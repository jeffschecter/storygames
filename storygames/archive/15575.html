    \n So that was a thing!

    Improv workshop, followed by three Fiasco sessions in two days. 18 games played:

    News channel Six, Transatlantic (x2), Dragon Slayers, Rainbow Mountain*, Nut People* (x2), The Ice (x2), Business Casual*, Manna Hotel, Poppleton mall*, La 1936, DC 1973, Objective: Zebra, The Zoo (x2), T-72.

    Endgame is the best store I have ever seen. It's hard to describe just how amazing the people and facility are.

    Mia, Karen and Matthew facilitated a gaming-focused improvisation workshop for around 30 people and it was excellent. I can't wait to hear more about participants thoughts and feelings on the techniques and format. I had a great time. how about you?

    *Unreleased

    \n So, I didn't attend the improv workshop, but I was there Sunday, and played two of the Fiasco sessions. They were only my third and fourth live Fiascos, and my first time playing with five players, and with three players. I had a blast. It was great seeing all the other playing styles, and great to see how established Fiasco veterans play the game. I liked walking away thinking that I had kept up.

    Interestingly enough, both sessions ended up using the softer aftermath table, which I thoroughly enjoyed.

    Thank you, Endgame, for hosting, and thank you Jason and Steve for helping to make it happen.

    Yours,

    Posted By: daw65Billy Zonkowski, former director of IT support for Pablum Inc., now CTO of Miyaki International, Inc.
    I loved what I heard about that game. I hope you'll see fit to do a Bite-Sized AP!

    I had two really fun games on Sunday, one using Manna Hotel, the other using Objective Zebra. Each had something to teach me. Our Manna Hotel game didn't bring in any of the Lynchian elements, and instead wound up being a crime drama set in a nowhere town, like the Coen Brothers in their darker moods. This was my third Manna game, and I was surprised to see how gracefully it became a genre-light, kitchen sink drama playset when none of the "Need: To gain respect... through the subtle exercise of mystic powers"-type elements were chosen.

    The Zebra game was unusual among my Fiasco games for letting me get into an actor mode. I usually don't get out from behind the camera when we play, preferring to script and direct more, but I got to play a cultist, and it's been a long time since I've gotten to do that. I was reminded that I kind of miss GMing Cthulhu, and may have to do that again sometime soon. Sorry if I got a little hammy, guys! Such are my instincts in these cases. Anyway, I think I'll make a concerted effort to try doing that more often, because it really was a blast.

    Posted By: Will HindmarchThat said, tell me more about how this went\u2014logistically, artistically, etc.\u2014including how players were organized into games.
    Player organization was handled in a pleasingly meta way - structured freeform, if you will. Someone said "Who's facilitating?" and hands went up, then players were told to find a table. The only larger structure was that slots started at 11 and 3. Maybe there was something in the air from Saturday?

    Of course, each person is going to have a different perspective on how things went artistically, but there were a lot of "what happened in my game" stories going on and people were actually listening, which is always a positive indicator! Some of the snippets I overheard, like Karen's lamentation about the baby giraffe was never going to know the joys of motherhood... or someone whose back was turned to me, in Jason's "Nut People" game, bitterly complaining that "Mother was never good enough for you, and no one else was either"... were pretty intriguing. And hey, any day where game-related chatter turns to "Crank Dat Pecan" and whooping crane masturbation (for science, so it was OK) is a good day. I learned a lot.

    EDIT to add - "Crank Dat Pecan" : pecan theft :: "How to Roll a Blunt" : rolling a blunt

    \n Hawkdogg represent.

    Endgame is a very community-minded store, both in the "gamers' sense and in the "Oakland" sense, so they set up Fiascocon as a food drive as well. people brought what looked like about 40 gallons of canned goods. The dubious incentive was that the top can-bringers got to play at my table, and it turns out that people who bring a lot of cans also tend to be excellent Fiasco players. I thought this worked well and would suggest wrapping in some tangible community benefit/competitive perk to this sort of event.

    The other sessions had facilitators randomly assigned, which worked fine. We juggled a little, splitting a couple of 5-person tables into more 3-person tables and so on. Everyone was very mellow and the general atmosphere was positive and energetic. Everyone I played with was great.

    Playing after a brain exploding improv workshop was fantastic. I felt more "on" and more excited than usual, having just had a physical and mental workout thanks to Mia, Karen and Matthew.

    The timing was perfect for both parts - a couple of three hour slots. We had half a dozen top-notch facilitators for each session and I could pick out players I could've grabbed to run a table if necessary, too.

    One Fiasco-y thing that took me by surprise - a lot of use of the soft tilt and aftermath. Not sure why, but two out of three of my games, and plenty I eavesdropped on, were using the soft tables.

    \n Here's what we actually did. Mia chose the exercises from a wider assortment.

    Outline of Workshop

    1. Very fast thanks and intro to the day, Fiasco, Endgame. 5 minutes.

    2. Exercises. 115 minutes.

    Introductions
    Sound ball/samurai chop (listening, warmup) (whole group)

    These games are demonstrated to the whole group and then participants break up into groups of ten:

    I'm a Tree (building and editing)
    Character game (collaboration and agreement)
    Fortunately/unfortunately (heightening, reincorporation, spontaneity)
    You Make Me Feel (listening, scenes, agreement)

    Building an environment (whole group)
    Slo-mo katana battle (whole group)

    3. Then lunch, 45 minutes

    4. Games, 175 minutes. Then people are divided up at tables, facilitators are randomly assigned, and everybody plays Fiasco fro three hours. After each session, an at-table debrief on how improv techniques were or were not incorporated into play (not everyone debriefed).

    5. Goodbyes, 5 minutes. At the end, everybody is gathered up for final remarks, thanks, announcements, etc.

    \n I think Chicago is a likely destination for this event. A local just needs to take the lead on organizing and publicizing. It needs:

    1. A location, probably a game store with lots of space, and a date a few months out.
    2. An improvisation instructor.
    3. Publicity and enthusiasm.
    4. A way for people to sign up and pay.
    5. Improv-savvy minions and facilitators, probably the same people, some of whom may be participants anyway.

    The instructor needs to get an honorarium and if you want me to come I need a plane ticket. Endgame was very generous in donating their space but other stores may not have the same kind heart.

    I'm sure Sean, Karen, Mia and Chris would be happy to offer advice as well!
