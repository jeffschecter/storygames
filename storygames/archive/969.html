System exchange.

Levi Kornelsen
July 2006 edited July 2006 in Story Games
On RPGnet, I see this stuff all the time - people converting games from one system to another.

And it, combined with the "game of mini-games" gave me a bizzare idea.

Imagine, for a moment, that you owned one of My Life With Master and Dogs In The Vineyard.

Imagine, in addition, that Vincent put out a little PDF, something like ten pages, that set up a situation in Dogs where a modified set of MLWM rules would be just the thing - a set that you could step a character from Dogs over to very, very easily. And imagine the reverse also existed - a follow-up to a MLWM game where the characters went out to pass judgement on other evil masters, tweking stats over to Dogs...

...I would want to buy the other game. Instantly.

This struck me as a concept worth noting.

    Your dogs / MLWM conversion cycle seems to border on the same ideas as a full course or curriculum of RPGs. As we design more focused RPGs, we can easily get to the idea that X RPG explores one set of ideas well, while Y RPG explores another. It's only a small step to realize that some of the first set of ideas could be a great primer for another way to encounter some in the second set.

    Grounding that, I find it very interesting with MLWM -> dogs, because you first explore abusive relationships, then you explore judging people morally. I suspect that there's quite a bit we can say from being abused to becoming an unchecked judge of others'.

    And certainly the possibilities do not end there.

    Mendel has a really good point...start thinking about not just what games you play, but the sequencing effects of playing them in a certain order, of treating a game not just as a mechanical entity that follows from a certain premise, but as a set of concepts that give us a perspective on other concepts. Design something with the intention of 'addressing' another game from within your premise...this already happens to some extent, but to open that box more fully...not just borrowing a cool mechanic, but treating that event as itself a statement of sorts.
