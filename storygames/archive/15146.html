\n We're planning on putting together theme based weekends for Play a New RPG month to expose local players to different gaming styles, with the hope they'll be interested and we can help them get started running a few sessions for their home games. I am looking for feedback on the classifications, and game selection. If anyone has any suggestions or recommendations please share them.

Week 1: New Perspectives (transitioning away from traditional games): FATE (probably a Star Wars themed game with pregens and fixed scenario)
Week 2: Succeed... Differently (looking at conflict vs. task resolution, and success with consequences): Lady Blackbird
Week 3: Let's be the Bad Guys (unusual roles, fixed outcomes, shared collaboration): My Life with Master
Week 4: Who Needs a GM? (looking at GMless games): Fiasco
Week 5: Dice, what Dice? (looking at alternatives to dice mechanics): Dread

    \n Ok, so.

    It depends on how you define task and conflict, now that I think of it. The way that Ron talks about task and conflict resolution, Rustbelt has both. The way that Vincent talks about it, it only has conflict resolution.

    The way it works is, for unopposed actions (termed "checks") that might conceivably fail and have a consequence for failure, you roll a d10, add it to an appropriate stat (one of Tough, Savvy, Grizzled, Slick, Cagey, Personable, Thorough, or Uncanny), and compare it to a target number. For opposed actions (termed "conflicts"), the people acting against each other compare their roll+stat.

    Whichever it is, if your roll isn't good enough, you must choose: Push, or Give. If you Give, you take your failure and like it. If you Push, you pay a Price and get to succeed anyway. In cases of "conflicts" where both intents could feasibly come true, if you Push then they both happen (the first guy 'cause he won the roll, you because you Pushed). However, when the intents are mutually exclusive, the other guy gets a chance to Push Back, in which case you go to Deadlock and there's one or more roll-offs (with more Prices paid each time you refuse to Give) until someone decides to back down because things are getting too heavy.

    Where it gets interesting is when you're performing a check and are in a conflict at the same time. There's a lot of permutations here, and things get very very ugly in a very Tarantino-esque way. But basically you only roll+stat once, and that's compared separately against the target number (in the "check") and the other roll+stat (in the "conflict"), and you can Give or Push separately and individually in both (with separate and individual Prices). Depending on the situation, Giving in the "conflict" may forfeit your "check," or vice versa, or it may not.

    (As for the Price, it can take many forms, ranging from injury to loss of important stuff to compromising changes in the situation. The GM names two or three Prices and the player chooses which one.)

    But, the thing is, those "conflicts" aren't conflicts, the way that Ron talks about Conflict Resolution. They're actually just tasks against another character -- it's all about character competence and performance in that moment, as to whether he can hit the dude or whatever. The actual Conflict resolves when one guy decides he's taken enough punishment and the other guy decides to stop kicking him, or they both decide to back off because things are getting too heavy and not going anywhere, etc. In other words, dice don't resolve conflicts; people do. But what happens is, all the stuff that's established via the Task Resolution -- what succeeded, what failed, and what Prices were paid -- is what informs the people in their course of resolving the Conflict as a whole. The Conflicts are basically resolved by negotiation, while the Tasks serve to establish one or another person as being in a better position to negotiate for what he wants. The process of the Task Resolution is so uncertain, messy, and thoroughly undignified that it casts a very interesting light on the very prospect of Conflict -- it's not uncommon in this game for someone to demand something, pull a gun, and get what he demanded, without dice coming into play: guns have consequences, and if you're not prepared to face those consequences, then don't go up against the gun. (On the other hand, if you are prepared to face those consequences, it's very easy to take the gun and turn the tables. 'Cause, y'know, if you're willing to take a bullet in order to take the gun, there's not much anyone can do to stop you.)

    Um, I hope that makes sense. Please ask questions if you need to.

    (By the way, it's very very easy to get Rustbelt out of postapocalypso. The book even tells you how and has three examples: a weird fantasy thing, a Guy Ritchie kinda thing, and a Firefly ripoff.)
