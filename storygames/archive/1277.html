    Quick off-the-top analysis:

    "Personality" and "When Something Happens" are linked. The reason the character will not ignore the thing that happens is their personality trait. Or, conversely, the reason they have that personality trait is because they need to not ignore the thing that happens. So that should really be a single step: "The thing that happens is that the protagonist's family is murdered. Ergo we know that the protagonist is vengeful," or "The protagonist is vengeful, so the thing that happens should be that her family is murdered."

    Likewise, "Profession" and "Opponent supplies adversity" need to match up with exactitude. The adversity should be the type of adversity that the profession of the character prepares them to address. That's also a single step: "The character is a princess, so the adversity should come in the form of power politics" or vice versa.

    Note how naturally it doesn't work when you desynchronize these things:

    A vengeful princess tries to get a date
    A nebbish superhero engineers the downfall of the Empire.

    Yes, you could make terrific stories that would be described by either of these, but it would be because you looked past the surface that you're describing to find the underpinnings that correlate (the vengeful princess is also a teenager ... so her getting a date is fit adversity ... and she's doing it to show her annoying older brother that he's not the only one who's growing up, so her vengeance makes it impossible for her to dodge the problem).

    Judson, Dave, I think you're breezing right past Tony's Basic Edition point and rushing onwards to the Advanced Edition special rules. If the princess is trying to get a date, then of course there's some reason that she needs or wants to get that date. That's what Tony's saying. That the princess-who-needs-a-date also has a personality trait of fill-in-the-blank provides fodder for exploration, but that comes second to the fact that the princess needs that date. The extra stuff is gravy.

    The reason why this is important is that tons and tons of folks have missed the first step -- that the princess needs a date -- and want to jump right into the dating game with a sharp-tongued / vengeful / whatever princess who doesn't need the date that the game is about. Which is the short road straight into dissonance and unsatisfying play. The impulse to make the character "interesting" must work on top of a foundation of the character being relevant. Otherwise, the character isn't ever going to be interesting.

    Your "character algebra" is an interesting way of framing a concept I've intuited for some time now. That you're raising it suggests that it's worth developing more explicitly.

    Specifically, a lot of the situation building that goes back and forth across a table amounts to constructing some kind of "character equation" and handing it back and forth until we don't have any variables left.

    Specific examples:

    In TSoY, I make a character with a Key of Unrequited Love. That's essentially a personality trait, with a connection to another character. I push my sheet across to the GM, who now has to work that Key into his situation. He is essentially solving for the goal (ish.)

    On the other hand, if I start out an Unknown Armies game with "We're playing a group of New Inquisition goons" I'm presenting a restriction on goal, which you'll then need to solve for a personality to match.

    I don't know if I like this better or worse than the 5 W's game, primarily because I find myself doing it fairly naturally anyway, and I think (to keep with the math metaphor) the range of both processes is a proper subset of the universe of possible characters and situations. If you need it though, probably best to know both tools.
