    \n For a serious answer, it depends, of course, on your definition of game.

    For Caillois' requirements - fun, separate, uncertain, non-productive, governed by rules, fictitious - it doesn't consistently meet all those requirements. It's fun, separate, and non-productive, except when it isn't. But it does little to generate uncertain outcomes. And it's mostly not fictitious or governed by rules (other than standard social rules which, if you focus on those, break down the "separate" factor).

    By Crawford's multiple terminologies, Facebook is a toy. It is an interactive plaything without goals.

    Most other definitions include a goal, which Facebook doesn't have.

    Facebook is a collection of multiple forms of communication that focus on networked sharing. And it's a place where people can play games.

    Is a coffee house a game? Cause Facebook is more like a coffee house. Except without coffee. But it can still be a place where you spend a lot of time and that causes you to lose sleep while producing little of value.

    \n I don't think it's a game.

    Most things have the potential to be approached playfully (that is, as non-real-pretend-real, fictitious, non-productive, with fiction-rules). "Everything is a game to you!"

    But there's a difference between being able to play with something, and that something being a game (you could say that you play the latter but play with the former).

    Facebook is not a game in the sense a hammer is not a game. You can transform the hammer into a toy with a thought and play with it. You can even play a game with it, but the hammer is not in itself a game. It's the hammerness of it, that makes it a hammer and which does not include gameness. For a game to be a game, it has to be designed as such (even if spontaneously).

    But that might also depend on one's specific experiences with facebook. Mine are very non-gamey. I have played with facebook, on occasion. But it's a tool for connecting and communicating in the sense a phone is. And unless you want to call a phone a game...

    Posted By: MatrixGamerBelow the picture is a statement "This is a game! Add to the story or challenge the last addition. If you challenge, flip a coin, heads you win. Say that in you post."
    \nThat's a game. Partly because you said it is, but it still would be if you didn't. It creates goals (though emergent goals are certainly very possible), it defines a space in which it exists (the comments section of that photo), it even has structured interaction (the rules for how to challenge another person's addition). It could maybe use the addition of a defined conclusion, but not having one doesn't make it not a game.

    But that doesn't make Facebook a game. Facebook is the place where the game takes place.

    My dining room table isn't a game, but it's a place where a game can sometimes take place. Dinner can also take place there. Or bill-paying. Or planning a vacation. Or sex. All kinds of things can happen there.

    For the owners of Facebook, Facebook itself is more of a game than it is to the users. They have a goal - they want to get people to spend time there, so they can sell adspace. To that end, they do stuff that will help them meet their goal. They try to make the site a consistently engaging experience so that it will keep drawing users back to it. There are certainly gamelike elements to that interaction.

    I think there are other websites that match a game model much more clearly, mostly because they have goals attached to them.

    Selling sites like ebay or etsy are kind of like a game - keep your star rating up so you don't lose potential customers, get people to link to your etsy designs on Facebook so more people will see them and potentially buy (or at least praise you for your awesomeness).

    Dating sites have a lot of gamelike aspects - OKCupid is super gamelike - you get to see how answering questions makes you more or less compatible with specific people and you get the opportunity to answer questions that they've answered, hopefully increasing your compatability rating if they interest you.

    I'd say that, overall, the "it's not for reals" element of gameness is the part that can be reduced the most and still have something recognizably gamelike. You can do things that are very much "for realsies" but use gamelike structures to help them be more engaging and successful. If I understand correctly, that's the main thrust of McGonigal's book. And I think she's on the right track, there. (Though my opinion is formed partially in ignorance - I haven't read her book. I'll add it to my short list.)

    \n Good question, Rob.

    I know people who have used OK Cupid as a game, but in different ways. One example is that every time you edit your profile, it gets put onto the stream on the main page. So, make an edit to a profile and if it gets a good response, then it's a good edit. If there's no response, edit that section again tomorrow until results are achieved.

    Faking it makes it more of a game than answering honestly. You should get matches either way. So answering dishonestly only works if you want certain results. Like playing a game, it's a strategy.

    There's no more strategy to answering honestly, hoping I'll get people who will like me, than picking an item on a menu, hoping to get something I would like to eat. Maybe a game argument could be made, but it's broadening the definition to uselessness for me.

    It's not a game for the same reason Facebook isn't. You could game the system, but that doesn't mean the system is a game.

    Things will get fuzzier as gamification spreads across the web and out into devices and interfaces beyond it. I don't really like the idea of taking things and asking if its a game using some definition agreed upon. If I define a dog as something with four legs, a back, furry, and bark, then someone could claim a wooden chair with rabbit fur armrests is a dog (obviously, I don't think this discussion is that ridiculous). Microsoft may add game elements to Office to make writing or presentation creating more compelling and motivating. I don't think that will turn Word or Powerpoint into games.

    We use words to define reality, but reality is not defined by words. For that reason, I like what Hans said. If something isn't marketed as a game, then it probably is not a game. If I make something and say it's supposed to be a game, then it probably is.

    You might try my "game" and tell me that it's a crappy game, and that could be because it doesn't fit the definitions in certain ways. You might instead tell me that it's actually a toy, or entertainment, and it's great at being one of those things. If I want it to be one of those things, I might call it done. If I want it to be a game, I will need to consider your input and determine if I went wrong somewhere.

    My point is, what I (the creator) claims it to be carries more weight in my book than definitions creates to describe these activities we humans carry out. FB and OKC aren't in danger of being mistaken for games. If Nathan hasn't asked the question most of us wouldn't even think twice about it. But most importantly, they were not created to be games. If tomorrow Zuckerberg announces they are rebranding FB as a game, then I will completely change my mind and say it is a game. I'll also have a few points on how it is a crappy game.

    \n Ok, so OK Cupid isn't completely 100% a game. But I wasn't trying to say it was. I was trying to say it had definite gamelike elements and that you can look at those gamelike elements as a way to analyze what works well about it. The fact that it works a lot like a game is a big part of the reason it's popular and successful.

    Facebook, on the other hand, is successful for different reasons. It does a few things really well. It provides a way for you to strengthen a connection you have just made to a friend of a friend whom you've just met, for example at a party. It provides a way for you to keep tabs on your network of friends by getting snippets of info on what they are doing when they decide to post such things. It provides an excellent platform for organizing events that occur within an established network community. And it provides an application development platform that allows application developers to leverage those network connections as part of the application (ie. visiting your firends' farms in FarmVille).

    It does those things really well. But those things aren't inherently gamelike, except for the game applications, which are a separate entity existing within the Facebook framework.

    I think you're getting stuck on putting things completely in the "it's a game" bucket. Obviously Facebook and OKCupid and Wikipedia are not 100% games.

    But I think McGonigal is right to analyze the gamelike structures of Wikipedia and say that those elements contribute to it's success.

    And I think that OKCupid lends itself to similar analysis.

    And I think that Facebook, when looked at that way, comes up with a pretty resounding "no".

    And I think the reason for that revolves around goals. Wikipedia and OKCupid both have goals that the users are participating in (for Wikipedia, I'm referring to the article editors, not the readers). And they both have gamelike feedback elements that encourage participation directed toward those goals.

    Facebook, on the other hand, is much more goal-agnostic. The providers have goals - get people to spend time there so we can sell more ads. Some of the users have goals because they are using the site for goal-oriented purposes, like marketing or recruiting. But the primary users don't have goals that are addressed in a gamelike way.

    I mean, I guess there's a tiny bit. If you want attention from your friends, you need to post often, so your posts will be at the top of their feeds and they'll see them.

    But I guess I see the more obvious inherent goal of accessing the site as "checking in on your friends to see if any of them have posted anything interesting" rather than "get attention".

    If you're an attention whore, I suppose it's more gamelike. I'm about to defriend one of those, whose been driving me crazy with post after post after post about nothing interesting. So I guess she looses (if I'm selfish enough to think she cares whether I read and enjoy her posts, I suppose).
