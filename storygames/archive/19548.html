\n I just finished my first session of The Quiet Year. It sucked.

I believe the system is to blame here. It seems designed to prevent roleplaying fun. Some specifics:

- The setting-building phase is very short and results in very vaguely defined settings. This makes it hard to connect with people and events, since they have no grounding or context.
- Players don't represent anything in particular, such as characters or factions. It's hard to care about a decision when you aren't on any particular side of it.
- The Czege principle is thoroughly violated: the player who initiates a project always decides how it resolves. This feels arbitrary and unsatisfying.
- The mechanics have no bite:
--- Scarcities are barely felt during play, and there are no conseqences for failing to alleviate them.
--- Contempt tokens have no effect on anything, including other players' reactions. This is mostly due to the second point above -- it's hard to care about another player's contempt when they clearly don't care all that much themselves.
--- There are no consequences for ignoring a threat. Indeed, ignoring a threat nullifies it.
- The rules limiting discussion make the game sterile and boring. The social aspect of story games is crippled. Players spend most of the game in enforced silence, twiddling their fingers.
- The cards encourage the creation of multiple unrelated storylines. This makes the story feel disjointed and shallow.
- The cards encourage creating characters, but the framework of the game prevents those characters from having any depth or any story arc.
- Projects are "fire-and-forget": players have to stop thinking about a project just as it becomes interesting, and pick it up again when everyone has forgotten about it. This prevents anything dramatic from happening.
- The game is designed to end with an unsatisfying anticlimax.
- The Frost Shephards are left so vague that the ending is totally abstract. "The Frost Shephards arrive. The game is over." Well, what just happened? There's no answer.

It's not a good game, is what I'm saying.

So... am I missing something? Were we doing it wrong? Does anybody want to defend The Quiet Year?

    \n As a person who was initially skeptically about the game but has come to love it, I gotta say that what you're reporting is what I imagined the game would play like.     Player 2: "Among the grumbling Elders, an old man stands up. 'Absolutely not! Let the past stay buried!'"
    Player 1: (still taking the POV of the Youth Brigade) *grabs a Contempt token*
    Player 3: "The Priests speak in unison: 'There may be relics there of our lost Ancestors. We agree with the Youth.'"
    Player 2: (still taking the POV of the Elders) *considers a Contempt token, hesitates, and decides that the Elders respect the Priests and will follow their lead. Puts the token back down*

    Something like that. Each player says what faction they're speaking for and then speaks for them. That's how we do it and it works for us.

    \n I think that a lot of the fun is of the "filling in the blanks" variety: you take these vague elements like "a forest with young spindly trees" and "a young boy digs in the ground and finds something" and use them in a way that surprises/delights/horrifies everyone else at the table. In a good game session, people keep picking up and re-incorporating the details everyone else has been inventing, which provides the glue between the previously established events and the new things. If you think it's fun to throw out a crazy idea and see what other people do with it, and to take someone else's crazy idea and see what you can do with that, you can have a great time with The Quiet Year.

    But yeah, there's not a lot of roleplaying in it. It's not really about creativity with characters and plot and situations, it's about creativity with setting and themes and brainstorming. And it plays fast, and produces an interesting artifact at the end (the map), which make it a fun pickup game to have on hand.

    (Personally, I enjoyed it more than card games like My Dark Overlord and Once Upon A Time, or RPGs like Primetime Adventures or Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple. Maybe you could take a peek at those to see if they land closer to your sweet spot?)

    \n It sounds like your group is fairly committed to a general notion of "ownership" analogous to the "each player controls his/her character" expectations of many traditional games. It also sounds like your play practices tend to favor procedures that control thematic/fictional elements rather than methods of communication and representation.

    As others have indicated, this approach may well cause TQY to fall flat for your group. If you invest even harder into this paradigm, you're not likely to find any more success. This just might not be a game that supports the style of play that you are looking for.

    In other words: be wary of universalizing your own experience. Neither you (and your group) nor the game are to "blame"--there's just a bad fit between the gaming experience you want and the gaming experience that TQY provides.

    \n I don't even know where to start, really. You keep saying the game stops you from doing things you want to do, but your only evidence is that it failed to force you to do them. If you want to create a coherent community where events build on each other and projects have meaning, you have to actually try to do that? If you want to feel like there are characters being developed and expressed throughout the game, you have to actually try to do that too. The thing about scarcities not mattering seems to be the pinnacle of the failure described throughout your experience: since the game didn't force you to do something, you decided it was impossible to do it, when in fact it was trivial to do so given the tools at hand.

    I mean, you have to care about what you're doing. You have to have more thoughts about what is going on than you actually express through play. The Quiet Year is a game where most of the play happens in your head because there are limits on your means of expression, and it makes specific use of that tension. If you are used to always being able to talk about everything you are thinking about a game simultaneous to playing that game, it is definitely an adjustment to suddenly not be able to do that. But it doesn't mean you stop thinking about the game, and it doesn't mean the things you don't express aren't true or real or meant to affect play. They are -- that's the whole point, that most of the inputs into decisions in a community are not known to the other members of that community. That this creates interesting/frustrating results on how that community views itself. That it creates alienation of individuals from their community.

    The only difference between the conversation Scrape portrayed and the rules as written is that the players speak out loud the name of the group they are representing. By the rules, the person or group represented is implicit in what they are saying; that person or group is the impetus behind the words, but they are not identified. If you decide that the only person or group you are going to represent is an analogue of yourself, inserted into the community, that works fine; if you decide you are going to represent a variety of viewpoints based on what you have imagined in your head about the community, that is also fine.

    Wanting these groups to be not only explicitly named, but named in advance, is normal enough (and there were named Factions in early playtests of the game), but it is a decision that the designer has specifically avoided. But if 'the game doesn't make us name factions in advance' translates into 'therefore there are no meaningful factions' then you are in trouble, and will probably struggle to play the game. When I play the Quiet Year I spend a lot of time thinking about what factions might exist, and how they might feel about what is going on; I don't worry about whether or not they have a name, or are explicitly acknowledged in discussions. When those groups that exist in my head want to say something, or start a project, then I do that. The other players are presumably doing the same thing. Usually consensus emerges about what sort of groups exist, and what sort of opinions they have, but that consensus is volatile and implicit and changes over the course of the game. In much the same way, I would suggest, that actual social groups and movements in a community change over time.

    I mean, the cool kids in my high school never sat down and wrote up a constitution, but their consensus opinions and influence were frequently expressed; they also changed dramatically over time, as the membership changed, or changed their opinions, or whatever. They didn't precede everything they said with 'as a cool kid, I think X' but we still knew which things they said were and were not part of that social construct. The cool kids didn't fail to exist, just because we never named them and wrote up a chart of their membership.

    \n If you want it, here are some ways to get conflict in TQY using the rules:
    Discover a threat outside the community
    Discover a threat within the community: a mystery, a crime, a Bad Thing
