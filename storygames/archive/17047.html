\n Guess what game is it ?



the complete article is here (in french: http://www.narrativiste.eu/2012/projet-pictoversel/). This is just an exercice de style.

What we have in mind is a complete (CC) -formalized- pictographic system for all games, including storygames, usefull for internationalization and general ergonomy. The purpose is NOT to "write" the rules of the games, altought pictograms could be used for memorization

Check also: http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/17016/rhetological-fallacies-what-a-game-it-could-be-#Item_1

    Adding a pictogram along with actual text can be really powerful, though. It can help explain things, especially for visual learners. (Different people learn in different ways, and straight text from a book won't work for all potential players.) It can help you visualize and remember how rules fit together. It can make it easier to flip through a book searching for the rule you need ("I remember it had a big blue arrow near the top of the page..."). And once the players have learned the rules and the pictographic representation, then you can have reminder sheets with few or no words on them.

    It must be one of those optional roles that people are always adding to the game. I can't quite figure it out, though.
    They are actual villager role cards in Miller's; they were invented by the Princeton crew decades ago for Mafia (though with different names).

    IIRC, Looney Labs' main designer Andy didn't like the add-on roles--he felt it spoiled the purity of the villagers, mafia, angel tryptic. So they weren't put into IANAW.

    ----
    I'll add to Nick's point: check out how Jason used pictograms in the Fiasco rules and on its playmat. You'd probably not be able to play from the pic/playmat... but you NEVER forget to "give away in Act I, keep in Act II" when you see it (something I forgot to do a lot, first few times around).

    And the "page with big, blue arrow on it" is an example of a very common graphic design trick to aide scannability of a text: avoid having any two pages with identical "blocking" (the placement of sidebars, images, headings, and text blocks that provide an easy to memorize "snapshot" of each page).

    \n I had to think pretty hard. I didn't get it until the last few rows.

    My thought process was: "Last row: Monsters greater than or equal to 1, and people greater than or equal to two... what? Previous row: Monsters greater than or equal to people... oh, monsters win. Row above that: Monsters equal zero... then... people win. Last row again... then time symbol? Could this be werewolf? There's 'don't look' and 'don't speak' symbols... sunrise, a person is dead, a person is removed from the game. Yes, it's werewolf."

    That was from having to figure out what the pictograms meant on the fly; I can easily see the RPG industry coming up with some standard pictograms to represent common concepts like rolling a d20 or 3d6, character sheets, damage, character points, and so forth.
