    \n Yes, absolutely.

    There are a few things that I'm trying to fix about Ganakagok. The basic bones of the game are sound, particularly the tarot-like deck that drives events in play, but there are game-mechanical and game text-related issues that I want to address before I'll feel okay about putting it out there again.

    The game-mechanical issues relate mainly to the economy of gifts and burdens that are the main character resource in the game. I think I've got a handle on that, but I need to do some more playtesting to make sure. I've also discovered that I need to make a decision about whether the game (a) needs explicit stake-setting, (b) doesn't need explicit stake-setting, or (c) can be played either way so long as everybody at the table agrees that that's how to do it. I don't think the answer is (c), by the way, but I'm not sure.

    The textual issues have to do with both form and content. Nathan Paoletta has convinced me that the current layout of the book is both ugly and ineffective from a readability standpoint. Also, the rules explanations don't really do the game justice: I want to re-write the rules in a way that bottles the awesome that happens at the table. And I think I owe it to the topic to make more explicit reference to Inuit culture and mythology as the source material for the game.

    Additionally, I'm looking into ways of producing the Ganakagok tarot in more than hand-crafted quantities (i.e., a dozen or so decks at a time, copied onto card stock and cut by hand), but I foresee difficulties.

    I'm going to playtest changes in the game through the summer, probably, and then finish updating the rules and have a completed text by the end of the summer. Laying out the revised text and putting it into print will hopefully be done by the end of next year, if I keep up my end.

    So that's where things stand. I appreciate you asking about it. I love Ganakagok, and when it's firing on all cylinders it produces a play experience that's literally fabulous. The people who play it really dig it when it works and see its potential even when it doesn't.
