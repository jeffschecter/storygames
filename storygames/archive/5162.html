\n This thread is meant to help me/us think about excellence in gaming book products. What makes a role-playing book excellent?

What I'd like you to do is name three gaming books - no more! - that, for you, in some way or other, are excellent. Not in the Bill and Ted meaning of "excellent", but meaning "of the highest quality".

Define for us, and for yourself, what fields the books excel in. Perhaps you think book X has the best editing ever seen in a gaming product. Or book Y has the most evocative art.

Here's an example of the type of posts I'd like to see:

Book A. The system has been polished to a gleam, and years of testing have shown that it can handle the most extreme cases.
Book B. The artwork, the page design, the typography - they all form a unified whole that takes the book to a higher level.
Book C. The author's voice, witty and irreverent, speaks directly to the reader in a way no other gaming text I've ever seen.

    \nSkyrealms of Jorune for its art. The artwork is unlike anything I've ever seen in other games. The mixture between classical references and alien life-forms, the anatomical detail, the textures all help actually bringing the vision to life. The artist Miles Teves said a while back that he'd never be able to draw like that again: The technique he used was apparently a great strain on his hands, and his work on Jorune was very much a labor of love.

    Ars Magica, 2. edition. Brevity balanced with completeness, rules innovation balanced with rules practicality, myth balanced with history; this book is lean and strong and ready to run when you touch it. I find it hard to define the field of excellence - balance of elements, perhaps? Concise completeness?

    Prime Time Adventures for its play procedures. With extreme sanity, the book gives simple and clear instructions for procedures that provide good, supportive, creative group play. Everything is transparent. With very few rules, the game transforms the gaming group's behavior in unforeseen ways.

    \nDogs in the Vineyard for its author voice. It truly engages the reader in a pseudo-dialogue (psuedo, since it's still one-way communication) and engages serious thought about what's said. In some ways, it takes the fact that game knowledge is passed on often orally rather than written (as in con games or teaching your friends who don't read the book) and uses that as a strength rather than a weakness.

    Don't Rest Your Head is excellence in design incarnate, and I'm not just blowing smoke up Fred's ass here. Combining player-empowerment, a consequence axis separate from success and failure, and very light GM responsibilities has left it feeling like the RPG equivalent of a car that has been stripped of everything unnecessarily to make it faster and more efficient.

    Polaris has some of the most beautiful imagery of any game I've read, next to perhaps Nobilis. When someone says games can't be Art, I hold Polaris (and, yes, Nobilis, but I'm only suppose to choose three here) as the counter-example -- not just as a game or as art, but as a game that is bettered rather than hindered because of that.

    \nNobilis 2nd Ed., for production values and presentation that feels very un-RPG-like. Lovely, understated cover art. Generous white space. An excellent typographical eye that leads one to clearly understand what they're about to read and how to navigate the work.

    D&D3.0 core books, also for production values and presentation, except that it feels like the very definition of an RPG production (as it should!). Rich, dense, evocative art, excellent organization, the whole thing feels like it's worth every penny.

    Burning Empires, both for production values (approaching D&D3's level of page-by-page richness, except for the binding on early editions) as well as the sheer scope (audacity?) of vision within the rules. BE's ruleset never saw a risk it didn't want to take.

    Based on the above, I'm obviously a sucker for production values. Unfortunately, on the rules/play front I'm still scratching my head trying to come up with examples of true excellence -- what I would define as, rules that produce the intended play experience with minimum ambiguity and maximum consistency.

    \nMutants & Masterminds (1e) - Top of my list for production values both as a thing of beauty and model of function. The look of that book is absolutely perfect. (The art for 2e declined dramatically.)

    Arcana Evolved - The tightest, most polished alternate rule system for D&D ever published. It strived to create a different take to reinvigorate the game and did just that. It's refreshingly novel, and the rules hold together amazingly well. No significant errata of any kind. (Compare to, say, Iron Heroes's magic system.)

    Ptolus - Like M&M, the thing is a masterwork of form and function. The book is like 1000 pages long, filled with gorgeous illustrations, and eminently usable. Details of note: three cloth ribbons of different colors, color-coded sections, annotations and cross-references called out in the text.

    \nBurning Empires - Absolutely top-notch production quality; powerful setting/situation generator to drive play directly to what most interests the group.

    \nDogs in the Vineyard: I'll put in a vote for author voice as well. Everything that Ryan said, plus I'll add that the text really feels (inasmuch as it can given I've never met the guy) like Vincent's having a couple of beers with you and explaining the whole thing.

    Shadow of Yesterday: Beautifully crystal-clear text for how-to-play information. The game goes step by step and explains itself all along the way, giving you all the tools you need to use the game for maximum awesome. The writing is cheerful, reassuring and uncluttered. Also, the setting info is full of evocative flavor, really conjuring the feel of the World of Near and getting you (well, me anyway) jazzed about playing in the world without going overboard. Every bit of flavor text-especially the Zaru introduction that ends "I would break the world"--just fills me with a burning desire to read them aloud to every gamer I know.

    Oh, and the actual game rules rock too.

    Paranoia: I think it was 1st or 2nd Edition I read, but never owned, alas! Anyway, this game is the absolute King (I can only hope that recent editions are continuing the tradition) of conveying the tone and color of a game through its text, teaching the mindset for play by simply staying in character (the miniseries by Adventure Comics is great for this too). Stuff like the blurb deep in the restricted GM section where the computer chimes in, "Enjoying the read, citizen? Good, gooood. REPORT FOR TERMINATION!!" helped me grok what the game is "about," and gave me a great desire to play, but a satisfying experience just reading, too. Uh, not that I would know, friend citizen.     \nPolaris, for physical product as well as solid writing. The single blue page at the very front of the book, the paper quality, the illustrations, the layout all contribute to a simple elegance. The Moments Frozen in Time is a brilliant way of promising something. The rest of the book delivers it. I love Ben's writing voice (which differs from his internet preaching voice, which I very much dislike).

    Dogs in the Vineyard, for clarity of writing. The book's writing style confused me and broke me at first. At once point Vincent says something to the tune of "I don't know, I'm just making stuff up!" My mind reeled as I realized that he was presenting things in a conversational, straight-forward, human way. It felt like a conversation and a form of real communication. The Initiations is a simple way of introducing a fairly complicated die system (because the base mechanics ARE fairly complex, when you imagine them as written by someone else). Dogs is simple, concise, compact, and inviting. And yet it's got such a wide scope and range of possibility.

    Covenant, for the character sheet as well as Conventions/Motifs. The character sheet has a relationship map in the middle. The entire page is designed so that things move outward from the relationship map, so that you can pick any random NPC and see how they tie into the world and the mechanics. There's also rules summary right there, meaning you don't need to look anything up. The sheet is brilliant. The other brilliant bit is Conventions/Motifs, which immediately brings the setting into the game and into the mechanics, as well as ensuring that the things which are recurring are recurring because the players WANT them.
