    \n J Walt speaks truth to graphic power.

    None of those books is going to teach you anything useful until you try to make a page of type and images into something readable.

    Your homework, my dear boy, is to find an example of said words and images that you find compelling -- a book, a graphic novel, a CD insert, a band flyer -- and try to recreate it perfectly (in InDesign, preferably). That exercise alone will teach a lot about colors, typography and design.

    \n
    \n
    \n

    That's a good plan, Luke.

    \n

    My recommendation is usually this:

    \n

    Lay out a page of your real, actual project, then lay out ten pages. By the time you get to 10, you'll have discovered all sorts of stuff you didn't know you needed to know. You'll keep discovering them as you go, but you'll have discovered 75% of it by 50 pages in. By the end of your next book, you'll have 90%. A few more books, and there will be the odd technique that you avoid thinking about because you don't understand it.

    \n

    The reason I suggest starting with a real text you care about is so that you'll see the flaws as real flaws. You'll have actual weird spaces, real orphans and widows, and will really have to figure out how to bridge chapters.

    \n

    Also, Bringhurst is yummy and a good page design primer.

    \n

    Technically, the things to get down first are master pages and styles. Otherwise, you'll dig yourself into a miserable pit of miserable misery and you'll hate the world. And you won't find yourself dug in for 20 or 30 hours.

    \n
    \n As another specific recommendation for "learn by doing", try making a character sheet for a game you like. Doing this (and more often trying unsuccessfully) has taught me a lot about how hard it is to strike the right balance between easy to reference and looking good. Cheating off of John Harper's sheets is a good shortcut.

    Seriously, though, I did a viking hack for Agon that included a character sheet, and I basically just copied every element of his excellent character sheet, but in a more rustic Viking-y idiom, and moved some things around, but even such relatively minor tweaks with the training wheels on forced me to learn things about the program I was using, spacing, legibility, et cetera.

    Oh, and find lots of fonts you like I find that typing up a prominent piece of text, like the title, and then cycling through lots of fonts can help to crystallize the aesthetic I'm shooting for in my mind's eye.

    \n yeah learn by doing, then read some stuff you will get more from it.
    \n
    \nif you want to expand your horizons i suggest "making comics" and "understanding comics" by Scott McCloud http://www.scottmccloud.com/2-print/index.html these will help you know quite a bit about how to present both words and images on the same page and will give you a basic arts vocabulary so you can better describe what you want to potential artist.
    \n
    \nI learned layout from comic books, photography and magazine adds
    \n
    \nIt's really difficult for me to separate the task of writing and how those ideas are presented with design, so much so that I have to thumbnail (quick ruff sketch) each page before I can actually write it. I cant know what my text will be unless I have thought about what diagrams, images, or context the text will be presented in. you can save a whole lot of words by simply putting information in the right place.
    \n
    \nNot saying any of this is positive or that it is a process worth emulation. it probably comes from my weaknesses as a writer.
