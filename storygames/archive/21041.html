    \n I wrote up a sketch of how this could be done with a combination of Sorcerer and Dungeon world concepts, but what I ended up with is not ultimately feasible for a structural reason that I'll lay out - might be educational for somebody:

    If you make it so that the players have a genuine choice about their defense in D&D, then that implies that they can gain some advantage by not defending, and that advantage is significant enough that they might really want to have it, too. The problem with this is that the value of the defense is variable: it depends on how likely the opponent is to hit, which is a function of your AC and the enemy's attack bonus. This in turn implies that if characters can trade their defense for doing something else instead, then the characters with high AC will want to defend (more than others, I mean), while those with low AC will want to trade out their less effective defensive actions for whatever other advantage you might be offering. At this point we're basically in min-max territory: if you're planning to not defend in combat, you should sell off your AC - you won't need it if you're not going to defend! And that's probably going to end up in a place that won't work in any sensible way any longer - glass cannon characters become an even more intractable issue.

    I "fix" this issue in my homebrew D&D by having both a fixed AC and active defense rolls, which the players can then balance in various situations in different, complex ways, often involving trade-offs of stuff like HP, initiative, attack and defense. That scheme's not relevant to other D&D versions, though.

    For 5th edition I don't think that there is an elegant way to achieve defense rolls with genuine choice without rebuilding the system from the ground up. No reason to not have the defense rolls themselves, but allowing characters to sell them for other advantages seems logically impossible within the framework.

    Anyway, I think fundamentally, you've got a bit of a disconnect here - taking damage isn't a decision/player action, it's a consequence. Your die roll is only there to see if the consequence happens or not, and how firmly. The "What do you do?" part, to me, happens on the player's turn. D&D doesn't really give you options for avoiding this without adding some sort of like, "reaction" and that involves in this case, building an entire subsystem like Eero suggests. I've heard people talk about giving PCs a "reaction" type action on saving thows - it works like:

    GM: "Joana, Thommen, you're advancing down the hall when you step on something and hear a 'click'. You've got a split second to react, what do you?"
    J: "I throw myself forward and tumble out of the way!"
    T: "I brace myself and ready my shield."

    And then, depending on what sort of something we're dealing with, Joana might roll a dex save, and Thommen might roll a con or strength save, but it depends on what the something is - if it's a pit trap, Joana gets a dex save, while Thommen just falls in the pit. If it's a bunch of rocks falling from the ceiling all along the corridor, Thommen might get that Str/Con save to block them with his shield while Joana gets no roll because she hasn't gotten out of the way of the rocks.

    I can't think of a good way to implement this for combat though. D&D just fundamentally doesn't work that way. =/

    \n Your example works, Paul, because you're still making the defense roll in each case, so the AC remains relevant. I would still be careful about the "I grit my teeth" option, because that's where the AC dumping comes into play if it does: a player chooses to forego defense both in their character build and in their tactics, and instead optimizes their damage output (or fleeing capacity, whatever), leading to more lop-sided character concerns. The double damage is harsh in this particular example, which I like; it makes that option more of something you'd do with a very good AC rather than something you'd choose because you feel you're going to fail the defense anyway.

    The reason why I personally forwent this strategy when thinking about this yesterday, however, is that now you're extending the length of each combat action to double size: instead of the attacker choosing to attack and [somebody] rolling to resolve the attack, you're having the attacker choose their attack, the defender choose their defense, and only then resolving the event. This means that each combat includes double the amount of decision-making points as before. Is this really a viable strategic direction to take in hacking the system?

    (This problem obviously is not a problem for Sandra's original conceit of having PCs roll defense rolls instead of monster attack rolls - that alone does not add steps, it just moves the dice-rolling around.)

    Having abandoned the idea that every defender gets a little "defender turn" every time they are attacked, I determined that if I want to keep the amount of decision-making roughly similar to the standard rules, the "defensive choices" need to essentially occur as part of or instead of normal character actions. Here I got stuck with the paradox I mentioned earlier: if we couple a character's actions with their defense (instead of having characters automatically defend as in orthodox D&D), then AC becomes a much more volatile resource, capable of being dump-statted, which may be undesirable for many purposes.

    For the sake of the exercise, though, here's how I would do this if I had to. This starts getting pretty far away from 5th edition D&D, though:

    Monsters always act first during a combat round, there's a "monster phase" during which the GM challenges the players with the threatening monsters. When a monster attacks a character it "sticks on" to the character, engaging them, but does not inflict their attack effects yet. If a monster is still engaging a character at the end of a combat round, they automatically deal their damage to the target. Essentially, there are two monster phases in each combat round, the "monster move phase" and the "monster damage phase", with the PC action turns sandwiched in between.

    PCs, on the other hand, can "shut down" monsters by successfully attacking them; a character can retreat from engagement with a shut down monster, avoiding their attack for this round. Only monsters that have not been shaken away and shut down in this way actually get to inflict their damage at the end of the round.

    Furthermore, a PC can choose to "dodge", making a defense roll to avoid all monsters stuck to them this round at the expense of doing anything useful. This works by rolling defense against every monster sticking to you individually, in the order you choose (or an order suggested by the fictional fight choreography, whatever); you get to continue dodging as long as you succeed, but if you miss on one monster, that's it - the rest are still stuck on you.

    If a character succeeds in dodging every monster assaulting them, they get to "riposte", taking a normal attack on top of their dodging. This is a reward for the character who's AC is not being completely overwhelmed by the enemy.

    What this system achieves is that monsters do not cause attack sequences at all during the combat round, which should help keep the handling time down. Players get the same amount of decision points they do in the orthodox rules, except now they need to make a stark choice between defense and attack. Monsters feel much more like an environmental puzzle than active characters that the GM is running against you. The tactical value of AC, and the character build logic, still goes completely haywire, but it does it in an interesting way that I feel is not necessarily thoroughly unbalanced.

    (If you're wondering about the DPS of the combat participants, I don't think it goes too weird here. We're just essentially saying that if you're fighting a monster and miss with your attack, the monster damages you instead. Somebody always gets damaged, either you or your opponent. Same as Dungeon World, roughly.)

    \n Take a look at The Black Hack ($2 on DTRPG) for an example of a 15-page D&D rewrite that has no GM rolls except for damage.

    It handles it with a single unified mechanic (roll under stat, e.g. STR, DEX) with two tweaks:

    1. Advantage and Disadvantage for situational modifiers.

    2. Add to your d20 rolls the difference between Hit Dice when dealing with foes more powerful than you.

    You roll to hit monsters by rolling under your STR or DEX (modified by HD difference if they're more powerful).

    All of this would not be easy to do with 5e without a major retool, granted.

    \n A very simple way of moving combat dice rolls from the GM to the players is just to get the player being attacked to roll on behalf of the monster. "The goblin swings her mace at you, whistling toothily to herself. Go ahead and roll her attack for me at +4. It hits? That's 1d6+2 slashing damage as the mace bounces off your skull, mate." This means you don't need to invent and balance a replacement for the AC subsystem; you don't even have to explain anything, as it's just the rules as is but with a different person executing them.

    This doesn't particularly achieve your other goal of encouraging off-menu choices, but I don't think changing AC to a Defence Saving Throw achieves this either. Saving Throws are obligatory: both within the D&D rules and within the fiction, because obviously players will always choose to defend themselves from being attacked so long as that action is free.

    In the rules as is, monsters follow exactly the same rules as characters on their turns. After introducing Defence Saves - and assuming that players are still using Attack actions to meet AC instead of the monsters rolling Defence Saves against the players on the players' turns - I would be worried that the monsters will stop feeling like independent entities and more like static obstacles.

    Obviously NPCs and monsters *are* second-class citizens in a D&D game, but one of the distinguishing features of D&D (for me) is its fundamental conception of the world and creatures in the game as pre-existing, independent from the players and their actions, although they might be affected by them. They don't just exist to serve the players' story. Dungeon World makes a (well deserved) attack on that core idea, but I think many of those drawn to D&D enjoy exploring a pre-existing world and its denizens - it's a sort-of proactive non-creativity.

    In the 5E rules as is, PCs are supposed to respond to being attacked by choosing an appropriate action when the initiative order comes back to them. I find my players will generally remember who has attacked them and aim to take revenge on that monster during their next turn. But even though I use a variety of techniques to keep combat fast moving (e.g. PCs lose their turns if they don't declare an action within six seconds) there is still quite a big table-time gap between between the monster attack and the player action, which in-fiction are theoretically instantaneous. Now I come to think about it this has a *really* unnatural feeling to it. I've always just accepted this as a consequence of the rounds/turn system...

    (I know I'm over-explaining D&D rules, but writing them out is part of my thinking process and I've just left them in to help people who don't know D&D follow.)

    I can think of a number of exceptions to this delaying effect, most of which are reactions (in the technical sense of an extra action you can take once per round, interrupting someone else's turn):

    1) The Rogue has Uncanny Dodge staring at 5th level, which means they can burn their reaction to halve the damage of a hit against them. (Rangers can also gain this at 15th level.)

    2) From 3rd level, the Monk can catch or deflect missiles as a reaction. (I've seen lots of RPing milage wrung from this feature.)

    3) Fighters and Rangers have various optional defence-related features or manoeuvres, which mostly boil down to:
    3a) use your reaction to reduce damage
    3b) use your reaction to make an extra attack if an opponent misses you
    3c) increase AC in certain circumstances

    4) Barbarians' AC changes when they are raging. They can also choose to attack with advantage in exchange for giving anyone who attacks them advantage until their next turn.

    5) Spellcasters have recourse to a number of warding/protection/shielding/AC increasing spells. Most of these aren't cast as reactions (Shield being a notable exception) but they do generally require the player to pay attention to monster actions in order to make sure the effects are applied. The Dodge action has a similar effect, in that it burns the PC's turn in exchange for an effectively passive defensive bonus.

    Perhaps the reaction mechanic is the key to closing the attack-defence/revenge gap in round-based combat?

    The simplest iteration of this idea goes, "The goblin swings her sword down on you. You're about to be hit for 5 slashing damage... unless you want to take a reaction?" This phrasing implies a) that the PC has the option to take an action right now b) that there is real potential to avoid this negative consequence and c) specifies that the mechanical cost of trying this is that you've used your reaction slot for the round so can't take an opportunity attack etc.

    If the players don't have a relevant reaction listed on their character sheet, then their only option is to go off menu - and we've given them an incentive to do this. "I stoop down and throw some sand up into the goblin's face."

    "Great, I'll reroll her attack as you've imposed disadvantage." So effectively, we've allowed the PC to use the Dodge action in the reaction slot. "The goblin's sword misses you entirely as she spits sand out of her mouth."

    Off menu damage reduction or riposting is harder to adjudicate, as we don't want to invalidate any defensive character features that people might have chosen. I suppose with player agreement it would be fine: you could declare that Riposte (make a return attack) and Parry (reduce damage by 1d8+Dex) are defensive reactions available to everyone from the off, and let people select their character's features accordingly.

    I'm intrigued by encounter checks. Does that work like a random encounter table or is it more of an in-fiction thing?
    \nYeah, here is how it works:

    You know how in D&D, many dungeons and wild places have a location-specific rule that you, every X time span, roll a dY and if you get a Z result, then you got to the random encounter table and roll on it with some die or combination of die types (Curse of Strahd uses a d8+d12).

    In my game from the beginning I made this a very explicit, overt mechanic, making a big show of making these rolls. It worked really well but after a while, I switched it to putting these rolls in the players hands, and that was even better. (I still have the table itself behind the screen but we talk explicitly about some rolls that have already come up "Is 12 the wolves?" "No, the wolves are on 13".)

    What I also do is if Z is high, I always switch it to low. I want the players to want to roll high. And if dY isn't a d20, sometimes I change it to that. Like if it's "1/6" I might go to having the party roll a d20, on a 5+ there's no encounter. I don't always change it to a d20 but I do always make it so that it's "roll high to avoid encounter". I'm ok with the frequency being low because we make a lot of these checks, sometimes rolling handfuls of dice for a long journey or stressful hours in a fortified corner.

    And I'm open about the time span, which varies from place to place, and the numbers involved. "OK, now that you've left the road, the numbers change to..."

    So it's encounter table based more than in-fiction based.

    Eero_Tuovinen said:

    That's my position in a nutshell: having defensive decision-making points is something that one can do in D&D, but you can only have two of these three virtues then:
    * The game's still similar to RAW.
    * The game's still elegant to play.
    * The game's still not trivially unbalanced.

    Now fly, clever designers, and prove me wrong

    \nCool! I gave it a shot. First session is May 12th but probably there won't be any fights then.
    So we'll see later. ABT\u2014always be testing.

    Three small changes from RAW
    1. I changed it so you can perform the dodge action, an action which already existed in the RAW, "in advance", "in response" to being attacked. That's the only "defensive option" I added. But then that costs you your turn for that round, basically you "jump the queue" if you want to dodge. If you previously have diegetically maneuvered the fictional positioning of elements in the SIS to get advantage on defending (or use spells for it), you don't have to dodge, you already have advantage. That's a second, and open ended, defensive option.

    2. I changed it so that if you are attacked, it becomes your turn right afterwards, unless you've already taken a turn this round. You jump the queue \u2014 the person who attacked you still goes, but then you can go right away. The point of this is queue jumping is to (more often) consolidate the response windows. You get to defend, then you get to do something.
    I'll add a key phrase "While you're busy with..." if you're attacked while you've already spent your action for the round.

    3. (Not related to the question of defensive options.) As per the DMG, I use the static list "initiative score" option instead of rolling for initiative. But I've also added that whoever diegetically initiates force jumps to the top of the list. This is something I had already been doing for years, but instead of jumping to the top of the list, I'd have the list just start with them and everyone above them just skipped that turn. So my new approach makes it less different from the RAW than I had already been doing.

    Elegant to play?
    Huh... it does look pretty clumsy. I want to test it.

    Not unbalanced?
    It's the same action econ as RAW but with Dodge action buffed \u2014 an action that has never, ever been used for almost three years of play, and it still, post-buff, looks pretty weak. But hopefully strong enough to be used sometimes.

      \n Seems pretty good to me, something like that probably pushes my little trilemma as far as it can go. I especially like the initiative hijinks - it's more exciting, realistic and does help with handling time by effectively following up the player's defensive choice with the rest of their turn, if any.

      The interesting question is whether "aborting to defend" is worth it for the defender, and when. Because it essentially gives you a second defense roll (a bonus die to defend), its usefulness is at its highest when enemies have a roughly 50/50 chance of hitting, and the consequences of a hit are worse than the gain you would have from attacking yourself (or whatever it would be that you'd do on your turn).

      Presumably character with heavy armor won't be dodging, because they're relying on succeeding on a single die. Likewise character who will get hit anyway due to low AC won't bother - they'll miss the defense roll on two dice same as one, so better just take it and try to get something useful done on their own action. And of course characters who expect to end the fight quickly, or otherwise have important actions to take, won't want to lose their action to dodge.

      I agree that this Dodge is still not too strong, and that canonical texts generally make dodging pretty weak (basically because they want you to take your attacks and finish the combat instead of dodging back and forth). Seems very worthwhile to try this.

      \n There is this design philosophy that we should make easy things easy but hard things possible.

      For example, let's say that a pretty uses Burning Hands to hit two player characters at once. Then their windows won't get consolidated that round. One will be able to fight back first, then the other. See, exceptions are natural consequences of the system, I like that.

      So instead of making a rule "the windows are consolidated" (like arguably Kutulu and PbtA does), I've found a rule that has the consequence of usually consolidating the windows but still allowing for corner cases and still feeling enough like 5e to me. Haven't been this happy about a design in a long time. My old "seamless initiative" thread looks like garbage now (from my part, no blame on other participants in the thread).

      \n So it took ages to get around to it, but I finally had my D&D group play using Defence Saves!

      The players found it confusing at first but got into the swing of it pretty quickly. I asked them afterwards and they claimed to be indifferent to the change, but when we went back to the vanilla system the next week they seemed less stung by being attacked than they had when they were actively rolling defence. So I think it did make them more enguaged in the encounter, they just didn't realise it.

      As a GM, I loved using DSs because it meant I had less dice to manage. There were sixteen vampire-bit satyr in this particular encounter and it would have been a pain to roll for them all. All I had to do was tell the players who was being attacked, and then they did the admin bits simultaneously while I took a moment to get ready for the next round.

      So yeah, I'm totally sold on DSs and will be using them in the future!
