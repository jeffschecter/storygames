\n Continued from this previous thread, this thread tracks the Qualifying Round that sorted and seeded the games into the tournament.

The initial Qualifying Round includes 10 match-ups for which I read all of the games and pick which game in each match-up I'd rather play. Since I am little more than a speck of dust in the roleplaying/story game world, others are more than welcome to contribute their own picks of which game they would rather play. I will give others' opinions equal weight as long as the contributor has read or played BOTH games in the match-up she is reviewing. Later rounds will require actual play, but a read-through will have to suffice for the Qualifying Round.

The 11 match-ups in this Qualifying Round are in two groups. Category Cinderella pits free versions of successful games against games in the tournament on the strength of one or two nominations but with little if any publically available playtest reports or reviews. Category Close Call matches up award-winning but little reviewed (and even some unplayed) games with games that have yet to receive awards but which have nonetheless impressed playtesters and reviewers.

Category Cinderella\n
    \n
  • Sports: Contenders vs XXXXtreme Street Luge
  • Heroes: Capes Lite vs Apocalypse Girl
  • Morningstar: The Shab-al-Hiri Roach vs Grover Cleveland
  • Wooing: Shooting the Moon vs Being a role-playing game on the topic of the High-Flying adventures of Beatrice Henrietta Bristol-Smythe, DBE, daring Aviatrix and accomplished Exploratrix, and her Gentleman Companion, who for a Modest Fee, accompanies Beatrice Henrietta Bristol-Smythe, DBE, when the Occasion warrants her an Escort
  • Wine: Bacchanal vs The Dinner Party
\nCategory Close Call\n
    \n
  • Great Debates: Sea Dracula vs Executive Decision
  • Bikers & Bandits: Mexican Standoff vs Cutthroat
  • In Five Parts: Crime & Punishment vs City of Brass
  • Absolute Power: General Mud vs Court of the Empress
  • Fantasy-Inspired: Archipelago vs Drowning and Falling

    \nMy Sports Pick - Contenders

    I agree with the reviewer on Play This Thing that the whim-inspired XXXXtreme Sports Luge might be better as an entertaining read than as a fulfilling game. I can see myself playing it once just to screw around, but ultimately the Ronnie Award-winning Contenders comes off as more interesting long-term. Compared favorably with its inspiration, My Life with Master, Contenders has impressed even those with little interest in boxing (or gladiator duels or whatever you want to use as the central form of physical showdown) with its ability to both evoke the emotional feel of Rocky-inspired fight flicks as well as the drama of climactic one-on-one fights. It might not end up as my favorite game, but I can see myself playing it for more than one session.

    Agree or disagree? As long as you've read or played both these games, feel free to chime in to change the score, which currently stands as:

    Contenders 3 - XXXXtreme Street Luge 0

    \nMy Morningstar Pick - The Shab-al-hiri Roach

    Jason Morningstar's bold assertion that all his games are "awesome" was no idle boast. Reading both the award-winning Shab-al-hiri Roach and Grover Cleveland got me itching to play them with my gaming group. Both are, typos aside, very clearly written and use thoughtful layouts to drive home their respective themes. They also seem to do a good job combining humor with other layers of mood, avoiding the one-dimensional goofiness of many other "humorous" games. Honestly, based on just my read-throughs of the games themselves, I'd probably play Grover Cleveland first with my gaming group because it seems slightly more accessible. However, in this case, it pays to be a favorite, as the buzz I've read about the Shab-al-hiri Roach is intriguing and makes me want to play it a little more. I'll split my vote, giving the edge to the Shab-al-hiri Roach.

    Agree or disagree? Feel free to post your pick if you've read or played both these games.

    Scoreboard
    The Shab-al-hiri Roach 2 - Grover Cleveland 1
    Contenders 3 - XXXXtreme Street Luge 0

    \nMy Heroes Pick - Capes Lite

    When I paired Apocalypse Girl with Capes, I honestly had no idea how closely the two were related, so much so that the author of Apocalypse Girl calls his own game "second generation Capes." Moreover, putting Apocalypse Girl in Category Cinderella was probably a mistake; it not only has an award (a Low Ronnie) but also positive playtests, two requirements for the "middle tier" games in Category Close Call. That said, Capes Lite barely gets the nod here due to its dirt simple presentation of some rather challenging mechanics. Apocalypse Girl seems to have the potential to surpass its forebear someday, but for now it still seems an unfinished, albeit highly ambitious, project.

    Agree or disagree? Feel free to post your pick if you've read or played both these games.

    Scoreboard
    Capes Lite 2 - Apocalypse Girl 1
    The Shab-al-hiri Roach 2 - Grover Cleveland 1
    Contenders 3 - XXXXtreme Street Luge 0

    \nMy Wooing Pick - Shooting the Moon

    At the risk of picking on Ben Lehman, I'm afraid I'm going to hand another one of his games a decisive loss. "Being a role-playing game on the topic of the High-Flying adventures of Beatrice Henrietta Bristol-Smythe, DBE, daring Aviatrix and accomplished Exploratrix, and her Gentleman Companion, who for a Modest Fee, accompanies Beatrice Henrietta Bristol-Smythe, DBE, when the Occasion warrants her an Escort" is an interesting read, but, once again, I agree with Play This Thing's perspective that this is essentially a quick "acting game." I might want to play it on a car ride just for kicks, but arranging a game session around it is another matter.

    In contrast, I was pleasantly surprised by Shooting the Moon, which I must confess I initially thought I'd find annoyingly silly. Instead, I'm itching to test this out with my play group, so I'm getting behind it with the full force of my votes.

    Agree or disagree? Feel free to post your pick if you've read or played both these games.

    \nMy Wine Pick - The Dinner Party

    Experienced game designers might be concerned that I'm picking the relatively unknown Dinner Party over the well-regarded Bacchanal, but unfortunately this contest puts the question "Which game would you rather play?" at stake instead of "Which game pushes boundaries farther?" or "Which game is more influential or revolutionary?" For some, the erotic explorations of debauchery posed by Bacchanal might be quite appealing, but I see the Dinner Party getting substantially more playing time with my gaming group. It's the first game that had me laughing out loud simply at the rules themselves, and, in stark contrast with Bacchanal, it could make an excellent entry point for non-gamers. I'll split my vote so others with passionate opposition can overturn it, but I definitely lean toward the Dinner Party.

    Agree or disagree? Feel free to post your pick if you've read or played both these games.

    \nMy Great Debates Pick - Sea Dracula

    Though vaguely linked through the concept of a "debate," Sea Dracula and Executive Decision are more closely related in how they push the boundaries of traditional role-playing, albeit in markedly divergent ways.

    Executive Decision is essentially a debate game with a President as a pseudo-GM and other players trying to score points by pushing their character's own agandas. Unlike some of the other games I've read (e.g., Contenders), the game itself does not seem to be interesting beyond its content, so, if you have little interest in politics, you'll have little interest in the game. Moreover, the only review I found for the game seems lukewarm and even questions whether it is a role-playing game in the first place. I probably should have put this game in Category Cinderella, where it would have lost to any of the favorites.

    I personally have little interest in playing the alternative in this match-up. Like Executive Decision, Sea Dracula seems less about role-playing per se, focusing instead on the ridiculous requirement that players (likely uninclined to bust a move in the first place) can win the game only through dancing. I have a feeling my gaming group will have more fun with this than I will.

    Agree or disagree? Feel free to post your pick if you've read or played both these games.

    \nMy Bandits Pick - Cutthroat

    Cutthroat gets off easy against Mexican Standoff, a beta version of a 15-minute party game. Although Mexican Standoff got some votes for Free Indie Game of the Year a couple years ago, I think it's best used as a warm-up for a different game. It occurs to me that maybe we could combine some of these shorter games that are getting rolled over in the Qualifying Round into a single combo session that goes head-to-head with other more involved games.

    The early version Cutthroat being used in this competition retains many of the flaws that subsequent ones presumably address. As such, I doubt it will last long in the tournament, unless a more recent version is made freely available online.

    Agree or disagree? Feel free to post your pick if you've read or played both these games.

    \nMy "In Five Parts" Pick - Crime & Punishment

    Both games consisting of five parts as well as pre-made characters, Crime and Punishment and City of Brass restore interest for me in the Close Call Category of match-ups. City of Brass is an extremely deadly (for the characters) competition enhanced by cards and partially constructed pre-made scenarios. Crime and Punishment also has a strong visual element through the brainstorming and investing process, but the game on the whole is far more collaborative. In my opinion, these games are in the same ballpark as far as quality goes, so I'm going to split my vote, favoring Crime and Punishment solely because its subject matter would be more interesting to my gaming group.

    Agree or disagree? Feel free to post your pick if you've read or played both these games.

    \nMy Absolute Power Pick - General Mud

    A trend is emerging that is making me change the ultimate use of this Qualifying Round. Originally intended to quickly weed out lesser games, all it's really doing is separating full session games from shorter, exercise-length games.

    Court of the Empress is case in point. I read it and thought it was interesting but rather simple, primed for another 0-3 spanking. In reality, it's just a different type of game, and the designer says as much: "This game is intended as a roleplaying exercise, in the same sense that actors use acting exercises to improve their craft." With this in mind, I think I'll do a MULRAH Tournament of session-length games and set aside shorter games for a "MULRAH Mini" Tournament.

    That said, the end is in sight for this qualifying round. Ronnie Award-winning General Mud takes this match-up for obvious reasons.

    \nMy Fantasy-Inspired Pick - Archipelago II

    Another trend is emerging from my read-throughs for the Qualifying Round: I'm rapidly becoming a Jason Morningstar fanboy. I didn't want to pick between the Shab-Al-Hiri Roach and Grover Cleveland. I loved the Dinner Party, not his game but his nomination. And I think Drowning and Falling is awesome.

    Both Drowning and Falling and Archipelago II seem like excellent games, inspired by the fantasy genre though this is a very loose similarity. I get a clear sense from Drowning and Falling of how the game can twist standard D&D-style gaming into a hilarious session, but I'm a little worried that Archipelago will be too aimless to start. I understand Archipelago's aim is to develop slowly, and I wholly intend to test it out, but it's a concern nonetheless. However, even though I prefer Drowning and Falling, Jason himself has noted that it's best for a short game, so I might send it to my newly developing MULRAH Mini Tournament. He also notes his love for Archipelago below, so I've adjusted the score to represent this.

    \n With the MULRAH Mini tournament underway, the full tournament likely will not get underway until 2010. Does anyone want to help play through some of these games? You would probably pick a bracket of four games, play them all, and select which one goes to a "Final Four" that would, in turn, receive even more actual play to determine the final champion. Please let me know if you're interested in this!

    As for seeding, I've tried to remove some of the guesswork and subjectivity by scoring each game. The score is the average of two parts the game's rating on RPG.net (5 if no score exists), one part a score from 4 to 7 based on my own opinion articulated above, and then an additional 6 for every award nomination and a 7 for every award won. Thus, Shooting the Moon gets a 6.1, the average of its 5.6 rating on RPG.net (taken twice), a 7 for my high opinion of the game, and a 6 for being a nominee for the Grog d'Or Award for Best RPG in 2007.

    I'll hold off on presenting the scores until the tournament actually begins. If nobody volunteers to help out, the scores might be the end of it; there's no way way my group can play through all the games in any timely fashion!
