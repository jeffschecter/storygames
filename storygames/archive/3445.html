\n Hey all: Just thought I'd share.

So, aside from local area meetups (playtest this, housecon that, Camp Nerdly, etc) I've got two regular RPG groups: One meets weekly, the other bi-weekly.

Recently, with work and stuff I'd been really stressed out, and because of lack of prep and general work stress, a mini campaign kind of bit it about 4 sessions in (it finished, but it dragged on and on). I started to realize that I was burning out. Back in my easier job a couple years back, setting aside a few hours for campaign prep and the like was no big deal, but since I joined my new company (which I love, mind) I've had less free time and more stress. Games with little GM-prep were much more appealing recently, until I just realized I had run out of steam.

So what I ended up doing was setting the expectations that Actual RPG Gaming is, for the forseeable future, going to happen every other week. That's more than enough time to recharge batteries and hit the gaming (be it PTA or GURPS) with renewed vigor.

In those other weeks, we still meet, but we BS for about an hour on politics and science, then we play 1-2 Crazy German Boardgames, which is our other passion.

We've fallen in love with some of these unique games, and together have been researching which ones to pick up next. Citadels, Ra, Tikal, Puerto Rico, BattleLore (occasionally) and Settlers of Catan (anytime gaming doesn't happen we throw down with Settlers) for now, later Princes of Florence, Traders of Genoa, El Grande, and maybe that new pretty Samurai game with the weird tower-thingy. Two weeks ago we even decided for gaming night to go on a Field Trip, all piling in a car and cruising to a local boardgame-focused shop to scope out the board games and RPGs there.

It was an experiment, started about 6 weeks ago. 6 Weeks Later, and I am way less stressed out about gaming, can still throw down and have a blast, and am now in a zone where everything's manageable, thanks to those crazy German board games, science chat, and the ability to take a step back and see what was and wasn't working.

Anyone else?

-Andy

    \n I tend to play shorter sessions, and chuck in some "other" gaming.

    For example, a while back my group appeared for our regular session and I really wasn't feeling it that night. So rather than run for three hours, I ran a short session (which was ok for me, I burned up all my energy in 90 mins of decent gaming rather than eke it out to 3 hours of mediocre gaming) and then we played a multiplayer Halo 2 game for another couple of hours.     Ah ... you've got to stay in for the long haul because people are counting on you.Social Footprint! I recognize that!

    Hrmm ... does a large social footprint correlate with burnout? Wouldn't surprise me.

    It probably does correlate somewhat. I also think that people get excited about a new idea for a game at the beginning and fail to think about how easy the activity is going to be to maintain after the first couple of sessions. This is (or has been for me) a bigger issue in those infinitely long campaigns rather than games like Great Ork Gods that last a couple of hours and are over. Still, with a little foresight you can make even long term games (infinite footprint) manageable in terms of burn-out. How? Here's some ways I can think of:

    1.) Stretch out the game cycle a bit. Why do we gamers feel like we have to play a campaign at least once a week? Or heaven forbid, more than once a week. Once or twice a month keeps the prep to manageable proportions and allows time to recharge batteries between sessions.

    2.) Distribute those social responsibilities that generally fall on the GM. Why does the GM have to host the game, organize the munchies, make sure everyone is going to be there, and all the other social crap? Doesn't he have enough to do just prepping the game? Give him a break and dole these other jobs out to everyone else. That way no one person gets burn out due to being overworked.

    3.) Make your game such that different players can sit in the GM chair from time to time. Nothing helps with the GM burn-out like being able to just play from time to time.

    4.) Do other activities (like boardgames or going out for beers) between sessions or even in place of a session here and there. Doing other things is sort of like clensing the ol' mental palate. My personal suggestion is paintball. Shooting your fellow players repeatedly is great therapy.

    Anywho, that's my take on the burnout issue.

    \n I'm seeing a simple and, I think, wrong-headed equation here between "burning out" and "not wanting to play." In my experience, "burn out" isn't when you don't want to play, but it's when you want the social-creative payoff of play but you don't want to deal with the pile of logistics (prep, hosting, food, etc) that play typically requires. "Just don't play" isn't a solution, here, because you're still going to be bummed that you didn't get the play payoff. Solutions to burnout need to find ways to get the play payoff without incurring the logistical mess that comes with it -- and keep in mind, a different logistical mess, even a larger one, may be preferable to the same-old logistical mess that you don't want to deal with.

    \n Interesting distinction, and I know just what you mean. If it's the logistical crap that's not any fun, then don't do the logistical crap. Have someone else in the group do it. If nobody else in the group wants to do it either, THEN play doesn't occur, and that's a shame, but it beats requiring someone to be miserable.

    Right now I am the only one in the group that sends out the weekly reminder e-mail of when we are playing, where, and what. About 3 years ago I tried to give that responsibility away and the reminder e-mail didn't happen. People ended up calling each other not knowing what was going on and everyone was unhappy. Except me. I needed a break, I took a break.

    I suppose this wouldn't work if you gamed with 16 year olds, or idiots. But if they're adults, they can do these things themselves if they want it done.
