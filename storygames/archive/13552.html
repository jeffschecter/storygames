    \n A three part part wiki-centered competition:

    1) Each contestant designs a short, easy to play, 3-8 page game on the wiki.

    2) Open time for playtesting of each others designs - because you'll have wanted to do it for part (3)

    3) Then those contestants design their final game using only the components of other people's games during the first part - using the wiki to say why you used this part, what you changed, and linking to the original game.

    \n Judges assign each participant a score in each category (presumably design and feedback), they are weighted as you like and then averaged, a winner emerges. You could write a mediocre game but provide stellar feedback and win, or vice-versa, but being above average in both categories would trend toward victory. Maybe, like the games themselves, feedback is discrete - you write a short essay on each other entry, or an assigned entry in round robin fashion.

    The more I think about the "essay" idea, the more I like it - if a requirement is "you need to write a thoughtful, 1000 word analysis of a randomly-assigned entry that will be judged on its usefulness and insight", I think that would be a good thing.

    \n Each time someone comments in your game's thread, award them points as follows:
    0 - Useless and shows no effort.
    1 - Trying to help,
    2 - and has clearly tried to grasp what your game's about and help you where you need it,
    3 - and actually succeeded.

    Presumably most responses would earn 2 points. It would also be fun to look over those that earned 3 to get a survey of what sorts of responses actually did help game-development efforts.

    Then the game judges would award points for games like this:
    5 - Submitted on time, intelligible,
    7 - and attempts interesting concepts,
    10 - and pulls them off in a way that could be playtested,
    15 - and that playtest might actually be a fun game.

    Game points + response points = total points. Most total points = winner.

    Or we could try harder to promote a culture of helping, and change the game's 5/7/10/15 to 1/2/3/5.

    \n Not fully baked, but: The contest is broken up into a series of rounds, with the expectation that you'll have a readable/playable draft for the first round that you'll revise for subsequent rounds. Each entrant is randomly assigned a set of other games to give feedback to. You secretly rank each game you're assigned for "design quality" (for whatever that means to you), and the various rankings are synthesized by the organizer to determine the design winner for the round. Each person receiving feedback ranks the people who were assigned to give them feedback based on how helpful the person was (for whatever that means to them, so there's an element of playing to the person in that you need to figure out whether the person needs cheerleading, challenging questions, advice, something else, etc.). These rankings are also synthesized to determine the feedback winner for the round. The next round is similar, but you have a different random mix of games to give feedback to / people to get feedback from. There are points awarded for placing at various levels for the various rounds which are totaled to determine the overall winner. (You could easily add other stuff to this system, too, like a playtesting component, or special judge's prizes awarded by individuals outside of the ranking system). You'd probably have to figure out how to account for some people flaking out in the middle of the process and not delivering their drafts, feedbacks, or rankings.

    \n Forum ate my post.

    The participants are judged chiefly on their feedback and contributions. It's not about your personal design work, but rather your ability to critique and help others in the community. The contest will persist over a span of 4 weeks, with each week associated with a stage of design. Work on your game via a forum and solicit feedback. At the end of each weekend, a poll goes up and asks which other participant provided the most meaningful and useful feedback for your game. Compare all 4 weeks of the polls to determine the winner.

    Week 1: Initial concept in line with the game-chef ingredients.
    Week 2: First draft focusing on mechanics (does the crunch work?)
    Week 3: Second draft focusing on story (does it encourage compelling stories?)
    Week 4: Wordsmithing (Does it teach the game and communicate it to an audience appropriately?)

    For bonus points, with a standardized format it might be possible to have all submissions donated to the community. Put together an anthology of these 5-10 page games and publish them in zero-risk pdf and Lulu combo. All profits go into a grant for a future game such as the one DivNull provided for Blowback.
