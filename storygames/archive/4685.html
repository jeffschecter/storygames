\n
\n I'm beginning to work on a game that is essentially an extended combat system. (I'm looking for the crazy backstabby fighting that characterized certain interpretations of Paranoia, to be precise.) However, often times, combat systems can be boring:

"I attack."

"Roll to hit."

"Yay I hit." or "Boo I missed."

"Next turn."

There have been exceptions to this. For example, while I never actually played The Riddle of Steel, I played the combat simulator many times. I felt engaged because of the meaningful decisions and resource management, tied to the unforgiving nature of the system. (Any miscalculation could be fatal.)

Another example, from the other side of the design spectrum, is Sons of Liberty. This is essentially just a combat system, but the real-time card play plus special abilities encourages madcap action, which is fun in a completely different sort of way.

So, here's my question:

What makes combat fun for you? What are some systems that have consistently brought the awesome for your combats? Got any good examples where a game system made your combat more gripping/exciting/hair-raising/betterer?

Seth Ben-Ezra
Great Wolf

    \n As a player, combat needs to have that satisfying crunch. When I was playing more than GM'ing, 3.5 was the order of the day for just cooking out some baddies and stomping mudholes with my minis. Glorified action figures, with mechanics thinly veiling the let's pretend aspects of narrative-injected wargaming.
    \n
    \nGM'ing, however, is a completely different story.
    \n
    \nMy best example comes from a d20 conversion of Battletech. The campaign lasted well over a year and a half, with lots of crowbarring to turn d20 Modern into an angst-ridden story-driven mech combat simulator. The scene involved mechs venturing into an abandoned hangar on a frozen-over ice planet, only to find five story tall monsters lurking in the shadows as they explored.
    \n
    \nWhat made the scene pop was the characterization. Each of these characters piloted these war machines for different reasons; and the mech was made to be another layer of the personality, a Freudian Super Ego whose physical limits and sustained damage reflected the mental states and attitudes of the characters. These monsters were far too massive to take on foot, even for a hundred men; and so the mech served as a metaphor of technology protecting mankind, even as it enslaved mankind (a recurring theme throughout MechWarrior, particularly the Dark Age material). At its' heart, it was merely a dungeon crawl; but the novelty of the scenario combined with a thick, almost stiflyingly serious mood of play (dark room, cold house, no one speaking, everyone caustically self-reflective) made the sudden combat all the more unexpected.
    \n
    \nWhat makes combat interesting to me isn't the combat, just like Robert Heinlein's Starship Troopers wasn't about a man who shot bugs. The action is intense, visceral, dangerous, and scintillating; but it also says something about the world, and how the characters approach survival in a world that's left them behind. That's a recurring theme in my games: characters on the edge of space, reality, the kingdom, sanity, whatever - dynamic choices are made when players, deep into a narrative, are faced with choices for which there is no right answer.
    \n
    \nLacuna is the best answer to that. With every swing, the Agents are brought one step closer to their heart exploding; even as they grit their teeth and reach their peak in Blue City their bodies are rupturing on the slab, moments away from a horrific, disembodied demise.
    \n
    \nCombat should represent something. Random encounters, when brief and deadly, punctuate the danger of the world outside the village. Think of every fight in those contexts, and people will foam at the mouth for murder, knowing that their fights mean something. At least that's my experience.

    \n Giving combat meaning surely is one way to make it fun, but not the only way. It's also the hardest to implement mechanically, if you're really talking about "meaning for the players" rather than "meaning for the characters."

    There really are different approaches to how to make the process of combat fun. The main one, for me, is just like for the rest of roleplaying: make players' choices matter. That includes giving them a good array of choices to begin with. When I played AD&D 2e, the choices were very limited. And out of those, throwing a couple of daggers, for my dex-based character, was always by far the best choice, so that it wasn't even really a choice anymore because the alternatives were not viable.

    The second way to do this is to make the narration matter. In other words, allow player ingenuity and creativity to become a focus of the conflict, of each action or at least several important ones. You'll notice that that's a particular preference of mine, as I've designed a game about that whole concept.

    Third way: allow me to kick ass. At times this is a whole different play style (wish fulfillment), at other times it's just what needs to happen for the character to realize their place in the story. I don't care about "realism" nearly as much as I do about being a protagonist. Remember how Malcolm Reynolds solves the hostage situation toward the end of the Firefly pilot? Most people do, for a good reason.

    Fourth: Make teamwork matter. If we're going to do combat together, allow us to make each other more awesome. Promote the group cohesion and tactics. Put in combos for multiple characters; something more than just buffing/hexing.

    I might have more later

    \nGrumble.

    The thrill of overcoming adversity comes for me from systems where the play moves swiftly, where I *do* experience problems and setbacks and losses because of the nature of the mechanics, and where I feel that there's at least a "gambler's hope" of me losing out totally.

    The fun that come out of carefully managed mechanics and resources hits me when there are at least two or three different valuable resources (health, spell slots, gnosis, attack bonus, whatever), where I can initiate tradeoff between them in differing ways (often with a variety of maneuvers), AND where the value of those tradeoffs changes based on the circumstances of the combat itself.

    The simple thrill of whackin' the baddies comes though best when there are clearly-defined rules for "you die now", and I get to initiate and work those. Also, the baddies have to be nice and simple, but with enough variety and novelty that I don't get tired.

    As for sort-of-catharsis based on the intensity of the stuff related to the combat... I'm actually really crappy at achieving that *reliably*, and can't speak well to it.

    \n I have to admit to a bit of the simulationist bug when it comes to combat. I like tactics to work. I want to see how a grappler fights a boxer, or pit gladiator with net and trident against shield and cestus. I want every creature to have developed tactics that best suits its niche in the ecology and the body that developed to fill that niche. Wolves bring down larger animals with pack tactics. Lion prides add ambush to that mix. Orcs and rats swarm. etc. I want all those techniques to bring different challenges to the table.

    I also like the narrativist approach which basically allows for various shades of "you succeed, now tell us how." The descriptive freedom can create fun stories of "can do" swashbuckling. So, I guess I'm of two minds here.

    \n Combat.... Mmmmmmm yummy. There are a few things that a combat system must have in order to be fun. This is regardless of whether combat is handled just as a part of the normal resolution system or as a separate thing in and of itself. **Note: This is all my opinion and not some statement of objective truth here.

    1.) Characters must always be effective. Non-effective characters = crappy, not fun play. This doesn't mean every character has to be the MOST effective, just that no character should be completely useless. If a character is ineffective in combat situations that player is essentially nothing but an observer and when we're talking about more gamist play being an observer sucks hind tit. TSoY does a good job at this. Everyone's effective. Bonus dice and cool Secrets make everyone a viable participant in combat.

    2.) Players must always have choices. Personally, more choices = better to me. Only having the choice to make a basic attack roll or run away is generally not terribly fun. In this situation you end up with combat that is roll to hit, subtract some damage, move to next player, rinse and repeat. YAWN. SNORE. Being able to make decisions that increase or decrease one's defensive and offensive capabilities from moment to moment based on the current situation is fun. Burning Wheel does this well. Fight! has a bunch of different options that are interesting and can help or harm your chances at success depending on how well you anticipate your enemy's strategy. Strangely enough D&D doesn't do this very well with most of the Fighter type characters but does do this with the Wizard, Sorcerer and other magic-wielding characters. A wizard generally has more combat options open to his player than a fighter due to spell selection. (Yes, level and refresh rate of spells and such have an affect here. Not trying to get that complex at the moment.)

    3.) Losing should be a result that doesn't end the game for the player. This seems to be a no-brainer to me. OD&D handled this by making character generation so simple that a player that loses simply takes 10 minutes to make a new character and then joins right back in. More hippy-style systems make losing not tied to character death and removal from play but rather tied to player intent for the combat. Thus if the player loses his character is captured or humiliated or something but not necessarily removed from play. Removal from play for any extended period blows.

    Well, that's enough for the moment. I'll let someone else talk for a bit.

Another is that magic items were significant, but you had to (mostly) work with what you found (I did allow some amount of trading).     \n A bit more on Yomi Layer 3:

    There is a long trend to design combat based on evaluating a situation of perfect or nearly-perfect information. You've got your minis on a map or whatever, you know what they can do, you can see your opponent's stuff, you know what they can do. You have a big list of options, and you sit there for a few minutes, grinding through decision trees until you figure out the right choice as best you can. It's a lot like a chess game. You lose if you lack enough good options (the fighter problem in 3.X DnD) or play too fast. You can certainly play fast anyway, like Speed Chess, but the basic result is that inexperienced players lose that much faster.

    A Yomi-based game allows much faster play, though the potential is not always realized. Anyway, I'd much rather my RPG combat feel like a round of Street Fighter than a game of chess, and the former is certainly a lot faster to play. Contenders is like this, with a choice/counter-choice game embedded in the stance selection. You have a rich play experience, but with a much smaller rule set: the opponent is providing the complexity, not the quantity of rules or the complexity of the board.

    \n It depends on what the game is about. In action-oriented games I like:

    1) Meaningful tactics. Your choices matter. In some games this is very lo-fi (WoD, where the biggest choice is spending Willpower).or based on hardwired character traits (feats, class/character abilities). In action games I like there to be choices available to everyone, regardless of character build and for there to be multiple viable options.

    2) The opposition's choices matter to you. *S/he* does meaningful stuff too and your choices plug right into his/hers, and vice versa. Being able to suss out how this will play can ive you a advantage based on reading your opponent.

    3) There's plenty of room for narration. One of the limitations of a GURPS Martial Arts/Burning Wheel/TRoS approach is that your system choices do a lot of the narrative heavy lifting ahead of time. This is very useful for lots of people and I enjoy it from time to time myself, but n many cases I personally prefer to have specific actions done in broad strokes so that the player has the most room possible to plug in descriptions.

    Ready 2 Run's combat system (the system used in Aeternal Legends) is designed for games with the above values. You build your own special moves round to round by spending Wits (which regenerates each round) and plugging them into your choice of single/multiple actions and defenses to make them better or worse based on what you think might happen during a particular phase. So your dice buffed opening move can be a downward blow or some kind of spiralling cut, or whatever -- you design the game characteristics of the move you want to do. From there we plug in Tricks and Preps, Expertise and special abilities, but all characters have options to pursue,

    Posted By: Great WolfRight, and this is where I'd really like to see some insights from folks. What mechanical bits have you encountered in a game that made combat "fun"? Again, I'm mostly looking at this from a gamist perspective (and phooey on you, Jason Corley ;-] ) where I'm concerned about the players being able to engage skillfully in the contest in order to overcome.
    \nWell, everyone else has covered a lot of ground already, but...

    I played a one-shot of True20 a few months back, and it was a real eye-opener. By eliminating various options people supposedly complain about (e.g., AoO's), combat got incredibly boring. E.g., since my PC didn't have to worry about getting into a flank, penalties for shooting into combat (iirc), or provoking an AoO for using a ranged weapon in a threatened square, our use of minis seemed pretty pointless. I was immediately obvious that walking up to a foe and popping a cap in his temple was the optimal strategy, as there was no risk. Damage in that system is also very random; you make a saving throw and your degree of failure determines the effect. It complicated resolution and made it difficult to gauge whether my PC needed to be cautious or not.

    I think that, in an effort to be "more about the story," yet still be recognizably d20, the system basically achieved neither.

    I dunno. I play a lot of D&D 3.5, so maybe I've become biased. It can certainly get over-complicated sometimes, but I enjoy the tactical aspects. I like that while there may be optimal strategies, they aren't so obvious that play becomes repetitive and boring. Figuring out the optimal combos becomes part of the game. And positioning matters, so it's not just "walk up and roll to hit."

    Like other people said, there should be a robust array of choices to be made, and those choices should matter. Realism needs to take a back seat to gameplay.
