\n I've been playing a fair bit of Dungeon World, lately, along with bits of AW and Lady Blackbird.

I really like the way these games play, particularly the components that invite player contribution. For example, I love it when in response to a miss the GM gets to say "Right, cool. So you've never been to this village before in your life, right? Why is it that the sheriff hates you so much?" Or whatever.

Basically, I'm talking about games where the conflict is driven by player failure, which is set up to occur often enough that the story stays interesting. Character failure is probably less frequent, as exemplified by, say, off screen moves in AW/DW, and GM discretion for how hard and direct a move to make.

Burning Wheel is almost in this category, because the probability of failure on a roll is generally pretty high (there's incentive for setting yourself up on the brink of failure all the time), and the consequence is not intended to be "okay, that doesn't happen, what will you do instead", unless that's somehow an interesting outcome. The main barrier I see for BW is that the GM rolls for, say, contested tests.

So, a couple of questions, here, moving in various directions:

1) Is it feasible to play Burning Wheel with a diceless-GM, or does that just fundamentally break the system?

2) What games use systems that have, or at least readily allow for, this feel of "dice failure is frequent, irrespective of actual character action failure per se" and "GM rarely or never rolls dice" and so on? Or to put it another way, which games support the implied GM style of Vincent Baker without bending to the breaking point? Obviously "anything written by Vincent", and then Lady Blackbird, and more or less "anything by Luke Crane". What else? In particular I'm looking for other *systems*. But exemplars are interesting, too.

3) Has anybody tried to run, say, nWoD games this way? 4e? Other games that don't at first blush fit the mold? I don't mean the fluff, here, I mean the system. So this would mean for nWoD you're still rolling d10s and counting 8+, but with maybe some changes to invite more frequent roll failure, allow the GM to bypass rolling, etc

I realize this is a tad stream-of-consciousness, but hopefully the basic intent of my question is clear enough to spur discussion.

    \n I pretty much play this way. I play Exalted with my current group (sorta like WoD)
    \n
    \nI have my players roll their defense vs static numbers for enemy attacks.
    \nIn fact I've pulled pretty much the whole front, threats, principles etc straight out from Apocalypse world and use them to run Exalted.
    \nThe biggest difference is that exalted uses a dice pools and "needed number of successes". So i think that not enough success is like 2-6 of AW. Up to 3 more successes like the 7-9. 4+ successes over the needed is like a 10+
    \nIf they (somehow, as they are effectively supers) roll 10+ successes over required (its happened!), then I give them the equivalent of the 12+ in the advanced moves.
    \n
    \nWorks a charm, and my players are loving the game.

    \n One thing to note - conflict is never only driven by player failure. The trick (for me) was in the fronts and threats. The conflict is driven by the difference in goals of the PCs and the Threats. The story is driven by the repercussions of the character's actions and choices.
    \n
    \nThe biggest thing that the AW results make crystal clear is that "if you try X, something WILL happen". In a lot of other games all that failing a roll means is that "nothing" happened. An example might be "I try to convince the guard to let me in". If you get a great success you get in. If you get a partial success (7-9, of difficult+3 success in my system), then you either get past and the guard phones in the event (alerts the others) or you are not allowed in but the guard doesn't mention it to anyone, or maybe you get in but the guard himself is alerted and running for help.
    \n
    \nIf you fail, it doesn't mean "the guard just says no". There have to be repercussions - whats the worst that can believably happen in the fiction because of a failed attempt to bribe/pursuade you way past?
    \n
    \nThat sort of attitude, that there are ALWAYS repercussions and that something always happens, is important. :)

    \n Wushu and Panty Explosion Perfect are pretty light on the GM die rolling. In Wushu, only a 'boss battle' Nemesis has a contested roll. All mooks and social/environmental hazards have a fixed value. In PEP, the challenged player rolls and the other players (who are rivals and best friends) narrate the outcome. The GM can challenge and assign modifiers to the die, but never rolls it. [Or at least that's the way I run it.] :-)
