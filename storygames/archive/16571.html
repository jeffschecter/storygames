\n I've been working as a paid GM since 1995, with 7 groups a week at most. Currently I'm on 4 groups, children 10-16 years old, playing campaigns through the winter. It pays reasonably well.

Last summer I hired four GMs to help me with a summer camp for children; playing the frpg Fabula for a whole week, eating sound food, and hiking the woods every day too (to keep healthy and rest our heads). 30 children attended. It was a great success!

This summer we're doing it again; young players signing up for one whole week, two sessions a day, one hike in the woods a day. We do it for 3 weeks this summer, a new group of young players each week, and a unique scenario each week. All of the players are paying customers out to get some serious adventure!

All GMs get good money to do it, and a lot of fun!

So;
- Have you done this?
- Have you considered that doing it may bring new blood into rpgs?
- Have you considered how wholesome this activity is for children and youths?
- Do you understand how inspiring it is, to play with openminded and eager children?
- If you have the skills, and the time; is anything stopping you from doing it?

    Posted By: ArpieThere is one, huge, thing that may stop me from being a paid GM: initial capital and interest in the activity.
    In the US, Games are considered a "zero-interest" activity, meaning investors are nervous about committing to such an idea.
    It is the same in Norway. I do not get any support in doing this.

    My initial investment in this scheme lay back in 1995, when I paid for leaflets to be distributed to local schools. It cost me approximately 200 bucks. Since then I've had around 1.700 $ in average yearly income from GMing (a conservative estimate, mind you).

    FRPG Fabula
    Mind you; I believe you have to use a great game to succeed. Most classical games I know of would fall through in the special circumstances that applies in professional GMing, especially with regard to the time-constraints.

    My fantasy-rpg Fabula is perfectly suited to run small sessions of 2-2,5 hours, with a speedy resolution system that allows for lots of action in that time, and with balance and robustness built in, to tackle any strange ways our game-play chooses to take us.

    Fabula was developed specifically for this use, by me, and went through a new revision after each half-year the first four years. I had to hone it to the purpose, and to be sternly critical about every aspect of the game, to get where I wanted with it. When it was published in 1999, it was in its eight edition. And the game I play today, is far better still.

    As the years have gone by, I have made a myriad of settings for the game, to make the game new to me each year, so I would not grow bored with playing. The freshness and variety in themes have made players follow me for up to 11 years (some boys started at age 9, and left me at age 19), getting great fun each and every season.

    So there is a lot of work put into this, more work than the yearly salary can cover. But rpgs are my hobby too, so I'm not complaining. \n

    \n I do both professional GMing and professional RPG writing/design as supplements to my day job income. My average yearly income from each source is in the same range as Tomas's, but the pay-per-hour is much better in working with kids. Calculating how much I make on an hourly basis even when writing for Wizards, the best-paying gig out there, does not bear thinking about. (Self-publishing may, of course, be more lucrative but also makes it harder to calculate all the time you put into it.)
    \n
    \nHowever, Becky Thomas of the Roleplay Workshop has been doing RPG afterschool and summer camp stuff, and I believe a group of adults as well, as her full-time living for decades now in the US. Last I talked to her she was using a game store as her physical space, which addresses some of the issues you mention, Jim. My afterschool program is likewise run out of a school, and as a parent who pays for my son to attend similar classes I can attest that they take the majority of the program's income in exchange for providing space, liability insurance, etc.
    \n
    \nBecky started as a teacher, I got my start as a parent, so both of us had an "in" with a school system. One could get started by offering services to home school families, which are a lot less regulated; you could even run the games over videoconference to avoid much of the liability. Also the Brooklyn Strategist hires professional GMs, which could be a good way to get started. Starting a business like the B-Strat is like starting a game store, maybe even more risky and capital-intensive.

    Posted By: Jim CrockerI think I'd probably burn out if I were GMing 40+ hours a week, though. Certainly, I don't want to spend that much time around kids, but that's just a personal preference thing.
    I wound spend myself too, if trying to GM 40+ hours a week. The most I have done is 20+ hours a week; eight groups professionally, and one or two free groups on the side. That took its toll on me. I did it for one whole year, but had to cut back the next year, not to burn out. My normal rate has been 4-6 groups, 10-15 hours a week. This year I've been doing 4 groups, 10 hours.

    In summer-camp though, we played 40 hours last year, in one week, with 5 leaders and 30 players. It was great!

    This year we will spend more time hiking (the woods are great here), and cut-back on playing time to 5 hours a'day. I believe that will be healthy for children and adults, and still give us the time to produce really hilarious adventures.

    As for personal preferences; I like to play, and I like children, so for me this is a great job. I believe you have to like both to do it really well.
