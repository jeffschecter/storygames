    \n It is very good. One of the suggestions for things you might like to do is 'Decide on some rules for resolving conflicts'.

    This has produced some great stuff in the games I've played. Depending on the intensity of a particular conflict, we've used Archipelago cards, dice-offs and votes. The difference between having to roll a 1 on a d6 to survive sliding off a cliff to avoid the dragon's breath vs. using Archipelago cards to see how threatening a village elder goes is pretty amazing.

    And any time we got confused about what we should do in the game or how long something would take, one of us just had to read out the title and we were all back on track.

    Looks good. It sets up a game but doesn't tell you how to play. I like the flexibility, I imagine a lot of mainstream players would not - it makes them work harder than they are used to.
    \nThat's an interesting point, Ross. In some ways I think you're right, in that it does require some on-the-fly inventing of procedures. On the other hand, in play I've actually found it's incredibly freeing.

    Because there's no "right" way to resolve conflict, or to decide which scenes come next, players tend to fall back on intuition and whim. That has all sorts of interesting effects, but one of them is that it tends to devolve to the easiest and most natural methods for the group.
