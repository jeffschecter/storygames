Thomas was trying to say something over there and things got derailed in a stupid internet way.

\n

There are different ways of identifying with characters, and game rules need to take those into account when they encourage one way or another of acting.

\n

A: So, let's say that we have a game in which you are to identify strongly with the character you're playing. When the character is sad, you're sad. When the character is happy, you're happy. You want this character to have a good life for the same reasons you want yourself to. You probably want this character to do things you wish you could do.

\n

B: Let's say we have another game in which our concern is for making an interesting character with complex flaws that may be irredeemable. You don't necessarily like this character as a person, but you have a great interest in seeing how things come out for them, for better or for worse.

\n

I see a lot of functional play with variety B happening these days.

\n

Variety A, I dunno. But it sure would be great. This goes back to the two way interaction of fiction with rules that seem so elusive right now.

\n

Let's look at what Vincent said about Immersion an astonishing 4 years ago:

\n
\n

[1] The affirmed rightness of your vision

\n
\n

This is social. Your fellow players share ownership of your character, remember; you want and need for them to affirm that your vision of your character is right. They trust you with your character. They won't step in and contradict, override, undercut.

\n
\n

[2] Permission to act with passion

\n
\n

Furthermore, whatever you have your character do, they won't react defensively. If your character threatens something they value, they'll deal with the threat passionately in response, but without ever carrying the struggle up into the social level.

\n
\n

You aren't constrained by the fear that having your character act might step on someone else's toes.

\n

[3] Faith in the robustness of the game's fiction

\n
\n

And you have to trust that the game has room for your character in it. You can't be worrying whether this decision that your character's making might break the game. You have to know, securely enough that it's unconscious, that even if your character transforms the game entirely, the game'll survive.

\n
\n

Charles proposes a fourth:

\n

I would add an additional underpinning, which is [4] the degree to which the mechanic requires a state of mind which is similar to the PC state of mind. If my PC is trying to be totally open and honest with another character, and the mechanics for doing so pit us as players against each other in a competitive bidding war where one of us will lose and one of us will win, then that would seem to create a conflict between what I am experiencing as player, and what I am experiencing IC, and that may act to the detriment either of my ability to effectively use the rules, or to effectively remain immersed in my character, or both. If the mechanics instead involved us as players working together against abstract difficulty, then that might better mirror the IC situation, and better allow us to remain immersed.

\n

I think that a game design that fulfilled these design goals would create extremely intense, profound play. Furthermore, I think that as a design challenge it's an excellent one: difficult but not impossible, at the frontier of theory, compelling and immediate, practically guaranteed to make a successful game.

\n
\n

As usual, this is a matter of properly matching specifications and system.

    \n Great quote and great things, Joshua. They are also very politely worded as being about "the other players" but what they are pointing at is a serious commitment from each of us to take deeply to heart the rights of others to do as they will, and to accept what happens to our character even if we do love them from the bottom of our little heart.

    Let's talk about how we might get them. Or what happens when we don't.\n
    [1] The affirmed rightness of your vision
    This is social. Your fellow players share ownership of your character, remember; you want and need for them to affirm that your vision of your character is right. They trust you with your character. They won't step in and contradict, override, undercut.
    \nEmphasis mine. That seems like the heart of it. What are the ways that people can and do step in, contradict and override? There are games (now) that support this mechanically. The High Card narration rights in Primetime Adventures can be used to do anything you with another players's character. It takes trust and shared vision for that to play out right. I've experienced that in my games, but it's a structural way for a person's trust, and their bond with/vision for their character to be broken.

    Let's also look at ways this can happen outside of mechanics, because that surely is often done, and may be more important. We rely on the other players to mirror, to support and to enable us to play our characters the way we will. In a game with a standard social contract (oh forgive me for that, but here goes) [players control: character actions/feelings/reactions--GM controls: world/environment/NPCs] even if you play your character in a given way, the other players and the GM have a tremendous amount of power to undermine your vision.

    example:
    Player A: Jedediah brings back the lost mustang, snorting and bucking, But he handles the rope with calm and muscles the horse into submission.
    Player B: Jedediah! You poor little thing, that monster's going to kill you! Here, I'll help! Frank throws another rope around the mustang's neck, and nudges Jedediah out of the way.
    GM:The mustang is snorting and a red light gleams in it's eye. You thought you had it handled, but it was just bluffing and now redoubles its efforts, aiming a well-placed kick at your eye.

    This might be helped by:\n
    [2] Permission to act with passion
    Furthermore, whatever you have your character do, they won't react defensively. If your character threatens something they value, they'll deal with the threat passionately in response, but without ever carrying the struggle up into the social level.

    You aren't constrained by the fear that having your character act might step on someone else's toes.
    \nIn the above example, there might be all kinds of reasons why the other players did that to Player A. Player B might have been angling for the roughneck position in the party for their character for sessions. Having Jedediah who was, say, the gunslinger, suddenly also bring in the mighty Mustang means not only does Frank get overshadowed in gunfights, but is also being outshone in his own (as defined by the player) arena, horsemanship.

    The GM may be pissed off because Player A brought in the mustang ahead of schedule. They were supposed to go out and search it out as a group later. Now that is tricky stuff there. We get into territory where the GM has a story that the players get to participate in.

    Whether that is passively and whether that is satisfying is a whole nother argument. I've been writing recently about Danish scenario play where the GM not only tells a story (as written by the game writer), but that players really literally are along for the ride. But how they flesh out what happens it all their own. It is another, different social contract [players control: character actions/feelings/reactions--GM controls: world/environment/npcs--game writer controls: outline of events of play]. It's not too different from module play, except that what you do while playing is almost entirely different. The emphasis is on the moments, the reactions, the interactions, not the outcomes of conflicts and rolls. It is much more like Type B play than Type A.

    So how do we get around these blocks? In adventure play (a la D&D), having well-defined character niches, as they do in 4e with the roles (striker, controller etc) functions to minimized conflicts like the one I outlined with Player B here. Danish scenario play takes the "you can't do that yet" "yes I can!" argument out of the GMs hands, since everyone knows that the story will unfold as the game dictates. Everyone is (hopefully) on board to flesh out that fun and to trust that the GM chose a good one. In some games, if you fail, you get to say what and how your character goes down in a blaze of glory, that's another way to foster trust so that if you get hosed it doesn't feel so bad. No on did it to you, you chose it and that's all good.

    I'd like to comment on the variations A and B given by Joshua:
    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanA: So, let's say that we have a game in which you are to identify strongly with the character you're playing. When the character is sad, you're sad. When the character is happy, you're happy. You want this character to have a good life for the same reasons you want yourself to. You probably want this character to do things you wish you could do.
    Why do you want your character to have a good life? The normal way to work your character is to strengthen it, but why?

    - Why do a great majority of players work for the betterment of their characters?

    - Why do most game-smiths take this stance for granted in their games?

    I believe we are prone to use our will to succeed in everything we do. This is natural, and express itself in a lot of ways. There are several arenas we strive to succeed in, the social one most prominent amongst them.

    Player A is, in a distorted way, the most "true" man in this respect. He will engage in his character, and work for its betterment throughout the game. He is still a social incompetent in the example of Joshua, a disaster waiting to happen. The example describes a sympathetic player with no other strategy than to identify with his character. It is interesting to make such a "player", for the sake of argument, but we need to know that such a player is a theorem, not a reality.

    That said; I like the idea that our player A is totally on the side with this character. He will fight claw and beak to make the character survive. As a starting point for character-play this is good. In a simple way it simulates the fact that all people strive to survive and succeed. All sentient beings do. By exploiting our natural tendency to do well, the game-smith may build a fictional environment which challenges us on the emotional level. By building on natural human reactions, the players and GMs will react in earnest and with ease as the characters. "With ease" is essential here; when players react and act with little or no hesitation, their emotional and intellectual energy is flowing into the game. As a result the role-playing game gains a smooth player ride into fictional flow. At best the interaction is driven and compelling, laden with positive emotional tension.

    So the stance of this "player A" is, however distorted, at the hearth of successful role-playing games. We need to identify with our characters to make the instant and recurring "jump of faith" required for truly great interactive fiction.

    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanB: Let's say we have another game in which our concern is for making an interesting character with complex flaws that may be irredeemable. You don't necessarily like this character as a person, but you have a great interest in seeing how things come out for them, for better or for worse.
    This immediately reads as a laudable stance. It makes grand historical ideals of research and refinement come true in role-playing games ... or so it seems.

    However; the interaction of role-playing games are of another kind than other fictions; you will create nothing of real value without engaging the players, as you need the players to identify with their characters, and to engage these characters in the fiction. If the friction between character and the fictional elements fails to engage the players, the game will produce fiction of shallow quality. Typical for such games are lack of emotional content, and lack of "enlivened" characters.

    Games created with the intent to create "interesting characters", or worse; "interesting stories", is often lacking in emotional content. They often feels flat, however clever they are, and falls flat on the surface of fiction. Often they will be experienced as construed, and leave the players with a strange feeling of being outside the real drama. The most widely known example of this is Vampire, a game dedicated to creating and playing "interesting characters with complex flaws that may be irredeemable". And a game where the said "flaws" seldom get any strongly felt consequences, due to the game being a bit clumsy on how to engage the feelings of the players. As a result Vampire often feels like "sex with the motions, but no emotions". There are more examples, amongst them some American indie-games with meta-mechanics that makes them more of an intellectual exercise in resource-handling than anything else.

    (not trying to provoke here, only referring to how me and some of my friends have experienced some indie-games, not all)\n

    \n John Kim; I believe you have to accept some talking on the meta-level. The game will not have a chance of being organized otherwise. The other points are based on facts as simple as that too:
    - Talk meta: organize the game, give instructions while playing, be social
    - Rolling dice: do the mechanical stuff, with dice or cards or resource management
    - Consider other players: be in tune with group, play up to initiatives of others, interact
    - Demands of fiction: give heed to genre, fiction-history and internal logic, avoid bad anachronisms
    - Pondering the character: step out of character shoes, plan actions/developments, stick with character

    All of these are pretty obvious, but essential. While being immersed in the game, and identified with your character, you will strengthen the game by doing all of the above. Most of them is based on a stance of acceptance.

    You may say that the three core elements of successful play is:
    1 - Acceptance ... of fellow players
    2 - Immersion ... in interactive system
    3 - Identification ... with character

    Go game!
