\n I have tended to think of game rulesets as things that players slot into. I now think this is wrong.

A game ruleset is a tool that people use to intervene in the social system that is a game group \u2014 to change the way that game group behaves. Game groups, especially long term ones, are much bigger, more complex, and more resilient than any mere ruleset they can be. Rulesets, themselves, are dead matter \u2014 they cannot compete with life unless lifeforms champion them.

The long form of this idea is on my blog. Any thoughts appreciated.

    \n Not so keen on the lower-status-job thing. It gives a wrong vibe on how you value other people, or at least does looking from this side of the language barrier -correct me if I'm wrong.

    Otherwise I'm so glad to see this written. I've been feeling for years that there was something quite inefficient on, as a designer, giving the readers advice on how to run your game outside the rules chapters of the book. The procedures and phrases the designer uses to run the game are as much important to me as the actual rules to know how it's meant to be understood and run.

    I loved how exact ritual phrases from the designers helped more than their explications to understand how their games are meant to be run, and loved even more when those phrases were a hard component of the system instead of an advice on how to run it.

    I felt the same when I had to read tons of advice in order to learn how to actually run a game right, or how it is that you only get system mastery when you have learned not only a big chunk of the game rules but a big chunk of your group ritual phrases to trigger things, as well as when to apply them.

    Thus, no wonder some people have a harder time with some games at the start but then things click when they try different things, read comments or just see it from a different point of view. It took me years to finally get the basics behind PbtA games, basically until I read World of Dungeons and a manual to understand Dungeon World. The hardest thing for me to get was how was the GM was meant to run the thing without rolling dice, until I understood that the idea was that only when the players failed a roll was that bad things started to happen; and then, they happened in two stages at least: a threat, and when the reaction to that threat failed, a consequence.

    However in general terms this would mean that designing a game is an exercise in convincing other people to change their ways, something so akin to railroading and control that no wonder people often dislike this approach to game design and keep telling me that advice is okay, that you can't force people into playing your game the way you want them to play it and that doing so is disrespectful to the reader. Subconsciously anyone will object to any form of coercion.

    So basically rulebooks are being written in the right politically correct way now and that does not need to change, nor it's actually an improvement to design them with this particular frame of mind.

    \n I think what you're basically describing is a dynamic which I think is very common in the RPG hobby, but not necessarily universal: Lots of tables have a sort of preconceived concept of what functions an RPG is supposed to be fulfilling, and when encountering a new system they frequently just default back to to the sort of "meta-RPG" they never really stop playing.

    This is encouraged/enabled by the fact that the RPG hobby is permeated by the meme that rules are disposable. Statements like:

    - You should just fudge the results.
    - Ignore the rules if you need to!

    And a widespread culture of kitbashing which is not inherently problematic, but does get weird when people start radically houseruling a system before they've even played it... often to make it look just like every other RPG they've played. (For example, I had a discussion with a guy who said he didn't enjoy playing Numenera: Before play he'd decided he didn't like the point spend mechanic for resolving skill checks; didn't like XP spends for effect; and didn't like GM Intrusions so he didn't use any of those mechanics. He also radically revamped the central Effort mechanic worked in the game. Nothing inherently wrong with doing that, but he never actually played Numenera.)

    As a game designer, I find it incredibly difficult to get meaningful playtest feedback from RPG players because, by and large, none of them are actually playing the game

    Where I find these memes particularly disturbing is when I encounter them in other designers: People who are ostensibly designing robust rules for other people to use, but in whom the response to "just fudge around it" has become so ingrained that they do it while playtesting their own games instead of recognizing mechanical failures and figuring out how to fix them.

    \n I was going to protest, and say that a good system forms what people talk about, but reading the blog made me realize that you're actually talk about something else. That people bring their own conventions about roleplaying games into a game system, no matter what the game system says.

    Some experiences from my side, and this has happened to me several times.

    - People not reading all the rules, because "they know how to play roleplaying games".

    - People saying "This is not an RPG" if it doesn't fit their way of playing.

    - Masses and masses of people not reading game advice. I agree with @WarriorMonk here. It's wack design to even include game advice. This is why I say that Oktoberlandet is probably the best written game that exists, because the information in that book gets different meaning depending on how the reader interprets the text.     \n If a ruleset is an intervention, I'm becoming convinced it's a terribly half-assed intervention delivered over maybe the worst possible medium.

    Someone's used to playing that way, and you want them to play this way. You codify as much of this way as you can in rules, write it down, and send it over.

    You've got two problems right off the bat:

    First, the rules you wrote down, and even the advice you wrote along with it, are, like, only a small fraction of what you need to play this way.

    Second, they're dead words on paper, and your reader will probably ignore most of them.

    (I really don't understand the attitude of, "a good rules set will produce the desired play on its own." That's a pipe dream. Even the most virtuous of your rules sets will only produce your desired play when the reader basically already knows how you want them to play and has a strong desire to play that way.)

    But text is easy to create, and traditional, so we all keep using it, and we all keep thinking that The Text Is The Game. And rules are easy to convey in text\u2014technique and attitude and so on are really terribly hard, in part because people tend to just skip over that bit while reading\u2014so we get this idea that The Game Is The Rules. But that's bullshit, of course, which I guess is the thesis of this thread.

    But maybe a a well-crafted ruleset that does the best it can to produce the desired play when someone reads it is a bit like a well-crafted essay that does the best it can to prevent the transmission of STDs when someone reads it.

    Well meaning, and not entirely ineffective, but the wrong damn tool for the job.

    What's better? The best I can think of is live demonstration, teaching by playing, but that obviously doesn't scale. Youtube video series, maybe. A sort of pyramid scheme of teaching where you train people to play your game, and they train others, and you have a central place where you can all talk about it, maybe. That's all quite a bit harder than writing some words down and clicking "Save as .pdf", though.

    \n We've got to remember that RPGs are a multiple media experience. There are game mechanics, there are ritual and freeform social interactions, there's a fictional world shared mostly orally, and all this without considering using support media like images, sounds and music nor performance arts to transmit the characters emotions or classical poetry/literature-level-like dialogue and descriptions.

    Trying to explain how all the simultaneously-moving parts work using just text is of course the less efficient way, beaten justly by learning while playing. You could say that video is the second best way to explain it, followed by audio, comics/flux diagrams, then actual play and finally explaining everything in written form. However I'd say that perhaps each part could be better explained in their native media.

    You would end up with something like a core game document made in mixed media, with written mechanics/flux diagrams that include video links of actual play with real people with some scenes in comic book format to explain what's happening in the fictional world. Then you could print the mechanics for quick in-game reference once you have understood how everything works by watching the video.

    The part with real people playing would help a lot to get players assimilate easily how are ritual phrases supposed to work and identify when those and the mechanics should be used -as well as the tone of voice and gestures that accompany them for best effect. This is where the spirit of how are the fictional elements negotiated is learned by the players.

    The comic-book part would be there for marking the difference between the real world and the shared imagined world, where immersion rules and mechanics as well as social real-world negotiations are meant to be unobstrusive, and explaining what goes there as a reflection of how and when are the mechanics implemented and the results of the fiction being negotiated by the players.
