\n I confess this is from another section of the forum, but it just does not stop itching.

I am looking to create, participate, enact, what have you a play by post rpg with no need for a dice-roller. I am wondering how a post itself, the very thing that one writes resolves the conflict or task resolution. I am not even sure if there should be rules for this.

At first I thought, number of words, number of adjectives, or borrowing from Wushu where the more description the powerful something becomes. As a former teacher I even mused, whoever writes the best wins. But that some seems underhanded.

Writing, narration itself as resolution, can it work?

    \n I've been tinkering with a stripped down, diceless Dungeon World variant for long car trips. The basic idea is this: every time you make a move, you get a 7-9, a partial success.

    Characters start with 6 HP and their gear. You only have the generic Defy Danger move for all situations. The player tells what their character does and if it triggers the move, the GM describes a fundamental success with a choice or a cost based on the situation.

    In your combat example the character would wound (if the monster has more than 1 HP) or kill the opponent since their attack is fundamentally successful, but there would be a cost. The cost might be losing 1 HP, it might be e.g. getting thrown into a river or it might be both. The GM decides based on the situation. A weak orc wouldn't both wound the character and get to throw him, but a strong ogre could do that. For simplicity's sake any time you take harm, lose 1 HP. You fall unconscious at 0 HP.

    Resting refreshes HP back to 6, but the next grim portent tied to the overall situation happens. This makes sure the players don't just clear a room, rest and repeat.

    I've playtested it once and it works. It's a bit more taxing for the GM compared to regular DW since you need to come up with costs and choices all the time, but that's not a problem in the play-by-post format.

    \n To make it work and be successful, my instinct would would be to go GMless. Players would have distributed authority and collaborate to create the narrative. I would also design it to support a Dramatist approach to play. I would create rules so that during the set-up characters would be put in relationships with other PCs, NPCs, and Organizations that would produce complications, drama, conflict by their different desires/goals, values, temperament, alliances, the most important thing to them, or some other type of intrinsic interest or situational placement. Perhaps a set up something akin to Hillfolk, but it would be designed to fit my games goals/creative agenda. I would strip it down to just the essentials and try to figure out a system to do it as elegantly as I could (ie effective yet simple).

    During play I would have players take turns narrating scenes. Everyone would share all the characters and tell the story in turn. I would use a narrative mechanic wherein the players would vote on the direction of the narrative after pausing during a critical point in the given scene. The players would vote on whether a "good", "bad", or "mixed" result would happen to the character(s) that was the most critical to, and affected by, the story at that given point in the scene. It could also reflect what would happen to another critical thing in, or element of, the story other than the character(s), so long as it was an important moment in the story and the outcome would create dramatic weight, stakes, or something interesting in the narrative. So the director would pause the scene at a critical point in their narrative, in which the outcome for the most relevant character/event/etc. would create the most drama, narrative interest, stakes, etc.--basically, on the cusp of when the most critical element of the story within the scene presented itself. At this point, after the director paused the narrative, the players would all vote on which direction the narrative would take for the character(s)/event/etc. that was most critical at that point in the narrative, and would create the most compelling outcome, etc. Would the results be bad, good, or mixed for the character/event/etc.? The players would all type: "bad" or "good" or "mixed.' based on what they thought would produce the best story. If the vote results were a consensus, for example, if everyone voted for something "bad" to happen, then that is the direction the narrative and the rest of the scene would go and something "bad" would happen to the relevant character(s), ect. If the vote results were "mixed," then it would produce outcome that was "mixed." Again, these choices would be made based on what was best for the story, the play-style would be a Dramatist play-style. If the result was "bad," the first player that had voted for a "bad" outcome, and had an idea for the rest of the scene could type "idea;" they would then create a narrative for the rest of the scene that was bad for the character(s), etc. involved. If the result was "good" then the rest of the scene would be "good" for the character, etc. involved; again, the first player that voted for a "good" outcome, and had an idea for the rest of the scene would create the narrative for the rest of the scene. If the result was "mixed" the first player with an idea, who voted "good," would produce the "good" part of the scene and the first player with an idea, who voted "bad," would create the "bad" part of the scene. Or instead of the first player with an idea, players could just take turns creating the second part of the scene, if it made playing by post easier. Play until everyone feel the story is finished.

    Hmm...I thing I may actually make this game.

    \n It might be a good idea to use narrative authority distribution mechanics as your resolution tool.

    For example, you can use the other players' input as a randomizer (like in The Sundered Land: a Doomed Pilgrim in the Ruins of the Future or in A Penny for my Thoughts) or you can rule that: every X paragraphs there must be a "challenge". You can continue the narration as long as you fail the "challenge" or may opt to stop an let other player continue the story (starting with the narration of your success).

    \nIt was just a half-cooked idea to illustrate my point (you can use other players' input as randomizer) and, yes, it gets better if people can interrupt your narration at any moment (it solves the problem of deciding who must continue the narration: the challenger!) but I was thinking about play-by-e-mail or play-by-post which makes necessary to put some limit about the length of each narrative contribution.

    I've been thinking about narrative rights distribution mechanics for a while and I'm quite convinced that they are the way to go if you want something minimalist, generic and easy to play. For example, in the system that I'm currently working on there are 4 outcomes:\n
    • Yes!
      • \n
    • Yes, but...
      • \n
    • No, but...
      • \n
    • No!
    \nI find most natural that the player gets to narrate the Yes! and No, but... outcomes and that the challenger (usually a GM) gets to narrate the Yes, but... and No! outcomes, because you have to be creative only half of the time and always for your own interest!

    So you can go diceless using the same idea (you can narrate a fail a continue the story as you wish or you can get a success by delegating the narration in other player) and the result should be quite similar.
