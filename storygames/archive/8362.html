\n
\n So the evening before last two of my friends and I had some free time, and I suggested we play In A Wicked Age. I hadn't played it before (and neither had they) but I was keen because of the whole quick-prep thing combined with the idea that it's easily extended into an ongoing thing without a big time commitment up front.

Anyway, we decided we wanted to play something intrigue-y, and picked the Nest of Vipers oracle. We came up with a situation involving a band of pirate raiders in a small town at the crux of some trade routes and their rivalry with the local merchants. It took a few minutes to get the idea of Best Interests across, but the both of the players (who I'll call C and R) managed to come up with what seemed to me like two good ones, both putting them into conflict with other characters in areas which weren't their best. I had four or five NPCs with Best Interests mostly aimed at making life a pain for the PCs.

So, we started playing, and stuff happened, and the story gradually developed, but it was damned hard to get any sort of conflict started. There were plenty of heated discussions and plotting and negotiating, but since none of that stuff leads to conflicts in IaWA, it didn't feel like it led to much in the way of development. We only had two dice conflicts, both with the same PC against the same NPC, one of which the PC won in the first round and the other of which the NPC took the advantage die in the first round and then ran away handily with the rest of the conflict. I looked for opportunities to throw PCs into conflicts, but couldn't come up with any more where I didn't feel that suddenly springing an action scene of some sort on them would have been a violation of what we knew about the status quo.

So, I'm thinking that we did the genre wrong. Does IaWA work when the characters are prone to talking and negotiating over everything and never getting their hands dirty? Should I have just pushed harder and had some NPCs try to start a fight or otherwise goad the PCs into concrete action, even if it felt forced?

    \n There were heated discussions, but the one side always just folded? That may be best interests not enough at stake (not crossing each other), may also be "do folks realize they signed up to be in a story and in a story it gets interesting when someone doesn't just fold." As GM, if you put the sworn enemies face to face over something they don't agree about, you're pretty much done your job - if they decide to have tea and split it evenly, well not much you can do.

    Also there has been some talk about what forms of force/coercion/violence you can represent in the game.

    I've had games where 1/2 the conflicts weren't knives and wrasslin, but rather trash talking in front of an audience whose views would essentially "force" the decision (it wasn't about convincing the other of your argument, it was about humiliating/tricking the other with your words so that audience would agree with you). Similarly, blackmail and psychological terrorizing are forms of coercion.

    \n What Joshua said is true. If someone isn't going to beat the fuck out of someone else then the rules aren't going to help you a lot.

    One of the things that I've found helps get the game into this mode is to have an NPC (or two, or ten) who is going to start punching first, fast, and always. It works sort of on Raymond Chandler's dictate of "having two men with a gun come through the door." When the PCs get too soft footed, to working things out through negotiation and open, honest dialog and all those things we want in real life and not in S&S fiction, someone comes and is going to kill the fuck out of someone else.

    The other thing about this style of game is that its all about fucking up the status quo. You've got merchants and pirates locked in a status quo? Dude, you've got to shake up the status quo. If the players won't do it, then someone else will. Someone is going to kill the pirate king (is it a pirate? a merchant? a pirate working for a merchant?), set fire to a merchant's house, and do something else. If you have a status quo situation, the story is going to happen when someone take the decisive action to fuck over the status quo.

    So when you say "I didn't feel that suddenly springing an action scene of some sort on them would have been a violation of what we knew about the status quo" it leaves me wanting details. When did you establish this status quo? Where were the best interests of the PCs and NPCs in relation to it? Who was being fucked by the status quo and would knife someone to change it?

    Also, in the conflicts you did have, how did you resolve them when the beat down happened? Because really, a conflict in which someone gets kicked around often leads to other conflicts, but it sounds like in your game they ended flat or ended things totally, without spinning off into further obsession and madness. So what happened with them? Who negotiated what, and how did it hit the fiction? Like, after the dude tried to stab the captain in the back, what happened to the dude and the captain and the rest of the crew?

    \n

    Believe it or not, that's functioning mechanics. The players negotiated as soon as violence was on the table. But to really engage fully, the characters are going to go one step further. What if the guy couldn't concede the argument that easily? What if he fought tooth and nail? That's when the negotiation gets really interesting.

    \n
    \n

    After they planned it all out, I didn't feel like it would have been fair to deliberately have the NPC screw up just to bring him into conflict with the merchant. Maybe I should have.

    \n
    \n

    You're not bound by anything but what negotiation leads to to bind by. If characters make a promise in negotiation, it's exactly like if a person makes a promise: they determine if they're going to keep it. If negotiation gives you a hostage, then they're more likely to keep it than not, probably. If you're promising things without being forced to make those promises, you're not getting the system to fire on all cylinders. If you're not actively extracting changes from characters when negotiating, you're not negotiating hard enough.

    \n

    I recommend that, if the characters are negotiating and not getting their hands dirty, that they make good use of particular strengths that give them far-reaching abilities. Spies, bribery, reputations, what-have-you. That way, the characters can """keep their hands clean""" (that gets extra sarcasm quotes) while their agents do the dirty work.

    \n

    If they don't have such strengths, you should be making them wish they did. In the game, assassins, spies, extorted ex-lovers and current seducees are either strengths or weaknesses. They are represented in best interests and particular strengths. Make sure they're there and the players care about them.

    \n My standard for playing In A Wicked Age.... is this: Is there a weapon involved? Where "weapon" includes anything intended to harm the person which includes emotional and psychological weapons.

    If you're in a negotiation and and it's just a heated exchange of ideas. No dice. But if someone says, "I saved your life back in the war, you owe me!" that's a weapon: guilt. If it helps think of it as this declaration, "I pull out guilt and use it to cut out your resolve." "Oh, no you don't!"

    Same with bribery. Money put on the table as a fair and even offer no matter how duplicitous? No dice. Money put on the table accompanied by "you wouldn't want your wife and children to starve would you?" Weapon.

    The person still doesn't have to give in even if they lose but beating off that kind of emotional attack sure is Exhausting.

    \n I think there is wisdom about conflicts to be found in the text. I quote the Book:

    For the Players:
    \u2026when you\u2019re naming her best interests, look at the other characters and guess where they\u2019re going to be strong. Cast your character\u2019s best interests against them there.

    For the GM:
    You\u2019ll take the lead by naming best interests that are direct threats to the players\u2019 characters\u2026Jump in with your NPCs\u2019 best interests opportunistically, whenever you can add threats, complications, or the potential for violence, or whenever two players\u2019 characters are starting to look too cozy together. If you look for the angles, the leverage you can use to wedge them apart, you\u2019ll find it.

    Playing With Conflict
    Choose two characters who want to do harm to one another. Arrange circumstances so that one of the characters has a sudden, momentary, immediate advantage\u2026

    Choose two characters who want to do NO harm to one another at all, but whose interests don\u2019t mesh well or overlap. Arrange circumstances so that one has the opportunity to pursue her interests, but only by threatening the interests of the other.

    Posted By: AlanHm. I initiated a dice conflict once by declaring "Your character falls in love with mine."
    \nJust to back up Ralph and for the benefit of the peanut gallery: By rule, this statement means nothing in IAWA. In the wicked age you say what your own character does and if someone interferes, then you go to dice over the concrete actions and consequences to determine who's injured or exhausted. You can say "Bob's character falls in love" but it doesn't mean anything. It's just table talk, and everyone is free to ignore it.

    Most critically, you never, ever "have a conflict" to "see if you fall in love with me." I see people play IAWA this way, and it makes me cringe.

    \n Coming at this from another direction:

    It is also worth noting that IAWA is difficult with only a GM and two players. Not that you can't play it that way, but that everyone has to work hard in order to do so.

    I think there are two reasons for this: first, there aren't enough combinations of different alliances--essentially the players are either working together or working against each other; and, second, because there are fewer players to initiate conflict with each other--the game really kicks off when one player goes after another, and the more players you have the more likely it is to happen. In both these cases, two players is not quite enough for the central dynamic to get going easily.

    \n Or here?\n
    In Wicked you don't roll dice for conversation. But that doesn't have to mean you don't roll dice for social conflicts.

    I think Wicked often works best when "conversation" means "not forcing." Once you have decided that you are going to do something, or make someone do something, and you no longer are interested in letting them have a choice then dice are on.

    So, if you're like "Sure, words are my weapon, but if bitch doesn't bow down and do as he's told, he's going to end up broken as a human being by the end of the scene" then dice may well fit.

    Cause really, its all about having dice only when the things being done are going to fit the consequences. If what you're doing can't result in the person being injured or exhausted, then what you're doing doesn't make mechanical sense as a place to be rolling dice. If you can't think how the person refusing to do what you want, or failing to stop you, won't result in them bleeding or unconscious or weeping out of control then you shouldn't be rolling dice just because what the system outcome will be won't make sense. But if you can see how that would happen, and are at the point where they are either going to do what you want or get that stick... then roll away.

    --Brand_Robins (Endorsed by the Vincent Baker)

    Posted By: jessecoombsIAWA seems very concrete as far as the difference between the physical and the not, and I'm having trouble understanding where the confusion comes from.
    \nI don't find it so concrete between physical and not, though that line probably works for some groups.

    I think the confusion over what you can and can't do with dice in IAWA comes from assumptions brought in from other games about what winning a conflict means. In Dogs if you go to a conflict because you want to make some other character love you and you win the conflict, that other character loves you. In Nine Worlds, ditto. In PTA, ditto. In Other Game With Stakes Resolution, probably ditto.

    So folks play IAWA and treat the initial condition that started the conflict as stakes. Which works, so long as you never, as a group, push on it in any way or have anyone have a problem with rolling with it. But once someone does that, things get wonky fast. (It can also limit the scope of your actions to treat the initial condition as stakes. You could start out trying to force someone to marry you, and end up half the world away freezing to death by the end of the conflict in IAWA, but if you limit the actions to resolution of the initial state you'll never get that.)

    Really, I think a good part of the confusion is that people expect IAWA to play like most of the other Story Games they've played, and it doesn't.
