    \n A situation that demands action, with a clear goal, and play that keeps moving forward.

    The key mistakes I see con GMs make, again and again, is wasting time with: a) getting the characters together in the first place, b) justifying their involvement, and c) making their way to the actual start of the freakin' adventure, i.e., figuring out what the heck the adventure is in the first place.

    To use a typical dungeon-crawl as an example, the players should not have to meet up in a tavern, get offered an job, equip themselves, and then make a long overland journey to before they get to the dungeon entrance. If the dungeon is the point of the con event, start the game at the entrance to the dungeon, just as the PCs are stepping downward into the darkness.

    From a story-games perspective, there should be a situation pregnant with crisis that hits all of the PC's flags and hits them hard. Whatever relationships the PCs may have should all point to the other PCs, if possible. For example, here's the r-map for "Fires Over Omac," a BE demo from Crane and co.



    You can barely read it with all the links connecting the PCs and NPCs to each other.

    So, basic summary, as expressed in an old song-writing adage: "Don't bore us, get to the chorus."

    Posted By: Adam DrayPlayers who care and get involved, despite the distractions.
    \nThis is the most imperative. It's gotten me through dozens of awesome games, be it AD&D 2e at GenCon back in the days of the giant hockey ring in Milwaukee, to Tenra, The Fantasy Trip and Blossoms in the hallway (during construction) at the latest GenCon.

    Personally, I find that (as GM, or player), I tend to push the chair away and stand during the session, particularly when high action/drama goes down. Kinda like the dudes who go All In in the Texas Hold-Em tourneys. It brings me more energy, too.

    Every once in a while, there's noise or screaming from the next table over. As long as they're being enthusiastic and not just aggressive shitbags, it's rarely a distraction.

    But all in all, it's aiming for enthusiastic players.

    I'd have no idea what to do if I was running or playing a game where The Pickle Guy showed up, though.

    \n Good con game checklist:

    - Everyone introduce themselves and shake hands! I know this sounds stupid and basic but man, oh man...

    - Get to it, give me the information, the hand-out or the process of chargen so that we can get right on the same page and get to gaming, dammit. If chargen will take a minute more than a half-hour...pre-gens with motivations, I say.

    - If the noise is too much, move the game somewhere else. There's almost always a quieter table elsewhere if you are willing to walk a ways.

    \n To reemphasize and add to what Buzz was saying, not only should you not waste a lot of time on introductory stuff, but your scenario should have a clear direction from the get-go and not expect a lot of difficult decisions from the start.     \n On what Judd said about shaking hands...
    \n
    \nSo we're at the table at GenCon, and a guy is looking for a game at Games on Demand. So somebody suggests that he sit in on one of my playtests of Other Worlds. So I try to be social. The conversation went something like:
    \n
    \nMe: "Hi, I'm Mike. Tell us who you are, and, could you tell us what RPGs you're into?"
    \n(See I'm trying to get some context so I can explain how OW works to him)
    \n
    \nGuy: (Fails to introduce himself) "Uh, I play all sorts of stuff."
    \n
    \nMe: "Cool... can you tell us what the last RPG you played with your group back home was?"
    \n
    \nGuy: "Um... we play so much stuff I can't recall."
    \n
    \nMe: "Uh... OK... what have you played at the convention so far?"
    \n
    \nGuy: (starting to look like a cornered rat) "Uh... mostly I've been wandering around."
    \n
    \nMe: (listening carefully to what's going on around me) "What's that you say Kat? There's a game of Faery Noir going on that needs more players? Well I have one more than I prefer to run with. Hey, dude, you should try out this other game that's going on over there. It would help us a lot!"
    \n
    \n
    \nI said to the people at the table (to whom it was patently clear as to what I'd done and why) that I felt guilty, but only because I had foisted this guy off on another game. Call me the big bad elitist, but if you don't have basic social skills, enough to give your name, and talk about the RPGs you've played previously, I'm sorry... but I don't want to play with you. I don't think this is a very high bar I'm asking people to get over, and almost everyone does just fine. I'm a shy person too... but I've always taken steps to try to be at least minimally social with the people I meet at cons.
    \n
    \nBecause if you're not comfortable with actually socially meeting with people, then how do you expect to have fun playing a RPG with them? It's not chess.

    \n Strangely, shaking hands was one of the things I took away from Gateway this past weekend.

    I get it. We're meeting strangers. Sometimes we know some people, but not others. Or we're the lone guy or gal at the table who doesn't know everyone else. I mean, con events are weird that way. We're going to meet up as strangers and then HIDE our identities and not talk about anything about us for four hours. There are parties... and then there's this.

    Aware of this, whenver I was at a game that wasn't filed with people that knew each other, I'd just jump forward and go, "Hi, I'm CK," and shove my hand before them. The first time I did it was just sort of out of panic, like, "Oh, shit we're not even doing the basics of being nice, if we can't handle this, the game's gonna suck!" Then it just became a thing I decided I had to do.

    What struck me about this -- the reason it made such an impression -- was that on three separate occasions this prompted this peculiar chain reaction where everyone suddenly shoving their hands in front of people they didn't know and started introducing themselves around the table. It was like there was this pent up desire to do the formalities of being nice, but that we had no context for doing them. But once someone broke the ice, the way people were making sure to introduce themselves was almost a relief.

    We talked about this on another thread -- the open socializing model that leads to gaming rather than jumping into gaming at cons. But I think we need to face the fact cons have YEARS of training behind them where social graces were left at the door, connection to actual people was left at the door.

    From my experience at this weekend, it's hardly a deal stopper. As far as I can tell all it takes is one person to start things rolling and everyone jumps to the chance.

    Now, I had no one like Mike's story. I was lucky, maybe. 80% of the people I played with I had never met before. But there were no jerks. Everyone was great.

    But I also think -- if my experience at Gateway is any indication -- there's a shift taking place in Con culture.

    CK

    \n Oh, and for better or worse, I arrived with no pre-gen characters and what not for either my PtA games or Sorcerer & Sword. I've come to believe that creating characters and situation is a vital part of play, and I don't want to cheat the con players of that part of play.

    I was up front. I said, "I can't guarantee we're going to reach a cool end of con session climax. I won't be rushing anything to get to the big finish. What we're going to do is play the game. And the game starts -- here..." and I'd distribute the character sheets.

    I don't know whether everyone was satisfied. But I was. As far as I was concerned we were playing, and as far as I could tell all the players were engaged from that moment on.

    CK

    Edited to Add: It occurred to me that the games I played in worked this way as well: Grey Ranks, Poison'd, Psi-Run, and Shock. We started from scratch with character creation. Not one game had pre-planned scenes or a prepped climax of any kind. So, that was my con.

    \n Speaking of reaching the end...

    I find that it's best to structure your games with optional scenes or potential activities. Some players may take a while to get through some challenges and/or get more engaged with interaction and other unplanned activities. But some will whip right through it. So to stay flexible, I make at least one "drop in" scene/encounter/activity.

    \n I've only been to two cons and a few game day events. Introductions are the best things ever, even if you forget the person's name halfway through the game. I had a pretty dull game experience, and it started from the beginning when I, a player, suggested that we all use the nametags the GM brought with him. (The badges were on yard-long lanyards, so they hung below the table.) The GM wrote his name on his. I wrote my name and my character's name on mine. The three others just wrote their character's names. I even tried to introduce myself to the other players. I have no idea who those people were.

    The other thing I like is to leave takeaways for the game. I let the players keep their character sheets and always point out the URL for dog-eared-designs.com at the bottom of my PTA character sheets in hopes that someone will pick up a copy of the game (it's worked once that I know of), or a flyer with info on how to pick up the game and communities like Story Games, or related things. (For my Lacuna game, I gave whomever opened the mysterious folder I left on the table 823 experience points to spend in any game of their choosing.)
