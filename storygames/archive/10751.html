\n In the Gonzometer thread, I asked Ralph:\n
Hey Ralph, so stuff like this scene from Batman: the Brave and the Bold would fit that definition, right?\n


"Are you seeing what I'm seeing? Because I'm seeing gorillas, riding pterodactyls, with harpoon guns, stealing a boat."
\nWhich in turn gave me an idea:\n
As the PCs roll into a new town, each makes an observation and asks "Are you seeing what I'm seeing?" That gives each player an easy way to confirm, clarify or augment that observation until everyone comes to an agreement about what that town is like. It establishes a baseline from which any deviation may be considered gonzo.
\nSo this is a very loose idea, but it seems like the start of Polaris-like branching discussion of ritual phrases.

"I see some elves meditating on the tips of branches overhead. Are you seeing what I'm seeing?"
"Yup. And over there are some priests of the elf god praying for the arrival of saviors from a malevolent entity. Are you seeing what I'm seeing?"
"Yup, but they're actually praying for elven saviors. Too bad we're all dwarves. Are you seeing what I'm seeing?"

Thoughts?

    \n While its great for roleplaying to be informed by proven improv techniques, I think its a mistake to assume that there's a 1:1 translation between the two. Roleplaying has different needs. While a "no blocking" rule might work well in some cases, in others...blocking is a good thing. There are only two ways to manage a consistant vision. One is the Vulcan Mindmeld where everyone at the table is so perfectly attuned to the exact same thing that it just works. The other is to block things that are too far outside of the vision to be absorbed effectively. If nobody has the power to block...then the vision eventually frays or is diluted to the point of being unrecognizable.
    \n
    \nIts different when everyone is just interested in riffing off of ideas and seeing where it goes, and every road is just as good as any other. But when you sit down at a table to achieve a specific thing but don't have any recourse response...then essentially everyone is held hostage to the individual whim of everyone else. Which in some applications is cool. It can be good to be forced out of a comfort zone and have to creatively respond to something out of left field. But other times its ass.

    \n <2cents>"No blocking" is important in improv because momentum is important. Blocking stalls momentum while the actors think of another idea, and all the while the audience is watching actors, well, think.
    \n
    \nThat's simply not an issue in roleplaying.
    \n
    \nBlocking is bad in RPGs when it happens all the time, such that a player is never able to make a meaningful contribution, or when it's done to deliberately make someone upset. So as long as you're not the Big Evil GM kinda guy who shoots down everyone's ideas, and you're not trying to make your fellow players upset, blocking is not a problem.
    \n</2cents>

    \nHey Daniel,

    In general, I love A Penny For My Thoughts.
    The prompting towards building on what others offer you is cool.

    But the "Guiding Questions" are a bit of a let down for me. Here's why: someone asks a question, and you reply "Yes, and..."
    They aren't questions. They are statements that the lead player is forced to build upon.

    And so if the second memory trigger for my character (who we've thus far established is a quiet, mousy married guy) is "the taste of semen"... perhaps you're genuinely curious about whether this means he's gay, or if he's just confused, if this is a first, if this is his regular Thursday routine...

    But asking "So, is he gay?" is the equivalent of saying "He's gay... build on that."
    Asking "So, is he confused and experimenting?" is the equivalent of saying "He's just confused and experimenting."
    "Is this his first?" is the same thing as "This is his first".

    And it's disappointing that you can't use the guiding questions to... ask a question.

    Let's say I observe the following:

    "I think I see a young girl playing with her pet bear cub in the forest. Are you seeing what I'm seeing?"

    Using 1001 Nights' method, you could respond in the negative or affirmative, but either way I would get some reward. In other words:

    Yes - I get a reward AND my contribution is accepted.
    No - I get a reward to salve my ego.

    Possible alternatives include:

    Option 1:
    Yes - I get a reward AND my contribution is accepted.
    No - I get no reward and no contribution. Total shutdown.

    Option 2:
    Yes - I get no reward, just the satisfaction of my contribution being accepted.
    No - I get a reward to salve my ego, but that might encourage me to keep suggesting things I know will be rejected.

    I'm sure there are other permutations, too.

    \n The other option, apart from Yes or No, is asking for a rephrase, in the style of Polaris' "You ask far too much".

    Done Polaris-style, you would have a key phrase that asks for a rephrase:

    Player 1: Yup, but they're actually praying for elven saviors. Too bad we're all dwarves. Are you seeing what I'm seeing?
    Player 2: I don't see it yet.
    Player 1: Perhaps they're praying for elven saviours, but none of us are elves.

    That does rather beg for some way to indicate what you don't like, though. The other way is to have a phrase that rephrases the other person's thing:

    Player 1: Yup, but they're actually praying for elven saviors. Too bad we're all dwarves. Are you seeing what I'm seeing?
    Player 2: What I'm seeing is that they're praying for elven saviours, but none of us are elves.
