[Experiment] Chain of Contrasts

TonyLB
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
Okay, I've got this idea in my head for a character brain-storming thing. I could get people together and do it, but that would require physical presence. I could do it on a MUSH, but that would require people to be online. Screw it. I'm usin' some volunteers from here.

Here's the deal: We are making the conflict-optional parts of characters for ... making something up off the top of my head ... a group of people on a lightly magicked boat in a heavily magical world of archipelagoes and peninsulae ... lots of ocean, lots of little snippets of land, frequent but sporadic social contact between them, resulting in a plethora of cultures and problems. Our people are do-gooders on the run from an expansionist empire. It's a classic stern chase scenario.

So we're making pieces of characters: We will do so in the following way.

- I will propose an attribute for my character
- Somebody will volunteer that their character has an attribute that contrasts with the attribute I proposed
- They will further volunteer a different attribute (related, unrelated, whatever) of their own
- Return to step two, but with a different person. So somebody else would say something that contrasts with the newest attribute
- At some point, five distinct posters (including myself) will have contributed. At that point the thread is closed to new participants. We stick with our five.
- However, the game continues, with old posters jumping back into the action to further detail and enmesh their characters.
- Nobody does more than three substantive posts about their character (for six attributes total) except me (see below).
- When all posters have had three substantive posts, I make one last contrasting attribute for my character (bringing me up to the common value of six) and we take a look at what kind of crew we've assembled.
- At that point, the thread goes wide open to anyone who wants to post, as I (at least) will be tearing apart the results and seeing whether the experiment generated something useful.

So, an example of how things might go:
Tony: "Honest"
Geraldine: "Diplomatic and selfish"
Hobarth: "Considerate and shy"
Jimmy: "Outspoken and impatient"
Hobarth: "Long-suffering and sad"
... etc. ...

And here we go: My character, Montgomery Olson (that's "Mr. Olson" while on duty) the first mate and general executive officer is Formal.

    So ... I've been thinking about this. It strikes me that as the traits became more specific, they rapidly became less useful to making a compelling character and (perhaps more importantly) less useful to connecting characters in contrast.

    For instance: In the early game we had Elbert's intimidating bearing contrasted with Polly's retiring demur. That's easy to get to, in any scene we play, ever. They can establish those two traits in contrast in the first five seconds of every scene.

    In the later rounds, we got that Shelton believes that if he can be pure then God will reward him, whereas Anur believes in the importance of works, not faith. I'd be amazed if that came up more than once every couple of sessions. It's a much harder target.

    So ... if folks disagree with me on that general idea, then let's talk about that. Do the particulars make it easier to bring things in? I say no. Someone else might say yes.

    And I'm not leaving out the people who agree as far as it goes: Maybe the more specific contrast-pairs are harder to get to, but worth the effort. Frankly, I think that the more general ones are, in fact, more useful to defining the character and making them likable. The specific ones strike me (again, my opinion) as being both harder to use and less useful. But I'm certainly open to discussing it.

    Darn.

    Okay. I'll try again.

    If you have an attribute which doesn't contrast with anyone else then you can easily get shoved into a situation where you have to choose: Do I play this attribute or do I support other people in playing up their attributes?

    If your attribute has a built in contrast with another character then it's much harder to find yourself facing that choice: If you play the attribute then it's hard to avoid supporting other people in playing up their attributes at the same time.

    The "either-or" choice leads to zero-sum games inside of your own head. You have to balance two things, and getting more of one means getting less of another.

    The "both at the same time" situation lets you avoid that. There's no balancing. You do your thing and it all works out.

    I suspect that if you do that then all traits end up in the same category, because people won't naturally cross category lines in trying to find something contrasting, and everybody will be doing much tighter links of contrast. If two things are loosely linked as part of my internal contrast then I'm happy to say "I'm smart, and I'm heavily muscled," and know that I can work that contrast. I'm confident because (a) I control both sides of the equation and (b) if I fail I only screw myself.

    But if someone else says "I'm smart" then I'm not going to reply with "I'm heavily muscled." I wouldn't have the same certainty that I could make the contrasts work as part of a team. So I might say "I'm stupid" or "I'm humble" or "I'm mature," but I'm not going to say "I'm big," or "I'm psychic" or "I'm scared." Those are enough of a category drift that they seem to risky.

    Which, BTW, JBR is why it turned out as a victorian novel. You started with the category of social role. Start with "I'm big and strong," for instance, and I think you'll see it evolve into a Mission Impossible style team description.
