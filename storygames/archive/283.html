Player-Group Dynamics, and their effect on pace of game and spotlight time

Random Nerd
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Play Advice
So, lately, I've been pondering what factors seem to cause the games I play or run in to significantly differ in feel. And it seems that one of the most significant factors is the ways that the styles of the players interact.

For example...
I run a Nobilis game. During those times when the flow of play is dominated by player-to-player interaction, it tends to be a slow-paced game. The biggest problem with it, that I can see, is that the group sometimes tends towards indecisiveness even over very minor issues. However, the spotlight time is generally quite even.

I play in a Buffy game. In this game, scenes that are based on PCs talking with each other have a lot more energy and dynamism, even when there's nothing on the line but interparty relationships. Also, debates about actually doing things tend to be resolved quickly in favor of "Well, here's a half-assed plan, which we'll do with vigor and verve. If it doesn't work, we'll just make up a new one on the fly." However, the spotlight time on this game is a lot less even, with some characters having more focus time than others.


As far as I can tell, both of these factors are based largely on the proactive/reactive stance of the players. In the Buffy game, one or two of the players tend to take much more active, dramatic, and flashy approaches to the game. This has a couple effects. First, it resolves "what-do-we-do" disagreements faster, since it's less considered weighing of options and more jumping hotheadedly into the breech. Second, it makes it so that those characters have more focal screen time, especially when they're arguing with each other. Now, when one of the more active characters is arguing with one of the more passive ones, the passive one tends to perk up some for the duration of the interaction, but interactions between the more passive characters tend to have less zest.

In the Nobilis game, everyone seems to have more the same stance. They're more likely to have their characters do nothing than to have their characters do something rash, and tend to be less likely to take the initiative without a push from the GM. Thus, the game has a more even feel in spotlight time since there aren't a couple of people who tend to be the first to jump ahead, but it gets bogged down a lot faster. The archetypal example of this in the game, which we joke about some, is when a group of Nobles spent half an hour talking about the best way to move a box a block and a half.

Now, I feel that if I could better understand these dynamics, it would probably make it easier to keep the feel and pace of games at where it should be. But any sort of all-way interaction between a group of people over a length of time will tend to be quite complicated and intricate, so it's tough to get an understanding of the subtleties of how these things work. The more obvious aspects, and the generalities... they're easier to spot, and they are helpful, but not as much as a closer understanding would be.

So, to what degree do my observations and comments here seem to be consistant with what all of you have seen? Provide me with additional information, and add your brains to mine in a grotesque analytical hivemind!

Or we could just discuss this. That would work too.

    Well, thinking back to recent experiences, I've noticed that splitting the party is, far from a bad idea, a great way of picking up the pace and sharing spotlight time. On the other hand, both cases that I can think of were sort of instigated by me as a player. In a Dogs in the Vineyard game, the process of arriving at the town and making social arrangements (everybody wanted the honor of putting the Dogs up) resulted in conflicting obligations that I resolved by volunteering my character to stay with his aunt while the other PC stayed with the town Steward. So the ensuing action was split between scenes with each player dominating a scene.

    Then in the Harn campaign I just got involved in, the PCs were summoned by their friends/patrons to hunt down a missing artifact. The scenario started with the entire party visiting the scene of the crime and very carefully questioning the witnesses. I'm not sure what the GM would have done, but it would have been easy to imagine the entire party moving from clue to clue. What prevented this from happening was the impression of urgency: it was believed that whoever had the item was going to take it somewhere where it wouldn't be possible to recover it. So I suggested that the party split up and carry out tasks that would be most appropriate to our skills and social contacts. I think this worked well because different people got spotlight time. Now, it worked very well for me since I got a big scene pretty much to myself, but I think the other players also enjoyed watching it play out.

    If I were a GM trying to actively encourage this sort of development, I think I'd bring it up with the group and suggest if they were interested in any of the following:

    --Playing scenarios where time was truly of the essence. I think some players really don't want to spend all day planning, but they'd feel more comfortable knowing that there's an in-game reason to speed up their decisionmaking.

    --Situations that work well with characters operating in parallel. Issues of firewalling should possibly be discussed. (In the Harn game, there was some effort at firewalling but it was also played pretty loosely at other times. And in fact some of the scene cutting was done in a way that tacitly encouraged collaboration and "fortuitous" timing. E.g., a magical message was sent from a character in one scene at exactly the moment to resolve a quandary for a character in another scene.)

    --Naturally, assuaging any paranoia about taking action separately or doing so without considering every possible angle. (Then again, this is only a problem if it's ruining somebody's fun. Including yours. But in that case, I think it'd be best to put it in exactly those terms.)

    --Going with the above, allowing some degree of retroactive planning, as in "Of course I would have brought a widget. Who goes to snark hunt without a widget?"
