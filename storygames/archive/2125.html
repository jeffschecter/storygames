Stars and bombs

Graham
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Game Design Help
I've been thinking about this mechanic for a while. Could you tweak it, suggest changes, add bits to it and so on?

So it's a GM-less game called Heist. There are crime-themed challenges, each with a difficulty number. For example: Security Keypad, Difficulty Level 3.

And there's a deck of cards. They're custom cards, consisting of:
  • No stars
  • One star
  • Two star
  • Three stars
  • One bomb
  • Two bombs
  • Three bombs
...and there are slightly more star cards than bomb cards. Every player gets dealt a hand of cards.

So, let's say someone wants to disable the security camera. He narrates how he wants to do it: "I wait for the guard to continue his patrol. The moment he leaves, I slip in, standing beneath the camera so I'm not in its field of vision. I yank the yellow wire out - that's the tampering alarm - then the red to disable the power feed."

Then each other player plays a card, face down, to show what they think of the narration: good narration earns lots of stars, bad narration earns no stars or bombs. Add one card, randomly dealt, from the pack.

Then turn the cards over. Bomb cards eliminate star cards, as follows:
  • If there are any one-bomb cards, take away all the one-star cards
  • If there are any two-bomb cards, take away all the two-star cards
  • and if there are any three-bomb cards, take away all the three-star cards
If the number of stars left is equal to or greater than the difficulty level, you win! If not, you lose.

That's it. It's not finished. I'm not quite sure how you get cards or how you refresh your hand: except that I don't want the hands to be continuously refreshed. Sometimes, I want a player holding one solitary bomb card in his hand, which he can't get rid of, and he knows will destroy the next challenge.

Can you either:
1. Add to it (e.g. "There's an Alert Level, from 1 to 3. Before the cards are turned for each challenge, take away a number of cards equal to the Alert Level.")
2. Tweak it (e.g. "Players don't get dealt cards. The other players give them cards as a reward for good narration outside of challenges."
3. Ask me questions and I'll make up answers on the spot (e.g. "What happens when you run out of cards?")
4. Make brilliant and incisive comments.

Thanks!

Graham

    Change it to 2 colors of dice in 3 sizes.

    Everyone puts down a green d2, d4, or d6 depending on how much you like the narration.
    or a red d2, d4, or d6 if you dislike.

    Roll all the dice. Subtract red from green. Result must beat difficulty number, for the action to prevail.

    All dice rolled are lost.

    --
    For each point by-which the difficulty number is beaten, the player attempting the action is rewarded a number of 'pips' equalling the (Difficulty #) x (number by-which the Diff# was exceeded).

    As pips are accumulated, they can be traded-in for the corresponding dice-- when the player desires: (2 pips buys a d2, Four pips buys 2d2 or 1d4. Six pips buys 3d2, 1d2+1d4, or 1d6). A d2 flip determines the color of the dice purchased.

    Difficulty numbers range from -3 to +3. Multiplying -3,-2,-1 and 0 by any 'number by which the Diff# was exceeded'... yields <0, so no pips are ever rewarded for beating easy difficulty numbers.

    No matter how much they lose, a player always keeps one last green d2 in their <s>hand pool.

    Or, to make it more of a tactical game, you either have open hands (everyone can see everyone else's hand), or you have a group of cards in the middle of the table that each person picks from in order - and keep the 3 bombs/3 stars, 2 bombs/2 stars thing. So then it becomes a tactical decision. If there's a bunch of 2-star cards and only one 2-bomb card, whoever goes first has to decide whether they want to have the hosing power, or take the 2-bomb so that no-one else can use it (and maybe miss out on the 3-star cards), or leave it and take another card that he thinks might be better.

    Other things to couple this with: everyone plays a card out of their hand to the center, and then you around and people pick cards; you get to keep unplayed cards that you pick (so, investing in big cards that aren't appropriate for the current round is a worthwhile stategy, and everyone will remember that you took the three-star, or whatever).

    Hope some of that helps. I love the # of bombs only affects # of stars things, but only if it works in a way that makes that a chosen, rather than a random, distinction.

    (Finished the level. It was hard.)

    So, on the scarcity thing. There's a couple of things I really like about it.

    Note that there'll usually be a choice to make. Most of the time, there'll be a choice between three or four different cards. Even if there's only two cards, there'll usually be a choice between the two, so you can signify good narration or bad narration. Now, that choice might be between two or three stars or between a bomb and a star, but there's still a choice.

    This has a couple of effects. Firstly, it introduces an element of randomness. The most brilliant narrator in the world can't guarantee everyone will give him three stars. Often, he'll only get two; rarely, he'll get a bomb.

    Secondly, it gives permission to play the low cards. If you narrate, and someone plays a bomb, it doesn't mean they hated your narration: perhaps they had no choice. Besides, you've got to play the bombs sometime.

    By the way, the technique with the bombs is to dispose of them when they'll do least harm. So, if you have a One Bomb card, the best time to dispose of it is during a brilliant piece of narration. Everyone else will play Two Stars and Three Stars and, so, there'll be no One Star cards for the bomb to eliminate. So there's an element of guessing what everyone else will play. And I like that.

    (This raises the interesting question of whether you can play two cards at once, of course, which could be very appropriate with Bomb cards).

    Thanks for all the ideas so far. If I haven't commented on them specifically, it doesn't mean I've ignored them: they've provoked a lot of thought. As you can probably tell, I've thought myself into a corner with this mechanic, and this is helping me organise my thoughts.

    I think that the objection Jason raised is correct. If there were some sort of tension between wanting to succeed, and wanting to reward narration, it might work. But it seems to me that these things both work in the same direction here. That is, I'm not seeing why anyone would, given the opportunity, reward tactical movement forward with less stars.

    That said, this might not be important. I'd say forget about the "narration" criteria. Just tell the player, "Here's your cards, you can play any of them on any contest at your whim for any reason you like."

    One thing you haven't made clear is why it is that at times somebody will have to get rid of the bomb card that they're holding. Unless it's simply that you don't get a new hand until you play all of your cards. That might work. Though one problem with such games is that there's a strong incentive to collude here, with no player involved having an incentive to detect it. That is players might just lay their hands down, and figure out how to get through play by applying the bombs at the right times to mitigate damage. Even if the rules say not to do so. Unless there's a GM or something to "catch" them at it.

    It might do to put some sort of competitive element in the game.

    On the other hand, Jason's original objection doesn't stand, as you've noted. The judgment here isn't "This is a three star narration." It's "Hmm, I have a two bomb, and a two star card here...which does this current narration merit." It means that you have to be considering the narration in the context of all others. Otherwise it's far too easy to just give your players the three star every time, or the three bomb if you're feeling nasty. With a limited hand, you have to consider the pacing of your play.
