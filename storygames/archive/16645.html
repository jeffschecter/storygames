    \n What tool you should use depends on the mechanical support you need. Skype is okay. Google+ hangouts can integrate documents, drawings or even dice rolling apps, so that might be better.
    \nIf you need heavy mechanical support a VTT might be a good idea. I - for example - did macros for MapTool, so you can "automate" the DitV conflict resolution and fallout and have editable character sheets. You can do without that, sure, but I find that I can focus more on the important stuff (the story/the play) if the mechanical parts flow easily.
    \n
    \nAnother thing is trust. All players could roll real dice and just post the results if there is enough trust - or you prefer some kind of integrated/online dice rolling.

    \n I play a lot of games over audio Skype, and whenever I can avoid the hassle of any kind of virtual tabletop I do (which means I never really use one, since there are plenty of good games that are perfectly playable without one). I prefer the tactile component of rolling my own dice, anyway. When I played DITV, we just rolled our dice locally and read them off, and the guys on the other end would just write the numbers down on a piece of paper, crossing them off as they were used up. I also played once where we typed the dice into the Skype chat window, but that ended up being more hassle than was worth it -- reading off / writing down a string of numbers is pretty quick and easy.

    \n I have an ongoing Burning Wheel game (starts in 11 minutes!) via Hangout - with the Tabletop Forge app running in the hangout - and I could not be happier with the setup*. It's very self-contained, a headset and a decent internet connection and you're rolling. Tabletop Forge includes a dice roller, and though the commands aren't super-intuitive, they're also nothing you'd find unwieldy in the context of, say, a MUD.

    *Unless, of course, the setup was that we were all in the same room, noshing on satay and joking. But G+ makes a more than acceptable substitute.

    \n@Orklord - This is something I've been talking with a few people about developing in the near future. With our current plans for Gen Con (specifically This Just In...) I've pushed back active work on it until after the convention, but getting that off the ground is one of my goals for the latter part of the year. We're basically planning on doing 'Games on Demand' for online gaming.

    The goal is to support one-shot or short term sessions - Gm's will list the games they can run and their availability, players will be able to search for games by timeslot or game system. Once a session is set up the players and GM can go and do their thing. I'd very much like to be able to emulate what GoD does in a convention setting.

    \n I realise this isn't the solution you're looking for, but after several attempts with Skype, MapTool, and other stuff, my group has begun playing a slow game via wordpress. Sometimes we get two or three "player turns" in a day, but it's more usual to get one (with occasional gaps when someone isn't available). I thought it would leave me as unsatisfied as a play-by-post forum game, but for some reason it seems to be working for us.

    If you're interested in how it plays out, the blog has dates on the posts and comments, which can give you a feel for the timeline:

    http://1337rpg.wordpress.com/

    If you're interested in the actual content, as usual with blogs, start at the bottom and read up.
