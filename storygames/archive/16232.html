\n In our current campaign, with rotating GMs, it's my turn to GM. Since last session was hugely climactic (the covenant exploded, one character died, gods were jumping in on scenes), I wanted to start a new chapter, and thought I'd start the next one some years later. Maybe 7-20 years.

    \n We did something like this in our long-running Exalted game. The GM asked us what sort of things we'd want to have accomplished in 1 year, in 10 years, and in 100 years (Exalted live a long time. None of you has ever seen a dead Exalted), then used our answer to the first question to create a scenario about six months in the future, with our goals in progress, but with things threatening their completion that we had to deal with. It was pretty sneaky!

    \n Why do you want to start the next chapter some years later?

    (I think the clue to answering how is to understand why)

    Do you want the next generation of characters in the spotlight? Do you wish to keep the same set of characters but give them time to evolve off screen to allow new values and new conflicts to appear and fuel adventures? Do you wish to link up with events in a canon timeline (Grand Tribunals, politics, wars, historical characters)?

    You obviously have to have everyone agree that it is a cool thing to leap forward in time to not break someone else's secretly planned fun - unless you introduce time travel or alternate realities.

    \n Maybe we'll chuck in some destiny points, or other forms of influence from other players.

    Hey! How about...

    1. We start out at the opening scene for the new epoch. Play a little bit with no comment on the past.
    2. At some point, the characters are gathered. Around a campfire, or lying in a ditch after a battle, or whatever.
    3. They start asking each other questions. "Hey X, I heard you did Y some time ago...?"
    For each question, we play out a scene.

    \nI think you have to start planning for the jump a few sessions beforehand, in much the same way as you'd plan for the end of a game. Figure out what everyone was hoping to accomplish, what scenes they wanted to play, conflicts they wanted to resolve, etc., and what things people were willing to leave unresolved. We did this recently in an AW game, leaving the door open to pick up the game again after a jump in time. Some stuff did not get neatly tied up, but all the players got to have the big scenes that answered the important questions for their characters.

    AW's kind of interesting for time jumps, because you can look at the stat improvements and other advancements not so much as actual improvements in the game, but as narrative control buttons. I'm not really getting Hotter, I'm just following a narrative arc of people falling under my spell. So after a five year time jump, my character resets to a starting playbook, because she's starting a new arc.

    Forgot to mention: If you were planning on a big time jump with radical changes, you could use Microscope to flesh out the gap. Have the bookends be the end of the last game and the start of the new one, use the palette to make sure you don't screw up the new setting (e.g., ban PC death if the PCs are supposed to continue).

    \n Acts of Evil is about player characters scattered across a landscape of times and places throughout human history. The ligatures mechanic is one way they affect each other. Here's the text:

    [A]ny player whose character is not part of the scene can, by revealing a ligature connecting his own character\u2019s narrative to the circumstances of the current scene, also spend Power to increase or decrease the size of the GM\u2019s pool.
    And a ligature is simply a material connection between the details of a player\u2019s prior scenes and the current scene that he wishes to affect, revealed as a short narrative interjection. It may be sorcerously intentional.
    ["Do you remember the wolves that paced about the courtyard below in Copenhagen as I argued upwards from the icy rootop with Fomalhaut? Well, you see me in my workshop with one of those wolves partially vivisected and trembling horribly. And now, as Benjamin closes in on Clarice, for a moment her eyes are those of a wolf.
    And I spend two Power to increase the pool by two dice."]
    Or it may be inscrutably circumstantial.
    ["So, this is in Loconfeur\u2019s secret room? The shelves are cluttered with nameless texts and the trappings of his stupid, quasi-occult pretensions, and among them is an oily, blackened skull. It\u2019s the skull of the father from the cemetery where I first became a homunculoid, from the grave the little girl was weeping over.
    I spend one Power to increase the pool."]
    Power spent is lost, whether the roll is successful or not.
