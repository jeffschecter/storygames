    Here are five different sophist tricks which, if I see employed in a debate, will piss me off and convince me that the other party either has no real strength in their position, is a jerk, or is mimicking someone else's ideas and presentation

    * Referencing extensive, obscure source material and refusing discussion until that has been absorbed, thus creating a barrier of entry and elevating the status of the source material artificially.
    * Using difficult to penetrate jargon, and dwelling on definitions rather than touching upon the point, thus deflecting legitimate questions.
    * Establishing definitions and then declaring any discussion of the definitions to be inappropriate, because the definition is 'clear,' ignoring whether or not it is appropriate.
    * Declaring that any argument about theory has "already been had" and, if pressed, referring back to that extensive, obscure source material, thus dodging legitimate questions perpetually.
    * Lantern hanging, which is to say, framing the weakest part of your argument in the premise and establishing debate about a secondary point, forcing participants to either concede the premise or refuse to participate, which may or may not be desirable depending on the secondary point.

    My response to these tactics, and others like them, remains the same across politics, religion, gaming and many other fields. These are, in short, warning signs that the other party is not looking for debate, but rather is looking to spread the good word.

    How applicable this is to the matter at hand is, no doubt, something people will disagree with me about.

      Lantern hanging is actually something out of politics. It was most clearly illustrated in Chris Matthew's Magnificent book of political advice, Hardball* as a piece of advice: "Hang a lantern on your problem" (though I suspect, like much in the book, it's sourced from somewhere else.)


      It's a pretty simple idea in execution. Do you have some issue that you do not want your opponent to bring up because it reflects very badly on you? Well, he's going to, so the only way you can short circuit that is if you shine a light ("hang a lantern") on the problem yourself. This allows you to "control the message" and when done right, it can move beyond mere damage control to turning those problems into assets, or into highlighting your assets. As an extra bonus, if you do it right, anyone who subsequently brings up the issue looks like they're being petulant or mean spirited because you already stepped up on this.

      Consider a job interview. You have a candidate who is lacking in some key skill, and that's probably going to tank his likelihood of getting the job. If you are the one who brings it up, or it doesn't come up, there's no way you're going to hire him. However, if he brings it up, and puts it front and center, and uses it as the launching point for why that skill doesn't matter for one reason or another, his odds go up. Why? Well, he's had a chance to make a pitch that he would not otherwise have done so, and on an emotional level it's hard not to respect his courage for going out on a ledge like that because, on a purely instinctive level, we recognize that pointing out our own flaws is kind of scary.

      This latter point is part of why this gimmick works so well on the internet - pointing out your own flaws online _is_ easier than doing it face to face, but still produces an emotional payout.

      One of the most pointed examples of this was in the second Reagan/Mondale debate in the 1984 election. Reagan had done miserably in the first debate, and had seemed very out of it. In the intervening time, that performance had raised the question of whether Reagan was perhaps too old for the job, and that that was starting to tell. When the second debate began, it opened with a question about the needs for endurance and clarity in the job, but Reagan was ready: "I will not make my age an issue in this campaign. I am not going to exploit, for political purposes, my opponent's youth an inexperience." He knocked it out of the park. It was funny, it was a 10 second sound bite, and it killed the issue (and at that point, Mondale's chances) dead.

      So, I should note, the tactic itself is no better or worse than any deflection - it is equally valid for moving past spurious issues and on to the heart of the matter as it is for skipping past substantial issues and onto fluff. As such, it is not the use of the tactic which bothers me (though it will always ping the radar when it happens) so much as the misuse. The question is always whether the issue the lantern's been put on is a real one or just a distraction.

      -Rob D.

      My personal take on the "theorists are all zealots" meme and (perhaps more directly) the "theorists don't actually play" meme. This is going to get long and rambly...

      Roleplaying is a really weird hobby. It's totally ephemeral, and totally contained within the heads of the participants at the moment they engage with it. It's a purely anecdotal hobby, a slightly more structured way of hanging out with a bunch of your mates. The experience of participating in a roleplaying game is utterly personal and utterly subjective. Afterwards, you remember very little of the *specifics* of the event, you only remember the general impression. You remember that your character fought the villain and won (or that you made a profound point about the nature of sacrifice, depending on your tastes) you probably don't remember exactly what was said between the people at the table, or exactly what led up to your making a particular in-character decision.

      To put it another way, Roleplaying games are a medium with no text. When you read a poem, your experience of it is purely subjective, and you can never know what somebody else experiences when they read the same poem, but you *can* give them a copy of the poem you read, and get them to read it. Sharing your experience of a roleplaying game is much, much harder. It's like trying to explain a poem to somebody without being able to use any of the actual lines.

      What this comes down to is that it is virtually impossible to find any common ground between your experience and the experience of another roleplayer who has not actually been in the same game as you. And what *that* means is that it is very easy to characterise other people as "never actually playing", because they may very well never have had the experience which you consider to be a roleplaying game.

      Take, as an example, the recent discussion about Force, in which more than one person (myself included) read the definition of what "Force" was and said to themselves (in so many words) "but nothing like that would ever actually *happen* in a game". It's not just a miscommunication, it's a genuine gulf in experience: your games are nothing like my games are nothing like his games. A common problem for you will be a non-issue for me (indeed it may be something I can't even imagine).

      To cut a long story short, what winds up happening is that Joe Theorist will talk about How Games Work, and Dave Non-Theorist will say to himself "this is completely alien to my experience, this guy must be making it up." And that then becomes "this guy must be making it up because he has a *sinister agenda* to *destroy roleplaying*"

      Or something.

      I think there's a lot of merit behind this; theory tends to be formed (at least, I hope it is) based on actual experiences. The problem is that these experiences are usually quite limited, usually to the experiences of the person or persons who develope the theory. The theory that forms is fine; it's just that the experiences it's based on are a very small subset of what roleplaying is considered to be. Observing or participating in lot of gaming helps to expand it - a bit.

      Another problem is that theories tend to produce some kind of Best Practises solutions (could be games, could be Tips and Tricks for GM's, or whatever) which are again based on small set of experiences. They rarely match to other people's experiences - some of it does, but not all. And since theory and applications are based on the small subset of experiences that have made roleplaying better for some people, somewhere... You're stading quite near of My Way, The Best Way -preaching, even with good intentions. And this aggravates people.

      If there would be a really universal theory of roleplaying, I'd bet that it would be painted on so broad strokes that getting practical applications out of it would be... Very demanding.
