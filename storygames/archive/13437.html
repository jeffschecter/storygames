\n This may sound like some sort of semi-rant, but...
Most manuals are supposed to be enjoyable for first time players. Unless they address advanced players (whatever that means). As such, most manuals need that section, chapter, or paragraph telling the reader what this game is about and what a roleplaying game is. The former is ok. Every story game is radically different from the other, so the "what is this game about?" question isn't to be taken for granted. The "what is a roleplaying game" question, on the other hand, tends to produce roughly the same answer, from my experience, so much that it's a paragraph I recently most often skim through.

Sometimes I wish there was a pre-constructed such paragraph that I could copy-paste on the manual instead of muttering the same old blah blah blah changing a word here, a word there, but without substantially changing the information I'm delivering.
Of course there are radical differences between what, say, a Storyteller system game says and a game coming from an understanding of The Forge theory says. Of course in my case I'm referring to the latter, I'm prone to take it for granted.

Wouldn't it be cool to have such a paragraph pre-cooked for you, where you only have to change a bt here and a bit there to adapt to the game you're creating?
..does any such thing already exist, perchance?

    \n I think this section is a symptom of faulty formating at best, poor design at worst.

    Like I always do lets compare rpgs to something relevant and successful, like board games.

    Most board game rulebooks have an overview section that very briefly describes the set up, play and end of that specific board game, this is helpful because it gives players a frame work of expectations to build upon while they finish reading the rules, you will note this is very specific information and not 'what is a board game'. 'what is an RPG' could be thought of as an overview but in practice they don't really serve that function. it's more of a "oh god you bought this book and are going to try and play it without having the critical experience you gained from years of trying and failing to play DnD?" In a sense most rpg books are written to a very specific crowd of people, people who already play rpgs.

    In trying and failing to play DnD people have learned the non explicit structures required to play an rpg 'what is an rpg' is an attempt to make these structures understood by a novice. board games avoid this because they rely on explicit structures, many story games do this as well.

    I understand the desire for a copypaste 'what is a roleplaying game' section, but so long as a game has an explicit structure there is really no need for one.

    Jokey Post:
    Here's my (much panned and criticized) "WIRP?" section from For Mature Audiences for Little Game Chef:\n
    What Is Role Playing?
    If you have to ask this question, please put this book down and find something more appropriate for your experience level. This advice is for your own protection. Inexperienced role players are far more likely to misconstrue the motives of their fellow players, interpret attacks on their issues as attacks on themselves, or abandon their fellow players when the going gets too tough. Conversely, an experienced role player can balance on the fine line that FMA asks you to walk between self-analysis and character embodiment. You are\u2014and you are not\u2014your character in FMA; and being flexible enough to flip modes when necessary is a learned skill that few possess intuitively.
    \nI thought it was both appropriate to the general subject matter of FMA (VERY adult, VERY personal) and a somewhat funny approach to that hackneyed tradition--much of FMA's core design to flip common RPG conventions on their head: character/player divide, the concept of a "character sheet" and "character history", common handling procedures (e.g. "don't touch my dice/character sheet/stuff"), and so forth--my foray into Looney Design. (The latter references how Andy Looney came up with the design goals of Icehouse.)

    \n One interesting thing which arose out of Steve's 30-minute Trail of Cthulhu demo to complete RPG novices, was that much of what was planned to be in the demo was completely irrelevant to them. For example, "At this point, you don't need to roll percentile dice to see if your character gains the information." I mean, why would it even cross their mind that it should? How would it be relevant that they wouldn't? Those players (utterly unfamiliar with RPGs) were concerned that "I was worried I would have to make up a story" and "I will be judged on my acting ability."

    So, there's "How to play this game", directed at everybody and those familiar with roleplaying games and, optionally, "How this game differs from other roleplaying games you might have played" to try to mitigate habits brought in from other games, if that's necessary. Another analogy - MS Word went big time for "Word for WordPerfect users" for a while, but that information was separated and distinct from "How to use MS Word."
