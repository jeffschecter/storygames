\n I've seen this word come up from time to time in regards to new game designs. Most recently it's cropping up in response to the "new" Mongoose initiative system for combat in Traveller. As in, "It looks okay, but I fear it might be too fiddly for my tastes." It seems to be a slight pejorative, along the lines of "meh".

1) What is your experience with this term? Have you used it? Seen it used? And, if so, in what context?

2) What's the underlying issue with rules described as "fiddly"? Too gimmicky? Requires rules reference in play? Too fragile/susecpetible to breaking?

I've never used the term myself (that I recall). Re: the Mongoose Traveller system, my concern over these kinds of "tick" systems is the need to reference the count modifiers of infrequently used actions. So, yeah, people will probably recall the basics (e.g., taking cover, firing, moving base rate, etc.) but forget or fudge the odd ones (e.g., tending to a fallen comrade's wounds, using a keypad on a locked door, etc.), which in turn ultimately discourages creative play.

    \n I use it as a shorthand for "It is not that this is too complicated to understand, but it has a great many moving parts/things to keep track of, probably more than I am entirely comfortable doing in play."

    -Rob D.

    EDIT - Which is to say, it's useful because if you say something is too complicated to _use_, people will assume you are saying it's too complicated to _understand_, and the conversation that follows tends to the frustrating.

    Additionally, something may be fiddly (have many moving parts) but not _too_ fiddly. DRYH is fiddly, but not too fiddly for my tastes.

    \n One small issue is that there are probably three major ways that fiddly gets used in game criticism, and they are close enough together to overlap at the edges.\n
    1. Fiddly means complex with a reliance on situational (or exception-based) rules
      2. \n
    2. Fiddly means rules that require a lot of handling time. A system with high points of contact
      3. \n
    3. Fiddly, especially among boardgamers, means a game that requires frequent manipulation of many physical pieces, often for bookkeeping reasons.
    \nSo, while the boardgame Puerto Rico is not really fiddly in the first sense, the captain phase can be a little fiddly in the second sense, and several parts of the game are fiddly in the third sense. You can see how this gets confusing.

    This particular case seems to refer to the first meaning, with (possibly strong) elements of the second as well.

    As for the advantages and disadvantages of "fiddly" rules, I discuss them at length in my series The Importance of Being Elegant on my gaming blog.

    Introduction
    Concerning Aesthetic Content in Games
    The Case for the Prosecution
    The Upside of Elegance

    As you can tell, I discuss this from sort of a "negative space" perspective, indirectly discussing fiddliness through the prism of one of its opposites, elegant design. Therefore, the "Case for the Prosecution" is about arguments for fiddliness.

    \n Huh. My definition is totally different.

    To me, "fiddly" means "can be fiddled with" -- specific to game design, it means there are enough pieces that can be manipulated to optimize certain facets of the game. Burning Wheel is fiddly because its got lots of stars and subsystems that interact in different ways to produce different effects. Agon is not especially fiddly because all the stats work in straightforward ways using a general dice system. PTA is totally not fiddly because everything is resolved the same way, and choices in play have little-to-no effect on the results you get out of it.

    Fiddly is not at all perjorative to me, as sometimes I WANT to have lots of pieces to manipulate and optimize. Sometimes I don't. It's roughly equivalent to sometimes wanting something sweet and sometimes wanting something savory. Fiddly is simply descriptive for me; "too fiddly" is more a statement of preference than a judgement of quality (and is more-or-less the same as "too sweet").

    I'm working on a fiddly game right now, and one of its features is that it's got lots of fun fiddly bits.

    \n For me the concern (as it goes) is that the British meaning of fiddly - that is, both the dictionary definition and the term in common usage across a multi-million strong linguistic community - does have a specific technical application to discussion of role-playing games. This specific community has an alternative definition - or rather, a loose pack of them, with "easily modified without breaking the game" a very different beast to "must be fiddled with to work right"; such meanings can be used in quite the same contexts as the original meaning yet convey very different things. I don't think this is true with the "owned" example. If instead, we were a community discussing slavery practises, I think this secondary use of owned would degrade the discourse.

    My guess is that the alternative meanings of fiddly didn't exist outside of the word being introduced (likely by a Brit) with its intended meaning, but it's meaning was unclear and was therefore reinterpreted by readers. I think this is a shame, and lies at the door of the sender as much as the recipient, but believe that it is a mistake to throw up our hands and accept this muddying when good words exist to give the other sense. Leaving aside modifiable, adaptable (dictionary words), tweakable (not, but with a generally understood meaning) as close but imperfect fits, the word fiddleable (or fiddlable) yields a number of hits with pretty much exactly the meaning that is being sought - of being able to play with/modify an item in a system in order to produce the results that please you more.

    I'm no linguist, but the -able suffix surely means "can do this to", as in timeable, stuffable, priceable
    whereas '-y' has different connotations, including "to an unwanted degree". Even without searching fiddleable, I knew what I was looking for, as the word has the sense of meaning what it actually does mean. Fiddly already means something different, has a clear definition, and discourse would be degraded if it took another meaning that it could easily be confused with.

    NB: I'm reading a lot of Dwight MacDonald at the moment, so I know I'm outside the zeitgeist....
