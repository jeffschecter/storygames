\n I've been running a Spirit of the Century game for the last few months, set in 1937 and based around a group of occult-oriented adventurers who had been crossed some years earlier by Nazi occultists and have sworn revenge. It's pretty episodic, with discrete adventures focused on undermining a different Nazi project or expedition, often involving the Cthulhu mythos.

For the latest adventure (with elements shamelessly stolen from The Attrocity Archive, Global Frequency and Quatermass), I'd set up a situation where German military intelligence had discovered a mathematical conjecture, the solving of which would open the human mind up to possession by extra-planar entities, and had leaked an almost-finished version of it to British Intelligence via a double agent. The heroes had discovered this plot, and had arrived on site at the Templar Barracks in Kent just as some of the possessed officers were starting to cause chaos and spread the possession using memetic warfare. Quelling the problem there, they used their uncanny abilities to detect that some of the possessed individuals had fled days earlier and had set up operations in the village of Avebury in Wiltshire, which is built in the middle of an enormous, prehistoric stone circle.

We left the session on a cliffhanger, with the heroes cresting the brow of a hill to see the stones of Avebury vibrating with an unearthly green glow that was beginning to spread outwards, threatening to create a shockwave that would engulf the worold and leave every human mind open to possession.

It turned out that none of the players had heard of Avebury outside of the classic BBC serial, Children of the Stones, and didn't realise that it was real. In my opinion, it's one of the most awe-inspiring places I've seen and knocks Stonehenge into a cocked hat. It occured to me just after the session that we should hold the next session of the game on site, among the stones.

After checking that the players were all free, I took advantage of the bank holiday and drove them down to Avebury today (about a two hour drive from here). We had a walk around to get the feel of the place, then found a comfortable place to sit, just outside the circle, and played through the climax of the game. We did get some funny looks from passersby, while we were describing gunfights, explosions and alien nasties happening all around us, but it made the whole game come alive. Being able to point at a building and say, "That just got taken out by mortar fire", or referencing a pen we had all seen a few minutes back as the scene of a battle with possessed, carnivorous sheep was quite wonderful. We spent most of the drive back chuckling and grinning.

Has anyone else tried anything like this? It's not quite the same feeling as a LARP, but certainly took an abstract act of imagination and turned it into something that felt oddly real.

    \n I've always wanted to run a CoC game at Bangour Village Hospital, which is about 15 miles from me. It was built in 1906 as an asylum, then it became a war hospital, then an asylum again, then a war hospital again, then a general hospital again. Its a pretty creepy place. You can see some of my photos of it here.

    Just need to dodge security when you're there though.

    There's also the former Boomhill TB sanitorium, which is pretty cool as well. You could run some bvery atmospheric games here if you chose to camp on the groups. Again, a small selection of photos.

    And I would have dearly have lover to have run a game of some kind (dead of Night, perhaps?) at Dalmore Paper Mill. Sadly, it's now converted to swank housing and all of the charm is gone. As always, I did get a bunch of photos before they ruined it all. You can view them here (first visit). And here (last visit).

    And yes, I do like wandering around old buildings taking photos. It's a thing.

    Cheers
    Malcolm

    \n My group and I have a periodic game of Shadowrun set in a ruined Manhattan (after the Quake of '29, don't you know), which features things like our actual apartment as our team's hideout, and local landmarks as the setting for tactical challenges. For example, our GM made us infiltrate the Apple Bank building on 72nd - which, for those who haven't seen it, looks like a friggin' fortress. Since we live in the area, we actually walked down there between sessions, took photos of the building, looked up plans online, etc.

    Now I still can't walk past there without that moment of, "Oh, awesome, remember what Rob did with the gas grenades?"

    --Jess
