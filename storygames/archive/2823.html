\n I've been thinking about the stuff that (in GNS) gets labelled "Sim." I'm ... not convinced that the GNS definition is tremendously useful, but I do think that there is something that is in that general direction that is very important.
\n
\nNow here's the thing: GNS is, fundamentally, a way of classifying people's actions. It is about the moments when they choose to do something. But there is more than actions to a story. There is a huge amount of stuff that happens before and after and around and between the moments of choice. There are moments where, in cinematic terms, things go into slow motion and you hold on a moment. You do this because the audience wants to pay attention to the beauty of the moment itself, in stasis.
\n
\nWhen a giant boulder starts rolling down on Indiana Jones, and he turns and looks up and his eyes get really wide, the camera holds there for a few solid seconds so that you can savor the "Oh SHIT!" moment fully. It's not really about Indy making any choices, or any of that jazz, it's about paying a good solid few seconds of attention to the situation before it gets (heh) rolling.
\n
\nThe movie where the boulder starts rolling and Indy starts running, all without that slow-motion moment ... well, that's a different movie. And, for some people, that movie might entirely miss the point. The "point" for some people is that slow-motion moment. If you try to figure out which action is most important to those people then you will inevitably miss the mark, no matter how close you come, in the same way as if you attempt to find the rational number which is exactly equal to pi.
\n
\nSo I've got two lines of inquiry here: First (but probably least important) do people think I've correctly characterized a mismatch between GNS's classification scheme and some styles of play? Second (and far more important): What tools do we have to encourage people to pay attention to these moments between choices? I know how to encourage people to pay attention to certain actions or choices: I attach rules to them. But how do I attach rules to a state of being? I can say "When you attack with a crazed shout of fury, take +1 to your damage," and it works because people want that +1 (in order to take actions). But somebody who is in the zen state of appreciating a moment ... they don't need bonusses for that. What kind of reward (if any) can I offer that would attract their attention? How can any mechanical benefit feed back in to help them achieve what they're looking for?

    Posted By: DirkGentlyAssuming that the event has already happened, which seems to be a necessary assumption from where you are going, you are just looking for how to entice the character to 'react' in a convincing manner to the event, correct?
    Not exactly. Character reaction is a useful tool, but the goal is simply to have the players pay attention to the fact that the moment is cool and beautiful.

    If the moment isn't cool and beautiful then clearly they won't pay attention to that. However, the inverse does not hold: If the moment is cool and beautiful, that does not imply that the players will pay attention to it. We miss cool things. In fact, I would say that we have to miss many cool things. There's just so much potentially-cool stuff happening in a roleplaying game that a player just wouldn't have enough attention to focus on all of it.

    I know how to focus the attention of the players on player choices. I don't have as many techniques for focussing their attention on things that they aren't choosing at that moment ... things that (in the context of the moment) simply are.

    In genre-terms, I've thought of a purer version of this than the "Oh shit" moment in Raiders: In Angel, Joss Whedon often sets up scenes that are nothing more than the characters walking down a corridor toward the camera (and, secondarily, toward a threat they're going to face). These scenes are completely irrelevant to the plot, total grace-notes, and entirely brilliant. They say "Hey, audience, check these guys out! Aren't they cool? Let's savor. Okay, done savoring now. Moving on!"

    That's the kind of thing I'm aiming at. Not so much "How do you make characters or situation cool?" but rather "Once you've got something super-cool, how do you encourage people to take a moment to notice that, rather than going straight to the next act of creating super-cool?"

      I disagree with Alan that these things are outside Big Model. Creative Agenda is basically what things you want to explore in the fiction to get your groove on. If your mental state is grooving on the slo-mo moment of the boulder about to crush Indy, that seems like a CA thing. So what-grooves-you is connected to CA because everything about play touches the "spear" of CA but the finger that points to the moon is not the moon. Those groovy moments are ephemera. The slo-mo thing or any other game design thing to get you to groove more is a technique. So let's talk techniques!


      The "slow motion" movie effect that you discuss has its counterpart in RPGs: rolling dice. I'll even argue that any point of contact with the rules is, in essence, the game designer's way of "forcing" the players to slow down and savor the moment.\n

      DM: The dragon swoops down and rakes his giant claw across your shoulder. *rolls dice* A narrow miss! The claw rips across your gorget, perforating it with a jagged scratch!
      \nRolling dice comes with a dramatic pause, for free.\n

      GM: What does Indy do?
      Player: I eyeball the statue to guess its weight and replace the idol with the sand bag.
      GM: You swap the bag and the idol. For a moment, everything is quiet but then a fifteen foot high bolder comes rolling out of the wall toward you!
      Player: Oh shit! I run!
      GM: Make a Tough roll to escape!
      Player: *rolls dice breathlessly*
      \nThe more times you make Indy's player roll, the more "slo-mo" the scene. Chase scene mechanics in RPGs can resolve the chase in a single roll or it can make it a cat-and-mouse game with more rolls and more points of contact. The tense combat between you and the dragon can be resolved in a single roll or the rules can have you rolling and rolling. D&D combat is very slo-mo but is it tense? Is it worth savoring?

      I don't understand why you need to reward people for appreciating the moment. I think you might want to create mechanics that facilitate a player's appreciation of those moments and, as you've said, you just attach rules to things to do that. Why do you think you need to attach rules to a state of being? To what end?

      I think you're asking: How do you make people stop and realize how cool their gaming moments are? Right?

      If so, perhaps you build a Reflect step into IIEE. I think rules like PtA's Fan Mail encourage continuous reflection about the "coolness" of the game actions but those rules don't require it. The awarding of fan mail that is the reflection you want, but the evaluation leading up to a fan mail award is. I get the feeling that you think that the outward expressions of "hey, this is cool" can disrupt the actual coolness of those moments. Maybe this can be fixed with some procedure. Instead of awarding fan mail as instant gratification, each player makes brief notes and can award it at the end of a scene and explain what was so cool.

      Or maybe you need an inner-thoughts mechanic to capture your "oh shit" Indiana Jones moment. Require players to describe how their characters react via descriptions of facial expressions or inner dialogue.\n

      GM: You swap the bag and the idol. For a moment, everything is quiet but then a fifteen foot high bolder comes rolling out of the wall toward you!
      Player: My eyes bug out! "Oh shit!" I'm thinking. Then I start running!
      \nIn your "If you really love me then you wouldn't be ashamed to say so!" example, those >dead silence< moments could be left silent or they could be filled with player dialog expressing what was going through their heads. I think you can codify that with game procedure. If that's what you want.

      I have a slight problem with your film and comics analogies because those are generally activities that are not shared with other people. You read a comic alone. You watch a movie alone, even if you're sitting next to your family. If something is so cool in a movie that you speak out about it, you could very well do the same thing in an RPG. Any parallel to extra comic frames or slow motion will require, in an RPG, to have people talking because talking is how stuff gets into the fiction. Well, and sometimes writing+reading.

      So here's a possible solution that uses talking. You describe something in the game. I think it's a really, really cool moment. To show that, the rules encourage me to add a detail to the scene or rephrase it from another perspective, perhaps my character's. It's a forced redundancy that says to everyone at the table, "I am grooving on this, and this is how I see it."\n

      GM: You swap the bag and the idol. For a moment, everything is quiet but then a fifteen foot high bolder comes rolling out of the wall toward you!
      Player who thinks this is AWESOME: Yeah. I plop the bag onto the pedestal and turn around arrogantly, walking away. Then I hear stone grinding against stone and turn around to see a billion tons of granite threatening to crush me. My eyes bug out! "Oh shit!" I'm thinking. Then I start running!
      \nI love the "be quiet for a minute" mechanic. I recall someone's game having a "soliloquy" rule. I've toyed with "talking stick" mechanics. Perhaps you always slide N beads forward, where N equals the number of players not counting you. Each player may slide one bead back. So you can get all your beads back if everyone thinks your moment was cool.

      Or, maybe tie all this into the reward system. This is how your characters grow, during cool moments. You gamble that your moment is cool. If people agree, you get trait points back, not blue beads. The trait you write has to pertain to your cool moment.
