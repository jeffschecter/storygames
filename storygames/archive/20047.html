    Hmm am I overstating it if I say that aversion to flashlight droppers is a sign (though not conclusive evidence) of a gamist CA?
    \nAssuming you're asking this question in good faith, I can offer a (quick) answer:

    Unfortunately, it's really not that simple.

    Creative Agenda, according to Forge theory, is a dynamic that takes place between all the participants of a roleplaying game, and shows up in the interactions between them: basically, what kinds of behaviours get rewarded socially at the table?

    This means that a simple in-game behaviour doesn't necessarily map to any particular CA.

    For example, a group could be averse to "flashlight dropping" because:

    * They are operating with a "hardcore" Gamist agenda which says it's better to face a challenge and all lose/get killed than it is to play it "cautious" and retreat to make a safer plan. "Cowardly" behaviour is sneered at.

    * Conversely, in another group operating with a different hardcore Gamist agenda (do everything in your power to survive and win), continuing a fight which you aren't sure you can win is considered bad form. In this group, any risky behaviour is evidence that you are not playing "optimally", and therefore failing. ("We win this game by making sure the GM never makes us roll the dice at all!")

    * They are playing a combat-oriented game like D&D 4E to tell a story about young adventurers becoming great heroes and overthrowing an ancient evil which threatens the world. The GM guides them from one combat to another as they progress through a fantasy epic. The game supports their Right to Dream agenda, because it's balanced so that the characters are able to overcome pretty much every encounter. In this game, dropping the flashlight would be so contrary to the CA that it would be an instant game-stopper. ("Heroes don't run! Are you a hero or not?" "The GM spent three hours preparing this set-piece... if you run away, we've got nothing to play tonight!")

    * They are playing a game about the value of honour, courage, and resilience in the face of greater evil. The characters are all different takes on what drives a person towards self-sacrifice, and they are all excited to explore what happens to such people (the characters are people like Rob Roy, Joan of Arc, and Malcolm X). In this game, dropping the flashlight could be a really cool statement about your character, or it could be a total game-breaker, if we were all playing with the assumption that this would essentially disqualify your character from being a protagonist in this kind of story.

    (Note that the last example could still be a Gamist game, if the point of play was to portray your character better or come up with wilder self-sacrifical, heroic behaviour than your peers, trying to one-up each other every night. It's really hard to say unless you're there and can see how the people interact.)

    Anyway, I'm not trying to distract from the thread; just answering a throw-away question. Back to D&D and flashlights!

      "Simulation heightens and focuses Exploration [in the big model sense, not the three pillars sense] as the priority of play". <- That's Ron.

      \n Whoops, I edited my post before seeing yours! Just added a note about the Forge threads.

      My basis for decisions... I think you and I have the same broad goals, but very different preferences about the details. I'll talk about my game Delve, which reflects my taste on these matters.

      I like games and goals and challenges and measurable progress, but I also like to spend as much of my play time as possible in a state of "feeling like I'm there" immersion, just communicating via fiction rather than interacting with formal play procedures. Every time I pick up a die, that's at least a tiny reminder that I still exist in the real world, so I try to minimize that stuff. So, my solution is to make the characters' actual lives be as much of a challenging game with measurable progress as I can. So that "just playing your character" is playing a game. I mandate at character creation that every character needs to have certain goals, and I've created a world wherein the only way to achieve those goals is to tackle the challenges and puzzles in the setting. (A lot of D&D games wind up doing this, but sometimes without the sync between goals and tasks it can wind up feeling game-motivated rather than character-motivated.)

      Players:
      Anyway, once all that is rolling, the players' basis for decisions is "what does my character value" at the broad, sandboxy, mission-choosing/defining level, then "what's the most optimal approach I can come up with" when there's something on the line to be gained (factoring in both short-term "I want this NPC to give me the key now" and long-term "but I also want him to like me later"). And then back to "what does my character value" when it's time to bask in victory, milk latest achievements, etc.

      Some people ask about which one trumps the other, values or effectiveness, but I don't think it's possible for the two to conflict. The ultimate decision rationale is, "What can I do that is most optimal, given what I value?" Just because character values provide part of the constraints of the challenge arena, that doesn't make the challenge-tackling any less fun or important.

      Because everyone is deeply in character, reactions are what they are -- if you freak out and run, then you freak out and run; there's no question of "if" your character "would" do that. No one's picking options for some purpose like spicing up the story. We just react as if we're there.

      Players don't have to literally play themselves, but they do have to create characters whose skins they can inhabit unconsciously, without effort. And there are no mental stats or mechanics to help with decisions, so your character is always as smart as you are, come problem-solving time. Every challenge-tackling decision you make is always the best that you, as a player, can do.

      As for player decisions beyond "what my character does", there are also coverage and pace-of-play decisions (basically, "the characters do X; speed through, summarize, or cover in detail?"). These are based on what mood everyone's in at the moment, and how much they think they could get out of events by playing through them in detail.

      Ephemeral stuff like, who talks in character -- there's no decision there. My rule is that, when we're playing through a scene, you must speak in character (and signal when you aren't, if not obvious).

      \n As GM, my basis for decisions looks like this:

      In prep, I use the tarot-card system I've devised for setting up problems, possible solutions, and ways to unearth those possible solutions. The cards themselves will then be accessible to the characters as vague hints.

      The game has few stats, and I do zero rules-based challenge-level calibration. The calibration I do looks mostly like this:
      - The new magical effect I'm going to hit the PCs with -- how exactly does it work? Is it a meaningful threat without being unavoidably lethal? If yes, then I'm ready, and also ready to accept improv thanks to understanding how it works.

      I also choose my magical effects based on where the characters are in their path of discovery -- which secrets they're in the process of learning, and what's a helpful next step.

      My basis for all the content I invent is the tone and the metaphysics of the setting. Basically, real-world history with modern accessibility and a few secret principles of magic, demons and the cosmos.

      I also like novelty and try not to repeat myself.

      During play, my basis for decisions is usually a semi-unconscious process of trying to inspire the players and then follow whatever they're enthused about, but it's always constrained by my vision of the setting (and what I already know about it, factually, from my prep). If they come up with a slick plan and they think it'll work, then it usually does; but when I know something they don't, then I will enjoy surprising them.

      When I'm running NPCs and monsters in the moment, I usually have an intuitive sense of how they'll behave, based just on my taste and mood when I invented them.

      When intuition doesn't guide me, I'll default to a stock character or situation that I've done before.

      When intuition guides me toward a danger zone, sometimes I don't notice. When I do notice, I'll pause my talking, review my notes (or pretend to) to buy a moment for reflection, and quickly figure out a better option. If I can't, we'll just play until we enter the danger zone, and then use my bailout tools.

      For a situation that's simply pointless to play through, my bailout is the pacing control -- speed through, summarize, cover in detail. Once players start down a dead end, I'll ask a few questions, then speed through telling them how it turns out to be a dead end.

      For a situation that's important but tedious, my bailout is a decision tree sheet to help the players organize their process and get the necessities out of the way as fast as possible. (This is a work in progress.)

      For a situation that's gonna kill them without fair warning, my bailout is to say "I described it wrong". The social contract in Delve is that the setting won't just randomly murder you -- the challenges of play are not about survival unless the players choose to make them so. The risks instead are your progress toward your goals -- losing magical items, severing useful connections, missing opportunities, needing a month to heal injuries while the world moves on. There's nothing in the setting that says the PCs must be killed without warning, so if my intuition ever leads me there, I view it as optional, not mandatory, and thus as my own mistake. "Sorry guys, I described it wrong, the flaming column doesn't fill the hall, it just bisects it. Oops!" Sometimes I'll even pretend to be reading from something, to minimize the chance that anyone might get their immersion dented by watching me obviously make up content.

      I will also use this "Oops!" if I accidentally do something inappropriate to the reality of the fiction, like forgetting the season or time of day or name of an NPC or how hard it is to load a crossbow. Only if someone notices, though -- the fiction just needs to be real to us. If we're all incorrect about crossbows, but none of us knows that, then whatever, man. "Oops!" is not perfect, but it's functional, and I don't have to do it very often (maybe once every few sessions).

      Patterns:
      That first paragraph of "during play" has all kinds of emergent effects -- I tend to adjust challenges on the fly to aim at the sweet spot of "most fun", for example. I don't do this as discreet decisions, though; it's more a function of being a fan of the characters and excited to see them progress.

      Still, in some forms of Right to Dream play, it could be very important (for verisimilitude, for a sense of "stick to what would happen"-ness, for maintaining the logic of the gameworld, or for following the expectations of the genre, just as some examples - a Call of Cthulhu game which seeks to emulate H.P.       \n Delve is immersive gamism, with the color set on High, the difficulty set on Low, and "what would happen"/in-fiction causality (physics, incentives, etc.) as the arbitration reference. The GM has various responsibilities related to all of these:
      - Prep is mostly about making sure that there are fictional challenges available for interaction, fictional rewards to be won, and signals to the characters about their opportunities. Some formulating a vision of the setting is also required.
      - Narrating the setting in play is mostly about keeping play immersed and colorful.
      - Deciding what happens when the PCs pursue stuff is an appeal to "what would happen", with the GM as the initial arbiter but not necessarily the final word (as per the comics).

      I don't think any of this is emblematic of "Gamism", broadly, but it all supports Delve's specific creative agenda (which does happen to be, among other things, Gamist).

      For instance, Eero's encapsulation of the role of "challenge" in the game becomes very useful in certain play situations:

      Let'say the players are spending a lot of time working out the logistics of how many mules they need for an overland voyage. In a typical game group, one player might be trying to do so as accurately as possible, another might be worried about "losing" by getting it wrong (and therefore the two might clash on the importance of certain details over others), and therefore trying to pay as much attention to it as possible, and a third might be sitting there absolutely bored. As a GM you're sitting there, and not sure how to contribute. Do you try to please the bored player by suggesting we skip ahead, or indulge the player who's interested in historical detail? It's not obvious.

      When the group knows what the game is all about (whether it's conscious or just implicit), it's much easier: we can all look at each other and say, "Hey, what's the challenge here? Are we interested in pursuing it? Is it meaningful?"

      Maybe there is no significant challenge, or maybe there is but none of us are interested in it. In those cases, the group might just abstract it away: "How about we just say that it costs you 2d10x10 gold coins to buy enough mules for the trip, and only about half of them will survive?" If everyone is happy with that, we move on.

      Or maybe there IS a significant challenge, but the challenge is about our water supply (because we're crossing a desert, or water is required for the upcoming adventure, or some such) and not about how much we can carry; if so, we can ignore the irrelevant details (e.g. the carrying capacity of the mules, how fast they move, or whatever we deem an unimportant challenge), and focus more on the details of the water supply (which will then presumably lead to interesting situations in the adventure itself).

      I've played in D&D games where we forget to assign XP or forget to level up at all. In such a game, clearly the focus is on something else entirely - that doesn't tell you which CA is in play, however.

      In contrast, I've played in (and read accounts of) OSR-style D&D games where a character reaching 2nd level was met with people jumping out of their chairs, laughing (sometimes even crying), and the fortunate player announcing that s/he will be taking everyone out for drinks. (Like the wonderful Fellowship of the Bling accounts.)

      Trying to run a game like a "simulation" is a very specific technique, but it does not tell us what the creative goals of a game are, or what importance various decisions and events might have.

      Like Dave and Matt (Deliverator) are saying, it's about the *why*. You still have to decide what you're simulating, how often, in what detail, and some kind of organizational principle, whether conscious or not, comes into play.

      Let'say we're playing D&D, and using "simulation" techniques to determine "what would happen".

      Rognar, brave 7th-level warrior, wakes up in the middle of the night with a full bladder and decides he needs to go make water under a bush.

      How do we deal with this? Just knowing that you're going to "simulate" fictional details accurately doesn't - and can't - tell you whether this is a worthwhile situation or a waste of time.

      A group which is grooving on some kind of Gamist agenda asks themselves a simple question: is this situation providing some kind of challenge we're interested in seeing this player face? If no, they'll probably skip it. If yes, they'll focus on the elements which are relevant to the situation.

      If anything will be simulated, it will be elements relevant to challenge. Perhaps it's how hard it is to maneuver in the dark without waking anyone up, or in the alien climate/landscape where they are. (Admittedly, it's a bit of a ridiculous scenario; it's fairly unlikely that there is challenge inherent in such a situation, and so most groups will be happy to conclude that "peeing in the middle of the night" is not something they're willing to spend valuable playtime on.)

      However, Gam challenges can take all kinds of forms - maybe with this group, the challenge is all about how entertaining you can be compared to your peers. In that case, describing how you get up to go pee in the middle of the night would be a very suitable challenge, and everyone would be grinning and watching anxiously to see how player C will manage (or fail) to entertain them by describing Rognar's midnight leak.

      The question is: will the player succeed or fail? (Or, alternately, *how much* will he succeed/fail. In some games, failure or success are presumed, and we compete over matters of degree.)

      Under a Story Now agenda, this would also be skipped over in most cases, but could become the focus of play if it reflected on a particular Premise relevant to the characters. Were I the GM in this game, I might jump in to say how Rognar's hated enemy's family grave is near the campsite, inviting him to show how much he despises them by urinating on their grave, for example. However, showing disrespect to the dead can disqualify you from a happy afterlife...

      Or Rognar's loyalty to the group might be tested: there is nowhere to pee, but his romantic rival's (who is an indispensable local guide, and unwise to piss off) half-empty water canteen is just lying there... will he go for it?

      In any case, the question here is: what will the character choose, and what will learn about his values in the process? If the question is obvious, we'll probably skip it and focus on something else, just like in a Gamist adventure we would skip any challenge with an outcome we already know.

      When it comes to Right to Dream, my personal interpretation (I have no idea if this is "canonical") is that we're dealing with Exploration, and therefore we will only play out this situation if we see a chance to a) show even further how something already is (add further detail to reinforce something we already know) and celebrate grooving on it together, or b) discover something new about what we're Exploring.

      The Big Model had different categories of Exploration: Character, Situation, System, and so forth. Let's say that our particular Right to Dream-focused D&D game was heavily about exploring Character.

      In this D&D game, we might like to play out Rognar's nighttime bathroom break to give the player a chance to show what Rognar is like. The player would portray Rognar and describe how careful or brutish or careless or noisy or whatever Rognar is, and we'd all grin and smile because, yeah, that's totally what Rognar would do! We'd be celebrating how *right* the player's portrayal of Rognar felt in that moment.

      I think D&D fights against mook opponents who are easily defeated are a fairly typical Sim D&D moment: we just want to see the heroes beat up these bad guys, even though we already know they can, just to celebrate one more time how good they are at it. A bunch of people playing canonical characters in an RPG ("You'll be Spiderman, and I'll be Gandalf!"), without trying to subvert them, but rather seeking to play them "straight" might be another example.

      Alternatively, maybe we'd use Rognar's nighttime bathroom break to learn something new ("Exploration" in a more traditional sense). Maybe we're curious to see if he's a civilized sort or a careless type. I, as GM, say, "Hey, there's a bush nearby, very convenient. But there is also a nice outhouse, with a washbasin, but it's all the way across the road." Which will he choose? Now we know something further about Rognar we didn't know before, and we enjoy learning more about his character.

      Or I introduce a feature of the local environment (prickly plants? a local nighttime predator?), and we enjoy discovering what that is like (Oh! Look! The prickly bush only grows in the moonlight, walk under the cliff and there won't be any). Through Rognar, we all learn more about some aspect of this shared fiction, and we all enjoy that (and can reference it later to add more exciting details in the story, building on the knowledge we have now established - "Obviously, there are no prickly bushes in this clearing, since we know the enchantment keeps the moonlight from touching the ground here" - "Ooh, yeah! Totally!").

      In a dysfunctional game, though, not everyone's on the same page. I'm GMing and I want to present Rognar with a moral dilemma, so I tell him he wakes up in the middle of the night with a full bladder, but that moving away from the group will the umbrella might expose his beloved to the rain. Rognar's player is looking for the challenge, and confused because he doesn't know where the challenge here is. He wants to know exactly how many feet away the outhouse is and how fast the rain is coming down.

      Everyone else is curious to see how much of a jerk Rognar is, so they keep asking whether he's just going to leave the local guide's canteen lying there. The player now has no idea how to contribute meaningfully to the game; Lucy is sitting there altogether bored, because she's only interested in using her new Power Cleave ability and so has zoned out until the next fight.

      \n That sounds very promising, Sandra. And thank you for such a lovely write-up: it's rare to see people describe their gaming with that kind of insight.

      I will add this:

      If your goal is to "encounter/create human stories", you might consider adding some other tools to your Technical Agenda (as you put it). If you want the *possibility* of human stories, then maybe you don't need to. But if it's something you really want to explore (as a group, I mean), that it would help to have some more (or at least different) GM tools at work.

      The easiest way is generally to get the players to do it for you. When I hear something like, "They also said that an issue with the campaign was that they didn't have any real ties to the various factions in the city," my immediate thought is: you can't force these things onto them. (Or, you can, but that's a lot more work, and there's the danger that the players don't buy it even after all that work.)

      It doesn't sound like a huge problem to me. (Certainly not a "starting the campaign over" kind of problem!)

      Just ask them: Hey, what do you think about this faction? Have you ever heard of them doing anything bad? Why don't you like that?

      Which is your favourite faction? If you could join one someday, which one would it be?

      If you could change one thing about this faction, what would it be? Do you think that's something you could accomplish, if you were on the inside?

      Or you could say to each player:

      Let's tie the characters in a little more. Come up with one person who's a close friend or a good contact. Which faction do they belong to? Come up with one person who's an enemy or a rival. Which faction do they belong to?

      You can also be more sly about it, of course, if those questions are too direct:

      "Hey, guys! I'm just curious: which faction do you think would be the most likely, in your opinion, to do something good? Why?"

      "Which faction do you think is the most likely not to be what it seems, or to be hiding something? Why?"

      You can do this while hanging out and having some pizza, just chatting about the game. (But do take mental notes!)

      Or you can do this in-character: have an NPC ask them the same questions, see what they say.

      I bet in 5, 10, or 15 minutes you'd have more than enough material to come up with sessions and sessions worth of interesting stuff. Sometimes you can use exactly what they came up with; other times, you can play contrary to expectations.

      -"Which faction do you trust the least?"
      -"Those Templars sure seem fishy to me. How did they get to be so strong? They must have some blackmail against the ruler of the region."

      BAM! Whether they're right or wrong, there's a ton of material you can pull from that. Why ARE they so strong? Is it a facade, carefully maintained? Or do they have some nefarious connections? Otherworldly powers, contact with demons, contacts with the mafia?

      Why would someone suspect the ruler's being blackmailed? Work that into your campaign. Give them some reasons.

      All it takes is for you to throw a hook into the next session: oh, hey, it sounds like so-and-so or this-or-that locale (could pick something from one of your available modules) knows something bad about the ruler, stuff no one is supposed to know. The players will likely jump on it right away!

      Or flip it around: yeah, sure enough, there's some blackmail against the local ruler (e.g. he or she isn't a legitimate ruler, and the real heir is still alive)... but it's the Templars who are trying to keep that from coming out. Hence the ruler's strong support of the Templars: take that threat away, and the Order will disappear very quickly, having no funds and no popular support.

      I can think of a million potential plot hooks/connections from something like that, too.

      And that's just from one sentence!

      Just a thought: I don't know if it's applicable to your game. Got off on a bit of a tangent there. Maybe someone reading this will find it interesting or useful, anyhow.

      \n So what has happened early on is that one player did have some faction ties pre-game but then was very secretive about it. You're probably familiar with the old "pages of secret backstory" trope that doesn't really come out in play. He did have his character join another faction once play started.

      Another player had an NPC relationship in town (his teacher) but then avoided seeking him out. I had a lot of stuff happen to/with that NPC but it didn't enter play since he didn't visit him. "He could seek me out" -- yeah, how? You're in the dungeon he told you to stay away from and he doesn't have Message prepared and he doesn't really know where you are. Now that I do think of it that way, through the familiar perhaps.

      As for the happenstance overlap: Yes, absolutely. But hence communication.
      It's also the case that I'm motivated to create a particular game experience, it's what interests me and motivated me to bring the group together and to prep for the game.

      The dialogue has two components. On my part, I want to be clear about what the experience is and how I run the game. For the first few sessions last summer, this even included me pointing out parts of the module, demonstrating how I was running the game "I say that because the module has this in it", "These goblin's stats come from this page" etc. Just for the first few encounters. This was when we had players who had never played an RPG before. It seemed to me that expectation setting was very important for a sandbox game. When I was a player (in a different group) I felt I've accomplished my goal in a coherent system, it's all the more satisfying than if the GM used quantum tricks and handwaving. YMMV! I want to bring this experience to the players.

      The second component of the dialogue is me seeing what parts of the game seems to satisfy them or frustrate them. My response to that could be things like "OK, they don't seem to like to talk to NPCs, maybe I don't need to work so hard on that (or I need to work harder)", or it can be things like "Of course they don't like book flipping, but they really don't need book flipping, printing out the stat blocks could be worth it". etc
      It's also a tradeoff vs my own laziness.

      On the one hand: Everyone wants to be the perfect hostess, the perfect DM to serve up the best experience for "their" players that they'll remember for ever. The old "they're my audience" thinking. On the other hand: I'm just one participant in this experience. The success of the endeavour isn't solely my responsibility. As long as I bring to the table what I need to bring to run the game, it's enough. It won't guarantee the best session ever, but who knows? My energy level for prepping and working on the game experience will fluctuate and I need to be mindful to it and not burn out or abandon the campaign. Sometimes "phoning it in" with a skeletal prep is better than cancelling the session.

      Prep can scale from "Just put in the monster's name", via "Put in the page number in the MM", to "Type up a short form of the monster's most important stats and traits". (And heck YES I wish they would make the full Monster Manual available in a digitally copyable form. I've already done lots of text/semantic programmatic transformations on the few monsters in the basic PDF.)
