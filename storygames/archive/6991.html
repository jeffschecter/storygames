    \n It just squeaks into your time frame, but Jan Hus (a proto-reformer) was active in what is now the Czech republic right before 1400 (he was arrested in 1414 and executed in 1415)

    In England, Wycliffe was very influential in the 1300s and his ideas spread to the mainland of Europe. By 1384 he had finished translating the Bible into the vernacular. The movement that shared his ideas, the Lollards, started in the 1350s.

    The earliest Waldensians were already around in the middle of the 12th century in France and even Rome. By the early 13th century they were being persecuted and burned as heretics. Peter Waldo (Pierre Vaud\xe8s) also commisioned the translation of the Bible into the local tongue.

    The Cathars might count, but one could argue that their gnostic leanings disqualify them from being considered Abrahamic and instead make them Hellenic. And you've got them covered under the "Gnosticism" category.

    Do the geographic divisions within rabbinical Judaism even matter before 1400?
    \nNot really. You don't really get a split in the geographic regions until after 1500, when the Jews of Sepahard settle mostly in the Ottoman Empire and slowly start to separate into the Sephardi traditions we have today (and more), and at the time most Ashkenazi Jews are all more or less following the same tradition (understand this is all simplified). Now that doesn't mean that there weren't differences between the Jews of Ashkenaz and Sepharad and Israel/Middle East, so you could technically keep the groups as you have them above for simplicity's sake. Maybe you also want to have a category for those that fall outside the Ashkenazi/Sephardi/Mizrahi divisions, like the Yemenites or the Jews of Rome.

    Hope that helps.
