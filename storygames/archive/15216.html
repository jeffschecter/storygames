\n Someone thought I was bullshitting when I submitted the Tarrasque monster manual entry for the "most influential paragraph of game text" thread. I wasn't. Here's my response:

I have never ever had a play-changing epiphany from reading advice* in an RPG text. What RPG texts have done for me is to show by example.

I was thinking of quoting the page from the Vampire book where one clan lists its attitudes toward all the other clans. That was a crucial step for me in terms of thinking about webs of relationships between protagonists.

But the monster manual entries came earlier in my gaming career, and were more formative. If you read that Tarrasque entry, you'll see a ton of implications for how one is supposed to play. Here's a monster that's unique, never around, utterly unapproachable until you're high level, invulnerable to all the shit you usually use to win fights, costs a friggin' Wish spell to kill... and its hide is not only the most valuable thing there is, but also a plot hook (if you're into dealing with master smiths, anyway). My group and I read the Tarrasque page over and over, fantasizing about how we might one day fight it. That and the Great Wyrm Red Dragon were possibly the biggest incentives to earn XP and level up.

Honestly, I'm surprised that no one else is quoting content like outcome options, plot hooks, faction lists, setting color, etc. What's more persuasive, being told or being shown?

*or instruction, really... but that could be because I tend to learn new games by playing them with people who've already read the book, so I don't have to

    \n Y'know, there is a big part of my gaming brain that agrees with this completely. I was usually bored with most of the Monster Manual entries, since most of the stuff they were writing about didn't really matter to me, or I had my own ideas on how their ecologies and histories would be cooler.
    But every now and then, you'd run across something interesting and/or original, that was written in a way that got the imaginative gears turning. The Tarrasque, a lot of the unique monster descriptions in Ravenloft, some of the creatures from Planescape, etc., would get the creative juices flowing when either preparing adventures or setting up goals for your character, or just in anticipation of something cool later on. Kind of like how some people get a kick out of finding those insanely-powerful monsters randomly on the world map in Final Fantasy, where you KNOW you have no way of winning, and it sets you back a bit, but after you've been ressurected, you think, "I want to get strong enough to BEAT that thing, next time I find it!"

    And I always enjoyed those Vampire clan write-ups and their relationships with the other clans. That was typically the way I'd choose my clan, by seeing how they felt they fit in with the rest of the Camarilla/Sabbat (with their powers as a tie-breaker).

    I think, in all, it's similar to how certain TV shows or movies will get someone daydreaming about what it'd be like if they were in that fictional world, and thinking how they'd resolve the situations their heroes/anti-heroes deal with.

    \n Man, WW splats are such genius. There's not even all that much going on mechanically with them usually: different lame powers, or a bit of an edge in one specific domain.

    But the colour text? The attitude-relationship box? The art? Sweet Jesus does that get the imagination going! Verbena! Malkavian! Glass Walkers! Technocrats! Red Talons! Brujah! I like it way better than the class/role model because its all colour-first design. I think everything else in a WW product is totally useless to me but the splats really sing. And I think that why WW is as popular as it is, is its strong focus on colour. You can just stay things like: "You're a team of Void Engineers tasked to track Garou", "You're the hit squad for the Prince of Montreal", "You're a group of survivors fresh from Faery, you all had the same Keeper and her Court was like this..., or "You're all Abyssal Exalts preparing the way for the invasion of...".

    I feel like you sort of get the same thing with Prestige Classes but not with the Basic Classes with D&D. I never really knew who my wizard was. I mean, I tried. But man, I knew exactly who the Incantrix of Shadowmoor was.

    The splats always seemed to come bundled so tightly with the setting, with a community, with relationships, with attitudes, with philosophies. With these in mind as creative constraints, one could easily come up with a character with motivation and beliefs.

    \n Jesse,

    Well, the insights may sound obvious now, but remember, I was 11 at the time!

    - Anticipation: not every bit of game content is for right now.
    - Illustrating advancement: as you get more badass, you get to fight more badass monsters. The biggest monster of all gives you a goal to shoot for.
    - Specialness: a goal means more to you if it's not interchangeable, and if opportunity is limited. If you get a shot, it might be your only one!
    - Challenging unfamiliarity: want to scare the players and wake them up? Take away all the tools they've gotten used to relying on (attack spells, psionics, etc.).
    - Pay for what you get: in AD&D2, I think a wish spell permanently aged or level-drained the caster or something like that. Some rulebook had a "going rate" for hire-a-wizard wish spells, and it was the most costly thing in the game.
    - Treasure isn't always just useful: sometimes it can require another fun quest.

    It's a broader view of how things are connected in long-term play. Quite a step forward from, "I stab it, magic missile it, take its gold pieces, and move to the next room in the dungeon."

    I think it wasn't too long after reading this that I first began GMing.

    \n Judging from the other examples in that pronunciation guide, their "tah-RASK" is the same as tuh-RASK would be. Most accurately, that's /t\u0259'\u0279\xe6sk/ in IPA. The first vowel might be /t\u0251-/ for some people but because of vowel reduction (and also perhaps the rhoticization), probably not the majority. For Southerners, Ladefoeged's rhoticized schwa would be the most accurate symbol for the first vowel ("ter-ASK").

    The -e at the end of French words isn't pronounced anymore, but used to be identical to -a endings in current English, so it would have sounded like Tarrasca.

    Rafu is Italian, so he might be more inclined to pronounce it the same way Spaniards or us Canadians do: "So, it's a Tarask, eh?"

    "Tar-ras-cue" (by analogy with barbeque, I assume) is a bit odd, but by the standards of common English (horrible, awful mis-)pronunciations of Ancient Greek names, fairly pedestrian. Or go back to the WotC pronunciation guide and look at the entry for Ixitxachitl. Damn.

      \n I'll speak on the functions I know about.

      First, that splats are (sub)cultural locations within society which transmit identity to the players. Before to you read Vampire, all you've got is 'I drink blood and can't go out in the son.' Not a lot to go on for character creation. Without splats, Vampire is totally different game, probably about a diverse isolated cast of vampires coming to terms and trying to live night-by-night as a vampire. With the splats you have societies, cultures, and groups. It's takes the characters from a social wilderness and into the city. It maps out the social terrain for the characters and gives them an identity to compare and contrast themselves with, it give them a community and a culture.

      Second, Sins/Monsters/Missions/Villains: it gives the characters something to work towards or against. I think I'd bracket them all under the literary sense of the word antagonist: something that causes change in the character in the story, but usually remains unchanged. In RPGs, good antagonists are also saturated with fiction or colour. The Tarrasque implies both a narrative/mechanical arc for the characters (we need to become powerful friends of dwarves / lvl20) but also a world that contains things such as Tarrasques. Sins, I think do similar work in a different way, as colour-saturated antagonists that the characters work against. How I am going to deal with Sister Abigail's Sorcery? I include Missions and Villains because they do the same thing: colour-saturated antagonists for the players because neither of these things are abstract (without colour) or colorful with purpose. They're not "steal the valuable things from the powerful" but "hack the geneteck from the zaibatsus." They're neither simply "There are zaibatsus and they have genetech" but "You're going to steal the geneteck from the zaibatsus." They're a one-two punch for handling the necessity for character antagonists and the need for concrete setting details.

      Character antagonisms that are too abstract are hard to role-play (I don't really know what it means that my wizard is lonely) where setting detail and colour without motive is pointless. (In Dalerran there are meadows the colour of sun-sets). However, combine the two (often with mechanical support) and you got an elegant design solution (My wizard seeks his true love in the mythical meadows of Dalerran).
