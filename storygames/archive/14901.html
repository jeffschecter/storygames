\nWell I went to leave twenty years ago, since then I guess I been packin' kinda slow. Sure did like that admirin' touch, guess I liked it a little too much.

So I was looking at Jason Morningstar's thread and I had a lot of thoughts on the preference of even his very inconsistent group for a very long campaign. This is something I've been struggling with with my current group. So let's talk about it!

My normal preference is for a campaign of about 6 months of weekly play. The reason for this is because around the 4 month mark I get some idea for a brilliant campaign that I have to stop and run OH MAN AS SOON AS I CAN. (Sometimes this even results in me running two weekly games at the same time. That is exhausting, though.) The only thing that keeps my interest in the existing game is when I identify an ending, and that heightens the game until I get there.

Of my current group, two people tolerate this and three others really dislike it. They want a game that goes for (this is a quote) years and years. Oh man. I struggle with this. But it's a challenge! So here's how I'm facing the challenge.

First, I wanted to pick a game situation with a lot of existing material that I could mine. I angled for the X-Men corner of the Marvel Universe, because when I was but a boy and a beardless youth, I read shitloads of X-Men stuff. I found the characters really bizarre and compelling (except Wolverine, fuck that guy), even though, to put it lightly, plot was not the strong point. There were times when nobody, nobody could figure out what the hell was happening or why. But there was always someone whose identity was causing them problems. There's only one other guy in the group who reads comics, and he hates all comics (he reads 10 comics a month), so he won't care if I tear it apart for ideas .

Second, I wanted to pick a game that could support virtually infinite variability within characters and character change. D&D's level-an-loot schema is really, REALLY good for long term games for just this reason. Pick the length of D&D campaign you want to run, pick the level you want them to end at, divide by the amount of XP you need, blah blah, you've got a perfect pacing tool, absolutely perfect. I picked Smallville for this reason because you can always shift around your Values, change your Relationship and Value statements, and also you can advance things if you get beat up enough. Sweet.

Third, I need to identify the boundaries of the campaign - things that are just not going to change. In a long-term Call of Cthulhu campaign, nobody is ever going to storm the Dreamlands and blow up the Elder Gods. Nobody is ever going to make D&D into an industrial age drama. And in my X-Men game, nobody is going to make humanity stop hating mutants.

Fourth, I need to identify the Big Pressures of the campaign. These are things that might change over the lifetime of the many many months we'll be playing, but that won't be solved in a single confrontation. Dresden Files City Creation has a lot of this in it. In my X-Men game, the Brotherhood of Mutants, Magneto's terrorist organization, is not going to be defeated in a single confrontation, even if they knock Magneto into the sun or convince him to change his ways. Someone is going to pick it back up and get with blowing up the government again. BUT, I want these pressures to change and refine based on what the players do. I like the term "fronts" (I like books about WWI, OKAY?!), but don't mistake this for what Apocalypse World means by fronts, because I don't really know about that game. It just fits what I think of in terms of the ongoing battlefield of conflict for the characters.

Okay, more soon. In this thread, post your methods for setting up and adapting to a Long Campaign.

    \n Well, yes and no. I think this is a bit of a separate topic.

    First of all, YES, if you want to play another game, with this group, in the place of the long-running campaign, you should be looked at as if you are advocating taking something away from the group, because you are. You are saying "let's stop doing this and do something else". If people don't want to, that's their right, just as it's your right to say "let's stop". It's pretty rare in a super-long campaign that anyone (other than the game organizer) has a veto on what the rest of the group is going to do. (The game organizer has the same veto in one-shots, by the way. "Get out of my house, it's late.")

    But if what you're saying is "I want to leave this game, I don't want to play it anymore even if the rest of you do", there has to be some attention paid, preferably up front, to what will happen if someone decides they want to leave. It's certainly not the responsibility of the person leaving to make that transition easy/satisfying - they're stopping, that's the whole point. So it has to be plans/expectations of the remaining group that are put into play. Yet the same is true for any game lasting longer than a session. If someone decides they don't want to show, that can disrupt certain games. The group should be planning ahead to think about this. Exit strategies are important to any enterprise.

    \n OK, I had a D&D campaign run for 20 years with the same characters so I am trying to think if I have any suggestions for longevity beyond, "Have players extremely attached to their characters and skilled at egging you on to keep GMing."

    I understand that urge to want to change pace and style for a time and I think finding a way to work other genre's into your game is a useful safety valve. With the 2nd ed D&D, Ravenloft gave me the excuse to have the Mists snatch them at any time for a little Gothic fun, Spelljammer allowed for D&D In Space!, and once you've got the ability to plane hop, a GM can pretty much make up just about anything and have his players wander through it for awhile. Now not every world and cosmology allows for easy genre jumping, but just as you have identified what boundaries you won't cross, it is worth looking at how far you can stretch your game's boundaries so that when that urge for a new game comes, you can fold some of that urge back into the Long Term Campaign.

    Posted By: Vernon RWow 20 year D&D campaign with the same characters and all the same players? That's amazing!

    How often did you play and how long did it take for characters to level up? Did you ever do anything different like have them run apprentices or hirelings/henchmen for a change up? How much of your game was adventuring and how much was downtime or building up a home base or politicking etc.?
    \nWell there was some ebb and flow of players. I started in 7th grade with a group of five players and we played roughly weekly until we graduated high school. We lost two players over the course of high school and picked up another and then that core group of four remained steady. Through college we played once a holiday and then near weekly through the summers. Then one of our players moved for grad school and the next ten years were monster sessions once or twice a year when we all were back in our hometown. We would still be playing at that rate now but I moved away and don't get back to the hometown much anymore.

    As for the leveling up, I want to say that they started at first in seventh grade and were somewhere in the high teens/low twenties when we headed for college. They are currently in the high twenties level wise. We started at 1st ed, converted to 2nd ed when it came out and stayed there since none of us were interested in learning a new rule set to play the game we'd been playing for a decade.

    As for the apprentices/retainers, yes we did do that but not as the main group. If only two members were available on a weekend or a few of us were hanging out and suddenly got an urge to game, they would break out their lower level followers and play as them for a while. A few of those hirelings actually became powerful enough to join the main group with either the players running two characters or me NPC'ing them. Sure, they died more often but they were still useful.

    Our gaming sessions were pretty much solid adventure. They did build a pair of temples, a castle and a city to go around them and I did work that city and its needs into their adventures, but my group wasn't much for "a day in the life" playing or high level politics. They wanted to kick open doors, slay demons and take their stuff. I did run a scenario where they had to gather an army to protect their town from an oncoming horde and they played out going to all the leaders and creatures that they helped over the years asking for help and calling in debts. That was really fun because they dug into their histories and each player remembered different highlights and places to look for aid. Reincorporation is definitely key in a Long Term Campaign.

    Scaling power levels became tricky over time if you want to keep a shred of believability to the world because you have to ask yourself just how many 25th level necromancers does this world have and how is it no one noticed them until now? Plane hopping became quite common at that point.

    \nYes, in D&D. I kill the characters with increasingly epic challenges, and they thus never achieve too high a level. The players start anew with new characters.     \nIt happens pretty naturally if the characters are entangled in the setting and consequences are meted out aggressively. For example, as Sam mentions above, seriously injured heroes get to sit out for one or more sessions, easy. Same for guys who have to look after their estates or engage in long-term training or research. It's also notable that you wouldn't take a belowed character to a desperate or suicidal adventure, you'd rather take one you are willing to lose (this is a psychological overture to outright retirement, I'd say). And of course there are the cases where "my guy wouldn't go on this adventure" - no problem, just make a guy who would!

    (That last one never grows old when it's the player's first character. Sometimes the player really does roll up a different character who apparently does have adventuring motivation where the first one didn't.)

    I execute a Gygax-style calendar myself, so time flows in real time in the fantasy campaign. A character who takes it to the chin takes 1d6 weeks to recuperate, while one at the gates of death will take 1d6 months. This, combined with real time, gives us a rough estimate of how long a given character's asylum stay might be. As might be imagined, a character stable tends to develop quite naturally in these conditions simply because your main man is not always available. (It's notable that I've made characters pretty "sticky" in comparison to canonical D&D, so most of the time you're out of a fight before you're dead.)

    If GM coordination is required, it might not be a bad idea to institute an outright rule where everybody needs to have characters ready to go equal to the level of the highest-level character in their stable. Then maybe some rules about when you're allowed to play your "main guy", if the above sorts of pressures aren't desired in assuaging players. For example, you could intentionally play a mix of high-level and low-level adventures, and forbid a character of level >N from participating in a level N adventure; this would quite naturally force players to play secondary characters from time to time after their main characters are of higher level.

    \nYeah, that's the time-frame you can expect - it's really on the heroic side of things to jump up after half a year of rest after a ghoul ate your face and took your jawbone home in a doggy bag. It might be shorter or longer in application due to any number of reasons. Technically this sort of thing could be construed as stopping the calendar (although I've usually just assumed that the first session happened a bit later or the last session a bit earlier than nominal - haven't had to really worry about it yet, as there haven't been really time-critical events in multiple theaters of operations going on simultaneously so far). Similarly there can be in-session downtime that accelerates the passage of time.

    In practice, though, we don't keep a "hard" rule on these things, recovery times and other downtime things are more of a suggestion and inspiration for how we frame the next adventure. If a player really really has to get to play this particular character right now, we'll usually work out an early release for him - I usually fuck with the character a bit, asking for gold or recovery checks or whatever, but in reality we're just negotiating an acceptable fictional positioning where everybody is willing to have a buy-in for adventure. (I suppose a childish enough player could really twist my arm in fictional positioning, as long as we wanted to play with him.) It's also possible to make an adventure out of the recovery - the player brings in his character's dear brother who insists that everybody together should help him carry his injured kin to this fountain of youth deep in the jungle or whatever. If the players want to insist on it and are willing to prove their mettle, there's no reason to not get out of the penalty box earlier than the doctor's prognosis indicates.
