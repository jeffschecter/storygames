\n Jason said we should fight about whether ashcans are awesome or not!

To start off, this is what I mean by ashcans: cheaply produced or handmade booklets or other materials with very small print runs (in the dozens) that are sold, given away, or traded. The games inside could be great or they could be terrible; they could be "finished" or they could barely hang together; they could be so experimental or conceptual that they are impossible to play.

No matter what they are like, I love them all: I think they are amazing ways to (1) try out indie publishing, (2) connect on equal footing with other game designers, (3) show off your crazy ideas, (4) maybe get some feedback on something, (5) maybe send a project out into the wild that you'll never work on again, (6) maybe see if there's enough interest in something to keep pursuing it (even lower risk than Kickstarter!), etc. Whether people play them or not, whether it goes anywhere or not, I think they're great. Years later, they stand as a monument to a specific moment in your creative life and a record of the ideas you were experimenting with.

I don't think they are necessary at all; I don't think people should feel like they have to produce them; but I love the little glimpses they provide and the way they make possible the publishing (in print!) of games that may not be feasible in any other format. Selling 6 copies of the game you photocopied at Kinkos is a super boss feeling, and I highly recommend it.

I suspect (though I don't know for sure) that Jason is upset about a particular moment in Forge indie games history (The Ashcan Front from GenCon 2007) and some of the ideological and publishing debates that happened around it. But hopefully he'll set me straight!

Jason, what's wrong with ashcans?

    \n I am not Jason, but will answer! The problem with ashcans is that you are commercializing your workflow. Like in your post, we need to define terms: game that you are done with and happen to be producing in a low-cost, lo-fi manner -- that is not what I'm talking about. In-progress, unfinished thing, no matter the publishing quality -- that is what I'm talking about. If you sell something that isn't done and isn't playable, that is problematic. I'm not going to go so far as to say wrong, because to each their own and I can see the artistic merit in it, plus it's an idea that's culminated in Kickstarter, which I think the jury's still out on. But, for me, it's unethical.

    The outcomes you mention, Jay-Wall, are good ones and I think they can happen without money changing hands. If I were going to a convention today and had a game ready for playing, but not ready for publishing, I'd print up some copies and put them in people's hands to check out. If I had a game I knew I couldn't finish, I'd put it up for others to use and do what they want to with (this, I've done plenty.)

    TL;DR: they make something about money that I don't think should be about money.

    \n I wasn't an organizer of the Ashcan Front but I was a close outsider. I sold my game Poison'd as an ashcan at GenCon, and the feedback I got from its ashcan's players made a stronger finished game.

    I still sell pre-release versions of my games, when I have a reason to. I made and sold a 25-copy "preview edition" of Apocalypse World, for instance. It was kind of gorgeous.

    Most or all of the ashcans sold at the Ashcan Front were ethical. Everybody was careful to be clear that these were games in development, not finished games. Most or all of them included the promise of a discount on the final game, when it came to be. I personally bought most or all of the Ashcans on offer, and I don't regret it a bit.

    The goal of the Ashcan Front was to be a solution to the problem of finding playtesters. The reasoning went that if someone invested a little cash in your game, knowing it was unfinished and still in development, they'd be more likely to invest time and effort giving it a try, and give you feedback.

    It turns out that nope! That's not what happens.

    You can see the exact effect today in some Kickstarters, when one funds beautifully with lots of buzz, delivers well, but then disappears from view because nobody plays the game once they have it. It turns out that a game that doesn't inspire people to play it doesn't inspire people to play it, even if they spent money on it. The way to find playtesters is harder than selling an ashcan, it's to design a game that people will be eager to play, even when it's still in development.

    -Vincent

    \n Creators commercialize their workflow all the time. If you bought Scrivener you bought into a commercialized workflow. The product works, but it's under development. Tinderbox sells its updates. So does Adobe. Artists sell their sketches. All but maybe one or two of the games done as an ashcan in the two years of the Ashcan Front was as complete and functional and as incomplete as an early release of Scrivener or Tinderbox or Photoshop.

    Getting people interested enough in what you're doing to playtest your game is hard. You pretty much try anything and everything you can think of. You can have your game up for download online and promote it to the best of your ability and still not provoke any playtesting, or enough playtesting. When you've tapped out your inner circle and your wider circle, how do you find new people? Gen Con is expensive. But it's the biggest gaming convention in the western hemisphere. The dealer hall at Gen Con is expensive, but the vast majority of Gen Con attendees will pass within feet of you and maybe give you a little of their time if you set up shop there and you can catch their eye. You could spend years online fretting out a couple of potentially interested people a month via social media and it'll all add up to maybe a day's worth of the conversations you can have in the dealer hall at Gen Con with people passing by who are hungry for something new.

    Except, it doesn't work. I'm not sure why. Danielle got no playtesting from her Kagematsu ashcan. The designers who participated in the Ashcan Front benefited from the relationships they made with others in the design community, and almost not at all from new relationships with the wider Gen Con attendees who bought their ashcans. The ones who completed their games would have done it anyway, with their existing circle of gamers, or connections they made in other ways, or the connections they made with others in the design community from participating in the booth made it possible for them to complete their games.

    However, you can do things with physical objects that are inspiring to some people, things that just might tip them into playtesting your game. A letterpress cover like The Smoke Dream. A splatter painted cover. A hand stitched binding. Selling ashcans at Gen Con might not be a solution for wider playtesting, but even within your inner circle and wider circle people still have limited time. You'll do whatever it takes to capture their attention for your game. John Wick does YouTube videos. I don't think I'll be doing an ashcan for The Clay That Woke, but I'd absolutely do one again for a game in the right future circumstances.

    Paul

    \n Sometimes an ashcan is necessary for the design process because the game has visual or tactile elements that need to be tested out. For example: the Finnish edition of Zombie Cinema was not quite an ashcan (that is, the game design was finished, and it was sold as a finished special edition), but it was a small run (100 copies), handcrafted, and basically a test run for the more ambitious 1000 copy English production run. The latter could not have happened without the prototyping and experience accomplished in producing the former. I could easily imagine myself doing the same thing again with a somewhat more unfinished game design, if the situation warranted it.

    As for why ashcans are sold for money instead of distributed in other ways, I think that's a good question. As has been said, ashcan sales per se do not seem to engender motivation for the sort of creative partnership that a designer hopes for. Ashcans and similar drafts in other creative media are often not sold, but rather distributed on the basis of social networks and status games and such. The equivalent would be if I sent my new game's beta draft to Ron Edwards, Paul Czege and then down the list of my personally important game designer idols in the hopes of catching their attention and garnering some feedback and playtesting. There's a disadvantage in how closed that sort of process is, but then what use is an open process if nobody's interested in participating?

    For myself, I suspect that selling ashcans is probably counter-productive for finding the right partners for your process, except if it's done in a large enough scale and high profile enough like my Finnish-language Zombie Cinema; that style works because the game gets in so many hands, gets reviewed and discussed as a finished game, so it produces the creative attention you need. However, for an ashcan any smaller than that it seems to me that you'd be better off producing just ten copies and choosing who to give one very carefully. Every copy you sell to somebody is a copy that goes into hands that feel not the slightest obligation to help you.

    Then again, I think that the Ashcan Front had solid reasoning: I wouldn't be willing to pay for that booth if the other side of the equation (the interested playtester) didn't participate in the expenses in some manner. It'd just be way too expensive, unless my game absolutely couldn't be finished without doing it.

    Regarding practical plans, I'm planning to produce about a dozen copies of an Eleanor's Dream ashcan once I finish the writing. These'll go to long-term Finnish (the game's in Finnish) acquaintances who've shown enduring interest in this sort of shit, and I'll ask first whether they're interested in taking a careful look and preferably playing the game. Very similar to how book publishers handle advance reader copies.

    \n The Ashcan Front, for me, had two things about it. Firstly, a homemade aesthetic to printed games. Secondly, a commercial model of selling 80% finished games, so you could get feedback.

    The first bit was great. It produced beautiful things.

    The second bit, for me, wasn't. There was, I think, nothing ethically wrong with it. People knew what they were buying.

    But it created a weird commercial incentive. For a while, there was much excitement around ashcans, and this tempted people to sell half-finished games under that umbrella. It was less work, it was popular and respected game designers were doing it: why wouldn't you do that?

    It is hard to draw the line between what was okay and what wasn't. For me, Paul's Acts of Evil ashcan was a cool thing, a game which he'd come to a creative block with, so he produced a beautiful object to inspire others to help him finish with it. Eventually, he even gave anyone who had bought the ashcan permission to finish the game themselves. All cool, all ethical. But they weren't all like that.

    You can see a similar thing happening with Kickstarters. They give people a bad incentive to sell half-finished games, provided they present it in an aesthetically pleasing way (i.e. with a nice video or a good logo).

    \n This is a really interesting discussion. Thanks to all who are participating.

    I'm generally a huge fan of ashcans because I think deadlines and products are great ways to move people to actually getting something out the door to players. I look at something like the Dungeon World red book, and I'm stunned by how much of the game's success hinged on getting the game into people's hands in a way that signaled that the game was worth the player's time. It's a totally different kind of thing than just putting up a game on a forum or a blog because the producer of the ashcan/early release took the time to produce a physical thing. And I think that's cool.

    I also think that designers are probably bad at knowing what value people are really taking from their game (h/t @JDCorley). My first game, The Play's The Thing, totally falls into @lumpley's description of a successful Kickstarter that no one plays; it turned out that people wanted to own the game much more than they wanted to play the game. I think this is 50% because owning a Shakespearean RPG is cool, and 50% about the actual game play itself. But I've also had people email me to say that they've used some ideas or resources in the game in ways that I didn't expect.

    But then... I'm sympathetic to @Jason_Morningstar's point that ashcans can ultimately be about status. And that's some ugly stuff, where our community is less focused on producing playable games than we are on producing saleable products. While I wouldn't call that unethical, it's clearly not an optimal outcome. I hadn't thought about ashcans through the lens of status before.

    I guess part of me worries here that this is really about design process, and I'm pretty loathe to make a lot of judgements on what other people do to bring games to fruition. Some people work on a game for 2 years, release the text for free, and put up a Kickstarter... others have an idea, print some ashcans to see if people are into it, and then self-publish a run of books if they get good feedback. I'm not sure I feel comfortable telling anyone how to put together a great game. (And maybe that the ashcans themselves might be the best outcome for the game, the best format for an idea that isn't actually going to go any further.)

    Do others feel like ashcans aren't a legitimate part of the design process? Like they are a detour from finishing something of worth?

    I think it's kind of inevitable that people are not going to be interested in playtesting stuff they paid for, though. Money is just a messy way to try to build relationships.
    \nIt's totally a thing in the political sphere though. A politician doesn't make you an adherent by promising you stuff. He makes you an adherent by getting you to do favors for him. These are a common inexpensive way for those who want to make their own comics on a very small budget, with mostly informal means of distribution. A number of cartoonists have started this way and gone on to more traditional types of publishing, while other more established artists continue to produce minicomics on the side. Comparable with indie music, the phenomenon shares ideas about autonomy of the artist because of the DIY aspect.

    \n So here's the problems I see with creating a hostile environment for ashcans:

    1) It creates a "your game better be finished/complete or else" environment for up and coming creators. Yes, we like to hold ourselves and each other to high standards sometimes, but there's been plenty of games released commercially that had some issues that don't get the flack that ashcans do. It makes perfectionism and unreasonable playtesting expectations a bigger problem than they already are, which seems like ultimately it creates an even bigger divide between "established" indie designers and everyone else.

    2) Publishing is increasingly not a big deal, whether in print (especially as a photocopied thing) or as a PDF or something else. The difference between whether something is formally finished or not is increasingly immaterial. At least out here in the PacNW, a lot of "unfinished" games by folks like John Harper (several versions of Danger Patrol, World of Dungeons, several versions of The Regiment, Ghost Lines), Ben Robbins (Kingdom, most recently), and myself (several versions of Geiger Counter, Metrofinal), and other folks get played regularly without anyone caring whether they "finished" or not. This isn't playtesting; it's just play. If I printed up some booklets of the latest version of Metrofinal to sell for $1-$5 at a meetup, nobody would bat an eye, and that's not because of my status or even because the games are playable and fun already, despite me not being satisfied with them. It's just not a big deal. You're paying for something that's enjoyable but ultimately disposable, like a beer.

    Resisting this and insisting on a firm divide between "finished" and "unfinished" games seems like (1) resisting the tide of where publishing is going and (2) trying to establish a firm divide between creators and consumers, both of which I find to be really wrongheaded and bad for indie games in the long run. The good news, I guess, is that it doesn't matter what I think and people can do whatever they want.
