\n Okay, so I've suggested Geiger Counter to those members of my gaming circle most likely to enjoy it, and there's a definite possibility that it will get played in about a weeks time.
\nOnly issue being, the most exposure any of us have had is me reading the rules and the play examples on this forum (though hopefully the more interested will check out the rules beforehand), so I'm concerned about making mistakes that might make things difficult or dull.
\n
\nSo, other than getting my friend to break out his wipeboard for notes and the map, is there any advice any of the people who've actually given it a go can offer to make a game run smoother or more entertainingly? Both with the game itself and the set-up beforehand (I'm tending towards coming up with the setting and at least the broad strokes of the characters collectively, rather than coming in with a setting and getting people to make individual characters).

    \n My quick comment would be to use the character creation / game set up rules from version 1, and then all the play rules from version 2 (except the keynote goals from version 2, which rock). Version 1 is much better at making an ensemble of characters, while version 2 reads more like a standard "each person has their guy."

    On that same point, it's important to make sure people are in it for the story, not for "their guy" to win or survive.

    Being on the same page about what genre you're trying to do is also really critical. Talk about it ahead of time.

    \n Like Ben says, the biggest problems I've noticed all come from bringing a "trying to win/survive" tactic from previous game experiences to Geiger Counter, instead of approaching it as "let's tell a story in which everybody dies." The ensemble-creation approach that he's pointing at, as opposed to doing standard character creation, might help with some of that.

    Also: take your time planning out a setting, characters, and a menace that everyone can get excited about. You should be really drooling to watch the action go down and see how everyone dies, just like you're off to the movies to see Stargate or Alien or Scream. If you're not excited and inspired by the premise, it'll be harder to riff on later.

    \n What Ben and Jonathan said. And:\n
      \n
    • For the setting generation, ask for genre types like teenage flick, zombies, aliens, sci fi, splatter/gory or low key, etc.
      • \n
    • Once you are on to an idea, get people start drawing on that map.
      • \n
    • Prepare a bullet point summary of the rules, e.g. the dice pool placed on the maps and the survival dice - they can be tricky do dig out from the text in the heat of the game.
      • \n
    • Ignore the advice on not framing scenes for your own character. You should be able to make the spotlight move around anyway.

    \n A nice side effect of not being able to frame your own character is that you're constantly forced to explain why some people are here but others (like your character) are gone. Why are Doc and Missy by themselves now? Clearly they just went down to the cargo bay to get tools for "the big plan." Now they get to talk alone.

    Those random combinations push you towards making it more like a movie, less like a party with a marching order. It's part of the solid geiger gold.

    (that's also why it's super-useful to have tokens with everyone's name on the map, so you can keep track of where people were last framed)
