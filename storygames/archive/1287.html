[rant]Is it time to kill a Sacred Cow: "Roleplaying Game" ?

komradebob
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
The very first time I went to The Forge, I encountered a thread titled something like "Killing Sacred Cows", which was all about asking some very fundamental questions about what was really necessary in an rpg, and what was simply in most designs by reasons of inertia.

It was mind expanding. I encountered ideas I'd never seen before. Character death optional? Distributed GM duties?! All kinds of good stuff.

So I'll bring up another Sacred Cow: The term, Roleplaying Game, and put it out there. Is it time to kill this one, too?

The history:
As far as I can tell, the main reason the term even exists is because there were grumpy miniatures gamers who wanted D&D, with its crazy hippy rules to go the hell away. So, between the folks pushing D&D out of the miniatures wargaming hobby and the folks trying to define this "thing" which wa sdeveloping, someone suggested "role-playing game". I'm guessing an undergrad with some psych background, but who knows? The name stuck.

Well, much to the chagrin of the minis gamers, D&D struck a bunch of right notes at the right time. And it grabbed an audience outside of the minis wargaming hobby.(It also gained a following inside the minis hobby, too. What's doubtful to me is thata the fans inside the minis and/or hex'n'chip hobby gave up their other pursuits. Rather, D&D became another game they played, along with more then-mainstream games).

So why kill the term?
Well, at one level, it cuts ties with baggage. D&D and role-playing are intimately related. It's the "type" game that defines everything else. RPGs have taken off in all kinds of directions since D&D made a splash, but at the end of the day, rpg=D&D.

Dropping the term lets a game stand on its own.
No more need of a "What is roleplaying?" section. Just tell players what they do in the specific game.

Dropping "roleplaying game" means the ability to recombine mechanics with other game types.
You want a board or cards or text or whatever? Fine. No problem. You want aspects that you associate with RPGs? Also fine. Put 'em in. But you get to concentrate on what you want for that specific game without having to tie it in to any number of other games.

Gamers still Get It.
Seriously. A gamer that likes rpgs will probably still see aspects of rpgs in your game, and like it for it. Players who've never seen an rpg can enjoy it without feeling like they need to get into the whole hobby.

Roleplaying Game sounds daft.
It sounds uppity and exclusionary and elitist. We're talking about Play Pretend with some structure and maybe a bit of luck. That's damn fun. "Role Playing Game" is intimidating. Many times, in actual practice, mainstream rpgs are more like party games than wargames. And they're fun when they are. So why not run with that aspect?

Sometimes, Size Matters.
But not the way you might think. The rules of most games are short. Really short. Monopoly is an aberrration for its length, and a bunch of the rules are on the cards or the board. Why have rpgs become so encyclopedic? Is it really necessary? Indie games tend to be much shorter than mainstream games, but even then we're still talking about an absurd length compared to most board games or card games or party games. The average book on Poker is shorter than most indie RPGs for heavens sake, and there's money on the line with that! Dropping RPG from the description inherently drops some of the assumptions of length.

Presentation.
Dropping RPG as a term unshackles us in terms of presentation. RPGs are books. Okay, they used to be boxed sets, but today, they're books. Even Indie games tend to be presented this way. And not only are they books, but they're books with a certain kind of feel: Lots of text, few pictures. Like a technical manual. I freely admit, especially where indie games are concerned, that economics somes in here. But, damn! Has anyone thought that the book presentaion is a barrier to wider acceptance? If you'd never encountered rpgs and taken them up as a hobby, can you seriously imagine yourself playing a game that had 50 pages of rules (much less 300 and an index!)with only afew illustrations? What is the length of rules you'd accept if you'd never seen an rpg? Would you ever consider playing something that required reading and absorbing a book of rules?

Well there it is. I've ranted ( again). I think maybe it's time to stop fighting the good fight, stop grinding uphill, and go out and let the good games that are being made stand on their own. I don't think the games being made that I like so much even need the crutch of "roleplaying game" any more.

    Nancy,

    Here's my point of view about this: In games that are "playing a game by taking on the role of a character in the story/adventure," the label makes perfect sense.

    But what about Primetime Adventures. Or Sorcrerer. Or the Pool. Or a gazillion games out there right now that are as much about narration as taking on the role of a character.

    This isn't to say that these games don't have role-playing in them as part of the fun -- it's just that taking on the role of the character is not the focus of many of these games. (It is, however, a requirement of the game Puppetland.)

    And just as important, each player and each group will have their own feel and style and joy about what mix of being in the role and simply narrating events they feel comfortable with. I could successfully and in a very enternaining way, play a whole session of Call of Cthuhlu or AD&D and never speak in character or say, "I do this..." Instead could spend the night say, "Tomak look down at the necklace half buried in the dirt, remembering his dead wife as his lifts it..." and all would be well.

    The label Roleplaying Game make it seem like the point of these games is to take on the role of the character. It isn't. It's one aspect of the game -- as determined by a mix of rules and playing style preference.

    I think it's a big turn off to people. Everyone tells stories. Not everyone knows how to "take on a role." (But note that, in order to tell a story, we often take on a role: "And then she was like, 'Well, FUCK! you bitch!,' and wasn't having any of that..." and so on.)

    Rather than talk about whether "roleplaying game" is a marketing term or not, or some of the other issues that have been raised, i'm going to focus in on one thing that isn't being discussed that really is a sacred cow.

    Nancy wrote:

    I like the term and think it aptly describes what we're doing: playing a game by taking on the role of a character in the story/adventure.

    And others, such as Tomas, have said things in agreement with this: a roleplaying game is a game in which you play a role. I've even said it myself, at times, in other places. But should we accept this, or is it holding back creativity?

    The characteristic that defines an RPG isn't playing a role, but playing with tokens and a gameboard that partially or completely exist in the imagination instead of as a physical artifact, which means they can be modified in play. The non-RPG they truly most resemble is Nomic, with its modifiable rules. It's what distinguishes early D&D, which didn't mention roleplaying at all or really require that players act out their roles, from wargames and minis. It's even present in games that attempting to borrow from board games instead of minis, such as The Fantasy Trip.

    Although playing a role may be the easiest way to play this kind of game, it doesn't have to be the only activity in the game. Things like collaborative setting creation and shared characters are attempts to explore modification of the group fiction beyond the familiar. There are certainly other ways we haven't even thought of yet.

    To what extent does the term "roleplaying game" inhibit exploring these other ways?
