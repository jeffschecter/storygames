\n I've been interested for a while now in running a road trip game using one of the *World engines, probably either Dungeon or Apocalypse World. I was thinking that it would be fun to draw on some of the great American road trip books for inspiration, plus maybe some genre fiction like Amber or the Dark Tower series to glue it all together in a fantastic setting. So you'd have a disparate group of travellers venturing into a weird version of Hunter Thompson's Las Vegas, then some snippets from The Road, then On The Road, and maybe some Huck Finn in there, too.

I'm wondering how to keep a game like that moving and not just have it degenerate into picaresque little adventures, and how to keep some kind of theme and Fronts since part of the road trip is that you keep moving forward and keep seeing new territory and new people?

    \n Two road-trip games that performed great in my actual play experience are (unsurprisingly) Ribbon Drive and (perhaps more surprisingly) Psi*Run. There's much to learn about road-trip play from trying out those two, very compact one-shot games.

    As for AW, theoretically it should run what you're proposing just out of the box, if you just have an appropriate mixture of character playbooks. However, my actual-play experience always focused on small sedentary communities as the most natural outcome of character selection... You could sidestep that by defining (and, to a degree, mandating) a nomadic community as the focal center of play, instead.

    The classic road trip property is Route 66, or maybe Alias Smith & Jones.

    \n JDCorley's post suggests me that The Drifter's Escape and all of the Trollbabe family of games might be relevant here: games about a main character wandering into a new place & situation, interacting with it and leaving anytime \u2014 but always leaving it changed. Dogs in the Vineyard is "the" Trollbabe descendant with an interacting cast of main characters instead of just a lone wanderer.
    To do that with AW, what you have to change is prep: instead of a single large all-spanning tangle, you prep small loaded situations, mostly self-contained, but each ready to explode. The instructions in AW are not especially useful for this, but if you don't jettison all of those then you'll be building overarching plots and hidden link between adventures, without even thinking of it: basically you have your Fronts, unaltered, but as an MC you don't go looking at those very often, and you look at those while prepping more often than while playing.
    The prep instructions in Drifter's, describing the building of a loaded situation seed on which the arrival of a foreigner without any ties pours instant gasoline, might provide you a starter for prepping the individual "adventure" situations\u2026 as could the general concepts in Trollbabe. The town creation procedure in Dogs is like a platonic ideal of what you could develop after some trial and error: a very tight "how to" to building the local situation, specific to your game.

    In my previous post I maybe misread you and thought you were focused on "road trip" as the coming-of-age or inner growth story of a small cast of characters, with the external backdrop of travel (meeting many different places and people over a relatively short timespan) providing the fictional instrument of such growth (but not being really important per se).
