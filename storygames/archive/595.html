A very small competition

Graham
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Stuff to Watch
We talked a while ago about a competition where I sent beer to people if they wrote games based on English authors.

I got rid of the beer (after huge amount of people said they didn't drink). And there's a revised version of the competition over in the Endeavour forum at the Forge..

The deal is now: if someone writes me a game based on an English author (i.e. an author born in England), I'll send them a postcard from somewhere in England connected with that author. And if I really like the game I'll send a remote-controlled Dalek or something.

This is partly an excuse to travel round the country, of course. I don't have much reason to go to Bletchley Park or Bath, but if I have to send a postcard from there, I've got an excuse.

In any case, I'm hoping I'll get a small number of games. What I'm really hoping is that people will enter another competition, then remember this, and write a Dickens game or a Hamlet game or, hell, a Wordsworth game or something.

Have a look at the Forge thread for the full rules. Do let me have any reactions, comments, questions, etc etc etc.

Graham

  • Graham
    April 2006
    By the way, let me explain the thinking behind specifying that the authors are born in England. There's two reasons, really.

    1. Often, authors from other countries are wrongly identified as English. I didn't want people writing Joseph Conrad or Roald Dahl or Oscar Wilde RPGs for a competition about English authors.

    2. It excludes Tolkien.

    So, yeah, I'm happy to include writers born abroad to English parents (such as J G Ballard, Mervyn Peake or George Orwell). But I want to be careful to exclude authors who wrote in England but who should really be claimed by the country they were born in.

    Graham

    rr23, I guess it depends upon the vision of England you want. Let's put aside history for a moment; from thor's comment, I had taken him to mean he wanted to capture something of modern england. Given this, you could watch almost anything half-decent (because total trash risks carrying nothing true within it at all) and from that draw things out that seem evocative. For me, I would mentally juxtapose between features of that program and what you would find in equivalent US media. The feature that you've captured might be a fairly universal feature (drunk people eating doner kebabs at 2am in a black cab) or a more local, narrow one (the church hall being the focus for community activities) but it will still evoke some England. What I was getting at before was that I almost think the less universals are better, because it draws together true threads to make a unique picture of england.

    With that in mind, here are a few. I realise now I've written it that a lot of these are comedies; it's interesting to think that the English like to be defined by their comedy...but it cold just be that I watch more US drama.

    Spaced - for me, this series by the Shaun of the Dead creators is bang-on in its depiction of north london life. The archetypes, the activities, the slang, the look of it all. Relating to the above point, not many people are in the Territorial Army, but the fact that Mike is splashes colour into the show. The fact that it is incongrous with many other aspects - the day-to-day mundanity and drugtaking - doesn't dispell the reality but just shapes it in an interesting way.

    People Like Us - A spoof documentary that followed people from different walks of life. In the main, the depictions tend more towards stereotypes, but should give a good baseline to work from. Also see the Office, Smoking Room, and Peep Show for other series that hold a mirror up to modern English life, very much warts and all.

    You could do worse than watch police shows. They tend to aim for verisimilitude and represent people from various walks of life, and tend to have the length to explore situations (and the accompanying setting) a bit more.
