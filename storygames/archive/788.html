    Just to make it clear, I wrote the extension for Dogs in the Vineyard with Kung Fu, not Dogs in the Vineyard for Kung Fu. My version of Kung Fu is a perpedicular way to escalate things. You still have talking and shooting, but now you can also have Kung Fu talking, and Kung Fu shooting.

    As far as Chi balance, that's the path of using exactly one Kung Fu trait per raise, and always using the Chi die as part of that raise. This really constrains your options, but on the whole can be quite effective at winning the conflict. What I particularly like about the Chi dice though is the room for communication. You can explicitly leave some good Chi dice after your raise, telling the other players to stay in the conflict another round, or to hit you hard, or just that you want some Kung Fu fallout. And then they get to choose what to do about it.

    - Mendel

    I was being partially facietious about the wuxia rules. But... Talking is fine. People do it lots in wuxia, when they're not in conflicts. But generally, conflicts in wuxia films start at the physical phase and only get to really serious talking once the characters have thrown everything they've got at each other. Fallout from a kung-fu match means you've been beaten up and you're suffering, but wuxia is all about people suffering while trying to make statements with their lives. Fallout from a talking contest, now, that means your statement has been compromised, and that's when wuxia characters die.
