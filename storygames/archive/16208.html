\n Hi.

Say you want a high-powered game, like Exalted or Anima Beyond Fantasy, but you don't want the crunch. You don't want the complex and sometimes wonky systems. You want the characters to matter more than their stats. And you want the character evolution to feel organic, not predicated on the best design for MORE POWER!

Is there a game like this out there? If not, how would you go about creating one?

    Posted By: Eero TuovinenSounds to me likeHeroQuestwould be just right for you. It's low-crunch, but deals well with variable power levels.
    \nYes. This. This is my choice for playing Exalted, or anything with a massive scale - where I want a wide range of action types to be meangingful.

    Godlike powers? Yes. Mortal skills? Yes. One on one fights? Yes. Party conflicts? Yes. Army battles? Yes. The workings of nations and politics? Yes. Wanna pull back and do a sweeping pass of history to jump ahead a few centuries? No problem.

    Although the book recommends (in 2nd edition), that you use the same starting stats regardless of the power level they're supposed to express, my preferred method is to use stats as a scale. This more easily lets powerful characters express their power against lesser powered characters. As power increases on something closer to an exponential scale, crunch only increases on a linear scale ONLY when you have characters of differing power levels. Most of the time the increased values cancel each other out before you roll.

    In my mind, it is the best choice. I've run a couple of games using HQ in the Exalted world. Players accomplished many battles and made lots of progress. In one game, in the course of 8 hours (spread across 8 1 hour lunches) one group got into conflicts with gods, Exalted, and swarms of other enemies, carved out a piece of Creation and were on their way to establishing their own small nation, making war with a nearby Dragonblood principality and demonic pirates. I. LOVE. IT.

    Ryan Macklin's Mythender is also a good choice, when that comes out. It's especially goodif you still want the pursuit of more power to be a major element of your game. It's only for very specific stories, however (that is, killing Gods and taking their stuff) but it's quite fun and over the top. Not as good as HQ for an extended campaign, but better for a one-shot or short series of sessions.

    \n No one's mentioned Amber, yet, so I will:
    * Diceless
    * Low crunch (but not fiated)
    * Shapers-of-reality power levels
    * Doesn't have to be the Zelazny universe, with a bit of filing and scraping

    On a similar note, Mage (full rules, including Dynamic Magic) kicks ass at the UberMaster levels, though you might find you spend a lot of time noodling over combining spheres in weird ways. Once you got Mages with 3+ dots in several spheres, there's a LOT of funky shit they can do on the fly (never mind world-shaking Rotes).

    Also, I'll toss out Hero. YES, Hero can be "low crunch," with some tweaking:
    * Superheroic rules (just to avoid money and equipment crunch)
    * SPD 5 max (to control combat lengths, but see below also)
    * No Pools (not even Elemental Control--NO having to recalculate shit in mid-fight!)
    * Pre-calculate EVERYTHING (front-end crunch, yes, but it makes handling combat rounds REALLY fast!)
    * Don't fear the STOP Powers (high-powered PCs ~= co-GM-authorship) - Penetrating Transforms, Extradimensional Travel, FTL, Time Control--all these make for serious high-power with no real crunch to speak of (well... Transform requires doing "damage" until the transformation is complete... but...).

    Finally, I like Sufficient Advanced for its awesome approach to varied power levels (which really brings to light how bad-ass the PCs are, when their tech levels exceed the natives').

    \n It would be wild to play Hero System one of these days, just for the novelty. I've never played a pure-blood pointfuck game like GURPS or Hero. The point buys in more middle of the road games like BRP already make me crawl up the wall (you should see me in the middle of it, it's sort of entertaining), but it's all probably because I've always had difficulty wrapping my head around the joy people get from being able to first imagine a character and only then execute that character in the system, with "complete freedom to create the exact character you want", as these things are often marketed. (Except not really, as the game's real selling point is the claim that by counting these points your creation will be "balanced" in some vague sense with another player's attempts.) Even now that I know exactly how the inner mindset works, I still end up ridiculously minmaxing characters just to get rid of that much too large point pool.

    Just occurred to me while reading David's paean. I think I've got some edition (a green-black hardcover, fourth edition or something?) lying somewhere out there, perhaps I should look at whether I could figure out what makes people interested in it.
