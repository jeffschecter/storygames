\n Something of a straw poll, this, but I'm interested in people's replies.

I've sent out very few review copies. I gave a copy of Play Unsafe to Ken Hite, once, and that's about it.

But this hasn't stopped my stuff getting reviewed. Both Play Unsafe and A Taste For Murder have very positive reviews on RPG.NET. My Cthulhu scenarios get reviewed in other places.

Also, some conversations with podcasters have put me off sending review copies. I've heard things like "People send us lots of stuff to review", which makes me think, well, I won't add to that pile.

What about you? Do you send review copies? If so, has it worked for you?

    \n As I described in the other thread, I don't send review copies. Thinking about this, a part of it is probably a dislike for the status games involved: I always feel like I should be deciding who's a real journalist and who's just a hack if I got into that game.

    My impression is that not giving out review copies has had some impact on the amount of review, but that it depends on the product. Zombie Cinema has had a fair amount of reviews and a huge amount of actual play reporting under this policy, while Solar System and World of Near have been all but ignored in this regard - neither has been reviewed anywhere as far as I know. This is most likely the sort of thing that depends on the nature of the product; I have a feeling that WoN would probably have benefited a great deal from artificial publicity, while ZC has found its audience without help. They are very different games all around, so one can make many guesses as to the crucial difference; perhaps it's that one is a light game while the other requires plenty of commitment, or that one is very focused while the other is more generic... difficult to say.

    \n I give copies of my games to Ken, but I don't think of them as review copies. I mean, he reviews stuff, and that's why I gave him a copy of my first game years ago. But now I give him copies because I think he's cool and I like talking to him about games. It's nice if he reviews them, but that's not something I specifically think about now. It's just "Oh hey, it's Ken! Let me give you my game!"

    I've sent out a couple PDFs at the request of some people who wanted to review the game. None of those have turned into reviews yet. Blowback also got a very even-handed, decent review at RPGnet today by BlackHat Matt, who purchased the game.

    I also just sent a copy of Blowback to one of the few people I go all fangirl over, because I heard he was pretty interested in my game. If he decides to review it, I'm sure that will lead to fame and glory for me, but mostly I'm just geeking out over the idea that someone I admire so much is going to have something with my byline on it.

    Mostly though, any free PDFs I've sent out have been in the context of a larger conversation, for literal review\u2014 to be looked over and dissected and inform a greater dialogue, usually over email or maybe in a blog. And that's worth it every single time.

    \n I get a bunch of stuff through my podcasting through Pulp Gamer, though I don't consider what we do on Out of Character to be "reviews" in the sense that I say "this worked, this didn't, this is what I think". Normally on Out of Character I feel my job is to find the game a home. In other words, I don't say "HERO is the most godawful system for adapting PS238 in the history of mankind" (though it clearly is), I instead say "PS238 uses the HERO system, and there's a big community for that and a LOT of superhero stuff that you can plunder for ideas, and it's clear that the author loves both the system and the source material". That helps PS238 find a home with someone who will like it, and emphasize its strong points. It's not a review, in the sense that I do a lot of critical thinking about it and pick it apart.

    \n I'm on the flip side. I've received some review copies, one I got a turnaround for very quickly, although I had some difficulty being balanced as it wasn't my thing, and I usually research books heavily before getting them so most of the time I'm reviewing something I like. Another it took me about a year to finish, a real life catastrophe got in the way, but I did finally put it out, and Fred Hicks was very gracious about the delay. I'm currently sitting on one because I really want to play it to do the review justice, rather than putting out just a capsule review, but it's damn hard getting people together to play it, so I'm settling with a 1 player 1 GM game in the near future to get it out without too much more delay. I find with a review copy there's some more obligation to be thorough, which makes it harder to put out quickly, and there's also the fact that if you don't like it you can't just ditch it and not review (if I buy a game that's crap, I usually don't feel like reading it all the way through to put the review out, as that doesn't benefit me in any way).

    Given that, it's worth considering if someone is just going to put out a review of the book without a playtest, or if they are going to do a playtest, if they've got an existing group that's game to try out new things.
