\n This is something that seems to me to almost be the logical conclusion of some of what had been discussed, or what people said they'd do with extra money, lately.

We all know the ransom model: Pay X money in Y amount of time, and the game gets released, either for free for everyone, or released period, with those who chipped in having already basically pre-ordered copies. Not enough people pitch in, no game-release.

Now, people say that with more money, they'd do more things: More art, more editing, better layout, more promotion, etc.
So why not combine these two things for tiered ransom?

I, or someone else, writes a game. For let us say $200, I'd release the game. I leave the ransom up for two months. If I get the $200, I move to the next ransom: $100, and I'd have editing (please, I'm just throwing figures here), if I get the editing money after a month, the game will get edited, and I'd move to the next phase.
Now, I'd potentially move to the next phase regardless of whether or not the previous one was met, and I'd let people know of the length of the cycle, so they won't think the game is released immediately upon the game being ransomed.

And that way I'd move on to art, layout, promotion.

The game would get released, so long the original ransom is met, but if you'd want the game you get, or gets released to be better looking, you'll need to chip in in additional places, or alternately, more people could chip in (we sometimes hear of people who heard too late, so didn't get to pre-order a copy this way, whatever).

This way people would know what their money is ear-marked for as well, you're basically generating free and ongoing word-of-mouth (though probably not all good!), and if the game has no art or editing, people will know why.

Now, I think this is the logical end of much of recent discussion, and I think it might actually work, but there is a certain amount of irony in my post. I don't think it's contradictory.

    \n I guess I was thinking that a corporation would exist that owns the game as an intellectual property and all the buyers-in are shareholders in that company and have voting and dividend rights. I'm not sure about vastly more complicated -- I mean, I'm sure there's a crap-load of stuff I don't know about forming and operating a corporation, but other than that, it seems like a pretty simple idea.

    ETA:
    Playing on your example numbers above, a designer imagines a game and predicts that they'd need to get paid $200 to bring the system to a play-worthy state. So an entity is formed to own the game. Maybe the starting author holds the 200 shares. Or maybe not, she's just an employee of the newly formed entity and once the entity has found some venture capital, it can start paying her for her design work. Once the entity has the $200 (or maybe before that, if the shareholders decide to proceed and the designer is willing to start before the projected needs are met) design takes place. The group then owns a game in rough state. $100 more is found for editing, another $150 for interior illustrations, $200 for a cover and $100 for layout. In stages or combined -- whatever. At this point, a real, finished game is there, not yet printed or distributed. But it's there and owned by the investors. Maybe they gather enough cash to print it or maybe they donate it to the public domain and release it to the net as an HTML document. That's up to the owners.
