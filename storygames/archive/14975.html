\n I read Paranoia 2nd ed. in the early 1990s. It's ethos of "never make a map so you can add stuff later" stuck, reinforced by Feng Shui and playing games and pre-made scenarios without maps. But when I stumble onto a good map in a scenario, I love it. I don't want a tactical exercise, but I want myself and the players to get a better visual of where they're at. Also, clue-gathering in Cthulhu feels cool if you can point out where you look.

I've made maps every now and then, but I think they suck. Maps in commercial products seem so professional and mine seem out of proportion, the walls look just like a pencil drawing instead of Something Greater, and the houses I make all look alike (that is, like my childhood flat).

Have you had the same problem? How did you get over it? And what do you appreciate in a map, as a player? Is this a silly non-problem which I can pass by just making one map after another?

    \n This has been one of the problems I've had over the years. I'm also in the Paranoia/Feng Shui camp of not making maps if you don't have to have them. Better to just put a few index cards on the table to represent general locations, distances, and positions if you really need to have one. But then, of course, they aren't very lasting.

    When I started a sandbox campaign with Ben Lehman's High Quality RolePlaying Game, I was originally toying around with the idea of taking a bunch of 3x3 inch blank cards (which I can make in obscene quantities at my work, just from scraps), draw little towns, forests, neighborhoods, and larger buildings on each card (in a style a la Tony Dowler), and then, as the players adventured and explored the world, I could just stick them down on a large piece of blank paper, give them a name, and voila!

    Didn't work out too well, mainly because I kept trying to emulate Tony's style too much (I don't know how he does it, since his stuff is both simple, detailed, and has a way of making them look so curious with a unique sense of perspective), and I got tired of it really quickly.

    Now, I'm making city maps that just show streets (sometimes alleyways if necessary), and then leaving everything between blank, so either the players or myself can write in a dot and notes about locations as needed.

    D&D With Pornstars also has some good advice on making simple, yet oddly detailed maps.

    \n Hello, I'm a professional fantasy cartographer, I work for many RPG publishers including Paizo and Mongoose Publishing, and a number of smaller publishers, plus I publish my own setting and create all the maps for it.

    If you're interested in creating maps for your games, and you want get better at it, use tutorials for your favorite mapping/graphics app, take part in monthly contests that is really a great learning experience for creating a map that fits a theme, I can offer no better recommendation than to joining the Cartographers' Guild - maps for novels and RPGs (free signup). All the pro cartographers, mappping software developers, many fantasy authors for the RPG industry are active members. Some use Campaign Cartographer, Photoshop, GIMP, Illustrator, Inkscape, MapTool - I use Xara Xtreme Pro. some create 3D maps, some hand-drawn - there are galleries of finished maps, there are Work-in-Progress maps where members and pros critique your maps as you post helping you to develop your particular masterpiece.

    Many members join as chicken-scratch artists and in 3 months are turning out maps that rival the professionals - really. The tutorials are awesome and the tutorial writers respond to questions asked of the tutorials. I am a senior member there, under the name 'Gamerprinter'.

    Check it out, there's no better place to learn about creating maps for game - worlds, regionals, city maps, dungeons, encounter scale maps, VTT maps, etc.

    Here's my latest contest entry - for the August Map Challenge at the Cartographers' Guild (there are already two other entries that meet or exceed mine!):



    Here's a link to the larger version of this map: Akayama Tengu Village Map

    Note while I do create completely digital maps as well, my primary style is a hybrid of digital (note the beveled effects, etc.), but it is placed beneath hand-drawn work.

    \n The trees, buildings, even the farmed land, but the main mountains are one time only hand-drawings. I started with a 24" x 36" blank piece of paper, scanned it into my large format scanner, imported to Xara Xtreme, made the white part of the scan, transparent, then placed beveled shapes with the water-color-like palatte, drop shadows, some feathering and transparency and built the map layer by layer beneath the hand-drawn elements both as a guide and the final hand-done look of an otherwise digital map.

    Long story, short - yes.

    Edit: oh, and this took me about 6 hours altogether over the last 2 days - I'm quite fast at what I do.
