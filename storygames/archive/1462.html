So I'd been mulling over the question of establishing an element, then reincorporating it at a later point in the story, as in improv.

How do you make a system that encourages that? How? How? It's such a complicated issue!

And, connected: What are scenes that don't resolve an issue about? Why are they there? What do players get rewarded for in them?

Then this morning I thought "Hey ... what about if, instead of them just rolling dice based on the level of a card, they put a die on the card each time they refer to the issue, then when there's a conflict they can roll those dice by reincorporating the issue into the current situation? So those early scenes are about them choosing where to place the resources they'll use in the eventual confrontations, of course."

Which ... y'know ... criminally obvious in hindsight.

    Posted By: Thomas RobertsonI assume that as soon as you choose to 'reincorporate' the issue and roll its dice, those dice go away? That seems cool.
    A little more complicated than that, actually. Here's how I'm thinking I'll elaborate it into the system.

    Each player has a color of dice. You will have dice of many colors in your pool, ready to place on your cards. If anyone (including you) narrates something that you (personally) think is a success for the character then you put a die of your color on the card. If another player narrates something that you (personally) think is a setback or aggravation for the character then you put a die of their color (from your pool) on the card.

    When you roll, the dice off of your card determine who wins. But whoever belongs to the color that, overall, beats all the other colors of dice gets to decide whether the victory (whoever has it) is the final resolution or a mere set-to on the way to either final resolution or leaving the problem unresolved.

    When you roll a conflict, the dice come off the cards, and are traded (winner takes loser dice, loser takes winner) except in the case of final resolution of an issue, in which case some set of dice (haven't yet decided ... winner dice? loser dice?) are removed from the game forever. The goal of the game is to be the first to get rid of all your dice :-)

    And (stolen from TSOY) ... you can't place the same type of die on the same card twice in a row. So either you've got to be bopping back and forth between two conflicts (all with the same color dice), or you've got to be doing a back-and-forth where you have successes and setbacks in order to use two different color dice.

    I think that the game of placing the dice (which corresponds to the game of consciously realizing the interactions and how your character is effected others and effects them in turn) is going to be, by far, the main fun and focus of the game. Resolving conflicts will just be the Maltese Falcon that draws people forward.

    I had similar thoughts along these lines last month, so I'll toss them into the mix.

    First I had this vague thought:
    Players have stuff they want to happen in the game written on blank cards. You write down a scene, a visual, a sound or action and the GM incorporates it into a narrative. When you bring out a card, the other players place tokens on it. Every time the GM incorporates a card into his narrative, he collects the tokens on the card and discards a token from all other cards presently on the table. Once a card runs out of tokens, it must be incorporated into the narrative, but the GM doesn't get any tokens for doing so. In other words, a card has to be put into the narrative when the tokens run out, but you can bring it in early and get rewarded with tokens for doing so. After you collect tokens from a card and complete your narrative, the GM duties are handed over to the person on your right. ...

    I just thought it was cool to have these weird narrative bits down on the table with countdown timers on each of them. "Aw crap, that shotgun's going to have to happen soon. Wait, here comes 'a true love betrayed.'"
    And then I spun this into a loose premise with a half-assed system:
    CSI: RPG

    The group is a team of CSI detectives putting together pieces of evidence in order to form a narrative of the crime and discover whodunnit. The players create the evidence by writing it down on cards.

    "A bloody fingerprint."
    "A shattered window."
    "An elvis impersonator costume."
    "A bank withdrawl slip."

    With each piece of evidence, an investigator postulates what happened at the crime scene. This is an adaptation of those quick "dramatic re-enactment" sequences on CSI. If an investigator takes a card while it still has tokens on it, he can spend those tokens to "tamper with the evidence." That is, to add twists to the other pieces of evidence, one token per detail. Any tokens you don't spend, you can keep for use as a foreshadowing/delaying tactic as described in the previous post.

    If you don't want any more twists added to a piece of evidence, you can spend a token to "seal" the evidence. That means you actually put the card in a plastic ziplock bag. If you want to tampe with sealed evidence, you have to spend a token to open the bag plus spend the standard token to do the actual editing.
    Not sure if this sparks any ideas, but I saw everyone riffing on this "writing stuff on cards" jam so I thought I'd join in.

    Okay, I've had the playtest, and I'm going to bubble a little. Okay ... a LOT. Executive summary: Everything I thought might work worked, and in addition the synergy of elements drove us to things that were intensely satisfying.

    Eric and I decided to just draw random (rather than choosing our elements deliberately). We pulled two cards from each category, and got the following: External: "Your World is under attack" and "People are being separated." Relationship: "You don't respect me," and "You care more for someone else." Internal: "Why am I so flustered?" and "Why don't I stick up for myself?"

    The setup: Two young kids have been drafted as pilots in a giant-mecha war against crab-aliens. Shima, is a cold, military, older girl. Yuki, is a hesitant, blushing young man trying to grapple with his own feelings. Shima trained Yuki. She's fallen for him, but can't express it. Yuki's convinced that nobody respects him, and is trying to summon the nerve to complain about that. It's the night before Shima ships out to detached duty, somewhere away from the space station they've been defending and training at.

    The "put dice on cards to prime for conflict" thing prompted me to start with a solo scene. Shima is packing her few belongings into her space-robot ... neatly pressed uniforms. A photo-booth slip of pictures falls out, with her and Yuki. Reminders of better times.

    Eric went with a scene we'd both be in, squaring up on the intersection of "Why am I so flustered?" and "You don't respect me." Bzz! Bzz! Alarm! We're under attack. Yuki is too slow getting his robot started, and the deck officer chews him out. Why doesn't he stick up for himself? They launch, and Shima, who can't express herself in words, finds a good medium in high energy plasma, blowing away any alien that tries to attack Yuki. He, of course, views this as proof that she doesn't think he can hack it.

    Next scene, after the battle, when they're prying the battered Shima from her cockpit, Yuki is already gone. He's stung, and doesn't wait to see if she's alright. That was sad :-(

    He goes off to his favorite sulking place. When he's had a chance to reprime dice in another solo scene, I introduce the character Fea, an energetic and outgoing girl who drags Yuki off to play some video games. Shima meets them in the hallway ... there is a brief set-to around the intersection of the stakes "Why don't I stick up for myself?" and "People are being separated." We resolve those two: Yuki doesn't stand up to Fea, and so she drags him off, leaving Shima all alone on her last night there, the words she wants to say dying on her lips.

    Lots of stuff is still primed. Straight on to more action. Next scene, video games. The screen flashes "Game Over" and Fea, the girl who always has a smile on her face, starts losing it. Not loud and messy, just leaning against the game with tears pouring down her face. Aliens are trying to eradicate the human race, and she can't keep up a brave front any more. She wants to look to Yuki for strength. We set a conflict right at the intersection of "You care more about someone else" and "Your world is under attack." My stakes are that Fea and Yuki kiss, and Shima (coming to confess her feelings) sees that as she turns the corner and quietly flees before they spot her. I win the stakes.

    Just two conflicts left: "You don't respect me" and "Why am I so flustered?" I prime a little melancholy by having Shima all gearing up to ship out, determined not to even care whether Yuki shows up to see her off. He doesn't. She cares.

    Then, Battle Claxons! Back out into space. The two of them compete with each other, with Shima unable to communicate how she wishes they could work together, and Yuki unable to play second fiddle to her any more, and in the end they can't get it together. Ego and fear stand between them.

    Hangar deck. Same conflicts, different context. Shima confronts Yuki about his "undisciplined behavior." They get into a heated argument about who's done wrong to whom, all in the context of their trainee/trainer, kohai/sempai relationship. I win, but refuse to resolve. We come right back to it, immediately, with the cards empty and needing to be primed in action.

    So we hit the same conflicts again, only much harder: One after the other, we say terrible, painful things (and, instantly, each of us primes a die on the card, because we see and recognize them as terrible and painful ... and we're saying the things in order to be able to prime the dice, it's an orouboros loop). Shima reveals that she saw Yuki and Fea kissing. "Is that what this is all about?" Yuki asks. "That's what this has always been about!" We do Naruto-style flashbacks to their training days, first seen from Yuki's point of view (Shima is always pushing him, never satisfied with any progress he achieves) then from Shima's point of view (Yuki has the heart to rise to any challenge, even when it's not easy for him).

    They feel something for each other. They both know it. They just can't get past their walls. Slowly, then quickly, the defenses slide back up. They salute angrily, both blaming the other for their own failings. They turn away from each other, and Shima leaves. The issues are not resolved, and as their values rise it becomes clear that they cannot be resolved. We don't have enough dice left. End of game.

    I have had whole campaigns that didn't give me the emotional punch of that game. And nothing big happened. A few minor robot-skirmishes. A kiss. People passing each other in hallways. Two people arguing violently, then turning away. That's it. And yet ....

    Elapsed time, from "Let's play Misery Bubblegum" to "And that's all there is to that": 60 minutes on the nose.

    Okay, mechanics-wise, here's what we did.

    Cards had paper-clips on them (starting with one on each card). You couldn't put more dice on the card than it had paper-clips. So you can only do so many introspective scenes before you need a conflict.

    When you make a conflict you stake cards, and you also state rough outcomes. Like, "I'm putting in 'Why don't I stick up for myself?" You're putting in 'You don't respect me,' eh? Well then, my desired outcome is that she treats you like a child, and you don't say a word about it. Oh, your desired outcome is that you're so effective as a punching-bag, without sticking up for yourself, that she can't bring herself to treat you bad? Cool."

    First each card that is staked instantly gets another paper-clip. So now there's some motivation to ratchet the tension up a little bit before the conflict is resolved (sorta like in Dogs, but lighter) in order to place that extra die on the card. You roll dice and tote up (as described in my second post).

    If the winning side had their card full (i.e. it had as many dice as paper-clips) then the conflict has the potential to resolve. If less dice were rolled then the conflict is automatically deferred. The person whose dice-color got the highest total (whether they're the winner by dice rolled or not) gets to decide whether or not to resolve.

    If the conflicts resolve then all dice involved in the conflict (as well as the cards) are removed from the game. Bye!

    If the conflict doesn't resolve then the issue on the winning side gets another paper-clip, and both issues go back to their owners. In that moment they are empty, but it's not uncommon for people to be loading dice onto those cards as resolution is narrated, just saying "Oh! That ratchets the tension ... and that ... and that."

    Now the simple way to be unable to resolve conflicts is that you simply get to a place where you have more paper-clips then you have dice. As it turns out, that wasn't actually our problem: I had six paper-clips, and six dice, but they were four dice of Eric's color and two of my own. Remember: I can't play the same die color on the same conflict twice in a row. The most of my dice that I could actually spend in was five. Heh. That's probably going to change when I revise how players use Motifs (a mutant hybrid of the past version's Seeds and normal RPG character-abilities) but I'm not getting into that just now.

    The instant we failed to resolve the issues, Eric pointed out that it was still possible to resolve them ... we could propose a conflict and one side or another could totally bag on it ... spending just a token one die or something. Now it wouldn't resolve that time, but that would put almost all of the dice in the game into one person's hands, rather than dividing them between two, and that would give them the potential to resolve. Long-term I want to look at the end-game strategies implied there, and figure out what I want, but in this game we decided that neither of us was interested in that kind of hail-mary pass.

    And I have mechanics answers.

    People do own issues. We auctioned them, one by one. Winner gives the dice they bid to the second-place loser (in this case, easy, since there's just me and Eric). The issue is then theirs, forevermore. So we started the game already intensely loaded with each other's dice (more so, in fact, than with our own).

    Right now there's no way for the system to do more than two-sided conflict. I'm thinking about it, but it's a bit of a stumper (as Andrew Morris has occasionally had reason to discover in Unistat).

    All the rest of your bullet points are correct.

    When to resolve: When nobody wants to put more dice on their cards.

    Who narrates: The GM ... what do you think this is, some hippy game? :-)

    PCs: Issue cards are not, on the card, tied into individual characters, but the process of dumping them into the game includes making those ties explicit. So "You care more about someone else" became implicitly "Yuki cares more about someone else than he does about Shima." That having been said, I anticipate that in three+ player games, one player will "go out" first ... removing all their conflicts (and probably their dice at the same time) from the game. Their characters part in the story is pretty much finished at that point, and the player becomes (effectively) appreciative audience.

    Scene counts: Nine scenes, of which four were solo scenes, four were one-conflict scenes, and the ninth was the scene where we hit the final two issues in two separate conflicts.

    Dice: Players started with twelve dice. We were close to going out, but didn't quite manage it. There were three phases of rhythm: In the early game we had way more dice than we could possible place, and so we placed with wild abandon. When everything was placed, we went to conflicts. In the mid-game, we had enough dice to fill up some of our conflicts, but not all, and things got a bit more focussed. In the end game, so many conflicts had been resolved that we were back to filling up a given conflict and jumping into it as quickly and effectively as we could.

    Hope that helps!

    So here's my question to solve for the next playtest: Motifs, as they're currently worded, are "You do X once, then you do X again, and you benefit. Lather, rinse, repeat."

    But things are, in fact, reincorporated most often in threes, not in twos. The T-Rex menaces the protagonists in Jurassic Park, then it appears again as part of the living nature diorama (eating some herd dinosaurs), and then it is not surprising when the T-Rex inexplicably shows up to save the protagonists from the attacking velociraptors. If it were, say, a pack of deinonychuses then you'd cry out "Oh LAME! Deus Ex Machina!" But you've seen the T-Rext that all-important two times before, so you buy it without question.
