\n An interesting little anecdote:

I was on a long car ride with a colleague, and we ended up talking about all kinds of things. He mentioned that he liked to play board games with some friends. I mentioned that I dabbled in game design, but that I had more quirky tastes, and was particularly interested in games which created stories, narrative, fiction.

He asked me a few questions, and we went on to talk about something else.

Another friend on mine had a brief discussion with me about randomness, and I tried to explain that randomness can be combined with human choice in various ways. As an example, I sent her a link to a Fiasco playset I'd written. She asked how it worked, and I pointed her to the Fiasco Tabletop episode on YouTube.

Yesterday, I find out that these two people know each other! They apparently sat down and watched the Fiasco Tabletop episode, were totally thrilled, and immediately went out and bought Fiasco.

So I guess I'll have to facilitate a game for them now! Interesting. I'll be very curious to see how much they learn and/or infer from watching that video, and how much they will wonder about or ask questions about. It will be interesting to see!

    \n Definitely. My own middle school roleplaying experience was entirely based on having been given two expansion books, one for Earthdawn and one for Shadowrun. We knew nothing about creating characters or gaining experience, so we played with an improvised system with no numbers. If we weren't continuing from the previous session, lunch hour would start with "Who has a scenario?". Different GMs handled combat differently and PVP easily devolved into juvenile arguments. After a couple decent scenarios other people started to get interested but summer vacation dissolved the whole thing. One player did approach me two years later in high school and ask if I had a scenario... most of his friends at the time were wrestlers, but it's certainly possible he's started playing "gamemaster scenarios" on his own.

    \n Nice!

    When we started playing D&D, we had a GM rotation, session by session, and it took us a long time to figure out we could have some kind of continuity from session to session (I think one of the players read about "campaigns" in a Dragon magazine or something like that). Oddly, that didn't stop us from leveling up characters and accumulating loot. A major part of play was trying to give out goodies to the PCs when YOU were the DM, so you could have those goodies when you were playing your character again.

    Unfortunately, we all tried to outdo each other in this respect, and pretty soon we had to "reboot" the whole thing and pledge to keep it more reasonable.
