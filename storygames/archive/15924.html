    VEGAS*RUN

    Sure, you could use Fiasco to play a game like *The Hangover*, or you could play Psi*Run, taking away your psychic abilities and adding Blowback From The Crazy The Night Before. Always roll Crazy:

    CRAZY
    5-6: Crazy causes no trouble.
    3-4: Crazy causes some trouble: married a stripper, aftershocks from drug use, stole a cop car. Probably won\u2019t get you killed, but likely illegal or very awkward.
    1-2: Crazy causes a lot of trouble: tiger in the bathroom, OD\u2019d prostitute, abandoned baby. Loss of life a possibility.

    And ... let's make it GM-less. Wherever it says "GM gets first say" change that to "Player on Your Left Gets First Say"

    So. You wake up in Vegas. You don't remember last night. You have questions, like, "Why is my tooth missing" or "Who left this baby here?" or "Where's the fiancee?"
    You have no powers.
    And someone... the mob, or the cops, or you're not sure, is after you.

    Take turns framing.

    RUN.

    \n Apocalypse World hack:

    The setting is highly fractured. There's a very highly policed city, and an abrupt change when you move to the wilderness. Play as normally, with the following rule: You may not go aggro or seize by force in the city (if you do, you automatically get your intent, but you are arrested by overwhelming force within seconds). In the wilderness, you may not seduce or manipulate or read a person unless you already know them from the city (they aren't going to stop to talk, otherwise). Make sure the PCs have needs that can only be filled by interacting with both of the spheres.

    \n Fiasco Bolt-On: The CLIMAX

    After the regular 4*number of players scenes, after the players roll dice to find out their disposition, frame a big mongo scene. The player who gets to frame is the one with the highest roll ignoring color, though they may choose to pass if they like. Come up with a reason why every player's character is in or will arrive into this last scene; and make the stakes high in this last scene. This is at least one and hopefully all of the PC's last-stand-opportunities to fulfill their needs / deal with their objects / cope with the tilt.

    How the scene comes out for the players depends on what they rolled - if they rolled low this might be the scene where they get killed or lose everything; if they rolled high this might be the scene where they 'win'.

    Then do aftermath as usual.

    Couple caveats:
    - Don't grind play to a halt looking for the perfect climax.
    - DON'T BUILD UP TO A CLIMAX - Don't save cool stuff for later because "that should be in the climax" - if you've got cool ideas, go there, and then try to do something even bigger in the climax, or have the repercussions from that in the climax, when you get there.


    [Ok, 5 minutes is up. Coen brothers movies often don't have climaxes (no shoot out at the end of Fargo - just Frances McDormand finding what's his name feeding Steve Buscemi into the woodchipper) so it makes sense that Fiasco doesn't. But I was watching Crazy Stupid Love last night - which is a soft Fiasco (and not a very good movie) - but it had a big old almost Shakespearean comedy-ish climax and I thought, "Fiasco doesn't do that." So, with this, it can. Maybe. If it works.]

    \n DRYH hack for playing in the Books of Magic setting. PCs are the players themselves when they had 12. Each player describes their character with three adjectives (in fact make that for every character created) and they start with no powers at all.
    Now describe your parents in 3 words and wrote those down on pieces of paper. Exchange parents with the other players at random. Check out what you've got and write another word to describe the kind of relationship your character has with their "new" parents.

    Older player starts framing scenes. The first describes the neighbourhood (use the one you grew in) and their routine day when they were 12, as they wake up. Remeber to add the "new" parents into the scene, enlist the help of the person who described them in the first place. Decide if your character feels one of these three: anger, love or fear. Describe how does your character reacts when you feel like that. That's the first power you develope: Example: you feel like you want to dissapear when you are afraid, your first power is that you can vanish in thin air.

    Frame scenes later at school and hanging with friends, in both develop a new power, up to three different abilities per person. Of course that powers belong to somebody else. And they are coming for it...

    \n EMPIRE OF THE FLOWERY THRONE,
    A hack for my own game Into the Dungeon in 30 Seconds.

    Don't roll Morale. Instead,

    Choose a caste. Scum, slave, refugee (Status 8), Streetsweeper, thief, immigrant (Status 10), Merchant, slave trader, courtesan (Status 12), Noble, priest, knight (Status 14). If someone tries to coerce you into doing something, refuses to accept something on credit, frightens you, etc., they try to roll over your Status. If you try to slum, steal or sneak, you try to roll over your Status.

    The armour options are: None (AC 8), Leather (AC 10), Hide (AC 12), Brass Half-Plate (AC 14)

    Some monsters can only be harmed by their own natural weapons and the natural weapons of their predators. If you kill a monster, you can collect its teeth and claws and hire someone to make them into a sword.

    You can play a priest. You cannot wear armour. You can perform rituals as a stunt; you list all the things that could go right and for each one the GM chooses (a) a sacrifice or (b) something that could go wrong.

    You can play a sorcerer.

    (Whoops, ran out of time. The inspiration should be pretty clear; I've only read snippets of Empire of the Petal Throne).

    Here's my hack:

    3:16 Spiritual Carnage

    You play monks, in our world of today.

    Weapons in the original game are converted to prayers or biblical-verses, and aliens are demons/devils.

    As a GM it is your job to help the chapters be influenced by evil, as they get more and more into the fight against it. Let them be influenced by their superiors: the higher in the hierarchy an officer is, the more tainted he should be. But all under the guise of being true believers. Examples of things to say:
    - When evil kill and maim, we must turn brutal to help good!
    - No servant of evil shall live! Kill them all!
    - If we kill one good soul to battle evil, the devil is to blame!
    - If evil lie and lure to corrupt souls, we shall purge them with faiths fervor, and holy fire!

    The church is in fact run by a pope who is a servant of evil. His "holy" goal is to give over Gods church and all of humankind to Satan. And God himself will not stop him. The players will have to do that, if they can, and if they are able to keep their faith in the struggle against evil.

    Have fun!

    \n Strip D&D
    (A hack of Strip Poker and D&D)

    Take the number of hit points that you have. Divide them between all the items of clothing you are wearing. Paired items (socks, gloves, etc) count as a single item. You cannot allot more than 20% of your hit points to a single article of clothing.

    Take masking tape and a sharpie. For each article of clothing, write how many hit points are tied to it.

    PVP fight!

    When you lose hit points, the person who struck your character determines which article(s) of clothing you lose. If you lose 30 hit points, and your socks are worth 10 and your shirt is worth 25, then the attacker can choose which article you lose.

    To ensure that everyone sheds some cotton, you can introduce the following rule: If someone wasn't attacked last round, there's a +1 bonus to hit them (cumulative if a person goes multiple rounds without being attacked).

    \nAbstinence Only

    Using Dread for satire. Your PCs are all college students in a Bible study group. You spent your lives hidden away in the safety of your small town homes. Possibly you were home schooled to avoid the evils of science education. You all desperately, seriously wish to remain sin-free until graduation. But your college life is full of temptations: sex before marriage, drugs, alcohol, cigarettes, swearing, homosexuality, all kinds of things are rampant in the dorms.

    Starting questionnaires start the player off defining their sins and temptations. The GM sets scenes and faces you with temptations. When faced with temptations, the player must make pulls from the Jenga tower as normal. If the tower collapses, then the PC has succumbed to temptation and fallen from grace. Maybe you have rules to try to repent and/or save the souls of your fallen classmates (but I'm out of time). And the PCs who have fallen from grace can keep playing, trying to tempt the other PCs into joining their life of decadence.

    Will you be the last true Christian from your high school graduating class?

    \nResistance is Futile

    Using Dread for satire. Your PCs are all college students at a liberal arts school. You spent your lives in the safety of suburbia and your parent's internet/basement. You were public schooled to avoid the evils of morality, education, and responsibility. You all desperately, seriously wish to remain slackers until (and after) graduation. But your college life is full of hazards: disease, substance abuse, activists, political correctness nazis, crushing debt, all kinds of things are rampant in the dorms.

    Starting questionnaires start the player off defining their time wasting skills/hobbies and anemic sense of responsibility. The GM sets scenes and faces you with responsibilities and moral dilemma. When faced with responsibility, the player must make pulls from the Jenga tower as normal. If the tower collapses, then the PC has succumbed to a sense of duty or purpose and fallen from slackerdom. Maybe you have rules to try to repent and/or save the souls of your fallen classmates. And the PCs who have gotten a job can keep playing, trying to tempt the other PCs into joining their life of responsibility.

    Dude, will you be the last true slacker from your high school graduating class?
