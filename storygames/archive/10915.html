    \n Yeah, you can gain Flow for winning a challenge in a group. There's a Flow cost. Each person in the challenge must pay it. The total Flow paid is then used at the end to compute efficiency rebates, so 25% not of the Flow cost, but of the total Flow paid. Then everyone gets that much Flow back. So if you've got 4 users paying 50 Flow apiece, that's 200 Flow, and 75% of it is 150, and they can each end up with 100 more Flow than they started with. If they can beat the Aggregate with a margin of at least 4.

    Flow isn't a material resources, it has no conservation laws, it's just what the Aggregate assigns. And the Aggregate deems working in a group == really good for Flow.

    I am a bit confused about "1 for the win, 3 for effect, 3 for efficiency". Isn't it "6 margin of success: 1 which must be put into effect, 2 more for a total of 3 effect, and 3 for efficiency" instead?

      Are there any guidelines for appropriate Flow investments for targeted challenges?

      We had a horrendously recursive Social Engineering challenge in our first session, where a PC targeted an NPC. I had no real idea how to pick an appropriate Flow investment for the challenge. I ended up saying 8 Flow, because it was about two thirds of what the PCs had.
      \nI'd have to double check to find guidelines, but I go with my gut. Does the person/thing the character is going against have a lot invested in this Challenge? Do they really not want, or want, something to happen? Those kinds of things tend to get more flow than if the target doesn't care that much. Of course, if the target doesn't care at all, it's not a challenge.

      It's become very interesting to me how there are two very different things going on in a Challenge. Setting the Flow Investment is about asking the User, "Is it worth this much to you?". Setting the Difficulty is about asking the User, "Can you do this? Will you get what you want?" My thinking on this hasn't developed very far, but there's a lot going on there.

      \n Last session tonight! It certainly went out with a bang. One session of Character/MRCZ creation, six of play. A brief summary to give context to my question (I also posted this in bite-sized AP June 2012):

      NOTE: The following story contains violence toward a pregnant woman.

      The MRCZ, Re/!/Mem, is all about "finding the perfect experience". They've danced around this goal for six sessions. Through an artistic performance they've made 6.3 Jen (a sometime girlfriend of one of PCs) want to get pregnant, on her own, and experience the bonds of motherhood. Forty days later, they bring her into Re/!/Mem HQ to ask if they can take her gestating baby's first memory. She's hesitant. They Socially Engineer her. "This will be a breakthrough in science. Knowing the first memory of a human infant will be an historic moment." She gradually sheds her hesitation and agrees to it.

      The baby's memory is "the shocking bliss of being brought into existence and being one with another being." A couple of the PCs pass the memory around, experiencing it. Jen leaves. After quick discussion, Re/!/Mem decides to death Jen, as she could be a threat to them using this memory for mercenary purposes (have people line up, sign a contract, experience the memory, and give it back*). It's a Perfect Death, 6.3 Jen being garroted, gassed, and bashed in front of Re/!/Mem HQ. When her body is re-printed, there's no baby.

      The session spiraled from there, with the two responsible for the deathing being frownied into oblivion after Jen's MRCZ--the Pie Shoppe--Thin Sliced the video feed of Jen's death all over the station. One tanked PC Switched at the last minute to avoid being booted off the station, one gave up in disgust and voluntarily left. The other two PC members of Re/!/Mem were left to pick up the pieces, one of them still holding the memory.

      So, I feel very conflicted about the consequences of deathing Jen.

      (1) On the one hand, the users were very aware of how deathing fit into life on the station. They knew it was hazardous, and that doing things that made people unhappy had the potential for mass-NPC-frownie (one user in a previous session had ridden along with a Breaking MRCZ, watched them get caught, tank, and booted off the station). I felt like the users were being anti-social according to station standards. They had this friend who had given them something very unique and beautiful. They wanted to figure out how to use that to get more Flow. Ok, a bit mercenary maybe, but that's fine; there are mercenaries on the station. But then they went and killed 6.3 Jen in cold blood because she might be a threat.

      As we were clear this was going to be the last session, I felt I had a bit of a freer hand and realized what the consequences would be: this horrible act, that didn't fit the social mores of station life (it wasn't a contracted death or a wetworker just doing his job), would be Thin Sliced by Jen's MRCZ (who knew she was heading to Re/!/Mem HQ when they last saw her), people on the station wouldn't find anything redeeming in it, and the users responsible would be mass-Frownied and tank.

      (2) On the other hand: Pretty harsh move. One user responded by Switching desperately and barely managing to save his ass. It was a tense scene; very exciting. The other user had felt the session had broken down after Jen's memory was taken (he felt that should have been the end of the session and game, but communication wasn't clear between us all on that issue), and somewhat in frustration he had his PC just give up and leave the station.

      (3) So: was I, as a Superuser, within my bounds on that call? It felt kind of like GM fiat, but it also felt like I was following the rules of the game and the rules of the setting and previously established fiction** in deciding that. What do you think?

      **for full context, there was a time previous when a user had been deathed after stealing someone's memory, but he and the Wetworker who killed him became fast friends afterward, so perhaps that may have colored the group's view of Wetwork.

      \n Interesting: I was reading back through some FM threads, including this one, and upthread I asked this question: Can the SU "arbitrarily" (based on the fiction) distribute Flow? And Luke said No. Which made, and makes, sense to me.

      And then I basically asked, in the post right above this one, "Can the SU 'arbitrarily' (based on the fiction) distribute Frownies?" No one answered, but Luke gave me a cryptic whisper, and, thinking about it more, I feel like the SU is totally within-bounds to distribute a bunch of Frownies for pre-established anti-social behavior.

      There's no mechanism, fictionally or in the real world, for characters or users to give each other huge Flow boosts (3 Flow from Attaboys is the limit--in our game we forgot that users can only receive one Attaboy per session), though there is a mechanism, fictionally and in the real world, for the Superuser to give users huge Flow drops. You can receive one Attaboy per session, but you can get Frownied approximately 80,000 times.

      You have to work to get the Flow you need, and it's easy as shit to lose it--depending entirely, of course, on how you play. By all accounts, the Users in our game rocketed up in Flow without too much difficulty: most of them were in triple-digits by the third session of play. And then, in the sixth session, issues that had kind of been festering in the fiction for a while exploded, and two characters fucking tanked--because of a truly soulless decision they made.

      Are you a member of the station? Does it change you? Do you try to change it? How? Do you rebel and break shit? Have fun on Mars, sucker. Do you tilt your group in the direction you want to go? Do you win friends and influence people?

      I don't know if I'm effectively communicating this, but I feel like I'm understanding something about this game (that I thought I understood) on another level. It's not coming out the way I want it to. Freemarket will not leave my brain.

      I'm running it this weekend for a round of fresh-faced newbies. I can't wait.
