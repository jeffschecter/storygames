    \n To triangulate radio waves, you need directional antennae. The Yagi antenna is the first one, and there wasn't an English translation of the plans for one until 1928. You can't just do it with your console set.

    Let me put it another way, because technically you can just use a loop antenna if you know how to do it. A broadcast signal is like a big spherical dollop. It's strong at the middle and weaker near the edges. There are also lots of places inside the sphere where the signal is weaker or stronger than you would expect from a perfectly spherical distribution because of terrain and atmospheric conditions and so on. So just listening to signal strength and when things start breaking up is untenable. (Not to mention that in the 20s, you're also talking about driving a car through areas where there just are no roads, a very bad idea.) A really good directional antenna will give you a direction from where you are, a bad directional antenna like a loop antenna (or even a 1920s Yagi antenna) will give you a range of directions: the signal is coming from (say, 10-20 degrees wide). The further away from the source, the smaller the range, because the difference in signal from pointing at the source or away from the source is more pronounced. But it also means that even 5 degrees of range in the direction determination encompasses a huge amount of area. Triangulation reduces the area, but it's still far larger than you would want to search on foot.

    No, triangulation really didn't come into its own until they put up non-directional broadcast towers that operated on known frequencies with known intensity so that you could reduce the variables involves greatly.

    Hope this helps? Is it a 1920s game? People often don't quite get how impressively different technology was then.

    \n *****************************
    TL;DR: JDCorley is right.
    *****************************

    I have actually done the whole "triangulation with loop antennae" thing as a senior physics project, except I was triangulating the electromagnetic waves from lightning strikes rather than radio transmitters. The technique is exactly the same.

    I built a series of carefully-made multi-loop antennae, along with circuitry to filter out noise and bands that I didn't want. My partner wrote computer programs to monitor the filtered signal, recognize the sharp spike of a lightning strike, and log when it occurred and record the signal.

    We installed these stations in several locations around the city and waited for a storm. It actually worked well, until somebody's cat knocked out a station by making a bed of it.

    Each carefully-made, computer-assisted station could localize the direction of the source to within a few degrees. Several stations taken in combination could localize lightning strikes that happened within a radius of a few hundred miles to an area of a few square miles. The lightning signals were then analyzed to try to understand strike patterns, what sort of strikes happened in what sort of areas, and so on.

    You could do the exact same thing with a radio station. You would just change your filters to pick a different band - the radio station's. Then you can locate that radio station's source to within a few square miles.

    Then you go back to 1920 and things get very hard.

    First of all, your ability to coordinate between different stations relies on very accurate time keeping. We had computers with internet connections that could stay within microseconds of the national time standard. The 1920's\u2026 didn't. Why is this important? Because knowing direction plus relative distance is super-important in improving your triangulation.

    Light travels at a set speed, so if station A recorded a signal slightly before station B saw it, then station A must be slightly closer. But light travels at about 30 million meters per second, so you need a millionth of a second in accuracy to get 30 meter resolution. If you've only got a hundredth of a second accuracy, then your resolution drops to 300 kilometers. Which is must less useful.

    Second, your ability to accurately record the signals that the loop antennae receive is important. The reason why is because your antennae are not perfect. What you actually do to correct for this is put multiple antennae at the same station, set at angles to each other. Let's say you have two set at right angles. The two loop antennae should receive identical signals at different strengths, based on which antennae is better aligned with the source of the signal and which is worse aligned. Of course, these antennae are actually going to report slightly different signals, since the antennae are not actually perfect copies of each other. You can thus use one to check the other, and vice versa, and this ultimately improves the performance of the antennae greatly.

    Again, we do all this with a computer, process the signals, analyze the differences, and so on. 1920's tech means analyzing the signals is much more tedious, but also that you have much poorer ability to record the signals in the first place. That's bad.

    Another (much more minor) problem is metallurgy. You could get uniform, high-quality wire in 1920, but it wasn't cheap. The cheap, uniform, high-quality wire we take for granted today is going to cost a lot more then.

    Finally, you're ability to even figure out how strong the signal is goes down dramatically in 1920. Like, are they just going to listen to the radio as they move around an antenna and say "louder\u2026 louder\u2026 quieter\u2026 louder\u2026 stop!"? Because that's going to be pretty bad.

    Summary: Mr. Corley is correct. Say they're in London. They drive to Bristol, pulling out their loop antenna and scanning for directions on the way. They're not going to localize the signal to "a certain block in Edinburgh." They're not going to localize the signal to "the city of Edinburgh."       \n The easiest way to prevent your players from getting a fix on the transmitter too soon is to allow the location of the transmitter to change (e.g. a home-built transmitter in a light commercial truck). Then the characters can zero in on a location; but, they\u2019ll always be a few steps behind the villain. When it\u2019s time to get the bad guy, stop moving the location. A 2 m unity gain antenna and a few Watts of transmit power can cover miles of ground \u2013 more if it is line of sight (LOS). As Jason pointed out, low, medium and high frequency (LF, MF, HF) can reach beyond line of sight (BLOS) due to skywave effects (see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skywave).
      \n
      \n
      \n****** STOP READING HERE ******
      \n
      \n
      \nIf you want a fixed transmitter location and a more technical solution, start with this short article and then read the rest of this post: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multipath_propagation
      \n
      \nIn urban, dense foliage, marine, or rugged terrain environments, multi-path can reasonably justify supplying misinformation to your players even when they "succeed" in detecting and measuring an azimuth to the transmitter.
      \n
      \nExample, take a look at the radar multi-path diagram in the wikipedia article. Now, for the sake of argument, block the direct path between the radar and the target. Now, only the two indirect paths remain. A character making measurements would correctly detect both of the reflected/refracted signal paths but not the LOS path. A successful measurement roll would determine (let's say) the ground-bounce signal path as the strongest signal. The character would deduce the wrong azimuth to the source even though they successfully measured it. This is how you deceive characters (players) that are not knowledgeable in physics or RF communications without actually lying to them.
      \n
      \nThis applies to surface multi-path in the same way. Just imagine buildings, water, hills, and ridges as reflective surfaces in the same way that the ground and the upper atmosphere are in the diagram. Realize that there are many, many paths that the signal will take from the transmitter to the receiver in this environment. However, most of those signals will be orders of magnitude less powerful than maybe a couple of multi-path signals and can easily be rejected as false detections. When a LOS path to the transmitter exists, it will be the most powerful signal \u2013 so give a character who takes careful measurements a reasonably correct azimuth.
      \n
      \nA note about wire loop antennas \u2013 they have a directional ambiguity. With a single loop, you can\u2019t tell if you\u2019ve identified the azimuth to the transmitter or the azimuth 180\xb0 opposite. You can resolve that ambiguity by taking measurements in multiple locations (e.g. with multiple detectors). In a low multi-path environment, measurements from two observers will intersect at a point near the transmitter (remember that there will be error in the measurement \u2013 consider +/- 10\xb0 for a low-quality antenna and a best case accuracy of +/- 3\xb0 for a good antenna); measurements from three or more observers will intersect in a region that should reasonably bound the location of the transmitter. In a high multi-path environment, measurements from two observers will intersect at a point (except in the case where both observers measure the same azimuth); but, this point will only be near the location of the transmitter by dumb luck. Measurements from three or more observers will intersect in nonsensical locations and may or may not bound the actual location of the transmitter.
