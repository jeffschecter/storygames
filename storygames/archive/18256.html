\n So, @komradebob suggested that the posts in a thread about Microscope might warrant their own conversation http://www.story-games.com/forums/discussion/18248/introducing-microscope-to-first-time-gamers#Item_4

The gist is that I played Microscope with some people who have never roleplayed and who would have resisted playing a "roleplaying game" if I had used that term. Based on that experience and another gaming experience I had with "non-gamers" I have noticed that there are certain "soft skills" that gamers take for granted, but that seem to create a barrier to entry for non-gamers. These soft skills are either daunting or boring or otherwise difficult sell to non-gamers. And, I'm not sure why. The "soft skills" include (but probably aren't limited to) the following:

-- maintaining the continuity in a story (i.e. remembering and respecting what happens scene to scene and even moment to moment within a scene)

-- playing a character in a story (i.e. considering what a character might actually do in a story including consequences of character action)

-- respecting rules (i.e. treating any rules as if they are important and not just pointless busy work)

These things seem obvious to my gamer friends, but are a real hurdle to my non-gamer friends. My non-gamer friends are bright, thoughtful, creative people who are certainly capable of playing a story game and would enjoy them I think (based on the Microscope experience). What's going on here do you think? Is there a way to overcome the barriers? Are gamers just hardwired differently?

    \n May it be that just like some people are shy at getting involved with a situation of real life they are not familiar with, some people are shy about prying in a wrong way into a fiction they are not familiar with? Complex rules (say, 3.5 level like) also seem to scare possible players away, while games that use skills they are already good at (like, uh, child-like pretending or common everyday storytelling skills) seem to be more approachable.

    There's also the emotional component. I wouldn't be as comfortable approaching a table of serious mathematicians at work, nor a table of professional actors and poets (except for just watching), as I would be at a table of normal people who seem to be excited at something that actually resembles a game. Even the art and other emotional components of the game draw people who could be repelled by the rules complexity or any degree of distrust towards the strangers doing strange things on the table.

    But you have to admit that even gamers are hardwired differently among themselves. I can read an RPG book and become interested and even excited about playing it just by imagining the possibilities. My best friend can't imagine these possibilities, he needs to play the game so it doesn't matter what I tell to get him interested. Another friend of mine doesn't care as long as we get to play something. The rest seem to be bent on trying to breake the game and have their own fun, but react surprisingly well to general consensus abut what may be more fun in every scene.

    My take about breaking these barriers has been to introduce people to the hobbie first with either games with high GM fiat -with an experienced GM of course- or storytelling games without continuity boundaries, where everything they say can go. People eventually learn to put their own limits about what is fun and what isn't regarding the fiction and the group - remember the social contract isn't totally developed as any game starts, but keeps rectifying itself as sessions progress and players get to know each other's reactions and ways to have fun. But giving newcomers total freedom to enact their imagination helps them develope their imagination and reduce stress about talking weird things in public, until they no longer feel weird about it.

    \n I've introduced a lot of total gaming novices to RPGs of various kinds (primarily D&D 3.5, Pathfinder, Fiasco, DW) and I don't think the barrier has anything to do with inherent skills capacities or whatever, it's entirely a question of experience. I make people comfortable by framing play as a learning process in which it's perfectly fine to make mistakes and introducing concepts one at a time, as they come up, rather that trying to impart my knowledge of the game in its entirety all at once.

    I find playing with novices who haven't internalized the conventions of RPGs (such as the "soft skills" described above) can be very revealing and exciting, especially in more collaborative indie-type games.

    This probably isn't super helpful, but I guess what I'm saying is treat it like any other learning situation, and let people find their way by acting as a guide, not a master.

    \n I'd say that many of the things you are talking about are skills that will be developed by the player over time, and that it's perfectly natural that you would see a lack of proficiency in a learner.

    I'll take a shot at a few of your specific points:\n
    -- maintaining the continuity in a story (i.e. remembering and respecting what happens scene to scene and even moment to moment within a scene)
    \nThere are a lot of new things happening when you are playing a roleplaying game. These things take up a lot of mind space. How to interact with the players is one of those things. How to interact with the system is another. How to interact with the story is yet another.

    For an experienced player, the "how to interact with the players" part is usually pretty easy. Think, however, of how this part is a little bit harder when you're playing with strangers at a convention. Then multiply that difficulty several times. That's what it's like for a person who is new to the hobby. So you can reduce this a bit by surrounding the new player with people they are comfortable with. But this will still take extra mind space because they are playing a game that takes place within the social interaction space, so they are learning how to do that.

    For an experienced player, the "how to interact with the system" part is usually pretty easy, though it depends a bit on how familiar you are with the system and/or how similar the system is to other ones you are comfortable with. Think about how hard it is for some experienced gamers to play a system that is drastically different from what they are used to (a Pathfinder player, for example, playing Breaking The Ice). Again, multiply that by several times to get a sense of what it is like for someone new to the hobby to interact with a game system.

    Between those 2 parts, which are usually pretty easy for an experienced player, you have a lot of mind space taken up.

    There's not a lot left at that point to keep track of story continuity.

    They'll get better at is as the first 2 parts get easier.\n
    -- playing a character in a story (i.e. considering what a character might actually do in a story including consequences of character action)
    \nSo there's these things that other people play, called computer games. And for most people coming in to the RPG hobby these days, that's the experience they have that is most similar to tabletop RPG play. In fact, they even call some of those computer games RPGs, which makes the association even more natural.

    For most people, when they play a computer game, they play an avatar rather than a character. What I mean by the differentiation is this - an avatar is an extension of the player into the game world, while a character is it's own person with it's own goals. An avatar is a set of tools, while a character is a set of motivations.

    So when those people come to the hobby, they tend to play using their "character" as an avatar, rather than as a character. That's totally natural.

    The thing is, many experienced players do the same thing. I know I personally do it a lot. I often play using my "character" as an extension of my own will, rather than as a being with it's own motivations. But I've been doing this shit for 30 years, so I've gotten better at being subtle about it. When I show up to a one shot convention game, I mold the character I'm playing to fit my current mood, so that I can use that character to be my avatar in the game.

    The new players you're playing with will also learn. The more often they play with people who subtly disapprove of obvious avatar play, the more they will either learn to be subtle about it or to embrace character driven play. Or maybe some of both.\n
    -- respecting rules (i.e. treating any rules as if they are important and not just pointless busy work)
    \nAgain, much of this is computer game training. In many computer games, it's fun to try to break the rules, or at least to try to tweak them.

    Many games reward you for doing bizzaro wacky stuff that your character would never want to do, like giving you an achievement for throwing yourself off of a particularly large cliff in spectacular fashion. So it's not surprising that players want to explore wackiness sometimes rather than staying tight to the rules.

    And hell, treating game rules as pointless busy work has a long history in the RPG hobby as well. It's a pretty rare group that actually uses the encumbrance rules in old D&D (though there is a recent revival in embracing that hardcore sim rules and finding the fun in them, which is cool).

    Again, I've been playing RPGs for 30 years. And I have an analyst's mind. I've been hacking systems for forever - I was playing a friend's homebrew game in 4th grade. It's only very recently that I've actually come across games that I've read and wanted to play straight out of the book.

    So it is not surprising to me at all that players would not see the importance in meticulously following the rules. It's only in certain games that the rules consistently drive the fun. And as new players, they have a hard time seeing how the rules are attempting to do that. Hell, they may never really see that. More than half of the people I play with can't analyze game rule system structures for shit.

    \n It's one thing to listen to a story. It's something else to imagine a dynamic fictional world generated by several people with different agendas -- none of whom are actively trying to create a narrative in any traditional sense. I don't think it's super obvious to completely new players who are accustomed to "making a move" or "taking a turn" in a board game that when they "make a move" in an RPG they are creating part of a cohesive fictional whole and that when their "turn" is over, their character is "still there" in the fiction.

    Gamers recognize that a character sheet is merely a shorthand description of a fictional construct that is separate from the player. I think that, for some new players, the notion that they are anything but themselves when they play the game can be an alien concept. It's a leap to understand that "making a character" is not the same thing as choosing the thimble when you play Monopoly. It's a leap to understand that the story in a game is not just a super complicated version of mad libs.

    People make the joke that Fantasy Football is basically just "D&D for sportsfans" or whatever -- but it's really not. I think it's easier for people to "get" fantasy football. It's a different mental exercise. It's not an act of actually generating mental images of fictional football games. Roleplaying games contain some aspects that can be confusing to somebody who has never done it.

    \n There are things about roleplaying games that are complicated or at least challenging for new people. But I think you're misidentifying them. Or identifying ones that are rarely a huge issue as being big huge issues.\n
    I think that, for some new players, the notion that they are anything but themselves when they play the game can be an alien concept. It's a leap to understand that "making a character" is not the same thing as choosing the thimble when you play Monopoly.
    \nRecent counter-example. I was playing Bang!, the card game, with some non-gamer friends recently. In that game, you have roles, and you have different goals based on what role you are. And nobody knows who is what role, except for the Sheriff. (It's similar to Werewolf/Mafia, but with more card game action added.)

    I was the Sheriff and my neighbor was the Deputy. The game says the Deputy's goal is to make the Sheriff win (by helping to kill the Outlaws and the Renagade). We were down to the end of the game and it was the Sheriff, the Deputy, and an Outlaw left. My neighbor, playing the Deputy, shot and killed the Sheriff. I asked her why she did that. She said "I thought I could become the Sheriff and take over." This wasn't just her misunderstanding the rules. She had started making roleplaying decisions based on her role in the card game - she was taking actions based on the kind of Deputy she wanted to portray.

    I see this rather often in board/card games that have strong character elements. Players who do not play roleplaying games will often make character-driven decisions rather than strategy-driven decisions. I think it is common for non-gamers to easily slide into making character-based decisions. I almost never see experienced roleplayers do this - it's easy for them to know that when playing a card game, you make your decisions based on victory strategy, not character goals.

    I think the difficulty that you are seeing with new players in roleplaying games has more to do with the fact that they are trying to juggle lots of new things in their head at the same time. The more complicated the other things are - a complicated combat system, a complicated mystery scenario, a complicated set of new people to interact with - the more they will struggle with things they would otherwise be comfortable with. As they gain competence with those other parts (or those complications are simplified by choosing simple systems and scenarios and playing with people they know), they'll have more brain space to spend on story and character stuff, and they'll handle it plenty well.

    -----

    A similar thing I learned about when learning to be an English teacher. When a student is writing using an unfamiliar format or about an unfamiliar subject matter, they will do worse with "basic skills" like grammar and spelling. When they gain more competence with the subject matter and/or the format, their ability to apply their basic skills will go back up again.

    I see the same thing as a professional technical writer. If I'm writing about stuff that I'm really struggling to understand, I make more spelling and grammar errors. And I'll use less optimal sentence structures and word choices. I need to do more editing of my work. But when I write about things I understand better, my writing itself is better.

    When you have competence at a complicated skill - and roleplaying is a rather complicated set of skills - it's hard to understand why people seem to struggle with the most basic parts. Often it's because they are focusing all their brainpower on the more complicated bits and have less to spare for the rest. Once they gain competence at the complicated bits, the more basic stuff will return.

    These soft skills are either daunting or boring or otherwise difficult sell to non-gamers. And, I'm not sure why. The "soft skills" include (but probably aren't limited to) the following:

    -- maintaining the continuity in a story (i.e. remembering and respecting what happens scene to scene and even moment to moment within a scene)

    -- playing a character in a story (i.e. considering what a character might actually do in a story including consequences of character action)

    -- respecting rules (i.e. treating any rules as if they are important and not just pointless busy work)
    I can guess why it's a tough sell! It's because it's not at all intuitive why these would be important. I doubt your noob friends lack the ability; they simply need to be shown why they should bother.

    Maybe the answer is that they shouldn't! Most of us Played Pretend as kids, and getting back into that as an adult can be a blast!

    So if you're going to ask them to bother remembering continuity, and thinking in-character, and using some friggin' rules, you'd better show them damn quickly how doing these three things will get them awesome fun times.

    My experience with this is that leading by example will suffice, but a dose of highlighting helps:

    -- "And he reveals his will! Remember the envelope he was hiding before? This is what it was!"

    -- "Oh man, my guy's about to get in so much trouble! He should get out of here and go home. But he's a curious guy, and he's compelled to investigate!"

    -- "Bad roll. Now I have to cross off a hit point, huh? Yikes. A few more of those will kill my guy; he may have to be more cautious from now on!"
