\n This is pretty Delve-inspired and really nothing new but I wanted to collect this stuff in one post that I can edit later and add things too. Feel free to contribute but I'll curate harshly what goes in the OP. \n
What do you do?
Always say this, all the time, a lot. It's is the "prompt" for the players. "You are standing in an open field west of a white house, with a boarded front door. There is a small mailbox here. What do you do?" Especially say this after some sort of block. "The stone slab doesn't budge. What do you do?" The one exception is when you've just said something to them as an NPC. "What'd ye fancy folk be wanting in an inn like this?" The second exception is when you said something that requires mechanical work for them, like giving them a DC.
\n
What are you doing, exactly?/How do you do that?
When players don't convey their execution clearly. "We search for traps" "What are you doing, exactly?"
\n
What are you saying, exactly?
When players shortcut over RP scenes that you want to play out. "We ask her for the map." "What are you saying, exactly?" Only do this when there's uncertainty. If there's no conflict, do still answer them in character as the NPC, but you don't have to dwell on what they said diegetically to initiate the conversation.
\n
That is how it is / That is how it is, now / Weird right? But that's what you see
When there's an object or event in the shared imagined space that's unusual or surprising and the players express doubt. "It's ridiculous that there would be a tiger here!" "Weird, right? But that's what you see. What do you do?"
\n
When you [part of multi-part action].../ As soon as you [part of multi-part action]...
When the players chain multiple executions and effects together in one statement, and you need to time rewind in order to settle the earliest uncertain one, if any. "We wrap the body in a carpet, go to the street, hail a cab to the harbor and dump the body in, looking at the black water as it sinks." "Would you make an encounter check please?" "It's a one" "When you're waiting for a cab, with the rolled up carpet, you notice a trio of greybearded men in short shoulder capes and black suits staring at you from the other side of the street. What do you do?" Don't do this if there's no uncertain executions. In that case, just go along the full chain. "OK, the body is in the water. What do you do?"
\n
Does that seem reasonable?
When you're not sure you've got buy-in from the table, or when you want to make sure you're making a good ruling. Use carefully; it can both increase or decrease buy-in depending on the situation. In my experience when you're making a more mechanical ruling, it's good to ask if it seems reasonable, but when you're describing diegetic elements, it's better to just stick to your guns.
\n
It's [time&circumstances]. What do you do?
A way to cut to a new (probably procedural) scene without speaking in camera terms like scenes and cutting.
\n
[Character], where are you, and who are you with?
A way to introduce a new (probably dramatic) scene without speaking in author stance / story game terms.
\n
Scratch off one... / Scratch one...
Scratch off one arrow, one ration etc.
\n
For how long?
When the players wait for something that you already know (through dice or timeline) when it's going to happen, if you just jump to "OK, three days later, it happens", it's as if they've always wait the exact right amount. What if the party would've given up after two days? By asking, you open up for "OK, we wait for three hours". You might know that the thing shows up after two hours, or four days, or whatever. BTW don't make the answer up after getting the time span from the players, that's cheating.
\n
How many times do you do it?
Similar to the time span question, but when they're doing something repeatedly, like "I smash the button over and over again".
\n
That's [time span], so that'll be [number] encounter checks. OK?
Also a way to request for a decision point to end, or fast-forward, a "scene" without camera terms. In finchian D&D a lot of things don't require skill rolls, but do cost time and are still tense.
\n
One hour passes. Make an encounter check. What do you do?
Alternatively just play it out [time unit] by [time unit].
While you're busy with...
If you're attacked and have already spend your main action for that combat round.

    \nWhat do you see that tells you that...?

    I just brainstorm what traits or events or etc. I want to have them perceive, and let them generate the imagery for the group. Seems to build a lot more atmosphere and more buy-in. e.g., if running a game where treachery has become an ongoing element thematically, I might just tag a city +treacherous. When they arrive, "What do you see that tells you this is a city where treachery thrives?" "What do you see that tells you strangers aren't welcome here?" "What do you see that tells you you it's safe to rest here for the night?".

    \n A few notes on how I use these:\n
    What do you do? Always say this, all the time, a lot. It's is the "prompt" for the players.
    Amen!
    \n
    What are you doing, exactly?/How do you do that? When players don't convey their execution clearly.
    I use "What are you doing exactly?" to imply risk and that this is a time for careful thought and that it's worth taking the time to get our communication right because there are serious stakes.

    I use an offhand, "How you goin' about that?" to express curiosity and my own un-serious desire to better visualize what's going on.

    In the case where there is danger but I don't want to telegraph that because the characters aren't aware of it, I try to start with the chill version. \n
    What are you trying to achieve? When the players seem to have a intent that's counterintuitive enough that you might suspect that you will misunderstand their execution or that they have misunderstood the circumstances.
    I think that situation -- significantly counter-intuitive, substantial risk of misunderstandings -- is the most important time to use that phrase.

    It's not the only time, though.

    I use similar phrases (often "Where you going with this?" or "What are you after here?") to gauge where the players' focus, interests and priorities are, to derive inspiration for my own content, to develop an eye for which sort of obstacles are necessary vs irrelevant, etc.

    Possibly my most frequent use of "Where you going with this?" is to figure out which character activities can be summarized and which should be played out.\n
    That is how it is/That is how it is, now When there's an object or event in the shared imagined space that's unusual or surprising and the players express doubt.
    I think my key phrase here is just to answer "Really?!" with "Yup!"

    Actually, that's only true when we're messing around. If the players express genuine confusion/skepticism, I have two answers:

    Weird, right? But that's what you see. When they are correct to be surprised, validate that!

    Actually, in this situation, that shouldn't be a shock. Followed by an explanation of why not. An opportunity to better cohere on the setting!\n
    That's canon now! To cement something very consequential that happened circumstantially.
    I actually dislike this for nitpicky immersion reasons which you can probably ignore, but for the record, my version is, "That's how it will always work," if referring to an extant setting status quo that's merely new to the play group, or, "That changes everything, going forward," if it's a new development in the setting.\n
    When you.../ As soon as you... When the players chain multiple executions and effects together in one statement, and you need to time rewind in order to settle the earliest uncertain one
    Great call to mention this. A very important GM task for sure! I'm not sure if this is the best way to label it, though. "When you.../ As soon as you..." could mean anything; I have to read ahead to get it.

    To me, the key GM action here is refusing to let the fiction advance past a certain point of relevant uncertainty, to which end I invoke:

    Wait! or Hang on! or Before you [subsequent action]...\n
    Does that seem reasonable? When you're not sure you've got buy-in from the table, or when you want to make sure you're making a good ruling. Use carefully; it can both increase or decrease buy-in depending on the situation.
    I'd specify when it can decrease buy-in. In my experience, that's only when the GM is asking the players to do the GM's job, rather than simply double-checking that they've done their job to the group's satisfaction. Do that latter. Don't do the former.

    Agreed? Or did you have something else in mind?\n
    It's [time&circumstances]. What do you do? A way to cut to a new scene without speaking in camera terms like scenes and cutting.
    I'd hesitate to include this without further clarification about when it should and shouldn't be used. I'm not sure if I ever actually use this outside of the player characters waiting for an event, and me saying, "Okay, now that event occurs!"
    \n
    You wanted to go see [person], right? What do you do? A way to introduce a new interaction scene without speaking in author stance / story game terms.
    I find this prompt broadly useful across all situations where the players had a goal or next step in mind:

    You wanted to [whatever they wanted to do next], right? What do you do?\n
    Do you...? / You're still..., right? Sometimes it's good to ask questions circumstances. "You're still wearing your armor, right?"
    For me this is a subset of What are you doing, exactly?/How do you do that? It's part of the conversation wherein I get the info I need from the players.

    I guess other times I might inquire just for the sake of making my own (or the group's) imagined picture more vivid... but that seems like a different endeavor than most of the stuff on this list?\n
    Scratch one... Scratch one arrow, one ration etc.
    I say "scratch off one". :P\n
    How long are you prepared to wait? By asking, you open up for "OK, we wait for three hours". You might know that the thing shows up after two hours, or four days, or whatever.
    Absolutely.

    I view time as an investment, and I handle other investments of resources similarly:

    How many times are you willing to get stabbed by the needle in the lock mechanism? How many magical questions do you want to use up interrogating this enchanted item? How long are you willing to grind away at this combination lock before you give up if you haven't opened it yet?

    So, uh, maybe... How much/many of [time or precious resources] are you investing in this? to cover all the bases?

    \n Sandra, seems there's a bit more of work you would need to do to improve my list (at least I would surely do it to improve my own list if I had the time) and that is to tell readers that they may phrase things in a different way (after all different groups and cultures have slightly different meanings and emotions attached to different terms) as long as they keep as close as they can to the intent of the technique.

    Also, that's something I didn't considered as well: these ritual phrases will often be accompanied by gestural signals imbued with subtle emotions, which shouldn't be purposely passive-aggressive nor remotely aggressive in any way. It's really important to explain that to the readers.

    I purposely put in the list techniques that are even conflicting themselves, as it was meant to be a list of different techniques used in different games by different groups to achieve different atmospheres and gameplay. I discarded a lot that sounded similar or that did a mix of things that two or more techniques already did, though that was more for the sake of clarity. For efficiency purposes, techniques that do two or more things should be welcomed. Adding different choices of phrasing to each technique should help readers a lot to understand how it's meant to be said and when.

    What are you doing, exactly?/How do you do that?When players don't convey their execution clearly. "We search for traps" "What are you doing, exactly?"
    \nMy favourite way to phrase this is, "What does that look like?" It helps encourage players to think visually about what their characters are doing. "In the movie/TV show version of this, we see..."

    \nI really like "what does that look like?" as the player prompt for more detail for a few reasons.

    Firstly, yes, it asks the player to visualise their character's actions, but it also gives the player permission to use this moment to present their character to the table. Manoeuvring the player to use visual language isn't necessarily aping TV, it's opening up a space where the player can be the story-teller for a bit and use some of the descriptive language typically reserved for the DM. These moments are important to give a player the spotlight in the group if they want it and encourages a descriptive agency over their character.

    Secondly, I think preferring "what does that look like?" over "what do you do, exactly?" enables players to provide those key clarifying details the DM needs but without backing the player into a place where they think they need a specific answer to advance. As a player this sudden demand for specific information can be slightly intimidating as it indicates that I'm risking some kind of failure state, and as a DM I've seen players shift their attitudes accordingly and mire play in micro-actions to try feel out the hinted threat.

    Asking what an activity looks like also helps players who might only know what their character's activity is in an abstract sense. As a player, asking me exactly what my character is doing to pick a lock is likely to get me thinking about a real mechanics of lock lockpicking - which I know nothing about! Hesitant guesses are likely to frustrate both me and the DM as we back-and-forth over an imaginary object with incomplete information!
    But getting the player to think about the "look" of lockpicking in a more abstract way will likely elicit workable information about how their character approaches the task without having to devote table-time to the real-world functioning of tumblers.

    Of course, some situations do need "what do you do, exactly?" and it can be loads of fun, but keeping the language in the visual for the most part gives players more chances to be the story-teller without triggering slow-and-cautious critical/technical thinking when it's not required/desired.

      So my initial impression when I meet this "What does it look like stuff" is that it's been a while since I experimented with that stuff but that it goes counter to the first-person-view experience I'm trying to foster.\n
      However, it's only rewarding if there is actually a failure state that the player risks through improper handling of the imagined activity and the imagined space is handled coherently by the DM. We can all see how fun it might be to have a character dismantle a booby-trap in a dungeon by describing their exact actions, but how painful that process could be if the DM and the player miscommunicate at any point.
      \nPainful in terms of hit points! >:D\n
      Frankly, I could do with a glossary of key phrases just to help me describe spaces and objects that players are interacting with precisely.
      \nGestures help a lot but yeah, it's difficult.\n
      ... roleplaying games aren't normal conversation and failure to be normal conversation isn't a criteria that roleplaying games can be judged on.
      \nIn Rickard's defense, some games might have more conversational qualities than others. But I'm with you when it comes to my particular game.
