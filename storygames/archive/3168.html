\n I'm reading Wild Mind: Living the Writer's Life, by Natalie Goldberg. And well on into the book she tells of a conversation with a friend, writer Jim White:\n
    "One day after we knew each other a while--Jim was ten years older than I, a veteran poet--he turned to me. 'Who gave you permission to be a poet? Was it Allen Ginsberg?' I had studied with Ginsberg the summer before. 'Someone along the way has to give you permission'

    "'No.' I shook my head. I was too shy to say, 'No, Jim, it was you."
\nAnd then later she breaks it down:\n
    "...there's someone further along on the path, who gives you the nod, who says yes, who adores literature as much as you and so gives you permission to love this odd thing all the way..."

    "When I say 'you ask permission,' I do not mean you have to go to someone higher up on the totem pole and inquire, Is it okay if I write?...As a matter of fact, never ask anyone...it is about relationship. You know another writer and this reinforces your own love and commitment....It's more like you stand shoulder to shoulder, looking out at the vista, and the older writer points and says, 'See,' and you nod and smile, knowing that the vista is good and sweet and you always want it in front of you."
\nSo what she's describing isn't so much about permission, per se, or mentorship, as it is being treated as a friend and an equal by someone who went there first, right? So, who gave you permission to design games?

For me, it's Ron, absolutely. (Via phone conversations. But I bet he's done it for others through the Sorcerer mini-supplement program. Which brings up another interesting question: do the design community's social institutions, like game design competitions, mini-supplement programs, open game licenses, Dreamation Game Design Roundtables, The Forge Booth, and The Ashcan Front give equivalent permission? Or not?)

Paul

    \n More than anything, it was my Rolemaster group in college. These guys were completists, having most every book for an insanely complicated system, but for all that, they had complete ownership of the game, cheerfully and aggressively retooling vast swaths of it to make it into the rukes that helped them play the game they wanted. Same group managed to start at Phoenix Command and end with something that looked like a John Woo movie, long before Feng Shui had crossed any of their radars. To call what they did drifting is a painful understatement, but the underlying lesson of the game as servant of the end goal meant that I promptly went forward and applied it to every subsequent game I played. To be completely frank, the line between corrupting a game into a homebrew and creating a new game is, so far as I'm concerned, mostly just a matter of degree.

    -Rob D.

    \n My father, at some dim childhood age, who sat down and played with me when I made 'pen games'.

    I'd draw a "cave" (a squiggly square), and each of us would draw three "bases" on the walls of the cave. You'd balance a pen tip-first on one of your bases, put one finger on the back end, and try to "shoot" a base belonging to the other player by drawing a line to it - and you drew by letting go with the rest of your fingers and pushing only with the finger on the butt of the pen. We took turns.

    I'm pretty sure I got the idea from somewhere else, but I made the caves, and we shot at each other's bases in those caves. Then really simple mazes, when you tried to "shoot" your way through, starting each shot from your last wall impact, and aiming for the lowest score.

    Those were my first "game designs".

    \n Cool question. For me, there's been several phases of my 'gaming life'. The first phase, the beginner, the two to give me permission or inspiration would be James Ward and Jeff Butler, of Gamma World fame and a comics and TSR artist, respectively. Although the game I was working on publishing back then was never really playtested or published, I enjoyed phone conversations with them about gaming hugely.

    The second phase, the 'punk', would have been Christopher Clark from Inner City games, and the two west coast punk rockers that published Die, Conservative, Die! and Brains - Hardcore Punks Against Dead Guys. The three games I designed during that period were tested and played extensively, but never published.

    The third phase, the 'Forge', was Ron Edwards, Tony Lee (author of Extreme Vengeance), and Jared Sorenson. Both of the games I designed and played were published online and even tho the games aren't online anymore, I got a lot of positive feedback from a lot of folks on the interweb.

    These days I'm in a personal phase. I fiddle with games to play them with my young nephew, or come up with a stripped down version of d20 to play with my old gaming group in Madison. I also kick around the idea of digging up my two Forge-era games and re-writing them so people have access to them - I get a couple e-mails or PM's a year for Dead Meat.

      \n > 'Who gave you permission to be a poet?'
      > 'So, who gave you permission to design games?'

      Sigh. I'm afraid this might come off pedantic and uncharitable and pointless nitpicking, but there's nothing for it. Just know I write this because I think it's important and has some value.

      The first question is an issue of identity. It's about who someone is.
      The second question is an issue of activity. It's about what someone does.

      I've written poetry, and I've designed games, but I don't think of myself as a poet nor a game designer. Maybe it's a permission issue, maybe not; it's not something I've really dwelt on, although perhaps it'd do me some good.

      All I know is that I've not yet been issued my artistic license.

      I will say I am a gamer, though. Who that comes from... I'm not sure. It feels like it isn't this ray-of-sunlight, moment-in-time threshold, though. It feels more like there's an intermittent dew of permission issuing forth from many people, to various degrees, across countless moments.
