Posted By: bunnyhilateProbably this also stretches to other types of content, but for some reason, I'm not concerned about the relative lack of good games that handle Mormonism or chivarlic tragedy.
\nWell, I think there's just a dearth of anime RPGs overall, much less good ones. I can't think of any other genre* where such a huge portion of the list of published games is mostly stuff that's out of print, which is especially incongruous given that there's such a huge fan community, and that community has enough of a minority of gamers (and potential gamers maybe?) that most American anime cons have a decently active tabletop gaming room.

On the other hand, about the only anime-themed game that got any real traction was BESM, which is very much a "System Doesn't Matter" game, that went from being free-form with a 2d6 roll-under rule to a wannabe GURPS over the course of three editions. It's hard to what kind of market forces were at work, considering that Guardians of Order started off with traditional RPG distribution, then was getting into major bookstores, then completely went down in flames. Given that BESM/Tri-Stat was by far the most successful "anime" RPG, we definitely aren't dealing with a demographic that puts a lot of import on the "game" aspect. However, that may be simply due to most of the combination anime fan/gamers out there being pretty mainstream in their tastes. The RPGs I most often see played at anime conventions are D&D and Exalted, and while you'll probably see more BESM and TFOS there, we're basically talking about mainstream gamers with otaku tendencies. Preferences, the influences of various subcultures, and just plain lack of exposure to other kinds of games are all potential factors, and the only data point I have is that a lot more people bought copies of Maid RPG because they heard about it from places like 4chan and Gaia Online than the conventional RPG sources, indie or otherwise.

The Japanese market is a little different partly because Japanese fans by and large don't think of "anime" as a distinct category the way Westerners do. In an interview someone asked me if Maid RPG's anime-style art was typical of Japanese RPGs, and my response was that pretty much everything remotely geeky in Japan is in what we would call "anime-style." Video games, light novels, anime, manga, TRPGs, etc. all have that general style, and there are parallels with live action stuff too. Japanese RPGs seem like they have a lineage that goes back to Sword World (Japan's answer to D&D), while for the color designers draw inspiration freely from whatever strikes their fancy. For example, Alshad ff lists among its inspirations: Nibelungenlied, Trinity Blood, Elemental Gelade, Negima!, Fullmetal Alchemist, D&D3.5, Arianrhod, Sword World, Final Fantasy VII: Advent Children, Star Wars, Gundam Seed, and Dragon Quest. Licensed games are also very rare there (and mostly done with bland universal systems apparently), and the "X with the serial numbers filed off" phenomenon seems largely unheard of too. That's probably in part the thing with Japanese trademark and copyright law, where fan publications can blatantly use characters for hardcore pornography if they want, but a copyrighted work will avoid showing the name "Sony" on screen by any means necessary.

Part of the lack of anime-influenced RPGs may simply be that we haven't yet seen the generations really influenced by this stuff come to their own just yet. It took a while for artists like Svetlana Chmakova and Bryan Lee O'Malley who have manga influence but are neither rare pioneers (like Adam Warren or Fred Perry) nor slavish imitators.

    \n I think that the need to clump everything together is a big part of what's been holding back "anime" games in the first place. When I ran a session of Maid RPG at Gen Con in 2007, I told the players afterwards that I hoped to bring it out in English, and one of them said that if it didn't, she'd have to use BESM for maid stuff instead. The idea that a system with the word "anime" on the cover is necessary just floors me. It seems obvious that if someone says, "I want to run an anime RPG campaign" or "I want to write an anime RPG," the first question you ask them would be, "What kind of anime, specifically?" There certainly isn't anything unique about anime that you could encode into RPG rules, doubly so if if we try to generalize into the whole medium. For off the shelf games, depending on the type of anime, you could easily arrive at Risus, Savage Worlds, or PTA as your system of choice.

    Considering that there have been maybe a dozen recognizably anime-inspired RPGs put out in the West, yet my copy of The Anime Encyclopedia is an 800+ page monster (with very small print), I feel pretty safe in saying that there's a lot of unexplored territory, and disproportionately so compared to fantasy or superheroes.

    \n The best games that I'd consider "anime games" have been some drafts by Ewen himself. BESM and OVA and such are all awful, and fail to capture anything I'd find remotely interesting. This might not mean that they're not anime, but I do like my share of Japanese culture, so at least those games fail to speak to the things I find interesting in Japanese animation. Panty Explosion is relatively passable in this regard, I guess.

    Also, it's completely ridiculous to try to make an "anime rpg" as some sort of general thing. The absolute best one could achieve would be something like Primetime Adventures (the Finnish version of which is quite explicit in recommending the game for anime-style series, too). Comparing the number of anime rpgs to the number of fantasy rpgs is likewise a bit wrong-headed, as all anime pretty much fits in some genre that is in turn covered by some rpgs. Pretty much the only genre that's underrepresented in rpgs but common in anime is urban fantasy without prewriting the game into some ultra-complex setting (read: Whitewolf). Something like half of all anime that gets any traction in the western hobby scene seems to be urban fantasy genre-wise (categorized as boys' or girls' adventure anime in Japan, incidentally), but there are relatively few games that do very well with the high-concept style of urban fantasy in those games. A game that worked well with a high-concept setting like "there are magical dolls and now you own one" or "one day you got transformed into a girl and now have to live with it" or "here you go, have a book that lets you kill people without leaving a trace" would be pretty close to the core experience of much anime, sort of. I'd recommend Mortal Coil, except it is aesthetically so very, very western that it'd be more trouble than it'd be worth to try to animize it.

    It's pretty obvious that an "anime rpg" needs to conform to the anime aesthetic in the details, too. I've played several successful sessions of the Mountain Witch with anime flavour, going as far as filling my demo set with anime art to market the game as "the choice for an anime samurai epic". There is nothing in the rules of the game that particularly prevents imagining the color as anime-style, after all. Something like Samurai Champloo (a popular, relatively recent anime) could easily be interpreted as a Mountain Witch campaign. Same for Samurai 7.

    \n Heh. Quite so. Here's more, let's see if there's some obvious connection:
    "There are death gods out there, fighting an invisible war to keep demons at bay. Now you're one of them, even though you're alive, and they hate you for it."
    "You know how to go into a magical land that has lots of different beasts in it. You befriend some and learn to take them to the real world, but now others are doing it, too, and they have more in mind than fun and games."
    "You were dumped into a magical spring, and now turn into a girl at inopportune moments. Your father turns into a panda."
    "You're a magical princess reincarnated into a schoolgirl. You fight crime."
    "You got an android in the mail. She loves you, maybe."

    Those are all from urban fantasy adventure anime of various stripes, picked randomly according to what popped to mind. As can be seen, they're mostly very high-concept and simple, and all feature the notion that the protagonist character is somehow special, often the only special thing in the world otherwise more or less like ours. From this viewpoint the most popular urban fantasy rpgs are almost the exact opposite with their elaborate mythologies.

    Continuing my thinking-out-loud, though, the thing I've liked about my best anime gaming experiences has been mutual appreciation of source material. That's what I liked about Ewen's inspired moongirl game (go back to that one, it was promising) - it didn't try to capture the whole field of Japanese media, it was just an in-depth look at one narrow feature, easy to appreciate for an audience proficient in the source material. I could imagine (and have been imagining) making a similarly narrowly focused game about this harsh fascist undercurrent series like Naruto have with their child soldiers who cheerfully endanger their lives in blind service of ambition and the national flag. It'd be quite like many boys' adventure anime series.

    \n I think Bret is onto something. I too think the thing that makes anime gaming, well, anime gaming... is really flavor and tropes. Just like D&D doesn't *really* do pulp Sword and Sorcery... D&D doesn't really do anime either,although a group could come along and agree at the table to explore those tropes together.

    First, Flavor: Hey, it is the art. pure and simple. While I liberally borrow from anime all the time (just look at my fantasy character's collars sometime, lifted straight from Lodoss), I don't think anyone would say my work looks very anime-ish. And it isn't. My own predilictions is more similar to American 30s and 40s illustrators crossed with a healthy dose of..oh... Alan Davis, english comic book artist. No one would ever hire me to do an Anime RPG.

    so, an anime RPG better be drippiing with really good anime/manga art.... like Weapons of the Gods nailed the comic wuxia world it was named after by using reprints from the actual comic.

    But flavor is SOOO freakin important in these games. Are mecha there?

    Tropes: Tropes need to be reinforced by system in play. Look at Sword and Sorcery, it is usually lone hero (Conan, Eric Stark, Tarzan) or duo (Fahfrd, Gray mouser, Elric and Moonglum, Elak of Atlantis and drunkard Lycon).

    Now, look at anime: Most often, it seems to me, there is the Main character surrounded by colorful cast of Support and back up (Bleach, Naruto, Witch Hunter Robin, 12 Kingdoms, Inyu Yasha , practically any Gundam). The lead is often "Special", something about him, her places him in harm's way, yet has hidden talents that must come out during the story. THAT trope has to be nailed. Some kind of scaling reveal about talent and power. Systems that reward pluck and persistance are a must. The support cast must be rewarded for playing 2nd banana roles... I'm looking towards White Hat rules in Buffy who get more Fate, Hero points to play with than the Slayer. (I don't remember what those points are called.). Note, sometimes the 2nd bananas are MORE powerful than the main character, but are hamstrung from taking the reins of the story in some fashion. Sasuke is more of badass than Naruto for much of the series...and it is telling when Naruto passes him by.

    Then we have the team concept: Samurai 7, Record of the Lodoss War, Voltron, maybe even Cowboy Beebop, certainly Samurai Champloo.... everyone seems to have a spotlight episode or two. This is more closely atuned to the usual D&D party and feels a bit more even. Some characters tend to be a bit stronger in how much we invest in them... and often, someone needs to die for the story to be pushed forward. But there is a even handness to how much we get to know these characters.

    Then there are exceptions, certainly the main character of Black Lagoon is powerless in a world of blood and bullets, but his business and people acumen pay off in strange, funny and really substantial ways. Last Exile is typical hero journey stuff of two kids thrust into a huge world of steampunk flying battleships and napoleonic tactics and sense of how war is fought. Last Exile, it is anime because the world is SOOO freakin fun and cool. Much of Miyazaki's work is hero journey stuff and again the visuals make it so imaginative and fun, while the heart of the storytelling and characters make it so fun to watch (IMO).

    So. I don't think one can really nail that last category down, the exception category. Unless you devoted an entire book to the world of Last Exile... it is a setting that shouldn't be bolted onto a generic can-do-any-Anime RPG. Nor Nauusica or Gundum or 12 Kingdoms. These are worlds with deep mythologies and are very different than what we have in western rpg settings.... sure, you can do Gundum with Mekton Zeta RPg, but not Battletech. Mekton is a direct homage to Gundam probably more than any other mecha anime (beam saber anyone?). 12 Kingdoms divine rule is so odd and delved into, and colors EVERYTHING in its world, it really needs to be its own setting book.

    But I would argue that Mekton could run Gundam, it isn't as good as it could be. Nor should it, it is a 20 year old game. We've come a long way in RPG-game theory in the last 10. A Gundam rpg needs to have rules for child soldiers falling in love. And how a "special" pilot blows the doors off of the norms. Those are pre-reqs for a Gundam game IMO.

    Anyway, I'll shut up now.

    I have a number of thoughts regarding this but it's rather difficult for me to specifically address them to any one person, so I'll just ramble here a bit.

    Disclaimer: I apparently am one of those dirty "traditional" folks that play rpgs. Sure I like, own, and even run a decent selection of small press / "indy" games, but from the discussions I see, it seems clear that what I like and am interested in has little overlap with folks here. *shrug*

    Anyway, "anime" seems to me to be just as much of a niche thing as rpgs are. From what I've seen, if you say "rpg" you're going to get one of three responses:

    1. "Huh?"
    2. "Oh, you mean like Diablo"
    3. "You mean like World of Warcraft"

    However, if you say "Dungeons and Dragons" (chances are "D&D" isn't enough) _then_ you'll probably get some people that "know" (have a very vague clue) what you're talking about. It's going to be really vague though.

    Anime seems to occupy much the same space.

    There's a chunk of people that think there's "natural overlap" or something in terms of rpgs and "anime". I think a fair amount of that simply comes from lumping the all the unpleasant stereotypes under the general "nerd" or "geek" interest category and being done with it.

    The "overlap" though is largely imaginary. That's not to say that there isn't some, just that it's really not a "significant" amount.

    Just like you can find a fair number of PnP rpg players that play videogames in general and something like World of Warcraft in specific. But if you start "in game" and find out how many people playing an MMO actually know anything about D&D or other roleplaying games, the number is extremely small.

    Each of the geek niches has a dominant aspect. RPGs it's fantasy, MMOs it's WoW, Videogames it's probably GTA or Halflife 2 or Guitar Hero/Rockband (system dependent), non-windows its the Linux geeks, comics it's Supers.

    Whether it's "right" or not, people think of "anime" as a whole thing. It's like saying "Drama" or "Action" Shorthand to sort of describe a big ol' collection of... stuff.

    I personally would say that 8 Mile, Legends of the Fall, and The English Patient are fairly different movies, but they're all lumped under the term "drama". Yes, I realize that "anime" covers a wide variety of actual genres, but it still doesn't change the fact that for most people they hear "anime" and that's just a category.

    If you talk about anthropomorphic animals and rpgs around some place like say... oh, how about rpg.net, you're going to wind up with a decent chunk of people that start great flames about how much they hate "furries". Talk about it to more casual rpgers and you'll probably get something along the lines of "You mean like TMNT?" Non-rpg people you're going to have to go for something like Charlotte's Web, Watership Down (old) or Redwall (newer), something like that.

    Personally?

    I am so bored to fucking tears with the whole "anime" and "rpg" thing. Any time this stupid topic comes up, it boils down to a bunch of people sitting around talking about all the different kinds of anime there are. Sometimes, you'll get people like Eero that apparently loathe just about every attempt there's been to do an "anime" game. Just like so many people loathe the attempts that have been made at "generic" games and want something that really focuses in and "captures" or "embodies" the specific pet subset of whatever game type you happen to be into.

    No Eero, I'm not trying to be a dick you just happened to express an explicit qualitative judgment so I'm using you as an example.

    Does D&D capture/embody "fantasy" roleplaying? For some folks, sure. Different editions of D&D are also going to be capturing different types of fantasy roleplaying too. Does Call of Cthulhu capture/embody "horror"? Does Traveller embody "Science Fiction"?

    People seem to miss the fact that there needs to be a "gateway" product (for lack of a better term) to get people into something. Some folks start with D&D and grow tired of it. They like roleplaying games, but they want something more. So they go looking. They might stay "mainstream" they might venture into the small press or "indy" side. Whatever.

    If the community of roleplayers that also happens to like different animes of genre want people to be interested in branching out into their particular sub-niche, make it attractive to do so in the first place.

    Instead of crapping all over the generic systems (of which there are damn few) of "anime", make sure there's games out there that answer/speak to the particular genre you like in the first place.

    Howl's Moving Castle is different from Ghost in the Shell. But to the person with limited experience, they're not going to be drawing many distinctions. Actually, I suppose "Lion King" and "Howl's Moving Castle" are probably closer analogies. But my point still stands.

    Does anyone here _really_ show up and start crapping all over D&D or Gurps and what pieces of crap they are to people that aren't already a member of this particular sub-community of rpg players? Well... I'm sure there's a few. But ok, let's say you do go off on a tear and start harshing on those games and call them pieces of crap. What then? Well, chances are you've got some pet system that you think is the greatest thing since sliced bread and answers what _your_ particular vision of an action/sci-fi/fantasy/whatever game is.

    Ok, so BESM and OVA are pieces of shit. Show me the wide range of choices that speak to being a game that "looks" anime and deals with one of the various genres you'll find under the "anime" category.

    Let's see....well, Maid is the only one that occurs to me right off the bat. And that's a pretty new one. Are there a lot of "anime" shows featuring maids? I dunno. I guess I've come across one or two, but certainly nothing that springs to mind. Maybe Big-O qualifies.

    What game is it that's answering Ghost in the Shell? Dragonball Z? Elfen Lied? Naussica? My Neighbor Totoro? Howl's Moving Castle? Vampire Hunter D? Rurouni Kenshin?

    Those are just the names that occur to me right off the bat that are lumped under "anime". Sure, you've got a d20 Slayers and a few other licensed bits that GoO had. Of course, people that are fans of the particular show usually dislike the system(s).

    But it's not like there's a lot of choices out there in the first place.

    What's gotten indy / small press rpgs to the point that they are, is in part a fair amount of failure from established companies. Companies that sank tons of money into various games and failed. Went bankrupt. People lost their jobs. Some left rpgs entirely. Slowly, out of all this failure, ideas began to emerge. People thinking seriously about what they like and don't like and why.

    Now, there's a small but seemingly fairly stable niche of small press / indy rpgs out there. There's still the mainstream companies putting out stuff that's changed the minimum amount necessary for the past 30 years.

    I'll also note that the fact that they've been able to keep on as they have, with minimal changes, means that they're answering enough of a need. If small press / indy games were the answer to everyone's rpg needs, they wouldn't _be_ small press, they'd have become "mainstream".

    Right now, with the small press side of rpgs going the way it is, _some_ of that cycle of failure is able to be skipped. But if you really want people to stop looking for an "anime" rpg and start playing games that tell stories that are shown in the anime style... there's going to have to be alternatives in the first place.

    And there's going to have to be failure. Time and money lost.

    It's kind of like in the movies, when the guy is standing there with a gun and holding off a lot more people than the number of rounds in the weapon. "Who wants to be first?" is the question he asks or something along those lines. Nobody in the crowd wants to be the guy that's gut-shot and dies, just so that somebody else can get the glory of beating down the hero.

    Licensed settings really doesn't seem to be the way to go generally. You'll have a small group of people that like it, but often what's happening is the licensed property helps to frame a setting and style of story that the participants like. In other words, while there are some folks that like the idea of playing Mal (or Jayne) from Serenity/Firefly, what the majority of people like is the kind of story, banter, and all that other stuff that goes along with the show. It seems reasonable to expect that if you pick up the "Serenity" game, you're going to be able to evoke that same sort of thing. Whether or not _you_ think the game answers that doesn't really matter. People are buying the game because that's what they want.

    Uresia: Grave of Heaven. You might not like either version of the rules, but it's written more as a sourcebook to playing a particular type of game. Honestly, you can take either version, strip the rules out, and run the setting using your pet system. But S.John Ross really seems to go out of his way to highlight the specific things in the game and talk about ways of tuning it to match a more serious or silly game.

    Like it or not, something like OVA or... well, probably not BESM since the GoO is out of business and people want in print games and supplements... maybe something else, are going to be the generic gateways. And while some people are going to hate the idea, that generic gateway? That's going to be enough for some people. They really are going to be fine with it.

    "Anime" rpgs are a niche of a niche hobby. People that want tightly focused games? Those folks are a niche of a niche hobby.

    Anime+rpg=boring pseudo intellectual arguments online. It's just like rpg theory. RPG theory (in my book) is only useful as long as it actually manages to _make_ something happen. Something very specific. Something that's actually taking place in a game. I don't give a crap about GNS or whatever. I care about the outcome of all that mental masturbation. Games like Donjon, Shadow of Yesterday, Trollbabe. Yeah, the intellectual stuff had to happen in order for those games to come about, but the point was to _make a game_.

    Any time "anime" and "rpg" get talked about, the point doesn't seem to be to actually make a game, it's just another round of spinning tires in the mud.

    And no, I'm not volunteering to step up and do a game that answers this. I pick and choose amongst the games I have for the themes/stories I want. I don't feel there's a lack of "anime" games out there (and I've got OVA and BESM both). I _am_ working at a PDQ thing that I'm hoping to get "anime" style art for and is all about action and all that but it's not an "anime" thing in my head.

    Ummm... I'm not really sure how to finish this. Reading back over, it seems like I've got a bit of a hostile/agressive/upset tone. I'm really not feeling any of that. It's just... I dunno... it seems like it's the same old thing. Maid came out. Groovy. I have absolutely no idea who I could play it with, so I haven't bought it. Is Maid an "anime" game? Being the unwashed, knuckle-dragging, dirty "traditional" fellow that I am, I'd say "yes". I just think there's little point to the whole "anime rpg" discussion unless there's actually going to be _something_ at the end. Rune Soldier (Magical Soldier Louie) using something like Donjon. Not a Donjon hack or conversion; a game that speaks specifically to running an action-comedy-fantasy game like that and a setting that evokes those themes. Or Slayers, or _something_. There needs to be a variety of the "anime genres" represented and there needs to be a variety of systems that try to answer the particular pet peeves of different rpg players.

    Sooooo.... yeah, not pissed off. Just... not caring? I don't see the point in caring? The lack of any games that seem to be trying to answer the "need" for some sort of "anime" rpg seems to me to be a bigger concern, you know? Yeah, a Porche is a hell of a lot better than than a Model T. But you've got to _have_ a Model T and convince people of the need for cars before you can get to producing the Porche. If that makes any sense.

    \nOK, sure. As I alluded above, I've played a fair bit of BESM, both as generic point-buy to accommodate a western-style fantasy campaign, and as a system for producing specifically anime-style stories. So, what did BESM do right?

    Well, for starters they nailed the appeal of full-color sourcebooks for popular Anime series, with episode summaries, setting details and character profiles--regardless of any utility for roleplaying. In fact, though he was already a roleplayer, my brother first got into the Tristat books for just such this purpose, buying the books primarily as fan appreciation of the series, though he was mildly interested in their roleplaying application, too. But having Tristat writeups for Tenchi, Ryoko, etc. wasn't very useful for actual play, since we didn't really want to play a Tenchi Muyo campaign; we wanted to play our OWN stuff. Which led my bro to track down the Big Eyes Small mouth book itself, and we then started applying it to roleplaying.

    And actually, the "mere point-buy" aspect WAS primarily an appeal. I'd bought Champions years before and never played, but I and my brother both enjoyed making characters in such a system, being able to custom-build and detail a heroic personage just how we liked it, instead of being bound by whatever Classes or O.C.C.s or whatnot a designer imposed on us. (This is the standard appeal for point-buy fans, I know. Just stating it for context.) And when we found a new group in college, BESM provided a much lighter, bookkeeping-free vehicle for that desired customizability. That was main reason our friend converted her fantasy campaign to BESM: when she'd begun it AD&D was simply the available system, but she had a detailed setting in mind where magic and mythology and such works a certain way, and D&D was always clashing with her desired effects. BESM allowed her to design from the ground up so that things worked the way she wanted, and allowed us the freedom in individual PC designs to do the same. My Bard, for example, finally had flexible song magic that could invoke and call up the forces he sang about, rather than being a half-rogue with a spell list.

    The "anime color" elements were cool for us as well. The mild skewing-toward-anime of the trait and ability names (Kensei, Gun Bunny, Own a Big Mecha, Guy/Girl magnet, and so forth) were strong enough to evoke Japanese when we wanted, but without inextricably tying us to that. You could easily reenvision Kensei abilities to whatever swordfighting tradition you wanted to have, for example, and Own a Big Mecha is just a animefization of "Own a Pilotable Vehicle." And as I said earlier, the list of genre conventions in the back of the book are fairly useful if you want to play to that.

    And of course it didn't cover, like, ALL of anime; as people have been emphasizing in this thread, that's sorta impossible since anime is so vast and diverse. They did cover "action/adventure anime in the juvenile-to-young adult range" fairly well, according to the mold of what was popular at that time. . .stuff like Tenchi and El-Hazard and Bubblegum Crisis and Sailor Moon, and some giant robot stuff. Like, if you want to build a Tenchi-type-guy or a Gundam pilot and fight it out with appropriate adversaries, with some fairly unstructured interpersonal hijinx in between, BESM's your go-toguy. But run Hana Yori Dango with it? Hell no.

    I think the question "what did GoO do right?" provokes another question, though: "right" to what purpose? I mean, if you're talking about "right" in terms of commercial success of any kind, then yeah, seems like they were doin' all right there for awhile. Though I wouldn't say that for certain without knowing some hard data; "really popular and widespread" doesn't ALWAYS equal "commercially sound." But anyway, if we're talking about some other metric, like "useful for roleplaying purposes," then. . .I wouldn't call it use-LESS, by any means. It filled a need for me at one point. But I found it was more of a stage in development for my roleplaying preferences and understanding. It did things that other available systems (D&D, mostly) at that time didn't do, and thus we were able to get some good out of it. But I look at it now and see a flabby, vague, and poorly balanced system. It's not bad as a zippy, slam-bang combat system, though I never saw much of that use since I play with gamers who "fill in" the 'gaps" in an abstract system rather than taking advantage of its light-footedness. But everything else, though there are a whole passel of "skills" that cover most tasks and interactions, are poorly defined in terms of what effect they actually have. though that's not much different than a lot of other mainstream systems at the time.

    So, I'm not really sure what lessons we can take from GoO for present purposes. As far as the lesson of marketing success goes, we already have the Maid import as an example of raising support as an anime-fandom-artifact. So lesson learned. What about design lessons? I mean, do we want to design another BESM? Is there a way "do BESM right" by this communities design standards, a la Solar System? Maybe. But even if an Anime-Solar game was designed, it would only cover a certain swath of action-adventure anime. I still wouldn't use it to run a lot of genres. So we're back to the supposition that we should design specific focused games for specific genres, a la Bliss Stage or Panty Explosion. I don't see BESM helping a lot there.

    \n I'm surprised that this thread developed so nicely, despite its very cloudy subject. I mean, if one discusses how to create a Gunslinger Girl, or Claymore themed RPG, I could understand that the discussion could get somewhere. But anime themed RPG? that sounds as hopeless as designing a Hollywood themed RPG that could cater for Aliens as well as Harry and Sally, and American Pie to boot.
    \n
    \nThe problem is that the tropes are too diverse to fit practically within one system. Now one can take another approach and see what happens in real life: I might be faced with a group of potential players whose only common element is that they like anime, and want to re-enact parts of it. In that case I guess the beginning would be to explore which particular genre best fits this particular group (mahou shoujo, yaoi, shounen martial arts, etc). I think PTA is the best option for that.
    \n
    \nOnce the preferred genre determined, and the desired tropes identified, THEN one might look for a game, or even design one that really helps rather than hinders the game. Probably (I haven't read) Maid RPG is a good example of this.
    \n
    \nNow I guess the discussion would be even more interesting if we discussed genres and how different mechanics might serve them. But probably before that one could discuss what genres are at all suited to be adapted to RPGs. For instance many shoujo I read are about romance and coming of age. How could you turn that into a RPG? Breaking the ice is one way of approaching a fraction of it, but you would be missing the typical tropes of the genre:
    \n
    \n- passing of time of seasons and its rituals, such as the festivals, Valentine's day, New Years temple visit
    \n- the different coming of age milestones, such as the theater play, the first year in high-school, the first kiss, etc
    \n- the inevitable rivals plus friends, etc
    \n
    \nA Shoujo RPG should provide those tropes, but in such a way that it still makes sense to play in a group, since RPGs are mostly social activity, BtI notwithstanding.
    \n
    \nSo which manga/anime genres would you find insteresting/feasible to adapt into a RPG, and how would you go about it?

    \nOne of my points is that although people tend to perceive it as such, anime is not a "special" category, and designing games inspired by it is no different than for anything else. Mechanics can play off of the medium (Raspberry Heaven, my Azumanga Daioh/Lucky Star style game has scenes structures after the 4-panel comics directly), the story structure itself (Tokyo Heroes, my sentai game, has sessions that go Prologue, Investigation, Battle, Epilogue, just like the shows), or encode memes from the source material (Maid RPG with its zillions of random tables is a perfect example of this).
