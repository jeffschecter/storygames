\n So, I'm trying to think of games which have what I'm referring to as 'Strength Through Adversity' mechanics or, more plainly, mechanics where character advancement comes from taking damage or advancing the plot in ways which are detrimental to the character. I know that Smallville's system works partially along those lines and DitV rewards characters who make the story more interesting at cost to their character, but I'm not sure of any others. Anyone?

    \n Also, in many iterations of FATE (Dresden, at least) you get a fate chip when you lose a conflict, though not all (Diaspora).

    Here's a speech I gave my group when switching from Strands of Fate to Smallville:

    "I know you guys like to have it pretty easy in our games. It's casual, we're enjoying ourselves, you like lots of adversity and problems but you don't like to actually get hurt. The perfect combat for you is one in which you plan something really clever, try to pull it off but there's a problem, you improvise to fix the problem, the bad guys go down, and you walk away without a scratch. If you do that, Smallville is just flat not going to work. You only learn in this game from getting hit, from getting angry, insecure, injured, exhausted or afraid, and then going and talking to someone else about it. If you're never hurt, if nobody ever beats you, you will never accomplish anything. This is very different and it's going to involve a switch. The first thing you need to switch is from thinking of an injury (it's called Stress) in Smallville as being something universally bad to something that's part of your character's situation. Much like you liked 'solving the problems' of Firefly combat in our Fate game, you should see failure and struggle as part of the 'problem' of getting to your goals in Smallville."

    \n In a Wicked Age rewards you for going up against a superior opponent and putting up a fight (even if you lose in the end). If you survive the first round of a conflict against a character who is rolling bigger dice than you, your character's name gets on the Owe List. Entries on the Owe List can be traded in for bonus dice, or to make your character an important part of the story in later sessions.

    \n A way I have toyed with doing this is to have the GM spend tokens to do stuff in the game. Once the players encounter that difficulty the player then take the tokens win or lose. If the GM used the tokens to make a bad guy you get the tokens after your first fight with the bad guy but the GM can still keep using that bad guy until you do something in game, like kill him.


    There was also a Kill Bill inspired game some years back that had damage you took convert to Fire points that you could spend in your next scene after narrating how you overcame your previous defeat.

    \n My favorite example of this is the With Great Power RPG: There is a plot track. At the beginning of the game, the GM has several decks of cards and ways to use them, and the PCs have one deck together and very little ways to use the cards. As the game progresses, you get into conflicts with the opposition. Conflicts where the stakes are things that the players care about.
    \n
    \nYou have to lose conflicts in order to advance on the plot track, and as you advance on the plot track you gain more powers. The last run on the plot track is "You can finally defeat the enemy once and for all" or something to that extent. In other words, it is impossible to beat the scenario/boss/story unless you fail at least four times. Might sound like a little, but the conflicts should really be about things the players care about. In a game I ran, there were about 8 total conflicts until the end (over 4-5 sessions or so?), because the players wanted to Not Fail more than Get That Rung. Which is awesome.

    \n Marshall Burn's The Rustbelt does not have character advancement (in the mechanical sense), but the general play mechanic is all about strength through adversity, so here it is:
    \n
    \nBasically, whenever there's something that is really *hard* to do, you make a roll. The difference between that roll + appropriate stat and a preset difficulty is how much you have to pay in Blood, Sweat, or Tears, to be able to do what you want to do.
    \n
    \nHere's the trick of it, though: you *never* roll enough to just do it. So, whenever your character really wants to do something difficult, he must pay through the nose for it - often, quite literally.
    \n
    \nSo it's not quite getting stronger the more you lose... But the more you lose, the more you put on the line, the more you get done. Which really rings as "Strength through Adversity" for me.
