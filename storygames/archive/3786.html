\n
\n So under the heading of "Scene Framing," I often see two (and sometimes three) things done at the same time:
\n
\n- Who is in the scene? (hereinafter: Scene Casting)
\n- Where is the scene set? What does it look like? What are the tactical/emotional considerations? (hereinafter: Scene Setting)
\n- (Sometimes) What is the purpose of the scene in the narrative?
\n
\nSetting aside that third thing (which is often absent anyway), I don't see any reason why those first two need to happen in concert. I actually think it'd be really neat to have them done by two separate people.
\n
\nI think different orders would serve different purposes, though I want to think a bit more about why that's so. My initial thought is that the difference is that scene casting is a decision with social ramifications (it matters whether you cast a given player's character in the scene, and so you'll apply some creativity to making the outcome you want, rather than the easy one) whereas scene setting is not as fraught.
\n
\nBut, in any event, I think it'd be interesting and neat to have an interaction like this:
\n
\n
Tom: Okay, the scene is Darla ...
\nSylvie (playing Darla): YAY!
\nTom: ... and Harry.
\nSylvie: BOO!
\nJed (playing Harry): Oooh ... Awkward.
\nSylvie: (glaring) Gonna be a damn quiet scene, that's all. Darla's not talking to him. Hmph.
\nRich: Okay, and the scene will be set in an elevator.
\nSylvie: Push my button. Stare at the numbers. Tap my foot.
\nRich: An elevator that breaks down.
\nSylvie: I hate you so much.

    \n Yes!

    I'm doing this with Medical Hospital - one player sets the location and a secondary character, and another player ties these into the main character's issue and a current event. The third plays the main character, the focus of the scene. Thus every scene has three contributors helping frame it.

    There are five bits of information in my proto-game, so there might be different ways of distributing it, but I think distributing it in general is really cool.

    \n It's the index card thing from another thread. A card card gets laid down and tells everyone which character will be in the spotlight. A second card relates another character's issue to the spotlight character. A third card tells you where the scene is set or an interesting event associated with it. They can go down in any order (the first card laid down might actually be the issue, or the location, for example), but they way they interface frames the scene in the roughest terms.

    \n I remember some parts of it ... but I don't remember a time when casting and setting were both getting focussed player attention at the same time.
    \n
    \nIn a mission, casting is decided by system (it'll be the mission guy, their anchor (sorta) and nobody else but horrifying alienses). Then a lot of attention gets lavished on setting the scene.
    \n
    \nIn interlude scenes, casting gets a lot of attention ... but then, to be honest, my experience was that people skipped gleefully past setting the scene, doing a sketchy sort of "Let's agree on a place to have our blowup" thing. Is there an assignment of authority there that I missed?

    \n The player with privilege (the GM or a pilot) chooses the cast.
    The players who own those cast decide, roughly, on the circumstances of their meeting.
    The GM sets the scene.

    If Tony isn't playing the game, this is a lot more important. If Tony is playing the game, we gleefully skip past things.

    In Medical Hospital you'll have strong incentive not to cast your own guy in the spotlight, and the order index cards are laid down will result in a little tactical bidding. If you want your guy to be featured, you want to lay down second. If you want to control the bulk of scene framing, you want to go last.
