\n This post is about planning for Games on Demand for the coming year. I'm taking the lead on organizing the event for Gen Con, and I'd like to gather any additional feedback or suggestions based on previous years.

When I was asked by Gen Con to better explain the concept of Games on Demand, here's what I said:\n

What are the goals?
To my mind, the event serves two main purposes: 1) creating an opportunity for attendees and volunteers to play new and unusual games, often with the designers, and have fun; 2) serving as a "long-demo" space for the booths and publishers selling the books, giving them a way to expose people to their games.

Who\u2019s in charge?
Games on Demand doesn't belong to a specific company, and is more of a community social event. It is a volunteer-run operation with close ties to the publishers and vendors (such as IPR, Pelgrane, Design Matters and others) that sell the games at the show. Vendors (such as IPR) have in the past supplied the event with badges, materials, and discount coupons, and many individuals and publishers have invested their time and energy to make it happen each year.

Which games are on demand, exactly?
This definition has always been shaky at best. Whether you call them "indie", "story" or "small press" games, it means roughly the same thing- small games that often feature unusual mechanics or ideas, from creators and publishers who are mostly unknown in the broader industry. To be fair, most of the games we're talking about come out of a specific community, but the event is by no means limited to people we know. We had some people running their independent games this year who were certainly new to the community, which was both weird and great.

\n I have a few specific goals for this thread:

1) If you've helped organize or run the event in years past, and would like to work with me to organize things a bit better, please chime in or drop me a line directly. I'm currently in talks with the Gen Con staff about plans for 2012 and I'd like a small team of advisers to help me sort that out (between now and January).

2) If you've run Games on Demand at another show (Origins, PAX, etc.), and would like to do it again, I'd be interested in coordinating our planning. Drop me a line or jump into this thread with your ideas.

3) If you have any specific feedback on the event, including what we should repeat from last year and ways we can improve, shout it out. One thing we have discussed is putting on a mix of scheduled events alongside the usual Games on Demand in generally the same area. Thoughts on that would be welcome.

4) If you have ideas about how to better define the event, it's goals, or the concept of which games we're talking about- in terms the general public will understand- I'd like to hear them.

NOTE: I'm not particularly interested in re-hashing old arguments about what "Indie" means or doesn't mean. Let's keep that talk to a minimum.

Thanks!

    \n At local indie cons like GoPlay NW, we pretty much always have a table with stacks of books and ashcans on it for people to peruse. And then you either leave money in a box or track down the creator and pay them directly. It's a bit harder at more commercial conventions that have their own rules about sales, but my sense is that people often sell out of backpacks on an individual basis. It's just harder to have an open sales venue distinct from the convention, because we'd likely get reported and then be in trouble with the convention staff that we want to work closely with in the future. Often, if you have a connection with a game store that's selling at the convention, you can get them to carry your games for you, even just temporarily, as long as they have shelf space or a place near the register to put them.

    Unless something crazy happens with my job, I'll be at PAX Prime this year and will be glad to help out however I can. Also, I have the poster files from the black-and-white poster we used for PAX, based on the color poster from GenCon.

    \n As an update, we've been working through the details for GoD at Gen Con. The plan as it stands is to make some modest changes from last year and doing what we can improve the event for everyone involved.

    Right now we should be getting a 12 table room in the Convention Center, probably on the upper floor overlooking the Exhibitor hall (though the details are still to be worked out). That gives us twice the space we had last year, in a more central location. I don't think we'll actually be able to double attendance and GMs, so we'll split the space into both standard Games on Demand open gaming and scheduled games, taking tickets for both as we did last year (1 specific or generic ticket / 2 hour slot).

    If you're interested in running scheduled games for the event, touch base with me before trying to enter it yourself. Likewise, if you want to volunteer to GM open games or help facilitate GoD, let me know. Volunteers and GMs who put in enough hours will get comped badges (and maybe a place to sleep). I'll be working out the scheduling details more as we get closer.

    \n Right now I'm planning to do what we did last year,which was 10am - 10pm on Thursday, Friday and Saturday, and then 10am - 2pm on Sunday. Derek asked me if we'd want to stay open later (from 10pm - 12am for example) and I said my only concern was covering the time and being able to take tickets.

    In general, I'd like to make sure our actual attendance is a large percentage of what we anticipate it will be. Empty seats during our event hours make it look less successful on paper, giving us less leverage for space, etc. That said, if we have the interest and volunteers, we can make the hours whatever we like.

    I should also note that we won't have to lock up the room at night, so people can stick around after 10pm and keep playing if they aren't drawn elsewhere in pursuit of booze.
