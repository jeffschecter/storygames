\n
\n In another thread Lisa Padol said "There's a problem Hillfolk is written to solve. If you don't have that problem, the game may or may not have other things to offer you."

This got me thinking about games that solve particular problems, and how disconcerting it is to play those games if you don't have those problems.

For example, my local crew largely plays to fail - we need absolutely no encouragement to make interesting bad choices and get our characters in desperate trouble. In fact, we need encouragement to do the opposite! So mechanics like Fate's compel feel very weird to us - we're being rewarded for doing what comes naturally. We end up aggressively self-compelling. One of the problems Fate is written to solve is the player who prefers success over drama.

What are some other games that seem written to solve particular problems?

    \n I would be inclined to think that nearly all rpgs are like this; the field is so vast that one man's naturally neutral game is another's hyper-specific crutch.

    If I had to name a game that wasn't written to solve specific problems, I'd be likely to point at the traditional commercial mainstream; most of those games have apparently been written to mimic supposed market expectations of what content a rpg text should have, rather than expressing the author's genuine art of roleplay. If we put that entire class of games aside, though, it seems almost axiomatic that all games are written for a purpose, and that purpose is to solve some problem the author sees in continuing roleplaying without writing that text. We write games to solve problems in prior games, in other words.

    \n There was also a host of post-D&D games designed to solve the problem of "unrealistic combat simulation". Games like RuneQuest (and the Norwegian MEGA), for instance, were pretty much a reaction against level systems, hit points, combat rounds etc. If you didn't have a problem with unrealistic combat - if, in fact, you liked the game-like monster-bashing experience of D&D - playing RuneQuest could be horribly dull, with few and way too deadly fights.

    \n My game GLASS 'solves' the problem most action LARPs have of being overly verbal (to the point of reducing comprehension in combat, not augmenting it), attempting to simulate impossibilities (teleport just *doesn't* work in LARP), and player disenfranchisement caused by simulating mind-affecting abilities. Most games of the mid-2000s were trying so hard to be 'D&D on your feet' that they'd bled out actual player skill--both physical and social/mental--and reduced play to a crawl of holds and (worse!) turn-based actions. I wanted the sport with some simulation for the fantastic: basically shifting the sweet spot on a spectrum of DO versus STAT.

    \n According to my nephew, Mythic GM Emulator was written for when everyone wanted to play an RPG, but no one wanted to be stuck GMing. I have a slightly more optimistic view on it, that it was designed for small groups when everyone wanted to play as well as take on minor GMing duties.

    Matrix Games are tricky because they're a family of games and mods to games. I one key, early form, however, they're a way for players to bring elements of interest to wargames that could potentially impact the level of focus without the need for intricate sub-systems for those kinds of things above or below the level of focus. ( Yes, I can unpack that last bit later if it's as clear as mud).

    Early versions of D&D have their weird XP system seemingly to deal with a situation where there are big groups of players all playing at different times with different frequency and in different combinations in a sandbox world where death is common.

    Archipelago seems to have multiple problem solvers built in to it. Key phrases point players to different types of GM type behavior and give them polite permission and direction in taking those types of GM actions. Destiny Points give players directions to push the game fiction for both themselves and other players. The spheres of influence ( I forget the proper name right now) give individual player authority over key important game world concepts without the need to pre-write huge amounts of text or have a huge amount of time spent hashing it all out before play begins. All of those things are key to success in a game where the group is making up stuff collectively with equal "game power".

    Not RPGs, but Games Workshop's various "gang campaign" miniatures games solved the problem of being a minis game + "killed" characters with their post battle "what happened to them really?" rolls.

    \n I definitely wrote Sufficiently Advanced to address problems in other sci-fi games (and settings). I wanted to have a world where technology was allowed to flourish and its implications were carefully considered. I also wanted to get rid of the "shopping trip" that happens in most sci-fi (and cyberpunk) games right before the plan falls apart in a firefight.

    There were a lot of other design goals too, but I think those were the only ones where I said "Man, I'm tired of this crap. Let's make sure that doesn't happen in this game."

    \n In less pejorative terms this is really:

    Games designed for an audience to achieve specific goals. Evidently the designer(s) thought those goals were worthwhile for that audience.

    If you are not part of said audience and/or do not really share those goals maybe the game isn't for you. Or maybe you get something else out of it.

    Is it really necessary to point to "superior" gaming habits and look down our noses at games designed for different people and for purposes that don't personally thrill us?

    - Mendel

    \n Oh, also: the premise of the OP is really important to bear in mind when talking to people about new-to-them games. I recently advised someone against trying out any indie games with his home group. They're long-established and mesh well together, according to his description, and routinely have those "we didn't touch the dice!" sessions of their ostensibly D&D3.5 home game. I said to him, Look, your system is basically your group, not what's in the rulebooks, and you've clearly established habits that work for you. People in your situation who try out indie games are not generally successful, because those games require you to both do and not-do very specific things. If you want to try out indie games, great, but do it with other people.

    \n So, the thing about Luke's games is that they solve\u2014or at least address\u2014the problem of both being too nice (problem I used to have) and, in general, being too mean (problem I've seen some other people have). That is, if you're following the rules closely, the PCs will experience a nice mix of success and failure, frustration and triumph.

    It's very rare, though by no means impossible, to find a GM who truly has the art of providing a meaningful mix of challenge levels within a game, whose game of choice doesn't actually provide the framework the way BW does.

    Note that 3E and 4E D&D also attempt to address this issue, largely successfully! It's just that the nature of what is "challenging" in those games is rather more narrowly defined.
