    Screens hide not only your secret notes, but also your body language, thus cutting off important communication queues. The Mehrabian myth, while not exactly true in the gaming situation where players have a strong context for shared communication, still applies to some extent: some information is shared via body language and the screen blocks that.

  • ffilz

    In the good ole days, I used a GM screen to hide my notes, and for the all important reference charts. I made my first one by taping photocopies of the charts onto an old Avalon Hill game board (it gave me a nice huge display - it was from the game Midway which had two separate boards that folded in half to fit the old style AH flat box, not the newer book case boxes).

    When I started running Cold Iron, the screen started to disappear. All the Cold Iron reference charts fit on one page. I did get a "generic" GM screen which had generic pictures on the outside and blank on the inside. I've never pasted anything to that.

    When I started running D20 (Arcana Unearthed), I took the 3.5 DM screen from Dragon Magazine and added some additional flip panels with AU specific stuff - though mostly I ignored the screen, still it was useful for some amount of hiding.

    These days, when I use a screen, it's on my side-table (a TV cart that handily has a 3" high raised wall on three sides so a DM screen is easy to make stand up on it, but without a DM screen, I still have a wall to roll dice against).

    Oh, and I have always been one to move around. In my current setup, I don't do too much standing up. My gaming table is coffee table height (though during the AU campaign, I did a couple times stand up and walk around). During a battle, I will often get off my easy chair and sit on the floor to be closer to the table.

    Frank
  • Rafael
    October 2006
    I play most games with a GM screen, because I don't want the players to see the illustrations or notes that I'm using (until it's time to reveal these elements). I've been GMing for twenty years, and in that time, I've found that all of my players enjoy the technique (although I don't count players that I met and gamed with at a con, for example, simply because there wasn't sufficient time to talk about that kind of thing). If I were gaming with a group that found the practice objectionable, I would employ a different method.

    I usually set the screen off to the side, so that the content behind it is visible to me, but not the players. However, I ensure that it does not obstruct my body language, or the results of my rolls.

    -- Rafael
