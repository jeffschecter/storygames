So I'm standing at the Forge booth and someone picks up your game and brings it to me: "What can you tell me about this one?" I know they want something different than what's on the back cover. What would you like me know about your game, in two or three sentences?

I'll start:

1001 Nights has two levels of characters, Court and Story. The Court characters use the Stories to say what they couldn't say openly in Court, and there's a neat little mechanic that makes colaborative Stories really easy. The GM power is shared around, and at the end of each Story, you have to check to see if you've angered the Sultan.

    carry, as the title says, is a game about war. It's a short-form roleplaying game that follows the story of a group of US Marines in Vietnam. The game focuses on the issues and problems that the soldiers carry (haha, get it!) with them into the war, and how those burdens shape their actions. The game is about both the actions of men in war and the memories and legacies they leave behind.

    Okay, here's me trying to pimp Fastlane since it'll be at the booth this year:

    "Fastlane isn't a game about the long run - it's about the NOW. It uses roulette - not just the wheel, but the entire web of bids - to create a game where you have a very fine control over how much you want to risk, with the rewards going steadily higher the more you do. Of course, the house always wins in the long run, so you might as well burn your candle at both ends, have fun while it lasts, and in the end, leave a good-looking corpse. And if you don't have a wheel, as long as you have some chips or other counters to make bids with, and some six-sided dice, you can still have about 95% of the fun (98% in Europe)."*

    (*This last bit is a joke that references the difference between the American and European roulette wheel - the American one has twice as many zeroes, doubling the house edge.)

    Just for reference, I hit on an interesting article about making an elevator pitch that has a lot of stealable points for pimping.

    For those not deep in wonkspeak, the elevator pitch is the 30 second or less response to questions like "What do you do?" or "What does your company do?". It's predicated on the assumption that people mostly don't care and are asking just to be polite, but if you have a good answer, it's an opportunity to get them interested. How applicable that is to pitching your game, especially to people who aren't already pro-indy, I leave as an exercise for the reader.
