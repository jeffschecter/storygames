    Pretty much all of the games listed so far have some sense of fairness even in their inequality. Stuff like "well you don't have to play Fighter, you could choose a better class." We need to dig deeper for games where the inequality is not a matter of choice or accident, but rather unavoidable (and intentional?) design. That's much more difficult to find, surely.

    Fables of Camelot has that quality, the PCs are explicitly ranked at the beginning of the game in an unfair (though mild) way. There's no trade-off involved, to be of poor social status is to be of poor social status (and there's no reward for it). It's not a major element, though, but rather just a minor drama seed.

    The Dragonlance campaign starts the players with noticeably inequal dramatic roles: some are chosen to be the messengers who usher in the new age of divinity on Krynn, while others are chosen to be the funny ethnic sidekick.

    Anybody remember how Poison'd determines the initial ship hierarchy? I seem to remember that there's some logic for determining who gets to be first mate (or if it's a NPC), but I don't remember how it goes.

    \n I'm not familiar enough with modern systems to give you a breakdown, but the concept has been going for a while.

    Back in the day there was the Hero System campaign 'Greek Hero'. Players were a band of Greek minor kings but could also play the lesser folk who accompanied them.

    Chivalry and Sorcery, Bushido and the Elric games all had random background charts which determined your starting status as well as starting wealth and your ideal skills.

    The Dr Who game saw one player as the Doctor and the others as companions.

    Even AD&D got in on the act, with the Birthright campaign seeing a noble scion supported by his lesser followers.

    Nathan_H said:

    But, do you think these games worked?
    Specifically, the ones that gave game/rules supported advantages, versus the ones that gave shared imagination status differences?

    \nYes, such games work, but you need players who are happy that they are mechanically disadvantaged compared to another player in the group. If you have a group of power-players then you are unlikely to have happy players if one is elevated above them.

    In the Hero System I mentioned, the 'king' player(s) started on more points than the followers. In Bushido, Elric and Chivalry and Sorcery the game granted higher status characters additional starting money and equipment as well as a better range of combat-related skill bonuses.

    It can work, but you need the players to be on board.

    \n In Hurlements, initiates have a major numerical (like X2 on % stats, plus fantastic powers) and an overwhelming fictional edge. Social injustice is mitigated because they are all in the same fraternity, but it's a medieval historic setting where some are nobles and others street orphans without hope of a rags to riches happy end. The numerical bit is made unimportant because players don't have access to the rules anyway.
    It delivers every time thanks to the perfectly designed tailor made solo introduction. Once you are on the path for your quest to discover yourself and the essence of the world, envy doesn't make any sense.
