    It is, though his jerky, sentence-fragment-heavy, hard-sell style of writing is a bit offputting.

    It's probably good advice for writing an action-adventure novel, terrible advice for writing a literary novel. Worse than terrible. (Now I'm doing it.)

    But I can definitely see using it to structure an action-adventure RPG.

    EDIT: Looked up one of his books on Amazon and (among several extremely positive reviews, I stress) is a review which speaks thusly:

    "The biggest problem with this novel is the writing style. Ingermanson writes in such a simple, elementary style I found it hard to relate to any of the three main characters. Their emotions seemed insincere at best and artificial at worst. I eventually found myself not caring whether or not they made it alive till the end. And the only true suspense in the book - the carjacking - ending up being quickly resolved in the most cliche manner possible."

    So, people's mileage with this approach varies. Probably not a big surprise to anyone.

    Second EDIT: His Snowflake Method also has some potential for developing RPG stuff, I suspect.

    I think the best thing to come out of this, which we've discussed before, is that not every scene needs to be conflict-driven. But I disagree that all other scenes need to be "sequel" scenes.
