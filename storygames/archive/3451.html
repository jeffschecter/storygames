    \n The biggest issues are the the actual printing.

    I've bought a few.

    The most problematic was BTRC's "Fuck This" game. It was designed to be printed onto a specific type of Avery card which was pre-punched into business cards. Which probably seems like a good idea until you realise that this stuff isn't available outside of the US.

    I bought Grim's school shooting game, whatever that's called ("Final Straw" I think). It was OK, but the layout of the cards themselves was a bit shit. The text was too hard to read. I told him that and he said he'd already made it bigger once to make it more legible. I suggest that once was not enough. Also, these came with no backs to print. That's no problem for me, because I print cards onto paper, then put them in sleeves with old Pokemon cards, so there's no backs anyway.

    I also got the Savage Worlds Adventure cards, which were a PDF. Printed on A4 and came out a bit small, but not small enough to be a problem. Although, again, I printed them on paper and stuffed them into deck protectors so I have no idea if printing the backs would have lined up with the whole A4/letter difference. Probably would have been OK, but I don't have any way to print onto cardboard easily so never tried.

    8 cards to a page seems to work well.

    Getting cards to print right on A4 as well as letter isn't as easy as all that when I tried it. I mean, you can get by OK, but you might need to make two versions. Not that that's the end of the world.

    Overall though, I've never bought a card game in PDF which was available in print. Card games have a much more tactile quality to them than RPGs, which are text to be read, understood and referred to during play rather than a thing you actually handle to make the game go (if that makes any sense?).

    \n That sort of thing is going to retail for USD 20 or so, maybe less, with professionally printed cards that will endure rough handling and a tuck box. You are taking away the quality cards and tuck box and adding both labor and expense.

    I think your biggest hurdle is going to be convincing people it has any value at all, given those parameters. If I played the game and was absolutely hot to play it again, I'd pay ten bucks for sure. But I'd much rather pay twenty and have the annoying bits taken care of.

    \nI *heavily* stress that you don't make your PDF game require color to work. Not everyone has a color printer, and of those who do not everyone keeps their color ink stocked or wants to use it on your game. Now, some might like this, but I would suggest a second set that would work as black & white -- when you're dealing with consumer-end setup, you can't make many assumptions.\n

    \n Wherever possible, I'd also recommend including icons that are associated with your color-coding, since those would work just as well in black n' white and color. So all the blue cards have the same icon, all the red cards have the same icon, etc. The words "introduction" "connection" and "finale" are a good first step, but icons can be recognized at a glance, upside down or right side up.
