    \n My situation is similar since I wanted a game to play with my wife. Since 2007, PTA is the only I have played consistently. 50-100 episodes.

    PTA does not work well with one player only because of the fan mail aspect. It is has to be given from one player to another without producer's interference. If you ply have one player, it is impossible by definition.

    We play PTA gmless/gmful. Each plays 1 protagonist and we take turns asking for scenes. You can ask for scenes for your or the other player's protagonist. Whoever's protagonist is not in the scene has the authority over the npcs. If both protagonists are in the scene, then you split the npcs depending on who you are interacting with.

    To find out how much budget the producer will spend on a conflict, we roll 2d6 and the difference is how many points the producer adds to the automatic 1 card he gets.

    This system has been working wonderfully well for us. Please let me know if you have any curiosity about our experience and I will be glad to try to answer any questions.

    \n I just played a one-player game of PTA with my girlfriend while we were on holiday. It worked brilliantly as an introductory game (the producer's budget gives the session a time limit; the scene framing rules give lots of structure to how to advance the story). As producer, I simply awarded her fanmail every time I thought she'd done something cool ...

    ... and she did lots of cool stuff: from minor points of characterisation (like her insipid accidental-murderer protagonist's choice of tea), to roleplaying with aplomb during a scene where two homicide detectives tried to crack her in an interrogation room.

    As for pitfalls to avoid:
    Reading up on advice about how to frame conflicts is always good.
    Be prepared to provide support and guidance when she's unsure how to contribute or what to do next.
