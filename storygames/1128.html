    The game that's come closest to the ninja feel, for me, was (surprisingly) a computer game: Thief. The main dynamic of the game is its stealth mechanic: for all intents and purposes, your ability to kill enemies is directly correlated to how well you've snuck up on them or (more commonly) hidden in just the right place to have them walk past you.

    One of the first challenges of the game is to find the right patch of shadow to cling to in order to have a guard walk right past you ... then you walk behind him, matching his foot-falls, until HE ends up in the right patch of shadow for you to take him out without anyone noticing. The overpowering sense of fragile superiority ... of being in a position that is intensely unfair because you own this guy 100%, but still totally just because if you ever got noticed you'd be dead in seconds ... massive wish fulfillment.

    I'd love to see that translated into a tabletop ruleset. Yep.

  • stevebarkeruk
    September 2007
    You could build the whole combat system around instant kills, whether the ninja has been detected or not; give each character a defense level which you must exceed with maneuver points at which point you can instantly kill them. Maneuvering for the kill is easier if the target doesn't know you're there or is distracted, and in a straight fight they are also maneuvering to kill you.

    For example a Samurai guard has a Defense of 10. If you can make maneuvers to a value of 11 without being spotted you can silently dispatch him. If he spots you, his Defense doubles to 20 and he is now also maneuvering to overcome your own Defense stat.

    Things like armour increase Defense by reducing the number of angles vulnerable to attack, but also decrease the ability to maneuver for attack. Characters like Samurai can have access to traits and items like ultra-sharp katana which can reduce armour bonuses, while Ninja could have access to distraction items like smoke bombs to reducing their opponent's maneuver total if they are close to being defeated.

    So the kill is the final resolution of the conflict, but the conflict itself is about stealthy maneuver.

    Strictly speaking, a game about historical ninja is a game about samurai special ops (which would be kinda cool). Here are some historical resources.

    http://www.ninpo.org/historicalrecords/bansenshukai.html

    http://www.ninpo.org/historicalrecords/ninpidenindex.htm

    There is some contention about the historical context behind ninjutsu (which, strictly speaking, has nothing to do with fighting techniques). Confirmed historical practices are usually a subset of a larger curriculum/set of strategies. The Tenshin Shden Katori Shint-ry is known to have preserved some historical espionage techniques, but nobody knows exactly what they are because they're secrets -- and mostly irrelevant in the modern day. The Koga-ryu is extinct; the last known master was an eccentric named Seiko Fujita who didn't teach anyone. Meanwhile, there's a guy named Masaaki Hatsumi (the links above are for a site belonin to the Genbukan, a splinter organization of Hatsumi's tradition) run by a who claims to have inherited three ninjutsu traditions but there's no proof, though he is a legitimate heir to a branch of the Kukishin-ryu. When you research authentic ninjutsu you'll usually find his name, but he's not necessarily a trusted source. IIRC there are ninja reenactment/performance groups in Japan.

    One of the things I wanted from Budo was a way to make a character by just transferring the curriculum from a historical school to the character sheet so I could take advantage of the massive amount of available trivia, but I personally am more interested in a cinematic style game.

    Oh yeah -- there are gritty instant death rules there too if you set that "dial."
