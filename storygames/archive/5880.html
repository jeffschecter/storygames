\n I'm working on a game which emulates simple children's adventure games and which uses a dice pool mechanic and semi-task resolution. Monsters have hit points equal to their dice pool, and get defeated when they have taken damage equal to that number. All well and good so far

What's bothering me is how to handle groups of monsters. The obvious answer is that each monster has it''s own pool, and players can fight them one on one, do damage, defeat them, and the like. It's simple, and I'm sure that will work fine most of the time, but it seems less elegant than I'd like.

The other option is to just treat a group of monsters as one monster with more dice, which is nice an elegant, but has flaws too. I'm looking for advice in which to choose, and I'll outline the pros and cons here.

Each monster has it's own pool

This is simple and obvious. It has the advantage that players can gang up on single monsters or face off against single opponents as they like. Also it allows a reinforcement mechanic I have (converting GM successes into more monsters) to work smoothly. On the other hand it makes encounters somewhat harder to set up, because I just give the GM a dice budget for each encounter, and splitting it only allows so many members of a horde. Of course the GM can have a small bunch of monsters to start with and hope to roll reinforcements, but in fact a single monster with lots of dice is more likely to get reinforcements than a bunch of small ones.

All the monsters are one pool

This seems more elegant, the threat is just one dice pool, and as you do damage it just loses members, and it avoids all the problems with initiative, pairing combatants and the like. On the other hand it just turns reinforcement into healing, which is a lot less cool, and doesn't allow players (or monsters) to divide and conquer in the same way. It makes the encounter setup a lot easier though

In writing this steam of consciousness I am leaning strongly towards the former ... but maybe someone has some cool suggestions for the encounter setup?

    \n Thanks for reminding me about the SOTC rules, I think that would work, allowing a group of monsters to all gang up together keeps things nice and flexible.

    My problem with the encounter design is that (like Donjon, from which I am shamelessly stealing), each monster's dice pool is it's own dice + a number for the chapter you are in. So a 2 dice monster has 3 dice in chapter 1 and 5 in chapter 3. This means that a 4 dice encounter with one monster rolls chapter + 4 dice, while splitting it into 2 monsters with 2 dice gives you (chapter * 2) + 4 dice, which is more.

    I'm beginning to think this might be fine, though, since two monsters with (say), 5 dice each have more hits than one with 7 dice, but less chance to win rolls, which balances out the advantage.

    What I want to avoid is making an impossible encounter by just having too many little monsters. My idea was that if you want a horde then you just have a few monsters to start with and bring in reinforcements as needed, the only problem is that, since excess successes are converted into new monsters, a bigger dice pool gets more reinforcements, so even if you want a horde of monsters you are still better of starting with one 4 dice monster than a number of smaller ones.

    I realise I could fix this with some sort of split pool, but the mechanics of what you do with successes are neat and work well right now, and I want to avoid a list of monster powers a la RBH

    \n I say make things easy on the GM. I mean, since the HP and the characters' pools are the same thing, your system seems pretty much set up to do this already. I'm assuming here that "pools" also represent the effectiveness of these monsters as well, which would decrease as, say, a gang of little ones loses members.

    I don't think that grouping the dice will reduce reinforcement to healing the way you see it. In particular, if you treat each die as one member, it's really easy to say how many members are remaining. It's even quick to do some simple division, if each member is represented by a small number, say 2 to 5 dice. And hey, if a creature has 13 dice you're probably not grouping it anyhow.

    Having a physical representation of the number of individuals left in the group (even if a little division is potentially involved), as well as an easy method of mapping group effectiveness to numbers gives you a one up on any traditional systems I know that try grouping. ^^

    \n I don't think multiple monsters should add their skills together, but they should be able to generate greater effects.
    \n
    \nThink of a mob of goblins chasing a group of kids in a movie. One goblin isn't very good at grabbing the kids, and a mob of 10 is no better. Once the mob does grab, though, they can grab a lot of things (the kid's hair, backpack, shoes, etc.), or their many hands can get a pretty good grip. In standard rpg terms this is a little like a damage bonus, but a bit more flexible.
