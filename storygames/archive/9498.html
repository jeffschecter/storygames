\n I adore the Bas Lag books (Perdido Street Station, The Scar and Iron Council) but I have no desire to set a game there.

What interests me about Mieville's fantasy writing is the anthropological thinking (at least that is how I think of it, having dated a lady who studied Anthropology). So, setting a game in the shadow of Perdido Street Station, not so interesting, but taking that novel's kind of thinking so that we play a game where we consider the effect of a series of man-eating undersea kingdoms on the coastal villages or how a planar invasion of Githyanki effects a confederacy of city-state's anti-slavery laws...yes.

The whole game doesn't have to be about these things but I like those kinds of socio-economic touches brewing under the surface of the settings where I play.

Richard K. Morgan's books have this kind of vibe too. The Steel Remains, his latest, a fantasy novel in the swords & sorcery tradition just drips with it.

So, those are my Mieville-ish thoughts.

    \n One problem I have with a lot of "steampunk" games & fiction is that it's all steam and no punk.

    Which is to say, the emphasis often ends up on the trappings of the fictional steampunk age (flying machines, engine magick, people wearing goggles for no real reason, etc), and not the social issues behind the fiction - class struggle, the social effects of technological revolution, and protagonists who are at odds with authority and lacking in social power or influence.

    Mieville's books, on the other hand, do this splendidly. The protagonists of Perdid Street Station and The Scar are outside the law, down on their luck, and generally victims of the power structure of the places where they live. They're not kick-ass heroes who hobnob with kings and generals. This theme is taken even further in Iron Council, where the protagonists are workers, slaves and prostitutes.

      \n N.B.: I'm somewhat devil's advocating, here.

      It seems to me that the discussion in this thread is mixing up the concepts of setting and theme. That is, the setting of Perdido Street Station is New Crobuzon, a diverse tossed-salad of races, cultures, classes, and ideologies. The themes of Perdido Street Station are things such as class struggle and power dynamics, as well as ambiguous moral grounds and the consequences of magic and technology.

      Now, New Crobuzon just so happens to be well suited to exploring the themes listed above, but that does not mean there aren't a whole range of other stories that can be explored within the bounds of the city. It's a city in which you can find anything and everything, so the range of possibilities is incredible. I can very easily envision a successful monster-hunting campaign being run in New Crobuzon. I can also see a family drama, a noir-style detective story, a war story, a comedy of errors, a mad-science misadventure, a romantic comedy, etc, etc. not one of which would have to touch on the themes of Perdido Street Station in order to make for entertaining gameplay. Hell, there are even characters in Perdido Street Station that are called out as being D&D-style adventurers. Those characters exist, but their stories haven't been written yet. Why not attempt telling them, if that's what suits your gaming group?

      If you limit yourselves to exploring only the themes already explored within the books, you're cutting out a whole swath of possible tales. If you want to replicate the feel of Perdido Street Station, sure, play a game that emphasizes those themes. But if you want to play a game set in New Crobuzon, you're going to need something that gives you a little more freedom in terms of themes you might want to explore.

      I'm all for an official New Crobuzon RPG. I'm hoping it's not just a D&D derivative, sure, but I wouldn't mind something that could handle that style if that's what I wanted to play.

      I have no disagreement that adding the color of New Crobuzon is fairly simple. But I propose that what you are trying to get at is not so much a matter of one-line character identities, but what the characters do, short and long term over the course of the game.

      For example, I suspect you would be equally incapacitated by a game where the protagonists where factory workers, remade, and ne'er-do-wells who band together to keep out subtle invading threats from beyond the city and to help the beleaguered mayoral government.

      But they are not 1) higher class or 2) putting down rebellions. This is the problem with defining things with negatives. This is why I kept asking what you want your Bas-Lag protagonists to do. Then it becomes possible to used the design of the game to encourage that type of play.

      For example, the hack I was considering can easily be structured so that every character is predominantly described by 2-3 dreams and the associated authorities which serve to block the achievement of those dreams. Over the course of play, as there are continual confrontations with these authorities (social, financial, physical, mystical), the dreams come closer to achievement and the authorities become more potent in their hostilities. Ultimately the dream will either be abandoned or achieved, but if achieved then the world changes - but hardly in a predictable way. There is, after all, always a cost for getting something which our heart desires.

      (Aside: The game in question already handles the literary feel well, with chapter and book break down of play, easy support of parallel action and the introduction and removal of characters mid-stream. And it meets one of my hardest requirements for a Bas-Lag, being able to play the Weaver in a balanced, fun way.)

      My question is would such a handling suffice. After all, regardless of your social class, if you are defined in terms of your conflict with authority - be it academic, political, etc., then you will be struggling against that authority as the main action of play.
