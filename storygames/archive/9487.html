ANNOUNCING THE BULLY PULPIT GAMES AEROMACHINE CHALLENGE

Take a look at this illustration and then write a game about it. Your game must:\n
    \n
  • Incorporate the content of the illustration in ways that are clever and impact play.
    • \n
  • Be playable and fun.
    • \n
  • Be no longer than 1200 words. Layout is not a consideration but clarity is.
    • \n
  • Be submitted as a text file or .pdf to jason at bullypulpitgames.com...
    • \n
  • \u2026by Monday, 15 June, at 0800 EST.
\nLook carefully \u2013 the illustration conveys an extraordinary amount of information about the subjects and their kick-ass world. Ignore or discard this at your peril! You can easily extrapolate tons of information, starting with the fact that their aeromachine is obviously designed never to land. The judges will smile on entries that use the implied characters, objects, and situation to their fullest.

Likely questions:

Can I use the illustration as an inspirational starting point for a game about the battle of Tarawa?
Sure. The more elements of it that you meaningfully include, the more likely you are to win, though. Don\u2019t throw away the coolness you are given.

Can my game be a scenario for another game?
Make something that is focused on your entry and what it is about. If that means you hack another game, that\u2019s fine, although the judges are biased toward elegance and innovation.

1200 words are so very few. My heart is breaking at the thought of it.
It was enough for John Harper, who used 1100 to write The Mustang. So it\u2019s good enough for you! You've got two weekends to ruthlessly edit.

I intend to submit my game five minutes late, is that OK?
Absolutely, but it won\u2019t be read or judged.

If I win, what will I get?
Something strange and wonderful, no doubt, as well as bragging rights and a fun game.

Can I complain about the format of the contest or explain to you at length why I am not interested in it and won't enter?
Definitely! That's what it's all about.

Go! Feel free to throw down the gauntlet in this thread.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarYou can easily extrapolate tons of information, starting with the fact that their aeromachine is obviously designednever to land.
    Some stuff I notice, upon perusal:
    * It's seemingly Victorian, with the very body-hiding attire; and YET...
    * The lady is driving; and the gentleman is watching her, not the way ahead: is he teaching her to drive, or learning how?
    * The lady is very poised: this is not an awe-inspiring situation for her, almost routine (hell, she looks half asleep).
    * What the hell is attached to the block-and-tackled line descending out of the frame?! Or are they simply anchor lines trailing in the breeze...?
    * Judging by the forward-facing lamp, they don't mind flying at night (in fact, it's near dawn or dusk, judging by the color and angle of the light on the clouds)...
    * ...and yet they seem to be moving along at a good clip (or into the wind). But not SUCH a fast pace that they need a windshield.
    * For that matter, they aren't so high that they need a sealed cockpit of oxygen... yet they seem to be above the level of cumulus clouds (at least 6,500 - 8,000 feet and probably far higher, to be above them)! Are these people impervious to thin air (OR maybe that's why the delicate lady is so sleepy looking--oxygen deprivation)?
    * Incongruous-seeming components: LOTS of brass (on a heavier-than-air craft without much wingspan of which to speak?! Maybe there's something funky going on above the frame, with its upper wing area?), but a wooden barrel for what appears to be the fuel tank... and remarkably fine lines for the fuselage (no sign that it's cloth over ribbing; is it plastic? carved from wood?).
    * The flag could be "nautical" or it could be national. If the latter, then it's somehow relevant who's from where (wartime?). If the former, then what does this flag signify to observers? (Oo! Bravo = carrying dangerous cargo! THAT's what's at the end of that bottom tether!) And, regardless, it implies that craft like these get within sight of either each other or of land-bound folks who need to be inform of... something... upon approach.
    * Looking again at the lamp: why does it have two levers on it? Perhaps one is for unfiltered light and one is for red light... which doesn't spoil night vision? Or maybe a semaphore shutter, for sending Morse Code messages? Whatever it's use, it's got either a small fuel reserve (if it's the brass bottle to which it's mounted) or an unknown power source (in the nose?)
    * Count the (visible) controls in/near the cockpit: yaw (steering wheel), pitch (lever 1), throttle (lever 2, ratcheted). This craft doesn't roll, if that's all there is.
    * And all those loose lines?! (Called "sheets" in sailing.) That would be a serious danger on a normal sailing vessel ("three sheets to the wind" is NOT a complement!) and downright murderous on an air vessel; but it seems commonplace in this craft's design.

    ...I may be missing a few other details, but I'd start with answering those questions, to one degree or another.

    \n Some other, minor stuff I'm noticing upon a second close look:
    * There's no insignia or rank or uniform on the people (MAYBE that spiral and stripes on her cuff is insignia, actually), nor is there and numbers or markings on the craft (other than the flag): this is a civilian craft, akin to a car more than even a boat (our boats--even jetskis!--have registration numbers). That's significant, I feel, because, hey, we don't have common civilian aircraft NOW. So their wealthy (hard to judge by the clothing) or... or there's a LOT of call for air flight. Something particularly dangerous about traveling over land or sea, maybe?
    * Huh. And now that it occurs to me, and assuming there's no way to see the rear of the fuselage through all the engines and pulleys and shit... this thing has no NAME. Nope, nuthin' special to see here, about this aeromachine, folks; it's just another red-and-brass model....
    * Something about that brass gauge (?) on the fore rail is bothering me.... Hmm... there's a lot of leg room, and engines are in the rear, which implies plenty of dashboard area... yet this ginormous gauge sits outside of the cockpit, in the wind and weather?
    * I might not know what to look for, but where's its airspeed probe? Do they just guess? Maybe that big brass gauge is part of it?
    * Canards are not exactly an alpha-level design, for an aircraft, especially starting from first principles (i.e. birds and bats). Then again, they were good enough for Orville and Wilbur....

    \nROMANCE IN THE AIR
    Bill White

    Choosing \u201cpre-generated\u201d characters, Monologues, Money, reputation or love, need, pointed at another player\u2019s character \u2013 a very solid set-up. Rife with possibility. I worry that the \u201cpointing at\u201d bit isn\u2019t structured enough \u2013 one guy with a colorful character might get everyone pointed at him, which I think is mechanically problematic and at a minimum not interesting in endgame.

    Fifth Business, GM-like scene-setting powers, positioning characters in the scene, elegantly handled.

    The reactant\u2019s choice for how to approach a scene is obvious \u2013 if the actant is vicious, be vicious. If the actant is virtuous, be vicious. There is no circumstance where acting virtuously makes sense numerically. I guess this means that the actant would have to angle to push the narrative in a direction where the reactant being vicious is unpleasant or unthinkable.

    I think scores would be likely to tie and a mechanism for adjudicating this is indicated.

    Bonus points for using the word recherch\xe9.

    I wish the coming war were more than a footnote!

    \nFLYING AWAY
    Sean Levethal

    This is a structured freeform game that takes place, almost exclusively, in the mind of a single character. That\u2019s cool! It\u2019s melancholy as hell.

    1823? Come on, the hats alone ought to tell you it isn\u2019t 1823.

    I like the two player set-up, and the default protagonist. I like the idea that the other player is really a part of her as well, as her memories.

    The use of the word \u201cpremise\u201d is a little confusing, but that\u2019s probably my problem. It reads more like \u201csituation\u201d or \u201cvignette\u201d to me.

    The dice rolled are identical for both participants, with no possibility of altering the scheme. The only reason you wouldn\u2019t just flip a coin at the end is that the individual scene sub-total controls narrational authority. This all seems a little inelegant to me.

    Why are sideline scenes optional? In terms of \u201cpremise\u201d they are really juicy and interesting.

    \nFAREWELL, MY YOUTH
    Nathan Russell

    Charmingly focused and stuffed with nice prop bits. I especially like the plot clock, which ought to keep things moving briskly.

    Barrack = support? It\u2019s a new use of the word to me.

    This game reads like parlor narration to me \u2013 not necessarily a bad thing, but the roleplaying aspects really feel optional. It\u2019s procedurally complex and I can\u2019t tell if the die and token handling would hang together or not. It feels like it would.

    I like that it is essentially a zero-sum game, that only two will survive, and at some point as a player you may be thrust into the roll of tragic kingmaker. I think that is the game\u2019s core and killer app and would like to see it revolve more tightly around difficult choices players can sink their teeth into. It feels like that only happens at all at the last moment.

    \nDREAMS OF A FLYING LADY
    Giulia Barbano and Luca Ricci

    Subtitle: \u201cA tale of adventure, emancipation, and humanity\u201d, which accurately sums up the design intention of the game.

    \nBEING A ROLE-PLAYING GAME ON THE TOPIC OF THE HIGH-FLYING ADVENTURES OF BEATRICE HENRIETTA BRISTOL-SMYTHE, DBE, DARING AVIATRIX AND ACCOMPLISHED EXPLORATRIX, AND HER GENTLEMAN COMPANION, WHO FOR A MODEST FEE, ACCOMPANIES BEATRICE HENRIETTA BRISTOL-SMYTHE, DBE, WHEN THE OCCASION WARRANTS HER AN ESCORT.
    Ben Lehman

    Hits the late Victorian supercilious tone just right. Fun to read. I like the fact that the game\u2019s text flows nicely as a unified piece of writing.

    I can easily see this game being played in multiple ways \u2013 to lampoon class and status with deadly accuracy, to skewer contemporary mores and beliefs through a pseudo-Victorian lens, or straight up as weird, humorous fiction. Either way she\u2019s always right about everything, and unflappable, and the humor flows from this. I suspect the more the Gentleman is mauled the funnier the dichotomy becomes, particularly at endgame where they are perhaps married.

    Perhaps best played in an utterly mannered way, with each participant rigidly hewing to the parts given them in the rules. Being a native would be fun, because you have unlimited authority to engineer uncomfortable and improbable situations.

    \n Upon reflection, I think the prisoner's dilemma thing in "Romance in the Air" would work better if it there were multiple iterations between actant and reactant on each plot point, with actant and reactant alternating first move.       \n >>I especially like the plot clock, which ought to keep things moving briskly.
      \n
      \nThe intention of the plot clock was certainly to keep the story moving forward and to have a defined end point. I am not sure if it is the right "size" or length - only play will tell. It will advance everytime a new Device is put in play and then potentially move forward if a tie is rolled. It is meant to put pressure on players as the game draws to a close - they must move to create friendships, gain devices or both in order to put themselves in the best position for escape.
      \n
      \n>> Barrack = support? It\u2019s a new use of the word to me.
      \n
      \nIt is true. We Antipodeans "barrack" for our teams all the time. It is a common question to ask "Who are you barracking for?" when watching sporting events.
      \n
      \n>>This game reads like parlor narration to me \u2013 not necessarily a bad thing, but the roleplaying aspects really feel optional. It\u2019s procedurally complex and I can\u2019t tell if the die and token handling would hang together or not. It feels like it would.
      \n
      \nThe die mechanic is very much inspired by Zombie Cinema. It is quite intuitive once you begin play, but deserves more explanation in Farewell, My Youth. Whether the roleplaying aspects are optional - they might be and probably depend on a groups style of play. I know my group would get into their characters and use that to inform who they support or barrack for. However, this needs to be better identified in the text. After I had finished I thought that at the start of the game players could make a statement about their character, as well as about the cloudship, but I didn't have the wordcount!
      \n
      \n>>I like that it is essentially a zero-sum game, that only two will survive, and at some point as a player you may be thrust into the roll of tragic kingmaker. I think that is the game\u2019s core and killer app and would like to see it revolve more tightly around difficult choices players can sink their teeth into. It feels like that only happens at all at the last moment.
      \n
      \nI agree, the pressure to make those tough decisions probably really only builds at the very end. The Flashback scenes where you choose who to sit next to (more on that in a moment) are meant to create a degree of tension, as players seek to put themselves near someone they think is going to "win" (thus giving themselves a chance to also escape). The idea then is that there is two ways to escape - to have the most devices, or help someone get the most devices (by supporting or barracking) AND be sitting next to them at the end of the game.
      \n
      \nIn an "earlier" (we are talking a matter of hours here!) version of the game
      \n
      \n>>I don't think I understand the purpose behind the seat-swapping thing, but it's probably something obvious.
      \n
      \nTo be honest, I don't think I would play with the actual seat swapping - I would do it virtually. I didn't have time or space to put in my "alternative" which is simply six circles around the plot clock and a token representing each character. The circles represent the "seats" and that way no-one has to actually get up and move. The reason for the seat swapping, however, is to show who you are currently trying to ingratiate yourself with. It isn't perfect (ideally, you would pick who sits next to you, not who you sit next to - if that makes sense). This is meant to create some drama beyond the grabbing of devices. You can frame a Flashback about a character/player and the two people on either side of that player will realise that they could be "swapped out". If they are sitting next to the current "winner" (the player with the most devices), they will very likely object! It is about positioning yourself for the end game - as well as sitting next to the winner, you still want to have enough people willing to vote for you in the final roll.
