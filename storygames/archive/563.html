    My LiveJournal is a mix of general game design stuff and specific game ideas (like for my games Verge and Pleiades). I think the main thing that sets my blog apart from others is that it is read by a large number of non-Forge game designers. If I post a rant about my D&D experiences or about immersion, I get very interesting responses from folks who don't frequent the Forge but may be published freelancers or pro game writers. My friends list is quite extensive and a large majority of the names there are other game designers. Poke at it (and especially the syndicated feeds) for pointers to LJ-accessible game design blogs.     I don't update very often. I'm a synthesizer of design philosophy rather than a creator of it.

  • J_Walton
    April 2006
    To answer T_G:

    I didn't know Mo and Brand at all before I started bumping into them on Story Games. So, they're a mixture of "these awesome people I should have been aware of before" and the New, New Thing in my blog reader. Mo's posts on Sin Aesthetics have been scorching hot. They're worthwhile to go back and re-read every one in a while, because she's dropping lots of crazy stuff about immersion and player interaction in high density, so you may not necessarily absorb it all on a first read. Both Brand and Mo are incredibly articulate and good at describing how they feel about situation X, which is invaluable when talking about the actual experience of roleplaying.

    Terra Nova is a blog about MMORGs. I think it's fascinating and invaluable to keep up with the kinds of developments happening in online virtual worlds and computer-based roleplaying. Terra Nova specifically focuses on issues of portraying a character, creating narrative, and other issues that are extremely relevant to what we doing in PNP roleplaying. Plus the guy who writes it is also very intelligent and articulate, which are the two traits you really want in a blog or podcast (hence, Paul Tevis being awesome).

    Elliot Wilen's blog is really just a place for him to comment, intelligently and articulately, on all the other roleplaying blogs out there. He rarely just posts his own thoughts, but is usually responding to someone else and taking their ideas in a new direction. I love reading his stuff because it's usually an exploration and expansion of stuff I've already read in other parts of the blog-o-sphere. It's like Take 2: cold pizza. And it usually tastes just as good the second time around. Different, but good.
