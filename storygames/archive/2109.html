    All hooks there guys. No "grabs." How you Awesomeofie the traditional bar scene is to actually make it pertinent to the characters in question. Let's say we've got one character who has defined relationships to a lover, and a political ally. You simply have them both show up...together. Another character is a holy warrior and there are some folks in the corner bad-mouthing his diety. But it's a holy day of peace! Another character is defined as quite greedy, but also compassionate about the poor. He finds a pouch dropped by a merchant, and two orphans who have snuck into the bar trying to find something to eat. Another character is a wizard who loves discovering new spells, but also has a bad drug addiction. He's got ten sheckels to spend, and that's what both his dealer wants, and the guy in the corner wants for his musty old book.

    Now, here's the fun part, mix them up. The Lover is the one badmouthing the diety. The political ally is the one who dropped the purse. The "orphans" turn out to belong to the drug dealer. And the guy with the musty old book is the brother of the lover.

    Don't use the above example! It's made for hypothetical characters. Take the characters you have, and make up an example that works off of what's important to the player about those characters.

    The reason the orc tracks to the dungeon is such a groaner is not because it's cliche. All of my examples are cliche, too. Cliche is fine. It's a groaner because it's the same old non-interesting hook that was used last time. Instead grab the players by the throat with situations that immediately involve the stuff that makes the character interesting to the player. So, actually, if the characters are all orc warriors, then perhaps orc tracks to the dungeon is precisely what's called for.

    It all depends on the characters. Any situation made up without reference to the chracters in question is simply not as awesome as one that is.

    Mike

    Tony, not to pick on you, but my response to this set up in my head would be like:

    "Eh. Neat color. But what does it have to do with my character? Maybe I'll just have my guy slip out the back...but then there's no scenario to play through...guess I'll just have to play along and have my character ask somebody about what the Staff of Ra is."

    See you're not leaving any real choices for the player here. Unless, of course, the characters have allegiances to both of these gangs or members of it or somthing.

    Here's what I'm saying: plot is dead easy to create. It's like some sort of comedy sportz routine: X comes into a bar and has Y problem. X says, can you help me with it? Filling in the blanks takes very little effort. But just isn't engaging until there's some real input for the player to make.

    If, in fact, the bar set up exists to get characters to something more interesting, do it in Media Res:

    "After a fight in a tavern in which the Deathskulls and Knifebrains had it out over something called the Staff of Ra, you all decided to check out a place called the Temple of Aremis. So now you stand before it considering breaking into it, but one of you notes that there's a symbol on the door that indicates that it's associated with the death cult you guys are involved with. What do you do?"

    Basicall skip any scene where asking "What do you do?" only has one viable answer. Even if there's lots of ways to implement it. Sure, in your scene one could approach getting the information by violence, or by trickery, or by persuasion. That's got a modicum of interest as a decision, especially if not established previously for these characters. You might get away with it as an establishing scene this way. But better if there are actually two or more interesting directions that the players can choose to go entirely.

    It's akin to those linear tournament dungeons that only have corridors from encounters numbered 1 to 20. Yeah, the GM asks, "Where do you go now?" but there's really only one answer. Answering it is perfunctory, and not really interesting. The interesting part is what happens in the next room.

    Always be framing to the places where the players have the ability to affect the play. Simple as that (not that it's easy to do, just that it's not hard to understand).

    Mike
