\n Didn't want to derail the Mind of Margaret discussion, so I figured I'd ask this in a new thread:

In what other games do all of the players control the same character?

I'd love to run a GMed campaign where all of the non-GM players share a single character, debating their actions a la Mind of Margaret (or perhaps acting via some sort of arrived-at consensus).

The only other 'everyone-plays-the-same-character' game I've encountered is Everyone is John, which isn't really what I'm looking for.

Do y'all have any recommendations?

    \n I wrote a hack of Inspectres many years ago, for emulating TV dramas with a lead character (Doctor Who, House, Blackadder, etc): as well as controlling their own PC, each player could buy control of the lead character by spending resources to do so. The lead character was the only one who could 'solve' the situation the other characters were in; in playtesting this, I often used a glove puppet to show who currently had control of the lead.

    \n@David_Berg Everyone is John is mostly a mental health joke, which is just one reason why I don\u2019t love it. It also happens to be competitive, and I\u2019d much prefer a radically collaborative approach (see below). (Thanks to @AsIf for the link btw!)

    @Hasimir Touched by Evil seems interesting, but I\u2019d prefer a system without turn-taking.

    The game I am envisioning mirrors the experience of sitting next to one or more someones on the couch while playing an adventure videogame like Dark Souls, Zelda, etc. Whoever has the controller is superficially \u2018in charge\u2019 of the character, but chances are you\u2019ll freely debate the best course of action (where to head next, how to build your character, which NPCs you should befriend, etc.). In effect, control of the character is shared, but the protagonist retains their singularity and significance in fictional terms.

    Mind of Margaret comes as close as I\u2019ve seen to successfully proceduralizing the \u2018debate\u2019 portion of that experience, but its design goals are clearly different than those I\u2019ve roughly sketched here.

    \n I've not played Bluebeard's Bride but that definitely seems to fit the description and is amazingly atmospheric from the AP podcasts I've heard.

    Trouble for Hire and Lovecraftesque share a protagonist between the group, but only one person controls them at a time. Lovecraftesque has people rotate who the player, GM and watchers are. I've only played it three player but it's a really fun game. Trouble for Hire looks interesting as a game starring a specific character, foil and antagonists. You all take roles and then those can shift in play. They include the character, the foil, the road, the strangeness, general crowds, the antagonist and the editor. Some roles won't be used in scenes and you use "RPM" to perform certain actions or change role. Once you hit certain amounts spent, episode events take place to drive you through the episode of a kind of weird bounty hunting TV show.

    On another note: Had a thought today: " everyone is John" could be played from the perspective of "the origin of consciousness in the breakdown of the bicameral mind" by Julian jaynes. It's a hypothesis that was first proposed in the 1970's. Speculates that in our recent past (about 3000 years ago) humans typically did not have our self reflective mentality. Instead we experienced executive function as a communication from a dissociated mind. People routinely heard voices that told them what to do when challenged by novel circumstances. Very speculative, but a fun idea that is perfect for the game (you can play the game without having to contextualize the central conceit as enacting a psychotic process!
