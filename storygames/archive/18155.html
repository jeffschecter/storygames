\n So there's this thing I experienced in Burning Wheel's Duel of Wits that bugged me.

Here's what play looks like: I look at my Oratory and other skills, I look over the sheet of Rebuttal and other actions, I guess at what my opponent might do, I pick a sequence of 3 actions, I announce I'm ready, we roleplay a bit and then roll some dice. Example: I choose the Dismiss action, and when it's my turn, I roleplay my character scoffing and turning his back on yours. Then I roll, perform some calculations, and wind up with 4 successes. Your guy subtracts 4 points from his total; if this brings him down to 0, the watching crowd decides he's lost the Duel.

Sounds great, right? And it is great, in many ways. The strategic decisions are meaningful, there's satisfying feedback, and there's all sorts of roleplay prompts (rebuttal! obfuscate!) for players who might want them. Players who are practiced at the system can handle it fairly quickly, focusing more on the mechanics or more on the fiction as they prefer.

However! I found that it interfered with some of what I like about roleplaying.

The way I learned to roleplay was based on taking in fictional stimuli, providing fictional responses, and then going to the dice (or whatever) if the rules told me that was how my response was arbitrated. I'm in character, I see something, I attempt to do something, and then we pause the fiction in that state of suspense (hopefully not for too long!), futzing around with dice, until it is revealed how my attempt turns out. I believe this general style of play (and rules design) is sometimes called "fiction first", for obvious reasons.

In the Duel of Wits, on the other hand, each time I roleplayed my character, it was an act of filling in the fictional blank for an already-made mechanical decision. "I scripted a Dismiss action for mechanical reasons; now I must invent a way to enact that with my character." No matter how colorful my enacting was, to me it felt like working as an actor from a (admittedly loose) script, rather than being caught up in the fiction and responding from within it (as my character).

No surprises there, right? Different players have different tastes, and for me personally, Burning Wheel has some things I really like as well as some things I vaguely dislike.

My question is: can I have my cake and eat it too?

Can I get that fiction-focused experience, where I receive fictional events in-character and instantly respond in-character, while also having social conflicts with meaningful decisions and satisfying feedback?

I'm imagining something like Duel of Wits, in that you can take different actions which have different odds of different outcomes, but where those decisions are made as the character makes them: as instant responses to fictional stimuli.

Example:

NPC: This meeting is a waste of time. We cannot discuss an alliance here. I must get back to my palace before the woeful security endangers my royal person.

PC: If only you had the elite bodyguards we ambassadors travel with. They're the best in the realm! And loyal to whomever we command. Sometimes, we give them to our most valued allies...

GM: That sounds like a Bribe action! Roll your Bribery skill, which we'll resolve with its own unique procedures.

The key here is that there's no separation between the player's thought "Bribery might work!" and the character's thought "bribery might work!" This is enabled by the fact that the player can't call for a roll by saying "I try to Bribe him"; only the relevant fiction triggers the GM to say "roll to see how that goes!"

Just a thought. Perhaps this would only result in a total loss of strategic decisions ("Oh, gee, bribing him seemed natural, but my character sucks at Bribery") or in short-circuited responses ("Security concerns, eh? Let me check my character sheet to see if I'm good at Bribery").

What do you think? Any other ideas?

    \n Hmm. I think perhaps I've derailed my own thread.

    These tips on nudging Duels of Wits toward a more fiction-focused experience are great, and I will totally try them next time I play, but that's not really what I initially intended to talk about here.

    My intent was that "receive fictional events in-character and instantly respond in-character" would serve as one hard criteria. Either you do or you don't, and "don't" is not an acceptable option, no matter how gracefully you can receive system events and incorporate them into character play.

    The other criteria was less specific: meaningful decisions, coherent feedback, good fodder for action. Duel of Wits was just an example of one subsystem that does this well.

    I think, historically, games that have prioritized (or just habitually adopted) "apprehend and respond in-character" have tended to rely on group-specific systems (usually "good GMs") to provide (a) reliable info for meaningful decisions, (b) the right narration for coherent feedback, and (c) various embellishments and improv for continuing action fodder. We're all probably familiar with GMs who do this well, and others who do it less well.

    What I think would be really cool would be a way to take that burden off the GM (and/or the group) and design a system that contributes in a way similar to the Duel of Wits, without at all impinging on the old-school "fictionally react to this fictional event!" m.o.

    Perhaps that sounds insane. The best I can say is that "through my character's eyes" is my favorite socket through which to experience RPG fiction, and having to switch to another POV (even for a millisecond!) every time important stuff goes down is simply a let-down. Good after-the-fact fictional transcript; poor in-the-moment experience.

    Is my desired combo possible? Thoughts on that in the next post...

    \n One criticism of traditional task resolution is that, though it will resolve an immediate pass/fail, it may utterly fail to resolve the matter of interest at hand, leaving the GM (or someone) to just decide all the important stuff. I'd say most modern conflict resolution does the opposite: it will resolve the matter of interest at hand, but it may utterly fail to resolve what specific events occurred. Which isn't a problem... unless you're intent on observing and reacting to those events as they happen!

    So any sort of reconciliation, I think, would have to sort this out.

    Brainstorm:

    - GM has an Interference budget. GM can only interpose so many hindering events between a completed Task and a player's Intent per session. The size of the budget is not perfectly known, and the GM needn't spend all of it. The point here is to assure the player that the GM can't repeatedly say "Okay, you complete that action, but before you can get what you wanted, something else happens..." If the GM does that at all, the player at least knows that a budget is being exhausted, making the road ahead easier. It might also be necessary to qualify Interference as "interfering with the Intent and requiring other Tasks, but never rendering the Intent unreachable by any Task".

    - Player announces Intent. Narration continues up until character attempts a minute Task which will bring them closer to achieving their Intent. Task failed? See below. Task succeeded? That directly means the character is closer to their Intent. Narration continues. If the GM chooses not to spend from the Interference budget, narration will lead to completed Intent with no further rolls.

    - Dramatic option: whenever completion of an action would result in immediate achievement of an Intent (character's Intent is to escape from prison, and they are in the process of descending the outer wall undetected), GM can cause Interference at reduced cost (or for free?).

    - Task failed: well, that didn't work; now you'll have to try your second-best option, which might have lower odds of success, plus deal with any obvious consequences of the failure. Perhaps failures help the GM's Interference budget: maybe a failure earns them a point, or maybe it's cheaper to Interfere if you can tie the Interference into the failure?

    - There would have to be rules about fast-forwarding through non-pressured, time-insensitive tasks instead of rolling for them. The rules should never force us to cover parts of the fiction we otherwise wouldn't.

    Could something like this act as a foundation for play that has both game-like strategy and coherence and fictional reactions to fiction?

    Other ideas welcome!
