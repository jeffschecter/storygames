    I really don't understand the 'bad WotC' thing, since after all the word 'tap' had zero association with turning a card sideways prior to Magic -- as far as I'm aware, M:tG is the only reason such a term would occur to anybody trying to describe that action. 'Rotate' and 'turn' are much closer to describing the action, whereas 'tap' is of course referencing the whole 'drain/use up mana' thing. Telling a player who hasn't played Magic to 'tap' their card would probably result in curious looks, or the player tapping their finger on the surface of the card.

    Nitpicking aside, any specialized verb should probably relate to the game, as has been pointed out -- Ben's suggestions seem the best for something similar to the M:tG 'spending resources' approach. What state is being indicated? For describing the mechanic, 'rotate 90 degrees' or 'turn to one side' seems about as clear as it gets. It also occurs to me that you only really need a specialized verb if players are doing this so often that it needs to be constantly referenced -- or if the action is being referenced on another card, but even there it seems like a basic icon would be the best bet (the usual card icon next to a circular arrow.)

    I think this needs some investigation, potentially. I had thought that, in fact, what WotC had done was to Patent tapping - actually patented CCG "Gameplay", with tapping being one element of that. That is, it's not so much that it's copywrited, or trademarked, but that they owned the "technology"of turning a card to the side and such. In other words, that you can't put that into a game at all, without theoretically violating the patent. Presumably games that have this sort of mechanic only do so because they came out prior to WotC getting the patent, or are paying WotC for the right to use "their" technology.

    Now, that said, I'd also heard that the patent was unenforcable to the extent that if you used a different term than "tap" that you could get away with it. And, perhaps, that the entire patent wasn't valid for some reason.

    But I'm neither well versed on this, nor a lawyer. I merely suggest some further research. Check out this thread on RPG.net: http://forum.rpg.net/showthread.php?t=137059

    Which includes this link (see the section labeled "Patent"): http://www.wordiq.com/definition/Trading_card_game

    All stuff to be aware of if you're going to be making a card game like this.

    WotC have a patent that covers various mechanics related to CCGs. I'm not a lawyer, much less a patent lawyer, so I don't know how much you have to overlap before you've got a problem.

    I do know that what you call it doesn't matter in terms of whether you're infringing the patent or not.

    As far as I know, they've never used the patent, even when in litigation with other CCG companies, and there are certainly CCGs out there that use the "rotate a card ninety degrees" mechanic who are not licensing the patent, and this doesn't appear to bother WotC.

    My personal opinion is that the patent in question probably wouldn't hold up in court (maybe. From talking with somebody who was studying patent law, I've learned that the legal definition of 'obvious' has very little relation to anything normal people mean by the word), but patent lawsuits are very expensive, so it's unlikely that anybody will ever find out. If WotC throw lawyers at you, you simply won't be able to afford to fight it. (On the other hand, they can hardly expect to cover their litigation costs.)

    The trademark and the patent are two different things - here's the original patent (5,662,332):
    From James Garfield's patent for Magic: The Gathering

    3. A method of playing card games, the method being suitable for card games having rules of play and multiple copies of a plurality of cards, the rules of play including instructions on executing turns, the predetermined number of cards in the players library and hand of cards, and the objective of the game, the method comprising the steps of:

    each player obtaining a pool of cards from the multiple copies of the plurality of cards by purchasing or trading with others or playing a card game with others for ante;

    each player constructing a library of a predetermined number of cards by examining and selecting cards from the player's pool of cards;

    each player obtaining a hand of a predetermined number of cards from the player's library of cards by first shuffling the player's library of cards and randomly drawing the hand of cards therefrom; and

    each player executing a turn until the game concludes in accordance with the rules of play, said step of executing a turn includes placing one or more cards on a playing surface for other players to see the playing face on the one or more cards and designating one or more of the cards on the playing surface for entry into play.

    4. The method of claim 3, wherein said step of designating one or more of the cards comprises rotating the one or more cards on the playing surface from an original orientation to a second orientation.

    5. The method of claim 3, wherein said second orientation is 90 degrees from the first orientation.

    6. The method of claim 3, wherein said step of executing a turn comprises the initial step of rotating the player's cards previously designated in a prior turn from the second orientation to the original orientation.
    There it is - turning a card 90Âº in a CCG is patented by WotC.

    CCGs as a concept (a deck of cards constructed from a pool of cards of varying rarity) is covered under this patent. I think in the early days they made various rumbling noises against other CCGs, but I don't believe anything ever went to court. I don't think it would have been very defensible, and by now it'd be impossible to enforce.

    As for 'tap' - I've searched the trademark database ... there are 262 records for Wizards of the Coast, with live or dead trademarks. 'Tap' is not among them. It's not listed as a trademark on their website, and the presence of the word in the patent doesn't cut it for being a trademark. Of course, testing that in a court of law is, as always, the real acid test. But I wouldn't sweat it, personally.

    If you got to the site: www.uspto.gov, and put in (wizards)[OW] AND (coast)[OW], you'll see all of their trademarks - and there are plenty.
