Brainstorming Session: Web 2.0 Apps for Games and Gaming.

Kuma
July 2006 edited July 2006 in The Best of Story Games
So, I have all this web space and a couple of domains. Ludolab was a crapout because it was (eventually) a paying enterprise. Instead, I've been looking to noodle out something new for the Ludolab.org domain that I still own. I've been doing a lot of reading and thinking about Ye Newe Web 2.0 (if you're unfamiliar, think Flickr, 9rules, Standpoint and other 'social web' applications), and wondering exactly what sorts of tools could be crafted for gaming and story-gaming in particular.

My (admitedly short) list is:

* Online playtest/dev environment, with whiteboards, database front-ends for character templates and whatnot.
* Collaborative world/gamebuilding.

I also own the domain elseworld.org, which I was planning to use to create an XML schema for world/situation building. But really, I think I'm stuck in some wheel ruts in my thinking (OK - I *know* I'm in a rut), and I'd like to get other folks' ideas churning in the mix to see what can come out of it.

    I have grand designs for a php-based modification of mediaWiki to make it into an online random generation engine. Anyone (just registered folks?) could go in & edit random tables (instead of articles), then save them off for later use. From the main page, you'd have a generation string box that would let you reference any saved table or combination of tables and put their results in the context of a paragraph or whatever. Like, maybe you're playing Shab-al-Hiri Roach and you need a quick professor, so you type in

    Name: [[CreepyCampus.MaleFirst]] [[CreepyCampus.Surname]]
    Subject: [[AcademicSubjects.Main]]
    Languages:[[CreepyCampus.DeadLanguages]], [[CreepyCampus.DeadLanguages]], [[ModernLanguages.Main]]
    Quirky Habit: [[CharacterFillers.Quirks]]

    and hit the Generate button, and Ajax gives you your little dossier in the bottom half of the page while keeping the scripting box open for you to modify whatever needs changing for you to get the results you want.

      Heh. Ok, to de-nerd a little, 37 signals is a company that makes a number of really clean, simple, and powerful web applications, of whihc backpack is one. They offer the capability to, for example, easily create a project plan online, and share it with the rest of your team. Similarly, it can be useful for shared writing projects, passing around files and other collaborative efforts. Campfire is their "business chat" offering, and it offers a lot of collaboartive services on top of pure chat, and while I think it's interesting, I dont' find it as compelling as their other offerings.

      They have also made a name for themselves on that whole "Web 2.0 Thing". Their design principles (rapid prototyping, minimalist design) are one of the influences on the direction web 2.0 is going (as well as one of its poster children), and they published an e-book which goes into their process. It's a good, if a bit overly self-congratulatory, book, but they charge way more than they should for it. It's a testament to their popularity that it has sold quite well.

      Anyway, they fill a slightly different niche than something like a shared whiteboard would. While you might want a whiteboard for someone you're sitting down and hashing somethign out in realtime with, you'd want backpack more for something that does not require that realtime component. Put another way, it's an upgrade from using email to pass a file (or files) back and forth, both because it puts it up online in a form you can work on, and it also provides tools (planning, to do lists, etc) that help witht he process.

      It's fun to experiment with, and things like it and Writely and a lot of the more user-friendly Wiki's are the first signs of a big change heading this way.

      -Rob D.

      Full suite of stuff here.

      The prototyping is an interesting confluence of programming and web design, which is to say, they design the front end first, if only as pure HTML, and build down from it. While they hardly invented this approach, they've defended it well, and they've emphasized it's combined strengths. Historically you would end up with weird hybrids, since pure developers usually built from the code up, and web developers understood the interface better than the systems, resulting in things like Cold Fusion to bridge the gap. 37 signals puts forward that there should be no gap, which is a nice, though maybe not practical, idea.

      It works for them though. They're reportedly very responsive to user input, and keep their products updated quite robustly.

      Hi

      Maybe it's not the most spectacular way to apply Web2.0 to RPG, but I'd like to show you my idea of play RPG via Wiki.

      I first just wanted to use wiki to store my campaign information, but as I got Quest rules, which claims to copy-and-edit the last email you get instead of just reply it... I got the idea of using these shared pages of Wiki to be edited by each player instead of send all these emails.

      The main advantaje of this idea is that you can get the whole history of every page's modifications. Being one page the main turn structure. Just using the Quest rules for email formatting from www.foreverpeoplerpg.com

      So, at my online-RPG at http://steamwolf.wikidot.com I don't share the world building (although I'll add a forum to share ideas) but just every turn writing.
      1- DM creates a new page for the gaming turn
      2- DM sends an email to players and lurkers with VERY BASIC information about the started turn
      3- Player 1 edits this page with his character's actions
      4- Player 1 sends an email to GM, players and lurkes with basic description of his actions.
      5- Player 2 edits this page with his character's actions
      6- Player 2 sends an email to GM, players and lurkes with basic description of his actions.
      ...
      #- GM writes the last changes to the text to end the turn
      #+1- DM sends an email to players and lurkers with VERY BASIC information about the ending turn

      If I had some authomatic tool to send these emails at every page edited I'd avoid steps 2,4, 6 and "#+1".

      And that's it. You'd see an example here if you want at my PBEM site:
      http://steamwolf.wikidot.com/acts-in-action

      I hope you'll like it and find it interesting.
