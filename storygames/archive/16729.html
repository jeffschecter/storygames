    \n What system are you working with Dionysus? Not knowing that, I'd suggest hooking in all the Player's Flags (character choice / skills / talents / traits / gear etc...) as PC's motivations or goals to your re-incorporated story elements: The mysterious power, the mysterious soldiers and competing groups. Make sure you have a motivation for your NPC's that antagonises the PC's interests. Aggressively scene the next session so as the players start with ACTION! a conflict or choice that leads in all sorts of exciting directions. If you can, allow for player authoring into the direction of these 'conflicts' and allow the system to adjudicate the outcome, not GM fiat. Always complicate on a failure instead of saying no.

    As for your question on using AW fronts and threats, of course it works on this scale! Just follow the principles and agenda, even the moves, they tend to be a good primer for most story games style 'play to see what happens'. Hope that helps?

    \n Noofy, he's explicitly not "playing to see what happens". That's the opposite of what the players have asked for.

    Dionysus, the only way this style of play works, in my experience, is if you've got a story you're really excited to tell the players, and they're excited to hear it. You turn up with a session's worth of material for them to play through, they turn up prepared to play through that stuff, rise to the challenges, act out their roles, and beat the bad guys. Are you on board for that?

    You don't neccesarily have to have a full story arc planned out ahead of time, you just need to be working with material you like, that's easy to come up with ideas for, and that you're comfortable improvising a little. For me, I can only do it with fantasy, because that's what my imagination likes. I don't think fronts and threats are the right approach here, except in as much as they give you ideas for surprises to throw at the players.

    My generic template for this kind of session (to play through in two or three hours) is three main challenges (set piece fights, social showdowns, sneaking/infiltration scenes) glued together with some clear clues and some unsubtle connections. Think of three cool images, and work out how to make them happen in the game.

    Then you just make up backstory and metaplot to explain it all, and it comes across as complex and compelling storytelling.

    The drawback to this kind of play is that it's exhausting to prep for, and it can be unsatisfying to play long-term, since it's not really player-driven at all.

    \n Doesn't Marvel Heroic do something lile that? Basically one-issue plots you play through. I haven't had the chance to read it through yet.

    Otherwise, maybe start with a villain and their plan, then the necessary steps to stop tem and then gradually reveal it in play and walk the players through the challenges they must pass to save the day. Create stuff you will be excited to show in play, give the players opportunities to showcase their characters and don't be afraid to mercilessly edit scenes.

    In AW parlance, when you're looking for something to say, your principles/moves should be something like.:
    *say what the story demands
    *celebrate the characters
    *reveal clues to the next step
    *tropes before randomness dice
    *more ninjas

    \n When I've run this kind of game in the past (Aberrant was my game, oh boy), at the end of each session I would think about what happened in that session that surprised and delighted me, and then build out "stuff to do" from that. So, like, if one of the characters got really into how the shapeshifter tried to give him a bad name, then I would build the shapeshifter's agenda out in more detail for the next session.

    My point is, instead of trying to come up with a huge overarching plot that would stand up to sessions and sessions of play, I would have a core conflict or two, and then "plot up" each session based on what stood out as the cool stuff from the last session, usually by asking myself "how can this be more complicated than it appears."

    It's zooming out and instead of playing to find out what happens right now (which it sounds like you're good at), it's playing to find out what happens next time.

    Does that help at all?

    \n When you need more stuff later (though it sounds like you're getting a lot of ideas from paying attention to your players, which is PERFECT and totally not cheating) look at those character flags you listed (and come up with more as the characters flesh out a bit) and see what sorts of things you can get out of combining 2-3 of them.

    Like Assassin + Face is pretty obvious - a chance for the face to distract someone while the sneak is sneaking and stabbing.

    But some of the other combinations are less obvious and may inspire more ideas.

    But of course, just because you created a challenge with roughly a solution in mind, for god sakes, don't let that be the only solution that works.

    If you present what you think is a guy that should be distracted while the assassin sneaks in and the McGuyver suddenly has an awesome gadget solution (and hasn't been dominating the game by having an awesome gadget solution for every goddamned thing), go with it. The player answer is the right answer. That's not cheating either. That's being a responsive GM.
