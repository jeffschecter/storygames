\n In the thread on whether or not Polaris is broken, there are some random ideas on what is and isn't roleplaying, and what sort of things can't possibly be fun in roleplaying.

Here are some examples of things that can't possibly be fun, taken from random conversations during the past decades:
- Trying to make your character fail
- Having no game master
- Using complex combat systems so that almost all the playing time consists of rolling dice and looking at tables
- Being actively antagonistic and/or competitive as a player
- Playing without dice
- Playing games about tragic stuff like slavery
- Playing without being allowed to move around and gesticulate
- Sitting around in a basement pretending to be orcs and elves
- Playing games without magic or fantastic technology

    \n RPGs were born from playing wargames "wrong."

    I believe the first RPG was developed by David Wesely in 1967 when he wanted to hack the rules to a Napoleonic wargame. The wargame supported 2 players controlling opposing armies and a 3rd person acting as referee. He wanted to include more players, so he invented non-military roles (mayors, bankers, university chancellors) that additional players could control. When these players wanted to fight in a duel, he improvised rules to negotiate the encounter. Later he invented role-playing scenarios that grew even further apart from his original wargames.

    RPGs were born from hacking other kinds of games!

    \n Was David Wesely playing his original Napoleonic wargame rules?

    In my opinion, no. Once you change something, it is not the same thing. That's not political. That's not identity politics. That's not a status game. That's not an attack on hacking. That's not an insult. He created something new.

    Why does that matter?

    I've had people tell me about how awesome a game was, I went out and bought it, tried to play and had a terrible time. Then I go back to the person who recommended the game and find out that they changed a crucial 20% of it with custom hacks that compensated for the weaknesses I experienced. I go back, play with their hacks, and have a great time. If I had known about those changes, I would have saved myself a lot of time and frustration. Not only did they misinform me, but they also are underselling their own amazing contributions.

    Posted By: MatthijsPosted By: TeataineAs I've been saying: Roleplaying is the medium, not the thing you do.
    \nWhat does that mean? Could you give some examples?
    Sure.

    I intend it as both \n
      \n
    • the means of communication, expression, entertainment in the sense of artistic media and media in general
      • \n
    \nCompare with music: "You listen to hip-hop? That's not real music!", "You're into metal? How can anyone enjoy that?!" In other words, people confuse the things they do (beatboxing, guitar solos) with being the true expression of the medium (music, or perhaps more accurately sound). There's also the whole (sub)cultural package that comes with that.

    and\n
      \n
    • in its more scientific-y meaning of substance in which something is immersed and transmitted in - a condition, circumstance
      • \n
    \nThe things we do (have a GM, roll dice, make characters fail, pretend we're elves...) are floating in this medium, but they're not the medium.

    The things we do are secondary and subject to what we're trying to do with (or rather within) the medium. It's blindingly obvious that even when we compartmentalize based on the contents, we're still doing the same thing - more precisely, operating within the same medium - but using it for different ends.
