    \n No, it's true -- there's definitely a high level of player skill still involved with the social engineering component of BW. DOW gives socially strong characters teeth but only in the immediate term. Socially strong players still very much have an advantage in the long term.

    In BW, I'm pretty lax about allowing DOW compromises to influence the outlook or attitude of NPCs. So if I have a thing where, like, a player wants to arrange a trade agreement with an NPC vizier and the vizier wants the player to sign himself and his friends on as members of his guard: the player loses the DOW but gains a compromise, so I'll offer that the vizier is positively disposed to the PC, impressed by his business acumen, and will be happy to hear more about the trade arrangement after they get back from doing a thing with his guards. I'll even make it mechanical and offer a linked-test bonus to the PC's dispo at that time.

    Once I've done that a bit with NPCs, I find that the players feel more-okay with adjusting their characters' attitudes toward one another as compromises.

    \nThere are many different kinds of Persuasion. There are a great many arguments without lies or threats where one side will concede to well-argued points - but only grudgingly and seeing themselves as pushed. For example, many though not all successful salesmen are seen as pushy even by the people who buy from them.

    In Burning Wheel, our Duels of Wits usually came across as this sort of barely accepted argument. The things that gave that impression include (1) I would always keep bringing up points to support my original position, and (2) I would never concede anything until I had won my main point. My character was never rude, but she always pushed for her way - and would only concede others' points basically after she had won her main point (i.e. final compromise).
