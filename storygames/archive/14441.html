\n Ok, so I was cruising around RPG.net as I sometimes do, and I found a large thread that was basically why someone thought all story games were terrible (You can't have a plot if you don't plan it first foolish story gamers!), and the poster brought up and argument I've turned around in my head a lot, that doesn't really sit right for me.
\n
\nBasically, that "Story Games" are bad because they can't be fun for a player who doesn't want to take active participation in creating the world and situations. Or fiction period. Or basically wants to engage in the game much. And whenever I see some dumping on the idea this always comes up.
\n
\nAnd I was wondering, what the peeps here thought about that. At the wonderful Fabricated Realities, there were a bunch of people (Candy comes to mind) Who'd never played such games before and ADORED it. Is this sort of thing conditioned by certain playstyles? Do some people just sit around at a table to socialize a little with friends?
\n
\nAnd really, above all, is this such a huge phenomenon it's actually a serious concern that should inform you at all times? I've NEVER encountered someone as opposed tp the sort of thing because they didn't want a creative role in anything? I mean, even people who want to be immersed (whatever that means to them) can't be opposed to their character actions being part of the fiction, right?
\n
\nSorry if this is half baked, I'm just grappling with this concept since it always feels off to me when it comes up.

    \n This isn't about "story games" specifically, but it is a real usability issue: some games handle passive players better than others.

    Some notes on the issue:\n
    • A game that requires everybody to contribute so the game won't implode doesn't handle passive players well. A game that is flexible about how and when everybody contributes is better from this point of view.
    • There is no problem if you don't have passive players in the group. For some people this is a completely unrealistic assumption, while others have never encountered a passive player.
    • Being passive is not necessarily a sign of a creative agenda conflict, but it can be. Sometimes people are just being passive because they like to engage less.
    • Just like a game being unable to handle passive players is not necessarily a problem, being a passive player is necessarily not one either. In Internet discussions some people will say stupid things about certain games because they don't handle passivity well, while others will say stupid things about some gaming groups because they don't understand that there are other reasons for passivity aside from a creative problem.
    \nThis usability issue has been pretty relevant to me lately, as I've been playing old school D&D. That's probably the most passivity-resistant game there is in roleplaying, so I've been able to teach the game in a very effortless manner, and I've been able to accept a very wide range of people into it: anybody can sit in and roll the dice now and then as long as the core of the group keeps the game moving. Over time the passive guys will learn how the game works and join in on contributing, or they won't, and then they'll probably drop out over time. This is easier than the games that you outright can't play if the group includes even one non-hip player, as is often the case when you do an open call for players. Something like Sorcerer, say, will simply not work if there are players who can't contribute. Just the nature of the game.

    Posted By: GrahamA lot depends on the game, I think.
    \nYeah, and there's also a wide spectrum between players who are entirely passive about everything (which is, for some unfathomable reason, what these threads seem to assume is the case) and players who are up for total collaboration on everything. Those all-or-nothing types are the extreme opposite ends of a long, long curve, many standard deviations away from the fat part in the middle where the rest of us live.

    Real people tend to try to do the things they enjoy and avoid the things they don't. In a game, that might mean that some will want to invent details about the setting and incorporate them in a scene, but won't want to determine the results of an investigation; or they want to have conversations with other characters, but don't want to frame the scenes in which those conversations take place; or they want to declare what happens when their characters win battles, but don't want to narrate what happens when their characters win or lose arguments with their families. So many things can be done in a game that it's kind of ridiculous to think that it's at all normal for anyone to love or hate ALL of it: most of us are going to fall in the middle, and given our druthers we'll stick with what we like and hope that someone else likes to do the stuff we don't. As Eero says, if the game is flexible about how and when everyone contributes to the game, it's easier for groups to work around the parts some players don't want to mess around with.

    Really, I'd rather say that players who are not having fun are the ones who kill collaboration-heavy games, because it's closer to the truth. A player who isn't into certain kinds of collaboration isn't a bad person or non-hip or stupid (well, not necessarily), they just aren't into it...and all of us have been that guy at some point. It sucks, but it happens, and on another day or in another game we'll have a better time. Figuring out what we like and dislike doing in games is a critical element to deciding whether we'll stick with this hobby in the first place, I think.

    So saying that a game is passivity-resistant is just another way of saying that it's flexible enough to accommodate a wide range of preferences without wrecking everyone's fun. If there's something that makes you miserable, you don't have to do it and your misery won't wreck everyone else's fun, too. Games that require total, devoted, uncompromising collaboration between everyone to work aren't bad games, they're just games targeted for a very, very narrow audience (i.e, the people who enjoy doing all the things that game requires of them).

    \n I recently started a thread on how too much freedom can kill games. Basically some players are very intimidated by having to make too much up. If they are presented the world and told "Have at it!" They don't know what to do and their creativity dries up. If you describe a room and some people to them and ask "What happens next?" and they have a much easier time of it. D+D does the room description real well and gives definitive rules that show you what to do to what is in the room (basically beat it up and steal stuff).

    If a game allows one or a few players to grand stand and take over the game then passive players can be run over. D+D is VERY vulnerable to this. The dominant player basically tells (their girl friend) when to roll the dice. Yes the passive player is carried along but if my only participation in a game is being asked "What do you do?" once every ten minutes or so - accompanied by a die roll (that I might not understand) - that isn't fun for me.

    In the end though anti-story games threads are just turf pissing matches. D+D players often only play D+D. The RPGA gets whole rooms at Origins and Gen Con to do this. They don't have to rub shoulders with the rest of us. They aren't excluding us - we could play if we wanted to - but we don't. We self segregate.

    Hey! I just noticed the change in the tag line for the site. Now we talk about roll play games rather than "Stabbing them in the hope with our ennui" A good move since I imagine most D+D players found that tag line off putting and archane.

    \n I have to think a lot of what comes across as "passive" isn't exactly that, too. I play with a group with a couple of people who are engaged, creative, active players - right up until they're directly asked to come up with content. As long as they're not thinking about it, great. Put them on the spot, and they freeze up and play safe.

    There's a great fear of creativity in our culture, a sense that it's hard and something only professionals or talented people can do. I think one of the things that RPGs do for a lot of players is give them permission to be creative. For some, that doesn't work anymore as soon as they notice what they're doing.

    \n A complaint about D&D 4E is that you can't just play a vanilla fighter anymore, the common choice of passive players (although this is now different with D&D Essentials).

    In older editions of D&D, you could play a fighter easily (albeit non-optimally) by just saying "I attack" every round. Whenever we played D&D with friends, if someone wanted to join in but didn't want to think too much, they would pay the fighter. But in D&D 4E they had at-wills, encounter, and daily powers to choose from. Additionally, often fighters were now Defenders. With the new focus on teamwork, if you played non-optimally, you didn't just hurt yourself, you hurt everyone. And playing a Defender was often quite tactical. You can't just get by with "I attack", you need to get into the best position to draw agro attacks from opponents and act as a shield for the rest of the PCs. I've heard quite a few players say, D&D 4E should have been called D&D Tactics.

    For me, this made D&D more fun. For more casual players, less so.

    \n As Accounting for Taste said above, most people aren't going to be completely passive, so the trick is to help those kinds of players find areas where they like to be more active while giving them downtime. I've been playing a lot of 5-person Fiasco lately, and one of the benefits of that game with that many people is that you don't have to be on for every scene. In addition, the Fiasco rules explicitly let players say, "I dunno, I'm going to Resolve because I don't know what should happen next." Around our table, that's an invitation for other players to say, "Hm, well, what if we went in this direction?" I've found that, if you take away the blank sheet of paper and give people a seed of a starting idea, often they'll happily run with that seed.

    Posted By: Mark WThere's a great fear of creativity in our culture, a sense that it's hard and something only professionals or talented people can do. I think one of the things that RPGs do for a lot of players is give them permission to be creative.
    \nTwo things:

    This view is common in general... and, I've found, very common among gamers. The whole, "Who are you to want to make stories when it's the shiny special people who should be doing it?" shtick annoys me to no end. They're just people, too.

    Second, even the pros get nervous with performance anxiety. Actors vomit before going on stage, the writer is struck with terrible fear as he sits there looking at the morning's blank page or screen. In other words, let's not get too high-horse on people who get stuck.

    Making things is human. Getting stuck in panic in the act of making is just as human. Accepting these two facts, I have found, has helped me move more swiftly through both making things and getting stuck.

    I have a Graphical Design degree. At college, we were taught that creativity is a "muscle". The more you practice it, the easier and painless it becomes. As soon as you do not let that idea out, that means he stops running, as soon as someone says no he stumbles and the entire cage will come crashing down upon him."

    It works. In our writing classes we would often be told to on subject x for 20 min, and you were not allowed to stop. Not allowed to think about what you were writing. If you went completely dry on ideas you were to wright about how you were dry, how it felt, what lead you to those previous ideas, and so on. It usually meant that you would think of something else in that process and could go back to the subject. As we did more and more of this we discovered it was increasingly easier to "Not stop."

    As for gaming I find that some many players are just afraid to speak up, either out of social akwardness, another player or GM judging their thoughts, or just an expectation that it would be stepping "On someones toes." It took some effort, and I still do not get the entirely desired results, but I have managed to pull most every player out of that mindset.
