Psuedo-Play: where is the shame?

Dave Younce
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
Breakoff from this thread.

So, uh, I do this. I have fun with Psuedo-Play. I have so-called "Lonely Fun." It's by no means the only kind of fun I have with RPGs - I enjoy playing with my group and I'm not dissing that here. But there's this attitude I see here a lot, that there's something fundementally wrong with Psuedo-play, and I'm bothered by it.

I play a mix of so-called 'Story-Games' and 'Traditionals' (in my case, D&D3.5). My life, and the lives of my players, mean we don't get to play as much as we did when we were teenagers, and we're OK with that. Sometimes its only two weeks between games, sometimes it has been months. In that intervening period, why should I be ashamed of spending some time playing around with the system when I find a couple of hours by myself one night? Nothing to show for it, you say? So what? What more would I have to show for it if I watched a movie or played a computer game? What's the stigma, exactly?

When I take the Briggs-Meyers test, I'm always an INTP. "INTP types are quiet, thoughtful individuals who don't mind spending long periods of time on their own working through problems and forming solutions. They are very curious about systems and how things work..." It appeals to me to spend time by myself, and I enjoy imagining elements of play outside of play.

Sometimes, here and in other online communities about RPGs, I see it said that "The only thing that matters is Play", but I don't agree. I think Fun is more vital than Play. I enjoy playing - it's Fun. But if I can also get Fun out of spending an evening rolling up treasure on Abulafia, why shouldn't I?

    I do think that there has been a stigma to the lonely fun. However, I think it has mostly been a self-imposed stigma driven by obsession over offhanded comments, perceived values, and focus on the value of actual play at a table with other people as being not only the primary mode of gaming, but the only mode worth doing.

    I mean, I know I've beaten up on myself in the past for doing endless lonely fun when I could be prepping for some cool game. Or when I don't have a game ongoing and I feel like everything I'm doing is silly and a waste of time. (As opposed to the silly waste of time that is playing "kill the dragon" with other people.) But the truth is that there isn't anyone else making me feel like that -- it's my own issues doing it.

    Of course, there are some textual issues in our discourse that support my issues being bitchy to me. We have, in this community, occasionally ranted about games in which there is more imagining of the cool play that could actually happen than cool play actually happening, or talked about the ways in which you can pretty quickly tell as a designer who is actually playing your game and who is just reading your game or making characters in endless cycles, or how reading and playing are not the same thing. None of these actually attacks lonely fun (or only do so rarely in extreme cases) -- but all of them do put an emphasis on areas of game other than it as being the valuable parts of game, and for people that already feel their lonely fun is dumb, it may advance their shame complex.

    Personally, I got over it a bit ago and now enjoy both group fun and lonely fun. And posting to the forums fun, which is neither game nor lonely fun, but somewhere in the middle.

    Josh is getting close to it there.

    Several times on SG recently I've seen arguments about whether certain ideals that seem to exist are correct. The problem is that the ideals are being fabricated from logical errors.

    Mainly it comes from taking Forge design ideals, or other such principles, and trying to decipher some play ideal from the design ideal or otherwise incorrectly creating conclusions. In this case we see people talking about solo play and such, and they say, essentially, "Forge design ideal says that we have to make games that are good for something other than pseudo-play, so they must be saying that pseudo-play is bad!"

    See the error there? Forge theory simply points out that certain sorts of designs make for games that are historically better for pseudo-play than for actual play. What's bad about this, for the designer, is that the game can't spread by word of mouth, since nobody actually plays with other folks. So the argument actually goes, if you want to make lots of sales, make a game that people will actually play.

    Which doesn't say anything about the value of pseudo-play to the pseudo-player.

    Which does make Dave seem quite a lot like he's projecting here - but we'll assume it's all a misunderstanding.

    The other one that I saw recently (it was Josh, in fact) is that people were thinking that Forge theory said something like "People should always do other things than play RPGs with people who they play RPGs with." What it actually says is that for games where you would not do other things with the people with whom you play, they tend to be bad games."

    Uh, I guess what I'm saying is to be sure that there's something to rail against before you start railing. Otherwise you're going to look like you're projecting. :-)

    I've taken to thinking of this as "solitaire play" since running into those damn hippie Forge ideas. There's a fine tradition of this:
    • Doing fantasy world building for your D&D campaign -- making up history & cultures, cartography, writing stories
    • Generating Traveller star systems, trade systems, etc. Even the chargen lets you tell a little story by yourself, with a protagonist who can die!
    • Gear-heading up a bunch of vehicles/characters for math-heavy point-buy systems like GURPS or Champions
    This is totally cool. If lonely fun is fun for you, rock on and don't let anyone piss on your (one-man) parade! But, it's really a different activity altogether from social table-top play, isn't it? Confusion arises, perhaps because historically you buy the same books and other merch to facilitate both hobbies. Appealing to both activities as though they are the same hobby is a sensible sales strategy. Hey! If you buy these paints/minis/dice/charts/software tools, you get a fun craft you can enjoy by yourself -- AND you can use it as a stepping stone for a game you can play with your friends!

    And that "AND" part can cause trouble. Sure, you can bring elements you've worked up into your solo games, to the same extent that you can bring elements from whatever cool book or comic or movie -- whatever tickles your imagination -- into the game. Stigma shows up when you expect from participants of the social hobby that your investment in the solitaire hobby must count for something in your social game, even if it's getting in the way of the other participants' fun.

    I mean, nobody writes fanfic with a real expectation the producers of their favorite show will incorporate it into future series. They write fanfic because they enjoy writing fanfic, maybe sharing it with other fans on its own merits.

    All this "Forge game design" stuff concerns itself with the matter of the social activity, with the understanding the solitaire activity is Some Other Thing Entirely. Because there's way too much Hey, why's my social game less fun than I was led to expect? going around in this hobby and not enough products on the market meeting the needs of these gamers.

    I leave it as an exercise for the reader as to why certain people with a lot at stake in catering to consumers of the solitaire hobby might become resentful towards the proponents of a design philosophy supporting the notion that the two aspects can be separated.
