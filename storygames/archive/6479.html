    \n I played Memoir '44, Man Bites Dog, Apples to Apples, Thou Art But A Warrior, and Something is Rotten. I facilitated a game of Valkyrie, The Upgrade, two games of Business Solutions, and played several large-group sessions of Derek Treuer's Magical Kitchen.

    Thou Art But A Warrior is great and I'm going to advocate for my local group to play it as soon as possible. We used the one-shot rules and I think a full game with four protagonists and everyone in every role will burn brighter than a pile of Christians.

    Kevin's weird Hamlet-in-Montana project, Something is Rotten, was challenging and fun and needs further baking. But I like the cut of this youngster's jib. I wish we'd got to see more of the skeezy meth kingpin version of Fortinbras.

    The Upgrade went very well - Clinton and I produced, with the capable and expert help of our production assistants Emily and Eppy. I was surprised at the juxtaposition of genuine hilarity with truth and pathos. I had expected genuine hilarity exclusively. There was some hot girl-girl action, too, and catfights, mansharks, Kevin Allen Junior getting clubbed like a harp seal, and jet skis that shot fire. Definitely the highlight of Nerdly for me.

    Valkyrie, a game I'm playtesting, works as written, which was more or less a huge relief. I need to fix the numbers a bit, but they are just numbers.

    Business Solutions also rolled pretty solid - I had two groups going at once, and they swapped a technicians mid-week, which was fun. It's a fun game to watch. Both groups opted for the "eccentric" flavor (as opposed to melodramatic or slice-of-life), so it played more like media properties with "Office" in the title.

    Derek Treuer's Magical Kitchen was another favorite. It's new, a very clean design, and delicious in play. Especially the vegan option. My favorite scenario was "fill a bowl with grapes", which I totally won.

    \n Played in the aschan of "oh frak will it ever stop raining" Friday night. That was one soggy piece of work...

    I ran 2 sessions of PTA and one of Ganakagok, and got to play Business Solutions.

    PTA 1 was Friday night. Series title - Unit of Worship. The pitch session started with a sci-fi bent about folks traveling through a one way gate to another planet where aliens who had previously been gods on earth still existed. And the gods would, after centuries without followers, compete to get these folks to worship them.

    In play, the sci-fi aspect was about absent, except for the player who's character was a robot, jealous of folks with faith, but unable to have any himself. We had 2 gods, a reluctant Bacchus and an ambitious god named Primus. A scheming temple priest, a teenage runaway, and a medic who found himself useless in the presence of beings able to heal any wound.


    PTA 2 was Saturday morning. Entitled "Tomes", it was a show about a book club that investigates the supernatural. The episode title was "The Portrait of Dorian Gray", so you can kinda guess where that went. It was good fun involving answering machine messages from the dead, a big reveal about a shared love interest of two of the characters, and a twisted art opening.

    Ganakagok was Saturday night. I subbed in for Don who went home sick with allergies, and promptly forgot the theme he had set for the game (song of ice - music and sounds heard on the ice). Instead I ran a generic game, and with the exception of one really weak conflict on my part, it was suitably mythic.

    We had an elder who fought the bear god, an became one with the ice. A whaler who fought the orca spirit to a standstill, and a hunter on a private vengeance quest to kill or become a cannibal ghoul.

    Sunday I played BSI and repaired copiers. It was fun, if a bit random. We chewed the scenery alot. Our calls took us to a bad side of town nightclub, a law firm, and a CIA front operation.

    The food was great, especially the cookies midway through the session Saturday night. Huevos rancheros on Sunday morning was bot unexpected (the menu called for a cold meal) and extra yummy.

    \nPrime Time Adventures, Robotech plus a 70-80s punk rock music rebellion, fascist police, zeppelins, and intelligent zombies. Remi is a great GM! Seriously, the most enjoyable game of PTA I've played in a while. We drifted the rules as needed and used the optional chase rules to great effect! There was wonderful reincorporation, role reversals, and significant character change all around. The game was very dynamic. Vegan Sharks will never die!
    \n
    \nMythos Hunters, I would buy this game when it comes out. Great mix of traditional and story game. It's a highly cinematic version of X-Men + Delta Green vs. Cthulhu. The relationship chart, leadership / strategy rules (leader can generate free successes that anyone can use when following orders), damage track (you pretty much control when you die and are rewarded for it because the closer you get to losing your character, the more powerful you become), attribute / skills system were all wonderful. My favorite part were the zero scenes. Very much like Dogs' initiation scenes. Ooohh... I also loved how the leader assigns roles to the team after watching the zero scenes and how you are mechanically rewarded for working within the team's structure. Mark was also amazing at clearly explaining the rules, pacing everything, and working with what we all brought to the table.
    \n
    \nMisspent Youth, I really love this game and will 100% buy it when it comes out. We sat down to playtest the game text itself rather than the specific rules. It was a grueling process but a much needed one for every game. We each took turns reading 2 pages out loud and passed the rules around. As we did so, we took notes focused on clarity, flow, communication, and expectation issues. We definitely found issues but all things that are all easily fixed and this text is simply the first draft of the ashcan version. That being said, I don't think many published games, traditional or indie, would survive unscathed going through this process. The Youthful Offender trait selection section is an absolute joy to read and use. I laughed repeatedly or was creeped out and it did a great job foreshadowing what's behind the corner. Really looking forward to this!
    \n
    \nAlso played Bang and Jungle Speed!

    \n I ran a half a session of Misspent Youth which was about an attempt by The Authority, a political movement called The New Morality (ripped off of Richard K. Morgan) which had lobbied to have passed a No-Child-Left-Behind-like law called The Initiative for The New American. This was essentially state-sponsored eugenics. The setting was sort of either an alternate-history 1960s with high-tech, or a recapitulation of the 60s in the future. The couple of scenes we played out revolved around a Patty-Hearst-like girl played by Joshua AC Newman, who had been impregnated by her senator father who had done so for greater genetic strength (at least, that was his own internal excuse), and a golden boy played by Rachel Walton who had hit the genetic lottery and had risen from low genotypic ghettos to become a mole for The New Morality. Once he was inside, though, he changed sides but hadn't figured out a way to get out, yet. His friends didn't know and the first scene resulted in this coming out and things getting way fucked-up. The girl got briefly arrested before they all affected an escape. It was really exciting and I was sad it didn't go forward. In this game were Phil and Rachel Walton, Joshua AC Newman, Patty Kirsch, and Phil & Rachel's friend whose name I'm blanking on right now (sorry).

    For the rest of Nerdly's gaming-events, the hallmark was me getting stress-stomach-aches (but in a very very good way). I sat (mostly) quietly while a bunch of people read the text to Misspent Youth extremely closely and out loud and came up with a bunch of things to fix in it. They got about halfway through world and character creation before we had to stop. It was stressful but helpful. That included John Stavropolous, Nathan Herrold, Terry Romero, Kevin Allen Jr., and some people whose names I'm blanking on right now (sorry).

    \n The only full gaming session I got in at all of Nerdly (not counting card games) was The Upgrade. I played Eric, an out-of-shape tech support guy who hated his job and had relationship issues with his wife (played by Rachel Walton) that were very similar to ones I had with my son's mother, and expressed similar issues with meeting women (not being comfortable approaching them blind, for example). Rachel and I had a lot of fun answering the couples questionnaire, and since our characters' friends filled it out for us we injected some uncomfortable truths and manipulations in there, since it was obvious her friend who initiated the process wanted to break us up.

    I decided that Eric, like me, hates reality TV and only went on because he'd already been signed up. I decided he thought it would be a good idea but then immediately began to regret his choice to attend. He wound up being the "lone angry voice" (well almost lone) a la the movie Network. I had about 20 minutes where I pretended to enjoy myself, then the wonderfully doofusy-yet-alluring Jason (played by Kevin Allen Jr.) walked up arm-in-arm with Rose, Eric's wife, and the facade quickly crumbled. I played the rest of the game raging against the evil of the whole process to very very little effect. I had a heartfelt moment where I confessed what a bad husband I'd been, where I confronted Rose on what she had been doing wrong (my failure to express my feelings had been one of the core problems with the relationship), and told her my plans to quit the job I hated so we could spend more time together.

    Given the way the scene played out, at this point I'm thinking, "Well, it's a bit too obvious and easy to see they're going to get back together. Let's inject some suspense." I told Rose that I was going to vote no on the Upgrade, but I didn't want her to tell me her vote, that she could have her time and think it over. Furthering this suspense aim, later on I asked for a scene where Jason tried to get real with Rose (he had done a "real moment" once before and even though it was funny, it was also quite touching).

    Oh, yeah, somewhere along the way I brutally knocked Jason out cold with a log-strike to the back of the head. This was especially bad because he was about to try to make up with me.

    Anyway, I go into the vote and we close our eyes and I put both my hands out, thumbs-down, to indicate I want to keep the relationship I came to the island with. I open my eyes and see Rose has her thumb up. I had played the game up til this point with a stress-related stomach-ache and the physiological reaction I had to this was even more extreme! I felt dizzy and I didn't see anything else in the room. I walked as far from her in the room as I could be and sulked (there were several occasions where I wanted to leave the cabin because my character would've totally stormed out, but I didn't want to miss anything as an audience member).

    It was incredible. I don't think I've ever felt such strong emotions from a game and I thank Kevin, Rachel, and everyone else for helping make it happen. It was draining and punishing and totally fucking amazingly good and fun.

    I have many many more thoughts on this game (so does everyone who played it, the session probably ran 2-3 hours but we easily spent 5 talking about it), and this style of gaming. It was great.

    \n I played in the nostalgia-fest of Tomb of Horrors, Capes, PTA, and The Shab-Al-Hiri Roach.
    \n
    \nTomb of Horrors went better for us than I expected, with only one PC death, and us making it about 2/3 of the way through the module. Funniest old-school bits: traps, traps, traps. So many that I suggested that the Tomb was actually a "demo dungeon" used by a company selling traps to other dungeon makers. Mad interior decorators. Gygax went nutty on the colors, with one room having things of about twenty different colors, including russet, olive, scarlet, and citron. Like we could tell color that well by torchlight...
    \n
    \nCapes was fun, with two people who were still learning the system, and myself, whose main experience was with the playtest versions, not the final product. We had a blast nonetheless, with some cool conflicts coming up (my favorite: a shapeshifting villain with one of the heroes as an exemplar, and a free event conflict of "Identity Revealed"). The click-n-lock chargen was used extensively, with us creating three or four characters during play -- including Batman!
    \n
    \nOn the PTA front, I played in the Tomes game that's already been mentioned. Another character and mine had the same girlfriend, but knew her by two different names, which made for a fun reveal. Coming up with the series and the summary of what had happened in the previous episodes was as much fun as actually playing.
    \n
    \nIt was my first time playing The Shab Al-Hiri Roach, and I had great fun. I chose to start unroached, but was roached at the first event, with Reverend Talley attacking me in the bathroom and forcing the roach on me. From there, we went through the usual roachy evil. Highlights: an NPCs death by pimp-slap, a self-destructive character who was described as "not being allowed near the Dean with forks", a wedding(!!) that kept being interrupted by roachy fun (and thus was scheduled to happen at each of the last three events), and Regina Sutton being branded.
    \n
    \nMy wife Sandy had prepped a game of Dead of Night, but it was slotted for 11pm, and all the signed-up players decided to go to bed instead of playing. :-(

    I made a tasty sandwich (not a game, but very important to me none the less)

    There were no less than three times in the course of the weekend where i though "Oh, i should show this to/ask/talk to Dave Younce." His presence was greatly missed.

    Something is Rotten - This was the first "lets try to play the game fully" playtest and it went pretty well, i feel. We stopped after one act, not because it fell apart, but because it was apparent that it could be made more awesome by changing some things around. "Something is Rotten" is an improvisational acting game where you perform Hamlet (but set in a modern day small town in rural Montana). My players were great and really got into the story. I'll have an AP post, or at least a critique/summary of this experience soon.

    The Upgrade - A lot has already been said here about the upgrade. It was a pretty wildly amazing experience. It brought out in me things that i've never felt in a game before. Clinton and Jason were really excellent hosts, and Eppy and Emily provided wonderful support. I didn't really know what to expect out of the game going in, and i was a little nervous to be honest. I ended up making a huge ass of myself (this was on purpose, by being a bombastic jerk-off character), and then not knowing how to temper that character into showing actual emotion. This confusion led to actual awkward pathos. It was like being in highschool again and not really knowing how to talk to girls. I was honestly nervous that rob was going to kill me, i was terrified i would get dumped and end up the big lonely jerk, and i found myself desperately desiring to make a connection with someone i've only known for a few hours. All this was made more strange by the fact the these rather strong emotions all wrapped up at a specific point when the game ended. It was like "Oh, it's ten o'clock: Rob's not the angry husband your fucking with he's a guy your friends with who you've been palling around with all weekend. Rachel isn't a toyed with object of desire that your courting in hot tubs and flame spewing jetskis, she's a pretty cool person that you've just sort of met and hopefully not really creeped out. And lastly your not that guy Eric from the first real world, you're you, so stop yelling and quit that don't give a shit attitude, it's not fooling anyone." A weird night to say the least. I would like to do more of these. It will be very interesting to see how these games influence the next wave of games people are designing. I know it will have an effect on my writing, to say the least.

    Sunday i didn't play anything, i went on a hike in the woods. It was muddy but fun. I miss being outside and despite the weather i was really happy we got a chance to do this. It was a wonderful bonding experience.

    Nerdly was great.

    Posted By: eruditusPosted By: Robert Bohl... I sat (mostly) quietly while a bunch of people read the text to Misspent Youthextremelyclosely and out loud and came up with a bunch of things to fix in it.
    \nHoly Fuck, I want people to do that for my game! That's a great idea!
    - Don
    It works really well. Definitely something that I feel the designer should be present for rather than listening to as a podcast or reading a review. Why? Because what the players say isn't as important as what the players do! You need to observe everyone's facial expressions. Where they stall. Where they get excited. Where they seem confused. Not everyone will communicate this with words. And sometimes what does end up communicated is directly connected to the last strong stimulus they experienced. For example, someone could make a character and have struggled through the first 3/4ths of it but had a blast with the last part and forget how tough it was at first. The reverse can happen, you have a great time but one specific issue paints your perceptions. It helps to be there to observe the exercise. Although it can be painful! It's also important to make note of everything that happens. Even the items you don't agree with. They are happening for a reason. It may not be happening for the reason the person describing it thinks, but there is probably something there to dig up and explore. Every problem is an opportunity. We playstormed a game where a mechanic literally made the players feel frustrated and angry. We examined why and turned that into a feature. Use it to frustrate them but then give them a resource to channel those feelings and beat down the source of their frustration and turn it into feelings of awesomeness. Rock!
