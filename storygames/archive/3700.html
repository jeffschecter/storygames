\n A friend of mine who's really into Magic linked me to a few articles. I searched around the M:tG site and found two articles that I thought explored Gamist design in a really interesting way. It's interesting to the the parallels to GNS, and also the divergences. Anyhow.

This article is about finding the intersection of fun/cometition in a casual game of Magic. The author talks about how player experience, and preference for difficult challenges affect how people play the game. It has charts!

This article is another look at the old "Timmy", "Johnny", "Spike" classifications, and gives them a more nuanced reading. I was always suspicious of those classifications, but the authr makes some interesting observations. It's interesting to see the parallels to Gamist, Narrativist, and Simulationist play, all within an ostensibly Gamist design.

Maybe these are good fodder for discussion here?

    \n Yeah, I guess what I meant was that their "psychographics" don't map evenly onto GNS, but do kind of cover the same territory. "Spike" and "Johnny" we'd recognize as players with Gamist motivations, while "Timmy" seems to have kind of Simulationist goals in some ways, with the emphasis on enjoying the process of play. I got all excited when he talked about "Jonny" "expressing something" through play, but I think the agenda is fundamentally different to Narrativism. What's being expressed is not a story.

    I think the biggest difference is that, while GNS talks about what actually happens at the table, in terms of what people do, "JTS" (As I'll call it) talks about who people are. I think this is interesting. Are there fundamentally different types of people who enjoy Gamist play? Or can your agenda switch?

    I think that Gamism is a kind of unexplored field in game design, in that it seems poorly understood how to make a properly satisfying Gamist challenge. Clearly, the designers of M:tG have done a lot of work in this field, and it would be interesting to see if we could learn anything from them.

    Posted By: Simon CI got all excited when he talked about "Jonny" "expressing something" through play, but I think the agenda is fundamentally different to Narrativism. What's being expressed is not a story.
    \nOh, I wholeheartedly disagree! I was very much a Johnny - more specifically, a "Deck Artist" - when I played M:tG (3rd and 4th edition, which was what - about a hundred years ago or so?). Long before WotC released "theme decks", I was building nothing but. Highly focused decks with one or two themes to them, such as the Goblin Invasion that would be pathetic until the Goblins found themselves a King to lead them and the Lightning Bolts started raining, or the Thallid deck that spawned a ton of weak fungi in defense until the forest could finally summon the dread Craw Wurm to strike back, or the Merfolk who fought a bitter battle using poisons, confusion and trickery to wear down the opponent's library.

    This would often frustrate my Spikey friends, who would endlessly wonder why I didn't just include this or that card to counteract their optimized decks of doom. Doing so would damage the artistic integrity of my decks, though, and I balked at the idea. To me, when I played, a story was generated in my head - when the Instants and Interrupts started flying back and forth, it was an actual magical duel of wits taking place, not just the result of a lucky draw. By including elements in my deck that didn't belong, I would mess with the consistency of that story and ruin my fun.

    Maybe that was a very unique experience - maybe other Johnnies aren't like that - but it was something I greatly enjoyed at the time.

    \n Ok, I don't want to be a theory snob, but what you're describing isn't Narrativism, it's Simulationism.

    But I know what you're talking about, it's how I always loved to play Magic as well. Unfortunately, the game doesn't really support that agenda very well. I'd say that even the classification that the article uses - "Deck Artist" doesn't really cover that agenda very well. It's about trying to make the game play out like some fictional events in the setting. That's quite different from making a deck that, for example, tries to make the opponent burn through their library (not an optimal strategy, but if you construct your deck elegantly, it can be done) but according to the article, it's the same profile.

    \nAgreed and seconded on all counts but the application of GNS. I don't agree with the idea of a strong disconnect between Narrativism and Simulationism, and this is a great example of why. To me, the story-in-my-head cannot take place if the world (i.e. the cards on the table) are inconsistent. I have seen this also in playing games that aim for strong Narrative elements at the expense of Simulationism, by randomizing elements and leaving it up to the players to make sense of it. Sometimes the randomness is simply too chaotic to make any sense of, and the resulting story falls flat. The best story-generating games are highly thematic, for a very good reason - the same reason I would refuse to include cards in my deck that could mess up the story.

    I recently got a chance to test the Game of Thrones CCG and it supported my playstyle much better than Magic ever did, because of the strong thematic components. My one complaint is that deck building was much more structured, which limits my abilities to design my own unique theme more than M:tG did.
