\n How would you use Dogs in the Vineyard to play Battlestar Galactica?

Why?

1. The release of the BSG's series bible made me remember how much I loved the first season. The show's original intent as described in this series bible reminds me of a session of Dogs.

2. I've run Dogs 100+ times for 200+ different people and I still love it. But after 2-3 intense sessions, many players retire their characters by using fallout to burn their "I'm a Dogs" Trait or Relationship (which is why i suspect it's mandatory to have one). Most players I've run for enjoy playing but it has a limited shelf life for them. I know many of those 200+ players are also BSG fans, so I'm excited by the idea of going back and running Dogs for them again in a new way.

How?

- Town = Ship
- Cylons = Demons, Possessed People, Sorcerers
- Conflicts are less about "demons" and more how people react under pressure
- The "Dogs" are people without the required leadership experience elevated to ultimate power as decision makers who must make sharp judgements to keep everything together when there is no time and the only hope they have is the hope they bring.

Thoughts?

Has anyone done this? Or how would you?

I've only run Dogs for Dogs. Never re-skinned it for anything else.

Rock,
John

    \n It occurs to me that Apocalypse World is a slightly more suitable system for a BSG-like series. Dogs strikes me as, essentially, episodic in nature: how do the characters come to grips with their own understanding of their place in the world by exposure to the problems of this town, that town, and that other town over there. The game seems to come pre-rolled with the understanding that the only really explicit links between the situations described by any two "adventures" are the PC/Dogs themselves. If you want to take a TV-SciFi series and Dogs-i-fy it, then it seems to me that you're looking more at Star Trek or Farscape as more likely models.

    But I suspect that Apocalypse World is ideally suited to the BSG setup. There is no central authority in the background; there is no "place to repair to for comfort and replenishment". The world has fallen to hell in a hand basket; most near everyone is dead; you pathetic train of survivors are being dogged by a poorly understood and seemingly implacable foe; and, of course, "something weird" is going on that's not really rationally explainable.

    I suspect it wouldn't be hard at all to BSG-ify the player's sheets/moves, and some characters leap out as nearly auto-translating:

    Angel == Doc Cottle, Fours
    Battle Babe == Starbuck, Sixes
    Brainer == Baltar Six, Twos
    Chopper == Chief, Helo
    Driver == Lee Adama, Eights
    Gun Lugger == Anders?
    Hard Holder == Bill Adama, Ones
    Hocus == Roslin, Baltar, Zarek, Ones
    Operator == Gaeta, Ellen Tigh, Tory, Fives, Threes
    Savvy Head == Chief, Cally
    Skinner == ? (not much focus on the "role of the artist")

    I remember being pretty impressed by the ambiguous choices people faced on just about every episode of BSG - I very often didn't know which way I would jump if I had to make those choices. I'm surprised that's not spelled out more clearly in the Bible, because for me that's what the show was About.

    The fundamental concern in BSG is survival. The moral question is this: What is worth sacrificing in order to survive? The Dogs are the characters like Lee Adama, Starbuck, Billy, and Tyrol, who are officers, crewman, or civilians given the authority to intervene in the affairs of ships in the fleet to keep it flying. Their job is to go into places where there are problems and fix them. They have the authority to, if necessary, draw a gun and shoot the troublemaker, but no one wants to let things get that far.

    The really interesting thing about the ladder of sin in DitV is that, often, the players decide that, in some cases, some level of sin is okay. That's exactly what happens all the time in BSG.

    BSG isn't really about finding Cylons. Any rules that focus too much on the cylons really lose out on the appeal of BSG, I think.

    In this fleet, all we can do is survive. We keep going and jumping and looking for some place called Earth that may or may not even exist.

    But something's wrong.

    Someone LOSES PERSPECTIVE. They forget that the needs of the fleet outweigh anything else and that nothing matters more than the survival of the human race. They start to think that their personal needs, or tradition, or democracy, or comfort, or anything else, come before the needs of the fleet.

    This leads to...

    IRRESPONSIBLE ACTIONS. They start to act out these unrealistic feelings. They withhold fuel shipments. They riot. They demand that the president resign and hold immediate elections. They show up to fly a watch drunk. In some way, they interrupt the necessary operations within the fleet.

    These interruptions lead to fleet to being exposed to...

    Cylon Attacks. The halts to smooth operations distracts the fleet and leaves it vulnerable enough for the Cylons to make some kind of attack, whether that means raiders, terrorist attacks from sleepers in the fleet, or other threats. These, like Demonic Attacks in regular DitV, shouldn't really take center stage. They happen and are resolved, but it's mostly about avoiding and recovering from them. They should more be backdrop for what the characters do.

    Eventually, these attacks, the worsening situation, and these bad beliefs lead to...

    UNHEALTHY PHILOSOPHIES. These ill-advised beliefs become well-formed coded. Feelings about fairness lead to demands for labor benefits and a true representative government. Desire for control become assertions that individual ship captains, not the Commander, decide where to go and how to fly. These doctrines are incompatible with smooth operations of the fleet and can lead to disaster. Weariness over running from the Cylons leads to beliefs that Human-Cylon peace is possible

    Left unchecked, individuals become RENEGADE LEADERS. They attract followers and destroy their resolve with their poisonous thoughts. They make problems even worse.

    Eventually, these splinter groups gain enough power and contemplate...

    CESSATION. The splinter group grows large enough to take control of one or more ships and threatens to leave the fleet, making themselves and the fleet itself weaker.

    The scale of escalation is essentially the same as regular DitV.

    \n If we are going to look at the characters in the show and say, these few are the kinds of characters that are "Dogs" in the game, I'd have to say that these are my favorites:

    Lee Adama
    Starbuck -- Both of these characters are military officers who often get assigned to solve fleet problems. But, at the same time, they have strong personal feelings about civil liberties and morality that lead them to decide that some things are not worth sacrificing for survival.

    Billy -- He is not military, but he is the President's closest adviser and is given a lot of authority to adjudicate problems. His feelings toward survival are much more liberal than either Lee's or Starbuck's, and he is a lot like the "redeemed sinner" kind of character in regular DitV.

    Chief Tyrol (especially in some episodes of seasons 3 and 4) -- The Chief is a character that really empathizes with the plight of the non-military citizens of the fleet, and he has difficulty separating his personal emotions from his duties as an enlisted military personnel. Everyone, civilian and military, feels they can rely on him, and that pulls on his conscience strongly.


    The equivalent to a "I'm a Dog."-type trait, which I'll agree seems most important when the player burns it as Fallout and retires the character, is something that describes the troubleshooter role the character holds in the fleet. For the characters above as examples, Lee ("I am the Commander of the Air Group" or "My father, the Commander, trusts me above anyone else."), Starbuck ("I'm the best damn soldier in the fleet."), Billy ("I'm the President's personal adviser."), and the Chief ("I'm the only crewman from a poor colony." or "I'm the Chief Deckhand."). The Cylon-type traits, while interesting, change the focus of the game from problem solving to Cylon hunter. While this is a perfectly valid game to play, I don't think it really captures the feel of (at least the first season of) BSG.

    For towns, I think Vernon hits it right on the head with towns being problem situations. The clearest example I can think of right now is season 1, episode 2 (or 3?), where Tom Zareg leads a prison break and demands a new government. Here, a survival problem in the fleet is revealed to really be a dispute about the necessity of democracy, and Lee, especially, is challenged to choose between neat order and fair representation. Another good example is the season 3 episode where the fuel ship laborers refuse to work until conditions are improved. The Chief is sent in to force them to work, but he instead decides that oppression is not a price worth paying for survival, and joins the strike.

    [Spoilers]
    The Chief also presents a cool example of the character's "I'm the deckhand everyone trusts." trait being burned. At the end of Season 3, he finds that he is a Cylon. Here, as Fallout, the Chief replaces his Troubleshooter trait with "I'm a Cylon 1d4." Later "towns" deal with him trying to handle problems while hiding and struggling with this fact. Eventually, he upgrades this trait to "I'm the liaison between the Cylons and the Fleet 1d6."
