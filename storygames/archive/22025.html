So board games have the same problem, when I say that I am going to my board games Meetup people often ask me, "what sort of board games do you play?"

I honestly don't know how to answer, because describing the games I play requires being familiar with a terminology that means nothing to someone uninitiated.

RPG's have a similar if not worse problem. If you want to tell someone you're playing something other than dungeons & dragons (not that there's anything wrong with that game) people look at you surprised not having known there are other RPGs.

How do you deal with this issue? What do you say to get people interested? (Or simply explain the experience in some way)

    There isn’t necessarily a good “jumping off point” for story games or indie RPGs.

    Your best bet might be Tabletop (there are good sessions of both Fiasco and Misspent Youth) and/or all the podcasts out there that do story game playthroughs.

    Some Kickstarter intro videos could be helpful, too! They tend to be brief and to the point.

    In the other hand, if you ARE asking about introducing and explaining:

    For trad gamers:

    “I’m interested in role playing games that have a more dynamic narrative; they are designed to worry less about winning fights and more about characters, their stories, and difficult personal decisions. Some of these games don’t even have a GM, so everyone gets to contribute creatively to what’s happening.”

    For board gamers:

    “The games I enjoy are tabletop games, like board games, but instead of manipulating figures on a board, there’s a strong component of imagined or fictional material. As a player, you’re not limited to the actions the game has specified for you, but can try a nearly infinite number of options to solve problems or get ahead. It’s like participating in making a tv show, movie, or novel, while also being a character within it.”

    I've always found that storygames are much easier to sell non-gamers on (in big part because they're very much designed to be parseable by non-gamers, whereas trad presumes gaming experience, and because storygames use skills more commonly held by non-gamers than tactics and strategy) as long as the DnD comparison isn't made, or as long as it's immediately shut down if it's made.
    "Is this like DnD?"
    "No, not at all."
    I can imagine it would be harder with neo-trad stuff and other more orthodox indie stuff though, since the answer with some of those games to "Is this like DnD?" is "ehh, sort of". whereas the stuff I play is entirely unlike DnD.
