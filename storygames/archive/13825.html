\nPlaytesting: Stop

Here is a snippet:\n
More playtest does not magically make your game better. Neither does it fix most problems that your game might have. It is not a replacement for skilled writing, a creative and technical vision, editing, inspiration, or game design work. It is a very specific tool for finding very specific problems, and attempts to use it beyond that are detrimental both to your own game design and to design culture in general.

It is also important when we embark on a playtest that we respect our playtesters' time and attention. Relying on playtest to point out textual, procedural, and mathematical flaws which could be more easily spotted with simple analysis is degrading to our playtesters and also simply bad game design practice. Stop it.
\nRead the rest here: http://www.lumpley.com/comment.php?entry=565

    \n Having worked as a video game tester, I can say that playtesting is a lot of work to reveal issues that occur in the long tails of play (infrequent or unlikely events). Flaws that are detected decline across the development of a game (consecutive builds) and the type of flaws changes from game breakers to fiddly things like seams in the landscape and improperly textured surfaces in out of the way places.

    With RPGs, the process is a bit different. I agree with Ben that RPGs are what you make them and that unfocused and non-deliberate playtesting is probably a waste of time because compitent players will compensate for the game's failings, obscure them, and then either not report them or not have solutions to the problems. You should know exactly what you want to get out of a playtest and work to get that information out of the session (e.g. a poorly controlled experiment will not yield useful or meaningful results).

    Regarding "textual" testing, I can see a valid use for crowd-sourcing the editing process for an unfunded project that the designer has not yet decided to invest money in to hire an editor. As a writer I find that the quality of my writing is a direct function of the number of people I've asked to review my work. I'm going to also throw out that two types of blind playtesting seem like a really good way to check in and evaluate how your project is coming along. I think running your game for a totally new group of people is a useful exercise because it gives you more experience explaining the game and seeing how best to convey rules, best practices, and how to answer likely questions in the text before they come up in play. Alternately, I think there's a use for asking someone else to read your rules, turn on a tape recorder, and play the first session of the game with people who have never played (without you being there). Being a virtual fly on the wall (listening to the tape) can show you where people have to stop to look up specific rules and what information to emphasize.

    \n A lot of good points here, actually.
    \n
    \nI've seen entirely solid, well designed games get torn apart in playtesting by groups that simply weren't prepared to understand and accept the designer's vision before the started to critique/criticize. I've also seen the confidence and game designers undermined (and had my own confidence undermined) by attempts to playtest games in groups that were already deeply socially dysfunctional. There is a real tendency to imagine that social/personal problems that have nothing to do with the game itself are crippling design flaws. I've seen good games get junked for this very reason. (And I may very well have junked good games myself for this reason, only I'm not about to make claims about the quality of anything I've designed.)
    \n
    \nI think often playtesting is a response to personal insecurity or confusion. If you lack confidence about your abilities as a game designer, or if you are afraid your game will be rejected, often playtesting offers a way to reassure yourself or to put off moving forward with your design. "It's not ready yet! Needs more playtesting!" is an excuse that allows you to put off taking bigger steps that frighten you. And, in many games, a new designer may simply not know what to do with a finished design. They may not understand how to polish and publish. They may honestly have no clue what to do next. In this case, playtesting is an alluring answer, because it entails doing something they already understand--playing their game--instead of struggling to do something that they don't, like layout or promotion or sales. (I know I'm definitely guilt on both counts. I have some designs that may very well be finished, but the next step is both frightening and bewildering, so I think, "uh, time to playtest?")
    \n
    \nThat said, I think there is a parallel threat to excess playtesting: totally insular game design. I've seen a designer take the philosophy that the process is personal, and refinement is internal to the extreme that he actively hid his game from play, sidestepping all requests by other players to try it. I don't think that game design can happen in a vacuum. (I know Ben isn't saying that it should. I'm just offering a possible alternative extreme.) I don't think that working privately for years on mechanical refinement--as this designer did--is necessarily a good idea. Individuals can have huge personal blind spots, and these blind spots can lead to design that perfect minutiae while leaving large, highly visible portions of the game unpolished.
    \n
    \nBen offers some really good advice for avoiding this outcome in the article:
    \n
    \n
    Ben Lehman wrote:
    \n
    Probably the best idea to to either let the game lie fallow for a bit, or to discuss the issue with trusted fellow designers, members of the target audience, or good friends (ideally: all of the above.)
    \n
    \n
    \nThere is one small point that I'd like to quibble with though. Ben advises designers to hire a professional editor. I understand his argument, and I agree that someone who is trained to look at the structure of the text is more qualified to give you textual advice than a group of gamers. However, many first time designers simply can't afford to pay a professional editor, and I wish Ben had offered an alternative suggestion for them. Personally, I plan to ask non-gamer friends to review my text and see if they can understand how the game is played. Of course, this wouldn't resolve grammatical or structural issues, but it will help me know whether my game communicates clearly as a text devoid of context.

    \n It's hard to sort out the passion from the message. Superficially I'm the poster child for what Ben says is bad playtesting, but when I burrow into the details we're on the same page in many places.

    I playtested Microscope for two years, putting me squarely on the dark side. I could have released it after one year, and it would have been okay, but instead I kept examining the parts that made me go "hmmm." As a result, I learned some things that I think were pretty fascinating, which opened doors in the game.

    One of my reasons for so much playtesting was (ironically) the same as one of Ben's reason for not playtesting: ensuring the intent of the design didn't get watered down in play. Seeing how players drifted unusual aspects of Microscope back towards traditional methods (in other words, when they didn't play the game as I intended, but as they had played other games) was invaluable in figuring out how to prevent it. Players are going to play the game sooner or later (if not playtesting, than after release), so if you can't get them to play the game you intend, skipping playtesting just delays the inevitable.

    Comparing Microscope now to the game as-it-would-have-been a year ago, I already know the time was well spent. We'll see if other people agree soon enough. Maybe I wasted a whole year! I could have been down at the pub!


    PS There's a big difference between "play and design for fun or art" and "design and sell for profit." If you're charging someone money for something, it's a whole different ballgame. We need a big flag we can wave to distinguish between the two processes. [EDIT and add to that, either way you're asking someone to spend their time on your game. Even if it's 100% free, if you release it you are asking someone to spend their time playing it.]

    \n Kind of wierd having a conversation in two places at once. I'll just cut and paste some of my comments here for anyone not reading through the entire anyway thread.

    So my conclusion:

    "Every place where Ben is saying "do these things they're an important part of design"...bold that...underline it...and do it.

    And every place where he says "don't try to use playtesting for this"...scratch it out...erase it...and ignore it.

    Replace it with..."when you use playtesting for this, it should only be after you've done these other things* and you should by this point have some specific ideas of what you're looking for in the playtest as a result of having done those other things...cuz otherwise...you're playtesting wrong and wasting your time...and THAT'S when you can mangle your design".

    So the premise of this article I think is completely wrong. It shouldn't be "stop playtesting because you're doing it wrong". It should be "keep playtesting, but here are the things you need to have done first in order to do it right."

    Rewrite it like that, and it gets two thumbs up for me. Leave it as is and its just another internet design meme waiting to be passed around and misused.



    *not referring to the practice of Playstorming here, which IMO is really its own seperate animal."
