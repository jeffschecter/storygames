    \n For me, it's a long line of British postapocalyptic fiction. Start with H G Wells' The War Of The Worlds. Then go forward to the 1950s and 1950s: the bleak cosiness of The Day Of The Triffids; the psychedelic strangeness of The Crystal World; the brutality and strange vistas of A Wrinkle In The Skin.

    Then 28 Days Later, which steals from The Day Of The Triffids.

    You'll find that British postapocalyptica is significantly different from other types. I personally find A Canticle For Leibowitz and The Postman tedious and almost unreadable, but you know, they're there. (The Road is excellent, though.)

    There's a long appendix in the back that's in Choose Your Own Adventure format (!),

    and then advises you about what you'll be facing and how to respond.

    \nTo anyone who liked the movie, I would urge/demand/testify: go and read Woodrell's book. Heck, read everything by Woodrell. Then read nearly all of Cormac McArthy (post Blood-Meridian, I'd say). Then maybe go and read yourself some Jim Thompson. And Robert Parker's Cole and Hitch books (Appaloosa, Resolution, Brimstone, Blue-Eyed Devil). If you're not by that point completely prepped to present some heavy Apocalyptica... well, you oughta be.

    I suspect that anything related to the Noir genre, especially when set in essentially rural areas with limited economies, deals heavily with "the world is broke and falling apart and we ain't got nothing but our word and each other" meme, and to me that's central to the AW aesthetic.
