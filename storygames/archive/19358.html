    \n My current preferred choices for large groups like that are OSR D&D and Fables of Camelot. Both are explicitly designed to deal. Adventure Castle, Executive Decision and any LARPs or LARP-like tabletop games are also designed to deal with it. In general, you want a game with either very short player turns (so wait is minimized), very cooperative player activities (so several players are engaged at once), asymmetrical player positions (that is, where some players can remain consistently more passive through-out a session), or multi-threaded simultaneous play (so several player turns occur at once).

    If I had to play a game not specifically suited for that situation, I'd rather split the group in two and execute two separate plays.

    \nUh? Forget the "live action" variant. The core, tabletop game is recommended for 4-12 players. I've never tried the upper end of the spectrum, but I've played with 8 or 9 people and it worked OK, while with only 4 it felt pretty weak.
    You have to remember the system is agnostic regarding which player characters are actually the main characters in the "movie": that's an emergent property of both screen presence and prolonged survival. It's likely some PCs will end up only being on screen for a single scene, then dieing: embrace it as a feature of the game. It takes more players, and thus more player characters, to generate more complex plots.

    \n I've done that with Zombie Cinema: start with e.g. 4 players to a table, with the tables representing different cities or work places or families or whatever, and then combine tables as characters get eaten (with the character-less players remaining as a sort of an audience nimbus, with voting rights, but without each of them taking turns anymore closer to the end of the game). A quite interesting way to play, assuming you don't have any dry prune players who will completely lock down in dramaphylactic shock when they realize that they don't know everything that's gone on before, now that some of the players at the table have been playing at a different table for the first half of the session
