\n My Were-tiger badass and your ninja saboteur team up with the flying starfish and elven sniper, and we all shoot recoilless rifles at the Pharaoh's goose-stepping thugs.

You are playing...


A system which suffers a little from over-complication (5-step geometric scale), but still has a little something for everyone: Mathematical Magic creation, consistent magical architecture rules, ghoulish curse creation, martial arts crafting, cyberdecking.

These rules, plus overly-complicated rules for returning the world to its previous state, are encoded in...


The first attempt to create a player community-defined metaplot. In fact, it might be the first attempt to actually create a metaplot at all: Write up your adventure summaries, send them to West End Games (in 1990) and they'll build your doings into the world-weave metaplot.

Group worldbuilding before the internet...


Anyone have memories of this game?

I get nostalgic for it every now and then, but the problem is that I'd want to use a system that's not as "robust" and "all-encompassing", and I'd want a character sheet where what you believe in gives you more dice or points or something.

TORG!

    There's also the fond memories of playing when it first came out. First I played a contract ninja for a campaign that only lasted a couple of sessions. Then in the next campaign, it was my kung-fu style Luke Skywalker character, a reporter, a werewolf, and a soldier of fortune.

    I wait with baited breath for the release of Generic HeroQuest and OtherWorlds as I think these two systems would be great for TORG, at least as starting points to drift. I'm just not yet sure which one is better, so I'll need to play with them more.

    \n Oh, look at Joe being all awesome and practical. I haven't even shared my Real Thoughts on the game yet. Yeah, if anyone else had "mini-reviews" of the product line, toss them here!

    * At one time or another I owned almost every single TORG book. Yeah, even the shitty novels.

    * Space Gods was the first supplement where it awakened feelings of "Metaplot is Dumb/Jumping the Shark" in me, but I didn't know it at the time. I loved the idea of a biotech entity in the Southern Americas, but I thought it was handled poorly. Everything was Too Rifts-Awesome: More Guns, More Damage, More Psychic Powers, etc. I wished they juggled the Cosm laws and stats to not be so awesome in all ways. Althoguh in my games I, probably like many others, simply ignored that supplement entirely.

    * My favorite supplement rules-wise was The Nile Empire. Gadgeteering plus Egyptian Feng-Shui architecture magic was hot. Hot!

    * I was most disappointed in the Cyberpapacy supplement. I loved the idea of the realm, but for some reason I didn't like the supplement as much. It was all maps and shit, and not enough exciting situations or potential.

    * Orrorsh was cool, too. Lots of juicy ideas to take. Curse magic was cool, too.

    * When I sold my TORG stuff (when I returned to America from Japan, and needed to get rid of lots of baggage) I copied a bunch of sheets from the game for inspiration. One of the ones I copied was the Aysle "dozens of circles and numbers" spell creation sheet. I loved that sheet, though I never once used it.

    * I really loved the axioms of The Land Below. That shit was hot.

    * Probably the most useful book that came out in that five-year range, RPGwise, was the Players Guide to the Possibility Wars: It was a true player's guide, a 15-page book that allowed you to quickly generate characters and play, with some extra rules and shit. It was really well thought-out: A useful player reference, not just another splatbook (guns, cars, etc). Delphi rocked too, for the 100 some adventure seeds it gave to the GM.

    * No one ever used the Sacrifice Drama Deck card. That made me sad.

    * I too lost my Eternity Shard Die. It was one of my faves. I think I lost it camping.

    * I never sent my own Infiniverse Updates, nor did I ever purchase them.

    * The first internet email mailing list I ever joined was Kansas Jim's TORG Majordomo list. That was also my first brush with Japanese TORG freelancers.

    * My own characters included a Ninja and a Cyberpapacy Sniper.

    Damn, this is making me want to find some old books on eBay and give a campaign a go. Hmmm.

    Posted By: jdrakehI nearly bought some old World of. . . box sets today to get a Masterdeck (the slightly refined, more universal, Drama Deck descendent). It's funny. The broadly generic Masterdeck was a really a good idea. Has anybody else created something like this for RPGs? Ever?
    \nRay Winninger and I were obsessed with Lion Rampant's Whimsy Cards made back in the 80's.

    We were both certain that we wanted to a) shake up the "I hit, you hit" nature of combat, and b) we wanted to give Players more control over color detail and, when possible, events. So the Whimsy Cards were a good inspiration.

    Greg, being Greg (and, as stated above, being a genius), insisted we make sure the cards tied in some how mechanically. I stil remembering him coming into a conference room with a box of business cards from the shoe import company WEG was tied to and all three of us brainstorming and writing down ideas on cards and building out the first deck. (Wow, I haven't thought of that for years.)


    Funny story: So, Torg is out, and I'm living in Chicago, and I'm part of a socially active group of guys who also game. And we start talking about games. And the brother of one of them hates -- HATES -- the Dram Deck. Because, he says, "If I'm the GM and one of the Players wants a Romantic Interest, he'll get it when I say so." And I thought, "Jesus, no wonder I hate playing these games so often."

    You can see what we were up against in terms of Player empowerment back then. It's not that some groups didn't function that way. But when even offering up romantic interest by a Players cause they want it for their PC is considered radical by a GM, that's some weird stuff. The Drama Deck, via the Whimsy Cards, were an attempt to funnel that kind of power to the Players.

    CK

    \nI loved TORG for a couple of reasons. First, like Jerry, I was (and still am) a fan of all of Greg Gorden's RPG work (I started in RPGs with DC Heroes, got my first professional (i.e. paying) writing gig writing for TORG, and worked as the Earthdawn Product Line Developer at FASA).
    \n
    \nSecond, my group and I were lucky enough to be playtesters on several of the world books, including The Nile Empire, The Cyberpapacy, Nippon Tech, Aysle, Orrorsh, and Space Gods.
    \n
    \nMy group loved the Drama Deck. They developed an "open hand" style of playing, where they all laid their cards out face up, so everyone knew what everyone had, thus allowing the team to strategize on how best to use their resources. This led to lots situations like "If I play my XXXX card, and you play your XXXX card to help me, then he can play the XXXX card on top of that." It was a little odd when we'd play at conventions, since it seems that few other players used this same approach.
    \n
    \nLastly, as I mentioned above, my first real writing experience was writing short adventures for TORG, including "Full Moon Draw" and "Breaking New Ground" in the Full Moon Draw adventure collection, and "Bad Business" in the Cylent Scream adventure collection. I also contributed to "Pixaud's Practical Grimoire" and I wrote "The Storm Knights' Guide to the Possibility Wars" (about which someone upthread had some very nice things to say, which is always gratifying, even 16 years later).
    \n
    \nI don't have time to play RPGs much at all these days, but every now and again when I get the urge to drag out some games, TORG is near the top of the list.

    \n
    \nI have mixed thoughts about TORG, but I liked it enough to commit a vile act of thread necromancy.
    \n
    \nI used to really like the Drama Deck and I remember thinking: man, this was all you really needed for the game, Greg Gordon, why didn't you take it just a little bit further (I complained about this when Mike Webb ran me through my first embarrassing session over a decade ago.) ?!?!?
    \n
    \nI always thought it would have been really cool to make the game more about all the cheap cliches that these pulp realities were dumping on the Earth, and maybe create more mixed potential zones.
    \n
    \nSo, like, you'd play cards that invoked specific cliches which had various powers or granted various bonuses depending on the local possibility energy or world laws.
    \n
    \nI also thought it would have been neat to scale it down a little and present the story of a single city where all these realities warped the skyline (so it's more like a disaster survival story, at first, then the global stuff might come later.)
    \n
    \nWe tried that once (one neighborhood was orrorsh, one was aysle, one was the cyberpapacy, etc.) It was easier to get players together and no less beleivable than the core premise behind TORG anyway. (We just assumed that the invasion had somehow cut off the city.)
    \n
    \n(For example, if you re-did the drama deck, subplot cards like Monologue might have the same effect in all realities, but you might have Technobabble cards that work best in Nippon or the Cyberpapacy, but not so well at all in the Living World. You could have Granola tropes that worked better there.)
