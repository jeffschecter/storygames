\n I'm looking hard at Monsterhearts, a game I'm very excited about, and wondering whether it might be missing some MC advice or procedures. You see, I'm very fond of the game and just about everything about it, but I'm really not a huge fan of the source material. (More accurately, I just haven't been exposed to much of it.)

However, I'm not sure whether it's actually missing that stuff--those MC procedures to develop material for play. Maybe I'm just not seeing certain things because I'm not very familiar with the genre? If that's the case, I want to know what some of your tricks might be--the stuff that makes your Monsterhearts games rock. I want to know which parts I'm missing because I don't understand the game, and which I'm missing because I don't know enough about the genre.

The Area I'm Looking At

Let me tell you what I'm seeing, and do a quick comparison to Apocalypse World, which I feel like I have a better handle on (even though I'm not super familiar with that genre, either).

* Monsterhearts provides great "higher-level" support for the MC: just like AW, there are Principles and Agenda which tell you where you should be headed as you play the game. Fantastic! These apply to the game as a whole, from start to finish. Things like "blanket in darkness/barf forth apocalyptica": these are over-arching principles of play.
* Also, just like AW, Monsterhearts gives you lots of tools for moment-to-moment play, in the form of MC moves. Great, once again. This tells you what you can say at any given point in play.

However, I can't escape this feeling that the intermediary level is missing: the stuff that's larger than the moment-to-moment minutiae of play, but smaller than the agenda of the game entire. The kind of stuff you might use to generate material for a session or two of play, for example.

How do you go about developing material for what Forge-speak terms "Situation" (significant story developments which push the whole thing along)?

The game positions the PCs relative to each other fairly well ("Backstory" and Strings), but not relative to the world at large (NPCs, institutions, etc.). The only real tool I'm seeing for this is the class seating chart, but putting it to use is not an obvious thing to me. (This is probably my unfamiliarity with the genre speaking.)

So, I suppose I feel lost when it comes to creating adversity, situation, and NPCs for the characters to interact with. Sure, I can bring in my GM tools from other games (e.g. Bangs from Sorcerer, certain character-Flag techniques from games like TSoY, etc.), but first I want to see if there aren't tools already present in Monsterhearts which serve this purpose better. After all, so many people seem to be having a great time with this game, right?

I know that "ask provocative questions" is a huge part of the toolkit, but that's enough without knowing a) what kinds of questions to ask, and b) how to follow up on those questions.

Differences from Apocalypse World

So, I started thinking about why this doesn't seem as difficult in Apocalypse World. When I initially looked at AW, I saw a similar issue, but the answer to it appeared fairly quickly. Here's how I can see AW answering this question:

1. Apocalypse World gives you Fronts and Threats. These are really strong forces baked into the Situation which the PCs cannot simply avoid. If they ignore the Fronts, threat moves will target them, and sooner or later those countdown clocks will turn their world upside down. Since many of the Front details threaten the PCs' lives, they are essentially unignorable.

Monsterhearts talks a little about a Menace, but suggests that a) it's not necessary to play, b) hints that you shouldn't let it totally take over what the game is about (and my feeling is that the source material also treats this kind of Menace as a backdrop rather than as a prime focus of the story), and c) doesn't give you tools like countdown clocks to make clear how the Menace will affect the characters' lives if it is left unchecked).

(My own gut feeling is that pushing the Menace too hard and too early changes the focus of the game pretty dramatically, and isn't necessarily desirable. I feel like I'd rather find ways to focus more on the inter-personal drama enveloping the PCs.)

2. The post-apocalyptic, violent, anarchy-ridden setting suggests a million logical threats to the PCs' livelihood. There is no obvious status quo: there is, instead, ever-present danger. When at a loss, you can have some raiders attack or some cannibal cult try to eat everyone. In Monsterhearts, in contrast, there is an assumed status quo: the characters have at least somewhat stable lives, families, a regular source of food and a place to sleep, and go to school together. Burning down the town where they live and having them kicked out of school would change the basis of the game dramatically: what do we do next?

3. On a related topic, the ever-present conceit of scarcity suggests multiple avenues for character goals: you must feed and clothe yourself, and fight for your own survival and safety. Simple moves like "make them buy" become very significant: spend your last 1-barter, and you have to start worrying about where you will find something to eat next.

This makes it very easy to ask provocative questions and set up PC-NPC-PC triangles. "Where do you get your ammo?" "Where does the tribe get its drinking water?" "How will you feed three extra mouths in your group?" "Who refuels your bikes? What do they usually ask for in return?" Pick a limited resource and ask a question about it.

Each playbook has some suggested or implied means for using their skills to procure barter/resources and survive. Pointing out any kind of basic scarcity immediately creates a situation which must be dealt with. (You're a killer-for-hire? How are you going to find someone to kill now that you're out of barter?)

4. Finally, many of the playbooks are seeded with start-of-the-session moves (Hardholder, Operator, Hocus, etc.) which are very likely to create unstable situations by themselves. If you have even one of these playbooks at the table, chances are good something tough is happening before you've even started playing. A single bad roll can give enough material for a long stretch of play.

So, all these factors (and maybe others I haven't thought of) provide an easy source of dramatic events, character motivation, and easy topics for provocative questions.

    \nThe Monsterhearts Equivalent?

    I'm not sure what takes the role of these tools at that intermediate level ("How do we establish unstable Situation in this session?") in Monsterhearts.

    The seating chart and the "have a fight, party, or disappearance" are great tools. But what are they tools for? Is drama guaranteed simply by having someone throw a party? What if a teacher disappears but no one cares? As MC, I'd like to have some idea of what I'm trying to push or pull at by creating those situations, so I can better repurpose them for those goals.

    What would I do?

    I know I would want to ask the characters to tell me about the kind of trouble they're experiencing:

    * in their school environment,
    * in their family or home environment, and/or
    * in their romantic lives.

    I would try to find out what kind of significant source of unhappiness is plaguing them. Is their monstrous nature negatively impacting one of those three things? What do they wish they could change about their lives right now? How could they go about that?

    Essentially, this would be not unlike establishing in-fiction goals for each character. Basically, I would want to write up a Kicker for each character.

    But those things are not really in the rules: it says, "ask provocative questions", yes, but leaves it at that. It's one thing you can do, but not something you *must* do in order to play.

    So, am I missing something? What do you do when you MC Monsterhearts? Do you do anything that's not in the book, but you feel is essential to your game working?

    I'm trying to find out whether my worries are about a gap in the text or just a gap in my understanding of the genre. If I watched more vampire teen dramas, would my concerns disappear?

    (Quick example: For instance, out of curiosity, I watched the first two episodes of Buffy the Vampire Slayer. From that source I can conclude that one way to run Monsterhearts is to establish a very frightening Menace right from the start. However, I don't feel that the story developed in those first two episodes really gets to the good stuff in the game. There isn't a whole lot of PC-PC interaction, I don't see anyone dealing with body horror, and I don't see a whole lot of Strings being used. It seems to me that the Monsterhearts *game* has a lot more depth to it than what I saw in those two episodes. Will I feel the same way about a lot of other source material? I don't want to watch and/or read a ton of stories that I'm not really that keen on.)

    My feeling is that the "scarcity" in Monsterhearts is all about:

    * Acceptance
    * Love/comfort
    * Popularity

    But targeting those is less obvious when a character, as written, starts out as a blank slate on those points.

    \n You have a pretty good handle on it Paul! I wouldn't stress it too much. Just play as the rules say: Create characters together, author your world with collaborative storytelling and establish the starting situation through the hooks in the playbooks. The seating chart introduces named NPCs with connections developed through provocative questioning. Questions that target what the characters think, feel and how might they act on those impulses? The party, fight or disappearance allow you to frame the stakes you are interested in as MC in a well known and familiar 'setting' using all the disparate information garnered through chargen. Re-incorporate that stuff like crazy. If the players authored something, then they are interested in it, so threaten it, take it away, make it part of an untenable situation or hard choice or give it long term consequences.

    I'd highly recommend writing emotional connections / physical dalliances / wants and needs / status arrows all over the seating chart. It needs to be your Rel-map of the tension and drama established. I like to physically hand out 'strings' too. We use little red stones for harm and lengths of chunky yarn for strings.

    Don't forget the moves themselves give you all sorts of little tidbits that feed the intermediate level of play that you feel is absent. Let things stew for a while, build on what's immediate then come back to some of the questions you are curious about.

    Aggressively frame scenes. Ask the players (by addressing the characters) what they want and where they want to be. Let them in on scene framing and antagonise the heart out of their intent. Once moves start snowballing, make the hardest MC move you dare, and generally narrative effects are far more juicy than mechanical ones.

    \n Paul,

    You're right that Monsterhearts works differently from Apocalypse World. Where Apocalypse World uses Fronts, Monsterhearts doesn't. Instead (a bit like Fiasco), it relies on a fizzing relationship map, which draws in both PCs and NPCs. That's where you get your Situation from.

    I think the tool you're missing is "Make monsters seem human; make humans seem monstrous". (There are probably others, but that's the one that comes to mind). Remember the example, in which the seedy, faintly-monstrous parent of one character is coming on to the underage student, who is another character? (I haven't got the book to hand, but it's the one where she bends over to get a beer from the refrigerator).

    That's the sort of situation you want. It comes straight from the relationship map. You do it by making the parent predatory, in a faintly-but-not-completely monstrous way.

    In a similar way, look at the PC's relationships and think how to make them darker and more monstrous.

    Once you've chosen your Skin, stats, moves, and so on, take a look at this list of problems:

    * My academics are really suffering: I'm in danger of flunking some important classes, or worse.
    * My family, my home, or my life outside school is a dysfunctional mess, unstable, hurtful, jarring.

    \n This does't directly pertain to your question. However, it is a piece of advice about MCing from one of my friends blogs that I think is useful. And since you've asked about tools to MC with I feel it is related enough to post.

    Things I'm still learning:

    Punch hard. Punch so hard they should see stars. I had said earlier you had to watch how hard you punch. It's all lies... if they take the time to freak out and cry about that, it's fine. The players are going to take those moments to go into a corner and cry a good, long hard time. However, the beauty of Monsterhearts is that you then as the MC say, "What do you do?" And a good player will eventually realize that while sitting in a corner and crying is a thing to do, eventually you come out of that corner swinging. And oh, how amazingly delicious it is.

    Tell them everything. The power of the "gotcha!" is a fun toy in the gaming toolbox, but it ends up getting used terribly, and it just pisses people off. I've definitely had to lean back and hand over information, stuff I would have been completely happy keeping hidden until later. I've found that the info I've given has made their lives generally more complicated and an overall better story. They know all sorts of things about the demon Rife, but haven't met him yet. Some of them... even seem to have some sympathy for his problems. Awesome.

    When you're done for the day, stop. I've now run a few times where I've kept going past where my brain was able to deal out enough stuff. In the writeup for the game, it says you'll do a session for about 4 hours. We've generally gone way longer, just because damn, they like to talk to one another. I've found my trigger from when I've gone too far is when I have an NPC show up and ask about something, for no reason. "Why are they doing that?" ... "I don't know," I reply to myself. I've found that if you don't find a nice stopping point, the best thing to do is just stop, nearly cold, maybe do a sum-up. It may feel like it's in the middle of things, it may feel awkward not having a nice episode end, and it's totally fine.

      \n This is a great thread! (What follows are just random observations and experiences YMMV)

      I don't think an MH MC will have a problem with material if they ask the right questions of the players so that players start the game with something their characters want. You really don't need much for an evening's play and trying to squeeze in too many issues can make the game feel rushed and superficial.

      You can tell from player responses to your questions whether or not the question you've chosen is going to be a hot button topic. They often seek to adjust, clarify or add to the elements created by the questions when they are interested in something. Once you hit on something at least one player really goes for, you have insta-story and the game will just about run itself in my experience.

      I've not had problems with material, I've had more problems with player disappointment about not getting to explore certain issues.


      I use the following a lot...There is nothing wrong with not detailing something cool beforehand. You can stop the game at some point to detail it or make it up on the fly once you have other material for context. This is kinda like "Draw maps, leave blanks" from DW.

      The best games of MH I've had involved the players initially playing playing a pure high school story with the monster thing on the periphery. Having it on the periphery means that one of the players (including the MC) can bring it back with a vengeance just when it's most convenient for the players.

      The not-so-great games of MH I've had have involved the players are trying to be monstrous to each other, to NPCs and in public from the outset with no build-up. I had one game where someone tore someone up in front of witnesses who then put a video up on the web. The game turned into "Monster Police Chase Across Sydney". *sigh*

      My games of AW are about relationships but are otherwise about the threats the characters face. There can be a strong external focus in AW. (This varies by player and exact setting and group dynamic of course)

      My games of MH are about relationships too but they have a far more inward focus and I really push hard to keep them that way. In my games, I find "What do you do?" to not be the best question sometimes. I ask "How do you feel?" instead or before asking the former.

      Sometimes ask players how their characters feel out of immediate context to really help the players into their characters heads. Teenagers have mood swings after all....

      When I'm a player in MH rather than the MC, I sort of treat my PC like a stolen car... When deciding how to respond to something, I imagine myself as a teenager who thinks the whole world revolves around them and who has very little self control. I have the best time in this game when I make bad decisions.

      YMMV of course. These are just my observations about what might be my favourite game.

      \n The short answer is that the old Coals/Flames way of structuring adversity was overly complicated and overly structured. Monsterhearts doesn't ache for lots of outside adversity, and so it doesn't make sense to have a big complicated structure for that adversity.

      Menaces feel like the right amount of complicated. You write one up when you have a really good idea for one. There are either one or several threats, and each has three moves attached to it (Craving/Offering/Capacity). Make those moves whenever you'd make any other moves, as you see fit. Add a custom move if you see fit. Done.

      Menaces are essentially a set of prompts/supports for introducing and handling outside adversity, should you feel the need to do so. Coals/Flames were a complicated management game for the MC to handle adversity, and they frankly didn't add anything or work in context.

      \n Thinking a little more about this, I've come up with a variation for a quick-start game:\n

      Monsterhearts: Setting Up

      Look at those five problems. Choose one.

      Describe your problem in basic terms, and then ask another PC one of the following:
      * "How are you the cause of my trouble or pain?"
      * "If I were to resolve this problem, how would it ruin your plans - or your life?"
      Happiness always comes at the expense of others.

      Now pick a different character and ask them:
      * "Why is it that I think I can't accomplish this goal without your help?"
      Loneliness is terrible weakness and terrible strength, all at once.

      MC, ask or tell each player what NPC might be involved in the situation, if they haven't said so yet.
      \nIt seems to me that after every character has done this, you should have some great material to start rolling with.

      We used "It All Ends in Tears", which really helped people get into the game right away without first having to discuss the genre or understand what kinds of problems were appropriate and which wouldn't be a good fit. It's not a bad way to start a game; I'm very happy with how it worked out.

      The IaWA oracle concept might be fruitful - in many ways, a really important aspect of M<3s (and which separates it from AW and a lot of other story games) is how the game is really fundamentally a PvP kind of engine.
