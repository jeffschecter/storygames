Who plays "them"?

TonyLB
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
You know ... "Them"! Who plays "them"?

Like "They say that Jessie and Hank were seen coming out of a sleazy motel ... you can totally believe it, seeing how they hang out so much."

"Them"! The "They" who say that thing. a.k.a. "Everybody" as in "Oh, everybody knows that Julio's a stoner. It's common knowledge."

Now I totally get that when a particular NPC (even an un-named NPC who only appears for a moment) starts circulating a rumor, then the GM is playing that NPC. And when a PC starts circulating a rumor then the player is playing that PC. But both of those are different from "Them." "They" are everywhere and nowhere. They aren't real people, no matter how passing their appearance in the fiction. They're virtual people ... an abstraction of a great pattern of socializing. And so, really, "They" aren't clearly in anyone's particular camp to play.

Y'know what's interesting? When I look back over my experience of playing social-intrigue-heavy games ... nobody plays "Them," and the absence shows. Is that just me?

    This is interesting to me because the last time I thought about this issue it was in relation to Capes. As far as I can tell, 'Them' is a perfect example of a non-person character -- 'Public Opinion' or 'The Public' -- and when I was trying to figure out how to adapt Capes into a Hong Kong action mode, I was particularly concerned about how this extremely important element to super-hero stories would be absent.

    For me the most obvious answer to the 'why' question is the classical Greek Chorus. Literally embodying 'Them' as a character with a voice -- but no body -- seems like it provides lots of opportunities for scenes that really revolve around character motivation and responsibility. Games where social ideals are important are going to benefit when those ideals are given a voice -- which I think is traditionally done through a single NPC or distributed through a total number of NPCs. Both seem to have their advantages, and fit into different genres.
