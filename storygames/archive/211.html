Setting Material and the Three Bears

James_Nostack
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
I've been looking over a lot of old gaming books lately--you know, the ones from the late 80's/early 90's, the whole D&D Boxed Set thing, where there are 200 pages of "setting material" to use. Dark Sun. Dragonlance. Planescape. Nobilis. Those settings are too dense!

In comparison, there's Sorcerer, which is a great game, but for the longest time I couldn't get into it because there's just not enough setting. It was only after I made it all up that I realized how fun it could be. Capes and Trollbabe are the same way--I think they need a lot of heavy thinking until there's a setting I'd like to game in. Those settings are too skimpy!

Then you have The Shadow of Yesterday, which has enough setting to seem enticing, but not so much that it's intimidating. That setting is Just Right! (I haven't read Dogs, but it sounds like it might be the same way.)

Two Questions:
1. Am I completely crazy, or is there something to the "some setting great, too much setting sucks" phenomenon?

2. If so, what does the Just Right amount include? What doesn't it include?

  • jhkim
    February 2006
    They make everything more meaningful, to me at least. So, for example, my James Bond 007 game was set in the mid-1980s. All of their espionage missions had intriguing irony to them played out from the context of 2005. So it made it more interesting that they went to Kamal Khan's hideout near his homeland of Russian-occupied Afghanistan rather than in some fictionalized country.

    Within the Harn game, it also makes a difference. For example, my Harn character recently converted from Peonianism to Laranianism. To me, switching out the Earth Goddess to the Warrior Goddess in most games religions would be dull. i.e. I erase the "Deity" box on the character sheet and write in a new one. In Harn, though, I have a better picture of the religions as cultural forces. Peoni and Larani are tied in ways that other religions are not -- both of them are part of the Emelrene culture, and their holy writings are both in Emela. Save-K'nor also has it's seat in Emelrene, but it is foreign in origin, having come from the Azeri highlands.

    Having the detail means gives multi-layered context to all the characters and events of the game. So they can be interpreted at different levels and reflected on.

    Mostly I'm answering the second question, as the first question has been handily answered with Ben's setting/situation/color distinctions.

    I approach the book as a set of instructions -- explicit and implicit -- to guide the players in playing the game. As such, my Setting chapters are all first-person narratives with the Color dial ratcheted to 11. Generally speaking, the Setting presented actually has very few actual, crunchy details; if you look deeper, it's all flash and bang Color. Each segment comprises a different Situation that might arise in play (although I don't ever expect anyone to actually play those situations, as I have other situation creation rules provided). So my "setting" chapters are actually 75% Color, 20% (unusable) Situation, and maybe 5% Setting. The important thing, though, is that it all (should) read more or less like people playing the game -- it's all examples, suggestions, and demonstrations of how to develop your own roleplay.

    The thing of it is, what you need in actual play is a Situation to address and Color to express that address. You can try to build Situation out of provided Setting details, but this runs into the problem that the Setting may not provide all the needed pieces or have pieces that conflict or subvert the Situation you're trying to create. I find it much more useful to create a Situation from player flags and then flesh out a Setting that that Situation might arise out of. As for the Color, I favor games that are incredibly loose with "canonical" Color and allow players to make up their own willy-nilly -- in which case all you need to provide in the book are guidelines and suggestions.

    I've definitely come to realize too much setting doesn't work. But setting definitely also needs situation. My take on various settings:

    Glorantha: this was cool when I mostly relied on Cults of Prax and Cults of Terror, plus various maps. Now I look at my >1 shelf of Glorantha stuff, and wonder if I could ever run it again. There wasn't a lot of situation in my early stuff, though there was the ever present Lunar/Orlanth tension, but I never really played that, Apple Lane and Snake Pipe Hollow provided some good situation though.

    Tekumel: Lots of grand detail. Too elegant. Didn't work. Definitely not enough situation.

    Diamond Throne (Arcana Unearthed/Evolved): Almost no detail. Almost no situation. Might as well have not been in the books. In my first campaign, by the time I dug up one possible situation, one player's background already contradicted my idea of situation (that the Mojh were against the Giants - PC was a Mojh who had become a Mojh in some way to protect a Giant).

    Blackmoor: I'm running my 2nd Cold Iron campaign in this setting. Very little detail in the original materials. Not a huge amount of situation. Mostly provided a neat map to build my own setting on top of.

    Harn: Too much detail. Not enough situation, at least not that I could recognize back in those days. Mostly flopped because Cold Iron just didn't match the setting well (though the campaign actually was quite fun - it just really didn't make much use of the setting, and setting stuff kinda got all messed up by PCs playing clerics and followers of the "more or less evil" deities).

    Nowadays, I've certainly got a keen eye for what I want out of a setting. I don't want a huge amount of detail, though I would want more detail for a sim game (Glorantha worked really nice for sim). I would expect the setting to be relevant to the game, so for gamist, kill things and get treasure, Cold Iron, I mostly want a map that has wild places to go find monsters in, provide religions that adventuring clerics would follow and gain cool spells from. Dogs has a perfect setting for it's game (though I've tossed around the idea of a fantasy setting, and replace gunslinging with magic or something - though that might blur things with demonic influence and such).

    Thinking about this makes me realize now one of the things that I always look for when I look for a module to inspire adventures (I use D&D modules all the time in Cold Iron and Rune Quest). I'm looking for situation that fits my play style - and I think I did actually look for somewhat differnt moduels in that sim RQ campaign than I used in gamist Cold Iron.
