What has been will be again, what has been done will be done again; there is nothing new under the sun. Is there anything of which one can say, "Look! This is something new"? It was here already, long ago; it was here before our time. There is no remembrance of men of old, and even those who are yet to come will not be remembered by those who follow.
\nI'm spending the day on my newly-built veranda, rereading Interactive Fantasy from 1994 and 1995. It's hot and sunny, and I see once again that all my seemingly original thoughts were thought by somebody else long ago.

* Role-playing as a way of processing archetypes, a form of unconscious therapy? IF issue 2: "Freud and Campbell: myth and archetype in RPGs". Oh yes, I remember reading that, actually.
* Education and role-playing, something we're focusing on at this year's Knutepunkt - surely a new trend? IF issue 2, again: "Special feature: Role-playing and education".

Oh, some things have progressed - we have new terminology, which is good. (Although, again from IF issue 2: "On the Vocabulary of Role-playing"). And Ron has defined narrativism in a useful way (well, I assume it was him). Still, though - \n
And do-on't throw the past away
You might need it some other rainy day
Dreams can come true again
When everything old is new again
\nBTW, what happened to the people behind IF?
James Wallis runs the games consultancy spaaace.com, and occasionally posts excellent stuff on his blog.
Andrew Rilstone has worked a bit on computer game design - and designed games for LEGO. I think he's currently a freelance journalist. His blog has precious little on RPGs.


(Some of the IF articles can be found online at The Oracle, which I'd never heard of before I started googling).

    The language you use perhaps is part of the problem. Its not just the terminology that has changes, but also the points of view, the body of available evidence, and the common practices that have changed.
