    Posted By: Hans c-oAlso, only the "B" series of modules works with Basic, right?
    \nRight. Or, more accurately, the "B" series covers levels 1-3, just like the Basic Set does.

    My (completely biased) reviews:

    B1: In Search of the Unknown. Avoid, unless you're specifically looking to push your player's nostalgia buttons. C-.
    B2: Keep on the Borderlands. A classic, but perhaps overused. B+.
    B3: Palace of the Silver Princess. Wacky and kind of loveable. If you look around, I bet you can find the original, unpublished, even wackier version as a PDF! A.
    B4: The Lost City. Atmospheric, and expandable to an entire "Lost City" campaign, if that's your cuppa. A.
    B5: Horror on the Hill. In some ways, a wilderness + dungeon slog. But it has some nice stuff, and has been surprisingly well thought out. I'm rather fond of it. B+.
    B6: The Veiled Society. In theory, a great sandboxy murder mystery. I could never figure out how to, you know, actually play it. C-.
    B7: Rahasia. Oozing with atmosphere, but some annoying bits (the teleportation thing is there just to make mapping harder). C+.
    B8: Journey to the Rock. Really kind of terrible, unless you are playing with ten-year-olds for whom this kind of thing is new and exciting. That's how old I was when I played it, and it was AWESOME. C+.
    B9: Castle Caldwell and Beyond. A series of dungeon crawls, each less interesting than the last. D+.

    Posted By: Pjack
    B6: The Veiled Society. In theory, a great sandboxy murder mystery. I could never figure out how to, you know, actually play it. C-.
    \nB6 is definitely odd. It comes with a bunch of fold-up paper cottages and stand-up character counters for running a dramatic chase scene through a dense urban neighbourhood, but since the route of the chase is fixed (no alleys or crossroads, just a single long crooked street) and the resolution of the chase is largely pre-scripted, using these props is not a challenge so much as a bit of puppetry. Its also an early example of a big authority figure demanding a mission of the party, giving them limited resources to do it, yet paradoxically employing vast resources to force the PC's onto the job. Not a device I like in D&D.

    You know, this is interesting. I love B1, and loathe B2. I am also of the generation before the Basic D&D generation - I came in with Holmes, which originally was packaged with B1 but changed to B2 once B2 existed.

    I think B1 is much more a "0e" little-books style adventure, firmly Gygaxian, dungeon-centric, and focused on player skill. It is one kind of "old school D&D".

    B2 is very different. It's made up mini-dungeons arranged in such a way that the players can pick and choose what to engage with IF they do some scouting. It has a framing device that presumes the PCs are disposed to do good - or at least take requests from authority figures. It has a very different feel to me, one that's much more in the fantasy adventure vein and less in the small-unit tactics with thin veneer of fantasy color that I identify with MY early-D&D experience.

    The "old school" truly is vast and contains multitudes.
