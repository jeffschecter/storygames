\nJason's suggestion was ignored by the people who were criticizing him in the "how to ignore" thread, but I think it might be a valuable thing. (Though I acknowledge that more likely it will end in tears, recrimination, and gnashing of teeth. But hey, it's worth a try.)

So it seems like a lot of threads devolve into specious arguments and poo-flinging here on Story-Games. Sure, sometimes it's to be expected - like with my JRPG thread. Hell, that's like going up to a biker in a bar and saying "hey, do you think your girlfriend's ugly or is it just me?". But a lot of times, people are just trying to have a conversation about something when other people invade the thread and start crapping all over it by declaiming anyone who agrees with the thread op and flinging personal attacks while tenaciously clinging to some imagined moral high ground.


(taken from XKCD, link here)

I think that comics pretty much sums up what happens here. People get so caught up in being RIGHT that they forget that other people might have equally valid perspectives, or that they might not be a native English speaker, or that they might have been tired while writing their post, or that they might have been distracted by a podcast while writing, or one of a million factors that might obscure the original intent. Hell, a lot of the time people spend a few days shouting at each other in a thread only to discover they were talking about the same thing.

Frankly, I'd like to see some civility. I'd like to see conversations that don't always devolve in horrible personal attacks where people dogpile the thread OP by calling them a racist, or a sexist, or a prude, or communist, or an elitist or whatever until the person who started the thread gives up and walks away.

But maybe that's asking too much.

    \n My friend Liam once said that the decline of forum etiquette is based on social currency. When a forum begins, you get social currency (attention OR good feelings) by posting thoughtful and insightful things. But the moment someone gains social currency by being unpleasant, it erodes the community. Because-- even if you try to only get social currency by being thoughtful and insightful-- everyone knows, deep down, the easier path to social currency is to be loud or unpleasant. That knowledge makes it more difficult to back down when someone else is a jerk, or resist being clever at someone else's expense. A community, as a whole, will always slowly gravitate towards the easiest way to get social currency. That's entropy at work.

    In an ideal world, people would be as disgusted by people thumping their chests over how horrible Viet Nam was as they were about the idea that it was a good intentioned thing gone wrong. Sam wasn't trying to get social currency by being provocative, I don't think. But the second the troll started baiting him, a lot of people realized there was social currency to be had with derailing the thread and dogpiling about the horrors of war, and that's what happened. And it did nothing but make the posters feel better, raise said posters in each other's mutual esteem, and lower the tone of discourse on Story Games in general.

    The only way to combat the entropy is to withhold social currency, which is difficult to do without a ton of groupthink or strong moderation, neither of which Story Games has in quantities high enough to do this.

    \n About 10 years ago, I (mostly accidentally) started following a specific strategy when someone would disagree with a post I made in a forum. It's made using forums more pleasant, at least from my point of view. On the minus side, it can be hard to do. On the plus side, it is an approach that is entirely in your hands and doesn't rely on changing other people's behavior (which isn't your job). Works like this:

    1) When confronted by a post that disagrees with what you are saying (regardless of the tone of that disagreement), make the assumption that the reason for the disagreement is that your original post didn't actually say what you meant it to say. That is, the person is disagreeing because they didn't understand what you are saying and this is not because they are stupid, but because your writing sucks.

    2) Regardless if there is evidence to support the assumption that you are being misunderstood or not, or even if the idea of it doesn't even make any sense to you, proceed as if this assumption is true, even if you are sure it isn't. Clarify the point you are making, totally ignoring any inflammatory stuff that may or may not have been in the reply. Use an example if you can.

    3) Repeat until any misunderstanding is cleared up or you are convinced that the person really does disagree with you. All the while, be just as polite as can be, even in the face of asshattery.

    4) Once you reach this point, the other person has heard what you have to say. You've heard what he's had to say. You disagree. So, most important point: now you're done. There is no further need to pay attention to this person in this matter or belabor the point. You will not convince him. He will not convince you. Just move on, without any further explanation or comment at all. If you are continuing in the thread talking to other readers, feel free to read the disagreeing guy's posts, but make no response to them, or even recognition that they were made.

    That's it. Now, when you read it said like that, it seems really stupid.

    But damned if it doesn't work.

    If enough people in a forum take this (or a similar) approach, the net effect usually turns out to prevent the "social currency from being a douchebag" from being gained, or even perceived. I'm not sure why this happens, but it's been the case more often than not.

      \n I notice the cliquishness here and one of the smarter gamers I know came here and posted a bit and felt completely dismissed and snubbed for a perceived lack of sophistication. He left and vowed never to come back. I think that kind of Forge-based hierarchy of knowledge has diminished a lot in the last couple of years, but there is still a hierarchy of status which I think is attributable to so many posters here being designers and having "names". This is also a phenomenon of this place being a spin-off of the Forge, which is basically a cult of personality and strongly encouraged that kind of status hierarchy. And the storygames movement started off being very self-congratulating, as a marketing mechanism ("Your game rocks!" "No, yours does!").

      This combined with a very general consensus on politics and gaming ideology means that it's not a very welcoming place for a newcomer who doesn't know either the personalities or hasn't already bought into a pro-storygaming ideology.

      It makes for a place where one poster can say something fairly outrageous or aggressive and not really get called on it and another can do something that goes against the general cultural grain and get a lot of shit for it.

      It's also a function of size. When a forum is really small, people welcome any newcomer. When it's really massive, people tend to look at content of the post rather than the poster. When it's medium like this, there is a tendency to pay attention to the important people.

      It really reminds me of my liberal arts college, where there was a similar social dynamic.

      \n I dunno about moderation for the forums. It seems that most of the bad behaviour here is based on posturing, ego-swelling and passive-aggressive sniping. That's hard to moderate because it's by nature a blurry line. There are people here who make posts for the same reason a kid takes a magnifying glass to an anthill. There are people here who just want to become the next 'It Designer' in this tiny sliver of a community and see others here as a means to that end. There are the people who think that playing edgy or uncomfortable games or character concepts that this makes them hardcore visionaries, and that they have to broadcast this constantly.

      These are the people that make the site tiresome for me. I don't even think they're the majority, but they do have the loudest voices, because they have specific agendas.

      What to do about it? I think that somehow the site needs to veer more sharply to welcoming community of gamers and away from intellectual forum. As stated, the latter already exists in the Forge and now Praxis. Maybe there should be more forceful shunting of certain topics to Praxis. This should be a place where everyone sees and respects each other as real people, instead of Olde Internette, where you adopt a persona and play it like you would in a game (see lordgoon's unintentionally ridiculous purple prose above). It's hard to want to contribute here when you wonder if someone is trying to sell you something or just toy with you.

      All that said, yeah, the place ain't too shabby at all in the grand scheme of things. I think Andy himself sets the proper tone for what the site should be like and is doing a good job.
