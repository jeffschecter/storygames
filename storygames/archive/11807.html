\n I have looked high and low both on the App Store and on the web looking for D&D adventures that take advantage of the smaller scale of the iPhone but I have found nothing. Is this becouse the task is too difficult or has no one else thought of it? Perhaps the market is just too small for adventure writers to bother or the process of writing an iPhone app or web app viewable on an iPhone is too difficult. What do you think is keeping these two geeky things from converging?

    \n There's only one possibility! Either
    1. Roleplaying geeks don't use apple products.
    or
    2. Apple users don't play roleplaying games.

    Ok, jokes aside, no idea. I tried long and in vain to find a dice roller app for my symbian mobile, too. Apparently nobody bothers yet, the tech is not quite there in terms of being really useful at the gaming table. Also, if you pull out a phone during a session, you're generally frowned upon. I believe we gamers are rather technophobic when it comes to the tabletop (even if we're gadget geeks when away from it).

    \n Clause 3 of the GSL:\n
    3.\tLicensed Products. The license granted in Section 4 is for use solely in connection with Licensee\u2019s publication, distribution, and sale of roleplaying games and roleplaying game supplements that contain the Licensed Materials and are published in a hardcover or soft-cover printed book format or in a single-download electronic book format (such as .pdf), and accessory products to the foregoing roleplaying games and roleplaying game supplements that are not otherwise listed as excluded in Section 5.5 (\u201cLicensed Products\u201d).
    \nAnd relevant bits of 5.5:\n
    5.5\tLicensed Products.\tThis License applies solely to Licensed Products as defined in Section 3 and to the specified uses set forth in Section 4. For the avoidance of doubt, and by way of example only, no Licensed Product will (a) include web sites, interactive products, miniatures, or character creators...
    \nSo all you could really do is an ebook, which IMHO isn't really woth theeffort. You couldn't do a D&D adventure that had a built-in die roller. All this is 4e only, of course -- 3e is somewhat more forgiving.

    \n Thanks Bryant, that explains alot! Seems like they were trying to stop anyone making a free version of the DDI. It kindof makes sense but it is a pain in the ass. E-books of games should be interactive with hyperlinked texts to the important rule bits. You could easily do the full text next to the abbreviated text that removes all of the rules into hyperlinks and also have an outline version that allows the GM who knows the adventure well to only bring up those sections that he or she wants. Fully index the whole thing and store items, spells, monsters, NPCs and even room descriptions as searchable data.

    If someone with access to a back log of 3.x material decided to build an app like that they could expand it so that it is not just an individual adventure app but rather a gateway app where the base app is free and each individual modual costs some ammount of money. With that kind of library you could make all of the user's advertures searchable together which would mean that each adventure they buy enhances the usefulness of the app.

    I don't know maybe I am crazy but it seems like a solid idea to me.
