I was talking to a friend once who works for the prison system, and the conversation turned to rehabilitation/recidivism. He made a comment which kind of took me off guard-he said that most prison employees simply don't believe that rehabilitation works, and that their job as they see it is basically to separate prisoners from society (for a certain length of time, anyway) in an attempt to keep people safe.

Of course, this got me thinking of game setting ideas. When did exile fall out of fashion as a punishment? Currently, when we want to keep criminals away from the population forever (and if we're too squeamish to execute them), we keep them in jail without parole until they die-at great cost to taxpayers (especially as the inmates age and require greater medical care). What if, instead, we shipped them off somewhere-say, a deserted island-and left them to their fates? What would happen?

I'm guessing you could only do this with the worst-of-the-worst: the truly violent, hardcore monsters.

Some things I thought of:

1. You avoid the capital punishment moral debate. Sure, they'll probably die-the place'll likely turn into a Lord of the Flies-type situation, or it will be difficult to find enough potable water/food/shelter to support the population, there's no medical care, etc...but you aren't the one doing the actual killing. Nature is.

2. Would the prisoners be trained in basic survival/living-off-the-land skills before being shipped out? What kind of equipment would they be issued...knives? Flint and steel? Would there be scheduled food drops to the island? If so, wouldn't those just be seized by the strongest gang?

3. What about the media? Would VICE news try to infiltrate to show the people back home the true story of how people are living and dying on the island?

4. How would the families and friends of the convicts deal with this situation? There would likely be no mail or other form of contact once the prisoner was resettled, for instance.

What do you all think?

    \n It sure sounds like you want to discuss the philosophy and politics of exile as an institution of legal punishment more than a game based on the concept

    Not that this wouldn't make for a valid game setting. For my own purposes I would go with Australia or some such historical location of criminal exile, for the added realism and organic shades of grey. A dystopic desert island reality tv murder-fest would go down well with fans of Battle Royale and such, of course.

    If I wanted an ahistorical setting, I would go with a prison planet. In the 23rd century Earth's criminals are shipped off to some moon of Jupiter to build their own society as best they can. At the time the game occurs this has been going on for a while, and there's actually increasing debate about opening another moon as a penal colony because the original choice has become too civilized and valuable in the process of colonization.

    \n Well, in the real world civilization emerges out of penal colonies for at least the following reasons:
    * No society has generally speaking been so dystopian as to exile people into certain death; rather, the land of exile generally has some value and hope, and therefore there are also colonists joining the colony voluntarily or semi-voluntarily, and governance of key resources (harbours, trading posts) if nothing else. Being a penal colony is a stage of development, not a static end-point. (Except of course for those penal colonies that literally are on barren islands in the middle of nowhere; however, those have historically been more like open-air prisons, rather than the hands-off kind of thing we're discussing here.)
    * Real people are almost always not "vicious criminals" as some sort of objective classification, and the idea that such couldn't start a community when left to their own devices smacks amusingly of 19th century Romanticist ideas. I would frankly be amazed if a random selection of criminals from any society couldn't do anything that a random selection of the population at large could.
    * "Civilization" is just the name for that which propagates itself in the social sphere. Take a million apes, let the less viable 99% kill each other (or whatever anti-civilized behaviours they might engage in), and those who are left will pretty much by default have some sort of "civilization".

    Were I developing a game about this topic, I would, as is my inclination, select a scenario with maximal ambiguity and possibilities, the sort where we can discover in play whether our characters are fundamentally unfit human specimens, whether the land of exile can be made fit for human habitation, and so on. (This, as opposed to a game where the designer predetermines these thematic answers.)

    I could see a random table for finding out what crime brought you to the colony, for example, and a secret low-probability secondary check to see if you were actually innocent of the crime. Some characters might become non-criminal officers of the colonial infrastructure, too, in this random chargen. Just set up a bunch of people who approach the situation from a variety of angles, and then put them through the wringer; see what they make of their circumstances.

    \n Yes, Durance.

    However, there is also potential for making this into a "reality TV" show, as Eero suggests - that was my first reaction to this pitch.

    As for why we don't do this:

    1. The Earth is getting pretty crowded. There aren't too many places we could "send" people to where they'd have a good chance of survival but ALSO couldn't escape from easily.

    Exile worked well when transportation was a difficult thing. These days... not so much.

    We can "exile" people from our country... but then some other country has to take them. Who will do that? (Actually, it's quite possible that someone would... but in the long-term it's not obvious that this system would maintain itself.)

    2. I think most people have a strong reaction to the idea of sending potentially fragile/weak people (women, children, the wrongfully accused, the infirm, the sickly, the pregnant, elderly, etc) into a dangerous situation with known killers/rapists or other hardened criminals.

    Would you send the young nerdy kid who decided to sell pot to his classmates and the old man who was sentenced because his skin was the wrong colour to be stuck on an island with three child-murderers/serial-killers/sociopathic gang members?

    To most people, this doesn't *feel* right, or like justice.

    It's not an obvious problem.

    Otherwise, however, I'm quite fond of the idea of taking the "unwanted" out of society (meaning that each society/community consists of people who have chosen to be a part of it and live by its rules), so long as they have somewhere else viable to go.
