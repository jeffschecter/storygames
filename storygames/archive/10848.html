    \n Aside from the number specifics -

    Hero Wars has advice on running a game focused on the player characters shaping the world- it's full of possible themes and ways to shape a campaign ("The Hero Wars are between X & Y"). The idea is that you pick a culture/area being shifted by politics, power, war, magic, whatever and here are these people with the power to change out that comes out.

    Heroquest 1 backs out from that a bit. The advice is kinda there, and mixed with advice on how to run a more traditional "GM creates the story, players 'go through' it" kind of affair. All of the sample scenarios are designed for the latter style of play.

    Heroquest 2 takes a different tack in the latter - the GM may not prepare the whole story in advance, but the rules are designed to try to emulate climax points, around which the GM improvises. It's also the most complex in terms of intersecting rules interacting- the GM typically has several, several options in terms of how to run a single conflict, and how hard to set consequences.

    \n@neil: ok, cool. Edges seemed a bit, I dunno, much. Kind of like "we already have these other things to think about; do we NEED edges?"
    *looks up Edges* Yeah, permanent or recurring Edges and Handicaps make sense, but you have to read the basic definition a couple of times lest you think there's some kind of "spontaneous" Edge to worry about too. \n

    (p.135) "An edge is a number that is added to AP staked when the character's opponent must transfer or forfeit AP."
    \nMaybe I'm weird, but that initially sounded like edges were "created" by such a loss of AP. But I guess that's just saying how they interact with AP loss. Anyway. Thanks for the tables and the rules summary therein (thereat?) - they'll prove useful ^_^

    @bankuei: it sounds like I got the version that I'd be happiest with, then, thematically. Hero Wars has some very cool (and surprisingly related to stuff I learned at the Forge) story-game advice in the Narrator's Book - it tells players to drop "I try to..." when describing actions in favor of making clear statements, it derides Fortune-at-the-Beginning as causing dull and listless play, and throughout the rules advice, it reminds the Narrator to make choices based on the needs of the story, trumping things like sound tactical responses and logical accuracy. It doesn't advocate fudging dice anywhere either, which is something older games suggested when they claimed to be "about the story". No Rule Zero here! ^_^

    It's pretty amazing to think that this came out around the same time as the Pool and the Window and other simple-system, "rules should be seen and not heard" design styles. It's got some crunch, but not a lot, and it's pretty firmly Story Now in what it's encouraging the group to do. You could play a Hero Wars game that centers on just discovering parts of Glorantha and all that, but that would require very careful choice of Occupation and Magic keywords, otherwise the Narrator would still have a bushel full of things he could do to keep the plot more coherent and shapely. "Yeah, the guard at the gate has the rune of Urvairinus tattooed on his neck, and when you hand him your travel papers, he sees your matching rune on the back of your hand. He begs for your help!"
    Cool.
