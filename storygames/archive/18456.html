\n I haven't posted much around here lately.

Some have been watching my Google plus threads and blog...those who have been interested in the progress of the game are probably already aware of what's happening. Walkabout will be getting it's first formal introduction to the public over the coming weekend. This will happen at the 21st annual Phenomenon roleplaying convention in Canberra, Australia.

It's been a long road since the game was a finalist in Game Chef 2010, it's blown out, been streamlined, blown out again, been streamlined again...and is now hopefully close to the idea I originally envisioned, while still being playable.

For those who are interested, there will be a final beta release just after the convention (with revisions based on anything I learn there).

Maybe I'll follow this thread up with new insights gained about the game, the way it seems to work, where it's strong and where it needs work.

    \n Feedback from the Convention.

    It went well. The system creates some great story.

    For those who remember the earlier incarnation of the game which was a finalist from Game Chef a few years ago, there are some subtle differences. Instead of rolling dice, tokens are drawn, and now there is a more communal aspect to play...relationships play a far stronger role than individual abilities (but a character can have a relationship to concepts such as "combat" or "stealth"...or they could have a relationship with their "firearm").

    We drew tokens from rusty tins, which two groups felt improved the post apocalyptic vibe of the game.



    The games also made played in such a way that I know how to tweak the system further to really approach the style of storytelling that I had originally envisioned for the setting.

    Unless people are interested in having me post more here, further feedback about the sessions can be found over at my blog Observations of the Fox.

    The games also played in such a way that I know how to tweak the system further. Those tweaks will really allow the game to approach the style of storytelling that I had originally envisioned for the setting. That style of storytelling is purely trait based, with minimal impact from numbers, dice or elements too far removed from the narrative. A group could easily play the game while taking a bushwalk, pulling out a few small pieces of paper or card from one pouch to reference their character's relationships and a few tokens from another pouch to resolve a challenge or a scene while they are walking.
