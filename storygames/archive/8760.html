    \nThose are the ones that come immediately to mind. HeroQuest and Dogs in the Vineyard have make your own abilities, but have some core values that have locked names.

    It works pretty well, as long as there us a good sense of what PDQ calls the prenumbra of a trait (how wide it spreads). This can vary from group to group and game to game so it is a good idea to have a good general grasp on the comfort level of the prenumbras in use in the game.

    An example of trait prenumbras would be "Driver" as opposed to the more narrow "Getaway Driver" or "Thief" as opposed to "Cat Burglar", "Greaseman", "Con Man", or "Cut-Purse". The ease with which the group can assay if a trait is applicable to a test is important to making sure you get the play you all want. It also lets a group have a wider array of specialists by narrowing the prenumbras of abilities (or just a select ability like "Criminal" or "Thief" that all PCs will have in some manner). A heist movie game will want multiple types of tighter defined thieves to let everyone shine a bit.

    \n There's also Wushu, which I quite enjoyed.

    Over the Edge is still one of my favorite systems for the simple elegance of the traits. You decide what your Central Trait and Side Traits are, what they mean. Your central trait is broad, while the side traits are more focused. In play, you explain to the GM how a trait applies to a situation and you get to use it. In addition to spawning a thousand indie games, it's spawned a thousand homebrew games. I've used it for Star Wars, Supers, Modern Paranormal, and Fantasy.

    Posted By: Josh RobyUnknown Armies still has the four core stats \u2014 Strength, Speed, Mind, Soul.
    Yes. I have Over the Edge, and I can't believe I didn't think of it. Unknown Armies is one of my favorite games ever, but isn't what I'm talking about, because I'm talking about something with player-defined equivalents of Body, Speed, Mind, and Soul as opposed to just player-defined skills.

    I've glanced at the Risus website, but not enough to grok it. And PDQ I'm totally unfamiliar with (except what I've heard about it on podcasts and such).

    my nascent game idea involves PCs accomplishing things through various "madnesses" (and possibly one non-madness trait), but i've been having difficulty coming up with a good set of them that's small enough to be manageable without them being too broad. but given that the official response if something doesn't fit under the umbrella of a madness your character has is "Sorry, you can't do that" i'm not sure whether allowing for player-defined ones will be a good idea or not.
