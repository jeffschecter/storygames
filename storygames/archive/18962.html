\n In this thread Simon Burley was asking about how his game Squadron UK, in some ways a very British game, would go down in the States as it\u2019s a supers game and he was saying that comic culture is very much tied up with US cuture. This got me thinking, what about all those other games that reference aspects of another country\u2019s culture? Many of them will be games from outside the US and Canada referencing those countries, since North American culture is so pervasive these days (forex my game about delinquent aliens set in a US college frat house), but not all. There are currently a whole crop of games that make reference to anime and other aspects of Japanese culture- how do/will they go down in Japan? Then there\u2019s the Marquis of Ferrara, from my fellow Collectivists the Pompey Crew. And so on.

How do designers who reference the culture of other countries\u2019, or set their game wholesale in that culture, even though they\u2019re not from it themselves, approach the process? Do they attempt to be as realistic as possible, either by doing a huge amount of research or employing other folks, perhaps people from that country, as consultants? Or do they just write the game and hope for the best? Or do they not aim for verisimilitude at all, on the basis that its only a game after all, and not an entry in Wikipedia?

And how do folks from countries thus referenced feel when designers set those games in their country? There must occasionally be some real howlers, how do they react to them?

There are actually a number of settings I\u2019d like to use for games that are at a far remove from my own culture, the African Congo for one, which could so easily, if I\u2019m not careful, turn out disastrously and be excoriated by people from that culture as a gross travesty of it. Etc. And to finish, here\u2019s Stephen Fry raging at Alan Davies on QI for suggesting it was bizarre that someone from America in the 1880s should write a book about Roman chariot racers.

Thoughts?

    \n I am from a relatively small and marginal country on the world stage. I have no particular intent to stop discoursing upon the world because I am not "from there" for most parts of it. It would be an awful standard if we only allowed the big and important nations to have a say about the world at large, requiring everybody to limit their discourse based on arbitrary lines on a map. Goes for game design as well.

    When I write about the world in a roleplaying game, there is generally speaking an intent to the work as a whole, and that intent determines how I treat the subject matter. So it might be a game about How the World Really Is (or how I think it is, anyway), in which case I would find it important to make my case convincing. The game or other discourse might be about perceptions or specific historical narratives or how the world should be, and in all of the wide variety of situations the standards of quality in writing are slightly different. What satisfies me in the quality of my writing depends on the purpose of that writing, not on an universal set of standards for writing about Other Cultures. If this were different, then logically speaking there is no reason why I wouldn't need to impose that same universal standard on all communication I do about the world. If there is or should be such a standard, I haven't figured it out yet - as far as I can see, needs of communication differ across the board.

    When foreigners write about Finland or Nordic countries in general in a fictional context, they usually get the details subtly wrong so that the outcome has a plastic quality to it, and the events described often ring falsely. Quite often the falseness is simply because it seems weird to see somebody discuss a topic without using the accustomed tropes related to that topic, so even if their superficial facts are correct, the way they tell the story seems weird. This doesn't particularly bother me, that's just how it goes. If I got offended by something like that, just imagine how difficult I'd find it to deal with actual personal offenses and other small setbacks of ordinary life.

    The actively negative stereotype about Finland is that it's an illiterate, boorish nation of alcoholics. I don't particularly mind that one, either, but that might well be because I'm too secure - and perhaps the Finnish national identity is too secure, more importantly - to mind about old stereotypes. Perhaps this would be different for somebody who feels that their national identity is more beleaguered, more under question, so that nasty bigotry actually feels like it has a bite to its bark.

    \n As a student/scholar of foreign cultures, I offer the following advice:

    1. Try to be 100% honest. To yourself, to your readership, to your topic, to real-world Tibet. Strive for perfect honesty, whatever "honesty" means to you.

    2. Question your own motivations. Why do you want to make your work about Tibet specifically? Double-guess yourself, learn about what Graham correctly calls the politics of what you're doing, measure yourself against all possible such political standards and discover whether you're actually honest (and weren't instead being unwittingly dishonest).

    3. Learn about Tibet, with a passion. If you aren't honestly passionate about the topic, then you've got no business writing about Tibet.

    4. Temper your passion with an extra serving of honesty. Try to ensure you've learned enough about the topic that you're never writing out of enthusiasm alone. Strive to get the whole picture. both upsides and downsides. Pro-tip: this is where the majority of well-meaning writers trips and falls.

    5. Do never attempt to erase yourself from the picture. Be honest about being yourself writing about these topics. Be honest about your limits. Explaining your own involvement with the topic is part of this, as is including a proper bibliography.

    6. Discover that no topic, subject-matter or culture is something you own, ever. More likely, it own you. Be honest about not owning it, and do what logically follows from there.

    To invoke a personally relevant example, as an Italian I was only amused, in the best possible way, and sometimes genuinely flattered at reading The Marquis of Ferrara, and that's because the genuine honesty of the authors so clearly transpires from their work.

    One of the things that I like about sci-fi/fantasy/transrealism is that you can analogize absolutely everything. Can you not write a game about an analogue of Tibet? Even so, being culturally knowledgeable and respectful will not necessarily guarantee the same response in your punters. Politically, Tibet is tricky - I come at it from a perspective that it has been colonised by an oppressive power. However, if I were a loyal communist party apparatchik I'm sure I'd be saying Tibet has been liberated from a hierarchical regime that foists fairy tale beliefs on its people.
    \nI'm quoting this here because I think it illustrates the implicit assumption quite well. That assumption is that offending somebody is somehow inherently not only bad, but also the baddest thing possible. I mean, here we are worrying about the hypothetical hurt feelings of a Chinese communist colonialist (I take it as a given that any recognizable nation in political communion with another, while not given credible political choice about it, is in fact in a colonial relationship), and musing about solving the problem by not talking about Tibet.

    I often get the sense that the origin for this extremely vapid standard of discourse is somehow in the old privileged club mentality of western European, particularly Anglo culture: because it's just a hobby, and we want everybody to have a nice time, let's not talk about nasty political disagreements at the cards table. This should, I think, not be the standard of discourse for any artistic endeavour; treating a roleplaying community like it was a Bridge club ignores the fact that roleplaying as a narrative medium cannot avoid talking about substantial issues. And with substance inavoidably comes offense, you can always find somebody out there who'll get offended by anything that matters. Trying to distance all possible substantial topics into genre literature la-la-lands just postpones the issue, ultimately; do it long enough, and people will start drawing parallels between the orcs and blacks - and with justification, too.

    Given that offense per se is not a reliable gauge for what's appropriate and what's not, I'd like to suggest a different measure: attempt to capture the core idea of your art in your work, and capture it well. Create something that others will find interesting and elevating. Choose a target audience, and speak to them of matters you find substantial and interesting. Sometimes your choice of audience and topic will mean that somebody somewhere would prefer you not to talk; they may even put nasty labels on you, and they may even be right. However, this is still better than trying to preempt offense by constructing elevated standards of discourse. Even if you're the most horribly ignorant, bigoted racist imaginable, it is still better if you speak your mind honestly, rather than have you try to fake attitudes and politics that the majority (or right-thinking people, however you want to think about it) finds acceptable. If you don't feel it, there's no point trying to fake whatever brand of political sensitivity some external party might try to push at you - that does not make you any less racist, it just makes you a silent one. Rather, by being honest and clear about your ideas, you participate in the great dialogue of civilization. Free exchange of ideas is the last thing we should want to limit, and pressing for elevated standards of cultural sensitivity is a perfect example of a measure that comes with chilling effects: people will maintain their silence rather than risk being labeled an ignorant racist.

    All this is, of course, not to say that you shouldn't research the subject of your work. I think that should go without saying for any endeavour where we reach for excellence. However, equating an offensive work with an unresearched one runs the grave risk of ignoring political disagreement: what if that person actually researched their work perfectly well, but what you're actually offended by is their conclusions or choice of perspective? What if somebody can disagree with you without being an ignorant fool, what about that?

    \n Then again, I would say that it's a meager understanding of reality to assume that merely experiencing something is to understand it in some quintessential manner. As a good scientist and post-modernist I of course accept that there are no final truths, only iteratively useful models of reality Upholding local experience as somehow superior is just as romantic as the opposite assumption. (Such a thing exists. For example, Finnish public discourse often puts great weight on foreign perspectives, somehow assuming that e.g. a British journalist writing about Finland has a more objective viewpoint and more worldly context upon which to characterize what Finland is really like.)

    Combine the fact that people are often discussing subtly different things (e.g. perception of a thing vs. the reality of the thing) with the difficulty of ascertaining the truth about complex human things, and we arrive at the sort of general skepticism that colors some people's (including my own) attitude towards topics like this. It just seems immensely naive to decide on a model of interpretation like "the native viewpoint is always correct, worship it" and then aggressively throw all weight of your social authority behind it. Reality is so much more complex that I wouldn't want to make such blanket judgements about the appropriate ways to create art.

    Keeping that in mind, I'll reiterate my suggested measure: when writing about other cultures, or your own culture, or other things between the heaven and earth, study your motivations, choose to work on worthwhile projects, and then throw your talent behind making your work excellent. It genuinely seems to me that you can't go wrong doing it that way. Of course that's massively generalized advice, but then, as I outline above, the topic is so complex that any more specific advice seems like it only applies in a parochial manner to a narrow range of cases.

    In other words: the possible problems in you writing about Tibet can all be grouped into problems with the quality of the work. It is not a problem as long as you do it well.

    Do you work, but look at the problem from both angles. In the case of Tibet, which can be quite a hot issue for some, look at the pitfalls of pre-occupied Tibet, talk about those, and talk about the pitfalls of occupied Tibet too. Neither of these places were perfect, because nowhere is. And in order to accurately roleplay, or facilitate a roleplay that takes place in an area the players need that information and that context. So doing your research, and accurately conveying your research to readers, devoid from your personal opinion, is what the best game in this kind of category should aim for. Anything less I think would be an insult to their intelligence, and also feels a little like being hit with an opinion over the head, which is never appealing.

    Think of it from a position of: If I came to playing this game from a position of knowing only what a few newspaper articles had told me about the situation, would I be able to come out knowing and understanding a great deal more about how people think and live in this situation?

    That isn't to say that every game needs to be 'edutainment'(Much as I despise that phrase), though. Nor is it to say that everyone who plays your game for three hours is as experienced and able to comment on the situation as someone who has lived it. But they should come away with a better idea of what it means.
    The important part of exploring controversial matter is to explore. Not to exploit. And I think exploiting actually counts if you try and push either side of a controversial dialogue too hard.
