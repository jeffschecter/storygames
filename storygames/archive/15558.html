\n Hey Friends!

So as a stocking stuffer, my friend got me a set of a dozen fudge dice. He said I would find something neat to do with them, and I'm up to the challenge. That said (and aside from the standard fudge mechanics), what are some interesting/neat/crazy things you have used fudge dice for?

    \n I used them for a little game idea that used fight scripting. You'd set 3 fudge dice to show the order of your actions for the round:

    [ - ] stood for "sword" or attack.
    [ + ] was "crossed swords" or counter.
    [ ] was "void" or dodge.

    The game idea never went anywhere, but I thought that bit was kind of neat.

    \n I use FUDGE dice for my holiday game trilogy: Christmas Ninjas, Valentine Knights, and Space Cowboys of Independence. All three games use them for matching, with the player required to match each symbol that appears on the opposing roll. The smallest number of matching dice for a given symbol that appears in the opposing roll is the level of success. (so if the GM rolls a + and -, then you want to have at least one + and one - to avoid failure, but if you have two of each then you get the next level of success. This is built on top of mechanics that let you manipulate the rolls (related to the River in Legend of Wulin and Weapons of the Gods).

    - Mendel

    \n You could easily add Fudge dice to any task-resolution system to add a little bit of narrative flair to the outcome of any die roll. For example, in D&D, roll your D20 plus your skill modifier, and determine success or failure as normal. However, also roll a single Fudge die: [+] means an added positive result to the action, [-] means an added negative result. This changes the standard Yes/No of most task resolution systems into something more dynamic: Yes; Yes, and...; Yes, but...; No; No, and...; No, but...".

    \n Graypawn made a shoot-em-up couple of games called Ammo: Revenant War & Amm02: Deus vs. Machina.

    I've only played the first one, but i played as a pizza delivery girl who'd been, y'know, abducted by aliens, rewired, and returned to earth again (like all protagonists in the game) and now the alien fleet was attacking hardcore. Dropships on the streets of seattle. SO i was out delivering pizzas and i opened my car door (careening down san-francisco-quality hills towards Pike Place Market), leaned into it, SHOT OFF THE HINGES and rode it down toward the waterfront. I was firing at aliens while doing this, natch.

    Each time i fired a shot, i flicked a fudge die from my hand onto the table. When you play the Ammo series, your hand is like a clip and you toss out a die for each shot fired, then you line up matches and then even more badass stuff happens.

    And its, um, free? And there's not a more-fun fudge dice game at all, anywhere, forever?

    \n I want to expand upon Bliss Stage...the cool thing about Bliss Stage is, well, actually, everything. But the cool thing vis-a-vis Fudge Dice, is that rather than generating a "success level" or anything like that. Instead, you're on a mission, with a bunch of priorities: mission objective, pilot safety, safety of the relationships you're harnessing to power your dream-mecha. Every "round" of the mission, you roll your pile of dFs, and assign the pluses to different areas, choosing what to sacrifice if you didn't roll enough. Do you disregard your own safety to achieve your goals quickly? Or do you play it safe and cautious? Do you allow your relationships to be stressed out by dream-trauma? You're making constant tradeoffs.

    I particularly like this because it's a thing that nothing but Fudge dice can really do well. Whereas the "basic" implementation (the FUDGE or FATE or Solar System method) is something you could do, roughly, with a regular numerical roll.

    Though I do like me some Solar System, I tell you what. In fact, I think Fudge Dice are a basic human good. I'm all for anything that can take the math out of storytelling, or at least reduce it.

    \n In my last Spirit of the Century game, the 4dF were four different colors, each representing a factor in the current action and whether it was favorable or not at the moment of execution. Red was offense (or anything involving the acting party's effort), blue was defense (the defender's efforts, the objective's inherent difficulty), white was time (the timing of the action, any coincidental events) and black was space (distances, environmental elements). The whole table got involved in interpreting the rolls, offering entertaining explanations for the various combinations of plusses and minuses that came up. It was great, I recommend it.

    I've been trying to come up with a catchy name for the technique but so far, I've got nothing. Colorful Dice? Has anyone used that one yet? Or FUDGE Swirl? Although it's not very swirly.

    Next time, I think I'll also use the FATE 2.0 version of Aspects, which required you to choose one die and change its value to a plus (instead of just adding +2 to your total roll as in SotC). The player would then be required to justify how that specific Aspect influenced that specific factor and turned it to their advantage.
