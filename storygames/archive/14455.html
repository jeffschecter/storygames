    \nIt's space opera. It's not Cthulhu in space. A bit of transhumany stuff.

    Think Firefly or Traveller, but glossier.\n
    Premise: I got nothin'!
    \nYou play Lasers, who are hired to go places and do things in space. A bit like Firefly or Traveller. Usually, those things are investigative.\n
    How it plays: Probably will hear "it plays like a GUMSHOE game," which is way-not-helpful to me. Sorry. So far my GUMSHOE experience is ToC, and I'm not seeing that experience map onto a sf/horror thing. But that's because my system and premise questions are great yawning chasms.
    \nBasically, it plays like a GUMSHOE game.

    Not really like Trail of Cthulhu. Trail plays eerie and immersive: the GUMSHOE system helps this, because it's simple. Ashen Stars is all a bit more shooty and procedural: more like Esoterrorists, if you've played that, which you haven't.

    Oh, you get cybernetic powers and stuff, too. And there are some space combat rules.

    I got a chance to playtest the game in Robin Laws' playgroup; Robin is the author. I can give some feedback, particularly if there are any specific questions.

    \n

    Apologies for the necro. I opened this thread before I left on a cruise, and realized I wanted to leave something. I still have a chunk of comments in a draft but figured I wouldn't inundate unless people had more specific questions.

    \n

    Basically this is the GUMSHOE system, but I hadn't played any other games with it so I can't speak to its peculiar reshaping to fit. The system basically uses character traits as narrative currency though - "strong" traits, or "investigative" skills, automatically get you past an appropriate obstacle, and then are exhausted for the story session. "Weak" traits require a roll.

    \n

    The setting is malleable, but includes a bunch of set-pieces (specifically, that it's happening in the shadow of a status-quo-shattering space war, analogous to World War I, and that certain species and technology levels exist). I played Mass Effect on the Xbox 360 shortly after finishing my stint in playtest, and found that it rang very true to the tone, though it was closer to Mass Effect 2 insofar as it felt a bit more noirish, grimy, and as mentioned, "Stuff Has Gone Down" prior to the game. The "Spectre/Laser" analogy feels pretty good.

    \n

    Our party crewed a single ship, and did a lot of in-game computer research where possible. "Facts" coined by players in-game would stick; Chris Huth (who also did a lot of the game's art) asked, at one point, if we could look up a suspect's personal information on some kind of network. Robin asked what he meant, Chris replied "I dunno, Spacebook." From that point forward we looked stuff up on Spacebook.

    \n

    Individual weapon shots tended to be deadly. We'd often incapacitate or kill suspects or antagonists if we weren't careful. I suspect this can be dialed up or down, but any weapon in the Weapons List - particularly a Laser's default firearm - tends to take people out unless you specify stun ammo. The converse was also true, and survival-oriented biomods, either cybernetic or viral, quickly became de-rigeur.

    \n

    Idiosyncrasies in the the inter-species dynamics and Robin's GM tone made the thing feel pretty laid-back and sometimes even comedic, for a space opera. Adventures vary, I'm sure, but it had more of a caseworker/beat cop feel than Mass Effect did. Closer to CSI than 24. But the serious lethality of the system made it feel even more like Dashiell Hammett, which I appreciated, having been neck deep in that stuff when we played.

    \n

    I am happy to say as much more about sessions as people are interested in hearing.

    Right, it's really not particularly "Cthulhu-ey". It's much more about the seedy side of space, the murders and smuggling and politicking and stuff that happen in the shadow of reconstruction.

    \n

    Although it comes from a similar place of existential contemplation as Lovecraft's postwar fiction, and certainly the individual monsters and ne'er-do-wells could be extradimensional entities, in the final roundup they're just another potential man-at-the-end-of-the-trail. Though that said, they could be anything from the muscle, to the fall guy, to the kingpin. Tonally, if there's cosmic horror, then it's in service to crime-fiction logic.
