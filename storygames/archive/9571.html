\n The Issue: I'm running a pretty trad-style World of Darkness game with my regular group. I encourage a lot of "realism" in their characters and therefore run into issues sometimes when one character is going to his kid's recital while the rest of the 'party' investigates a murder scene and otherwise interacts with plot points. Their believable characters believably split up, A LOT, and I hate seeing players left out of interesting or pivotal scenes because their character is off taking care of some solo business.

The Question: What's the best way you've seen to involve Players when their characters aren't involved in the scene? I'm not expecting a definitive answer, but I'd love to hear about some of your favorite methods. What I'm not interested in are PTA-style methods of having the characters "jump into" a scene they aren't otherwise around for. Sometimes, that just doesn't make sense from a narrative standpoint. What I am interested in are different ways that you've seen games handle this particular issue.

When it just doesn't make sense for Joe's character to be here right now, how do we keep Joe involved and entertained until his character shows up again?

    \n There are a couple of options; how well they work depends on the expectations and conventions of the group.

    (1) Play opposition. Give the OOS player(s) an NPC "monster" to run. This works best when the NPC's options are clearly delimited: a mook in a combat scene, a walk-on bit part rather than a "boss." Some players will find this unfun if they feel they don't have any real chance to affect the game or the fiction, or represent their character.

    (2) Be an active audience. To the extent that some in-game or meta-game resource can be available to be distributed by OOS players (e.g., fan mail), those players can be given the job of recognizing "good form" by the in-scene players. This falls down if any PVP strategizing enters the picture.

    (3) Activate sympathetic connections. It may happen that OOS players can be given the ability to introduce some resonant fictional counter-point or actual game-mechanical advantage during play despiter their absence from the scene (via flashback or some other narrative device, perhaps mediated through FATE-like Aspects). "He remembers the training I gave him" is the simplest example. Some may find this phony or prone to abuse.

    (4) Play simultaneously. On his or her turn in the "round" the OOS player takes action in his or her scene. This only works when there's enough to do in both locations to warrant such a set-up, and can get complicated quickly.

    That's about all I got. Any others?

    \n I often have scenes where there are only 1-2 out of 3-4 PCs in a scene.

    The other players, I have them play NPCs. Not just "the barkeep", but Major NPCs. Including protagonists and antagonists.

    Sometimes I ply the major NPC once or twice to have the players get a feel for my vision of the character before I "hand them off" to another player who isn't in a scene. But sometimes I'll just as easily hand off a major NPC to a player at the start.

    I did this in With Great Power, where the characters were all in the Seattle Night Watch: The first time they met their boss, a powerful (but not Mary Sue) protagonist, I handed the character to a player. Told him "Here's a few things about the Boss: X, Y and Z. Go." and he took on the role with no problem.

    If there was anything that I needed (as GM) to happen, like a secret, or a plan, or something, I would make corrections as needed. Like, for the most part I'd turn over major antagonists and protagonists to the players, but if they accidentally said something that directly contradicted my plans, and there wasn't a way to make them commensurable, I'd jump in with a suggestion, or just take over.

    Matt (as Boss): "This is the worst thing I've seen since the riots in Cambodia in 1970..."
    (and it's really important to my notes, and incommensurable, to have him NOT in Egypt at that time)
    Andy: "Ahhh, actually he wasn't in Cambodia, he was in Egypt at that time. That'll be important later."
    Matt: "Ah, ok."
    (thinking)
    Matt: "This is the worst thing i've seen since a string of political assassinations back in Egypt."

    or:
    Matt (as Boss): "I'll tell you what. Go investigate the murder, and report to me what you find."
    Andy: "Ah, actually I've got this part, if it's ok. I've got something in mind"
    Matt: "Ah, ok"
    (mentally, ownership of the character passes back to Me As GM)
    Andy (as Boss): "I'll tell you what, you've had a hard day today, go ahead and get some rest. I sent Alex and Nicodemus to look into the murder, they'll let us know what they find later. Seriously, you need some rest, get the hell out of here."
    Other PCs: "Alright, boss."

    Just an example of each method.

    I love this method, because often the players will come up with things that are
    * Unexpected,
    * Yet totally work within the framework of the scenario.
    That is, my plans for characters and The Story will often change based on the players' portrayal of major NPCs, in a cool way. And yet, if there's something that I just need to have happen (not like constant needling of the players, "Nope, he would say X instead. Nope, he wasn't in Cambodia. Nope, he doesn't know that") and it absolutely cannot change, then I simply either chime in, or I take over.
    And if it's possible in the system, I reward the player for taking on a major NPC role for a scene, usually with a Fan Mail style chip or something.

    \n Two tricks:

    1. By establishing certain types of NPCs as "examples of the everyday" the players learn to watch those types of NPCs for cues as to how the world/culture normally works. Even if a character isn't involved in a scene, the player can watch for the everyday NPCs to gain insight into the world.

    2. Some mysteries are built for the characters to solve, but sometimes I'll create a second mystery behind it, something that isn't necessary to conclude the story, but functions as a puzzle for the players to work through. If they solve it they often get some general insight into how the game world works and establish a good frame of mind for approaching problems within the main plot. To do this the players must be able to listen in on any scene, which isn't appropriate for all types of story.

    \n Let them affect the game in different ways. Playing NPCs is one way, but here are some others:
    - Narrative outsourcing: Let the players describe stuff for you. Very helpful for the GM. "You walk into a bar. There's something weird going on. Bob, tell us what's going on!"
    - Character impulses: Let non-scene players affect characters by pushing their emotions. "You look at him, and suddenly you feel apprehensive." "That gunshot should have scared you... but instead you feel icy cold hatred for the gunman."
    - Dreams: Let players narrate other characters' dreams.
    - Constrained control: Give players specific elements to control. The weather. The fire consuming a building. The erratic behavior of an insane robot.

    \n I can't speak to WOD, but in D&D I'd "activate sympathetic connections" by saying something like, "Okay, Oscar, while they're fighting the evil baron's minions, you're talking to your father about preparing your son for his initiation ceremony, so you're not here. During this fight, though, I'll give you an action point (up to once per round) if you reveal something about how baron's triumph will undermine family ties, loyalty, and tradition--preferably by showing us something about the baron and his minions in the scene. Also, other PCs can ask you to spend an action point to give them an extra action, but they have to let you establish something about the relationship between the two PCs. Okay?"

    The point of this is to keep the player involved by allowing them control of some game-mechanical currency which ebbs and flows based on resonances between the in-scene and off-screen action. It's not full participation, but it's something.

    Posted By: JarvisI remember hearing in your podcast that you guys got some really good use out of that technique, even to the point of handing major villains to the players. I really dig the idea, but does it work in a mystery story?     \nGood question! It made me think about my situation a little bit more.

    So the game I did this heavily in was the With Great Power (rules) game of Night Watch. In Night Watch, there's always something that happens - sometimes even a murder. But the thing is, even though there is definitely a person who did the murder/thing (and they are outed/revealed later), the stories are always about what happens in the wake of that event.

    So for example, the campaign began with a murder, a human forced by the hand of a mystery NPC to become an "Other" (a superhuman, in this case a Water-based Mage), and a harbringer of apocalypse arriving in town. The murder was there, but rather than the game being all about the players quizzing the NPCs as to whether they did the deed or not, the game quickly spiraled into a series of events that happened in the Wake of the murder.

    So, basically, I had in the back of my head these NPCs in a fictionalized Seattle: The boss of light, the boss of darkness, a few light Others, a few dark Others. In my head, I decided that the conspiracy was all due to the lieutenant/assistant of the Boss of Darkness, and came up with some "puzzle pieces" as to the reasons behind the murder. And yet, I also decided that the characters would not really interact with that character more than once or twice in the entire adventure; Sure, he was the hand behind everything, but he was really not that important to what was happening in the wake of what he did.

    Here were my puzzle pieces:
    * A harbringer of historic apocalypses (nice girl, but a little skittish) shows up in town. She doesn't have any plans, she just happens to be visiting.
    * The characters receive a vision of a natural apocalypse which is going to strike Seattle within the next month.
    * Later, they realize the apocalypse is water-based (which cues back to the reasons behind the turning of the Water-Mage character)
    * The water mage is being spied upon by some Dark Others, but the Boss of Darkness knew nothing of it.

    Meanwhile, a lot of other "not mystery plot, but totally interesting" events were going on:
    One character, a kind of muse, was having a falling out with her boyfriend, and at the same time was being hunted down by an organization called the Inquisition for things she did a few decades ago.
    The other character, a kind of Harry Dresden with No Magic (all trinkets) character was coming to grips with his magical weakness, and a side-story about his paternal grandfather, a liche, trying to steal his body.

    As the side-events were wrapping up, and events surrounding the puzzle pieces were put together, only then were the last pieces thrown out:
    * The characters learn that whomever set up the murder and water-mage-transform is the same person planning a tidal wave that will hit Seattle in two days.
    * The murderer believes that this will lead to the creation of a huge amount of supernaturals ("Others") in one event.
    * (and to make the villain sympathetic) the final piece is that the dark others are not planning some "create a bunch of dark others and bum rush the light others" as expected, but rather that the villain's family were killed my humans who couldn't understand them, so he simply wanted to turn all area humans into supernaturals in order for people not to fear the supernatural anymore.

    But literally, the characters interacted with that villain twice: Once halfway through the campaign, when they needed to ask him a few questions about Other Events (he was in the city planning hall, and they had old architectural questions for him based on other puzzle pieces). Then, at the end of the scenario, they figure out who was behind the whole mess, and they go to his stronghold and fought him.

    So basically, some of those Puzzle Pieces I planned out in advance (the general reason for the murder, why the villain did it, and what the villain's goals were), others were made up later on the fly.

    BUT, TO MAKE A LONG STORY SHORT (too late):

    The important thing wasn't the mystery. I mean, there was a mystery, and I had chosen the villain. And I pawned the villain off on the players when they first met him (to answer those innocuous questions about city architecture), but at the endgame I took the role.
    The important thing was what the mystery leads to.
    In a nWoD Vampire game, I could see running a similar event. The Prince of the City dies (or could be a peon, something not so groundbreaking). It's not like the PCs are simply going to don hats and magnifying glasses and look for clues: Rather, what's more likely to happen is that the villain had plans; he put events into motion, and the PCs, and everyone, are caught in the whirlwind.

    A prince dies. The PCs aren't going to go all Sherlock Holmes on it; I mean, they have interest in finding the killer, and may even be tasked with it. But most likely, the adventure should instead focus on the complications and events that happen next, as three people suddenly vie for power over the vacant throne, and each PC has their own subplot to deal with at the same time... It's like they won't have time to solve the mystery without interruption. They're not going to go from person to person asking "where were you the night of so-and-so?", but rather will be visiting various major NPCs trying to bring stability (or more chaos) to the situation... meanwhile, every now and then a clue is offered to the players, a reason behind the bigger puzzle of Why the person was murdered, rather than Who Did It (which in the end, is far more interesting, right?).

    Make sense?
