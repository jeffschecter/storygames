\nA Grail Epoch is a hack of Matthijs Holter's Archipelago.


This hack is played with dice and minis, and there are a few other notable features:
1. It's designed for three players
2. There are exactly 12 characters and 9 starting locations
3. New areas on the map are unlocked as the cast of characters changes
4. Characters can only move a certain number of spaces on the map per turn
5. Each player character's destiny is designed by the two other players

The most significant change is that the players will roll to see who wins a conflict: so if there's a verbal altercation, or an attempt to steer a ship through a hurricane, or a knife-fight, two players. High roll wins, and then the winner chooses the Yes card or the No card (depending on which player won).

    \n Rafael, I plan to steal all sorts of stuff from this game. You've managed to come up with some great answers to things that have given me fits trying to figure out when I was looking to make a minis using hack of Archipelago.

    I am, naturally, immensely jealous!!!

    Some of the stuff I liked:

    1) It's a 3 player game.
    I read that bit and felt like I'd been smacked between the eyes with the clue bat. Sheesh! Of course!{bonk! *stars*] I kept running in circles trying to figure out how many people my hack was supposed to work for. Three is a great number that works with some natural dynamics.

    2) There are three main characters (PCs) and 9 supporting characters (NPCs) and that's it.
    Again, clue-bat time. Really, having some spare characters around to add flavor is a must have. It's important for scenes to work, It's also next to impossible for anyone to follow a cast of millions. Yeah, another bulls-eye. Just enough extra potentially interesting characters to provide interactions and contrasts to the main characters.

    2a) I thought the requirement to go with simple names was brilliant.

    It also helped me clarify something I was running into problems with: Sometimes extra minis are really just "terrain". I mean, i'd like to do some action packed stuff with soldier-y types, but those things really don't even add up to mooks. Really, they're more scenery that's dangerous than anything else.

    3) Dividing the charaters into factions, but those factions can be uneven or even amazingly lopsided and that sets up the story.
    Oh hell yes. There are charater minis and a setting, and even a situation, but the players need to be able to decide kinda naturally what the focus is going to be and where their interests lie. Good stuff.

    4) The various dice as player resources not in-fiction capabilities of the characters.
    I tried something similar in an earlier design attempt and badly bobbled the explanation. You've nailed it.

    5) Hvaing the least involved player acting as a temporary referee/ decision maker in contests.
    The 3 player nature of the game ties right back into that perfectly.

    6) Having 9 locations and ties between them, and movement between them.
    I have no idea how I'd translate that to a more minis wargaming style table layout but the idea is great. Less locations than characters means that somone is always interacting somewhere with other key characters, and being able to physically move the pieces uses the play space more naturally than jumping from scene to scene and explaining the space/movement interations by just narration.

    7) I liked that you talked about setting set ups beyond just the islands from Matthijs original game.
    I was very much struck by your suggestion of a ship as a setting and having locations based on that. The core idea of the map can be seen a whole bunch of different ways. With your use of a big sheet of paper you could even do things like social circles or cliques as conceptual locations, and even make those linked with the more obvious idea of physical locations. Think, something like a High School drama. On one hand, there's probably a few key physical settings, maybe even with subdividsions. Swirling around that thoug are the cliques that act a whole lot like locations of their own, and may even go beyond simple factions. And all of that is interacting together. On a big sheet to draw a map, you could have both types of things acting as locations.

    Jeez I wish i wasn't loacted half a continent away from you guys. I'd love to try this out in person and then have a go at busting out some toys and a table and showing y'all what my take on this would be.

    \n Hi, Komradebob!

    Right, so the connections do not count as a space. They're just lines that connect spaces, so that you know which spaces you can move to from a given location.

    Yeah, when we added connections, we found a way to justify it (someone's dug a tunnel, a collapsed tree has created a makeshift bridge across a canyon, et cetera). Your ideas are kickass -- secret passages, smugglers' routes, all that. Great stuff.

    I think your idea for 'extras' is a great one. Mechanically, I'd say they're just there -- easily moved around whenever necessary, not named or capable of actions. In a regular game of Archipelago, you'd just handwave it away -- "There's a crowd watching as you battle in the streets" -- but with minis, you could actually show the crowd, as needed. Could definitely make for a more fun experience.

    I play this game with a posterboard, but I've been meaning to do a proper job -- I've got this eight-foot table that I have to assemble, along with a few dozen structures (buildings, towers, a painted mural background), and I'd like to run a game of A Grail Epoch using that setup sometime. I'll let you know how it goes!

    Yeah, I'm working on a printable playmat. I'm trying to make it fairly modular, so you can move locations and connections around to create a custom map. The setup you describe is perfect!

    I'll let you know if I make any progress on that front anytime soon.

    \n I thought a bit about the ship question, and about locations and connections more generally, trying to figure out some ways to make it work on the tabletop.

    Then I realized I was jumping ahead a bit, trying to figure out a physical solution without considering the core point of the layout.

    Here's what I'm getting at: The point of having some stuff seperated and accessible/inaccessible early on is mostly so individual players have a bit of a chance to spotlight characters and situations within the game context a little bit on their own, before it all comes together later in the game.

    That very first round of scenes is usually a chance for each player in turn to show off, get some spotlight lovin', and also give an invitation to the other two players to come play with something they find very cool. It's an isolation thing on one hand and a focus thing on the other.

    What i'm getting at is that the initial round of scenes is really very important, and that a moving location should be fine, provided it doesn't stomp on anyone's ability to spotlight a bit and isolate scenes if that's where they want to go with things.

    [Anyway,for tabletop layouts, it might be just as useful to declare anti-connections/blockages or even double cost moves. Or maybe just come up with some practical SOP, like wherever a location marker is placed defines the center of the location and location centers are somewhere between 8" and 24" apart with physical visual indicators of rough boundaries. At that point, really just use common sense and roll off conflicting ideas/draw cards just like anything else. And, ya know, talk to each other a little about intent.]

    BTW, has anyone else considered doing a re-assessment after those initial round of scenes? I ask because a couple of times playing Archipelago I've found that the initial choice of either element or PC was not really as interesting as something else that was developing, like an interesting NPC that had blossomed through play or an unexpected element was needing some guardianship, while an earlier chosen one really didn't because everyone was actually already on thr same page with that.

    \n Right, you've nailed it. The other thing to consider is that, over time, the spotlight intensifies. NPCs are disposable -- they exist to provide conflict, illumination, and opportunity. But as the game progresses, it's inevitable that some of those NPCs are going to withdraw from the narrative, leaving the core cast -- the main characters -- on the screen to face their destinies.

    To make that easier, you create additional connections. And maybe you have locations that are accessible from new places -- like a ship that suddenly can travel throughout all of the islands, effectively granting teleportation abilities to characters. This makes it possible to reach new locations quickly during those final rounds, which can facilitate cool storytelling without making it tedious ("I'd like to have a scene at the Dragon's Lair, because it would be cool to have him face his nemesis, but it's five locations away, so I guess we'll do a scene here, and then next time it's my turn, I'll be able to go there...").

    Early on, the limitations of location and connection force all characters into a small group of spaces, resulting in repeated interaction and conflict, but probably with a slightly different cast of characters in each scene. Later in the game, you want to open things up, make it possible for the last remaining characters to hammer it out and face destiny -- and a moving location sounds like an awesome way to do that!

    \n I guess part of it comes down to whether the vehicle (or whatever) is actually a location in itself or just a connection doesn't it?

    I mean, The Millenium Falcon or a circus caravan are both locations that can move but also have a whole lot to offer in themselves for scenes.

    It's undoubtedly overcomplicating things in a straight forward game, but maybe if a vehicle ( or equivalent) is going to do that double duty in a game, it starts with two "Ports of call"* and then an additional one can be added after any scene that takes place in the vehicle (or involves that vehicle in an important fashion). The vehicle is then either "at sea/in space/on the road" or can be moved to one of its ports of call. When it moves into a port of call, that's the only connection it has at the time.

    No, I don't have brilliant ideas about how it would work when multiple locationvehicles interact in at-sea situations either.

    *edited to add:
    Or just one port of call and an open slot, to be decided the first time it comes up. Which is probably better anyway.
