      Fred,

      Eh? The sentences are not that similar... or else it's late and I'm not seeing it. Btw as far as I remember the word is "pika" and not "tschuka" (which means "pike", but refers to the fish - not the weapon... :) ) I think you also have an extra "buil" there. Properly this sentence would AFAIK be something like:

      Ya khotel bui uvidetz vaschu razyedinennuiu golov(k)u na kontsye piki.

      Heh, yeah, now we got "pique". It's also "spade" in Russian, as in Queen of Spades (Dama Pik, of course... :) )

      Russian is quite... eclectic. Since France was considered by Russians the height of culture (pretty much during the entire 19th century IIRC), an ungodly amount of French words were adopted into Russian verbatim. The Russians also adopted some words from German (e.g. apfelsinen), probably to become more "European", or sometimes only by chance. Also depending on the words involved, a linguist could point out that both Romanic and Slavonic languages are of common descent (as they're both Indo-European). I guess an example of that in the sentence above would be "Videt" in Russian ("Vidi", "View", "Voir", "Vista").

      Btw English is quite eclectic as well, but nearly all of its non-aboriginal word roots (or direct etymologies) are Romanic (Latin, French, Germanic/Saxonic, Scandinavian), with Greek possibly the notable exception. Native English speakers, and especially those that do not know other languages, are usually totally oblivious to this. When I first studied French, I suddenly saw it in Russian and English, and then finding other stuff like it was quite fun.

      MarK.
