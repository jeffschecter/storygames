    I know that everytime someone does a review of Chruch and State I sell copies.

    In the first 6 month cycle every reveiw would sell 15 to 20 copies. Even now, more than a year after release in a demographic that slows way down after time, a review will sell a few copies -- Chad's recent overview gave me a sales spike from an average of 2 copies a month for the past several months to something like 7.

    The best places to do reviews for RPGing folks are probably RPG.net, Ogre Cave, ENWorld (if you can get them to review non-d20). However, you may also want your reviewers to post Actual Play reports to RPG.net and the Forge -- as good Actual Play reports probably sell more games than any review ever will. I've actually considered giving away comp copies for Actual Play posts rather than reviews, as they seem to be the better way to push indie games.

    As for the Lovecraft folks, I'd suggest getting on one of the forums and politiely asking for someone who would be willing to review the game. If you have any friends that are already members of the community you can also ask them to do it, as having an "insider" often softens the transition.

  • Josh Roby
    February 2006
    Keep in mind that I do 'this shit' professionally, by which Brand means I produce textbooks that other people sell to school districts and state boards of education, completely bypassing the actual consumers. So our business model is just slightly different than most game publishers. That said.

    o Build Brand Identity. In the indie design biz, you are your brand identity. People will buy a game because it's written by Vincent Baker, John Wick, or Clinton Nixon. Make sure your name is next to any mention of your product. This is a long-term deal, yeah, but start now.

    o Samplers are better than Samples. Start thinking of the content you produce as assets that have more than one application. Packaging some of the assets from a larger, flagship product into a smaller, free-with-order product is good marketing strategy. Look at Capes Lite -- I doubt Tony has numbers, but I'm sure he's sold copies of "real" Capes because people could freely download "Lite" Capes, and wanted more. Now consider if, when you bought Sorcerer, it came in the mail with a little sixteen-page booklet containing excerpts of the other books in that line. Think you'd be more likely to order the other products over the next couple months? Specifically, Jason, what would you think of putting a trio of Roach cards in each copy of FLFS that went out (with your website URL on the back of each card or something)?

    o Reviewers don't need physical books to review. This is important for two simple reasons: first off, if you wait until you're done manufacturing before you can start getting reviews, it means you're sitting on stock, and that's bad. Get reviews by sending out manuscript, first pages, or F&Gs/galleys, not finished product. (Somebody mentioned pdfs, which is great cause that's no cost to you, and you'll probably have pdfs long before physical copies. Just be sure to downsample them to manageable resolution first.) Additionally, reviewers' remarks are most effective on the product itself, and it's kind of hard to add that when the book's already printed.

    o When advertising (which is a small, small aspect of promotions, which is a small, small aspect of marketing), focus on features offered, not ephemeral things like quality or comparisons with other products. Say: this game does X. It has tools that let you do Y. It has art by Z. If you can't put each feature in ten words and say it in fifteen seconds, it's too long. That doesn't mean that you have to dumb it down -- it probably means that you're explaining how it works, rather than what it does.

    o The games market is a hugely fractured nightmare of a market; the RPG market is an even more fractured subset of that. Promotion strategies that have worked generally fall into three groups: (a) internet, via forum activity and maintaining your own site, with an emphasis on being open and friendly, (b) conventions, either Luke Crane flavored or Indie Press Revolution flavored, of which I know next to nothing personally, or (c) organizing a grassroots demo-and-games organization -- the Cammarilla sells books, pure and simple; other companies have organized groups of fans to run demos and usually lavish them with praise and teeshirts. There's no reason in the world why indie games can't start up something like (c) and I think Brennan's experimenting with something like it on IPR.

    o Going back a bit, notice how you can do (a) and (b) with free samplers instead of comp copies? Try and separate marketing materials (samplers) from product (books). One's to help you sell; the other is what you sell. It's relatively simple to create marketing materials, especially once you have a product, so there really is no need to use product as marketing materials. For every story of a guy who got a comp copy from a con and got his friends to play at home and buy more copies, I've got another where my friend Lem got the entire line of In Nomine for 'winning' a con game, and then never played the game again his entire life. I also bought 7th Sea off of eBay, and was surprised to find John Wick's signature and a con date on the inside front cover.

    o Definitely explore your crossover market with the Lovecraft folks!

    o Don't worry too much about good reviews / bad reviews. From all reports, the only hit from a bad review is to your pride -- sales spike anyway. Hell, that Satanis game sold copies off of its abyssmal reviews.

    I've gone on way too long already. Mostly, though, try and develop a sort of sense of how you want to present your product and to whom -- the means by which you do that is secondary to the those two bits, which rarely get nailed down well in marketing gaming.
