    \n All dice are compared to 7. Difficulty is the number of dice >=7, rather than the number you look for on each die. You botch only if you roll zero successes and at least one 1. Tens explode if you're invoking one of your specializations, or whatever you call it when you have a trait at 4+ and you describe it more precisely. I think that's all.

    I'd add in the following story games bits:
    - Additional successes can be rolled over to later, related tests, or used to narrate in a fact, Donjon-style
    - You must fail a roll with a given skill to advance it.
    - Hard: Rewrite the adjectives on the wound levels track so that they're distinctive Conditions, ones that can be roleplayed constantly. Use strong adjectives like Exhausted, Enraged, Unconscious, that are easy to portray.
    - Specializations are flags, right? Anything a player spends more than, say, 3 build points on, that's a flag too, right?
    - Character creation must be done collaboratively. In fact, take away 5 build points from each player. When each player finishes building, have him hand his sheet to his right-hand neighbor, who will spend 5 points to finish the job.
    - Play "I don't see it" before you begin the game.
    - Write a kicker, or a Mouse Guard-style Belief. Reward its use with XP.
    - If you succeed, you narrate. If you fail, someone else does (GM, usually).
    - Really stress the crap out of Nature/Demeanor and the Willpower reward cycle. Maybe you get more than one of each? Go after their Willpower with nasty supernatural powers if you must, get them to spend it for auto-success if you can, but somehow, make them ping their keys Nature and Demeanor to stay in the game.
    - GM: Fish like crazy, say yes or roll dice, empower players with narrative authority constantly, say "yes, and..." a lot, don't get lazy in your IIEE, be willing to zoom out and do conflict resolution, cut scenes aggressively.
    - Players: Accept narrative authority, say "yes, and..." a lot, don't get lazy in your IIEE, suggest a lot, cheer a lot, don't think of the GM as the entertainer, keep the pace up, and let the other players use your character to make things more awesome - they'll return the favor.

    \n Trail of Cthulhu would work well. It does a particular thing: if you've got a mystery, it gives the investigators a way of progressing through the mystery, and the game becomes about how they uncover it and what happens to the investigators as a result. Would you be using Sanity and Stability? Because that would be cool.

    Inspectres is possible, too, and that does a very different thing.

    Colin, dude, you can't just graft on bits from other indie games and expect it to work. That's exactly what indie games aren't about. The mechanics are meant to support the game, not be generally usable tools.
