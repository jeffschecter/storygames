\n Hey, so lots of threads about the Forge's Big Model just now, and my name keeps coming up in them.

The Big Model was nifty when it was current. It was fun and useful to me, and I owe it my games from Dogs in the Vineyard to Apocalypse World, but rpg design has left it behind.

Among many others, but most particularly, I don't think that the idea of Creative Agendas stands up after all, let alone G, N, and S as its representatives.

We used to think that RPGs were one game. In, like, the 90s or whenever. We would say things like "what is the object of a RPG?" as though all RPGs would have the same one.

GNS was a real step forward: "RPGs aren't one game, they're THREE!"

But, of course, RPGs aren't three games either. Every RPG, like every other kind of game, is its own. You can taxonomize them if you want, but then you're constructing artificial categories and cramming games into them, not learning or finding out something true about the games themselves.

Just so you know!

-Vincent

    \n Well, it is illustrative that while I agree with Vincent's sentiment about taking games on their own terms, I still find the Big Model a most useful analytical tool. As Rob says, vocabulary - and the underlying mental analytic vocabulary enables - is what you get from the incessant modeling of complex behaviours. For me rpg theory, Big Model and GNS and whatever, is comparable to e.g. military science, which despite the lofty name, is in the exact same place with rpg theory in attempting to model, analyze and predict complex human phenomena with infinite variation. One could condemn both for coming in the way of natural intuitive understanding, but it's probably better to take them both as what they are - tools of mental construction, to achieve better ways to [kill people] / [play games].

    \nPaul: If I were going to do that, I should have done it back when the Big Model was current, instead of waiting until it's obsolete. The Big Model's been totally static for the past 5 years or more, you'll notice, and rpg design hasn't been.

    But in a few words, sure.

    You know how you can assign a given rule to Drama, Fortune or Karma, if you want, but it tells you absolutely nothing about how the rule works, or why, and it creates illusory clusters of rules instead of fostering real understanding? And the same thing with FitM vs FatE? And the same thing with Effectiveness, Resource, and Positioning? They're convenient stand-ins for what's actually going on, when what's actually going on defies such simplistic taxonomies?

    Same thing with GNS.

    I was developing the idea of technical agenda as the technical component of creative agenda, and the further I developed it, the more patent it became to me that G, N and S were arbitrary, not reflective of real divisions in actual design or actual play. That while you can, if you want, assign a given instance of gameplay to G, N or S more or less consistently, you do so by asserting false similarities and ignoring some true similarities between other instances of gameplay. GNS is a convenient stand-in for what's actually going on.

    If any of you Big Model theorists want to check my work, I'll be happy to lay it out for you, but it's long and it's technical, and I'll ask you to follow along carefully. Hit me up elsewhere.

    -Vincent

      \n Well I believe I called it in 2009 :-)

      Of course, my own ideas have moved on since 2009. A Hell of a lot. And I'm probably calmer about it too, possibly something to do with being older and presumably having less testosterone.

      But I don't think that the real problem with GNS was that it involved taxonomy. Sure, a taxonomical scheme will always struggle to capture the vastness of the human experience (and a Hell of a lot of the human experience can enter into RPGs). But without an effort to simplify concepts and categories, without mental short-cuts, without boiling the things we observe down to their most basic versions, we can't even formulate general statements for guidance, let alone apply them and learn. So everything becomes trial and error, instinct, preconceptions, tradition. Now often those haphazard learning methods may surpass theoretically informed efforts in their result, but if we want the best from our games, we should probably try both.

      So if the problem with GNS wasn't that it involved taxonomy, what was it? I believe it was what it became, in people's minds. I believe there are actually people I have met who have been totally put off some styles of game by the prejudices that they have built up in response to GNS. People who have closed their minds off entirely to the fun they might have with things that fit into category X, just because they've heard bad things about category X, or because there have been some games that fit category X which they haven't enjoyed, and they attribute that to their Xness, and not to some other quality which might better explain their non-enjoyment. And that closing off of minds makes me sad. Especially when it means some people close their minds off from the stuff I personally prefer.

      I don't think this has affected a large proportion even of avid roleplayers/storygamers, but I think it has affected a few people I personally have met, because of the circles I move in, and it has impacted on my life personally, and as I say, it makes me sad.

      So I'm glad to see V.B. moving away from the rigid theoretical models of the Forge and adopting a more analogue outlook for the time being. If that means more people shaking themselves loose of the shackles of GNS, that's great. But I can't help wondering with a hint of cynicism where exactly all this is headed now, as far as V.B. is concerned.

      On the other hand I think there's still room for progress in a theoretical understanding of RPGs and storygames. What I would say though is that people should try looking at them from some totally different angle, not related at all to the sorts of categories Forge theory was primarily interested in. My money's on proper academic psychology being the best place to start looking if you want to be able to say something truly meaningful about RPGs. For instance, how does the capacity for empathy (affective or cognitive) affect a person's enjoyment of RPGs, what they look for in an RPG, how they behave during an RPG, etc.? But that's another thread.

RPG Showmanship is an expectation that each players sitting down at the table will act as mature responsible individuals. Rules can't fix players who display poor sportmanship.

      I chose to use sportmanship because out of all the different ways of handling small group dynamics it has the most applicable techniques in handling both cooperation and competition in small groups.

      Tabletop RPGs are about the experience not the game. The experience being doing something interesting and fun in a setting as an imagined character. RPG Theory works when it helps players and referee how to craft a better experience.

      That an important parallel is cherry-picking useful information from what know in forming and maintaining small groups of people together in a leisure activity. But with the understanding that it is a preregister to play like having a roof, a bathroom, and adequate heating or cooling. It not the point of playing a RPG Campaign.

      \n Ron Edwards' essays had plenty of cases where he would describe certain game designs as fitting certain modes. Sometimes he phrased it as the game design lending themselves to certain modes of play, but often it would just directly associate. For example, below is his description of the "High Concept" subcategory of GNS Simulationism ( from http://www.indie-rpgs.com/articles/15/ )\n
      This sort of game design will be familiar to almost anyone, represented by Arrowflight (Setting), Pax Draconis (Setting), Godlike (Setting), Sun & Storm (Setting + Situation), Dreamwalker (Situation), The Godsend Agenda (Character-Setting tug-of-war), The Collectors (applied Fudge, Situation + Character), Heartquest (applied Fudge; Character), Children of the Sun (Setting), Fvlminata (Setting), and Dread (Situation + Character), Fading Suns (Setting), Earthdawn (Setting), Space: 1889 (Setting), Mutant Chronicles (Setting), Mage first edition (Character), Mage second edition (Setting), Ironclaw (Setting), and Continuum (Setting with a touch of System). Many Fantasy Heartbreakers fall into this category, almost all Setting-based. Some of the best-known games of this type include Tekumel, Jorune, Traveller (specifically in its mid-80s through mid-90s form), Call of Cthulhu, Pendragon, Nephilim, Feng Shui, the various secondary settings for AD&D2 like Al-Qadim, and quite a few D20 or WEG games which rely on licensing. I am coming to think of D20 as a kind of High Concept chassis, a very new and interesting development in RPG design.

      Also, most incoherent game designs are partly or even primarily High Concept Simulationist as well, with AD&D2 and Vampire (first edition) as the best-known examples.

      But to truly get rid of GNS we need something to replace it with as a vision.
      \nThe way I remember it, the initial question was why do people play RPGs. So... why do people play RPGs?
      \nExactly.
      Why?
      For they want to share fictional content with other human beings, agree about what is happening and "let it true to imagination" (and that's true from the beginning on narrative). There are a lot of different ways to achieve this goal (systems in the lumpley meaning), some of them "work" some of them do "not work". In this case we should identify if "things going wrong" lie in the group of players using the system or in the system itself.

      The 'real driver' is what the single player desires to be true in the fiction that is created between all the players. [Added]: Things that the player desires to be true, regard different elements of the fiction. Some of them regard what they "own" in the fiction. In most cases there's a "special player" that owns more fictional elements than the standard player, who usually controls his/her "playing character".

      \n So some time ago I was helping a friend in designing his own RPG. He had the setting figured out, and needed help with designing the hard rules. His idea was to play it with a specific group in mind, not to publish it or whatever. He also has a story in his head, parallel to the events in the game, that intersects with it sometimes but is pretty much stand alone.

      He tried to think it right up the dice rolls, hit points, stats, etc.; he doesn't/didn't know very much about RPG theory and certainly doesn't know a lot of games except D&D (3.5, 4E, Path), Fiasco, and VERY LITTLE of BW.

      I asked him what kind of game he was trying to design; what's the f***ing object of his game and all that. But he didn't understand my examples and explanations. So I changed the question: is your game going to be about telling stories, about experiencing the fictional world, about facing challenges, or about something else? (In my mind, I asked: "is it N, S, G or what?").

      So he understood, and answered that his game was going to be about telling stories. Yeah, cool, we have a start. But we can't start designing a game until we know *what kind of stories would it tell*. So then we got to the "goal", "object", or "whatever-name-you-want-to-give-it" after that discussion.

      I think that the creative agendas might be an obsolete concept altogether, but the ideas behind each one of the three (G, N, S) can be thought as macro-goals. These'd help define the actual goal of the game.
