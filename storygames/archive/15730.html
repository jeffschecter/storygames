\n The cafe I'm working at today has a slate-topped table with thick pieces of chalk for patrons to use. Of course, this being a hipster cafe, they have covered it with Twitter hashtags.

But imagine playing a game of Apocalypse World around this and using the chalk to draw big maps on? Or using it to draw connections between NPCs on 3x5 cards for a Fiasco game?

We've had threads before about "cool" gaming tables. But tell me about the truly USEFUL gaming tables you've seen/used/owned. What makes a gaming table truly useful?

    \n Having it be smooth, and lined with easel sheets or butcher paper. Cleaner than the slate option, and you can save a game state, set it aside, and then bring it back later. Everyone being able to scribble on a shared resource is really useful. You can keep your r-maps there, your geographical maps, notes on NPCs, things to revisit, idle sketches. stickers, scores, whatever you need.

    Having a little rim around the edge is nice too. Keeps low- to medium-energy dice on the table, keeps writing implements from rolling off, contains spills, deters squirrels.

    \n My preference in tables is very no-frills. I don't need to be able to write on it or have room for minis or have little shelves for drinks and dice and whatever; just a nice surface, good overhead lighting, no sharp edges, plenty of legroom, and ample space in front of me for whatever we're using to play, plus space in the middle that everyone can reach if necessary. Really, any good kitchen, dining room, or conference table is enough for me; I'd rather have a generically-useful table that I can add things to when necessary than a specialized gaming table with a lot of built-in features that I might not need to use in whatever our current game is.\n
    Posted By: Steve SegedyWhichever, appropriately sized tables are important.
    \nYes! Also, an appropriately-sized room to put that table in. If it's difficult for me to stand up and do anything (pace, move closer to another player to talk quietly, go to get a drink or to use the bathroom, etc.) because other people will need to move for me to get by, that's not a good place for us to be gaming. Seriously, I'd rather have NO table at all than be totally jammed in at a great table.

    \n 4'x6' with inlaid "Project board"
    It's the cheapest way to cover something with whiteboard. 4'x8' sheet from the hardware store costs less than an 8x10 inch whiteboard anywhere else.

    I've even provided 6 11x17" pull out shelves to keep character sheets on, and there's a lip on one of the short ends of the table for dry erase markers and such.

    We don't utilize it as much as you would think, but it's nice to have the option. And it makes games like Sea Dracula and Fiasco and Puppetland completely awesome.

    I would REALLY like to do the same thing to the round table out on the living room, because I think the smaller table would be better for some games/crowds, but it's not technically mine to modify.

    \n Sitting on the floor is cool with me. As is a living room, really.

    We play at a table right now, but when it comes down to it, the table isn't what's important to me. What's important is: being able to see and hear everyone, being able to be seen/heard by everyone, being able to get up and move around without making other people get out of my way, having a place to put my game stuff/roll dice/whatever, and being able to show everyone something efficiently. A table can satisfy a lot of those things, but so could a lot of places.

    (Our current table actually doesn't do all of those things; it's in a dining room, and the only way anyone on the windowed side of the room can get out of their chair is if the person next to them moves. We played in the living room a few times and it was SO MUCH BETTER in that regard, and did fine on the other criteria as well. The only problem was the two players with overcrowded schedules would get drowsy and start to fall asleep after 9pm if they were on a couch...so we went back to the table.)
