    \n May I usurp a tale of Seth's? He had just taken the train to NYC from upstate,
    had narrowly escaped a 4-hour delay due to flood,
    and was trying to get from Penn Station to Grand Army Plaza:

    "Excuse me, how do I get to Brooklyn?"

    'Take the subway'

    "Where do I buy a ticket?"

    'You need to buy a Metrocard'

    "Where do I buy one?"

    'In the subway'

    "How do I get in the subway?"

    'You need a Metrocard'

    'Where do I buy one?'

    "Look pal-sie, I already toldja, inside the frikkin' subway! Oy. Fuggetaboutit. Raspberries!"


    This set the stage for all of Camp Nerdly 4, in my opinion. Silly disarray that constantly risked
    total derailment, but ended-up being fine & hilarious.

    "Wow. We're making really-good time driving from NYC to Nerdly. Let's stop and eat"
    'Wow, there's a lot more traffic now than before we stopped.'
    But, hey, now we can have a really fun conversation! Yay!

    -Not dividing the number of chores to be a multiple of the number of attendees
    -Not writing specific times on the chore sheet
    -Not using the clear chore-description checklists from Nerdly 3

    -'Hey Todd, are you running Puppetland now?'
    "I thought I'd wait & see if somebody's running something I want to play, first."
    [Everybody else is also waiting-to-see what's being run, before they decide whether they're running anything.]

    -No way to assure the-game-you-want to play doesn't happen during the-chore-you-have-to-do

    And so on. Up to:

    -Sunday, noon: somebody decided it was time to throw the chore list in the recycling/
    so the list couldn't be consulted to make sure final chores were all accomplished.

    NONE OF THE ABOVE MATTERED!

    From all that I saw, the Jenga tower never fell.
    The disarray gave me an unpunctuated experience, where one thing just sort of flowed into another.

    When did lunch end,
    and the game at the lunch table begin?

    Are we still playing, and this is a conversational aside/
    or is the game abandoned & now we're having a prolonged conversation?

    Is it time to wrap-up the long conversation? There's nowhere else in-particular I have to be...
    but I did come here to, y'know, play games.

    I now look forward to the structure of Vinny's upcoming Double Exposure indie-design-minicon thing.
    But it's nice to know the world doesn't end, if the trains don't run on time.


    As long as the Schmiedekamps bring cupcakes.

    \n Friday, some Cornerstone, then Jeff Collyer's first session of "Last Train out of Warsaw."

    Sleep. And at a reasonable hour, no less. Odd.

    Sat with Adam Dray's "Let's make a Fantasy Heartbreaker" and got some good ideas working. I'm not sad that we didn't finish, I think most of the discussion was worthwhile.

    Instead of playing Puppetland, we had enough for two tables, so I ran one and Todd ran another. Ours ended in an epic fashion, with all the players in silent tension until the final phrase was uttered. Fan-freaking-tastic. Easily the highlight for me, and I thank everyone involved.

    More cooking. I'm glad that the reputation was well deserved; the folks at Nerdly take their chores seriously, and everyone pitched in and made things move fast.

    Afterward more small games, Cat and Chocolate, Catch the Lion, Ascention, all good stuff. Then I twisted Bill White's arm into a short Ganakagok came, and it was epic. Showed off all that I like about the game, and ended on a rather karmic and funny draw from the deck.

    Kagematsu Sunday morning. Our story was working well, and we watched a good number of failed attempts and successes (as both are equally important, I feel.) Stalled a bit by nervousness, lack of sleep, or my sometimes desire for a cheap laugh, we didn't actually get to finish, but I got to walk everyone who wanted to know through what that process looked like, and all was well.

    Goodbyes and dismantling, then away. All in all, a fantastic time.

    \n My feelings on Camp Nerdly are little mixed.

    On the Positive Side

    Great games... I stalked Don and Serafina through three games (Carry, Chronica Feudalis, and Everways), was introduced to a game called Cornerstones that seemed fun.

    Great people... saw old and new faces.

    On the Negative Side

    Scheduling Snafu -- I spent a week preparing a game that no one signed up for. Leaving the game scheduling to the last minute did not work, at least for me.

    Another Scheduling Snafu -- Moving Lunch back an hour on Saturday completely messed up the Saturday afternoon gaming schedule. We recovered, but it meant one less game I got to play.

    Conclusion

    All in all, it came out on the positive side, as I had a great time with the games I got to play, and Camp Nerdly is still a great time, but I don't think that we should leave the game scheduling to the last minute, and we should stick to the schedule rather than try and make changes at Camp.

    \n The weather was fine, and I had no complaints. I was escaping the Florida heat all weekend, so anything else was welcome. Earlier in the year could be nice too. I like the cold.

    The food contributed to the schedule slippage this year. (This was my first year, so I don't know if this is common.)
    Saturday's breakfast was so intense, that lunch prep was pushed back an hour, which blew the first afternoon slot away, leading to delaying dinner, destroying the schedule further.

    I want to say that the schedule could have worked itself out. Had we begun the weekend with an individual page per slot (those huge 28x36 pages taped to the windows) already out and ready for editing, and had the food not been so epic, I believe everything would have worked better.

    A lot of people were holding back what they offered because they didn't want to get stuck running a game the same time as something they wanted to play. Myself included. No amount of scheduling rules are going to change that.

    Also, the common stuff table was a little confusing. Were those boardgames/books just open to anyone who wanted to play? Were they there for trade? Were they there for sale?

    Maybe a little sign for "all items on this table are ___" would help in the future? Then anyone looking for something do to could just grab them and go, no questions.

    \n This was my first Nerdly, and my first time meeting anyone there. It felt a little intimidating at first, but it was a great group of friendly people. I got a chance to play a number of games that I'd only read about before, and I hope I get a chance to play with a lot of you again. Dreamation/DEXCON are looking more interesting now, as is trying to sync up with the rest of the community in the DC area.

    The disadvantage of being fairly unconnected to anyone else was that when the first line of scheduling fell through, there wasn't much of a safety net. So I found it somewhat hard to get into a fallback game. I think a tiny bit more structure here might help -- something like a "LFG" signal that says you're willing to be pulled into a spontaneous game, or a blank sign-up sheet for "available now for anything". Just something to provide that extra bit of activation energy to get games rolling, especially for us introverts.

    Also, hand soap in the bathrooms. That'd be good.

    But overall there were almost too many good things to count. Great weather, extremely friendly people willing to explain new games, impressive food, and a great community spirit for tackling the chore list.

    Games played: (Apologies if I've munged any of the names)
    - Apocalypse World, run by Sean Leventhal. A great introduction to the game, with a special player appearance by Ranger Todd, who was probably the only walk-in Camp Nerdly attendee.
    - Montsegur 1244, run by Patty Kirsch. Another great game, though it would have perhaps been better with another player or two. I felt my focus was spread a little too thinly over four characters, and ended up not doing justice to my "main".
    - Keep on the Borderlands, Basic D&D, run by Adam Dray. This was a riot. In the course of a few hours we charted the sort of degradation into mindless villainy that took me almost two years of play in middle school.\n
    GM: "The sign on the cave says 'Welcome all adventurers! Please come in and enjoy meat and ale with us!'. Inside there are three bugbears, smiling. They carry metal skewers with chunks of meat on them."
    Sean & Dave: "This seems like a trap."
    Ben, entering: "F*cking metagamers."
    \n- Last Train Out of Warsaw, run by Jeffrey Collyer. Nicely atmospheric. I think we could have used another player or two here as well; one of the players ended up largely segregated from the rest of us. But the game still delivered on the promised experience.
    - Fiasco, with a bunch of people. A good Sunday morning game. I could only play half before I had to leave, and I don't think that those of us who were new to the system were really hitting on all cylinders until the end, but I could certainly see the potential.

    Thanks again to everyone I interacted with.

    \n I ran a game of Apocalypse World at Camp Nerdly. I felt it went really well given that I had no one who had played before. In fact among my players were two people who I believe were new to the story game community and attending their first Camp Nerdly, and a walk in. One of the staff at the campground stopped by and got hooked up in my game of AW. This was awesome.

    We had a Hoarder that was clearly inspired by Ariel from the little mermaid. This combined really well with the Quarantine (Fry) played by the walk in. The hoarder would put a label on the quarantine whenever he fell asleep "Genuine pre-apocalypse human male" and Fry always had something to fall back on. There was also a brainer floating around as well as a chopper.

    This was my first attempt at running a one shot of Apocalypse World. Looking back I made a couple of mistakes. I should have pushed harder to set up inter-character conflict. Even with the brainer out there the players got along pretty well, they were even willing to go to the brainer for medical aid. Contrast to my regular game where the Savvyhead's medical technology is rightly feared because when he heals people aquarium tubing and pumps seem to dissapear and their stomach just doesn't feel right afterwards.

    The other mistake I made was not spending an extra five minutes figuring out character motivation and hook. I could have easily let people socialize a little longer and it would have done a good job covering for some of the inconsistencies in the NPCs, and uncertainty in their goals.

    The walk in took away a copy of his character sheets, as well as a moves pamphlet and information about where to find the game. I took that as a good sign. Since I have received some positive feedback so I think it went pretty well.
