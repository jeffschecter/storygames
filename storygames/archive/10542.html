\n I asked about his improv/RPG-ish projects, and the notorious Marc Majcher wrote:\n
Absolutely. We did have a WoW-ish based show called Guilds of Steel, and there was talk a year or two back about doing a Lovecrafty-type thing based on our experience with the Roach, but it never came about. Play-wise, there's a bit of crossover here and there (in a Play Unsafe kind of way), which I think has really enriched things, from both directions. As far as games go, my first (very amateur) attempt at a story game design for one of the Ronnies was strongly influenced by an improv exercise (the Story Spine), and I've got a bunch of notes towards turning one of the other long-form narrative formats we did last year (Cochise!, the improvised 70s cop show) into an RPG, but that's in the very early stages.

There's been some cross-pollination in the social circles - some of my gaming buddies taking improv classes, and dragging some other improvisers into playing some games - which has been pretty fun, as well. The two things are so close, in my mind, that I'm constantly surprised that there isn't more of an overlap.
\nAround here I know we recruited our dear friend Joe Stanton straight out of improv into our gaming group, and there's been a little crossover there. I also have "non-gamer friends" who are glad to game with us. They are really good gamers.

So let's talk about social cross-pollination a bit - how do you get the peanut butter of gaming mixed up with the chocolate of other parts of your life, and vice-versa? The improv connection is pretty strong and obvious, but surely there are other social circles where the Venn diagrams cross nicely - book clubs? Gun clubs? We're multifaceted people with lots of interests and I'm curious which ones intersect most happily.

(I will buy the first copy of Cochise!, by the way. Sight unseen.)

    \n I've been working to bring more of my non-gaming friends into the fold over the past year or two. The best way I have found is to learn what their hobbies and interests are, then find a game with a premise that incorporates their talents or knowledge.
    \n
    \nFor example, I have one friend who was a HUGE fan of Neil Gaiman, particularly American Gods and Sandman. I introduced her to RPGs with Nobilis.
    \n
    \nMy wife loves fantasy literature, Dune and Game of Thrones among them. She has a theater background. Her first real exposure to RPGing was a BW game where she played an orphaned noble involved in political intrigue in a desert wasteland city-state.
    \n
    \nI think that keeping people within their comfort zone is key.
    \n
    \nPerhaps the best example in my life came about relatively recently. My band offered up one of our demo tracks to be included with Joe McDonald's game Ribbon Drive. None of my bandmates have had too much exposure to gaming in the past, but are now all A LOT more interested, especially after they looked through the copy that he sent us (where everyone can create their own music CD and bring it to the table). I truly think that Ribbon Drive could do wonders for this kind of 'cross-pollination', especially amongst indie music and indie film circles.

    \n For me, the cross-pollination is between tabletop game folks and MUSH/MUX folks. Both are a kind of role-playing but there's a definite difference in style and expectation. Steph and I started FiranMUX after running a Firan tabletop game for 18 months for some local friends. Over the 12-13 years of the MUX, I've made dozens of good friends and many more great acquaintances. A bunch of them ended up gaming with me on the tabletop at least once or twice.

    I use what I know about tabletop GMing to make Firan better. Similarly, what I learn about running a large LARP-like game (the MUX) gives me interesting ideas for running tabletop games. Steph and I (mostly Steph, okay) wrote a LARP that entertained 40-70 players at a time for four hours. That was quite a learning experience.

    A lot of what I read is centered around MMO design, too, so I bring those ideas to my tabletop gaming and vice versa.

    \n A fair number of my usual gaming crowd are actors, and they all know each other from theatre school. At least two of my group are also in another, strictly-D&D group, run by a director of a show they were both in (I say at least, because there was a third, but she dropped out of that game). Having a large contingent of actors tends to lead to a fair bit of in-depth character work, since they like to "get into their characters' heads", but it also has its drawbacks because they are often less-good at branching out and creating new hooks for themselves without some guidance. That said, once you point them in the right direction, they will creative your ass off.

    I only know two lawyer/gamer, both of whom I met in law school. Both tend to be more Trad-oriented, preferring D&D to all else (although one of them was pretty passionate about Mage back when I was running it). They aren't, generally, rules lawyers. Nomenclature aside, I've found that gaming with lawyers tends to lead to less table-dispute, because the argument structure is so formal: each player makes a pitch for their case, and then both happily agree to abide by the GM's ruling.

    \n My boyfriend (Deliverator, for those not in the know) always says that I'm better at making and keeping non-nerd friends than he is. (I don't think of them as non-nerdy; I think of them as being nerdily into politics or organic farming or art or librarianship and not gaming/nerdy media.) These are generally not the friends I drag into playing RPGs. I have, however, dragged friends who started out interested in other "traditionally" nerdy things (sci-fi novels or TV, board games, etc) but had never or rarely played RPGs into indie games. These friends have generally enjoyed themselves.
    \n
    \nCurrently though, I'm working at a tiny, politically radical non-profit where I probably wouldn't roleplay with any of my co-workers. I mentioned offhandedly to my supervisor one day that I was going to a board game night after work, and while she was very sweet about it and asked me all the right questions (who are you going with, what are you going to play), there was definitely an undercurrent of "well, that's kind of weird and nerdy." To be fair, board game night may sound totally un-fun to her because so many mainstream board games suck. Still, it's really really hard for me to explain role-playing to people with absolutely no context, especially if they not be nerdily inclined themselves, and if she was incredulous about board games then there's no play I could do roleplaying justice. So I've made the decision to keep quiet about my nerdliness at the office. I love my boss and the work I do, and I know she's liked my work so far; since I have plenty of opportunity to do nerdy hobbies outside of work, I don't feel the need to broadcast said nerdiness at work and risk the good relationship I'm developing.
    \n
    \nMy activist tendencies and my gamer nerd tendencies often feel rather at odds with each other, but that is probably a topic for a separate thread.
