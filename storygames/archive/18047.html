    \n Maybe you could create a "single meta-set" of stats for each playbook by looking at the range of stats?

    This means that each playbook would get a basic stat defining it (the one at +2), and then secondary stats which are possible to have above 0. (Ideally, they would also be placed furthest from stats which are negative, if you can do that spacing visually, but maybe that's too much of a stretch.)

    Here's the battlebabe:\n
    \u2022 Cool+3 Hard-1 Hot+1 Sharp+1 Weird=0
    \u2022 Cool+3 Hard-1 Hot+2 Sharp=0 Weird-1
    \u2022 Cool+3 Hard-2 Hot+1 Sharp+1 Weird+1
    \u2022 Cool+3 Hard=0 Hot+1 Sharp+1 Weird-1
    \nSo, ranges:

    Cool +3
    Hard -2 to 0
    Hot 0 to +1
    Sharp 0 to +1
    Weird -1 to +1

    Averages:

    Cool +3
    Hard -1.33
    Hot +1.25
    Sharp +0.75
    Weird -0.25

    The averages seem to reflect the playbook overall better than the ranges. What do you think?

    \n Not that this is going to help terribly, but the way I myself read AW playbook stats is like this.

    1. One stat it always has at max.
    2. Two stats it has at varying mid levels
    3. One stat it always has low, except
    4. For that one stat-line where it has it's usually low secondary stat high, but all the other ones are now jumbled up.

    So AW statlines have a core, three variations and a single twist.

    \n There's a set of network analysis techniques you could use, where you transform the class-by-stat averages into class-by-class affiliations and then use multi-dimensional scaling to visualize the resulting positions. It would look something like this--

    Taking the class-by-stat-average matrix and transforming it into affiliations by row produces this class-by-class matrix, where the intersection of each row and column is a measure of how similar one class is to another. The diagonal must reflect some sort of measure of the class's "stat power," in the sense that it will probably be higher for playsets that have smaller total average penalties, but I don't know.



    Anyway, having done that, it's possible to use the matrix as an indicator of the "proximity" of one playset to another in a multidimensional space. That gives you this:



    The only thing that doesn't give you is what the underlying dimensionality is, but you could maybe indicate the top stat for each playset and that would give you a sense. But at least you can tell that the Angel and the Gunlugger are more similar to each other than either is to the Hardholder or the Battlebabe, and a quick scan of the playset means seems to confirm that.
