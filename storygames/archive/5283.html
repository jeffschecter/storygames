\n I'm just wondering if I'm the only person who, when he picks up a new RPG, has to take the time to write up extensive notes for myself and my players.

In essence, I find myself trying to sell them on the concept of the game (doing this for an abstract game like "Nobilis" is essential); the character creation rules (as simply as possible, with page references if they have the book, and short summaries otherwise); and drop in a few pieces of the best art, if possible, to give them a feel for the genre.

I spent years (years, I tell you) over two editions doing this with Exalted before I realized that it was WAY more complicated for myself and my group. [No offense to people who can juggle hundreds of Charms in their heads].

A lot of the people I game with are terribly clever, but they like things clearly explained. I do some of my best explaining on paper, especially if it involves rules.

It's as if game writers come up with the concepts and rules from their design notes and playtesting, then they get layed out accordingly with flavor text and art, etc., and then the reader has to extract the essentials into something that can be digested by the players.

[I'm not suggesting players don't buy copies of the game they're playing - I make sure that everyone who plays in a game I run has a copy of the game. I'm just saying that they always seem to need a handout that summarizes things].

Thoughts?

    \nNot really. If that much is required, I probably won't play it or pick it up. I like cheet sheets, but those are usually available in a GM screen or PDF format. I often can't be arsed to make my own if its more than what I can give verbally.

    For Nobilis, I simply print up the "No You're EnNobled?" pdfs and handed those out. That was enough.

    For Exalted, all I find I have to do is give a basic premise of the setting - only a little more detailed than "You're demigods who are out to change the world" and talk a little bit about the brief history, types of Exalts, potential antagonists, and the potential of magic and weird setting elements.

    I'm more and more drawn towards games with light settings, specficially stuff that is informed and influenced by the players, so there's less and less I need to fill them in on before hand.

    If I need more than that to run the game, I'll convert it or, again, just not run it. Like for Exalted, the charms are just too much book keeping. But it's fairly easy to convert to HeroQuest, so that's what I did. For Wild Talents or Spirit of the Century, there are a lot of rules options for combat that I don't remember, but the systems are simple enough that I can wing it based on what's going on and look up the rule to remember later if I want a more official version.

    \n I've had groups where this was pretty much a requirement to get anything started. It had to be boiled down to bullet-points and hard-sold. Pretty much anything new for some groups, in one case even anything AT ALL. I try not to play with people who need to be chivvied that much now.

    I've had game texts where I needed to do this - I had to re-state and outline the rules in order to get it installed into my brain. This is okay - sometimes a designer and I just aren't quite on the same wavelength - but it reduces the likelihood I'll actually play the game.

    And I've had game concepts where I needed to do this. That trick never works. If my idea for a really cool game can't be sold and communicated successfully in a short conversation, it's not going to fly anyhow.

    \n Yeah I'm with you Vasco, I do rules references for everything I play. I don't like flipping through books at the gaming table, plus I usually end up learnign the rules by creating the references.

    However, I also create game pitches to sell the games to my fellow players. This is usually something I do if a game really gets my creative juices going, but we're currently playing something else. Then my sales pitch is already online and ready for when we next start a new game. I also do some if I expect the game to be a hard sell. Another huge reason is the language barrier. I post my selling points in both Swedish and English. While my players all speak English, they are much more comfortable with Swedish, and having translated some of the basic concepts and terms from the game is a very good prep exercise for actually playing the game.

    \n We certainly use the Dogs cheat sheet in play. I have also compiled my own cheat sheets with photocopies at Kinkos, cut-and-pasted up and re-copied (in particular, for Hero and for Warhammer FRPG). Going further back, every Hero System campaign I have started has made extensive use of the campaign Design Sheet, which lets one mark out Powers or limit how they're bought so that you get the desired "feel" for the setting without having to negotiate every single player-made power. That summary also made makes it REALLY easy for a newer player, because they only had a subset of the Hero System to "grok."

    But I never do what I thought you meant when I read the thread title: "translate" game terms into other terms that may or may not be as evocative. I think that just leads to longer learning time, as one must grok the game in these "familiar" terms and then do it all over again with the "specialty" terms the game text actually uses. It also kicks referencing the game text in the nuts, because only those who know the "translation key" can reference the rule text thought its Table of Contents or Index.

    Now, on a more general level, I'd certainly associate game system elements, to speed teaching (ex: "OK, Coins are used almost like Dice Pools"). But it's pretty rare with my regular (ha! as if!) game group that I could do a lot of cross-game associations with "indie" games, because of their lack of exposure (i.e. none--buncha conservatives trying to relive the 1980s with games that never deliver).
