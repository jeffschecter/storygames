\n I just had a conversation with Norther on #indierpgs that made me realize there's a missing indie game.

Stoughton: I'm planning on running varying sessions of In A Wicked Age and Camaraderie in the same setting - one is conflict among the players and the other one is external conflict.
Stoughton: The stories have two different paradigms of destruction in that space. But nothing gets created. So yeah, there's the random draw that can create empires and whatever but building a faction, a castle, a religion - there's nothing generative in the stories the game tells.
Norther: are there any games that inspire creation?
Stoughton: That was MY question.
Stoughton: Uni's a good game but it's not targeted enough at what I want.
Norther: i dont think I know of any actually
Norther: although I always do it as a player
Norther: create safehouses, businesses, empires
Norther: a game about a criminal empire would be creative
Stoughton: I mean, I want a game that you create SOMETHING and it's a criminal empire, kingdom, religion, whatever according to taste. It includes making a map.
Norther: criminal organiation, start with a bar, work yourself up...protection, smuggling, etc
Stoughton: Exactly.
Stoughton: But I don't know of any games like that.
Norther: wow that actually sounds cool
Stoughton: Yeah. It's strange that there's nothing for that niche besides some board and videogames.
Stoughton: At the end of the game you'd have these locations full of NPCs and stuff that would be fodder for the intra-player or extra-player conflicts of the other games.

    \n Wolfe has it.

    Right now I'm considering playing around with Carcassone-like tiles and a die.

    Take turns, roll the die.

    1 - Add an NPC to somewhere, connected to at least 1 other NPC
    2 - Lay a new tile of your choice and tell us about what is on that location
    3 - Narrate an event that happened on the map, caused by a previous event.
    4 - Narrate an event that happened on the map, which caused another event already mentioned.
    5 - Point at a location and make a "link" to another map, such as a staircase (on a dungeon / castle map), a town (on the world map) et cetera. The link goes on a list of unfinished maps.
    6 - Your choice: Decide the map is finished, in which case all players add tiles until it's properly bounded OR Decide the map is unfinished and challenge the other players to add something to the map - whatever you want to challenge them is fine.

    \n How about add a layer to Carcassonne?

    Meeples in fields are "common" NPCs. Regular people, farmers, blacksmiths, soldiers. Meeples in cities are important civil figures: rulers, generals, viziers. Meeples on roads are important trade figures, ranging from powerful merchants to bandit/crime leaders. Monestary figures could be mystics and recluses: hermits, sorcerers, priests, survivalist gun nuts.

    The big problem is that the default map has nothing else: no forests, bodies of water, mountains, etc. And the cities are rather huge if you're considering it for a continent map, and even for a regional map, the city sizes can be odd (two massive cities in close proximity). The Hunters and Gatherers might be better. It has forests, rivers, lakes instead of roads. Also a new placement thingy, huts, which could serve as cities. Another way, is to use the basic game and "cities" become something else, like, mountain ranges, forests, or well, whoever places a meeple there gets to decide what that is, and monestaries might be cities.

    All in all, I'm not sure if the results are much better than Dawn of Worlds. Although NPC generation is built in and it's more focused on whatever scale you need it for, you have to make some conceptual tweaks to make the map work.
