\n I am looking for techniques, exercises, or specific questions to encourage players to look out for each other's fun.

Something akin to asking each player to highlight something they appreciated about someone _else's_ performance during debrief.

I'd like to shift the focus towards that direction, to prioritize that over self-centered or self-congratulatory behavior.

Can anyone point me towards something that's worked for them?

    \n WHAT I DID: When I produced "Ghosts in the Machine" (a Hypertheater piece described in more detail here), I began with a "prehearsal" for the actors that was literally a party. We had food, alcohol, music, etc, and the only stipulation was that the actors had to remain in character the entire time. This was done months ahead of the actual show. In that first party we generated many of the character details that later peppered all three levels of plot. But that was a long time ago, and my grasp on design theory was very nascent.

    WHAT I WISH I HAD DONE: I wish I had specifically asked them to focus more on their connections and relationships to other characters, rather than simply viewing the world from their own individual characters' perspectives. I wish I had told them to look for some "big picture" themes in those relationships. We could have easily made a simple game out of it: For instance, write all the characters names all over a whiteboard and go around the group; you get a reward every time you propose a connection for your character that the other party accepts. Draw a line between those characters on the board and label it. Every line drawn will end up in the script or prep materials. Yay You!
