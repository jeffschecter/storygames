    3. Will you offer feedback on my interpretation of the CA's?
    \nSure. I'll start now, at risk of turning this thread into another one of those infamous threads.

    I think you've misunderstood something about Creative Agenda in general. CA describes an emergent property of an instance of actual play. Thus you can say that that game of D&D 4E you played with your friends was (or was not) Gamist. You're on slippery ground as soon as you start talking about individual players as having Creative Agendas or talking about games as having Creative Agendas. Sure, the articles talk about this game or that game being Narrativist or whatever, but it's a shortcut for saying that the game is a good tool for producing Narrativist play. Likewise, when you say a person is Simulationist, you're talking about an individual's general preference for a certain kind of play. I think, if you watched me play different games for a year, you'd have a hard time putting a GNS label on me. I play all sorts of games and these actual play sessions see different Creative Agendas at work (and some probably without any CA at work, unfortunately).

    Most of Forge theory / The Big Model describes behavioral patterns, not rules text. The essays sometimes analyze how the theory intersects with rules as written, and that gives people the impression that TBM is a good tool for analyzing rules text, but I think it's not well developed for that.\n
    4. I have general worry about repetitiveness. If we're looking at common mechanis will a given CA's react in the same or similiar way and if so at what point does it stop being noteworthy?
    \nMechanics taken individually fall into the Techniques layer of The Big Model. Creative Agenda is a spear that punches through all the layers. That means that you cannot predict CA as a function of a single Technique or game mechanic. You can predict how collections of game mechanics might influence the Creative Agenda of a group at play, but the other layers (especially Social Contract) will play a big part in defining the CA of the instance of play, if it even has a CA.

    \n Hi Fred,

    When I first read Ron's articles and tried to apply them to my experiences and to facets of games, Ron told me I was doing it wrong. Apparently this is the norm. "G or N or S?" says nothing (directly) about the experience of play, or any given moment of player choice/action in play. CA is intended to describe the behavior of a play group on a larger scale. So, really, the strongest statement you could make about a game mechanic would be something like "this game appears designed to foster a narrativist CA, and this mechanic expresses/supports that as follows:".

    I know this sounds nit-picky, but given your intent to "solidify these concepts and help learn to design for them", I thought it might be relevant.

    I've found that going over my past play and asking others to help me figure out what type of CA was in operation was helpful in "solidifying". As for designing, I'll throw in my opinion that picking G or N or S as a starting point for design is tough. There are so many diverse CAs within each of the 3 types, that just picking a type still leaves you with a blank slate. It makes more sense to me to start with an inspiration for play first, and then to troubleshoot your emerging design for CA coherence as you refine your vision and how you're communicating it.

    As for reading a CA-based game analysis, I'd love to! But only by someone I trust to know the theory. And knowing how long it took me to "get it", I'm skeptical on that front.

    \n Two things:

    (1) To reiterate Adam: I think it's important to recognize that "Creative Agenda" as an empirical thing emerges in play as a consequence of what can be called the "reward cycle": how game-mechanical advantages, fictional consequences, or social benefits (and the utility associated with each by players) are allocated as a result of the actions people take at the table. It's a social fact, in other words. The game-text arguably "underdetermines" CA, although it's possible to talk about the text's CA assumptions (in other words, how the author thinks people should play the game). Likewise, individual players may be said to have CA preferences, although it's probably closer to the truth to say that our tastes (or "sockets" as some would have it) allow us to plug in to a variety of different games and be happy with them as long as it's rewarding to us on its own terms. So, for example, as long as the high-fantasy flavor is there, I might be reasonably happy playing either D&D or Burning Wheel. Does that make me a "Sim" player? Not in any useful way, since it suggests that I and the Lovecraftian genre purist are closer in tastes than a hack & slash dungeoneer. Better to say that my high-fantasy preferences might contribute to a Simulationist CA on the part of a group that I play with.

    (2) If you're going to indulge in textual criticism of RPGs, you might want to read these reflections on the craft:

    Ahlroth, J. (2008). "Leave the Cat in the Box: Some Remarks on the Possibilities of Role-Playing Game Criticism." Playground Worlds. Available online.

    Laws, R. (1995). "The Hidden Art: Slouching Towards A Critical Framework for RPGs." Inter*action #1. Available online.

    They suggest that the critic needs to engage with play and people's participation in play; more broadly, they suggest that the critic has to be self-reflexive about the theoretical apparatus he or she is bringing to bear upon play.

    \n It might work, but you're outside Forge / Big Model theory if you go that route. You're applying your own "GNS as a psychographic profile" theory then.

    The Big Model only recognizes Creative Agenda as an emergent property of a complete instance of play with a set of players, often measured over multiple sessions.

    The main reason that GNS doesn't work as a player profile is that players like different things at different times. For example, you can categorize food as sweet or savory, but you can't categorize an eater as sweet or savory and have it mean the same thing. You might categorize an eater as sweet-loving or savory-loving, but it isn't a very good profile scheme because most eaters love both savory foods and sweet foods at different times. The best you can do is find out what kind of food they're in the mood for right now.

    One way to look at Creative Agenda is outcome. Because you need to see the complete instance of play -- which basically conforms to a complete reward cycle -- you cannot judge Creative Agenda (except for guessing) until you see that people got rewarded for playing in the way they wanted to be rewarded, as a group.

    Another way to look at Creative Agenda is a shared set of goals and aesthetic concerns for creating play. "Hey, guys, let's play a certain way with the promise that we'll get this specific kind of reward for it at the end, and let's all make sure that everything we do drives toward that payoff." You can't have that collaborative reward-aiming-and-sharing without other people on the same page. You can't share goals and aesthetics with yourself. That's not sharing...

    Does this help?
