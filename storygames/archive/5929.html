\n Six guys of diverse skills and backgrounds get together. They leave civilization and invade an underground complex filled with traps and hostile animals and people. They are patently risking their lives in this endeavor. The payoff? Some material wealth, some experience/life lessons/practical training, and some gear that will help them do it again the next month. Now, the payoff is not so great that they can retire for the rest of their lives or anything -- they are supporting themselves for maybe a month or three.

Is risking your life for three months' livelihood a worthwhile trade? What would make it worthwhile -- is mundane life really that bad? Is the payoff actually better than it seems? Is the payoff something that can't be got any other way and is essential to keeping their families at home alive? And this is something that they do regularly -- this isn't for the one big payoff that can buy back the farm from foreclosure; this is a way of life. They will do it again, and again, and again, until they die, or... or is there some long-term goal that makes all the risk and work worthwhile?

    \n Once I worked out some of the basic assumptions in D&D 3.5. ~13 level-appropriate encounters to go up a level, and 4 level-appropriate encounters per day.

    Which means it takes about 2 months of solid adventuring in general to go from 1st level punk to 20th level world-ruler. Heck, be generous, and call it 4 months: give yourself some downtime for natural healing and travel (before you get teleport) and whatnot.

    But where will you find a place where you can find dozens, nay, hundreds of potentially dangerous encounters, arranged by difficulty, passively awaiting you to meet them when you feel like it? Oh, hey, dungeons.

    Yeah, there's a really good chance that you will die, permanently, long before you hit 20th level (especially at those low levels). But getting that sort of power, in that sort of time frame? There's a lot of people who would consider that worth the risk. (Even ignoring the average expected wealth by character level, which give you insane returns for your time invested, compared to just making profession checks)

    Our fateful heroes arrive in town. Just a couple of average joe's. They stop in at Town 1-A, grabbing some weapons and grub before they go on their first expedition together. And while they're in town, news of what they're about to attempt leaks into the village (after all, it always does). Of course they scoff.

    The heroes take their leave.

    A week goes by, and no one heres a peep from our fateful heroes.

    And after two weeks, people start to worry. They start to wonder. The children play make believe about how these heroes have surely defeated the red wyrm. No one quite believes it, but they prefer believing that to believing that they let innocent youth walk to their doom in some abyssal vacant lot.

    The heroes gave an expected return date. And on that date, the townsfolk just happen to gather in the town square. And they just happen to bring with them celebration supplies. Not that any of them are expecting anything. And... oh?... what's that on the horizon? Six bloodied, battered, young snot-nosed punks. And one of them is holding the head of the great red wyrm.

    The bards take the story on the road immediately. By the time the heroes are done with their celebrations, news has already hit Town 1-B. And when they get there, the villagers gladly give them maps and supplies. These are the fabled warriors! And the celebration this time is bigger, because the villagers expected them to succeed. By the time they hit Town 1-E, they are famous and beloved and highly spoken of.

    But then they hit Town 2-A. And no one knows their name. No one believes in them. They are most certainly not celebrities. But they hear rumor of an albino troll that's been menacing the countryside recently.

    And already they can feel the surge of excitement. The taste of glory.

    God only knows how good 2-E is going to feel when they hit it.

    \n Even in D&D modules, there's usually some sort of set-up that explains why the PCs are doing what they're doing. In the Giants modules, there's a background blurb that explains that the Duke has sent the PCs. OK, why he didn't send a larger army is a good question... the Fellowship of the Ring is explained in the volume of that name, as requiring secrecy, hence it's small size (no army could be large enough).
    \n
    \nBasically there's no way to make economic sense of this model in any realistic way. In the real world, nobody would do it. David's example of cavers notwithstanding... what is the actual percentage of caving outings that end up in death? A careful caver can expect to survive. Since his "foe" is the cave, his odds of success lie mostly in how careful he plans and proceeds. With a mortal foe, you have something working actively and creatively against you, and that's just too unpredictable.
    \n
    \nThere has to be some rationale. It's not hard to come up with one, however. For one, the adventurers could simply go in always not expecting too much opposition. Consider that Howard Carter could be relatively certain that there would be no monsters in his expedition to find egyptian mummies. The fact that so many people associated with the discovery died does not deter anyone from being an archeologist today.
    \n
    \n
    \n"Dungeoneers" would simply have the same sort of assumptions. That they aren't going to run into trouble that they can't handle with near 100% success rates. That is, I think that they think that the dungeons are basically empty. And are surprised when they find things living there...
    \n
    \n...after all it's usually pretty surprising. I mean why has that band of gnolls ended up living here in this old crypt, anyhow?
    \n
    \nEarthdawn, if I'm remembering correctly, goes somewhat in this direction. The "kaers" that people go looking through, are expected to be abandoned, perhaps even long looted.
    \n
    \n
    \nNow, this leaves two problems:
    \n1. Hey, there are GNOLLS here! When the group discovers the gnolls, the fact that they decide to take them on is interesting. Perhaps they figure that they can ambush the gnolls, and that these three here are the only ones in the crypt. The rationale for attacking, however, is viable... banditry has been an occupation forever, and taking people's stuff is good enough reason for some. Given that they're "evil" beings, it's not even really banditry from their POV. But they do have to have numbers, or surprise, or something, else rushing a 7 foot tall hyena-man sounds like a bad idea to me.
    \n2. Why is it that in every crypt we delve into, there are GNOLLS! For no particularly good reason, other than the phenomenon seemed to smack me most with this show, I call this the Magnum PI effect. Why is it that week after week Magnum is falling for yet another beautiful client, and getting into shootouts... with folks in Hawaii? I mean any one of his adventures is pretty plausible, but week after week? Don't you think he'd switch careers to nighttime security if he knew that he was going to get shot at so much?
    \n
    \nOr maybe this is better thought of as the "ER" effect. Huh, somebody got shot in the ER AGAIN? Wow, how many does that make now? Almost as many as explosions that have rocked the ER. What sane person would work in that hospital with it's record?
    \n
    \nThis is simply an intrinsic problem with the episodic format. I think you simply have to hand-wave it. Yeah it's damn odd that there's horrors in every Kaer you come across. That every crypt has gnolls in it. But, hey, what can you do? The next one will be easy, right? Has to be. We've certainly killed off all of the gnolls in this area, no?
    \n
    \n
    \nThere's another solution, too. Which is that these adventurers happen to know that they are simply better than their likely opposition. This has three problems, however:
    \n1. The system usually doesn't support this. Fact is that even in D&D, things can and will go wrong. In this case the promise of ressurection is what must keep em going (don't get me started on what this would mean culturally).
    \n2. It drains some of the heroism. If they know that they're able to kick ass, then it's not nearly as brave for them to charge the 2HD gnolls.
    \n3. It's just weird. Why do they keep coming up against foes that are precisely the right difficulty for them to take out? Good research?
    \n
    \n
    \nA while back there was an idea that somebody had to use dungeons as resolution systems. The reason I bring this up is because the dungeon was always spurred by some rationale that was created to support the need to go into it. Want to win the affection of the princess? Then we'll put her at the end of the selected dungeon, and if they get there, her hand is won.
    \n
    \nI like this. Just create a rationale that's compelling for the character, each time a dungeon comes up. Make it an explicit part of the process - one that usually happens implicitly anyhow, where a player says, "My character is itching to hit the road again..." or whatever. Modify the dungeon to take these things into account. Is the character's goal fame? Then there's always some idol to get that owning it will make him famous, or some deed to be done in the adventure that will make his name known far and wide.
    \n
    \n
    \nBut what's not going to work is to try to come up with some economic model that explains folks traipsing into mortal danger for cash. Either they don't expect much trouble, or they know that they can take care of the trouble.
    \n
    \nIt may simply be easier to just handwave the whole thing... given that's what people have done for decades.

    \n Wow, thanks for all the responses, guys!

    So yes, you can create a metaplot thing that creates a justifiable in-character reason for going into each dungeon every time. We already have a lot of these games. I think we've got this one handled. Everybody on this forum can easily bullshit a reason for their character to risk their lives.

    Thing is, that's appropriate when the band of PCs are basically the only dungeon-delvers out there. Maybe there's one other rival team for plot interest and character foils. But, by and large, what we're talking about is individual and personal reasons to go dungeon-delving. I want to reconfigure things to create a cultural, economic reason to go dungeon-delving. A reason or reasons that are broad-based and ubiquitous, so that dungeon delving is a recognized (if not respected) sort of career track.

    To that end, we have the following suggestions so far:

    The Libertarian "The System is Irrevocably Bad for the Human Spirit" thing -- Life in medieval times is nasty, brutish, and short! Dungeon delving is a preferable alternative to a life of drudgery. Yes, subsistence farming sucks. In medieval times, the farmers are usually serfs, and the reason they don't leave is because they're not legally allowed to, and if you try the baron's knights will run you down on their horses and surround you with pikes and march you back to the farms. Now -- if you had equipment for dungeon-delving (a trusty sword, a little armor, and the ability to cast magic missile), why the heck aren't you mounting an insurrection? Serfs stayed at their tasks because they did not have the resources to overcome their oppressors. If they did have those resources, why in the blue blazes would they go tearing off into a dungeon?

    The Modern Libertarian Version: Or, alternately, real life is terrible, and adventure is categorically better, because it's exciting! Ron says that if it were economically viable, he'd choose adventuring over his current life. Well... would you really? Or put it this way: if you went through your first 'adventure' and had to put a sword in the gut of two dozen 'monsters' who are clearly civilized, would you really do it again? And if you would, do you think you're representative of a large segment of the population?

    Remi's Monster Pogrom I can see this, and Roger's holy war variant. I'm not sure if I'd want to play it -- or at least, I don't know if I'd want to play it without the ability and systemic support to turn away from the genocide and try to subvert the system that put me and countless other youths in that situation. Which, admittedly, sounds like a fun game, but it's veering off from what I'm aiming at.

    Here's Tristan, who I'm just going to quote:\n
    Posted By: TristanSome do it for the fame ("people will tell my tales, bards will sing my name, children will play to be me!"), some for the money (greed is a funny thing: once you hit the big pot, you are only thinking about the next big treasure there is... ask Bill Gates!), some for the thrill of adventure (I think of them as the professional extreme sportsmen of the time), some out of boredom and monotony (we have had enough "pig farmer" examples, haven't we?), some out of "scientific curiosity" ("Haven't you heard? Albino Troll's blood is the prime ingredient in KFC secret "dragon wings" recipe...") and some can even have some obscure, personal reasons ("My name is Inigo Montoya. You killed my father. Prepare to die!"). Just as in real life, not everyone chooses his profession by the same reasons.
    \nSome of this is the individual and situational stuff that I'd want to get away from, but the others are all different takes on essentially the same thing. There is value to be gained in the dungeon that isn't available elsewhere. There is the fame that I'll talk about in the next bullet point. There is material wealth, there are rare substances (and presumably, phenomena), there is excitement. Now the question becomes -- what makes the dungeon the locus of those things, and more importantly, what makes it the exclusive locus of those things? Why is there no better, perhaps less-riskier, option, for accruing fame, wealth, knowledge, excitement? What makes the dungeon special?

    To Become a Famous Hero (note the caps) Okay, I can totally get on board with this idea, especially if there's a culture that celebrates these "dungeon heroes." But -- the games that we have so far do not support this very well. I would love to play this game, but I can't think of a system that would actually reward me for becoming famous. So here's the question -- what would that system look like, and how would it be mirrored in the world and setting?

    Adrenaline Junkies I think there's a very large difference between caving, which is certainly seriously dangerous, and fighting your way through a band of gnolls, a cave troll, and countless traps designed to kill you, only to pit your mettle against a fire breathing dragon. Yes, caving has serious dangers that require respectful care, etc etc -- but can we all agree that there's an order of magnitude difference between caving and "adventuring?" Extreme sports and adrenaline-junkie pasttimes are relatively safe -- they're spiced with danger. Dungeoneering is dangerous, period -- it's not spiced with danger, it is just popping the cayenne pepper of danger into your mouth. Some really crazy folks might do it exclusively for the thrill, but I'd think they'd be in the minority. This wouldn't be enough to support an entire lifestyle of dungeoneering.

    I'm really liking where the thread is going, and you can be sure I'm taking notes.

    \n Sorry, I wasn't damning it.

    I may have stated my views overly negatively -- being a pacifist and anti-war -- but I do think there are some really valid justifications for dungeon-delving, even as there are valid justifications for being a soldier in Iraq. (I may not agree with the justifications, but they exist and they work for many, many people.)

    One way to make it "not murder" is to make it "war." Another is to make it "self-defense."

    Dungeon-delving almost always includes killing things, and what's more, knowing you're going to kill things -- or at the very least, run into things that want to kill you. (And the best way to prevent them from killing you is to kill them first.)

    In your list of reasons for dungeon-delving, I see that you've left off "community defense." I think that's a big omission, because one of the standard tropes of the fantasy adventure setting is not just that the monsters down there are big and bad and dangerous, but also that if they're not destroyed, they will come and get you.

    They're not merely situational enemies, which, if you leave the dungeon alone, won't come and bother you. They're not the haunted house on the corner which only gets you if you enter through the creaky gate out front -- they're the graveyard full of the living dead which will shamble out at the next full moon and eat your friends.

    A dungeon is basically a den of evil; a terrorist training camp. If you allow it to fester, the evil cult or archmage or lich or orc tribe or dragon will come and kill you. The only, only way to prevent this is to take the fight to them before it's too late.

    I'm not damning this, I'm celebrating it. The setup is clearer to me than to some other people, apparently: dungeon-delving isn't just about getting rich and doing a job, it's about saving civilization (or parts thereof) from an existential threat. You don't do it because you want to make money -- become a merchant or a craftman, for crying out loud! -- but because otherwise, the dragon will come out of the ground and burn your home down.

    (One of the reasons I say I'm celebrating is that I have a strong, strong preference as a DM for heroes who do take that kind of "heroic" view of the world around them, rather than those who only operate for financial gain. I don't believe that dungeon-delving can work in many other circumstances. The only good for-profit justification, as I see it, involves the establishment of some kind of large "industry," such as an Adventurers Guild or a Royal Library, that pays people to go in dungeons and report back to them. And that seems too contrived.)

      \n There are lots of good ideas here, but to me they each miss one of several criteria that I think Josh has. I'll list what I think the criteria are, and Josh can correct me if I'm wrong. When posting, consider whether or not your idea meets each of these criteria:
      \n
      \n1. Good Incentive - There has to be a strong incentive to go, good enough to overcome the risk of death. Frankly all of the things mentioned can be the incentives (fame, money, sex, annonymity, etc, etc, etc). The model just has to make the incentive strong enough that we'll believe that people will risk death for it repeatedly.
      \n
      \n2. Not Too Good an Incentive - if the incentive is enough wealth that you can retire, or will be famous forever, or get the woman of your dreams, etc, then why would you go back and do it again? Somehow the incentive is not great enough to retire once you get it. Lots of people are proposing ideas for incentives that, if achieved, would lead the character to never feel the need to go again. Addicted to it? After just once? Sure, I understand the halcyon feeling of ozone in the air where you get nostalgic for the "good old days" and sitting on your rear seems a terrible fate... it's just not as bad as death.
      \n
      \n3. Small Bands - for some reason, despite the high level of incentive, we don't send armies, we send small groups of people.
      \n
      \n
      \nThe problem I have here is that these seem to be mutually exclusive. Basically there's a cost-benefit analysis. According to the show "Deadliest Catch" this job, considered phenomenally dangerous, results in .3% (300 per 100,000) fatalities each year. This is the level of danger that results in a higher rate of pay...
      \n
      \nWhat would you estimate as your chances of surviving as a team of folks going into a place with lots of deadly creatures? Where you don't know what you'll encounter, but it's sure to be something monsterous in terms of size, natural weaponry, or it might even be intelligent, and weilding weapons? I think that the natural tendency is to just think 50%. In fact, if the dungeon is balanced, shouldn't it be around there?
      \n
      \nHeck, let's say that somehow we're experts in this area (how one gets to be, I don't know), and well prepared... call it only 25% chance of dying. That's two orders of magnitude larger a chance to die. Hell, let's say that the guy is really confident in his own abilities and is using only a 10% chance of death. The crab fisherman, intending on fishing for only ten seasons before retiring has better than a 97% chance of surviving to this retirement. The adventurer going on ten adventures has only a 35% chance of making it all the way.
      \n
      \nOh, sure, each success means that the next one is still at 90%. But tell me one activity that people do that results in 10% death rate, outside of being in an army and fighting a battle? In which case the incentive is (even abstractly or putatively, we all know that there are lots of exceptions in history) survival. Because you can't expect to enjoy your riches if you don't live to do so. This is a level of danger at which the only way that I can see people accepting the risk is if it means that the rewards will satisfy most of their dreams in one shot.

Instead the job of retrieving these valuable will be an honored profession. And they'll send as many people as possible to get these things, because it means a higher rate of success, and a lower rate of death. Yes, you're risking more lives, but the fact is that, tactically, there's relative safety in numbers.

He's the daydreamer, or the kid who's mad all the time for no reason, or the one you can just tell thinks she belongs somewhere else, you know?       Let me tell you about the most successful game of D&D I ever ran:

      I figured out a while ago that fiddling with the rules of D&D was a dead end. Sure, I could design something that worked better for what I wanted than the original rules, but they'd always be house rules, and it messed with the essentially gamist premise of D&D, which is mastering the given rules.       Player Characters became superheroes. Hit Points made you well-nigh impossible to kill. You could hit harder, faster and more accurately than anyone else. And not anyone could go up levels. Only certain gifted people could do it, and only with training from the right organization. There were a handful of recognized "paths" to this power, which gave different abilities - classes.

      And everyone in the world knew it. There were standard rates for hiring these people. People would carefully assess the level of the threat they were facing, and would hire the right level of adventurer to deal with the threat. Well-known dungeons were mapped according to level.

      Adventuring parties are a business. They need to stay small, so they get a good return on each mission, and so they can keep expenses down. Roughly four people ensures a good mix of skills and power to deal with most threats. There's some competition between the different parties - competition for lucrative jobs, and also some rivalry. Some groups will take any job to get ahead. Others are more scrupulous.

      \n Simon, what you're describing is very similar to how we have played our Forgotten Realms campaigns. I'll indulge myself and talk about that a bit in the hopes that it will help the JBR with his game.
      Basically, as per Simon's game, we assumed that the game rules are the physics of the D&D world. The fact that you can shoot a fireball a precise number of feet based on your mage level and that there is specific spell access based on level means that there are in-world measurable ways of determining the level of spell-casters. As such, it makes sense that this sort of idea could be extended to fighters and rogues, etc. though it would probably range within about 3 points of to-hit bonus since that should be easily detectable. For example, by watching fights between untrained village youth, you could determine that the average untrained person hits someone not wearing armor about 50% of the time, but a "third-level" fighter hits about 65% of the time.
      Now as far as fitting into the world, one of the assumptions of Forgotten Realms is that the reason there are so many tiny nations and independent city-states is that having a couple of 20th level adventurers living in a city or ruling it, means that they can effectively hold off armies and subjugate nearby towns at whim. Which TOTALLY MAKES SENSE if you accept the D&D rules as the physics of the universe. After all, even a mid-level mage is far harder to destroy (has more hitpoints) than an iron door.
      So if the physics of the universe decree that certain people are essentially super-powered and that there are similarly super-powered threats, a lot of the assumptions of dungeon-crawling begin to make sense. Why send 4 people instead of an army? Because the army would be decimated, even though they might eventually slay the dragon by sheer force of numbers.
      In at least one campaign we played (based out of Waterdeep) there was an Adventurer's Guild whose job it was to match up adventurers with appropriately difficult work. As such, they got a 10% cut of any rewards the adventurers got. Similarly, they acted as a clearing-house for old adventuring gear sold at a discount by people who no longer needed it because they either had something better or were retiring. We worked this as random treasure rolls for magic items that could sometimes be purchased on the cheap.
      In almost every campaign we've ever played, the party ends up hiring or acquiring through the Leadership feat a PR-person/manager. This person's job is to hire bards to sing about us, to set up deals with inns so that we can eat/drink/stay there for free, and to get our likenesses put on mugs, tabards, and similar. Occasionally we'll cut a deal where we promote a blacksmith's services or a leatherworker's goods as the ones we all use. At high levels, these kinds of 10-gold freebies don't even matter, but it makes everything more "believable." Why WOULDN'T you go out and set up those kinds of deals?
      Lastly, our party manager is usually tasked with opening up a store to sell party memorabilia and to act as a sales center for all the junk we pick up in dungeons including statues, furniture, tapestries, anything nice, really.
      And that's how we rock the economic model. The party ends up being super-stars who get a lot of things for free and who like being famous.

      One of the things we've also done that I haven't seen very often is we've played lower-level dungeons. Like we did the Sunless Citadel when we were 12th level. Not knowing what was in the dungeon, we started out cautiously and ran it like any other dungeon. But by the end we were picking up hobgoblins and throwing them down mineshafts and impaling multiple kobolds with a single arrow. In short, we were murderous super-heroes fighting evil 3 year-olds.
      By the time we reached 18th level, most of the members of the party were never going to die of old age. Many of us only ate or drank for pleasure and only slept for 2 hours each night (Rings of Sustenance). We only feared death for the momentary pain it brought and the hit to the wallet, knowing that we would be back the next day at full strength. We completely wrecked the economy of a small town for fun by giving the local orphan 2000 gold. And some of us started to worry that we weren't human anymore. Because in a lot of ways high-level adventurers aren't human - they're demigods.
      And knowing that if you can get past those first two or three dungeon crawls and become a demi-god or a rockstar or wealthy beyond the dreams of avarice seems like it would be a huge incentive. Especially because once you have your stake saved up you don't have to fear violent death unless all of your friends die with you.

      \n The further along this discussion goes, the more I'm beginning to realize that yes, dungeoneering makes no sense.

      On the other hand, things that make no sense often make extremely fun games. I mean, starfighters make no sense. Giant mecha make absolutely no sense. Punching a Nazi off the top of a burning zeppelin makes no sense. Massive supernatural conspiracies make no sense.

      My interest in things that make sense is dramatically lower when I'm sitting down to play a game, apparently. All those fun games in all those settings that were totally ridiculous, and not once did I ever stop and say, "Why would anyone ever do anything like this? What kind of world would I need for this to be a reasonable activity?" But then, I've always liked games that felt more like fiction than nonfiction, so maybe my tolerance for goofiness is higher? (Or my antipathy towards grittiness, for that matter?)


      This is still a very interesting thread full of great material, but I can't help wondering if anyone else is starting to think "...so what exactly is better about a setting where there's an economic basis for adventurers looting dungeons, anyway?" Or is it just so people who want more grit and less goof in their settings can still take their PCs in to plunder Undermountain without being annoyed at how nonsensical it all is?
