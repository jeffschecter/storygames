\n This summer, I got a chance to dabble in the arcane arts of jeep form role playing.

The opportunity came when I went to Ropecon in Finland. While there, I was asked a lot of questions by Finns, Swedes and many others who were intrigued but suspect of the Forgian emphasis on heavy mechanical structure in our game design. The jeeps among them. But when I got a chance to play a jeep game, it was alien, yet strangely familiar. Like they had a lot of the same goals, but were doing things in the most different ways possible from forge--and its relations/diaspora--style of play. Along with some dangerous agendas.

I thought it would be a nice gesture to continue the cultural exchange on a more public forum so we could continue learning from and about one another. I've invited Tobias Wrigstad to come debate--or rather discuss, the jeep way, and give him an opportunity to ask any questions he might have about our strange and alien ways as well.

    Wow. I didn't know we were going to get personal.

    \n

    As I remember it, I got in touch with role-playing in 1983, as I
    \nwas about to turn 7. Then I played because all the cool guys (who
    \nwere all older, incidentally) were doing it. It could have been
    \n1984, but I'm pretty sure it was in '83. We played the Swedish D&D
    \nequivalent. BTW, 2007 marks my 10th year as a freeform-only
    \nscenario wright. I feel I should have developed much more than I
    \nhave. It is a bit shameful, actually.

    \n

    But never mind that, let's fast-forward a couple of years. In the
    \nearly or mid 90's, I was starting to be active in the Swedish con
    \nscene.

    \n

    Around this time, larping started to be popular in Sweden.
    \nLarping meant dressing up and simulating what happened when elves
    \nand orcs incidentally picked the same spot to celebrate summer
    \nsolstice or something like that. Most importantly, larping was
    \nagile -- everything was done without a character sheet or a GM.

    \n

    It was now that freeform started to get really hot and interesting
    \nat Swedish cons. People were also larping and wanted to keep that
    \nagility and also act out. People also started to come clean about
    \nnot really using the rules anyway, because they were to cumbersome
    \nor just messed up the story because a critical person would die
    \nfrom a crappy dice roll.

    \n

    I immediately took to freeform. It felt like the stuff I had been
    \nwanting to do -- create good drama -- and was frustrated by the
    \nlack of in the games we played at the time. I also liked to use
    \nthe entire body for acting, not just sitting around a table
    \ntalking. It made it easier to feel, and it was apparent to me that
    \nit was more powerful medium of expression than just the voice.

    \n

    Jeepform arrived on the scene around 2000. We didn't call it
    \nJeepform until much later. Jeepform is a silly name, but it is way
    \nless overloaded than freeform. And you can google it. Which is
    \ngreat. Anyway.

    \n

    Jeepform for me is a point of realisation. What I/we had wanted to
    \ndo with our freeform games. For me personally, the recognition of
    \nthe importance of the meta-play was a big thing. I had realised
    \npretty early on that I was interested in stories and people. I
    \noverdosed on fantasy as a kid, and wanted to avoid that, and also
    \ndeal more with everyday drama than epic drama. But I was also
    \ninterested in form (= techniques for playing, "heavy mechanical
    \nstructure", etc.). And to me, as I have written elsewhere on Story Games
    \nrecently, freeform has been about creating the best possible form
    \nfor the story you want to play.

    \n

    I feel that freeform in Sweden stopped experimenting early on, and
    \nJeepform almost requires experimenting and moving on by
    \ndefinition. At least if you want to tell different stories with
    \ndifferent messages or premises. I want to develop form to fit
    \nstory. Thus, I'm a Jeep.

    When we started using the term jeep form, we used it to mean "a
    \nsubset of freeform games", but maybe it should be "an extended
    \nsubset of freeform games". Let's stick with the former for now.

    \n

    We wanted a term that was more narrow (and thus more useful) than
    \nfreeform to be able to say "this is (not) jeep form" about stuff.
    \nOut of politeness, we didn't want to hijack an existing term. I
    \nstill think this was a wise choice.

    \n

    So let me explain what I mean by freeform by triangulation.

    \n

    Historically, I think that in freeform, the story (or the message
    \nor the premise) is the arbiter. In table-top (and forgean games?),
    \nthe arbiter is a (general) set of rules. In larp, mostly for
    \nlogistical reasons, the story cannot be arbiter as everyone is the
    \nmain character of his/her own story-arc, and any over-arching
    \nstory is subordinate and incidental.

    \n

    For me, freeform has been about finding the best possible form for
    \nthe telling of the story at hand, which means that form is "free"
    \n-- it changes from scenario to scenario. Elsewhere on Story
    \nGames
    \nI wrote "[The free in freeform does not mean] free as in free of
    \nthe shackles of simulation games (like D&D) [but] free as in Indie
    \nor free as in do what's best for you."

    \n

    Most freeform scenarios that I see today don't tailor form to
    \nstory. Freeform effectively isn't free anymore, but fixed. There
    \nis a silent agreement on what it is (or the different forms a
    \nfreeform game should have) and people stick to it. I'm sure this
    \nis a Scandinavian thing more than a global thing.

    \n

    In a sense, jeepform is freeform that refuses to become fixed in
    \nthis sense. It doesn't have to be more complicated than that,
    \nactually. Whenever you want to play, you start with a blank sheet
    \nof paper (rather than with the silent agreement of what freeform
    \nis) and then you only bring on-board form/techniques that fits
    \nthat game. This is why we keep repeating stuff like:

    \n
      \n
    • Character ownership is optional
      • \n
    • Role monogamy is optional
      • \n
    • ...
      • \n
    \n

    Because we have to bring everything in by conscious choice. Not by
    \ndefault or old habit or because we're safe with those techniques
    \nor because we think the players like it.

    \n

    IMHO, tailoring form to story often requires experimentation. If
    \nmy next game is going to be nothing like my last, then I probably
    \nwill make use of completely different techniques to make it
    \nhappen. And since we haven't all been doing this forever, those
    \ntechniques may well have to be invented. Or at least modified.

    \n

    So what do I mean by techniques? I mean pretty much anything. It
    \ncan be "a character must always interpret things in the worst
    \npossible way for herself"     or "people bid on GM power for
    \nindividual scenes using system X"     or "no physical interaction".
    \nThe key is understanding why and what you'd get out of it. The
    \nfirst "rule" could be used in a game about distrust and loyalty,
    \nthe second could be used simply to distribute GM power in order to
    \nmake the game richer (more influences from different persons) and
    \nthe third can be useful in a game about isolation. You are likely
    \nto end up using several such rules to get to your point.

    \n

    For me, Jeepform also stresses a few other things like "no heroes
    \nand villains"    , "play subtle" and "always have a premise or
    \nsubject"    , things which are controversial for some. To me, the
    \nquest is as dead as the party. Last, a big thing for me, is the
    \nrecognition of the meta-play as being equally important as what
    \nhappens in the shared fantasy.

    \n

    If you go to http://jeepen.org/dict/, which is where we try to
    \ndescribe what it is we do, there is a list of "truths", which we
    \nbelieve are mostly true for all role-playing. Just below this
    \nlist, there is a list called "The Jeepform Piece of the Cake",
    \nwhich is an attempt at capturing some core values of jeepform.
    \nSince I know how hard it can be to click a link, I'm repeating it
    \nhere:

    \n
      \n
    1. How you tell the story is as important as the story itself.
      2. \n
    2. Form should be tailored to the telling of the story at hand.
      3. \n
    3. Form should support good flow with few interruptions.
      4. \n
    4. Rules for deciding outcomes of actions, etc. are allowed only in
      \ncorrespondence with item no. 3, but should generally not be
      \nneeded.
      5. \n
    5. Focus is on the story, which takes precedence over both total
      \nimmersion and play-for-show.
      6. \n
    6. Transparency is important to facilitate collaborative play
      \n-- there should basically not be any secrets. For this
      \nreason, and others, split party is banned.
      7. \n
    7. The meta play is as important to the game as the actual play [=shared fantasy].
      8. \n
    8. Meta discussions and meta information are banned to the largest
      \npossible extent unless they are a deliberate part of the meta
      \nplay to affect the actual play.
      9. \n
    9. The existence of characters is almost mandatory; characters
      \nand story should be made to support each other.
      10. \n
    10. Character ownership is optional; a character can be played by
      \nmore than one player.
      11. \n
    11. Role monogamy is optional; a player can have more than one
      \ncharacter.
      12. \n
    12. The inner thoughts and feelings of the characters should
      \npreferably be expressed in the play using appropriate
      \ntechniques.
      13. \n
    13. More than one game master is generally a good idea, especially
      \nin the presence of complex form. Game masters should preferably
      \nnot be players also.
      14. \n
    14. A high degree of player freedom is desirable.
      15. \n
    15. Actual props and well-prepped gaming locations are generally bad
      \nthings as they become obstacles to the immersion (what am I allowed
      \nto do with this prop?) and constrain the game to move outside of
      \nthe prepped location (which is sometimes desirable).
      16. \n
    16. That a game is not a rail-road is not an excuse for a lousy
      \nstory.
      17. \n
    17. The most important aspect of a story is how it affects the characters
      \nin it, not whether the characters manage to save the world in the end.
      18. \n
    18. Everyday drama is more interesting than epic drama.
      19. \n
    19. There are very few situations where the Game master should be
      \nplaying the extras. Extras are good. Non-player characters
      \nare not.
      20. \n
    20. "Breaking" any of the "rules" above is only allowed in order to
      \nmake a point.
      21. \n
    \n

    If you want me to expand on any of these points, I'd be happy
    \nto. But I feel this post is becoming too long already, so I wont
    \ndo it unless asked.

    \n

    I'm not sure I've ever said jeepform is supposed to be "cutting
    \nedge". What I do like about it is really the emphasis on pushing
    \nform and form development and that form considers meta-play and
    \nshared fantasy equally. A rule that says "you cannot ever comfort
    \nanother character" is naturally targeting the player and her
    \nnatural urge to comfort someone, not the characters.

    \n

    Maybe this all means that jeepform games are more likely to be
    \ncutting edge than other games. I dunno.

    \n

    There is one more point I'd like to make, which is the first item
    \nof the "truths" list mentioned above:

    \n
      \n
    1. Restrictions foster creativity.
      2. \n
    \n

    I was surprised to find that people view this as un-freeform. To
    \nme, this is the most obvious truth. And finding a balance where
    \nyou have the right restrictions to keep the game focused on the
    \npremise but free enough to still be interesting is a key problem
    \nin game design.

    One more thing. I've been surprised to see jeepform labelled as
    \nlarp. It is labelled thus because we generally stand up and act
    \neverything out. To me, jeepform has always been more table-top
    \nthan larp, but perhaps I'm wrong.

    \n

    In any case, we don't wear costumes and we don't use prepped
    \nlocations. What we do do, is move about, and map the world in the
    \nshared fantasy onto the physical gaming location. Because then it
    \nbecomes obvious that I'm stepping out of the car, or exiting the
    \ncave, or is trying to open the door, without me ever having to
    \nbreak character and say it. More stuff can go on in parallel
    \nwithout the need for out-of-character communication. This is a
    \ngood thing. This is part of what is meant by form should support
    \ngood flow with few interruptions    . Breaking away from the
    \nrole-playing to resolve a challenge event using dice is almost
    \nalways a bad thing IMHO, because it directs your attention to the
    \nwrong things. Plus there is so much more you can say with your
    \nbody than when you're just using your voice.

    \n

    I'm trying to hold back on posting during the working day, but
    \nhere goes.

    \n

    Maybe we are using the split party term wrong, but what we mean
    \nis that the players should never be forced to exit the room just
    \nbecause their characters are not present. I always hated this,
    \nboth as a GM or a player. It happened a lot in the bad old days.
    \nI'm a big fan of not all characters being on screen at every point
    \nin time, but characters and players are two different things.

    \n

    I want all the players to be in the same room always (unless there
    \nis a really good point to it, like to create real distrust among
    \nthe players). IMHO, freeform role-playing always benefits from all
    \nparticipants, players and GM alike, agreeing on what the game is
    \nabout and (to some extent) where it is going. If people are forced
    \nto leave the room, that wont happen.

    \n

    Transparency in our terminology means no secrets. If I know your
    \ncharacter is the traitor, then I can help you get into situations
    \nwhere that can create good drama. There may still be secrets for
    \nthe characters, but not for the players. And people do know how
    \nto separate what they know and what their characters know.

    \n

    So, yes. It is an issue of physical space -- a logistical
    \nissue. This is why I prefer freeform to larping. In larp you
    \n"cannot" time-travel over here because someone might burst in and
    \nnot have a clue on what is happening, etc. Plus when a lot of cool
    \nthings happen all over, the over-arching story is lost.

    \n

    I hope I managed to answer you question. If not, fire again.

    Per wrote:

    \n

    Add to that point 7 and 8. They seem to me to be completely
    \n contradictory - or do you see "meta play" and "meta information"
    \n as two very different things? If so, what's wrong with the latter?

    \n
    \n

    Meta play and meta discussion are two different things. Meta play
    \nis whatever goes on between the players in the room. If I'm
    \nkissing you (remember, we act things out) in a game where my
    \ngirlfriend is also playing, then stuff happens in the room that is
    \ndefinitely not part of the shared fantasy, but is still important
    \nand can be leveraged on in the game.

    \n

    Meta information or meta discussion is really whatever information
    \nthat needs be disseminated outside of the characters. Like "is the
    \ndoor locked?", or "I position myself so that I can see through the
    \nwindow", etc. I think that sucks because it can almost always be
    \nsolved by role-playing, like the player trying to open the door or
    \npositioning herself somewhere while muttering "I wonder if I can
    \nsee through the window from over here". Cheezy examples, perhaps,
    \nbut illustrative.

    \n

    I think every break from character should be motivated. In some
    \ngames, the meta discussion is an integral part of the game, and
    \nshould be there. Like in Doubt, the preplanning and between-scenes
    \ndiscussion is equally important as the actual role-playing,
    \nbecause the players are talking about relationsships, prejudice,
    \netc. Which is a goal of the game. Which is what is meant by the
    \nlatter part of #8 and all of #7.

    \n

    For me "What does the room look like?" and "You enter into a blah
    \nblah..." is unnecessary. In the relatively few cases where this
    \ninformation is needed, the GM can always give it through voice
    \novers that could be e.g., in the mind of a character, and that
    \ndon't interrupt the play. The GM is broadcasting stuff and you can
    \ntune in if you like, or not. We're getting into another
    \ndiscussion, so I'll stop it here.

    \n

    Basically, if the information you want to disseminate does not
    \nsupport the premise (e.g., it just facilitates play like my
    \nexamples above), it can be solved without talking outside or
    \nbreaking character.

    \n
    \n

    What do you mean with "meta play" exactly?

    \n
    \n

    I took a stab at that when I answered Per's questions: "Meta play
    \nis whatever goes on between the players in the room." It is
    \nanticipation, expecations, reactions, etc., etc. in the
    \nplayers as opposed to the character.

    \n

    Put differently, a role-playing game has (at least) two levels,
    \none where the characters interact -- the actual play, and one
    \nwhere the players interact -- the meta play.

    \n
    \n

    You say that jeepform isn't larping. How do you define the
    \n differences and why you Jeepform as more similar to tabletop
    \n rpg?

    \n
    \n

    I'm going to take the easy way out here and quote myself. This is
    \nme explaining freeform about 18 months ago.

    \n
    \n

    If I was to describe freeform role-play to a larper, I would
    \n describe it as a larp for a few persons, 4-8 maybe, that
    \n physically takes place in the same room with few or no props, no
    \n rules and most likely with a gamemaster.

    \n
    \n

    This means, as I've said previously in this thread, that we can do
    \nmore cool stuff that require all players to be in sync, like
    \ntime-travel (which incidentally is not so cool), use symbolic
    \nprops, and use the room to denote whatever game location we want
    \nand change it every so often. Just like in a table-top.

    \n
    \n

    If I were to explain freeform to a table-top player, I would say
    \n that it is a table-top game of the usual size, 4-8 players maybe,
    \n that physically takes place in the same room (regardless of where
    \n the characters are in the game world) with few or no props, no
    \n traditional rules, and most likely with a gamemaster. Also, there

    \n

    I'm not a theorist, but here goes:

    \n

    The reward in a freeform game, as I see it, is peer recognition.
    \nYou add stuff to the story and the other players take that in and
    \nappreciate it. When you do something cool in the game, you feel it
    \nin the room. That's the short-term reward. Plus the cool scenes
    \nwhen they happen. Doing something cool could be taking the story
    \nsomeplace unexpected, or making a great character interpretation,
    \nor having great timing, or ... You get the picture.

    \n

    The long-term reward is creating the best possible story or
    \nwhatever the game is about. If it is about contemplating
    \nrelationships etc. (more about that below) then doing that and
    \ncoming out on the other side as a person with new perspectives or
    \nexperiences, or just having gone through a personal catharsis is
    \nthe long-term reward.

    \n

    Development of character could be there too, but story is still
    \nking, or the intended personal/group experiences.

    \n
    \n

    So, let's talk about some of the deeper, darker bits of
    \n jeep. You've mentioned several times how meta-information should
    \n not be set aside, but can be seen as some of the (most
    \n important?) material of the game. Could you talk about playing
    \n close to home? How do you deal with things when it cuts too
    \n close to the bone and goes awry? And then there are the ethical
    \n issues it raises.

    \n
    \n

    Yes!

    \n

    (I'll return to meta-information tomorrow or later depending on
    \nhow well I sleep. I didn't find a good way to integrate it in this
    \npost.)

    \n

    Playing close to home is about using stuff from real life and
    \nplaying about things that are real and that you can relate
    \nto. Doubt, which I have mentioned already, is a game about when
    \nyou should break up or fight to save a relationship, about
    \ntemptation and about what counts as infidelity. In Doubt, players
    \nare encouraged to play close to home. They have minimal
    \ncharacters, and the game is quite hetero-normative and
    \nstereotypical. Thus, there is ample opportunity for the players to
    \nuse stuff from their real lives -- old relationships and old
    \nboyfriends/girlfriends. They have a chance to use them here as
    \ninspiration, or to say stuff to them that they didn't get to
    \noriginally, or realise why stuff happened the way it happened, or
    \ntry alternative routes. Finally, I get to tell you the story about
    \nmy cheating on my girl friend in my early twenties, but in this
    \nanonymous setting, where you don't know what's real and what
    \nisn't.

    \n

    I also get to look into the eyes of my (real-life) girl friend
    \n(who is also playing) and hold a monologue about how a marriage
    \nproposal is an evil ultimatum and how marriage is a futile attempt
    \nat ownership of another person. It does not matter what I believe
    \nor not in these matters. It is thought-provoking, developing and
    \ninteresting. In a sense it can also be a game of "I dare you"
    \nbetween you and yourself.

    \n

    Basically, playing close to home is about consciously using or
    \nseeking emotions that are rooted in you, that you can really
    \nrelate to or that you have perhaps not processed fully
    \nyet. Depending on who you are, it can be threading dangerous
    \nground, or not. For some it is just a cool thrill, for others, it
    \ncan be very challenging or even life-altering. There is really no
    \nway to tell, and it will vary with the electricity of each
    \nevening.

    \n

    So, how to deal with things when they explode? I don't know. My
    \nadvice and general strategy for this is to just be a human being.
    \nEveryone knows it is a game, and all players know we are taking it
    \nas close to home as we want. Generally, things don't explode,
    \nbecause it is very hard to tell what is close to home or not,
    \nunless you know each other very well. And if you do, then your
    \nfriends are there if you would burst. Which is great.

    \n

    I've had players break and not being able to continue playing. Or
    \nplayers bursting into tears seemingly for no reason. I have never
    \nhad a player regretting that he or she was in the game from the
    \nstart (with exceptions that don't belong in this discussion).
    \nGenerally, when players break, it is due to some problem that the
    \nhe or she had that was just brought to the surface by the
    \ngame. And then you're probably in a good crowd to be comforted.

    \n

    Sometimes, you'll feel that you're going too deep for some people,
    \nand then you can just pull out.

    \n

    Granted, I've done stupid things in role-playing games that I
    \nregret, but that's part of the deal. If we want interesting games,
    \nthen that comes with the territory. I was talking to a person
    \ntoday about running a game with him and he immediately asked "What
    \nis your hidden agenda?" That was fun. (I'm definitely not like
    \nthat always, but in this case, he correctly had a feeling I wanted
    \nto use the game to work on something in his life. In the end, he
    \ndidn't want to play, which is sad but OK. Just talking about some
    \nthings in the game was too close to home, and I ended up sending
    \nhim monologue texts from the game that quite well fitted his
    \ncurrent situation. He thought that was scary.)

    \n

    So, what's the ethics of this you ask. This is murky waters for
    \nsure. But I think that as long as you are not evil, not abusing
    \nyour powers or have a too shaky hidden agenda, almost everything
    \nis playable. Me and another guy did a game in 1996 where four of
    \nthe players are turned against the fifth. All the other players
    \nwould leave the room to plot just to create a "bad feeling about
    \nthis" in the fifth player. That was criticised back then because
    \npeople expected role-playing to be about people collaborating, not
    \nback-stabbing each other. Basically. Today, I don't think people
    \nwould think this was weird.

    \n

    Whenever I go all-in, I commit to picking up the pieces if
    \nnecessary. That's not so great, but it is the best I can do. And
    \nit seems to suffice.

    \ncharacters in the same room but 12 years apart. Doubt is a 4
    \nplayer game (two couples). Travellers is a 3 player game. (I was a
    \nbig fan of 3 players for a while.) Just to name a few. 4-6 I think
    \nis most common.

    \n

    Two player games work but are generally more stressful for the
    \nplayers -- more screen-time per person, so to speak. For dynamics,
    \nthree is a lot better.

    \n

    I've played Slivovica, a completely unserious train-ride through
    \nJugoslavia, with as many as 18 players, which worked quite OK, but
    \nin my experience, things tend to gravitate towards larp when
    \nyou've got more than 10 players, unless you have a very rigid
    \nstructure that makes it possible for all players to keep track of
    \nwhat's going on without becoming boring. All in all, 3-8 is my
    \npreference.

    \n

    (We did have about 150 people when we played The Upgrade! at
    \nRopeCon in 2006, but that was cheating.)

    \n

    On the question of multiple sessions, the answer is yes, but not
    \nso much. I've done pretty long-lasting (about 15 sessions is long
    \nin my book) freeform campaigns. But I'm not a campaign guy. I
    \ndon't have enough time and am too scatter-brained to not want to
    \nchange everything completely every 2-3 sessions.

    \n
    \n

    most of the times I read about Nordic larp, they're mostly
    \n one-shots or games that take place over a single
    \n weekend. They're not games that you continue every week

    \n
    \n

    I'm not really a larper, so I couldn't really tell you about
    \nthat. I think you are correct, but then again, there is a lot
    \ngoing on in the forest that I would not know about. I think there
    \nare fantasy campaigns as well as vampire campaign stuff. These
    \npeople don't read the Knutepunkt books. Sadly, there isn't a lot
    \nof interaction between fantasy larpers and "art-house larpers".
    \nAFAIK, art-house larps are generally one-shots, and I think those
    \nare the ones most likely to be described in the Knutepunkt
    \nbooks.

    \n

    Changing the rules over the course of play is something that I've
    \ndone several times, for example in the form of talking about what
    \ntechniques we should use tonight to bring out what we want in this
    \nsession. And this will vary over time.

    \n

    Generally, the people I play with are experienced freeform
    \nplayers, which means that we don't really have to talk so much
    \nabout this stuff. Things evolve quite organically, and at some
    \npoint something might happen and someone will say "let's make that
    \na rule!" or it effectively becomes so. That's happened on a few
    \noccasions. But I personally really don't do much extended play, so
    \nI cannot give you a really good answer on this one.

      \n The question I now have -- since my other Jeepform inquiry has been answered thoroughly in another thread -- is how suitable this playing style is for more traditional sorts of roleplaying scenarios. For example, there's a lot of talk about Doubt and its essentially realistic tone, but less about something like White Out: Las Vegas and none at all about zombies attacking a convoy of post-apocalyptic warriors. The last being the sort of thing that many RPers like to see in their games.

      This is not an attack on Jeepform's general bias toward reality, but I have to wonder if there's genuine potential for it to spread into other kinds of stories and genres.

      I think this is mostly a consequence of my own preferences, and not so much a limit or particular strength of Jeepform.
      **I** prefer social (sur)realism over vampires, Elvii, and zombies, but I would like to think that you could do most things jeep style. For example, we played a somewhat jeep-esque by Andrea Castellani today (I'm at ModCon in Italy) which was set in the stone age and me playing a warrior.

      If I remember correctly, Ken Hite commented on our "Nuts and bolts of Jeepform" talk at RopeCon last year that most of our stuff can be used in table-top role-playing (in the American tradition), and that the techiniques are very modular -- you can just add one, or two if you like, you don't have to buy the whole package, so to speak. This is an (albeit very anecdotal and name-dropping) indication that jeepform applies to whatever you want to apply it to.

      I'll try to point Olle (the White-out author) in the direction of this thread.
