\n I wonder if people could give me some advice?

I'm looking for a game which I'm beginning to believe doesn't exist. Ideally, I want a game that meets the following requirements:\n
    \n
  • Capable of doing Science fiction with spaceships.
    • \n
  • Able to deliver an Iain Banks or Firefly-level mood rather than Flash Gordon or anime.
  • Offering a range of possibilities like Traveler rather than a predetermined scenario like Lady Blackbird
    • \n
  • Able to deal with more than just fighting.
    • \n
  • Players don't die easily.
    • \n
  • Narrative
    • \n
  • Capable of serious play
    • \n
  • Two-player.
    • \n
  • Emergent play/GM'less.
    • \n
  • Rules light.
    • \n
\nThe reason for the last three are:

a) I'd like to play it with my wife.
b) Neither of us want to spend a lot of time in prep. Also we'd both like to play at the same time.
c) We're getting too old to learn yet another huge set of rules.

There are a number of games that seem perfect, except they need someone to do a lot of prep work:\n
    \n
  • Fate
    • \n
  • Sorceror
    • \n
  • Dr Who
    • \n
  • Mortal Coil
    • \n
\n
And a number that seem close but not quite right:\n
    \n
  • Traveler: characters die too easily. The rules appear sim-based. Tech appears old-fashioned.
    • \n
  • 3:16: Seems to be mostly about fighting.
    • \n
  • Cosmic Patrol: Pulpy
    • \n
  • Shock: Needs more players. Also, I can't make head nor tail of the rules.
    • \n
  • Universalis: Seems a bit abstract, somehow. I've never played it, though.
    • \n
\nI've found two that are absolutely perfect - just what I want - except they only handle one type of play:\n
    \n
  • The quiet year.
    • \n
  • Mars Colony.
    • \n
\n
None of this is set in stone, though. If I've got the wrong idea about something, or one of those games would actually be perfect for me, I'd love to hear about it.

At the moment, the nearest game I can find is Archipelago, which has worked well for us for fantasy so far. If nothing else is suggested I'm going to go with this, but I just thought I'd ask. Are there any really good rules-light GM'less science fiction games out there I'm missing?

    \n Hi
    I'm suggesting to you Dreamwake because...


    Capable of doing Science fiction with spaceships.
    Check.
    There are no spaceships in the original Dreamwake setting, but they can be handles as any other vehicle... which means as any other item... which means you just need to note down your idea and then you can add as many details as you want, or stay simple, or anything in between.
    And it will be relevant to the game as a Tool in relation to what your PCs are doing.


    Able to deliver an Iain Banks or Firefly-level mood rather than Flash Gordon or anime.
    Check.
    That is up to you as the game offers very clear and powerful tools to define what the tone/mood/style of your game should be, and then to KEEP it that way.
    Also, the game assumes al PCs function as a united team with a common goal.


    Offering a range of possibilities like Traveler rather than a predetermined scenario like Lady Blackbird
    Check.
    There are clear and very simple rules to play in any setting you want with just a few minutes chat.
    Then, the game revolves around the completion of missions that are both Player generated and totally unpredictable.


    Able to deal with more than just fighting.
    Check.
    I've often ended up playing investigations, explorations, undercover infiltrations, rescue missions, social confrontations ... and fights, that too


    Players don't die easily.
    Check.
    Although PCs can get very badly injured, true "death" can only happen by choice.
    Otherwise you are beaten up, you are exausted, you are at your limit... but you are still in play and can participate to the game.


    Narrative.
    Check.
    The rules managing Player communication help everyone contribute and participate, shaping the game fiction in a rich and detailed way (100% tailored to the Player's taste).
    But they also shake up things, produce unexpected twists and turns, and make the story be emergent.


    Capable of serious play.
    Check.
    Same as the "mood" point


    Two-player.
    Check.
    There is no functional difference between a 2-Player or a 3-4-5-Player game.


    Emergent play/GM'less.
    Check.
    I use to say that this is a "true GM-Less" game, rather than a GM-Full one.
    That is because you almost never play in an Autor Stance or are otherwise allowed to act as Director/Narrator/GM.
    You play your own Character, and that is it.
    The game will ask for other input (bite sized chunks of it, very specific, very focussed) from time to time, a bit more in 2-Players games, a bit less in 3-4-5-Players games.
    But that is all.
    At any rate, after the first time SetUp phase (meaning setting and character generation) it is 100% prep-less.


    Rules light.
    That depends on your definition of "light" in regards of rpg "rules"
    I consider it quite light and simple... but with a joyful amount of crunch XD
    There is almost no mathematic or numbers.
    You only use pools of d6.
    It is strongly fiction-first.
    But then the core "conflict" mechanics are dice-y enough.

    I've seen that the game appears complex and with lots of moving parts at first sight, but usually everyone at the table becomes almost an "expert" by the end of the first session.
    Without a facilitator present that may take, I don't know, two sessions and an actual play in between them?

    If you're interested I can provide a few Actual Plays, both old and new

    I'm not personally convinced yet by Traveler (World) / Diaspora. There's something really peculiar about science fiction (and I say this as someone who writes a lot of it). Unlike fantasy, it's really easy for technology to become out of date. Last time I played Traveler, it assumed a future the eighties might have thought we would have. There was no social media, for example, and computers were bulky items. I think it's best if a science fiction game that intends to be played across the decades doesn't hard-code tech levels into its rules.

    \n Thanks everyone for the suggestions.

    Noofy and Akooser: I've actually just bought Star Trader to take a look at it, and it's generating ideas.

    The difficulty I find is that some of the design goals for Traveler seem strange for emergent science fiction:

    1. You can't make any character you want. The last time I played, my first two characters died whilst being generated. The third came out with the skill "Cutlass," which just didn't feel very science-fiction-y.

    I've got the Psion supplement, and it even has random rolls for losing your powers before you play the character.

    2. Even if you could play any character, they seem awfully fragile. I've never gone a game of Traveler without the dice being fudged or someone dying rapidly. Maybe that's because I'm trying to play Firefly in a universe more like Apocalypse Now?

    3. It has strange assumptions about technology. Computers are rare and heavy. Communications is limited. Social media non-existent. Unfortunately, while they were great assumptions for a future extrapolated from 1980, I'm not sure today's science fiction can do without technology at least at the level of our own.

    Somehow, I keep coming back to Traveler. I feel I should love the system more than I do. It's hard-science-fiction, with variable technology levels and dozens of sourcebooks (many of which I have). I really, truly want to love it, but it seems to fall down each time I play it. I think I'm ultimately looking for a game with a different philosphy.

    What I'd love is for there to be a two-player emergent game that produces science-fiction stories. Something like Shock:, but for two-players.


    As for the other suggestions:

    Jason: I'm certainly going to play Archipelago again. It's one of the best story producing games I know

    J_Walton: I have Microscope and I'll go take a look at it, but my understanding is that it's really about events, not characters. So it might be useful for playing out a science-fictional history, but not really for following a couple of protagonists. But maybe it can drill down to that level?

    sbrplaysrpgs: I bought Intrepid (which looks like a hack of Archipelago), but unfortunately, it's for 3+ players. I'll buy World vs Hero and take a look at it, though - thank you

    Horn_head_o: I read Lasers and Feelings and it seems great. I'm going to give this a try too
