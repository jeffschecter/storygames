\n When GMing a recent game, I began with an ironic monologue. That is, I pretended to read some prepared text from my "scenario": "As you arrive at the church, the early sunlight breaks through the fog. It lights the spire in eerie colours..."

Much to my surprise, the players liked it and asked me to continue.

It was an effective moment. It gave a moment of ritual, a definite signal that the game was starting. It gave focus and description (we often don't take time to describe worlds in detail). And it gave that sense of theatre, which always interests me.

Have similar things happened in your games?

    \n A GM I know used to read out little pieces of text before the game. It didn't really work for me, but perhaps for the rest of the group.

    I played in "The Grand Mechanism", a sort of performance larp, at Knudepunkt 2011. It was amazing fun. It was sort of a long improv exercise where we made up a disaster that affected the theatre, and then had different scenes where we played out conflicts in the theatre hierarchy. Each player selected & rehearsed a few Shakespeare monologues beforehand, and tried to insert them in the game. Of course we were all horrible ham actors, but it was still a very enjoyable experience. When someone started a monologue, the rest of us were like "oh, okay, time to listen", and they had the time & space to do their thing before the action moved on. (And I loved reciting Shakespeare).

    \n I usually include a "story opener" in my Matrix games. A brief bit sets the mental stage and basically tells the players what to do.

    For example: From Jump the Shark - my new game

    Have you ever been home on a Saturday night watching some horrible B movie on TV and thought to yourself "I could write a better movie than this!" Well here is your chance!

    Jump the shark is a game about a bunch of writers, locked in an office, forced to write the worst most outlandish scripts imaginable. They work for the B Movie network so good writing is unimportant. Plot development and continuity are irrelevant. The only thing the producers want is a finished product. The writers want out (because the poo bucket in the corner is getting a little too full) so they write, but to keep themselves sane they make it fun. They decide to make a game out of it. They write outrageous scenes and then make them more outrageous. To keep things interesting they introduce an element of gambling. The end result is a gambling story telling game.

    or from Thunder Hamsters and the Temple of Cheese I have...

    After hundreds of adventures the Thunder Hamsters have discovered the way to the fabled Temple of Cheese. The hero who can recover the most holey cheese will win fame and adoration. But only one hero will win. The race is on!

    The Thunder Hamsters work together to reach the temple. They sail across a great ocean and then trek over boundless wastes. They are the greatest of friends but also the bitterest of rivals.

    \u201cWe are here\u2026\u201d Says Master Kibbler. \u201cSince I am too old to race I will be your referee. The rules are simple. The hero who can bring out three cheeses wins! The \u201cBook of Holey Cheese\u201d tells us that the cheeses are spread out around the temple. It goes on to say you must make a choice: \u201cSlow and Certain\u201d or \u201cFast and Unpredictable\u201d. Beware! The temple is full of traps and guards but do not forget to help one another when in need. With that I wish you good luck. May the mightiest Hamster win!!!\u201d

    Which I usually read in a high nasally voice with much enthusiasm.

    I hadn't thought of it as a ritual but I guess it is.

    \n Interesting. So I have mixed feelings about these answers. (Don't get me wrong, they're interesting.)

    On the one hand, ritual! Interesting! Invocations!

    On the other, I wonder if we've taken an interesting technique and storygamed it out of existence. To some extent, certainly, the opening monologue is about one person who focusses the session. It's clearly about authority. As in Anders example, there's a sense of anointing: the players invest their authority in the GM, who performs the opening ritual for them.

    I wonder (as I often do) whether, because of indie GMphobia, we're afraid to consider the power of investing authority (and ritual) in one person.

    \n One of my long term games utilized items that each player brought with them to signal 'entering the game.'

    Each person had a little pocket ritual (a candle, a key, a book of matches, an amulet, etc.). When i started the game the play began one of two ways: If i wanted them to fall into the game a specific way/feel i would play a song and describe for them (with their eyes closed) what it was like to transition into that game world. If i wanted them to jump in on their own i'd ask who had an item to take them in.

    Some items were individualized and only worked for one person, others were group-takers. (my favorite was my friend Silver's candle, which required that we turn off all the lights so that it was solid darkness save for the candle, and then he would blow it out and narration would start in utter darkness).

    \n When I GM, I often like to model sessions as episodes in a TV series. As such, I carefully choose an "opening credits" and "closing credits" song.

    Sometimes I start with something before the opening credits and other times I start of right with the theme song and frame the first scene after that.

    I like to choose obscure but hip songs that strongly reflect the themes in the game. My current game is based on fairy tales. So my opening credits song is Fairy Tale by Melodies From Our Nameless Hearts and the closer is Happily Ever After by He Is We. I keep them on a playlist on Playlist.com. http://www.playlist.com/playlist/21106900491

    (I don't play the whole song. They both have a break about halfway in them, which is where a TV show would cut them anyway, so that's where I cut.)

    Also, in the last session, I started off by reading part of a fairy tale story. It was the story they were going to be playing out part of. And I framed it as the story being told to one of the characters by Baba Yaga when she was younger. Because part of the concept of the game world is that fairy tale stories are both history and divination - they are things that have happened and will happen again, though the details change each time.

    The players really liked it, and I'll be doing that again.

    \nSo, my friends and i were pretty into the immersion and sugery-goodness of really theatrical play. The 'Crux Items,' as they came to be known, grew organically. First were the 'sliderstones' which three of us (the only three playing at first) bought from a vending machine on a roadtrip, somewhere in the frightening parts of northern Arkansas. It was just a Bridge to Tarabithia thing. But it worked. Then we started riffing on the idea, and that became Silver's Candle, skeleton keys, a magic bracelet, a chesspiece, you name it. Each was a little thing that said 'i'm going there.' Then, inevitably, we found ourselves with some time to game, a game to play, and everyone had forgotten their Crux Item. So i played a song to 'crux us in.' I started narrating "The other side of this song smells like garbage..." One of the players (it was actually his first game with us) loved it. And Cruxing In by Song became a Thing.

    All that's to say, i dunno. I wish i did, but we were making it up as we went along. Probably just ask your players what they think would be a cool 'key' to the gameworld...It's become a fun exploration for us. All i know is that, to me, the best part of the ritual happened when we had so many people being invited into the game that we made it a 'tradition' that your first GM for the game provided you with a 'temporary' Crux Item. After you'd been at least once you were allowed to make your own. Which i loved because i have a handful of little crafted things that i've given away to people as they joined, and i've been given other little things by friends that invited me back, into their own space in the gameworld.

    I always start the actual roleplaying part of IAWA with "In a wicked age, , ". The fact that I have to pause for a moment to think through what exactly I'm going to say probably helps add that extra bit of "now we're starting".

    Conversely, the ritual opening of Polaris or A Flower for Mara provides the same sense of "opening" without the stage fright. Maybe it's because there's not fiction being created in that moment. Dunno.

    Seth Ben-Ezra
