\n The concept of ritual space as it relates to gaming is fascinating to me. To kick things off, I will define/describe it as best as I'm able.
\n
\nRitual space is a clearly defined location and period of time in which a given activity takes place. This doesn't mean that it's always the same place or always the same time. Rather, it's a clearly defined instance of place and time. The purpose of this is to set the activity apart, give it an other-ness. What this should accomplish is to help remove distractors from the participants minds, and to provide a sort of 'safe place'. It's an aid to focus and openness. How this is accomplished varies greatly. A few things appear to be common, however. The first is a definite beginning point. Some action makes it clear to all present that the activity has begun, that the ritual space is open. The second thing is a clear ending point, some action which makes it clear that the activity is done, that the ritual space is closed. The third thing is a bit harder to define, but it is something which constantly reminds the participants that this place and time are separate, and it should last from the opening to the closing of the ritual space.
\n
\nMy personal experiences with ritual space and gaming predate my knowledge of the concept. I had begun a game of Vampire: the Masquerade with friends. Either shortly after beginning, or shortly before, I bought some crushed velvet and had it sewn into a table cloth. I bought a couple of tallish silver-tone candlesticks, and a handful of long black tapers, and a couple of marble platters to place under the candles to help catch the melting wax. Each session began with the setting of the sun, which I would describe as I lit the candles, usually in somewhat overblown prose. We would play until we ran out of time or hit a good stopping point, and the vamps would scatter to their respective dens to shelter from the dawn. I would describe the fading of night and the rising sun, also in melodramatic prose. Then I would blow out the candles.*
\n
\nThese were not the very best gaming experiences I've ever had, but they come very, very close. Sidebar conversations were fairly minimal, as were distractions from play. Everyone enjoyed themselves, and kept coming back week after week. They told their friends about the game, who would then ask if they could play. I had the hardest time saying no, so that at the height of the game, I had twelve PCs. One night, almost all of the players showed up. It was crazy and exhausting, but the preternatural focus of that game made it doable, and somehow even more awesome. The ritual trappings seemed corny when I thought of them, and they seem cheesy looking back, but I cannot deny the difference in that game from every other game I've ever played in or run. It may be simple coincidence, that focused players, time and ability to focus, and engaging fiction all dovetailed over the course of that campaign. But I suspect it was not. After that campaign broke up, I never seriously tried to do that again. I've had better gaming experiences since then with no memorable instances of ritual. But I can't shake the belief that the ritual aspects were a large part of what made that game so successful.
\n
\nMeguey Baker, I believe, was the first to talk about ritual space in gaming over on the Forge. She talked about how she used techniques from counseling sessions she'd done, and how it helped to create an environment of trust and openness. I think that such a space is essential for exploration of sensitive or uncomfortable topics in a mature manner. In a flip way, it's like "What happens in Vegas, stays in Vegas." You can remember what happened and how you felt, but the actual feelings and happenings happened in a separate place. It allows for more intimacy within the space while lessening the discomfort that frequently follows unexpected intimacy. I cannot speak for Ben Lehman, but I think Polaris is intended to take advantage of this same concept, where you can tell earnest stories of tragedy without feeling self-conscious about it.
\n
\nPoint of Discussion #1: If my understanding differs from yours, or if you think I'm just completely wrong, please chime in. My definition is taken from a mishmash of what I remember of Meguey's posts, and my own understanding explicit ritual space in other venues. I associate ritual space very closely with the concepts of IWNAY and Playing Passionately.
\n
\nPoint of Discussion #2: Talk about your experiences with ritual space. Describe the specific techniques you used in as much detail as you wish. Talk about what effect it had on your gaming. Feel free to talk about either explicit, intentional use of ritual space, or habits that formed over time that ended up creating ritual space organically.
\n
\nPoint of Discussion #3: What else can such techniques help with? What sort of play might they be harmful to, if any? What are the pros and cons of any specific techniques mentioned, or that you've seen?
\n
\n* frequently the blowing out of the candles would be followed by a light cry of pain from one of my friends, as I would almost invariably spatter him with a little hot wax when I did so. I didn't do it on purpose, but it happened often enough that the closing of the session is associated with the sound in my memories.

    \n General thoughts... ritual doesn't have to be intentional. As I said, my own experiences weren't done with anything in mind other than "setting the mood". I imagine that's the same sort of thing your DM had in mind too, Anders. Whether the liminal space (to steal the term from Matthijs) was intended or not, it happens. Of course, ritual doesn't have to be serious, either. When you're sitting down to enjoy time with friends and play a game, you can't afford to be *too* serious, really. Maybe your ritual might include everyone telling a joke before play starts, to "get it out of their system".
    \n
    \nCome to think of it, a TRoS game I ran had elements of ritual as well, though I didn't really think of it until today. The ritual involved one of the players summarizing the events of the previous session before play, and then me invoking the actual play with the phrase "When last we left our insipid heroes..." sometimes followed or even preceded by a brief discussion of whether or not the PCs had become "intrepid" yet. At the end of the session, I would have the players roll a die, then ask them a question from a list, based on their roll. The questions included things like: "What would you like to see happen next session?" or "What was your favorite thing about this session?" Even then, familiar with the idea of ritual space, I didn't intentionally use it as such.

    Posted By: MatthijsThe rest of the game also uses ritual techniques - ouija board, dream visions.
    \nPoint of order, Oujia Boards have only been ritualised in recent years. They were originally created as a kind of board game and had nothing at all to do with contacting the dead. However over the years a whole load of folklore has sprung up around the use of the Ouija board, which at one time was no more than a harmless party game. And that's one of my points too: I don't want to see RPGs go the same way.

    Personally I do not like the use of the term 'ritual' anywhere near my role-playing, except as a purely mythical element of the story. If the characters get involved in a ritual, that's fine. If the players have to perform a ritual, that's where things start getting iffy. There's one game, 'The Everlasting', that actually provides a spooky and unnervingly authentic magic ritual for the beginning of play, and that suggestion in particular gave me the creeps. But let me outline my misgivings more clearly.

    Firstly, there's the problem of misunderstanding from outside the hobby. We've all heard about BADD and its idiotic claims that D&D promotes witchcraft. If we start putting rituals into games, we might as well be admitting it's true.

    Secondly, there's the question of intent. Some genuine practitioners would say that by ritualising the game you are in fact turning it into some kind of spell; you're using it as a means to generate energy. At the end of the game, where does the energy go? By providing a 'dismissal' at the end you're effectively sending away the energy you've created. But has the entire thing been formulated properly? Are the usual precautions in place? In a proper magical ritual for instance, there are usually precautions that must be taken when entering or exiting the ritual area to prevent energy from escaping uncontrolled. For that matter, where's your grounding ritual? Vital in magical practices to bring the participants back down to earth and remind them it's the real world, but not always obvious because it's the precise opposite of the other forms of ritual - the magicians (or whatever) do ordinary things like chat and eat some food to remind themselves that the magic is over and they should come down now.

    I'm no huge believer in magic, but those who do might question how safe the rituals used in a game are, especially when the participants see the entire exercise as a game not a serious endeavour. And when you've got both the rabid religious fanatics AND the neo-pagans against you, who's going to defend you?

    Thirdly, I worry about the psychological effect upon the players. It has been pointed out that ritual can be used in psychological healing and councilling, but these are conducted by trained professionals who know how to control the events they create. What if someone became so fixated on the ritual that whenever they heard the wrong sequence of words they suddenly snapped into character? These are advanced psychological techniques, let's not use them randomly if we don't know what they might do. We already have enough crazies and psychos generating negative press for the hobby, I don't want to see a practice become prevalent that might create more.

    For this reason I would only play a game with a ritualistic element if that ritual was a) not called a ritual, b) contained no ritualised elements intended to bring on a state of altered consciousness, or c) that resembled genuine magical rituals in any way, shape or form. By all means set the atmosphere by lighting candles, playing appropriate music and saying 'OK guys, let's play', but if you start getting mysticism in my gaming fun, I'm outta there.

    -Ash

    \n Ritual doesn't have to be a formal, quasi-mystical thing.

    For example, when we played Primetime Adventures many moons ago, I located proper music to be the theme music for the show. (For the record, it was "Also sprach Zarathustra" done on the kazoo, which proves that you can find anything on the Internet.) Then, before we'd play the game, we'd put the music on and laugh maniacally. I'm gearing up for some PTA now, and we're doing the same thing.

    Lance, have you read anything on my game A Flower for Mara? I took some of the ritual lessons I learned from reading Meguey and from playing Polaris and put them into play in the design of that game. A couple highlights:\n
      \n
    1. There's a formal opening and closing of the game, called "Raising the Curtain" and "Lowering the Curtain", because it's an improv play. Each is performed in just about the same way: the players gather around the place that has been designated as Mara's grave. Then the Director (kinda the GM) reads a passage from the burial service from the Book of Common Prayer.

      Each player carries a flower, which is a symbol of the grief that his character carries. At the beginning of the game, each player writes down his Grief, which is something in the player's life that caused him sorrow, grief, or pain. Then he attaches the paper with his Grief to the flower. During the game, the player carries the flower. If his character decides that it's time to move past his grief, the player puts down the flower at the graveside and gives a brief out-of-character monologue about the Grief that he wrote down.

      2. \n
    \nThe ritual elements of A Flower for Mara are vitally important to allow the players the proper space to address some really heavy issues.

    (If you're interested, there are links to actual game play footage here and here. This was a playtest, by the way, though the only major change I can recall is that the rules no longer require music as part of the opening and closing.)

    Is your ritual pretty much the same thing done in the same way every time, or is it just a casual time/game time divide? Does your group consciously think of this as the routine? What do you think would happen if people showed up and the GM was like "Okay, sit down and let's get started," without the casual time?
    \nIt's casual, but it always happens, and it always happens in pretty much the same way. It's definitely a routine, and I'm sure a lot of us think of it that way. The thing I notice that seems to be the most critical is that it's the GM (or whoever's running the game) who waits until everyone's gotten their social chatter out of the way and is ready, and then announces to everyone that we're starting now.

    This is, I'm sure, partly practical...if the GM isn't ready, the game suffers, so don't rush the GM. Also, the GM is already paying attention to the players, and will probably notice when it's a good time to stop talking and start playing before anyone else does. There's probably also an element of respect there, in that everyone is agreeing to grant the GM the authority to make calls like "Time to start the game" and "The target number is 20" and what have you.

    When we haven't had that casual time before the game, it has been a BIG PROBLEM, because everyone does their chitchat during the game instead, where it disrupts everything. We usually need fifteen to thirty minutes to get caught up with each other about our lives and share whatever interesting news we've heard, and we're going to be taking that time at the beginning whether or not the game is started. If doing that up front and then specifically saying "Let's start playing" when it's over counts as a ritual -- and it might, albeit of a very casual and haphazard variety -- then it's one that our group absolutely requires for a great game.

    \n Wolfe - I'm not familiar enough with Polaris, but from what I see in the rpg.net reviews, it uses a number of ritual phrases. The introductory one draws on a set of deeply ground in ritual phrases - "once upon a time", "long ago" and so on. Still, I think I might be wrong in supposing that ritual depends on repetition. But I'll hang on to the idea that it needs to draw some sort of weight from somewhere else - some mode of discourse the players are prepared to allow themselves to be influenced by.
    \n
    \nNow that think about it, I remember claiming something similar in a discussion on a norwegian board - that ritual is binding and limiting, because of the need for repetition and conformity. But someone else pointed out something that made me realize I was thinking of ritual from an "apollonian" perspective, when ritual can also be dionysian - wild, inspired, out of control, provoking startlingly alien and unexpected acts. So, no, not necessarily repetition - that's just one of a handful of clever little mindfucks that can be used to unlock our brains and make us think, feel, act and react differently.

    \nI think one of the big things I'm seeing from examples and anecdotes is that ritual is either explicit or formed gradually. Note that explicit doesn't necessarily mean intentional.     \n
    \nI guess I'm largely interested in the explicit type of ritual. On the other hand, the gradual type is interesting because it's more practical, since it emerged from practice, and can be a great resource when thinking about explicit ritual techniques.

When implementing things, explicitly or gradually, you never have to call it ritual. Hell, you don't have to call it anything. You can just that there's this thing you want to try.
