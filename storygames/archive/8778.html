\n The lack of precision in game texts is one of my ongoing disappointments. I'll offer this as an example that hopefully other game designers can take to heart when they set about writing their texts.
\n
\nSummerlands mechanics are fairly straightforward and not at all hard to grasp the mechanical bits. They don't fill me with glee and have me yearning to grab dice and start playing but they seem functional in that "rules light", "get out of the way" utilitarian kind of way that was big a few years ago. But there are significant gaps in the instructions for how to apply them.
\n
\nI'll summarize the rules which start out with basic opposed stake setting which some people really have an issue with but that doesn't bother me:
\n
\n1) player states intent and what his character gets if he wins. Player will narrate victory
\n2) GM states consequences and what the character suffers if he loses. GM will narrate failure (or if in conflict with another player the player's will have opposed intents)
\n3) A score is calculated by determining appropriate attributes and tags and help
\n4) GM determines difficulty which is the number of dice to roll trying to get below the score.
\n5) Player rolls
\n6) Theres some damage type stuff (called distress) that happens on failure in addition to the stakes based on the difference in the rolls.
\n
\nSo here's the crucial parts that are not explicitly stated in the text.
\n1) What is the allowable scale of the stakes? I've found no text that directly addresses this. The only place that even touches on it confuses things further by asserting that the entire conflict is resolved by a single roll. Other parts of the text use fairly routine tasks as examples like "repair the car". So 1 roll to repair the car and 1 roll to fight another dude are actually stated. So is that also 1 roll to fight an entire feud between neighboring villages with stakes to destroy the losing village? Is it 1 roll to rebuild the entire village that's destroyed? Rules as Written the player sets the intent. So if the player say their intent is to rebuild one building its one building. If they say the intent is the village its the village. If they say its the entire world...its the entire world. Neither the GM nor group consensus is given any explicit input or over ride authority over the player's stated intent. Rules as Written player states ANYTHING THEY WANT as their intent and there is no authority other than non rules based social pressures to set standards. There are some game designers that I would interpret the lack of any further process as meaning exactly what is written. Player sets intent, no one else has any input into the player's choice whatsofuckingever. I suspect, based on the horde of indie games I've read and played (often with their designer) that there is actually a whole process that happens at Greg's table to decide stakes that just didn't make it into the rules. If you mean for us to kibbitz and discuss before the player sets stakes and the GM to have final veto over stakes he doesn't think are appropriate...tell us that. If you mean for the player to be able to do whatever he wants with no input from any body else and no recourse for anyone who doesn't like it...tell us that. But for god's sake...tell us SOMETHING.
\n
\n2) The GM sets consequences. Based on what? Is the intent for the GM to set consequences that are in balance with the scale of the intent the player established? Or is the intent for the GM to set consequences that are some degree (perhaps substantially so) harsher than the player's intent in a sort of "even now?" fashion. Is this meant to be a dramatic dial that the GM can use to control the pacing of the game? A later part of the rules mentions scripted scenarios as an acceptable way to GM this game...are the consequences meant to be the way the GM keeps things on script? What...
\n
\n3) Can the player back out? The GM sets both the price of failure AND the difficulty...specifically setting the difficulty AFTER seeing what the player's target number is. So if the intent is X and the consequence is Y and the difficulty is Z can the player say "Whoa, I'm not willing to risk paying Y to get X, especially not with difficulty Z...screw that, I'll do something else". Rules as Written provide absolutely no provision for the player to back out once the process is started. It may seem like an "of course players can choose not to roll once they see what's at stake" kind of thing but if that's the case fucking SAY THAT. I can think of numerous cases where its just as valid (for an "in your face - suck it" kind of game to not allow that. Which is it?
\n
\n4) What order to player's go in? This is completely and utterly completely absent. The conflict rules briefly touch on the idea of PCs being in conflict with each other...one would think then that who gets to go first would be a rather important thing to know. Does the GM just decide based on what's reasonable in the fiction and the descriptions of actions the players have given? Then SAY THAT! The only thing the rules mention is that there are only ever 2 sides to a conflict, a side consists of 1 character acting and at most 1 character helping, and that if there are more characters involved (in a "dramatically important way") to split the activity into several rolls. Ok, on what basis, which order are they resolved in, and can the results of the earlier rolls influence the difficulty of the later rolls? Do all the rolls have to be established and calculated before any of them are resolved or can you just figure out each roll as it comes up? Or what?

    Roleplaying games are not special. They don't get a special pass on clarity because we talk in funny voices when we play. You must communicate your process clearly, otherwise it is bad design.

      3) Post here either in its own thread (in "Game Design Help", or append a message on the end of the Cool Stuff of the Month threads) asking people to read/critique/drop bombs.
