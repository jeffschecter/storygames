\n Some recent probability-related threads rekindled my interest in an idea I've had for a while. I posted a similar message to this one on the Forge at the start of the year. I'd be interested to hear what this crowd thinks of it.
\n
\nAlthough it has slowed a bit lately, for a while I spent an inordinate amount of time publishing ad hoc web articles about probability in RPGs, from some basic stuff to some not-so-basic stuff. Partially based on the reaction I've got from these, I am contemplating writing a "probability for roleplayers" type book (probably a free PDF).
\n
\nI'm thinking it will be about half theory/general principles, half specific examples from particular (openly licensed) games, with the examples getting progressively more complicated. I have a pretty decent list of the topics I would want to see in such a book. I also have a pretty clear idea of how I want it to look.
\n
\nI'm curious about some things though, which hopefully this community can answer:\n
    \n[li]Would you even read such a book?[/li][li]What about probability do you wish you knew more about?[/li][li]Are there particular examples of situations or mechanics in specific games that you've wondered how the probability a) works and b) can be figured out? If so, what are they?[/li]

    \n Not a bad idea, this. I'm not in the target audience directly, being a designer and math alumnus myself, but I'm sure there's plenty of people who could learn lots of interesting stuff from a book of rpg math.

    One thing I'd put in such a book would be simplified methods of estimating probabilities for commonly occurring rpg situations. Statistically most rpg procedures are repeated so few times that even a 10%-unit throw in the accuracy of an estimate wouldn't be a big deal. Using that as a guideline you could eliminate some of the smaller terms from the common probability calculations and give people some interesting rules of thumb for everyday needs. Also, of course, tables of probabilities.

    \n Yeah there are a couple basic tricks for calculating and estimating that will cover sooo many cases. For example...\n
    Posted By: thadrineTell me this.

    What is the average totals of a roll and keep system using d10's?

    So if I roll 7 and keep 3 what is the average total going to be?
    Compared with?
    Roll 5 and keep 3?
    \nRoll 7 keep 3 highest sum them? You can estimate the average by the following procedure. First, we're about to treat the range of results as continuous rather than discrete (you can "roll" a 3.4, for example). To do this in a nice way, give each real result (like 5) a range of numbers around it - 1/2 and + 1/2 (so 4.5 to 5.5). Now put the 7 dice on the range distributed uniformly - split up the range from 0.5 to 10.5 into 8 equal parts, and put a die between each part. So the first (lowest) goes at 0.5+10/8=1.75, the next at 3, then 4.25, 5.5, 6.75, 8, 9.25. Grab the top 3 and add them together, and there's your answer. 6.75+8+9.25 is 24.

    Roll 5 keep 3? Split into 6 equal parts, take the top 3. That's 5.5+7.166+8.333=21 average.

    Those estimates are pretty close to right, though not exact. Let's see... the real answers are 23.94 and 21.46 (simulations). This method would really benefit from a better layout, pictures would make it crystal clear.

Divide number of sides N (10) by 1+number of dice (1+7=8) to get the length L (1.25), count the Kth highest die as worth N+0.5 - K*L. So the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd of 7d10 are 10+0.5-1*1.25=9.25, 10+0.5-2*1.25=8, 10+0.5-3*1.25=6.75.

    \n An even "dirtier" version of Guy's esti-method is just to imagine a few dice in "typical" outcomes, spread throughout the range, but centered on the average.

    For example, rolling 5 d6s and taking the two lowest:

    Imagine you have a roughly even spread. We have five dice and six possible outcomes, so we have to leave out one outcome. Which one? Five dice could show up 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5. That's a low outcome. Or maybe 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. That's a high outcome. Let's use both!

    That gives you totals of 3 (low) and 5 (high), and a vague sense of the average result (something around 4). And if you do the math, the average roll of 5d6, keep the lowest two, is something like 4.07.

    If you have 4 dice, keep the lowest two, it's easier: just imagine a roll of typical results clustered around the middle -> 2, 3, 4, and 5.

    That's a result of 5. Let's check the math (using anydice.com here): it gives us an average of 4.66. In the ballpark, right?

    If you've got more dice than outcomes, keep "imagining" results around the middle/average. So, for lowest two of 8 dice, I might "imagine" 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6, and then add another 3 and 4. That hypothetical roll would be a "3" (keeping the two lowest). The actual average is 3.11.

    However, obviously, using this method in this case you'll get the same result for 6 dice as you would for 8 dice. But you can also "guesstimate" what, say, 5 dice, 7 dice, and 9 dice would look like and then get a sense of the range. In this particular example, this won't help you much, as you can see, but often you can draw a rough "curve" of what the probability might look like by estimating this way.

    It can give you a (very) rough sense of what typical (or average) rolls might be like, but then you'll have to do the actual math if you're serious about using that in a design. You'll need to know what your extremes are like, as well, and those are much easier to estimate mathematically (there are fewer ways to roll "unlikely" results, so it's easier to do the math). Still, can be a handy "estimate" tool, just like measuring a distance by "pacing off": useful as long as you remember how easily you could be way, way off. You're essentially taking a guess. You'll need to get to a calculator or a spreadsheet or both then to see if your guesses are in the right "zone", and whether that's a mechanic that's worth pursuing for what you want to do.
