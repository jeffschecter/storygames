  • Adam Cerling
    August 2006
    Once upon a time I ran a Silent Hill / Little Fears game. It ran in a closed story arc of five sessions.

    We played in the basement, lit by a bare lightbulb. I sat my players in oversized armchairs. To each one I gave a signature prop, prompted by the Little Fears character sheet: an action figure, a stuffed dog, a stuffed bear, a special handkerchief. I allowed no food, but did offer pitchers of Kool-Aid and big heavy tumblers.

    I prepared playlists of Silent Hill background music on a nearby PC: one low-tension playlist, and one high-tension extra-disturbing playlist that I used for the Dark World.

    When the kids found a discarded walkie-talkie near a crash site, I handed the players a walkie-talkie. I had its twin rigged to transmit static when I needed to imply that there were monsters getting close.

    Every time they saw a new monster, I had a big sketch of it prepared on 12" x 18" paper -- all scribbly, in black charcoal and red crayon. I taped the sketches to the wall behind me where the play. By the end of the game, the wall was covered in the monsters they had seen.

    One session ran in a Silent Hill hotel. In one room, the kids found a TV that randomly turned on. I prepared a real TV in the basement to turn on, muted, via remote at that moment; I had it playing the final scenes from the Shining.

    One session ran in a Silent Hill pet store. I prepared a scene where a girl's favorite stuffed dog would get shot. I said that in-game there was blood, and that the girl could feel a bullet in the stuffed dog's head. The player, cradling her stuffed-dog prop, declared that she had to get the bullet out. So I slid a real pair of scissors to her and nodded at the dog, telling her: "You really do feel a bullet in there." So the player cut the dog's head open with the scissors, pushed her hand inside and found the stuffing wet with red food coloring I'd slid inside with the bullet the night before. She went home with red stains on her hands.

    The players still talk about that game today, four years later.

    Four keys that made it work:

    One, it was a brand-new player group. Nobody knew how I'd GM.
    Two, we nonetheless had a good Social Contract conversation up-front (before I knew what Social Contract was), establishing simply that we were there to play a scary horror game, so no wrecking the mood.
    Three, the game had a huge amount of GM Fiat. I railroaded like a ninja master, so they never noticed, but how else was I to get them into all my cool scary moments?
    And Four -- I was unemployed. There's no way I could pull off all that prep these days!

    1001 Nights is all over the props. Silk scarves, brass bowls, dice that make you think of gems and want to touch them, a menu in the game, etc.,etc. I seriously think bringing in actual tactile, sensual experience helps a game. Theme music that's not fiddley or intrusive can be fantastic.

    In several years I have not run a game that did not have a soundtrack with character theme songs et all. There is an art to using a soundtrack, however, and it takes practice to keep it from being distracting. (As with scene framing its something you can get cues for by watching how they make it work in movies.) I find that done wrong it is annoying, but done right and with a group that has a strong emotional response to music it can add a lot to the game.

    I also use mood lighting and/or candle light in almost all of my games. I find that it helps take the edge off, especially if the room is at that level where you can easily read dice and character sheets, but everything else is just dim enough that it doesn't grab focus. Plus, I find that such lighting tends to draw the eye to the eyes of the other players, which is very helpful.

    The only other prop, besides sound a light, that I use regularly is the character sheet itself. I tend to funktify my character sheets and add pictures, poems, whatever to make the sheet a point of focus that constantly refers me back to the character's themes and issues in a visual, emotional way (as well as the mechanical, rational way that the stats work).

    Once in a while I've been able to run games in fully different settings, and the results have been mostly good. Playing Tribe 8 by firelight in the middle of the Canadian shield at night, or Polaris in High Park in Toronto as autumn is fading into winter added an ambiance not easily gotten in other ways. This is the only area where I've ever gotten close to anything like "dangerous use of props" -- running the post holocaust game in an abandoned neighborhood construction zone was probably not the wisest move I've ever made.

    I've never been a big one for costumes, handouts, or other types of props. However, after reading Meg's comments I find myself intrigued.
