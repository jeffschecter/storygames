    \n Yeah, looking at the Intarweb, it would seem that "Country House" includes actual converted castles - or at least they're listed alongside each other as "historical houses." There's a period following the medieval where England no longer really needs castles, but the nobility still want their country estates. So these are really small palaces built to meet these needs. In some few cases they simply converted existing castles, instead of building in the "Palladian" style.
    \n
    \nHere's a great example: http://www.nationaltrust.org.uk/main/w-vh/w-visits/w-findaplace/w-tattershallcastle/
    \n
    \nNote that many of these places are too costly for anyone to own any more, and so are held now as national monuments, or the like. This is actually the case, too, for the Hearst estate that I liked to above. It's far, far too expensive for anyone to own individually today (the amount of wealth embodied in San Simeon is staggering). Note that San Simeon is sometimes refered to as "The Hearst Castle" as you can see from the name of the website. Though it's main building's facade is modeled after a Spanish mission, than a castle, it has lots of castle-ish features. (If I recall correctly, Hearst also owned an actual castle in Wales).
    \n
    \nHere's an English palace of the same magnitude, one so large it's called a castle without really being one at all: http://www.britainexpress.com/counties/yorkshire/Castle_Howard.htm
    \n
    \nFrom that one, I get a new entry:
    \n
    \nMuseum Room
    \n
    \n
    \nNote that, while many of these places have more rooms in total number than the list above, no real place is going to have all of these rooms. Most of the rooms are redundant bedrooms and attendant rooms in suites, with some cases of multiple kitchens and dining rooms, and other such rooms. Consder San Simeon again with it's 38 bedrooms.
    \n
    \nAgain, what's fun about this is to name them. Each bedroom for it's occupant, in many cases, or the occupants role. So you have "The Lord's Bedroom" or something. And "the Lady's Bedroom" why neccessitate sleeping in the same room if you have dozens of bedrooms? Castle Howard seems to use colors to differentiate between various dining rooms and drawing rooms, etc.
    \n
    \nI'm not sure why you're getting this list, Simon, but what comes to mind is not only the game clue, but also Kill Dr. Lucky. Which has many more rooms than clue, each being a space. And, hey, one of the expansions is based off a real Country Home and Castle (Canada is still part of England, right?): http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craigdarroch_Castle
    \n
    \nI'm definitely seeing some fun game here about traipsing all over the grounds trying to uncover some mystery. Hyper Clue, perhaps (Cluedo to you brits). If you don't make it, I will!!!
    \n
    \nUh, perhaps pushing the definition, but here is, of course, the ultimate "Country House": http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles
    \n
    \nNote how it started as a hunting lodge... It inspired this actual country house in Ireland: http://www.powerscourt.ie/
    \n
    \nYet another converted castle. :-)
    \n
    \nI recall my first encounter with the idea of a country house, which was in the movie Candleshoe (which interestingly centers around the problem of the cost of running such an estate), filmed at this place: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_Wynyates
    \n
    \nThe place is noted for it's secret staircases, and spaces, including what is apparently a priest hole.

    \nOf course there's the country house from the movie Gosford Park: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syon_House
