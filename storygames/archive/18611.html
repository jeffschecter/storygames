    \n I'm all about carry - I like it a lot! I've been GM and player on different occasions. These were the things that helped me:

    Create your own outline of the rules. I found the text a little tough to crack, and having everything spelled out in a step by step few-page summary was really helpful.

    Pass over lots of time in between scenes. If you take this moment by moment the personal changes that happen won't make any sense. Your story will take place over weeks, months, or even years. Be sure you are going to the Next Important Thing, and not just following the grunts around.

    Personally, I think this game really shines over multiple sessions. Like, two or three. One is tough.

    The more detail you can put in the small things, the better. Like, don't go crazy and bog everything down, but the more real the better. The GM I played with was a veteran, and just the way he described a medical kit or a radio really did it.

    Hope that helps!

    Tayler

    \n That all sounds good!

    There's a rules summary (made by Adam Dray) you can download here (scroll to the bottom), either to use or as a starting point for your own.

    The most important thing, when I play, is to make sure stakes (in Squad scenes) are being set that really matter, and usually that are about how the character feels about what they're doing or about the relationship of the character to the other characters. "If I win, I guide us through the minefield without anyone getting hurt" is weaksauce. "If I win, I show the Sergeant that I actually know my shit about where the VC put their traps" is way better. It also makes it a lot easier for the GM to invert the stakes and make failure really hurt "Ok, well then if you lose, it'll obviously and clearly be your fault when someone gets blown up following your guidance".

    I've never played a game that went for more than one session! I think it's because I tend to kill off Fodder quickly in squad scenes or off screen, and push each scene to snowball into the next. But there's nothing wrong with going slower (and it's cool that other folks are having that experience).

    \n Color is important. Steep the game in colorful details whenever you can. Learn a little about the weapons, electronics, tents and living conditions, food, countryside, and military chain of command and sprinkle it about.

    Go back and forth between squad scenes and battle scenes. Doesn't have to be one-to-one, and probably shouldn't be, but definitely pace things so they feel the tension between war and downtime. Battle scenes are a yardstick of the breakdown of the military command as squad relationships fall apart. These guys are supposed to trust each other, and squad scenes put pressure on that trust. When shit goes south in a battle, that puts additional tension on subsequent squad scenes.

    Admittedly, as a GM, I am terrible about pushing against Burdens. I encourage the players to push those and I try to follow along.

    Have a few "set piece" scenes in your head. Some of mine:

    * battle: a gully with a creek at the bottom, lots of trees, VC ambushing from the other side
    * battle: crossing a bridge in a convoy, mortar shells coming down and destroying the lead truck, blocking the way
    * squad: "that's my cot" -- a pointless posturing fight between a PC and one of the big fodder -- other people playing cards
    * squad: the PCs get permission for some R&R and go into town -- bars, prostitutes, drugs
    * squad: someone's on guard duty (I usually pick someone irresponsible)

    \n Oh, I tried to run carry as a Battle of Hu\u1ebf scenario. In my mind, it was a failure.

    It was too battle intensive. I didn't leave enough room for squad scenes, because the pressure was on constantly. I've run it twice now and I'm not sure that it's salvageable as a scenario. What seems to work best is cycle of: a mission that leads to a battle scene, then some down time and squad scenes, over and over. You can time-lapse between these things ("a few days later, you're all out on patrol..." or "so it's a few days after your patrol and you're hanging out in the mess...").

    Personally, I think this game really shines over multiple sessions. Like, two or three. One is tough.
    \nOne thing with a lot of games of this type (high emotion, serious themes) is that in order to squeeze the maximum goodness out of them they often need 5-6 hours play, meaning that they fall shy of a typical 4 hour con or club session. So yes, I can see that you might need multiple sessions to do the game justice. This is not to second guess the designer of course- multiple folk facilitating a game means multiple practical ideas about how to do so.
    \nRunning it over multiple sessions/a longer period allows you to put in scenes that don't have instant pay off. Trying to shoe horn a lot of this style of game in to a single session doesn't necessarily mean the game won't run, but the intensity doesn't have time to build. As the game moves on scenes between characters are a lot more powerful if it's obvious what these characters have to gain and lose and if there has been time for the depth of the characters to be portrayed. WIth limited time the game tends to distill to instant gratification scenes, probably partially because players are aware that their 'screen time' is so limited, and the groundwork scenes get cut.

    To get a similar effect with a single session game seems to need a much more focussed and smaller game. Ash seems to be adept at these. Family Affair (which I played at ConDamned) does just this.
