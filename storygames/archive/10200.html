\n Game Chef 2009 will start on Monday, Aug 31. This should allow folks to finish and submit whatever they're doing for Jake's contest.

The weekend before, Aug 29-30, I will release info about the structure of the contest, but not the ingredients or theme.

Game Chef 2009 will accept competed games on four different deadlines:

- Monday Sept 7 (one week later),
- Monday Sept 14 (two weeks later),
- Wednesday Sept 30 (the last day of the month), and
- right before the start of Game Chef 2010 (approx. one year later).

Participating chefs are encouraged to use these submission dates as milestones, interpreting them as they like (for example, I might do something like "basic playtest draft, revised draft, alpha, beta") and, furthermore, to resubmit games that have been significantly revised or improved between submission dates. Submitting games requires emailing me a link to a location where the game can be downloaded or purchased (yes, you can sell your GC games if you want, that's none of my business, though I don't necessarily suggest it, especially before the One Year deadline).

Note that, of course, you can just submit your game once and walk away. There's no need to continue to develop a game that you may or may not be fully invested in, but some very vague structure will exist for those who do want to continue after the first week or two.

Cheers! I will post more details on the structure of the contest and link to the new website as we get closer to the start date.

    \n Seeing as how none of the games that won Game Chef have ever been released in "finished" form, remaining in playtest drafts (except maybe 1984 Prime, did that ever come out?) or seen much sustained play, I figured it was time to reconsider the role of judging. I was further inspired by the amount of time and energy that gets spent on judging an ever increasing number of games, time that would be much better spent, in my opinion, playing and revising the games after they have been created, not losing that momentum while we wait to find out who won (since being in the top tier of games is nice but doesn't necessarily mean anyone will ever play your game).

    So we're going to try something a bit different this year. While there will not be formal judging per say, I am attempting to structure things so that it will still be possible to look at the results of the competition and judge, for oneself, which games are likely to be the most successful or, more importantly, the most interesting to YOU, games that you might want to play.

    That said, this will be my first year running Game Chef and I'm anticipating an above-average amount of estrangement as I make a few changes and try to implement a new structure. Lord knows I complained enough to Andy over the years. Thankfully, I think the format and tradition of Game Chef is strong enough to survive a bit of meddling by the host.

      \n Also, one possibility which people didn't seem to jump on (but it was totally left out there) was for folks interested in the social aspects, the peer feedback and feedback groups, to set up such an arrangement themselves: Perhaps ask for folks interested, then start up an email list or Google Group for the contest, or perhaps create a forum somewhere (maybe ask over at 1km1kt).

      What I noticed in years past is that when I created feedback groups and even participated in them anonymously, I enjoyed reading and giving feedback to folks, but I don't think I really helped them in a significant way (maybe that's just me). And my cycle of design with my games (notably Hegemony and Take Back of Freedom) was "no inspiration no inspiration no inspiration no inspiration no inspiration SUDDEN BOLT OF INSPIRATION; IGNORE FORUMS COMPLETELY UNTIL GAME DRAFT IS TOTALLY DONE, usually at 11:50PM on the last night of the contest". So I didn't make much use of my own feedback groups, I'm afraid. Although when I did toss out a question or an idea, I usually did get people chiming in. But while I saw that others were clearly getting helpful input and feedback on their designs, I personally came to realize that the process tended to get in my way of finishing a game on time (especially because of my work schedule and the like).

      Since it was clear to me that feedback groups help some people and hinder others, I figured that writing them out officially, but leaving room for people to organize such feedback circles themselves, was the way to go.

      However, it seems that maybe I didn't shout that publicly enough ("HEY YOU! You want a feedback group? Make one! People with similar interests and needs WILL join you, and get involved!"). I saw folks complain online here and there that their ideas didn't get a lot of feedback or commentary. Some thought it was because they were a "nobody" in the social hierarchy of game chef or something; but that's just plain bullshit. The reason folks didn't get commentary is because that's not what the blogroll was for: The blogroll is a chance for folks to keep their own pace, to blow their own horn about what they're working on, the steps they climbed. If folks were looking for support, feedback, or insight, they should have organized with other participants to create small communities of support (even if it was nothing more than a pact between 3-10 people who promise to read each others' blogs and respond) so they would get the feedback they were looking for.

      All in all, good lessons to remember for next year's contest. I'll try to beat the drum early and loud of, "Feedback groups are great! If you want to participate in one, step up and organize it!"
