    \n I want to try to be as constructive as possible, because it's a cool thread concept.

    Weapons of the Gods: Re-describe things like Hidden Arts in a way which make sense on the first read-through.
    Also, create "loadouts" of campaign secrets, basically packages that give you a certain character type at-a-glance, so you don't have to spend several hours reading through each and every one for the few that appeal to you.

    Artesia: Simpler rules system for "everyday things": Boil all actions down to a few skills and attributes.

    Cold City: Take Partial Successes out behind the shed and shoot them in the head.

    Noumenon: A guide which tells you what you should do with the game.

    Solar System: Replace Fudge dice with numbers again.

    HELLAS*: Less on planets, more customized lifepaths for the races (lifepaths work for humans well, but with some of the races there are things that conflict with their racial background).

    Play Unsafe: Needs to be put together in a way which reduces customer cost 50%.

    More small-ish Games: Look into Landscape printing. Personally, I love it, even if it increases the wear on larger games.

    Blue Planet: A book that gives you 10 Campaign Situations, pregenerated PCs and NPCs for those situations.

    -Andy

    (asterisk indicates the best RPG released in 2008)

    \n There was a request to explain what I meant and rather than just whisper it, I think I'll share it.

    After the pitch session in Primetime Adventures (PTA), I take the TV show metaphor a step further and we all come up with a list of what characters ought to appear in the show. It's something I call a "casting step" and I included it in my game, Misspent Youth (which owes a heap of debt to PTA in many, many ways). So for example, in the plucky teenage girl fights monsters and is a chosen one and has a gang of friends we might come up with this list of concepts:

    nerdy kid
    wise elder
    sexually frustrated kid
    secretly gay
    tough girl
    chosen one
    sexy monster
    etc.

    Then these concepts are claimed and, in some cases, combined.
