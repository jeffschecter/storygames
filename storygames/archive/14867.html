\n So, I've been sort of playing Pathfinder recently (it's complicated), and I've been looking at d20 stuff, and I keep getting weird little ideas for hacks I'd like to put together.
\n
\nBut more than just, I've been thinking, how could we REALLY mess with it? Ages ago, for instance, there was an RPG.net thread I saw that turned D&D3e; into a sixties protest game.
\n
\nMore recently, a coment I saw about wondering what kind of ways d20 could be hacked popped up.
\n
\nAnd so, I've been thinking about some kind of Story Games, maybe other places too, d20 hacking challenge! It could be some good outreach to more d20 oriented places, and I'd love to see the d20 get pushed and shoved in all kinds of crazy directions, like Jenskot did here.
\n
\nBut, well, I'm a little scared because I've never tried to coordinate anything like this before. And I'd like to gauge interest before I attempted it.
\n
\nI was thinking, it would propbably be judged, although I have no prize to speak of, but because systems push stuff in interesting ways, criteria I was thinking about was
\n
\n*completeness
\n*Amount of OGL content used
\n*Compatability with other d20 games
\n*Complete incompatibility with other d20 games.
\n*Innovation
\n*Style
\n
\nJust stuff to make it interesting, and maybe push away from just grabbing the core mechanic and writing something wholly unrelated.
\n
\nQuestions? Comments? Suggestions? Interest?

    \n Oh, I totally forgot. I had a campaign idea I called Dawn of FIre - mashing up the post-apocalyptic movie Reign of Fire and with-the-apocalypse movies like "Dawn of the Dead" or the new "Rise of the Planet of the Apes".

    The premise is that on a typical D&D world, suddenly the surface is taken over by an enormous number of dragons. To survive, a band of people seek deep underground areas for shelter. This means that while you have adventurers coming into dungeons, they are coming in there to find a place to live for themselves and the other survivors coming with them. For example, gold is worthless - what they will be looking for is food and water. I thought it could be an intriguing twist on the concept, that I might well adapt for Pathfinder.

    \n When I first saw Wilderness of Mirrors it immediately got added to in my DnD game years ago. Basically, make a skill check compare to a Narrative Control scale like in Wilderness of Mirrors and go from there. Every time a player did something cool or created a complication they got an action point (which was an extra d6 they could roll for anything). Rewarding for good RP worked really well for that group. It was still basically DnD, but played drastically different.
