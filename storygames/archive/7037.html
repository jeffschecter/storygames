    \n Actually, Lee's dialogue in the early 60's was miles ahead of anyone else's in comics. It's just one of the differences in popular culture between now and 45 years ago.

    One thing to note is that this dialogue isn't primarily conversational. It's a combination of framing scenes, setting stakes, providing exposition (because the artists had to work fast and didn't always illustrate things clearly), and providing characterization. The fact that it doesn't sound like the way people actually talk is probably to be expected.

    American superhero comics have moved away from this style of presentation, but at a cost. If you can't use thought-bubbles (oh, the thought bubble, how I missed you!), or narration captions, or clumsy dialogue balloons, to explain what's going on, you have to include a lot more scenes to show the reader what the hell is going on. In the Silver Age, it was pretty common to just jump from Scene 1 to Scene 4, for reasons of space, and just hand-wave the intermediate scenes through exposition. You also have to figure out other ways to show what's at stake in a scene; if it's a particularly subtle or non-obvious point, that means providing a lot of other scenes to develop the emotional tension.

    One result is that Stan Lee told a trite story that wraps up in 22 pages. Modern superhero comic-writers tell a trite story that wraps up in 110 pages. Today's six-issue-miniseries would have been a single issue for Lee & Kirby. (One of the few writers who can still pull off a self-contained, 22-page story using modern-style dialogue is John Rogers, who's doing DC's new Blue Beetle series. Not coincidentally, he's a gamer.)

    If a cartoon like that were game, it's the equivalent of something like: "Hey, we've got Conan the Barbarian over here, fighting guys on the Black River... Oh, hey, yeah, we know he's going to take over hte kingdom - let's just say he does, and handwave through it, and get to the fight with the Dog-Demon."
