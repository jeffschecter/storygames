Player Skills -- Okay, Now What?

Josh Roby
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Play Advice
So we've identified a lot of player skills in the thread Player Skills -- Brainstorm. There's been some awesome discussion there and if you haven't read that thread, you totally should do so, if only to see Jess unpack HARDCORE!

However, now that we've got this list, I'm at something of a loss as to what we can do with it. Each skill is used differently in individual games, so I don't think it's terribly feasible to talk about them in isolation or out of context. We could write drills for them, but again, the drill might teach the skill in one way and a game use it in another. We could use it as a checklist of design, but it's pretty long and unweildy for that.

So I put it to you, people of Story Games. What can we do with this very juicy list now that we've got it?

    I see what you're saying. In the interest of fragmenting the discussion in confusing ways, I've posted a comment to your blog. Basically, I think these skills can be discussed in precisely the same way that we talk about skills in sports: there are general skills, and then there are specific tricks you can do with them.

    Would it be feasible to outline a sort of "generic" version of each skill and then follow up with specific examples from specific game designs? Then maybe some specific techniques? Sort of like:

    Scene Framing is the act of setting up a scene in a game. The scene framer selects a few characters and props and decides where they are (set) and what they are doing. Blah de blah blah, a little more detail here.

    In Primetime Adventures, scene framing is done by determining if the scene will deepen the character or develop the plot. Stakes are determined, and cards are dealt to determine if the stakes are won. Then the players roleplay through the scene.

    In Dungeons and Dragons, scene framing is the exclusive purview of the Dungeon Master, who regulates the player characters' progress by describing each step and challenge that they must overcome to proceed.

    Tips and Tricks: An engaging scene can be framed quickly and easily by creating a conflict (see: conflict creation) based off of player flags (see: identifying flags).

    Contributing to theme
    Accepting character development in unexpected directions
    Being unafraid to take narrative risks that alter the state of the fiction in important ways
    Setting up future conflict or difficulty for yourself
