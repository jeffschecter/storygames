    \n My brother Mel says that the Duel of Wits is the thing about Burning Wheel/Burning Empires that he uses to sell it to new players. As luck would have it, he's just posted a twenty-minute podcast where he talks about that and gives examples from a game he's run. It's here at Virtual Play.

    Here's Mel's summary of the show:\n
    My favorite part of the Burning Wheel and Burning Empires games is the \u2018Duel of Wits\u2019. It\u2019s the mechanism used for social conflicts between characters. I know that some players don\u2019t like social conflict mechanics as somehow detracting from \u2018roleplaying\u2019, but I don\u2019t see it that way. In any social conflict for which there is no conflict resolution mechanism, the conflict will only end when one player or the other gives in\u2013regardless of the merits of the argument on either side. As a player, it\u2019s easy to \u2018not be convinced\u2019. I\u2019ve played in games that ground to a halt because players couldn\u2019t agree to a course of action for their characters\u2013I may even have participated in such disagreements! So the Duel of Wits strikes me as a great tool for the Burning Wheel. It\u2019s one part of the game that I like to use a lot and to show new players because I think they will like it as much as I do. This episode focuses on three important elements for the Duel of Wits. First, players cannot be forced into a Duel of Wits. Second, the players have to agree to what\u2019s at stake in the Duel\u2013not only what they get if they win, but what their character will do if they lose. Third, players must adhere to the terms of the Duel. Somehow abrogating or not carrying out the terms of the Duel is nothing less than cheating! Ultimately, the Duel of Wits is a negotiation and agreement between players for the direction of the story. The characters are a vehicle for the Duel, but it\u2019s the players who are bound to the results.

    \nAs Bill mentioned above, the Duel of Wts--a mechanism for social combat--is what draws me to Burning Wheel. I gloss over the detailed tactical fighting rules. Although it's not defined as such in the rules, the Fighting 'switch' in BW has three settings: a single roll of dice ('Versus Tests': for example, Sword Skill versus Sword Skill); rolls of dice pools divided into offense and defense (Bloody Versus); and the Fight! rules (which itself has various degrees of detail). So you can pick whichever level suits your liking. I generally stick to Bloody Versus--we resolve combat in two rolls of the dice. That leaves more time for Duels of Wit!
    Mel

    \n Also, fights in Burning Wheel are deadly and you should only enter one if the stakes are high enough for you to risk your life. As a result, they tend to be somewhat rare and generally are linked to important beliefs and plot points. There are no random encounters with goblins to reduce hit points and equipment. Thus, when a full Fight comes around, the group doesn't mind spending the time to work it out. Once you get the system down, Fight doesn't cause any drag time in your game.
