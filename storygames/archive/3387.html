The captain of the guard is standing point blank with an drawn bow pointed at your unarmored chest.

Here the game is about being in jeopardy.

You push aside the body of the dead orc and in the chest you find a glowing sword. As you pull it from the chest you see the torchlight reflect off of it in an iridescent sheen which takes the shape of a serpent traveling up the razor sharp blade.

Here the game is about mystery and magic.

You stand before the doors of the Temple of Badassery, the doors are 50 feet high and the corridor extends beyond them into the inky darkness.

Here the game is about adventuring into unknown dangers.
\nI've dreamt of games like the one described above. Has anyone ever had a game like this? What system? How many people? How long did it go? Was it a fluke or a planned event?

    \n Yes. I have had games like that.

    I was young (15 or so) but we had all RP'd enough that the system was second nature, both of which helped. The GM was a bit older. He had an archeology PHD and had done some drama in school. His dungeons made sense and his descriptions made the scenes come alive. Things would get so tense that we would be sweating on the edge of our seats. We were playing 1eAD&D with low level characters. We had the feeling that we had stumbled into something miles above our heads, but when the real fights happened it would generally be something that we could just handle.

    One of the things i remember was that when we found an item of note (magical or otherwise) he would describe it. So we would see a dagger with lapis lazuli and gold inlay and carved ram's head on the pommel or a small crystal vial of some viscuous white substance that seems to shimmer with its own eerie light, or whatever. The dagger was neat, the potion was cool. It wasn't "you got a +2 dagger and a potion of neutralize poison."

    \n It looks like you've got a lot of Immersion going on there, too. I didn't see that before but it makes sense. I'm not necessarily into the Immersion of it all (the total sensory and emotional interface concept).

    However, there are two things I'm interested in:

    1) Low-level characters = This to me means 'choices with consequences'. Low level characters really have to judge what's important to them.

    2) Identify = This to me means 'no rules that afix properties to characters or furniture'. Specifically for this example, no spells that permanently note a +1 weapon or that a character is a Dragon or what-have-you.

    Good. More examples like this and I might be able to see a conceptual framework.

    Posted By: noclue+1 sword is a metagame abstraction for what the sword does in the story. The character shouldn't get player information.
    \nIt's certainly a metagame abstraction if magic is mysterious, sure, but not if magic is a commodity. If magic is a commodity, then "+1 sword" really could mean the same thing in-world and at the table: repeated testing has indicated that a weapon of this quality is 5% more likely in most cases to deliver a telling blow, and inflicts blows that are, on average, more telling to a degree that you can actually (but just barely) make a clear distinction between such a weapon and a lesser weapon. If magic swords have the same cultural role as modern firearms, then a short, efficient, specialized terminology is likely to emerge to talk about them. We don't talk about "partially vacant metal shells that have the strange property of deforming upon impact when fired from a piece of musketry, creating a more brutal wound thereby," we just say "hollow point slugs."

    That commodification might not be your cup of tea, but its the whole point of, for example, Eberron (at least, that's the designer's stated intent with regards to magic), so its got at least some fans out there.

    On a related note, if you want to do the whole magic-is-mysterious thing, you probably need to go beyond making magic hard to identify; you should probably also make it unreliable. Repeatability is the death of mystery. It's hard to not nail something down to its particulars if it always does the same thing all the time.

    \n I don't think I agree. "+1 sword" is specifically a reference to +1 on a dice roll. People will develop shorthand, but they will not refer to guns (for example) as improving dice rolls without a game structure that uses dice. We quantify everything, that is true, but +1 sword is an abstraction and a player's shorthand not a term that is inherent in the setting.

    When I said magic an mystery in the original post I wasn't really saying that magic should be mysterious, but that the world should be full of images and imagery (magical in the poetic sense). Although, I do believe that a good dose of mysterious makes magic better in gaming.

    As for unrealiable magic, personally I find it a bit annoying. I would rather play with the cost of magic, make the choice to use it an important one, or make the cost unreliable rather than the affect. Personally, I also like making magic rare rather than commonplace (yeah, Eberron has too much of it for my tastes). But mostly, I think you can get some of this feel if you create limits to what characters can touch in the magical realm. So even the wisest mage feels like he's only touching the surface of a great ocean. He knows what he knows, but he also knows what he don't knows. This makes great sense to me. Theoretically, these folks are toying with the very stuff from which the cosmos was fashioned.

    \n That's why I prefer TSOY's system. As the pluses get larger, the narrative focus gets smaller. You'd just say you had a weapon, or a good weapon for the fight, or a great weapon for the duel with the captain. It doesn't lend itself to an economy, and means you aren't looking for code words to tell the group what you've got. What you've got is narratively obvious.
