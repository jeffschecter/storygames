But what happens in social conflicts? You can't break a leg trying to convince someone of something (unless things go very, very wrong) But people can start distrusting you, call you a liar, murder, thief, etc. So I'm trying to make a list of "social injuries".     \n What if you had a stack of physical (red) chips and social (blue) chips. When you take an injury, physical or social, you or the GM says why it hurts and you move one chip of the appropriate color forward. If you every move all your red or blue chips forward, you're basically out of the game. The catch is that whenever you say something you want to do, the GM can use one of the pushed forward red chips and say, "that's what you tried to do but you couldn't because it hurt too much - what did you do that didn't hurt so much?" If you narrate something social or say something in character, the GM can use one of the pushed forward blue chips and say, "that's what you wanted to say but it didn't come out that way - what did you really say?" This way you narrate the injury but the GM can decide when past injuries come back to haunt you. Injuries make you do suboptimal or second best things in the fiction but don't shut you down. You can be hurt the same way, or not, but you don't have to track specific wounds. It's different than injury lists, I know, but that's what jumped into my head after reading your post.

    \n I don't really like social mechanics that are modelled on combat. Rules matter to the feel of the game. If you model social interactions as combat, then any relationships which are significant enough to include rolls/tests/duels of wits may end up feeling hostile.

    It's not much of a problem when the recipient is an NPC, but can be grating when the target is a PC and the game is supposed to be immersive. You then get people frustrated about being forced to do something (because, yes, such a conflict is beating the target's character on the head - with words - until they agree to what the initiator wants).

    I would be interested in a different social mechanic: one in which you have to earn someone's trust. Perhaps the target could first rate how important the issue is for them (say, from 1 to 5) and list this many "checkpoints". Each checkpoint might be assigned a task, perhaps from a ready-made list (something like: "prove you're reliable: do this task in time" => buy me a dress for today's ball; or "prove you're trustworthy: keep this secret" => deliver this letter without opening it). Then, after all (or most) of the tasks are fulfilled, the initiator achieves their goal because target feels they've earned it.

    I look at it like this. If someone likes you already you don't have to do much to convince them of something. But to your point, what if you wanted them to like you more? You would carry out tasks for them, get them gifts or if you're Ben Franklin you ask them to borrow a rare book.

    What does this do to the person? They start to feel pressure to upgrade your status with them. If they agree, then you start dating or become best friends or whatever the desired outcome was. If they don't want to, even if you're being nice to them, they start to feel stress because you're putting pressure on them. It's the ultimate outcome that the person being pressured has to accept or reject and a PC should always have that option. However if they reject you, there are lasting hard feelings and the stress doesn't go away.

    \n Interesting that you brought this up, WarriorMonk. It's mostly brainstorming, but you might see something of interest. I'm working on a hack for World of Dungeons that includes "social attacks" during Arguments.

    An Argument occurs whenever people start yelling at each other, insulting each other, debating with each other, or whatever, and there are significant people around to witness it or there are some serious stakes involved. Anything less in a social sense is equivilent to pushing and shoving. A true Argument is more like social hack and slash. The participants are making vicious and meaningful verbal attacks towards one another and someone's reputation is on the line.

    Scrutiny is a measure of how well a character can stand up to social pressures in an Argument. It is to social what Wounds are to physical. You roll 2d6+Cunning to make a social attack and do 1d6+Cunning Scrutiny damage. When your scrutiny is depleted, you immediately take a Stigma and further scrutiny damage results in exacerbating that Stigma. The Stigma is a semi-permenant flaw in your character's reputation or social status within a group of people significant to your character.

    Thadius Brent is locked in a verbal duel with another Highblood infront of a crowded ball room. The opposing noble, Bringer Stoneman, insulted his mother in such a nasty way. Brent decides to go on the offense and try to embarress the noble infront of the crowd. Brent rolls+Cunning. He gets a 4 and 3, +2 for his Cunning, so a 9. He runs into a Problem, and the GM decides to Exchange Scrutiny. Before Brent can pull off his insult, Stoneman lands another verbal blow against Brent's mother's purity, but Brent returns with a jab of his own by shedding light on Stoneman's own infedilities. Brent rolls 1d6+2 for his attack, rolling a 2 and adding the bonus. The GM rolls for Stoneman's attack and gets a total of 7. The GM had noted earlier that Stoneman's Scrutiny was 4, so Brent's insult destroys Stoneman's reputation infront of the crowd and he takes a Stigma. However, Brent only had 7 Scrutiny points which leaves Brent with a Stigma of his own. The GM decides the crowd reacts badly to their argument and kicks them both out of the ball.

    Brent earns the Stigma: Instigator (the Highblood see you as a troublemaker - you're no longer invited to the ball)
