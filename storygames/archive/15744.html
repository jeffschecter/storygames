\n I love animated skeletons. I think they are weird and terrifying. They carry certain unsettling implications and they want to kill you.

Here are some scary-ass skeletons. Note that the rules are hard to understand - these guys are tenacious, they are fast, they jump around like frogs and use tactics. The only one to be defeated has to be beheaded, but others go down after a punch in the face. They are magical and ferocious and not necessarily evil - they are defending the golden fleece, which seems like an OK thing to do.

OK, Story Games global brain trust, let's make skeletons cool and unnerving. What are the key components in skeletonry? What will make them badass and memorable? I want players to pee their pants when they hear that dry rattling down the corridor, not snicker and pull out their blunt smashing tools.

Dungeon World moves and color earn extra points.

Go!

    \n The necromancer trick of small army of skeletons is "suddenly, you are surrounded by all sides by a quick-moving force of armed and skilled warriors." That's what skeletons should do - there never should be a 'room full of skeletons' encounter you can know before in advance.
    \n
    \nWhat skeletons do is surround you, cut off exit routes, appear from the least expected location, leverage their higher numbers tactically (attack someone from the front and back simultaneously, for example), attack the weakest and pin the strongest.
    \n
    \nSo that's skeleton tactics.
    \n
    \nBut what about their motivation? I'm all for the 'angry spirit' camp of what makes the fellows tick - the more angry, the more tick. But what makes them angry? Clearly, their (or their fellows') tombs or burial places being desecrated. What counts as desecration? Well, the usual stuff - being there, for one, and also taking stuff you shouldn't. But also probably harming what's left of the physical body of the skeleton.
    \n
    \nSo the more you harm a skeleton, the angrier the spirit powering it gets, and the more dangerous it and other skeletons become.
    \n
    \n
    \nAn all-skeleton dungeon could be an inversion of the usual dungeon-crawl - the problem is not getting deep into the dungeon to get the Fabled Sword of the High-King, it's fighting your way out of it after the High-King and his hundred guardians arise from their slumber after you try to take that sword.

    \n I like the idea of skeletons used for surprise tactics in war:

    They can do things and get places that are very unexpected.

    Maybe they walk across the ocean floor, crossing the Bay and entering the besieged city at night through the Port.

    Or maybe they can be packed into a small space, without air or food, and emerge, attacking, three months later (like the Trojan Horse).

    Or maybe they can be thrown over the wall with a catapult, and are taken simply to be bones (launched over the walls to demoralize the defenders) until they reanimate a few hours later.

    Or maybe the enemy general creates huge walls of flame to deter your troops from advancing... but the skeletons walk right through them, attacking you when you least expect it, their bones glowing with embers, their weapons smoldering with heat.

    (Do they need to see? They have no eyes, after all. You could fill a room (or a castle) with smoke, and send them in to slaughter the blinded (and choking) men and women trapped inside.)

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarWhat are other factors that would make skeleton-smashing a difficult, interesting choice?
    \nEach bone is covered with intricate carvings. Intact they're worth quite a bit to the right collectors, smashed they're worthless dust.

    The centers of the bones, where the marrow would be in a living skeleton, have been surrounded in necromantic energy for as long as the skeleton has been animated. Who knows what's been incubating in there that could be unleashed if you crack the bones?

    These skeletons are from a group of people whose religion dictates that defiling a body after death jeopardizes the immortal soul of the person. Permanently harming the skeletons would count as defilement. An adventurer who defiles a corpse would surely earn the enmity of the person's family, etc.

    Depending on the magical physics of the world, animated skeletons could be very light compared to a person, and encountered in places where it's hard to get the leverage/hard surfaces to smash them (e.g. rope bridges, ship rigging, etc.).

    The bones for these skeletons were leftovers from a magical experiment that was meant to replace the bones of powerful people/creatures with something more durable, but the souls came along with the extracted bones, animating them. If the skeletons are smashed the souls will be able to return to the original enhanced creature, waking it up.

    \n In a fantasy novel I was working on that had several necromancer characters I decided that the match between the soul and corpse be relevant to the kind of undead you could create. So if you bound the matching soul you could create a revenant who was smart and fast. If you bound some random human soul to a human body it would be a traditional zombie, slow and lumbering. Or you could just start with a skeleton and bind an easier-to-bind animal spirit (there's less stuff to animate without the flesh on the corpse so you don't need as good a match). As a result, the skeletons would be orderly and regimented when necromancers were actively directing their actions but savage and beastlike if left to their own devices.

    \n Skeletons are simply a set of bones that hold a curse...a curse that is triggered upon proximity to a living being. Once activated, the skeleton feeds on the energies of that person's connection to a particular friend or family member. As the skeleton is energized, the person's connection, no matter how far away they are, becomes tired and sick. Doctors will be mystified as to what is causing the condition if it persists, and no cure will help them. They will eventually die if the skeleton remains animated...at which point the skeleton, too, will "die" as the there will be no energy left to feed.

    Defeating the skeleton within a short amount of time will, of course, let the connection regain his or her health. If their sickness has gone on for any length of time, the medical practicioners will declare the recovery a miracle. However, a price is still to be paid, as defeating the skeleton also breaks the connection; what was once a happy, energetic relationship will soon be found to be dull and uninteresting. Marriages will simply dissolve, and friends will quietly part.

    If the hero does not make contact with his connection for a long while after the skeleton is destroyed, the memories of each other will simply fade away, leaving a "hole in the soul," a feeling as if there was something they lost, but they can never truly remember what it was.

    Posted By: Double King
    From a Victorian era, skeletons get to represent death and the horror of what lies beneath.
    \nI definitely agree with your points Double King, but this goes back war before the Victorian Era. The use of skulls and skeletons as a reminder of death probably goes back to the birth of symbolism, but it really hit its stride during the Middle Ages and the Black Plague.

    I think its depictions of the Memento Mori and Danse Macabre that really get at what is scary about skeletons by either juxtaposing them with mundane life (paintings of skeletons and skulls next to domestic objects) or showing the skeletons indulging in behaviors that we associate with the enjoyment of life (dancing, drinking, feasting, etc.) It's the contrast between death and life that really unsettles us. Skeletons that refuse to be simply dead, that refuse to give up their humanity and act like monsters, those guys are scary.

    I think the RPG that I've played that best gets this is Bret Gillan's Cold Soldier, although it doesn't deal strictly with skeletons.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI like the idea of personalizing them.
    \nI love all the comments here, but these ones fit best with an idea I've had while reading this inspiring thread.

    Skeletons constantly recall the happiest (or most memorably emotional) moments of their lives, and while no living creature is around they can indulge in this fantasy. Once adventurers arrive in all their fleshy glory, the skeletons' lack of life becomes clear, the dream is shattered and the skeletons blame the living intruders. Skeletons attack with their hands and teeth, even if using weapons they try to grab you.

    Upon being touched by a skeleton, their memories flood into you: playing with their children in a park; marrying their wife; the satisfaction of a hard day's labour and drinks with friends at the tavern. Their touch also saps the flesh from your bones; it crawls off your bones and onto the skeleton, restoring it to a semblance of life but in your guise (or a strange mongrel mix of many people it has sapped). It's disguise is nowhere near perfect. It holds up as human from a distance, but close inspection reveals some irregularities, and it begins falling apart within hours or days. They can't talk, but make rough, throaty noises and their eyes are unfocused or work independently. They can look sort of normal but always kinda wrong or creepy. Like they're wearing an Edgar suit.

    If the skeleton saps enough flesh to become whole again it seeks out living people and tries to recreate its memories. It approaches children in a park; it crashes a wedding, believing itself to be the groom; it pulls up a chair at a tavern and joins the living there, pouring a drink down its disconnected throat. In close proximity it doesn't take long for others to realise something is wrong. In defence or in anger at the spoiled recreations of its memories, it attacks, drains more flesh and moves on to find another place where it can try again to relive its memories. Hopefully those adventurers it attacked but didn't kill will use the flashes of its memories they saw to work out where it would go and track it down.

    \n For interesting encounters, I think Mockingbard's nicely covered "here's why you don't just kill them". I think Dan M.'s ideas above would cover some additional bases, for when memory-sharing is easy or unnecessary: every bone is valuable, every act of violence is desecration.

    For scary encounters, only the "your flesh crawls onto them" part is doing it for me. And for that to work, I need (a) the skeleton to have a decent chance to actually touch me, even if I've got a staff or other long weapon, thus they probably need to be fast or ignore impact; and (b) my flesh to stay messed up somehow even if the skeleton is defeated and dime-a-dozen healing magic is used on me.

    I think the thing that makes the skeletons initially scary in that Argonauts clip is the fact that the heroes retreat from them. And then they get REALLY scary when they actually KILL a guy. I don't think any amount of eyeballs or glowing or moaning can compare to actual CONSEQUENCES.

    If the flesh crawl isn't good enough by itself, maybe:
    - each time you're touched you experience a horrifying flash of some thing (object, creature, or situation), and now you have a permanent phobia of that thing, and must Act Under Fire to deal with it
    - they curse you so you very slowly become skeletal (plenty of time to reverse it, but until you do, eww); or Graham's idea
    - they become walking voodoo dolls of every person (or the last person) they've touched/cursed/drained/killed, so harming them harms that person or people
    - if they grab you or hit you with a certain move, they gain the ability to impersonate you, and the desire to go live as you (make a beeline for your home?)
    - they can go from peaceful to violent instantly due to something the players probably won't anticipate, like talking or exhaling or using proper names

      \n Skeletons are infected, and contagious. The plague fills their bones; crack one, and the plague spills forth. If they touch you, you stand risk of infection. If they tear into you with their fingers, then you are almost certainly infected.
      \n
      \nWhen infected, plague quickly lodges in your own bones. You feel hotter and hotter, all over, with no clear source. Your only chance to stay cool at first is to take off your clothes, but soon that's not enough. You can sit in cold water, but in no time it feels like it's boiling (and maybe it actually is). Snow and ice can't soothe the heat. Aching for cool, you begin to tear at your own flesh. With each bloody handful, you feel a moment's release, so you keep tearing. You tear and tear until there's nothing left of you but your skeleton, with the fiery plague still burning inside. The only source of comfort is to tear into others, to pour the heat from yourself into another body. But it never ends. Even when they smash you up, and you are little more than broken pieces of oozing bone, the heat never ends.

      \n Yes, simple kidnap and sacrifice would be the easiest way to get more troops for your typical Necromancer in a world where there wre anti-skeleton burial measures in place.

Think isolated woodcutters living with illicit skeletons of their dead wives, grieving mothers futily suckling slowly-grasping skeletons of their dead babies. He needs them to go kill that dragon, he OWES them that, but you're interfering with the mission.

      The other angle I always liked was the idea that they might be entirely constructed. They're a mish-mash of bones from different bodies, all wired-up and pinned at the joints, maybe a few animal bones thrown in when needed, nothing like clean ranks of uniformed soldiers. They move like the kludged-together marionettes they are, and the life force that animates them is the rent fragments of souls in all those bones raging against each other, a dozen bodies all rejecting each others' grafts. They are ANGRY, and in pain that will never go away, and your busting open the tomb just gave them something to vent it on.
