    \n Paul, my best advice for AW is just to do what it says in the book. That's actually pretty different than what happens when you play D&D or some other games, right, where you -- or I at least -- generally start by assuming I know more about good roleplaying than the book does and look for the book to build on what I already know how to do. For AW, the book very clearly tells you how to run it, what to do when you don't know what to do (make an MC move!), and the rest.

    In every instance where someone has had a problem running AW (or where I've had a problem understanding AW), it generally seems to revolve around people not doing or not reading carefully the stuff that's actually in the book. This isn't to say that there couldn't be best practices that aren't encoded clearly in the book -- theoretically that could be the case -- but AW has encoded its best practices in the text better than most RPGs. The principles, GM moves, list of things to do, etc. They are all gold.

    \n Paul,

    Here's some things I've learned from my first (and still ongoing) Apocalypse World campaign. These are all in the rules but are parts tha I tripped over. I hope to run more campaigns to totally utilize what I've picked up:

    - Ask more questions. The book tells you to ask questions and give the players room to answer. I wish I'd done this more. GMing impulses and traditional authority setups will leave people looking to you to fill in setting details, and your impulse will be to do that. Look for more opportunities to have the players fill in those gaps rather than doing it yourself. Do this as much as possible.

    - Make all NPCs Threats. After the first game you'll have a bunch of NPCs. They all need to be Threats. Do not handwave this. If you're like, "Well, they don't seem to belong to any of these Threat types," either make them a member of a Warlord's cast and make the Warlord the Threat, make them a Brute and figure out a larger group that the NPC belongs to that's a Threat. After every game when an NPC is introduced, go through after the game and make sure they are Threats.

    - Wait. Make everything a Threat. Think about all aspects of your world as threats. The town the PCs live in? Landscape threat. That religious belief that came up in play? That's an Affliction - Delusion. The strange characters that populate the Psychic Maelstrom? Threat. The Psychic Maelstrom? That's a Threat too.

    - Fill out the world with your Fronts. Different people have different numbers of Fronts that work for them. I generally have three - one that's fully manifesting, one that's being hinted at, and one in the back of the fridge. In each Front you're supposed to have four Threats. To begin with you'll maybe only be able to stick one or two existing Threats in there, so make sure to fill it out with two or three Threats that you've made up. Spin up a Warlord nearby, a Grotesque lurking in the town tht nobody realizes, etc. Once you've been playing for a bit though and if you follow the last two steps, you'll have more than enough Threats. Still, leave maybe one spot in each Front open to introduce something new.

    \n The world is full of threats to the players. It's not manipulative to say, "Okay, now you have to deal with THIS shit." A dust storm, flocks of blood drinking birds, slavers, a cannibal - all of those have reasons to get all up in the PCs shit and all you have to do is a make a move with them to cause problems, and usually "announce future badness" or "announce offscreen badness" are the best way to start.

    So really that's exactly how you handle it, or you can make another move, but you don't really do it any other way. Maybe make a custom move or something.

    If they're not interested in it, then that's fine. Advance it along the countdown timer. Eventually it'll reach its full expression and they have to deal with that. Usually, though, the full expression is something they shouldn't want to see happen. And don't be nervous about not being ready. Just have some Fronts hanging around with Threats ready to go nuts. The moves will tell you what to do.

    \n Another thing is that Threats don't always manifest as confrontations. A Threat can be a disease causing people to slowly deteriorate, or a dangerous cult slowly growing, or people eating way too much and using up all the food, etc.

    So there's a lot going on, and usually a lot for players to choose to deal with depending on interest/urgency. However, when they have shit they want to do and are like I GO THERE, I DO THIS, etc. then roll with it. You don't always have to be slamming them with Threats.

      \n I like to talk about the moves after the game, mostly because I'm so excited and thrilled at how much fun it makes MCing the game, and I want to share some of the nuts and bolts on how the fiction was shaped and created. But I try not to talk about it during the game, because it takes everyone out of the moment. The game rules on moves say "Misdirect" and "Never speak it's name", and I think this is important, because it avoids the twitchy vibe that Paul is talking about.

      In terms of success and failure, Burning Wheel and Apocalypse world play in a similar fashion. Burning Wheel has the concept of "two directions" for the fiction to proceed when rolling, whether you pass the roll or fail the roll. In AW you have three directions for fiction: pass, pass with some failure, and failure. In AW you actually have a list of moves to help you figure out what happens after you roll, but, similar to Burning Wheel, you need to make it logical from the fiction.

      Misdirect is all about keeping a move loyal to the game fiction, and keeping it to events happening in game, and "never speak it's name" keeps the fiction flowing smoothly. I look at it as cause and effect: a player does something, and this thing happens, so they do something in response, and then that other thing happens. The cause and effect, staying to the fiction in the game, is really important, and makes for a much smoother game experience.
