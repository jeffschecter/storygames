\n My girlfriend thought of the worst dice mechanic ever.

You roll a D20 and D%, together.

The result of the D% is how much of the D20 roll you get.

So thus, if you roll a d20 (8), and a d% (29). Your result would be 2.32.

    Posted By: shreyasTwo operations: Roll all the dice you have. Find the product.
    \nI will see your mechanic and raise.
    Roll all the dice you have. Find the geometric mean result (find the product and then take the Nth root, where N is equal to the number of dice you rolled).

    Or better yet, multiply each result by the result of a fudge die (to generate positive and negative results) and then find the root mean square(square each number, add them all together, multiply by 1/N where N = the number of dice rolled, and then take the square root of that). That is your result.

    In fact, you could generalize this approach and say that more mathematical complexity makes any mechanic worse. Since there is no upper bound to the amount of mathematical complexity of a mechanic there can be no such thing as a "worst" die mechanic. You can always create a worse mechanic by taking some nasty logarithm of the previous mechanic.

    \n Also, now that I'm at it, the truly intriguing probability space of Justin's girlfriend's dice (divide by 100 to get the proper results). Anybody trying to figure out a game that utilizes this dice distribution will do well to note that the probabilities of this deceptively simple dicing operation are apparently jumbled by factorization - results that can occur with several factors in the multiplication are drastically more likely than eg. prime numbers. Don't make the mistake of assuming that it's more likely to get 0.13 than 0.12 from these dice.

    \nJunkyard

    The world is a junkyard, and you are a mechanic/tinkerer/savvyhead/salvager trying to build a beautiful scrappunk fortress and defend it from the Beetle Legions.

    When you happen upon gizmos or vehicles or contraptions or damaged structures, you can choose to work on them. Flip over a card from the Junk Deck, and that's what junk you find. Roll a d20. That's how much Fortress Defense they'll have if repaired. Roll a d%. That's their current level of repair.

    Now there's a fun, skill-dependent, trick-building card mini-game. You're trying to repair the contraption. At any point you can abandon the contraption and move onto a different one. If you do, flip a new card from the Junk Deck, roll the d20 + d% again, and begin a new repair job.

    There's a timed component as well, and suddenly you hear, "Beetle Invasion in 10... 9... 8..." When it gets to 0, if your contraption isn't fully repaired, you've got a choice: Stick it on your fortress as is, or abandon it. Either way, the beetles now attack! The other player flips 3 cards from the Beetle Deck. They attack your fortress and you have a few defense powered tied into certain contraptions.

    Fire Beetles and Beetle Bombers both have the ability to score Catastrophic Damage! against contraptions that were never fully repaired. Other beetle types have the ability to score Catastrophic Damage! against specific contraption types - Zap Beetles can blow up electronics, Thickshells can rebound bullets and blasts, etc.

    Posted By: Adam DrayKeep-worst and keep-best of N dice tend to ramp very quickly (exponentially) towards the ends. If you're dealing with small numbers of dice and large die-sizes (2d20, 3d12), it's less noticeable. Roll 10d6, though, and I'd put money on your best die being a 6.
    \nYou can fix this by starting to count how many sixes there are, if you want to. Count two sixes as a "seven", three as an "eight" and so on. This way adding more dice still gives you a bit of return, and it doesn't add to the handling time that much.

    \n This has already been done, and much better. I'm sure you all remember Metascape II:\n
    The main die system is perhaps the most advanced ever made. So what? Well, it allows any roll to have a chance of success or failure. So, the proverbial dragon can be slain in one blow. Or, the mighty warrior may fail to slay a mouse. Roll results range from 1 to infinity, though the largest recorded was several million (not bad given a base roll of about 20)! This is a true, open ended die system.
    \nThe system used a special "d16" marked "c, t, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 4, 4, 8, 16". I don't remember much about how it worked except that the "T" stood for "TREMENDOUS".

    (There was also reportedly a table in the book which allowed you to generate the same results with a d20... although I have never seen the thing, and suspect it may be mythical.)

    The game was lovingly described as the "diceless Die system".

    (If you wonder what that means, there was some explanation in their pitch for the game:\n
    Diceless GM: Yea yea, we have all heard these empty promises before. The GM may easily choose to not use the die as a decision aide. Yes, the game is diceless for the GM. We have played for about 15 years without the GM rolling a single die other than mentioned above.

    \n

    Choose a mapping from the colors to the following meanings:

    \n
    * Power\n* Grace\n* Truth\n* Wisdom\n* Beauty\n

    The base value is "Power".

    \n

    The result of "Grace + 4" is how many dice you may reroll (normally, 0 to 8 dice, unless Wisdom has added new Grace dice).

    \n

    If the "Truth" value is negative, invert the results of all the other dice.

    \n

    The absolute value of "Wisdom" specifies how many dice you can change the color of (e.g. "-3" means you can interpret 3 other dice as another color.)

    \n

    \n

    You may apply the operations in any order.

    \n
    \n

    The value of the highest possible value is left as an exercise for the reader.

    Posted By: SavageHominidYou roll a D20 and D%, together.

    The result of the D% is how much of the D20 roll you get.
    . . .
    What would you do with this?
    The D20 is your spellcasting roll, to which you add your magic skill. The D% is the Power of the demon summoned by your spell.

    The more powerful the demon, the more of your own spell manifests in the world. Multiply the rolls to find your spell success level on the scale of 1 (not at all) to 30 (exactly what you wanted the spell to do). Fractions don't matter.

    You have now pissed off a demon. Write down the D% number you rolled. If you ever roll that number again, you accidentally invoke that demon, and it comes to kill you. Its Power is its number of hit points (or the amount of damage it does, or some other combat quantity).
