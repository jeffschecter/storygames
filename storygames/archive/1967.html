Unstructured Play

When I was in sixth grade, the school placed half a dozen tree trunk stumps on the school yard. They were wide enough to stand on, to various degrees, and about two feet high. I assume they were meant to be used as stools.

In no time, my friends and I were hopping from one to the other, and soon we developed a game: weâ€™d count to three and all jump to the next one, last man standing sort of wins. Soon people figured out that you could lean back and therefore lift the stump onto its edge, then move back and forth, sideways, to basically walk the stump around. Other people kicked back when they jumped to make it harder for the next person to land, or even to kick the stump over. People didnâ€™t always count, or didnâ€™t always jump, but we had really no rule in place to disqualify anyone.

Over the next few weeks, we had a ton of fun playing that way. We played every day, in almost every recess. However, the game sooner or later always went awry when someone moved their stump to the other edge of the yard, or kicked too many over, or did something else that frustrated the other players too much to continue playing. It turns out that we were playing by different rules, and in fact, we were playing different games: some of the kids aimed to be the last one standing. Others wanted to show how far they could jump. Yet others just tried to screw with whoever was around. And finally, there were players who didnâ€™t know what was going on, but enjoyed the yelling and shouting and people falling over; passive participants and voyeurs, if you want.

After one of our fights over the game, when we accused each other of cheating or not playing right, a teacher ordered us to sit down, agree on a set of rules, hammer out what exactly the game was, and then all play by those rules.

We never played again.

    Parallel play. As long as my idea of what the game is and what the rules are doesn't actively interfere with yours, we can play together. Once they start to interfere, a single vision needs to emerge, players need to retreat into parallel paths again, or play becomes not-fun and stops. My (offensive to same, apparently) feeling is that a large amount of roleplaying (indeed, a large amount of social interaction in general) is fundamentally parallel, toy-play, without a significant collaborative element or functional conflict resolution method. People don't cooperate well at anything without some kind of rules. Kids are better at it because they haven't formed firm identities yet.

    One (male) person would walk up to you and cradle your face in their hands. And then, just when you thought they were being amazingly tender and sweet (or just fucking weird), they would give you a wicked smirk and yell "Nutface!"

    The implication was that they had just scratched themselves (under their clothing) and, consequently, had just rubbed fresh ball sweat all over your face. Whether they actually did this or not was never really clear and was, in fact, kinda immaterial to the whole game. It was more the idea of how disgusting that would be. That's what drove play.
