    \n The 800 lb gorilla is Wizards of the Coast. In most cases, it's the smaller companies and products which go open rather than the big ones, so the Open Game License and System Reference Document are extraordinary in that sense. I suspect that open licences give the greatest competitive advantage to smaller companies, but the rising tide of the d20 boom did seem to lift Wizards' boat along with everyone else's.

    Of course, they rejected an open licence for 4E. That strongly suggests that they think the OGL was a bad idea. Now that Pathfinder is such a powerful competitor, I wonder if they're even more disappointed by the OGL.

    I do wonder what would have happened if 4E was open. I suspect that more publishers would have chosen 4E over 3E and Pathfinder would have lost a lot of its third party support. Necromancer Games is an obvious example of a very enthusiastic publisher who ended up rejecting 4E altogether.

    Other Games
    Eclipse Phase was released under a semi-open licence, and its creators are very happy about that.

    Anima Prime was released under an open licence as it was published; parts of Beast Hunters were retrospectively released under an open licence. Hopefully Christian Griffen will chime in with his experiences there.

    Time & Temp is under an open licence. I know nothing about it's sales, but it's not the first game Epidiah has released under an open licence -- so I assume he was happy with the commercial success of the previous games.

    I know nothing about the commercial success of indie games vs Monsters (open), Wushu/Wushu Open (open), The Dreaming Crucible (open) or CRN Games (Clinton Nixon's games; some are open) -- but depending on how interested you are, those are all leads you could follow up.

    Ransom Model Games
    These games/supplements are only released after being funded -- in some cases, they're only written after being funded. Obviously that's a model where an open/semi-open licence is a lot less risky, but I think they're worth discussing.

    4C System was released into the public domain.

    REIGN supplements have been released under a semi-open licence, and there have been over thirteen of those -- so that licence has presumably benefited Greg Stolze (but not enough for him to release the original game).

    'Third Party' Stuff
    A big chunk of the open gaming market is made up of retroclones, d20-based games and FATE games. These are by third party publishers, so that's all I'll say about them.

    \n My understanding is that Wizards of the Coast's D&D 3E sales benefited due to the OGL. But it hurt Wizards of the Coast's financially outside of D&D 3E.

    The OGL allowed third party companies to release brand new games based on the OGL that had no connection back to D&D 3E.

    Companies were able to release OGL Superhero and Modern RPGs before Wizards of the Coast did. I don't know the numbers but the feeling at Wizards by some was that Mutants & Masterminds and Spycraft hurt Wizards financially as they had plans for expansion beyond the fantasy genre (and did expand in the cases of Modern and Future D20).

    But an even bigger impact that Wizards probably never expected is that the OGL allowed companies to make money off of their earlier editions when D&D released new editions. Pathfinder has allowed D&D 3E to stay alive in the wake of D&D 4E and has hurt Wizards dearly. To a lesser extent, the OGL allowed small publishers to release clones of Original D&D after Wizards stopped selling PDFs of older versions of the game.

    Now the OGL wasn't the only factor. Mutants & Masterminds and Spycraft were excellent games. D&D 4E was a large departure from what existing fans were used to or wanted. And there were easily several other dozen business factors that shook things up.

    That all said, Wizards is likely the biggest and most extreme example. I'd love to hear how the OGL has affected games like FATE.

    \n It's also interesting, at least to me, that the easiest way for a publisher to be hurt (rather than helped) by opening up their content is if they aren't prepared to compete successfully with or cooperate with other publishers who want to build on their material.

    Can you imagine the superhero game that Wizards might have eventually put out? Can you imagine, in your wildest dreams, that it would be better than what Steve Kenson put together? I can't. Green Ronin didn't just release some half-assed d20 supers game, they released one WAY BETTER than what WOTC could have done. And that's way more of a threat than "d20 Cybernetics Tome" or some of the other releases.

    Really, the 4E GSL doesn't restrict publishers completely, but it does enough to make it impossible for third-parties to compete successfully with WOTC at their own game. I think if the GSL was more open, you'd have a lot more indie game designers (since a lot of us were really excited about the direction that 4E took, design-wise) coming out with content and hacks for it that would have fixed a lot of its problems. And that could have helped WOTC, or it could have further split up the market if the hacks were stand-alone and functioned a lot better than straight-up 4E (which wouldn't be all that hard).

    So I think making decisions about opening up content partially depends on looking at your publishing plans and deciding whether having third-parties make their own products is going to make some of your plans irrelevant or give people the tools necessary to compete successfully with you. And maybe you don't even care about that if what you really want is there to be high quality products for you game, whoever's making them.

    \n Agreed that the D20 STL (System Trademark License) benefited D&D sales more than the OGL. But there is no STL without the Open Gaming Content which was governed by the SRD (System Reference Documents). The STL allowed 3rd party companies to brand their content with a D20 trademark as long as they followed the STL guidelines (requiring the use of the D&D Player's Handbook) and restricted what material they referenced or used to Open Game Content defined by the SRD. The OGL was a separate license that drew on the same Open Gaming Content but did not require the PHB at the cost of not having access to the D20 trademark. There was also OGL content that benefited D&D sales (I bought many OGL feat books that stopped using the STL because the OGL was easier) but STL content was by far the most impactful.

    There were a long line of decisions that hurt Wizards. From D&D 3.5, to pulling Dragon magazine, to discontinuing PDF sales, to the virtual tabletop being years late, to misleading customers, to terrible marketing, to abandoning popular editions of the game, to changing the focus of their main products, and alienating existing customers. But many companies make similar mistakes. It\u2019s the belief of many that although these mistakes lost customers, the existence of the OGL made the impact of these mistakes much more damaging\u2026 probably more damaging than the benefits of the OGL.

    Let me be clear\u2026 I'm a huge fan of the OGL. These aren't my personal opinions.

    \n Yeah, Anima Prime is completely open. Not just SRD like, but every word in the full game.

    It doesn't seem to hurt sales. It's sold around 70 copies in its first month (though this second one is notably slower; but there also aren't reviews or AP threads yet).

    So, it's a total win situation for me at this point. I'm definitely doing this again for future projects, if any.

    \n That's great to hear, Christian!

    I would be very reluctant to describe 'open by asking' games as open; requiring the author's permission goes against core principles of openness -- as does not being able to duplicate large chunks of the text. One of the reasons for open licences is to allow a project to be 'split' -- "We're not happy with the stuff you're writing for Apocalypse World, Vincent, so we're going to release Disaster World and market all our products as being compatible with Disaster World only". Obviously that's not possible if it needs Vincent's permission.

    On the other hand, I don't think using someone else's open work puts any kind of moral obligation on you to make it available free-of-charge in any way. In other words, I don't think Green Ronin's refusal to release an SRD or it's request for OGC M&M material to be taken down is in any way hypocritical or unreasonable. (To put it simply, there's no necessary connection between gratis [free of charge] and libre/open [you're free to use it however you want]).

    I should have explained myself when I called Eclipse Phase 'semi-open' -- 'open source' and 'open content' works can always be sold by anyone, so semi-open is my term for works that cannot be sold. Others in the movement just lump all semi-open works in with 'proprietary' works (all rights reserved).

    People have been talking about FATE. However, FATE is a 'third party' game in that it had to be open because Fudge (its progenitor) was open. My impression was that Simon would want us to talk about whether Fudge benefited from the open licence, not whether FATE did. Was I wrong, Simon?\n

    \n After 30 years as a gamer and about 20 or so as a retailer, I can honestly say that I have literally never sold a game based on wether it had an open lisence or not, and certainly never based my purchasing decision on same. Indirectly, the presence of a body of work by other publishers might possibly be one of a long list of selling points, but honestly, if theres one thing that the d20 glut and even the current Pathfinder glut has taught me (and every other gamer I know), it is to be very cautious of any third party material for any system, because it's probably mediocre.

    At the end of the day, especially for Indie games that are only going to sell a few thousand copies, this is a question that is nearly entirely up to personal preference. I can't imagine more than a few dozen designers or loopy open source pursists who will refuse to buy a good game solely because the author wants to retain some control over their creation, or who will buy a game they otherwise don't care about solely because it is open source. My honest belief is that this is a decision that will have no material effect on sales for anyone. There is a lot of WotC talk, but figuring out what effect the OGL had on their sales involves a huge counterfactual exercise that is pure speculation. If you ask Ryan Dancey about the OGL, he will tout its huge success in making DnD the 'default operating system' for gaming for nearly a decade, retailers will tell you it did as much harm as good, Paizo will be cagey about the fact that they were basically handed their company and success on a platter by WotC, and 4e DnD sells just fine with no OGL, just not to people who were already playing 3.5, but to NEW players (and the board games have been a hit, too), the vast majority of whom just want to play DnD and do not care about whether it is open or closed or creative commons or whatever.

    Make your game. Make it good. Then do whatever you want as far as how open it is, because that's mainly a political decision and not a sales and marketing decision.

    Posted By: sageThe thing with "open" licenses where you can use the text is: who does that? If someone actually made the game that included the entire d20 SRD, word-for-word, they'd get called out for it I'd imagine. So what does the open gaming license really do, since mechanics aren't protected?
    \nIt basically functions as a statement that says, "We won't sue you, honest." It's permission to use mechanics, the supporting structures and particular expressions free from worry of legal action. Just because mechanics aren't protected doesn't mean a company will refrain from getting litigious over them, effectively squashing anybody that doesn't have the money to mount a defense. So from that perspective, a large company like WotC/Hasbro putting the game under an OGL is pretty significant.

    Posted By: sageWhich, yes, gets into the whole murky area of creative rights and game design, copyright, patent, etc.
    \nAs far as procedural mechanics in a game go, this area is not that murky: game mechanics cannot be copyrighted.

    Other stuff gets murkier. The actual text detailing those mechanics can be copyrighted. Game mechanics can be patented if the patent office is feeling particularly stupid that day (hint: they are). The mechanics themselves cannot be trademarked, but the "product identity" attached to them can.

    And, lastly, someone violating your intellectual property rights may actually be good for your intellectual property.

    EDIT: Oh, one other thing. All this stuff really only matters between parties that have roughly the same pocket depth. Typically, if one side is much bigger than the other, they win, no matter what the law actually says, as they can force the smaller guy to spend enough in legal fees to render the prize moot.

    To my knowledge, no open source gaming licence has ever been defended in open court.

    \n
    \nI think I wouldn't make the decision to go open content or not from a sales perspective. As Jim Crocker noted above, as a retailer, he doesn't believe he's sold any games on the basis of them being open content. What matters for sales, I think, is "simple" -- a quality product, and gameplay and other experiences around the game that create good gaming stories that gamers feel a need to tell each other.
    \n
    \nIt is possible that for some Fate players, the openness of the system is one of those other experiences. Even though it's simply true that every system there is is open to a little or a lot of homebrew tinkering for your own table of play, explicit permission to tinker still has an effect on people. When they get such permission, the potential exists that they're also getting an added sense of ownership of the game, a personal relationship with it that gets a stamp of validity. That's not nothin'. I don't know if it adds sales potential for the product itself, but I would surmise it certainly adds a greater depth of satisfaction with the product.
    \n
    \nThat said, I'll get specific about Evil Hat for a moment here. Here's what Fate's release schedule would've looked like without an open commercial license behind it:
    \n
    \n2003: Fate 2
    \n2006: Spirit of the Century
    \n2010: Dresden Files RPG
    \n
    \nAnd ... done. That's it. Instead, we got (just to go by the RPG.net game index):
    \n
    \n2003: Fate 2
    \n2006: Spirit of the Century
    \n2008: Starblazer Adventures
    \n2009: Diaspora
    \n2009: Awesome Adventures
    \n2009: Free Fate
    \n2010: Dresden Files RPG
    \n2010: Legends of Anglerre
    \n2010: Limitless Horizons
    \n2010: Strands of Fate
    \n2011: Bulldogs! (coming this summer)
    \n
    \nThis list is likely incomplete (and definitely doesn't include Fate influenced non-Fate games like ICONS, Houses, Chronica, etc). So from the perspective of meeting demand that exceeded Evil Hat's ability to produce (I'm reasonably confident this has happened), the OGL worked, and since Fate is not at the core of the most popular/highest name recognition RPG product in the world, the "pile-on" volume has stayed well below the d20 glut phenomenon of yesteryear.
    \n
    \nAgain, commercially, the most benefit I can say Evil Hat *might* have gotten from this is "sustained interest" and/or an increased sense of Fate as a "real system" that's getting "support". (I put quotes around those simply to highlight my uncertainty about their value.) Would the DFRPG have gotten fewer sales if there'd been zero Fate activity between 2006 and 2010? Maybe? Maybe not?
    \n
    \nDownsides: Even though we inherited our system's open license stance from its predecessors, I don't regret the fact of it. But I have at times gotten to feeling sick of it -- see my December post for that: http://www.deadlyfredly.com/2010/12/confessional/ -- simply because there's been the occasional offshoot effort that either (more charitably) doesn't match my own tastes for what's to be done with the system, or (less charitably) which I think is adding nothing new or is missing the point.
    \n
    \nOpen licensing is a rough stance to take if you feel like your vision needs to be preserved. No matter how much you bake it into your system, your vision, your vibe, will get compromised when it gets munged by third-party hands. Sometimes that's for the better (Diaspora caught my attention because it was *not* the Fate I knew but was doing some new and different and in places better things), and sometimes it's not, and those "not" times may weigh on you a bit.
    \n
    \nWhen it comes right down to it, I think it's about figuring out what compromise you want to strike between control and community. I like elements of both, so Evil Hat has often tried to sit in the middle of that compromise continuum, whether it's been via an open license or not. Too much control and you end up with fans who feel shut out, I think, which leads to waning interest. Too much community, and it gets difficult for newcomers to figure out what's wheat and what's chaff in what's available.
    \n
    \nThere's no one right answer.
