\n Seems like there's nothing but love for Fiasco here. My IndieMN group played it twice over the past three weeks. It was a bumpy ride. I enjoyed it, for sure. But I'm not sure how much the other players did and there was a lot of not really knowing what to do in the moment. I think all the people I played those two games with read here, so maybe they'll chime in too.

"OK, so what kind of scene do we come up with?"

"OK, are we supposed to set stakes first or define the central challenge first or just start narrating and see where it goes?"

"Are we describing this third-person or acting it out?"

"So wait, he's going to resolve and he wants to collect white dice; so I want to direct the scene so that there'll be cost involved...uh...is there a way to do that?"

"What exactly is the role of these damned valuable mice in the story?!!?" ;-)

And our process of answering these questions was usually flipping to the right part of the book and reading a quick paragraph that sorta feels like it thinks it's answering but then we still didn't know what to do. Or it's like, well, each group will find their own preferred way. I don't recall reading anything less than stunning praise for the game and the play that it engenders and I'm basically wondering what's wrong with us? Following the procedures, we set up great dysfunctional situation, but the what next part was rocky.

So, anyone else have these kinds of issues? Any suggestions for resolving them?

    "OK, so what kind of scene do we come up with?"
    \nAsk yourself, what does that character want? Why can't they have it? (Actually this could answer most of the "what do we do now?" questions...)

    The relationships and needs and whatnot should practically scream out what scenes to have. If they don't, well, it's possible to have an off combination every now and again, and at least one playset suggests firewalling off some options. And it might be worth trying one of the fast setups. \n
    "OK, are we supposed to set stakes first or define the central challenge first or just start narrating and see where it goes?"
    \nNarrate and see where it goes. It's hard to go too wrong. And see above. \n
    "Are we describing this third-person or acting it out?"
    \nWhichever you want to do. Whichever way you'd do whatever other RPGs you play. \n
    "So wait, he's going to resolve and he wants to collect white dice; so I want to direct the scene so that there'll be cost involved...uh...is there a way to do that?"
    \nPush him hard. And see above. \n
    "What exactly is the role of these damned valuable mice in the story?!!?" ;-)
    \nNow I'm envisioning a playset that involves white mice and black mice, and the color of the die used to resolve the scene signifies the appearance of one of the mice.

    This is pretty much the heart of Fiasco: What does that character want, and why can't they have it? As the rabbi said, all else is commentary.

    \n (disclaimer, played fiasco once, may remember details poorly)

    I think fiasco does what it does very well, it's simply not doing what you need. Fiasco is worthy of praise but those praising it are people who do not need what it does not do.

    Was that confusing? There is sometimes a blindness in this community as to what skills are necessary to make this stuff work.

    I found playing it quite stressful in the ways Christopher spoke of. As for ways to make it work for your group I don't know, experience? drama class? watch more directors commentaries, TV tropes?.

    What fiasco does not do is cue specific and significant character action, it literally builds most everything else of the story, how it starts and ends and a bit of the middle. But it does not cue method or action. Games like burning wheel have skills and checks, AW has moves, these cue players into taking specific actions or employing methods. Fiasco not so much.

    If anything I would add a simple method descriptor to play sets. In addition to relationships, goals, locations add means/method.

    Do any more then that and i don't think it's fiasco anymore.

    \n I suspect that when there are problems with Fiasco it is because people are uncomfortable with the total freedom of making it up as you go along. Jason and I talked about this at Origins 2010 (when I foolishly didn't play in the Fiasco game he was running - Doh!) Once you do it you see it is easy - but until you do you don't know you are allowed to do it. Then once you know that you can you have to figure out what you want to do - and that kind of freedom is also hard because it means you have to own yourself. You make things happen.

    What I noticed when I played this Origins was that while I'm comfortable making shit up - I didn't know what limits the rules set on that. I found out there are no limits.

    The limit that does exist is the non-verbal negociation between the players not in a scene on whether or not the outcome was good or bad. I tended to jump in early and present an oppinion (by showing a colored die to the other players not in the scene) I found that some players were witholding of expressing an oppinion. That was more the conflict for me than the conflict in the story.

    Once thing I did find annoyingly interesting was how plans I layed out for my character were shot down because they were too logical - too reasonable. At my core I'm not a person with poor impulse control with high ambition and that reflected in my play (though I did try to get the girl by urging her to date my son, I did view my son as a threat to me by having a gun in our house that I had used in an old hit, and I then did sell my son out to the police cos someone had to go down and I didn't like the little shit anyway!)

    From a design point of view I really like the use of random event tables to customize each play session. I included that in Cthulhu Madness after seeing that.

    \n I was the nephew in the first game. The biggest issue I had with it was the cheese that I managed to pull. At the Tilt (iirc) the two factors were something goes missing (the weapons) and someone isn't really innocent (the nephew).

    (We pretty much decided that was what the Tilt meant before starting the second act - is that the right way to do it?)

    At the end of the first act, I had been given a single white die. The goofy rules thing is that I was able to choose to resolve in the second act, guaranteeing that I finished with 3 white dice, which is a pretty decent chance at a good outcome. The difficulty Chris is talking about is that it was hard to make ma want to take a black die. The first conflict they set it up so that I succeeded in killing my friend to keep the weapons secret (which I didn't really have a problem with, cause I figured hey - not so innocent). I forget the second conflict, but they were generally having trouble setting up a conflict in which I could possibly be motivated to take a black die.

    System-wise, I don't think there is a solution, but possibly we were making a mistake in advocating for our characters instead of making decisions based on artistic sensibilities. Since you can choose to resolve in the second round, by giving yourself 2 identical dice, you can weight yourself fairly significantly in favor of a good outcome. Chris managed to get a good outcome by picking black dice when he was resolving, but that was narratively speaking much more painful, because the other players could set up some horrible situation, and by picking a black die, he'd be making it worse for himself.

    \n I've had mostly good experiences with Fiasco, but there have been off nights. Usually, it's something about the players not gelling as a storytelling unit (if that makes sense).

    The worst experience was with a difficult playset (a Cthulhu-on-a-submarine one) at Games on Demand last year. We didn't really know how to tell a crime caper story with those constraints and it showed. It didn't help that the players didn't really know each other and some were new to Fiasco.

    Although Denys' comment about overthinking it, above, is harsh, I think there's some truth to it. If you import ideas from other games (e.g. stakes, driving scenes towards conflict), Fiasco can fail. It relies on you playing in a loose, freeform way, with the dice ending scenes for you.

    I also think that Jason's made a deliberate decision to leave decisions to individual groups. For example, the answer to your question about narrating vs acting it out is: do whatever your group likes to do. He could have spelt it out in much more detail, but didn't, and I think that's a valid choice. This, of course, makes the game more annoying for people that like their rules spelt out.

    Posted By: TylerTI think fiasco does what it does very well, it's simply not doing what you need. Fiasco is worthy of praise but those praising it are people who do not need what it does not do.

    Was that confusing? There is a some times a blindness in this community as to what skills are necessary to make this stuff work.

    I found playing it quite stressful in the ways Christopher spoke of.
    \nThis was my experience, as well.

    The fiction was quite interesting, and, if re-told, would make a great story or movie. Everyone was very much on-board with the "Coen Brothers movie" concept.

    However, the actual process of playing was very awkward, and it felt like the game did not make clear what we were supposed to do. I think some groups have this stuff figured, or it just happens naturally, but I played with a group of semi-strangers, and we were all unsure how to engage with each other.

    Imagine getting together a group of people and telling them: "Ok, now, you guys will all tell a story together, with no rehearsal or takebacks. It's about a hairdresser, a con man, and a young widower. Go!"

    What would happen? Often, people start talking all at the same time, all over each other, or everyone sits there quietly, or some combination of both. That's what happened to us.

    What we lacked entirely was any kind of tools to resolve conflicts between the players: for example, let's say Jane wants the action to focus on the hairdresser's seduction of the con man, but Jim keeps pushing the story towards the con man's engagement with the mafia, and it completely disinterested in the seduction plot. Who gets their way?

    The most common problem was, actually:

    Player One: "OK, I want you all to Establish a scene for me."
    The other players look at each other, and no one's sure who should talk and how much they're allowed to say. Sometimes you'd sit quietly and find everyone else was waiting for you to speak, other times you'd jump in with your ideas and you'd find that you'd tread all over someone's elses, and so frustrated another player.

    We were trying to establish some concept of how the social interaction was supposed to go at the table, and it felt like we were hashing it out from scratch, which can be pretty painful.

    I'd say Fiasco demands some pretty serious group freeforming skills.

    The Setup, Tilt, and Aftermath were like a breath of fresh air in comparison, and tons of fun. Given those constraints, the players all felt free to make stuff up to their hearts' content, and a great time was had. Within the scenes, however, it would sometimes flow and sometimes... it was like pulling teeth or knocking heads.

    \n Referring to Christopher's questions and comments in the original post...

    Fiasco is structured freeform, so I built in wide latitude for individual groups to reach their own conclusions. Some of the questions you raise revolve around the common points of contact where these decisions are usually made organically. Third person or acting it out? Up to you. What's up with the mice? Up to you. The only wrong answer to this sort of question is an answer that isn't right for your group.

    Other common decisions left to the table - what constitutes "positive" and "negative" outcomes? How does the initial series of Setup inputs gel into a coherent situation? How much does the group pre-plan and telegraph as opposed to discover in play? All up to you. I've seen these questions answered in different ways based on local play culture. I believe that not being explicitly told how this stuff is supposed to be makes Fiasco more flexible and approachable. Occasionally, when people look for specific answers that are not there, it also makes it more difficult.

    So that's my answer about procedural ambiguity - feature, not bug! I totally understand that this is not everyone's cup of tea.

    Some of your questions are answered in the rules - you do not set stakes, for example (See page 32). Two things I've observed that may or may not apply to your experience, Christopher - first, learning as you go is a recipe for a rocky session, rather than having someone who has thoroughly read the rules and can facilitate. Second, I've seen groups plug in "what we always do" well beyond the decisions prompted by Fiasco's deliberate ambiguity. This leads to all kinds of weirdness, like nine-player sessions and sessions with a GM.

    The circumstance you mention, where somebody wants to succeed and is Resolving in Act Two, is always challenging if you'd prefer that they fail. We talk about this in the Companion and give an example of one way to handle it. You and your partners in crime need to craft a situation they desperately want to fail, which isn't easy. I usually ask myself "how can I make getting what they really want also be unacceptably awful?" and go from there. If a Resolving player is truly fixated on success, this isn't a fight you can win. When I see this I just encourage a situation where they can have a spectacularly positive outcome, which is what they want at that point.

    In any case, take heart! I think it's been pointed out that a somewhat rocky first session isn't unusual. The nice thing is that once you've hammered out how your group is answering the ambiguous questions and internalized the procedures, further sessions typically become both easier and more fun.

    I hope this helps! Thanks for starting this thread, which is really informative and useful to me. I'm happy to talk about your specific issues or those of other posters, but I wanted to directly address yours first.
