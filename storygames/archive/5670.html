    \n In addition to Once Upon a Time, then, I can think of a couple more that are similar; they're more boardgame than RPG or CCG, but the mechanics are similar:

    Dungeoneer - Not collectible by any means, but each pack included everything needed for a GM and one to four players. It even had map cards, though I'd think those could be discarded if one wanted to focus specifically on quests and character.

    Descent - While another board game, get rid of the board!! Character creation, draw three cards from a deck or three (combat, magic and stealth). The Overlord has threat tokens and draws from the Overlord deck each turn. Spend threat tokens to generate traps and encounters. Items? Cards. Treasure? More cards. It was a lot of fun; even played a campaign using the boardgame. Discard the board and just use the cards is not only possible, but sounds kind of fun.

    \nThose weren't collectible, at least not by my definition. Collectible games are driven by random boosters, so you have to collect a lot in order to get the cards you want, and usually not all cards are commonly found. You could buy the pack of bunks and that was all you would ever need. Same with Once Upon A Time. You can simply by the game (and the expansion if you want to count that) and you'll have all the cards.

    It's an interesting idea though. Would you have different types of decks that you'd need to collect (character, action, quest) or would each card carry all those options? The latter seems particularly interesting, since players would have to choose if they want to use a card on their character or save it for an action. You could also have a mechanic where defeating quests gets you the card (although not permanently, or GMs would be hard to come by and they'd be reluctant to use good cards), basically working like experience and treasure.

    Posted By: twilightOr an RPG with a CCG mechanic? Which, I think,Once Upon a Timewas supposed to be. I could be wrong on that, though.
    \nThe Once Upon a Time I have from Atlas Games is more of a non-collectible storytelling card game. You tell a story, trying to get rid of your story element cards, and other players can grab control and take over telling the story by playing one of their story element cards if you mentioned something that fit the content of the card. (i.e., they have a "Wedding" card, and you say the prince and princess got married, they can take control). If you use up all your story element cards, you can play your one ending card and win the game.

    There's a Japanese RPG called Meikyuu Kingdom where the various crunchy bits (skills, items, monsters, etc.) are represented as cards that you can either photocopy out of the rulebook or buy (and thereby get in full color) for about 1500 yen. These aren't collectible, and they aren't strictly speaking necessary to the game.

    Back in the day I remember seeing a couple of attempts at making a Magic: The Gathering RPG, some of which involved the players actually using their Magic decks.

    \n Something that came close was Throwing Stones, which was a collectible dice game. You'd buy a tube of dice, which I think had six or so cubes in it, and choose four of those dice to build a starting character. The dice were specially printed with various symbols representing different abilities, and I think certain dice were considered representative of certain classes of abilities. Thus you could build a mixed character with two 'warrior' dice, I 'red magic' die and 1 'scout' die, for instance. Or just 4 ' warrior' dice to keep it simple. Obviously, you needed to own the right dice to manage certain character builds, which encouraged buying more tubes. Character growth was, I think, a simple matter of adding more dice to your character. Never played it, and though I thought the simple modularity of char-gen was quite clever, my impression was that keeping track of the dozens of different made-up symbols would have been a pain.
