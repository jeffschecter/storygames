\n So, the new game that I'm releasing at GenCon is called A Flower for Mara.In this game, you take on the role of various relatives of Mara, a woman who died suddenly, and play through the first year after her death. I have various design notes here. In addition, this is a rough trailer that I've put together, and this is a sample of play from a test run back in January.

This game is in the tradition of Jeepform LARPing, which means that there's lots of walking around and very little mechanical interaction within a scene. (Almost all the mechanics are dedicated to scene structuring.) It also has a Grief mechanic, which basically says that the only way for your character to overcome his grief is for you to share one of your own Griefs with the rest of the group. It's all very powerful in play, and it works wonderfully...

...

...and I have no idea how to sell this thing at GenCon. I'm at the Forge booth, which is demo-focused, which is very cool and all. However, I'm not persuaded that a 15-minute demo would actually capture what is nifty about this game. Even worse, I'd be afraid that it would make the game look silly. "So, it's time for a scene. Um, talk to each other for a while. Yeah, that's good."

The one idea that I have right now is to focus on a Family Gathering (as seen here and here). Essentially, the family gathers for a holiday of some sort, and the game cuts back and forth from the meal to soliloquies delivered by the characters to express their inner thoughts at the time. Working this inner/outer action could make for a workable demo....

So, anyways, I'm turning to the Internet for help and assistance. Thoughts?

Seth Ben-Ezra
Great Wolf

    \n Hey Seth, the whole idea of the demo seems a bit forced in this context - you can't really replicate the experience, so why try? Instead, going with your initial impulse, how about this:

    Give three participants each a character card with something they need to communicate, something they want to know or understand, and something they are reluctant to share.     \n Seth,

    I think it's important to keep in mind that delivering the experience of the game is NOT the point of a demo. The point of a demo is to showcase the features of a game such that the audience understand what the *potential* experience of the game is.

    The key feature of "A Flower for Mara", to me, is that it is NOT simply a scenario for free form role-playing (my less charitable self calls this socializing while pretending to be someone else; my mean spirited self calls this in-character yattering). It is very much a game, with rules and a structure and a point.

    So to me the demo should (a) show that a structure exists (b) shows how the structure limits and informs play such that the game is not just "free form" and (c) hints at without necessarily delivering the full experience.

    \n Okay, you're getting hung up on (a) showing people something and (b) showing them the mechanics.
    \n
    \nYou don't want to do either of those things. Educating them is only one possible tool in pursuit of the goal: The goal is to let people experience why they desperately, slaveringly, need to play this game, and then to say "So, can I ring you up?"
    \n
    \nIn practical terms: It looks to me like the fun of this game is going to be 90% anticipation ... knowing that you've got this Grief simmering inside of you, just waiting to bust loose, and savoring the feeling of restraining it. If people like that feeling then they'll want your game, if they don't then they won't. That sound about right?
    \n
    \nI'd think that what you need to demonstrate is character creation: Make them create their own wonderful, horrible, juicy, agonizing Griefs. Praise them for how wonderful said Griefs are (or, if they're not wonderful enough yet that you can honestly praise, push them to make it better ... they know it's lame too). Then make them invent the reasons they would keep those Griefs hidden. Then give a general sketch of a heart-rending scene that accents those Griefs.
    \n
    \nThen, when they can feel the anticipation building inside of them, but before they get to roleplay a word, you say "And that's the demo. I hope it gave you a feel for what the game is about."
    \n
    \nHow's that sound?
