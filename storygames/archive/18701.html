    \n I have a question...

    What's so difficult about playing the Ghost?

    The Ghost was my first Monsterhearts skin. The GM warned me that it might be "hard to play". We just had our season one finale yesterday and at no point did I find the Ghost difficult to play.

    Perhaps it's just my play style tends to be somewhat in sync with the Ghost's? Or it could be that I just had a lot of great characters to play off of and a great GM to put things down in front of my Ghost to react to when the other characters weren't there to bounce off.

    But I keep coming across the idea that the Ghost is difficult to play and I don't get why they'd be any more difficult to play than some of the other Skins. (I can understand that some of the Skins can be the easiest to play, since their goals are fairly self-defined. Just not why there's the idea that the Ghost is the/one of the hardest.)

    On-topic of the link: That is one of the saddest things I've read. (Though little or no relation to how my Ghost turned out. It's much closer to how our Hollow ended up.)

    \n The Ghost requires grokking, in my opinion. If you "get" the Ghost, it's no problem. But, like most of the skins, the Ghost revolves around an economy that you have to tease out and puzzle out from either their moves...or else by playing them until it makes sense. In particular, what's up with the "blamed" condition threw me off a bit, in trying to figure out how the Ghost made use of it. I think it's less obvious what to do with a Ghost. Other than "BOO!"

    I thought I witnessed a pretty decently-played Ghost, but not hugely so.

    \n Thanks for the thoughts! For context for how my Ghost played out...

    My Ghost was aware he'd been murdered (but not of who killed him or why) and the attack was directly tied to why his mother was in the hospital in a coma, but for four years, he didn't seem to feel the urge to find out more. It was a few years of a bit of denial/avoidance of any issues (while simultaneously being bitter over anything that reminded him he was dead). Including his crush on another boy in the class (the Fae). But as the game started, he was slowly forced to face his own death (including brief outbreaks of cold, frustrated rage when confronted with violence or put into his Darkest Self), though it took until the season finale for him to discover who killed him, what happened to his body, and how he could save his mother. And I think even that was because he was pretty well dragged over to see his own body and sat down to be told about what happened. He was too distracted by the crazy things going on to have much time to focus on himself, what with child services figuring out he's been living four years on his own and placing him in the foster home where his even more awkward new friend (the Hollow) lived, the Ananzi meddling, the murderous Queen looming, and the Fae getting entangled in it all, and his best friend (an NPC we eventually find out is a witch) being used as a pawn in the power plays between the Ananzi and the Queen. (Also, since his murderer, the Queen, was killed before my Ghost even knew that's who killed him, his only real revenge scene was his fruitless attack on the Queen's father because he knew just enough to know the Queen's father knew something.)

    My Ghost was so focused on the troubles of those he cared about, when the NPC witch sacrificed herself at the end of season one to give him new life, it made sense to change his Skin to the Calaca for season two. Though I did consider keeping him as the Ghost because his personality is still so strongly, "Avoid thinking about upsetting things (though never move on from them)."

    I think the nice thing about the Ghost's skin is that it's very versatile to the type of ghost story you want to tell. Revenge, confusion, denial, misdirected rage, lonely wandering, etc. I never felt that I was playing my Ghost "wrong" because there weren't really guardrails to stay between. Moving him over to the Calaca keeps bumping me up against, "But he's not really ready to let himself be happy because he knows he's a monster... But the Calaca's meant to be fairly cheerful, all-told. Is it 'okay' if my Calaca is more positive about there being things his friends can do to improve their own situations than his own life?"

    The more restrictive-seeming a skin seems to be, the less interested I am in playing it. Especially with players who've seen the skin played before, because I'm more conscious of the idea that I'm retreading the same material. (I know that at one character-creation session one of my groups specifically asked for the Vampire not to get played because they'd seen it so often that they felt the skin was threadbare.)
