\n The Boarddiagram and something that mcellis wrote when helping me to devise a FATE hack for my upcoming Earthmen on Mars campaign gave me a vision of a FATE layout like a roulette table. I envisioned a green leather-grain vinyl mat covering the table, with spaces marked in paint like those on a roulette layout. Persistent Aspects of the genre, setting, and campaign might be marked in paint near the middle, but scene aspects would be written on plaques in dry-erase marker and placed in spaces on the layout. Each player would have an area on the layout with spaces for plaque that indicated his character Aspects, current Tags, and current Consequences. Compels and tags would be indicated by laying chips on the layout as in roulette.

Okay, so that is wildly extravagant and probably impractical. But maybe a layout could be produced using laminated printout, or a table topped with glass or a dry-erase surface.

If we were making a FATE layout, what features and organisation would suit it?

    \n Draft objects are ready for playtesting next Friday night.

    The central part of the layout is a "workboard" with a dry-erase surface, 453 mm by 601mm (about 18" by 24"). This has been through the wars: the other side has a meticulously-ruled 25 mm hexagonal grid drawn on it with paint-marker. The new side has a three wide by six deep grid of rectangular spaces ruled on it with a permanent marker, and the columns headed "Setting", "Campaign", "Scene". The spaces are slightly wider and deeper than a standard 3" by 5" index card. I have written persistent Aspects of my setting and campaign into spaces in the appropriate columns using dry-erase markers. Each one is lettered twice so as to be readable from either side of the table. Scene Aspects may be written into the spaces in the appropriate column and wiped off when the scene is done, but I plan to letter some index cards with Aspects that I expect to use often, which can be set out in the "Scene" column to save the time of writing legibly. Perhaps I will colour-code the cards, using buff for lighting conditions, red for footing and surface conditions, green for people present etc.

    I plan to put the workboard in the middle of the table when we play, with a poker chip pre-loaded on each Aspect in play. When a character-player takes the free first tag of a persistent Aspect we will remove the corresponding chip to stock. Thus there will be a constant visual reminder of Aspects in play and what free tags are available, which will (I hope) encourage the use of those mechanics.

    The other part of the layout is a character placemat for each player. This is sort of like a character sheet, but it is designed so that the character's name, language list, and Aspects may be read from the other side of the table, for the convenience of players (including GMs) who might need to address a character, compel an Aspect, or refer to whether a character can understand what might be said. If you print out the placemat at 71% size it will fit onto an A4 landscape page it has about as much space and about the same-sized type as a character sheet, and the circle marked "Fate" is large enough to put a stack of poker chips in it. But the mat is intended to be printed on an A3 landscape page. When this is done it is possible to write the character's name and Aspect large enough that they can be read across the table by a middle-aged man who doesn't quite need bifocals yet. More to the point, the boxes provided for Aspects will be large enough to play a poker chip in one.

    During playtesting I'll simply print the mats out, write permanent features of the characters on them in pen, and use soft pencils for checks and consequences. But when I have thrashed out the bugs and am happy with the layout I will get a set of blank A3 mats laminated. Fixed characteristics can be written on with a permanent marker (it is erasable with solvent) and transient ones with dry-erase.

    My hope for these mats is that players will be able to use them to indicate gameplay using tokens and gestures, with minimal intrusion into the narrative. For example, any player might indicate a Compel by taking one of his chips and placing it on the relevant aspect. The player of the character affected can either take the chip into his stock to indicate accepting, or put a chip from his stock on it to indicate refusing. A player might indicate an Invoke by taking a chip from his stock and placing it on the relevant Aspect on his own character placemat. Perhaps a player could indicate an Accidental Compel by tapping a forefinger or a chip on the Aspect that is being Compelled.

    I might need spaces somewhere on the table for "power a Stunt" and "Bonus +1" chip discards.

    \n Never mind the hex grid: that was a try-out for what I did on the back. What you see there in red is "permanent" marker (though it would wash off with whiteboard cleaning solution). The writing in black and green is dry-erase marker, though later I will replace it with wet-erase OHP marker. That way I will be able to remove scene-Aspect markings &c. with a dry eraser between scenes without disturbing the persistent setting and campaign Aspects, or using a damp rag I will be able to re-task the grid for a different campaign.

    \n Okay, new photos now.

    I've made a change to the ruling of the grid to clarify that "Stunt", "Minor declaration", and "+1 bonus" are not in the "Setting", "Campaign", and "Scene" columns of the grid.

    I've re-written the persistent Aspects in wet-erase marker so that they will not simply rub off.

    I have put in a few familiar objects for scale. The scene aspects are written on standard 3" by 5" index cards. Those coins are crowns, and are the same size as standard poker chips. The table is a 36" card table, much smaller than I would use for playing FATE at.

    \n The layout turns out to be too large to use at most dining-tables. It will fit on mine, but my dining-table is 1050 mm by 2100 mm, which is really inconveniently large.

    So I've re-drawn the central board at A3, and now I can fit places for four character-players into 960mm by 770mm. I think the minimum size of any table I'm likely to be playing at is 1500mm by 900mm, so that ought to do. With A4 character placemats I could get down to 840mm by 507mm.
