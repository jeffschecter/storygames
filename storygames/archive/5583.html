\n I was reading this thread over here and started thinking about my own experiences with the game:
http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=5581

Quick story: I've owned both editions of Exalted, and at one time or other purchased/owned every single 1st Ed Fatsplat. I ran 2 one-shots and a mini-campaign of Exalted 1e. I was interested in playing or running it again, and went to Matt Gandy for advice on it (as he had just finished running a 1e/2e game for something like three years). He shuddered in horror. Every piece of advice I asked for was returned with a horror story on running the game: 2e being much better, rulewise, than 1e but just as onerous on its front-loaded requirements:

GM needs to know all the charms in order to present villains that aren't too powerful, or more commonly aren't too weak that they get punked by some PC combo they didn't take into consideration. This is the majority of pain.
Therefore, writing up NPCs takes a small mountain of time.
Finally, the inelegance of "i roll to hit" "i roll to defend" "i roll for damage" "i roll to soak" ... every single attack roll ... tends to set sail into "too fucking much" territory.

Setting, theme, flavor and all that: Love it.

In the end, I realized the following, lining up my own experiences with Exalted, and games like D&D:

* Combat in Exalted is boring, for me.
* I like the idea of knowing and manipulating the raw data behind the charms: "Gaming the system". Doing the MtG thing in an RPG, stacking effects or manipulating charm combos for maximum effect in a situation.
** Problem is that there's too goddamn many charms for me to be able to do this without exploding. I could have done it if there were, like, 4-5 charms per skill, not but not 10+ for some 25-30 skills.
* The knowing and manipulating of the data behind the charms is a mini-game in itself. And again, under other circumstances, a fun one.

Then, I started thinking about D&D, and how it basically does a similar mini-game thing, but the knowledge is of a limited number of combat manuvers on a square tile map. There's the "Great Cleaves" and the "AoOs" and the "Flanking Bonuses": They're there to be manipulated and gamed. And unlike the Charms from Exalted, there's not 250+ of them (per fatsplat!), there's maybe, MAYBE 40 total. And only about 10 that are universal, that you would use often at a given level.

Anyway, coming to that realization, both that I like manipulative mini-games, and that some games' mini-games are much more vast than others, got me into digging D&D again.

Currently, I'm in a D&D 3.5e game (using the E6 hack). I've got an Ice Elf Spirit Shaman 1/Rogue 3. Out of Combat, I'm Roleplay Central (in fact I think I took too many "story liberties" in the last adventure, basically creating my own side-plot). In Combat, I'm Mistaa Kombatto (Mister Combat), throwing down flank/hidden attacks, pointing out when I or others on my team can use Attacks of Opportunity, Zones or Scenery to their advantage, etc. I'm loving it.

So, uh, this was just a little story. The morals might look something like:
Viva D&D!
Viva Exalted, with its cool background and setting!
Hopefully someone will come around to make an Exalted-ish game, but with Less Stuff to keep in "active memory"!

-Andy

    \n Ahhhh, I left "spells" out of my D&D equation, actually. That's probably a big nearsighted error on my part: I was specifically thinking solely of feats, combat manuvers and class abilites.

    With E6 (the hack where you play levels 1-6, and then freeze at 6 forever, only gaining feats and nothing more), here's the thing: You never have to crack the spell books past 3rd level. So the chances are very good that you only have to pay minimal attention to spells in the balance (just read the PC casters' sheets). I'm guessing that as the PCs advanced to higher and higher levels, that created a geometric amount of things to pay attention to?

    That might be the big difference.

    The thing is, I'm not sure you can do the same in Exalted: You can say "no growth!" and lock down all characters at chargen, ensuring that everyone from PCs to NPC baddies have only 10 charms. But that's still choosing from hundreds of charms that have wildly different effects. Probably the only way you can "e6-ify" Exalted is to say "rewrite the charm list so that anything requiring more than 3 essence is removed from the game", but somehow that feels kinda ill. Not comperable to E6, in some way.

    \n In practice, I have found that spells are not necessarily a pain in the ass. They take some time preparing, of course, but since the most basic and common ones are pretty simple in nature (boom! 20 ft radius, 10d6 dmg, ST for half), most other "alternative" and exotic ones can be used more for flavor.

    I use the same premise for NPCs. With the uncommon exceptions, I simply use standard NPCs (10th lvl kobold ranger, 7th lvl orc barbarian, 11th lvl human wizard...), and make them cool by role playing.

    And the thing about maneuvers in DnD is that most of them work under very similar circumstances (in some cases pretty much the same ones). Mechanically speaking, a trip works practically the same as a grapple, a parry, a bull rush...
