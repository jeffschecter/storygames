    \n I'm pretty sure the relevant moral choices come from deciding how to handle the situation, once we know there are demons afoot. The people affected by the demons are still human beings, and human life is sacred, so the onus is on the Dogs to handle the situation as painlessly as possible.
    The mechanics deal with one side of a conflict or the other choosing to up the ante, thus increasing the chance that one side will not walk out of things alive. That is definitely a crisis for the Dogs, as everyone in the community has friends and loved ones, and would be affected immensely by the death of someone they cared about. Villagers, in their suffering and grief, might start doing stupid and sinful things; thus, attracting demons (okay, or maybe the other way around), and thus the Dogs want to minimize the presence of demons by minimizing the presence of death.

    It doesn't matter that you know for a fact that someone is demon-tainted; the problem is that it's really complicated to just plug someone in the gut and end things right there.
    Does that help?

    \nI think the trick to situation generation is to make sure that just "living morally" going forward doesn't fix the problem. Somebody's wife still cheated, something has still been stolen, etc. The wound shouldn't go away just because you pull out the knife. My players had a tendency to treat the game like an "investigative game" where the goal was to treat the town like a puzzle and find the root cause of the sin. Things started to work better when I got them to engage with the NPCs emotionally by making them established relationships (either kin, or named NPCs from the relationships set at chargen) or by making them sympathetic, so the players actually wanted to help fix the problems rather than just berate the NPCs for getting into whatever mess they got into. Also, I initially had a hard time with with the "push toward conflict" stuff because I didn't want the NPCs to initiate conflicts with the players to force them to resolve the town's issues the way the NPC wanted. Now, I have the NPCs try to resolve things on their own or try to escalate tension in the town and give the players a chance to get involved if they want, because that tends to prompt the players to start choosing sides.

    \n I like to think about it this way:

    Pride and sin are very personal and specific. A particular person thinks they're better than other people, which leads to them breaking the social norms and pulling other people into sin (either intentionally, or as a reaction to what they do), which is still very personal and specific, and that snowballs until you get false prophets and the demons arrive.

    The demons themselves are NOT personal and specific. They're not into any particular person's suffering so much as wanting to burn it all down and make everyone in the community as fucked up and miserable as they can be. So they're a big flaming sign that things are very wrong, definitely, but they're not a flashing arrow pointing out where the problem is. It's up to the PC's to decide what the problems are and how they should be fixed, which isn't always a straightforward task.

    Who do you have to kill? Who do you have to make repent? How do you make them do that? Etc. etc. The moral dilemma is in the who, what, and how questions. You know, as Dogs, what you are supposed to do. But who's responsible and how do you make justice happen?

    \n So, I'm a Dog.

    I come into a town that's gone wrong because two guys are very much in love, and because of that started fucking. A bunch of other guys didn't like that, and so are now plotting to kill one or both of those first guys.

    All of these people have sinned in one way or another. But how do I fix it?

    Should I kill the gay guys? Kill the guys who were going to kill them?

    Or, should I make what the gay guys are doing not sin by the simple expedient of saying, as someone with Authority, that they are not sinning so long as they are really in love?

    And when their parents won't accept that decision and come screaming at me that I'm a sinner in front of the whole town, possibly giving those who still think it is a sin probable cause to doubt my authority, how do I deal with that?

    And when the plotting dudes now want to kill me because I'm doing sin, do I kill them? Forgive them?

    In order to make the wrongness (the demons) go away, I can do any or all of these things. But which ones do I do?

    The question isn't "is something wrong." Something is always wrong. The question is "how do I fix the wrongness, and what is the cost of that to my character."

    \n S orcerers are evil, and need to die. That's not very morally ambiguous, but that's ok. The trick is to make sure the root causes are far more sympathetic.

    The last town in our recent game involved a girl who had all the qualities to be a great dog, and who wanted the freedom that comes from being a female dog. Unfortunately, the nepotistic steward overlooked her for personal reasons, and decided that his sweet-but-useless niece was going to be a dog instead.

    The first girl's grandma knew exactly what was going on, and had no patience for such bullshit. She held a ceremony in which she blessed the girl as a dog. Blammo, false worship! Grandma's a sorceror! She has her demons start sending the girl true visions of evil things going on in the town, so that the false dog can kill the evildoers.

    How are the dogs going to deal with all this? Grandma is evil now, but it's hard to blame her for opposing the corrupt steward. There's a nepotistic steward to deal with, but he's not leaving quietly, and are you really going to shoot him over some slightly shady decisions? Most importantly, how do you help the girl, who is the most sympathetic character in this mess, but who is currently in a cult, defying her appointed steward, trying to rise above her place as a woman, and getting help from demons?

    \n What I do...
    \n
    \nDemons aren't something you can attack directly if at all. You deal with people.
    \n
    \nDemons don't control people's minds. They influence. People make their own decisions.
    \n
    \nMany of these people are your family, friends, and lovers. Make Dog's relationships the main NPCs.
    \n
    \nGood people do bad things for good reasons. And bad people want them punished.
    \n
    \nBad people do good things for bad reasons. And good people agree with them.
    \n
    \nPeople use bad things as justification to do bad things themselves for good and bad reasons.
    \n
    \nVictims bad things may do a bigger bad things in reaction to what was done to them. They didn't invite it and they didn't start it.
    \n
    \nGod doesn't speak to the Dogs. The GM can't tell the Dogs what is right or wrong.
    \n
    \nEven the strict rules of the faith have grey areas.
    \n
    \nBut some people will follow the rules strictly, regardless of their personal feelings. And what the Dogs says is true! There interpretation is right!
    \n
    \nExcept if 1 Dogs says 1 thing, and another Dog says another, they are both right? How do the Dogs deal with this? How do the NPCs react to that conflict? How do the NPCs take advantage of that conflict?
    \n
    \nPlant the seeds immediately that it is ok to split the party. Splitting the party means Dogs can form individual opinions. Individual opinions pull away from group consensus. If your cousin did bad things for good reasons they probably want to talk to you in private. They probably want you to advocate for them to the other Dogs.
    \n
    \nFear escalates. A Dog shoots my neighbor because he claims he conspired with demons, I may not believe them... my neighbor helped raise my family! Or I'm afraid the Dogs are coming after me next! I didn't do anything but I thought my neighbor was innocent to. But if the Dogs say something... it's true. But I don't want to die. Towns are small. Everyone knows everyone. A Dog shoots my father, mother, wife, husband, son, daughter, grandmother, grandfather, grandson, granddaughter, lover, best friend, teacher... things get crazy fast.
    \n
    \nDogs benefits from multiple sessions. Sometimes morality doesn't come into the first session. People can act like a military unit dealing with objects, not people. But in the next town you can deal with the fallout of their decisions. Explore the consequences. Humanize those "objects". And now you are armed with history! You know what decisions the Dogs made so you can create new situations that challenge those viewpoints. Maybe the person they punished in Town 1 really was a bad person doing bad things. Maybe in Town 2 a different bad person is doing the same bad thing for really good reasons. In Town 3 a good person is doing that same bad thing for good reasons. In Town 4 no one thinks that's a bad thing at all or the person in question is your family, friend, teacher, lover! And as Dogs go from Town to Town they may end up being the person doing the bad thing for the good reasons. Roles can be reversed. And in reflection things don't seem so black and white.
    \n
    \nPlus what the Dogs say goes. Even if someone thinks the Book of Life says different.

    \n Don't mind a little mostly-coherent rambling from me.

    I listened to Clyde and Vincent's conversation from a few yearsmonths back a few days ago and Vincent said some stuff about what demonic influence and sorcerers represent that I found incredibly helpful when thinking about this stuff. Mind you, this is for low-magic "real world" type games. Demonic Influence is really just a metaphor for Bad Stuff Happening - blight, dry well, sickness. This is the stuff that befalls every town but since we are dealing with superstition, one thing (Mark and Sally having sex out of wedlock) gets linked to another (the Jones family cattle all dying from a disease) and it gets blamed on demons let in by Mark and Sally's illicit love. And Sorcerers are simply the bastards who take advantage of a lame situation for personal gain.

    Now, the game sets up an escalation path so that one thing leads to another and sooner or later the demons are able to come in, but really it's just shitty things happening: if your town is sin-free and the well dries up it's a problem to deal with like any other frontier issue, but if you have some false worship going on and the well dries up then suddenly everyone is talking about demons and sin, and Jeremiah the butcher who is taking advantage of people's hardship? He's the sorcerer. There's no real difference in the situation except for that two things are getting linked together fairly arbitrarily by an angry and frightened populace.

    Because of this, what the GM does is make some stuff happen that's bad (dry well in this case, your "demons") and sets up some issues that townsfolk have decided are sins. It's up to the Dogs to say "yes, Mark and Sally's immoral love is a sin and it needs to be stopped before things get worse" or say "Sally and Mark are pure in their love, and it is Jeremiah the Butcher who needs to be brought to justice for passing false judgement against these lovers." As I said, all the GM has done is create a problem and put a morally ambiguous thing out there as a possible cause, and it is up to the players to decide which way they go. The thing to remember is that the answer that the players give IS THE MORAL TRUTH (emphasis mine): if they decide that it is against the teachings of the King of Life to practice love outside of marriage, then that is true for your game. If the GM sets Dogs up as vehicle for giving a moral teaching, it robs the characters of one of the really cool parts of Dogs which is getting to create (and then be tested) on a morality.

    Oh, right, one other thing. Once the players have started creating a morality for the game, the GM should test them on it. If the Dogs have said that sex out of wedlock if immoral and punishable by death, hit them later with kissing, then holding hands, then talking to each other without a elder male guardian present. The GM should challenge them on their decisions and their morality. If a Dog says that all thievery must be punished by death, hit them with a father stealing food to feed his family through the winter, and later an 8 year old kid stealing candy and ask them "even now?"

    \n The book has some possible relevant advice...\n
    GMing Between TownsRemember how, at the end of character creation, you went \u201cmmhmm\u201d like the good doctor? Here\u2019s where you angle the game to hit those issues. In the town just past, what were the characters about? What positions did they take? Which sinners did they judge harshly, and which did they show mercy? What did they say, I mean really say, about themselves and others?

    Your goal in the next town is to take the characters\u2019 judgments and push them a little bit further. Say that in this past town, one of the characters came down clearly on the side of \u201cevery sinner deserves another chance.\u201d In the next town, you\u2019ll want to reply with \u201ceven this one? Even this sinner?\u201d Or say that another character demonstrated the position that \u201clove is worth breaking the rules for.\u201d You can reply with \u201cis this love worth breaking the rules for too? Is love worth breaking this rule for?\u201d

    But Dogs isn\u2019t abstract or academic! This love, this sinner, this law\u2014 those are real people, real characters I mean, in real concrete situations. Create the people and the situations, don\u2019t pose the question in some sort of theoretical way.

    Most importantly, don\u2019t have an answer already in mind. GMing Dogs is a different thing from playing it. Your job as the GM is to present an interesting social situation and provoke the players into judging it. You don\u2019t want to hobble their judgments by arguing with them about what\u2019s right and wrong, nor by creating situations where right and wrong are obvious. You want to hear your players\u2019 opinions, not to present your own.

Maybe we can make a few less babies per village, if it means a few more women and queers are happier, and we can demonstrate the community is not harmed, or that it can take it. Gathering evidence to demonstrte this to the P&A would be a long term objective in the campaign.

    The Dogs can't just come into a town, sanctify it right away and move on. That doesn't work. It's sloppy. In order to declare an instance of sinning is not actually sin, you need to know about it, to be aware of the details, you need to investigate. You also need to go through to the roots of the problem, the underlying pride, and fix the injustice. If you don't, it'll just lead to more sinning in the future.

    They also have to make sure the social order will be able to support the change. If you allow one woman to have multiple husbands, that's cool. It's no longer a sin, no longer attracts demons. But this will lead to jealousy. Other wives wanting multiple husbands, husbands not wanting to share their wives. The Faithful may accept that this case is different, but not understand why. You've opened a can of worms. A lot of Pride, Injustice and Sinning may come from that one call you made. If and when you come back, you'll have to deal with it, and fix the mess you made. And try not to contradict yourself. See, there is still one person in the world who can actually say you were wrong, and that is you.

    If a Dog is being sloppy, it is his or her own damn fault.
