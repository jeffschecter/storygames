    \n I've read the game, it's relatively straightforward - 90% setting fluff, the system is basically GM-led freeform, so what you're buying is a confused description of a surrealistic fantasy city which could easily be used with any other game. The setting material is literarily legit (I'm not intending to sound lukewarm, merely precise) for surreal fantasy, although I personally think that the illogical presentation makes the game no favours; it would be easier for the GM to appreciate, adopt, adapt and apply if the presentation was arranged logically instead of having all the history and political background of the city scattered here and there in the form of random vignettes, with no overview whatsoever. (Surrealism in actual play is not served by confused presentation to the GM, I think.)

    Were I Jeff, I would purchase Itras By if it being a game in the freeform tradition were not a dealbreaker in itself; for me personally it nearly is (I find freeform generally vapid), although Itras By is definitely one of the better offerings in the generally underwhelming genre of Nordic freeform. For somebody who feels differently about freeform, I believe Itras By to be a thought-provoking experience, particularly if they're not annoyed by the aforementioned deficits in presentation. I could totally see somebody thinking that the shardy, vague presentation contributes to the magically surreal atmosphere of the city, which is probably what the designers had in mind when they decided to forego all overviews in favour of a vignette style.

    \n I did consider whether my capsule review was appropriate, but concluded that the three main facts I mentioned are correct (it's got legit surreal fluff, imperfect organization, and it belongs in the freeform tradition), and it is not inappropriate to be clear about my subjective experience, either; it might be useful for somebody who knows my tastes and can therefore decide for themselves whether they might like it. I've nothing against the game and its existence per se; it's an interesting little book even if it's not to my tastes, and I believe that for somebody who likes that sort of thing it's one of the better offerings out there.

    Also: freeform does not, obviously, mean that a game does not have a system. At least, not to anybody who understands "system" according to the lumpley principle Whatever else one might communicate with the label, I merely meant "freeform" in historical sense: it is a game belonging in the tradition that is commonly called "freeform". I doubt that anybody would claim that Itras By does not belong, speaking of cultural history, in the tradition of Nordic freeform gaming; it's all but an emblematic example of what that sort of thing looks like in book form.

    Other than knowing the rules and setting, and having print-outs of materials, what else does "well prepared" entail?
    \nA carefully prepped adventure is what you need, of course, when the time is limited. The set-up of the game is basically traditional, so for a 2-hour slot you'll need a tight setpiece scenario with a strong initial premise and no wheel-spinning secondary scenes. By front-loading the interesting (surreal fantasy) stuff you'll make space for follow-up developments to form the main arc of play. (As opposed to a meandering adventure that begins with exploration, goes into b-plots and gives up the beef in a climax 3 hours later.)
