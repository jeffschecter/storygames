[Do it in Your System] Tank Man

Brand_Robins
November 2006 edited November 2006 in Story Games
I was recently talking with a friend about different games, resolution systems, and the unexpected moments in real life (as opposed to the fiction I almost always concentrate on in RPG discussions) and how they help, hinder, or change the experience of play and what you can and cannot author as a player.

Part of the discussion ended up focusing on how resolutions systems based on character abilities can end up putting GMs into real binds when the type of action being taken is clearly not something the system supports. We came up with an example and argued it back and forth, coming to no final conclusions. So, I thought I'd take this one to the forum to celebrate my return to Toronto.

I'm now going to give you an example of something that happened, and I'd like you to tell me how you would resolve it if you were the GM (or opposition/narrating/guiding player) in a game using a couple of your favorite systems.

The game is a story of political revolution, of a country with a history of brutal human rights abuse on the eve of a possible cultural revolution lead by students, philosophers, and unions. The powerful regime has sent in the military to end protests and public gatherings, authorizing use of lethal force. Tanks are rolling down the street on the well to quell the movement, and by the end of the day thousands of people will be dead.

Then one player announces "I step out into the street, still holding my groceries in my hand. I walk into the path of the tanks and raise my hand for them to stop. If they try to go around me I step into the way, but I offer no other resistance. They will either stop or they will have to run me over where I stand. I face the world with courage and compassion, and hope that it is the sort of place that can face me back with honesty."

What do you have the player do in order to meet her intent?

(And yes, it'll be obvious where this example comes from. And yes, if it is part of your normal system of resolution you can reference the real world outcome of the above action - but it isn't neccisary. We all know that it could have turned out differently.)

    Brand,

    This is an interesting question, and I can see the edges of the problem here (I think). In any game I played I wouldn't care about whether or not the character can stand in front of the tank. Most systems could handle it with an Athletics roll (or similar), but that's a pretty lame contest. As I see it, the conflict is only interesting at two levels: 1) does the tank stop?, and 2) does it matter?

    So looking at my stand-by system of HeroQuest, I'd be rolling one of two contests (or perhaps both, with one being a follow-up).

    1. Does the tank stop? It'd be a psychological battle. I'd be rolling in stuff like, 'Civic responsibility', 'Violence solves nothing', 'Passive resistance', 'Looks like your favorite uncle' (who wants to run over their favorite uncle?), and so on. This works because I play HQ a la Mike Holmes. So I sort of ignore in-world justifications. If I lose the contest we figure out why after the roll (guided by traits in play). Same thing if I win. Usually it's pretty obvious how the victory or loss will go, but it doesn't have to be.

    2. Does it matter? This is where the real meat of it is for me. I'd probably be rolling pretty much the same abilities as above, but with a different eye on winning/losing narration. Maybe you stop the tank but it doesn't matter because no one sees the dramatic gesture and other tanks go around you and kill everyone. Maybe you're run dramatically over and no one sees you, or maybe someone snaps a picture of your courageous stand. Maybe you stop that one tank, and so save a dozen lives that otherwise would have been lost in the slaughter that follows.

    I think HQ does this well because it's not worried about the mechanics of how you get from point A to point B. You start at A, you get some idea of where you're going, you roll some dice to narrow down B, and then you narrate your way to it. In a more 'traditional' game the abilities you use to roll dice are supposed to act fairly rigidly on the path from A to B, so if you don't have an ability for it it becomes unclear how you can create a path.

    I'm sure you already knew all this, but I wanted to talk myself through it

    Thomas

    In Sufficiently Advanced, you could do it two ways.

    One, using the more "standard" game mechanics, has you starting a Metatech contest with the guy in the tank. Metatech is the game's social stat. You add in a bonus for your Core Value of Peace (or perhaps Life or Nonviolence), and another circumstance bonus for your audacious surprise action. The guy in the tank uses his Metatech to resist, but you roll high enough to blow him away and zero out his Reserve. He bows to your will, at least temporarily, and stops.

    The other way uses your Plot Scores, which measure how important you are to the story. You spend your Plot Point for the game through whichever Score is most appropriate. Perhaps Plot Immunity to avoid dying, perhaps Empathy to connect with the guys in the tanks, perhaps Magnetism to sway them to your side. (It seems likely that this guy didn't have a Plot Point, and had to take a Complication to get one... which might explain why no one's seen him since. Perhaps his Complication was "thrown in Chinese prison.")

    I'll tackle it from a d20/D&D perspective,since that's the game system I'm most familiar with.

    I'd break it down into a couple parts. The first part is a Bluff check. Yeah, I know the player isn't bluffing per se, in that he really is going to keep standing there and get squooshed, if it comes to that. But the tank driver doesn't. Usually Bluff is opposed by Sense Motive, because usually you're trying to fool someone. In this case, I'd sorta reverse it -- the player wants the driver to have a good Sense Motive ability and to roll really well, so that he senses what this guy's motives really are.

    The basic issue being resolved at this stage is: Does that tank driver believe you're just going to stand there and let yourself get run over? Some of the standard modifiers that could potentially apply:

    * The target wants to believe you. (Makes task easier.) Is the driver morally-against this whole thing, and is looking for a way out? Or does he really hate these people, whom he blames for killing his whole family, and so is itching for the chance to run them over?

    * The bluff is believable and doesn't affect the target much. (default)

    * The bluff is a little hard to believe or puts the target at some risk. (Makes task more difficult.) If the tank driver knows he could get demoted or thrown in jail for stopping, I might go with this.

    * The bluff is hard to believe or puts the target at significant risk. (Makes task much more difficult.) If there's a morale officer inside the tank with a pistol pointed at the driver's head, I might use this one.

    * The bluff is way out there, almost too incredible to consider. (The task is really tricky now.)

    I might be inclined to cut the player a break here, all other things being equal, unless there's some good pre-existing factors in the SIS that would lead me towards one direction or the other. I probably wouldn't use a randomizer here, but that's a personal thing.

    So... either the bluff works, or it doesn't. If it doesn't, the driver just doesn't believe the PC is going to hold his ground. Hand the player a new character sheet.

    If the bluff works, the driver figures this guy might just be crazy enough to just stand there. So what does the driver do about it?

    This is the second part of the resolution, for which I'd use one or more Diplomacy checks. The first step here is to figure out the driver's current attitude. By default it would be Indifferent, which implies he would undertake "Socially expected interaction". Again, there's some need here to know the SIS and whether the driver feels the expectation to stop, or to run him down.

    Off the top of my head, I'd probably go with him being Indifferent and inclined to stop, but only to think things over. So, in a minute, another Diplomacy check. If the driver's attitude changes for the worse (either by PC action, or the tank commander screaming at him, or whatnot), then it's new character sheet time again. If the driver's attitude changes for the better (either by PC action, or a crowd of people rallying behind him, or whatnot) then he puts the tank into park and they all get to think it over for a while. If there's still no change... hmmm. I'd probably increase the difficulty by, say, 1 per minute, just to reflect the increasing psychological pressure on this guy.

    Some folks might make the player make some sort of roll, representing bravery or resistance to fear or whatever, to get that PC to actually go out there and risk his neck. I'm not really inclined to do that, but the ruleset would support it in a general way, I'd say. Also, some folks might be inclined to weasel the PC out of his glorious death via various mechanisms. Not me. If a player says "They will either stop or they will have to run me over where I stand", that's good enough for me.

    Now, I might just be reading something into this scenario, but it seems like the bigger conflict resolution here might have something to do with the issue of whether the military as a whole restrains itself from mowing down a whole lot of civilians. That would be trickier to model.


    Cheers,
    Roger

    City of Birds offers two methods, largely up to the player.

    Method I:

    The Bird says, "I want to stand in front of the tank." His goal is thus to stand in front of the tank, making it an internal conflict. He talks about his confidence, hope for the future, etc. The Snake gets to say whether he stands or not. Let's presume he says yes. The Tiger then gets say on the ramifications of that act. It would be up to him whether the tank stopped or not.

    Method II:

    What if the player doesn't want his standing to be in doubt? He wants to stand, period. In this case, his intention is clear, but not whether he gets what he wants. The Snake could say something like, "Your buddy leaps out and pulls you to the side," or, "Sure. You do," again passing the final decision to the Tiger. The Snake would also be within his rights — and I think this is the preferable choice — to bite off a bit more and actually decide whether the tank stops or not. Then the Tiger says what that means: maybe, "You die but it provokes a thousand more students to riot." Or "You wake up in the hospital with no legs."

    In Revolutions, standing in front of the tank means you've largely resolved things. You are coming home with your groceries and see the agents of the old revolution (the tanks). Now, you want to do something, so you declare you will interpose yourself and stop them.

    There are several outcomes -

    You fail your unrest - you move to help, but you can't bring yourself to take sides.

    You succeed your hurdle, but fail your roll - you arrive too late.

    You succeed your roll, and beat the hurdle - move in front of the tanks, they stop.

    You succeed your roll, but fail to beat the hurdle - you move in front of the tanks, but they do not stop.

    In any case, the next scene is when your family learns what has happened.

    I have been mulling this over for a while now, and it is a tough question. The thing that is so striking about the events surounding Tank Man is...well, the incredible intimacy of the moment--it's two men, tank man and tank driver, standing before each other. We know so little about it--only that he stood in front of the tanks and refused to yield, and the tanks did.

    A lot of resolution mechanics, on the other hand, gets on the table when we know the why and wherefore. We see this in the examples--is he trying to stop a tank, fuel a revolution, embarass a nation? As soon as we do that, I feel like we are missing something. This is a guy walking in front of a tank, carrying with him what can only be terribly personal, human hopes and fears. Is there any real hope of a resolution when that man did that?

    Let me take the D&D example again. I would leave it as straight Diplomacy, if we *wanted* to take it to that level, but let's imagine something different.

    DM (setting color, tone): You are returnign home from the grocery store, see the tanks rolling down the street, knowing in your heart where they are headed, knowing the danger...

    Player (interrupting): I step in front of the column of tanks.

    DM: What? It's a tank, you've got groceries...

    Player: I step in front of the column.

    DM: ok, it starts to turn to go around you...

    Player: I keep moving, staying in front of the tanks.

    DM: *rolls intimidate check, player holds steady* Shit, I, I mean, he doesn't want to run you over, the tanks back up and drive off. Why did you do that?

    Player: *shrugs* felt like the right thing

    I feel like if it isn't two *people* looking at each other in that conflict, even re-enacted, there is something going a little awry in the portrayal. Figuring out the fate of a nation or revolution just seems out of place. The resolution mechanic, outside of the character's courage, seems out of place.

    It only makes powerful sense when, even just for a moment, the DM takes the tank driver's perspective and just can't do it. If the DM takes the opposite tactic and does run the character down, well, that is a different sort of powerful statement.
