\n In this episode, I talk to Luke Crane of the Burning Wheel Headquarters, about their RPG adaptation of the comic book Mouse Guard. I'm really happy with how this interview went; it's one of the best I've put out so far. Luke has some really interesting things to say about the use of art and the way layout and editing work. We also get to talk a bit about how the HQ is constructed and who does what.

NOTE: This was obviously recorded before the release of the Magic Burner, but it's funny to listen to Luke toy with me.

This episode is 64.5 MB big and 1 hour and 10 minutes long.

    \n Luke gave Terry and I the first copy of Mouse Guard as a wedding gift! Luke is a beautiful man!
    \n
    \nI will stand by this statement: Mouse Guard is one of the best written, organized, laid out, useful, easy to use, and streamlined RPGs I've ever owned.
    \n
    \nIt's easily as readable and clear as Dogs in the Vineyard. Possibly more so in certain ways. From what I can tell so far, it's one of the easiest games to GM I've seen. It comes with 3 sample adventures and pre-generated characters tailored for each adventure!
    \n
    \nYou know how amazing the Dungeons and Dragons 4E Dungeon Master Guide is? Imagine it was part of the player's handbook and the advice was actually supported by the game itself! That's what this is like.
    \n
    \nI really like D&D; 4E. Our Thursday group has played over 20 weekly sessions. In terms of a game that is accessible to new players and GMs in terms of rules, instructions, and reference... Mouse Guard tops it. And production wise is on par or even better. It's that good.
    \n
    \nAnd it draws people in! My sisters and family who are all non gamers couldn't stop looking at it and asking questions about what it is. There is just something about it.
    \n
    \nThe chapter on Seasons is beautiful. The Index is extensive and just infinitely useful. I wish all games had an Index like this. I wish D&D; 4E had this index! It uses iconography to highlight important rules and text for the GM ad the player. It's so well organized!
    \n
    \nIt's the best version of Burning Wheel I've played. It's like the Burning Wheel crew took most of the fan requests for a streamlined Burning Wheel and delivered perfectly! And the game is very hacking friendly. I could easily see playing D&D;, Shadowrun, Earthsea, Conan and more with Mouse Guard!
    \n
    \nWow.

    \n I really really love the twist and conditions rule.
    \n
    \nIt basically means that you are never making a succeed / fail roll. You are never testing for "do I succeed".
    \n
    \nRather than succeed / fail, a test is "suffer a down side / do not suffer a down side". Since not accomplishing what you wanted would be a down side, making the roll means you must actually succeed. But not making a roll doesn't mean you failed...it just means you suffered a downside...which may be failure, or may be something else.
    \n
    \nA simple distinction that sounds like mere semantics but actually completely changes the nature of making rolls in play.

      \n Yeah, I just finished skimming a few chapters and it looks and reads great. The layout is sharp and I can't wait to see what it looks like printed. The dimensions of the book and the size of the text (which makes it easy to read) means that there's a limited amount of information that can be fit on any given page, but Luke's done a good job of breaking things down into bite-sized chunks and coding different sections to help you find what you're looking for.

      The mechanics appear a bit intimidatingly dense at first glance, especially looking at the combat reference sheets, but once you begin to understand how to decipher them (and the text is very readable and helpful in this regard), it's clear that the abundance of information is actually to the players' benefit, in the sense that you shouldn't need to look anything up during play. Seeing the example character sheets (included with the PDF) filled out is also great. Looking at the red text, it's easy to see that there's actually not that much you need to keep track of at one time (at least in the beginning), that most of the text on the sheet describes options that you reach for in certain circumstances. All in all, Luke's done a bunch of things that make the 300+ page book and the extensive (if greatly streamlined) rules set highly accessible.

      The seasonal framing thing is grade-A awesome. That definitely stood out as being very cool, an unexpected (for me) reincarnation of the Burning Empires scene-structuring guidelines, maybe?

      Can't wait to delve into it further, but it definitely looks like Luke and the BWHQ have knocked another one out of the park.
