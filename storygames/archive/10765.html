\n Greetings,
\nI just started working on an idea for a short game about... well, about fatness. The goal of the game (which I named "Fat Chance!") is trying to be the fattest character of the group in order to earn something. All characters are in fact involved in some kind of challenge (players can decide anything about it before playing) that lets the fattest one win.
\nThere is no GM: every gamer is both a player with authority over his/her own character, Scale and Hunger for the near other gamers. The role of Scale is to try and make other characters abandon the challenge, while the Hunger tries to convince them to keep gaining.
\nEvery rule is created to make the players themselves feel more and more uncomfortable with their characters becoming fatter and fatter: the more they get fat, more dice have to be thrown in order to achieve a success, and the number range of success gets narrower.
\nHunger and Scale create Embarrassing Scenes which have to be "won" in order to avoid being frustrated; when the frustration level gets too high, the character leaves the competition.
\nI have yet to translate the whole game (since English is not my mother tongue), but if you feel interested into it I can do it.

    Posted By: MatrixGamerIf it has to be fatness then what about tying it to a weight loss camp or contest where the overt reward is for losing weight but where the real emotional rewards are for gaining weight. I bet you could find good anecdotal info by checking at "Overeaters Anonymous".
    \nWell, to be honest, I wanted the characters to have to face an inner conflict: they feel compelled to gain weight in order to win the competition, but in the meantime they feel they are wasting their life with it, losing their relatives' love and relationships, losing the ability to live like anybody else and so on. I ventured a little bit into the Fat Admirers' and Feederism world to have some informations on what a gaining life could look like, and that's how I managed to develop some parts of the game.
    Each character has five stats, some of which take care of their relationships and of their self-image; and each Embarrassing Scene should be a serious conflict between the will to win and the will to keep living a satisfying life. All of this with a "gamist" approach: as Hunger, you want to push your challenger's character limits in order to stress him/her, and as Scale you just want him/her to leave the competition in order to let your own character win it.

    \n Here's some food for thought (pardon the pun) for this game, from a real life fat person...
    \n
    \nFor many, if not most obese people, the obesity stems from deeply rooted emotional issues. Many have serious self esteem issues; some aren't unaware that their real problem is self esteem. Most have been conditioned since childhood to associate food with pleasure and happiness. So when they eat, it makes them happy for a short time; but they gain weight. Their self esteem takes another hit; so they eat some more for the feeling of pleasure; and the cycle goes on and on.
    \n
    \nSometimes, the compulsion to satisfy the desire for pleasure or happiness is much, much stronger than the conscious knowledge that one should not eat so much. For example, I once felt a compulsion to get something to eat that was so strong I went to the fridge, got some food, and ate it despite consciously and energetically telling myself that I was not hungry, and that I should not be succumbing to the compulsion. It was like someone had implanted a mind control chip in my head and was controlling my actions via remote control; it was an almost surreal experience.
    \n
    \nMost diets and so called healthy living plans, unfortunately, just don't address the real underlying causes of most peoples' obesity problem - that being the low self esteem and the association of food with pleasure and happiness.
    \n
    \nOne system I found that did address those causes, and can be quite effective is Paul McKenna's "I Can Make You Thin" system. It's a simple system that doesn't actually prescribe any particular foods, require one to change one's diet; rather, it provides a method to recognize the body's natural physiological signs of hunger and fullness, to recognize and suppress the purely emotional hunger signals, as well as to redirect the emotional associations of pleasure and happiness towards healthier activities such as exercise.
    \n
    \nPerhaps amongst all this rambling you might find some inspiration to ratchet up the meaningful exploration in the game.
