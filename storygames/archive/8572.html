\n So I started designing a new setting. No, this isn't the one I wrote about a few months ago. That crashed and burned for a variety of reasons. This is brand new and potentially may lead to something being published.

Nota bene: I will steal your ideas. If you post really cool things in this thread, I reserve the right to steal them, twist them, and use them in my setting, and that includes selling it and making money and not paying you anything for it. Seriously. But if I think your idea is brilliant, I might work something out with you and have you write it, then pay you typical, lame-ass game-author rates for your work.

I felt that disclaimer was necessary. I really don't want to steal people's ideas, but I also don't want people yelling at me, "You stole my idea!" when I do publish this thing. I'll do my best to credit everyone's ideas in the colophon, okay? I don't want to be a dick.


Concept

"Points of light" in a city. High fantasy with horror and post-apocalyptic themes.

A city from a non-magical late-medieval world somehow ends up in the middle of a high-fantasy jungle world. A small number of the city's original inhabitants develop magical powers and transform in weird ways. The magical jungle devours the city streets. Monsters of every type invade every shop, apartment, and alley. The heroes must help their fellow citizens survive in this terrible, monstrous world.

Verothi was a booming city. Situated at the junction of three rivers and several trading routes, the metropolis was the home of the Hathnian Kingdom's palatial seat, a college of science and the arts, numerous grand guild halls, and fifty thousand people. The citizens ranged from the queen and her court to nobility to rich tradesmen -- and so on and so forth -- all the way down to the lowest beggars.

One night, the world blinked, and Verothi found itself in a different universe. Farms used to stretch out to the horizon from the city's walls. Now dense jungle pushed up around the defenses. The city streets, before kept in order by regular patrols, now filled with monsters of a kind only mentioned in faery tales. Most everyone died within a few weeks. Indescribable things scaled the walls -- or flew right in -- and devoured people. Nightmarish beasts crawled up out of the sewers to murder people in their homes, or worse, drag them down into who-knows-where. The survivors holed up in safe places, hoarded food and water, and tried to stay alive.

Two months after the city displaced, it got a new wall made of magical force. Of course, no one from the city had ever seen anything like it. No one knew who had created the barrier. Was it meant to keep the people in, or keep monsters out? In any case, it seemed impenetrable.

Some of the inhabitants began changing. They developed magical powers for fighting and healing and for understanding the magic that weaves the world together. Some of these people devoted their power for the cause of good. They helped people where they could, bringing food and supplies to the needy. Others turned their abilities toward evil.

This is the metrocalypse we live in. The way home is uncertain. There are still monstrous things here, some of them us.

    \nProduct

    For now, this is just something I'm gonna run for some friends. Reaction to these setting ideas so far seems to be divided into the "okay, fine, whatever" camp and the "oh my god, I want this now" camp. That leads me to believe that there's a market for this, so I am developing it with an eye towards eventual publication.

    I envision the setting as a series of PDF products. There will be a core setting product that describes the city and situation at a high level and gives advice for making characters that live in this setting. I imagine that's free. Yes, FREE. If a DM wants to download that and make her own stuff for that setting, good on her. Then I will support that core setting idea with additional products. These are things you could plug into other settings with a little work, so they're not just Transit things. Small location-based adventures in the city. New monsters from the jungle. New powers and feats with a Transit flare.

    \nVerothi

    So imagine the city of Verothi as a typical river town in Sixteenth Century Italy or Germany or something. The architecture is a mix of creepy Gothic stuff and Classical styles. There are markets and shops and apartments and houses. Most of the thing is walled up. There's a palace and some temples. The people there are pious but they never see real magic. Life centers on commerce, craft, and service. A poorly-organized Guard maintains order and collects tolls at the gates.

    Suddenly, there's an earthquake and a flash of weird light. That day, weird monsters start roaming the street. It turns into some kind of zombie apocalypse, but with unspeakably weird creatures hunting people instead of mindless undead. Every day, the jungle itself eats another city block. The vines grow over the walls overnight and start filling the cobblestone streets with tall plants, chokers and creepers, nettles, and sometimes man-eating plants. The city itself is unsafe for travel.

    People huddle in small groups in safe buildings, but there's no food. People have to go out to hunt for supplies. Most never return. Sometimes the monsters break down the doors and feed on the citizens.

    The city is transformed. A flight of dragons makes a new nest in the palace on the hill. The once-beautiful hanging gardens become home to harpies. Strange and malicious brownies move into apartments and rifle through shops for trade items. Every day, new, weird monsters appear.

    One day, a shimmering globe pops into existence around the city. It seems to stop more monsters from coming into the city. It also seems to keep the city's populace from leaving. Still, it doesn't eradicate Verothi's pestilence of evil and cunning magical beasts. Who created the barrier? Whom is it meant to protect?

    \nCharacters

    When you make a character for Transit, you make a normal D&D character. You were a normal (human) person a few weeks ago and had no magical abilities. Being in this new world has changed you, perhaps awakened something that was already in you.

    Some people just develop new powers (e.g., they are still human in race, but gain a class and level and all the powers that go with that). Others transform more dramatically. They polymorph into different shapes (races), some as strange as the "dragonborn," who weren't actually born with a dragon form...

    Creating a character for this setting requires you to think about who you were before the change, who you've become since, and how you deal with it.

    More on the Creating Characters for Transit page on my wiki.

    Posted By: Adam DrayIf you play D&D 4E,would you play this thing?
    \nSo this setting sounds really awesome, Adam, and I'd love to play it with you, but recently I've been thinking, like, "Why would anyone play in a D&D setting that wasn't something they made up together with their friends?" I've been thinking about this because I'm working on a Shang Dynasty-inspired thing for D&D4, based on some of the ideas we put forward in Hard Boiled Cultures and have been trying to decide if there's a One Bad Egg product in there somewhere. And my conclusion is like, maybe? Not because I really think other people want to play in my Shang Dynasty fantasy setting (though they might), but because I think the way I'm implementing the setting in 4E might serve as an inspiration for other folks who want to hack 4E into a more "new school indie gaming" style experience. So, yeah, while I'd totally love to see what you're doing with Transit City, I'd be interested to steal ideas for implementing them in a setting that I created with my friends here, not because I actually want to play in Transit City.

    In my mind, the way most D&D products work is ass-backwards. What you want, in my mind, is to inspire people to create their own super cool stuff and provide them tools that allow them to do that in a way that is satisfying and effective at the table. Setting stuff can do this, sure, but, as we've discussed here recently, setting is most inspiring when it's a largely unfilled map that sets out the boundaries of a playground in which players can let their own creations loose. Sure, it may feel a bit less exciting as a game designer to not have hundreds of folks roaming around an imaginary place that you created, sending you emails because they're demanding that you write a product about secret society XYZ, but, honestly, I think there really is something in teaching folks to fish instead of just selling them fish.

    Ultimately, I'm more interested in doing awesome stuff the way that you do awesome stuff, without doing the exact same awesome stuff that you're doing, if that makes sense. Just my 2 cents.

    \n Logos, thanks for your comments! Lemme try to address them without cutnpaste madness.

    After a week, the city isn't idyllic at all. The streets are clogged with jungle, monsters are hunting people, and some of the people are transforming into magical superheroes. Yes, everyone starts reacting to the metrocalyptic events that occurred! The city changes.

    I probably won't save the PHB races for natives. My goal is that any D&D character will fit in this world. Really, the weirder you are, the more you have to role-play about your transformation process, all the while kicking ass and taking names. This is still a typical D&D game with a focus on combat. The backgrounds just offer some grist for the role-playing mill. I do not at all envision this as some kind of hippie-storygaming drift of 4E!

    The barrier does stop people from getting out. The hunting occurs in the city. Since the jungle flooded the streets, there's regular fauna and flora for the hunters and gatherers to eat. There's just also, you know, orcs and giant spiders to deal with, too. Yes, the population is reduced almost to nothing. Normal people (assume they are minions, statwise) are no match for monsters. I do see a huge potential for adventure in trying to get out of the city through holes in the barrier (perhaps the barrier doesn't go underground...)

    Yeah, there are points of light. You have the gist of it. A secure temple whose gods (or someone) now actually answers prayers. The guard armory. Even an old city block that happens to be pretty defensible, even if it's largely run by the old organized crime syndicate. These are points of light in the city.

    \n As a potential player in this game, you already know I think it's awesome. Though it will mean revising my ideas, I kind of like the idea of rooting it in an actual city, especially one that something mysterious actually happened to. Combining that with D&D sort of puts me in mind of the re-tellings of folk tales in Hellboy (the comics, rather than the movie). However, I'd say that I'd want it to be done in a way that didn't require me to read 700 pages of history - a one sheet with the "hollywood" version of the history would be ideal for me. I'll read up on a lot of stuff that I'm interested in for its own sake, but I'd have trouble getting into doing a lot of research on say, some city-state of Italy that you were jazzed about. It'd be cool, but only if you do most of the work.

    As a potential buyer... I think it'd be hard to sell me on a setting, or even setting elements. I think of, e.g., OBE's Shroud stuff - a lot of it is cool, but I've pretty much totally ignored the Shroud concept. There are *some* settings that have grabbed me enough to make me want to play them (for me, that would pretty much be Dark Sun and Eberron), but the sort of half-way "world seed" setting-bit idea seems harder to grab on to.

    What I would be more jazzed about is something like *functional* bits of setting. So, OBE's Unbroken is awesome in my mind, not b/c it has anything to do with the Shroud, but because it's a "Paladin who has lost his faith but does not seek redemption," which is a great character archetype. I would be much more likely to buy a $2 product that was just billed as "100 awesome monsters from a savage jungle" or "14 bastions of civilization against an encroaching wilderness - with interesting interactive battle maps," without much in the way of explicit setting material (or whose setting material was brief and ignorable so I wasn't feeling like I was paying half of that $2 for stuff I was never going to use).

    I could also get interested in a line of "a real city adapted to a fantasy world" products, especially ones from non-standard cultures (for fantasy RPGs), like Russia, or South Asia, or Africa (I will buy all your Africa world books, even the ones that suck). Especially if they were written in such a way that they could be used either as, "here's a city for your Mythic Africa campaign, that was always fantasy-ish" *or* as, "here's an African city ripped through space-time to a fantasy world." E.g., there could be lots of overlap - fantasy monsters, how PHB races might be interpreted in cultural terms ("Dragonborn will be revered, because the snake is a symbol of wisdom"), etc. - but also some points of difference having to do with the trauma of re-location vs. things always being that way.

    Also, pogs. I want sheets of pogs that represent stuff that's hard to find with Google Image Search, like African paladins.
