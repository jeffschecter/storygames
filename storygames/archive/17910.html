\n I'm looking for a system that you think works well for a campaign (5 sessions minimum). Genre is unimportant, simply looking for a game that works better as a multi-session campaign than a one-shot. There are a few parameters:
- No D&D, Pathfinder, and the like
- None of the Apocalypse World or assorted hacks

My goal is to discover an overshadowed quality system that deserves to be played and can provide a fun campaign. Don't worry about the demographics or interests of my group. I want to know what *you* enjoy. Also, please just don't drop a title. Share your thoughts about it, I want to know what you love about the system and maybe even some anecdotes about your experiences running/playing in a campaign.

    \n Primetime Adventures is one of those games that only really works over a longer campaign, and it's super fun. It's a very well-written rule book that really boils serial TV narrative down to a clear and easy to play formula. It's all about embracing the tropes and form of the medium - theme songs, cliffhangers, flashbacks, etc. The system encourages character change over time, and keeps your series fresh from season to season.

    \n Such a quirky request.

    The campaign-length games I've been playing this winter are Solar System and Delta Green. The former you probably know (The Shadow of Yesterday, etc.), the latter is basically rules-free conspiracy horror.

    The thing I enjoy about Solar System is that it's got all the modern tools in a flexible universal framework. This means that we can invent our own, ambitious game setting and apply the system to it. There's creative danger to this, you don't know how things are going to fall out in advance. I imagine that GURPS-players get similar support out of their chosen system, although of course the specific utility is quite different.

    The thing I enjoy about Delta Green is that it's a very serious and elaborate setting. The systemic background is deeply problematic for me, but I enjoy wrestling with that as well; what we've been doing in practice is reinventing the Call of Cthulhu system to make it focus in the right ways for the game. It's a process of game development via play.

    Looking at this pair, it seems that I spend quite a bit of my roleplaying time in various campaigns that exist at least partially for the purpose of investigative, creative work. Even Solar System, which I play basically for fun at this stage, is predicated on exploration of formerly unknown mechanical and fluff territories.

    \n Yeah - actually D&D is a bit more long-form in terms of its mechanics than games like Delta Green/Call of Cthulhu, where you probably are doing the same things late in the campaign, just doing it better/more effectively/faster/under worse circumstances. In D&D you will be flying and laser beaming dudes.

    Burning Wheel and Burning Empires both have similar long-term mechanics that really change things up. I would add Houses of the Blooded and Blood and Honor (the second one is better/clearer but both are good.)

    It's interesting: games like FATE and Smallville support long term play by having a very "in the moment" character sheet. Your character is expected/encouraged to change over a long period, and (virtually) all advancement is tied to that. You won't be playing the same character in Smallville after a year of play, in the sense of "character" meaning beliefs, values, relationships, etc. - you just can't do it, it's not mechanically possible.

    \n Technoir really gets interesting when you introduce the 2nd or 3rd City to your story... The connections and loose ends still left hanging yet interconnected via the transmission mechanic are gold for campaign play. The characters advance in simple ways (via their verbs) which is satisfying, but its the development of the shared milieu and the NPCs within it that make the game sing.

    Oh and Burning Wheel takes character development to a whole new level of AWESOME after the first few long term beliefs are achieved.

    \n I've been running Other Worlds for almost a year now, and it's still going strong. The characters change either according to the fiction by piling up consquences, or very, very slowly in terms of experience. It's very character-centric, completely universal, and the mechanics move toward conflict resolution very quickly, but offer a few meta-resource tweaks to die rolls that makes them a little gameable. The character generation rules explicitly call for a ton of relationships and personality traits out of the gate, which offers a ton of story hooks. In practice, there's been almost too much to work with, if you can believe that.

    \n What counts as "the like" for "D&D, Pathfinder & the like"? If you mean to rule out famous traditional games, then my list is short, but if not, there's always Stormbringer, Vampire, Werewolf, RIFTS, and most things one would do with GURPS, just to sample my high school campaign playlist. Pendragon also sounds amazing from what I read here. Most games with significant character improvement are fun to take on that long arc from noob to badass.

    Indie-wise, Sign in Stranger is awesome for reasons of dramatic and rewarding change in the characters' relationship to the alien world they're exploring. Mostly in terms of discovery & understanding, but you also might get mutated.

    \n There are also a lot of campaign-based games that came out of the early Forge community, like Anima Prime (which requires campaign play, at least "by the book"), in which young children and teens fight for the survival of the Earth and the human race while dealing with their interpersonal relationships, rivalries, and romances. My Life with Master is another good example, although that's more like a "closed story arc" - similar to Primetime Adventures, it doesn't give you an open-ended campaign but something in the 5-9 sessions range.

    In a Wicked Age... is a brilliant campaign game, if you play it right. (To me, the cool stuff about the game only comes out after a handful of sessions.)

    Eero's right on with The Shadow of Yesterday/Solar System - it would be one of my top choices for a long-term campaign.

    \n I've recently become obsessed with Spellbound Kingdoms, which is formatted like a trad game but is indie to the core. However it is intended for sorta-traditional campaign play, complete with classes and levels. It could use a tad more guidance on how exactly advancement works, but the other goodies\u2014the genuinely unique Renaissance clockpunk setting, the way the system and setting are tied together, the indie-style flagging, the fun exploding dice\u2014more than make up for this small deficiency. I've run a bunch of short one-shots / combat demos, and am two sessions into a campaign with my girlfriend and another friend, and it's pretty great.
