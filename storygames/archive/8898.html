    \n Fortune in the Beginning:
    \nI don't really know what it is I'm trying to accomplish or the means by which I'm trying to accomplish it, but I can roll* and then use the system to help me answer both questions.
    \nex: Roll: Hey look I got a lot of movement options with this roll...I guess I should use those...I think I'll use them to jump to the top of the wall.
    \n
    \nFortune at the End:
    \nI know exactly what I'm trying to accomplish and I know exactly the means I'm using to accomplish it. After describing both and acquiring any pertinent modifiers I roll to see how well I did.
    \nex: I want to get to the top of the wall. I'm going to use my Climbing skill to do so. I get bonuses for my rope and climbing gear. Now I Roll.
    \n
    \nFortune in the Middle:
    \nI know in a general sense what I'm trying to accomplish, but I don't specifically know the precise means I'm using to accomplish it. After stating my general goal I roll, and on the basis of that roll we'll decide how it happens.
    \nex: I want to get to the top of the wall. Ok I Roll, barely made it. So we know I made it to the top of the wall, but how in the fiction does that happen. Let's say that I threw a rope over one of the crenellations and just as I was pulling myself over the top the rope broke, and I had to stuggle to get up the last bit.
    \n
    \n
    \n*where "roll" here means "engage in whatever mechanical randomizing system the particular game uses".

    \n From the Forge Glossary:\n
    Employing a Fortune Resolution technique (dice, cards, etc) prior to fully describing the specific actions of, physical placement of, and communication among characters. The Fortune outcome is employed in establishing these elements retroactively. This technique may be employed with the dice/etc as the ultimate authority of success or failure (e.g. Sorcerer) or with the dice/etc outcome being potentially adjusted by a metagame mechanic (e.g. HeroQuest)
    \nI cannot believe that people think they can describe this better than the Forge glossary, which is by definition correct because it is the Forge glossary.

    Posted By: Nathan HWhat's a metagame mechanic?
    And how is that different than a game mechanic?
    \nThat's an excellent question, Nathan.

    I thought I could answer that. I thought I was like "oh, I know what metagame is." All mechanics are metagame, aren't they? The assumption is that "game" in this regard means "in-character" decision making. All mechanical decision making is metagame, technically. However, the confusion comes in that we use the term "game mechanic" to describe the rules of the "game." Metagame mechanic might be a little redundant in reflection of the term "game mechanics." All game mechanics are metagame.

    Another option would be to consider metagame to not be game mechanics, rather social and real life considerations. So to make a metagame decision you're making it based on real life such as "I'll wait until Jon goes to the bathroom to talk with his in-game adversary." Or, "I know the GM wants us to have fun and worked hard at this adventure so I should bite at plot hooks."

    I guess, there could be mechanics for that (like flags). The question becomes when that is turned into a mechanic is it still metagame?

    Interesting,
    - Don

    \n Story games have blurred the line between game and meta game mechanic.
    \n
    \nTraditionally a game mechanic is a mechanic that impacts the system in a way that is consistant with the fiction. Its designed to represent the impact of some actual in-game (in-fiction) thing. When you roll your climbing skill to climb a wall that's a game mechanic because in the fiction it makes sense for your character's ability to climb to impact how well they climb the wall. Similarly getting a bonus for having a rope is a game mechanic because of course having a rope would help you climb.
    \n
    \nA metagame mechanic is one that impacts the system because the player (who exists outside the fiction) wants it to, there may be some attempt at a fictional justification, but most often its "just because"
    \n
    \nFor instance, earlier when you were fighting the duke you made a witty quip and were rewarded with a hero point. Now you've failed your climbing roll and decide to spend your hero point to reroll. What causal relationship did your earlier witty quip have to your ability to climb? None...its just because...ergo...its a metagame mechanic.
    \n
    \n
    \nOne of my favorite blurry ones is PTA fanmail. Is it meta? After all, why does some other player liking what I said an hour ago have to do with my ability to succeed now. On the other hand...if you view PTA as a game within a game, with the over-game being the production of a tv show than one can imagine a character who receives alot of fan interest being given a bigger role on the show...so maybe its an in-fiction causal thing after all...

    \n Despite my tongue-in-cheekness, I do think the Forge Glossary definition is very good. Paraphrasing only a little, you get: "Rolling dice before you fully describe what happens". That's pretty good, I think.

    Fortune at the End is the method used in traditional roleplaying games:

    1. I want to pick the lock.
    2. I roll to see whether I pick the lock.
    3. If I succeed, I pick the lock, like we decided before the roll.

    Fortune in the Middle is more like:

    1. I want to get into the castle.
    2. I roll to see whether I get into the castle.
    3. OK, now how exactly did that happen?

    Fortune in the Beginning is something more of a problematic term, in my interpretation. It's:

    1. Roll dice!
    2. OK, now what do the dice tell us about what's going to happen?

    It's problematic because it's essentially Fortune in the Middle: there's always something established before the dice roll, then the dice tell you how it changes. It's used very effectively in Bacchanal.

    To come back on Andy's point: as I understand it, Psi Run and Otherkind aren't the only real examples. Fortune in the Middle occurs in any game where the dice don't decide exactly what happens. So that's Dogs, My Life With Master and just about any indie game you can think of.

    Graham

    \n It's probably worth noting that "metagame mechanic" is outmoded in Forge jargon. These days, us hip Forgies call 'em "Positioning mechanics." Which relates to "Positioning" as a component of Character (along with Effectiveness and Resource). Basically, "Positioning mechanics" allow the player to adjust Positioning outside of the character's scope of effect (e.g. unallocated relationship dice in Dogs), and/or convert Positioning into Effectiveness (e.g. Artha in the Burning Wheel).

    \n We've been playing Pathfinder with FatB in full swing.

    1. Roll a d20.
    2. Use this information to figure out what you'll be doing this round.
    3. Narrate your badassery.

    I've enforced it, too. "I'm going-" "Tsch! Roll first, then tell me." It's made a world of difference in making d20 a universally pleasant experience. The two gamists in our pack love being able to plan for a better tactical situation, and the two hardline narrativists really appreciate a tarot-like direction in their options that round. I might splice this into another thread, because I'm really interested in seeing if others have tried this or something similar to it in a trad-game.
