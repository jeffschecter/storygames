Why I design games without playing games

Adam_Dray
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games
Yeah, the topic name is intentionally inflammatory to get your attention. Ha. The fact is, I totally agree that play experience (and specifically, playtesting) is, like, the most important thing I can do as a game designer.

I want to talk about all the stupid fucking rationalizations I and other people use to justify sitting around and writing armchair RPGs.

I don't have time to play!
Yeah, but I have plenty of time to fuck around on Story Games and The Forge and RPGNet and LiveJournal/Blogspot. I apparently have enough time to twiddle with my dice mechanics and rewrite my rulebook for the ninth time.

I ran a one-hour demo of my game, Verge, for some fellow Forge Twunts at a small con in North Carolina a few months ago. I got valuable playtest feedback out of it. That made me realize that I don't need to run a 6-hour Saturday game to playtest. Maybe I could have one or two friends over on a weeknight, eat some pizza, and game for an hour or two.

I can't find people to play my game!
Whine. If I can't find people to game at all, because I live in Montana or Nunavut or something, but I have Internet access, there are ways to play games online. If I have a regular gaming group or know a bunch of Vampire or D&D players, but I can't convince them to give my weird indie game a whirl, why should I expect anyone else in the world to play it?

At the very least, post a link to your PDF on the Forge or something and ask people to play it for you. If your game is grabby enough, someone will try it.

But it's not done yet!
And it never will be, at this rate, huh? I need to get my ass off LiveJournal and finish it. I have to put a "good enough" version out there on the net for people to read. Why isn't it done? Probably procrastination. Why am I procrastinating? Oh, the usual reasons: fear it isn't good enough, stuck on some aspect of the reward system, can't figure out how to explain the rules properly, rehashing and rewriting till my fingers bleed.

The best cure for all of those things is playtesting. It isn't good enough? Playtest and figure out why. My playtesters know it isn't done, or it wouldn't be playtesting. I'm stuck on some aspect of the rules? Playtest and figure out how to fix it. I can't figure out how to explain the rules in your manuscript? Playtest and explain the rules to a complete noob. I'll come away with a better idea of what is important to understand in my game. Rehashing and rewriting? I must be stuck on something or maybe I don't understand it well enough. Cure for that is -- yes, that's right -- playtesting.

What other dumb excuses do we use to justify armchair design, absent of playtesting, absent of playing other role-playing games?

    The surest way to have lots of bad gaming experiences is to play lots of different games with lots of different people. Playing one RPG with the same group you've been gaming with for twenty years isn't going to make you a great designer.

    And a "bad gaming experience" doesn't have to mean that the game is interrupted by a fistfight. Maybe most of the game that afternoon was good except for this one part where this particular rule just totally sucked. Inspiration results.

    Should we intentionally subject ourselves to bad gaming experiences? Well, no, not intentionally. How about we subject ourselves to diverse gaming experiences -- and some bad ones will probably result, but that's not the goal -- and the diversity is what gives us a broader perspective on design.

    When all you've seen is Ford minivans, you're probably not going to design a Volvo sedan or a Ferrari sports car or a VW Beetle or a Mac truck. You'll probably design shorter four-door minivans, really fast minivans with fins, cute little round minivans, and giant minivans for hauling freight.

    If you have perfect gaming sessions, why do you need a new game? Well, you might have the perfect Fantasy gaming sessions, but still need a good SF or Horror game. If you have the perfect game for all gaming you do, you probably won't design any more games. Other people might hate the game you use though, so they'll still design games. Not every game appeals to every person, no matter how well it is designed.

    Let's face it, this is about science and the scientific method. We are game scientists.

    Characterizations
    We measure. We observe. We quantify. Mostly we observe. Mostly we observe in actual play of game X. Something doesn't go right and we observe, "Wow, this really doesn't work in game X." Maybe the problem is the game rules don't do what they're supposed to. Maybe the problem is that the game rules don't do some other thing it was never supposed to, but is the kind of experience you really wanted.

    Hypotheses
    We make some wild-ass guess, maybe even an informed guess, about why X doesn't do Y. Call it a hypothesis. Call it a crazy idea.

    Predictions
    We reason. We think. We reflect. We say, wow, Y happened because X didn't do Z. Or X is going to cause Y to happen, but game W won't. Maybe W is a new game idea.

    Experiments
    We play. Actual play. We play our new game W or X-prime or whatever. Does Y still happen or does Z happen? We test our hypothesis. We tell people what we found. We iterate.

    There's a reason we do this. It's because Ron's a fucking biologist and he lives and breathes this stuff and he tricked us into being game scientists with the structure of the Forge because he's a fucking genius. And there are days that I want to shake my fist at him for something he says or does, but then I take a hard look at myself and I realize why I'm getting all *shakefisty* and I admit he's right. God dammit. I put my game religion aside (not forever, but for the moment) and go back to the game science.

    Observation, hypotheses, logic, experimentation.

    January 2006
    Much depends on the order you place those elements - as listed, they're pretty much in descending order of how applicable scientific theory will be. if you view it as engineering first and foremost, then scientific method becomes a powerful tool, but if you view it as theatre first and foremost, scientific method becomes an interesting afterthought.

    This is going to get very rambly, so hang on tight.

    Anyway, there's a definition gap to bridge. The scientific method can, probably _eventually_ do anything that any other model can (and vice versa) if only through simple persistence. If you keep trying things you will eventually hit on _something_. And, frankly, if that's all the standard it needs to be held to then sure, rah rah, go scientific method.

    Assuming we're a little bit more demanding, then we can say that the scientific method is fairly, for lack of a better word, methodical. It's approach is thorough and incremental and it depends on having metrics that can be measured (because without them, any determination of which is a better outcome is just a judgement call, and if that's all we're doing, then cool, but best not call ti scientific). Now, god knows that we're all big fans of inventing entirely bullshit pseudoscientific metrics and terminology, but people have been paid far more than us to measure whether a game is any fun, and still suck at it.

    "But are we measuring fun?" one might ask. I, naturally, say "Of course we are, what the hell else are we looking for if not that?" This is hard, of course, because you can't measure fun, and it's hard to codify the way you can certain design goals or even those slices of fun one may peel off, like "engagement" or "energy". Thing is, ultimately, the standard by which one comes up with those metrics is the same standard by which they are judged, and in terms of pure process, that's dirty pool.

    So if the question is instead about something with the trappings of scientific method, but is really just a way to make "my good judgement" sound more authoritative, that's totally cool. Viva la pseudoscience.

    But if you really are sticking to that which can be measured and incremental improvement, the weaknesses should be as obvious as the strengths. It's harder to discard an entire branch of thought based on gut. It's hard to throw in an entirely new idea since you can't fairly metric it yet. It can't measure fun, only its shadow.

    And here's where we hit the real rub: is this art or science?

    It's probably both, depending on who you are and what you're trying to do, but the danger emerges (as it always does) when science says "Can you scientifically prove that art is the better path than science?" (as it does when art asks "That's nice, but is it art?"). If you accept the premises that make scientific method the best possible tool, then every test you run will prove it is so, and again, dirty pool.

    So let me change the criteria to something a little more answerable then - how much does a given design _trust_ a given GM, and by trust I mean expect that the GM will take the game and make it his own.

    The scientifically proven game is very low trust by its nature because it has _science_. It's the _best possible game_ for this, at least by all of its own standards. Given that, the question is whether this _should_ be a standard.

    There's an underlying assumption in a lot of games that they're finished. That they should be played according to the text because that is the _best_ way to play them.

    And _that_. That right there. That is not designing to people. That is designing to the game. The goal is a great game for people, but it makes no consideration for who those people might be.

    So what's the alternative? Clearly we can't design games to be customized to each individual player. It's simply impossible. However, every _GM_ has this ability, so the question then becomes "Can we give him the tools to make the very best game _for him and his group_ (with all the potential chance for failure that comes with this) or do we want to give him the best damn game we can make and remove that chance of failure?"

    There is a real difference between those games. One is sloppy, one is restrictive. Both work, but does one work better than the other? I dunno, but I'll certainly say that one is more popular locally. :)

    Part of the problem here is that we have this crazy idea in our heads that playtests should be fun. They shouldn't. The ideal playtest is miserable, awkward, and painful for all involved.

    Why?

    Because the goal of playtests is not to have fun. The goal of playtests is to find problems with the system. If you have fun, you're likely to overlook problems -> failed playtest.

    Thus, it is best to start playtest as soon as your game is at all possible to play. I take Shreyas's IRC Torchbearer playtest -- where the game broke down completely before the first conflict even finished -- as an ideal. Why? Because it gave him a clear idea about what needed to be done with the game. Miserable game, good playtest.

    Whereas my recent Bliss Stage playtest was fun, and confirmed that I'm doing some things right, but didn't light a huge fire under me about what needs fixin'. I've got one or two bits out of it, but that's it. Good game, so-so playtest.
