\n Here is the dungeoncrawl game I want to play some day.

Everyone creates a character with a particular speciality. There's the brave leader, the lithe climber, the careful healer, the studious journalist and the romantically-challenged expedition sponsor. Each has a different set of skill focuses (ranging from morale boosts to scaling sheer cliffs to buying new supplies).

In addition, everyone has a cultural background. Maybe that brave leader is a member of Polish royalty, and the careful healer is a native to the Himalayas, and the studious journalist is from the Far East. Or perhaps this is a fantasy setting, and lithe climber is descendant of the Tundra Elves, and the brave leader comes from the Besiegers' Empire, and the romantically-challenged expedition sponsor is a Dragonkin from the Serra.

And then you take these people, with their specialties and their backgrounds, and send them on expeditions through dark caverns and treacherous mountains. And they encounter there difficult situations, like food shortages and difficult passes and unstable ravines. Occassionally they even encounter a wild beast or a madman hermit. These situations are some of the most difficult, as they require a lot of communication, bartering and even compromise.

Ethical and moral decisions will enter play, threatening group camaraderie. When the expedition sponsor is near death, ill, do you forge on or retreat? What if he commands that his money not be wasted, even if the trip costs his life?

From one expedition, these adventurers may go on to find another. Some do it to earn money, some to spend it. Some are there for the physical challenge, and some for the scientific discoveries. Together, they are an eccentric and ragtag group brought together for a tactically-challenging adventure which repeatedly challenges peoples assertions about themselves, their skills, and why they came.

Oh, and there are no dragons.

    \n Johnstone,
    This isn't really anything out of the ordinary. I was just thinking about how in dungeoncrawl games, the focus is always put on the dragons. Never the dungeons.

    And then I started thinking about how if the challenges were all static, it would allow for the characters to be dynamic. (Rather than having static characters facing dynamic conflicts). And I paired that thought with the fact that in real world explorations, archaeological digs, spelunking, etc... there is always a lot of time spent planning, setting up tents, conversing, mapping, doing laborious tasks... And as a result what you would have would be situations where characters were challenged by the crossroads of their task and their being. The interesting dilemmas that would arise in a dungeoncrawl without the hackable, slashable villains would be ones that threatened a characters' understanding of motivation, skill, team and task.

    And I was thinking about how cool that dungeoncrawling game would be, compared to what I might perceive as the "status quo". Plus, I think a game about adventuring against nature would make an awesome teen game.
