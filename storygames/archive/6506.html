\n We did a round of these last year and I know that, eventually, at least one game was published as a result.

Choose a designer and challenge them to write a game that, in some way, you think would be good for them to write. Or that they should write. Or that would be a useful challenge for them to write. Something like that. Look at the games they've written already and go: ah, yes, this is what I'd like to see them write next.

If you're the designer, you're welcome to ignore the challenge, take it up, or merely let it sit in your brain bugging you for the next ten years. Which it will.

(It's understood that this thread is merely a brainstorming thread, a source of ideas, and that anything that comes out of it will need to be playtested, revised, etc, etc.)

I'll now demonstrate the correct way of phrasing a challenge.

Jason Morningstar! You write historical games that focus on small groups of ordinary people (c.f. Grey Ranks, Night Witches).

I challenge you to write a historical game focussing on the decision makers! For example, a game that portrays the Russian Revolution, the Great Leap Forward or the Second World War through the eyes of the people who made it happen!

Graham

    \n I'd just like to say: I finished one of my game designer challenges, which was issued by Jonathan Walton. The result is Bleeding Omaha, a game about struggling to find poetic beauty in the Omaha music scene during the '90s. It involves writing and performing songs as part of play, and the physical product is fantastic.

    I'm also working, IN SECRET, on another challenge I received.

    So very much props for initiating this last year. And again this year.

    Guy Shalev: You write games that are quite morose and bleak, with a competitive bite to them. I want you to crank that up a notch and write a game which is intentionally over-the-top melodramatic, filled with character.vs.character savagery. Bonus points if you include the phrase "Monologues of Infinite Bleakness" in the game.

    Ogre: I challenge you to write a game that captures the bizarre, satirical, retro-future feel of Brazil, City of Lost Children, and to a lesser degree A Clockwork Orange, Starship Troopers (the movie), and The Truman Show. Bonus points if you have mechanics based on the volume of your voice. Why? Because I think you can pull off the cheesy element, and the serious-content-with-whimsical-delivery.

    Tony Dowler: I challenge you to write a game that requires asking questions as part of conflict resolution, and that makes both the questions and the answers mechanically meaningful. I think this would be good for you because... I don't know, I see you as this really curious and collaborative person. And if I'm on the mark there, then I'd like to see you build a game that rewards those qualities. Bonus points if the game uses an egg timer.

    I will critique, playtest and support any game that is written out of one of those challenges.

      \nNathan: I'm slowly pondering a game about Blackbeard's brief retirement. He was good friends with NC governor Charles Eden and accepted a royal pardon at some point in the early 1700s, settling down with his 14th wife just across the bay from the governor's estate. There, he entertained local visitors with tales of his exploits and was a frequent guest at social occasions all along the coast. But Virgina governor Alexander Spotswood wasn't too happy about having such a notable pirate hanging around, plus Blackbeard eventually got up to some of his old tricks, having become bored at home or just vexed by the desire for more wealth and bloodletting. In the end, Spotswood hired a few privateers to track Blackbeard down and kill him, royal pardon or not.

      I'm not sure what the game's about yet, exactly, but I like the idea of doing a game about pirates trying -- and failing -- to live normal, civilized lives. Like a pirate version of Buffalo Bill's Wild West and Congress of Rough Riders of the World, but taking place over storytelling sessions in parlors of the colonial gentry, in stark contrast to their past exploits raiding ships along the coast. Maybe there could be a kind of tension revealed through flashbacks, where all the excitement would take place? And then there would be the present life, with all the mundaneness? I guess you'd take the role of Blackbeard's infamous crewmembers, like Israel Hands, Black Caesar, and the like.

      Still pondering.
