A note on play styles:

While playing a role-playing game, you and your fellow players, including the GM, will create an imaginary world that you share. It is something like the board in a board game, or the tabletop battle map in a war game, only much more vibrant. That is because instead of creating a visual representation of that world, you will imagine it, together, by describing it\u2014through conversation about the world, through acting out the characters in it, and describing where they are, what they do and say, and sometimes, even what they think and feel. When you describe your characters in action, you will need to place them in context, and to do that, you need to create, expand, or utilize that shared imaginary world, which becomes the setting of your game.

Not all games ask you to imagine that setting in the same way. I don't mean whether it's a fantasy game or a sci-fi game. That's genre. Role-playing is such a vast and flexible medium, there is room to imagine your setting as an environment, or a narrative.

Because role-playing developed out of war games, the original conception of the setting was as an environment. A place, or a location. A virtual environment, actually, before video games introduced them to the world. Players took on the roles of characters in that environment, and moved through it, exploring it. When players imagined events or characters in that setting, they judged them based on how they imagined their setting worked. If they thought that something was not functioning according to the way they thought it should function in that environment, it was not considered fair. It broke the constraints of the game.

Over time, another style developed, that of story. Instead of imagining the setting as an environment, people began to imagine their characters moving through a story. Instead of asking "would that happen in this environment?" they started asking "is that right for this story?" This type of setting allows players to use different play techniques.

Let's take an example. Say you've just been reading a Raymond Chandler novel, and it's got you all excited. You want to play something in Chandler's style! But there's a substantial difference between mid-20th century Los Angeles as a setting, and a Raymond Chandler novel as a setting. You can only play mid-20th century Los Angeles as an environmental setting. What would happen in mid-20th century LA? Who would be there? What kinds of things would the see and do? There is no one story to a city, only the place. To get that Raymond Chandler feel, you have to think of your setting as a story. Since the only things that are appropriate in a Raymond Chandler novel are the things that Raymond Chandler wrote, you have to judge events and characters by whether they are appropriate for those stories, not whether they fit into mid-20th century LA. Chandler's novels may have been set there, but they were not typical examples of what happened in Los Angeles, ever.

Now, if you were to play in mid-20th century LA, you don't necessarily need to have characters from that place and time. You can put a robot or an alien into that setting, still thinking "what would happen to a robot or an alien in mid-20th century LA?" without ever considering why that would be interesting. A story setting tends to bring the players closer together. They have to decide on what kind of story they are playing, and what it means to them, and for their characters. Players have to ask, not only "what would my character do in this situation?" but also "what would my character do in this story? and "is that right for the story?" Naturally, some players will feel this is too much added responsibility, while others will find it easier than maintaining the authenticity needed for a great environmental setting.

Flowers in the Fall demands a story setting. There is some leeway here, and of course, you are still expected to create a colourful, immersive world for your characters to inhabit. But the way opposition is structured is meant to give a sense of dramatic momentum, while player characters relate to the GM's supporting cast in ways that are determined by their place in the story, not their place within an environment. Characters are intimately connected with the setting, because it revolves around them.

Because play is focused on the players' characters, and specifically their struggle against the forces threatening the world they live in, the game plays better when you think in terms of story arcs, scenes, and dramatic tension. The game might not be exactly like your favourite novels, but when you play with them in mind, you can come pretty close.

    \n Who is your audience? You've combined the What is Roleplaying? section with some game theory/playstyle information, and it seems to be presented as if to people entirely new to roleplaying. If it's intended for newcomers, they don't need to know about other styles of play, they just need instructions on how to play this particular game. Adding descriptions of other ways to play just confuses things. If the text is intended for experienced gamers, they don't need explanations of roleplaying, but they do need to be told, clearly and directly, that your game won't work well with traditional, character-centered play.

    For either audience, you've cluttered the explanation with unnecessary information.

    To answer your questions directly:

    It makes sense, but it is not particularly clear.

    I don't think it would help me to play your game if I didn't already know the difference between traditional roleplay and story-oriented roleplay.

    It is too long. I think some of the information is extraneous, and I think it needs to be more direct.

    I think you'd be better off asking and answering a series of questions, with the questions as headings. Good questions might be:

    What kind of game is this?
    Do I always have to make realistic choices with my character?
    What if the best thing for the story isn't necessarily what my character would do?
