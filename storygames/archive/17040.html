    \n Cool! I don't get hurt feelings about stuff like this, so no sweat there. Also, here's some unabashed praise: I think that Misery Tourism Games is really cool. I dig your mission statement, your games are interesting and provocative, and I hope you have serious success. This shouldn't surprise anybody at all.

    When you design a game, you naturally have an audience in mind, and you design a game that you think they'll be drawn to. What do you do if your audience doesn't seize upon the game you've designed for them?

    You have to figure out (a) whether your game can hold their attention in the first place, and if you're confident it can, (b) how to get it there. It's competing for your audience's attention with other games, and they enjoy some advantages yours don't - they always do! - so you have to figure out how to make your game competitive. You can't expect your audience to indulge you or your fellow designers to give you a break, that's not their job, it's yours.

    Complaining at your audience for not paying attention to the games you wish they would (including yours) is a marketing technique you can use if you want, but I've never seen it work well. I can tell you that when I tried it on behalf of a friend's game, here, it didn't seem to help. I think it made more bad will than good.

    So that's my thought: you can't expect an audience to stop liking what they like. In the case of excellent games like Monsterhearts (ha ha go frack yourselves!) you shouldn't expect them to. No, to win an audience over, you have to win it over.

    -Vincent

    \n This seems like a self-correcting problem, to be honest. Cut-and-paste jobs aren't going to be popular, because they aren't interesting. But because he knew exactly what he was trying to design, he was able to copy on the rare occasions where it was possible, and innovate when necessary.     \n 2012:
    "Oh no, not ANOTHER Apocalypse World hack! Why can't everyone be more creative?"

    Back in 2007, one could've said:
    "Oh no, not ANOTHER stakes-setting game! Why can't everyone be more creative?"

    Or 1994:
    "Oh no, not ANOTHER stat+ability resolution game! Why can't everyone be more creative?"

    Or 1984:
    "Oh no, not ANOTHER D&D-like game that substitutes different arbitrary charts from Gygax's! Why can't everyone be more creative?"

    In running Monsterhearts, I've discovered an entirely different game emergent than that of AW: one that rewards failure, insecurity and impulsive behavior.

    I think our main issue with this current pattern in TRPG design is that it has a specific point of origin \u2013 Vincent Baker's Apocalypse World \u2013 and that hey! Vincent's just an ordinary bloke like you, me and your brother. What's so special about him?

    But AW is such an intuitively useful tool in understanding TRPG design that we've learned a lot by seeing it in action, and generate Internet activity around it.

    So fans of the pattern are using it to reboot/reinvent/restart genres which have been otherwise subjected to conventional mechanical solutions, while detractors focus on the fact that these reboots seem not to take the genre on its own mechanical terms beyond those of AW.

    But Vincent appears to loom behind a framework that Joe McDalno, Adam Koebel and Sage LaTorra, Jonathan Walton, Elizabeth Sampat and, y'know, many others are creatively using to problem-solve player agency in genre TRPG interaction. These are mere appearances \u2013 you can still design whatever you want.

    This AW-dominated indie development era too shall pass.

    \n@Johnstone has some really good points about Internet positioning strategy. At the same time, I do sympathize with you @whduryea about the underexposure dilemma. It turns out that we actually do exist in a star system, which is often counter-productive at circulating new ideas and alternative content. New things that come from the stars have an aura about them; new things from everyone else is either filtered out as noise or compared with the shadow of the stars' games. That being said, it's a sometimes very generous community, and people will definitely go to bat for you if you're willing to be transparent about your agendas, etc.

    Also I've learned that occasionally low people on the totem pole have way more agency over their fate than they think.
