MikeRM requested that this be spun off to its own thread, which is fair so that others can suggest their own ideas for awesomifying Dark Sun on that thread. This thread will be focused on the "bowl of beads" system we've been discussing. To recap:
There is a bowl of beads in the center of the table. At the beginning of the session, these are all blue beads representing the water in a community and the raw potential it holds. You pull handfuls of beads when you want to do something.

You want to do X. X calls for one color: black, white, or red.

You pull out some beads, and if the appropriate color predominates, you get what you want. If it doesn't, but the color PLUS BLUE predominates, then you can turn those blue beads into the appropriate color to get what you want. You can use the runners-up to generate various effects, like fallout.

Once blue beads are counted for a different color, say red, the blue beads in your hand are removed from the bowl and replaced with an equal number of actual red beads. This represents the water being polluted, metaphorically and perhaps literally.
None of this is set in stone, but the primary points of discussion I'm curious about are:

So let's say this is something like Dogs in the Vineyard and each session has a larger overarcing goal, like saving a town, and this big bowl of blue beads represents the unresolved situation. As the group does their thing, the blue is replaced with other colors until there is no blue left. At that point, the session is over.

Would it be apropos for the group to collect those beads in some sort of experience mechanic or is that tying too closely to the D&D roots?

It may be useful to define the number of colors the system uses and the actions (verbs), methods (adverbs), motivations (adverbs), and/or stats (adjectives) that those colors represent.

    There are 'experiences' in a lot of games.

    It occurs to me that you could build a pretty straightforward, um, death spiral effect into the pool system:

    At the end of every (structure unit), count up the red, black, and white stones. Subtract the smallest number from each colour. Then add blue until you restore the size of the original pool.

    That way, you can do something different and clean up your pool, but it resists you; it's easier to go with the flow.

    You might want to rethink that 'blue stones are a mission timer' thing too; it is problematic for two reasons: You have to look in the bag to do it, and it's pretty strongly unpredictable.
