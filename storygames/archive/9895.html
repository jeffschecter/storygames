Does great con play = a great game

\n
\n
\n
\n\nValamir \n\n\n

\n\nJuly 2009 \n edited July 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Here's an idea that's been bouncing around in my head: the trustworthyness of actual play reports from cons as playtests.
\n
\nSome background. So I've been to Dreamation a couple of times now and just attended GPNW. From what I can tell of Jiffy Con, Nerdly, Go Play Beach Party et.al. the same is probably true of them. What I've noticed is you have tables full of amazing story gamers coming together with the expectation of having great fun with each other at one of the few times many of them get to get together to play. And invariably...they do. They do have great play, and they write about what great play they had in AP reports.
\n
\nBut I got to wondering...given those rarified circumstances...wouldn't they have had great play, playing...pretty much anything. I mean these are some of the most incredible improvisational roleplayers on the planet. You could pretty much give them the crappiest set of rules ever written and as long as they can come up with a compelling initial situation they'll find a way to have a fun time...because that's what they're there to do. They know how to make characters interesting. They know how to recognize compelling conflicts. They know how to engage in dramatic and interesting dialog. They're going to do that regardless...cuz that's what they do.
\n
\nSo here comes a designer with a new game design. And its the new hotness. And its getting some play at these story games cons. And the AP reports are fantastic good fun.
\n
\nSo here's the discussion I've been having with myself in my head that I now wish to have with you.
\n
\nAre those experiences really valid data points for the designer to use when judging how solid their design is? Does the specific situation of con play where people are interested in having fun playing with people they don't normally get to play with automatically disqualify the session from being a real "play test". In other words, are those sessions too much "play" and not enough "test" to count? How easy is it to distinguish when the talent around the table is making the play work inspite of the rules instead of because of them? When you're just playing to maximize fun with your friends are you self aware enough to recognize when you're actually using the rules and when you're just making it work because having fun improv roleplaying is what you do?
\n
\nTo what extent then do amazing AP reports from such conventions have to be taken with a grain of salt because they are not representational of typical play circumstances?
\n
\nThoughts?

    Posted By: ValamirHow easy is it to distinguish when the talent around the table is making the play work inspite of the rules instead of because of them?
    \nI don't believe that experience and talent invalidate the test: if you were making a new kind of cello and got Yo-Yo Ma to play it and report back to you, would you scoff at his report because he's so good that he can play a shitty cello and make it sound awesome?

    No, of course you wouldn't. Because Yo-Yo Ma knows what makes a good cello: he has the experience and he has the talent to make an honest, expert appraisal of the instrument's worth. If he plays it and says it's good, that means something.

    So if you're a game designer and you hand your baby over to people you think are great players, and they come back and say "Yes, this was a really good game," you should be pleased. Because I promise you, if they've got the talent and the experience, they'll know what's making the game more fun and what's working against the fun. If your rules suck, they will tell you (assuming they're giving you a playtest report and not just being nice 'cause they don't want to say mean things on the internet), and then they will tell you what they did to have fun DESPITE your suck-ass rules. And you can use that information to repair your mistakes and incorporate any useful feedback they gave you.


    The flip side to this is that games are more a matter of taste than a cello is. A game which is roundly loved by your story games cognoscenti may be a total flop when played by every other gaming group on the planet. Getting your game played by as many different groups as possible under as many different conditions as possible will give you more useful information than just reading the Big Story Games Hootenanny Actual Play report. Maybe you don't want to make the game that appeals only to them: maybe you want to put in rules they think are unnecessary, but which other gamers in other places will find invaluable. It's up to you how much you want to cater to their specific tastes: you may discount or ignore their opinion whenever it fails to match up to whatever audience you DO want to cultivate, and feel just fine about it.

    \n What I\u2019ve observed:

    Sometimes a game sucks so bad, player skill won\u2019t save it. But often these games don\u2019t get written up as APs. I\u2019ve walked out on a few games.

    Sometimes a game\u2019s concept and core system are good but those involved hack out rules intentionally and often unintentionally. This happens with PTA quite a bit. So the game has something to it but it may not be clear what.

    Sometimes a con isn\u2019t the best environment for the game at all and hacking or streamlining is needed to make it fit into the time slot. This used to be the case with Burning Wheel. The con experience and the at home experience were very different.

    I know as a player I have stealth-GMed to save a game. Meaning there were games going bad that I used my PC as a tool to subtly co-GM to up the fun factor without anyone knowing. For all I know people have done that to me in other games!

    The converse can be true. Sometimes storygame baggage can make a good game go wrong. You saw this with In a Wicked Age early on. In those AP reports, was the game really bad or was something else going on?

    To complicate things further, often people know they had a good time but don\u2019t know why. The AP report might accurately communicate enjoyment that can be attributed to a system but not the specific reason for it.

    Luke likes to watch his games playtested without participating. That way he can make observations based on how people react in game rather than their interpretations after the fact. For example, I\u2019ve seen people have a horrible time 75% of a game but the last 25% was so awesome they forgot the first part. And vice versa.

    I think APs are useful. I wouldn\u2019t look to them for actual research into the details of the matter. But they can be a useful barometer for general enthusiasm. The fact that someone bothered to write an AP, good or bad, is a sign worth exploring. Ultimately, I would look to patterns in a series of APs rather than focus on just one or two. It\u2019s like how I read the news. I\u2019ll read the same news reported from different perspectives to see what patterns and contradictions shine through.

    I think what APs are very bad at is communication the value of a game\u2019s text. Often many of us playing haven\u2019t read the rules and are riffing off of someone else\u2019s interpretations.

    \n Ralph, are you asking "Should a designer trust con play for playtests?" or "Should we trust con playtest reports as accurate representations of a game?".

    The first question's a good one. But we're veering on asking the second: and, arguably, we shouldn't be using playtest reports as accurate representations of a game anyway. If a designer posts a playtest report, con or not, we shouldn't judge the game from that report. It's a playtest report. It's for gathering feedback and developing the game, not advertising it.

    (Of course, playtest reports do get used as advertisements, and that's another problem.)

    \n I think Ralph is pointing to something very useful: a clear confound present at this sort of con play. The introduction of a new rule set accompanies the presence of a non-standard group, one that may have particular qualities (high level of play, anticipation and excitement to make the most of a rare opportunity to play together) that may swamp or mask some or much of the impact of the rules.

    Now, this is not to say that other confounds don't exist in play-test situations, possibly moreso than here. But it's pretty clear to me on this description that the risk exists, as opposed to a simple vanilla situation where regular game group takes on a playtest.

    \n Don't know if wnyone's mentioned this point already, but another important thing to take into account is that convention games are often run by the creator. This can have a strong effect on the experience. Firstly it's often the case that the players haven't even read the book because the creator is telling them how to play and instilling in them his own sense of enthusiasm for the product, which can be infectious under the right circumstances. This means that you can't be certain the text of the book is clear, concise, and lively enough to enthuse the reader with the game. For a proper, proper test you have to give the book to someone who's never played the game and see how they get on running it. Annoyingly this is one of the hardest things to persuade playtesters to do.

    Conventions do tend to get some people excited too, and under such conditions they may be more easily pleased than they would be normally.

    \n I was about to make sort of the same point, Robert:

    1) System matters.
    2) The system is defined as the means by which the group agrees to imagined events during play.

    The first is to say that one can't discount the effects of a game's rules on the outcome of any play session; the second is to say that the game's text is never identical to the game being played. Even when run by its creator, and especially when played by people who haven't read that text. We all have that much down, right?

    It's hard to answer both Ralph's original question ("to what extent does AP have to be taken with a grain of salt") and Graham's clarification ("should a designer trust con play for playtests") because they're both asking for answers on an undefined scale and all the evidence at hand is anecdotal. I've personally had mostly amazing sessions at good cons. I've also played a couple con games, with awesome fellow players, run by their designers, whose other games I like... and had no fun whatsoever. The nice thing about that is that I know the problem isn't with the social dynamic or the atmosphere or player skill level: the blame lies squarely on the system. That's valuable. And therefore, the good games are valuable data too.

    One thing:\n
    Posted By: ValamirWhen you're just playing to maximize fun with your friends are you self aware enough to recognize when you're actually using the rules and when you're just making it work because having fun improv roleplaying is what you do?
    \nEven in specific playtest-focused mode, you should never not be playing to maximize fun with your friends. Games that do not have this as their chief objective will always fail, and that makes their data useless.

    \n Several things:

    \n I'm not sure I agree Brendan Unless you're allowing for the "fun" to be had when you're sitting around trying to figure out why the game you're playing is so damn broken.

    If your primary goal is "having fun" then most likely you're going to overlook flaws to keep things moving. You're not going to dwell on the rough parts. When you encounter a hole you're not going to let it derail the game, you're going to get past it as best as you can and keep playing so you can get to more fun bits. I think this is especially true when you're talking about improv heavy games being played by improv skilled people since its exceptionally easy to have fun just by enjoying the experience of improving with friends...and pretty easy to get completely off the reservation in the process.

    When you're actually play testing you don't skip over the flaws...you intentionally seek them out. You stress test it by doing exactly the sorts of things that aren't expected and see if the game survives. You catalog what went wrong, when it went wrong and spend some time trying to figure out where the source of the problem was. Sometimes it was you not playing right. Sometimes that's due to assumptions you brought, other times sloppy text. Much of the time its a situation the rules hadn't considered yet. Usually, unless the game is pretty close to being done...the actual play part is NOT fun. If it were, it wouldn't be so hard to find people willing to playtest. The TEST part can be fun, if you like that sort of thing. But its a different sort of fun...not the sort of fun that makes for entertaining AP reports.

    Now I may be in a minority here, but I'm actually pretty suspicious of playtests that are fun. For me there's a big difference between Play, and Play Test. If a game is getting alot of Play and that's being treated like Play Tests...ehhh...I'm not really so sure that works.

    Like I played Fiasco at Go Play NW. It was a ton of fun, that game is really really close to being completely baked. I don't know whether or not Jason considers that session to have been a "play test" but it definitely wasn't from my perspective. We were playing...there was no testing going on...we were just playing. When my group actually playtested Fiasco a few months ago...we play TESTED it. We poked it, we prodded it, we intentionally tried to see if we could find weak spots either in the rules or in the text. And I think the feed back we gave wound up being pretty helpful. But if we'd just played...we could have made it work...but I don't think the feed back we'd have given would have been that helpful. I don't in any way consider the hours spent play testing Fiasco to have been not worth it...but I also wouldn't call those hours...fun...not in the same way the session I played at GPNW was fun.

    Posted By: ValamirBecause the goal is to have a smooth ride, you go out of your way to have a smooth ride. Over there is a pot hole they failed to fill in, but because you're trying hard to have a smooth ride you navigate around it. Here's a place where someone left a careless shovelfull of blacktop to harden in a crazy pile, but by gliding to the far left you manage to avoid bumping over it.     You report back to the contractor that the road is smooth and ready to go.

    \nWhy report that? That's not at all what the results of the road test said. The report should have been "It's smooth, so long as you don't drive over that pothole there, that bump over here, and slow down a lot when you get to this part," since that's what the test revealed. Going out of your way to have a smooth ride just can't happen by accident on a road with as many problems as that.

    Really, that just doesn't sound like a very good road tester; apparently he's bright enough to spot and navigate around the rough patches, but not bright enough to notice that he's doing it? Is he some kind of asphalt savant, possessed with mysterious and inexplicable senses to find the smoothest path but incapable of translating those revelations into our feeble mortal language? Or is he just blowing off the contractor, saying "Yeah, I'll test your road, whatever," and then not actually reporting what he found to him?


    Clearly, there's some responsibility on the tester's side to accurately report what they did, and if that included steering away from the rough parts, that ought to go into the report. Good testers -- and, I would argue, people who are just good at playing games in general -- will be able to identify and describe not only what they did, but what they avoided doing and why.

    \n Here's another deviation in what I consider good playtesting... Ralph's road tester analogy is exactly what you should do in terms of how to test. The report was ass, obviously.
    \n
    \nBut note that pothole, and go around it. Note that lump of asphalt, and go around it. Note the grading scrape, and go slowly over it. Make sure you note HOW you went around or over all the rough spots. It's not your job to say "Dude, you need to fill that pothole, scrap down that lump, and smooth out the grade." It's your job to say "These are the problems I noted, and what I had to do to keep playing." The very worst thing you can do* is hit that damned pothole, go back and tell the road maker that his road is borked. You have failed to test the majority of his road at that point. You've pointed out one problem and quit.
    \n
    \nIt's the road maker's job to decide what to do about the pothole, lump and scrape. Maybe he'll just put signs up and say "go around this pothole" and "go slowly across this scrape". It may not be the best choice (obviously) but that's his choice. If all of the drivers follow the instructions and have a smooth ride, then that's what really counts. If the drivers see it for a half-ass fix, and don't like it, then the drivers will let him know that they don't approve of his choices.
    \n
    \n
    \n* Okay, obviously the worst thing you can do is not test it and say you did, or test it and give a false report. But besides those...

    \n If I may tweak the analogy: it's not the road you're testing, it's the car. What I was looking for was not so much how fun the game was, because a good GM can make almost any game fun, but how well the mechanics worked and how well the game flowed. I have still yet to run more than a one-session game of it, which makes me a little sad.
