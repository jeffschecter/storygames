Jason's character sheet, from the Reverse Engineering competition, has been bothering me. So, I thought we could all throw out some ideas about how to make it into a game.

So there's three dances: Waltz, Polka and Mazurka. That's going to set the period fairly nicely: both the Mazurka and Polka are common in late 19th or early 20th century ballrooms and the Waltz wouldn't be out of place either.

Location-wise, we could be in Europe, the United States or somewhere in Europe. We're almost definitely at a formal ball.

Note that they're three very different dances. The Waltz (by this time) is an elegant, courtly dance; the Polka is an exuberant, unrestrained gallop; and the Mazurka is a flashy, complicated dance. We could use them as attributes, if we wanted: so if a character has Waltz 3 he's a. Good at the Waltz and b. Elegant and courtly.

What about the characters on the sheet? Do we just put a tick on one character, indicating that that's the character we're playing? Or perhaps a number, indicating our relationship with that character?

And what the hell do we do with the Mysterious Strangers?

    The combination of these three dances is a mark of a cotillon, and this lends itself to the idea of the presentation of debutantes at a ball. I imagine players pairing off as men and women and sitting such in a circle or semicircle. Indeed, the central picture suggests as much.

    The dances themselves can be taken literally, as in how well does one perform the Waltz or Mazurka, but I take them as flavorful analogies for social skills, as you've suggested. Waltz is how one conducts himself or herself with grace in society. Mazurka is one's creativity and spontaneity. Polka is tougher, as its character is so much like the waltz, but perhaps more straight-forward. Let's call Polka one's ability to be direct and forceful. Dance experts can correct me, if they like.

    I would draw upon the figures of the Cotillon as various challenges within the structure of the game. The leader calls out a figure and a pair of couples, who must perform. The reference I have given lists 26 different figures, each of which requires one or more of the waltz, the mazurka, and the polka. This is just a small set of the figures available for a cotillon. I suspect Jason had something like this in mind when he made the sheet.

    Each of the characters on the sheet is available as a player character. Those not chosen by players remain under the control of the Gentleman Leader (game master). The mysterious strangers are off-limits as PCs. The object of the game is to find one's Love and arrange a marriage.
