\n I've often seen the question raised online: "if people want to engage in particular behaviors in their roleplaying, why do they need a reward system to incentivise them to do it?" I thought I'd share what I personally enjoy about dedicated reward systems in the hope that it might shed some light on their purpose.

See, I've had the experience, time and again, of wanting to do things in a game that go against the grain of the rules, and/or what the other players are pursuing. Like, "Hey, I'm gonna make a guy who's not very strong or tough, but he's a charming devil, and manipulates others to get his way." And the other players are like, "That's nice, we'll be over here slaughtering orcs by the truckload, while you miss two out of every three swings." And everyone would get better at the slaughtering, while I wouldn't get much of anywhere, 'cause while I might get a "good roleplaying" bonus here and there, everyone else is racking up piles of XP from their massive kill count and E criticals.

Now, nothing in the game prevented me from pursuing what I wanted. There's some manner of system support for such actions, though not nearly as much as for the others' goals. And with sufficient GM and fellow-player support I could pursue off-the-beaten-path goals in a satisfying way. That's all cool. But if the reward system doesn't back me up, then it puts a damper on the play I want. Not necessarily killing it or ruining the fun, but any fun will be had by overcoming the friction of reward un-support.

But isn't the play itself the reward? Sure, it can be, and that's probably why I've often pursued that un-rewarded play often. But see,the thing is that RPG reward systems aren't pure, enjoyable-in-themselves rewards.You don't turn in your XP for candy. You turn them in for increased effectiveness in the game. So if you're playing to your eccentric, unsupported goal, instead of the systemically central one, you're not (unless the GM just likes what you're doing and just sorta gives you the XP anyway) gaining effectiveness at the same rate as other players. And, especially if increased effectiveness is needed to better pursue your goal, that can suck.

So being rewarded for my preferred play isn't an incentive to do so (I already have that; it's the desired play itself). It's a resource for pursuing that play. Some games are pretty specific with that resource, having a dedicated reward system for the kind of play the game is designed for. (Like, D&D's calibrated for defeating Challenges, and Dogs in the Vineyard toward complications and difficult choices, therefore the former grants increased challenge-beating oomph while the latter piles on more life-complicating traits.) Which is great if everyone's on board for that particular kind of play. If the group's more diverse, I'd prefer something more flexible. The Shadow of Yesterday basically has customizable rewards that allow each player to design their own experience sytem. You want to get XP for killin' stuff? Great! Key of Bloodlust. XP for pursuing the unapproachable princess? Bing! Key of Unrequited Love. Machiavellian scheming? Key of Power. Avenging your father's murder? Key of Vengeance. Or you can buy extra Keys for infinite combinations.

I'm unaware of any other game with that degree of flexibility. D&D has its Story Awards as an alternative or supplement to the monster-killin' awards, but I find them terribly vague and of limited use. Aside from mission-based goals, they all say to me merely, "GM, wing it." Which is difficult, 'cause the GM isn't the participant who's invested in a given PC's "thing"--the PC's player is. And the player and GM may have difficulty communicating how to address that "thing." Whereas if the player can set a secific area of focus (like, "political scheming--on!") communication can be more clear and satisfaction of that goal more secure.

Anyone know of other games that address this? And how do the range of possibilities map to folks' experience and play preferences?

Peace,
-Joel

The only conclusion I've been able to draw from my pondering is that I have yet to see a reward system that encourages the kind of play I'm after. And beyond that, most of the systems I've seen are very incoherent about what kinds of play they reward. So:

    Why do we need a reward system?
    What is the function of a reward system?

    This is what I mean when I say your defense doesn't do much defending, because it doesn't do much to answer either of these questions. And I don't blame you! They're very hard to answer. So hard, in fact, that decades worth of games have struggled with them.

    This is very much along the lines of the thread around here a while ago that asked whether or not character advancement was necessary. Many, many people said "I don't need advancement, but I do need change." I'm with them. Change doesn't require advancement. There may be some systems that reward players in ways completely unrelated to advancement, but I haven't seen many. So if we don't need advancement, why do we need rewards? Maybe it isn't the reward system that's broken -- maybe it's the idea of having a reward system in the first place.

    From your description, it sounds like the barrier that keeps you from viewing the game this way is that other people get rewards, not that you insist on getting them. If we ditched the idea of rewards, this barrier disappears and the play itself becomes the reward.

    \n Rewards are a signaling mechanism that points toward fun. By watching what rewards a player pursues, you find out what is important to them. By encoding rewards in mechanics or social contract, you tell players what kind of fun you're offering them and where to find it. By leaving reward mechanisms unspecified, you're letting players define their own rewards ad hoc, or leaving space for a social reward method to fill.

    Does that satisfy Max's sidebar and let us go back to the main thread, which I find interesting in its own right - what happens when there's a conflict between what the player is pursuing and what is on offer from the system?

    Joel wants to be rewarded for clever social-fu, but the only rewards available that have a mechanical impact point away from social-fu and towards killing everything you meet. Sure, you can pursue non-mechanical rewards (personal satisfaction, strokes from your peers...), but by doing so, you only hurt the group experience, because your character is becoming more and more marginal as the killing-things characters power up by earning mechanically-reinforced rewards.

    The thing isn't actually "have a mechanical reward system" vs "don't have a mechanical reward system". It's "have a mechanical reward system that can encompass multiple kinds of player fun" vs "everyone pursues the kind of fun encoded in the mechanical reward system" vs "don't have a mechanical reward system". IF you want to have a mechanical reward system (to point toward fun and reward playing together instead of in parallel), THEN you need to think about what you'll be rewarding. IF you don't want to have a mechanical reward system, THEN you need to figure out how to keep everyone playing the same game over time OR you need to accept that play will have large parallel stretches.

    Posted By: Mark WSounds to me like you're in the "accept that play will sometimes have long parallel streches" boat, then. Keys do facilitate that nicely.
    \nI don't want to be. Honestly, our group hasn't played a game without a reward system, and I don't expect it to happen soon. For the purposes of this conversation, let's assume I do in fact want a reward system. It's not that I don't want to see a good way to make multiple payoffs fit into the same reward system, it's just that I haven't yet.

    One of the major challenges seems to be that some things are easier to reward than others, by virtue of being more concrete. It's easy to see who won the fight between the party and the goblins just by looking at who's still standing. It's not as easy to see who "won" a social encounter, and involves more possible degrees of success than the flat out fight. None of that is new, it's been discussed here before. And things like "being a hero" or "upholding cultural standards" or "challenging beliefs" are problematic for the same reason - judging what a success is, and how partial a partial success was are not concrete. I think they can be made to be concrete and easily verifiable, and keys do quite a bit to move along this path, by requiring that the victory conditions be defined a bit better.

    The parallel problem is that, as Joel pointed out, the reward doesn't encourage the style of play necessarily, but instead provides the resources to pursue it. And in order to support all these different goals that we're opening up to be rewarded (with whatever hypothetical method we choose), we'll need to distribute a resource that can be used equally well to achieve all of them. To see the issue more clearly, consider D&D. Experience lets you level up, which increases your BAB, maybe an attribute, some skills, and maybe a feat. Those resources can be used very effectively to pursue an agenda of "killing monsters and finding loot." The application is simple, direct, obvious. What about "being a hero" or "upholding cultural standards?" How would you use these resources to pursue those goals? It's possible, but it sure ain't simple, direct, and obvious.

    This is all really by way of defining the problem, which seems to consist of (at least) these two questions:

    How are we going to measure success consistently in a wide range of activities?
    What resource can be awarded that will equally foster a wide range of goals?

    I don't have answers yet, but the wheels are spinning. How do or would y'all address these issues?

    \nI think you've answered your own question--"it" is precisely "insist[ing] on some chance of failure. . .for the sake of keeping it interesting." And I don't think keeping the chance as small as possible is exactly the goal. . .rather it'skeeping the chance of failure just on the cusp between "too easy" and "too hard." There's, like, a million considerations that go into this, and I'm certainly no expert on balancing them. I just know as an end-user when the result does or doesn't suit my taste. For instance, Big Eyes Small Mouth, which my group plays a lot, produces a lot of failure results--until you buy up a certain amount of skill and can practically never fail. Neither of those conditions is desirable.

    I'd also like to point out that the nature of failure conditions themselves play a big role here. If failure means "eh, nothing happens," or "you're dead! New character!" then either you really, really don't want to fail, or it doesn't matter much at all. TSoY has stake-setting rules that basically let you put interesting failure options on the table that everyone can live with and which drive the story in interesting directions. Death isn't on the table unless it seems appropriate, and can be reserved for circumstances of dire and climactic import. A couple of stakes I set when running TSoY recently: [fighting a giant crocodile] "If you fail, the crocodile wounds you and makes off with one of the hapless soldiers." [confronted with a couple of tribal warriors who have no beef with the PCs, they're just caught in the middle of their raid] "If you fail, you're wounded and they capture you." See how that works? The players still may want to win (they did in the first example, but not in the second), but if they lose it's not unfun.

    I think that may be sort of a tangent, but I hope it addresses some of what you're pondering.
