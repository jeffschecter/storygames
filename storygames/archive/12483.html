    \n This is a rather hippie suggestion, but I think an award convention (or series of conventions) would float my boat. That is, nominated games are scheduled for review, discussion and play during two day events around the world.

    The categories would include Best New Game and Best Free Game. Games would be submitted and then short-listed by the submitters themselves. Finally, the Organising Body would schedule X conventions (one in the UK, two in the US, or whatever) to occur in a space of two or three weeks.

    At each convention, each of the X games per category would have two slots. The first day would be taken up by Review Slots (people learning and discussing the games in mini-lectures). The second day would be taken up by Play Slots (people playing the games).

    The convention attendees would vote:

    * Best New Game - Review
    * Best New Game - Play
    * Best Free Game - Review
    * Best Free Game - Play

    After the X conventions, the combined results would determine which games won the awards.

    The convention would be called Verge.

    \n I really don't have any problem with the gaming industry giving itself awards for the sake of giving itself awards. It works for the film industry, the music industry, and the TV industry, and besides, recognition from your peers is as valid as recognition from the public. (Maybe even more so, since they're supposed to be better-equipped to tell how awesome something truly is.)

    But I guess it's a good idea to decide what the actual bottom line of the whole shebang is supposed to be. Is it a promotional tool to drive hits on websites, get discussions going about the best games of the year, and give publicity to the winners and nominees? Is it a professional award, designed to enhance resumes and encourage good designers and companies to keep excelling in the field? Is it a celebration, meant to look back fondly over the work of the past year and make everyone feel good about what was accomplished? Or something else? Or some mixture of all of these?

    As a spectator, I tend to like awards that are a mix of celebration and promotional tool. I like hearing people enthusiastically discuss what was great and not-so-great, and finding out about things I missed out on earlier; I also like seeing people being happy. I think those goals can be accomplished both by a popular-vote award and an industry-expert award. At a guess, I'd say that having a panel of experts might lead to better, more meaningful discussion, but possibly with less enthusiasm and volume.
    \n

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI like the idea of predicating awards on actual play. I am deeply dubious you could find people willing and able to run (or even play) five different games in a thoughtful way over a reasonable time period. Especially since a few of those games will be agonizingly terrible.
    \nThis is something that I did with this year's ENnies submissions. I ran "test-drives" at local businesses with an open invite. Anyone who was interested could sit down and play. I ended up gaming with a lot of people I had never even met before and when all was said and done I collected opinions on the game which I used when making my nominations. Obviously it could be biased as the quality of the game would be based on the experience of the person running the system, but I made an effort to only do the test-drives with games I had never played or run or using settings (in the case of the Savage Worlds setting test-drive) that I had never previously used. Colonial Gothic was first, followed by Mutant City Blues using Hard Helix, Dragon Age using the adventure from the boxed set, Gaslight (using Savage Worlds - a system I have a lot of experience with), BASH Ultimate Edition (another I had experience with) and Dr. Who using an adventure written by John Bromley, an author who is contributing to a Dr. Who short story anthology. With Savage Worlds and BASH I was careful to only have players in the group who had never used the system since I had experience running it, that way I could still get the first impressions that I was going for. That's not to say that those were the only games I played but I took the stance last year that the best test of a game is to actually play it and I did my damnedest to follow up on that. The other games we played were all done with my regular groups. As to the agonizingly terrible part... I admit that I didn't do a good job with some of these. I don't run canned scenarios very well so it was kind of awkward for me to refer to a module and I think that may have detracted from the experience a little bit. But I learned a lot and now that I know what to expect I think I'll be able to manage the test-drives a little better if I'm picked to go again next year. The goal is to hit at least one public, open invite game day a month using the games that are submitted.

    But that's enough of my puffery. I wholeheartedly agree that the games that are judged should be played and I think that you can find people willing to play far more than five games in a given period of time. CW is taking the stance this year that every game that is submitted will see actual play from at least one judge. I think that's a fantastic decision.

    \n I rather like the character, mystique, and broad reach of the Diana Jones Award. I like that it nominates all manner of things, that it is about an opinion of excellence, and that it doesn't worry about being comprehensive\u2014just complimentary. It's an award that is, in my opinion, genuinely great to be nominated for. Winning becomes an extra bonus. Something about its narrow field of nominations (and the way its jury avoids making the award about them personally) means that it's flattering to be nominated, but that it isn't seen as implicit commentary when something is not nominated.

    I would be happy if various outlets offered a modest annual award in a similar fashion, without any one of them accepting the onus of being our Oscars. The notion that the ENnies are meant to be comprehensive is damaging, I think. When they are seen as just one part of an audience's arsenal for appreciation, the stakes come down a bit and it's easier to enjoy the awards as what they are: formal compliments, not official decrees or validating mandates.
