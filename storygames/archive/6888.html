    To be exact, the Big Model don't define a meaning for "immersion", because it mean very different things for everybody (the flamewars on rpg.net between people who argue that what THEY do is "immerasive" and what other people do isn't... is the stuff of nightmares...)

    To be exact, this is the definition of Immersion in the Forge Provisional Glossary:

    Immersion: This term has no single definition. Some uses, among others, include: (a) undivided attention to the Shared Imagined Space, (b) the absence of overtly stating features of Social Contract and Creative Agenda, (c) strong identification with one?s imaginary character. See Why immersion is a tar baby' and 'Immersive Story by John Kim.

    (I added the links to the thread cited, absent in the Glossary)

    "what some people call immersion" (whatever that is, meaning very different things for different people) can have two "places" in the big model:

    1) as techniques, usable for every single creative agenda (and I can assure you, I never noticed any difference in immersion playing with different creative agenda. And the people I know who most go on and on about "their superior immersion" are as gamist as they come, playing always, always dressed as barbarians in LARPS based on defeating another squad - but they insist that they aren't gamist because they are so much "immersive". So, really, your first post in this thread made no sense to me, apart in the usual "my fun is better that your fun" way...)

    2) as a technical agenda (in the Lehman version of the diagram, not visible in the usual diagram I put below this message), as is, a preference for some techniques (some people mistake this for a creative agenda, but it isn't)

    So, really in the diagram they are "below" the CA (not that it matter so much, you could always draw the diagram upside-down...)



    As an advice: if you want to deepen this discussion about immersion and the big model, the best way to do so would be posting an actual play or one of your games (chosen because it shows what you want to say, or because you think is problematic in Big Model terms) in the "actual play" subforum at The Forge, and ask about it. There you will find many more people more knowledgeable about the Big Model, and discussing theory in the story-game forum tend to "disturb the zen".

    If instead you only want to talk about your interpretation of "Immersion" without discussing the theoretical works about it contained in the Big Model, you should refrain from using Big Model technical terms.

    Posted By: Tomas HVMJane McGonigal talks about "immersing yourself in an interactive system". I find this way of putting it very interesting. It implies that whatever way you play a game, you are always immersing yourself in the "system" of the game.

    What a bummer for the would be "gamist" and "simulationists" out there. Kind of makesimmersionking of the game ...
    \nUm. Immersion doesn't equal narrative play (in fact, by what I call the RPGnet definition that seems to dominate, it's closest to simulationist play, but is actually more like method acting than anything at all game related). In fact, looking at McConigal's wikipedia resume, I doubt she's heard of any facet of the big model or anything of the infant RPG theory. But I could be wrong there.

    When you get away from the limited definition that appears to dominate RPG discussion (and the complete lack of definition from the Big Model), and looking to general gameplay, immersion means forgetting about facets or the world. Psychologist Cs\xedkszentmih\xe1lyi called it the "Flow" - which is used else where as being "in the zone." Immersion can happen in video games, chess, soccer, baseball, pictionary, etc. However, that's the ideal state. It rarely fully engages. Instead most games rely on offers and rewards to draw the player in. And really that's all you can design for. If players are compelled to play to get that next experience point, collect that next coin, or claim that next shot at narrative rights then it's successful. Immersion is like that optimal state, an Oscar award or Nobel Prize, you can strive for it in design, and the better the design fits together the more likely it can happen, but even if players consistently fail to reach it, it isn't a sign of a failed game.

    So, your post was way off base, but your title was spot on. Immersion is what all players strive for. Roleplayers, soccer players, chess players, solitaire players, Super Mario Bros players, etc....

    \n Without diving too deeply into any theory discussion, I have to say that I share Alvin's viewpoint about the alignment of the typical definition of immersion with Simulationist play more closely than Narrativist.

    The more general definition of immersion discussed in his second paragraph is what I've always thought about when I say the word. If other people are getting other meanings out of the word, that would explain some of the consternation I've had trying to discuss it in the past. It boggles me that people wouldn't want a high level of immersion in their gaming. My most memorable roleplaying experiences have been when near fully immersed, when everything outside of the game ceased, for a short period of time, to exist for me. Food, sleep, finances, relationship and work problems all briefly gone, replaced by the fictional world created between myself and my fellow players. Even the rules of the game, though still present, didn't get in the way. In some cases, the rules actually added to the immersive factor, though this is admittedly more rare.

    \n I think that everybody look at what he/she is invested in the game, and the way he/she address it with passion, and call that "immersion".

    So, everybody try to immerse in the game. But that simply mean "everybody try to be interested in the game". Then, somebody is very interested in "feeling" like his/her character and call that "immersion", somebody else is very interested in the fiction created and call that "immersion", other people get very interested in the working of the rules and call that "immersion", other people try really had to "win" and call that "immersion"...

    So, saying "I like immersion" is a good way to get everybody in these discussions to say that they agree with you, even if they would not agree with you about what that does mean.

    Posted By: Thunder_GodAlvin,
    Do all players strive for Immersion? What about when you are trying to create a semblance to the external world (political commentary) and have players aware of it being that?
    \nWell, general absolutes always seem to devolve into semantics debates online, so I'm sure there's some players that don't strive for immersion. If they don't, it's probably because they don't enjoy the game (imagine a baseball player who loved the game in college and as a rookie, but only continues to play because of his multi-million dollar contract or, riffing on your example, someone who plays the ReDistricting Game solely to learn about the process of gerrymandering, and not the reward the game offers). To put another way, they are "playing" for another reason than play.

    But, ulterior motives aside, tork interpreted me right. Now, being in the zone doesn't mean you are ignorant of the outside world, or cannot learn from it. I played the ReDistricting Game because it was fun. And now, months later, I still carry with me the lessons in how corrupt gerrymandering can be. Now, I'm not sure if I got immersed in the game. If I did, it was only for short moments. So, it could be that immersion is either overused or the importance is overplayed. I lean towards overused, judging that most RP gamers seem to use it to describe a level of character connection, but it's definitely a mix of both. I'm not blameless, despite more recently consciously trying to divorce my usage from the method acting definition, I still overuse and over play the importance of immersion.

    I only recently discovered "the flow" as a psychological term. And using that as a basis, I'm not sure if I've ever been really immersed in an RPG. Other games? Absolutely. I've got a good number of days spend playing Civilization or Alpha Centauri where I start at like 10am, and next thing I know it's midnight and I haven't eaten a single thing. That might be a dangerous (certainly unhealthy) level of immersion. And yet, despite that, table top RPGs are still my favorite type of game. There's a lot more to strive for than immersion.

    I got a bit sidetracked there. Back to your point, I don't think immersion, even at that level, breaks any chance of learning, even real world politics. I think the way kids play is a lot more immersed, at least more easily, than us jaded adults. It might be why we look so fondly back on those days of games and wish to recapture the "innocence". And yet, play, child or adult play, is a central human method of learning about the world. So, a well designed game, be it a sport or political commentary, should be able to make its intended impact regardless of whether or not individual players achieve immersion.

    \n 1.Q: - What is it really toimmerse yourself in an interactive system?

    We are kind of immersed in a global interactive system in our daily life. So it is possible the interactive system of a game replace this bigger system, and gives us the chance to interact in new ways. Kind of: now we play outside the interaction of our life, immersing ourselves in an altogether different interactive system. So we are escaping our life and world, to play with the idea of something completely different.

    The agreement between the players to do this, enforce the system on each one of us. We buy into it and play with full immersion. And if we don't buy into it; we will not play at all or we be disruptive to the games we "participate" in.
