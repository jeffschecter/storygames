\n So, I'm running Trail of Cthulhu at Conception, a small UK convention. I asked Simon for a copy and he, very kindly, sent me a PDF, with obvious instructions not to pass it on.

Problem was, it was 250 pages long. It'd cost a fortune to print. It was cheaper, in fact, to upload it to Lulu and print it. So that's what I did.

Now...here's the thing. Simon didn't send me the cover. The cover, by Jerome Huguenin, looks like this:



It's beautiful. But I didn't have it. So, making the best of it, I drew an approximation of Jerome's cover. I uploaded it to Lulu and used that as the cover.

So, when my copy arrived this morning, it looked like this. I think you'll agree I've captured the dark, brooding horror of the original.



On the back, I made sure to credit Simon, and squeezed in a subtle advert for my book:



I flicked through the book on the train. Now, I must admit to some bias here: we're in the same game group. Still, I'm being honest, and it's not like he pays me money for saying nice things.

This book looks really good. The explanation of the Gumshoe system is the best one yet. See, in Fear Itself, I thought the explanation of scenario design was pretty poor: it made the game look railroady. Here, it's clever, and explains exactly what the system can do.

The background's great, too. There's loads of Mythos stuff. Now, I like Lovecraft, but not the idea of a "Mythos": for me, when you formalise it into a Mythos, you take away the idea of unknowable terror. ("Oh, yeah, that'll be Nodens, then").

But this is superbly done. It's got alternative, contradictory explanations for things: Azathoth is described in the conventional "idiot god" sense; as nuclear chaos beyond angled space; as a term meaning "primal evil too horrible for description"; and as a deception. It's great. I'm reading it and enjoying it.

And there are really nice touches in the rules. They sound stupid if I say them (Credit Ratings, the Big Reveal), but they really are very good.

So tonight's job is sitting here, with a glass of wine, reading through. I have steak, too.

Oh, as a courtesy: you can get Trail of Cthulhu, albeit without my updated cover, from Indie Press Revolution or Pelgrane Press.

Graham

    2) The style of play that the game encourages, how is it different than classic Chaosium Cthulhu?
    \nFor a start, it's more focussed on the Investigator. So everyone's got a Drive, the thing that drives them to investigate: academic interest, revenge, adventure. You've got Stability points as well as Sanity points (Sanity is how ignorant you are of the underlying pointlessness of the Universe; Stability is how well you're holding your shit together. You can be a devoted Cultist and appear calm).

    So there's a big theme of what uncovering the Mythos does to you. Very Lovecraftian.

    There's a wonderful rule called Anagnorisis, which describes the moment when you realise the underlying pointless of your life and go to pieces. It's connected with your Drive: you realise, at that moment, that your Drive is pointless. You wanted academic glory, but now everything thinks you're a nut; you were doing it for revenge, but you realise you're powerless; you were doing it for love, but the person you loved is a cultist. I'm excited by this: I love stories like Dagon and The Shadow Over Innsmouth, so the idea of playing out that "going to pieces" moment is great.

    Gumshoe's big thing is that you spend points to discover clues, rather than rolling dice. I like this and find it takes the focus off the investigation as an investigation, moving the focus to a Simulationist-like "Let's explore this strange, horrific world". So, obviously, in Cthulhu, that's brilliant.

    Hope that helps. I'm rambling a bit.

    Graham

    Graham, say I hate the Cthulhu mythos. (I don't as fiction, but have never enjoyed it as a game setting.)     \nLet's see. Gumshoe's portable, of course. Whether it's worth picking up for Gumshoe alone, aside of the Cthulhu setting, is a hard call. There is lots of setting material.

    The thirties part of the setting, with the depression, is pretty good. That'd stand up by itself. If you're into pulp, it's really very good.\n

    See, I like Purist, so I'm the wrong person to ask. And I don't really like the Gumshoe combat system, but the reason I don't like it is because I don't like combat in an investigation game.

    That said, there's some lovely things in this combat system. Quirky, crunchy firearms rules and damage tables for molotov cocktails. I might well enjoy a pulp gunfight.
