    \n In terms of redemption: you can confess and repent, and have your devil drops to 0. Your soul stays where it is, with the implication that your sins erode your soul forever. If you go back to your own ways, you get a new sin: backsliding, and your devil jumps back up to where it should be.

    If you die with 0 devil, you can go to heaven, otherwise it's a contest between soul and devil. The outcome is peaceful rest and directly to hell. That's after you check to see if you'll become a ghost or not.

    The best first taste I can think of for Poison'd is this: The first decision you make about your pirate is which of a list of a dozen sins he's committed. In there are rape, murder, idolatry, adultery, and blasphemy. Two of your stats depend on the decisions you make. Something to a game that right up front asks you: "So, is you character a rapist? A murderer? How about blasphemy?"

    \n The game isn't so much about dealing with your sins. It's about sinning. If that distinction makes sense.

    The players make it happen by using the game system. Everything always comes to a fight, says the rules. When you end up in a fight, you want X's on your sheet. More X's means more power in the fight.

    To get X's, you want your character to do stuff that requires dice rolling. When you win dice rolls, you get X's. When you lose, the GM can bring the fight right then.

    So, what stuff can you do that will give you a chance to win the dice roll? You look at your stats. Most of the time, your pirate is really, really good at hurting others and being a treacherous bastard. If you do those things, you will probably win some X's and have a chance to survive when the King's men come for you -- or your crewmates decide to toss you overboard (or much worse things).

    So it's a pretty vicious cycle. A fight is always right around the corner. You need X's for the fight. You need to be a no good nasty pirate to get those X's. Or do you? How much do you care about this fight? How much do you care about your rapid slide into villainy? You get to decide, and take your lumps.

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonAlso, I was never sure how to set the Brinksmanship of opposing groups. Should I treat them as one NPC or several? If they just one, the PCs can easily best them by cooperating, unless I intentionally try to drive wedges between them, Jayne-style.
    \nThis is my instinct, based on Judson's running of the game, but unless it's a duel, or the PCs are explicitly not working together, I'd make the aligned opposing forces as one, or at least as few as possible*. If the PCs are working together, they are using the Captain's (if there's no Captain, whoever is leading the foray) dice, and the Captain can distribute dice to those claiming they'll help. Each die given out gives the helping player two dice to roll. My suggestion, again, just having played and not read the book, make the opposing side outmatch the leader's dice so that help is required to have a shot at winning. This also opens up for players withholding their help (not rolling after taking the dice), risking group success for their own benefit (they get X's).

    *I say as few as possible, because, we were chasing down two merchant vessels, and since each one was trying to escape and survive, each one was seperate. We couldn't chase down both at the same time. So, figure, however many of the NPCs the PCs can fight at once, that's how many should be lumped together. In a typical fight, a bunch of mook sailors would be one group, I think.

    As for what else to do. We found the drive to debauchery was natural. You get X's to help out in fights by being a nasty pirate. One player had the highest soul at the beginning, with the apparent intention to be our moral center (went by the moniker of "Father" and leading us in worship of the sea gods). By the end, he was murdering helpless passengers and trying to cut off my hands (which I almost accepted to improve my brutality!).

    After the Resolute, longer term play is up to the players, trying to accomplish their ambitions. I wanted to Own Land, Live Forever, and Spit in God's Eye. Only the last one came close to happening (as far as I can tell). If we were going to play a second session, or had time to continue, we were set to raid a Governer's town and steal his daughter to achieve the ambitions of two of the characters. We also got a taste of Leisure and were ready to attack more ships in order to get some decent shore leave (enough to buy fine clothes and get into high society parties to accomplish other ambitions).

    So, after the initial scenario is dealt with, the pirates go on to pursue their ambitions, rape, murder, and steal, get hunted down by even more British ships, die and become ghosts cursing and blessing their former shipmates, or perhaps even try to find some redemption.

    \n Hey Jonathan,

    There's no general conflict resolution in Poison'd. There are success rolls, and there are fights. Players can't call for a fight -- that's the GM's job. The GM may call for a fight when a player makes a success roll and takes no advantage from it (by tying or losing the roll).

    So, there's no "between conflicts" period, exactly. The PCs take action to pursue their ambitions and when they're being brutal, treacherous, going into danger, or enduring duress, you roll dice. So when you say, "People just wanted to get on with pillaging or killing each other," it makes me think that you wanted to always have a "conflict" over that stuff. That's not quite right. You can say, "I push down the old priest and strip every bit of gold from the altar," and if you succeed at your brutality roll, you do it. If you don't, you lose your nerve, and/or the GM brings the fight right then.

    Does that help at all? I'm not sure if I'm on the right track.

    \n John, I think you are on exactly the right track.

    Vincent has a Q&A thread up on Anyway, right now, but this bit from the introductory post pretty much says what John just said, only more exactly:\n
    2. There are a couple of key things the GM needs to know and remember to do. These are the "roll dice or say yes" of Poison'd.

    2a. Whenever the players look kind of blank, say out loud the four things they can do to get Xs for the upcoming fight (there's always an upcoming fight):
    * Go into danger (Devil vs Ambition);
    * Use deceit or treachery (Ambition vs Brutality);
    * Attack someone helpless (Brutality vs Soul);
    * Endure duress (Soul vs Devil).
    "Those are the four things you can do. Which do you want to do?" Then help them find or create the opportunity to do the one they choose. Don't forget about flashbacks.

    2b. Pretty soon, one PC pirate is going to attack another. This is awesome. Have the first player roll Brutality vs Soul for her Xs for the attack (if appropriate). Then say to the second player: "okay, so he clubs you in the head / stabs you in the back / heaves you over the side. Do you want to bring the fight or endure duress?"

    Ask the player if her pirate is enduring duress. Ask it whenever it seems to you that he might possibly be, or might possibly could. This is the best technique in the game. It turns a horrible situation for the character into an opportunity for the player, getting the player to buy into her character's suffering.
    \nThe other thing I'd note is that it is worth looking carefully at the Cruel Fortunes and seeing how they dove-tail with each other. In many cases they directly suggest what should happen next, particularly in the context of the Prizes section in Opportunities. In combination Cruel Fortune and Opportunities offer all the macro-level actions that the players can take. When I run the game, I'm tempted to print out each of these on a separate index card to remind me what I can do and what the players can do.
