I was composing this long, half-funny ramble about mixing esoteric practices into game sessions, when it occured to me that, well, for once I am right; of course people have done stuff like this for real.

Matthijs has his ouija-board game mechanics, Ole Peder and Martin based seven years of game design on an automatic writing exercise. The cult classic... ehm Kult is basically a near-psychotic interpretation of gnosticism, isn't it? As is the old World of Darkness mythos.

So I'd really like to hear about it. Got any stories about occultism at the gaming table? Might make a great second article...

    \n I know of one game that used an (optional) ritual, and in my opinion it was an ill-advised and ill-executed attempt. The game was 'Everlasting: the Undying' (first edition only, I think.) In it, it was suggested that the players enact a ceremony before play began, involving the lighting of candles and introducing ones character to the others in a ritualised form. There was also a 'closing ritual' for the end; the analogy with modern neo-pagan rituals was extremely obvious. I mentioned it here once in another thread, along with a lengthy quote, but that was a long time ago so I've no idea where to find it now. To the shrewd observer, it was uncomfortably close to ritualistic means of invocation, albeit of fictional characters rather than 'genuine' demons or spirits or what-have-you.

    My concerns are mostly of a psychological nature. Role-Playing is not a religion, and I worry what effects it might have on the players if it were somehow treated as such. A ceremony enacted seriously as if it was a part of preparation rather than part of the game itself might begin to take on too great a significance for the individual. We occasionally get reports of mentally ill people who turn to role-playing and wind up in terrible psychotic states. The press LOVE them, never mind that these people are usually affected by mental health issues before they even start gaming. So to be frank, not only would I worry about the affects the regular performance of such ceremonies might have on the players' minds, I would also be somewhat concerned about meeting a player who was enthusiastic to include such pseudo-religion in a game. That goes doubly for the ritual found in the Everlasting, which could be read as an attempt to actually become one's character rather than merely portray them. Also it might offend genuine practitioners who may be at the gaming table.

    Naturally this doesn't extend to pretend rituals that occur in-character during the course of a game. Those are fiction, and anyone who can't tell fact from fiction should not be playing RPGs. I also don't include the 'rituals' from Beast Hunters since those are more like somatic signals and gestures to aid play, not anything in any way ritualistic in the occult sense of the word.

    -Ash

    \n Side note: A guy I know was called by a major Norwegian newspaper to ask about role-playing (larp, I think) and occultism. He told them in all honesty that he didn't know of any larpers doing occult rituals. However, he said, he knew of a sect performing ancient middle-Eastern rituals, drinking the blood and eating the flesh of an ancient spirit. Then he went on to give the journalist the phone number of his local church.

    Side note 2: I think people attach too much significance - or, rather, fear - to ritual, hypnosis, real/pretend religion in games. I believe there's a marked difference between the US and Northern Europe role-playing cultures in this. Easy for me to say, of course; I find it hard to really believe in anything, though I think I believe in god at some level. But I just don't get all these warnings and worries.

    Posted By: Anders NygaardJDCorley - would love to hear more about that game - what was it about; is the ceremonial intro part of the fiction, or a separate procedure/game? How did you introduce it to the players, and how did you convey the procedure?
    \nJust to add to JDCorley's comments, the ceremony in 'the Everlasting' is basically like a ritualised 'getting to know you' session. The GM introduces the session by telling the players that they will 'be eldritch' (the game's name for supernatural creatures) for the duration of the game. Each player then introduces their character (from an in-character perspective) one by one, while symbolically lighting a candle to indicate that the character is now present. The GM finally rounds it all off and starts the session, using more archaic and flowery language naturally. It's fundamentally similar to certain neo-pagan invocational and transformational rites wherein the participants 'become' their characters for the period that the game is in session. It's entirely optional, and its only intent is to adjust the mental state of the participants and add atmosphere to the proceedings. There's no mini-game in the process and it has no further effect on play beyond the purely psychological.
