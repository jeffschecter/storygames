    \n This actually came out of Fred, Lydia and I sitting around shooting the breeze. Some unrelated craziness had Lydia looking to start a new MUSH and I'd signed on. Fred has pretty much left it all behind him, but he's unable to resist a good mechanics discussion.

    So, we initially decided that there's nothing we'd like better than a pared-down Polaris negotiation system for conflict resolution. In my heart, I *still* think it would be best, but we hit upon a very practical roadblock, and that is that MUSHers fear change. They fear it like giant electronic bunnies.

    As such, this has been a fascinating experiment in keeping things looking familiar enough that people don't run away screaming, while inserting soem more contemporary ideas of RPG design. To this end, we have *absolutely* ripped off a number of Story Games. Elements that some folks may recognize include:

    * Conflict resolution involves explicit discussion of consequences, with an implicit discussion of stakes.
    * Props are designed to have charters, which are written up in the style of Mortal Coil. This is cooler in theory than in practice, as MUSHers are also lazy.
    * War is a result of Lydia playing Ganakagok at Dexcon and going "neat" and stealing a lot of the medicine pushing. Blame Don.
    * The game has no secrets, though it has hidden information. That information is codified into Lore Sheets (abbreviated from Weapons of the Gods) and while it's not possible to get _all_ of them, all of them _can_ be gotten. Player investment in these also gives us a direct measure of player investment in the props they're tied to.
    * "Storybuilding" is a polite way to say "Ripping off Polaris".
    * Strong metagame currency in the form of "focus"
    * Stats are not what you *can* do (Strength, Warfare, etc.) but rather how you do it (Grace, Wits, Force, Resolve)
    * Talents are presented as things like "swordsman and strong", but the reality is that they equally easily support "Revenge" or "In the name of my Father"
    * Power pricing totally ripped off from Everway
    * We have an expectation of awesome

    And to top it all off, we're actually doing playtesting before we go live.

    Honestly, this may all explode horribly, but it's proving an interesting challenge in the meantime.

    -Rob D.

    PS - And so props are given where they are due - Lydia is our editor, and through a strangely MUSH related turn of event is also the original hat of evil from which we take our name. She is also our resident super genius, and one of the players in the very first Fate game.

    \n Joshua, if you look at one of the main portal sites you'll see about 260 games listed. Some of these games aren't active any longer, but there are other games that aren't on the list; I think there are somewhere around 200 active games.

    How active is active, however, is the important question, and I don't think anyone has ever tried to get an accurate count of how many people play mushes right now. If I had to guess I'd say 75% of those games average fewer than 10 people online at once, with the rest somewhere between 10-50 online; of course some people play many different games. The largest games have several hundred online, but aren't exactly considered 'family-friendly'.

    So on any given night, my totally unscientific estimate is about 2500 people are mushing.

    The overall text mud community however, is much larger -- people usually throw out the 50,000 to 100,000 range -- and a small portion of those muds are also considered 'RP' muds, but I would be surprised to see story game influences in something that wasn't a mush.
