    \n I would draw back from the setting a bit - my Star Wars RPG would invite the player to join George Lucas and other creators in imagining the setting and the stories freshly, from a perspective freed of setting canon. The Death Stars and whatnot would still be there, but only as incidental inspirations and examples of what may be done. The game would probably devote only a few pages to setting, and even there it'd probably mostly focus on a bibliography and filmography, discussing the differences and creative choices made in prior Star Wars fiction; the goal would be to prepare the reader to make their own music, not just to replicate faithfully a setting that does not in fact have an a priori existence detached from a given story.

    I would, of course, provide the basic mechanical support for jedi powers, droids and all that sort of thing. The mechanical basis would probably be one of dramatic adventure - in fact, if I had to work under a deadline and just get a good game out quickly, I would personally probably work with the Solar System just because it totally can do it and I already know how to make it work. I would resist the temptation to make the game narrow and strongly structural in nature; better to provide tools and procedures for taking on the whole menagerie of setting and ideas that's been created for the Star Wars brand over the years. Loose enough rules so that they can be used equally well for replicating the style of the '80s comics, '90s novels and recent movies. A substantial part of the product would consist of ready-made adventure and campaign frameworks to show the reader how to do it. Some of this material would be explicitly counterfactual as regards the "canon", the flagship campaign would probably be something along the lines of doing the original movies without the original cast. It would all be very explicit about not getting any more hung up about setting consistency and pre-established canon than the original creators were. An important part of the work would be to encourage the reader and the gaming group to learn about the subject matter and enjoy using it in play: implied references to pre-existing works, clever cross-overs, smart choices in what to use and what to discard, this is what creativity on the basis of existing culture (which is all creativity, when you come down to it) is all about.

    As might be expected, the above is pretty much how I would do a Middle-Earth roleplaying game or an Arthurian roleplaying game, too: provide the aesthetic components or at least source references, provide analytic tools for understanding how to replicate the style and the feel, provide a gaming engine that can do adventurous drama, and then leave it for the gamers to apply the stuff. I've never seen truly satisfying licenced-world gaming happen by offering endless amounts of setting canon for reference, with neurotic rules for replicating that one event in that one movie; the magic is definitely in encouraging creativity inspired by the original work. If that should result in a "Luke Starkiller" or "Skywalker Paradigm" or a campaign where Ewoks do not exist, where's the harm?

    (As might be imagined, I have a bit of a chip on my shoulder about roleplayers putting their faith and hope in magically satisfying genre gaming by throwing more study and less creative honesty at it. It's a particularly annoying blind point when a license-based roleplaying game is again and again interpreted to mean a game that allows you to be a pedantic jerk about a setting on the basis of pre-existing texts instead of making the choices that are right for your game.)

    \n For years and years I've toyed around with various hacks and homebrew systems for Star Wars, but keep coming back to the classic D6 system. It worked well enough, but could stand for some major rules simplification (to get that feel of just being concerned about the heroes and major villains, and everything else are just cameos, extras, props, or window dressing). And fixing the Force rules.

    One system I was seriously considering hacking was Feng Shui. It was the game that, perhaps, best represented the action and heroics and banter of the original Star Wars movies, and even more so with the action scenes of the newer movies and video games. Seriously, the rules for Sorcery in Feng Shui would've ported BEAUTIFULLY into the Force.

    Another that I thought about, particularly for a grittier, Episode 4 style game, would be the Barbarians of Lemuria system. Particularly with the way they deal with occupations and combat abilities, they keep it simple, and still keep the flexible feel of advancement, so that a scoundrel can eventually become a General in the Rebellion, and a farmboy can eventually become a Jedi Knight. Its magic system also would work well as the Force in a Force-light campaign.

    \n Mechanically, I think I'd try to focus on Difficult Choices, Sacrifice, and Challenging Characters' Ethics, as those seem to be the primary themes. I think that's why DitV gets drifted to SW so often and easily--tough moral decisions and the authority to make them stick. But DitV isn't a PERFECT match, in my opinion, because the characters are not challenged by its play, the players are (after all, the PCs are Immaculate [in the Catholic sense]).

    For Force mechanics, I'd look to emphasize Trust and Will versus Fear and Temptation--maybe something like opposed attributes on tracks or similar.

    Definitely want some mechanical benefit for color and creative narration--that's how all the "gee whiz" scenes come about, all the little touches of cleverness or spectacle. Fan Mail (PTA) and/or Feat Dice (Exalted) seem to be the main ways that such narration is incentivized.

    I'd probably (mechanically) de-emphasize blasters, lightsabers, and ships. As I see it, they're all more-or-less equal, just specialized for particular arenas of conflict. It's the rare (and, thus, frequently noted/emphasized) item that is better than the run-of-the-mill: the Millenium Falcon is the fastest of her kind; Darth Maul's saber-staff is (was, before all the Jedi porn) frightening and potent, allowing him to handle a Jedi Master and his nearly fully trained Padawan with relative ease; the Sun Crusher is the final word in solar system destruction (the end of a long line of otherwise-serviceable Star Destroyers, Battle Stations, and Death Stars). I see those as mechanical bumps (or maybe even trumps?), not the BFGs on laundry lists of stuff to buy, minimally distinguished by stat bonuses.

    Finally, there's The Alien... and one really needn't hunt much further than Shock for mechanics to play out the weird and woolly encounters with both sapient NPCs and monsterous threats. Maybe some kind of bidding or buying process, a la Univerrsalis Facts, to initially generate the alien morphology and (thus) home world/climate and behaviors.

    Fun concepts to ponder, actually! Every SW RPG has approached the property's mechanics with crunch and numbers, its action in the form of task resolution, and its diversity and variety as laundry lists of feats and traits. It would be nice to see a more narrative treatment that actually pushes for the themes (and thrills!) of the original trilogy.

    \n I wouldn't. I don't want a single "Star Wars" rpg. Star Wars is vast, and contains multitudes. That is what makes it so appealing: different people see different things in it, and I find new things in it every time.

    So I want a multitude of lenses for interacting with Star Wars. High action heroics, sure, but also a game that looks at the story as Anakin Skywalker's tragedy, or focuses on the Han and Leia romance. Or about how tribal Ewoks misunderstand the events of ROTJ and form a crazy cargo cult worshipping the Golden Man and his hairless ape buddies. Or games about Imperial politicking, or about the downfall of the Old Republic or whatever else is appealing at the moment.

    I want a lot of little games, rather than one big one. (Eero's ideas are great, too.)

    \n If I was going to make a game that delivered the spirit of Star Wars it would probably be highly structured and focused on pacing, like a good adventure movie. There would be a mechanic that encouraged dropping undefined but evocative words or phrases into conversation to make the setting seem deeper and richer than it actually is (e.g. Kessel, Clone Wars, Old Republic, etc., were just words on a list before someone worked them into dialog). Planets would be thinly sketched, and would be characterized entirely by the introductory visual (Desert! Ice! Swamp!). There might be a humor element where you could have characters that speak a language only you understand deliver a "line" that you could shape for the audience by selling your reaction to it. There would be no need to get the Star Wars license for this, and actually getting it would probably be harmful to the concept.

    \n I had a thought a while back to do Star Wars SAGA (using the DL5A/Marvel system) combined with WEG's D6.

    I got this idea after reading the article on doing Ravenloft with the SAGA system, and the powers checks seemed to work very well with the idea of having dark side points.

    I assume everyone's familiar with WEG's D6 system, so I'll just do a quick rundown of SAGA. You have a certain number of attributes ranging from 1-9, and actions are completed by playing a card from your hand and adding the attribute to the base score. Difficulty ranges are roughly the same in both systems, so conversion would be easy. How SAGA handles experience is as you get experienced you get a larger hand, this doesn't increase your absolute top, but is roughly like going from 3d6 to 4d6-1 to 5d6-2, and with the trump suit mechanic it works pretty much as you'd expect a 3d6->4d6-1->5d6-2..etc system to work if it has exploding 6s.

    Then the dark side comes in. If you play a dark side card (roughly equivalent to major arcana in frequency) it means you're drawing on the dark side, and the card you replace it with goes into a separate dark side hand, and this hand becomes automatically trump - making it very tempting from a power perspective. Each time you play a card from the dark side hand it increases by one, and if your dark side hand grows larger than your regular hand you become consumed by the dark side and turn into a villain. There would naturally be ways of whittling it down - for instance if playing an action from your regular hand would mean you fail, because the cards are all too low, but playing from the dark side hand would allow you to succeed, if you resist temptation you get to discard one dark side card, and slowly get back control (of course you fail, so there are consequences).

    This ends up making a very real seeming and interesting battle between the light side and dark side, and rather than just having points that you draw on, you constantly have a visual reminder of the struggle in the cards you're holding.

    Also, the WEG system of having mechanics as a base attribute takes into account what the game is about, and the SAGA system integrates well into it (probably Marvel would work better given the modern nature). Sorcery & Mysticism also translate well into the force, particularly with the extended universe featuring Jedi with specialities in different areas.

    Alternatively: A square grid combat focused system as a great excuse to use Lego Star Wars figs.

    \n I think there's a huge space between a "Star Wars RPG" and "an RPG that takes place in the Star Wars universe."

    The Star Wars MMO had this same problem -- folks wanted to be major movers and shakers, Jedi, Sith, whomever, but instead spent their time doing MMOish things like building blasters and selling bantha hides.

    I'd probably start with the same creative premise as Burning Empires: The dial is set on "you are the most important people in the universe" and, with some work, you can dial it down from there.

    Personally I don't think I'd have much fun playing in or running a sandbox-y, poke-around-and-be-a-tourist type Star Wars game. Just like I'd rather play a Knight of the Round Table and all that implies, than just some person in the same time and place as Avalon, where all the good shit is going down.

    Social mechanics would be huge in my SW game, some method to win people over at least temporarily. I'd also make sure every character had some defining, even crippling, flaw, or perhaps some profound need that one pursues, by default, in a really petty, crippling way. "What the characters want" would totally trump "what the characters can do" in my SW game.

    Keeping with Lucas' own aesthetic, I'd probably come up with some way to make up the Force's capabilities as you play, rather than a list of powerz. There'd be some fundamental truths to it, like the fact that using the Force in the pursuit of certain "good" motives looks different than using the Force in pursuit of certain "bad" motives. The motives might feed into a recharge mechanism in some sort of reinforcing cycle: you have a "light" and "dark" battery, and they both enable the Force powers in the same way, but doing "good" stuff when you use the FOrce recharges the "light" battery and doing "bad" stuff recharges the "dark" battery. Something like that. Totally spitballing.

    The Force could be strong in people and those people might not even know it, in-fiction. Out-of-fiction it might represent "luck" or something, maybe also one's susceptibility to others' Force-powered efforts. No midicholorians, kthx.

    I'm not persuaded you'd need subsystems for busting out blaster fights, lightsaber duels and/or dogfights. What works on-screen may not work in-game.

    Defined factions, WOD splatbook-style, would probably help a lot to get players' heads into the setting. It's depressingly simplistic in the movies but man it does work in RPGs. Merchant League, Rebel Alliance, Imperium, Jedi Order, the Hutt, yadda yadda. Probably a package of obligations and resources that come with belonging to each.

    Oh! A way to build out the setting's planets on-the-fly, which is how watching the old SW movies always felt to me. Oh hey, here's an asteroid field where caves are actually giant mouths, wild! Oh hey, this old Jedi teacher lives in an hallucinogenic swamp! And so on. Rather than providing a map full of details and facts. I liked when Han looked through his starmaps and came up with Bespin but, oh shit, he's got a pretty complicated relationship with the boss man there. Probably tie locations to NPCs, and it's a map of NPCs that you move between. And if there's *not* an NPC there (Bespin, Dagobah), there's gonna be some kind of complication (Hoth, asteroid field).

    I'd spend a lot of time focusing on making sure the players are all bought into A Major Conflict of some kind, rather than the open-ended "let's tramp around space earning our fortune" thing that Traveller already does quite well. I guess this is me striving against the sandbox again.

    The third area I think would really benefit from the make-it-up-as-you-go thing (the first couple being the Force, and the setting) would be the gear and vehicles and, I suppose, alien races. I'd love to capture that "whoa" you got the first time you saw big walking things stomping across the ice fields, and then seeing them reincorporated, sort of, on Endor via those two-legged jobbies. Or landspeeders! Or all the variations on TIE fighters. Or a light saber that has blades coming out both ends! Just like how an established star map would just feel like a huge canonical creativity sink to me, a big book o' gear would also feel like a huge drag. It might be funny/cynical to tie setting ephemera bloat into some kind of benefit for the GM -- the more crazy aliens and ships and guns you come up with, the better the GM is doing. Call it "licensing rights" or something.

    Anyway, I think SW has never been done especially well and this is where I'd start. I'll let y'all know when Lucasarts and I have worked out the details. ;-)

    I'd do what Eero does: start with The Hidden Fortress, The Guns of Navarone, Oedipus Rex, and Flash Gordon. What is the Force really? No one knows but ancient masters who sit on high cliffs and speak in riddles. What makes a hero? The willingness to lose everything to make something. What makes a villain? The drive to sacrifice everything to own something. How fast does the ship go? 12 parsecs.

    \n

    Your character begins as a member of society (farmboy, diplomatic princess, member of the fraternity of smugglers), then is forced to leave society in order to exceed its expectations (your family is burned alive, your home is blown up, you get a bounty on your head). So you start by drawing your power from your place in society (or struggling against it \u2014\xa0same thing), then start to get called.

    \n

    Every time you use your calling, it makes it stronger, while making your place in society weaker, until the call itself is the tool for the job every time. Once that takes place, you start getting opportunities for choices (fuck your sister/friend's crush vs. getting out of their way, fuck your brother vs. fuck your friend, abandon your friends to safety vs. help them and get your ass tortured).

    \n

    Those choices build consequences, where each road taken, and each road not taken, still impact the characters' lives.

    \n

    Your character proportionately die and establish a legacy. Legacies generate new characters in the forms of children, proteg\xe9s, revenge-seekers, and the like.

    \n

    Your options are always combinations of two of: Fighting to preserve; Fighting to destroy; Falling in love; Making a deal.

    \n

    Different forms of conflict use different combinations and have different minigames. Starfighter combat works differently than a diplomatic lie, which works differently than fighting with blasters. When everyone in a conflict is fighting with a lightsaber, the consequences are different than when one of them has a blaster.

    \n

    You play in trilogies. Each chapter takes roughly one evening.

    \n

    (There aren't many copies out there, but my edition of Beowulf does some of this, though over a longer period of play. I'd love to see someone use the Three Wyrds, recolored for Star Wars.)

    \n I think it really depends on which half of the saga we're talking about, episodes 1-3 are essentially a story of corruption: the corruption of an individual and the way it mirrors the corruption of the larger society. Episodes 4-6 are about redemption in the same model, though Luke himself isn't being redeemed, as he grows as a personal force of redemption, so the rebel alliance grows as a force against the empire. So I would give the players civilizations or factions to stat out, have everyone decide whether or not they favored a corruption or redemption tale, and then have them pull characters out of the created circumstances, stressing all the time classic mythological themes and tropes from pulp-era science fiction. There would need to be a protagonist, who represented the society as a whole, but maybe he would never be the PC, instead, the PCs would be all the people around him who shape him and shape the nature of his rise or fall. Also, lots of pre-discussion of what makes up the "Star Wars" aesthetic for the group, maybe with a list of flavor/color points that people could drag and drop into the game.

    Hey, something that's important to me: The Force is not something Jedi have. The Force is something that binds the universe together. Jedi learn to use it powerfully. Anything that's even alive as a rock is its conduit \u2014\xa0like, say, a droid. It both guides someone who listens and follows their lead. It most certainly guides those who don't listen, though; you can call it luck if you want, or pluck, or whatever, but the point is that it's the fundamental life energy of the universe making things happen, and you can choose to participate in it or just be subject to its whims.

    \n

    So I wouldn't make The Force a score. I'd make it something you choose to interact with at any point. Sometimes the Force is what you use to deflect blaster bolts. Sometimes it decides it's time for you to die. Do you defy it? It's a literal, and potentially nonproblematic, deus ex machina. I'd love to see someone make that fly.

    You get, maybe 15d6 to divide between them, so you might have

    Diplomat 7d6 Scoundrel 3d6 Jedi 1d6 Soldier 3d6 Pilot 1d6

    It should be relatively clear when you roll each thing.

    There's a pool of d4s on the table that are light side dice, and d8s that are dark side dice. You can draw on them whenever appropriate, and you really ought to be able to tell what's appropriately light or dark. Here's the catch: whenever a character uses dark side dice and they come up 1 or 8, the character keeps them and MUST roll them on any future roll, thus tainting all future rolls with the dark side, and if you have dark side dice, then you aren't allowed to take light side dice, unless your light side action is 'light side' enough to cancel out all your dark side dice.

    Some kind of rudimentary chart showing acts of light and dark and how they scale would be needed.

    Some rules for redemption (a la Vader sacrificing himself to save Luke).

    In a game with no Jedi, you'd still want the Jedi trait, probably, but you might be able to do away with the entire force-dice mechanic.
