    \n I've played with it some. It has some serious shortcomings, but also some amazing potential.

    Shortcoming #1: this is a walled garden WITHIN a walled garden. Your potential customer has to have the device, then decide to download iBooks, then search for your book. So far as I know there's no way to create a web link directly to a "buy this book" page.

    Shortcoming #2: no custom fonts. You can design with custom fonts, and custom fonts show up in the table of contents page view, but they don't actually render. (This gives a little insight into how Apple does the ToC view where you can see all the pages: they're actually snapshots taken when the book is "compiled.")

    Shortcoming #2: the interactive books still seems like second class citizens. They render much later than everything else. The default set is fairly limited too, but as Lester mentioned they are highly extensible as long as you know some HTML and Javacode.

    Shortcoming #4: you can't start from scratch or even from an empty set of chapters, you have to start with a template. A slight mitigation: the templates are quite nice looking.

    Shortcoming #5: It's a proprietary format. Not only does it only go in the iBooks store, but you can't do anything that isn't supported by the Author tool. I'm pretty sure HTML is underlying this somehow, but you can't get at it in any meaningful way. There isn't even a chance of porting.

    Shortcoming #6: I haven't found an easy way to import text or synchronize text from another doc. This is the biggest deal breaker for me.

    Opportunity #1: The design interface is a very nice. It fits a nice middle ground between InDesign and Word, something that anybody can learn pretty quickly but with a fair amount of advanced options.

    Opportunity #2: The created books are automatically multi-orientation with the landscape being a page-by-page embellished view and portrait being a continuous mostly-text view. I wish some of the other frameworks did similar.

    Basically, it makes the best-looking ebooks I've seen, on par with PDF, but lighter weight and multi-orientation. To get that you have to take a whole lot of Apple caveats and you won't easily be able to develop in parallel for another platform. Unless Apple opens it up in some way I don't see this taking off unless the education market really drives it.

    \n Yeah, this war is getting interesting. As someone who cares what documents look like in electronic format, Apple wins by a mile. Kindle layout is so, so ugly in comparison. But Amazon destroys Apple when it comes to distribution, sales, and accessibility (the latter due to the price of their device). I bet the next step in the arms race will be pretty Kindle ebooks (rather than cheap iPads), but who knows how long that will take. I guess Fire makes that possible, though. And Apple made iPods ubiquitous before by coming out with a cheap version for everyone.

    All in all, though, pretty exciting that Apple hasn't ceded the ground on ebooks. A multi-horse race will always be better for consumers, since competition will (hopefully) lead to better options.

    Posted By: J. WaltonI bet the next step in the arms race will be pretty Kindle ebooks (rather than cheap iPads), but who knows how long that will take. I guess Fire makes that possible, though.
    \nAmazon has already announced the new KF8 format that builds on top of the Mobi format (I think). Their solution seems to be to provide a command line tool used to take some marked up input and produce KF8 out the back end. As inputs, it apparently takes HTML, EPUB, and (not very well, it admits) PDF.

    To me this is a really interesting counter-approach, and one I personally applaud -- "we're not going to really tell you what tools to use for creation, we're going to give you a compiler and tell you (a) what it accepts as input, and (b) maybe suggest how you can get best results out of the compiled output".

    KF8 is specifically built, it seems, to go after simple but layout heavy texts (like children's books, and presumably, text books).

    Apple's approach is intriguing: I have friends who claim that independent, small-genre publishers will leap on this especially if they're willing to give away books and make money in some other fashion (the EULA specifically forbids monetization through "subscription", but I wonder how far that goes -- for example, I wonder how they will address crowd-funded projects? If I kick-start and say "once I get 1000 bucks, all contributors will get a free iBook", is that fundamentally a subscription? Apple's lawyers would no doubt try to argue that it is, but I'm not so sure they can make the case, especially if the iBook is only one of the chotchkies made available to participants -- crowd-funding subsidizes the work, explicitly, and not the output I'd say: of course, they can out-starve any opposition that might use crowd-funding in the first place, so I'm not sure I'd be keen on being a test-case).

    But I really wonder, exactly who does Apple think their customers are? No doubt, they'll have to juice platform adoption by signing up big-business adopters. But I'm not sure I'm convinced the business model works here. Will MIT Press or the like really want to be asking their authors to use iBook Author as their composition platform? It seems this really is like iWeb or GarageBand: who really does/did professional, commercial work with these tools?

    This tool is not a pro-tool, it's an iTool -- this makes one wonder if Apple will follow up with a for-pay, more featured pro-tool in this arena.

    Posted By: doho123
    So, if any major textbook publisher uses iBooks Creator to create an e-book of their textbooks, then Apple maintains the rights to that textbook? Something tells me that the big boy publishers probably have a slightly different contract.
    \nNo. The EULA maintains that Apple retains commercial distribution rights for any "Work" produced by iBook Author. The copyright to the content is specifically retained by the content creator, not Apple.

    It's not clear to me that the EULA restricts you from distributing works at no cost while you sell the work through Apple's distribution.

    What's most disturbing to me about this EULA is not necessarily its terms ("we're giving you this software for free in exchange for exclusive, commercial distribution rights to anything you produce with it"), it's that it unreasonably allows Apple to unilaterally decide what works to distribute, with no recourse on the part of the creator. Should Apple decide to accept your work for distribution, and then decide to yank it, you have no recourse. There's no indication on how long Apple may take to decide whether or not to distribute your work.

    It would seem more reasonable for the EULA to grant Apple right-of-first-refusal on commercial distribution.

    But then, I suppose Apple's (quite sensible) response is going to be, "Hey! Don't sweat it -- if you don't want to lock in with us, just don't use our awesome, easy-to-use tool!"

    I'm pretty darned sure they're not going to close the loop to the extent that they'll insist on iBook Author as the only way to get iBooks into their distribution network -- that would be insane on their part, I should have thought.

    Posted By: Christopher WeeksPosted By: viktor_haagit's that it unreasonably allows Apple to unilaterally decide what works to distribute, with no recourse on the part of the creator. Should Apple to accept your work for distribution, and then decide to yank it, you have no recourse.
    \nI think I'm not understanding this. It sounds just like a book store. Doesn't Amazon unilaterally decide what works to distribute, with no recourse on the part of the creator in exactly the same way? And if they aren't going to distribute the iBook version of your masterpieceNazi Demon Pornographyor whatever, you can still distribute it in any other format you like through any other means. You can package it up as a PDF and sell it at Lulu or whatever. Yeah?

    I don't think it's like a book store. Of course Apple can decide what to stock and what not to stock, but typically book store chain's decisions about what to stock, and what not, don't restrict a publisher from flogging the book through some other channels.

    In the case where there are exclusive distribution rights agreements reached (for example, perhaps when Walmart or something like that signs up suppliers to distribution agreements), I suspect those agreements guarantee a certain amount of revenue passed over in exchange for exclusive distribution: i.e. guaranteed purchase of so many units per month, or so many months, and such and so. I also suspect that, should the distributor suddenly cease distribution, there might be penalties involved they'd have to pay. At any rate, I totally suspect that a lawyer might advise producers not to sign any agreement that doesn't offer them something in the way of this kind of protection.

    But with Apple's agreement, you cede to their terms the minute you use their software -- at least with respect to the works you make with it.

    The provisions in the agreement give Apple the right to totally stifle a person's right to make money off their work, if they use Apple's software and I've read at least one op-ed piece (Ars Technica I think?) that suggested this might get them into anti-trust difficulties -- they're basically using the license for one thing to leverage acceptance for a separate thing (their software leveraging acceptance for their distribution services).
