Pulling out from the Character Algebra thread:
Posted By: neelkNow, contrast does not have any necessary connection with the conflict in a story. You can use contrast to reveal similarities between the protagonist and the antagonist just as easily as differences. For example, suppose the *tagonists are a Jedi knight and a Sith lord, and we see them continue to duel on an exploding starship, even as the ordinary soldiers around them run for their lives from the pyrotechnic CGI effects. The conflict is between the two Force-wielders, but the contrast in this scene is between them and the mooks -- we see that the depth of rivalry between these two is deeper than even the human desire for self-preservation. The stormtroopers and rebels don't even neednames, as long as we can imagine them as average human beings with a typical desire to live.
Wow ... how awesome would it be to have a mechanic where you could only get the advantage of your traits when they contrasted sharply with the behavior of another character ... and where other players (and the GM) had reason to give you those contrasts?

Like, you have a character who has a motif of being determined, and there's a fire. If he just leaps straight into the fire to try to rescue something then he gets no benefit. If the GM has a fireman yell "Sir! Stay back! You can't help her!" and then you jump into the fire ... that's when you get the benefit, by contrast.

Man, that would be incredibly cool. I'm so psyched about what that could do to a gaming group, in terms of teaching them to give each other a chance to shine. I don't know if Misery Bubblegum is the right place for the idea, but it's got to go somewhere.

    Man, all I need is a Go Play flag.

    I find that getting the core of the character down on the character sheet and easily referenced by everybody else at the table goes a long way to facilitating all of this. It's a procedural way to say, "My guy is about this." And then the other players can help you portray your character as a guy about this. Contrast is certainly one way: "Man, all your this-ways are totally silly in the light of my that." But it is one among many.

    There is also:
    Similarity: "Now that we are united in thisness, let's go kick some ass."
    Implication: "My my, look at what happened to that poor sap NPC who was all about this." *significant look*
    Assumption: "Okay, where's our specialist in this?" *significant look*
    Recognition: "I thought you'd want to know that Random NPC George is working to undermine this."
    Manipulation: "Hey, I'm going to make that other thing look like this in order to get a rise out of you."
    Questioning: "Tell me more about this." (My favourite playtest example is this right here -- the PCs laid the groundwork for the situation without prompting from the GM.)

    But it's all player interaction, and it's targetted player interaction, which is the key for me. Painting a big red target on your character and saying, "Hit me here" opens up tons of possibilities for very awesome play.

    Tony, I don't want to give the impression that this is the one true technique, hallowed be its name. It's just that contrast is one of the small handful of techniques I understand well enough to explain clearly.

    The reason I can explain it is because Emily Care explained to me that the way things become important in an rpg is when the things we make up are accepted and elaborated upon by the other players, and I know how to put using contrast into that framework. Like, one way I can take "your stuff" seriously and engage with it is to challenge it with a conflict. Another way is to highlight it with contrast -- if you want to play someone brave, I play someone cowardly, to make your PC's bravery easier to see.

    Yet another way is to put land mines into the game (this seems like what Joshua is calling Implication and Manipulation). Like, if I know that your PC is prone to jumping to assumptions, I can have my PC start having regular lunches with your PC's husband, and making clear to the players (but not the characters) that they're just friends. Now, I've set things up so that you can have your PC jump to the conclusion that my PC and your PC's spouse are having an affair. You don't have to do it immediately -- you can save it for when sometime in the future you need something to do. And in the meantime, everyone can savor the potential for disaster. (I will bet hard cash money that the other players will immediately start looking for apparently innocent comments that will sow jealousy in your PC's mind.)

    Now that the giant disclaimer is out of the way, let me talk about what I've done in the past. I've used this idea during character creation, pairing off complementary traits like Bravery/Cowardice, Wisdom/Impulsiveness, Brick/Martial Artist, and so on. Then, the rule is that someone can only pick up a trait for their PC if someone else has the complementary trait. I got this idea from Greg Porter's EABA, which had a set of advantages that modelled each of the traditional roles of quest fantasy, like Chosen One, Wise Mentor, and Rebel, but which had the condition that every PC had to take one of the roles, more or less. This ensured the Wise Mentor would have a Chosen One to mentor, and the Rebel would have a Mentor to make fun of, and so on.

    This worked really well, but isn't nearly as flexible as the examples you're talking of, with the reversals happening dynamically. That's a really cool idea.
