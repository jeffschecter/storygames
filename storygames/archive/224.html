When small things loom large

Bruce Baugh
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
I was thinking this evening about features that end up looming larger than their users intend in triggering others' responses. (Adam Dray may recognize some of this... So I'd like to compare notes with others on things that bug - or delight you - disproportionally.

For me, I've realized, one of them is capitalization. This is of course in part the legacy of working with White Wolf and fighting a years-long internal battle to reduce the number of capitalized terms. (In the end, we pretty much won, too. You can see the evolution from 2nd edition to revised to the nWoD. But damn it was like pulling teeth.) And this affects me unduly with a lot of Forge stuff - for me, an unnecessarily capitalized word has almost the same kind of itch as a perpetrated misspelling. My head tells me "this is just a stylistic issue and it's not wrong, you know", and my heart says "like hell!" Which means that I end up doing extra internal work when I deal with a lexicon that seems to me like it could do with a lot fewer capitals.

Anyone else got that kind of trigger that makes it hard to justly appraise someone else's argument?

ADDENDUM: Note that this is not an effort to say "ha ha I'm right you lamerz" - I regard this as an identification of a weakness inside myself, because I'd prefer not to have the kneejerk, but have trouble with it anyway. So I'm not looking for triumphalism here.

    Jason, I think that some capitalization is always in order, and more may be depending on the particular project. My own drive to lower casing is quite possibly now on the other side of "what our broadly construed audience is comfortable with", or may soon be there.

    For my own taste, Pendragon has long been a showcase of good typography: a clear, light serif font for the body, clear, light sans serif font for headings, sidebars, and such. It looks good. So, I think, does Nobilis. (Including the cover. Remind me to tell the story of the bill for that in an art thread. It's funny.)

    My own theory right now is that readers in general will get whether a term has a game-specific meaning in a particular passage by context, if you write it clearly. Historians don't generally have to set off "class" when they're referring to the socio-economic categories of people with common engagements and outlooks, even if they refer in a single passage to that sort of class, and what students study in class, and maybe even to other kinds. Tracing it back, I think that capitalization in early rolegaming is partly a military legacy and partly a morbid fear of reader confusion. But readers may well be better than we often think.

    (Or, snark, the readers of the games a quality person such as myself makes are. )

    Good typography - including sound column widths and such - improves reader comprehension and retention for us as it does for, well, any subject at all, I think. I'm not right now convinced there's any gaming-specific wisdom of typesetting so much as a pressing need for more of us to learn what typesetters already know.

  • Thor_O
    February 2006
    As an editor, I recommend that all designers establish a style guide before they write. The guide should determine how you handle headings, subheadings, special rules text, examples, pull-outs, page references, capitalization, various punctuation styles (e.g., serial commas or no serial commas), etc.

    The style guide should also provide guidance on use of italics, boldface, indentation, and number use.

    I recommend that capitalization be used sparingly. For the most part, capitalization should be reserved for the beginning of sentences, proper nouns, and important words that are used in a manner contrary to the expected definition. If you're not sure whether to capitalize a word or not, leave it lowercase.

    For instance, in Burning Wheel, we capitalize the following: Stat names (Will, Perception, Forte, etc.), Attribute names (Steel, Faith, Reflexes, Mortal Wound), the names of individual lifepaths (Augur, Knight, Courtier), and the stocks when they refer to a group of people collectively under the racial descriptor (Dwarves, Men, Roden, Great Spiders, etc.).

    We do not capitalize the following: shade, exponent, trait(s), lifepath, obstacle, action, script, volley, test, versus, combat, wound.

    Use your style guide and stick to it. Give it to your editor and copy editor (especially your copy editor)! Your copy editor will probably be happy to work with you to develop a style guide if you need the help.

    As with any other aspect of language, you should do what you do to accomplish your goal in writing the damn thing down in the first place.

    If I'm writing FLFS, set in England circa 1890, I use 'one' instead of 'they'.

    If I'm writing street punks in a modern day setting, I don't use 'one' instead of 'they'.
