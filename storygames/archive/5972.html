\n I'm not sure whether it's a good idea to start a new thread for this, and what category it should go in. But I'm doing it anyway. This is about Ryan Stoughton's unnameable game. It's also kind of a Red Box Hack thread, since Ryan's game borrows so heavily from Eric's game. If you're not interested, don't read any further.

Ryan,

I just read both drafts of your TGCHNN (yeah, I'm sneaky that way). Very neat stuff!

There are a couple of things in there that are simply beautiful:

1. The way attributes are randomly determined, but don't put you at a disadvantage if you roll poorly.
2. You took Red Box Hack limitations and made them into TSoY Keys, dude! Awesome. That's just what they needed.
3. The Strategist/Commander features are hothothot. Just sweet. It's the best version of organizing/planning/strategizing rules I've seen, and they make me want to actually play them.

Now, a few comments and questions:

1. There doesn't seem to be much of a way to get more Conviction in combat. In the second version, you can steal it, which is cool, but it still seems like it would drain away over the course of a fight. Do you foresee a problem there?

2. Why such a narrow spread for stats? (Granted, the 2d6 scale does suggest a practical limit of +5.)

3. You mentioned in the other thread that something about the rules sucks. What was it? Have you fixed it?

4. In the second draft, it looks like the loser of a roll decides what to do with successes. (What about failures?) How do you keep this from getting frustrating for the winner, if I've read you correctly?

I know this is unsolicited. But you posted the rules, so you'll just have to take it. You're onto something pretty cool, I think.

Cheers!

    The spread thing is just a question of whether it allows for enough difference between PCs.     Posted By: JarrodRyan,yourplayers do it, but formyplayers to do it, you've got to hardcode that shit!
    \nWell, regardless of who the players are, Conviction is a very precious resource. So players naturally spend it when it's important to them. Sometimes "surviving this fight" is important, and sometimes "killing that guy" is more important.

    What I'm saying is that if players decide what's important to them in the moment, rather than in advance, it's still going to work. I'm leery of write-them-in-advance because that process doesn't capture some stuff that can happen in play.

    For example, say my 3 Drives are:

    1) Avenge my father's death
    2) Slay orcs
    3) Win the heart of the elven princess

    Now my buddy next to me, he's got his 3 as well:
    1) Come to terms with my troll blood
    2) Find my lost sister
    3) Be respected and honored in human society

    Now we're in a situation where we may kill the orc that killed my father. My half-troll buddy wants to spend Conviction but none of his Drives are relevant, so he can't.

    What's happened there is that something implicit has become important to the character over the course of the game (maybe even just 1 session). I don't want to kick out of the experience of the scene to have a discussion about changing Drives, nor do I want to have everyone fiating "Well, I guess the desire for respect and umm... troll blood kind of help you kill orcs."

    \n I think Paul is correct on the balance thing. I saw it too, but didn't mention it (or did I?).
    \n
    \nThe problem with his solution is that, on looking at the potential combinations of abilities that armor gets the bonus for defending with, you get one of two possible outcome arrangements:
    \n
    \n1. Each type of armor only has one weapon that it gets the defense against. In other cases the attacker can choose to void the armor.
    \n2. The attacker always has the ability to choose so as to avoid the armor.
    \n
    \nNeither sounds good. Yes, if the character is wearing heavy armor, you can choose to make his Grace and Insight high to counter... basically heavy armor becomes a hedge against a low Might or Resolve. But this seems counter to the intent of the design.
    \n
    \nIf you allow the defender to choose how to defend, then you can make it so that the armor works against three of the weapons, but he only has the choice against one. Against the other two, he must choose the correct stat to get the bonus. So, for instance, the way Paul's system works now, if I put on heavy armor, against reach weapons I can choose the higher ability, and get the bonus. Against Light I have to select Might, and against Heavy I have to select Resolve, in order to gain the bonus.
    \n
    \nThis is nice, because it means that I have to consider both stats. I have to consider the other two, already, for the one in four chance that my opponent has a Ranged weapon. So I have the option to stack behind the armor, or balance out, and behind the armor I have to decide if I'm better against light or heavy, or to balance.
    \n
    \nThat's a pretty reasonable set of options, with no one method really superior to another.
    \n
    \nIf you don't like the weapon match-ups, it's a simple matter to switch which ones they attack, and/or which abilities armor gets a bonus for defending against. The only problem with this is that the results might not be intuitive in terms of what ability is used with which weapons, or with either sort of armor.
    \n
    \nBy the way, using Paul's method, generally, you could include two other options: No armor and medium armor. Or something like that. Each option of which can just have a different combination. If you don't do this, then you may see everyone choose Reach and Ranged weapons, hoping to run into folks in the wrong sort of armor 50% of the time (the other two sorts always leave the opponent a choice of ability to defend with). If you have all four sorts of armor, then all options for weapons are equal.

    \n OK, I'll change this if there's a problem but it is readable and easy, I think.

    ---WEAPONS---

    HEAVY WEAPON
    You carry a Heavy weapon. Heavy weapons aren\u2019t fancy or elegant, but deal lots of damage. You take a -1 penalty on your attack, but when you hit, you deal an extra point of damage. Heavy weapons use Might.

    LIGHT WEAPON
    You carry a Light weapon. Light weapons are small, easy to wield, easy to conceal and handy for getting chinks in an enemy\u2019s armor. You get a +1 bonus on attack. Light weapons use Grace.

    RANGED WEAPON
    You carry a Ranged weapon. You may attack targets in nearby Zones with a ranged weapon. Ranged weapons use Insight.

    REACH WEAPON
    You carry a Reach weapon. Reach weapons are very long and you can use them to keep your enemies at bay. When you defend and are using a Reach weapon, add 1 to your defense. Reach weapons use Resolve.

    ---ARMOR---

    LIGHT ARMOR
    Light armor is very basic, and is fairly thin (like leather armor) or covers just the part of your body you intend to show your enemy (like a shield or a breastplate). Light armor uses Grace. Light armor is very mobile and gives a +1 bonus against Heavy weapons.

    HARD ARMOR
    Hard armor covers most of your body and is typically made from metal. Hard armor uses Resolve. Hard armor is good at deflecting attacks and gives a +1 bonus against Ranged weapons.

    THICK ARMOR
    Thick armor can be made from lots of different materials, and covers a good part of your body. You use Might to defend in Thick armor and ignore the first point of damage in every battle. Thick armor gives a +1 bonus against Light weapons.

    STRANGE ARMOR
    Strange armor includes heavy robes and magical tattoos. Strange armor uses Insight to defend. Strange armor is hard to gauge an attack on and gives a +1 bonus against Reach weapons.
