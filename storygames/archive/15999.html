Hacking D&D

I think everyone who has played more than a couple of sessions of D&D has their own version of the game within them, waiting to come out. As a system it\u2019s so amenable to tinkering that it\u2019s almost impossible to resist. These hacks though don\u2019t often seem to go very far. Most people seem to end up back where they started, playing their favourite published edition of D&D rather than their hack. The \u201cFantasy Heartbreaker\u201d - an author\u2019s \u201cimprovement\u201d of (usually later edition) D&D is a notoriously difficult sell. OSR products are usually modules and materials for existing editions, or re-statements and reorganisations of older games. Why should this be the case? Why is D&D at once so hackable and also so resistant to change?

Here\u2019s part of the reason, I think: The central metaphor for the strategy and challenge of D&D isn\u2019t the pure cerebral planning of chess, or even the calculated risks of backgammon. It\u2019s Mao. For those who don\u2019t know, Mao is a card game of a very simple shedding type, but with additional rules of play which are kept secret and which players must guess or intuit during play. One player is responsible for maintaining adherence to the rules, and administers penalties (drawn cards) to players who infringe. Play is about remembering an unspoken set of arbitrary and often arcane rules, and about maintaining a shared commitment to an ever-increasing body of knowledge. It\u2019s fun because each play of a card is a step into the unknown, trusting that you\u2019ve remembered or interpreted the rules correctly. It\u2019s fun watching newer players stumble and be penalised for reasons they don\u2019t understand, but which make perfect sense to you. Each group of players has their own set of rules, constructed over time, understood to each of them, but almost impossible for an outsider to grasp.

D&D is the same. The basic rules of play are simple enough, but the \u201cmeta\u201d rules, the rules about how to keep one\u2019s character alive in a dungeon, are unspoken, arcane, and endless. Players learn the rituals of checking for traps, of which monsters can be fought and which must be avoided, of how to manipulate the fiction of play to their advantage. The DM arbitrates, punishing players who have not mastered these secret rules, rewarding those who have. The players are complicit. They know that they will not understand all the rules, and that even doing everything right there is an element of risk. You make your move and you take your chances. Each play is a step into the unknown. Each group has their own understanding of the \u201cright\u201d way to behave in a dungeon, their own unspoken rules built up over time. Unlike Mao, however, encounters between groups with different rules are less likely to be productive and enjoyable, and more likely to cause friction. This is perhaps because of the persistent illusion that there\u2019s only one D&D, while Mao players don\u2019t have that same belief. Different rules are part of the fun of that game. D&D is also different in that the relationship between infringement and penalty is much less clear. In Mao, an infringement is instantly and unambiguously punished, while in D&D, especially less functional D&D, the relationship is less direct. The biggest difference is that Mao is at least nominally competitive. D&D is not competitive at all. The joy of play comes entirely from testing and affirming the groups\u2019 mastery of the unspoken body of rules and rituals. Watching people fail is usually not part of the fun.

What implications does this have for hacking D&D? I think that it\u2019s enormously tempting, and generally quite easy, to modify the procedures of play of D&D to more closely fit with the unspoken rules of survival and with your aesthetics of play. This is done instantly and painlessly for some of the less central procedures. Some people play with a \u201ccaller\u201d, but many do not. Rules for ability checks are implemented very differently for different groups. It\u2019s also easier to go further. A typical tension is a friction between how the rules adjudicate outcomes (especially combat), and what the group expects \u201cshould\u201d be the outcome, based on their unspoken rules. Often, the unspoken rules take precedence, and the procedures of play are altered. More often though, the fiction exists in an uneasy d\xe9tente with the rules, and the players accept a certain amount of cognitive dissonance.

So why don\u2019t more groups hack D&D? Why do so many return to playing a published edition of D&D or a close clone thereof? There\u2019s certainly some truth to the argument that these are well designed games and that hacks usually create more problems than they solve in attempting to improve on them. But I don\u2019t believe that there have been no improvements in the art of game design in the forty-odd years since the game was published. I don\u2019t believe that the game as it stands in its original incarnation delivers on its goals better than any other conceivable design. I think that the chief advantage that D&D has over any hack of D&D is that it is D&D. That is, the design is improved by the cultural cache that comes with the name Dungeons and Dragons. If you have accrued skill and experience playing some homebrew D&D hack, that cache, that cultural capital is restricted to your home group, but skill and experience playing D&D is transferrable \u2013 it is cultural capital that spends, at least within certain circles. The unspoken rules, rituals and procedures you learn playing D&D are valuable (even if others\u2019 groups have very different unspoken rules), because other people recognise and respect them. Learning the same or similar rules under a homebrew system renders those skills suspect, and less valuable.

So what does that mean for D&D hacks? Are they doomed to fail? Should we give up on trying to improve on Moldvay or Holmes or Gygax or whichever edition is your favourite? I don\u2019t want to say yes, but I don\u2019t see a way around it.

    \n Isn't the difference simply that you're playing a hack if you think that you are? If the group thinks that they're playing by the rules as written, and strive to do so in fact to whatever degree they find practical, then I'd think that their behaviors will align in that way typical of by-the-rules play: they will care of what the rules say, and respect them. The group that recognizes that they're playing a houseruled version of the game will be more prone to question the rules and substitute ones they perceive as better. It's not about the amount of changes you do, but about why you're doing them: a GM who strives to play by the rules could make more changes in his effort to make contradictory or non-functional rules work than the other guy who decides that he's going to fix the game by hacking it, and one would still be a basically lawful group, while the other is hackery.

    Could still be that practically nobody plays D&D by the rules, at least older editions. I've only encountered real-life by-the-book attitudes regarding 3rd edition and up, but I remember reading in the Internet how some players of the older editions apparently find it important to think that they're playing by the official and correct rules.

    Regarding Simon's central thesis, my own take is that the important key elements of D&D are nearly non-mechanical, and nearly unrecognized in the traditional discussions, obsessed as they are with game mechanics. The true method, as I understand it, requires types of behaviours that essentially will lead a group to recreate D&D over time (I'm thinking of things like precedent-based rules-development and game prep combined with player-controlled game direction here), utilizing very nearly the same organic, play-based processes that were originally used in Gygax's campaign that was spun into the early game texts. Worrying about adherence to the game text is a red herring when the game text only relates in passing to the core nature of the game, and mostly just regales us with plentiful detritus of the process of play.

    In other words, I don't think that a D&D hack is doomed to "fail", I think that playing an older edition game successfully is tantamount to hacking as you use the term here.

    \nSo part of why one can\u2019t simply say \u201cD&D\u201d anymore and expect any sort of real communication:

    Edition wars over D&D actually make sense, in a strange sort of way. You see the rules in Basic Mentzer/Moldvay really are apart from AD&D1e which started to include more and more complex rules and even a different focus. Then the shift that AD&D2e started completely changed things. I mean there isn\u2019t even XP for gold anymore! So 2nd edition is really where things started to move away from what I think of when somebody says, \u201cD&D\u201d. Monsters are pretty much the only source of XP, and now the Reward System starts to break down - the system needed more and more rules to cover combat and not dying and not being able to run away and... don\u2019t even get me started on \u201cKits\u201d and the focus on \u201cstory\u201c. 3rd edition moves even farther a field with battle maps and Attacks of Opportunity. With 4th edition going, yet again, even further astray by making death pretty much impossible and loading up characters with what is tantamount to super powers.

    But you see, all of this was a natural extension of the culture and oral traditions that were being passed down. All of the \u201cfixes\u201d of the editions created that dissonance that you speak of. The metaphorical dominoes start to fall and unexpected \u201cdissonance\u201d becomes unavoidable.

    Why? Because people started to \u201cexpect\u201d an outcome. They expected their character to live, in part because without stable characters there is not as much room for story and plot. Basic D&D doesn\u2019t promise that as the rules imply. AD&D started that promise of character focus, and it came to fruition with 2nd edition, gained more life in 3rd edition, and with 4th death is nearly impossible.

    And that example is just a \u201cD&D is about combat\u201d example! But then that is what D&D has become. What D&D was\u2026 that was entirely different! One did not simply go around and kill things. A smart player avoided combat and got the loot! That was, after all, the real source of XP.

    EDIT: I believe this is why people think that D&D is about combat and not exploration of the fictional space as well. The weight of the rules started to create this sense of "wait the rules don't match the fiction!", just look at 4th edition. If one has rules-bloat then most people will naturally turn to rules as written for answers. Just look at thieves: anybody could pick a lock or disarm a trap, but once one has these "skills" the players stop to innovate and describe and fall back on, "my dude picks the lock. I rolled a 10", or whatever. (And since I'm starting to sound like a Grognard anyway I might as well). The DMs job was not to obfuscate the dungeon! How would the players interact with it then? It really was/is a "Fiction First" game: "tell me what you want to do and we'll figure out a way to make it happen". But then, once again, this cultural oral tradition sprang up and people stopped reading the rules and just played the way they wanted to - even if the way they started to play didn't make sense and they complained about it!

    Conclusion: D&D had stones that were stepped upon to get to where it is today. If one follows the trail.

      \n@Vernon: I mean, all the characters in GoT *need* gold. I figure you could look at them as being Level 9 PCs, i.e. "lords" with property and responsibilities and such. I think it sells D&D short to look at it as a perennial dungeon-crawling game - - you crawl around in dungeons until you don't have to any longer, amirite?

      In terms of 0e/1e, anyway, this seems to be reasonable. A powerful warlord might decide to go tomb-raiding again with the fellas, just for kicks, but the player guiding him is more concerned with paying the men-at-arms and levying taxes and that tricky land war brewing in Asia.

      Monte Cook has been writing about 5e of late over on the Wizards website, and he been saying how he likes the 4e system of three-tiered play, and that it's a feature of play to treat different level ranges as quite different game experiences - early levels for the sword-hobo aesthetic, middle levels for the growing power (but still adventuring), and high levels for the land ownership and political intrigue.

      One of the big features of older D&D, imo, is that a lot is completely left up to the DM's discretion and inspiration so that it can kind of go wherever. Yes, that's kind of a cop-out, but historically this interpretation does pan out.
      At high levels, I think the internal reward system changes up - - once you own property and have followers, you will likely be compelled to do what you can to either hang onto, or build upon, your new power base. The mentality of "up, up, up!" has gotten you this far, and now that mentality moves into the fiction and away from the explicit mechanics.

      Of note: a lot of GP=XP dungeon crawl games make it pretty clear that you only get XP from cash that's found on an adventure; robbing random travelers and taxing your vassals are not "adventurous", and therefore don't grant XP... but gold is gold, and Lordly characters have bills to pay. So while surface-world activities won't keep your character level going up, they will help you to maintain what you've accomplished (at least enough to pay for it all).
      Different stage of the game = different priorities.

      On the other hand, my first thought is: why not just have a game where you start at the Lord tier? And my answer to myself (haha) is: yeah! Why the hell not? Eero has pointed out before that monsters keep getting tougher with every level, so on a personal level your character can only really feel powerful if she goes back to lower level dungeons to kick some ass.
      The answer to the riddle is this: not only does a high level character have a lot less to worry about financially, but she also has a lot less to worry about in terms of finding henchmen, porters, torch bearers, specialists, etc. If your power doesn't grow in the fictional realm over time, you're being short-changed. This is a divide or angle (fictional vs. personal power) that AW touches on quite a bit, and I think working that understanding into D&D could be quite fruitful when considering what high-level play is "all about".

      As for the lack of mechanical support for stuff, well... that complaint is as old as D&D, and I solved that problem for myself by simply playing other games. OSR games treat this lack like a feature, and relish the sheer amount of adjudication required by the DM, while more recent D&D editions have simply expanded to cover more spheres of activity with concrete rules. [edit: for what it's worth, adjudicating is a lot of fun for me. And I like doing so really transparently, too - - it feels a lot like taking a pause to make a ruling in AW. Can you tell I've been playing a lot of AW lately? haha]

      EDIT: Have you read the Old School Zen Manifesto? Basically, it's not an argument about "doing it wrong" so much as it is (imo) an attempt to give old school dungeon crawling games their proper context/mentality.
