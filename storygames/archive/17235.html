    \n I have only played it once.

    I'd really like to play it some more and facilitate it because I think it's pretty damn hot for cyberpunk role playing.

    Some people find it a hard read because its organization is a bit unorthodox.

    If everyone is really familiar with the genre, GHOST/ECHO, Tears In Rain (Trollbabe), & Shock can all work well too. But Remember Tomorrow does some things that help get people on the same page and has some systemic conceits that really pay tribute to the genre in a way those games do not. That said, I really need to play it again.

    Another obvious choice,Technoir, mostly did not work for me, (apart from Transmissions, which are shiny tech).

    \n Remember Tomorrow's greatness lies in several things:

    First, it's a bring-your-own-cyberpunkness party. Sure, there are some neat oracle-style tables there to make suggestions, but it all comes down to the players to decribe the level of technology and how it gets used in their shared world. Since all the tech is colour, players can freely narrate their cyber-eyes, tactile holographic interfaces and Harley-Davidson Orbital Insertion Capsules to their hearts' content.

    Second, you have to weave multiple story threads together. Every character and every faction has its own agenda, with a clear win condition created by the mechanics, so all the time, in every scene, someone is pushing to achieve something. As all these elements bump up against each other, you can't help but produce a rich, deep narrative.

    Third, the hotseat GMing. When it's your turn, you don't play your character (OK, you can, but the scene won't really be about them), you pick on someone else's! Choose the storyline that interests you most or the one where you have a great idea for what happens next or the one that's had no action for a while; frame a scene for the character most involved in that story, putting an obstacle in their way, then see how they get over it.

    Fourth, the freedom and power the above gives you. Want to drop a new character into things? BOOM! Done. Realised which faction might really be behind things? Play a scene and reveal their nefarious plans. Love two of the characters and want them to cross paths? Then do it!

    Fifth, everything else about the game. The simple stats, the conditions that get traded about as game currency, the reward system, etc. It's all tightly woven together within the pages of a suprisingly compact little book, but gives you vast amounts of potential to play with.

    \n I tried Remember Tomorrow once. The multiple intertwining plots are gibsonian but also a nightmare to keep track of, which is why I doubt I will ever try to promote it again to my group. The mechanics felt quite gamey and abstract, and curiously devoid of cyberpunk trappings, but I feel like I didn't give them a good enough chance. For a 50-page game it's jam-packed with rules and looks great to boot.

    Technoir always felt oddly lifeless to me when reading it. I didn't feel like cyberpunk themes were really addressed, but I haven't really tried the game.

    \n I really like Technoir. It's more noir with cyberpunk trappings than it is cyberpunk, though, IMHO.

If you want to put the meta-narrative and plot twists in the hands of the players, then maybe Shock:. Remember Tomorrow I can't comment on, but by the sounds of it, it seems like it'd be a good fit, too.

    \n Yah, Remember Tomorrow is the most Gibsonian* RPG experience I've had, for the reasons already stated. It's more reminiscent of his more recent stuff (Spook Country, Zero History) to me, but that may be because I actually like that stuff more than the Sprawl stuff. If you have more than three players, probably mindfully taking notes about what-character-has-done-what-with-what-faction may be useful, if you're not used to keeping that kind of thing sorted in your head.

    * I know, Snow Crash isn't by Gibson, but it's the most Gibsonian of Neal Stephenson's work, to me. I dunno if I've played anything that reminds me more of Stephenson's stuff, though.

    \n I'm gonna double up on Technoir being better at delivering that plot development kick a bit better than Remember Tomorrow, in my limited experience. RT is very narrative based, so having players that are skilled at narrative based games (Fiasco at least, Polaris at best) is better. My players had an awful time trying to narrate events off the cuff, because that's just not their style. Technoir created a better framework and set of rules for players, while still allowing for player agency (control over characters) and cool GM stuff (the Transmissions allow for an exploding plot that just keeps growing with minimal work).

    Good luck with Remember Tomorrow and let us know how it goes!
