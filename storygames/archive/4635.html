    \n I actually started a thread on this at the Collective Endeavour: Creative Commons: Is It Bollocks?.

    Briefly, though, I think the creator-owned publishing community is about:

    1. Making your own decisions about your products
    2. Making a bit of money from selling your stuff

    Creative Commons works against both of those: because it leads to people sharing their stuff "but, hey, only if you share your stuff, too, and don't sell it".

    What I particularly hate is that Creative Commons is the new hotness: so everyone releases their stuff under it, because it's cool, thereby placing restrictions on anyone who uses it.

    By the way, to forestall an obvious objection: of course, I realise that I could email the author of a Creative Commons licensed-thing and ask if
    I could use it under another license. But I'd prefer that be the default: if I find something I want to use, I contact the author, and never mind all the Creative Commons stuff.

    Graham

    \n I'm a lover, and here are my hates about the CC licenses:
    \n
    \n 1. The fact that there are so many options to choose from makes the notion of being "CC Licensed" almost meaningless.
    \n 2. The "non-commercial use only" clause is a mess. It's a fuzzy concept with no strict definition, and it's just an arbitrary limitation on field of endeavor. Might as well have "no girls allowed" licenses.
    \n 3. The licenses are rewritten for each jurisdiction, meaning that even once you specify a set of license clauses, you end up having to figure out what that specifically means in your legal jurisdiction.
    \n
    \nIf you ask me, they should have just made a permissive license (the Attribution one), a weak copyleft license (attribution-sharealike), and called it good. If people really want to restrict freedom of commerce, they should hire their own lawyers.
    \n
    \nThe sad thing is that the WTFPL is all most people need, even if bowdlerized:
    \n
    \n "DO WHAT THE FUCK YOU WANT." is a perfectly acceptable license. It makes absolutely clear what terms the author releases the work under. It also avoids misunderstandings about the state of official "public domain" status.

    \n It's funny watching this discussion

    And I say this without really wanting to be snobbish: it's just that I come from the free software world, and this thread is practically a perfect replica.

    Basically we have two camps: the BSD camp prefers a license that sets the content free, and pemits any use of it to those who want, even closing the license on the derivative works (the attribution is only a formality, and was in the BSD license until some years ago).

    The GNU/GPL camp prefers a license that sets the content free, and forces whomever benefits from it (making derivative works) to re release it with the same kind of license. Thus, the "bunch" of GPL licensed stuff grows.

    These two positions have not been reconciled in 30 years of computer science, don't expect to do this in this thread
    Just recognize it's two different philosophical approaches.

    History, so far, has taught one clear lession, though: releasing with BSD-like licenses does not help the makers of the original content, at all. Big vendors take the content, tweak it 10%, close it and resell it, without ever contributing back those changes. The BSD world, even if only rarely and grudgingly admitting it, are now seeing that they would have probably been better off with a more stringent license (like the GPL).

    Oh, for the purpose of this model/example: CC-Attribution is BSD-Like, and CC-Attr-ShareAlike is GPL-like.

    Non commercial is an anomaly, yes. The GPL like licenses work because they don't restrict the USE at all. Gpl-like "non commercial use" does not make any sense.

    \n Some sidenotes:

    If you do not choose a license of some sort, it's straight copyright that applies (the one with the c with a circle ). That is, nobody can do pretty much anything legally with it, unless you give them explicit permission.

    Collaborative projects and licenses: normally it's stated in the project policy that contributing means accepting a license transfer/agreement of some sort. Sometimes it could pay to look at the fine print: it could mean that you automatically transfer the intellectual property to someone else (yes, gleemax, I'm looking at you). More often, you simply agree to license your part of the work with a license that's permissive enough for the whole body of work to be used and released.

    But changing the license later on, if it's not a "do whatever you want" license like CC Attribution or BSD, means contacting each and every contributor and asking for agreement, or removing the relevant part.

    Public Domain vs CC: Public Domain means close to nothing, expecially outside the USA. CC licenses, otoh, have been written by license expert lawyers, and have been translated and checked to be compliant with the local laws in many languages. For example, I can release a CC licensed doc in italian, but not really set it in the public domain.

    CC confusion: yes, it's there, and it's people using the name inappropriately to blame. That's why in the software world we are so picky when license names are wrongly cited. GPL is very different from LGPL, for example.

    Stuff "stolen" by the big dogs: yes, it's not that relevant for rpg stuff, but... it is not really that important. I mean, the fact here is that creating new content/rules/games/whatever based on your game does you (and the rest of the community) zero benefit. OTOH, if the new content gets released back, everybody has benefits. The original creator and the community get access to the new stuff. The creator of the new stuff gets the benefit of starting from some really rocking base instead of doing all the heavy lifting.

    And I'm not thinking about the CC at all, here. I'm thinking about the OGL. Fate is under the OGL. Spirit of the Century is under the OGL. An SRD is made, and all of a sudden a half dozen derivative projects start, some of them with commercial purposes (Starblazer, Far West, Vegas After Midnight, New Orizons... I probably forget some). And guess what, when they will be under the OGL, and more stuff will be available to Fate players, developers and tinkerers.
    The Starblazer's large scale starship, planet and organization rules will be out, available for integration in new games. And the fine guys at Cubicle 7 would not have been able to do so much work starting from scratch such a short time, and it's hard to come up with a new system as good as Fate 3.0, if you ask me.

    Bam! Everybody wins.

    ...IANAL, but I play one on TV :-P

      \n In my other life, I'm an attorney in the software world, so I'm somewhat familiar with the nuts and bolts of BSD, GPL, etc. The analogy with software developers and the various open source licenses is pertinent but inexact. Many developers are selling services and expertise, such as Apache or Red Hat. The BSD crowd, by making their license essentially (a) attribute me and (b) please don't sue me, have a better chance of having their software adopted widely by for-profit developers whose clients are chary of the GPL's requirements in the event the technical relationship of the open source software with the commercial code OR derivative changes to the open source component triggers the forced-release of all the code. When I advise companies, I tell them GPL is safe for use if you keep it partitioned from the commercial code, but it's still risky because governmental clients, for instance, have pretty stringent requirements about inclusion of open source

      It's all about creator intent. If you want your stuff widely adopted so you can leverage your expertise, a less-restrictive license like BSD is better. If you want to restrict commercial entities from incorporating your work into other products without releasing it as open source, GPL is better.

      With creative content and RPG game engines, the comparison breaks down a bit. There's no ongoing service aspect to leverage--for instance, Clinton probably can't get paid to answer questions about TSoY based on his particular expertise. Most of this is the smaller scale and lower complexity of the indie RPG market, part is simply the close-knit nature of the RPG gaming community where Q&A happen freely. The OGL seems to have struck a decent compromise and from the perspective of kicking the market into producing a huge volume of d20 product, it has succeeded admirably. Whether this flood has helped Wizards' bottom line is another question.

      I'm not familiar with Creative Commons, but the jurisdictional heterodoxy and patchwork of different licenses would put me off. If the intent of the authors is to derive income from their games, a standard license that encourages collaboration while preserving some degree of copyright sounds optimal. If the intent of an author is to have his/her game adopted as widely as possible into secondary products without regard to taking a cut of the sales, a BSD-style license would probably work best.

      \n I discuss this elsewhere at length but, basically, many of the CC licenses are so nebulously defined as to render them useless for their intended purpose. Many are, specifically, subject to retroactive revision by the issuing party, allowing said party to remove content previously declared as open by virtue of not spelling out very specifcally the terms under which the content was made open in the first place. With the exception of Attribution, the licenses are prone to be fairly disingenuous. Now, granted, I can't recall any CC licenses being abused in this manner, though the possibility is most definitely there. And it shouldn't be.
