    \n I couldn't find "official" word from VB on a cursory search, but google reveals someone named Abzu made a comment here.

    He says:\n
    Not to be a pedant, but it's "Roll the dice or say 'Yes'." I think it's signficant. Dice come first. If you're not going to roll for it, just agree to it and move on.

    That slight tweak nips the ridiculous requests out. Why? Because "rolling dice" is when we go to the rules to resolve a situation. Rolling to resolve in the rules provides context for the narration. You don't roll to resolve out-of-context problems because ithey're not a part of the game. Therefore, you don't Say Yes to anything that's outside the context of the game either.

    \n It's not a deep and meaningful truth or anything. It's just that thinking, "Do I want to say YES? No, then we'd better roll the dice..." suggests that the GM saying "no" is the way we engage the system. Whereas thinking, "Should we roll dice over this? If it's not worth rolling dice over, say YES..." puts engaging the system up front, with "saying yes" as a good way out from there.

    Given a statement that is "outside the game", like flapping one's arms to fly to the moon, the first version can lead some people to roll the dice over stupid stuff like that. The second version reminds people to consider regular resolution first, and then reminds them that just saying yes is an option. Hopefully, if they do that, they'll remember not to say "yes" to things they wouldn't normally engage the resolution system to resolve. In other words, the intents or statements should make sense in the scope of the game played.

    It's just a little mental trick that could help some people "get it". It's not more meaningful than that. It's a wording thing, not a fundamental truth thing,

    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanLook, guys, Dogs was written in 2004-2005. One of Vincent's primary concerns was that players were avoiding mechanics because they were used to the mechanics telling them "no," but negotiating around them being effective. "Roll the dice or say yes" is apromisethat it'll be interesting either way.
    \nThis is interesting because it reminds me of the discussion that often comes up among trad gamers about whether to "role play out" social conflict or use the stats on the sheet. For various reasons, a lot of trad gamers feel perfectly fine that roll out, rather than act out physical conflict, but they reverse themselves when it comes to social conflict. (And there's the opposite camp too, who ... after someone has built an eloquent and persuasive argument in character ... say, "that's great, now roll your diplomacy stat" ... or charisma or whatever.)

    I don't know where VB comes down on social conflict mechanics, though given the way Poison'd works I think I can guess.

    I think the order of the phrase is critical because you have two decisions and it matters which comes first. The two decisions are a) should we engage the mechanics here because there is a conflict? and b) if the answer is "No," then let's accept the fictional proposition and move on.

    To reverse the phrase implies a value-loaded judgement of someone's attempt to add to the fiction PRIOR to the question of whether the mechanics should be engaged. So whether or not a player's attempt to move the story in a certain direction works is up to GM fiat or group consensus, first, regardless of whether the mechanics could be used to resolve the issue.

    I don't like putting that decision first. If the mechanics apply, why preempt them and jump to a purely subjective judgement of someone's attempted fictional input?

    EDIT

    Oh, and the answer is always "Yes" because deciding the mechanics aren't necessary is the same as deciding there really isn't anything at stake. Nobody cares to "roll against" what the player is trying to add to the story, so accept it!
