\n So recently there was a review of Sorcerer & Sword that criticized the book because if failed to include *bad* Swords & Sorcery fiction and therefore failed at being "complete." Besides my many issues with that review this particular comment struck a chord in me. It made me think of the link Paul Tevis pointed out to me about the Gorilla Wingman in the SotC thread I started where I said I'd never seen a robo-zombie-nazi in a pulp story. I put all this together and I realized what my beef with pulp gaming is.

All the pulp games on the market (that I know of) are about celebrating the *whole* of pulp fiction *including* the bad stuff. Robo-zombie-nazis are funny so let's get together and cheer them on. No one has done for the other pulp genres (western, crime fighting, war stories, exploration, non-Lovecraftian weird horror, etc) what Ron did for Swords and Sorcery. No one has dug into the fiction and thrown out all the bad stuff and really examined what's left over and made a game about that.

I'm reminded of the self-directed study I did of Gothic literature. I was shocked to discover that Ann Radcliffe's The Mysteries of Udolpho is held up as the key work of the genre because it contains every element typically touted as being *bad* about the genre. WTF? Seriously, The Mysteries of Udolpho is the pinnacle of a genre because of how *BAD* it is? (And they're right, that's 800 pages of my life I will never get back). Especially when Ms. Radcliffe gave us Romance of the Forest which is 18th century awesomeness!

So....yeah....that's my beef with pulp gaming.

Jesse

    \n That's an interesting point. I come at pulp gaming from a bit more of a deconstructive angle - i.e. we are snickering behind our hands at the racism and sexism of the form, or eliminating the more objectionable parts - and as a result, I don't mind keeping the "bad stuff" in. I share a birthday with Sax Rohmer, creator of Fu Manchu, which is both fundamentally pulp adventure and truly awful in its treatment of race, sex, and even class. If you enjoy Rohmer's stories, you have to address those issues somehow in your own mind, and if you're playing Thrilling Tales or Two-Fisted Tales or Adventure! in the style of such a story, you have to be able to do the same. Mere lack of literary merit doesn't bother me nearly as much!

    I see the weakness of pulp gaming in a different area. For example, sword and sorcery gaming has done virtually nothing to help people tell or play in games that are like pulp sword and sorcery stories. People said Eberron was "really pulp D&D" but that to me is highly redundant. D&D is pulp fantasy, it has to be. You fight snakes with swords. That D&D has passed over the tools developed in the pulp fantasy genre does not change its essential nature. But there's virtually no pulp gaming that is based on, say, pulp romance, or pulp Westerns, two ridiculously gigantic areas of pulp that even survive and prosper into the present day! Instead, people are reaching back to zeppelins and tough guys fighting Ratzis, in which little significant has been done in a long time.

    \n I think that if you strip all the "bad" period stuff out of pulp, you get something that doesn't work real well - the cardboard stories are too thin without pastiche-appeal holding them up, and an awful lot of the appeal of the genre is precisely in the pastiche. If you do a Sorcerer & Sword on them, and try to get to the heart of the genre... well, I think what's left IS mainstream adventure fiction - in other words, the same thing that drives pretty much all RPGs. "Pulp", in the Indiana Jones robots & nazis pastiche form that the genre is typified by in gaming circles, is all color.

    Why don't you see romance pulp, western pulp, etc? Because those tropes don't have the same "geek appeal" as robots & nazis? Or because they're not Thrilling Adventures full of Daring Heroes and Dastardly Villains...? (okay, westerns are sometimes)

    I love "pulp" as much as the next geek, but I don't think that "pulp" in gaming terms has much to do with anything thematic in the way Sword & Sorcery is still an identifiable Thing even stripped of the pulpy trappings.

    \nPulp, "good" or "bad" is at bottom an exploitation of archetypes, isn't it? Hero, villain, lovable scoundrel, hapless victim, incompetent minions of the dark lord, competent and scary ones, wise men and wizards... these are also the very things that give us a starting point for (and sometimes, let's admit it, all of) our NPCs and player characters. It's also a big shared part of the common experience that brings us geeks together. Without it, we have little or no basis for those tentative establishments of consensus and connection that let us game together and, if we're lucky, become friends. Good gamer-types move upwards and onward from this just as good readers do.
    \n

Not that I prefer to game in, say, David Eddings' worlds instead, but his crap gives touchstones for the basic stuff -- it's "Hot Cross Buns" to Wolfe's gigantic jazz improv-scapes....

    You might want to take a look at Justice, Inc. a pulp game using the Hero System, written by Aaron Allston, Steve Peterson and Michael Stackpole. The book was pretty good on at least identifying specific source stories and characters.

    But before 2000, fictional source material usually meant grabbing stuff from the covers of paper back novels and saying, "Hey! we're playing X!"

    \n It seems obvious, thinking about it, that games created with the express purpose of CREATING A KIND OF STORIES (can I say "narrativist" without being targeted by some inthaweb defense mechanism?) would contain essays (or at least some words) about what of human value these stories told us. I disagree a little with Christopher about the scarcity of examples: there is Sorcerer and Sword for sword and sorcery, but there is Sorcerer's Sould and Dirty Secrets that talk a lot about crime fiction. My Life with Master contain, intersected with the rules, a deconstruction of the literature that talk about minions and masters. Even the new edition of Dust Devils has a chapter about western movies. And Spione raised again the bar, with its treatment of "spy vs guy" fiction (and reality). So it's not something so rare in narrativist games.

    What all these have in common is a focus on the heart of the literature, about "what they really talked about", and not on superficial genre trappings. This differentiate them from the awful lot of games that list hundreds of pages of meaningless details about some franchise: these are useful to create pastiches, imitations of the old works, not new stories.

    (I am ignoring for simplicity the rather common case of game manuals that have BOTH, because this would mean opening the can of worms of the quality and quantity of the analysis in these books, that is very variable)

    A possible reason of the lack, in pulp games, of a critical analysis about the genre, could be the focus on pastiche: to homage a genre, you have to homage the bad as the good, or your homage isn't truthful, isn't "on target". This would differentiate their function from the one of Sorcerer and Sword rather drastically, and would render any comparation of dubious utility.

    \n Hi Jean,

    Thanks for the reply.

    Well, here we disagree. I think I see what you're saying, but from my observations about play and from the texts, I'm not sure that the game Cyberpunk (fun game though it was, especially the character creation), did more than hand people mirrorshades and cyberware. In my view (and it's just my view, and the way I like to play) if some thought isn't put into why or what a genre or game is "about" there most often won't be any "about" there.

    I don't think most people were interested in this, by the way. That is, I don't think what Jesse ranted about, or that I agreed with, was a secret longing on the part of players that the games failed to deliver. (It was an open longing on my part and some players, but we were the minority.) Most people were happy to push around and explore the genre elements. But to get at what Gibson and Sterling were up to, for example, would require approaching the books at fellow mechanics and looking under the hood of how the stories worked, what the themes were, and so on.

    I want to add that this, too, is fun. There is a great fun to having taken apart thematic concerns and then built them into games on purpose, with awareness of the players. Games I've played that have delivered exactly this in spades are Pendragon, Shock:, Primetime Adventures, Sorcerer, and Grey Ranks, as well as a home brew Greg Gordon did using his DC Heroes game for a cool setting, some sessions I ran of AD&D that pretty much blew off the rules. (That's MY list off the top of my head, early in the morning).

    Now, I'm not saying people didn't do exactly as you're saying, and I'm sure there were some fucking awesome Cyberpunk games. I mean that. I'm saying that in most of con experiences, college year games and post college year games, most games were about nothing. Pastiche genre elements mixed with some power fantasy and puzzle/problem solving was the bulk of the gameplay -- which is a good thing if that's what the players wanted! I simply wasn't one of those players. When I did hit a game that was about Issues (to reference a nearby and recent thread) the magic of RPGs hit for me.

    Stories, if they're going to be interesting, have to have such issues woven into them. We can often appreciate them as audience members even if we're not appreciating them intellectually. The analogy I often use when talking to beginning writers is that we can enter the cool lobby of a building and be awed by the lovely marble, high atrium, and the skylight -- but never give a moment's thought to the support girders holding the building up. But as a screenwriter, you must be aware of those girders. In the same way, to build RGP sessions to have strong thematic content, the game and the players using the game should have, in my view, a little understand of how narrative works and so on. (The games listed above all do, as so others. There's nothing crazy or scary or no-fun going on here.)

    And if you're going to tap a genre, I think you need to see behind the marble and the skylight and get to some of the support girders. Otherwise you often end up with what I saw: People in mirrorshades running around with guns and cyberware, who might as well been running around with guns in any setting. Because strong genre elements are there to pictorially represent the thematic material underneath the picture. Cyberpunk was about much more, and was more interesting than, "corps bad, cool outsiders good." Even the guys who started the genre said it was pretty much over by the time it began and that everyone following after them were just grabbing the pictures and pasting them into stories to ride the gravy train. If it can happen in the fiction, it can certainly happen in the games.

    So, my point is this: If one loves something, why not love it more by trying to understanding it? Such an act of deeper reading of a genre doesn't remove any of the fun for the material. It deepens one's understanding and appreciation for the material. And if one wants to play a game to "recreate" or "tap" or, best of all, explore more deeply what one loves by bringing that material to an RPG session with friends, why not do it with more knowledge?

    Now, I see that you're saying that was happening. And, again, maybe it was. Maybe people were successfully producing interesting thematic material in intuitive and unconscious ways by grabbing trope and cliches and images. But I seldom saw it.

    Given all that, can we agree that one's odds are better at producing thematically interesting content (and all the best work in the world is thematically interesting) if one does a little bit of work at understanding how the source material was constructed and that's what one wants? That is the difference you said you're not seeing. And in my view, bringing that effort to the table as a kind of creative-carpentry is a huge difference.

    \n One thought:

    Many (most?) mainstream RPGs present an undifferentiated pastiche that covers a broad swath of genre. D&D includes nods to everything from Excalibur to Conan; Traveller is centered on Imperial-style scifi but does Lensmen pretty well too. And of course there's Exalted. It's one of the fundamental principles of trad RPG design -- put as much color into your game as will fit, and figure out how to rationalize it all into one setting.

    My understanding of the reasoning behind that approach has always been a) to develop a creative setting not tied to any specific (and probably expensive) license or property and b) to provide such a big, colorful toolset that a DIY GM can pick and choose from to recreate the focused game he cares about.

    Well, that was great back when DIY GMs were the norm. I'm not convinced they are anymore. Now you've got big, heavily developed settings where the pastiche isn't a toolset, it's the actual and complete setting. If you can't stomach including magical mecha in your Exalted game, you're leaving out a big piece of the canon setting. The canon has become more important than the toolset, and respecting the canon -- as any Internet forum will show you -- has at least as many ardent followers as old-skool DIY play.

    So, sure. Pulp gaming revels in a genre-that-never-was when it throws together zombies, Not-zis, robots, Oriental mystics, masked vigilantes and American patriots. But including them all -- esp. in a SotC type of game -- is merely implied by the art. It's not mandated or IMO particularly expected by the game's text. There's even the step where you have to determine what your setting is generally focused on (weird science, magic, fisticuffs, futuristic, etc.). But it works perfectly well if everyone signs up for two-fisted patriots vs. protoNazis. Or gadgeteers vs. robots. Hell, it almost feels like more work to try and retcon Barsoom (say) into a game centered on the mean streets of Gotham.

"Inspiration" is the subject you're bringing up. Jesse's first post is about... mmmm... I don't even know what word to use. "Emulation" would be wrong, because there's no point in simply "re-doing" a kind of fiction. But sometimes there's something about a story of type of fiction and, I don't know, you're not done with it, or there's more to be done with it, or something.

    As an example, I think this is the case of Greg Stafford and Pendragon. The man really, really believes that in those myths there's something really, really valuable for all of us. He went after a game not to "do King Arthur" again, but because he thought a game that served as a delivery system for those myths, a means for us to interact with them would be of great value.

    I woke up this morning, conflating this thread with the nearby "Tell me about King Arthur PENDRAGON" thread and thought, "You probably can't play that game well without reading Thomas Mallory first." That's the kind of thing I'm talking about here. That there something in the fiction that has to rub a nub in our brains when we hit the fiction that might prompt someone to ask, "Do you have an itch from reading that fiction? Is there something else you want to do with it? Is there more to explore in the act of that fiction?" I'm not talking the "I play RPGs so I can do the smart thing and make the right choices the characters in books and movies never make" line of logic. Stafford built a game where the PCs would be making exactly the same mistakes the knights in Mallory make. Why is that? Not to recreate the genre, but to have the Players sink into the fiction directly and experience it again, in a new light, more directly than they even can as readers of the book.

    I think Ron Edwards was after the same thing with Sorcerer & Sword. That's why he was judicious in his book and author selection. Because he wasn't after inspiration or source material. He was after specific qualities of weird sword & sorcery fiction -- qualities that he truly believe are worth delving into deeper.

    But all this is different than grabbing at color and tropes for inspiration and visual substance for tales. You're right -- we grab at anything we can. We should! But both Stafford and Edwards picked and chose which stories and which authors and which kinds of stories and what qualities of these stories that mattered. I think this is what Jesse was talking about when he started.


    I'll close by saying I'm not sure simply doing a detailed under the hood game write up for any given genre makes any sense. I don't think it can be done as an exercise. It must be done, as touched on above, as an element of the obsessive for it to make any sense or to work. Stafford and Edwards really love the stuff of the fiction they went after, and in particular the stuff under the hood of that fiction. I think this quality of real, sincere interest must be present for the kind of work Jesse is talking about. And, in turn, the Players must already have an interest or a deeper buy-in to the material as well to make it work.

    Posted By: Christopher KubasikWell, here we disagree.     \nI think part of what Jean is getting at is that in the case of many of these games, it was the designers' intent to explore the themes central to the genre and get at what it was all about, but that at the time, hard-coding such things into the rules was largely seen as undesirable. Typically, games of the time were designed to allow thematically appropriate play, but would only push the game in that direction indirectly. Forcing of genre was left to the gamemaster, because forcing groups to play a particular way would be "wrong." This attitude is still pretty prevalent among mainstream gamers.

    I think this is unquestionably true of Mike Pondsmith's design of Cyberpunk. Pondsmith is a dedicated fan of the genre, and often has sensible and intelligent things to say which I think display a sound understanding of the themes and significance of the literature, but he also has a design bias toward wide open toolkit games that are dependent on gamemaster control and the players 'behaving themselves' for genre emulation to work. Games are going to be thematically appropriate because everyone at the table works together to make it so, and because the gamemaster punishes inappropriate play. You see this thinking everywhere in Pondsmith's GMing advice, and throughout the rules themselves.

    For instance, If you look at the Roles in Cyberpunk 2020, many of them can not work together in play, and some require campaigns built around them in order to function at all. This is barely even mentioned in the rules; the GM is supposed to handle it on his own, and the players are expected to be sensible about conceiving and building their characters. The roles are there to support the widest possible variety of genre-appropriate games.

    To come back to my point, I think games like Cyberpunk and Call of Cthulhu were designed with serious thought toward capturing what the genre was truly about, but that the state of the art and the prevailing prejudices in game design at the time did not really permit tackling such things head on.

    \n Interesting discussion. I always viewed the "everything and the kitchen sink" approach pulp games took to be offering alternative takes rather than "you must have all of this in your games." I figure if combining peanut butter and salami makes the folks at the table turn green, then you just don't do it, even if the menu would allow you to make that combination.

    As someone else has said, Justice, Inc., despite having come out in the mid-80s, handles the pastiche element pretty well. The game came with a rulebook and a setting book, and the latter had a brief discussion of all the different genres pulp includes and how you might mix them. "Just do all of it" wasn't their take on it at all. For example, one of the sample adventures in the book (a haunted house setpiece based mostly on untangling this odd family's R-Map, hehe) is written so that you can run it with or without supernatural elements. (One bit I really liked is that the adventure offered how to run it if the ghost is real, a hoax to steal a family inheritance, or both, and the "combo" approach is pretty deftly written.)

    So I admit that that particular book is probably where I got my take on how to "do pulp". Now that I think about it, there's a lot of things rattling around in my head from old Hero sourcebooks I read as a teenager. Ah, Danger International, the only RPG that told you how to do A-Team, V, 007, and The Road Warrior, right out of the box.

    Posted By: JDCorleyI don't think Dust Devils is a dead-on delve into pulp westerns. Importantly, there are plenty of pulp westerns where the heroes (or antiheros) never face up to any particular inner driving force, let alone have that as the center of their story. Not that Dust Devils is bad, it's very very good. It's just focused very tightly (pacing, characters, plot, etc.) on a very particular sort of story arc.
    \nOk, I'm back. But, I don't think to derail. After thinking on it some more, I don't think I'm cool with the idea that Dust Devils is "just one kind" of Western. I think this is actually tied in to what Jesse and Christopher are discussing.

    Specifically, I cannot come up with a single Western story or film that DOESN'T have elements of what Dust Devils helps players create. Maybe they're out there? But, um, they're the BAD ones. Sort of like what Jesse is talking about with Pulp and the kitchen-sink-include-the-bad-ones, too.

    Every single one I can think of does indeed have protagonists "face up to any particular driving force." I think that pigeon holing Dust Devils as that one kind of gritty Sergio Leone fellatio-fest is sorely lacking in imagination and experience playing the game. It is much more robust than this. This isn't aimed at JD specifically, really. I really don't think he's the kind of role-player who's bound to agree with me or particularly enjoy Dust Devils as I do. Which is fine. But, neither do I think he's pegged Dust Devils very well.

    So, Christopher and Jesse, I wasn't kidding. It does what you're talking about with the genre of the Western. It gets under the hood, tosses color out on its ear (and then invites it back in its proper role later), and helps players actually craft that "magic" that Christopher's after. After a couple years reading your guys' posts about your interests in gaming, I'm sort of baffled how little you appear to recognize it. Maybe not your cup of tea, which is cool. I do think it's worth a look, of course! And, sure, that's me as fucking selfish as I can get. But, hey, I think it's relevant here.
