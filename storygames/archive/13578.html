    \n People talk more about game design over on the game design section of the site over here ---> http://www.story-games.com/praxis/ (or the Game Design link above) (yeah you have to re-apply as it's a different database, same site; later I hope to consolidate the two databases again).

    Many threads on similar subjects (someone want to link the last?) sort of aim at a thought of:

    * Don't worry about "being original", worry about making a game you like.
    * Steal ideas all the time. If you like something, use it.

    Also:
    "Hack" is something where you take an existing game and change some small bits of it to do something else. Like "hacks" of the old DOOM computer game that reskinned it to play ALIENS (the movie). So, like, "Using Dead Inside or Dust Devils to play Jedi from Star Wars". It may or may not involve adding house rules or changing how some rules work.
    But it's been also used as a catch-all for a conglomerate of ideas pulled and jury-rigged together into a huge gleaming pile, like the "Red Box Hack" by Eric Provost.

    But the mantra again is "aim to make something fun, don't aim to Just Be Original". Original ideas develop in playtest as ways to fix problems that your game exhibits in play (or where it doesn't meet your goals, etc).

    \nThis was the last thread we had about originality in game design.

    If you write up a document that allows you to play somebody else's game in a slightly new way, that's pretty much always called a hack (like a setting hack or whatever). Otherwise, I don't think there's hard-and-fast rules about it, just perceptions about what's derivative and what's influence and what's novel. Though heartbreakers do get a lot of flack.
