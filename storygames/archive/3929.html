\nThis site features an activity in which the participants "play a social game about writing and letters, and the end result is an actual piece of text which will (hopefully) make some sense and be completely determined by the gameplay itself."

The creator calls it an "experiment in useful gameplay", which he defines as "gameplay designed to, through the act of playing, leave some useful or interesting artifact.

I think this could be a pretty apt description of rpg in general, or at least least story-games in particular. What do you think?

    Posted By: komradebobWouldn't an artifact be useful as a device for remembering those games years later?
    \nDefinitely, and our games sometimes leave behind artifacts (usually props I've created or notes I've taken). But shifting the primary goal of the game from fun to work seems to me like it would damage the experience, resulting in artifacts that help you remember a game that might have been more enjoyable without the added burded of artifact creation.\n
    Can creation of an artifactneverbe a form of entertainment?
    \nCreating an artifact can be a form of entertainment. My kids do it all the time with construction paper and markers. But they're not playing an RPG at that point, they're creating art. As soon as I take the time out from gaming to shift gears into artifact creation I've stepped out of the game. It happens when I take notes, and I can only assume that it would happen even more if I were to be expected to take notes in a way that created something designed to last.

    But maybe I'm just burned by all those so-called "games" my high school and college English teachers wanted to play.

    Posted By: James McMurrayI play my games for fun. The useful artifacts left behind are the shared memories and stories that can be recounted years later.
    \nOk, that is what I was referring to when I made the original post. You are all right, 'artifact' in it common usage refers to an actual physical object, whereas in my fatigue induced haze I was simply thinking of the remembered experience created and shared by the participants.

    That said, the idea of games that actually do produce physical artifacts as a byproduct of play fascinates me. The closest thing to this I've had was a (modded) Otherkind campaign that I participated in that was co-GM'ed in a round-robin fashion. All players were encouraged, as they were so moved, to write down or draw names and features on a piece of paper that sat in the center of the table. Before long, it developed into a map of the shared world we were creating by the process of moving through it in play.

    While it became a integral part of the play experience I don't think it distracted from the primary focus which was the enjoyment derived from collaboratively crafting an interesting story around dynamic characters. In fact, I think the play experiences was the better for it, as players were, for example, able to use it as a foreshadowing tool of things, places and entities that would possibly feature in the story sometime in the future. The idea was totally cribbed from Jeff, Judd and Storn's Elves Illustrated campaign.

    Has anyone else done anything similar to this?
