\n
\n
\n

\n
\n

\n
\n So I'm starting a new group to discuss game design and identity, and in the process I've been looking at the various moderation policies existing groups use and the kinds of discussions that end up taking place there. And what I've found is that those policies have a profound effect on both what is discussed and how. In fact, it resembles how a set of RPG rules affect what the players focus on and think about during play.

With that in mind, I'm trying to create a policy which limits what/how things can be discussed to better focus on design and identity issues, much like an RPG does.

For example, the subject of sexual assault tends to take up all the oxygen in a room because of associated personal values and experience. But instead of forbidding the topic entirely I want to limit how it must be addressed to create an environment that's more like Monsterhearts and less like Tournament of Rapists. And yet I also realize that any moderation policy (even a non-existing one) will exclude potentially valuable members and contributions, not because it's enforced, but simply because of the environment it creates.

I'm still fumbling in the dark with these ideas and not even sure how much of my goal is achievable, but I'm posting here because this is about the only place I know where people would actually understand what I'm getting at and might be able to help refine it.

    \n I don't know how many people here are familiar with Stack Overflow or Stack Exchange. I use it as a programming reference and the general currency of the site is that the question asker awards a checkmark for the answer they find most helpful. Simple enough. The aspect of it that I think might be relevant is that in addition to the "answers" there are also marginal comments which allow a back and forth discussion between the asker and the commenters. This is most helpful in clarifying the original question or asking for more information.

    So for the situation here, you likely won't have a single definitive "right answer" in each thread, but you could have a way for the asker to highlight a handful of responses that together form a coherent position or frame the issue for future discussion. At the same time, the asker could read the marginalia and refine, clarify, or expand their original question. It can be difficult to know what you really want to ask sometimes, so it would be much more effective in the long term to be able to go back and edit the question. Another thing that can make discussions of complex issues more challenging is that forum threads are typically linear and static, so even if the commenters reach a breakthrough, later readers have to find their way through all the twists and turns of the original discussion to get to the conclusion.

    Such a format would, however, give the question asker a great deal of authority to shape the discussion. Perhaps the asker, themselves, maintains a point score for each question that would (ideally) reflect how productive and interesting the community found that question to be.

    \n Tod's favorite solutions, plus the notion of involving extra system of some sort, has led to this random thought:

    Thread-starter gets total moderator powers over that thread. They can effectively delete any comment they want. However, deletion is actually just purgatory, which is a state in which the comment is hidden from everyone else (except the comment-poster) but not actually deleted from the server. Thus, the thread-starter can guarantee that their thread won't go off the rails with people replying to that comment, but the commenter also doesn't find their post deleted, full stop.

    While a comment is in purgatory, the thread-starter must explain why they've removed it, either by writing out an explanation, or just by clicking on one of the pre-made "Reasons" that come with the interface ("fight-starting", "derailing", "off-topic", etc.). The commenter then has an opportunity to edit their post. The thread-starter will be auto-notified of edits to posts they've "deleted". If the thread-starter likes the edits, then they remove the comment from purgatory and it re-appears in the thread. If not, they don't.

    \n Sure - but consider the Forge. There's a community not based around discussion in any way; you had to be there to make a game if you were going to get anywhere in any thread on the Forge. Discussion was the format, but the restrictions on the discussion made sense in the context of the goal of the group.

    That's why rpg.net and, inevitably, here too, fora just based around a shared enthusiasm for a set of products, will eventually fall prey to consumerism and the next-greatest-thing. Not a lot of Primetime Adventures discussion here; and why would there be? What's there to sell us this month in Primetime Adventures? (This is also why these "communities" can't manage political conflicts, because any psychopath can use a credit card and buy a game and have a thought about it.)

    It should be noted that my proposed ruleset (ban people who you perceive aren't contributing well, and sometimes ban the ones who are, randomly, for no reason), even combined with a $10 user fee led to one of the biggest and longest-running fora on the net, SomethingAwful. At various times and in various subfora over the last ten years it's been a 4chan-a-like, a harassment engine, a ridiculously productive technical support forum, a pre-youtube video sharing site, and a crazy-lucrative barter forum. It's phenomenally successful primarily because it understands that on the Internet, "someone with an opinion" is fundamentally an endlessly renewable resource and banning one or (as of today) 20,974, simply doesn't matter either to the bannee or the rest of the forums.

    Naturally if you're trying to have a community and not just a place to discuss things, this won't work. But if all you want is discussion, if community is not on your list, fuck it, go hog wild and run it as your own authoritarian fiefdom. It doesn't matter.
