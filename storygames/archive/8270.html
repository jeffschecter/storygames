    \n Another option would be to release a supplement for an existing game. Without knowing anything about your friend's book, it's hard to know what to suggest, but there are numerous systems out there that handle classic space opera fairly well (depending on your taste and what you consider important about the genre), such as GURPS, True20, D20 Modern, various editions of Traveler, Thousand Suns, etc. Games that cover out and out speculative fiction are rather rarer: there's Shock, but adapting a single book to it seems like something that would yield a few pages of notes that you might use to run one or two sessions -- nothing like the usual setting book that doubles as a guide to the book's universe. Somewhere in the middle is Sufficiently Advanced which covers the near-singularity/new space opera genre.

    \n Function follows form--what kinds of stories does this author tell, because the system will need to encourage that or it won't "feel right" to fans of the fiction.

    For instance, you can't do Steven Baxter or Iain Banks with d20. Period. You could maybe do most of Niven and, obviously, do Lucas and other Star Wars Universe authors. If your friend writes in a similar vein as those latter two, d20 would be fine and, yes, would have the largest market share/player base. If, however, he writes like the former two then d20 is going to suck--a bunch of moving parts that don't go anywhere his works leads.

    Posted By: gmdiceSomething that is already used by a lot of people is also a great advantage so that it will be easy for others to just pick up rather than learning a new system.
    \nThat's one point of view. I'd say selecting a system that best matches the source material is the greatest advantage. If he has no interest in doing the design himself (as Mark Smylie did with Artesia), I'd suggest enlisting a game designer to do the work with a license (as David Petersen did with Mouse Guard and Jim Butcher did for Dresden Files). Because whether an existing system is used or a new system is created, there's going to be new rules and mechanics (much more so, and more complex for OGL/d20 than, say, Shock or Starblazer Adventures or HeroQuest) and play testing will still need to be done. And a game designer will be a better judge of what system would work best, or even if a new system would work. So put it in the hands of a pro (or at the very least, someone who's actually interested in doing the work - it doesn't sound like he has any interest). The economy is right to find game designers looking for work, too.

    If all he's interested in is seeing his property stamped into a game book "that he can just fill in story lines", then yeah, just go OGL. Sure, without testing of the new mechanics to go with those story lines it will be a piece of shit, but it's the most popular free option, and if it looks cool enough it will be purchased anyway, for story ideas at the very least. Although judging from third party sales the past five years, that doesn't translate into very good sales, but I'm sure there will be some and maybe that's all he wants. The sales probably won't be any more than putting some effort into it with a quality design, regardless of the system, that might generate simply from word of mouth and the right "indie" buzz, backed up by solid execution. But if he's already got a large readership that he's trying to milk, instead of a small readership that he's hoping to please and grow, then I can see the appeal of putting in as little effort as possible. Obviously, not a choice I would make, though.

    I suggest that the OP, or ideally the author, answer these questions:

    \n
      \n
    • What's the story about?
      • \n
    • What do the characters in the stories do?
      • \n
    • Pick one of these three things for the players to do: Have their characters making the kind of meaningful, hard decisions the characters in the stories do; See if they can make the characters more effective/solve the world's problems; Play characters who look and act like the ones in the book, using settings from the book, and maybe meeting characters from the book. Only choose one. Don't worry. The other stuff can happen. But we need to know what's important. If you say "All of it," you're going to have to write an essay after class.
