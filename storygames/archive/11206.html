\n I posted this idea on my blog last week, which led to some very cool suggestions in the comments. \n
Roll a red die and a blue die.
Blue 1-3 = "Yes"
Blue 4-6 = "No"
Red 1-2 = "and"
Red 3-4 = blank
Red 5-6 = "but"
\nAfter seeing these tutorials on transferring printed graphics to wooden objects, I'm going to actually take the next step and make a couple of these babies.

If there is an "advanced" version of these dice, I think I will take Jason's suggestion to add some more outcomes like "if," "because," and "when." Perhaps I will replace "and" with "then."

Any ideas about how you would use these kinds of dice? I imagine them being a very simple introduction to story-games. The equivalent of Candyland teaching the basics of roll-and-move board games.

    \n I was talking this out with Ryan earlier and had some thoughts.

    The Clause Die has a lot of possible words that could be used, so here's a list of clauses I pulled from a grammar website:
    and, but, for, or, nor, so, yet, after, although, as, as if, because, before, even if, even though, if, in order to, since, though, unless, until, whatever, when, whenever, whether, while, also, consequently, furthermore, however, moreover, nevertheless, and therefore.
    \nI just have to choose the six best clauses.

    I don't want to get really specific "subjects" in this thing, like "I rolled puppy, so a puppy has to be in the story now." I want to keep these dice abstract enough that they apply to any game where you might be stumped about where the story goes next.

    But pretty much every game already has its own way of saying "Yes" or "No," even if it's some shade of "Kinda." That being the case, having a Yes/No die is a little redundant. You could still use a die in this manner, if you like, but a coin flip would work just as well.

    Ryan suggested something called a Theme Die, that doesn't specifically call up subjects like "Puppy" but could call up a more versatile theme like "Loyalty" or "Friendship" or "Duty."

    This got me thinking about what you could do with three dice: Two theme dice and a clause die in between.

    [Theme] [Clause] [Theme]
    [Loyalty] [If] [Love]
    [Duty] [But] [Violence]
    [Friendship] [So] [Hope]

    Thoughts?

    \n I'm experimenting by first making special dice for Happy Birthday, Robot! using the tutorial I found here. (Sorry the pictures are so blurry.)



    Step 1: I spread a thin layer of glue onto a sheet of card stock. This is just a mixture of water and Elmer's I had leftover from an earlier papier mache project.




    Step 2: My wooden cubes are 3/4", so I set up each word at the center of a 3/4" square in Macromedia Freehand. You can use your preferred graphics program.




    Step 3: After cutting out the shapes, you can see more clearly how I've arranged the words. There's going to be one word on four sides of the cube, with two blank sides. So I just arranged the words onto a cross-shaped template and left the center square blank.




    Step 4: I put a roll of scotch tape on the center square so it would stay put when I put it on the cube. I then spread mod podge evenly onto four sides of the cube. IMPORTANT: Leave the top and bottom of the cube dry.




    Step 5: While the mod podge is still wet, I stick the taped part of the transfer onto a dry side of the cube. It sort of looks like you gave the cube a hat.




    Step 6: Once the transfer is securely taped to the cube, I carefully press the printed sides of the transfer down onto the wet mod podge. The tape should keep the transfer from shifting around too much on the cube, but still be careful. You don't want to smudge your print at this point.




    Step 7: I wrap the cube in a couple of rubber bands so there is constant pressure on the printed transfer. I let it dry overnight like this.

    And that's where it is now. I have no idea if it's going to work.

    Step 8: Remove the Rubber Band
    I pulled off the rubber bands that were keeping the print in place over night.



    Step 9: Rub away the paper under running water.
    The tutorial says to rub away the paper under running water, but I could immediately tell that the water was loosening the mod podge layer. As a result, I was rubbing away the inkjet print from the surface.



    Step 9a: Peel away paper.
    I had better luck just peeling away the paper, but the running water still made the print bleed a bit.


    VIDEO of final result.

    Overall, I'd give this process a B+. Not bad if you're just making your own dice, but they're kinda sloppy looking. I'll probably try just using an ink stamp next time.
