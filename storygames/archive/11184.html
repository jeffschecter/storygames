\n One of the unwritten rules of fantasy settings for roleplaying is that there will be many intelligent races and at least a few of these races will be available for play as PCs. Certainly the prime example of this is D&D which always a selection available, but everything from Burning Wheel to World of Warcraft does the same thing to some degree.

Let me be clear: I'm not saying this kind of choice is a bad thing. I am asking what's wrong with keeping the racial choices to a minimum, or eliminating choice altogether? Also, is there any benefit to a single-race play environment?

I can think of two different (admittedly embryonic) fantasy campaigns I've planned that restrict players to a single race. One involved telling the story of three dwarf kingdoms, with all the PCs naturally being dwarves. I wanted to explore the culture and motivations of dwarves minus the Tolkienesque bickering between elves and dwarves and the dwarf's usual role as jokester in a mixed-race party. The second idea involved a kingdom of merfolk, a choice that obviously cuts air-breathers out of the equation and opens up a whole host of questions about life beneath the waves.

Both of these ideas were driven by culture issues, but could accommodate what we might consider more or less standard fantasy gaming, with adventurers and such. With at least these two examples I don't see any down side to the restriction, while at the same time play is opened up to some new ideas. Feel free to chime in with your own ideas about what does and does not work in relation to fantasy races and their availability/scarcity.

    \n I've toyed with a couple of single race fantasy settings over the years.

    I like the idea of them because it allows a group to really plunge into the difference within a race rather just simply relegating the different races to their stereotypes (or choosing to play "AGAINST type").

    But I find players latch onto archetypes, and if you don't have different races in your game then it makes sense to have your single race expand into a variety of factions that fill the various race niches.

    Consider an all dwarf game versus a mixed race game.

    The mixed race game might have bow shooting elves, while the dwarf game might have a steampunky rifle shooting group.
    The mixed race game might have a race of crafty goblin tinkers or gnomish alchemists, the dwarf game could easily fill these niches.
    The mixed race game might have standard humans who embody "normality" for the setting, these would just be replaced by the regular folk of the dwarven people.
    In fact the role of the dwarves in a mixed race game might be replaced by a faction of miners who embody the "dwarf's dwarf".

    You could spread the idea across all the races commonly found in a setting, but just turn them into factions of your single race. Consider the Spycraft version of d20 (of which I'm really only familiar with the Stargate RPG), instead of races it give you factional backgrounds...different types of military groups and cultural origins. You get your bonuses from these.

    Another huge advantage to a single race game is the fact that players can't say "I want to play a half X-half Y", since everyone's the same race the issues of incompatible biology never arise and you can get on with the actual depth of a character's personality rather than the quirkiness of their heritage.

    \n Which do you consider to be standardization, Droog?

    As Sam has proposed in his first post...the default "standard" for fantasy is typically mixed races.

    While another perspective might be to say that standardization is the elimination of anything beyond a certain standard...thus the elimination of races beyond a specific type.

    The fact that you've specified fantasy as a genre in your post implies that you accept genre tropes and genre standards inherently as a part of fantasy. So your very comment is self-contradictory.

    I'd always considered the core of the fantasy genre to be a contrast between two standards...in many folk tales it's the conflict between humans and the otherkind (call them fey, spirits, ghost, whatever). Some kind of morality lesson is then gained from comparing the perspectives. Thus when I run a single race game, I try to play up the contrasts of a setting that isn't our own, and then try to get a players perspective on how those contrasts apply to their character (and through the character, the player).

    Not that every fantasy game turns into a morality tale, there's always a lot more action and adventure than moralizing.

    \n Fantasy to me, as a genre, is the rejection of science and modern civilization as a world view; the re-envisioning of myth, folk-tale and legend. That encompasses Tolkien, Clark Ashton Smith, M. John Harrison, R.E. Howard, Fritz Leiber, Ursula LeGuin and many more. Swords, mythical beasts and archaic technology feature heavily, because these things are part of our European heritage. Beyond that there are few assumptions.

    So to say that the default standard for RP fantasy is such-and-such is to say that that RP fantasy is impoverished, limited to one writer and his imitators as a model.

    \n Let's go back to that first post for a moment.\n
    Posted By: Sam HOne of the unwritten rules of fantasy settings for roleplaying is that there will be many intelligent races and at least a few of these races will be available for play as PCs. Certainly the prime example of this isD&Dwhich always a selection available, but everything fromBurning WheeltoWorld of Warcraftdoes the same thing to some degree.
    \nWell yes, if we restrict ourselves to high fantasy, the defining feature of which is multiple heroic races, then yes, they all have multiple heroic races. That requires that we ignore such fantasy stories and games as King Arthur/Pendragon, Stormbringer, Conan, and more. Let's assume, for a moment, that we want to explore a high fantasy setting, while removing or severely restricting the choice of character race/culture. That could produce a few types of story:

    1. Explore the one race/culture in greater depth
    The problem with this is that most races in high fantasy aren't given the right sort of detail to create adventure within the culture. You might have an extra sourcebook that helps a bit, but likely you'll have to make up a lot of stuff. The question arises, if you have to make up a lot, could you have done it easier with a better game? I'd suggest looking to Earthdawn, specifically the Denizens of Earthdawn books, for decent examples.

    2. Play the characters' race/culture off NPC races/cultures
    I've done the second type of story, not in D&D, but in Legend of the Five Rings, by restricting race and clan (clans in L5R serve a similar function to races in D&D). It worked remarkably well, but I was up front as to why I was doing it, and also had players new to the game, who didn't have a lot of expectations as to the choices available. An advantage of this is that it promotes teamwork, since there's one less thing to come between the characters.

    3. A game where racial/cultural differences are irrelevant
    I'd suggest avoiding this option unless you know your group. Taking away choice without letting players know there's a good reason tends to make them feel oppressed. If, however, you sit them down with a swords & sorcery game, such as Pendragon, that expectation won't be there, and there won't be a problem.
