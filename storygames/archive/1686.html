Let's do it first.

Throughout the middle acts, I saw a distinct structure of one magician setting a scene (or a performance) and the other magician setting up a sabotage. Even with the three-part structure of pledge, turn and prestige, and all of the dialogue about obsessions... there was so much stuff.

Suppose that a Prestige-inspired game is about obsessed rivalries where murder or direct violence is not an option, or at the very least it isn't a possible outcome until a big climax. The goal is to discredit, embarrass and otherwise humiliate your rival.

The whole magician angle is just one interpretation of this basic premise, but it could be translated into any competitive field in pretty much any genre.

    Alas, I don't have a copy of Sorcerer on hand, but I'll toss out a barebones premise for a system, following my usual reward/price/neutral scheme.
    You are rewarded for: screwing yourself over, if it screws over your rival even more.
    You are rewarded for: creating good things for yourself, with some avenues through which they can be taken away.
    You are rewarded for: attacking anything good the rival has created for himself.

    There is a price for: screwing over your rival without screwing over yourself in the process.
    There is a price for: creating good things for yourself without also creating the aforementioned weaknesses.

    There is neither a reward nor price for: being satisfied.
    Not exactly subtle, but there are probably some elegant ways to make this emergent.

    Further mechanical thoughts:

    Scene: One of the rivals has a performance, the other rival seeks to sabotage it. It would be nice to use coins for the mechanics, but they're notoriously bad replacements for dice rolls. In any case, the dice are effectively d2s.

    Step 1: The performer puts forward a number of dice up to his Perform skill, but no less than his current Obsession.

    Step 2: The saboteur puts forward a number of dice up to his Sabotage skill, but no less than his current Obsession.

    Step 3: Both dice pools are combined and rolled together. The performer counts all even results as successes. The saboteur counts all odd results as successes.

    Theme: When you put more effort into a deed, it also fuels your rival's efforts.
