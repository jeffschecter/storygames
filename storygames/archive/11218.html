\n My son is nine years old and has autism. While he's a very sweet and personable child, he has a tendency toward what's called "parallel play," which means he'll play near someone and around someone, but not with someone. It doesn't mean he doesn't enjoy the time spent, only that his engagement isn't what it might be for you and I.

I've been trying lately to play more games with him and his mother. Mostly board games as mentioned elsewhere. He enjoys these and has repeatedly asked to play Galactic Heroes, which pleases me. He's even tried to expand that game's play into his more general collection of Star Wars toys.

Anyway, I've been wanting to run some RPG stuff for my wife for a while, and figured I would do something very simple just to get the GMing rust off. I settled on The Matrix by way of Wushu: jack in, get something done and head for an exit. My wife was working out her character when my son started asking questions about what we were doing. I asked him if he wanted to play and he said yes, so we made a simple character for him. I didn't argue when he said he wanted to carry a hunting rifle under his jacket.

I honestly didn't think he would understand what we were doing, but he took to it immediately. I simplified the already straightforward scenario and put them in an office building with a computer mainframe that needed to be hacked. My son is fascinated by elevators and security systems, so the break-in got him very engaged and I added details like motion-detecting sensors at his suggestion.

I have had neurotypical, adult players who've done less roleplaying than this kid. He referred to his mother by her character's name and when it came time for the Agent to show up \u2014 because an Agent always shows up, you know \u2014 he was all, "I get my pistols!" and acting out the kind of two-gun shooting that would make the Wachowski Brothers proud. He was into it and coming up with more ideas that I incorporated into the ongoing story.

We did jumping from building to building. We did taking out a helicopter in midair with nothing but fists and knives. We also did a spectacular crash-landing in the middle of a busy intersection that ended the scenario with a literal bang.

Now granted this was a game that lasted maybe 30 minutes, but I considered it a big success. My son immediately asked if we could do it again, and I told him we'd try to play the next night (tonight). I'll likely keep the action on a tight track because his mind tends to wander, but the mere fact that he wants to play again right away makes me very happy.

Are we likely to do lengthy, intense roleplaying? Certainly not any time soon. He's not just autistic, but he's nine years old. Think of the kind of roleplay you did at that age and then dial it back a little bit to account for his condition and you have the right idea. Maybe as we get older we can put more into the games, but for now if this is the kind of thing that lets us share the pastime together, I'm more than pleased.

    \n That is cool! How great that you found something that clicks with your son \u2014 I have a 14-year-old son who's kinda Asperger-y and kinda ADHD-y, and I know it can be hard to find the right thing that engages him together with others.

    I've been wanting to do some roleplaying with both my kids (he has an 11-year-old sister who's basically neurotypical, if a bit flighty) but keep being blocked by my own perfectionism, wanting to create some killer campaign scenario. Maybe I should emulate you and just set up a crazy one-shot situation to go wild in. (Hm, maybe I could try out Danger Patrol...)

    The funny thing is, my kids have been avid story gamers since they were about 4 and 7. They sit around and make up totally absurd stories out of a dense gumbo of every pop-culture favorite (Sonic the Hedgehog, Pikachu, Strong Bad...) and a bunch of their own characters. They alternate narrating what happens, and act out the characters in first person with funny voices. Very Narrativist. The main difference from, say, Universalis is that conflict resolution happens not by dice or karma but by raised voices and threats to leave the game. (Although as they get older there's been more consensus-building.)

    I'm not sure how to build from this into more, uh, traditional roleplay, though. I'm afraid that any scenario would derail into the crazed adventures of Dr. Robotnik giving Munchlax a haircut with a circular saw while the Cheat stuffs them both into the crisper drawer. Which sounds awesome on paper, but gets totally migraine-inducing after a half hour or so, believe me...

    Posted By: Sam Hhe has a tendency toward what's called "parallel play,"
    \nThis bit, in the context of your post, makes a lightbulb immediately go off.
    There are certain games that support something akin to parallel play - games which focus on single protagonists at a time, and cut between them, with spotlight scenes.
    If your son finds the "party interaction" component of play difficult, it's an interesting option.
    Frame a scene for your son.
    Then, frame a scene for your wife.
    Then, frame a second scene for your son.

    The nice thing is that it allows for tight, focused play... it removes the party aspect of play, which your son might find to be difficult or uninteresting... it also allows your son to get up and wander off during your wife's scenes if he so chooses.

    Anyways, just a thought that went off, and one I wanted to share.
