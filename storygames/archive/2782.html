    \n Another mod that I actually used at one point about four years ago (holy crap, it's 2007... it's been that long???):

    For all rolls, no more 1d20. It's 2d10.

    A roll of 1-1, 1-2, 2-1 is always a failure.
    A roll of 0-0, 0-9, 9-0 is the critical success rate.

    Any roll where you get "doubles" (4-4, 8-8, etc) except for 1-1 means that you pick up the dice, roll again, and add your result together.

    ie: Roll 5-5. Pick up the dice, roll 7-2. The final total is 19.

    -Andy

    \nDEATH MAGIC! (based loosely on Defiling Magic from Dark Sun)

    Every time your mage casts a spell: Find the spell level. Then, take that number of hit points away from any number of PCs that you want. Only PCs, not NPCs. The PC doesn't even have to be anywhere near you.

    Ex: Cast a 6th level spell. Then the mage's player decide that Hogart the Thief takes 4 damage, and Mary the Destructonaut takes 2 damage.

    \nHere is my quick attempt:
    \n
    \nWhen casting any spell, rather than use a spell slot, you can invoke the 'Laws of Magic' and have the DM assign a consequence. At that point you can either accept the consequence or lose the spell slot.
    \n
    \nAlso, ignore any assumed 'flavor text' granted by the spell description. Describe it however you want (magic missle could be bolts of lightning, lightning bolts could be beams of destruction from your eyes). Cool and 'in character' descriptions maximize the mechanical effect.

    \n I ran a D20 one-shot once; I believe I called it 'A Dungeon and also a DRAGON.' (One of my main beefs with D&D as typically played is that it's false advertising. Plenty of dungeons, but I never seem to encounter dragons. I like dragons. So I was determined that there would be a dragon.) To make this work I put together a deadly castle with two levels and a basement area with a maze. Traps, weird obstacles, sadistic cursed treasures, etc. Then I handed out level 5 characters, and 5 coins to each player.
    \n
    \nCoins were used as extra lives. You cashed one in, like a "continue," and then you could start over. So basically it was D&D as cribbed from Paranoia.
    \n
    \nCampy, but fun, since the extra lives made the players willing to try different things and be bolder, and even take down an actual dragon (green) despite their levels so long as they could reset after a death every now and then. There was also, as I recall, a wishing well at the bottom of the dungeon where the players could toss in their coins for something potentially good to happen, but I don't think anyone took the bait.

    \n These needn't all go together. Some are dumber than others.

    You get feats ALL the time. Like 1/level (maybe 1/2 levels if you want a tamer game). Take a few extra to start out with, too.

    Steal the HP rules form that new d20 Star Wars.

    You either know a skill or you don't. If you know a skill, your skill rank is 3 + your character level. Every level, you pick N new skills to know, where N is half of the number of skill points you'd usually get for leveling up.

    There's no non-artifact magic items. Instead, you can buy innate special abilities as if they were magic items, using the wealth-by-level chart to see how much stuff you can get. The GM should give you tons of gold pieces, which are only really used for drinkin'. If you feel the need to have a magic item in the game, make it an artifact.

    \n Skills: first, collapse a bunch of 'em. Balance, Jump and Tumble down into Acrobatics, Spot/Listen into Notice, Hide/Move Silently into Sneak. . .maybe Appraise can be folded into a Knowledge skill for thieves, woth other broader applications--and of course folks can appraise using their relevant Craft/Profession skill too. Probably other possible condensations.

    Then: keep the Max ranks for class vs. cross-class skills, but none of that double cost business--1 point for 1 rank, any skill. (Essentially, EVERYONE gets the Able Learner feat for free.)

    Finally: allow gonzo, legendary, limit-busting "greater successes" for every 5 points you beat the DC by. Like, not ONLY did you successfully Tumble (Acrobatics) through his threatened space, but you've outmaneuvered him for a +2 bonus on your next attack roll against him. Or +10 feet of movement if you're just trying to get away. Maybe if you beat DC by +10 you actually slipped behind him for a Sneak Attack, etc.



    Also, Action Points. gotta love Action Points. But I've not seen a cool system for disbursing them. I'm currently playing around with cribbing Bill White's experience system for use with APs instead. The players have enjoyed it, but it's still too soon to tell if it works well. In any case, I think something TSOY-like (Pursue X with your character, get APs, get a big one-time bonus for dramatic changes) is in order.

    \n PCs never die. Ever. Instead, the powers of evil sustain you because they want champions. It goes like this:

    Zero HP: lose half your Charisma because you're all beat up.
    -1/2xHP: Drop to Cha 0.
    -HP: Shift one step towards Chaotic Evil and recover half your Charisma.
    -2xHP: Shift two steps towards Chaotic Evil and recover all your Charisma. You no longer heal naturally
    -3xHP: Your creature type becomes Undead. Magical healing no longer works on you.
    -4xHP: Temporarily destroyed. Have fun regenerating from your phylactery.

    Don't tell the players this beforehand; just tell them you're implementing something in place of the Death's Door rules, and let them figure out where the game's main villains come from.

    \n Disclaimer: I don't know from D20, I've only ever played D&D 1e. So to you more knowledgeable peoples this may be entirely laughable, but anyway:

    One roll for to-hit and damage. Works like this:

    You have a skill rating with a weapon. Your weapon has an attack rating. Add them together and deduct the defense rating of the opponent (what used to be called armour class, maybe still is, made up of what armour they're wearing, magical bonuses, dex bonuses and what have you). This is your target number. If you roll under it, you've hit.

    Check how much you've rolled under it and add your weapon's damage modifier. This is how much damage you did. Really powerful weapons might multiply rather than add, maybe.

    Go insane with the modifiers for all these numbers. Maximum crunch, minimum grind (since mostly your specific numbers won't change much between encounters, so setting it up takes lots of calculation, but using it is quick and easy).

    You'd probably need to rework the hit points system a bit as well.

Is it based on a utilitarian, deontological, or tribalist moral system, or on a rigid and arbitrary code in a book somewhere - or is it a terrible mishmash of all of them (that is, the current state of affairs in the official rulebooks). What the hell does chaotic mean, given that the core rulebooks variously describe it as identical to being lawful, neutral, or both?

    Bleh. Spontaneously turning undead sounds like a plot arc. Spontaneously having to (maybe) act differently based on some fabulously poor standards is a game-stopper.
