\n I'm playing Fallout 3, at the moment, and it reminds me how much I like skill lists.

There's something about buying that first level of Lockpick. Because now I'm a lockpicking character. That can pick locks. My conception of the character's completely changed: before I was a fighter, now I'm a sneaky fighter.

And then the second level of Lockpick. Because, actually, I know I'm just buying to pick the harder locks later in the game. There's no technical difference between picking a hard lock with Lockpick 75 and an easy one with Lockpick 25. But, for some reason, it's satisfying. Now I can pick difficult locks.

All the different skill choices are like a menu of exciting, fun things my character could do. For some reason, having constrained choices is more fun than, say, making up freeform Traits in Dogs. In Dogs, I can make up anything. But, here, I have to choose. Do I want to disarm bombs or pick locks?

The only problem, really, is that I must pick fighty skills. Otherwise the monsters kill me. And that feels, slightly, like the game's betraying me. It told me I could pick any of the skills. But actually there's some that I need to pick. And it didn't tell me that.

Perhaps I will get another level of Science, so I can hack more terminals.

Graham

    \n Sure, but there's something more than "Skill lists are great when they reflect the genre".

    I like skill lists because they force me to make a choice. With freeform traits, they'll essentially be used in the same way. Oh, sure, I can pick "I learned to shoot with my father" or "I understand how computers work", but they'll all be part of the same resolution system. When I'm faced with a door, I can shoot the lock off or hack the computer to get past. Either way will work.

    With skill lists, I've got to make a choice, one that seals off options in the future. If I don't choose Lockpick, I won't be playing that Lockpicking minigame and there are doors I will never get through. But if I choose Lockpick, and don't pick Speech, there are people I will never manage to persuade. I can't have everything. I've got to choose.

    (Note, here, that there's effectively no option about choosing Fight skills. If I don't choose one of the Fight skills, I'll be killed by monsters, and the game will be rubbish. So I must choose some fighty skills: after that, I choose the more specialised skills.)

    Graham

    \n Ideally, choosing skills shouldn't make your character more playable, it should change the way you approach the game. Classic example: two-weapon ranger vs. archery ranger in D&D4e. One is highly mobile, hitting and running. The other is all about ducking behind cover while firing at range. A more extreme example (also from D&D) would be the artful dodger rogue vs. the brutal scoundrel. In all these cases, the feats and exploits are part of the character's skill set, and the player's as well. In 4e D&D, the main challenge in the game is combat-related, so all the classes (even the wizard) have combat abilities on par with each other. But each approaches the problem of combat in a different way suited to that class.

    In FreeMarket, all the skills ("experiences" in Donut-lingo) have the same net result: they help you earn flow. But each does it in an interesting, different way with all kinds of benefits and drawbacks. And best of all, there are no dud experiences. Your bad ass mercenary character and your peaceful pumpkin farmer both have a place in the game and in the setting.

    \n The first thing I thought of when I saw this title was Cyborg Commando.

    It took a kitchen sink approach and turned almost any human endeavor into a skill. You had a list of hundreds of skills. Now the irony was that it is largely a game about war and you are trapped in the body of a cyborg so in the vast majority of the skills, that list of skills was meaningless.

    My Lesson from Cyborg Commando: If it ain't important, don't include it.

    I think a well chosen skill list is essential. Now, skill can be used to define characters and help shape a concept, but in games like Rolemaster with its massive lists, it allowed your character to be distinct but it greatly limited what you could do. It seemed to create more barriers and obstacles with such a large list of skills but with so little mastery.

    My Lesson from Rolemaster: The larger the skill list the less characters can do

    And then there is the danger that there are some things that probably shouldn't be skills. Skills should be ways to distinguish characters and provide options. If the game requires something, than perhaps it shouldn't be a skill. Here, I'm thinking about Whispering Vault. In this game players are supernatural beings pursuing unnatural things undermining reality. There was a three step process used to stop, bind and exorcise the entity. Every character needed it. And when I ran the game my players probably didn't invest as much as they should have in it and basically couldn't carry out their objective. If it is something that important or that central, it shouldn't be hidden in a skill list but emphasized somehow, being produced out of a different pool of points or treated as an ability or as an intrinsic component of the game.

    Lesson from Whispering Vault: If something is integral to the game, don't make it an optional skill, integrate it straight into the character

    Now we're discussing skills in a computer game. The game was designed to take into account those skills and to allow those skill choices shape the emerging narrative. So you've got a single player and one single vision of how that particular game is going to play out.

    Now what happens when you've got three characters playing the game and choosing three different sets of skills with three different objectives in mind? In Fallout, you used to be able to take a Pacifist Talker who could basically talk their way out of most situations. That's not going to work with someone who bought all their points in heavy weaponry.

    Posted By: MarhaultIn F3, the world is huge but finite, so the skills chosen customize what pieces the player explores. In an RPG, the world is infinite so the skills chosen should customize what getscreated.
    \nAaaaaaaaaaah!

    First of all, you're using an unqualified 'should,' so your imperative is contextless and therefore meaningless.

    Secondly, however, the sentiment that RPGs are infinite is bollocks. Your game, at your table, is finite. You will only play a finite number of hours, do a finite number of things, fight a finite number of monsters, et cetera. Your game experience is and always will be finite. An RPG as a published product is also finite \u2014 put together all of the finite actual plays of the game, and you still have a finite amount of play and a finite amount of experiences.

    What RPGs are and computer games (generally) are not is potentially infinite \u2014 you can do any number of things, but you will always only ever do some of the things that you can do. Now, you can take skill selection as some sort of indicator as to which things the players will end up doing and plan accordingly, sure. That will get you play similar to, say, Spirit of the Century: high-action, lots of attention on individual characters and a focus on character competency. That is one of many flavors and there's a ton of other ways to play.

    Posted By: GrahamThe only problem with Fallout is that I must pick fighty skills. Otherwise the monsters kill me. And that feels, slightly, like the game's betraying me. It told me I could choose any of the skills. But actually there's some skills that I need to choose. And it didn't tell me that.
    This is a conundrum for a designer. I've worked my game Fabula for over a decade now, a game with many and broad skill-lists, and still I face this conundrum.

    Thinking aloud here:
    Thing is: even though a role-playing game is broad in its skill-spectrum, the scenarios actually played may be narrow in theme, so you need to focus on one part of the spectrum with your character, to fulfill your role as a player in that scenario. This goes for all characters in a game, and certainly in a computer game too (to make the characters interact in a meaningful way). The skills are important connection-points between your character and the scenario, and thus the characters needs a minimum of operative skills. When you are equipped with operative skills your actions will feed the drama of the game, making any conflicts run with depth and excitement.

    In most computer-games the scenario is fixed, while in a tabletop rpg it will vary (to some extent, depending on the scenarios published and/or the GM). My game Fabula has a variety of themes (I've played 30-40 campaigns in it myself), but still I get campaigns where I see that the theme renders parts of my skill-lists un-operative. Some campaigns has a focus that excludes some skills.

    Players are moaning about this, and the only answer I can give them is that the campaign/scenario dictates what is useful and what is not. Even though this may render some character-types less useful as a whole, it has to be accepted by the players. But it also has to be delt with by me, by trying to be in the forefront of any problems arising in the wake of a focused theme.

    One way of dealing with it: I take great care in stating the focus of the campaign at the start, and give the players good tools for fleshing out their characters to suit the campaign. The characters still carries some dead skills around, but as long as they are operational in the narrative, it is mostly ok.

    Another way of dealing with it: I use a special character-set well suited to the campaign (Fabula got 11 different character-sets), so as to make the players make their choices within a confine that suits the campaign we're embarking on.

    A third way of dealing with it: in long campaigns, where time and XP is ample, I leave it to the players to transform their characters during the campaign, to get them in tune with the theme. As the characters transform (into fighting men, or artists, or traders, or whatever), I start turning up the heat in the challenges, and focus even more on the theme. This often leads to some frustration, especially with players choosing the "wrong" character-types initally, but as long as I know what I do, and why, I always manage to lead them through the transforming stage of the campaign in a fulfilling way.

    To have a clear vision, and to communicate it in a positive way, is essential. So the game you are talking about makes ONE big mistake in my view: it fails to tell you that fighting-ability is a requisite for the character. Aside from that it sounds like a fine game.
