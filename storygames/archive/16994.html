\n So, I've been writing[*] a system that uses playing cards. The various categories of card have been really helpful in producing a game that isn't just win/lose. But I've come to a bit of an impasse and I'm wondering if a fresh perspective might help.

The basic system is based on a target number. You set the stakes, if you equal or beat the number you win, if you don't you lose. So far so standard. But instead of dice we use cards.

The player draws three cards and plays one, adding a stat. The GM draws one card and adds it to the difficulty number. (The cards are revealed simultaneously.) There are some specials:
- An Ace is worth 0 when the GM plays it; if a player plays it it's an auto-fail but the player escapes the consequences of failure.
- A Jack, Queen or King is worth 5 played up front, but can also be played on top of another card to add 5 to it.

After you play, you discard your hand. Periodically the deck is reshuffled.

Also, sometimes a card can trigger additional consequences when played by a player (the "yes/no, but" or "yes/no, and" of this particular system). Specifically, face cards do this; and in the current iteration, any spades card does it too.

Ok, so to the meat of the problem. We were really keen on the idea that the suit of the card represented something about the action being taken. Specifically, you should describe the action you took in accordance with the suit you played:
\u2663\tPower. Overwhelming force, raw intellect, sheer talent, willpower, charm, status.
\u2666\tControl. Finesse, skill, strategy, planning, preparedness, logic, manipulation.
\u2665\tInspiration. Cunning, creativity, dirty tricks, invention, innovation, the unexpected.
\u2660\tSacrifice. Taking risks, taking the hit, sacrificing one thing for another.

The trouble is, you don't know what suit you're going to play until you've played it. And generally, you don't draw any cards until you've decided what you're going to do. We tried asking players to declare what the stakes of their action were, but only describe the *action* after drawing the cards. But that turns out (unsurprisingly) to be really counterintuitive.

One other thing: up until now everyone has had a trump suit based on their prefered MO from the above list. Normally the GM picks any consequences and makes them be stuff the character is going to find unpleasant. But when they play a face card from their trump suit they get to decide the consequences instead.

The thing is, the system works best when you have one suit (spades) that always generates consequences. But that doesn't make any sense unless spades means something special (currently, sacrifice), while other suits mean something else (as above). Similarly, the trump suits are a really cool idea IMO but they don't mean anything unless they're tied to action as described above. The only other way I've thought of to make this work is to say you draw cards in advance - say at the start of every scene - but that has obvious problems, namely that you can deliberately burn through poor cards on actions that don't matter (or at least, that are relatively low-stakes).

I've run out of ideas for how to deal with this. Anyone else got any thoughts?

[*] With Frax and Chrestomancy.

    \n Your game is very much like Castle Falkenstein, which, fortunately or unfortunately, my pals in Portland Oregon and I tried to squeeze a variation out like this (but not quite so well thought out) back in the mid 90s.

    What we found was that it's better to define your Trump suit as one that allows you to BREAK AWAY from it's normal type of action. In other words, if I pick Hearts as my Trump suit, I can make a Hearts card mean anything I want, including the normal problem-solving meaning.

    An alternate solution we came up with is to find a way to turn cards INTO your Trump suit. Some point system or side reward which allows you to play, say, a Spades card and turn it into a Hearts card. That way, if a player is becoming frustrated at, say, being a manly-man who keeps getting non-action-suit cards, you can invoke/pay/mojo a less desireable card into one of the suit you want.

    (One method we had was to simply allow players to pay for this change by lowering the effective rank of the card. For instance, if I want to play a Ten of Spades card as my Trump Suit of Hearts, I have to count it as a 9 of Hearts.)

    Actually, I think your suits are throwing you off in more ways than one.

    Making each suit represent a category of action/problem-solving causes a whole heck of a lot of problems and doesn't offer the creative parameters you might think.

    But more importantly, most stories come down to a choice between temptation and sacrifice, with maybe a third option thrown in if things are supposed to be more exciting than realistic (or you don't want to make your audience think too hard.)

    That's why Sacrifice/Spades always seems to be your strongest suit. The push towards sacrifice is an important element of dramatic momentum.

    Power, Control and Sacrifice are all methodologies. Sacrifice is a price, a fuel for methodologies.

    You might want to think of changing your suits to two kinds of sacrifice (physical/lifeblood vs. hope/mental stablity) and two kinds of temptation (again worldly vs. inner.)

    ...OR change them all to methodologies/sources of power:
    Coercion: Force, Threats, Intimidation, Explosions, Violence
    Expertise: Knowledge, Clues, Secrets, Eavesdropping, Experience
    Materials: Money, Supplies, Provisions, Jury-Rigging, Scrounging
    Influence: Trustworthiness, Charisma, Leadership, Empathy, Family, Tradition

    In other words, the cards suits ought to be about EITHER the price you pay OR the way you solve the problem. Right now they're kind of jumbled.

    \n Ok, so players draw to tell them if and how they overcome adversity or obstacles or when they take risks. You want them to engage the drawing mechanism. What if drawing cards was the way you got experience? Then players would want to draw cards. So, you just play with a freeform style. If you want to throw up an obstacle or situation and offer them a chance to draw great! "You come to a door and it appears to be locked - do you want to draw?" If they don't want to draw then they don't have a consequence but they have to do something else. "We don't have much time, lets look for another way instead." "She's a sly negotiator and we could lose our shirts, let's hire a lawyer." "It's so big, let's just run away - maybe we can set a trap for it instead." Even if you don't, players can, at any point, say that they want to draw for it. "What if the door's trapped, I want a chance to draw!" "She could be trying to trick us, I want to draw to find out!" "It's twice my size, I want to see if I can get the jump on it." Those who are willing to risk a negative outcome opt in, others opt out (but don't get XP). "The door might be trapped, you try and get in and I'll keep watch." "I've only got a few bucks left, you negotiate but I'm not putting my money on the line." "I'm hurt, you fight and I'll stand back." Those who opt in all get experience, jointly draw three cards, jointly pick one to play, jointly decide on how to narrate their attempt/action, and the GM narrates the outcome. This way, either the players or GM can identify a moment of adversity, an obstacle, choice point, or a risk, based on the fiction, and call for a draw before anyone takes action (even if it wasn't meant to be one).
