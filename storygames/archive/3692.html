\n As mentioned in another thread, jim pinto and I had a conversation about convention games and risk aversion. As one of the RPG coordinators for a large, regional gaming convention, this is something that I have an interest in figuring out. I had maintained that, by and large, there's a substantial population of gamers at the Strategicons who are willing to try new stuff, while jim maintained that even among indie gamers, people want games that are "safe" and "predictable." It took me a while to realize that he's largely right, even for me.

When I go to a convention, I'm extremely reluctant to take a chance on something completely unknown. My time at these things is too valuable to spend four hours not having fun. There are several factors that can mitigate my risk-aversion, but the most important for me is whether or not I know and trust the GM. If the person running the game is someone who I've gamed with in the past and had fun with, I'm willing to play less safe and predicable games. Some of my best gaming experiences have come from putting myself into the hands of a GM I trust and trying new things.

So my questions to you folks are:

How risk-averse are you when it comes to convention games?

What would make you more likely to take risks with signing up for games at conventions?

What would keep you from signing up for a game at a convention?

Thanks,

--Paul

    I tend to sign up for the games that I think will be the most fun for me.     \nHow risk-averse are you when it comes to convention games?
    Relatively; time slots are precious. At a weekend con, I have six or seven time slots. I am going to end up sleeping in too late and miss one of those. I'm running three to five games of my own (I don't attend without running; it's a thing). That leaves me with... yeah, one to three slots for actual playing.

    What would make you more likely to take risks with signing up for games at conventions?
    a) I know people already signed up, or will sign up with friends.
    b) A game system that I've been wanting to try, or have heard of (Paul, it would be SUPER FUCKING COOL if the game descriptions on the Strategicon website could have links to the websites of the games).
    c) Low buy-in. Either short sessions (~2hrs) or "taste tests" like Mark V ran recently. If it sucks, it'll be over soon and then something else might not suck.
    d) Clever game description. This is mostly me and my fetish for wit and brevity, but I find that if you can write a good game description, you can probably run a good game.

    What would keep you from signing up for a game at a convention?
    a) Poorly-written game description. If you can't distill what's cool about your game into fifty words, I'm going to assume that you don't know what's cool about your game. Which quite possibly means that there isn't anything cool about your game.
    b) Competition. Seriously, I usually run-or-play every time slot, with the exception of the one I sleep through. I very rarely don't play anything at all.
    c) Because I'm a snob, anything with D&D or World of Darkness attached. I might play a Savage Worlds game, but only if there was nothing else running and the description was keen. But this is me: I am totally a snob.

    \n I should preface this with: I don't play in scheduled con games as often as I'd like, as I'm often working the con.

    How risk-averse are you when it comes to convention games?

    Actually, fairly high. A four-hour block is a block of time I can't just throw away without thinking. I have to consider seminar or panels that are going on, talking with folks around the convention (that whole "networking" thing), making sure I have enough time to get lunch or whatever, things like that. Given than I also tend to work a con in some capacity (and prefer that to being a pure-attendee usually), my during-the day time for play gets scarce.

    For after-hours play, I loathe signing up to run or play games then, because that's when all the fun, spontaneous stuff happens -- lively conversations with friends, pick-up games, stuff like that.

    What would make you more likely to take risks with signing up for games at conventions?

    At least one degree of familiarity: the GM, another player, the system, the author of the system -- those are likely to put the game on my radar in the first place.

Yeah, occasionally you luck out, make some aquaintances at the table, and have a lot of fun. But just as often you end up wasting your time.     \nHow risk-averse are you when it comes to convention games?

    It depends on what convention!
    A convention in or near my home town, where I don't have to pay for a hotel room or travel for hours, and the people I play with are folks I play with regularly (or at least at the con the year before)? High risk! Trying new stuff becomes my utmost priority! I usually join all the demos and pickup groups I can find.
    A convention with considerable time commitments and travel expenses? Low risk! My priority becomes meeting new cool people while doing things I know and love. I would hate to return home and tell the family I blew a thousand bucks and a weekend on slog.

    What would make you more likely to take risks with signing up for games at conventions?

    Well written, appealing blurbs that appear to resonate with my own interests.
    A respected name in the role of referee (GM).
    Traveling events I've heard good reviews of ahead of time (i.e. Jamie Chambers' tour on the heels of the Serenity RPG release)
    An opportunity to do a mini-demo of the game with the referee ahead of time, before committing to 4+ hours

    What would keep you from signing up for a game at a convention?

    Before anything else, system would not be a major factor for me - even 4 hours is too little time to truly appreciate a good / truly suffer from a bad system.
    The referee is far more important. If s/he seems like the type to have really over- or underprepared for the game, warning bells go off.
    The con can help with "referee profiling" by cross-referencing the games and referees. If I see this really awesome blurb for an epic high-drama D&D game, but the DM runs nothing but Munchkin card games for the rest of the con? Yeah, I can probably guess what it's going to turn into. On the other hand, if I'm really not sure if some particular horror game is going to be any good, but the GM has a reputation for running awesome games in a genre I enjoy, I'd probably go for it.

      \n Speaking of Marketing....

      The idea has already been floated for having links to the web sites for games. This way I'm not trying to cram the notions of, say Primetime Adventures into a 400 character blurb. It can be done. But I'm probably not the guy to do it!

      By linking to a site we're saying, "Here, look around, check out the forums and whatnot." If someone likes what he sees, he might show up, allowed to make a better informed decision.

      Another though I had was links to pages GM makes. These might be as simple or complicated as the GM wants. But, essentially, they bust out of the 400 character limitation. The GM can use text and art to evoke the setting, can place basic rules concepts right out there in the open... And provide links to other forums and such...

      The appeal for something like this is that it lets me say what they heck I'm offering.

      Cons were built on the back of Dungeons & Dragons (and its children). People who went to the Con, even in the cargo cult days (or because of the cargo cult days) kind of had an idea of what they were in for.

      But Dogs? PtA? The Roach? Sorcerer? The Mountain Witch?

      For some of these games, it's almost an unfair and unethical bait-and-switch!

      If we're going to be introducing new wares, it might be nice to let people see ahead of time what some of the cool games are. My guess is many people would say, "No, thanks." But others would say, "Really? We can play that?" And jump right in.

      My overall approach might be different if I was attending a big con far from home. I'd try to book a lot of events to justify the time spent, and I'd probably go for a more even mix of familiar and experimental.
