    \n I'll comment on your post a bit so you can see what sorts of reactions it brings up for me as I go along. No need to take these thoughts as counter-points or rebuttals, even as some of them necessarily come out that way in the context of your post.

    You have some interestingly mangled ideas about the GNS theory, at least, which is sort of an expected genre element for a good old fashioned anti-Forge rant

    The most visible weirdness is that for some reason you have the GNS theory saying that a game can support two GNS agendas at once, but not all three. The orthodox idea is that you can only ever play towards one agenda at once, and thus a game that might facilitate several is at best doing useless extra work and at worst just being more confusing than it needs to be.

    You also have some sort of a point about character immersion that doesn't ring that familiar to me - the "brain damage" debacle wasn't about character immersion nor about "Immersionism" (the suggested creative agenda of desiring for immersion), and properly speaking the orthodox idea (meaning, what Ron thought about it at the time - I don't think that others really developed any clear stances on it) about Immersionism was that it's more of a subjective perspective on "being really focused in playing the game" than a creative agenda per se. (This notion resembles the Nordic "flow state" analysis of character immersion, which might be useful to look into for further understanding.)

    Regarding your cult thing, I don't have any strong disagreement about the particulars. As you say, some cult-like - or equivalently, clique-like - social dynamics could be observed around the Forge at times. I don't personally think that it's such a big deal, though, because I don't hate efficient cadre dynamics in an artistic work environment, and you can't have those (at least not with homo sapiens) without having at least a little bit of the insularity you mention. What's it to you if some other people like to gather together to develop rpg design and publishing in a serious way? Nothing substantially specific in the Forge itself caused it to be cult-like, and it never had any sort of official cult-like structures, so railing about it is basically just railing against any successful work-group activity at all. Which, of course, always was a big motivation for complaining about the Forge the way I saw it: the very idea that other people are having fun accomplishments without them must have stuck fiercely to some serious roleplayers' craws at the time.

    (Note that it's not the Forge's fault as a community or organization if somebody came there, got their heads full of stuff and then made an ass of themselves at other forums. There was, again, no policy about that sort of thing whatsoever, because the Forge was always a public web forum providing a platform for exchange and distribution of ideas. Despite the crack-headed punditry one would hear at times, there never was a conspiracy council at the Forge sending out missionaries to convert people - it was all individuals reacting to the workshop and its ideas in their own ways.)

    Regarding your attempts at differentiating your game from the Forge, my own gut sense is that it's something of a dead horse to beat - probably not many people around anymore who'll care, so it's probably not a first priority marketing-wise. Of course if it's important to you to make your own peace with the subject, then rock on. To me the accusations about other people's jargon being bad and yours being good, as well as the arguments about how your game is not a "story game", seem frivolous, but I'm sure it's easier to ascribe good intentions to yourself than others.

    I'd like to hear more about Retrostar, personally - sounds like an interesting game to me, and the cover art's gorgeous.

    Finally: could you make a list of those story games that have an agenda and push it in a crappy way, and are apparently part of some sort of a Forgite/Scientologist conspiracy? I've found that it's easier to discuss things if you're specific about your claims, and that one's unfortunately vague: "Some games somewhere that somebody called Story Games are bad, but my game's not like them" is pretty unhelpful, if you stop to think about it, right? I'm not trying to play gotcha with how difficult it is to make comprehensive lists - if you could just name two or three titles, so as to establish a pattern, I'm sure that other people could help fill out the list if it's a trend for people to make propagandistic story games. Besides, it would probably help people in understanding where you're coming from if you really dropped some names of games and designers you really hate for their cultic brainwashing habits

    In summation: your blog post doesn't offend me, and I don't think that it will offend many people in general. There are really thin-skinned people out there, but I don't think that very many of those are in the habit of carrying a flag for the Forge, so you're not likely to get a mob after you or anything.

    \n Ok, I had the idea for a generic game like this before deciding to use Kabbalah. It was back in the 90s. I spent a long time and a lot of false starts trying different ways of doing it. At every step, I'd seem to be getting close, then fail.

    Eventually, I decided I needed a model of reality to crib from. I figured I needed some sort of pictographic alphabet or symbol set to name for my "Elements". I wanted to take the logic of (whatever system) and turn it into game mechanics. Easy.

    I eventually found myself reading up on Phoenician, which incidentally looks a bit like pathways for energy- sort of circuits, at least to me. This was the kind of thing I was looking for. But I had no real context. What does the character meaning "House" mean? Containment for sure. But many of the other characters, like the one for "fish" were opaque to me. What did a fish represent to an ancient people like the Phoenicians. Food obviously, but what was its significance as a symbol? In their myths and aphorisms? I hit a dead end.

    This all led me to (Lurianic) Kabbalah, of which I was only familiar with via the tree of life. Suddenly here were reams of essays on each letter, along with the sephiroth of the tree of life. This is important. This means something. But I had no cultural or religious background to really grok it.

    13 years and two trips to the mental institution later, along with some consulting with a famous Kabbalistic Rabbi, I got it. I've tried to present earlier versions that weren't ready for prime time, and found myself very, very embarrassed by my inability to explain the game. Surely, those incidents of mental illness weren't because of the reaction I had to optical steroids. I really was crazy.

    But again, I eventually got rules for my game that made sense to most other people who read them. Here's how I did it, more or less.

    I made a bunch of tables based on the Sepher Yetzirah's model of reality, as (the claim) literally spoken into being by the creator.

    This meant the big table had three "mother" letters in it- the basic sounds of Hebrew speech, 7 "Double letters"- which have two sounds each and stand for contrary qualities like life and death (a determining factor of whether you have that thing), and twelve simple letters, which represent simple concepts like the "source of laughter", or "seeing".

    All told, I tried to learn about each force- letter or sephirah, and to find an English Key word that said something "true" about the idea, but didn't lie about it. This Key word had to be both a noun and a verb, because I wanted you to be able to "House a Counter", or "Counter a House".

    Incidentally, I was right about the House meaning- it's about containment, specifically the world's containment inside the creator, but also every copy of that concept, through all the layers of reality. An actual house is a kabbalistic "House", but so is you body, or your mind. Tribute defines it as any container that is mainly a container, and the act of bringing something in, or Housing it. Plain English words for the crazy ideas. The rules extrapolate into what they can be used for in the game, but their actual definition is basic English, translated into mechanics. I try to bring the big idea down to earth, then discuss how it can be more interesting. I probably do a much better job than I'm doing today, because I take great care with each card.

    I can go further on this, without the prologue, if you'd like. It's not a great elevator pitch, but it describes how I did what I'm claiming.

      \n The way I understand it, the antagonistic impulse so familiar from rpg discussions has its origins in the way individual people succeed or fail in handling their own enthusiasm for their art form in a world populated by other people. This goes back to the fundamentals of what it means to be human among other humans: emotions inherent to human-monkeys, self-control and self-understanding learned as a child, and social skills that built upon those. While there is a variety of coping strategies that adults develop for these situations, the variety is also pretty predictable once you've observed humanity for a while: some people swing this way, some swing that way, some start an artistic school and others throw rocks at its windows.

      An important observation in understanding the phenomenon is that it is nothing special to roleplaying: every last form of art has similar internal social dynamics, both in history and present day. I believe this to be caused by the particular way artistic enthusiasm, feelings of ownership and social affirmation of expertise play out in an artistic setting: a person who loves their art and yearns for affirmation as an expert of the field will naturally feel resentment towards anybody else they perceive to be competing for the same social niche - in this case the niche of the "rpg guru", or however you'd want to term it. There is only so much attention to go around in a given social setting, after all.

      Not everybody reacts to recognizing a potential guru with hostility, but there are always some in a sufficiently large population. Some have these feelings (because they'd also like to be gurus themselves) but fail to react with hostility, possibly because they give up out of timidity, or because they're self-conscious enough to get out of that mind-set, or because they're hierarchy-minded and don't mind expressing their own guruship by subordinating themselves to the other guru. Others will indeed react to a potential guru by showcasing themselves as alternate gurus, but there is wide leeway for humans in how they express this: it doesn't need to be in blind hostility, many rpg gurus adopt a "live and let live" attitude towards others they perceive to be striving for a similar social position. Still, as long as we have naturally grown human individuals, it's likely that we'll continue having people who choose empire-building and competitive guruship as their social reaction.

      All this is a thoroughly common experience for anybody with an artistic hobby, such as roleplaying. Thinking back, I think the last time I felt threatened by another person's bid for social credit was a couple months back when a player in our D&D campaign expressed a sudden desire to run some Call of Cthulhu for the group: he was, in a certain primitive monkey-sphere sense, co-opting my group of followers and undermining my guruship, my position as the deliverer of play. I have much too much social discipline and virtues of various sorts for this sort of thing to end up in a poo-flinging monkey tantrum, of course, but the psychology is precisely the same as the one that makes a person form a strong emotional attachment to resisting other people's roleplaying in the Internet.

      I think that the best kernel of wisdom I have to offer about this phenomenon is that understanding it helps a lot in dealing with it in both yourself and others: if you understand that the other guy becomes angry and snide about your gaming because they care and love their hobby so much, and because they just might be a bit insecure in how they feel about their own position of expertise, it should also become easier to maintain your own calm and empathy towards them. This might not help you make friends, because some people actively need that antagonism as part of their identity makeup (giving it up would be much too scary to do just because you're being friendly or whatever), but it is an achievement of humanity to maintain your own calmness and empathy even where the other party to the interaction fails. Definitely worth it, even if the other guy wants and needs the fight.
