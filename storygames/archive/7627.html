    \n Believe it or not, clay crystals apparently exhibit highly complex evolution-like behavior. Some exobiologists even theorize that clay is the origin of life.

    So a planet with immense flats composed of slightly damp clay in a position to form persistent crystals. The matrix as a whole is a living creature capable of responding to stimulus and possibly even sentience. The clay flats are home to some rather bizarre thermal/chemical reactions that help make the planet a good environment for clay. There are also highly valuable organic molecules buried in the clay. The energy input is photosynthesis, through symbiotic chlorophyll-bearing organisms embedded in the clay. On the boundary between regions, there's a turbulent zone where the two clay organisms cannibalize each other for genetic material.

    \n Liquid water oceans below the surface of ice moons like Europa. Heated by possible radioactive decay at the core plus tidal forces from the gas giant it circles (Jupiter in this case). Lower gravity means pressure doesn't build up as fast with depth as it does on Earth. I *think* the ratio would be directly proportional to the gravity. On Earth, water pressure increases by 1 atmosphere about every 10 meters (9.8 for salt water). On Europa with a surface gravity of .135 of Earth that should mean the pressure builds up about 1 atmosphere for every 74 meters depth. Marine animals and submarines would use fins more for vertical maneuvering than swim bladders or ballast on/in Europa than in Earth's oceans.

    \n How about a solid "smoke ring"? Think of a big thin noodle of magma, rock and soil as thick as the Earth and wrapped once around a star at the proper orbital distance, both ends joined up. It turns "inside out" like a smoke ring once per day. Add water, atmosphere and life. The (relatively) small difference in the radius of the inside of the ring compared to the outside would mean that that continents would move less from the daily turning than from normal Earth tectonics. So you have a ringworld without impossibly strong materials, dynamic instability or need for shadow squares.

    \n A world that rotates very, very slowly on its axis. The habitable zone near the boundary moves equally slowly.

    Nomadic societies that follow the habitable line as it moves, or even walking cities.
    Ceremonial, resource, or other important sites that only show up in the habitable zone once per cycle.
    Some native creatures evolved to hibernate during non-habitable periods - like a huge field of 9-foot tall flower trees that springs up in a few hours when the sun rises again in the spring.
    Permanent habitable zones near the poles, which become focus for conflict and have their own unique flora and fauna
    Predators evolved to live for brief periods in the hot or the cold zone, entering the habitable zone to prey on local species then flee
    Human societies evolved to do the same thing

    \nI don't think you even have to worry about the rotations and all that for this one, as long as you make the sun sufficiently dim and the gas giant sufficiently bright. In such a case, the day/night cycle can actually be based off the position of the gas giant in the sky, and the sun would merely be the equivalent of Earth's moon, visually.

    As for the walking cities on Mercury, check out Blue Mars, by Kim Stanley Robinson. There's a city that actually rides on rails that circle the entire planet. As the one side of the planet moves into the sun, the rails heat and expand, pushing the city further along into the dark side (where the rails are cold and thus smaller). This keeps the city forever within the planet's terminator, and the friction also becomes a never-ending source of energy.

    Tulpa, I recently read that book. You've got it pretty much correct. Additionally, once every seven (IIRC) years or so, certain plants mature and oxygen levels and so on hit a peak, resulting in a veritable firestorm, rather than the normal, relatively placid brush fire.

    Other creative pseudoscience sci-fi stuff:

    From Hyperion, by Dan Simmons, come the Treeships. Quoth wikipedia:
    "Treeships are living trees that are propelled by ergs (an alien being that emits force fields) through space. The ergs also generate the containment fields (force fields) around the tree that keep its atmosphere intact. There are only a small number of Treeships in existence." If you're looking for a cool Spelljammer-esque ship, this would most certainly fit the bill. Alternatively, could be large enough to be a small "planet" in and of itself.

    Various books in Alastair Reynolds' Revelation Space universe include reference to the Jugglers, a microbial life form that is found on many different ocean worlds, and no one knows quite how they got there. What's interesting about them is that those who go swimming in the water often have their brains' reconfigured by the Jugglers, imparting (permanently or impermanently) things like heightened senses, or increased mathematical understanding, etc.

    Dyson spheres, ringworlds, and hollowed-out asteroids are all fairly common sci-fi tropes, as have already been mentioned. Hrm. How about a completely rogue planet, having been knocked out of it's original orbit, and slowly making it's way through dead space? Would be completely frozen (read:solid hydrogen ice and such) and even it's geothermic and tectonic activity would have stopped, due to the lack of tidal forces and so on (depends on how long it's been rogue).

    \n The L1 point (the one you're referring to) is inherently unstable in anything other than a three-body system (that is, the sun, the gas giant, and the planet), as any slight gravitational nudge would knock it out of orbit and send it careening towards one of the larger masses. We can (and do) throw satellites up there now, but they have to constantly make minor adjustments to their orbits. However, if you have an otherwise empty system, it would work. Check wikipedia if you want an in-depth explanation.

    For starters, with that huge temperature differential, you might get all sorts of weird weather patterns, winds, storms, etc--and the atmosphere itself could store up energy, like static electricity in a giant capacitor. You might have lightning storms like nothing we could ever imagine on Earth.

    Also, how about some lifeforms that have evolved to take advantage of this? Maybe a long burrowing creature that leaves its head on the day side to feed, and its tail on the night side, using its body to store up energy or run some weird metabolic process?
    \nAssuming the planet had an atmosphere, then you'd have constant radial winds running to the point closest to the Sun (call it the Suck Pole, or something). The air gets heated at that pole, runs back to the terminator and starts to fall/cool, whereupon it would fall to the ground and start rushing back to fill the void made by the superheated air. The dark side could, with a thick-enough atmosphere, stay warm by simple heat transfer and convection.

    There's a gas giant ... 2007 TrES-4 ... that's much larger than Jupiter and orbits incredibly close to it's primary. It'd be like this, only as a gas giant.

    As for the heat-pump creature, you wouldn't need a tidal lock to use this method, but with constant sunlight, you could have a creature that has a large organ that absorbs heat at one end and has the other buried in the ground. The temperature differential provides convective circulation around a sort of hollow cylinder with a tube up the middle - it's hard to describe. Anyways - line the inside with cilia attached at the other end to a sort of flagella-in-reverse. When the cilia moves, it moves the root around inside a bioelectric jar, creating a current. By Brownian Motion alone from the fluid, you'd have a constant supply of electrical current for running processes.

    This could be modified by the creature's biology by peristaltic stops to the flow, creating more turbulence in the fluid. Add in little things like blood cells floating around and you get all sorts of motion.

    But really, with constant sunshine, just go for the photosynthesis.

    \nTrue... and that's INCREDIBLY useful.

    The tilting centripetal forces wouldn't be a major engineering problem: just have the inner surface slowly shift from flat to terraced land at the appropriate angle, and it will seem "flat."\n
    Ring worlds, of course, would work perfectly in this regard, and wouldn't require other means of gravity generation.
    \nOh, they have their own problems, for sure. You lose a lot of tensile strength, when you go to a ring from a sphere (but you avoid sheering forces, so that might be a "wash," depending on the base material from which they're constructed).

    But their main problem is detectability, as compared to a sphere. At least one solution/explanation of the Fermi Paradox makes it VERY important that one become unnoticeable to the universe as a whole. A ring world screams "high tech over HERE!" while a Dyson sphere is going to look like some kind of dead star, if it is detected at all (gravity detection, lensing).

    And efficiency--you're only capturing a minuscule fraction of emitted radiation. And space to build--GOD, there's a lot of space on a Dyson sphere. A LOT, times two (inner and outer surfaces).

    But that said... both are impossible without some serious pseudo-science and/or materials with properties we've yet to imagine.

    Posted By: tony dowlerWhat about a planet that goes through a periodic planet-wide environmental fluctuation?

    A mars-like world with a surprising amount of ice buried not far beneath the crust. A periodic orbital wobble causes a worldwide flood followed by an amazing bloom of fast-growing short-lived plant and animal life.
    \nHrm, that would be an interesting one to pull off. The wobble would have to be huge to in order to cause such a massive change in temperature. You could easily have the planet in a pronounced elliptical, like many comets and such, which would take it close to the sun for a short period of time, and then away from the sun for the majority of its year. This would result in a short but hot summer, and a long, extremely cold winter.

    Another option would be a moon in a very slow orbit around a gas giant. If it's not tidally locked, it could have a normal day/night cycle in reference to the sun, most of time, but there would be long stretches of time where it would be within the gas giant's umbra, thus having absolutely no sunlight. In fact, the seasons of this planet could be determined by its position around the gas giant (sun, umbra, or penumbra), rather than caused by its axial tilt.
