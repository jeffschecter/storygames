Okay, so in role-playing games are we all the protagonist?
By "we" I mean our characters, you know, what we call player-characters.

Or do we(players and player-characters)just secretly think we're all the protagonist?
That might mirror life a bit closer then story, or most story? I shouldn't generalize story so, but most stories have one protagonist, at least at one time. I'm sure story will change over time, and likewise game. Life. Story. Game. Those are some incredibly big, broad, and uncertain words.

I'm almost fine with role-playing games not having a protagonist. Or some kinda collective protagonist, made up of player characters. I'd say that the recent Avengers films have that kind of protagonist.

    \n Since "protagonist" just means: the leading character or one of the major characters in a drama, movie, novel, or other fictional text. Then yeah. All PCs are protagonists. Even if they aren't the "important" people in the story. Because that's the character(s) we are focused on, which means they are the leading character(s).

    Note also that protagonist in no way means "morally good" and neither does "antagonist" mean "morally bad". (Not to get into what good vs. bad means, or even what morality means, ugh.)

    And sure, more than one person can be the protagonist, like Nathan said in the OP about the Avengers. The protagonist is only "the most important" by the definition that it is the character(s) getting screen-time.

    If a story were to be written about a janitor on the original Death Star, then that janitor would be, by definition, the protagonist. Not Luke, nor Leia, Han Solo, Darth Vader, or any other "important" character. It's the janitor.

    \n All the PCs in an RPG are the protagonist -- TO THE PLAYER PLAYING THEM. This is fundamental to the core experience of what makes a RPG a RPG rather than some other kind of story game--that most players take on a role or roles which are their lens into the action.

    Fundamentally, the definition of the protagonist is the same as that of a PC (to the playing player):
    1. They are a primary viewpoint character.
    2. They have agency, and thus their actions and decisions is one of the primary causes of their story.

    Of course, in a functional game, the other PCs will often be strong secondary characters -- just as in a book or movie, other characters than the viewpoint character -- the protagonist or progagonists (if there is more than one viewpoint character) will be strong secondary characters and it will make the overall story stronger. But they might be antagonists, or supernumeraries--from your POV.

    This dynamic is one of the things that makes LARP design challenging. A good larp generally has -every- PC as a protagonist -- from their own POV. Of course, the player may well know that they are a villain, or that their primary role in the game is going to be spying on people and serving drinks. But part of what the player brings to the table is--given tools to work with--the ability to have their character act in the world such that, when they tell their actions back to themselves, their character was the protagonist of their own story.
