    \n I can look over his game if he's looking for atelier critique from neutral sources, Joshua.     \n Where do I buy a ticket to get aboard this train?
    \n
    \nI had 3 boardgames I designed as a kid and played with my friends.
    \n
    \nOne was based on the book Interstellar Pig. It was funny that I had these fears about copyright, so my little construction paper game was called Galactic Bomb.. It was basically the game from the book, simplified with the serial numbers filed off. I made the figs out of a bunch of things.. model paint, foam, Gack (does anyone else remember Gack?) marbles, whatever I could lay my hands to.
    \n
    \nOne was a riff off of HeroQuest (the board game, not the RPG by the same name) It was all construction paper, but it probably had the most complex rules of all of the games I made.
    \n
    \nThe last was made using a board with some tanks, a fuel dump, an ammo dump and an HQ for each side. It didn't have rules with it, and we had toy soldiers as well. I don't remember much of the rules, but we played in for hours on end sometimes.
    \n
    \nThat was back shortly before I inadvertently created my first RPG, and played my first published RPG, in that order. I think I still wanted to be a toy-maker who designed amusement parks on the side.

    \n I'll plug BGDF, though I've never been heavily active there. I'm an indie boardgame designer, myself, and have played with several designer groups here in Madison, WI. If you want off-line support, which is always a good idea, since playtesting usually requires a bit more effort than printing off the rules, I'd recommend looking for a local boardgame players group. Anytime there is a group of game players, you can be sure one or more of them have though about designing their own and if nothing else, they will often be able to point you to the local designers.

    \n I've been told it's not uncommon for more than 100 board game-related patents to be issued in a given year. If you can show that a board game concept is new and not obvious in view of other board games, you can attempt to patent that idea.

    So people tend to be secretive when it comes to boardgame design.

    \n Idea theft is not any more prevalent in the European boardgame scene than it is in the roleplaying scene, according to professionals I've talked with. People do have more weird hangups about it because the communities are weaker and more local in nature. Personally I've tried my best to foster an open design environment here in Finland by patiently explaining to every designer how little there is the community can do for them if they don't reveal anything about their game to the community.
