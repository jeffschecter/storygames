[White Wolf] is adept at marketing in ways that have left an indelible imprint in the hobby. <code>Josh, I'm with you, but can't really articulate the specific choices WW makes to facilitate this. You're closer to publishing and have obviously thought about this - what triggers are they pulling?

In old skool WoD stuff, there was the relatively blatant "we are the elite of roleplaying games; everything else is drek" which worked for a few years until it got old and somebody called them on it.

They also had a sort of natural tie-in with the goth crowd, which is already (tends towards being) insular and elitist. Whether they exploited that or learned from it is a question for the history books.

White Wolf was one of the first publishing houses that started doing their own teeshirts as fashion products -- not as advertisements or something, but OMG so fucking cool black teeshirt that just so happens to advertise White Wolf products. That a lot of the teeshirts (and other merch) allowed you to declare your factional loyalties (Lasombra are kewl) only accelerated this. Teeshirts have great penetration value -- they go everywhere your consumer goes -- but this also turned into a membership badge.

Out in the wild, LARPers might run one game and then when that was over switch to another; White Wolf organized the Camarilla to ensure that their LARPers played a never-ending campaign using White Wolf products. Steady revenue stream by isolating their customers.

Most recently, nearly everything that they release is crippleware -- you would be hard pressed to make a "capable" character out of just the core book of Exalted, for instance, especially if you're at a Con or LARP or competitive home tabletop group where other players are bringing in material from other books. At the Vampire LARP I was recently at, for instance, somebody was playing some very big and monstrous splat, and another guy tried to stake him, and everybody mocked him because of course you can't stake that splat, the stake just turns to ash! Haven't you read that book? It's so embedded that they run ads for the additional books in the first book that doesn't have the complete rule set.

Once you've invested a few hundred dollars into a single game line, you're lots more likely to want to play that line, and more likely to buy even more books because you're going to use them, because that's the game you play -- until you identify with the game line: "I am a Vampire player." Which also means that you're less likely to buy other books -- why get Riddle of Steel, after all, when you're a Vampire player?

Now, I actually don't really fault WW for most of this -- save the crippleware books, which is great marketing but poor publishing, this is all good business practice. Disneyland sells annual passes for the same exact reason -- consumers that identify with the brand are a steady revenue stream.

    It all scales to some extent, although some portions are only really applicable to mass media. That all but requires an infrastructure of writers, designers, printers, et al, working full-time to keep those 128 page books coming out every four months.
