    \n Nathjan,
    \n Wow, that's a real span, you know?
    \n I mean, the experience of being 6 is totally different from being 16, right?
    \n But, to answer your question more directly, I guess, there are two common threads, right?
    \n1) Restriction, As you progress through life, there are certain things that you are not allowed to do, drink, get a job, vote, drive, date, etc. And that changes as you get older. I think many games miss that feeling because they handwave those restrictions for the expediency of adventure or whatever...
    \n2) Exploration, Again, there is time, desire and even necessity to explore the world and discover those things that are foreign to you. Like getting your first job or making your first girlfriend. Also, there is a theme of discovering what you are good/bad at. All the classes you have to take sort of force you to do a little bit of everything (some of it you might be able to do well, some not so much). Also, Until right around this age, you won't have enough electives to try stuff you think you might like...
    \n Is that the sort fo thing you are looking for?

    \n When I think back about my adolescence, I remember wanting to be a whole lot older, trying to act a whole lot older, but feeling the whole time like I was still ten years old. I remember coming to terms with being transgender, and I remember being in the closet.

    High school was important for me because that was the first time I started feeling really at home with my friends. Friends started to be people who cared about me, not people who cared about the same stuff as me. And a few of those friends lost me when they discovered they cared more about pot than me.

    As for what helps me remember that time? In terms of personal memories, hearing about my old friends or being closeted again.

    My adolescence was about finding myself, and finding people who I was comfortable with. I think that a game about adolescents would need to remind people that most people aren't sure of who they are, and that you're going to be a totally different person three years from now.

    If you're up for a challenge, base your character off a high school friend.

    \n Cards that prompt certain behaviors? For children? GAH!

    The reason you want children in a game about abductees is because children have a much more fluid and adaptable world view. They are used to re-training their perceptions, whereas adults have dug a lot more mental ruts for themselves with repetition and reinforcement. Children are in the UNIQUE position to see themselves being abducted and respond with QUESTIONS. Holy fuck! That is absolutely and completely amazing. Because you know what an adult would do: formulate a plan of forward motion based on their immediate surroundings and past knowledge, and act. They would ask the bare minimum of questions, and only the ones that their little ruts reinforced them asking.

    In other words, adults would only ask the same questions they had trained themselves to ask in new situations. Watch Cube, perhaps. The adults all ask questions that they assume they are specialized to answer. Safe, pointed questions.

    Children are there because they can question, wonder, adapt, grow, and create at an alarming rate. They are maleable enough to weather the storm, whereas adults would either make or break.


    If you aren't including children in your game about abduction for those reasons, you're doing it for the wrong reasons. Adults are absolutely more controllable.

    \n It's rather hard to say how to play children in general, over such a wide range. Different children are, well different, and children of age 6 are vastly different from those of 8, 10, 12, 14 or 16.

    I think that concentrating on a certain age range has a better chance of working, especially the sort of age that is full of transitions and new pressures, like when moving from one school to another. The age at which kids move (or start to think about moving) from primary to secondary school (to use UK terms) is perfect for that, 10, 11, 12, 13, it's the age of Harry Pottery, Jennings, Swallows and Amazons kids, the kids in the Famous Five, Dark is Rising, and so forth. I can well remember what it was like to go to big school for the first time, the fear, the uncertainty, the new responsibilities.

    \n I think one of the more important things is a child's tendency to act as though fantastic things are real and mix reality with imagination. The old woman in the old, sagging house with bats all around it - she's a witch! And as soon as someone's come up with that, everyone acts as though she is, in fact, a witch. You creep or sprint past her house, if you see her walking down the street, you duck and hide, dare each other to go and knock on her door and run so she doesn't turn you into a toad. That she's never done anything of the sort and that it's blatently impossible is irrelevant. See also 'don't step on a crack or you'll break your back'. I'm sure there's a way to encode this way of thinking into the mechanics.
    \n

Also, read lots of children's literature for inspiration (I just finished Charlotte Sometimes by Penelope Farmer, and can't recommend it enough - though something more like the Famous Five/Secret Seven sounds more relevant to your interests).
