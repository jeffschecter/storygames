\n I was reading the rules for Polaris, and I was captivated by (among other things) the conflict system. In all other games I know, conflict is numerical comparison. Perhaps I compare the Attacker's Orange score (+ 3 if the player makes a silly face) to the Defender's Blue score (+ 1d6 if the Defender gives the Attacker a nickel.) In Polaris, the conflict system is driven by key phrases like "but only if":
\n
\nA (hero player): I draw my sword and slay the woman's demonic assailant.
\nB (demon player): BUT ONLY IF it turns out that the woman was the demon all along, so you killed the wrong guy.
\n
\nIn this case, A can now choose whether to accept this price, or to decline the price. If A declines, then neither statement happens.
\n
\nI like this idea, perhaps because it feels closer to improv theatre which I've always wanted to emulate. I've been thinking about whether there are any limits to this sort of thing. Polaris has several properties that I'm not sure how to change, and I'm wondering if other games of this kind exist that differ from Polaris?
\n
\nProperty #1: Player inequality.
\n
\nOnly two players, the hero and the demon ("Mistaken") can use the 8 key phrases. ("but only if", "and furthermore", "you ask far too much", ...) The other players have minor roles, and may not use any key phrases. [edit] The game is "fair", because the major roles rotate quickly throughout a gaming session -- but nevertheless, this means that Polaris cannot accommodate more than 4 players. (Well, it can, but only if players take turns sitting out. :P)
\n
\nThis is the big problem for me, because my group has 5 players -- which is why we haven't tried the game yet. Perhaps we should just try it anyway, but ... more generally, I think it would be interesting to see what kind of story players could tell together, if they all had equal powers in conflicts.
\n
\nProperty #2: Character degeneration
\n
\nPolaris is a tragic horror setting, and it has characters advance along a "zeal / weariness" track, as they become more and more corrupt. It seems to me that this track is used as a "stick", to prevent the character from using his/her abilities all the time. (You can't refresh your abilities without also risking corruption.) Could this kind of game take place in a less tragic setting? If so, how would it control the use of abilities?
\n
\nProperty #3: Vague setting and characters
\n
\nThe setting is poorly defined in the rulebook, giving narrators freedom to invent whatever sorts of demons / relationships / societies they like. Is this necessary, or could you run the game in a well-defined setting with well understood characters?

    The setting is poorly defined in the rulebook, giving narrators freedom to invent whatever sorts of demons / relationships / societies they like. Is this necessary, or could you run the game in a well-defined setting with well understood characters?
    \nI think that this comes as a consequence of it being a GMless game. Since there's no one player with authority over the setting, everyone has equal ability to define the setting. So either the setting needs to be sketchy and largely incomplete, so everyone can fill in those details, or you need everyone to have familiarity with a large amount of information. The latter option works pretty well if you're using a pre-established setting, like Harry Potter or Star Wars, and all the players are already fans.

    \n There are a few other "ritual negotiation" games out there. Mist-Robed Gate is probably the most prominent, and it works for any number of players.

    The others that I can think of are mostly Jeep/Nordic-influenced games like Montsegur 1244, Sign in Stranger, A Flower for Mara, and the like which don't do ritual negotiation so much as structure player inputs in other ways, much more like improv. You might check those out too, if you're interested in non-random mechanical systems, which seem to be more and more prominent (well, compared to before, when they were usually casually dismissed as "freeform").

    You might also check out Kazekami Kyoko Kills Kublai Khan, for a fairly different, highly structured take on ritual negotiation.

    \n The "jeepforged" version of Ganakagok is numberless; it has a number of interesting features that contrast with the categories mentioned in the OP.

    In terms of #1, player inequality: the largish group of players (6-12) is divided up into "spirit tribes" (groups) with different responsibilities: one group does scene framing, another assigns characters to players and introduces them into the scene, a third directs the "on stage" action and describes its larger consequences. One player is the Sun, and is responsible for moving the game/story closer to its conclusion.

    In terms of #2, character degeneration: characters have thus far been mainly static, but I am thinking about giving one of the groups the responsibility for indicating "transformations" of characters as a result of the action: e.g., the "dreamy youth" is now a "zealous prophet" or something like that. I imagine some characters will grow stronger and others will grow weaker if I implement this change.

    In terms of #3, vague setting: yes definitely, but this is finessed since the game uses a Ganakagok deck with quasi-Inuit ideas and motifs (e.g., one card is the Ancient of Tears (Polar Bear): to master or overcome) to inspire the contributions of players in the "plotting stage" of the game.

    So it may be better to speak of "role differentiation" rather than "player inequality," as a feature of "numberless" games, is my conclusion.
