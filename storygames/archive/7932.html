\n I had the opportunity to play Werewolf a bunch of times this weekend with a dozen people and I've never seen a more effective, engaging, and enjoyable ice-breaking activity for a disparate group to get to know one another. By the time we'd played two games, we were all friendly and full of energy and enthusiasm for more "serious" gaming.

One fun moment - Clinton, as a villager , made a very cogent argument about who the werewolf must be, based on some mathematical deduction. I immediately cried "He's talking in numbers! Kill him!" And we did.

In this case it was an informal house-con, so I asked "who has something to run?" and we put up sticky notes and voted on various games. One of the suggestions was "Five more games of Werewolf!".

    \n I run and play Are You A Werewolf (Looney Labs) at many cons. General comments:

    * Perennial Kills - Fact: If you are wearing a red shirt, you're about 80% likely to be eliminated in the first lynching. God help you if you're particularly hirsute.

    * Alternate Productions - The Mafia theme was the original, but I think the werewolf theme is a good one for play at cons (kids, non-gamers). Also, it's worth noting that the Werewolf thing was actually a part of Princeton's "official" rules, for playing with really massive groups or for a variant--there would be werewolves AND mafia in the same game, in opposition to each other and the villagers. Further, being open source means that, frankly, there's a TON of alternate themes/productions possible (I want to do a Cthulhu-themed one with Cultists, the Psychic, Scholars, etc--another ripe one is a sci fi "body snatchers" one).

    * Optional Roles - The biggest problem with these is when to add them--what populations of players (similar to the "when do we add another werewolf" metrics). That's why, I think, the MAIN value-add (other than evocative art and role rules on the cards) would be testing to figure out what roles should be added in what order, based on population. I have also discussed (on RPG.net, with Mike Young, IIRC) a game in which EVERY villager role has some kind of day or night function, to avoid the "dull," do-nothing villager role. This may or may not be doable (could require much less powerful roles).

    * Best Optional Roles - The Masons roles are pretty sweet: basically, they get a "wake at night" stage, first night, during which they get to identify each other, thus reducing the number of possible werewolf suspects. They are sort of like "mini Seers" in that they can self-sacrifice, to reveal that "we're the Masons, so those other folks must be the wolves" (and the wolves can fake that), and in the way that they become a prime target if they admit their roles. Little Girl is OK, but it can be a bitch to adjudicate--the wolves as often as not reveal themselves to neighboring players by the mad gesticulations they do to show they've caught the Girl. And meanwhile, she's the ultimate Seer--it's common for her to reveal herself and say, "Him, her, and her are the wolves; I'm the little girl; I'll die tonight, I'm sure." (Then clever wolves DON'T kill her, and she gets lynched... giving them only one "free" night.)

    * Best Possible Play - Yet, as it works, the "pure" game is generally the best. it's VERY hard to balance roles against population, and some roles lead to potential stalemates (Leather Jacket and Mayor being the two worst culprits). And other roles just speed the game up too much, reducing the potential for interaction and deduction (Vigilante, Devil, Archangel). Andy Looney (generally considered a strong game designer and theorist) concurs with the basic game, which is why Looney labs is reluctant to release an expansion for AYAW (or a new version, with more roles).

    * As An Icebreaker - Yep, it's a game that even non-gamers love after one or two games. Old, young (not TOO young, though--wee ones often ruin a game), male, female. And it's SO easy to run anywhere--use playing cards, like Mafia. Or Keep my soon-to-be-released thin card pack in a pocket or purse.

    I could, obviously, go on and on... I think Mafia/AYAW/WOMH is a bit of genius in game design, development, and accessibility.

    \n Hmmm.... I'll give this a shot, Jason.

    * I Have A Secret - The fact that the wolves and Seer have a duty to perform which, perforce, means they should not reveal themselves, seems to generate the fun of being a wolf or seer (or other, optional role).

    * Real Skills - We aren't pretending to fly or use magic; we're using our actual observation, memory, deduction, and persuasion skills to play and, hopefully, win. On that....

    * Zero-Sum Gamist Play - It plays fairly fast, to an obvious conclusion, and typically has a very small "winner" group (often only two villagers or one wolf remains). Winning carries with it an obvious social reinforcement, because you won with Real Skill, not dicing or min-maxing a system (which appeals to grognards, sure, but not to most "casual" players).

    * Physical Interaction - The noise-making by everyone at night, the wolves trying to coordinate their unanimous kill vote at night, some optional role rules (Little Girl, in particular)--all these make for engaging play, something to occupy yourself with during what might be a dull period (night). Also, there's some metagaming that can be done with the noise-making--one of my favorite things to do is to play a sort of contretemps to the group's hand-slapping, making unusual rhythms out what normal ends up synchronized (folks tend to slap their thigh or the floor in a common rhythm).

    * No Distractions - No rule book to pour over, no character sheet to consult, no math to do, no dice to count out and sort and roll and resort. Instead, just eye contact and conversation (and demagoguery).

    * Emergent Complexity - First game with newbs is one way... but by the fifth or sixth game, folks are developing strategies for play; and strategies emerge in specific groups that won't emerge elsewhere, with other sets of players. For instance, I tend to drift into being a very BAD logician and accuser (intentionally) in order to accomplish two things: (1) make the wolves happy to keep me alive, as I am "clearly" off base and so (2) I can often, late game, watch reactions to my totally wrong ranting and figure out at least one wolf, if not all of them. Basically, anyone who agrees with me or looks overly satisfied with my ability to sow discord is likely to be a wolf.

    I'll give it some more thought... but I think the above captures a lot of it. [Plug: Re-reading that, it occurs to me that For Mature Audiences does many of those things: Real skill, physical interaction, no gameplay tools, zero-sum (but not gamist).]

    \n The beginnings of the Werewolf theme for this game were with Andrew "Zarf" Plotkin, whom you might know as author of a bunch of well-liked interactive fiction games. He has a page about it here. As Mafia, it's origins go back to Dimitry Davidoff in 1986, in Moscow.

    There are hundreds of thousands of variants:

    * wolf/villager ratio, number of seers, etc.
    * do you reveal the identity of dead players?
    * do you remain quiet during the night or do you make noise? either way, is listening for movements during the night phase part of the game, or should players endeavour not to discover anything that way?
    * inserting new roles is very popular - characters who can protect other characters form the wolves/mafia, characters who get a one shot instakill of a player they select, and so on...

    There's loads listed on that link above.

    \n For my son's middle school history class a few years back, we put together a Julius Caesar-themed version called "Are you a Conspirator". Each player had a card indicating which character they were from the Shakespeare play, and if they were a conspirator or not. Additional rules (since this was supposed to be educational) was that you had to explain who your character was if he or she was killed, and if you couldn't, you had to read a page of the play out loud instead. Also, if Caesar's player was killed, the game ended immediately. It was a big hit with the class, and they spent more time playing it than the teacher had allowed for.
