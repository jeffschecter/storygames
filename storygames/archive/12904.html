    \n AW makes a particular style of GMing an explicit part of the game. It may be a style that you have already discovered on your own; it's not that it's a new discovery, but that that style is codified and made explicit.

    Also, if you play it as written, it makes it difficult to plan out story arcs -- all you can do is set up the conflict and see what happens.

    \n The most interesting thing about MCing, to me, is that it makes me view running the game as a creative process bound by constraints. You don't get to do whatever pops into your head in the moment; you have to use a move from your list and follow the principles. I'm sort of into constraints, so those rules are incredibly powerful to me.

    I realize that not everybody who MCs is going to go mute except when they have a line of dialogue that conforms to a move, and I'm certainly not a perfect adherent myself yet. Constraints make creativity both harder and easier, but if your brain likes to latch onto problems, they can generate drive much more consistently than the normal blank-sheet starting point.

    \n Hey Neil, here's my thoughts on why Fronts are so nice:

    Apocalypse World is one of those games where characters are the main focus, and players haven't out-and-out accepted that they showed up to follow the GM's plotline and that they should explore every single hint the GM drops. In older games of this style, the GM has the awful job of throwing stuff at the players until something sticks and they get interested. In my old games, I've found this leads to a resentful GM, after the players ignore all of his cool ideas.

    In AW, the fronts are formulated so that if the players ignore a hook the MC throws out, he can just take it back, advance it (ie make it more of a problem) and re-introduce it later. If the players continue to ignore the front, it grows and grows until it becomes a problem they simply can't ignore. That's part of what fronts are for.

    So in the old days:
    GM: "So there's this wolf, looks like it's infected by some kinda fungal mange."
    Players: "Yeah, whatever, we got things to do."
    GM: grumble, grumble "Always ignore the good stuff..." grumble

    In Apocalypse World:
    MC: "So now you can see people with that fungal mange infection."
    Players: "Huh? What infection?"
    MC: "Y'know, like you saw on that wolf you avoided."
    Players: "Oh, right. People are infected now? Uh-oh. We give them a wide berth. I don't want to catch it."
    MC: "Okay."

    Later:
    MC: "The fungal mange zombies attack your convoy."
    Players: "Aaaagh! Drive! Drive!"

    Make sense?

    \nLook through crosshairs is a great example.

    It's one of the many dials in the game. For your game of AW, does it mean, like Brian said, to hold on lightly? To play your NPCs with passion, but ultimately let them meet their fates and not wrestle the game around to protect them? Yeah, it means that. But the dial can be turned way, way up, if you want to. Like, MC, do everything you can to tear the NPCs down, ruin their lives, burn their hopes and dreams, bury them in the poisoned ground. When you turn it all the way up you show the brutal realities of the apocalypse world (but without "punishing" the PCs to get the point across).

    Like every principle and move in AW, look through crosshairs requires a judgment call from the MC (and sometimes the players, too) continuously, case-by-case. Even though the game has very specific instructions and advocates a specific style of play, the implementation of those instructions is very diverse (since different people = different judgment calls).

    Further example: when you do something under fire. When do you call for that move? What counts as "under fire"? That's a very interesting space to make judgment calls. It varies from person to person, group to group -- even in different campaigns with the same group of players. In one of our games, the warlord's withering gaze would count as acting under fire. In a different AW series with the same people, not so much.

    And then, when you hit the roll with a 7-9, what's a worse outcome? A hard bargain? An ugly choice? It's all context-specific, dependent utterly on the MC's vision, judgment, and personal style.

    There's huge room for diversity in that apparently specific game text. I would even argue that the game demands diversity, given its reliance on group- and context-specific judgments.

    \n I have to agree with a couple of people in this thread - MCing is a BIG change for some people.

    For many many years the games i played and ran were basically - a "plot" has been made, and the players will go along it in some way. Often there were cool side tracks and weirdness, but there was STORY to be found and followed.

    One GM I play with is notoriously bad (or good) at this. He will start a game saying - "this will be about X, make some characters", and then flesh out the plot based on the characters. The games are a lot of fun and all, but I've often gotten a feeling of being swept along and not being able to make really big changes. Its almost a joke at times that "plot demands" something happens and referring to Plot as another character, or the BBEG itself.

    Playing Apocalypse world is completely opposite to this method. There is no plot, just a situation that the players' characters interact with.

    I have tried running more "sandbox" games, but with the group I play with normally this basically demands that there be a bunch of "plots" that can be picked up or interwoven. Unless i made those plots then the game would often start to get unfocussed.

    MCing is a set of rules - explicit rules of do this, dont do that, keep track of X etc. When i ran a series of session with Apocalypse World it really was instructions for running a sandbox game where the players' characters are the main protagonists. There is no plot at all - in fact one of the great joys I had was in discovering just what in heck was really going on That was something I'd never experienced as a GM before!

    The game is also a pretty much "sit down and run". I literally just printed out the playbooks, an MC cheat sheet with the principles and moves, and the 1st session sheet. We all sat down, and within 5 mins we were roleplaying these whacked out people in dangerous frontiers. I spent 10 minutes after that session organising my scribbles into the fronts, and never again did any game prep. We would sit down each session, have a brief recap, and launch into playing again.

    Compared to my previous pre-game setup of statting out NPCs, preparing plot points and scenes, and laying out clues for the players to pick up - I was spending more time preparing than actually playing, and was loosing my enjoyment of RP in general.

    To me, the MC rules were a way of clearly doing what I always wanted to do with RP but never quite knew how. Run a sandbox game without much preparation and get straight to the "good bits" of RP.

      \nThey're different sets of GM/MC Principles, but the overall method is the same: make a provocative move, listen intently to the counter-move, resolve a conflict when necessary, make a new move building on the counter-move, listen to the counter-move, etc. Always use the principles (whatever they are) to inform your next move.

      That is a far cry from: tell the players what happens next, check if they act according to plan, if they don't throw an encounter or problem or twist at them, if they do tell them what happens next, and always use the pre-determined plot and/or reality-based logic to determine what should happen next.

      Many games use the latter method and do it well. That method is right for those games. The method used in AW and DitV is, clearly, intended for Story Now play. I think what people are talking about is that AW very clearly spells out how to do Story Now play, not that AW will change how they play (f.ex.) WFRPG3; nor how AW's principles (let's take "look at NPCs through crosshairs") will necessarily be right for all other games, narrativist or otherwise.
