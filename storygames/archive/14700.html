\n Reading Knuth's *Art of Programming* where he's defining algorithms - long story short, algorithms are things you can get a computer to do, recipes are not so definable or cut-and-dry.
Recipes say things like 'separate the egg' - assuming you know how to do that; or 'boil until tender' - leaving a subjective judgment up to the chef. You have to already be a decent chef to read a recipe.
It occurred to me that board game rules are algorithms and rpg rules are recipes.
"Make apocalypse world seem real", "Say yes or roll the dice" (which still leaves it in your chef's hands to decide when to say yes and when to roll the dice), "Kill the bastards" (which ones are bastards?), "Keep things moving" (how slow is too slow?)
Does it have to be that way? Is it possible to write rpg rules so clear they become algorithms? Or is rpg instruction always going to have to have a non-text component, where somebody shows somebody else how to separate the egg and what constitutes tender?

    \n on anyway, Vincent speaks of different ways to change people's behavior.

    Procedural cues can be algorithmic. Mediated cues cannot.

    Mediated cues are rules to adjust things in an imagined space, using things in physical space. Procedural cues just adjust things in our shared physical space.

    Note that procedural cues might influence the fiction of a game - "No one at the table can use the word elephant in any context" == a game that probably doesn't include elephants, at least explicitly. But that rule can be twisted "sure, in our world they're called Oliphants."

    \n My take is that there are many people who like seeing the algorithm play out, and enjoy it even more when they are helped to suspend disbelief and can buy into having "ownership" over the process.

    For kids, Candyland and War are sufficient.
    For some adults, Solitaire is indeed engaging and fun.

    I remember a boardgame from when I was in high-school. It was about sending WWII bomber teams out on missions. Everything was roll, consult chart, roll, consult chart. About the only meaningful user decision was which direction the ball turret gunner faced. The older guys I gamed with LOVED it. Drove me batty.

    Peggle has a very thin slice of user input, one that is totally swamped by the random chaotic nonsense that happens after you've chosen. And yet people love watching all that nonsense play out.

    Diablo is full of algorithmic decisions, and then a lot of window-dressing that doesn't really matter. You could train a 5-year-old to beat that game. Click this. Click this button too. Drink potions when these orbs get low. Run back home when you run out of potions. And yeah there's some outfitting and skill stuff, but the core game is mindless. And people love it.

    Monopoly is 95% just watching the algorithm play out. Most popular board game ever.

    It's also interesting to note how much more enjoyment many people get out of games of chance when we give illusory opportunities for player input. Like picking your own lottery numbers. Or hitting the button that tells the wheel when to stop. Or spinning the price-is-right wheel with your own hands.

    \n It seems like there is some splitting hairs even though there is a plain distinction. Calilng for player choice isn't the same as the game procedures being algorithmic. The rules of chess are algorithmic. However, the rules don't tell you what piece to move.

    While there can be ambiguity in the rules for boardgames or card games, that isn't the same as the procedures being intentionally designed for judgment calls. For example, Once Upon A Time requires that the story make sense as a fairy tale as part of the procedures of play. That can't be programmed into a computer for the foreseeable future.

    It's worth noting that D&D, Champions, and possibly other RPGs have been adapted into computer games. However, the rules had to be changed in many cases for the computer to be able to operate as a moderator.

    \n There's a difference between the rules of the game being algorithmic and the player/GM's decisions being algorithmic. In some of the more combat-oriented D&D games the player's actions are basically down to an algorithm, more or less. However when some of those get cut off (say, magic doesn't work) it can turn into a pure math game.
