\n So over here on Justin's thread about designing for the "mainstream", there was a discussion about using playing cards in RPGs:\n
Posted By: JDCorleyI like the commentary about the difference between games where you hold cards in your hand as compared to those where you throw cards all over the place, but I think there's a lot more there than has been said and definitely more there than I can grasp with my feeble intellect. Anyone volunteer to keep the topic alive in another thread?
\nI've actually been keenly interested in this topic for a while, and I've been meaning to start a thread simply to hear other's opinions on the issue. So here it is. This is less about designing for mainstream players (an issue which is pretty loaded on it's own), and more about wanting to hear folks' experiences and intuitions with using playing cards in games.\n
Posted By: Jason MorningstarMaybe try out a few card-based games (Contenders, Dust Devils, especially Spione) and see some of the ways they are being used. I like them for all the reasons you describe. I know people who dislike them because they have very specific connotations - I guess if you play poker a lot, it's hard to look at a deck of cards and think "gritty spy thriller" and not "Texas hold 'em".
\nThat someone is me- Jason and I have been going around and around about this lately, because he loves designing games that use playing cards and I think they carry strong connotations of their own which make their applicability somewhat limited. I think they're great for Dust Devils, for example, because the pre-set expectations of playing cards (poker, gambling, cheating, etc.) reinforce the setting and tone of a Western story. I haven't played Contenders or Spione, but I have played Drowning & Falling and other smaller games using cards. My experience has always been that the hundred-or-so years of cultural baggage attached to the cards contradicts the themes of those games, or at least my associations about them. For example, D&F is essentially a dungeon-crawler, so I expect dice. That clash of expectations is distracting to me, creating a barrier for me getting into the game initially.

I recognize that much of this is in my own head- these are my associations, my expectations that are getting in the way of my game experience. I'm pretty sure I'm not alone in that, however, and unless it's the intention of the game designer to create that discordance, it seems disruptive. \n
Posted By: komradebobI'm not saying you're wrong at all, Jason. My point was simply to recognize that there are a couple of different groups in there with different expectations.

As an example, I was talking to a friend about Universalis, and got to the bit about coins. Jumping ahead, she immediately asked if the goal was to win all of the coins. I wa a little at a loss, but it was a fairly reasonable question, as this is a common goal of games played with coins.

Likewise, in a game played with cards, people often hold a hand of cards...
\nI thought this was a good example of what I'm talking about- players bring a set of deep, cultural associations to the table any time they play a game. If they've ever played an RPG before (or heard of one), they'll probably look for dice and character sheets. If there's a board, they'll expect to have a representative token they can move around. If it's a card game, they'll want to pick up and look at their cards as a hand, and if they're playing cards, they'll probably start matching Poker hands. As a designer, do you want to design your game to run with the grain of those expectations or to cut across them?

    Posted By: Jason Morningstarwhy is one weird thing acceptable and another (that isn't even as weird!) dissonant?
    \nBy my reading, you answer your own question: the dissonance comes about because cards are not weird--folks have years of experience of fairly rigid interaction with them, and deviation from those general techniques means jumping out of a mental rut to new ground.
    -----
    That said, I don't think that's such a major jump, for all but the least imaginative people in the world. Magic tricks, for instance, have gotten folks used to a variety of "pick one," whether looking at it or not, behaviors. War is a fundamental "pick and play and compare" game that everyone knows on some level. Even poker has variations with visible cards, unheld hands, and shared cards (Stud and Hold'em, respectively). Heck, even building them up into a teetering stack is a common occurrence, as are plus-one/minus-one, alternating colors, etc (Solitaire).

    I mean... I would think that, for someone to argue Steve's side, he or she would have to come up with an example of card use or interaction which does not yet exist in a common game. (I just tried, and it's hard!)

    In the end, what's a standard playing card deck? A d13 which simultaneously "rolls" a d4 each draw... but which can only yield four cards of any given value without reshuffling (or multiple decks) or thirteen of any given suit. So if a resolution system is served by this direct coupling of a d13 and d4, and the inverse relationship of availability, then a deck of cards is a perfect randomizer. Of course, they also have uses as a changeable game board:
    http://www.wunderland.com/WTS/Ginohn/games/Gnostica/GnosticaRules.html

    Therefore, I'd take the exact opposite side from Steve: barring some radical new way to use the cards in play, the use of playing cards as a systemic element in an RPG should make for less of a barrier to entry, for new- or non-gamers.

    \n Is it possible that I agree with both Matt and Steve about this?

    No method of fortune is without its own connotations. Sometimes you can make those connotations work for you, when that happens you can strengthen the color of your game. Sometimes you have to ignore those connotations because the practical applications are better suited than anything else for what you need, when that happens you encounter the barrier that Steve describes.

    The closest thing to truly color-neutral fortune you can have is customized objects, like Ganakagok or Legends of Alyria. That way you can always be sure the connotations of the mechanic reinforce your color.

    \n Joe Prince uses regular cards in Contenders (and in his upcoming Labyrinths & Lycanthropes), what is key is that he doesn't just use them as a d2 (red/black).

    Prince also publishes Swansong, which uses Tarot cards. Here they don't just flavour the experience of playing the game. The Tarot cards are used to give power cards that match best with abilities on your character sheet, encouraging you to bank those cards or keep them in your hand. That's a nice tactic brought into play (games with dice rarely have you keeping a rolled result, for instance).

    Ashok Desai's Fearmonger use regular cards but also does that "keeping your cards" thing that Swansong does. It's a kind of resource management. Fearmonger uses it to help with the tension of rural horror (Deliverance, Children of the Corn, Wicker Man are what leap to my mind when thinking of it).

    Finally, Iain McAllister's Mob Justice uses a regular deck and an almost-poker mechanic to introduce raising and _bluffing_ (on behalf of the GM and players). That's a pretty cool idea.

    Posted By: Steve Segedyit's about the inherent meaning attached to sets of playing cards that might have nothing to do with a given RPG. For some folks (apparently not as many here) those associations jump to mind immediately when looking at the cards.
    \nI look at it two ways. Bear in mind that I want to design games that non-rpg'ers and rpg'ers alike would enjoy together, or that a non-rpg'er would pick up and get excited to play. Existing associations in cards are useful design-wise for me. One of the things I think turns people off to trying rpgs, besides the false assumption that they're all about wizards and elves, is that there there's extra stuff you need that you have to get at special places. It turned me off for a long time, and perhaps why it's an important design idea for me. I frequented our local game store for years before I actually played a role playing game. I even ventured out of the board and card games section and bought a couple of rpg's that seemed interesting. But it seemed like I would need extra equipment (available at the store) and seasoned role playing gamers. Sure, but I'll have to wait until next payday. At the time--in my late 20's and early 30's--I didn't know any who still wanted to play. At lot of that was perception. Fancy dice sounded cool, but....

    So, given my personal (and admittedly perceived) barriers to getting into rpgs, I like to incorporate the existing card associations into the game. So mixing familiar "story" elements into a role playing game, makes said rpg even more accessible. Cards are really fertile ground for story telling, plain and simple. No need to let that interfere with the story you want people to tell. There's enough there to enhance your game.

    On the other hand, even if you know a thousand card games, when you're playing a game you may never really look long at a card and think about the story behind the suits, the details in the pictures, or the visual triggers you get when you look at the faces. It's possible to disregard those associations or create new ones.

    We do that with dice, even if those associations aren't part of a game. Certainly some games have specific colors you use for specific reasons. And aren't we all attracted to pretty dice?

    Posted By: David ArtmanIn the end, what's a standard playing card deck? A d13 which simultaneously "rolls" a d4 each draw... but which can only yield four cards of any given value without reshuffling (or multiple decks) or thirteen of any given suit. So if a resolution system is served by this direct coupling of a d13 and d4, and the inverse relationship of availability, then a deck of cards is a perfect randomizer.
    Basically--as always--don't design for some anecdotal or hypothetical customer who will have a negative reaction to a "non-traditional" randomizer (non-traditional ONLY in the eyes of RPGers; very traditional to most others). Rather, design what works best and promote its application unapologetically. It's far from ""making a focussed effort to overcome" card-use; it's celebrating their use for both accessibility and for the thematic or systemic strengths of the deck of cards as used in play.
