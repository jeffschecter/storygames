\n This thought occurred to me on a long drive this weekend. I'm not sure how it would work but it seems really interesting to me.

So you play D&D 3.5, and stat up a tenth level character. Or you run a traditional campaign until everybody is tenth level, even better. At that point, a switch flips and all experience you earn becomes negative experience. You go down levels, lose hit points, are stripped of feats, and eventually become weak and frail again, and then die. Your character has a tragic arc and, probably, an ignoble end at the hands of a kobold or something.

One interesting thing about this is that you'd actively try to avoid fighting, since even victory tears you down. You'd avoid wealth, becoming an ascetic. The GM's role would be to push conflicts where fighting was worth it, despite the irreversible pain. With the right kind of set-up, the degeneration could be played as karmic debt, or the burden of your murderous, genocidal "adventures", or the curses of your many, many victims.

My first thought was that this could be its own thing, but it seems to fit so well with D&D ... but I'm not a D&D expert; disabuse me of that notion if I'm way off base.

    \n Perhaps the backwards trend is some sort of curse? The more they do, the more they forget. And not just in terms of abilities, but in their social connections as well. The more time spent with friends and family, the fewer memories of them they have. In order to stop the curse, they have to go adventuring, but the more they adventure, the quicker they unspool. It becomes a race: can they discover the source while they still have power to stop it? It could be a purely personal attack, or it could be part of a larger plot. Maybe the adventurers are the powers that be in an area, and were cursed so that another power group could move in to replace them, or to leave a community vulnerable to an invasion.

    And, of course, as they unspool, enemies completely unrelated to the curse are going to take advantage. And the relationships with junior associates will change. High priests find themselves slipping below their acolytes' power, commanders become weaker fighters than their lieutenants. Characters with animal companions that are level-dictated have to deal with losing them.

    Perhaps they're getting younger, too, which could cause some awkwardness, especially in romantic relationships. How does a spouse feel about watching a wife or husband grow younger every day?

    \n This is a bit tangental, but for a time I considered running a D&D campaign that did this a little differently - the PCs all went forward as normal but continued to run across another character (or characters) who seemed to be devolving/losing levels as described above. The hook was going to be that at some point (level 10/name level), one of the PCs sacrifices themselves to save the others in some sort of time vortex. Essentially, that PC starts to live backwards in time, and secretly helping his former allies and former self, gaining experience as they do. But from the campaign's point of view to date - it looks like they were losing XP because of the whole backwards time thing.

    I love the idea of the decay of characters - particularly if there is a good explanation for it and you have people willing to buy into it as a concept.

    \n Huh. I'm glad people are enjoying this idea. Personally, I fail to see the point.

    D&D is a game specifically designed to be about tactical combat, rewards, and increasing personal power / personal challenges. As such a game, it actually works surprisingly well. It just so happens it's not exactly my cup of tea - if I wanted to do that, I'd play World of Warcraft instead, since it also caters to the exact same needs and is much more approachable and convenient.

    To take such a ruleset and reverse it seems to be the worst possible thing you can do. You'd be dealing with all the challenges, rules crunch, and minmaxing in addition to the constant spectre of failure. The better you do, the worse off your character is in the end. Not only does it rub the Simulationist in me the wrong way - aside from the Curse idea, I can't see any reason why this would make any sort of sense - but the idea of all that work for a negative payoff is just painful.

    There is a certain aesthetic and irony inherent in the idea that makes it interesting, but as a game I can't imagine it'd be anything but torture. For being a group of people near universally united behind "system matters", I don't get the enthusiasm behind taking a Gamist ruleset and thinking it'll turn Narrative just by turning it on its head, as some posts above imply.
