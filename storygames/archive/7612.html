A few weeks ago, I started a game of D&D 4th edition with my local group -- all reasonably familiar with 3rd edition, having just finished a long game by another GM, but no one else had previously expressed much (positive) interest in 4th edition. I was particularly interested in the skill challenges system, but I wasn't happy with how things turned out, probably much due to my own bad reading of the players' energy and motivation.

\n

I started with the cliched-but-heartfelt "meet someone in a bar who asks you to do a job" opening scene. After a bit of light roleplay (lightly interrogating the noble woman who's sending them on this quest), the group starts discussing next steps ("we should try to find this guy, talk to these people, maybe see who knows about the macguffin!")

\n

I interrupted this chatter, and briefly outlined things: this was an example of a skill challenge, everyone could participate, some skills "counted" while others were just for "assistance", they needed N successes (on skills that count) before X failures or they would "lose" the challenge -- while that wouldn't end the adventure, it would lead to a fight or other negative thing.

\n

This kinda destroyed the otherwise initially positive mood at the table. I made a few mistakes in setting up the challenge -- for example, tying skills to what "made sense", rather than pegging them to things the pregenerated characters were already good at, which meant that it was slightly more difficult than I'd intended. But, judging from chatter among the players (and one, who seemed to completely sign-off at this point), I think something else was amiss.

\n

Essentially, the set-up -- and past experiences with this kind of thing -- suggested an open-ended skill romp. The players thought we were playing "hide the clue" -- I know I've played this before in other games -- where they stumble through all manner of "likely" strategies until the GM dispenses the next plot point. Moving to a model where outright failure was an option (nebulously defined though it may be) came across as a big energy drain.

\n

To make matters worse, they had a terrible run of dice luck. I've never seen so many sub-five roles on so many 20-sided die. The skill challenge system seems to give even skills PCs about a 50-50 chance of success, as first-level characters, most of the variance is in the die roll. More frustration seemed to set in when they realized that the system didn't give them many resources to mitigate this string of failure. They were asking if they could use action points to for roll bonuses (this was how it worked in the previous game) or if there were any items that gave bonuses, etc. (They didn't make the most of the "aid another PC" mechanics, but I think that the run of bad luck had them underestimating their odds of success.)

\n

I pushed them to continue, and eventually we moved on to the rest of the story (and fun was had, etc.) but for many in the group, this put a cloud over the evening. I'll probably be more cautious about skill challenges in the future -- I'll certainly pay more attention to tuning the challenge to the skills the PCs possess, but that makes it rather unlikely that I'd be able to make one up as I went.

\n

I'm curious how others' experiences have gone? How did you introduce the system, and transition from table chatter to running the skill challenge?

\n

-- Joshua

    \n Skill Challenges strike me as a tool for GM preperation for the most part. It's basically there to stat out the situation the players deal with before the game, much like you would stat out an NPC. I don't really see the point of announcing a skill challenge, the primary or secondary skills (which is something a GM I played with did) or even disclosing the rules used in one.

    You just describe the situation like you always do, maybe drawing more attention to what the characters perceive as opportunities to act on. But instead of coming up with DCs for what the players try to do on the fly or using GM fiat to determine when the players have failed or succeeded, you have all that written down in your notes. This way you can focus on colourful descriptions and acting out NPCs, and have less to worry about picking the right numbers, coming up with consequences for success or failure or figuring out how much XP it's all worth.

    (BTW, are you using the errata'd Skill Challenge rules? The new DCs in particular allow non-optimized characters to contribute instead of having to constantly fall back on their one maxed out skill.)

    \n Georgios, yes. That's the way I'd do it to.
    \n
    \nSkill Checks are like trails of bread crumbs. Make a roll and then narrate something related to the task at hand that either moves the party closer to the ultimate goal (on a success) or introduces a new obstacle or an interesting tangential twist (on a failure). Each roll is like discovering another breadcrumb...but one 40% made up, 40% influenced by the skill roll used and player description of what they're doing, and 20% by some seed ideas the DM came up with in prep.
    \n
    \nCollect enough breadcrumbs and you wind up at your destination. Successful breadcrumbs means (in this case) finding the guy you were looking for. Failure breadcrumbs means finding...something else equally fun, but potentially more dangerous (like drawing the attention of some big bad who's also looking for the MacGuffin).
    \n
    \nBut I'd keep all the rest (what's secondary, what skills have special bonus rules, how many of each you need) behind the screen. Not for DM illusionist/fudging reasons...but because those things totally aren't entertaining. All the skills and powers and kewl fighty stuff is entertaining and thus players should be encouraged to engage with that stuff. The Skill Challege (mechanically) is dry and boring and not at all fun...so don't make the players suffer through it.
    \n
    \nJust let them talk and when they say something that ties into the Skill Challenge, call for a roll and narrate something fun and keep track of how close they are to getting where they need to be.

    \n I've used skill challenges a fair bit in my D&D 4e games.

    First off, are you applying the corrections to the Dungeon Master's Guide? The skill challenges, as printed, are broken and tend to make groups fail way too often. The errata are supposed to fix the math that creates the rampant failure, but I haven't tested them.

    Second, I started using a modified system, created by Stalker0 on ENWorld. His system also fixes the broken math. In addition, it adds some drama to the game with rules for "guiding light," "heroic surge," "bold recovery," and "time of trials." I tested it in my last game and it was a lot of fun. We even had a bold recovery, which made the challenge (a court trial accusing the PCs of stealing a day of time from the universe) very exciting.

    In general, I tend to play very loose with which skills are "appropriate" for the encounter. I may determine a list of appropriate skills before the challenge, but if a player comes up with something clever, I allow it as a main check.

    Take a look at my report about my "The Scryer" games, where I did "police procedural" play in D&D. These made strong use of the skill challenge system and, at the time, I was using the one in the DMG as written and I had no problems with it, though the players did use aid another fairly often.

    The "table chatter" you refer to -- is it fun for the players? Is it fun for them but not you? If so, can you get involved and make it fun for you? Because this, to me, is an important part of role-playing. It isn't the stuff you do between "real" role-playing. As long as it's about the game (and it sounds like it was), and the players are having a good time, why stop it? Yeah, you can gently prod them to get to the mechanics with a polite, "Ready to roll some dice?"

    When they're talking about how they're going to find the guy or talk to people, you can suggest how that translates into a roll. "You want to talk to some people to find the guy? Are you using Diplomacy for that? Maybe Intimidation?" Better, transition to a scene where they're doing what they're table-talking about. "Okay, so you are out in the alleys, talking to some really seedy people and asking about the guy. One of them is this older woman who looks like she wants to gut you with a dagger. She knows something, but she isn't talking. How do you convince her to give you the information?" Maybe it's a Diplomacy check. Maybe Intimidation or Insight. Maybe they have some other idea. Maybe you fight a combat instead and a win gives a success. The important thing is that you went from fun table-talk to REALLY fun role-play and combat. Woot!

    I'm gonna flag something you said: "eventually we moved on to the rest of the story." Whose story? What do you mean there? How does this relate to your feeling that the game was stalled in table-talk?

    Whiffing (rolling bad and feeling like a doofus) can be a problem in D&D. I have handled that with great success using what I call Blood Tokens. Every time a player fails a d20 roll, they get a blood token (I have red glass beads). They can cash these in (all of them, not just a few at a time) for +2 for each token they turn in. They can do this after they fail a roll, if they want. This makes missing kinda fun. "I swing and -- crap -- miss. But I take a blood token. I'm getting angry. I swing again. Crap, another miss! I'm turning in my 4 tokens for a +8. Fuck you, elf. My frustration turns into pure black vengeance. Die!"

    \nHaving played both ways as a player, I can tell you what the point of it is. To give the players clear direction and get everyone participating. I've played it where the GM, as you say "describe[s] the situation like you always do, maybe drawing more attention to what the characters perceive as opportunities to act on." And one or two players, whichever ones have the skills that seem to fit best (in one encounter it might seem like Athletics and Thievery on the only ones useful, in another Diplomacy and Bluff) and everyone else sits back, and in my case, wishing I could contribute but as a Fighter with very few skills, unless the two I'm really good at come up, there's no point.

    When its run more structured in that "This is a Skill Challenge" you can go around the table and get participation from everyone. Now I can use my Intimidation or Endurance because it's my turn and I have to do something! Intimidation while drinking with some dock workers? Hey, yeah, I'm showing how menacing I am by drinking them under the table. I could have used Endurance, but it's not a preferred skill for the challenge. And I actually see Religion and History get used, as well other many other skills, in a variety of creative ways. Because when your turn is up, you have to do something!

    Now, maybe once the group gets used to the structure, you can let it blend in more, but like combat there still needs to be a "roll initiative" moment (which might as well be rolling initiative or some skill to serve as initiative) where you know that you're in a Skill Challenge, and having a solid structure for that as you learn the game is immensely helpful and rewarding as a player.

    \n I think the preference for explicit or implicit Skill Challenges has a lot to do with how you're used to roleplay. As in, it has a lot to do with when you see "an opening for you to contribute to the game". I have played with people who followed a very clear and almost strict turn/speaking order even when they were roleplaying freely. They responded only to specific, game-related cues in the fiction and would otherwise either stay quiet or fall back on out-of-game chatter. It works very well for them. Most of the time I'm playing with people who carry on roleplaying and making decision for and with their characters regardless of whether the GM has given them an explicit situation or cue to respond to. It's simply something people grow accustomed to.

    The people I game with fall into the latter category, so implicit Skill Challenges would fit into our roleplaying habits much better. An explicit Skill Challenges would feel intrusive and somehow constraining. So while we'd still use Skill Challenges, we prefer not to draw attention to the mechanics and structures involved.

    \n I used the formal, "Now for a skill challenge" method of play the other night in the game I ran, and it worked very well, since the number of rolls acted much like hit points do (as a pacing mechanism). I used a simplified blend of the 4ed errata and Stalker0's mod, and the degree of "tactical" choice as well as the flexibility of description (e.g., a wizard player escaping a lava-like flow of concentrated evil by using Arcana to recognize the evil rune the flow was spelling out in gargantuan form, and so knowing where the safest spot was) enabled thereby made those portions of the game very satisfying and very enjoyable.

    Now what we need are rules for incorporating at-will, encounter, and daily powers into skill challenges, and "combat challenge" rules that allow for the same kind of narrative freedom as the skill challenges...

    \n Stalker0's system isn't much harder than the one in the book, I found. Yeah, it looks more complicated on paper, but I think that's due to average (not poor) technical writing.

    In my own words, it looks like this: Run a typical skill challenge but use Stalker0's chart instead of the one in the book. You can designate certain skills as "helpful" (+3 to aid another instead of +2), "secondary" (you need to "unlock" them with another skill check first), or "bold" (get bonuses in certain situations). Pick one player each lap around the table as the "guiding light," and they get to Aid Another with some special benefits. Spend healing surges for a +2 or +3 bonus to checks. If you're about to fail your last check ("time of trials"), another player can make a "bold recovery" and save everyone's bacon.

    Most of those rules can be ignored until they're useful or relevant to play. Just run it as a normal skill challenge with a guiding light. As DM, manage the helpful, secondary, and bold skills quietly and behind the scenes. When it's the time of trials and the players fail, offer the bold recovery.

    I've run it once and it was a LOT of fun and very tense.
