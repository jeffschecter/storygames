    Posted By: JoelI'm finding that I'm learning a lot about giving effective reviews, both by comparing my reviews last year with those of others, and by observing the reviews I receive for my own games. I think this is a skill that transfers beyond Game Chef.
    \nOh, for sure. It's very similar to being in a writing group or class with a peer-review process. I feel like I learned a ton about writing by being forced to be critical about other people's work. It's easy to say "I like that" or "I don't like that," but it's not always as easy to know why a piece of writing makes you feel the way you do. Having to drill into that is a valuable experience. I've been careful in my reviews to separate what might not be for me from what I see as actual problems, and finding that there's always a line between the two.

    (Not that this stops me from feeling like an asshole, BTW.)

    \n I really appreciate JWalt's guidelines for review writing: \n
    I\u2019ve reviewed a LOT of contest games over the years (more than 100, certainly) and, while I haven\u2019t always done an equally great job on every review, these are the principles I try to more-or-less stick to, which I think have served me well and are among the most helpful things for designers to hear.

    1. Try to avoid reading other reviewers\u2019 comments on the game before reading it yourself. Be your own reviewer!

    2. Read the games in a cooperative spirit, not like a harsh English teacher looking for flaws, but as someone who\u2019s potential interested in playing the game with their friends. If you can, print the games out and take notes on the print-outs as you go, so you\u2019ll have a record of your initial reactions.

    3. When you\u2019re ready to write a paragraph or two of feedback, start off by mentioning what you like about the game, what excites you, what the designer has done well. In addition to what you would normally look for in a game, consider how well the theme and ingredients were incorporated, how much the designer was able to accomplish with a limited wordcount, and how brave the designer has been in attempting new or difficult things.

    4. Then, talk about the parts of the game that you don\u2019t think are clear, that you don\u2019t fully understand, or that you feel uncertain about. Think more about how you would present this game to your friends or organize a session of play to try the game out. Do you have the information and instructions you need to do that? What clarifications or additional tools might you need? Try to ask questions or admit your own confusion instead of criticizing: \u201cI felt unsure about the part where\u2026\u201d or \u201cWhat did you intend this part to do?\u201d

    5. End with a few positive thoughts about the game as a whole and how it could move forward to be even better or more \u201cfinished\u201d (whatever that means). Is it ready to be tried out? Does it just need a couple things before it\u2019s ready to hit the table? What\u2019s the minimum amount of work that the designer needs to do to get it to the next level? Don\u2019t list a bunch of things or talk too abstractly. Suggest one or two concrete steps or improvements.
    \nThis really helps me hit the points that will be most useful for the designers, and put comments in the context of what would help the game, not "what I like."

    \n
    \n Yeah, I would be interested to hear what people think about my guidelines AFTER they've had the experience of reviewing and being reviewed. I find that it's really easy to complain about stuff you don't like or don't understand, but it's more useful to ask for clarification and admit to your own confusion without doing anything that looks like it's assigning blame. Also, next steps in a big one. It's easy to look at a whole batch of feedback and be overwhelmed at all the new work you have to do. But if people can point out one or two places where you could start chipping away, that's pretty great.
