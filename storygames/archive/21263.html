    \n I know a few people who, similarly, have a low emotional threshold like that. Some I know intimately, but obviously we don't know whether yours is a similar psychological case to theirs, so please don't take my commentary as claiming anything about you - the best we can do is reflect upon similar-sounding experiences.

    What I am immediately reminded of is one horrifying My Life with Master session years ago, where a particular player broke down in tears due to the game content being too stressful for them. This was directly caused by, I think, the same phenomenon you describe: the player is on board with the game's idea, but they experience the events of the game so strongly that the unpleasant experiences are too much for them. MLwM is a horror game where the GM is basically in charge of playing an abusive parent or lover to the player's character, so something like this is quite horrible when the creative development is not arrested in time.

    In my experience being sensitive like that isn't a mental "problem" so much as a feature of how you interact with the world, and perhaps - perhaps - a lack of experience in recognizing and handling your own emotions. The player in that anecdote was and is what one might term emotionally unstable or "fragile" - easy to excite, prone to emotional judgement, easy to bully, and so on. In their particular case I feel comfortable saying that my experiences with their bad bleeds are due to a combination of being a sensitive, empathetic person, and not really having the wisdom tools and emotional maturity for handling negative emotions.

    Finnish culture features a baseline of stoic machismo in what it expects (of both men and women, although particularly of men). This means that when (particularly male) roleplayers get emotionally uncomfortable, they may crash hard, because everybody is by default inclined against expressing their discomfort and dealing with it through social processes; instead of telling the other players when they feel uncomfortable, your average Finnish roleplayer ignores or hides their own feelings, because they've internalized the idea that expressing discomfort is childish and selfish. It is noble to suffer without complaining. This obviously means that Finns, taken as a cultural stereotype, do not know how to come down gently from emotional stresses (or physical challenges for that matter); you're either stronger than the challenge, or you break upon it, and if you do break, better make it fucking good, or others will think that you're soft. Preferable mode of expression for how bad it hurts inside is, on a certain unspoken level, to kill yourself - if it's not bad enough for that, it's not bad enough to complain about.

    The above description is relevant as a background for you to reflect on: does your own local social culture have similar features? How easy is it for you to talk about your creative experiences and the feelings they elicit while playing? Finns don't have any more problems than anybody else with positive emotions, but nobody wants to be a killjoy, so they'll bottle up the anxiety. How is it with your culture, and you personally? Are you allowed to think of yourself in terms of being an emotional being, or are you supposed to be more of a noble machine?

    The reason I ask is that I think that the way to handling your negative bleed is to grow more comfortable with your own feelings. This is a useful life skill in general, and my own experience growing up has been that the reason I can handle scary movies, intense roleplaying experiences, arguing with loved ones and other similar emotional stresses is that I've grown to understand myself and my emotions pretty well. In theory I'm capable of calling a stop to a game that threatens my mental equilibrium, but in practice I don't actually even need to, because the simple fact of being aware of my own emotions and being able to verbalize them does much to rob them of their power: if a game feels scary, it's generally speaking "good scary" because I can self-reflect on the emotion, and essentially, choose on a moment-to-moment basis to partake of the emotion or not.

    More on this angle of approach later, perhaps, if it seems useful. Others will no doubt have good points on this as well. I don't think that you're alone with this experience, really; most of the actually inspired and excited roleplayers I know have similarly strong emotional experiences with gaming. We take this shit really seriously, and that sometimes means getting upset by it. That's why we fight in the Internet all the time, too.

    \n It also helps a lot, when playing intense games like these, to establish a "cool down" period after the game. If you have to get out of the host's house by 11 PM, then resist the temptation to keep playing right up till 10:59. Stop at 10:15 and go into a different room, away from the character sheets and dice, and sit and talk about the game in meta terms. Talk about how you're feeling. Hug!

    Search for "I will not abandon you" as a term here on Story Games to find some more thoughts on one way to handle hard-hitting emotional role-playing.

    \n I suggest trying to change your focus from seeing things from behind you character's eyes to trying to create a great story. Become the director rather than then embodiment of your character. Play your character constant with their personality, motivations and so on but for the sake of the story not them. Perhaps, challenge yourself by playing characters that you don't relate to or emphasize with. Don't try to "win" with you character, let go. Focus on building on other's ideas and bring story threads together so that you can have a great coherent and satisfying ending. Focus on the verisimilitude of the story. Perhaps, by changing your focus you can enjoy the game but in a different way.

    \n Great posts here, very thought-provoking. As another comment:

    Sometimes feeling hurt by what's happening to your character isn't so much about the terrible nature of the fictional situation, but about a feeling of being alone and unsupported - it feels like no one's on your side.

    If that's the case, then you might find it will greatly improve your experience of you can cultivate a culture of taking about the characters and the game (basically, as Eero and Adam say above).

    Something as simple as the other players saying, "oh, poor Jenny! Your character really got the short end of the stick in this session. I just can't wait to see her come out of it stronger than ever! She deserves better!" can make all the difference in terms of your experience.

    If that sounds right to you, you can start by sharing this with your fellow players and then modelling the behaviour you'd like to see (until it "catches on").

    \n@Edgargso , this is going to sound judgmental, and I'm sorry about that, but here it is.

    You didn't actually answer my question about what your relationship is like away from the game table. I'm going to guess that that means there isn't one.

    The more I read your posts, the more convinced I am that your "good vibes at the table" are an illusion. You are more willing to bare a deep personal issue to a bunch of strangers on the internet than you are to share it with your (theoretically) close friends; friends who are supposed to be your collaborators in the experience.

    I realize we are expert strangers but even so.

    In this discussion we've passed from "I'm having strong feelings during play," through "my feelings are hurt because the other players are ganging up on me and not taking the game as seriously as I do," to "I can't discuss this with them because it will Ruin Their Fun (TM)." That is a textbook case of serious social disfunction. It's not a gaming issue. We can't fix it for you with technical advice. There are only a few things that you can do:

    1 - You can carry on as things are and continue to suffer.

    2 - You can leave the group.

    3 - You can give up the kind of "deep feelings" play you like and approach the game casually like the rest of the group does.

    4 - You can trust the rest of the group enough to talk to them about it and see if they're open a more serious style of play. They may surprise you. Maybe there are others in the group who are, as you are, dissatisfied with the game, but, like you, don't want to be the "squeaky wheel" and Ruin The Fun (TM) for everyone else. If they don't surprise you... well, options 2 and 3 are still available.

    But you cannot get the kind of play you want with a group of people who do not share your aims. It leads only, as you have discovered, to pain.

    Do you happen to know what other kinds of games (besides Monsterhearts) this group has had experience with? I feel like they would benefit from a really tightly-run educational series of In a Wicked Age sessions.

    \n@Edgargso to echo some of what is being said here already, you do need to talk to your fellow players about what's happening to you. Part of this kind of play is that you need to be honest about your experience with the players, before you can bring emotional honesty into your character.
