\n For those of you who have been generously playtesting Technoir, I wanted to let you know that I've posted a new version of the beta on the website.

There's been a lot that's been reworked and rewritten\u2014especially the Contention chapter\u2014so I invite you to give everything a fresh look.

There are certainly some major rules changes (3 Push kills are gone), but a lot of it is a more about framing why and when you roll dice and how that affects the fiction.

Thank you to everyone who has provided comments here at Story-Games. I think the text is much stronger for your feedback.

EDIT: And if I've missed anyone in the playtesting credits (or misspelled their name), please email me with their names at jeremy at cellar-games dot com.

    Posted By: Ice Cream EmperorIs there a list anywhere of the major mechanical changes? We just started our game two weeks ago but I am not sure if I will have a chance to compare & contrast the new document before our next session. (Or if I could do so productively, ie. notice what is a change and what is not.)
    \nHere are the big things that I can think of off the top of my head:

    \u2022 Objects themselves can be tapped with Push dice. This is because a few of the basic function tags like melee and short range have gone away. Tags now describe how the object is different from other objects in the same category. This makes many items cheaper than they were previously.

    \u2022 I removed the 3 Push dice kill, the rules for being forced-out, and giving-in.

    \u2022 I allow you to give henchmen adjectives like unconscious or dead, but not protagonists, heavies, or connections: you'll need to focus on more creative solutions for getting what you want rather than eliminating your enemies.

    \u2022 There's a random chance that, after the scene is over, adjectives that describe physical damage could lead to the character gaining a sticky dying adjective or a locked dead adjective. Both can be recovered from. See the Lethal Consequences section in the Contention chapter.

    \u2022 I've added rules for affecting multiple targets with one roll, ignoring inappropriate negative adjectives, interfering with actions against targets who aren't you, and applying adjectives without rolling when actions aren't opposed. See the Action Options section.

    And there's several things throughout that are clarified, made more explicit, and framed better. You shouldn't need to remake characters at all\u2014just decide if you want to give them Kreds back for any tags their objects might have that have been removed.

    \nI don't know if that's necessarily true. Whoever attacks first has the advantage of setting the narrative tone and shaping the situation. If I give you the fleeting adjective of grabbed, now you have to choose between using your action to remove grabbed or do something that still respects grabbed (which wouldn't be making me lost at that point).

    Also, I think the mechanical advantage usually goes to the attacker as well. Base verb ratings, for your best stats, tend to hover around 3 or 4. Because it's a keep highest dice roll, once you get 4 or more dice, you can usually count on a 5 or 6. Bringing Push into play, defenders can push their reaction ratings up to 6 or higher if they save their Push for defense, but that iron defense isn't a tactic that sees much play unless someone manages to hoard 5 or more Push dice. \n
    (Also, we have to come to agreement about who has to do what. Do you have to succeed at inflictingcorneredon me or I get away? Or do I have to succeed at inflictingloston you in order to lose the tail? Both make sense. Who's the primary actor? Who decides? "It depends entirely on the specifics of the fiction," is an acceptable answer. But examples to cover cases like this would be good. )
    \nI think you need to start with a base assumption that the chasers are always keeping up with the runners, and the runners are always just barely out of reach of the chasers. The adjectives modify that base assumption. And then when those adjectives are removed, they return us back to that base assumption. So, if I fail at making you cornered, the base assumption is still in effect\u2014you're still just out of my reach. If you make me lost, that's when you've gotten away. I'd love to do some full start-to-finish examples of chases and other conflicts with the system, but I'm thinking that's a series of blog posts leading to a free pdf type of thing.

    \n I'm curious what the rationale was behind removing the ability to mechanically kill people in a straightforward (if costly) way -- particularly the ability of PCs to kill NPCs. As it stands there is no way to guarantee someone's death within the contention rules -- though presumably I could shoot someone in the head in some fiction-only way if they have no ability to resist, or something of that sort.

    I'm concerned about adopting this change for our group, because we are using the rules to run something closer to a military espionage game than a typical cyberpunk story -- and in general, when it comes to high-stakes espionage, counter-terrorism, and the like, people die a lot. It's important to be able to kill or incapacitate people quickly -- or at least, as quickly as is reasonably possible in some parallel to reality. Having to inflict four to six negative physical attributes on someone in the hope that they will then start dying -- at which point if you are the only person around you could just kill them, I assume -- seems extremely laborious.

    It also seems gravely unsuited to any genre where someone can at any moment step out of the shadows and kill a crucial informant moments before he is about to reveal something important -- or where you might want to do the same to the man or woman who is about to betray you. Noir and cyberpunk both tend to include a lot of sudden, swift, unexpected death -- the above scenario is nearly clich\xe9 -- so I'm curious if you see that sort of action as still-present in TechNoir, and if so how you would go about implementing it in the rules? Does NPC vs. NPC murder simply happen, as suddenly as I want it to?

    If a PC discovers that their Connection, Jamal, has betrayed them and is about to sell them out to the big corporation, and when they arrive at the scene of the info-drop they see Jamal walking up to three men in suits, about to spill his guts... maybe they want to shoot him in the back of the head? And if they do, what does that look like, mechanically? I would be tempted to run it as an unopposed skill roll that simply results in Jamal's death, but that seems incredibly easy.

    And what if instead they confront Jamal before he gets there, but their words fall on deaf ears, and so after some arguing back and forth they decide that the only solution is to kill Jamal, here and now, before he sells them out. Do they really have to stand there and shoot him for six to twelve subsequent rounds of contention, then wait and make sure he rolled at least one 6 and nobody is coming to help him out? Jamal is just a shopkeeper, and they're a bad-ass with a flechette gun -- surely they hold his life in their hands, in some more direct way than that? Should the GM simply employ fiat to determine that the PC can kill him easily, or what?

    \n My reason was that it seemed to be one of the elements that was distracting readers from the core of system. My players and I rarely used it in our own games. Yet, it was this big concern that kept coming up in the playtesting reports. It just became easier for me to take it out rather than try to explain it as a last resort.

    Feel free to add it back in as a hack if you like. I just think if you use that option too often, you're missing out on the game's sweet-spot.

    Note that you can still one-shot kill henchmen. You can give them dead as a sticky or locked adjective.

    And, yes, NPC vs. NPC death should happen just as you want\u2014offscreen or onscreen\u2014no dice rolls or Push dice spending needed.
