\n We\u2019ve tried a number of approaches to the lack of a gender-neutral third person pronoun. In every case, we\u2019ve attempted not to draw attention to our technique, that is, those people who are highly attuned to what they perceive as political correctness do not notice, while still attaining non-sexist language. My preferred approach, using singular \u201cthey\u201d is therefore not acceptable, despite antecedents such as Shakespeare and Austen. Using the plural sometimes works, but can be tricky when you really want to have a singular person. You can also name individuals and then refer to their gender, though with players and characters and the GM, it becomes rather complex. Another approach is that the GM is female, the players, male, and the second person.

What techniques do you recommend which fulfil these criteria?

    \n One of my physics professors told a story about this sort of thing. He was part of a group (men and women) that was designing a physics test for high school students. One of the questions involved a boy and a girl carrying a heavy metal object (basically a long pipe) and the point of the question was that one of them did more work than the other, based on how each carried the object. The committee fretted about how to set up this question. Do they show the girl doing less work (because it shows she is smart, knows physics, and is using it to her advantage)? Do they show the girl doing more work (because it shows she can outperform the boy physically)? They got into heated arguments about it.

    Eventually, not able to reach a real resolution, they decided that they would just change the position of the boy and girl each year, so that, if anyone objected to the the girl doing more or less work, they could tell them of their dilemma and their compromise solution. He showed slides of the problem they eventually published.

    According to the professor, they only ever got one complaint about the problem: an objection that they showed the girl walking behind the boy.

    The lesson I took away from this was, perhaps, not the one the professor intended to convey when telling the story, because when he showed the slide of the problem, I was struck by something else: the problem itself sucked. It was horribly worded and a bit ambiguous.

    The moral of the story is: spend your energy making your text legible and coherent. If you spend any time worrying about gender-based language, make it about how you can use gender to make your text more clear (as Daniel's "he and she to refer to two interacting players" does).

    \n For RPG books, my favorite solution of the ones I've seen implemented is to refer to the GM consistently as a she and the player as a he.

    If you're going to have to describe conflicts between players, set up an ongoing example. Susan is the GM. Joe is the player she interacts with by default. And Kari is the other player that Joe sometimes comes into conflict with.

    When referring to characters instead of players, you can make the choice to always use the character name and never the pronoun.

    Also, depending on what you want to encourage, have 1 or 2 of your sample players crossplaying - especially have a male player playing a female character. Because, honestly, I think that's the biggest taboo left in tabletop gaming (and much of society in general) and, personally, I'd like to see it subverted at every turn.

    \n I know this isn't what you're asking, but here's what the AP Stylebook says,\n
    Do not presume maleness in constructing a sentence, but use the pronoun his when an indefinite antecedent may be male or female. A reporter tries to protect his sources. (Not his or her sources, but not the use of the word reporter rather than newsman.)
    Frequently, however, the best choice is a slight revision of the sentence: Reporters try to protect their sources.
    \nIt's English, so I, personally, don't believe using a male pronoun makes a text sexist, but using gendered words when a neutral one exists might.

    I think alternating pronouns works better if you want to address this. As a reader, I find alternating pronouns from paragraph to paragraph confusing. Alternating from section to section works better for me. Second person works the best for me.

    \n From the Oxford English Dictionary:\n
    What should you do in sentences such as these?

    If your child is thinking about a gap year, ? can get good advice from this website.
    A researcher has to be completely objective in ? findings.

    In the past, people tended to use the pronouns he, his, him, or himself in situations like this:

    If your child is thinking about a gap year, he can get good advice from this website.
    A researcher has to be completely objective in his findings.

    Today, this approach is seen as outdated and sexist. There are other options which allow you to arrive at a \u2018gender-neutral\u2019 solution.

    \n I don't really have a stake in this, English not being my language in any emotional sense, but it seems to me that there aren't any better solutions than "they". It's what I'd use if I had a horse in this race. (In practice I seem to often use "he", but that's just because that's how I've been taught, and as a non-native I don't truly have any gut-level hang-ups about this, so I don't think about it most of the time.) Some people will complain, perhaps, but it's more important to be right than to be popular, it seems to me. Besides, the complaints are not based on any objective reasoning anyway, so it's just a matter of getting used to the logical usage over time. The more people use the gender-neutral option, the less it'll bother people. Women are not exactly going to go away, or at least I hope they won't, so English-users will basically have to spend the next eternity struggling with this, or they need to change their language to enable gender-neutral expression. Not even a choice in the long run if you ask me.

    I discard the artificial constructs ranging from avoidance of third person singular to assigning arbitrary genders as unworthy of quality writing: write the truth and write for the ages, and resolve this issue in the language you are committed to. It's a losing strategy to try to write your way around the issue if that causes you to write in a difficult or hilariously artificial manner. Like, consider that practice of setting arbitrary genders to imaginary people who are place-holders of the generic reader/player whose gender is actually indefinite and irrelevant - how is that elegant and skillful writing? When faced with new needs that will persist over time, adapt the language - the coming generations will thank you for it.

    In other words, I don't know that Simon's conundrum has an solution in the English language, so "they" remains the least bad of the imperfect solutions. It's actually almost acceptable even for the unnoticeability criterion, as many folks won't particularly notice it anymore. There is no solution that somebody won't notice, so the mere existence of people who'll complain can't be cause for dismissing the best solution.\n

    \n Two points

    The first, to give priority to your actual question: In Blood Red Sands I did quite a bit of "As the GM, you ", "as the Player, you "
    I tried to use You as much as possible. Also, use specific examples where it is clear what gender the person you are talking about is. Its perfectly PC to say "He" when "He" refers to Jim, and to say "She" when she refers to Jill without being hokey, awkward, or non-grammatical.

    The second, I advise avoiding framing not only this question but also the way you're thinking about this issue as "sexist vs. non-sexist"
    For me I didn't use "you" in BRS as an attempt to avoid sexist language. I'm totally not interested in jumping into that mess of "bigotry vs. political correctness" nonsense. I did it because it makes rules texts much more personal and interesting to read...more engaging and less like a text book. I wrote Universalis like a text book...I really like it (I also like reading text books) most did not. Saying "you " is much more interesting to read than "the GM " AND has the added benefit of avoiding the whole He/She thing altogether.

    So my advice is to write in a way that sounds like you the author are excited about what you're writing...cuz that's way better than trying to write in a way that seeks to avoid offense and winds up boring.
