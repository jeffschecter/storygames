    \n\nSeptember 2009 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Liera:
    \n
    \nLee + 'e' in 'fear' + rah
    \n
    \nExcept the 'r' is a "rolling" 'r', as in scottish (?). And the 'ah' is short, not drawn-out. Emphasis is on the 'e'; three syllables.
    \n
    \nNytt:
    \n
    \nThe 'n' is like in English, but the 'y' sound isn't in the English language. You make it by saying 'ee' and pouting your lips. The sound is short. And then, y'know, 'tt'.
    \n
    \n---
    \n
    \nWhat makes it sound grammatically awkward is that the verb is usually used together with a "sig" and a "med", meaning "oneself" and "with", respectively. Just like in English, you say "ally oneself with something", not "ally something", which is what it says now. "Ally new", literally, except that "ally" can't be a noun here, and that "new" must refer to a something, not a someone (but that's just cool).
    \n
    \nHowever, you could make a case that it's part of a longer phrase, with the "sig" and "med" somewhere else. In modern Swedish, you'd put it right after more or less always, but in modern Swedish, you wouldn't use the word at all (you'd use "Alliera", the modern version). In older Swedish, you could play around more with the structure, and you could say "Med sig liera nytt", which would mean "ally oneself with something new". That'd totally work in 1913 or so.
    \n
    \nActually, I'm beginning to warm up to the name. As a title, it'd work, I think (since you've got some artistic freedom in titles). To me, since the "sig" isn't there, it suggests that you're not allying yourself with something, but rather that you're creating alliances between others. It could be a succint way of saying "forging new alliances".
    \n
    \nYeah, I'll stop raving about it now. Language is a favorite subject of mine, and it's not common to get to talk about Swedish to foreigners.
