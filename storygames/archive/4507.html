    Posted By: Bret Gillan(I notice a discrepancy between me and Judd's character creation. I know that Resources are Physical or Mental, but are they also Offensive or Defensive like Traits?)
    \nAh. Yes they are. To summarize:

    Traits are used in Maneuvers. They can always be used in any description, and they give bonuses if they're the right domain and have been activated. Offensive traits for offensive maneuvers and denying resources, defensive traits for defensive maneuvers and resisting resource denials or recovering a resource. All of the active ones of the same type (all activated PO's, frex) stack.

    Resources are used in Strikes. They can always be used in any description, and the highest offensive one of a particular type adds to your damage rolls while the highest defensive one subtracts from damage rolls against you. So your highest PO resource adds to your damage roll in physical conflicts, and your highest PD resource subtracts from an opponent's damage roll against you in physical conflicts. If your highest resource of a particular type is denied, the next highest one (if you've got another one) applies. They do not stack.

    Posted By: Bret GillanI'm interested in how hunts tie into story goals, and how you can pursue an adventure without a hunt. It seems, well, undesirable. That tattoo is a powerful lure. But from the book it seems as though you can pursue a hunt without a critter at the end if you want to achieve something in the narrative. Is this right?
    \nOops, overlooked that one. Yes, instead of "fight this beast and gain the tattoo," your adventure could be "kill off the blackskull tribe" or "retrieve your tribe's spiritual totem from the ruins of Ysvey." Or whatever. You might hit a point when the beasts are just too powerful for you to go straight from one to the next; doing a different adventure is one way to gain reward points and beef up in the meantime. Also, if you're just plain more interested in following a story element that's come up in your game, you can do that. Oh, and because there are few multiplayer beasts out there (two as of now), it's a way to have a "regular" adventure with more than two players.

    Also: I assume you guys haven't used Secondary Goals yet. Let me know when/if you do and how it goes; it tends to add a new layer to the experience.

    \n You know, I haven't mentioned it yet outside of my Livejournal, but my favorite thing about the game is how it's wired into the rules that the player can give a solution so good the GM can just say, "You know what? That makes sense. I give." I feel like that's not encouraged in enough games.

    I mean, say yes or roll the dice is used to refer to conflicts that nobody cares about, but what about conflicts where the players have such a badass idea that you're stunned? And by wiring that into the rules, it encourages me as the hunter to try and come up with some really really good ideas

    In other games, it doesn't matter what your idea is, you still gotta roll and go to the numbers. Maybe you'll get a bonus, but feh.
