    Actually, the discrete approach is tricky. Ultimately, you'd like to find the root set of basic groups, since you can then multiply by (2 * 3! * 4!) to get the complete set of squares. That's about 288, not allowing for duplicate rows which have to be factored back out.

    Since we're dealing with permutable rows and columns, we need only find a single example of each kind, and really we only really need to know the possible numbers that sum to the correct values.

    For the top and bottom rows, we need at most 4 numbers that sum to 4, so the possibilities are 4x1, 2x1+2, 2x2, and 4.
    The the middle rows, we need 4 numbers that sum to 8: 2x1+2+4, 2x2+4, and 2x4
    For columns, we need 5 numbers that sum to 8: 4x1+4, 2x1+2+4, 2x2+4, and 2x4

    If top and bottom are 4x1, then the middle rows have to produce either 2x1+4 or 2+4 in 3-place columns - 114 24 - so the only possible middle rows are: (2x4, 2x1+2+4, 2x1+2+4).

    Ideally there'd be a way to compute the number of middle row sets based on the top and bottom, but at present I'm at a loss as how to do that except by brute force. Trivially, there are 8 pairings of top and bottom rows, and I can't see any pairing to exclude. The question then becomes: for a pair of top and bottom rows, how many sets of legitimate middle rows exist? My intuition is that the number tends to be low - 2+-2, say. In which case I'd estimate the number of row sets to be about 20.

    That being said, I'd make an estimate of 4000 possible grids to satisfy the problem's peculiar constraints. Still a trifle high.

    Jason, here's a question for you: I'm still not sure I see how the grid maps to a story. What would the effect on the story be if I swap two of the middle rows, the top and bottom row or any of the first 4 columns? It may be that a certain set of canonical grids is what you want, possibly with the option to swap rows and columns around...

    Heya,

    I was passenger for a long drive today, so I took this problem with me. I didn't manage to count the number of possible variations, but I think I managed to come up with a way of randomising them.

    First up, here are the possible combinations for splitting the 8 points in a scene:

    A - 0,0,0,4,4
    B - 0,0,2,2,4
    C - 0,1,1,2,4
    D - 1,1,1,1,4
    E - 0,2,2,2,2
    F - 1,1,2,2,2

    First thing to notice is that F can never happen, as FLY has to be a 0 or 4. For the same reason, D can only happen when FLY is 4, and E can only happen when FLY is 0.

    So, get yourself 4 different cards:

    A - 0,0,0,4,4
    B - 0,0,2,2,4
    C - 0,1,1,2,4
    D - 1,1,1,1,4 (INSERTION or EXTRACTION) or 0,2,2,2,2 (any other scene)

    For the first round, pick a card, assign 4 to FLY, and either assign the other scores randomly or someone gets to pick. Return the card to the pile and shuffle.
    For the second round, do the same, except you assign 0 to FLY.
    For the third round, do the same except you cannot allow the total for any "column" to exceed 8. So if DESTROY got a 4 in the first round, and a 1 in the second round, it cannot get a 4 in the third round. I think it is possible to do this without having to redraw the card, but if not, then you redraw.
    For the 4th round, same again, except the totals for all of the columns (SUPPRESS, DESTROY, EVADE, DETECT) must add up to either 4,6,7 or 8 so that the last round can be legal. This may require you to discard you initial draw and draw again. I am fairly sure (but have not proved mathematically) that there will be at least one legal choice.
    For the final round, add 4 to FLY, and whatever is requires to the other "columns" to bring them up to 8.

    I've also checked the outine, and I've got a question for Jason: how do you determine if the mission succeeds or fails? Or is this not important (for example, the mission is FUBARed automatically, and the game is all about the interaction between the characters)?

    Jason,

    If mission success is judged scene-by-scene, you need to impose more constraints on the Adversary each turn. As it stands, the correct play is always to spend adversity points at the lowest possible multipliers and match these to the group's strengths; unless you are the Traitor, in which case you want to hit the party with a 4x multiplier at the most appropriate moment. This isn't very interesting strategy.

    What if you were to have a much larger challenge deck, with each card containing a specific random challenge (eg 2 point EVADE)? The adversary has to draw a certain number of cards, and choose to play all but one or two of them. The discards are not revealed to the other players. Non-Traitors will want to discard high numbers, or challenges where the group are weak. The Traitor will want to do the opposite, but still keep their head down. If the Traitor betrays the group, ALL cards are played.

    This may work even better if the group gets to pick one of their number as the "advance scout" who gets to draw. Then the Traitor's job is to appear trustworthy enough to get the majority of scouting draws, until they get a high-danger hand to dump on the group.

    This is quite a way from your original set up, but I want to give you some ideas for incorporating less transparency into the game. I think this is the key to a successful game with an inquisitorial "find the traitor" mechanic.
