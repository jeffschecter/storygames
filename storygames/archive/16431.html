\n This is what started as a improvised Lady Blackbird hack for Shadowrun. I had promised to make the translation from French around March and never got around to it - for which I apologize.

Original purpose was to introduce old players to new mechanics and new players to an old settings. I took many liberties, but overall everyone seemed to have a blast the various times I ran it.

Next I'll need to expand on narrative power for the side-lined players. Whenever I have time to play again.

Anyway, hope this'll prove interesting to some.

    \n I had meant to comment on this earlier (like, several days ago), but kept getting distracted by other things (aka work).

    Overall, I like what is going on with this, though I had to read through the whole thing twice to really understand who was connected or conspiring with whom. I think I'd be willing to use the characters and the basic premise to run a game, though the whole "every 45 minutes someone dies" thing is...well...hard to take. While in a way, yeah, it adds a certain amount of suspense when K'ien blows up, and there's the danger of everyone else dying a similar death hanging over their heads, but having it be so set in stone doesn't really make me want to use that rule. I would probably have some separate condition track that measures how close an individual is to exploding, that the players could then work against if necessary.

    I do like the separate Fatigue and Tracer condition tracks.

    \n There's an old running joke in my gaming circles that goes: "If M. is planning a one-shot, schedule your next year of gaming for it"... (I'm only really good for long, detailed structures I guess).

    So this time, I had 5 hours, six players, and I designed the whole thing so it would end at the counts of those five hours, whatever happened. Probably the only one-shot ever I can finish in one session. Amusingly enough, it seems most players I ran it to enjoyed that. And that's about the only reason for this rule.

    What interest me most - and has been taking most of my gaming prep-time since - is the realization that some players were having more fun as side-line ghosts than they were as characters. Really need to work on that one. Audience Play vs Character Play.

    Anyway, thanks a lot for the comments (oh and having re-read the documents since, sorry for the typoes and questionable English).

    \n Check out Always/Never/Now - it's a cyberpunk adventure using the LB rules. It uses pregens, but they might give you some inspiration if you're looking to make your own, and the game itself is a lot of fun.

    Generally, you're looking at 4 traits with 6 tags each, plus 3 unlockable tags per trait. Balancing the numbers isn't hard, but balancing the versatility of what the tags and traits allow each character to do requires a lot more thought and is greatly helped by playtesting.

    \n Yeah, in general, the care and feeding of LB traits is a delicate business.

    For example, Naomi's Pit Fighter trait should have a big red DANGER sign around it. In any combat situation, she gets 7 dice almost all of the time. That's cool in Lady Blackbird, because it represents that Naomi is just so incredibly badass that she just breaks everything. But it means that it's hard to challenge her dice-wise in combat situations. (It's easy to challenge her fiction wise, because she has to protect Natasha, and she's got her key of vengeance, and she's just too awesome to be on one page alone.)
