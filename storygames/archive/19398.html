\n I was trying to make me a table that will randomize different types of scenes. I know there are initial scenes, climatic scenes, and final scenes but those are only done once in a given story / adventure. The others below may be done multiple times. I also made a rule that two of the same type of scene should not be next to each other (that means I will not have a dramatic scene right after having played out a dramatic scene for example.) What I have so far is below.

Investigation Scene = Characters looking around an area or gathering clues and information.
Dramatic Scene = The most common scene. Characters interaction, dialogue, and emotional conflict. Characters coming head-to-head. Put something at stake.
Recognition Scene = The character finds out something important.
Action Scene = All about action, reaction, and physical movement. It can be combat (harm, delay, or put an end to something), escape (trying to get away, avoid, or hide), or pursuit (follow, capture, or secure something).
Unexpected Visitor - Someone unexpected shows up. Problems arise.
Meanwhile / Revelation Scene = We, the players, find out something important that the characters probably may not know about. A remote location and does not involve the characters being present. This should be immediately randomized as a twist and kept quite short.
Flashback Scene = This should focus on the following: action, information, and character interaction. The information contained in the flashback must have some bearing on the main story. Use when the past directly affects the main story. Use flashbacks to deepen the understanding of a character.

Questions:
1 - Should any of the above scene types be explained different? Do you have suggestions if so?
2 - Do you know of a scene type that I did not mention that would be good for an RPG?
3 - Any suggestions or thoughts?

    \n What is this for?

    The first thing that comes to mind is that these "types" seem like they come from different... families.

    For example, Investigation Scene and Dramatic Scene tell us about the nature of the action within the scene. However, Dramatic Scenes are a very basic staple of (almost) any type of fiction. Whereas Investigation Scenes seem to me to be somewhat more genre-specific. (Or at least shouldn't appear as often in, say, a comedy sitcom, as they would in a detective novel.)

    In contrast, Flashback Scene and Meanwhile Scene seem to be types of scene frames: "Now, we're watching a flashback." It doesn't tell us anything about the content of the scene itself: presumably a Flashback scene could have Dramatic, Investigation, or Action (etc.) content, no?

    I'd say there's a big difference between the frame of the scene (how the scene opens), its main "meat" (the content), and its ending/transition. For example, I can envision a scene opening as a Flashback, including Investigation for most of its duration, and ending with an Unexpected Visitor (or some Action - "And then a shot rang out and Simon dropped to the floor, lifeless. Everyone scrambled for cover!").

    I don't if that's helpful, but it's my first reaction to this! It's definitely a good start, and a thought-provoking list.

    \n The fact that this is Solo play helped to crystallize my thoughts as well as Paul_T's observations. Maybe there's something here you can use. There's a school of thought that in concise, tight fiction a scene must Focus on either (or both):
    Drama: which progresses Character.
    Conflict: which progresses Plot.

    Anything else hits the cutting room floor. (Conan punches a camel because he's a Barbarian hopped-up on black lotus; not because the camel presented a threat).

    Each scene has at least one important Element (an essential person, place, object or event/relationship). Think Chekhov's Gun. It must be there for a reason. But why? That is the Reveal.

    In this sense, every scene is an investigation scene. We (the audience) must learn something new about an Element or be introduced to a new Element. This Reveal happens through Drama or Conflict.

    A Scene is framed in time (Now or Then) and place (Here or There) in relation to the main character. Here/There can also be thought of as being immediately connected to the main character, as in Me/Mine or to someone else as in Them/Theirs. So:

    Scene Type --------------Time--------- Placement

    Flashback/Memory    ...........Then..................Here (directly connected to Me/Mine)

    Exposition/Narration........Then...............There (Them/Theirs;others)

    Current.................................Now............... Here

    Meanwhile............................Now............... There

    and also Fore-Shadow.........Later................Any

    Thus a Scene takes place in a Time (Now/Then) and a Place (Here/There), has at least one important Element (a person, place, object or event/relationship) Revealed or about which new info is Revealed through either Conflict or Drama.

    Examples:
    Flashback: growing up (Then) with my happy, care-free sister (Here or Me/Mine) who is introduced to
    drugs by a small-time hood (Element). Do we Focus on Drama or Conflict? I'd choose Drama to set up
    the next scene.

    Meanwhile: across town (There), my sister - a moll (Element) to the mob boss (Element and reinforces
    Them/Theirs) - is trying to escape (Now). Conflict, I would think. Note that even though she is
    still my sister, in this scene she is an Element of Them/Theirs; I am not directly connected or
    involved in the scene. Does she get away? Is she killed in the attempt?

    From this, one could develop random tables and flow charts to combine and direct the various parts, drive the plot towards a climax, or fail forward to another scene (Character development can be negative as well as positive) or introduce unexpected Elements. Fiasco meets AD&D(1st ed). Advanced Fiascos and Fictions!

    \nI think it could be a lot of fun to try this if you rolled for the second scene type only halfway through the scene. For example, roll a six-sided die every few beats in the action: on a roll of 6, the scene type becomes a different (randomly selected) type.

    So you would have many scenes play out as expected (the Investigation scene remains an Investigation scene), but some transform at some point, sometimes early in the scene, sometimes late. (The Romance scene becomes a Revelation scene! "Oh, Hank! I'm pregnant with your evil twin's child!")

    You could also have it linked to whatever other mechanics you use. If you roll 2d6 for conflict resolution, on a roll of doubles the scene type changes. Something like that.

    \n Something that seems missing to me here is the kind of scenes that Lady Blackbird calls "refresh" scenes. I see these as asides that develop a character or relationship between two characters, but they are not necessarily dramatic, or having things at stake. TV series have lots of these little scenes and moments between characters that do nothing for the overall plot of the episode, but do much for the fleshing out of the characters.

    \n Just a little bit out on a limb but still a very useful and often-seen trope:

    Dream/Nightmare/Reverie Scenes
    These are scenes which appear to be real at first, but then turn out not to have been real after all. May be similar to Flashbacks. But there are differences in their tactical applications, of course:
    \u2022 Dreams - revelations of information symbolizing a character's feelings, hunches and questions
    \u2022 Nightmares - revelations of information symbolizing a character's fears, doubts and suspicions
    \u2022 Reveries - revelations of a character's pleasures, wishes, altruism/selfishness or vengefulness

    \n Here is something I've used calling them four scene modes:\n
      \n
    • Narration: Describe what happens, establish facts verbally
    • Action: Focus on a conflict or goal, resolve using dice
    • Interaction: Focus on a conflict or goal, resolve using verbal interaction
    • Discovery: Focus on something unknown, resolve either verbally or with dice
    \nI would map it to the initial post like this:
    Revelation, Unexpected Visitor, Flashback, Dream, Foreshadow --> Narration
    Action --> Action
    Dramatic, Refresh (Flashback) --> Interaction
    Recognition --> Discovery

    It's just another idea if the scene options are too many. Maybe something like simple "scene modes" along with time parameter (now, past, future) could simplify the options.
