\n What kind of dice mechanics feel "lucky" to you?

This is kind of a two fold question in that it is what kind of things that can be done with the dice in the game to make it seem like the character is lucky or what makes the player feel lucky.


For me it is Call Ons in Burning Wheel. A call on lets you re-roll the failed dice in a roll. It feels like snatching victory from defeat.

    \n To make me feel lucky as a player, I need that 100-to-1 shot to come off: when everything comes down to the wire and only the best result will work. I've had that situation come up a few times, such as needing to roll a 6 on 1d6 in order to catch a bystander before she falls off the roof of a tall building. So, a game design that encourages situations like that would make me feel lucky as a player.
    \n
    \nI'm not sure what can make a character feel lucky: most games base the die rolls on the character's skills and abilities, so their successes feel like a result of effort and discipline rather than luck. Something like the Fate points used in many games, I suppose: a currency the player can spend when success reallymatters to them. That can be unsatisfying though, as re-rolling often sucks: I want to roll my dice and know whether I've succeeded or failed, not dither about with commodities, bargaining for a better result. Perhaps a tweaked version, where you spend the point instead of rolling, for a guaranteed success, would make me feel like my character's luck was with them.

    \n So there's this alleged game - it's quite possible it's a joke - called Metascape II. There are a few fun things in it, and one of them is an exploding die mechanic that provides a moderately high likelihood of a very, very extreme result, possibly with lots of repeated rolling. If you get a result in the thousands, you will (justifiably!) feel pretty lucky. The game is calibrated such that you'll do that a few times a campaign. If you've played Werewolf: The Apocalypse, and rerolled all your tens on a damage roll, you have a sense of the casino mentality at work.
