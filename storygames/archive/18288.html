    \n OK.
    lets unpack a little - situation, thief neaking into a castle, runs into an armed guard. Thief wants to get past somehow...

    In, say, ghost echo you have a goal (get past guard) and the dangers are :get harmed by guard and :draw more attention
    now dice roll - say 6, 3, 1. This lets the player decide what is more important - eg, get past 6, harm 3, attention 1 (gets past, slightly wounded, lots of attention)
    so: fiction (surprise guard who attacks, want to get past) -> quick mechanic check -> describe a short fight where thief gets hurt, but gets past guard into castle but now everyone is up and looking.


    A more trad game (in my experience) would have a great many checks to mechanics where you are needing to describe(and check each time) a more blow by blow cadence.
    say - roll initiative -> describe guard getting the drop on the thief -> roll attack and defense -> describe thief getting wounded but staying out of killing reach -> roll athletics check -> describe thief making a break through the room, throwing stuff behind to block guard -> roll stealth check -> describe running through corridors but hearing more guards starting to mobilize..

    I'd never seen much correlation between the dice mechanisms or the feedback patterns in the two games; but so many times I've heard people say that they were closely linked, and that Apocalypse World was a direct descendant of Otherkind. (Especially around here.)
    \nWell, the obvious connection is in the "choose-from-a-list" design of many/most moves. Look at a move like "seize by force": it gives you a bunch of options (not unlike the Otherkind categories), with one of them being your goal ("take definite hold of it") and the others being dangers to avoid ("suffer little harm") or additional objectives ("frighten or dismay your enemy").

    On a decent roll, you get to choose two of those. On a great roll, you get almost all of them (like on a very good Otherkind roll, it's very rare to actually be able to choose everything you want). On a bad roll you don't get to choose any a'tall.

    It's very Otherkind-like, but with some important differences: you don't get the full range of results, just three tiers, and on a bad roll you don't get to choose at all, it's all up to the MC to make a hard move (for which the MC isn't restricted to the original terms of the roll, unlike Otherkind--it's more open-ended here).

    The method to generating how MANY choices you get to make is different (add up the dice and refer to the move, rather than each die having a specific value), but otherwise it's a similar process.

    However, AW also has many, many moves which deviate significantly from this structure.

    \n You totally can have a blow by blow combat using Otherkind rules. (Just as you can have a much larger sale action in a single roll in a traditional game, e.g. many mass combat systems or rolling Gather Information instead of a dozen different Bluff and Diplomacy checks. I do the same one roll to replace inconsenti)

    Michael Weston: I pop out from cover to shoot at the bad guys.
    GM: Okay, what's your goal in shooting them?
    MW: I want to keep them from approaching the house long enough for Bruce's PC to finish improvising the bomb.
    GM: Excellent. One danger is that you get hit by a bullet when you pop out. What another thing that could go wrong?
    MW: Maybe my cover gets shot while I'm out from behind it, leaving me out in the open?
    GM: Awesome. Roll for it.

    And then Mike rolls and has to pick whether he's hurt, whether he's in danger or whether he failed to delay the bad guys getting into the building. You can move the granularity of the system up or down as you like. (So long as you don't have Apocalypse World/Ghost Echo type predefined results involved. Those lock down the scale of granularity.)



    PS: I made a quick little Otherkind hack a while ago for playing incompetent, washed up blue collar superheroes. It was a lot of fun to play.

    \n We've been playing the new Star Wars, and you're right. The system is vaguely Otherkind in nature...at least as much as Apocalypse World is.

    You roll and you get successes, failures, advantages and complications (not those specific terms, but you get the idea). Successes and failures cancel out until only one type is left, advantages and complications cancel out until only one type are left. So you can end up with:

    -Plain successes
    -Successes with advantages (that give you your intended result and twist the story in your direction)
    -Successes with complications (that give you your intended result but twist the story in the opponent's direction)
    -Plain failures
    -Failures with advantages (that mean you fail the immediate effect, but something in the story twists your way)
    -Failures with complications (that mean you fail the immediate effect, and the story twists even further to the opponent's favour)

    There are a few types of dice, with different chances of rolling up different symbols. The system is pretty intuitive. I'd actually say that the Star Wars system is one of the less fiddly systems I've encountered recently.
