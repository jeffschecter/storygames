\n Last night we played some Archipelago II, ending our session with the crew of the Argosy thawing out their backup crew in a blind panic, paranoid that the machines were taking over (I own The Mystery; they totally are). We had established an enigmatic message encoded in a stream of neutrinos coming from Europa - last week we sent an AI to check it out since we didn't have the reaction mass to divert the whole ship. This week we decoded the message which I (owner of The Mystery) declared read:

KILL YOUR AIS DO NOT SEND AIS HERE DO NOT GO TO EARTH

So, you know, uh oh. Did we just kill the last surviving humans in the solar system?

The other deeply weird thing (this is why playing with other people in an equal-authority system is so fun): Rafael's guy Lt. Cheng is going mad, he's a violent freezer-burned sociopath who is hallucinating about his dead wife. My guy, the watch psychologist, gets him restrained and puts this fMRI device on his head, a diagnostic tool that can be illicitly tuned to basically read people's minds. I illicitly tune. I can see through Cheng's eyes on my monitor. And I see his wife - not a hallucination, but a fucking ghost.

    \n Played a great game of the In a Wicked Age / Archipelago II mash-up yesterday. Lots of great great stuff happened, but I wanted to praise the 'If you're in doubt, draw another card from the Oracle' rule because it totally amped up our game.

    We used the Nest of Vipers oracle, and our four cards were:

    - A wizard, jealously guarding her magical territory

    - A fur-trapper, simple but good-natured, and his daughter

    - A poor home, shared by many families of beggars

    - A vicious gang of cut-throats and alley thieves.

    We had to discuss how all these elements fitted together - with the fur-trapper implying a wilderness setting, but the beggars and cut-throats being more urban. Eventually we settled on a huge city, sloping down from the noble houses on the peaks of the hills into the slums where the fur-trapper lived.

    At that stage, though, I realised I had a little bit of doubt about the set-up and suggested we pull a fifth card. I said we could discard it if we didn't feel like it added anything to the story.

    I pulled the card.

    - A ghoulish eater of dead-flesh, driven by unnatural lusts.



    Game on.

    \n Short attendence at the Fear the Boot's Writer's Guild (Fear the Con is this weekend), so idle minds will play.

    Three Mass Effect fan boys sat down and we created a Mass Effect playset on the fly for The Trouble with Rose.

    Karr'zal (quarrian engineer), Sidonis (reformed turian), and Bel Trane (human soldier/merc) were sent on a rescue mission to save a team salvaging a Reaper derelict in a post 'red' Shepard universe. Things starting going pear shape before we even got to the airlock in the hanger. Ambushed by husks, bashed by brutes, shot to bits by cannibals. The survivors, and two of the players were betrayed by the quarrian who was sent to blow up the wreck. Both Sidonis and Bel were in love with Ereba (the blue rose of Illium), but Sidoni's last act of redemption was to give his own life (double blanks in the fifth act) to save her and Bel. The survivors barely escape as Karr'zal is left drifting in space only to be betrayed himself in the end.

    We used the Silverlight powered and free Taebl virtual table program to handle the dominos, cards, and scoring. Easy peasy. I believe I've made a few more converts to TTwR and story games in general.

    \n Microscope, alt-history Elizabethan era. The second Spanish Armada conquers Britannia but the Gloriana Invicta resistance ("Elizabeth unvanquished") continues the fight for queen and country.

    We've already seen that the British heir-in-hiding Prince James was secretly part of the conspiracy that murdered his own younger sister, Princess Matilde, even while keeping up appearances as her loving brother and protector. Jerk! We go back to before she was killed to answer the seemingly simple question "how was she murdered?" The scene is the conspirators plotting to get rid of the princess. It's set in the gardens of a noble who has secretly supports the resistance and has been hiding the prince and princess from the occupying Spaniards.

    Folks are picking characters. It's the usual choices: the prince, the noble, the cutthroat who is biding his time as the princess's bodyguard but is really waiting for the order to kill her.

    Hmm, this scene seems too easy. So I pick the princess. The scene framer didn't think to ban her. Go ahead and do your plotting right in front of the poor girl you heartless bastards! Kind-hearted Princess Matilde spends the scene strolling through the garden innocently plucking flowers and bringing them to her loving brother and future assassin. Each time she's out of ear-shot the prince and their two-faced host discuss how best to murder her.

    And then to really put the icing on the cake-of-pain, we have the princess's protector and secret love-that-cannot-be (and devoted agent of the resistance) wander into the scene and watch unwittingly as she is handed the poison that will kill her. Which isn't so bad, because we already played the scene years later where he uncovers his masters' treachery and exacts brutal revenge with his own hand. Seeing how callous her murder is actually explains why he was/will be so ruthless in the earlier/future scene.

    \n Our Archipelago game ended in a surprisingly down note last night - the bad guys won, our robotics engineer Childress made contact with the machine intelligence and surrendered to it. Everybody on Europa died, creepily represented by the last surviving human holding down the F key on a keyboard as his or her life ended, crying out to the void. The surviving humans landed on Earth as planned, to find themselves in what amounted to a zoo where Guyana used to be, their future unknown and unknowable. One guy, the engineer Beausoliel, stayed in orbit with 60 days of consumables and no hope of anything at the end of it. And as icing on the failcake, they had tipped their hand concerning where they had come from.

    One nice thing about this mini-campaign was that it was a little incoherent - we never knew exactly what the machine intelligence wanted, we never understood its motivations, it was genuinely frightening. Some people it killed horribly, some people it co-opted, a few it seemed to avoid. That, layered on the weird atmospheric stuff about ghosts (combined with ghosts in the machine) gave the whole game a menacing air of unpredictability and terror.

    \n I got in two playtests of a new version of my old basically abandoned sword & sorcery game Bl\xe4ck this weekend.

    In the first one we saw three unlikely heroes taking on a necromancer in a tower before wandering of to a djungle in search for the Fountain of Wisdom. We don't really know what Valdemar found out there on the island, but it seems he was kind of self counscious when he came back. In the last chapter one of the heroes beat the dreaded captain of the Red Riders and got his sword for a price.

    We had fun, but that was definitley the unsuccessful playtest of the two. After making some changes I had another go today with other players.

    We saw the undefeated gladiator Valkan fleeing the palace guard, ending up in the Lion Garden (which held lions of course) where he fought for his life. Soon a thief showed up only to be spotted by the already alert guards. They soon made their escape to the other side of the river where they could be patched up and have some wine.

    After that they fought assassins in the streets of the Floating City (city block built on stilts in the river), young misbehaving nobles who attacked the poor who lived in the cities great graveyard, and finally the Red Cult at their sacrifice ritual in the old catacombs. It turned out that the Masked Whisperer, the cult's mysterious leader, was no one other than the lord of the city \u2013 a city in which only worship of the Nine Dancers was allowed.

    After less than two hours of playing (including drawing the map and making characters) we left our heroes as they were fleeing the now burning catacombs.

    By Crom, that's what the game is supposed to be like! But what was more important \u2013 rules changes, or the fact that the second set of players were actually sword & sorcery fans? Maybe they were equal parts. I'll revel in the success for a while anyway!

    \n Darius, the Witch, has run completely run out of String, has blown all his Tokens, and has taken two Harm and 5 conditions. Pretty much completely deprotagonized, at this point, as no dice rolls will end in anything but failure.

    Jessamine, the Vampire, comes over with an offer that Darius can't refuse, literally, and Darius finds himself being forced to "go first" into the house of Janel, (on which Jess wants revenge.) Darius tries to roll Dark to figure out where Janel is hiding, and blows the roll (how couldn't he?)

    Darius spends the next few minutes commediacally flailing about, having taken on another condition (on top of terrified, confused, and others... now Darius has "trapped" going on in his brain).

    Jessamine does what any good manipulative bitch would do, leave Darius behind to die.

    What happens instead? The MC turns the situation on its head, and Janel starts to offer Darius everything he needs... removal of conditions... even offering to find some... Tokens to get leverage on the other PCs... especially Jessamine.

    I love this MC. Turn a completely discarded and "useless" PC into a real contender again, it the space of five seconds.

      Eli and Kali sneak into the second floor of a soup kitchen. On the bottom floor soup is being served, and the patrons have no idea that it consists mostly of failed experiments. While the people shuffle through, they are scanned and profiled for future... work.

      Now Eli and Kali creap down the hallway to a barely open door. Eli cautiously opens it, remembering her assassin training. Kali, in another world, proceeds blindly in, staring at the anomolous flesh detector instead. It beeps more steadily, more loudly as Kali crosses the room.

      Eli is paralyzed with fear. An abomination, unworldly limbs, peeled eyelids, patched together from organic material and things better not talked about, is grinding powders, mixing chemicals, bubbling tubes full of ichor and blood. It moves to the two of them, and Kali finally looks up to see it.

      Hungry, angry, curious to dissect the two women in it's room, it's mouth creaks open with a slow inhale. Kali interrupts, pulling from the void and the greater darkness beyond and speaks into the creature's mind. "Your work is too important to notice us." It's will is strained, it wants to remember, but it cannot. Back to grinding and mixing it goes, more furious now. It reaches over Eli as she regains control of her own body.

      "It is when there are two that we must worry. Hurry, let's take what we need and go."

      \n So, I'm running Kerberos Fate, hitting that spot where I'm falling back on my source material because we're sort of off the borders of what I actually prepped, and the players aren't yet going in some utterly different direction. And, I'm reminding myself "This isn't about your source material or your ideal movie. Watch it."

      Two PCs and two NPCs are off to try to rescue someone, and I'm paying attention to who's where, how quickly people are going to the right place, all that.

      And the two PCs look at each other and decide that both NPCs are potential recruits for the Kerberos Club. This means they must Be Tested. And, this is a perfect test situation! So, they deliberately let the NPCs get a crucial couple of minutes ahead of them. One of the PCs changed clothing (for perfectly valid reasons) without even the slightest thought of a suggestion forming itself in my brain because it never occurred to me that anyone might do that..

      And we are simultaneously totally off my script, which is good (if it ain't, I should be writing novels, not gming) and doing something that means I can use exactly the material from my source that will push on the current plot arcs and Aspects that the players have decided are important. The two NPCs sent ahead are Nowhere To Be Seen -- obviously captured by the enemy, and the players and PCs are hoping the NPCs will rescue themselves because a) they want to be able to induct them into the club with a clear conscience and b) we've realized that one of them, the sex magician, is the best match for the proper young heiress PC (the one who stopped to change her clothes lest she be recognized doing anything unladylike). He's also the lover of the somewhat scandalous shapeshifting PC.

      This is totally not where I saw any of this going, especially the "no, we want them to rescue themselves" part. Oh. And, I think I know how to play with that idea such that the PCs don't lose their agency.

      \nRisk: Legacy, games #4 & #5 [minor spoilers, but I've deleted anything not written about in the initial rulebook]

      After game three we got both Event Cards and another set of cards that complicate how you begin the game. The latter set was really cool, ensuring that different players got priorities as far as (1) having the first turn, (2) choosing which territory to start from, (3) choosing their faction first, (4) starting with a different number of troops, or (5) starting with 1-2 extra coin cards. The Event Cards, on the other hand, seemed to consistently reward the player with the highest population -- the one that tended to be already closer to winning -- and we pretty much universally disliked these. They certainly speeded up the game, though. Perhaps there are future packets that we'll open that will make Event Cards less lame, or will give us more Event Cards so they don't consistently reward the person in front.

      The game board itself is much more interesting now, due to placing most of the stickers on the board. There are new Scar Cards that allow you to [REDACTED], which is pretty awesome and crazy. In general, the board feels like it has much more character and interest now. South America is essentially barely inhabitable now, due to a series of scars and cities (since you automatically lose troops when you take over cities), but Australia is still a beast of a continent.

      In the first game, we managed to trigger 5-6 Event Cards in rapid succession, and Anton -- who was already pulling off an effective Australia strategy -- beefed up on troops really fast and just steamrolled most of the board. Since it was over so fast, we decided to play again and the second game was much more interesting.

      The second game started with:
      * Anton - Australia, again
      * David - Iceland
      * Erin - Kamchatka
      * Jonathan - South Africa

      We ended up only getting one or two Event Cards the whole game, which made it pretty exciting and much more balanced. Plus, having just played a game, we were all in Risk mode and really dug in. Unfortunately, Anton used his victory in the first game to neutralize my attempt at [REDACTED] Australia, so he started out in a strong position.

      I took over Africa and Anton expanded out of Australia to attack David's forces in Europe, dissuaded from attacking me by the empty megacity of Davidopolis in the Middle East (which would have eaten 2 troops if he tried crossing it). Erin slowly expanded across East Asia and eventually into Alaska to assault David from the other side. Eventually David dissuaded Anton through a great series of rolls and Anton went south, though the weakpoint in North Africa (which has had an Ammo Shortage scar since game 1, I think) to take out my headquarters. I partially escaped into the deadly continent of South America, but David swung down from Iceland to eliminate me and take my 7 coin cards (bastard!). Erin attempted small assaults on both David and Anton but was outnumbered and kept wanting to consolidate her troops, but she had the faction that rewarded her for expanding, so her moves early in the game meant she was less concentrated in any one place. In the end, Anton won again, though he did it by taking the last of the coin cards (the ones not attached to territories), which automatically means that the player with the most territories gets a red star.

      So, in the end, a closely-fought game and one where a new rule was invoked. In both Erin and my cases, if we hadn't been annihilated and had our cards stolen, we would have dropped them for a bunch of troops and become major problems, so it was very back-and-forth.
