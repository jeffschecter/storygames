\n I love Apocalypse World. Like a lot of people, when I love a system I want to run things in it. All sorts of things. Every thing. Saying "let's run it in Apocalypse World" becomes a reflex reaction, and the fact that it's so delightfully hackable - as evidenced by Dungeon World, Monsterhearts and all the rest - doesn't do anything to temper that reflex. But my better judgment has to put its foot down eventually and say, "No, this is not a universal system, and you shouldn't treat it like one."
\n
\nSo what is Apocalypse World for, really? What sorts of themes, situations and people are its mechanics intrisically good at dealing with? By contrast, what doesn't it do well, either at all or without so much hacking that you'd be better off using another game? I've been trying to answer these questions myself, but I've realized that I simply don't have enough Actual Play experience with AW or its offshoots to come to an informed conclusion. I'd love to hear what you folks think. Especially welcome are your AP experiences with Apocalypse World hacks, both positive and negative, and what made them go down the way they did.

    \n I have 3-5 sessions each of AW, Dungeon World and The Regiment, and two sessions of Monsterhearts under my belt now. And I've been thinking about this very question.

    My feeling is that AW is best at telling stories at the table that are inherently inward-looking. There may be a goblin or a guy with a wrench or a bully in front of you. But sessions that mostly deal with external threats are not the most satisfying ones in my experience. To me, the game is about what those threats meet to you and the relationships you have with the other PCs.

    Dungeon World at the surface may look like the exception. But in reality, it's one of the most inward-looking games. You NEED those other party members around you. Separate for too long and any one character will get their ass kicked. And bear in mind that Dungeon World has had a LOT of work put into it. It may use the core of AW, but it's fully its own game.

    Plus, I think the game needs to be "personal" to really work with the AW engine. For example, The Regiment has rules for platoon combat. But honesty, those are the creakiest rules for me because they are getting away from the core of AW that treats a "gang" of say 15-20 individuals like one (powerful) person for combat's sake. Once you are dealing with more than 6-8 individuals it's difficult to give any one screen time. AW gets it right where it just gives NPCs a name, a "job" and some kind of want. And stops there unless and until the NPC justifies it's development more.

    So for example, if you wanted to play Cthulhu with AW, I think you would need to modify your expectations to really make it sing. Make it less about the investigations and great external threats. And more how those things are ripping at the relationships between the PCs.

    Am I off-base here? What's do others w lots of AW experience think?

    \n It really depends on how much hacking you want to do and how in-depth your understanding of the AW framework is. Don't forget that Murderous Ghosts is an AW hack too, and it does some very different things.

    If you take the core of AW as:
    - setting expectations
    - agendas
    - always say
    - principles
    - moves / stuff that's not moves
    - playbooks
    - a few other things

    That's basically all of roleplaying, so asking what AW is good at and what it can't do seems a bit silly at that point. It can do anything, if you approach it from that level. You just have to do all the work at the front end that makes possible the thing you want to do.

    If you're hacking relatively close to the tree (which Monsterhearts, Dungeon World, Sagas of the Icelanders, and such all do), not straying very far from the core of AW, then you're talking about a much more specific set of things.

    I've got a PAX Dev proposal in consideration that examines AW as a "tabletop development framework," but even if it doesn't get approved, I want to give that talk at some point, because -- in essence, and if you step back enough -- AW is a systematic, modular system for designing any game you can imagine, something like a programming language for RPGs (in a way that's much more robust than what Fudge attempted to be, for example).

    \n I feel that 'World games explicitly uphold 'play to see what happens', both mechanically and narratively. The emergent story and intrinsic drama isn't a side effect of play, its the point of play.     I agree with a lot of what's been said up to now and disagree strongly with a couple points, but doubt it'd be fruitful to go into that.

    I like the "World" games because they give almost all of the initiative to the player characters while giving express understood permission to the MC to bone the PCs when the dice dictate. This characteristic is especially important for games like Apocalypse World, Dungeon World and The Regiment. This feature is less important to Monsterhearts and Sagas which do benefit from it but which are great mostly for other reasons, some at AW's core and some hacked or added on.

    Apocalypse World does a lot of things well (see above), but to understand the core of it, ponder this (maybe) aphorism:

    "Seven to Nine is the Heart of what some have termed 'Story Games'."

    \n You know those games where PCs lie on a really large sliding scale, power-wise? Like when they start they are level 1 and they can wander around the map and discover a challenge that is way beyond them, and then they have to leave and come back later when they are level 10 and it becomes a fair fight? Yeah, AW is pretty crap at doing that. Sure, you get more moves and better stats, but the stat scale is pretty short, and doesn't react well to "challenge levels" and stuff like when you start making certain actions more difficult by adding penalties, and having more moves just gives you more options, as opposed to keeping you relative to a challenge rating. AW is built for a world where everything is generally on the same scale--some people are more skilled than others, but they are all still people, and if the PCs are "heroes" it is by dint of being PCs and the subject of the story.

    Also, AW gives very little support for exploration style gaming (where PCs go places that are dangerous and unfamiliar). Sure, Dungeon World sorta focuses on that, but the whole "leave spaces in the map" thing wouldn't go over very well with the people I play D&D with, because they expect all the important things on the map to be set in stone beforehand. Instead, AW gives you lots of support for playing a game in a setting that the characters are already familiar with (because they live there), by telling you (the MC) to ask questions about their characters and their lives, and telling the players that they are responsible for answering those questions.

    Apocalypse World is best suited to games focused on drama. Situations where PCs inhabit a world they are at least somewhat familiar with and live in, and are constantly interacting with and butting heads with each other and other characters, as well as problems that threaten their way of life and the things they hold dear, are AW's bread and butter.

    These interactions, conflicts, and threats also work best when they are clear and in the open, at least in part. AW is not a game that lends itself to stealth and cloak-and-dagger operations. Stories that involve stealth build tension through the threat of discovery, and the conflict in that moment is between the stealthy actor and the difficulty of staying hidden. The AW rules don't really focus on that kind of internal struggle very well, they are meant to focus on an active protagonist versus an external threat. Things really get going in AW at the moment of discovery, not so much before that.

    So, like Monsterhearts looks a lot different, because it has totally different moves and it has strings and stuff, but it's still about the same kind of drama as AW. And Dungeon World looks like a dungeoncrawl game, but it takes that focus on drama and switches up to "adventure" instead of going all the way into exploration. In DW the point is to go on an adventure, not to make an unfamiliar environment familiar through exploring it.

    Posted By: JohnstoneLike when they start they are level 1 and they can wander around the map and discover a challenge that is way beyond them, and then they have to leave and come back later when they are level 10 and it becomes a fair fight? Yeah, AW is pretty crap at doing that.
    \nAW is all about having the fictional positioning necessary to make moves, right? So it actually handles things that are outside of your PC's scope really well. You just can't make moves against them, because you don't have any leverage over them. Like, in DW, you probably have to do some serious work in order to be able to roll Hack N Slash against a dragon, because they're not vulnerable to the normal weapons or tactics that you're using. This even happens in standard AW with stuff like the Psychic Maelstrom, which you can't normally target with your moves. But if you use +workspace or +augury (moves you only get later in the game), or do something else to get fictional positioning over the Maelstrom, all of a sudden it becomes more possible to target it with moves. It's pretty easy to imagine something similar happening with Corporations in a Shadowrun hack, for example: you can target individual agents but not the Corp as a whole until you can get some fictional positioning that allows you to do so.

    +workspace is also a great model for investigation and exploration games, actually. "When you want to get to the bottom of something, tell the MC what you want and they'll tell you what you need to do in order to do that." Perfect. I'm not sure what else you need. You could say: "I want to know what happened to the Holmston Expedition and why they never returned." Or "I want to know the spell of power that opens the gateway to the Plane of Fire." Or you could say: "I want to figure out who murdered Jesse Smoulders and find some hard evidence that they did it." Ideally, in a game that was centered on exploration or investigation, +workspace would just be the beginning of something. Because the GM might say: "Well, you probably need X and Y, but in order to do that, you probably need to talk to someone in the Won Sing Triad. Do you know anyone involved in the Chinatown gangs?" And moves snowball, etc. (both MC and player moves).

    I mean, it's easy to be distracted by what the existing AW moves do and how they are arranged, but even core moves are easily hacked or replaced with other moves. What happens when you make something like +workspace or +augury a core move? What if it's something like "An arresting skinner" (i.e. a move that just happens, without rolling)? Those are pretty simple hacks and they completely change the nature and tone of the game.

    \n Jonathan, those are valid insights, for sure, but they don't counter my point at all.

    You're talking about narrative positioning, which can happen at any point in a campaign. I'm talking about character effectiveness versus variable challenge ratings. The difference between 5 hit points and 50 hit points is not something AW does. It also isn't able to differentiate between obstacles that require different levels of skill. Your examples are all narrative problems that need to be taken care of before dice are even rolled. But the situation where you see an Ob 8 test to break through the Black Gate and you realize your dice pool is nowhere near big enough and you need to increase your skill first (or collect a bunch more artha) is not something that happens in AW. You can always take on any challenge at any time.

    Even though you can get new moves and stuff and you can use workspaces for quests and whatnot, AW is more like starting at level 3 and going to level 5, and then picking up the special abilities of other classes, but not their hit dice or attack bonuses. Which isn't to say a lot of that stuff can't be hacked into AW, or that specific parts of AW can't be removed from AW and fit into games that do that style of play, BUT the OP wanted to stay close to the original, so: with AW proper, yes you can have problems that defy certain types of solutions, but there's no place for scalable skill level or effectiveness past that -2 to +3 range, which is pretty tight.

    \n So, things the AW engine definitely cannot do:\n
      \n
    1. Provide a layer of mechanical challenge to engage (Step On Up against, if that's your purpose) which is sort-of-removed, or partially shielded, from the fiction. All of the challenges are solvable through fictional positioning, because everything mechanical is mandated to begin and end in the fiction.
      2. \n
    2. Employ a mandated, pre-planned narrative arc or rythm that fiction has to conform to \u2014 like, for example, an n-acts structure, or a climatic progression. The kind of drama you get is instead such that anything could happen at any time, up to and including anti-climax: you've got to embrace the unexpected.
      3. \n
    3. Describe an unmovable status quo which stays in the background, out of reach, and can't be affected by play while in some way affecting it.
      4. \n
    \nNotice that:
    • all of the above three are related, maybe strictly;
      • \n
    • all of the above three are reasonable tools that a number of designs (including explicitly Story Now designs) have employed toward their own ends to great effect.
    \nAnd since I mentioned Story Now, it goes without saying that - speaking in "canonical" Forge theory terms - the AW engine is laser-focused on the creative agenda/family of creative agendas of that name (or a subset of it, actually).

    \n I like Apocalypse World because:

    - As the MC, I don't roll anything. The less I have to manage, the more I can listen to the players.

    - How to run and play is part of the rules rather than fuzzy advice that sometimes conflicts with the rules.

    - New players don't feel overwhelmed, almost everything they need is in their playbook. Choosing words from lists is easy.

    \n John, great points. I've been noticing these things too. I'm still fairly new to the game, and it's taking me some time to really, really grok it, but having clear, limited choices in the beginning is great and makes it easier to get right to playing every time.

    So here's something Apocalypse World (and certainly also Monsterhearts - I can't speak for other hacks) is intrinsically good at: sex.

    I've talked to some people who say that they or their groups don't really engage with the sex moves or do so infrequently. But that seems kind of lame to me. I get that a lot of people (and I've been there too) are uncomfortable with introducing sex to games in an obvious way - there are exceptions, but most people don't want their game to feel like porn or whatever. That's fine - that's actually not what AW world encourages with sex moves. What it encourages is sex that matters.

    The game really does this with mechanical reinforcement and by having it front and center on your character sheet. At it's most basic level, sex gives you power over each other. And depending on the skins it may do other things as well. If you actually roleplay mechanical affects (which you should, otherwise the game tends to lose its humanity to a bloodbath), then you and that other person may never look at each other the same again. For better or worse, but something changed. And now you have something on them and they have something on you. And that can effect the outcome of future rolls and most certainly the narrative.

    So sex is a thing. And not having sex is a thing too. It's sort of the conceit of the game that your characters are more or less sexy, competent, and passionate. If your character abstains, it should be an interesting intentional choice - maybe even an agonizing one. Maybe The Operator just can't afford to have one more thing to look out for. Or in Monsterhearts, maybe The Infernal still has a conscience and can't bare the thought of letting the Dark Power gain a string on his lover. Interesting, right?

    I'll go out on a limb to say that if your AW game does not include sex as a thing (just as if you ignored other Moves, and perhaps more so), you're missing out on something this game was designed to do well.

    -Rachel

    \n Ivan, difficulty level in AW is composed of basically three factors:

    1. The fiction. This is the stuff Jonathan Walton mentioned. Difficulty is measured by how easy it is for a character to find the means to do something, which kind of nullifies the point of difficulty. Like when you discover that the psychic maelstrom is being run by a satellite in low Earth orbit -- you have no way to get to that satellite, because you are just people. Maybe you can build a spaceship or discover a space elevator or hack into it's comm link, but until you discover a way to do that in the fiction, it's not difficult, it's impossible. And you need to find those specific solutions in the fiction. It's more like a test of a player's creativity and problem-solving skills, not a test of the character or the player's ability to tactically or strategically manipulate rules.

    2. Stats. Like Zac mentioned, this is the basic determinant of how difficult it is to do something. If you have hot-2, good luck talking somebody into doing something. If you have weird+3, the psychic maelstrom is your bitch. But that's a personal scale, it measures how difficult is is for your character to do something, and there's five of them. So you can build up one of your stats over time, and go from a tongue-tied misfit to a suave heartbreaker, but you can't build overall effectiveness like you can in D&D. Plus, the GM has nothing to do with it -- she isn't introducing obstacles of varying difficulty, it's just the player choosing what his character can do well and what he can't.

    3. Finally, there's modifiers to your roll. So, the GM could say "this is difficult and you get a -2 to your roll." Vincent advises against this in the book. Part of why this doesn't work so well is that the stat scale is just 5 units long (-2 to +3), so you can't create a challenge that is impossible for beginners but challenging to experts and easy for long-term veterans.

    All of this plays into AW as a fiction first game. Expertise isn't supposed to be reliably codified. Nothing should be absolutely certain, neither success nor failure, and the story's supposed to be about people. The MC is playing to find out as well, so you need spontaneity at all times. So while you can get the story of "zero to hero" in terms of how much social influence a character has, you can't do it in terms of personal expertise.

    (Just as a math note: While it's possible to manage a total of +5 to your roll, thus reducing the chance of a miss to 0%, it's not possible to get a +8 with no chance of misses on the help rolls you would need. So those 7-9 results are always on the table, no matter what.)
