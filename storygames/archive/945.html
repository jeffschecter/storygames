OK, so in Grey Ranks you have a GM-ful game where everybody takes on different roles in every scene. Totally yanked from Polaris. You are, variously:

1. The guy in the spotlight doing cool stuff. Primary focus is being extremely awesome and sexy.
2. The guy who paints the scene and provides both interesting color and adversity. Primary focus is setting and situation. Current name, stolen from Face of Angels: The world. It fits but I'm open to change.
3. The guy who steps up to play #1's sweetheart, best friend, or other prominent NPC. Primary focus is people and relationships.

I'm struggling to find a set of names that are unified in theme, intuitive, and not ridiculous when said out loud. Thoughts? Best set of terms gets a cookie at Gen Con or thanks in the game, your choice.

    Thanks very much for all the feedback, everybody. Here's the current explanatory text:

    "There are three different roles players assume in a game of Grey Ranks, and each player will be in all of them at various times.

    Lead: Sometimes your character will be center stage. You will have great latitude to describe how he deals with the challenges in his path.

    World: The player to the lead's left plays the world for him. The world is tasked with representing the physical space and painting the scene. He should create a descriptive environment that is detailed and interesting for his fellow player, taking care to listen to his vignettes and working to further his goals and make him look good. The world is responsible for realizing the setting and playing necessary supporting characters. The world has the additional responsibility of rolling dice to challenge the lead.

    Partner: The player to the lead's right is the partner, who during vignettes is probably - but not necessarily - his love interest. Many mission scenes and all vignettes will be about relationships, and the partner plays the important person opposite the lead. The only exception to the "on your right" rule is if a player character's love interest is another player character, in which case those two players are each other's partner regardless of seating order. If practical, just skip them in determining partner roles."
