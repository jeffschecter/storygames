    \n I use carefully drawn maps for lands and cities to give some feeling for the setting, and I want those maps to be as clear in writing and as evocative in design as possible.

    But I prefer not to map specific situations in a scene. As a GM in classical fantasy I find it better to frame the scene verbally, and demand action on the fly from my players. It makes for a faster game, and more fun, even though they may be acting on very sparse info sometimes.

    It happens that I make a situational sketch anyway, if the game somehow demands it, but it is far between.

    The reason I use situational maps so seldom, is that I do not believe in the so called "tactical" doctrine; that you need to see your characters in relation to enemies and friends, on a battle ground/grid, to make realistic decisions and have the fights play out to the satisfaction of everybody. The minute you put something down on a map like that, it stops being realistic, in my view, and makes the tactical element totally un-founded in any real experience of close fighting. The dynamic, flexible and wildly raging elements of a fight is lost, when your focus is drawn to some static plastic counters on a board. You may move them, but they won't move you.

    What makes for realistic decisions, is that you (the character) are highly subjective in what you see and understand, and react on base of a fleeting impression of what goes on (beyond the simple fact that a fight is on). It also makes for a more realistic decision by you, if you are subject to the simple and effective workings of a living GM.

    The GM using voice, mimics and body language to communicate the volatile situation, while also communicating danger, fear and rage, is far better. It makes for a better simulation of "me being in danger", in my experience. It makes the players focus on you, as a possible enemy. And that's a key; you (the GM) can easily portray a dangerous enemy; by standing up, leaning forward, talking loudly at your players, demanding action AT ONCE!!! (OR I WILL SMOTHER YOU ALL!!!).

    "AT ONCE" is another key: by upping the tempo, bringing it into a frenzy of descriptions, actions and reactions, you end up with a way of doing fights that is far more efficient in spurring inspired and realistic interaction. The tactics applied by stressed players will be closer to real life battle tactics, as it will include some reminiscence of real life reactions and emotions. While a fight is all physical action on the surface of it, the inside of a fight is a churning psychodrama of adrenaline, and other fear and rage induced reactions. Tempo is key to making such things happen in the players.

    And you don't get tempo with combat-sketches and plastic figures.

    \n To answer the original post, I don't think maps are bad. But I do think certain maps can disrupt certain player's imagination when used at certain parts of the game.

    The same is true for words. For some players, too many words, means to much specificity, which means limiting their imagination. But not always. It depends on the players. For some players, having a number next to a stat is more evocative than a verbal descriptor. Strength 8 out of 10 is more evocative for them than Amazing strength.

    The amount of maps and the amount of verbal descriptions I use depends on what the players ask me. If the players ask me what something looks, feels, smells, sounds, or tastes like... I will start to get more detailed. If players start to ask, where exactly is the exit and where are all the guards situated, I will draw a map. For me the key is not to do more than is necessary since the players may not care.

    The other key is that I accept that different players are imagining different things. Very few things I say or draw will be more vivid than their imagination. So I try to limit what I detail so we are at least on the bare minimum same page while still allowing different people to imagine different things.

    I do find though that if I write something down, even if the players didn't ask for it, it helps ensure the players will give it extra care. Of course if I write down too much, it has the opposite effect! Jotting down a brief list of clues or a list of environmental features or a list of major NPC usually means at minimum players will reincorporate many of those elements.

Because we are not face to face, I find having a visual in our shared Maptool space really, really helps. And it happens that a couple of the players in that are real minis lovers.     \n I'm another one for whom maps often work well with role-playing. I really like visualizing the scene, but it bothers me when I can clearly tell that my visualization is different than other players' visualizations.

    For me, there are two common cases that bring play to a halt in mapless play:

    1) Assumption clash about what is going on. For example, I describe how my character grabs the crystal and prepares to run - assuming that the others are some distance away, and another player says that he grabs me - assuming that we are all tightly bunched. However, I wouldn't have chosen to grab the crystal if someone was standing right by me.

    2) Extended Q&A about position. To prevent #1, sometimes I or someone else slow things down with a lot of questions, like: How wide a space is there here? How far are we spread out? What direction(s) are everyone facing? etc.

    I tend to prefer having questions answered by a map up-front rather than slow down play with Q&A or backtracking later.

      \n I like those kinds of uses for maps Tomas, and have done similar things. It can work a treat, and I also have used them more for informing play than for tactical fiddliness.

      ___________________________________

      I like games played, not just with maps, but also with evocative minis and sometimes with whole table layouts.

      I know that's not everyone's cuppa tea, but bear with me a moment. I have actually learned a few things over the years about how to use that stuff in a positive way, a fun way, and a way more in line with rpg style play than with it's cousin wargaming play.

      First off, inspiration.

      I know I come off as a bit of a horrible elitist when I get in convos about minis use and table set ups/maps because i'm actually one of those people who doesn't think that using whatever small coin or marker or randomly available mini is the best way to go about things, nor do i think that the randomly drawn map on the erasable grid mat or hex map is the way to go.

      That stuff works decently if what you really care about is some kind of tactical fiddly position mongering.

      I don't see it s terribly inspirational though, and I find it a bit backwards or out of proper order in approach.

      A bit like brushing your teeth with a lovely minty toothpaste, then having a big glass of orange juice. Wrong order if you want to have a pleasant experience. If you always do it, it may well lead you to the conclusion that orange juice is a terrible drink.

      If I hear you've been doing that, suggest a different order, and you continue to do it in the wrong order, I'm going to conclude that Dain Bramage has occured, especially if you complain about how unpleasant the experience is and continue to do it anyway.

      Start with the maps, minis, an open mind, and draw inspiration from them first.
      Second, keep the freeflowing nature of rpgs as a core method, even when you're playing with the toys.

      Th first part means...choose your minis before you make the game.

      For a player, choose a mini first, then make your character. For a GM it means pick out the baddies minis and the map(s) or table set up elements, then use them to inspire the adventures (or at least them beginnings of them).

      Already by using that order of things, we begin to remove one barrier people find between imagination and the physical stuff.

      The second part, use the freeflowing methods one associates with RPG fun during the game means just that. Fiddly exact range measuring and movement and even order of declaring actions can mostly go and get stuffed.

      So let's talk about why maps are good. It's not just for tactics. It's for any game where action is important. In an action film, the protagonist tries to accomplish something physical. The tension of the scene comes from whether they accomplish it or not. So it's important for the filmmaker to establish a visual layout of the scene.

      Let's watch such a scene now, from Die Hard 2, not the greatest action movie of all time, but a pretty solid, middle of the road action scene. Especially note that we can see the location of the second bad guy at :08 (even though this obscures the impact of one of the characters). A reverse shot at :18 shows us another view of this relationship (along with the reason the bad guy doesn't fire). At :20 we see a vertical composition of the space - the characters are above a jet engine and the second bad guy is somewhat elevated from them. And so on. Throughout this scene, the filmmakers use visual cues in order to create the space for the characters to act. John MacClain's character is not the greatest fighter, but he has tenacity and improvisation. Note that both characters he fights get the better of him and put him in situations where he has to use those character traits in order to achieve the goal. In fact, the interior of the plane, where the ultimate badguy is, is in sight literally from the first second of the fight, even though MacClain never enters it.

      To look at a failure to establish the physical space of the action, look at Mission Impossible 3's bridge scene. I love this example because it's clear that the set designers and art people knew that you had to establish physical markers to help the audience orient themselves in the scene - see if you can spot them as we have yet another tight close-up on Tom Cruise running or bad guys looking through a sight. In this scene it is absolutely impossible to see what each participant (and how many are there?) is trying to accomplish physically, or to place that physical action in a larger context, or feel any tension that they may or may not accomplish it.

      Turning to RPGs, action RPGs again have a similar setup for tension - we have a physical goal and we may or may not accomplish it. This is the setup for D&D, Spycraft and many many other games and subgames. In order to create that tension for how a physical action may or may not take place, the players must have some sense of the physicality of the location, the reality of the location. Maps, broadly or specifically drawn, help with that.

      Now, if your game isn't an action game - if what you're trying to show is the chaos and horror of war (for example), then the physicality of the space isn't really an issue, what you're trying to accomplish has less to do with a physical action and more to do with chance and fate. And that is why Saving Private Ryan's famed Omaha Beach landing scene only shows us the full physical space of the scene over the jittery-shot, out-of-focus shoulder of a German gunner who takes up half the screen, and only rarely. We never see a character we recognize in anything further away than a medium shot. In these sorts of games, Tomas' technique is very effective. When everything's awhirl with chaos, a good sense of physical space may actually hinder the tension and release of the scene.

      - debating the likes and dislikes of people as if they are arguments in each others disfavor, is null and void. It's a matter of taste.
      - but debating how games work for people, and don't work, due to inherent strengths and/or weaknesses, is very useful. We need to deepen our insights in role-playing games, their methods, and how they work on human beings. It's a matter of knowledge.

      It is necessary for us to make a distinction between good games and bad ones, and to do that we need to analyze what people do when playing them, what kind of experience they have, and what the basis for that experience is. One part of that is to analyze our likes and dislikes.
      - Why do so many people find map & minis style of play so boring?
      - Why do so many find it frustrating to have the battle-map removed?
      - Why do som many find it liberating to play high tempo and simply resolved action scenes?
      - Why do so many love the rolling of dice, and the release of pent up tension that a successful roll gives?

      - How can we design to counter these weaknesses? And to strengthen game-play within a style of play?

      So I'm proposing to design for a better solution in how we are doing rpg action scenes (and other tactical scenes) without the battle map. I'm preferring to seek out solutions in other kinds of methodical tools. I've debated this since the late eighties, and worked on it continually in my design of Fabula since the mid nineties. I've had some abysmal failures, and some very bright successes. Most conflicts are really felt after they're over, so I make a method where players are encouraged to explore that part of it very thoroughly. That is the gist of it; we make much more out of the aftermath, that being the time when conflicts really hit us, socially and emotionally.       And that's done with the player in the drivers seat, usually by letting him frame his own scenes. By individual scene-framing and other means we facilitate that the player is telling himself what impact the conflict have had on his character. It is largely up to himself to narrate how his character deals with the fact that he stabbed a friend of his to death, in the heat of combat. Will he rejoice in the victory, or mourn his comrade? Will he shake it off, or go into a dark mental hole? How will he deal with the heros welcome back home? When his father tells him he is proud of him, will he break down and confess then? And when his girlfriend giggles and cuddle him, will he feel too old and scarred for her, suddenly?

      There is a lot of beautiful and exciting interaction to be had here, issues both emotional, social and moral, fueled by the conflict we have had.

      - etc.

It's as much a shift in focus as a new way of doing conflicts, in my game. We make dirty conflicts fast and furious, and then we focus the main part of interaction on the dirty moral field created by the conflicts.

      By some strange reason this kind of play is as fun as anything ...

      I imagine that this way of doing it can be used for other purposes too, so please read this as my very special way of working this field at present, in this one game, nothing more.

      \n Let me offer some clarification on the substance of my position in this discussion:

      There is no doubt that the use of maps are popular, has been and still are. What we are discussing here is not the popularity, but the inherent weaknesses and strengths of map-use.

      I've argued that the use of maps are very good for role-playing, them being great sources of inspiration, and a good way to frame the setting. Maps turned to good use in role-playing games are feeding the interaction, and result in higher quality game-play. I'm a great proponent for the use of maps this way, and have used considerable energy to explore the possibilities in practice.

      I've also argued that the use of maps in specific conflict-situations, as tactical terrain for miniatures or other counters, are problematic. So I advice against using maps that way. It tend to bog down the tempo, and steal focus from other elements which are as important to the situations at hand. I have experienced that the use of sketches to clarify terrain in a planning situation is very good, but transporting those sketches into the fray of battle is to invite a stiff and slow way of playing the conflicts, and that's not very "action", nor very close to the actual sense of a conflict. To use sketches to clarify positioning in the rare instances that there is a real need for it, is good, but put them away when it's done. You don't have to base the whole situation on the use of such sketches, or formal battle-mats. That's my point of view.

      On the other side; designers exploring the possibilities for using battle-maps in better ways, is welcome to prove me wrong in my assumptions. D&D4 has had a go at it, with a lot of resources, but failed to convince me that this is a wise direction of classical rpg-design. Still: I'm not against people trying to make this kind of games. Anyone may make games of any variant for all I care. I only advice against using your creative strengths on a direction of design that I see as ripe with old-school weaknesses. I fear it would be a waste of energy. But please do, if you want to.

      Posted By: TomasHVMI've argued that the use of maps are very good for role-playing, them being great sources of inspiration, and a good way to frame the setting. Maps turned to good use in role-playing games are feeding the interaction, and result in higher quality game-play. I'm a great proponent for the use of maps this way, and have used considerable energy to explore the possibilities in practice.
      \nI can share a quick example of this.

      A few weeks ago I GMed a fantasy game where the players needed to fight some bandits, who had a camp in the forest. Before the game began I laid out some Dungeon Tiles to fill a card table with the map.

      One PC can turn into a small bird. This exhausts him so it is not helpful during combat. But his aerial scouting a few hours prior served as an excuse for the players to be familiar with the map and the location of the bandit's tents, wagon, campfire, etc. Of course, the PCs did not know where the actual bandits were at the moment the game began (a few hours later).

      The players were able to plan an approach and attack that used the terrain and how it meshed with their PCs' abilities. Much more time was spent (and fun had) planning than in the action combat. The fight did happen on the map, but we play a diceless game so the action still moved quickly.

      I think it's a gimmick that could work if you want the surprise of putting something cool out on the table (an unexpected mini or, sorry, map!), but any sort of prolonged darkness just leads to annoyance and discomfort, a lesson I learned during my theater education that's stuck with me since.

      \n One of my favorite play styles prioritizes subjective experience, as well as tactical decisions as a subset of that. In trying to support this is Delve, I've tried out maps. They help immensely in clearly establishing who is what and where, but they pretty much ruin the subjective experience.

      My solution has been to ditch the maps because of their downsides for this play style, and to replace their virtues with a different system. That system is verbal consensus.

      Basically: when a moment of decision arrives, clarify relevant factors first, then choose. It's a hard habit to teach, but an absurdly easy one to practice once learned. I've found that it takes the place of a map quite perfectly for the purposes of deciding whether to grab the artifact and run for the exit based on the badguy's location.

      Posted By: David Berg[Maps] pretty much ruin the subjective experience.
      I want to clarify here that I'm not talking about ruining engagement, investment, fun, or ability to envision the fiction. How maps affect those concerns seems to vary greatly from person to person. The "subjective experience" I'm talking about is very specific, and is only a concern when prioritized. I've known a lot of players and groups who did in fact prioritize this, and I personally love it, so that's why I'm acting as if it's relevant.

      So what do I mean here by "subjective experience"? I mean a sense of transportation, so that the "me" that looks out at my environment and decides what to do is firmly located inside the game's fiction. In absolute terms, this is impossible, but, y'know, the closer the better. For this goal, physical props are pretty much definitionally a hindrance, and I've found maps to be among the most hindering of the bunch. (Unless the characters have a map, in which case the map is awesome.)
