Embracing retcons for surrealism

\n
\n

\n I was "reading" (listening to) Robert McKee's Story: Substance, Structure, Style and the Principles of Screenwriting, and he was talking about how if your story doesn't feel right after you make some decisions later in the writing, you have to go back and fix things so they will feel right. It's an acknowledgement of the fact that the creation of story requires that you go back and reevaluate what you've done so far, and change it.

At first glance, this would appear not to be compatible with roleplaying games, but I got to thinking that this would totally be appropriate in a game where you wanted some surrealism. I love the notion of going back and changing what actually happened (without time travel) \u2014 something utterly bizarre \u2014 to "heal" story problems.

I'm not sure how this would be achieved necessarily, but it's the seed of something for me, I think.

    \n I wonder if a plot point system could be used to tag failures in the ongoing game and then those events could be changed in future play. I think that changing just anything might lead to choice overload. By forcing people to tag them it makes them think about how things would be different if this didn't happen, which might be a very useful tool toward being better players.

    \n I think this could introduce confusion into the game for the players in that what they remember happening is not what "actually" happened. Now, if the bizarre elements you are going for is a feeling schizophrenia (I don't think this is the term I'm going for it is close) then it could work well. How far back you go in play might be a factor: changing an event from this play session vs from a session over a month ago would have a different feel.
    \n
    \nI'm reminded of the third novel in the Dark Tower series by Stephen King. In that, the Gunslinger ends up going back in time and saving someone who was died from his point of view in the future. This created two sets of memories in the survivor that overlap. This is what I would imagine a player would feel when recalling the events, and this could then be realized and enacted by the character.
    \n
    \nI don't know if that is the best example, but multiple memories of the same event could create a sort of psychedelic feeling in a game. It might be a way of bringing to play stories similar to the movie 12 Monkeys (not the time traveling bit, but the corrupted memories that leave the protagonist questioning his reality).
    \n
    \nI'm trying to remember an example of actual play that would be appropriate, but can't come up with one at the moment. I'll chew on it for a bit and see what turns up.

    \n Wow,

    This is slightly eerie, but in a new game I am play-testing with my regular Wednesday night group, we encountered (and if fact, were actively moving towards) that point in the story just last week.

    Background: The game features loose rules for paranormal abilities that allow characters to manipulate the story itself so long as they remain true to their character's M.O.D, so, in this game, Ichi the Master Swordsman can do whatever he wants to the story so long as he does so within the confines of being kick-ass with his sword (or something tangential, like being uber noble). Ichi's masterswordsmanship is not reflected by having more attack bonus or hit points or whatever - the game is not a reality simulator. So, simple.

    The game's fiction (and rules) also suggest that there are people who are somehow ultra-aware of this and can manipulate the world without reservation. Ichi the Master Swordsman might have uncanny martial timing, but Ichi the Awakened Monster can actually tell you he's already killed you 5 minutes ago and it will become true.

    In our game, the crew of a space ship ran afoul of the big bag corporate entity and spent a season's worth of gaming running away, looking for something to clear their identities. Ultimately, they ended up on wasteland Earth, were they found rumors of some guy that could help them get their lives back. What they assumed to be a master hacker or something turned out to be one of these "Anomaly" people who threatened to change what they had done to draw the ire of the company in the first place. It was a very interesting game.

    Now, it would seem as if this were a normal "Time Travel" situation - either the players get a replay on what happened, or they find that time is immutable, or that THEY were responsible for everything they thought was coincidence the first time around - but that's not how this game is playing out. In the weeks before we met the Anomaly, we would spend each game following two plot lines - one that had the crew trying to find the guy that would clear their reps, and the other that would take one or two crew members and examine how the got to know each other.

    It was surreal - these characters weren't heavily defined or developed before play, but as we played through what was going that week in either "timeline", it informed us of what had already happened. One week they were at odds with each other because some crew members wanted to kill a prisoner and the others did not - the next week we discovered that the reason tempers were so high was because some of the crew members HAD BEEN prisoners themselves. None of this was scripted or developed by a GM, the game forced us to fill in the gaps of WHY things were working out one way versus another, and in a surreal way it seemed as if the story was actually designed to accomplish this.

    I suspect this comes from pareidolia (not Andrew Morris's game, but the phenomena) but it worked extremely well. If I can bottle this shit, I'd make a mint.

    - Frank Hablawi

    \n If you remove the linearity of the narrative, in effect removing strict causation, players need to have something on which to base their decisions apart from their character's desires which tend to become meaningless under such circumstances. Under such "dream logic", I think things could work but I'm not sure what the status of conflict/task resolution would be.

    That said, in our just finished game of Trail of Cthulhu I retconned the contents of some document so that the characters would have a reason to do something (travel in time actually). The reason wasn't apparent at first but the plot soon resolved as the players engaged with it without worrying about a solid justification, except that "the document explains it".

    This isn't the first time we've done this in Trail games under development.

    \n This game...

    Recursive Tales of the Infinite Multiverse

    ...involves going back to any point in the story where something could have gone the other way and finding out what happens when if it does go the other way. It outlines a way you could "mark" a certain choice for this kind of change in another game without too much note-keeping.

    Also, one thing that happened in an Otherkind hack I ran with my students once: Players introduced facts that changed everything about what was going on -- an evil prince wasn't actually evil; he was being possessed by the evil magic of the crown he wore, and once the crown was knocked off his head he became a good guy again. It was kind of like a retcon, except it took the form of a new piece of information people just didn't know before, and it fit into the story nicely. Some sort of flashback mechanic could be used to similar effect, as in the game 3:16.

    \n Considering Wave is dead, the coolness of your idea is tragic, Frank. I could totally see that working.

    If I were to do this I would emphatically not want it to be a time travel story. Maybe a story of liars, who can lie so well they change the past. Really, though, what's key is the surrealism of it. Time travel doesn't necessarily feel surreal.

    Steve's right, though. Without some way to ground people all their decisions are meaningless. In other words, this would need some serious game design behind it to make it a fun thing to do.

    I think what I'm getting at here is consciously acknowledging that stories need revision. Allow yourself to be painted into a corner freely, then change what you need to so that the final story is cohesive if you look at it by stepping back and seeing what is "true" at the end. But I'd want to preserve the what-the-fuckery of having that change happen and having people be aware of it.

    A thorny knot.

    \n I'm with you on the lack of desire to make this about time travel. From the vibe I got in actual play, I think that we've all pretty much garnered a healthy idea of the various ways time travel stories work - so there really isn't anything special in seeing "revisions" expressed in this way.

    So, putting that aside, I see two ways of allowing revisions in a game and acknowledging them.

    1. Mulligan.
    We screwed up, forgot something, or just plan missed a chance to add a plot point earlier on. Let's all be grownups and pretend that we didn't, ok? So, maybe the Trail game Steve mentioned would be a good example of this. That ancient text we got a few games back is actually full of plot-hooks that were previously untranslated or mistranslated. I find this solution or use as pretty straight forward and not too exciting to talk about. It's fine, of course, but pretty basic.

    2. World in Flux.
    This is more of the meat of what we're possibly looking for - making the game be about the change. The world is ever evolving and nothing remains forever as it had originally been recorded. In my notes for my game, I had the players as Librarians guarding (and sometimes manipulating) the Scripts of Life, so they were the authors of their own and the world's history - and the conflict came when dark sorts worked to rewrite history in their favor.

    I also really love the idea of using this to rework Barron Munchhausen; a game of storytelling liars where you can not only plant the seed of the next player's story, but you can modify what had happened as well. I tried to work on this for a year, but I got stuck on the fact that the game just ended up feeling like a really fun improv routine - hence the refocus on Librarians and the nature of writing a good story.

    Rob, I was slightly upset when I heard Wave was dropped... but I know that lots of its source code has been released as open source. Oddly, maybe now is the time to actually attempt to develop for it...

    \n Compare one of my favourite movies of all time, Hoodwinked (or presumably a lot of the detective noir fiction it's based on, but I'm no expert on that). The narrative repeats four times, as retellings by the four protagonists, but they all have different stories of what happened from different points-of-views.

    I've been thinking a bit about "do-overs" in RPGs in general. I've tried the technique a couple of times, in DRYH and Lacuna (both surreal games, of course), and I think there's a lot of mileage in the idea of playing something over again but making different choices. Similar to Braid, I guess.

    \n I have an example!

    When we played Trail of Cthulhu at Dragonmeet, someone went mad. When someone goes mad, you send them out of the room, and you all decide their madness, without them knowing. So, for example, you decide they've always been a Hobo. And when they come back in, they behave as if they're a doctor, but everyone else treats them like a hobo. It's fun.

    Now, our game was originally set in 1930. When someone went mad, we decided that they only thought it was 1930. In fact, it was 1318. So, for the rest of the game, she talked about guns and cars, where everyone saw crossbows and horses.

    It was surreal.
