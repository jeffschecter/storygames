\n So what with all the kerfuffle over my game about C20 scientists, I've rather neglected one or two other projects that I've been working on recently and should really be talking about. So here's the first one. SkipJack does for character creation what Microscope does for world building. In the game, the players choose from a list of everyday household objects - cheque stubs, umbrella, computer monitor etc. - and weave a story around them by taking each in turn and framing scenes involving them which attempt to tell us something about the mysterious Protagonist at the heart of the story.

So in other words the player group creates a character from scratch, and roleplays that character and any salient NPCs that may appear along the way. I've only had one playtest so far, but it was amazing how the players were able to build up a whole life trajectory purely from a bunch of objects. I think I need another two or three playtests before I can release a beta version into the wild, so if any o' y'all fancy giving it a spin, feel free. Naturally, all playtesters will be credited in the eventual book (if you let me know who you are!).

    \n People are very good at constructing meaning and connections between things that dont really much if any by way of actual connections. In a way that's how conspiracy theories work and I have read a novel where that is what the writer does and the conspiracy is based on something totally mundane, in fact a shopping list. So there is a parallel there with what you are doing with SkipJack.

    Its also a little bit like the power you can get with something like film editing. There is a film joke about Charles Bronson and his lack of expression:
    https://pbs.twimg.com/media/BkTsZoSCMAEmcnI.jpg
    But actually for some directors his lack of expression actually helps them to convey emotion in the film because they fill it in with what they are cutting to or from and music. The editing of the film and the atmosphere of the music creates the meaning. Charles Bronson looks at an innocent being attacked to dramatic music - the emotion reads as anger. He looks at a starving child while mournful cellos play - his emotion is sadness.

    Its all ultimately about juxtaposition and the way we learn to read events by cause and effect. If you put two objects together and tell people they are related, people will construct a relationship and invent that cause and effect relationship.

    I think you would have a lot of potential there and a bit of a fun twist to the sort of approach already taken by something like Rory's Story Cubes.

    Have you tried having players each bring several objects or pictures of objects to a game and using those in the same way?
