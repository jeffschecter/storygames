\n So I recently entered the 200 word RPG challenge with a little game called A House Near Mother.

It is the first time I have ever completed a game I started working on.

I was wondering if you good people would mind giving me some feedback on it.



A House Near Mother
Haha (Mummy) isn\u2019t well, so you move to a new house in the countryside, close to her hospital. A Monster lives in the woods nearby, who takes you on Adventures to cheer you up. Many spirits live in the woods.

You are a Neko (Patient 3, Determined 2, Reliable 1), Tanuki (Determined 3, Reliable 2, Patient 1) or a Kitsune (Reliable 3, Patient 2, Determined 1)

These are your Virtues.

Doing your Chores and going on Adventures require Checks. Roll 1d10+Virtue. 8+: you succeed.

Each Check is tied to a Virtue.

Example: cooking requires Patience.

You must do one chore before going out to Adventure, or more to be helpful.

For each Check, you spend one Wakefulness. When you have spent 10, you fall asleep. In the morning, you regain all Wakefulness.

The spirits Favour you for doing your chores. Gain 2 Favour per Chore. Favour lets you reroll a Check, without getting more tired.

You gain one Sorrow each day. If you complete an Adventure before bedtime, the Monster eats one Sorrow.

If all Sorrow is below the number of Children * 2 after 5 days, then Haha comes home. If not, then Chichi (Daddy) has bad news.

    Could you perhaps give me an example of what you mean by a structure of play in this case?
    \nIt's what @2097 said. Structure for the participants, not the characters.

    For examples, look at the winners and almost all the finalists from last year. LOVEINT is pretty close to your game, when it comes to how the structure could look like.

    Here is a good example of one step in the structure of play from the 3rd place All Fall Down: "Play in scenes. Shine the spotlight. Everything is broken. There is never enough. Everyone is desperate."

    It not only tells what to do but in what way to do it.

    Personally, I'm also a little in love with the finalist Ring: telling the tales of trees. Be sure to also check out No Sleep Tonight, because note writing in advance could be a way of create tension through uncertainty, and it's something you could implement in your game - as a step in your structure of play. Notes used in that way not only brings uncertainty, but it's uncertainty from the players instead of faceless randomness from dice.
