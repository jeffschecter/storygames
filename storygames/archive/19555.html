\n Setting: 10,000 Stars - a science fiction wuxia inspired (ie not accurate nor ancient-china-in-space) setting with various character types, all of which are likely to be mutually exclusive in terms of rules.

Alchemic Heroes are genetically modified/engineered characters who manipulate a resource called 'Element 5' (mcguffin substance of choice ) to enhance their bodies to give them kungfu-esque powers. There are several builds a hero can choose from, specialising in the usual rpg types: combat, social, stealthy/shadowy, etc. I don't intend for there to be a thousand and one choices because of a) analysis paralysis and b) it's way too much work.

In creating this character type I amd stuck on how to make it unique. If the player is merely presented with a list of abilities to choose from, then what makes this character type unique? I can't think of a way to represent genetic modification in an interesting way. The simplest way is for PC's to acquire E5 to power their abilities, but this isn't Vampire - it's not a resource acquired by killing people. It's a rare resource in the setting used in the process of genetic modification. That unfortunately makes it somewhat mundane (unlike drinking blood which, while macabre, creates the kind of narrative that Vampire thrives on). Also having to periodically acquire this stuff will just put players off - it's not fun if every other session the player has to go through a process to acquire the resource to make his character fun to play.

Thoughts?

    \n Instead of making the alchemical process a resource-hunting issue, make it an internal system of integration and growth (as historical alchemical systems have been, and as a wuxia alchemy should be). Alchemical heroes do not need more Element 5 because they already produce it themselves, as part of the continuous process that brews in their gut ever since it was began by the initial injections. The challenge - and choice - for such heroes is internal in a not-wholly-spiritual way, as the continuous, unstable alchemical process moves forward relentlessly, taking them from humanity to superhumanity, or subhumanity, or some sort of stable plateau.

    In practice you break your ideas for alchemical super powers into building blocks of which beginning alchemical heroes get a couple to begin with. After regular intervals the character's internal alchemy produces more, depending on what they already have, and what manner of internal alchemical balances the player opts to emphasize. This is a semi-controlled process that might involve a special diet, meditation and whatever, dice rolls included. Successful control means that the player gets to choose which alchemical building blocks to integrate next, while with failure they get random alchemical processes occurring within them instead.

    Ideally a wuxia hero of alchemical bend will be able to avoid "plateauing" into a stable state where all of the Element 5 in their body gets tied down in unreactive specific processes and compounds. These "plateaus" are specific, named fantasy critter archetypes basically, recognized from prior alchemical hero experiments. Perhaps roughly 4% of all alchemicals grow wings and stultify at that stage, for example. Character to which such happens are basically no longer open-potential alchemics, but will from then on have to forge their heroic destiny as wookies or whatever sort of critter it is they stabilize as.

    However, keeping the process on-going is not without its risks either, even if it is the only way to achieve the true goal of alchemy, the absolute immortality: as long as the Element 5 yet brews within, there is always the danger of uncontrolled mutation and unwanted change that is capable of transforming the foolish and weak into most lively awfulnesses. Rampant cancerous growths are the most mundane of such possibilities; even more dangerous are ill-fated cyclic processes that destroy or mutate the internal regulatory organs - such as the brain - that the alchemic hero uses to hold onto their humanity.

    \nWell, that's a basic tradeoff, I think. If you're going to condition a PC's abilities on certain behaviour (such as gathering a resource), then it either feeds into the narrative, in which case you need to balance mechanical issues and story goals; or it doesn't, in which case it's going to be boring to everyone except the player involved, and possibly to that player as well. (I mean narratively boring; it could still be interesting from other viewpoints: visual appeal, optimization, etc.)

    (Incidentally, In my experience blood in Vampire falls largely into the second category - gathering it is generally boring, and therefore usually ignored.)

    The trick would be for the GM to understand when E5 needs to be straightforwardly available, and when to make an issue of it. Even if only one out of ten adventures threaten the Alchemic's supply, that's more than enough to make the E5 economy a defining feature of the class.

    \n If you want Element 5 to be a resource Alchemic Heroes manage, there has to be some risk or consequence to running out. Otherwise it's not actually a meaningful resource. Here are a couple of ways you make it meaningful without making the game revolve around gathering Element 5 or severely handicapping Alchemic Heroes:\n
      \n
    1. The ability to stockpile large quantities of Element 5. e.g. the setting should allow for plenty of enemy stockpiles, veins, giant mutant beasts with hearts of Element 5--anything the PCs can go after as a quest. Large treasure rewards should contain it. Alchemic Heroes should also be able to hold on to large quantities of Element 5 once they acquire it--if they're capped at a small quantity, they'll need to refresh often.
      2. \n
    2. Have a low-friction way to get small quantities of Element 5. Maybe the setting bad guys or some monsters have small quantities of Element 5 in their blood, which can be extracted by someone with the skill. You can get back enough for one use or half-use in the time it would take to skin a beast, or take several hours to get a few uses worth. Higher skill means better returns.
      3. \n
    3. Allow Alchemic Heroes to recover some of the Element 5 they used recently instead of health. Alternatively, if they're out of Element 5, let them spend health to fuel low-level abilities.
      4. \n
    \n#1 means that adventuring with an Alchemic Hero makes Element 5 important, but in an interesting, situation-generating way. #2 and #3 give the Alchemic Hero a way to limp along between jackpots if they blew all their Element 5. They make Element 5 management similar in scale to D&D wizard spells/day management.
