\n I'm working on a mechanic where damage received is translated to "Scars". Scars may only be healed by buying them off. Each Scar will have its own buyoff condition stated in terms of a goal.

For instance, here's what a Scar would look like on the character sheet:

Scar: Convince Clive to forgive you

or

Scar: Send a message to Jasper on behalf of Paul

These would be placed on the player by the rest of the table, decided as a group. So you take damage and receive a Scar, Tom, Jeff, and I are going to openly converse about what the buyoff should be, you can of course provide your own input but have no final decision power. If we can't decide unanimously we'll vote as it shouldn't be a lengthy process.

Would this seriously miff you, if the group placed a forced buyoff condition on your character? I imagine group dynamics and the social contract would play a huge part. I really think it would work for my group, but would it work for yours? Does it even matter?

    \nGood questions. I'm still trying to work things out but yes, Scars do have other effects. The primary concern is you can only have so many Scars before you hit rock bottom. What happens when you exceed your upper limit is your character will leave the story perhaps through death, perhaps through physically leaving.

    Also, I'm flirting with the idea of making the number of Scars you possess determine how potent you are in Social Conflict. I imagine the opposite of a death spiral where you become more potent the closer you get to oblivion - hence if you buyoff a great deal of your Scars you'll actually be less able to get what you want. This should cause a cycle of get into combat and take some damage -> buyoff Scars/accomplish other goals -> Fight recklessly again -> and so on...

    \n Charles, since you're polling, I'll say, "Yes, I'd be miffed." Maybe not all the time. But I bet it would happen. So, it's no an issue of principle for me -- it's a matter of risking a deflation of fun.


    I have a question:

    Why do it this way? (That's not a rhetorical question. I'd love to hear what you're going for. I don't know how all your pieces fit together.)



    I have some comments:

    Some great conceptual innovations have come down the RPG turnpike the last nine years.

    The greatest might be, in my view, the notion that we want to jigger the fiction so that the Players get to play fiction with their characters that they -- the Players -- are actually interested in. (One common (but not universal) default used to be: "This is the story, so your character is interested in it, because your character is interested in it, so play a character who interested in the story whether your are or aren't, because being a "good" player is the ability to do exactly that.")

    These days the mechanics of games allow the Players lay down Issues on their character sheets to tell the GM the things they want in a game; Players author the inciting incidents that kick off play; Players create Beliefs, Attributes and so on -- that inform all the adventure to come. The beauty of this is that the GM doesn't have "lure" the Players into an adventure or subplots that might not interest the Player -- they're genuinely, already interested.

    Would it be possible for the Player to create his or her scar, with input and kibitzing from the other players?

    Again, I don't know what you're going for or how the pieces of your game fit together. You might have the coolest thing going in the world. But if my fellow players give me something they genuinely think is the coolest thing since Selma Heyak, and it really does nothing for me... then I'm stuck with this thing that doesn't interest me that the rules force me to deal with.

    You make some good points. What I envision Scars doing is informing play and allowing the group to flag what they think is interesting concerning the given character. My experience concerning this sort of thing with my group is that even the dudds aren't all that bad and can be worked with - sometimes turning out to be much better than you originally expected.

    \n I think in some cases I would miss the feeling of spontaneity that I get when, having suffered some kind of setback earlier in a session, I suddenly see an opportunity to turn it around in my favor; having the group say "okay, this is what you need to do to buy off the damage you just received" means that now I'm driven towards doing exactly that thing, and maybe even only that thing. If getting Clive's forgiveness is the course that's been set for me, then I have less freedom to decide that, no, I fucking HATE Clive and I don't want or need his forgiveness...in fact, I'm going to make things worse with him and destroy his life to make myself feel better.

    But like Christopher Kubasik says above, it's not really a matter of principle for me, it's just an "I doubt I would be 100% happy with this in every situation" thing. I suppose that could be mitigated by keeping the buyoffs relatively generic/flexible ("Resolve things with Clive" rather than "Get Clive's forgiveness"), or by periodically renegotiating the buyoffs ("I know we said I need to get Clive's forgiveness, but the way he treats Juliet just pisses me off -- how about we change the buyoff to 'Ruin Clive utterly' instead? That's much more interesting to me.").

    Of course, at that point it may no longer be worth putting into mechanics in the first place. I dunno.

    \n So on a more productive note than my previous post, I'm working on a design in which other players can change your character's traits. Which is basically the same kind of deal as your Scars, since it happens as the result of conflicts. However, in that design, you get rewarded for playing out the changed traits. That's in my mind a somewhat better solution, because as Vincent would say, it aligns the interests of the players.

    In your case, think about Scars as Keys (from The Shadow of Yesterday). What if, instead of having to work off a penalty, they get a bonus whenever they address or ultimately resolve the Scar?

    Say you're the golden boy, head of the Kingsguard, one of the best swordsmen in all of Westeros. Then, a mercenary decides to cut off your swordhand. What would be more fun to play out for you as the player, having a big-ass penalty until you retrain yourself, or getting rewarded every time you play out how hindered you are, how your ego is more hurt than your body, and how you have to reshape your identity?

    Edit: Oops, I misremembered the death spiral. Opposite effectiveness is good, but I still think a key-like effect could add to that.

    \n Sometimes I'm a narrativistic masochist. Sometimes I love to be forced. Sometimes when the game tells me "your character is going to do this" I say "Oh no! What are you doing to my character, you evil system! This turns my concept on its ears!" and I secretly love it, because now I have to roll with that and make it make sense. If your game is that way and I know it from the start, I'm gonna love it.

    Sometimes another player wants me to do something with my character that I'm not interested in, and I do it, and I try to make it awesome, because that player wants me to, and I want to make the game fun for that player. And then she will make the game more fun for me some other time. If your game does that, and that "other player" is actually the rest of the play group, I'm gonna love it.

    Sometimes I encounter a rule that I don't like, but that's really easy to change. Then I change it and it's fun and good. I suspect your game will, in groups where this makes people uncomfortable, be drifted this way so that it requires the consent of the player. If people are assholes they can abuse the rule, but you can't and shouldn't try to assholeproof your game. Besides, the rest of the group has to agree, so the group will have to consist of pretty much all assholes, and then who cares?

    Also, I agree that you will lose some things by doing this. As Accounting for Taste says, you might lose the spontaneity of finding a way to react. But then again, you gain the choosing of how someone else's character will react. So I don't see it as a net loss, just a different way of doing things that will do different things.

    All in all, I say keep it. Though the ability to remove a scar through some other, very costly, way sounds awesome, too. I WON'T forgive Fernando. I CAN'T! But to avoid it, I have to sacrifice my Compassion. Then it's a bang, and everyone loves getting banged.
