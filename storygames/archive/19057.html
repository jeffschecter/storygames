\n
\n Success is an important thing to fiction and to gamers alike. We roll a lot of dice to determine success or degree of success for many kinds of actions. What is a good ratio of success to failure in your game? There are certainly times as a GM that a player ends up in a situation where I REALLY want them to make a roll. Not just because it would further the story but also because there are times where, narratively, it would be SO COOL. It's not just a numbers game either... there are always CERTAIN rolls that we want to go one way or another.

Failure is pretty crappy if it comes at a bad time. I've seen and run games where the player tries to do something really cool which advances the story and would make them look pretty badass... and they miss. Everyone feels a little let down. If that were a movie... people would have left the theater at that point. So failure is pretty terrible. You could make a game (and it would still be a game) where the players never fail. (You could use a, "Yes... AND" mechanic.) However... without the expectation of failure we would lose a lot of thrill with our successes.

There are a lot of games that deal with this in different ways. What games are more successful at this? What have you done in your games to make the successes and failures come at better times?

    \n To explore this issue, I think you need to understand the nature of failure as well as success.

    If by failure all you mean is a "miss" then I totally get how that might be dramatically disappointing. That's why games with simulationist combat systems do nothing for me. If a failed roll advances the story somehow however (eg, leads to capture or some other way in which the stakes are raised) then it isn't disappointing at all in my view.

    I quite like the philosophy behind a lot of the moves in Apocalypse World and it's derivatives in which a success is always achievable on a failed die roll, but at a price. I like that a lot better than systems which have "hero points" or similar in which you can just spend a point to turn a failure into a success.

    \n Let's take an example of the classic situation: Picking the lock.

    1. You succeed, but the lock is an absolute bastard, and you have several moments of incredible frustration. Mark off the "Angry Condition" (Which prevents adding potent bonuses to future rolls, and can only be recovered later on in a set resting situation).

    2. You fail, and the tromping of armored boots and sudden shouts mean that you've been spotted by the Guard.

    ---

    Benefit of the first situation: You get what you wanted.
    Drawback: You are at a disadvantage for future rolls.

    Benefit of the Second Situation: No disadvantage to future rolls
    Drawback: You failed, and there is a new situation to deal with.

    The idea is that in the first case, you are mortgaging your future to pay for the present. In the second situation, you are accepting the failure and dealing with the next issue that's come up, causing the story to be pushed in a totally new direction.

    \n Well, they certainly don't get to choose whether they succeed and take the condition or fail with a complication. It's less "they can" and more "the GM chooses". That might be pedantry, though, so bluh.

    Conditions are fierce for a number of reasons, some of which CG touched on. They're pretty fierce and take away your options and resources. For example, Hungry saps away one point of precious disposition in conflicts, which puts you one step closer to defeat in a conflict. Afraid disallows you from attempting skills untrained, or helping others (my players learned to hate this one more than anything). Dead is a condition, but can only be inflicted when you're already either Injured or Sick, or if you fail particular types of conflicts. Additionally, you accrue a Condition at the end of every 4th turn, in increasing severity based on the conditions you already have, and as CG points out if you already have the rest you get Dead. Before Dead there are only six conditions, so even if some of them are trivial to take care of you want to make sure you're not in a position to get the Dead condition.

    With all of that, even though a condition comes with success, sometimes it's nice to know that the only thing you have to worry about is that, well, not only were you unable to pick that lock but now your lockpick is jammed all the fuck up in the lock, bent to shit. We can still kick the door down if it's that important, right? Hmm, there's another passage though, and this is turn 4...

    \n Options abound! Here are 3 I like:

    \u2022 If all failure is unacceptable, then play a game where you roll only for how much you succeed, and what complications arise.

    \u2022 If only certain types of failure, like character death, are unacceptable, then play a game with different stakes -- insanity, mutilation, humiliation, loss of status -- whatever gets your adrenaline pumping without producing outcomes you can't accept.

    \u2022 If moments of unacceptable failure arise unpredictably, then use metagame currency spends (fate points, story points, hero points, whatever) to nullify them.

    Pushing the bounds of what you can accept is fun too.

    @Golux, it sounds to me like you want stakes that players care about but which won't derail play. For this, I'd avoid rolls for vital progress (Plot Hallway), and instead use rolls for character impact. You can still roll when you pick the lock, but the roll is not about picking the lock. The lock is going to open either way. What's at stake is whether the character gets level-drained in the process.

    You could use this rule either (a) always, so players get used to it, or (b) only when failure isn't acceptable, in which case that fact would need to be called out.

    I don't think there's any way that the specific suspense of "can my character accomplish this task?" can coexist with "only one possible answer is acceptable" in the same moment. If you want to split the difference, though, you could just put resources at stake as a second step -- if the roll fails, then the player needs to spend something they didn't want to spend. But if the roll succeeds, you get that "Yay I won!" experience*, and the prospect of not getting that experience introduces some tension.

    * unless you're too system-conscious, like I am

    \n Agreed, this is something players will care about. I see a number of possible approaches:

    1) Just get buy-in up front from the whole group that sometimes the GM will remove pass/fail from a roll for the good of the game. Then when it comes up in play, GM announces it so no one gets blind-sided. (I wouldn't try this unless your group communicates extremely well about this sort of thing.)

    2) Break the expectation that the skills on the character sheet dictate what gets rolled. Make it clear that nothing gets rolled outside of certain game criteria, such as risk, difficulty, stakes, or whatever is right for your game. For example, "Never roll unless the thing you're doing could injure someone." If you're picking a lock and no one could be hurt, don't roll. If someone could be hurt, then you get to care about the Lock Picking number on your sheet. Probably a bad example, but my point is that it's consistent and easy to apply, and players won't be confused or surprised.

    Another example: Sorcerer says "Only roll when there's a conflict between characters (NPCs included)." I think that's an interesting one. Might not suit you, but maybe illustrates some possibilities.

    3) If your group can handle the above, then you get more options with Deliverator's suggestion about Scene Types. Yeah, lockpicking works differently at different times, but those different times are all explicitly labeled.

    4) Manage your target/difficulty numbers. If you've accidentally GMed yourself into a situation where it's okay to injure a character but not okay for them to fail at Lock Picking, just establish that this lock is not hard to pick ("difficulty 2"). This should probably be used in tandem with the next approach:

    5) Standardize a consequence system beyond pass/fail. Whether it's "see if you get hurt" or "see if you need to spend" or "some unrelated bad thing" or a roll on a random table -- do this every time dice are rolled. So, even when it's okay for me to fail at picking a lock, and even when I'm mostly excited about whether or not I succeed, there's also a chance for other stuff to go wrong (or to go well!). Just as the GM controls difficulty numbers for pass/fail, the GM can also control likelihood numbers for good/bad consequences (if you find that fun).

    If this sounds like too many steps, there are ways to streamline it -- results of the pass/fail roll can double as consequence triggers (even, odd, matching, highest, lowest, etc.).

    6) Something that might make the preceding ideas more intuitive to some players: Use broader skill categories, like a Thief Background without any specifics like Lock Picking.

    7) I've saved this for last, as I assume it's farthest from what you want, but it's also the easiest option, so I'll say it just in case. Make Lock Picking not be a thing. Instead use other sorts of events to trigger rolls. Example: character stats/skills in Danger, Fear, Precision, etc. will be rolled when the fictional situation is dangerous, or scary, or requires precision, completely regardless of what task the character is performing. (I'd consider this a simpler and less half-assed version of #2 above, but it does eliminate some stuff you might not want to lose.)
