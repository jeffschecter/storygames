So, this is taken from the "What Don't You Get About RPGs" thread.

The question raised was:
What would a RPG look like that didn't portray the PCs (or all the PCs) as the protagonists?

I have a fairly vested interest in this, because in Boulevard (my game of Punk Rock Tragedy) it's becoming obvious that the characters aren't often protagonists. I'm reading Requiem for a Dream right now. It's a version put out at the same time the movie was being shot, and director Darren Aronofsky wrote one of the forewords, and in it he wrote:
The novel had amazing structure and it translated very well into three acts. But something was strange. While breaking it down I realized that whenever something good was supposed to happen to a character, something bad happened. Because of this, I couldn't figure out who the hero of the novel was.

After sketching out all the character arcs I realized they were all upside down. So I flipped them over, and suddenly I had a "Eureka!" The hero wasn't Sara, it wasn't Harry, not Tyrone, not Marion.

The hero was the characters' enemy: Addiction. The book is a manifesto on Addiction's triumph over the Human Spirit.
First of all, he's right. Addiction is the protagonist.

Second of all, that's what Boulevard is sorta like. The PCs lose. That's an intrinsic part of the game. Their addictions, causes, places, and failures consume them and move on. Being destroyed is hard-coded into the game.

So... are the players ACTUALLY playing Addiction, Despair, Circumstance and other such concepts? Are they playing a protagonist at all?
...does a game where the PCs aren't protagonists simply devolve into a shit-kicking session, where players beat fictional characters to death?

    Going by the definition of a protagonist as someone who wants something
    and is taking action to get it--

    I've been messing with the idea of the protagonist being an NPC who is
    defined entirely by what they want at any given moment of play. And
    the rest of the party then defines themselves in relation to that motive.

    To explain this I usually use Luke Skywalker, because he's easy to see
    as blank-- not very deep-- beyond a series of impulses that he chases:
    wants to finish his chores and go to town/ attend the academy/
    find the escaped droid/ check on his aunt and uncle/ escape Tattoine/
    save the princess/ learn about the Force. Also, it's easy to convince
    players that playing Han Solo or Obi Wan or Darth Vader-- isn't a booby
    prize compared to getting to play the 'official' protagonist.

    Scene framing would begin with the players brainstorming possible current motives
    for the protagonist NPC, and then using a combination of bidding and fortune
    to determine which motive is played. (So that the highest bid is favored, but
    a lucky roll could let a lower bid prevail.) Then each player controls their
    own PC's relation to the protagonist's trajectory.

    This ought to work like a cross country game of 'fetch'. The pack agrees which
    way to throw the 'stick'. Then everybody decides whether to help chase it or
    whether to impede progress. Repeat across varying terrain. The immediate
    goal of the story should always be clear, as should the over-arching story direction.

    I know that the "GM PC" is a much hated railroading device. I hope to avoid that by
    keeping the protagonist as an empty shell/ and distributing his direction ouija-board-style
    among the players.

    Me and Segedy are tossing around an idea that seems relevant - a way to model the "Big Man" dynamic seen in Doc Savage or Buckaroo Banzai. Every player has a character subordinate to the Big Man, and the Big Man is also a fully-fledged communal character, whose "ownership" can be bought with some resource currency. When you are in charge of the Big Man, your own sidekick sort of fades and you drive the action for a while - until your Big Man mojo fades, and another sidekick takes over. The Big Man is functionally immortal but his sidekicks are very much not, and his goals should drag them into bad and exciting situations. So there are layers of competitive mini-game within the social heirarchy, and only the Big Man ever gets any credit for the amazing adventures.
