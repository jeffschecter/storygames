    \n In Solar system, you imagine what your character is (the character you want to play), then you create them in the system using skills, pools, secrets and stuff.
    The rewards come fast, and you can make a lot of gains if you "role play hard". You can make advances to skills you never use.

    In Burning Wheel, you have to use the system to let you decide what kind of character you play, to some degree.
    The rewards come slower, and focuses on getting better over a long period of time by doing what you want to become good at. You can only advance skills you use.

    \n Personally I mostly find that Solar System is cleaner and simpler - I understand what each part is doing, and I can change it around effortlessly, and it's nice to use because each rule is exactly where it should be. Burning Wheel, on the other hand, has lots and lots more crunch and material, but it's also a bit all over the place, and I need to consciously ignore the nagging voices in my head that demand me to rip off the guts and reinstall them in a cleaner, more pretty manner. I get the same reaction with most editions of D&D.

    Overall the two games do a lot of the same things, but at slightly different scales and with slightly different tones. I vastly prefer Solar System for my own personal practical play, but that's because I prefer the virtues it's strong at against the strengths of the Wheel.

    Magic Burner is great, by the way, because it has some articles that clarify the principles upon which BW is built.

    \n The original question concerns design intent and not details of the execution, though. Looking at it in those terms, I think the key difference between these games is that Burning Wheel doesn't seem to facilitate such a dramatic rising power curve as Solar System does - the whole point of Solar System is that your character is thrust into key positions that allow him to make important choices, simply because he grows more powerful pretty quickly, and perhaps even more importantly, because in the Solar System everybody matters.

    I don't have enough experience with Burning Wheel to say for sure, but to me it seems that the game is explicitly built to preserve a certain sort of conservative power curve over long term. Characters are intended to grow influential and powerful, even awe-inspiring, but they do not wield the sort of power Solar System bestows upon even the least significant player characters. Solar System moments are often made out of characters just going completely zany and breaking all expectations for what they should be capable of, while it seems to me that BW does a lot of work to ensure that all results are reasonable and fit well into a matrix of choice and consequence.

    \nI think both games drive towards character transformation. TSoY flat out rewards your character for changing their Keys with buy-offs. Burning Wheel gives Persona Artha for finishing a Belief.

    BW has cogs in its machine that don't turn until you have played a long time, where TSoY's entire machine (chargen to Transcendence), to my eyes, will run in a few months of play at most. This is not to say that TSoY has no long-term capabilities, only that BW is more set up with long term play in mind. An epiphany isn't going to happen without a whole lotta play behind it.

    Hope that makes sense.

    \n In tSoY, I feel like I can create anything which makes me unfocused. With Burning Wheel, I feel the actual playing of the games forges who my character is. I'm often surprised who my character becomes through logical progression. I also feel the mechanics of Burning Wheel inspire immersions and deep roleplaying for me. Duel of Wits really pushes team work. But the Refreshment Scenes in tSoY are out of this world. But burning wheel has much more built in situation generation.

    In play, I don't find either game similar at all. Especially in terms of how characters work together. tSoY feels much more light hearted and over the top action oriented to me with few consequences. That being said the difference in character skills is much less forgiving in tSoY.
