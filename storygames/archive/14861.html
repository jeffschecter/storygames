\n Over in this thread JD Corley said:
\n
\n
We're in this position because it is to nobody's financial advantage to improve the quality of your play. Nobody gets paid when a GM does a great job. Nobody loses money when a GM does a shit job. Nobody gets paid when an Apocalypse World game goes great. Nobody loses money when an Apocalypse World game goes off the edge and dies a horrible shitty death. Nobody gets paid when a Polaris game really clicks and our four (presumably ideologically pure non-GM) players knock it out of the park. Nobody loses money when a Polaris game fumbles and slops around and doesn't go anywhere. It is to nobody's financial advantage for RPG play to be improved. Only that it be "good enough" to get you to buy the next thing.
\n
\n
\nLet's talk about what kind of financial incentives would lead to an improvement in play experience. (Let's set aside for now the reasons that things like designing a new game are pursued when doing a support product for Pathfinder, or an e-book on how to make money writing RPGs, might earn more money but less respect.)
\n
\nI don't think that simply charging for gaming experiences rather than gaming products is enough. A gaming services company, like the Abantey Workshop in Berkeley or a myriad of similar afterschool programs in Israel, has an incentive to make an employee training manual that invests effort in making its staff better facilitators of play experience. So far as I know - and I hope someone knows why I'm wrong - this hasn't been done; training of new GMs happens along the same lines it does in a non-professional environment, and while I don't doubt that someone like Abantey's Becky Thomas is a great GM as the result of having done it as a full-time job for decades, I don't know of any way to benefit from her experience short of going to work for her one summer - which is not different from the way as an amateur I seek out GMs whose gamesI want to play in so I can learn what they know.
\n
\nI do think that indie publishing leads to an indirect incentive to improve play. As a freelancer for WotC, no effort has ever been made to get me to run games at conventions, and it seems clear to me that the way to be financially successful would be to devote all my time to writing and none to playing or playtesting. As a freelancer for a smaller outfit (Goodman Games), the company has paid my plane fare from NYC to Houston to run at conventions, and doing a tournament to support books I'd worked on was highly encouraged. And as a self-publisher, I am hella incentivized to run games of Adventurer Conqueror King wherever I go and over Google+ when I'm not there, and I can attest that this actual play feeds back into the texts I write for myself much more directly than it does when I write for WotC.
\n
\nGame stores that host gaming events are also profiting from GMs who do a good job, and stand to lose money when people suck.
\n
\nConventions benefit financially when GMs do a good job, but so far as I know have not developed a good system to encourage skilled ones to come back and discourage bad ones.
\n
\nI was told that a professional GM attempt in Brazil foundered over the question of how to measure player skill and compensate players for what they bring to the table. It's clear to me that one of the most important assets I could have as a professional GM is a professional player working towards the same job of facilitating a great play experience for the paying customers.

    \n Adam Smith pointed out in The Wealth of Nations that you don't earn good money doing what people of leisure do for fun. The market for doing fun things is always glutted with people willing to trade off low wages for having fun. The development of copyright law and of technology for enabling huge numbers of people to enjoy a single performance (printing processes, improved and larger theatres, records, movies, radio, TV, amplifiers) created a temporary exception for a small number of star writers and performers, but most writers, musicians, and I think actors still struggle financially. And the erosion of centralised control of master copies and reproducing equipment means that the wealth of star writers, actors, and musicians may not be with us for much longer.
    \n
    \nThus it is with gaming technique, I think. It's very difficult to make commercial because it is a leisure activity, and the service is not reproducible nor suitable to deliver to a large audience all at once. Professional GMs or other players are always going to be poor.
    \n
    \nThat leaves improving mechanics and content, i.e. publishing better rules sets, settings, and modules. Does doing that well count?

    \n I don't go to conventions or hang out at game stores, so I don't know if people who do spend any time at all talking about improving their play and/or getting other people psyched about improving their play. Does this happen?

    Because I tend to think that Agemegos (and Adam Smith) are right, this just isn't a thing that can be made into a commodity. But if you could tap into the same hobbyist, social-networky groove that brings people into gaming in the first place, that might work. Find those places where the community-minded gamers are already congregating and talking, and get them talking about how to be better at playing and running games. Give them the space and the encouragement to run with that, and they might use it. I mean, people apparently sign up for GM Conferences whenever they're available, so there's at least SOME interest floating around out there to get this ball rolling. Maybe start by setting aside space and time for people who are already interested in this stuff to get together and talk about it, with the expectation that if it goes really well, they'll be taking what they've learned back to other people and talking about how wonderful the experience was so that more people will show up for it next time.

    If it's true that being better at gaming is highly rewarding -- and I think it is -- then this should produce a lot of very enthusiastic pro-improvement gamers who will in turn share that enthusiasm with everyone they play with. There still won't be any real money in it; maybe someone could write a book and give presentations at cons and pay a few bills with the proceeds, and a few game companies could figure out a way to use it for marketing purposes to move some inventory, but that's about it. If it's going to sustain itself, it's going to have to sustain itself because it's fun and because people want to do it. If neither of those are true, then there's definitely not enough money to make it happen despite that.

    Posted By: johnzoTo Tavis' question: I wonder if any game company has ever done an affiliate program where GMs who drive sales are tracked and rewarded?
    \nAlmost. Since it is too difficult to directly track "that GM caused that sale", instead the trick is to say "that GM caused play to happen publicly, so she gets swag". To work, that strategy has to assume that any exposure is better than none.

    An alternative would be to reward the GM based on player feedback. This, naturally, creates incentives to collude between the players and GM, but that might not be the end of the world (hey, the customers are "engaged" with at least some aspect of your product if they are colluding). Does add an extra layer of cost, though, to administer the player feedback.

    I also note that at least one company is running an apparently successful program that is similar to D&D encounters, without using any incentives at all. Just the production of content appears to be enough. (I should note, however, that this company also has a get swag for demoing our games program as well.)

    Again, the point of these programs seems to be about creating new play and new players, rather than improving existing play. On the other hand, if a program takes someones "rpg skill" from "nothing" to "a little", I guess that technically "improves play".

    One thing these programs could add is something like a "player of the session award", where the GM would give the "best" player for that session, and the GM herself would be rewarded for doing so. (Years ago, a D&D 2E campaign I played in did this, where each player had to nominate someone else for the award. If nothing else, it certainly made the players aware of play quality. The knowledge that you had to come up with a nomination at the end of the game meant that you paid attention to the play quality of others.)

    \nIf you assume that "financial incentives" involve utility and not necessarily money, "financial incentives" and "cultural shifts and social organization" really aren't as disconnected as you might think.

    In other words, it is a delusion to think that "cultural shifts" happen totally absent of changing incentives. Behavior (i.e. "culture") only changes if a) available incentives change or b) your weighting of what is important (i.e. utility curve) changes, making existing incentives more or less attractive. As an individual, you typically only can control the latter, whereas someone outside your brain can only hope to control the former.

    \n You know, there are many many professions out there where the people who do the work and get paid for it love doing it. I'm a musician: I know.

    Generally, what makes the money necessary is not the nature of the work itself (you don't have to hate doing something to get paid for it!), it's the conditions under which you work.

    As a professional/paid GM, you're not getting paid to just play D&D. You already do that on your own! You enjoy it, it's fun, maybe even a labour of love.

    You're getting paid to:

    * Run games (work) at a specific time, at a specific place, of the client's choosing.
    * Run the game that the client likes, not the one you feel like playing.
    * Run the game in the setting/genre of the client's choosing ("We want an adventure where we're guaranteed to kill a red dragon and score magical items, with no chance of death... but make it feel like there is... AGAIN!")
    * Run the game in the style or situation the client demands (e.g. playing in one-hour chunks over lunch versus hours-long all-night sessions, or playing in a smelly basement vs. playing in a loud public space)
    * Run the game for people who are not friends, and probably wouldn't be friends--maybe people whose outlook on life you strongly disagree with
    * Run the game for groups who don't get along, so you need to police or discipline them
    * Accomodate special requests ("I want a game of emotionally-laden romance using D&D4E... but can you hack it so it's in the setting of Frank Herbert's Dune?")
    * Run the game as often or as infrequently as desired (but in either case, you will be expected to keep it running just as smoothly, which means keeping notes or whatever else you need to do)
    * Accomodate for annoying circumstances, like players dropping out, showing up late, or missing sessions
    etc.

    ...and maintain an expected level of enthusiasm and quality despite all those things.

    It's not the activity itself that's the trouble. GMing may be a lot of fun for you! We love gaming, right? Even prepping for a game is tons of fun when you're excited about the game.

    No, it's when you find yourself in a stinky, hot basement with a bunch of people you really wouldn't normally hang out with, with the player of the one character whose role the current session hinges on missing, and you've been forced to hack your D&D games to include little ponies yet again... on the same night when you were supposed to take your boy/girlfriend out to dinner...

    That's when you realize you'd better be getting paid for this.

    Look, in the original thread, I was lamenting, not calling to action. To answer the OP, I don't know what it would look like. A union, a revolution? Maybe, I don't know, I'm just a gentleman farmer retired to the country. Smart people come up with things like that.

    As I mentioned in the other thread, we had a GM's Conference, it was amazing, we did it with zero budget, no investment whatsoever except my time and the time of the presenters. But eventually I got tired of doing it, and nobody else wanted to do it, so we stopped doing it. (And what good would a conference like that really do for the play of games without GMs?     There are other hobbies where people do it for free, but who have a lot of investment in improving what they're doing. Music's a good example. You rehearse a LOT in music, you play with many musicians, you try to get good with your fellow collaborators and in tune with what they want. You might read some books that a music book company sells you, but nobody in their right mind would say "I wonder how a music book company can financially incentivize getting amateurs to play better music." Even with the possibility of beer money not in the cards for most amateur musicians, they already want to get better and the means of getting better are well established.

    How about something closer to home? How do you improve your World of Warcraft play? Well, you play a lot, and you vary the builds, and you go about the same kinds of situations from various directions, and you play with good players. You might read some World of Warcraft books a World of Warcraft book company put out, (and yes, talking on message boards!) but you're also going to be watching videos, improving your equipment, experimenting and testing.

    I think of my great grandmother, a painter. I say "a painter" although she never sold a painting and speaking objectively she probably never could. It was her abiding hobby for the last thirty years of her life. She couldn't really gain from reading painting books, she liked videos, classes, and travelling to locations and painting there.

    She had no "reason" to improve. Maybe plenty of painters are like the people Rob describes - perfectly satisfied with how they're painting and seeing no reason to change. But being satisfied is not the same as not wanting to improve, to push harder. Michael Jordan practiced basketball, A LOT. Bobby Fischer played a shitload of chess when getting ready for a tournament. Pearl Jam practiced their songs a hozillion times.

The development of copyright law and of technology for enabling huge numbers of people to enjoy a single performance (printing processes, improved and larger theatres, records, movies, radio, TV, amplifiers) created a temporary exception for a small number of star writers and performers, but most writers, musicians, and I think actors still struggle financially. And the erosion of centralised control of master copies and reproducing equipment means that the wealth of star writers, actors, and musicians may not be with us for much longer.

    Thus it is with gaming technique, I think. It's very difficult to make commercial because it is a leisure activity, and the service is not reproducible nor suitable to deliver to a large audience all at once. Professional GMs or other players are always going to be poor.
    \nUnless something very very weird happens to the world, you shouldn't and probably won't be skipping dinner with your loved ones to GM a Pony-fied D&D with awful people in a terrible basement when you could be having fun prepping and running a game you're excited about for your friends on your own schedule instead. There's no realistic way that you could be compensated enough to offset all those inconveniences you mentioned there and earlier, because there aren't enough people willing to pay the kind of money you'd need to offset all of those things. (And if there was, maybe you shouldn't be getting money for GMing, but rather getting money for therapy, for them or for you. )

    Are there independent, professional GMs in the world? Maybe, probably, I don't know. But I also don't know if there are any who could pay their bills by running actual games for individual groups the way you describe, though, which means that any dream of inspiring better GMing/play by appealing to the profit motive ("get better and you can charge people to do this for them!") seems a bit far-fetched. Worth a shot if that's what you really want to try, I guess, but unless this hobby turns into some form of gambling (look at the rise of televised poker), I just don't think there's enough money there to make much of a difference.

    \n The main real-world varieties of professional GMs, as far as I know, are:

    1) people who work with kids. This hits lots of the things on Paul's list, often including "Run the game for people who are not friends, and probably wouldn't be friends--maybe people whose outlook on life you strongly disagree with" and "Run the game for groups who don't get along, so you need to police or discipline them".

The public face of this is when people bid to play with celebrities at charity auctions, but I gather it also happens in private on a purely for-profit basis.
