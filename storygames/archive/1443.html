DevP
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games

I'd love to make a game design about my whacky personal Ideology X. For the sake of example, let's say that my X is the Labor Theory of Value. (It's not, but go with it.)

I don't want a game where we talk about how awesome a world with LTV would be, or a fantasy where we fight the man and bring LTV to the world, or a game that's a sunday school parable on the virtues of LTV (and the sins of anti-LTV). That all sounds remarkably tedious towards to pro-LTV players, and maybe even insulting to anti-LTV people.

Nonetheless, suppose I still want to socially engineer an outcome by which those who play are either (a) more secure in their proto-LTV beliefs, or (b) more understanding or aware of others' LTV stances, even if they are very certainly not holding that belief.

So. Can this be done? Are there examples?

    Well, RaHoWa sets out to do this, I suppose, but it's not exactly the cutting edge of game design.

    Now, if I were to make a game to spread the LTV, I'd probably make it a competetive board game. Each player would take on the role of one group of workers and one group of capitalists and they'd try to maximize their amount of money by hiring each other out to form temporary firms. Each firm would get some production function based on the amount of labor and capital put into it. Labor would be measured by little chits and capital would only emerge from this process - the first firms would be all-labor - and be reprsented by, say, some clear plastic container holding chits, because capital is ultimately just stored labor. And I'd let everything emerge from that. We could even have a pool of cards representing various market niches that slowly got used up, along with a bonus for monopoly position, to represent the falling rate of profit. And such forth.

    But it really depends on what X is. I mean, the LTV is all math-y and stuff, so a Monopolyesque board game makes sense for it. Other ideological positions better warrant an actual RPG, because they're more personal - sexual politics, for instance. So it's really impossible to say "here is a formula for producing a game around X" because building a game around X is going to by definition be a function of what X is.

    It is possible because I've done something along those lines.

    I ran a LARP at work because I wanted to demonstrate a particular point and I called it a strategy game for policy staff. I work as an analyst in central government in the UK and I did a talk about strategy and then set up the game with policy people playing three groups of participants, Policy, Analysts and Delivery.

    The aim of the game was for the in-game policy people to create a new strategy to deal with a particular problem and have this policy delivered. This would have been fine had I not had been feeding bad news to each group at regular intervals to interfere with their smooth running - much like what happens in reality.

    The upshot of the game was that there was a big bottleneck on analysis as policy and delivery people kept changing their analytical requests at short notice without defining them terribly well. At one point one of the policy people who was playing an analyst told another group to "piss off!"

    This demonstrated the value of a proper long-term analytical programme to support both policy and delivery.

    I think if you follow the schema that Daniel outlines you should be able to create something that delivers what you want. I think also if you have lots of things happening quite quickly and people don't have enough time to think about alternatives (such as hiring consultants in my game) that these pressures are more likely to make people go the way you want them to.

    I think a lot of games have a political component (maybe all, but I won't go that far just yet). And I certainly think what the OP is describing is possible. Players can buy into the politics of a setting without coming to believe them out of character, so I can certainly envision such a game for a particular political theory.

    The most overtly politically-informed game I ever ran was (and is) an Adventure! Gotham City supers game. Gotham City is a politically conservative dark fantasy. The government is corrupt, criminals go unpunished, only through the special training of a small group of people willing to break "unimportant" laws to enforce the "important" ones can civilization survive and not fall into anarchy and chaos. (Hell, the iconic character is a wealthy white heir to a massive fortune, that should tip you off right there.) This is approximately as far from my actual political beliefs as can be imagined, and most of my gaming friends are left-of-center as well (my wife the exception - but even she recognizes that the fantasy is based on very extreme assumptions.) Yet we are able to get into the point of view of the hardline "dangle them off a building and maybe even drop them a few times to get them to talk" characters and setting by celebrating the source material and by me making it seem like everything is at stake with every decision (not true.)

    There are highly conservative roots in a lot of game setups. "We few see the world for how it really is and we must take action ourselves using the abilities that put us above the mass of men, to protect them, the power we gain is merely a side effect of the protection we give."

    I've been told by several Marxists that my designs for Merchant (running a fantasy merchant house, basically) was quintessentially Marxist. I've finally read Das Kapital all the way through, and you'd think that I'd based it on Marx's work, which of course I hadn't.

    I think the point I'm trying to make is that game mechanics are distillations of the way the world works - very basic and abstract simulations and models. This is exactly what the raw stuff of the sociological sciences is - distilling the way the world works according to some paradigm of thought. So it's really no wonder that you can either accidentally stumble across, or purposely create, an ideological model of society in designing a game. You're simply doing what a sociologist or anthropologist is doing, only in a top-down fashion.

    A social scientist observes behavior and asks "What are the principles and rules behind human interaction."

    The game designer says (or asks himself, then says) "These are the principles and rules behind human interaction." And the play of the game exhibits behaviors based on those rules.

    If your game has a mechanics for "Might = Right", you're exhibiting a model of social behavior. Whether you intend to do it or not.

After I get FLFS out the door, I'll be taking a sledge to the game and seeing if I can reconstitute it to more accurately hit "How shall we live?" rather than "What will you risk to force people to live like you want?"

So again, ideology will be there, but as a designer I can either retain authority over it or hand it over to the players.

    Jonathan, I think that's an interesting question. Because, speaking as a potential player, I don't really want to end up sacrificing my character because the game is built to lead him (or her) up to that moment of sacrifice. I want to sacrifice my character because I personally think it's a cool and/or interesting thing to do in the situation.

    Which means that, in my opinion, if there is going to be thematic design at work then it has to create tense or interesting situations but it has to not just specify a single path for getting out of them.

    I think D&D is a fairly "straitjacketed" game if run with a set of by-the-book assumptions. What you're really meant to do in almost all situations in D&D is to fight. That's why the preponderence of rules text, Feats, Spells, etc are all focussed on combat. (I'm not denying that D&D supports a lot broader play than this, just saying that that's where its focus lies.)

    But I couldn't imagine playing a game where the predominant way to solve conflicts was by heroic self sacrifice. (No duh.) I suppose what an interesting design could do would be to work to prohibit certain choices and then see what interesting alternatives players come up with. So I could imagine a kind of anti-D&D where physical conflict was absolutely the worst thing you could do. And then you could use that game to have all kinds of ornery conflicts where people can't really choose to fight and you have to see what other options they avail themselves of.

    The challenge, in my eyes, is in providing enough freedom to let the players actually come up with meaningful options to resolve these conflicts, while providing enough structure to resolve them and to make the different choices actually feel like ... well, like distinct choices really.
