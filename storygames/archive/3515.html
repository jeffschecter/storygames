\n
\n
\n
\n Y'all know Buffy, right? In that show there is very strong synergy of A-Plot (There's a monster! Kill it!) and B-Plot (There's a personal crisis! Brood about it!) Depending upon how you choose to look at a show, it can either be the show where they fought the body-stealing skin-demon or the show where Angel confronted his loneliness and isolation. Actually, of course, it's both. It only becomes one or the other when we abstract things down to one story, because that means accentuating one set of connected ideas and glossing over the unconnected bits.
\n
\nSo ... same-same happens in a lot of RPGs. There's an A-Plot (There's a monster! Kill it!) and a B-Plot (There's a personal crisis! Brood about it!) They're intertwined, and influence each other, but you can still see the outlines of them as separate things.
\n
\nCan the GM determine the A-Plot? Oh hellz no. Ain't no way to stop the players from turning it into "There's a monster! Let's reason with it!" or any other damn thing. But what the GM can do is to provide ingredients for the group to construct the A-Plot in-game.
\n
\nIf you provide people with pasta, vegetables, spices, oil, maybe some wine ... that's a bunch of ingredients that go together. The folks you provide them to may use all of them, they may use only some. They may create pasta salad, they may create a three course meal. You can't really tell. But you can be pretty sure that they aren't going to make hamburgers ... or that if they do then it's going to be a really funny looking hamburger.
\n
\nIf you provide a monster, a dark and spooky mansion, mysterious omens and a whole bunch of downed phone-lines and cell-towers ... that's a certain set of ingredients that go together. The folks you provide them to may use all of them, they may use only some. They may create a survival horror, they may create a zom-rom-com, they may create an occult investigation. You can't really tell. But you can be pretty sure that they aren't going to make a political intrigue ... or that if they do then it's going to be a really funny looking political intrigue.
\n
\nSo ... you want the controversial bit? I think that a GM can help make a solid game by providing sets of plot-ingredients that strongly constrain what the players can make of them. I think that deliberately ignoring calls for out-of-type ingredients can be a strong technique.
\n
\n"Man," the player says, "If only we could talk to the spirits in this place ... we could do some politicking, make this into a supernatural intrigue..." Well, tough noogies. The GM ain't giving you the ingredients for a supernatural intrigue. There's nothing wrong with pulling in new ingredients, but today that's not what the GM is doing. Make something from the ingredients that she is giving you. Get creative.
\n
\nY'know what? This is walking awfully close to saying "It's totally cool for the GM to determine the plot," and I'm okay with that. Choosing a set of ingredients isn't the same as cooking it. Even a very small set of ingredients can inspire people to any number of recipes ... as long as they get to apply their creativity and make important choices about what the story turns out to be, I do not have a problem with telling the players that there are countless stories that they cannot tell this session.
\n
\nHave I finally gone over the edge? Crossed to the Dark Side? Or is this just obvious?

    \n Sydney: But the thing is, the GM locks up when presented with a blank page, too. When that happens, it is very easy (at least for me) to merely react to the players, in a way that doesn't (in turn) provide the players with a strong set of ingredients to build a story.
    \n
    \nExample: Last wednesday I set out to provide the ingredients for a Rivalry story. I presented an obnoxious character (Hiroshi!) who would accept nothing less than total victory, and who had an advantage (she already had an organized team) that you guys could not overcome.
    \n
    \nBut I was missing an ingredient. I didn't realize that I needed to deliberately give Hiroshi a vulnerability that you guys could exploit. I didn't have a scene of her aggressively bullying the reluctant members of her team (to give you guys a chance to talk with those team-members in private, and maybe recruit them away to your (marginally) more friendly group), I didn't have a scene of her being reprimanded by a teacher who was "keeping an eye on her" (so that you could frame her for some sort of trouble-making). I didn't provide a full set of ingredients, and the result (though gonzo and fun and wonderful) lacked overall structure.
    \n
    \nIt's not that you guys latched on to some specific structure I didn't intend, and ran with it. It's that I went half-way to providing a full structure, and then I went into reactive mode, trying to suss out from your activities what sort of extra ingredients you wanted. The result was that I added a little of this, a little of that ... Metaphorically I started out giving you pasta and vegetables, then I said "Hrmm ... they look like they want Worchestershire sauce ... and maybe some pickles..." instead of saying "Okay, I'm not going to worry at the moment what they want, what the set of ingredients needs is the oil that will let them saute those veggies, and spritz that pasta." You guys did the best you could with the ingredients I'd given you, but I'm pretty sure you would have been able to create something better if I'd provided you with a more complete (and restrictive!) set.
    \n
    \nMy desire to react, to incorporate your ideas and feed them back, overrode my ability to clearly see that I had not finished my own contribution.

    Posted By: TylerI guess I'd like to hear what you mean by "ignore", because that seems a rather harsh punishment for not stepping in lockstep with the vision of some soda-chugger running the game.
    I probably mean less than you're assuming. Let me try to phrase it less vaguely.

    If someone contributes something, and it's clearly important to them, the GM shouldn't ignore it completely. "No, you can't do that ... next!" isn't any way to play with your friends.

    Contrariwise, however, just because someone contributes something doesn't mean that it's important to them. Making a big deal out of every little twitch or doodle from the players ... it's crazy-making. If Sydney says " ... and my guy brings a six pack of soda along!" that doesn't mean that I have to wrack my brains trying to figure out how to make that soda vitally important later in the game. Sometimes a cigar is just a cigar. And if it's more important to Sydney, he has the power to make it important. It doesn't have to be the GM's job.

    So somewhere between those two extremes there's a balance: There's stuff that you leave to the players to do (or not do) as they see fit. Then there's stuff that, when the players put it out there, you need to actively embrace, support and make your own. Having that balance anywhere other than all the way over at the "embrace" end is going to involve deliberately putting some of the things that the players contribute into the "Not my Job" drawer and getting on with the things that you, as GM, want to do.

    Posted By: Mark WOkay. So more of a formalization of "the pitch" than anything tightly constraining. Along the lines of Tenets in Universalis?
    Well, the idea of a Pitch and the Tenets seems (to me) to be "Let's decide, all together, what we're going to do, all together."

    The idea I'm putting forward here is older and simpler: "I'm gonna decide, by myself, the input that I'm going to provide, by myself." It's less about collaborating, and more about delegating: not "Let's get on the same page, so that we can all do this thing together without conflict," but rather "Dude, I don't need you to be on the same page, because you aren't the one who's going to be doing this bit anyway."
