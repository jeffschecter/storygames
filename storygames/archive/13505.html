    Posted By: TomasHVMSome players of today grows up with more modern games, the games we discuss here. What will happen when these players start designing on their own?
    \nI don't think anything different from what is happening now will happen. And right now a constant expansion and maturation is what I witness happening.\n
    - Will they outgrow us?
    \nI certainly hope so. Players with no brain damage are achingly cute. On my modest part, I contributed to the spreading of the hobby with two or three people.\n
    - Will we all be left on the scrapheap of history?
    \nSeriously, is that a facetious question or you really want an answer? Mine can only be a question: why should one think that?\n
    - Will our games be left for broken, or insufficient to tell the really good stories?
    \nDon't think so. They may be improved and evolve. Trollbabe did, for instance. But if the premise is valid, I don't see why a game should cease to be a good game.\n
    - What flaws in our design will be revealed by time, and by their superior understanding of the medium?
    \nWell that is truly one question to which, by its formulation, nobody can possibly answer without taking a guess so wild it doesn't make much sense to take it. In short: only time will tell.\n
    I don't know anything about these long-term effects of our games. I can't even begin to guess. Would you dare to hazard a guess?
    \nThere are games which are inspired by other games, often. And they become great games by themselves. I'm thinking of what games Trollbabe and My Life With Master inspired, and they're all bloody masterpieces. I think that's the first and most widespread evolution pattern that can be witnessed.
    The second pattern is way more uncommon, and it only happens with frikkin' geniuses, who create games that are both cool and completely new.
    The second pattern then becomes inevitably source for the first pattern: genial games spur the creativity of people and inspire the creation of other games.
    I think this interaction of patterns will continue, at least for some time.
    So what should slow down an escalating growth in novelty, quality and beauty of games?
    The fact that the game designing trend expands quickly. Which has a downside. Basically, many people start designing games just because they can. Here in Italy, I see people creating hacks upon hacks without even trying and playing the original game first, just to make an example of what I mean. It's an expanding trend which has some damaging potential to the hobby.

    \n I think that the future of play that meets the same needs (social, creative, and narrative) as RPGs/story games will only include what we do in communities like this if we overcome some serious blocks.

    We need to embrace the social and ritual elements of our play and start exploring how our play can affect and shape lives. In a real way, this seems to be richest vein of potential art within our media.

    We need to take deep journeys into how we interact with media (from game text to fora to podcasts) and use this to exponentially enhance the reliability of our play experiences. The game text has always been a compromise between what is possible and helpful for play, it may very well need to die so that the games might live.

    We need to embrace the fact that play causes learning, and that as designers and players we have a responsibility to understand how that affects our ideas and perceptions (ideally before the next "brain damage" kerfluffle). It would be far better if we could actually do something positive here, but even some damage control would be nice.

    - Mendel

    Let me be the first (false?) prophet!

    How will future rpg-designers make themselves a new design-paradigm?

    - I predict that future rpg-designers will reject "mechanic" as a term, and make use of a more holistic language. That in turn will open the field for more heartfelt and intuitive games.

    - Simple, holistic design will dominate the field, and make it a public and popular field for self-investigation, personal growth and happiness. Luck is dead! Love your game!

    \n I think this is the most wonderful answer that can be given to those who tell you "roleplaying? bwah, get a life". Really, i'm serious. it's perfect and beautiful.

    Anyway, i totally agree with it. i haven't had a regular group in something like 2 years, and it's excruciating.

    As for the new generation... i don't really know, i don't think they will be as different from story gaming as story gaming is different from D&D: a huge leap has been made in conceptualizing the structure of the hobby, in thinking about tools and dynamics, and this was probably the biggest break between trad and story gaming*: such a "paradigm shift" is not needed anymore, and i think new generations of gaming will keep on expanding, in different directions. Most probably, GNS/Big Model will lose its "hegemony": as for now, it's more or less the only model of roleplaying theory, and, as such, it is the model, probably the future will see different models rising, some competitive, some complementary, and this will obviously generate a lot of games geared to get specific results and to reproduce specific dynamics, which we may actually not even notice, right now.

    This IS far-fetched, maybe, but i sure hope it happens: as valid as i think the GNS is, it is a model, and, as such, a simplified vision of reality. many social sciences show, in their academic history, that teh development of more, diverse models brings to a richer, healthier approach to the matter, while a single dominant model makes for a rigid discipline and constraints the possible evolutions.

    *even though i'm still totally convinced that there is no "us" and "them": it's two sides of the same coin, and more often than not there will be some powerful similarities.
