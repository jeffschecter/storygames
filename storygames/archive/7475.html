\nSetting / Color Stuff
\nSo I read Empires of Dust and I get the wierd feeling that Clint and Amy crawled into my head looked around and then designed a game based on all the cool Sci-Fantasy Tropes that I groove on.
\n
\nArid desert world, check
\nNoble savage warrior people, check
\nWierd alien "indistinguishable from magic" technology, check
\nLots of excuses to blow shit up, check
\nLots of cool weapons and vehicles to blow shit up with, check
\nBackground history kept simple and straight forward enough that I don't fall asleep reading the umpteenth version of Dune the Requiem, check.
\nCrazy commentary on religion and its role in a high tech future society, check.
\nCharacters who are the movers and the shakers and not the scrubs, check.
\n
\nIts got it all.
\n
\nSo I'm reading the opening fiction, its nice and short so I don't get bored and set it down to read something else. I get to this part about King somebody-or-other and his Gunknights. <boing> I don't have any idea what the heck they are but I know whatever they are that's what I'm gonna be. I'm already calling up images of King's Roland crossed with a Paladin. And immediately I start thinking..."Gunknights need armor...I wonder if this game has armor". So now the image in my head is Roland crossed with a Paladin crossed with Iron Man and I'm flipping through the book looking for armor. "Oh...here's the Trait you have to take to be a Gunknight...and here's something called 'Siege Armor' they get for free.." And while I'm flipping around I find the trait for Landed Nobility which instructs me to flip to some other page and roll up a settlement.
\n
\n<bing> so now my guy is Lord Redguard the Hammer of Dessereck. Cuz I saw some place named Red Canyon flipping around the book. And I'm figuring that Dessereck is some place where some people were holed up Alamo style and the King's army couldn't root them out until I led the assault that breached their defenses. "Oh look, here's a picture of some Yeti looking dudes...Bruta...yeah...those guys. I totally whooped up on a bunch of those guys." Now I need a cool gun, cuz if you're gonna have some kick ass armor you need a kick ass gun. <flip> <flip> <flip> Ooohhh here's a cool gun. Ooohh this one's cool too. Ooohh maybe I need one of these. <flip> "what's this...its wicked cool...its called a Jackhammer...hey my guy is the 'Hammer' of Dessereck this gun is made for me...what's it do...hey its the standard issue weapon for Gunknights.
\nGET OUT OF MY HEAD!!!

    \nsystem stuff
    \n
    \nSo the systems got lots of cool bits to it. A wicked collection of Traits to build your character out of, some of which are Unique and actually tie you directly into the setting and important NPCs (the one where there's this Angel chick who's fallen in love with you and now you're being hunted by the church for corrupting her is especially cool...but there's also one where your friends with the ghost of some kid who follows you around and tells you stuff...or the one where the Grey Man is after you and you don't know why).
    \n
    \nAnd the weapons all have Traits that have mechanical impact some are good like Armor Piercing and some not so good like Recoil which makes you lose an action before you can fire the gun. But you can upgrade the guns and add new traits or buy off bad ones and stuff and that seems cool.
    \n
    \nAnd there's some cool random encounter generators when you try to travel overland from place to place and run into enemy patrols and stuff and bits where time passes and the war continues and when you get places you find its been conquered by the enemy and then you can muster armies to go take it back.
    \n
    \nBut while I was reading it a few red flags went up for me. The core system is a pretty simple d20 roll under system (and I tend to be biased against linear distribution curves), but the highest stat my character starts with (barring using Traits to buy it up) is 12. That's like a 60% chance to succeed at the things I'm best at? Whispers of the Whifftastic Basic Roleplaying System start dancing in my head. I have a pretty low tolerance for whiffage.
    \n
    \nBut there's other stuff in the game that harder to evaluate. There are medals you can earn that you can trade in for a bunch of stuff, including auto success. But its hard to judge how fast and furious the medals flow...whether they're so rare that don't really help with the whiffage except once in a while...or so common that you can auto succeed pretty often...even for skills you're not good at.
    \n
    \nAnd its also possible to raise stats...basically any roll that ties your score gives you another level. But on a d20 you could make 40 rolls in a session and statistically only raise 2 skills...and I can't remember the last game that I had to make 40 rolls in a session for...so I don't know how fast that really is. And you can also train and get a free roll for everything to try and level something but I don't have a feel for how often that happens in the game.
    \n
    \nAlso injury is pretty brutal. If you take damage you're down, unable to do anything but crawl away. More damage and you're "gored" unable to do even that and you have the choice to either die or live with a scar (which is pretty cool). I can't tell if the system is as random as it looks on first blush, and if that combined with the damage rules will turn the game into "flip a coin to see if you die today".
    \n
    \nSo that's the direction of my current questions. Currently just red warning flags, because there's enough stuff going on in the system that its hard to evaluate how all the interactions actually play out. So...how do they actually play out? What does the system do to make your character more badass than a 60% chance to succeed would suggest?

    \n My apologies for not showing up sooner (my wife and I have been on a much-needed vacation),

    To Valamir:
    Glad the setting stuff clicked with you! Whiff factor can be an issue (especially if you're sensitive to it), but is largely balanced out by the way medals work. Get used to bantering during combat (which takes some getting used to) and it's a good idea to give your character a trait that will allow him to gain medals outside of combat. Medals tend to flow pretty fast and furious. If, after you take it for a spin, the whiff factor is too high for you, there are two easy solutions: 1) start the PCs off as "elites" (Base score 16, or 80% success rate at your best stats) or 2) start the PCs with two medals instead of one.

    Injury is also largely balanced out by medals. That said, it's also good to snatch up traits like "I Will Not Be Broken!" or "Final Stand" to mitigate the effects of being wounded. Still, it's pretty brutal stuff. Characters will go down from time to time. We try to use characters being gored to spark new, interesting situations (as per the defeat table). Also . . . use cover!

    Epiphanies are very random. Again, some people will hate this. We found that for our tastes it's really fun and instills each stat roll with a lot of tension.

    We pushed the system toward extremes, and it's definitely not for everyone . . . but it's a lot of fun if it is your cup o tea. Let me know if you come up with more questions!

    To Jonathan:
    That's "The Widow." It's a unique trait that you can take in the game. It's also a tongue-in-cheek response to the idea that RPGs should strive to be politically correct in their artwork.

    \n The map is important to the travel mechanic. The points on the map are called "waypoints." They are an abstract representation of the more important, defined regions of the game-world. Characters travel across the world along the abstract routes denoted by the dotted lines that connect adjacent waypoints. When you want to travel to an adjacent waypoint, you make a navigation roll. Success means you're there. Failure triggers a random encounter. You keep trying (and having encounters) until you succeed. The encounters cover all kinds of stuff like enemy patrols, settlements, military outposts, weather, etc . . . Essentially, it's a thinly veiled situation generator.

    Edit: Oh, and the "Kimo" thing, that's awesome! Amy will get a kick out of that. (It's actually a fitting name according to the way our playtest campaigns played out).

    \n I'm definitely not asking folks to cater to everyone's (or even my personal tastes), though these kinds of conversations often seem to go that way. Like, "Hey, I'm really bothered by this thing and wanted to point it out" and then the response is "Don't censor me! Don't tell me what to do! If you don't like my game, that's fine; don't buy it!" I think that's about as productive as annoyed customers going "I can't believe you did that with the game! You should change it right away!" I'm not asking anyone to do anything. I'm saying "This bothers me a lot" and creators can take that into consideration or not, and I can continue to buy / play their games or not, whatever else happens. If I'm not part of the target audience for a game, then I'll probably end up having to expend more effort to make it work for me. That's a cost I can choose to pay or not. I can definitely still respect people and strongly disagree with the decisions (including artistic / marketing decisions) that they make.

    I do think it's possible, though, that the decisions we make have unintended consequences that may or may not have been fully considered before we make them. If that's not the case in these particular instances, if all possible reactions were accounted for and decisions were made anyway, cool. But, honestly, I think it's pretty impossible to account for everything. John and I shared the same reaction in this case, so it's likely many other people did as well. How many? Who knows. Would you have made a different choice, given this thread? Maybe not. That's not for me to decide and it sounds like it's too late for this particular case, anyway. But I do think it's important to have conversations like this so that people can make better informed decisions in the future, even if the decisions themselves don't change. That's all.

    \n Hey, this game does sound very interesting here. I'm pretty interested right now in what is the "right thing to say" to get me (or somebody else, for that matter) to pick a game up and give attention and perhaps even a buy to that particular game. (I wrote about that yesterday at my blog.) For what it's worth, if somebody had explained that map mechanic to me at Gencon, I'd have bought this game just on that basis. The same for the setting-unique traits.

    As it was, I did look at Clint's games and he told me a bit about them, but as I remember it, the only thing I took with me about the Empire of Dust was that it's a boxed set and Clint uses the same house system he used for Bizenghast. The convention being chock-full of mediocre fantasy games with generic systems, me not knowing Clint's level as a designer at all (really, first time I heard about him, as far as I know), I didn't feel like buying a big game with a house system. (I instead bought Roanoke and Urchin, which convinced me post-convention that I'll need to start paying attention to Clint Krause.)

    Anyway, to complete this customer survey: if I'd even noticed the chainsaw babe when looking at the game, it'd have strengthened my initial impression of mediocre geek adventure game, just like similar art in Dread: 1stBoP did. In the latter's case I fortuitously happened on a cool bit of the book (the demon descriptions) on my own when checking it out, so ended up carrying a load of them with me. Empire of Dust, on the other hand, slipped through my fingers this time.

    As for the political correctness or whatever, I'll go with Jonathan - it's a straw man, and irrelevant to whether customers like what you're doing. If your customers do, that's swell, but I for one would have misinterpreted the game's quality and its cachet if I'd have judged on the basis of its art. It's an interesting challenge to try to figure out the right things to say about a game to sell it, not the least because there are so many different market segments out there. I'd have nabbed the game right away if Clint had harped on the bits that I find cool, but then, how could he have known what the cool thing would be for me? That's quite a challenge for anybody trying to sell a game.
