    \n Two quick comments:

    1. Check with a lawyer, not with dweebs like us on the internet.
    2. There is no such thing as "legal parody". You don't have a right to parody; rather, parody is an accepted defense, which is balanced against other issues. This is important if you are looking for ways to avoid a lawsuit rather than ways to defend yourself in a lawsuit. The legal precedent for parody is good for the latter, not the former.

    \n The best advice here so far is to consult a lawyer. Don't rely on anything posted on this or some other web forum for legal advice. That being said, fair use or "expressive use" is a defense (or, more accurately, merely one factor among others to be considered) with respect to claims of trademark infringement under the Lanham Act. Parody or satire is a recognized form of expressive use. Parody has popped up in several recent trademark cases, such as a one involving a satirical website falwell.com, also "Chewy Vuiton" was an appropriate parody of Louis Vuitton, \xe2\u20ac\u0153Timmy Holedigger\xe2\u20ac\x9d was an allowable parody of Tommy Hilfinger, an artist was allowed to make a parody of barby trade dress, a humorous look alike of Barney was allowed at a sports event, and so on. But there are numerous factors that go into figuring what usage of a mark is allowed and what is not. Is your parody a form of critique or is it merely a humorous substitute for the original, is your use commercial or non-commercial, is your use likely to be confused with the mark, and so forth. Taken to its full extent, there are big downsides for getting this wrong. So, if you have a fear or concern, either remove the reference to the mark altogether (cheapest solution) or obtain some actual researched advice from a professional.
