\n For the longest time I have been hating on roleplaying incentives and roleplay controlling mechanics. Recently, however, I've decided that I'd like to try my hand at designing such a system for one of my smaller projects with the intent to see how it affects the game.
\n
\nWhat I don't need...
\nInquires as to how this system fits into the game, just know that it does and have faith in me.
\nDoubt regarding the necessity of including something like this in any game whatsoever.
\n
\nWhat I do need...
\nConstructive criticism on the mechanics presented.
\nSuggestions on terminology. Specifically regarding the list of Traits at the bottom.
\n
\n~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
\n[align=center]The Mechanics
\n[/align]~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
\n
\nCreation: Distribute the following values amongst your Traits; +3, +2, +1, +1, 0, 0, 0, 0, -1, -1, -2, -3. With your GM\u2019s permission, you may apply more negatives but you must balance it with an equal number of positives.
\nSetup: Whenever your character's reaction to an event is in question, the GM will call for a Judgment Test. You may then select three Traits, you should choose at least one positive and one negative Trait. The GM will then select one of these Traits based on how what sorts of reactions he suspects should be produced. Situations may come up where the GM deems one Trait, or specific Traits, to be the only applicable choices.
\nJudgment Test: Roll 1d10 and add the Trait\u2019s value to the result. If the result is 6 or higher, your reaction must lean towards the right side (Virtue). If the result is 5 or lower, your reaction must lean towards the left side (Vice). You may refuse to react in the manner that you rolled but doing so accumulates one Stress Token.
\nStress Tokens: Stress Tokens are removed over time, very slowly. Usually one per week unless the GM deems a situation of great happiness capable of wiping away your stress. For every Stress Token over 4, apply a -1 to all of your Judgment Tests. If you reach 8 Stress Tokens, your character will be so depressed that he may end up committing suicide.
\n(while not noted in this section the game text... Stress Tokens also lower the character's Synchronization Level, which makes it more difficult for him to control machines with his mind)\n
\n
Example: The player\u2019s pilot, Neivann, is searching an abandoned warehouse for his brother. He comes upon a man beating a teenaged boy. The man turns to him as soon as he enters and says, \u201cListen, buddy, you can just forget this happened and take 100 credits for your trouble. Easy money, right?\u201d The GM calls for a Judgment Test, the player chooses Wrath, Greed and Apathy.
\n
\n
\n
Example One: The GM chooses Wrath. Neivann has a -1, he is prone to violence it seems. The player rolls a single d10 and gets a 6, reduced by 1 to 5. He must react in a violent manner. Neivann, with anger in his eyes, draws his pistol an opens fire with no mercy shooting to kill. On the other hand, if he had scored a 6 or higher, Neivann may have accepted the money and went away, or offered the man money in order to secure the boy\u2019s safety.
\n
\n
\n
Example Two: The GM chooses Greed. Neivann has a +2, he is not easily swayed by material wealth and is more prone to give than to take. The player rolls and gets a 5, plus 2 is a 7. He must act is a charitable, non-greedy way. Neivann says he will not accept the money, instead he tries to intimidate the thug with the threat of the police. If Neivann had achieved the opposite result, he could have accepted the money and left or asked for more money in order to keep things a secret.
\n
\n
\n
Example Three: The GM chooses Apathy. Neivann has a +1, he is prone to action and emotional attachment. The player rolls, gets a total of 8. Neivann must act with passion and action! Neivann decides he cannot let an innocent boy be treated like this with no recourse, he pulls free his pistol and fires at the thug\u2019s limbs hoping to take him out with lethal shots. If Neivann had achieved the opposite result, he may have shrugged noting that this has nothing to do with his brother and walked off to avoid further involvement.
\n
\nMental Scars: When characters are presented with tragic, frightening, embarrassing or any other emotionally damaging event the GM may apply a Mental Scar to one of your Traits. The Trait chosen is either relevant to the event that caused the damage or the character\u2019s highest Positive Trait. The next situation that comes up involving that Trait causes the die result to be a 1 automatically. Once that situation is resolved, the Mental Scar is removed. Upon this removal two things occur; the player may decide to decrease that Trait by 1 and increase a different Trait of his choice by 1 and he must roll a d10. If the result is equal to or lower than the number of Stress Tokens his character currently has, he gains another Stress Token.

    \nSetup: Whenever your character\u2019s reaction to an event is in question, the GM may call for a Judgment [1] + Trait Test. You must select three Traits, you should choose at least one Positive and one Negative Trait. Keep in mind when selecting Traits; how do you want your character to react and how do you think your character would react? Also, try to select Traits that relate to the event at hand. Conscious attempts to utilize Traits which do not apply may result in the GM choosing a Trait of his own accord.
    \nThe GM will then select one of these Traits based on what reaction he suspects should be produced. This assumption can be based on any criteria that pleases the GM. Situations may come up where the GM deems one Trait, or specific set of Traits, to be the only applicable choices. It is completely acceptable for the GM to select a number of Traits and then allow the player to select the Trait to be used. However, this should indicate a great amount of trust between the GM and the player. Another alternate, and very arbitrary, method to choosing a Trait is rolling 1d10 and counting from the top down.
    \nJudgment Test: Roll for the Test. If the result is 6 or higher, your character\u2019s reaction must lean towards the right side (Virtue). If the result is 5 or lower, your character\u2019s reaction must lean towards the left side (Vice). Regardless of whether the result is Vice or Virtue, you must incorporate it into your reaction description. The GM will decide whether or not the reaction you have described is appropriate or not. If you willingly ignore the result of the Judgment Test, the GM may give you a Stress Token.
    \nStress Tokens: You may refuse to react in the manner that you rolled but doing so accumulates one Stress Token. Stress Tokens are removed over time, very slowly. Usually one token may be removed per week unless the GM deems a situation of true happiness capable of washing your stress away. For every Stress Token over 4, apply a -1 to all of your Judgment Tests. If you reach 8 Stress Tokens, your character will become so depressed that he may end up committing suicide or collapsing and developing a psychological disorder.
    \nMental Scars: When PCs are presented with tragic, frightening, embarrassing or any other emotionally damaging event the GM may apply a Mental Scar to one of his Traits. The Trait chosen is either relevant to the event that caused the damage or the PC\u2019s highest Positive Trait. The next situation that comes up involving that Trait causes the die result to be a 1 automatically. Once that situation is resolved, the Mental Scar is removed. Upon the Mental Scar\u2019s removal, the player must roll 1d10. If the result is equal to or lower than the PC\u2019s current Stress Token amount, he gains one Stress Token. The removal of a Mental Scar also allows the player to decide whether or not to decrease the previously affected Trait by 1 and increase a different Trait of his choice by 1.
    \nInspiration: When PCs are presented with an event so pure in morality and goodness the GM may place an Inspiration on one of your Traits. Inspirations work very similarly to Mental Scars though in context they are the complete opposite. The Trait chosen is either relevant to the event that caused the inspiration or the PC\u2019s worst Negative Trait. The next situation that comes up involving that Trait causes the die result to be a 10 automatically. Once that situation is resolved, the Inspiration is removed. Upon the Inspiration\u2019s removal, the player must roll 1d10. If the result is equal to or lower than the PC\u2019s current Stress Token amount, he loses one Stress Token. The removal of an Inspiration also allows the player to decide whether or not to increase the previously affected Trait by 1 and decrease a different Trait of his choice by 1.\n

    \nFor an example of non coercive personality mechanics, I recommend checking out Pendragon. There are some who've used the Pendragon mechanics coercively...they're actually using the system wrong (hugely game breakingly wrong IMO). It also served as a guide for how the GM should have NPCs respond to you. If I have have a high score in Lust than the GM is likely to have chaste and polite company look down on me and priests rail against me for my sinful nature. If I have a high score in Bravery than the GM is likely to have my peers praise my courage and my lord select me for dangerous missions because he knows I won't run.
    \n

If I have a high enough reputation for being honest (i.e. a score of 16 or more out of 20 earned after several sessions of behaving honestly ) I will earn Glory. But being dishonest is now more likely to drop my score then being honest is to increase it, so inconsistent (i.e. honest when its convenient) play will keep my score too low to earn Glory. At Glory earning levels the GM can use some coersion on me (in the form of forcing a roll before allowing me to lie) but presumeably if I was willing to let my score get that high I am willing to accept that possibility. If I'm not, its a simple matter to not let my score get that high.
    \n
    \nSo...instead of a "dance for me monkey" mechanic where the GM or a random die roll tells me how I must now perform...consider a mechanic that tracks how I actually do perform over time and use your Stress / Synchro mechanic as a carrot similar to Glory to make me want to perform (i.e. look for opportunities to demonstrate my honesty) and to perform consistently (i.e. struggle to be honest even when its not convenient).

    \n Matthew, nobody like the "GM picks from players list of 3 combo" for two reasons:

    1. It's not a particularly meaningful choice for the player. The difference between the GM picking from the full list and the GM picking from the player's list of 3 isn't very large. It can also lead to frustration: if a player really wants to roleplay one particular vice/virtue, and the GM keeps not picking that one, the player is going to get really frustrated, and the GM may not realize why.

    I'd suggest the player giving the GM a choice between 2 options, or the GM giving the player a choice between 2 options. I think the second way would be better, personally -- it still gives the GM a lot of influence, but the player will feel better about having the final choice -- though you won't know for sure unless you play both versions out.

    2. It's counter-intuitive. I'm guessing you play in a more traditional style where the usual structure of that kind of play is:
    GM: This is what happens/this is where you are. What do you do?
    Player: I do this/I go there. What happens?
    Repeat.

    Essentially, the GM lays out options, the player picks one. But your system turns that around -- the player presents options, and the GM picks one. It cuts against the grain.

    Hope that helps.
