    \n Yeah, I think overlapping start times is a good idea, particularly if GMs plan accordingly. Probably a good strategy for GMs is to plan for some regular games (for 4 hour slots) and some short-form or trivial games that can start and finish in a two hour window. That might include board and card games and short runs of other games (half a Fiasco, a single planetary raid in 3:16, a demo fight for Dungeon World).

    I don't think people should really expect to come to the room, pick a game up off the table and be ready to play. If you have any inclination to run games at GoD, come with something (or several somethings) in mind to run. That's especially true for anything new (i.e. debuting at Gen Con).

    \n We assume many of the games will run for 4 hours, not 2. However, we expect new games to start on the two-hour marks. Also, anybody who does have short games ready to go is welcomed, nay, encouraged to bring them by! And the room doesn't open until 10 because we're running it alongside our booth and stuff, and we have a lot of stuff going on in the evenings as well so we have to sleep sometime. Anybody interested in running at GoD who hasn't contacted me already, go ahead and do so and we'll start building the formal side of the schedule. There will always be the informal side as well, though.
    \n
    \nWe tried for a bigger space from Gencon and weren't able to this year. A lot of this is "proof of concept" as far as Gencon themselves are concerned- if we pull this off and have lots of happy customers and don't lose them money, I'm pretty sure we'll get considerably more buy-in (and a bigger space) in future shows.       \n A couple of points:

      \n Hey all: I did Games on Demand for years, and this looks to be a make-or-break-it year. I've got Thoughts and Directed Questions.

      THOUGHTS

      * I talked with Derek Guder ("GenCon LLC Guy") when we hung out around GPNW, did you know that you can still schedule GenCon events now, even while at the con? Contact them with your requirements and where you want to run. They can't get it into the print catalog (a lot of events will be left out this year), but if you suddenly are able to go to GC and didn't register events back in Whenever, you can still round up people online, find a timeslot that works for you, then ask GenCon where they can stick you. If you really want a place to play and know that you have a Time and People, they'll work for you to get you a spot.

      And unlike Origins, the staff isn't ineffectual dumbasses** so you will actually be able to be helped if you make these kinds of requests.
      ** (this is my commentary, based on my own and others' experiences at Origins)

      * In the future (NA for this year, too late) if you register 8 events (32 hours?) over the course of the five days of GenCon, you will receive a free hotel room right next to the convention center in the GenCon LLC block. So, run those 4 sessions of Fiasco, 3 sessions of PTA and one Polaris over 5 days, and you can get into GC for free basically (the biggest expense is traditionally the hotel fees).

      * Every year there is talk of designers sending their books to the GOD booth for people to use for pick-up play. Just FYI, it never works: The folks facilitating don't have a full day to read your game, get their heads around it, get ready to facilitate it, and so on. Some folks provided "full demo kits" in the past, but it's basically the same thing: However, full demo kits are good for the people who have run the game in the past just don't have the prep work: It just won't get people who have never run your game before to suddenly bone up for it and run it.
      --- To that end (a little late in the game, sorry) if you want people to run your game at Games on Demand, you'll need to either:
      ------ Produce a game last year
      ------ Run it yourself, if you are attending. Maybe for friends who will then learn enough to run it for others, or
      ------ Have close friends run it, people who have run your game in the past.

      * Folks complain about "the new hotness" not being run at GoD, or at scheduled events. Can't be helped: Some games are too new and not enough seed time for people to run them locally and get familiar with them. But what can be helped is to get a tally of some potentially popular games and make sure you have enough people able to run them. Jason mentioned Apocalypse World, which is going to be pretty hot still (last year all the people who knew how to run it at GC were in one of two categories: 1 Tied to the Forge Booth, or 2 John Harper). Other games that will be pretty hot are Do (probably the best combination of Hot vs Easy to Learn), Dungeon World, Fiasco (maybe the companion playsets), Dresden Files (if you can prep stuff for it), Smallville might still have juice in it, Technoir (unpublished, but Phat Sponsored through Kickstarter), Micrsoscope, Mouseguard, Freemarket, A Taste for Murder, maybe Burning Wheel Gold, maybe Panty Explosion. The more of those you know (or a few, solidly) the more useful you will be to the people coming in off the street.
      * There's still time to learn some of the games above: If you dedicate to learning/running some of them, most of the designers/publishers will be happy to score you a free copy in return for running 2-3 sessions of it, at the very least a PDF. Just hammer that all out in advance.
      Games that will *not* be hot are Your Playtest Game Thing. So don't be offended if people pass over it and ask to be run published stuff on the above list.

      * Tickets, tickets, tickets. I know that I've personally done the dumb thing of:
      --- "Let's just throw down and worry about collecting tickets later" (then forget to collect them)
      --- Collecting tickets, then stuffing them in my bag, forgetting to turn them in, then seeing them in my bag a week later at home. "Ffffuuuuu..."
      --- Just not knowing/caring about ticket collection
      --- And so on
      Tix are important to collect and turn in, and fuckups like my own on a grand scale (ie Everyone doing this 1-2 times or more over the weekend) tells the GC organizers that giving X tables away to GoD is basically throwing away good tables that could be used for real events with actual players; next year you *may* get X-2, X-4 tables because you proved that you weren't able to fill the tables, that your sessions were cancelled with no players, that your events were failures, even when they were not!

      DIRECTED QUESTIONS TO THE ORGANIZERS

      * Who are the GMs/facilitators who are going to be running games this year at Games on Demand?

      * What are the games that each of them will be prepared for?

      * What times have they committed to being there (their "schedules")?
      (I saw the doodle, but there's a lot of blanks of "X might come by and help for a bit" etc. And as Jason said, looks sparse at times for ostensibly 6 tables at all times... I see only 4 "Yes" max for six tables for the entire weekend)

      * How will people anounce/schedule their tables so that people know when games are available (Like, Me: "I'm running Freemarket from 10-2PM at table 4." You: "I'm running Microscope at table 3 from 10-12PM". How do we post this so that passers-by who want to play but can't cause all tables are full, know when they should return?).

      * Who are the folks who will be onsite (and when) to facilitate the GoD area (collecting tickets; making sure tickets are collected and handed to GC staff), making sure folks are getting organized into games, etc)? (NOT the GMs)

      * What is the contingency for too many people wanting to play at a time when there are too few GMs available? (that is, say 10 GMs/facilitators show up on schedule but there's a flood of 80 people who want to play Anything). What do you do then?

      ======

      Thanks guys for attempting to make this work, hopefully it'll all work out this year and be successful!

      -Andy

      \n So we gathered a total of 613 tickets for roughly (the data is a little shaky) 413 players over 40 hours of play. By "players" I mean a 2 hour seat at a gaming table, costing 1 generic ticket ($2). In other words, people paid for their games and gave us several hundred dollars extra just to make sure we get to do it again.

      We kept six gaming tables full most of the time, and occasionally had to expand to other quickly-assigned tables in nearby rooms. Usually that was one or two extra tables, but at some point on Saturday we had eleven tables running at once.

      The fact that we the schedule was broken into 2-hour slots created some confusion, as people bought tickets for specific times and were confused about why we couldn't "meet their demand" when they showed up. We promised to make good on their tickets, and I think most everyone was understanding about it. For next year, I'm thinking we list the event as an all day slot ("Saturday 10am-10pm Games on Demand").

      Alternately, I think we might consider doing a fund-raiser (Kickstarter or the like) to let people buy in and sponsor the event up front, buying a room outright from Gen Con and forgoing the tickets altogether. That needs more thought, but it seems like a possibility.

      For what it's worth, the Crown Plaza location was much more convenient this year, considering the exhibit hall move and the opening of the skywalk. I would do it there again assuming we could get two rooms together and get out from under the train tracks...
