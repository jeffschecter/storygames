    \nExample? Sure, sure. (Also, this might not be a good question for your purposes anyway. I asked it because it's always been floating there, present at the game table from my earliest days. In fact it was much more present then than now.)

    So, say I'm gaming with my friend Dirk. He's got a snack that i want him to share, but he's not sharing. Should i do him a favor in-fiction in order to get the snack out-of-fiction?

    Or, better even (& from the direction that my question is pointing; from out-of-fiction to in-fiction): say i'm gaming with my friend Margaret. She's a friend of mine from way back. We're playing a game that supports some amount of pvp, say. The fiction is leading toward her character allying with the guy across the table, and that would be disadvantageous to me. Should i try and guilt her into having her character ally with mine instead, leveraging our long friendship?

    Or, say i was gaming with Todd. Todd and i live pretty far away, and i drove him. Classic, right? We're playing a game where Todd's role comes with an ability to give or withhold a benefit to my character. Is it fair play & part of the game for me to mention that i too have an ability to give or withhold a benefit (like, f'rex, i could leave my pal high and dry to navigate the night buses.)


    It's easy to say that this sort of issue is for each group to decide independently, but i don't think groups talk about this kind of thing objectively. If someone leverages it, either someone shouts them down ("Don't be a jerk, you have to give him a ride home. If you don't, you're an asshole") or it totally flies.

    Should it be part of playing a game?

      \n
      \n I have a suggestion for a topic.
      \n
      \nOriginally RPGs were inspired by fantasy fiction. More recently, movies/television and video games (i.e. the constant plaint that D&D; 4E is an MMO) have been major influences on tabletop RPGs.
      \n
      \nYet, tabletop RPGs are none of these. An RPG session is not a book, nor is creating it like creating a book; nor is it like creating a 47 minute television episode or playing/coding an MMO.
      \n
      \nI know a number of fantasy authors that have pointed out that many young aspiring writers don't seem to know how to write any more - that they describe the action as if they are describing a visual scene on a screen to another person, because so much of the media they consume is video.
      \n
      \nTranslating books to film, or vice versa, or the entire works of Uwe Boll demonstrate that the media matters.
      \n
      \nSo here's the topic: how does the fact that the "medium" of tabletop RPGs is unlike any of its sources of inspiration affect the end result? Are there things you see in source media that are impossible or unwise to try in a TTRPG? Are there techniques - drawn perhaps from improv theatre, oral storytelling, corporate brainstorming - that better match the medium that aren't part of the gamer lexicon?
      \n
      \nTL;DR - for all we talk about "framing the scene", there is no scene, because we're not actually looking at anything. Discuss.

      \n A few years ago I was in a game that was set up in a spaceship the size of a city which ticks some of the boxes you guys talked about - travelling platform so there were reasons for absent players to be absent, a big sprawling universe so that a given episode would have a subset of the available characters, and fast character creation (stripped down version of Fate.)

      It was also set up as troupe play, with people running multiple characters each (bonus points for making a player talk to themself), and it was set up so that it was very easy to do side stories and flashbacks. So if someone couldn't make it, maybe we'd go back and do an episode about something that happened to a character before the nominal timeframe of the game started, or an away team mission, or do a one off on a new planet with standalone characters. Sometimes they'd stay as a one off or sometimes they'd build themselves into the plot and become really important to the campaign. (Planet creation could also be pretty fast - someone would have an idea for a planet and other people were really comfortable with adding in new details and building something fun in 5-10 minutes). It was epic!
