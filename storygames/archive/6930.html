    Kagematsu is the role-playing game I\u2019m currently designing.

    In it, one person assumes the role of Kagematsu, a wayward samurai who wanders into a village beset by a terrible evil. Everyone else plays the village\u2019s women, who see in Kagematsu a potential savior. The game plays out as a series of romantic (or not-so-romantic) encounters as the women attempt to entice Kagematsu into staying and defending their little village. In the end, they must ply the hope he provides against the thing they fear most, or be lost entirely.

    Kagematsu is designed to be played in a single evening. It plays best with around 4 people, and you absolutely can not play it without out at least one female in your group.

    \nThe important thing is that you play opposite your gender. A woman plays Kagematsu and the men play the women of the village.

    Interesting. I picked up Colonial Gothic last year. I love that period of history, so for me the idea is hotness, and the simplicity of the core system appeals to me. Just haven't cut the time to run it yet (maybe late this year or mid-next). Could you perhaps break down your thoughts on the game in a mini-review in its own thread at some point?
    \nI will put it on my extensive "To Do" list. Short form, combat runs like the wind, it desperately needs quick reference charts, and I had to immediately house-rule that everybody on both sides gets 2 actions, not one, after my clever playtesters just walked away from the fight... I wish there were more pre-made adversaries to give me a better idea of what's balanced. I haven't even *looked* at Secrets (the first supplement), and Poor Richard's Almanac is coming out soon. Also, the author is working on a revised edition to address some of the problems like charts and indices. They have an excellent wiki with loads of keen info, and that is helpful.

    The playtesters liked it enough to request more games.

    \n I went as an attendee for once! It was a lot of fun.

    I got a little taste of D&D 4e with Clyde Rhoer running and Luke and I playing. We got briefly interrupted by a tornado (it may just have been a microburst, but tornado sounds better). It was neat!

    Ralph Mazza, Mike Holmes and I had a chance to try out the new Tribune board game. It was fun! I completely flubbed my first turn and got trounced.

    Ralph, Mike and I also tried Mansuba, which was a wacky chinese checkers kinda thing with an egyptian theme. It did have some cool randomizer sticks though!

    Ralph ran a playtest of Blood Red Sands for me, Mike, Danielle and Luke. I think I showed Ralph that Blessed Purple dice are busted! Oops.

    Paul Czege ran Avengers of Justice! for me, Clyde, Mike Miller and Ralph. I got to play Brick Red. It was an interesting game from the early 90s with a handful of cool ideas and a few stinkers. The core idea of the comic flowchart was very cool. I'd love to see an execution of it that works.

    I ran a game of Mouse Guard for Paul, Danielle, Ralph and Mike Holmes (who subbed for Clyde when he ran to do an interview). I think MG is the easiest game I've ever run.

    I played a game of Marvel Superheroes (FASERIP) run by my friend Nick Logue (who's written some great material for both WotC and Paizo and who now runs his own indie D20 module imprint, Sinister Adventures). I got to play Kid Nitro, an adrenaline junkie with a snowboard and twin freezie pistols powered by liquid nitrogen tanks on my back. Jared played my sidekick Shepherd (a brilliant scientist who lost most of his intelligence when his genetic code was mutated by the introduction of the genes of a German Shepherd). There were also a bunch of other folks at the table whom I didn't know that well. Nick is an incredibly entertaining GM. This was a lot of fun!

    I ran a playtest of Dark Pages with Clyde and Jared. Great stuff. Clyde played a sinister adept who'd sold his best friend's soul to a demon by mistake, and is now working for that demon as a fixer in an attempt to win the soul back. Jared played a vigilante guerrilla journalist obsessed with a female former coworker. It was creepy and fun!

    On Sunday, Luke and I had our flight home canceled by nasty thunderstorms in NYC. Fortunately Fred Hicks, Rob Donoghue and Paul Tevis were incredibly gracious and allowed us to crash on their floor before catching our flight home early the next morning.

    All in all, it was a blast!

    \n I had a great time. I playtested Acts of Evil on Thursday evening with Clyde, Don Corcoran, and Michael S. Miller as the GM. It was a pre-arranged game so I could see play from the player perspective to inform how I write the final game text. Then later that same Thursday evening I was interviewed by Luke Crane. It was less about promoting Half Meme Press or The Ashcan Front than prior interviews I've done. More about my design and creative aspirations and what I think about design trends. It was great.

    Avengers of Justice is a superhero RPG from the turn of the 90s that I think has some thought-provoking mechanics. It was in the schedule for Friday as an IGE event with me as GM, but no one had signed up for it. Fortunately, Thor Olavsrud, Michael S. Miller, Ralph Mazza, and Clyde L. Rhoer bombed into it with generic tickets. One reason I run scheduled games is because as a designer there aren't enough opportunities to run games I've never experienced before. (Whoa, violation of the social contract to run an unplaytested scenario for a game you've never run before? Nobody rat me out!)

    Thor ran Mouse Guard for me, Danielle, Ralph, and Mike Holmes on Friday evening. Remember the boring ass outdoor exploration-via-skill challenges gameplay in a lot of 80s era games? Roll repeatedly to climb a cliff face? Roll a skill challenge to use your tricorder to find the mineral deposits the company wants? Mouse Guard solves that bullshit.

    Had a very thought-provoking conversation with Mike Holmes on Saturday morning about the relationship between the "story game" social culture, the mechanical signature of the games of the culture, and how games rely on or challenge the social culture. I already had a pretty good plan for the final text of Acts of Evil, but this conversation totally re-galvanized me in my plan. Ran my Nine Worlds "Kill Oedipus" scenario that afternoon to a full table of people I'd never met before (one of whom was Bill White). Another reason I run scheduled games is to meet new people. And two of the Nine Worlds players then went on to participate as playtesters for Danielle for Kagematsu that evening. This is awesome. And that playtest was a lot of fun.

    And then on Sunday morning I playtested Clyde's Silence Keeps Me A Victim, which he's developing as an ashcan for Gen Con, and gave him feedback. And that was fun.

    I really wanted to meet up with Grant Davis to playtest Secrets and Lies. It will also be an ashcan at Gen Con. But. There. Just. Wasn't. Time!

    Paul

    \n I ran Dungeons and Dragons on Wednesday night for Thor and Luke. I was impressed at how easy it is to whip something up if you have a module to crib from. Thor had the Shadow-whatever module Wizards has put out and I never saw it before, but it was easy to steal from with those maps and stat blocks. I was also impressed at how easy it was to change the difficulty of challengers based on the number of players you have. We did two encounters and called it a night. That second encounter had Thor's human ranger at one hit point, and Luke's Cleric low on resources also. I simply chose the appropriate number of monsters based on their xp, and we had a fairly close game.

    Thursday I played in Acts of Evil. I enjoyed the game even though I tend not to play it correctly. One day I'll have to map out a strategy, and give up my characters humanity to play the game. Acts of Evil is pushing for a different kind of play than what I'm used to, or a style other than what I want, despite my words otherwise, and it beats you up until you "get" that kind of play. For now I'm still fighting the game. Perhaps permanently, which makes me wonder if I don't need to learn the secret lesson of the game.

    Friday I played in Paul's Avengers of Justice game mentioned above. It had some really cool ideas that were occluded by it's mechanics. I couldn't just punch someone, so when my two attack powers powered down, I was left with no meaningful way to contribute to the fiction. The most fun part of the game is where we basically defaulted to a freestyle form of play to do something else other than fighting, or as traditional folks might say, we made the system get out of our way for awhile. Someone should pirate this game for good ideas and make rules that actually work.

    I also played Mouseguard for a brief bit, and I yearn for this game.

    I played Darkpages also and had a bit of a hard time at first as I don't have an understanding of the fiction that inspired the game and was too tired to be very creative. Once I got that we were thinking in squares I started to get the game much better.

    Paul playtested my game Silence Keeps Me A Victim, and gave me a lot of useful feedback.

    I also interviewed Andrea Sfiligoi creator of Song of Blades and Heroes, Peter Atkinson, Thor Olavsrud, and my microphones might have recorded other secretive audio.

    I also got to chat with quite a few folks, which was good.

    #4 minus cilantro for the win.

    \n Fuck yeah...if you're going as a civilian Origins has always been a better con for gamers then Gen Con (with the possible exception of Magic players and RPGA living X players). The dealer hall is smaller and much more manageable, and has a bunch of crafts, costumes, and non related geeky stuff instead of blaring electronic nonsense. Open gaming is a breeze to find space for just about any time. There are a good 4 or 5 solid hotels immediately adjacent (and often connected to ) the convention center. The Big Bar on 2 is a fantastic evening hangout. Food choices aren't as diverse as Indy, but the Food Court is a reasonable alternative.
    \n
    \nYeah, I used to go to Origins every year before the GenCon Forge Booth made me choose. Its only been the last 2 years I've been back. Its way more fun.
