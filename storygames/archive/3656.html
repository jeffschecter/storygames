The use of the word "jargon" as a buzz word

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTyler \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJuly 2007 \n edited July 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I was reading a review of Burning Empires the other day, and mixed in with what was otherwise a decent review was this comment:
\n
\n
Jargon. Oh my, yes, the jargon factor is high. Much of it is inherited from Burning Wheel, of course. For example, the physical stats include "Forte" (toughness), "Power" (strength), "Agility" (manual dexterity), and "Speed" (agility). For reasons I can't fathom, a handful of N six-sided dice is noted as "Exponent N" instead of "rating of N", or "[N]d6", or "roll [N] dice." Dice that are not successes (i.e., rolled 1, 2, or 3) are called "Worms" instead of failures. The points used for advancement and special effort are called "Artha" instead of experience, or hero points, "Field of Related Knowledge" (FoRK) instead of related or complementary skill, "Wise" skill instead of knowledge skill or lore, etc.. This is compounded by the fact that the sci-fi setting also uses its own share of specialized vocabulary, of course; newcomers to both the setting and the system have a hard time remembering all these terms.
\n
\n
\nAll of these terms (and presumably more) are referred to as "jargon". Jargon is usually defined as terms or language for a specific field used to intentionally confuse those unintiated with the language. First, I do not believe the above is really "jargon" at all, but beyond that I've seen a somewhat distrubing trend to label any terminology that isn't from a traditional gaming text as "jargon", which has undertones of pretentiousness and of being outside the realm of the normal consumer. I've begun to believe that this has simply become a buzz word by those who aren't necessarily indie game supporters, much in the same way "liberal" was a buzz word in the mid-2000's, and that it has the same net effect of pushing people away who might otherwise be interested.
\n
\nSo am I off base here...would you call this stuff jargon? Do you think we should hew closely to the well worn terms of other games, even if different terms might be more effective communication tools? How do you turn the tide on buzz words, like this?

    Me: "Okay, so, what's your Municipal Politics-wise exponent?"
    Player: "What the fuck as an exponent?"
    Me: "The number by 'Municipal Politics-wise'"
    Player "Uh, 3."

    My players don't enjoy having pure-replacement terminology forced down their throats. Honestly, for later games, I dropped the terminology and just said things like "Roll your [whatever] skill and get X successes.] With yhat said, I think that's a valid complain for Sophie to make in her review.     To answer the question, no, I wouldn't call that "jargon," but "game-specific terms."

    \n Okay. I'm familiar with both BW and BE and the terms "worms" and "exponent" were chosen for good reasons, at least in my estimation. You can ignore them, of course.

    Exponent - A skill rating doesn't just have a number. It also has a "shade" of black, gray or white. Thus, exponent was a term coined to differentiate one part of the rating from the other. You could ask "What is your rating?" and have the person answer "B5", if you wanted. But if you are talking about the number specifically, the term "exponent" is more precise. After all, some things affect only the exponent and some things affect the shade of the rating.

    Worms - Pure color. Worms are what the Vaylen (the bad guys) are called in the game. I tend to ignore this term but color is a valid reason for choosing to use it

    \n Thanks Tyler!

    How about this: Not only indie games, but many games make use of specialized terminology that can make understanding and presenting the game more difficult. White Wolf is a classic example of a game producer who tends to do this. In order to make a game as accessible as possible a designer should avoid special terminology unless it adds a lot of flavor, is clearly explained and useful, or really does represent a more unique concept in the game. Not to pick on anyone, but take Seth's Legends of Alyria as an example. I think that game is loaded with cool stuff, but I had to go over the rules several times to finally figure things out because of strange terms that I simply couldn't wrap my head around. That's a problem - if I, a guy who reads tons of games, can't figure out the core mechanics until I draw out sketches then there's something wrong. In the case of Burning Empires, the word 'exponent' flat out scares my math adverse players. While I love FoRKs because I think it's intuitive once you understand the concept, and I can understand the reasoning behind exponent, I find it to be another conceptual step to teaching the game.

    Keep in mind I'm just wanting to illustrate how new terms can be a problem. I like special terms fine, but there are some circumstances where they can be a problem (such as redefining common terms in an already robust game). I liked Burning Empires, I think it's a great game, but in this reviewer's actual play experience (she tried running a campaign) these terms were a problem for her group. I think that's a pretty good basis for saying the terms may be a bit much - she's not just guessing and trying to illustrate a concept like I am, but it *was* a problem in her group's play.

    Ultimately I think we agree but are kinda talking past each other. No one wants a game so loaded with weird terms that people have trouble understanding it, and no one wants to read Vanilla: The RPG of Attribute + Skill Dice Rolling. As with most things, there's a range and some games may push

    All that being said - I think if the reviewer had said "special terms" instead of "jargon" a lot of the discussion here would disappear. Don't do the geek thing and get hung up on definitions and what the right word is where you understand what the reviewer is trying to communicate - that these terms made things more difficult for her group.

    \n The Jargon thing is usually aimed at Indie Game Theory, not the games in particular. That is, I've seen as much bashing of non-indie RPGs for the use of original terminology as I have of indie games. And I agree with it by turns. Hollyhock God for GM in Nobilis? That's just more color than I want to swallow. I think there is such a thing as taking things too far.
    \n
    \nAnd, actually, I'd say that Luke totters on that edge. But, as Jason says, Luke does that intentionally. His games might be a lot dryer without some of the color he adds with these terms. Playing BE with him at GenCon, we were regularly bemoaning the number of worms we'd rolled. It's just fun to say, "Aaagh! Look at all those worms!" right after you drop the dice for a bad roll. These terms certainly have a lot of weight for Luke, at least.
    \n
    \nAs far as the term Jargon itself goes, it's often given a negative connotation, but, in fact I believe that Jargon has legitimate uses beyond the social exclusion that's often associated with it. I mean, sure, doctors could refer to the Gluteus Maximus as the butt, but, whereas the butt is ill defined, the Gluteus Maximus is not. It's not just a shorthand in this case, but terms that have agreed to specific definitions that make it possible for discussion to occur about a subject with less miscommunication. And it can give focus to a discussion.
    \n
    \nTo the extent that something in a RPG is unique to that RPG, it makes some sense, then, to have a new term for it, so that people can understand that referencing the term refers to that particular specific defintion, and not some similar concept. I think we all agree that terms of this sort make sense. It's only when the term is created clearly for color that we all start to wonder. And sometimes it's iffy.
    \n
    \nFor Sophie (or whoever wrote the review) apparently Luke went overboard in her estimation. That's a valid criticism, to me. A subjective and local one, but that's part of criticism, too.

    \n This discussion has added at least this much value (at least!): I'm going to be carefully reviewing my use of specialized terms in my current design, and killing off or making clearer the ones that are not immediately obvious. (Also, if something is the same as an existing concept, I'll make sure I use the existing term.)

    Another point, related: as more games are produced, a lot of everyday words get "used up". I originally was going to call my reroll mechanic Twists (an uptwist when rerolling failures, a downtwist when rerolling successes). However, Colin is using Twists in Sufficiently Advanced to mean something completely different (and more intuitively obvious, I think, though I can't just now recall exactly what it is). So my term Twists has two strikes against it now: It's not immediately obvious what one is, and it will potentially confuse people who've played Sufficiently Advanced.

    Hence, Twists become Rerolls, divided into Rollups and Rolldowns. Or even "rerolls of successes" and "rerolls of failures", but I think Rollups and Rolldowns is probably clear enough once explained, and more concise.

    IMO this is less important than "owning" the terms you use. E.g., I've seen a tendency to borrow terms from other games, give credit and maybe quote a passage, as if this economizes on the amount of rethinking, rewriting, and relearning that has to happen before the player gets the concept.     \n Good point, Elliot, thanks - when using an existing term, that doesn't mean I shouldn't explain the existing term as if my readers haven't encountered it before (as I mentioned on another thread, talking about the terms "GM" and "NPC" and how they get used without definition in game texts). If they have encountered it before, they'll quickly realize as they read the explanation, "Oh, this is like in TSOY" (or whatever) and move on; if not, the explanation is necessary for them to understand it.

    Shreyas, I'm not sure what you mean by "complimentary distribution". Rollups and rolldowns in the Pentasystem are complementary, yes, but the distinction is important. You invoke a rollup by describing an advantage you have relating to the attribute you're using, it costs you a point, and you reroll a failure; you invoke a rolldown by describing a disadvantage, it gets you a point, and you reroll a success. I think that justifies making a distinction, and having specific terms.
