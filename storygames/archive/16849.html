\n It's been awhile since I've popped my head in the indie gaming community, but I thought I'd drop by and share some interesting moments from the past year. I've been working on an experimental comic project called Dragon Pulp. The gist is that each update ends in a poll where the readers choose which direction the story goes... it's somewhere between a webcomic and an old choose-your-adventure story where the choice is crowd-sourced. I try to give at least three options that lead in different directions- sometimes the difference is a matter of nuance, but I try to make the story hinge on each decision as often as possible without hurting the pacing.

Needless to say, the readers/players consistently chose the option I was the least prepared for. Ahh.... just like gaming.

From an RPG design perspective, the system is crude: the creative control from the readers is limited to the options I give to them, and even then their vote is only one of several. As the writer, I have the ability to choose the consequences of the group's choice- which theoretically means I could easily railroad all of the choices... but half the fun is sharing creative control.

I started out assuming that the best approach would be to have event-based votes: 'what does character X do?' or 'Is action Y successful?', but I've been learning that the more fun approach is to let the readers/players dictate characterization: 'how does character X feel about what just happened?'. The latter option has the extra benefit of being a huge indication of how rounded the characters feel ("character X is obviously the villain, eh? let's see if we can sneak in something that makes it possible to empathize with him" or "character Y is obviously the hero, eh? let's see if we can sneak some moral ambiguity into him").

This first story had a bit of a learning curve to it, so I imagine the second one will be a little stronger, but still... it's an overall success so far. If you'd like to join in, you can hop over to www.Dragonpulp.com. You can vote through the website, but I also send out a weekly e-mail with the installment and vote embedded into it (which is how most of the readers get it).

The first story is barreling towards its climactic conflict (since the tension of the story depends on the uncertainty of the vote, I had to make sure the hero was developed enough that the final conflict would have him torn between several different choices).

    \n Thanks! I originally wanted to make it a series of short stories, with each being 5 installments or less so people could easily jump in at any point- but when I came to the end of the first one, the popular consensus was "wait a sec... he's not dead yet... come back and finish this story." It turns out the readers wanted to spend enough time with a character to develop an attachment to them. Shoulda seen that one coming.

    When this story ends, I'll probably switch gears and start a new one. Since I'll be aiming for novella length instead of short story, I'll be able to structure it a little better...

    The current story's structure was cobbled together last minute since I had planned on ending it around installment 4... the readers caught me off guard... but that's what happens when two groups with disparate creative agendas share creative control, eh? I might've handled it a bit better, but I'm chalking that up to the learning curve.
