\n I have been tinkering with a homebrew RPG system for some time and have run into a snag, though one that's less about the system and more about its appeal. Specifically: Player characters are meant to be young -- 10 to 14 years old. Probably street urchins or orphans of some kind, though they could simply be young adventurers. I like the system I've devised to represent this: certain skills are handicapped when you're younger, and you are less able to lend assistance to other characters, though on the flip side you heal more swiftly, enjoy better "luck," and get second chances on more occasions (plus may throw a tantrum from time to time). And if you play a long campaign, this shifts over time as your character ages.

My problem is this: As I back up and look at who the audience might be for a game like this, it occurs to me that there may not be a lot of people who are interested in playing as young / weak characters. While I haven't yet trotted this system out to friends, I'm finding it hard to imagine someone getting excited about taking on the role of an underpowered 11-year-old street urchin (even if it's in an interesting semi-magical, semi-steampunk, semi-Victorian setting, though that might help). I personally think it could be interesting, I'm just not sure how broad a gaming demographic I represent.

I've given just a little thought to alternate frameworks for a similar system (e.g., you're cursed by angry gods and are on a quest to throw off your handicaps while also completing whatever tasks they've set you -- or something), and while I'm seeing some promise in some of those, I still find myself questioning the premise of playing as a weak character.

So I guess I have two questions:
1. Can you point me to any other games in which players take on weak / underpowered characters?
2. Do you think such a game could be at all broadly interesting? (I.e., certainly not as broad as Pathfinder or D&D, and perhaps not even as broad as Fate or Savage Worlds, for example, but maybe approaching something in the junior leagues of the latter.)

Thanks in advance! And welcome to the end of my first post ever here

    \n Welcome!

    It's funny that you mention D&D, because in the early editions, player characters were extremely "weak"... (And I find that kind of D&D very enjoyable, personally.)

    In any case, when dealing with "weak or incompetent" characters, the real question becomes:

    * Are they capable of making meaningful decisions and achieving goals in the context the game presents?

    Is the idea, for instance, that these "incompetent" characters manage to accomplish great things, as in a lot of fiction about children or young adults (e.g. fairy tales, Harry Potter, or the hobbits in Lord of the Rings)? Perhaps through luck, courage, insight, moral strength, or by cleverness and collaboration, they manage to achieve meaningful victories.

    Here, the appearance of "incompetence" is belied by an actual ability to do great things: the incompetence is entirely a facade created to make the victories all that more amazing. In game turns, I'd expect characters in such a story to be anything but incompetent (although a good game designer could make you feel like you had little chance of winning, if that was desired).

    That's entirely different from a game where you fully embrace your character's inability to achieve anything meaningful, like Nicotine Girls. Here, the entire conceit of the game is to find meaning in your character's struggles and failures.

    Which one are you talking about? They're very different, to my eye.

    \n Thanks, Paul. While I really enjoy the conceit of games like Nicotine Girls, this is definitely meant to be in the other camp. The so-called "incompetent" characters should be able to achieve great things through (to borrow your list) "luck, courage, insight, moral strength, or by cleverness and collaboration."

    That list is actually very interesting. I have accounted (mechanically) for a couple of those. Luck and collaboration, certainly. Cleverness, to an extent. But courage, insight, and moral strength would be interesting to incorporate as well.

    To explain briefly: In some situations, you have the option to improve a failed or poor roll by using a different skill. You'd have to narrate how you're able to use your Investigate skill to defend against the slingshot stone that's coming your way, of course, but if you can do it, and if you have any more of your (fairly abundant) luck currency (Slugs), you get a chance to improve your result.

    Your list makes me think that this mechanic, which at the moment is almost exclusive to combat, could be interesting if it were available throughout. Any time you want to try to supplement a roll you don't like, you can spend a Slug, bring another one of your skills into the narrative, and roll to try to improve your result. In that way, you could let a good storyteller use his or her moral insight, for instance (Empathy skill?), to sneak by the dozing factory boss. A player who likes to fight but has rolled poorly in a negotiation could figure out how to use Strength to add conviction to an argument (short of literally strongarming someone, which would presumably be its own roll).

    I don't know if that completely answers my questions, but it helps me think about this in a different way. And hopefully helps focus my request, for anyone who's read this far.

    \n I think there's a sizable audience of gamers who don't care about fictional character power level for its own sake. What really matters is effectiveness in the situations at hand. If you march a bunch of 11-year-old orphans into a battle designed for Space Marines, now that would suck. But if your 11-year-olds have to deal with the sorts of things that 11-year-olds can actually deal with, then "underpowered" isn't an issue. I would love to play an 11-year-old n a game about stealth and lies and courage and teamwork and friendship and imagination. Maybe I'd even get into some combat -- against rats, dogs, small monsters, asshole 11-year-olds, etc. -- though that wouldn't be my first choice.

    If your aim is a broad appeal, then no, I don't think roleplaying kids will get you that. However, many games that shoot for "broad" wind up achieving "generic" and get no audience. At least with kids your game is distinctive! (Not offering any "do"s and "don't"s here, just matters for your consideration.)

    Other games where you play kids:
    - The Magical Land of Yeld (start as mundane and very young, but can gain very effective powers reasonably quickly)
    - Monsters and Other Childish Things (your monster effectively makes you a badass, but, y'know, there'll be times when you're just a kid)

    I can also name you games where you play wimpy goblins, second-class citizens, dungeon cannonfodder, slaves, minions, toys, puppets, and many more "underpowered" characters. Most of these have audience numbers that many sole creators would love to get for their games. Nowhere near being on the Fate / Savage Worlds map, though. Few games are!

    \n OK, so there's Little Fears obviously, about children surviving in a horror fantasy milieu. Princes' Kingdom is about the young princes and princesses of a fantasy kingdom. Dreaming Crucible is about a single pre-teenager getting stuck in the fairy kingdom. Grey Ranks is about teenage boy and girl scouts fighting against Nazi Germany in occupied Poland. Under the Bed is about a child and the toys that help them adapt to the problems in their life. Urchin is about homeless children and adults in horror fantasy New York severs. Ars Magica has a whole supplement dedicated to playing child apprentices of wizards, and it's far from the only game attempting to cash in on the Harry Potter craze.

    That's just a quick run-down of what came to mind with two minutes of thinking - there are a lot of games out there that feature children, and even more games that feature characters in positions of weakness. Not everybody likes these games, but those are generally the same people who basically aren't interested in dramatic games anyway, and always and only want to play adventure games in the D&D mold, so I don't know if that demographic is relevant to a designer who isn't making one of those games. "Broad appeal" is a red herring, because being compelling is so much more important that a game designer can rarely afford to sacrifice their originality and vision in exchange for supposed broadness of appeal. As David said, broad appeal is often no appeal at all.

    \n I'd like to add that the idea of "11-year-olds fighting space marines" is actually quite interesting to *me*. (I'm using it as a metaphor, mind you - actual 11-year-olds fighting in war sounds terribly grim.)

    Once again, it's not that different from old-school D&D: a group of underskilled and underprepared bumpkins go to confront enemies much more powerful and dangerous. (Consider that in old-school D&D a typical character has 1d6 hit points, whereas a typical monster has at least 1d8, and better odds of hitting in combat, never mind rules like doors opening automatically for them, but being stuck when the PCs try to open them.)

    The key, if you're looking for an adventure genre kind of thing, is to give the "underpowered" characters some way of overcoming that difference. Then they can really feel heroic in action (if that's the design goal here).

    Here's another example: in Mouse Guard, you play little mice, who often go up against far more scary opponents, like cats or owls. You have little chance unless you're clever and can use the mechanics to your advantage. In Mouse Guard, that means working together as a team to get bonuses - so you can actually win most of the time, so long as you work as a team. The mechanics support the theme.

    Exploiting human cleverness is also a good technique. The opponents may be more "powerful", but they have relatively little smarts. They probably just do the predictable thing - the GM has limited attention and time to devise anything better, after all. The players, however, all put their heads together and come up with the smartest plan they can, and thereby have an advantage. This is how a lot of games (especially old-school D&D) work, and it's quite satisfying when the group is committed to it.

    Puppets - Puppetland

You have to poke at the environment a lot, but if you're unlucky in that, you can quickly wind up mutated in a way you might not like.

    \n Actually, having listed so many other people's games already, I might as well list mine. It's not published, but I can certainly describe it at length. In Delve, you start as mundane medieval peasants, armed with only your wits and your willingness to face perils others won't. By facing such perils and learning from them and acquiring magical stuff along the way, you eventually grow to be anything but mundane.

    Perhaps there's some commonality there, in that you want your 11-year-olds to "achieve great things".

    Some of the other games I mentioned have a similar "start with nothing, work your way up" arc -- Goblin Uprising, Sign in Stranger, and most old-school D&D clones. Land of Yeld also, sort of; you start as mundane kids, but there's a big jump in power level that presumably happens fairly early on.
