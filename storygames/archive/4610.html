Adversity vs. Suffering

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2007 \n edited October 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So over here I talked about the fun of making and playing characters who are loaded down with misery. Now I'm up for talking about the craft of actually making it happen.
\n
\nSpecifically: There's two types of scene-fragments that both start out looking very similar ... but which actually seem (to me) to have wildly opposite intents. In both cases, character A (we'll call him Abe) is coasting along when something comes out and smacks him in the face with trouble or sadness or difficulty of some kind. His happiness and freedom are threatened. If something isn't done, his life is about to get much worse.
\n
\nNOW ... on the one hand this might be a adversity. The intent might be to set Abe up to try to overcome this conflict and change himself and his situation.
\n
\nBut on the other hand, it might be suffering. The intent might be to establish that Abe is a person who has bad stuff happen to him, and that there's nothing within his power to be done about that.
\n
\nSorta important to know which you're aiming for: Adversity absolutely requires that Abe have the power to fight back against his circumstances. Suffering absolutely requires that he not. If two people are trying to play out a scene with Abe, and they don't agree on whether it's adversity or suffering they will butt heads, sure as anything.
\n
\nSo, adversity is a well understood value in games. We're all used to doing it ... indeed, there's a pretty well understood formula for how it goes: Stakes, conflict, resolution, outcome.
\n
\nIn order for suffering scenes to get any traction, do they need to be equally well understood? Is it possible to get on the same page of "This is suffering, not adversity" if we understand how to do adversity but don't understand how to do suffering?
\n
\nAnyway ... a first draft of a formula:
\n(1) Character's bad circumstances are shown (e.g. "Keichi has no money")
\n(2) Some external pressure acts to make those circumstances immediate (e.g. "Keichi goes to buy ice cream for Belldandy ... but has no money")
\n(3) The external pressure is relieved (e.g. "Belldandy has money from her day-job, and offers to buy the ice cream instead")
\n(4) The circumstances are expressed again, at more intense pitch (e.g. "Keichi knows that without money he is not a man ... he cannot even buy ice cream for his girlfriend!")
\n
\nSound right?

    Posted By: cydmabI was going to ask whether the victim has to know if he has the power to fight back. Hmm, maybetragedyis when you think you have the power to fight back, but really you're doomed.
    I certainly don't think the character needs to know that they're powerless. I think that the player needs to know (or at least explicitly learn) that they don't have the power to fight back.

    The obvious benefit of this is that they don't get all emotionally invested in winning a conflict that they're foredestined to lose. Suffering is, after all, a micro-scale use of Illusionism: You're all getting together to railroad the character towards helplessness, as part of your overall (empowered) play.

    The other benefit that I (personally) take from knowing that I'm not going to win is that it's a safety net: When I want the character to lose (because I want his suffering) I like to be assured that no matter how hard I have him TRY to win, I won't accidentally sabotage my own fun by actually having him succeed. That way I can pull out all the stops.

    \nOkay, so there's a mechanic where if you take upon your character, no-contest suffering, there are mechanical effects, which may or may not be seen as beneficial, it really depends on what the player's goals are. So, you can say "Fine, I'm stabbed in the eye, I'm now Mutilated and I get tokens to help me in my next fight. By the way, you're a bastard." Note: You can also say "Some bastard stabs me in the eye. The results of the fight might be mutilation, death, injury, whatever to you or the other guy. The choice to fight or not could be anything, whether your character believes he can win, whether you do (your fighting ability might be crap), or whatever.

    But, looking at your draft, that's still probably Stage 1, "Bloodbeard is pissed that he's been mutilated." You can either make the choice to suffer stage 1 suffering and get a mechanical reward, or try and overcome the adversity and possibly have worse happen for no mechanical reward.

    To take a non-game example track which I think matches John's question: What if the player can choose between suffering and adversity? What if you can choose whether or not your character has the power to overcome it, does that make is suffering instead of adversity?

    And a question of my own: does having the power to solve the suffering have to negate the suffering?

    Like this:
    1. The problem: Keichi has no money.
    2. Immediate: Belldandy wants him to buy her some ice cream.
    3. Relieving the immediacy: Keichi get a loan from the local Yakuza and buy ice cream with it.
    4. Reinforce the hurt: Keichi was able to buy ice cream, but now he's in debt over his head.

    Or perhaps even something less obvious:
    3. Relieving the immediacy: Keichi gets a job as a law clerk so he can afford gifts for his girlfriend, and buys her ice cream first thing.
    4. Reinforce the hurt: Keichi's boss is a huge jerk and he hates his job, and now has no free time to spend with his girlfriend or pursue his own dreams, like race car driving. But if he quits, he has no money and is back to square 1.

    Couldn't you still focus on suffering even when you have the power to overcome the immediate problems? Couldn't this be more about "right sizing" the conflict?
