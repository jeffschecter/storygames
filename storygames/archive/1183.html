    40+ sales of a well-priced book probably means that most of the initial costs got covered.

    100+ sales means that the game is a good-sized success!

    1000+ sales means that you're Luke Crane or Vincent Baker, or another member of a relatively small list.

Indie folk tend to make more of their sales directly to the consumer (or through a "lightweight" fulfiller like IPR making sales directly to the consumer). This may mean lower sales volume, but it *does* mean that the money made per sale is larger than it would be "traditionally". And that means that a smaller number of sales still yields up enough to cover the break-even costs -- which is a decent benchmark for sustainability. Looking ahead to Spirit of the Century, I think we're going to make a fair chunk more than the per-book print costs on any direct-to-consumer sale, and that's exactly as designed. All Evil Hat *really* needs to happen with any product is to have it sell enough to cover the costs of pre-printing and anything we didn't do ourselves in-house (which usually means "the art budget", but sometimes means a few other things, like "$15 to license a font" or "several hundred dollars to get Genevieve Cogman to write a section of the Dresden Files RPG for us").

    A kind of off-topic but important note for game publishers/sellers that I thought I'd drop in since the issue is cropping up.

    Creative and ideals-related goals aside, if you are at least willing to state that you are publishing your game to make money, then a whole new world of business-related tax deductions opens up to you so long as the IRS believes you (or doesn't audit you). You can even take these deductions (and offset them against your other income) if it turns out that your game publishing business produced a loss for the year. But as soon as you say that your publishing is a hobby or that you don't care about making money, then you have effectively cut off all possibility of taking deductions for your game publishing-related expenses to the extent they exceed the money you make on sales. That's damn important. So watch what you say out here on the Internet because you never know who's going to see it.

    Note: Of course, this is a simplification of the business / hobby divide, and I'm not offering anyone legal advice here or telling you how to handle your business expenses. Go see an your accountant, your lawyer, or Turbo tax for that.

FASA? With the goddamn battletech property? Nope, couldn't keep them afloat. When they were up, it was almost all based on the board/minis game anyhow, not their RPG entries like Mechwarrior. GDW? Despite having Traveller, and some of the best minds in gaming, they couldn't stay afloat.

    Let's see, so we've got the "Big Names" like WotC, WizKids, and Games Workshop (not to forget your side of the pond, Max). Well, all of these are afloat on Miniatures or CCG sales. Yeah, Games Workshop has a RPG. Does it make money? I'll bet not. Oh, they'll make a mint on Warhammer Online, sure. Wanna bet whether they got that going based on Warhammer Minis sales, or based on WHFRP?

    In any case the owners of D&D, which represents half or more of this "market" have gone bankrupt. More than once. White Wolf? Dumped by WotC when they owned it. One guess as to why they dumped the number two gaming line.

    The problem is that no RPG line can continue to be profitable indefinitely. D&D does make money, sure, when it releases a new edition of it's core books. And then only. And then the "line" bleeds red for several years until they come up with the next edition. There have been, what? 8 major editions of D&D now? You think that they're not aware that this is the only time they make money? And they do it by, basically, selling to all the same people again and again.

    So, to put it simply, the idea of being a "Full Time RPG Designer" is a pipe dream. I mean, let's say that you design some really innovative game that, in fact, does like Magic: The Gathering did for WotC, and opens up gaming to a whole huge new crowd of people. Sells millions. Gets you rich. What will you do then? Will you call it your day job, and just keep selling the same game? RPG here, not CCG. Let's see, that's what Gygax did, and he went bankrupt. The man was a millionaire, and he lost it all, because he thought that there was such a thing as being a "Full Time RPG Designer."

    Ain't no such thing. What does a smart fellow do, when he sees that these sorts of bonanzas are one-time phenomena? He sells WotC to Hasbro, and makes GenCon his sole business. Then Hasbro subsequently cuts the staff down to a fraction of it's previous size.

    So...if one can be a game designer, and make money at it, come out in the black? Well, then one has done about as well as one can do in this "market."

    Is all of this somehow depressing? Yeah, it's depressing that it took people more than two decades of failure to realize these facts. If, however, you understand what RPGs are all about, the size of the community, how they sell to a limited group and are then done forever, for practical purposes. If you do that, you can make being an RPG designer an interesting and profitable sideline.

    That sounds like success to me.

    Put another way, why should one game, The RPG, support more people than Monopoly does? Given that Monopoly outsells all RPGs put together every year. Expecting RPGs to do this is just not realistic. The error wasn't Gygax's pretending it could be done. The error was everyone else thinking they could reproduce what he'd done, and failing to notice him go bankrupt.
