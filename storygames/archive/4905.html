    \n Order a draft copy well in advance. Make sure it all looks OK.

    Judging from my experience: get friends in Europe, UK and the US (whichever two you're not in) to order draft copies, too. Make sure they all look OK, too.

    Then you can resolve problems before you release it to the world.

    In short, really, the best way to ensure you have a hard copy in your hands in January is get a hard copy in your hands in December. Then, if there are problems, you've got a month to sort it out.

    Graham

    \n As a first, very important step: make sure your PDF is in the old 1.3 format for European printers. They don't support newer formats, including transparencies.

    Second: pay close attention to the bleed if you have a border that reaches the edge of the final book.

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonOriginally, their covers had the tendency to print a fair bit darker than you would expect. According to recent reports, they've made efforts to make covers print brighter, but I haven't seen anything to compare since the change.
    \nOn a similar vein, excepts colors to be off some in general, if you're used to what you see on the screen. The Finis cover was recolored after the first proof, because what looked like "plain dark blue" on the screen turned into "dark purple" because of the red added to cyan to make blue.

    Fred removed a great deal of the red in the cover's color, and while it was too cyan for my tastes on the screen, I took a chance on the second proof. It turned out close enough to the intended color for my tastes. Now, this printing effect could be partly due to the darkness of the cover printing that Jonathan's talking about -- something I didn't know until now. But that's my experience with Lulu & covers.
