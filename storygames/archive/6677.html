    Posted By: JuddAny thoughts on how to do this or sites I should be looking at...
    \nHere's another DIY approach: Patrick Crusiau has put out some gorgeous paper figure art. Look at the bottom of these pages:

    http://www.gwindel.eu/MSPE.html
    http://www.gwindel.eu/GT.html

    There's also a how-to PDF linked here:
    http://www.gwindel.eu/Ressources/howto.pdf

    I love his figures because they're simple colored line drawings -- the details on busy art tend to vanish when you print it at 1", so if you have two tokens with similar colors you might be squinting a lot. Also, they're side-on figures rather than front-on figures, and I think those look way more dynamic than front-and-back Cardboard Hero type figs. You can trim away some of the whitespace for extra dynamics. You can size and scale them any way you like, up to the limits of the image resolution, or even flood-fill recolor them if you want an army of guys with different tunics or what-have-you.

    I printed up some of these and laminated them before trimming. They're crazy durable -- the weapons spring right back to true if you bend them -- and they pack flat. For bases, I use pennies and stick-tack. You can put colored yard sale dots on the pennies if you want to add distinguishing marks.

    You can also find good figure images at Clipart.com if you dig a bit. I used this one when my players sacked a monastery.

    Here's some action shots from that monastery. I don't think the paper figs look out of place compared to their plastic counterparts.

The bases I use are made by some company called George Bases (or similar, Google is of no help finding them) that I buy from the FLGS. They're some sort of wood/particle-board and come in D&D-approved dimensions: 1", 2" 3", etc.

You can also find the various collected sets for cheap; I bought tons of the metal-tin collections at GenCon for like $5 a pop, so now I have hundreds.       \nAbsolutely, I agree with you. I also like being able to take other games and use them in an RPG. Like using a card game, or tokens from something else.

      Best trick I ever pulled during a role-playing game was when the characters were trying to find the parts of a key to assemble to shut down a magical device. When they found the first segment, I handed that player a plastic crystal. As they gathered all of the "pieces" in-game, the players realized it was one of those star puzzles where all the pieces fit together somehow.

      \n Ok, so I tried a few different approaches, and I've settled on one that seems pretty versatile. I have a sheet of magnetic plastic, which you can get at most craft stores. If I later manage to get a magnetic surface to play on, I just pull the washer off and am still good to go. The 1" magnets listed above would also work to this purpose, but this is a little cheaper.

      Additionally, the _easiest_ solution I've found is felt. You can get packs of 1" disks that are designed to be put under furniture. They're a bit too light, and not terribly cost effective, but since they already have adhesive backs, it's just a matter of peel and stick!

      \n I don't think anyone linked to this site which I'm finding helpful:

      http://dndcounters.tripod.com/

      If others get the 1" wood tokens from Woodworks Ltd., you should know that the 1" craft punches are a bit too large for those (due to the beveled edges). I've found, though trial and error, that a 0.95" x 0.95" square (copy pasted into Word documents) with a 0.90"x0.90" circle Autoshape pasted over the image (to show where to cut using scissors) works exactly right for me with these.
