\n When our group has had disappointing sessions when playing collaborative, GMless Story Games, the issue has always been tied to not building upon each other's ideas for the story and trying to push ones own story idea which results in the detriment to the overall story. Instead of producing a story where things weave together and one another's ideas are integrated; this instead, produces a tree affect where the branches go everywhere and are unconnected and it becomes impossible to tie the disparate ideas together to bring the ideas back together in a coherent and satisfying ending or whole. I also believe that game design plays a role; for example, I have played Fiasco many times and this is never been an issue. I have also played Follow many times and this has never been an issue.

However, when one hasn't played a game very often and one has this branching, it is hard to know whether to attribute it to the game design or to the way the group played the session. Obviously, most of the time it is probably a combination of the two. If I feel lukewarm about a game's subject and the design seems lacking when one of these less satisfying sessions happen, I'm hesitant to give the game another chance. If I'm extremely interested in the game and the design seems interesting I definitely want to get the game another chance, albeit after a bit of time has passed.

Two games that I am very interested in, which others have reported having great sessions of, ended with these results in the first session we played. This obviously could've been an issue that was completely based on how the group played, but it is hard to know what percentage of this was due to the group or the game design. The two games were "Okult" and "Society of Dreamers." I am especially interested in playing "Okult" again.

When I play these games again, I want to play them in a way that is least likely to produce this tree branching affect. Besides sharing the story and building up on each other's ideas, as well as not throwing in huge twist that can overly complicate things and are typically hard to manage on the fly and get good results from, what are the best ways to ensure a good session? I'm most interested in ideas about how to get the best session out of Okult. @Wilhelm I'm especially interested in hearing from you about what we can do and about your perspective in general. Also, I would appreciate hearing @Ben_Robbins perspective and others who have played a lot of Story Games. By Story Games, I mean them in the sense that Ben and I define them. The definition can be found here, if you're interested:
http://arsludi.lamemage.com/index.php/460/defining-story-games/
I think that this is a very common problem in the broader sense and would be an interesting and useful topic to most of us on Story Games to discuss on multiple levels. Any help or advice would be very appreciated :-) thanks :-)

    \n This is an important issue, indeed. However, my current belief is it's only obliquely a design issue.

    In a lot of ways, this is the core of the roleplaying activity, and its main difficulty as well. Therefore, a typical answer would be: people need to train together, as a group, to achieve optimal results - even then, they won't succeed 100% (but close). That's more-or-less implied in a lot of Ron Edwards texts, for example.
    If you look at Archipelago, by Matthijs Holter, that's a role-playing game where all mechanics are geared not toward achieving the optimum but, rather, toward training players in those basic techniques and best practices which help achieve it. That was even more explicit in Archipelago II than in the current, 3rd edition, due to the inclusion of a small essay about teamwork.
    Granted, in games with a GM, MC, whatever, it generally suffices for the text to focus on teaching one player, who will then teach the others.

    But I do believe that design has something more to contribute - specifically, the generally tighter the game, the harder for play to go astray or lead nowhere at all. That's why I callled it obliquely a design issue.

    \n Total agreement here. A common issue, and design can address it, but isn't the entirety of the issue.

    A creatively open/collaborative game needs procedures which generate material (the "branches" of the metaphorical tree) you can then pursue and build upon, but, afterwards, needs to tie things back together.

    Those procedures can be part of the game's design, or they can be brought in by player skills and techniques.

    I don't have any brilliant wisdom or insights to share, except to say that I see people and groups struggle with this *all the time* in collaborative story games of all types (and sometimes also in hardcore RPGs!).

    For what it's worth, Jeff, I felt it happened a lot in our Follow game, even though we managed (just barely) to pull things together at the end.

    Hi, happy to hear that you played Okult. Sad to hear that it didn't click with your group.

    Fundamentally I think this is a player side issue. Players have to be committed to the same experience, or there will be several different agendas at the table. The game can only do so much to help align them.

    Have I played too much? Whenever I play I can almost see the branching story tree before my inner eye. Past choices and possible outcomes of the current ones. And most importantly future intersections where they can join up again. If I play with beginners I can often manage this reuniting of plots by proposing small things to the players, eg a key NPC that appears in multiple functions.

    But Okult doesn't leave you entirely on your own when it comes to this.

    Already in character generation you are forced to invest in each other's stories. The prompting play flow mechanic where every one is encouraged to contribute in play helps you to highlight points where stories intersect.

    When playing OKULT online I have added a step to the setup. We have a short talk about the nature of the threat. It prunes a couple of branches from the tree, but also makes it easier to go in the same direction.

    \n Some Players tend to not be good listeners or to not care (enough) for the integrity of other's contributions, which in most games results in less than optimal and cohesive storytelling.
    It really just takes a dissonant idea to create the "branching out" effect that Jeff talks about.
    Or to send the story in a direction that, although compact, is not to everyone's satisfaction.

    As Rafu mentions, the only real long term cure is to have such players "train" the skill they are obviously lacking. They'll get there, eventually
    Problem is, everyone else who plays with them has to suffer the pains of such training, and depending on how well this is communicated and enforced at the table it might be a longer or shorter process.
    Assuming the problem player enjoys the game enough to be willing to put in the work.

    Most of my FIASCO games have been unfortunately like that ... with at least one person putting a wacko idea out there, thus spinning the whole session into a gonzo direction. Or fixating on their own personal head-movie, no matter what else was happening around them.
    (note: I usually play with strangers)

    Currently my go-to game for newbs and strangers is The Quiet Year as it is much more difficult to fuck up in this way. Still, it can happen from time to time. Also Montsegur 1244 has had a good success rate.
    Still, on occasion the problem surfaces again, and the branch generating idea makes its way on the table. The only ready solution I found is to have a short out-game conversation to negotiate a more acceptable contribution.
    But this takes expertise and ability from the facilitator, especially with people new to rpgs that you would like to impress with a fun and positively memorable session, but are also wary of bashing down ... course correction takes skill and care :P

    One thing I noticed about a lot of story-games is that most share a common philosophy. Which in theory is all nice and good, but in practice often leaves them incapable of addressing the kind of problem Jeff talks about.

    The feeling I get is that... usually the message is "let's all be nice to one another".
    This works ok for some people, but fails when falling on somewhat deaf ears. Mechanics like the code-words to have a say about each other's descriptions (which I've seen in Archipelago, Hell for Leather and a I believe also Love at the Time of Seyd) do help, but only up to a point.
    Somehow the system tries to have Players avoid confrontation, avoid expressing their preference, avoid spilling their beans, in favor of a softer approach where (ideally) everyone listens to and cares for everyone else.
    When people don't listen and care, the system fails BUT the game does not break, thus producing a substandard experience.

    I personally like more, and find more effective according to my personal experiences, a diametrically opposite approach. Especially when rules complexity increases or when mid-long term play is desirable over one-shots.
    It is the approach exemplified by the FateLess rules system.

    The idea is to convey, through the overall mechanics of the game, a message more on the lines of "be honest, hell be egoist ".
    We all play together, sure, but instead of tip-toeing through every action and description for fear of maybe somehow possibly saying something others don't like... instead of this the mechanics try to ease and facilitate people to "care less" and just go, speak up their mind, and then for their voice to be impossible to ignore as it will have an obvious impact on the game.
    Let the game bring people to confront and face one another.
    Then see what happens.
    The most common outcome I've seen happen is that everyone's ideas and tastes and intentions become clear to one another, and then minimal route corrections on the fly are all it takes to make everyone happy.
    Other times an actual social problem will come to light, like when a Player truly does not listens or cares for other's ideas. The mechanics tend to exacerbate that problem until, in very short order, either the Player understands that his behavior is problematic and self corrects, or the game brakes forcing people to take a step back/out from the game and talk openly about what just happened, as humans.
    In my experience this is pretty much what the aforementioned "skilled facilitator" ends up doing: find out the problem, bring it to light where it can be clearly seen, stop the game to address it.

    I personally favor having a stark interruption that might lead to an actual clarification and a good game continuation, rather than a softer approach were everyone is kind of uncomfortable but not enough to speak up and keeps on shuffling and fidgeting until the session ends with various degrees of internal unexpressed cringe.

    I've seen this approach work wonders on the actual gaming tables I have been part of.
    But as an explanation, does this make any sense to anyone except me? XD

    Your description of our game of Follow sounds spot-on to me! That's how I remember it, too.

    Hasimir,

    Your post is interesting, but fairly hard to understand for someone not familiar with FateLess. Can you share *how* and *why* you think the design of the game creates the dynamics you're describing? It's all very abstract just now.

    Dave,

    Great thoughts about "what is it that excites you about this?". I'd love to explore that some more; perhaps there are cool and smart ways to do this somehow.


    On the general topic of the thread:

    My favoured approach to this problem in design has been... well, two thoughts.

    The first is through mechanics which really force people to drive towards a common destination (my game Musette is pretty consistent in this, for example, because the mechanics force the players to keep adding different events and details which are all headed towards the same decision point; Dogs in the Vineyard does some of this by encoding so many of the themes of play into the conflict resolution mechanics).

    The second way is to build your fiction so that most of the inventions being bandied around demand input from multiple players. I don't know if it's in the rules (because I've never read them), but when we started a (sadly) aborted game of Okult with Wilhelm, he had us do this very nicely, each asking questions and adding information about the fiction and its denizens.

    I like to do this in a lot of designs, where the final product occurs as a synthesis of other peoples' ideas. (Dave, if you're reading this, you may now better understand the whole Questions & Elements thing I was going for in our Inconceivable playtest!)

    For instance, in Musette you and I start the game by each generating a story element (for example, a character) we want to see in the coming game. But then it's a third player who grabs two of our ideas and says, "Hey, these go well together - let's use them."

    I find this helps generate non-obvious outcomes and encourages us all to have an interest in them. If someone had a "darling idea", chances are it's been recontextualized or transformed, so they're not likely to keep pushing it in that original direction. If someone was trying to run off into the deep end, their input has been thrown out or redirected.

    It would be really cool to find clever ways of doing this with more freeform fiction/roleplay, as well. I can't think of any, but that doesn't mean they can't exist!

    \n The Trouble with Rose can go that way. To head off that problem, I like to write play sets where there are are multiple triangles that keep everyone connected. There's the central NPC (a Rose by other name) that each character has a relationship with, a central mystery/trouble, each character has a tie-in with another character (one that usually causes dramatic friction), and then everyone has their own secret agenda. The blend seems to create much more successful games than duds. When it does create branches, there are usually one two triangles circling each other and there still tends to be interaction between the two.
