\n Hey everyone! I've been reading discussions here for a while, first time poster, etc.

I was reading this interesting discussion, and I realized I felt like something was missing. It seemed better to start a new discussion than to derail, though, so here I am.

My main question's this: is there a reason why we don't just ask each other what we're comfortable with in the moment? I don't feel like I've seen any games or guidelines recommend that, but it seems easy enough to do. Has anyone had any experiences with this approach to safety issues?

For context on where I'm coming from, I've made some posts on the same topic, and I think they're relevant. Part 1 is a short introduction to how I see the state of safety/consent practices in the RPG scene, and Part 2 is how I actually design around safety and consent in my game (which might be more interesting).

    \n The main difference between "just talking it out" and "designing a solution" is that in the latter you introduce the topic of discussion in a game text and give it a snappy name in the hopes that the reader might remember it when playing the game and coming face to face with a situation where this "rule" applies. The actual substance of the advice/rule could be precisely the same, and actually is in the case of several game texts that explicitly instruct the GM to observe their players and react to signs of discomfort by broaching a constructive discussion (of whatever kind the game's author thinks fits the bill).

    In other words, it's not such a big difference. Various gaming traditions just differ on what should be called a "rule" and what should be called "advice on best practices".

    \n There are a couple ways to edit content at the table:

    1. Do it before play (lines and veils discussions).
    2. Do it during play, right before it happens (X-card or a verbal "stop").
    3. Do it during play, after it happens (discussion).

    All of these are great tools and a group should employ any and all safety tools they have at their disposal to make their gamer friends feel comfortable and included.

    The risk of doing it all before is that it requires discussion. People don't always want to discuss their issues in detail. Saying "no child molestation scenes" says a lot about a person's history and they might not want to share that. It takes a certain amount of safety at the table to be able to express things.

    Doing it (only) after takes a huge safety net and a lot of trust, and you're basically setting yourself up for pain at the table. Most people don't want that.

    Doing the editing during play has the benefit of being able to deal with actual issues, not theoretical issues. You're not saying, "I don't want clowns in our game," when it's really unlikely that clowns will feature in your dungeon crawl. You're saying, at the moment it comes up, "I don't want killing orc babies in our game," because someone just talked about killing the orc babies.

    The X-card adds a safety layer to that. It makes the act of editing out content as quick and unobtrusive as possible, so people can do it and move on. No discussion required.

    Not talking about it at all is the default for a lot of groups. The default works for people with default problems, like you and me. It fails to work for people who are more at risk. A lot of people do not want to work it out at the table, or feel unsafe to work it out at the table.

    A person who was raped probably doesn't want to stop play and have a discussion with people about why X in the scene makes them feel sad or unsafe, especially because the other people don't already get why that content is unsafe for them.

    A person who is gay or transgender but not "out" doesn't want to explain why they don't want a bunch of cis people tromping all over that subject.

    Heck, you might not want to talk about why giant spiders freak you out, or why stories about dead grandmothers makes you incredibly sad, or why the inclusion of cigarettes makes you irrationally angry. It's a game, and you shouldn't have to explain that stuff at the table if you don't feel like it.

    DeReel, I'm gonna respond directly to your last comment, "It feels like there won't be much time to play..."

    I say this with love, but honestly, if you can't find a set of content for your games that is safe for everyone at the table, then maybe that group shouldn't be playing. And if you feel that your game-fun is more important than someone's earnest request to keep something out of a game, you should re-examine your priorities.

    In practice, having used the X-card and lines + veils discussions in games for years, there's always plenty of fruitful content for whatever game we're playing. No one abuses the tools. There are never any issues, except for people like you and me who don't apparently have many hang-ups at the table and want to nitpick the theoreticals to death.

    \n I've been thinking about this a lot since playing with a variety of strangers at a convention over the weekend.

    I know this is a slight derail of the discussion happening, but I feel it's related:

    I really think one of the best things we can do for safety at the table is to encourage and use positive reinforcement, instead of relying only on "Cut!" and the X-card.

    I played with people who always made a point of indicating when they enjoyed something and wanted more of it, and it made gaming with them so easy and fun. For instance, in one game, a stranger was playing a character of a persecuted race. I made a character who was responsible for the murder of her sister (and general atrocities committed against that race of people).

    This worried me; I was trying to fit into a fictional universe I was unfamiliar with and to contribute some exciting drama to the game, but I didn't want to antagonize or upset a stranger. She looked angry and upset, playing her character's hatred of mine, which was scary to me, since I wasn't 100% sure whether it was entirely in-character or not, and was tempted to pause the game and check in with her.

    However, we had a little piece of paper on the table called a "Support Flower". As she played out her character's hatred for mine, she kept putting her finger on "Yes! Keep going!".

    It was a lovely way to communicate that she was still enjoying herself, and felt like a warm hug.

    In that environment (we all joined in, fairly quickly!), it would be quickly obvious when there was a chance someone wasn't enjoying themselves (a lack of positive reinforcement), and that would allow us to easily "spot" moments when it might make sense to "ask" or "check in" with each other.

    \n I Enjoy your contributions so far, but let me just throw this thought into it.

    As in BDSM, isn't the main issue that we need a form of communication tool that can't be misinterpreted? I mean, normally when people are not playing roles you can interpret their behaviour and maybe see that something's wrong, or when they say "I don't like this" you might see that they're serious about it.

    The problem with playing is that the whole point is to dilute what's real and not. But as long as there is a way to make sure the safe-word communication is under no circumstance misunderstood, then you should be alright. Right?

    Also the deal with safe environments is that in larps people can leave the fiction and go somewhere else (the off-tent or whatever it's called) when they need to take a break - in tabletop you might not be able to walk away (if you're in someone else's apartment for example). That's why the table has to be safe to begin with and the Safe-word instead removes the fiction from the table. But, if the table is not a safe environment as you do not trust the people you play with, the Safe-word won't work. Because even when the fiction is gone you're still not safe.

    So I see discussion as a necessary complement to Safe-words in tabletop play. And that discussion can take place either before, during, or after the activity.
