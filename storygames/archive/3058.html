\n Thor wrote:

"Personally I think BW's complexity is more myth than reality, which I lay at the feet of form factor. I suspect that many people would perceive its mechanical density differently if it were a 128-page 8.5 x 11 RPG book. But that's really for another thread. "

Here's that thread. I want to talk about physical formats and how they shape experience - as a purchaser and as a player.

First of all, Thor, will BW fit in 128 letter-sized pages? How would the experience of using the book/s as a reference text be different? If you know, why did Luke choose the format he did?

For the rest of you, do the same formats that are attractive and eye-catching work well at the table, as reference books? Many small press games have settled into a 6x9" or digest-sized format - are there significant disadvantages to this for you as a consumer or player? What about density of text and tabular data? What about perceptions of value?

Let's talk.

    \n I prefer digest sized books.

    Why so? Partly simply because they take up less room, partly because they are easier to transport to the gaming venue, partly because they just look sort of neat.

    I don't find them harder to reference, if the internal layout is uncluttered and there is a decent index. I don't think size, within these parameters, matters.

    What does impact is other details, margin width for example. I did not buy Seventh Sea precisely because the margins were so large they left the actual text squished in the middle, that made it look cluttered to me and hard to read. Thus that format reduced the books usability for me. Similarly, when Chaosium released the new Keeper's Compendium they included huge margins with art in them, again it made it harder for me to use the book.

    So, size does not impact my usage greatly, digest size if anything improves usage for me and creates for me an illusion of accessibility which the game may or may not deliver. Large margins reduce usability for me.

    Similarly, multiple fonts reduce usability, as they make the page busy. Art buried within text, so that the text moves way for that art again breaks flow.

    Tables need to be clearly laid out with good use of space and a decent size, I have lousy eyesight and we frequently game in places with lowered light levels for mood. Small print in tables means I can't read it, which is not ok.

    But generally, size is not the issue. Clean, uncluttered layout with a good balance of text to space is key. PTA for example is a model for me of good layout in many ways (or I'm forgetting the bad ways I suppose). BW is clean, easy to read and not overfussy.

    Is that the sort of thing you're thinking of?

    \nI cited 128-pages off the top of my head and had to go back and check what Luke had told me. It would actually come out to roughly 150 pages in a "traditional" letter-sized book. For anyone reading this thread who hasn't seen Burning Wheel, it comes in two 303-page digest-sized books. One is the system book and the other is the Character Burner.

    It is my understanding (I wasn't around for this choice) that Luke chose the form he did for several reasons:

    1. It harkens back to the early form of role-playing games.

    2. He found the traditional letter-sized books unwieldy and the digest size much more aesthetically pleasing.

    3. The digest-sized books fit nicely in the hand and are easy to carry around.

    Further, it was split into two books and organized the way it was for ease of reference in play.

    Good stuff so far, many thanks. Retailers complain about non-standard sizes (sizes other than A4/letter). They claim these are hard to merchandize. I counter-claim that they are lazy, citing the retail universe.

    Usability is key, as Max notes, but I think you can structure a usable text in many sizes. For Burning Wheel, I can really imagine a quantitative improvement at a larger size, because you'd have more related information connected visually - in my experience there's a ton of flipping from page to page in play. At the same time the smaller books have all the aesthetic and functional advantages of digest.

    One thing no one has mentioned is that a digest format allows a small game to have some physical heft - if you only have 64 pages of letter-sized content, converting that to digest means you have an object of pleasing dimensions and tactile appeal, instead of a magazine.

    I have some comments on form-factor, but as a brief aside on the original point (that people think Burning Wheel is more complex than it is because of the form factor): actually, I think of Burning Wheel as complex mainly because of the way lifepaths work. Most people would cite scripted combat as the complex part of Burning Wheel, but you have the option to use more simplified resolution, which turns the basic mechanics into a very simple game. However, the lifepath system is not optional, if you want skills and traits, and there is not much of a pattern that I can see that would enable you to guess, based only on the name, what skills or traits a particular lifepath will grant, or what a trait will actually do. This means You have to look up multiple lifepaths and multiple traits to properly burn a character, and may have to look them up again when facing specific opponents. That's OK, if everyone buys into that level of complexity, but let's not pretend that it doesn't make Burning Wheel a complex game. Maybe not D&D 3e's level of complexity, more like AD&D 1e (or OD&D without the scripted combat.)

    \n

    Does format affect other opinions? Certainly. But mainly in terms of usability and perceived value. Small form-factor books are easier to tote around, fit into bags, and feel heftier (and thus more valuable) than they would if they were a larger form factor. However, sometimes you need the larger form factor. I would love to put the dungeon crawl game I'm developing into a digest size, because that feels right for this game; but I'm using a technique where players roll dice on a special sheet and determine some results based on where the dice land. This makes the size of the special sheet important: it should be 8 1/2 by 11, since that's what I'm using to test the technique, This means that if I go with digest size, customers can't just photocopy the sheet; they either have to use a copier that allows enlargement or download a PDF and print the sheet to the proper size. I'm undecided which way I want to go with that. In some other games based on the same technique, I may even want to avoid photocopying and let the players roll right on the page in the book, which may make a large, spiral-bound form factor the better choice.

    \n

    In general, I want to follow the advice Kibo gave me: a game book should not only be fun to look at, but fun to read and use. Fun to look at sells books, but a lot of games aren't fun to read and use, which is why they aren't.

    \nTaking another tack on this... I think that Burning Empires is, no matter how you cut it up, pretty complex. I'd be surprised to learn that people find BW daunting either in form or rules. But BE is daunting on both.
    \n
    \nOh, perhaps not complex in that you have lots of arcana to dig through every time you want to do something. It's complex in that the system requires you to refer to it for parts of the play process that most RPGs do not require.
    \n
    \nWhat's the next scene? Usually that's just GM fiat. In BE, you have to carefully reference where you are, and what the scene type is going to be, and what the current dispositions are to be sure that you're going for the right sort of scene, etc, etc. Even deciding what the next scene is going to be can be mechanically intensive in terms of the choice made.
    \n
    \nAnd that's just one part of play. BEhas rules to be checked whenever you want to gain an item of equipment. Special rules for contacts and what is produced from them. I could go on and on and on.
    \n
    \nThe perception problem is not, IMO, only from the form of the book. BW is a complicated game to play. Period. Perhaps one of the most complicated ever.
    \n
    \nNot in the sense that it's difficult. Just that it's involved. They say of Go that it's easy to learn, difficult to master. BW is not precisely like that, not quite that elegant. But it can be learned, and learned complete. What you can't do is get away with playing a session in which you're not doing a ton of things mechanically.
    \n
    \nNow... as it happens, I'm a junky for exactly this sort of complexity in play. I don't like "light" games where there aren't lots of opportunities to fiddle with the rules in play - or, even better, where the system requires you to consult it a lot (HQ can be fiddly, but the one thing I don't like about it is that it doesn't force me to use the rules often enough). So, for me, this sort of "complexity" is great.
    \n
    \nSome people are going to see it as a whole new learning curve, however. Which it is. One I'd highly suggest people overcome as very worthwhile. But it's not a game that I would say is simple to use.
    \n
    \n
    \nFrankly, I never refer to a book in play if I can memorize everything I need from it. If I can't do so, I probably won't play. I'm pretty sure that I could memorize everything that I needed from BE after running it for a while. At which point the form of the book goes from being almost unimportant to me, to being completely unimportant. As I've said before, give me a PDF to read (oh, here I have one of BE... better yet, give me HTML) and I'm happy. I'm sure some are daunted by the shape of the brick. But they're right to be daunted so. They just need to overcome that.

    \n I don't want to turn this thread into all-BW all the time, I swear. But Mike, I think you are getting to my point rather well (although I think your comments about Burning Empires reflect the experience of someone that has read the game but not played it, specifically in reference to the scene types).

    I've run a fair bit of HeroQuest and like the game quite a bit. I play and run a lot of Burning Wheel. HeroQuest has a lot more rules text (my book clocks in at 288 pages, 8.5 x 11). I also reference the rules text in HeroQuest quite a bit more often in play than I do the rules text in Burning Wheel when playing that game. The table with bids and multipliers and all that stuff is a must when running extended conflicts, for instance.

    In my experience, in terms of complexity, they're roughly on par, with the caveat that HeroQuest expects rules mastery by the GM whereas BW expects mastery, or at least a certain level of competency, on the part of the other players too.

    But to tie this back to the topic of this thread, I think there is a difference in perception of these games that is, at least in part, based on form factor. HeroQuest, I think, is seen as somewhat esoteric, but not necessarily rules dense, whereas Burning Wheel, which would be approximately 100 pages shorter than HeroQuest if it came in a form similar to HeroQuest's, is seen as a pretty crunchy system.

    Now, I don't think that's necessarily a bad thing. I, too, much prefer pervy games like Burning Wheel, Sorcerer and Dogs in the Vineyard to more vanilla games. I like to call them technical games, because greater mastery of their rules and the implications of those rules tends to create deeper, more dynamic play.

    Furthermore, I think the way the game is perceived is beneficial to reaching our core market, which is other gamers that enjoy technical games. It is perhaps unsurprising that a sizable minority of our dedicated fans are older gamers who fondly remember brown book D&D or Judge's Guild or T&T or Classic Traveller and the like. These are gamers that feel nostalgia for the form factor, enjoy rolling their own settings, and also enjoy a bit of complexity in their rules.

    I also think the perception of Burning Empires is beneficial. It would be a much less intimidating book in a traditional form factor, but I think that intimidation is probably good. I agree that Burning Empires is a complicated game (though perhaps not to the extent that you claim Mike), but I will certainly agree that Burning Empires is a demanding game. I know a number of people on this forum have tried to play it with their groups and failed. For many, the techniques which have been made into rules in Burning Empires are new or even alien. Burning Empires demands that you change the way that you play to conform with its structures. To play it as written requires that you allow your brain to accept a different pattern for role-playing games. We knew from the beginning that some people would absolutely hate it, and we hoped that some people would love it.

    I think everything about Burning Empires' form factor is a combination of dare and threat. If a gamer finds the size of "the brick" too intimidating, the game probably would have been overwhelming to them anyway.
