\n I went to a panel on The Future of Table Top RPGs at PAX. Chris Pramas and another dude from Wizards (who shall remain nameless) gave the panel. Chris was actually rather open-minded and even-keeled. Mr WOTC, predictably, talked like there was no other god but D&D. He also talked down to the audience and spoke in gamer lilt.

During the panel, a show of hands was taken. By my count there were 100 people in the room. 85 men, 15 women. 80% the 100 identified with D&D in some way. 5% identified with small press games in some way (that included me and Jared!). The other 15% seemed to identify with WW, SJG, GR and Mongoose games.

At one point Mr WOTC gave us a long knowing speech about how hard it was to be the GM and keep the players on the rails (I kid you not). After this reverential complaining went on for at least 20 minutes, I shouted up, "If the role of the GM is so problematic, why not get rid of it?" The room went quiet. There was one plaintive cry of, "Because it would be a board game then." Mr WOTC then came back to life and rumbled on, "Well, I like GMing..." I like GMing, too, but that didn't answer my question.

The actual Q&A format for the panel was ass. You had to get in line in front of a mic, wait for your turn, speak your piece and move aside. Bleh. Once the line formed, all you could see was gamer-butt.

Anyway, one wily little gamer jumped up to the mic and stunned me and Jared (and our other friend). He was clearly annoyed, "You talk like D&D is the only game. You talk like there's no other style of roleplaying except for putting players on rails. Have you heard of these games?" He held up a copy of Bliss Stage (!), "Have you heard of what Ben Lehman and Vincent Baker are doing with games?"

Jared and I cheered for him.
-L

    \n"At the "Future of Tabletop Games" seminar, a young man asked Chris Pramas and me a question, the answer to which wasn't very satisfying to the young man or, frankly, to me. The question was, in essence, "What's the secret to being a great Dungeon Master?"
    \n
    \nChris Pramas and I both took a crack at the question. My answer went something like this: A great Dungeon Master is a master of improv, which is to say that when the adventure runs off the rails, the great DM can "go with the flow" and improvise to keep the game interesting and fun for the players. However, a great DM also knows how to get the game group back on the rails. Sometimes this means presenting an illusion of choice, but knowing full well how the players are likely to respond, whereby the DM prods the players so gently in the right direction that the players think they're still masters of their own fate.
    \n
    \nI was horrified when the young man who asked the question resented the idea that the DM would railroad the party in such a manner. On the drive home after the seminar, I thought about my response, as well as my own DM-ing experiences. Was I wrong? Did the young man misunderstand me? Then it occurred to me that my answer my incomplete, that I'd failed to mention something crucial: The true secret to being a great DM is to surround yourself great players. Even the best improvisational DM in the world won't be able to sustain a campaign if he or she is running a game for a bunch of doorknobs.
    \n
    \nWhen a Dungeon Master decides to run a D&D game, he or she enters a social contract with the players at the table. Under the terms of this social contract, the DM agrees to run the best adventure he or she can and give the players the freedom to make real choices. Meanwhile, the players must acknowledge that the DM has invested a significant amount of time preparing the adventure, and while some distractions from the main plot are acceptable, there may be occasions when the DM needs the players' help to get the adventure back on track. Players who ignore this social contract, who ignore the DM's subtle hints or deliberately resist the DM's attempts to run the adventure at hand, can make the DM's experience quite miserable. And when the DM is miserable, nobody is having fun. On the other hand, great players can inspire the DM, as well as provide fuel for future adventures in the campaign."

      \n The railroading part of this discussion has already been had; more than once, I might add. Or whether Chris is right/justified in saying that the "alternative" methods that we use are only suitable for a certain segment of people.
      \n

On the other hand, I couldn't disagree that people have their own proclivities and preferences, and that not everyone will end up playing "storygames." The only question I'd have is whether Chris' rhetoric, or rhetoric from others like it, are preventing anyone from trying out other sorts of games.
      \n
      \nOne side of me wants to say that it's a tempest in a tea-kettle. Another says that it's this sort of thinking that allows people in the USA to sub-consciously keep minorities from having the same opportunities as white Americans. It's the "PC" issue. I'm quite sure that Chris has no malice in his heart when he says this. And given the size of the audience, I'm pretty sure that this single act did little direct "damage." But such comments might be part of an overall culture that works to prevent people from trying new things. The subtext, intended or not, is like, "These games are hard, and flakey experimentalism, in which you're going to have to put a lot of work in to only occasionally get some fun... and that's only if you're into such hard work." Which couldn't be further from the truth.
      \n
      \nThere is part of me, too, that says, "Who cares?" It's not like we're talking about some actual minority being damaged here. We're talking about people not being exposed to certain games, or being prevented from doing so. At worst this causes some small economic damage to those designers. Which, in a capitalist society is all fair, no?
      \n
      \nAgain, not saying that Chris is intentionally vying against "indie" games or something. Just that this may be the end result.
