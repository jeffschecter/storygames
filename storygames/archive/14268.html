\n OK, so we had some threads about matters relating to this lately. We talked about trying to define an RPG, and most people agreed that was a bad idea, and you should stick to explaining how *your* game worked. And we talked about "going beyond minimum instruction" with...wideranging but sometimes interesting results.

My question is how to write an RPG text for an readership that may simultaneously include successful RPG designers and someone who has never even seen an RPG before in their lives.

Most of the instructional texts out there in the world pitch themselves at complete beginners and leave the experts to take care of themselves. But is that really the way to go for a small press RPG that will probably be read 99% of the time by people who have played similar games for years? I am sceptical. Maybe because in my professional life I write technical stuff, mostly for fairly informed audiences. I *prefer* to write for the average person, but I'm very aware that the informed audience doesn't want to read that stuff, on the whole.

Your thoughts would be appreciated. Thanks!

    \n Good question!

    The system that people use in play emerges from a dialogue between the game's text and the players' expectations of that text, their existing social structures, and their preferences for play. When your write a game text, you're in part trying to anticipate that conversation. That means identifying what people will expect from your text - what people think a roleplaying game should be.

    My sense is that people's expectations about game texts have a parabolic arc as they gain experience. People start out with few expectations. They don't know anything about roleplaying, so they won't read much into the text that's not there. As people gain experience, they often form a sense of what roleplaying "is", and at this point are likely to come to a text with a lot of expectations. People who have played a lot of different games though, tend to be more aware of the range of possible ways of playing a game, and have fewer expectations.

    In other words, if you can successfully anticipate what the expectations of the reader will be, what "normal" play looks like to them, and those expectations match the game you are designing, then yes, you can pitch your text to an "expert" reader. I suspect that's an unusual situation though.

    \n Organize your text so that each chapter:

    - is composed of subsections that are clearly labeled
    - the first sentence says what it is about and who it is for
    - include a bullet point summary at the beginning
    - restrict each subsection to 2 page spreads

    This way experienced users can easily skip past anything they don't want to read.

    Additionally, have a section that says, "if you're experienced with RPGs, feel free to skip X, Y, and Z but note the following ideas that may run counter to your experience or expectations.

    \n "why" is pretty powerful. I try to say at the top of each section why the section is there. Sometimes the why is implicitly obvious, in which case what and/or how is OK.

    There must be some links to good sites on 'pitching' stuff out there from people with sales skills. Maybe we should link to them here.

    I remember someone saying something along the lines of "the first reaction to a proposal is to look for reasons the proposal is wrong". Because when you propose something as 'better, more efficient, more fun etc..." you are effectively saying "righter" and the implication is that anyone not using your proposed idea is "wronger". So you make 5 bullet points about why your game is funner, better etc..., and their very first reaction will be to look for THE MISTAKE - the thing they don't agree with because they don't have a problem with that thing. So if you have a problem with that thing, then you are wrong and therefore your whole proposal is WRONG. And that conclusion probably takes about 5 seconds to reach.

    \n Why write stuff if you're expecting people to skip it?

    If you're writing a short game for an existing roleplaying audience (like, say, Lady Blackbird) and you're asking them to mostly do stuff they already know how to do, then you don't need much of that instructional stuff. Just write what they need to know and get going.

    If you're writing a game for non-roleplayers or a game that asks players to do some things that they might not know how to do (like, say, Apocalypse World or Polaris), then that's a critical part of writing the game. Make it both informative and fun to read! How else will players know what to do! If you can't bring yourself to do this, then maybe you should think more about writing a game more like Lady Blackbird, for a different audience, asking them to do what they know.

    I'm increasingly of the mind that there aren't many games in between these. I mean, I guess there could be games aimed at new audiences where you're just telling them how to do stuff that most roleplayers already know how to do, but then, yeah, that seems really mind-numbingly dull to me. I'd rather write something more exciting, like either of the two options above.

    Posted By: J. WaltonWhy write stuff if you're expecting people to skip it?
    \nMy enjoyment of roleplaying games was delayed for years simply because the AD&D books didn't fully explain how to read 4-siders. They went into bell curves and such, but assumed that everyone knew how to use those crazy dice. Yes, it would have been a section that all experienced roleplayers would have skipped, but I needed it because I didn't want to ask an experienced roleplayer for that basic piece of information and look like an idiot.So, those 1st Ed books that I got when I was 19 years old went up on the book shelf and are in almost pristine condition.

    My point is that if you are writing a roleplaying game that may include those new to roleplaying, you need to have the basics. The nice thing about an rpg is that the instructions are modular in nature. Its possible to put sections in there that the experienced can jump over, but an idiot that is just starting out can find out that you look at the number on the top point or bottom side of a 4-sider to get the number from it. The basic information doesn't need to be very long or involved, and ideally, it should be something that rules later in the book build on.

    Also, you need to keep the nature of your game in mind. If it is an oddball that doesnt' follow a lot of convetentional rules, those basics are going to become very important to everyone, beginners and experienced players alike. A good example would be Dirty Secrets by Seth Ben-Ezra. Its "one player and many GMs" approach turns the whole roleplaying convention inside-out. Every section of the rules is important in order to learn the game. My own Silver Screen Dreams also shakes up a number of conventions. With this next iteration of the game, I am being extremely careful so that each rule builds on the ones before it. I don't want to ge the only one that can play the game and have fun with it.

    So, take your chosen audience into account. If you want to just write some little thing for experienced players, leave out as much info as you like. You may even be lauded for it. However, if you want to write a professional-grade product that you can sell outside of an online lemonade stand, take the beginners into acount and write for them, too.

    \n So... I don't want to spoil the happy consensus that seems to be developing here: that you have to write boring, painful stuff in the hopes that a few people might read it. But that's total crap! I really, deeply want to question a lot of the common wisdom that's being shared.

    I have nothing but loathing for the idea that you have to compromise your intent or the stark beauty of your game in order to appeal to a broader audience. Good audiences do not respond well to watered-down crapola and patronizing hand-holding. They are smart and cunning. They can see right through crappy attempts at pandering to them. They are capable of doing amazing things as long as you tell them how or point them in the right direction.

    If the instructions required to tell people how to play your game are boring then either 1) your game is boring, or 2) you are a boring writer. If one or both of these is the case, or even if you think both are pretty solid, you should do you best to make improvements in both those areas (I know I am!).

    The basic behaviors and mechanics of roleplaying are inherantly exciting. You're creating awesome things, with your own hands and minds! You're creating exciting stories about crazy places and people! If you can't describe those behaviors and mechanics to people in a way that is exciting to read and makes people excited to play then -- quite frankly -- you're doing it wrong.

    \nSteve: Color and style in your writing isn't what I mean by pandering to the readers, necessarily. Some of that is necessary for just having your writing be interesting and fun. In my mind, pandering is when you use a whole bunch of words to explain something to death, trying to hook your audience's attention when you don't have anything interesting to tell them.

    The way I like to think about it is this:

    When you're writing a game, you are empowering the reader to create awesome stuff, giving them the tools and advice they need to do that. When you look at every sentence in your game, or at least every paragraph, think about how that sentence or paragraph empowers the reader, and try to focus the text on that task. Both you and other readers in your target audience (since you're a reader too, and presumably in your target audience!) should come away from every sentence or paragraph feeling energized and excited to do something creative. If not, then consider rethinking or rewording things.

    Another thing is this: not every person is in your target audience. Maybe John and I aren't in your target audience; maybe it doesn't matter what we think. But your text definitely needs to connect with somebody. It doesn't really matter how good your game is if you're trying to write a game for people other than yourself. If you're writing for other people, the point is communicating what's awesome and empowering about your game to them, as clearly and efficiently and enjoyably as you can. So maybe the place to start is with basic questions like the classic three: 1) What's your game about? 2) What do the players do? 3) How does your game support both of the above?

    \nDave: I think picking your target audience (which is basically what assuming a level of knowledge is) and writing game text that is exciting are two different things that are only kinda related. You can write exciting game text, period, whatever your intended audience is.     \n Making content exciting is nice, but where should that rank on the priority list? I think it depends on the content.

    My dream RPG product distinguishes between inspiration, instruction, and reference. Inspiration must be exciting above all else. Instruction must be clear above all else, and exciting is nice. Reference needn't be exciting at all as long as it's usable.

    \n RATE MY PITCH!!! OK, Im re-writing my pitch. The section after that will back up each and every claim, and have a diagram!

    So... on a scale of 1 to 10, 1 being "you owe me 60 seconds of my life for reading this tripe" and 10 being "ZOMG when will this awesome game be available becaue I must have it now!!?!"...

    INTRODUCTION
    [Ingenero is for action-oriented role-playing in your favorite genre and setting. It empowers the players to drive the pace and focus of play towards the things that matter to them. It ensures the story never grinds to a halt or bogs down in irrelevancies. Your Ingenero sessions will rock!]

    Ingenero is the vehicle, and the players are the driver.
    The GM is the open, unwinding road.
    Character decisions are the steering.
    Character goals are the accelerator and brakes.
    Reward points are the fuel.

    Ingenero is a little system with a big engine. Its primary purpose is to align the players and GM in the same direction, and propel the characters towards dramatic action.

    The GM prepares, but doesn\u2019t plot \u2013 Ingenero calls for improvised GMing. But the system helps out by ensuring the players drive the story forward with their character motivations and goals they set. GMs experienced at improvised play will find Ingenero a snap to run, while those less familiar will appreciate how the system guides them.

    The game is played in two alternating phases, with the first focussed entirely on story development, character decisions and their consequences. When the characters advance closely on their goals, play switches to challenge phase and zooms in on those decisive moments of achievement or failure.


    CINEMATIC STYLE, DRAMATIC ACTION:
    A principle of the system is the focus on the rules of drama rather than the rules of physics or probability. The game takes its cue for \u2018rules of drama\u2019 from books and cinema. Rather than ask \u2018How would that really happen?\u2019, the question is \u2018How would that happen in a book or film?\u201d Drama and practical, satisfying gaming trumps realism.

    Characters are defined by descriptive outcomes they can generate, called plays, rather than skills and equipment. Conflict resolution uses plays to provide well-paced, tactical play full of immersive imagery.

      \n After much whispering with Steve Hickey, who has some great ideas on this subject, I have got the following: (still needs to be reworded for more active voice, but the detail is there)


      Ingenero is for action-themed play in any setting -- players describe their characters in terms of actions they can take, rather than skills or equipment they possess.

      The most important feature of the system is goal-oriented play for pacing and focus. This is achieved using two alternating phases -- the first focused entirely on story development, character decisions and consequences, and the second zooms in on those decisive moments of achievement or failure when the characters advance closely on their goals.

      An important principle is adhering to the rules of drama rather than the rules of physics or probability. Characters always get a chance to at least compete for their goals -- they are never completely knocked out of a conflict prematurely.

      A key aim of the system is cinematic conflict resolution. Conflicts are resolved using descriptive, competing actions which can be used to target other characters, maneuver for advantage, and even aid or interfere with another characters actions.

      Posted By: Dave Halletta small press RPG that will probably be read 99% of the time by people who have played similar games for years
      \nYour initial post suggests to me that you've already decided who your audience is. Write to that audience. Preface the book with a short paragraph called "WTF is going on here?" (or whatever concise, idiomatic heading matches the flavour of your game). While you write the book, note every term, every procedure, every concept that you're not explaining because of your audience decision. When you're done, write the Noob's Appendix and go back to write the "WTF" paragraph to point to the Noob's Appendix. This should also help you to avoid overloading terms, to be clear about terminology, and leave you with a glossary / reference chapter. I would wager it exposes some mechanical corner-cases, too.

      While I wholeheartedly agree that it's possible to make a text interesting or exciting, there is no getting around the fact that if your game text does not describe how to play the game, you have failed.

      As someone upthread suggested, get reader feedback, at minimum from three people: a writer of instructional texts, a player of games, a non-player of games who you think would like the game.
