    \n You should seriously check out Lady Blackbird and Archipelago. They're short, they're awesome, they're free and they encapsulate a lot of important mechanics and techniques in one concise package.

    From your list, I'd say you're maybe missing Polaris and maybe Smallville...but there are just so many cool games out there.

    Prioritizing your list I'd say if you read Strands then you can leave Dresden, Spirit otC, Legends of A and ICONS for later because they're all basically FATE with minor cosmetic changes, unless you want to really dig in deep with various variants of the system. Houses of the Blooded is FATE at heart, too, but brings some funky stuff to the table like Wagers.

    If you've got the lumpley suite totally read Apocalypse World. Especially if you're looking for techniques in practice.

    Fiasco, Microscope and Penny are all exercises in group storyjamming. PTA is a classic. Reign is important because of its Company rules. Esoterrorists (and other GUMSHOE games) are important because of how they deal with investigations.

    \n This may not be the advice you're after, but I'd urge you to read less and play more. Pick one of the games you've got. Make sure it's one you're excited about. Just play it. A one-off game is a low-risk investment for other players and many of the games will have sample scenarios available for you to try out. Get a feel for how it works. Then, if you want to, try a few more sessions of the same game or try a different game.

    The thing is, these are games, and are designed to be played. If you're just reading about the cogs and various moving parts, you won't get to see them in action and so won't really understand what the games are going on about.

    Nothing on your list is bad. Pick one, play it, try it out.

    If it was me, I'd probably first try out Dogs in the Vineyard, Spirit of the Century, or Primetime Adventures as games that reliably give a good game. PTA is the furthest from a "trad" game. But your preferences will vary, so just go for one of them.

    \n Hm...okay then!

    With Great Power for structured genre-based pacing, i.e. "this is how this sort of story is supposed to be paced"

    Shock: for GMless play built around collaborative situation-/world-building

    Burning Empires and, to a lesser degree, Mouse Guard for codifying a central tension most RPGs have: getting everyone on the same page about The Big Thing versus individual players actualizing their characters' aspirations. Actually leveraging that to good effect versus sweeping it under the rug, or simply prohibiting it.

    I thought 3:16 did a nice job of evoking its source fiction without directly mandating it. Flashbacks and competing for rank both were nice ideas toward that.

    Dust Devils is still my favorite game for laying out a very simple "get paid for your character's central conflict" type thing. Terrific teaching game to get trad players into that head space. The Devil is just great.

    Burning Wheel is enormously more nuanced than Dust Devils and you have to have quite a lot of rules mastery to get there (via engineering moldbreaker opportunities within your BITs), but that's probably the best-in-class I've found for playing deeply conflicted, literary-seeming characters.

    Posted By: jjafullerI have been thinking about making a "Where to Start" flow chart for people coming from traditional games. Something that maps out traditional games, and interests that leads to various indie games
    \nI think the most important thing is to show people a game that does a genre of fiction they really love, really well. Because trad gamers don't generally care about mechanics, or don't care about them in well-informed ways*, don't try to advertise the indie-ness, just focus on how "It's just like Starship Troopers!" (3:16) or "it's a lot like Redwall" (Mouse Guard). From my experience that's much more helpful than trying to give people a sort of curriculum or order in which to play indie games. (Though obviously for people interested in the history of indie game design it's maybe cool to have some knowledge of the trends and the direction of the influence arrows.)

    For my money, I think all you really need are Mouse Guard for a GM'd game with heavier mechanics, PTA (or maybe My Life With Master) for a GM'd game with lighter mechanics, Polaris (or maybe 1,001 Nights**) for a rotating-GM or GM-ful game, and one of Jason Morningstar's games for a GM-less game.

    Matt

    *Yeah yeah I'm going to get a ton of flames for that sentence, but it's still true.
    **Haven't seen that game mentioned elsewhere in the thread, I don't think\u2014it's brilliant. I really hope Meg writes another game someday because 1,001 Nights is super distilled mega-awesomeness.
