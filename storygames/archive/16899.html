\n Hello everyone. I'm new to the forums, but I thought I'd dive in head-first.

I've been involved in the hobby for more than a decade now, and most of that time was spent GMing. Most of that time was spent GMing homebrew games. There are very few things my group has played without tweaking it half to death in the process.

Recently, I've been really wanting to do something as a real project from the ground up - ending in a real book, with art, layout, and the whole shebang. I'm a graphic designer by trade. That will be easy. What will be hard is making the game itself.

I'm choosing my topic as a labor of love - I want to make a late bronze, early iron age game in the sword-and-sandals(-and sorcery!) genre. I've always loved the era and genre and I'm a big history nerd, so parts of this won't be too hard. On the other hand, I recognize what a big project this will be and so I thought I'd turn it loose to the public and get some feedback along the way. This is where you come in.





Here are my guidelines so far:
* It's going to be loosely based on the system used by SOTC and Chronica Feudalis. My current group discovered CF and loved it. I have been keeping a campaign/changes bog here: http://polyhedralpleasure.blogspot.com

* I want to keep the core flexibility of Chronica Feudalis, but I don't mind slight additional crunch. Even our medieval group (in the link) expanded some on the core rules.

* I'm not going for absolute historical accuracy. I want it to be a "bronze age world" in the same way middle earth is early medieval. Thus we have a lot of room to play with.

* Greek settings, whether in movies, books, or games, tend to fall into one of three categories in terms of realism. The first is simply historical fiction. There are quite a few books that go into this style of setting. HBO's Rome is a good example. The second is mostly historical, but in some ways over-the-top or represents supernatural forces in an obscure, dark and sinister way without totally proving them. 300 and Spartacus are both excellent examples of almost film-noir style larger than life settings. Troy did this as well. The third category goes all the way into mythic elements, with the central characters often demigods, or with great monsters and things walking the earth. Clash of the Titans did this. Not well, but they did it. All of Homer falls into this.

I want the setting to fall somewhere in the middle, with elements of dark and gritty fantasy - quite probably with a Robert E Howard Hyborea / H.P. Lovecraft Cthulhu Mythos undertone. Never quite falling into the "wizards guild" level of fantasy, but with weird and unsettling occult and supernatural elements blended in.

* The setting will be drawn from elements of classical Greece, Homeric Greece, early Rome, and some of the bronze age civilizations - Crete, Egypt, Babylonia, Israel, etc. I'd also like to include some bronze-age celtic and germanic flavor as well, but I'd like to avoid direct YACAPE scenarios even though I'm not entirely opposed to them.

* I want to write it from the ground up to capture the feel of the setting

So. here is the first topic of discussion. What can we do to make this feel like a bronze/iron age setting, beyond changing the available weapons and armor? This isn't necessarily strictly a mechanics question at this stage, but a general area to brain storm.

As a secondary topic, does anyone know of an existing game that has done this well? I have read Agon, but it wasn't quite what I was looking for.

Thoughts?

    \n Hi Jackson,

    I'd focus on what the players spend most of their time doing in play, and Bronzify that.

    If there's lots of politicking and intrigue, establish customs, rank, etiquette, etc. Search (by asking here, or finding some history books) for anything really unique, distinctive, and different from people's expectations and assumptions.

    If it's a fighting game, what was the Bronze Age attitude toward combat? How did their equipment affect the combat experience, as opposed to using rapiers or claymores or wearing plate mail? Are there any particularly Bronze Age ways in which warriors work together? Spear guy behind sword guy behind shield guy or some such?

    If it's a game where you spend a lot of time praying to deities for favors and powers, what does that look like? What locations and materials are used? What events are occasions for prayer?

    If there's social play about the characters' homes and families, what unique circumstances exist in those domains? Who owes loyalty to whom? Who has sex, who controls the money, who owns the property? I dunno if you wanna open this can of worms, but slavery is huge. Who are your slaves, where are they from, how are they treated? Are you a slave?

    If your characters spend most of their time going into dungeons and killing monsters, then why would a Bronze Age warrior do that, what would their priorities be when they do it, and how would society (and any applicable deities) react to them doing it?

    If I were you, that's where I'd start. At the very least, as a source of a narrower question for the forum.

    Personally, when I think "feel like Bronze Age", I think of nuggets from various periods of Roman society, gleaned from a mix of historical reading and silly movies and comics. First things that come to mind: small offerings to the god of journeys on the way out the door, little places of worship around the home, slaves, military obligations, voices in the senate, dudes having sex with their nephews, the layout of a palazzo, robes, and sandals. So, yeah, I bet you could get better feedback by narrowing your sights.

    Hope this helps!
    -David

    \n This sounds fantastic.

    The next step is to narrow the premise down a bit. What kinds of stories are we telling? Who are the characters? Is there a "party" (a problem for a lot of games which seek to emulate certain types of fiction, and particularly something like Game of Thrones, although it may be well-suited to a group of Hellenic heroes traveling together).

    As for the supernatural issue, my personal interest would in maintaining the gods as a source of power and influence, but making it very ambiguous as to whether they really exist, or there's something else at play. (A bit like religion in George R.R. Martin's stuff, perhaps.)

    Have you read Ilium, by Dan Simmons? It's not strictly related to what you're doing, but has an interesting "modern" take on the Iliad. You might really enjoy it, if you aren't familiar with it already.

    What is a YACATE scenario? That reference lost me.
