\n I'm running Whitehack, with classical published adventures from Drakar och Demoner. Drakar och Demoner (Swedish for "Dragons and Demons") was extremely popular in the 80s. A lot of people in Sweden think that Drakar och Demoner is a translation of Dungeons and Dragons, which it's not. Instead it's based on Basic Role-Playing. New editions have been released steadily, I think there are nine editions now, but I haven't read or played the recent ones.

I didn't play any Dungeons and Dragons growing up, and I still haven't, neither has the players in my group. When I think of nostalgic fantasy adventures, it's for Drakar and Demoner. When I got a copy of Whitehack it was my first introduction to the OSR movement, but when I thought about what old-school adventures meant to me, it was Drakar och Demoner. I'll write some on Whitehack and the old Drakar och Demoner adventures, and I'll be happy to answer questions on either one.

The first adventure we played was Spindelkonungens pyramid/"The Pyramid of the Spider King" (article in Swedish, but perhaps Google translate can help), originally published in 1982, but I have the re-issue from 1984 that came with the second edition rules. The second adventure was Skelettbyns hemlighet/"The Secret of the Skeleton Village" (article in Swedish). You can find both adventures at the website of the current copyright owner, Riotminds. It's safe to say that most Swedish roleplayers above a certain age knows these two adventures.

I may write about the above adventures in the future, but now I want to talk about the most recent adventure, called Fritagningen/"The Rescue" (also available at Riotminds' website). It was one of two introductory adventures for the fourth edition of Drakar och Demoner.

The first session of The Rescue started with character creation. We have decided that new characters start at the same level as the lowest level of the group, and since the other player had a level 3 character, that was where the new character started. One player had to miss the session, so we only had two players.

The old character was (using the layout from the descriptions of example NPCs on the back cover of Whitehack):\n
Assim, Level 3 Deft Halfling Treasure Hunter
Str 11, Dex 10 (Halfling), Con 7, Int 12 (Storytellers Guild), Wis 8, Cha 15 (Halfling); HP 8, AC 6, ST 12, Move 25, To-hit: +1; Attuned to: Sling, (Rope); Sling, Elven Rope with Grappling Hook, Crossbow, Compass, Studded Leather Tunic; 442 gp, 536 sp, 219 cp, jewels worth 200 sp.
\nThe newly created character was:\n
Wengel, Level 3 Deft Duelist
Str 12, Dex 10 (The Whirling Cape), Con 13, Int 9, Wis 12, Cha 7 (Engelkreutz); HP 10, AC 6, ST 12, Move 25, To-hit: +1; Attuned to: Father's Rapier, (Boots); Rapier, Rope, Lantern, Mule, Studded Leather Armor; 23 gp.
\nThe adventure description starts with a full page that the GM is supposed to read aloud to the players. It tells how the characters reach the small village Zerim, walks into the village center and into the tavern, joins a village council meeting and agrees to help the villagers get rid of some bandits that haunt them. Three times during the monologue the players get to say "yes" if they want to join the meeting and if they accept the mission. We all thought this was quite humorous, and the players didn't mind getting railroaded into the adventure. I'm using Whitehack's prices, but all the rewards and treasures from the Drakar and Demoner adventures, so there are some mismatches. The PCs were promised 20 gp to share, which would amount to six days of dried food for one person and a sleeping bag. Of course they agreed! One of the players said it reminded him of Seven Samurai, which is looking at it positively.

They knew that the pirates had kidnapped a village girl during their last raid, so the next morning they left for the bandit fort in the forest. One player, the only one with previous experience of OSR games, tried to ask NPCs to join them, but they were too afraid. At the time I didn't think about all the precedence for hirelings in OSR games, but even if I would have, it was nice to focus on the PCs. As soon as they reached the forest there was the first random encounter, a baby bear that came up to them. The bear's mother wasn't too happy about meeting the PCs and attacked.

I used the bear from the full spread of monsters included in Whitehack (Bear: HD 5d+10, AC 6, Move 30). I rolled 26 HP on the 5d+10 Hit Dice which, if you paid attention to the PC stats above, was more than theirs combined. The bear also won the initiative and attacked the duelist by biting his stomach. I rolled 1d20+5 (monsters have a to-hit bonus equal to their HD) against 19 minus the duelist's AC (19-6=13). Natural weapons do 1d6 damage and I rolled a 6. The duelist was down to 4 HP.

Meanwhile the halfling had climbed a tree, using the halfling group assigned to dexterity to make a double positive roll (roll 2d20, choose the best one). I had tried to invoke the species group negatively to force a double negative roll (roll 2d20, choose the worst one), but the player referred to Frodo and talked about that time he climbed some tree in the book, so I let the player have it his way. The duelist missed his attack on the bear.

Next round the bear attacked first, still chewing on the duelist's stomach. I rolled another 6 on the d6, but this time the player used his constitution save to lower the damage. He succeeded, and rolled a 3 on his d6. With the damage reduced to 6-3=3 he still had 1 HP. He tried to climb the same tree as the halfling, but failed his roll. The halfling, meanwhile, used this day's special attunement feat to hit the baby bear with his sling, drawing the mother's attention away from the characters. He didn't want to do any damage, had he killed the baby bear they would never get rid of mother bear. I let him succeed without a roll, he's deft with his sling after all, and the plan worked. The two bears left the characters in the tree with no XP for the encounter.

Here we had a fun realization when it came to the first aid rules of Whitehack. They require a trained skill roll, which means that if you don't have a relevant group on your character sheet you make a double negative roll. If you succeed you regain 1d6-3 HP from the last battle, but if you fail you lose 1 HP. This would put the duelist on 0 HP, not death, but since they didn't have access to a deft or wise healer he would get a permanent disadvantage. "Better to let it bleed out," said the duelist. "Yeah, you never know what happens when you start messing around in there. If you push something in from one side, something else will stick out on the other side," said the halfling.

Instead the decided to camp for the day to regain HP (it was still late morning the same day). The kidnapped village girl would have to wait! I like how the first aid rules in Whitehack forces the players to make a decision on how to treat wounds. If they had brought a medic or healer there wouldn't be a problem. I guess that's another OSR wisdom learned.

Next morning the duelist had regained 3 HP, by making his constitution roll in the evening, but failing in the morning. A friendly centaur showed up, another random encounter from the adventure. He showed them the way to the bandits' fort where they spent a couple of hours scouting the grounds. The fort was in a clearing, with 100 meters of open grass between the edge of the forest and the 4 meter high palisade. There were two bandits guarding the outside of the palisade, with one guard outside and one inside the gate. Thanks to a traitor in the village the bandits had been warned. The centaur had told the PCs that he thought there were 10 bandits in the fort.

Here the session ended.

    \n There were two things I could have done better:

    At the tavern in the beginning of the adventure, according to the text the PCs can detect the traitor if they pass a perception check. This is common in Drakar and Demoner, but I have the impression it's usually not a big part of OSR games. I guess it depends on the OSR game, but in general there's the idea that if the players say their characters pay attention to what's going on the PCs will notice it, otherwise not. I made the players roll wisdom, which the duelist passed and the hafling fumbled. It felt very forced to come up with in what way the PC fumbled, but the player graciously suggested that he wouldn't believe whatever the duelist noticed. The duelist never said anything, so it didn't matter. I have since decided that I'll let the players initiate any perception activities. If the player describe that they search in the right place they will find whatever it is, and if they decide to roll a perception roll I will suggest places they can look. They still have to describe that they do it. It feels like a nice trade off between player skill and character skill, and I'll try it out in future sessions to see how it works.

    The other thing was that I just quickly read aloud the description of where the important people in the village lived (hunter, miller, and so on). The halfling-PC player enjoys experiencing the world through his PC, so he felt cheated. I've decided to make more of an effort to describe things from the PCs perspective in the future. I think it makes for a richer game, and it's something I always struggle with. I'm usually not very interested in describing the environment, but I think it improves the game when I do, so I'll try. I do want to avoid the PCs spending all session talking to villagers without getting to where the action is, but I'll have to keep some of the exposition, and not just cut to the next piece of action.

    Last Tuesday we played the second session of The Rescue, joined by the missing player's Strong Barbarian. But I'll save the report of their commando strike on the fort for another day.

    \n Thanks! I usually don't post actual play reports, but I'm so into Whitehack at the moment that I really want to discuss the game.\n
    Neat! I'm glad to hear that people are playing Whitehack -- I've been reading it and thinking about what kind of game I want to play with it. And reading your account reminded me of several little rules; good reinforcement!
    \nI know what you mean when you say "several little rules". We've played five sessions so far (Spider King: 1, Skeleton Village: 2, The Rescue: 2), and between sessions I've re-read the rule book. Each time I've spotted new little wrinkles to rules I hadn't considered before. It's almost like we started with the core ideas, and have added new things each session. Because of the short length of the rules text each sentence includes something important, and there's almost no redundancy.

    Three examples from the above session:

    1. Previously we haven't used inactive slots. The players have only noted their active slots on the character sheet, and have left the inactive slots to be decided later. This session I required them to also decide their inactive slots, because they may want to switch their active attunement during the session and I wanted that also to be a tactical choice.

    2. The halfling above has the inactive attunement "rope". We discussed if attuned objects had to be a specific object or if they could be a general type of object. We decided on a type of object, thinking for example of Robin Hood being able to pick up any bow and be proficient, but after I talked to Christian Mehrstam (creator of Whitehack) I decided it had to be a specific object. So, at the beginning of the next session, the halfling specified that he was attuned to his elven rope. I said that if they lost the object, it would take a day of training to attune themselves to a new object.

    3. Combat advantage is important to Deft characters, since it gives them the option to do double damage. Before, and including this session, we only used the simple roll-to-hit system, but after the bear attack I re-read the rules and found that some combat specials (feint and trip) trades this round's damage for a combat advantage next round. That gives +2 on to-hit and damage the second round, with double damage for the Deft combat specialist. Re-reading the rules after session 5 I also found that if the Deft rolls a successful double roll they get combat advantage next round. I keep finding these nuggets, and it feels like it improves the game from session to session.

    \nLast session the halfling player convincingly argued that the once-per-day limitation should allow even more spectacular feats, since the character won't get the same advantage over and over.     My own guideline is that if it's something anyone can do with a roll, the Deft character can do it without a roll. If it's something that no-one can do, the Deft can do it with a dice roll, within reason. For example in the Skeleton Village, the halfling ricocheted a rock with his sling around a corner, hitting a skeleton he knew was there, but it required a regular to-hit roll.     \nThe Rescue, part II

    We left the two adventurers in the forest, scouting the bandits' fort, and looking for a way to rescue the kidnapped village girl, find some loot, and perhaps kill some bandits.

    The two players were joined by a third player, who has been playing from the start, but missed the previous session. He's playing a Strong Barbarian, extremely inspired by Kronar (NSFW) from the web comic Oglaf (NSFW!). The barbarian even have a mule called Oglaf. The PCs name is Malena-Igor, son of Malena-Ivar, which is like being called Barbara-Igor or something. It's some kind of gender commentary from the player that I'm not sure I understood, possibly a reference to the men in Kronar's tribe giving birth.

    Anyway, the stats:\n
    Malena-Igor, Level 2 Strong Barbarian
    Str 17 (Barbarian), Dex 13, Con 15, Int 12, Wis 6 (The Medicine Men of the Steppe), Cha 10; HP 8, AC 7, ST 15, Move 25, To-hit: +2; Hydra leather loincloth armor, Battle axe, Mule; 71 gp.
    \nThe session started with another round of rule fixes and additions on stuff I'd read since last session. I originally thought that picking a species (humans, elves, dwarfs, orcs, and so on; there are no races in Whitehack, only species) was mandatory at character creation. It turns out that if you play the standard species of the setting you don't get any attribute groups. The barbarian had assigned "Human" to strength and wisdom, which actually felt weird when he invoked during previous sessions. It's telling that I would have no problem with a dwarf that rolls double positive rolls for strength, but that it seemed strange that humans would do it. I think it exemplifies a subtle point in Whitehack, that all species are being compared to humans, or whatever baseline your setting has, to make a point about who is being privileged.

    The main thing the barbarian player had used "Human" for was for wisdom, when doing first aid checks. So after a discussion with the group he removed "Human" and added "The medicine men from the steppe". It was important that they were all men, to keep up the Kronar references.

    The other thing was picking a language. I hadn't bothered before, even though they met and talked to a band of goblin and troll treasure hunters during the first session, in the Pyramid of the Spider King. I decided in retrospect that the goblin chief had spoken the common language, but in the future I want languages to matter. It makes Wise characters more important, since they can learn extra languages. All players chose "Common" as the language.

    The barbarian joined the others and the players started planning their assault. I had decided beforehand I would let this take whatever time it took, as it was the main focus of the session. I leaned back and listened to the players going over different options, answering questions when they wanted to know details about the guards' timing and so on.

    I'll set the scene:

    The fort was an old stone hunting castle, six meters high. It was surrounded by a 4 meter high wooden palisade, with equally high wooden towers in each corner. The palisade was 60 x 40 meters. There was 100 meters of open grass between the edge of the surrounding forest and the palisade, so it was placed in a big clearing. The PCs had seen 5 bandits entering the fort, two patrolling guards, one guard outside the gate and one on the inside. The bandits had been warned from the village and were expecting some kind of attack. The two patrolling guards started at the front gate, walk in different directions around the outside of the palisade, met in the back and returned to the front each on one side.

    The players' (and PCs') final plan, after many alternatives, was to wait until dark, split up into two groups and sneak to positions close to the two back corners of the palisade. On one side the barbarian would wait. On the other side the halfling would attach his rope to the palisade and trip up one of the guards, which would signal to the duelist to attack the downed bandit and the barbarian to stealth attack the guard on his side. Stealth attack with a battle axe, that is. They hoped the guard in the front would go look what had happened, so they could kill him as well. The last thing the hafling said was "If something goes wrong, it's each man for himself. We'll retreat into the forest and regroup at the large oak tree we passed before." "Each man for himself, I like it!" said the barbarian.

    I required a dexterity roll from each PC to sneak up to their positions without being detected by the guards. It was dark and they would sneak carefully, which should give them a bonus, but on the other hand the guards were expecting trouble and the PCs would have to get quite close, so I didn't give any modifications either way. The halfling passed his roll, as did the duelist. I wanted to activate "halfling" negatively on the dexterity roll, but once again the player argued it should be a double positive roll. I gave him that, but said that next time he would *have* to accept a double negative roll. The species are supposed to be both good and bad for the PC. The barbarian failed his sneak roll. The guard had spotted him and shouted that he should stand up.

    Did the barbarian follow the plan to retreat? No, he didn't. Instead he ran towards the guard with his battle axe. The patrolling guard on the other side ran to the front gate to get to the other side, and hadn't detected the halfling and duelist. The halfling shot his crossbow at the running guard, getting an advantage for attacking an unaware enemy, but a disadvantage because it was dark, so it was a straight roll. He missed, but the arrow kept flying and the guards didn't notice. The duelist ran around the corner towards the front gate.

    The barbarian's fight went spectacularly bad. During the next couple of rounds he missed the guard twice, while the guard hit him two times. He was down to his last few HPs.

    The duelist rounded the corner and was attacked by the guard at the front gate. In two rounds the duelist missed twice, with the guard hitting twice, putting the duelist passed out at 0 HP.

    The running guard rounded the second corner to help his friend with the barbarian. I asked the player if he wanted to retreat. I've heard that's what you sometimes do when you're outnumbered in OSR games. But the player didn't want that, but to stand and fight. Also, had he retreated both guards would get a free attack of opportunity anyway.

    With two enemies flanking him they rolled with combat advantage (+2 to-hit and damage) and immediately put him at 0 HP, also passed out.

    This left the halfling standing. He had run up to the palisade to peak around the corner towards the front gate, but realizing his odds were bad he retreated into the forest. He saw how the guards opened the gate and dragged the unconscious barbarian and duelist into the fort. Here's one of the drawbacks with the PCs falling unconscious at 0 HP, but not dying. If they die immediately, it's out of my hands, but with them being unconscious I must decide if they live or die. Some monsters will of course kill and eat them by default, but with human enemies I have to be an asshole. On the other hand, I like that I can go all in as GM in battles and leave the life or death decision for later. This time I thought about having the bandits immediately slit the throat of the barbarian and duelist, but that felt a bit extreme within the fiction. I decided they would alert their boss and let him decide, postponing the decision.

    The session could have ended here.

    \n One huge incentive for the halfling to get back to the village was that we have decided that new characters start at the current lowest level of the party. Since he was level 3, all new characters would be level 3.

    But he wanted to try to rescue his friends and had two bottles of 1d6 HP healing potions, so he snuck back to the palisade. He used his rope to climb the palisade, but failed. I used the almost classic fail forward "halfway a guard spots you". He decided to keep going, and jumped onto a roof in the compound. He entered the building and found the stables, and tried to get the horses to panic to make them trample the guards in the courtyard. This failed without a roll, since I didn't feel like a hafling could frighten the horses that much by just punching and kicking them. The horses left the stables, but they didn't stampede.

    After sneaking around a short while he decided to enter a large second-story window of the main building. Once again he failed his climb roll and once again he was spotted. He entered the window anyway, and saw the bandit leader with a bandit sidekick at the large table in the main hall. The halfling tried to jump to a balcony inside the main hall, when I said "I wonder if halflings really are that good at jumping?", invoking his species group negatively. He failed his roll, and fell to the floor 3 meters down with 1d6 damage.

    This is a neat thing with how species work in Whitehack. The group now knows that halflings are good at climbing and sneaking, but not at jumping. Also, it's not certain it applies to all halflings in the setting. The next halfling PC might say "Pfft, jumping is easy for a Took, it's the Baggins who can't jump!" Suddenly we learn even more about the halfling species, without having to decide all this before playing.

    Back to our halfling. The bandit leader and bandit sidekick drew their swords, and the bandit sidekick ran up to the halfing. We rolled initiative and the halfling won. He wanted to leave the room, but the bandit sidekick landed his attack of opportunity, putting the halfling at 0 HP.

    \nThe Rescue, part IV

    Before the session me and the players designed our own encumbrance system. In Whitehack all equipment and treasure is measured in pounds, and a carried weight of 76, 101, or 151+ pounds lowers the character's movement rate. The basic movement rate is different for normal sized people, like humans, and smaller people, like dwarves and halflings. Strength doesn't affect carrying capacity, but you can roll strength to improve the movement rate by one level for one round. The players all said it would be too tedious to track the weight of each item, so we designed a slot system inspired by Lamentations of the Flame Princess.

    Each PC has a maximum movement rate based on their species. Normal sized species have movement 9 meters per round and small species (dwarves, halflings) have movement 6 m/rd. Armor decreases movement, but do not add to encumbrance, which start at 9 m/rd for all species. You carry five slots per encumbrance level. Slot 0-5 give movement 9 m/rd, slot 6-10 give 6 m/rd, slot 11-15 give 3 m/rd, and slot 16+ give 0 m/rd. Strength 13+ give an extra slot per encumbrance level and 16+ give two extra slots. Small items (coins, rings, jewels) can be carried 200 per slot. Normal items (sword, shield) take up one slot. 20 items of ammunition (quiver with arrows, bolts, sling stones) take up one slot. Large items (two-handed sword, halberd) take up two slots.

    A special case is the adventure kit, which is a backpack and a sack with basic items. It only takes up one slot. The items are (items and prices from Whitehack):

    Backpack,\t5 gp
    Bandages (5), 2 gp
    Flint & steel, 5 gp
    Rations, trail (5), 5 gp
    Rope hemp, 15 m, 1 gp
    Sack, 1 gp
    Sleeping bag, 2 gp
    Torches (6), 1 gp
    Waterskin, 2 l, 3 gp

    Total cost is 25 gp. Two benefits with the adventure kit is that it makes it more probable that the PCs will have stuff they need to do their adventures, and it's faster to buy at character creation. We avoided special equipment like lock picks and a grappling hook, so that interested PCs can get them if they want to.

    Armor decrease maximum movement according to the following table:

    Shield, 0
    Cloth, 0
    Leather, -1
    Studded Leather, -1
    Chainmail, -2
    Splint Mail, -2
    Full Plate, -3

    For example, a dwarf has movement 6 m/rd. In chainmail that's 4 m/rd. If he fills 0-5 slots, his movement is still 4 m/rd, same with 6-10 items, but at 11-15 items his movement is 3 m/rd as the encumbrance surpasses the species and armor movement rate.

    \n One more thing: I've expanded the encumbrance slot system a little bit since last session. The idea is that slot 1-5 is carried on the body, 6-10 in the backpack, and 11-15 in the sack in at least one hand. This means that if you ditch the backpack and the sack to run faster, we know what items you left behind for the orcs. You'll probably carry your main weapons, up to 200 coins, and so on, on your body, which is good to know if you lose everything else.

    The adventure kit is again a bit special. It takes up one body slot, but since it contains the backpack and sack it enables the use of slots 6-15. All other items in the adventure kit count as if they're carried in the backpack, in case you drop it. Moving an item from one slot to another takes a turn in battle. Moving an item from the backpack to the hands also takes a turn. The hands doesn't count as body slots though, so you can still keep the rope noted on the backpack even if you're using it at the moment. Since you'll only hold one or two things in your hands at a time it's easy to mentally keep track of, so there's no need to erase it from the backpack and put it in a body slot.

    \n A list of observations after seven sessions of play, four of the session in the above adventure The Rescue:

    * Even though I've suggested it a couple of times in fights, no player has used the special combat options push, charge, feint, trip, disarm, fight defensively/offensively, protect, or grapple. I think that, since most of the options forsakes damage this round to get a special move next round, they don't fit with the "must kill the enemy as quickly as possible" desperation that colors our fights. Level 1 PCs go down on a good hit or two, so there's less time for finesse. Players of Deft characters should be interested in the special combat options, because some of them give combat advantage next round, which is very good for a combat oriented Deft PC.

    * The book says "The base experience points awarded for monsters are listed in Table 10", but it doesn't say exactly what they are awarded for. Facing the monster, overcoming the monster, killing the monster? This is not a bad thing, since it enables different types of play, but it leaves some game design to the GM. My current rule is that the PCs get XP when they overcome monsters, because I don't want them to have to kill every last enemy. Monster XP is low anyway, 30 XP for a 1 HD monster like skeletons, goblins, and human bandits. For a level 1 PC they can be tough to kill, but a Strong PC needs 2000 XP for level 2, which equals 67 human bandits. Also, the book doesn't say if every participant in the fight gets 30 XP or if you divide it between all PCs (and NPCs). Our current rule is that the XPs are divided, how is totally decided by the players. They can give all XP to one PC to level it up, spread them evenly, or give it to the PC who did most of the damage, or anyway they want. If NPCs have helped out, I take an equal share, say 25% if there's one NPC and three PCs.

    * In the beginning it was difficult, and new players are still confused when they start playing, that there're two different resolution systems for tasks and combat. To perform a task you roll 1d20 equal or under the attribute. You want to roll as high as possible, since higher is better, up to a point. If you hit your attribute score exactly it's a perfect roll, but a 20 is an abysmal failure. In combat you roll 1d20 + base hit bonus + modifiers, equal or higher than the opponents 19 - AC. Rolling a 20 is a critical hit (not an abysmal failure as with tasks) and a 1 is a fumble. For example, a starting Strong character with strength below 13 that hits a human bandit (AC 6) would roll 1d20 + 1 equal or higher than 13. But as we've played for a while I've gotten used to switching modes between the resolution systems, and don't find it difficult anymore. It's actually quite fun to go into "battle mode".

    * We haven't used auctions yet. This one's on me, and as GM I should push for auctions when they're relevant. We're playing modules written for a game without auctions, so it's easier to translate the rolls into straight task resolution. I'm sure we'll stumble on a situation where an auction will feel perfect later on. When we do, I'll use the author's suggested change and add a hidden 1d6 to each side to make the auction more interesting.

    * The species system, the Deft attunements, and the magic system all require "game design" either while playing or between sessions. As GM I find myself thinking about how I could've handled past situations differently and future situations better. We don't have any Deft PCs at the moment, so I don't have to bother with that. The species system clicked when we had a halfling PC. Now it's the magic system, and assigning an HP cost to spells, that has me thinking. The game allows me to either come up with a strict system and present it to the player, or come up with the HP cost for each spell on the fly. Even if I come with a prepared system there will be a negotiation, since the player can increase or decrease the cost by increasing damage or duration, or by performing a ritual with expensive ingredients. The main thing for me has been to let go of the notion that I need to be perfectly consistent with the HP cost. If I realize a spell has been too cheap I can always increase the cost later in the name of game balance. If I, by mistake, let the PC summon a mighty demon for 1 HP and the player starts messing up every fight I can always increase the cost and say that the available demons are running out. This limits analysis paralysis while assigning HP costs at the table; now I just go with what feels right at the moment and postpone worrying until there's an actual game balance problem.

    * Combat oriented Deft characters can do double damage in combat, and critical hits also do double damage. These two stack, according to the rules. But it's not clear if "double" means that you roll damage once and multiply by 2 or if you roll damage twice and add together. A Deft character getting a critical hit could either roll once and multiply by 4 or roll damage four times and add together. We're using a middle path. On a critical hit you roll damage twice and add together, and if you're a combat-oriented Deft PC you roll damage once more and add, so damage is rolled three times. I like that damage from critical hits lump together in the middle of the distribution curve, rather than swinging wildly from one end or the other depending on the roll. Using multiplication it's more likely to roll a 1 for 2 damage than it is if your roll 2d6 of damage and add together. It's also harder to get an extremely high result, but on average a critical hit is a lot better than a normal hit when using addition.

    * I really like the included setting and I want to use it, but for the foreseeable future I'll run classical Drakar och Demoner modules. I don't want to convert all of them to the eternal winter setting, or to the nice and weird vampire orcs, so when I run out of modules I'll probably teleport the group into the setting or start over with new PCs. If I were sandboxing and improvising I would use the default setting, but doing it now would get in the way of experiencing the modules as written.

    Can't you use an auction in any multi-party conflict? I'd look for even combats where the general shape of the outcome is certain (usually, players will certainly win), but the side-conditions are variable. If goblin A wins the auction, you still defeat them but he's succeeded at banging the sacred gong. If goblin B wins, he's successfully lead the poisoners into you and now you're suffering from their attacks. If you win, none of the above. That kind of thing. Am I misremembering how that works?
    \nYes, that could work as an auction. Another way to get an auction in is when a PC tries to sneak past or backstab a guard. Instead of a straight dexterity roll for sneaking or a regular attack from combat advantage, you could alternate bids and describe how the PC sneaks and the guard guards, followed by one roll to resolve the situation, probably wisdom for the guard to see if the guard notices the PC. What you're doing is basically deciding if the situation is resolved by the PC's dexterity or the guard's wisdom. But so far we've used the previous methods, since we're more used to them.

    Also, we haven't had a situation where the PCs were certain to win; even a fight with a single orc is dangerous since their 1d6 of damage can put a PC close to being out.
