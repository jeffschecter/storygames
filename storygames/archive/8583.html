\n The Durham 3 (5th edition) had a player out last night, so we couldn't play our normal game. I threatened around lunchtime to run OD&D, and got taken up on the threat, so I had to whip up a dungeon quick. Tony Dowler's For the Love of Dungeons saved my bacon. Over a bowl of pasta at Noodles and Company, I was able to take his "Haunted Mine" map and whip up a fantastically fun adventure.

From our experience, you can make a 4-6 hour adventure out of each of these maps. We played for about 2 hours and 15 minutes and finished about half of the Haunted Mine. Anyway, I just wanted to throw big ups to Tony for this product: it's a great game aid.

    \n Dude, we had numerous cool encounters. We freed this no-account miner from a collapsed room, and made him a hireling. When we found the room covered in webs, he told us that he and his (dead) miner buddies left the giant fucking spider that lived there alone, and that we should too. We laughed at his cowardice and burned us some webs, fighting the enraged beast! My elf, Palladia Mosswolf, slew the thing and then fell into a pit, losing 2 of her 3 hit points! My barbarian companion Korg cut the thing open and found a dead Kobold inside! And a platinum dagger worth 300 gp!

    Later we crossed a gaping cavern and kicked a wight's ass! There was a dwarven treasure trove, and some cups filled with potions! Korg drank poison and almost died! It was pretty great.

    I think the map is too complex, though - we couldn't see it and had to make our own map, of course, and Clinton was all "There are seven corridors, one is at a 115 degree angle..." Our mapper wept salty tears of map rage.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarCan you talk about how you used what Tony provided to create a full-fledged adventure?
    \nMost definitely! So, I already had this giant map of the underworld I'd made last weekend with How to Host a Dungeon. I didn't know I'd be running D&D on Monday, but synchronicity happened.

    There's a specific thing in role-playing that I don't think has a name, but happens a lot. If you take your own preconceptions and add outside input, the input seems to match your thoughts and expand upon them perfectly, even if not related. On my big map, I had an ancient cavern that'd been opened by dwarves 1000 years ago. It's not far from the surface and was part of their mining complex, so I figured I'd use that for an adventure. I dug through my adventures and nothing fit, but then I flipped through For the Love and saw "The Haunted Mine" and thought "ok!" There's names in all these rooms, and they instantly start connecting. I saw "Mind Tombs" and remembered that elsewhere, deep on my big map, I had a horrible enemy, the Thought Lords. That clicked, and I realized I could use that as an influence. "Tombs" and "Haunted" made me remember my undead dwarves elsewhere on the big map, so I added them here, too. A room called "What Lies Here?" with a secret room to the side - well, it had to be a wight king laying there, with his hidden treasure room.

    Even though none of that was on either map - mine or Tony's - the two together generated a lot of meat. I was able to come up with an adventure over a bowl of pasta, complete with hooks to a bigger campaign, in 45 minutes. That doesn't really reflect on me as much as the evocativeness of the map and the technique I described.
