\n So, I have two reward paths of a fantasy game that I need to integrate.

The Setting:
Mundane medieval humanity keeping supenatural pure evil at bay. In the eyes of most, "magic" = "bad". You play a regular nobody, who advances through play to become something more.

Reward Path 1:
Go solve mysteries & problems involving the supernatural. Get clues in the process that gradually reveal how you could do some magics yourself. Yay! Learning rare, special knowledge is cool! Now we can use it for... uh...

Reward Path 2:
Go solve mysteries & problems that beat back evil and benefit humanity. The Empire rewards you with gear and authority, and you can eventually be granted land and lordship over an area. Yay! Becoming someone important is cool!

I want these to be one cycle that really feeds back into itself strongly on both a per-mission level and a large campaign level. I think a good answer to the thread title, given the constraints of the Setting I described, oughta do it.

Brainstorms welcome. Pointing out fatal flaws is welcome too.

    \n If your reward cycle for avoiding and defeating evil-magic is all about public power and external reward (you get land, titles, castles, recognition, a place in the magic-hating imperial bureaucracy) then the obvious counterpoint is that learning magic is all about internal and private power, the things you can do in secret, unseen, unnoticed.

    Putting it in terms of what character need is answered by doing it, maybe magic is the tool that answers the murky inner dilemmas, greed, jealousy, hatred, lust and the like. It's a selfish power (which is why everyone hates it), but it can solve those things that public power are no use for. It can make another man's true love sleep with you instead. It can make your hated rival have an accident. It can cure you of your recurring nightmares from the crimes you committed.

    For parallel cycles you can just make sure that characters have goals that could only be answered by one sort of power or the other. For interwoven cycles make sure that character goals really need to be answered by both of them. A good way to do the later might be the Houses of the Blooded way, magic is totally forbidden to everyone ... but the more mundane power you have the more people will look the other way. This is especially good if dabbling in magic to solve your inner problems leaves some sort of external mark, stigmata or consequence that others could ignore if you are of sufficient rank.

    For a more arthurian sort of bent you could imagine that the stigmata is something that happens to your land, like famine, plague, or best of all, occurrences of evil magic that you need to go and fight. Then all the reward systems are bound together in a rather dysfunctional relationship. Personal need drives you to learn magic, using magic creates external threats you need to fight, fighting them gains temporal power, gaining temporal power lets you get away with using more magic, using more magic creates more threats ... and so on

      \n
    • What do you know, people can use magic and still remain themselves. Who knew?
      • \n
    • Hey, that senator has a magical aura. How come?

      • \n
    • Hey, cool, I joined them.
      • \n
    • Mindblowing! They're telling me that the emperor is actually a magical construct and not the son of god we were taught. Magic actually controls the empire.
      • \n
    • Damn, now this ecclesiastical secret society is telling me that my secret society is full of lies and the emperor is actually just a man. Luckily they want my magic for keeping the world together.
      • \n
    • etc... the truth might be out there, but it's not going to come in for a couple of seasons yet.
      • \n
    \nAnyway, the basic problem of how to make magic matter in social climbing seems obvious to me - magic is understanding of the nature of the world and it might allow you to sabotage enemy rituals as well as, ultimately, create your own. Of course it's going to give you a social cachet. Science did that as early as the Middle Ages, and science does not give you special sight for perceiving when somebody is using his amazing science powers. Even if we dropped all possible religious power, information power, influence over weak minds, martial enchantments and all other practical uses magic might conseivably have, it'd still be something rulers would want to possess and sponsor just for the prestige as long there was some vague promise of value in it. Just consider how much a medieval university costs, and then remember that the only practical reason for having such at the time was to get a place to school priests in - priests who in turn are largely useless. So if a medieval ruler will finance an university for priests, why not wizards?

    How positioning in the power structure of the empire would feed your magic is somewhat less obvious, but only if you're stuck with D&D-like utility magic. Start talking of rituals that require shaping cliff-faces into totemic shapes and the concentrated worship of hundreds of acolytes, and we're again in the area of public works projects that can only be attempted with the blessings and financing of the ruler. In an environment where magic is reliably teachable to anybody with sufficient leisure, but expensive to use, the only people who are magicians will be the rulers and their advisors. Others are limited to cheap hedge-workings and observing what the high and mighty attempt.

    In other words, I sort of don't perhaps quite see how this would be difficult to achieve in play. Both magic and social position are clearly useful and preferable for ambitious characters, and they both allow for utility and threaten attacks on fronts that others are vulnerable to without their own resources. So it seems obvious to me that unless genre convention or specific rules limit it, of course power-seeking characters will find it immensely preferable to be both magicians and nobility, using the strengths of one to gain advantages in the other role.

    Another thought:

    A setup that generates opportunities for those who learn metaphysical secrets can easily be prepared.

    Things like:

    * The secrets you are discovering allow you to control a resource or find a way to prevent a problem.

    For instance, the poisonous plants you mentioned with the yellow aura? If they are not identifiable in any other way, and their presence is devastating a whole agricultural society, whoever has the means to sniff those out can use that ability to advance themselves, make demands, etc.

    * Enough in the world is based on magical secrets that learning any gives you leverage to move up in society

    You'd have to keep the secrets pretty subtle to keep your "low magic" vibe, but otherwise it's solid. Make sure the characters are interested in achieving power upfront, then give them opportunities to discover secrets. If each secret gives them a way of understanding how someone who HAS power already can be manipulated, you're tying magical discovery to social advancement. For instance, if I learn that the General is only successful in battle because of a magical charm he keeps in his boot... and perhaps what kind of magic would counteract that charm, I have the means to become more powerful/influential/successful.

    In other words, those who have power in your setting have achieved it through some magical means. They don't "play fair", which is why they are dominant. Learning their secrets, though, allows you to compete with them, blackmail them, and eventually take their place.

    \n There are cultures (mostly Eastern, as I recall) where only someone who rejects mortal, temporal power can have magic. Either you stay a public figure, heaped with rewards, or you sink into the cultish life and gather beneath the dungeons in quiet halls where greater powers than those of Kings are called to - perhaps by those self-same Kings.

    The choice then becomes one of how you respond when you find out you are not killing EVIL magic, you are killing UNTAMED magic for the people who have tamed forces that may be just as evil, if not more, than those you opposed beyond the walls of the Keep.

    \n Maybe magic lets you see and interact with the demons, daemons, spirits, and other supernatural entities roaming the world that no one else can?

    Then the problem changes from "how is magic is cool/useful?" to "how can I make cool and interesting stories about supernatural entities and their motivations, and how the characters can impede or assist them?" But without temporal power, you can't fight the evil done in real life, or enlist real-life allies. Since you can't fight just one half of the evil and achieve any lasting success, you have to pursue both goals.
