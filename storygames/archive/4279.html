\n ...because they're not. Freeform can mean...\n
    \n
  • An English LARP, non-boffer style
    • \n
  • That Nordic weird style of LARP
    • \n
  • Playing without written rules, just player agreement
    • \n
  • Online play
    • \n
\n...and all of those are different. Any attempt to generalise over them is going to fail.

It's as if we started a conversation about card games and included Monopoly, The Shab Al-Hiri Roach and Canasta, because they all use cards. You just can't do it.

Really, I think we should stop using the term "freeform" at all. It's meaningless: of the activities listed above, only "playing without written rules" is free from form, and that's arguable.

    More elaborately, several, if not all, of these different groups say "freeform" when they talk about their play. I believe that it's staggeringly unproductive to try to redefine some group's terms when you're an outsider trying to learn from them\xe2\u20ac\u201dwhich is the only sensible goal here, right?

    \n

    So you speak their language when you're talking to them, and when you're talking about them, you consider that they may be listening, and you use terms that are close enough to their language that they are translatable\xe2\u20ac\u201dNordic freeform, boffer freeform, unicorn freeform, whatever. You certainly don't deliberately throw their vocab out with the bathwater; that generates confusion and jargon barriers that don't need to exist.

    \n

    Trying to change common use is dumb, stupid, pointless, ill-informed.

    \n

    Mindfully tuning common usage so it works better, that's wise.

    \n I agree with your core argument, which I'll interpret as "The term freeform is loose and confusing."
    \n
    \nI've never heard it used in a description of actual play, or a game, or a game system, and found it a clear, useful term; this is for reasons outlined in the first post: it means a lot of things, in different contexts, and often these contexts aren't clear. I've heard gamers claim that their campaigns are 'very freeform in scope' or that 'combat is handled freeform' or even such nonsense as 'the game is run as freeform 3.5E D&D'. To me, each of these claims not only doesn't tell me anything about the game, it also sounds like the claimant is trying to sound cool by bashing around a word.
    \n
    \nWhile it's pretty harsh to consider banning a word on this basis, or asking people to change their nomenclature because we find a term they understand in context confusing, it's worth pointing out to them that we don't understand it, and it doesn't (at least to me) make them sound cool.
    \n
    \nAnd I don't agree that it's the same as the term 'role-playing'. Role-playing has several meanings, most of which I understand the meaning of. I can interpret from context if somebody invites me around for an evening of 'role-playing' or describes their leisure activity as 'playing RPGs'. Vast deeper meanings of the word may be lost on me, I'll admit, but I'm happy with my current level of understanding of it.
    \n
    \nAnd also, in my (limited) experience 'playing without written rules' has always sounded like a better idea than it actually was.
