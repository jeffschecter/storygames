\n To those running or planning a kickstarter, I've got a problem with your stretch goals.

I don't want another hastily-written fan supplement on a niche topic. I don't need it, I probably won't read it, I almost certainly won't play or use it. I already have far more "content" than I'll get through in a dozen lifetimes. I'd literally rather you didn't have it than you did. I don't want content; I play to make content. That's basically the point.

What you can do for me, as a game designer, is playtest your rules and make them perfect. That's what I want to pay for. That's why I use published games.

So, how about making your next stretch goal - "After the first PDF release, we'll take six months and $10K consultancy budget to really batter the game in play and through modelling and iron out the kinks."

If you can name your paid consultants ("Baker's agreed to run ten sessions on video, Crane will build 50 PCs in a hunt for dominant builds, and McDaldno has promised to find eleven serious ambiguities in the text") then I'll raise my pledge for sure.

How about it?

    With a game (or novel or movie) there's not some external criteria I can grade. Until we have units of fun I can't say that this game produced 10.2 mega-fonzies or whatever.
    \nAlso, part of the problem is that what people will report (which can't be numerically or mechanically measured) will not always reflect the true problem, or a problem at all.

    I don't have a link at the moment, but I remember an interview with the Borderlands designers. They mentioned a few cases in the testing phase where numerous people would complain about some flaw, but deeper examination revealed no problem (or a different one).

    For example, many players were complaining that the reload times were too long, although they were as fast as or faster than most other FPS. However, when more animation steps were added to reloading (giving the illusion that more was actively happening), these complaints dwindled.

    Furthermore, many times a playtester's feedback can reflect only their own (sometimes very specific) tastes and opinions. Player A can say X is too strong and Y needs to be improved; player B will say Y is too strong and X needs to be improved. And this can go on over many people, leading to a confused, bland, or simply dysfunctional system in the goal of pleasing everyone.

    Now, this isn't to say playtesting isn't good in some ways, but it requires careful consideration and a steady hand.

    TL;DR: Playtest feedback can be contradictory, subjective, and even plain wrong. Be careful!

    Designing games, thinking about new terrain, crafting mechanics? That stuff is fun and compelling and invigorating. Tweaking, editing, re-thinking, re-writing?     I don't speak for all designers.     If folks have less community support, their development process is going to be less informed and their playtesting crew much more limited. I think the thing that is going to be way more helpful in most cases than $10,000 of consultancy will be people making an effort to spot fledgling designers and playtest their stuff, and talk to them about their stuff over skype, and to ask questions like, "So, what other designs are you working on? What's their biggest departure from this one?" Single person design teams are the norm here, and they have limits. The support doesn't need to enter the picture when the game is essentially done. The support needs to come early and stay throughout.

    \n Theory:

    Playtesting can provide lots of different things. Playtesting is more expansive in purpose than what Ben talks about in his inflammatorily-worded essay. But in order to make playtesting sing for you as a designer, you need to know what you're looking for and create a playtest arrangement where you'll actually receive that. Creating a workable draft and then getting people to play it and "see how it goes" will not necessarily improve your game. But I'm hard-pressed to imagine a game that wouldn't benefit from playtesting. The key is to playtest smarter and with more intent. As a community of designers and players, we'll do well to keep sharing our best practices and ideas about how to playtest and what to watch out for.

    \n I've actually found intent-less playtesting useful, but I think it varies from game to game.

    For DW instead of asking for playtesters we made the game free (in one form) and told anybody who played it and shared their thoughts they'd get the whole game.

    That actually worked really well for us, because instead of people playing with the intent of testing something, they were just playing. We got our feedback, including things we couldn't have expected like actual play recordings.

    We also built up a publicly visible collection of feedack, which helped attract more people to the game.

    So it worked for us, and I have a hard time imagining a more directed playtest meeting our goals so well, but it certainly doesn't apply to all games. We weren't pushing the envelope much. For other games I can certainly see directed playtesting working better for other games.

Our internal testing was much better about helping with the few fundamental issues we had, but told us nothing about how other people would or wouldn't react to them and how they'd understand them.
