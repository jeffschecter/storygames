    \n Jailbreak is presented heavily in actor stance, because it's, again, largely a "reveal" scenario. That is we the players are entertained by Stolze's story, and really are given only one big bang (there's a prelude bang, too, but the real one is the best I've ever seen). Play is suspenseful leading up to the bang, as we get hints of what it's about, and then, once the bang happens, the reveal having been accomplished, then things really get wild as players have free reign to create all sorts of endings.
    \n
    \nThe whole thing works because of the nature of the hidden information, and precisely because of how it's hidden. That's the ingenious part. I don't normally recommend this sort of play, but done this well, it's a ton of fun. I myself have used a very similar structure for my Con scenario called "The Ending Season." I only just now have realized that.
    \n
    \nIt's definitely not something I'd ever do in long-term play of a game. But for a one-shot (that's the name of the book, after all), it's great fun.

    \n Man... I dunno. On the one hand, I'm all about allowing players to run the plot off in their own direction. But on the other hand, this is a set-piece, and it makes sense to run it to the big bang. That's the point of the scenario, and it's not really much good without it.
    \n
    \nWhat I suggest is using more "illusionisty" or even "narrativisty" techniques to accomplish the same goals, yeah. But using strong GM control of situation to make sure that the cool bang occurs. No, don't quite railroad, but do the GM job in such a way as to not let the subject matter get on another track.
    \n
    \nI talk about this seeming paradox all the time. People think that play must either be "GM controls the Plot" or "GM sets up a situation and let's it go." Thinking that any control of the evolving situation amounts to control of plot. As I always say, what you want to do is use as heavy a hand as neccessary to give your players something interesting about which to make decisions.
    \n
    \nSo... let's say that the hostages somehow manage to get out of the house and run. OK, that's cool, they got to demonstrate that their characters weren't going to sit still for this treatment. It's a simple matter for them to run into a previously unseen fence (maybe a special one created by the house's owner), that prevents them from getting far. Heck, allow them to arm themselves and maybe even take out the prisoners. It's not neccessary for any one group to be in control. Just have that interaction go on, and be revealing more and more. If the action moves out into the yard, then that's where the daughter is playing. And the bang can be heard banging from inside a shed they find.
    \n
    \nAlter the world to meet the evolving situation, such that you get the same effects. It's not railroading or illusionism (though illusion may be involved), because you haven't forced the characters to make any decisions. The player can still feel creative to an extent up to this point. And you get to do the reveal, and then that's when players get to go gonzo with their responses, and the remainder of how the scenario plays out is up to them.
    \n
    \n
    \nTo this extent, Stolze's advice is good, if taken from another perspective. As GM, simply keep the events occuring fast enough that the players are constantly being creative in their responses to the events in-character. Don't let them start thinking strategically or such. Keep the characters on their heels, so that the players aren't taking play away from the fun stuff, but reacting to it.
    \n
    \nSo, a player decides to have his prisoner character go down to deal with the hostages when they're deciding to break out, creating that cool sort of scene you mention. Heck, let's even propose that the sympathetic prisoner decides to help the hostages escape. Simply hit them with the next bit of information after making this decision. Allow the decision to be made to escape, but do not allow action to be taken relevant to the decision before you present a change in the situation that moves the focus of play back onto the stuff that's going to lead to the end of the scenario.
    \n
    \nIf the players are playing using narrativism as you suppose, instead of being all like, "I run from this freaky stuff!" more like, "Wow, that's some crazy stuff... I have to check it out..." You know, using the logic of folks in horror movies... :-)
    \n
    \nAnd if/when the do flee, then you can use my fence idea above, etc.
    \n
    \n
    \nNote that it's been quite a while since I've read the scenario text. I could be forgetting a lot about it. In fact I'm certain that I am. I recall, for instance, that one of the van occupants (hostages) is a reporter, but not who the other(s) are.
