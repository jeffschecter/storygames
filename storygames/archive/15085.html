    \n Sounds good to me!

    Another way to go is to present the players with your plan from the beginning. Say "Let's make the City of Theives", and make sure they're making characters with traits that are appropriate to that setting. Writing a list of traits is a good idea. There's a list of "recommended" traits in the game, and you could add to and subtract from that to get the flavour you're going for. Then you can make the map as normal, drawing on all the players' ideas about the City.

    Having a strong pre-decided aesthetic (like "City of Theives, in the Hyborian Age) is a really good thing for the game. The game resists too much more canon than that, though, so be prepared to have your idea of what the City of Theives is like challenged by the players' contributions.

    I hope the game goes well!

    \nYup. That's exactly how it works. Your ability determines what you might know. What you know determines what you can do.

    Items:

    Say the characters find some golden liquor in some vials. One of the players rolls lore to see if they know anything about it. If they succeed, they can tell you about it, and use a degree of success to remove an injury, if that's what they want. If they fail, we don't know anything about it. They can still drink it, if they like, which sounds dangerous to me. You say "The danger is that it's some kind of poison" or "The danger is that its effects overwhelm you", or whatever you like.

    The same is true of all other items. Generally speaking, there is no mechanical enforcement of magical abilities. A thing does what it can do. You don't need special mechanics to tell you that the Beast of Zotar can only be killed by a magical blade, just like you don't need special mechanics to tell you that water is wet. Magical properties of items, just like their nonmagical properties, tell you what is possible in the world.

    That said, go ahead and make up rules for magic items if you like. The game is not a delicate flower. It's pretty robust to making up new rules, doing things wrong, and playing things the way they make sense to you.

    Sorry I didn't see this sooner!

    Yup, that's some confusing writing!

    All the GM's characters, major or minor, have a single attribute that they roll in every conflict. For minor characters, this is usually a d6 or a d8. For major characters, it's usually a d10 or d12. Major characters, in addition to their normal attribute, get two abilities (at d10 and d6), which you can make up whenever you like, and use in any relevant conflict.

    Does that make sense to you?
