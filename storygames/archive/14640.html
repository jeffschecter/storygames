\n Two quick questions (Done without specifically looking for rules related but having read the whole of the rulebook).

1) Unopposed but difficult actions - how do we do them? Jumping across rooftops, cracking a safe, etc. Do they "just happen" or is there just a Verb + Push Dice roll vs a set number rule that I missed somewhere?

2) Hurt Dice from Unrelated Negative Adjectives - Do they apply no matter what? If I'm "Exposed" due to a hacker outing my private records to the Interface, does that Hurt me in a gunfight? If so, why? If not, why - the rules always say "your hurt dice".

Thanks!

    \n 1) They just happen. Hardboiled stories are about people vs. people, so I want to gloss over people vs. environment as fast as possible. Jumping across rooftops and cracking safes could be something you roll for if you can frame it as an adjective-asserting action against someone. For example, jumping over a rooftop might make your pursuers hesitant to follow you.

    2) Hurt dice represent pain and distraction. So the stress of having your records exposed could distract you during the gun fight. However, in the revision I'm working on, I've added an option to ignore Hurt dice from negative adjectives that plain don't apply (unless someone at the table can come up with a reason they should).

    \n So, on a related note, my players keep doing the perfectly intelligent thing and keep setting up defenses, or covering their tracks.

    Technically, there's no opposition, but their could be, later. I don't want to sit there while they roll to wipe their prints and hack the video logs and nail the doors shut, but letting them wall themselves in for free seems like the road to boredom.

    I feel like this is a gray area. Any ideas?

    \n While they're busy make sure they've covered their tracks, I'd concentrate on what confrontations they still have in front of them. Focus on what the antagonists on your plot map are doing and create some urgency there. Give them a clue a that leads into the greater mystery.

    Also, your players have to understand that they get stronger when they get hurt. That's how they get Push dice back, that's how they advance verbs. The player buy-in section is there for a reason. Watch movies like Brick and Miller's Crossing and Strange Days. Hardboiled heroes don't take too many precautions. They charge in and provoke their opposition. That's the kind of play the system is set up to reward.
