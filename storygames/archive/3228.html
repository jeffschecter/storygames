    \n Here's me:

    Grey Ranks is in final layout. Here's a sample page. A few tweaks and it is off to the printer.

    \n Great idea updating this, Jason.

    The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries, my no-prep, GMless storytelling game of pulp-inspired exploration is getting its text finalized and laid out this month, with pre-orders to follow shortly thereafter (fingers crossed). I'll be at Gen Con demoing and selling the game.

    In Frankenstein's Wake, one of the winner's of the BibliOdyssey RPG Design Challenge, is next on my development list. It's an RPG-board game hybrid that is no-prep, GMless, and meant to be played in a single sitting. Think competitive grave robbing, grubbing for funds, and pouring over esoteric books in a race to create your own blasphemous monster before your fellows. I'm looking for interested playtesters, and will be bringing a mock-up to Gen Con in hopes of getting a game in. So a strong alpha, but really only about 20-30% done, I'd guess.

    Utopolis: Policing the Retrofuture, my entry in the Reversed Engineer Design Challenge, is probably next in line. It's a police investigation game set in the future as imagined by the 1950s where police detectives are actually brainwiped criminals serving their sentence. Playtesting is up next, so there's a lot of development left to go.

    The Big House, a game of prison drama, has fallen to a lower level in the development stack. I've got a kernal of the mechanics and some throughts on structuring game play using scenes as currency inspired by Burning Empires. There's still a hell of a lot of work left, though, and several games likely to be completed first.

    Children of the Magi, my Game Chef 2007 entry, is a children's game (ages 5 to 8) set in ancient Persia where magi-in-training move about the emperor's palace helping others and learning what it takes to become one of the Magi. My daughter is much too young to play, so anyone with kids of the right age would be welcome to playtest, drop me an email or whisper.

    \n I had a playtest of Outside Men, my rock-bottom losing entry in Morningstar's undead-software-alaska-etcetera contest. It was one of those great playtests where we had a great time and completely didn't follow the rules, so I obviously have to distill what we did do into a new set of rules. Not everything is being thrown out, but most of it is, including the Yes-But cards. Sorry, Jason; they'll turn up somewhere. Interestingly the rules I'm coming up with have a lot in common with the stuff I tried to distill from the BSG-esque Fudge game I was in last year.

    I haven't quit on You Gotta Do What You Gotta Do, my also-not-winning entry from Morningstar's other contest. That's right, the owlbear one. And it, too, stepped out of playtest quite clearly wanting to be a completely different game from what I thought it was. I have lots of ideas for how it's gonna come together but the system just won't gel. James Ernest wrote an essay about game mechanics vs. their themes, and wrote that he always makes himself come up with themes first and builds systems to fit because the other way is too difficult for him. I think I work the other way. I know exactly what at-the-table experience I want to have happen but can't sort how to get there. The only reason it's going okay for Outside Men is because that experience is a little more conventional.

    I really like how Shreyas and J-Walt are doing their process with Secret Wars. I'm scheming. This is my scheming face.

    \n I'm most actively working on Bone White, Blood Red, my Game Chef '07 submission about the Pueblo Revolt of 1680. It's a game which uses leather cords threaded with beads of various colors to represent characters. Recent changes to the rules (from the GC version) include replacing the dice mechanics with a bead pull, better explaining the system of narration, and the use of a designated Story Cord to represent in-story opposition or challenges. I've had one playtest, which went great, and I'm looking for more locally. I need to work on writing up the rules revisions as well as creating material for teaching how to play the game.

    Wandering Monsters High School was my 24-hour game that I wrote in 2005; it's set in a generic, D&D-style fantasy world...but in high school. I haven't done much on this lately; the game needs rewriting and updating based on not only having more time to work on it, but also what I've learned about game design lately. Another delay has been that my artist for the project had her house burn down, and I haven't been big on bothering her to finish my commissions yet. I probably should kick that into gear. She did some great roughs of character designs for me last year. WMHS needs some more playtesting.

    The next game I'm going to write is called Descanso Highway and it's a ghost story game in which you try to decipher why you died tragically on the road, based on what people have left behind on your roadside memorial, using scrapbooking as the game mechanics. Your "character sheet" is an ever-evolving sheet of scrapbooking paper that is passed around as other players add to it and ask you questions that lead up to "why did you die?" -- the answer to which frees your soul from this world and allows you to continue on the next. It's not quite a roleplaying game but more of a storytelling exercise with props. I've played it once.

    \n Oh, thanks for starting this thread, Jason. Not only are we seeing notes on a lot of cool games, but it gives me an opportunity to announce officially:
    \n
    \nOther Worlds
    \n
    \nMark Humphreys, Scott Mathis and Mike Holmes, after working on the Questworlds project (until it's subsequent reassignment to the
    \neminent Robin Laws), have teamed together to create a new role-playing game called Other Worlds.
    \n
    \nOther Worlds is very much based on the lessons we learned as a result of that earlier project. As such, it will undoubtedly have some similarities to the base Heroquest system. But it will also be significantly different, reflecting our vision of the sort of game that we always hoped HQ would support (but which it didn't always provide, at least without some modification, and a lot of technique in play). Not a simple imitation of Heroquest, then, but a departure in a specific direction.
    \n
    \nOther Worlds will be released as a core rulebook of around 100,000 words. Most of that material will be guidance; guidance on playing the
    \ngame, guidance on running the game, and guidance on adapting the game to any genre or setting you see fit. We will also be including 'genre
    \nsnapshots' for a wide variety of different genres - Fantasy, Science Fiction, Wild West, Pirates, Horror, Superheroes - to enable you to
    \nstart playing more or less straight away. The game will be released with some sort of open license, and will be followed up almost
    \nimmediately with a few more detailed books on how to play with the rules in specific genres, and even specific settings meant for play
    \nwith the rules. Others will, of course, be able to create their own products based off the core rules as they wish as long as they abide
    \nby the details of the specific license.
    \n
    \nWhen will Other Worlds be released? Given that we already have a very strong direction, we're hoping it won't take too long for us to take
    \nwhat we already have, and fashion the text to what we want it to be. We aren't making any promises, but it shouldn't take too long for us
    \nto get to an advanced state. That said, we're committed to thoroughly playtesting the game (both internally and independently), and it
    \nwon't see the light of day until such time as that has happened - however long that takes. Our priority is to get this done right as
    \nopposed to getting it done quickly.
    \n
    \nFinally, we'd like to say a big thank you to everyone who contacted us with messages of support and encouragement. While we are
    \ndisappointed that we won't be able to continue working on the Questworlds project, we are looking forward to creating our own game
    \nfor publication. We have spent the last few months re-examining our design goals and making sure that we have something new and
    \ninteresting to bring to the table. The response from all those who have seen our playtest manuscript has been an unqualified 'yes'. From
    \nthat perspective, we thank Moon Design Publishing for pushing us on to create the game that we really wanted to make. This is a real
    \nlabor of love for us and we hope it shows.
    \n
    \nIf you'd like to discuss this project with us, please post about it here: http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=3247

      \n I'm still very early in writing a first draft of Pentripolis. You can see it develop right there on GoogleDocs, if you want. The idea behind it is simple: take some of the aspects of traditional gaming that are fun but underrepresented in indie games and boost them up. It's meant to look, on the outside, much like old-school games. There'll be a GM, secret knowledge, a detailed setting, dozens of NPCs, lots of different tools and abilities to mess with, and so on. But the inside is quite a different beast.

      For example, the resolution looks much like task resolution: use a skill and get an effect. But the only available effects are those that impact other characters (or create/influence tools for this purpose), and the way this works is the same whether you're using your sword or your sexuality. So all of the rolls are directly related to conflicts (but there's no independent stake setting; the scope of rolls is given by the skills), and social skills finally have a great deal of value.

      Also, every character has secrets that can be discovered and used as tools, multiple openings to connect other characters to (relationship mapping happens in play), and so on. Basically, virtually everything in the game will be about connecting characters and getting them into and through conflicts.

      Characters are based on passions like fear, rage, faith, lust, and so on. And you can tap into those for a bonus to something, but also exposing vulnerability to others (use your anger, Luke! (and thereby become vulnerable to corruption)). Character development doesn't depend on killing things or overcoming obstacles but on playing through Bangs.

      Basically, this is the game I've always hoped I could discover somewhere. I'm just going to have to write it myself, and it'll take a long ass time to do that.

      \nWhat am I working on?

      Verge is a post-cyberpunk role-playing game with a conspiracy-story edge.

      Players invent the setting during play by drawing a network of interconnected ideas, organizations, and people on a big piece of paper. Then they take the role of a character in that setting and struggle for control of the setting elements to accomplish their goals. To control a setting element, they mark it with dice rolled from a pool. The result looks something like RISK but with dice on a relationship map instead of armies on a geographical map. This process tends to create stories about the smashing together of greed and other human frailties, ideology, and technology.

      How close to done is it?

      I have rewritten this thing about six times in the last 2+ years. The rules went through three major changes, where the result was barely recognizable from the last revision. I think I'm close to the end now.

      The first draft of the first four chapters is done. I need to rewrite two more large chapters. Will probably do that this week or next. It'll be ready for playtest then.

      I have cover art but not a cover. I don't have a back cover. I don't have layout.

      What can you do to help?

      Mostly right now I need reader/critiquers and playtesters. Gimme a week or two and I'll have a ready document for you, with some specific questions I'd like a reader or playtest group to answer. I'll be doing some playtesting myself, too, of course.

      Once I feel like the game is in an okay state, I need to find someone to do layout for it. I need someone to take my cover art and add titles and turn it into a cover / back cover combination. I'd like to arrange an editing trade with someone else (you edit mine and I'll edit yours).

      \n since my dear friend JoePub keeps outting me in my joke thread about RPG Mashups, i guess it's high time i wrote a little thingy here describing what's what.
      disclaimer: i wrote this all out before and instead of posting it, my friend stephanie's computer just ate it. And it's 1.30 am, and i had four hours sleep last night and my feet smell hella bad (oops went to a different town didn't bring socks.)
      onwards:

      wha am i workin on? top three:

      1. ready for alpha playtest, just waiting there patiently on the shelf is Dungeon Master Showdown, a narrative control stealing game for about five people. one of the participants writes up a character (which is to say makes up some abilities and disabilities, a name and a description; little else) and the other participants (called Dungeon Masters) prepare a person, a place and an item to later try and work into the narrative.
      the game goes like this: the player (character-running participant) starts off the first scene, describes what's going on. then a dungeon master takes over. the other dungeon masters add in little bits of colour and detail, and if the player likes these things, then that DM is able to draw tokens (or rather, ge to keep the tokens they attempted to draw). if the player doesn't like, then bad things happen.
      after a while, one of the DMs will have enough tokens to steal narrative control of the game, and takes over as the new facilitator of the character's story. the player, too, has a method of drawing tokens which is not always successful. if the player dislikes something that a DM is putting forward, then the player can spend a token to have each DM (including the active one) present an alternate version of events. play continues with the DM whose version the player chose facilitating.

      DMS is pretty much a mashup of Cheap, a game of Joe's which he ran for me in Vancouver, and Dungeons and Dragons. of course i would never actually SAY THAT, for legal reasons. i guess it's a heart breaker, but whose heart is being broken i'm not sure.

      that's number one.

      number two i'm not taking the sheet off of. it's one of my babies, one of the two game models which i've been working on the longest, and the fourth project (which i also won't be discussing) derives from it. this i can tell you. it seats three. it's a game about people facing internal crisis. it involves active scene framing, is "GMless", and has a greater than 1:1 character:player ratio. i'm hella stoked.

      number three: it's an rpg companion to joe's (potentially collectible) card game Models Of God (or is it Models For God? fuck. too tired to search out the truth. i'm pretty sure it's "For" actually.) let's see. that's joe's game about pubescent biker gangs in a dystopic future who are confused and angry and brimming with poor communication skills and homoeroticism. i'm not sure how faithful to the material i'll be, however. i do know that it will take place in a future which is under threat of whatever-comes-after-nuclear destruction, or perhaps that destruction has already happened and we don't know it yet. "whoa, trippy". there'll be radiation-infected folks which will kind of behave like zombies; focused-applicability A.I.s, nanobots, and miles and miles and miles of partially broken-down national interstate highway system, spanning a continent which speaks a derived japanese, a country called Ameruka.
      though it could be played for laughs, it's not intended that way. this is a game about fucked up interpersonal and group dynamics. it's about not having had role-models so you don't know what you want or how to get by or what to fucking do with yourself. it's about having a gang of friends that you have to trust because you have no one else to turn to, but you mess them around and they mess you around and not all of you are going to come out of this ok, or even at all.
      this game is called Morning of the Sun.
      how ready is it? well, you've now read more than half my notes. the system is similar in its details to number two, inasmuch as it seats three, "gmless", operates with a similar but operationally different microcosm principle... greater than 1:1 C:P ratio. damn i love those.

      i kind of playtested a stripped-down version the other day, it went well.
      as a side note, i took a step back from my Sentai fighter game (forgive me, Jake and Matt and japan, if i spelled that wrong) Lucky Fighter Heartbreak and realized it's pretty much angling for the same things as Morning of the Sun, so i'm subsuming it into that project. there'll also be notes to play MOTS as a DnD-exclusively-dealing-with-the-dynamics-of-the-party-while-they're-traveling sorta deal. "camp time".
      i will be seeking out help to playtest this stuff before too, too long.

      this is a big step for me. i usually don't tell people about what i'm doing. usually telling people means i lose interest in the project. hopefully that won't happen in this case (just watch, i'll put down all my notes and begin working on #4.)

      um, yeah. i'm going to sleep.
