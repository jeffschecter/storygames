\n Over on The Forge, mjbauer asked what roleplaying game theory he should read before designing a game. The consensus seems to be: don't start with theory.

With that in mind, what's the point of theory? If it can't make us design better, and can't make us play better, why bother? The theory we've got seems remarkably impractical.

Despite the glib tone of this initial post, I'd really welcome thoughtful and considered answers. I say this to forestall one-line rebuttals.

Graham

    \n "Hitherto, the philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways. The point, however, is to change it."

    Theory allows us to step back and take a different look at things (I don't say objective because that's a liberal fallacy). I think that it's a bit glib in itself to say "don't read any theory", because really that just means "don't read somebody's formalised viewpoint." In fact theory and practice interact at all times.

    But revolutionary theory only comes to its fruition through revolutionary praxis. The proof is in the pudding.

    \n Reading, thinking, and talking about theory is great and definitely helps a lot with both game design and play. What's terrible, especially for new designers, is to have somebody point you towards a pile of theory discussions, posts, and articles and say "Read that and then design a game." Theory should be something you participate in, not something you consume. So the best way to get started in theory is to contribute something, starting a conversation or joining one in progress.

    Like: "So this other day I was playing this game and the GM did something and it really made me feel shitty and, now that I think about it, that kind of thing always makes me feel shitty, so I was wondering what other people thought about it? In my mind, I think it's because of XYZ and maybe, next time we play, I should recommend that the GM maybe try to do more EFG instead, because that really works for me. Has anyone else had issues with this? What are solutions that work for you?"

    That how to get started with theory.

    \n Theory is an explanation based upon observation. The theory makes more sense once you've got some experiences from which to draw your own observations. You've got to be able to recognize where you are in the theory's ideas to be able to see what the theory offers.

    When is best to start reading game design theory? When you want to push your game designs (I recommend at least 2, so that you can come to recognize commonalities between them) in new directions or refine them and are unsure how to do it. It starts, not with a need for the knowledge, but with a desire for the knowledge.

    Theory does more than just pass on the lessons others have learned while designing their own games, it inspires people to explore new possibilities, even beyond those in the theories. It is those that deny theory's usefulness that are most often trapped in habits they don't even recognize.

    \n Graham,

    I guess that would depend on what "theory" is. Which I'm not trying to say as a one-line rebuttal, but to offer why, I'll share an anecdote:

    After Dreamation, Ralph Mazza & I were talking about Master Plan, and I said "I don't do a theory show." He called bullshit on that, and after a long discussion we came to the idea (paraphrased & possible twisted thanks to memory) that one person talking about practical application & experience is theory to another. When I talk about, say, the tangible nature of mechanics in role-playing games (as people who know me & talk with me often know I'm prone to discussing, as it's a favorite subject of mine), I'll talk about this experience or that experience and how it's lead me to think about mechanics in a different way.

    To me, that's practice. To others, that's theory -- the moment I move away from a story of experience to what I extrapolated from them it turns into theory. Theory comes from practice distilled into discussion, at least in this sort of touchy-feely realm.

    Now, that might not pass a bullshit test to some. I don't know, and I suppose ultimately I don't care -- I'm all for the thought of the individual. I don't really like thinking of myself as a "theory guy," because I used to take the stance that there was no such thing as actual RPG theory. However, at this point in time that notion scans as lazy thinking for me. But I digress. (And for a momentary further digression, I only buy theory that's backed by evidence of practice, and then only enough to try it for myself. Touchy-feely indeed.)

    That said, I think this can be applied to Actual Play -- at least when AP goes from glorifying & bitching about a game to sharing with others what can be learned or extrapolated from a given session. That's also theory. More importantly, it might be "play theory" rather than "design theory." The former only seems to get love when we form it in the latter, or as "practice" rather than "theory." I don't know if that's a useful distinction or not, but I suppose that's for the individual to consider. (In fact, Graham, I would say that Play Unsafe is play theory.)

    ...All of which is a long-winded way of saying: "The point of theory in the RPG realm is possibly to communicate core ideas without the baggage of constantly explaining how you arrived to them or the practice behind them every single time." Theory certainly isn't a substitute at all (not even a poor one in my mind) for practice. In fact, I would even go as far as saying that theory requires context to full appreciate (not "understand," but "appreciate") and that until one has built up enough practice to provide that context, theory scans pretty much as bullshit. It can help us become better designers or players, but not right out of the gate. Theory is the upper-division of gaming, and it has nebulous, undefinable prerequisites, and sometimes it makes sense to skip ahead without all the prereqs.

    That said, knowing theory doesn't make you a cooler, better, smarter, or whatever person. It just means you've obsessed over this subject to a point far beyond what most people would consider casual, for good and ill. (Bolded for emphasis, and yet I still think this is understated.)

    (And yes, I'm probably talking a bit of half-assed communication theory here.)

    - Ryan

    \n My design process is something like this:

    1. Some weird inspiration hits me, unformed.
    2. I start playing with ideas. I design game stuff.
    3. I mentally touch back to some theory. More on this later.
    4. I write a game.
    5. I playtest the game. It inevitably breaks.
    6. I analyze why the game broke using theory.
    7. I redesign the game and repeat the playtest, analyze, redesign cycle until it's good enough.

    Design is not divorced from play. Game design requires playtesting or else it's just game writing. The Big Model (or the body of it that I have adopted and changed in my own mind) is a theory of play and is well-suited for improving game designs during the playtest phase.

    Now, in step 3, before I've played the thing, I am still touching on theory. I am imagining how people will play my game and making predictions using the theory of play to determine what might happen.

    There's also a part of the theory that is more descriptive than predictive. For example, TBM has the five parts of Exploration. When I design, I consciously think about character, setting, situation, color, and system and how they interact. I think about points of contact. I think a lot about IIEE. I think about stuff from other models, too, like sockets.

    Just having names for parts of a game help me design, because it's a mental checklist of stuff to think about. I have a mental vocabulary to slice up the cow into different cuts rather than simply calling it all "meat" (or "murder," if you prefer).

    \n Yeah...totally. I'll go even further than Matt.
    \n
    \nPlaying Games /= Thinking hard about games and play. If it did, theory never would exist in the first place.
    \n
    \nI think the number of people who think hard about games and what it is they're doing and why is such a tiny tiny minority of people who play that the very concept that Playing Games could be equated with thinking hard about them seems utterly absurd to me. That play is important to theory is not in dispute. That play is the same thing as theory is nonsense.
    \n
    \nPeople who play games and who want to think hard about them, generally do not discuss their hard thinking at the table while they're playing. They do so after they've played.
    \n
    \nSharing the experiences of your play and what worked and what didn't and what could make it better next time with people who were not at the table with you = theorizing. More accurately it = hypothesising which then turns into theory when those ideas are tried in future games and found to work / not work / sort of work.
    \n
    \nPlay -- leads to talking about play -- leads to thinking about ways to make play better -- leads to trying those ways out in play -- leads to evaluating the effectiveness of those ways -- leads to codifying the ways that reliably worked -- leads to sharing the resultant codification -- leads to better play.
    \n
    \nThis is the cycle of theory. A cycle in which play is featured prominently...but is not, in and of itself, sufficient.
    \n
    \nOne category of ways to make play better is "play with better rules" -- which requires someone writing better rules -- which requires someone to have thought about those ways to play better so they can be included in their rules. This is why theory is important to design.

    Posted By: Paul BPlaying games = thinking hard about games and play.

    Reading and participating in theory = maybe other stuff going on as well: earning points, winning arguments, ruminating on what-ifs that don't actually come up between real people in a real game, misusing theory to lay blame in a bad game, etc. etc.

    \nIt seems like nearly everything you have added to the second category can also occur in play. Theory does not magically produce negative social interaction, any more than playing games magically avoids it. People 'earn points' and try to win arguments and blame each other for things not working in play all the time.

    On the other hand it is entirely possible to play a game without thinking hard about it, or even thinking about it at all. (This is only marginally more difficult to do when discussing theory.)

    \n So, if I'm designing a game, and you have some helpful experiences and observations to share with me, then maybe:
    1) our shared understanding of terms like IIEE and Gamism is useful, allowing us to nod and continue discussing
    2) our ignorance of theory requires us to spend longer talking about when to consult mechanics and how some games are more challenging than others, stretching out our conversation and taking us longer to get to specific points
    3) we argue about what IIEE and Gamism really mean and never talk about design or play at all

    So, I'd guess (1) is theory at its best and (3) is theory at its worst, with respect to design.

    \n...Except that you have to do some (3) before you can really do (1), and you may have to repeat (3) when new people enter the discussion. In the end I think it pays off, but I think the payoff (at least at the community scale, individuals may progress faster) occurs years further down the line than most people expect. For example, the theory developed at the Forge resulted in a lot of repeated explanations of what they meant. This went on for several years and only a few games got made. Now, at Story Games, we see a lot more games made. It's not the switch from the Forge to Story Games that did it, though I suspect it was a nice symbolic trigger, it's the time everyone invested in doing (3) now producing a payoff in our ability to do (1). Even our ability to do (3) has improved, thanks to practice, bringing new people up to speed faster.

    So, regarding the original post's question of when to read theory, the counter-position to my suggestion of "after making 2 or more games" is more-or-less, "after trying something (even once) and wanting it to be better." There also seems to be a few that dislike theory altogether, which might count as a "never" votes. Are there other positions?
