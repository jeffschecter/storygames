So, another side-track off from "Intentions in Task-Based Resolution".

So I have a beef with intent-based resolution. First, I'll outline my interpretation of the problem of taking into account intentions. In any resolution system, there is a potential problem if the player doesn't know what steps need to be done to get what they as a player are after.

Here's an example. Suppose the players know that someone was shot in a Dogs in the Vineyard game. They want to catch the killer. However, they can't simply resolve finding the killer in a single conflict -- and in that case they don't have a definite number of steps to get what they want.

So the intent solution is that the player says what they're trying to eventually accomplish, and the GM guides them towards what steps they need to get there. As Brand put in a late post on the thread:

Brand_Robins wrote:
The big thing here is that because the Player isn't letting the GM know what his end goal is the GM can't help steer him at it until the end -- assuming the GM would want to steer the player. The player never knows which roll will end up with him at the sewers, or near the sewers, or if he can even get to the sewers. So pretty much he keeps trying different things until he finds one that will work. Task by task, no clear intent.
While this works, it's not ideal to me. The problem is that before the example even starts, we have player ignorance. The GM knows what the PC would need to do to accomplish his goal, but the player doesn't. Both as GM and as player, I'd prefer to give the player enough information to accomplish things before resolution begins. Then I don't have to steer the players, they can steer themselves.

As a small-scale example of this, if I'm going to play out a combat in detail, I will generally lay out a diagram of the area and describe it. Now, I could instead have my own vision and tell players what they would need to do to, say, surround an enemy. But I find it is more interesting if the players can come up with plans that I wouldn't have thought of... as opposed to me steering them to a plan based on what they tell me they want.

A particular pet peeve of mine is times when hiding intent is effective. In my experience, when informed of the player's intent, the GM often makes it hard for them to get what they want -- in order to be challenging. This leads to the intent paradox that the players can accomplish some things more easily if they lie (or at least are deceptive) about what they're trying to do. In traditional games, this would often involve being deceptive about the PCs' plan. In some recent resolution systems, this moves onto a meta-level where it can be easier to effect something if it isn't the stakes.

    Interesting stuff. I've been thinking a lot about frustrated player intentions and tasks, as you might have noticed, and those thoughts underlie a lot of my design approach. I've seen all of those things you talk about, including players with hidden agendas, and to me, that's counter-productive because it undermines open communication.

    The foundation of the traditional way of figuring out appropriate tasks is the idea that the GM is the decider of what works and what doesn't. Coincidentally, this is one of those foundations that Beast Hunters blows out of the water. In BH, because the "GM" has to offer a minimum advantage point gain for every action of the "player," any action works to at least some degree (unless the player decides to reject the offer). The GM's offer reflects how well the GM believes it would work, but if the player disagrees, she can always resort to a roll of the dice. In other words, it's no longer up to the GM to decide which actions fulfill the player's intent, it's up to the player. At the same time, the GM retains a guiding but not deciding role. And what you said here:

    I find it is more interesting if the players can come up with plans that I wouldn't have thought of... as opposed to me steering them to a plan based on what they tell me they want.

    That's exactly what my design is for.

    In other words, you can achieve a more open-communication style in traditional games the way you describe, by laying out how things work and providing all the necessary information. Or you can fundamentally change the distribution of authority through appropriate game design. And then you leave less room for those kinds of frustrations.

    Does it open the door for other kinds of frustrations? Possibly. Time will tell, I assume

    Adam,

    I think it has to do with the stages at which intent is parsed, and the fact that intent done at a scene or conflict level can still break down at a plot level. That is to say, many games with intent based resolution have perfectly valid and servicible ways to get what you want out of this conflict right here right now, but can then become vauge on how to get from that conflict to an end goal.

    Example: If you're in a task-based non-intent game and you want to find the sewers you have to go through the process described in the thread John linked at the begining to get there. You never know exactly what you're doing or if it will get you there or farther away or what. OTOH, in say... Burning Wheel, where intent clearly matters, if you want to get to the sewers you'll frame it up and have a roll or series to get to the sewers. Boom, done.

    But what if getting to the sewers was part of bringing down the king, which is one of your beliefs? Is that going to be done in one roll or series of rolls? I suppose you could, but most GMs and groups won't want to. Instead you'll need to get to the sewers then get the chancelor to help you, then get past the captain of the guard, etc. At that point even though you have a clear intent in each conflict to guide you the chain of conflicts can get into the same problem task resolution had -- you don't know if each conflict is getting you where you want to go in the larger scheme of things.

    Burning Empires game structure, otoh, fixes this. Want to permanently defeate the Worm's threat? Well you know exactly what you have to do and exactly when you have to do it because the whole game is broken up into stages that guide the whole plot into a plot level intent based conflict resolution.

    Most games, however, don't do that and I don't think it is always the preferable solution. (Though it rocks out in BE)
