    \n Hmmm. Sorry, can't help, but perhaps I can give direction? (I'm interested in this, too):

    I thought of an avenue that may help, though it's kinda sketchy. Some of the best GMs that I learned under were great at:
    * Making you feel like your choices mattered.
    * Were great at explaining the rules/setting.
    * Were particularly good about making you feel awesome, or that the things that you put forth were awesome.
    * Were really organized.
    ...a lot of this stuff is common with public spakers, motivational speakers and the like.

    So maybe if there's books/youtube talks out there on how to do public speaking, perhaps especially focused on motivational speaking, that might have some pointers.

    \n Andy, your list is super helpful! Point three resonates with what I was going to share:

    Michael Rabiger's book, Directing, has an interesting section on working with actors. (It's probably too short to make buying the book worthwhile for this alone, however.) I've skimmed this and what stood out for me is the commentary on the role of the director being to remove emotional blocks for the actors, who are in quite a vulnerable position (raw displays of emotion which are being silently judged by numerous others, for example).

    Another resource I've really enjoyed is the Greenlight Improv Wiki, especially its bit on various 'unfunny' habits (joke-telling, defending, pre-planning) are all ways that we try to hide ourselves from others.

    I confess the idea of showmanship raises a red flag for me, mostly because it seems (at first blush) to run counter to my own goals of transparency and creative freedom. (I mean, putting on an entertaining act means the others aren't seing me, and it runs the risk of sending the message that's what I want them to do also.) Can you explain what you have in mind?
