    \n My semi-informed opinion:

    Console Questions:

    1. The PS3's games are not region-specific. However, you may run into video issues. I believe that NA is on the NTSC standard while Europe is PAL, correct? I don't believe this would be true for HD - (SD: 480i versus 576i, HD:720). I think all PS3 titles would support 720p, so if you have an HD set you should be, uhm...set.

    The Xbox 360 is region-encoded/locked, so I don't think games purchased in Europe would work.


    2. The answer to this question depends on what you're using the machine for. If you're simply playing games that you buy on-disc - i.e. no video streaming/playing, no demos downloaded, no PSN or Xbox Live games, than get that cheapest one that comes with any sort of HD. With the Xbox 360, the Elite used to matter with regards to HDMI output, but the newest "basic" versions have HDMI-out.

    If you plan on getting demos, or downloadable content, then the hard-drive size matters. How much depends on you. I know there's hacky ways of getting an external hard drive hooked up to these boxes, and it's a much cheaper alternative.

    If you're planning on playing PS2 games on your PS3, and you want best backwards compatibility, then the version you choose very much matters (see below).


    3. Depends. Memory is hard-drive space above, with the caveats listed. Extra controllers depends on how much you play multiplayer games. There's other options (wireless 'net adapter, wireless controllers), each of which comes down to "how much does this matter to you?"


    PS3 specific:

    1. This gets ugly.

    Check out the "Retail Configurations" chart here:

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PlayStation_3

    If PS2 compatibility *really* matters to you, you'll need one of the models that lists "Hardware" under "PS2 compatibility" - the 20GB NTSC, or the 60GB NTSC. These might be hard to find in stores. These boxes have the literal hardware from a PS2 in them.

    The others either have none (the 40/80 PALs) or software emulation (60GB PAL, 80GB NTSC). I've heard mixed reviews on the software emulation support. Some games simply don't work or look incredibly ugly. The good news is that this *might* be improved in future firmware releases.

    \n The red ring of death is a spinning pattern of 3 red lights that appear around the 360's power button when something has gone tragically wrong. Sometimes (sometimes) you just need to power your console down and start it up again. Other times, it means that your box is bricked, and you need to send it to Microsoft for repairs/replacement.

    When the 360 first launched, there were significant numbers of these that happened for seemingly no reason. Over time, with various updates and hardware revisions, they're significantly less frequent. A new 360 purchased these days is going to be fine. I can think of 2 main causes of the red ring outside of obvious things like spilling liquids all over your machine or kicking it down the stairs, etc.

    1) A power surge has a chance of bricking a 360. I've seen brownouts brick 1/3 of the 360 dev kits in a developer studio.
    2) Old age. Something just goes wrong and your xbox has an aneurysm.

    \n Just to clarify a few things:

    360 games are sometimes region locked and sometimes not and sometimes partially locked. I live in the UK and have a UK 360 (first UK console I've owned in years...), and it seems about half the games which come out in the US are region locked and half aren't. Electronic Arts lock all their games, for example. The 360 also plays SOME original XBox games, but the list is quite small and all XBox games were region locked, so you'd only be able to play US XBox games on a US 360.

    All PS3 games are region free. That's it. You can play any PS3 game on any PS3. I have a Hong Kong PS3 and it plays UK games perfectly. I've also set it up to buy from the UK online shop and to display all in English etc etc. But just because the PS3 games all work and are region free, that's not the end of the story. If you get one of the premium (in the US that's 80Gb) consoles, it'll play PS2 games (and PS1 games IIRC) but is region locked. So you'll only get to play US region PS2/1 games on a US PS3. The 80Gb console has software emulation, the 60Gb console had PS2 hardware inside,2 but is no longer available. By all accounts, there's not really much in it in terms of play experience. You will need a PS2 memory card and an adaptor in order to save PS2 games on a PS3 though. In there wisdom, Sony decided that you wouldn't want to save PS2 data onto the internal hard drive. I honestly think it'd be cheaper to buy a PS2 that get all the upgrades to the PS3 to play PS2 games.

    The other thing about the PS3 is BluRay. BluRay is region locked much the same as DVD. The US and Far East is region A, Europe is region B, Asia and the Middle East is region C. So if you buy a US PS3, you're only able to play region A BluRay disks. A lot of disk for sale here in the UK Are multiregion (ABC), but US ones tend not to be. I don't really care about the multiregion UK disks, because they're still 150% or more of the price of importing the same title from the US, and since HK is region A, all US disks work on my PS3. Of course, if you don't have an HDTV, you probably don't care about BluRay right now.

    Both consoles also play DVDs, but are very much region locked. So a US PS3 are 360 will only play region 1 DVDs.

    Both use wireless controllers. The 360 uses 2 AA batteries, the PS3 has it's own rechargeable, non-replaceable battery. The PS3 controller charges from a mini USB port on the top, but only if the PS3 is switched on. This is (IMHO) a ridiculous design flaw, but someone obviously thought it was a good idea. I mean, if I've got the thing on, I need to be using the controller, right? But I can't, because I have to have in plugged in to charge if the battery is flat... I'd say that for this reason a second controller is essential if you buy a PS3, so that one can charge while the other is in use.

    Voltage wise, you can plug a PS3 in anywhere in the world as long as you have the right cable. It's a standard 3 pin D shaped connector like you use to plug in most computers on monitors. The internal PSU can run on anything from 110v - 240v. I've got my HK PS3 plugged straight into the wall here, and it's fine.

    If you get a 360, you'll need a hard drive to make use of the online features. New 360s will start coming with 60Gb drives as standard soon, if you're going to get one, wait for that. If you don't get one with it, get one.

    Headsets are pretty much essential for online play. The 360 uses semi-special ones and the decent pack come with a wired one included. The PS3 doesn't come with one, but you can use any* bluetooth headset with it.

    The 360 Elite has a 120 Gb hard drive and HDMI port on the back. Also, it's black instead of crap grey. Thing is though, that 120Gb is nice and all, but even the cheap 360 bundles now come with HDMI ports on them. So really, I don't see the point since the new "standard" drive is going to be 60 Gb. Unless you really really want one in black.

    360s are notoriously unreliable. This is the only reason I was willing to pay the extra and get the UK model this time around, to get the 3 year warranty. I'd suggest that importing one might be ill advised. I don't know anyone with a 360 who actually uses it regularly and has had their more that a year without it breaking. I know a guy who's still using his launch console, but he only uses it once a month if that and considers 2 hours gaming a marathon session.

    Wireless: the 360 doesn't come with built in wireless, you need to get an add on or connect it wired. Some PS3s come with wireless, one which don't can't be upgraded, but you could always run it wired off a wireless bridge.

    I hope that helps.

    * "Any", as in some don't work, but they don't need to be special PS3 ones,

    \n Okay, I sell these systems for a living, so let me kick in with my two cents on a bunch of the issues being discussed here.

    First, to answer your initial questions...

    The 360 is region-locked. If you buy a region one box it'll only play region one games, or any number of third-party region-free titles. None of the systems are HD-players. That was a separate attachment built for the system that is no longer available. You shouldn't have much of any problems with the playback on your television, as has been previously stated, though the voltage could be an issue. What country do you live in, and what's the standard voltage there?

    The PS3 has no region lock to it. You'll be able to play any game from any region with no problem. Voltage will still be an issue, so you should research that for sure. Same as above, playback shouldn't be hampered by your television as long as it has appropriate international playback support.

    \n Alright, let's now move on to the questions that came out of the answers.

    The Red Ring of Death
    Is not actually a ring, because it only fills three of the four status lights that wrap around the power button of the 360. What that signals is a serious hardware malfunction. Normally, the RRoD is a result of a design flaw that was made in the layout and construction of the motherboard. The processor chip was located on the motherboard in an unusual place to accommodate all of the wild shit they packed into the box, and had a cooling fan plugged on top of it. The problem with this is that the brackets used to fasten that cooling fan on were a bit "odd". Here, "odd" reads as "stupid".

    Imagine that the cooling fan is sitting right atop the chip, just like it would with most computers. Now, imagine that the fastener rods that hold said fan in place reach through the motherboard itself and latch in on the other side of the motherboard and chip. They latch together, forming an "X" shape across the back of the chip.

    The problem with this configuration comes up when the system runs hot. And the 360 often runs hot. You know, like any fucking electronic equipment will tend to do. The motherboard bows a little, just like any other motherboard would do. Just a fraction of a centimeter. But that's enough. The cross-shaped latches for the cooling fan then apply pressure to the back of the chipset. Over time, this pressure pushes away at the back of the chipset and its seating. Until finally, boom, the soder holding the seating in place starts to come undone and the system's processing chip just sits there, stupid and disconnected.

    Microsoft's fix for this? A reconfigured motherboard. Known amongst fans as the "64-nanometer chip" for the new size of the processor chip that was installed in the new config, this setup moves the placement of the chip itself, uses a different cooling fan entirely, and replaces the chip with a smaller one. This greatly reduces the problem that leads to the RRoD. The 64-chip was initially released only in the halo 3 xb360 bundle, though rumour is that it is now available in any box that has an HDMI port in the back of it.

    \nPS3 Backwards Compatability
    ...is a joke.

    When the system was first put on the market, the PS3 was available in two SKUs. The smaller 20-gig and the larger 60-gig. Originally available for 500 and 600 USD respectively, both were hardware-driven backwards compatible. This amounts to parts taken from the "slim" ps2 box and integrated into the ps3. These boxes had very reliable BC that played the vast majority of ps2 games, and a good eighty percent of psone games as well.

    Then the boxes just didn't fucking sell. Partly due to a terrible fucking ad campaign, but mostly because of sticker shock. People couldn't justify spending USD500 for a "tard box", and USD600 was just un-fucking-believable. Match that with a very poor initial release window, and a good eight months of crappy games to follow, and no one on the planet wanted a ps3.

    In what many industry pundits can only call pure arrogance Ken Kutaragi, lead of the Playstation brand at Sony, believed that people the world over would buy "his" system because it was Playstation and that meant you had to buy it. There were no other options. Even if that meant spending upwards of USD700 just to get the system, a game, and an extra controller. This led to the worst system launch that the industry had seen since the sad, lamented Dreamcast.

    Ken Kutaragi was asked to resign his position. new guard replacing Kutaragi came up with the idea to lower the price point on the system. Now, because this was only one year into the cycle of the ps3, any price drops had to come out of lowered hardware costs. Out comes the hardware-driven backwards compatibility. In go different, cheaper hard drives. And in goes a software emulator, only available for a limited time on the new 80-gig SKU of the ps3.

    The software-drive 80-gig plays about 75% of ps2 titles, with a markedly smaller 55-60% of ps1 games. The software is being updated irregularly to open up more backwards compatibility, but there are signs that those updates will stop coming pretty soon. The largest of those signs is the newest SKU that Sony is introducing come the Fall.

    Sony will be eliminating all previous SKUs from the market and trotting out their new 80-gig model. Available for USD400, this system is a nice set-up. Including a DualShock3 controller, it's actually not a bad price point. The only problem is, the system will not be backwards compatible. Ever. No hardware, no software.

    Dust off your ps2s, people.

    Here's where I wrap things up with my own opinion...

    I have all three systems. PS3, Xbox 360, and Wii. Used to be that the Wii would only get turned on when friends were over and there was drinking. Now I own Rock Band, so there's no need for the Wii.

    Between the 360 and the PS3, I play the 360 more often. Much more often. Microsoft has more exclusive content that I like than the PS3 does. Don't get me wrong, I played my little ass off when MGS4 came out, but there wasn't much more to love about the system. Ratchet and Clank and Uncharted are the only other games on it right now that I like. And most cross-platform titles I will buy for the 360 instead of the PS3 because I find the 360 controller more comfortable.

    I'll tell you the same thing that I tell everyone else when they come to me to buy a new system. Look for the one that has the games you want to play. That's why you're buying the damned thing, so try and get the most out of the money you're about to spend. Look at the titles, the upcoming releases, and whichever system has the list that makes you the most excited, that's the one to buy.

    And now I'm done. I hope that helped. And feel free to ask any other questions you might have.
