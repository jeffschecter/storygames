    \nBizarre Self Promotion Department

    Last year I was contacted by someone in France who wanted to translate a series of articles I had written for White Wolf's Inphobia Magazine.

    The series was called The Interactive Toolkit, and was published in four parts in 1994.

    It covered such topics as:\n
      \n
    • Why pre-published "story" modules were doomed to failure, since if you wanted a character worthy of a story you had to let the Player go where the Player wanted to go, making strong and dramatic choice that mattered to that particular character.
      • \n
    • Why the obsession with building a world simulation was a strange goal to pursue if you wanted story, since that's not the focus of story.
      • \n
    • Why building a story often depends on the character making the wrong choice, or losing conflicts, and not about maximizing the result of all actions for the characer.
      • \n
    • Shifting the role of the GM from "the guy who has the story" to a person who facilitates the narrative for the protagonists, jumping in to play whatever parts are needed to provide conflict and hard choices for the Players, and letting the Players be the drivers of where their characters are going.
      • \n
    • Dumping most of the mechanical traditions of RPGs -- the uses of finely divided stats, skill obsessions, lists for the particular effects off one kind of gun's damage to another.
      • \n
    • Why elaborate combat mechanics that might make great game were story-killers for the time they took up at a session against the amount of narrative generated
      • \n
    \nAnd other details that can only make me compare then and now and go: "Wow, and here we are with Sorcerer, Dogs in the Vineyard, Universalis, Primetime Adventures, The Mountain Witch and a few dozen other games that deliver exactly what I wanted.

    Again, I wrote the articles in 1994. There was no Sorcerer. I wouldn't bump into Ron and The Forge until six years later. I didn't know anyone who was looking for this kind of play. The only rules that I'd bumped into at this point as inspiration were the Passions and Traits of Pendragon, the unexpectedly rich character creation possibilities of Castle Falkenstien and Cyberpunk, the incredible settings of Dark Sun and Al Qadim and so many other RPGs that should have provided great fodder for compelling characters making dramatic choices, and Vampire's bold stake in the sand that an RPG could be about the stuff that mattered to stories. But always with rules and cultural assumptions about what an RPG was and what the role of the GM had to be that gummed up the works and stopped the best part of what a story is about -- character and character driven choices.

    The response to the first part of the series at Gen Con of that year was -- mixed, if not hostile. "Who's this guy to tell us we shouldn't have 40 minute battles where we pick through the rules looking for modifiers!" (Mind you the article was for the new Inphobia, and the editorial slant was "in the reader's face" -- so it's a caustic bit of writing!)

    And now look.

    What I think this speaks to is simply that there was a hunger out there in the community for different kinds of games. This hunger wasn't being met. But that over time games have been produced that did fulfill that hunger. This was thirteen years ago, and now the games are here! The market wasn't "manufactured" as some people claim. It's meeting a consumer need.

    So thanks, game designers, artists, editors, convention organizers, and entrepreneurs... You're delivering what people have been waiting for for years!

    Here's Part One of the series, in French!

    \n Hi Mark,

    Lou Prosperi contacted me years ago and asked if he could post the articles on his The Oracle site. I was surprised and flattered and of course said yes! (I knew Lou from days back at both Mayfair and FASA.)

    Here's the link to the first part of the series.

    As noted above, the editorial direction of the White Wolf Magazine re-launch (Inphobia Magazine) was an attempt to crack a few eggs. The focus was going to be much broader, with articles on music, movies, TV and more. The editorial slant was "in your face" (as in: "get the hell out of the basement! there's more to life!")

    Whether or not that was a good idea (and I had my doubts), I did my best to oblige, and probably match some of Ron's more caustic moments in print. If I could do it again I might change the phrasing. But probably not.

    It looks to me like the articles at The Oracle were scanned into a software program -- with all the strange typographical errors that procedure often creates. I've always meant to go back through them and provide a proofed version of the articles to Lou... But since they'd been written so long ago (and Lou asked me about this before I bumped into the Indie scene in 2000), it remained a low (if dead) priority.

    But the gist is clear, even if there are eye-sore spots.

    Posted By: Christopher KubasikBizarre Self Promotion Department

    Last year I was contacted by someone in France who wanted to translate a series of articles I had written for White Wolf'sInphobia Magazine.

    The series was calledThe Interactive Toolkit, and was published in four parts in 1994.
    \nSerendipity.

    I literally just finished reading part four of the series for the first time after owning the Inphobia originals since they were published. Due to some quirk of distribution, I never got the finale. This had always gnawed at me, because I loved the series, and the final part was where everything got drawn together. Grrrrr.

    Chris's idea that characters need to be trouble magnets has long been an influence on how I create PCs for any game I am in. Some GMs have appreciated what I was up to, others haven't, but it did a lot for my enjoyment.

    It's a little eerie seeing how well Chris's ideas still fit the basic idea of a Story Game all these years later.

    Posted By: Mike HolmesWhat's "eerie" about it?
    \nWhat's eerie is that Chris described Narrativism, and its fundamental procedures years before the Gaming Outpost discussions, or the birth of what would become the Big Model. I think it even predates Usenet's GDS. He also did it clearly, without excessive jargon (well, okay, Fifth Business, but he does a good job of defining it), and without being overly academic about it.

    While narrativist game design has come a long, long way since then, the theory of what it is all about is all there, 13 years ago. A bit has been added around the edges, but nothing there has been changed by all the theory discussions since, that I can see.

    I find that level of vision eerie. Cool, but eerie.

    If there's more to say, we should probably split this off into its own thread.

    \n
    \n
    \n Wow. going back and re-reading those articles after all this time made me realize how much of them I actually had absorbed without realizing it!

    One odd thing caught my eye: Story Entertainment. Chris offered it as an alternative to role-playing game. Now, in itself, that wouldn't stick out too much. But there was a further odd coincidence or two.

    First, I have a little historical rpg/minis game hybrid on my desk called Science versus Pluck. It's a later version of the game, and the author has changed the description on the cover to read "A Victorian Entertainment" as opposed to earlier versions, described as a wargame.

    Second, I've been thinking about Matthjis' threads regarding non-game roleplaying.

    Now, I tend to have a pretty lax and flexible definition of "game", but it's gotten me to wondering if another term ( like [blank] entertainment) might not actually be a better term for some of this stuff we do?

    \n\nChristopher Kubasik \n\n\n

    \n
    \n Paul,

    When I have a moment I'll be starting a new thread on all that.

    Robert,

    I believe that's a new thread, too. (Though I doubt any good will come of it. This nudging around labels never produces much of value. Terms grow organically and stick because they were organic, as far as I can tell. They made intuitive sense. No committee ever gathered to come up with the term "Role Playing Game.")

    As for the term Story Entertainment, it was inspired by the phrase "Adventure Entertainment" that Mike Pondsmith coined for Castle Falkenstein. His term had a certain breezy fun that perfectly matched his setting.

    More importantly, it conjured the idea of people sitting around making up entertaining stories for each other in a parlor game setting. To me at least. And the feeling that book created for me in my desire to play it that way -- if only the rules were a little different and I could actually find players who didn't want to hide behind their character sheets and create elaborate backstories they wouldn't share because they know GMs just crush those things like bugs to make room for the "real" story.

    More than anything else, however, it was a placeholder term: I was just trying to shift everyone's frame of reference drastically (some of the comments above illustrate why) and I wanted to jog everyone's brain a little over to "this place over here," to something that wasn't quite and RPG.

    Frankly, I'd forgotten about the term. But now that you've brought it up, I do kind of like it.

    CK
