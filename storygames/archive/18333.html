    \n Burning Wheel Gold isn't actually that hard to pick up, so long as you don't try to absorb the whole thing in one chunk. Start with the first 74 pages, and then have players go through Character Burning together, with the intent of starting somewhere and then going on to come up with cool character concepts.

    This post is actually a really good sum-up of how to adjucate combat without constantly resorting to Fight, which is good--because Fight is only really meant for major combats. It's also harder to get into when you don't have a strong handle on the system.

    I'd recommend starting with the core rules (and reading the GM advice at the back), then adding in modules as needed--Duel of Wits may be the easiest to add, rules-wise, and then Fight. I still haven't grokked Range & Cover.

    Remember this: failure is fun! It really is. Because failures are explicitly not allowed to be whiffs--you have to use a failure to deepen the story...just in a way that makes things difficult for the characters. A failed Circles test in a game I'm running has introduced a creepy rogue sorcerer into the game, a character I hadn't even envisioned existing until then.

    You definitely want BWG for what you're running.

    \n Dungeon World might be the best fit overall. It's rather hardcoded to fantasy, but that's where you're starting out. The rules are very approachable, especially as the character sheets and the two-page Moves handout contain most of what the players need to know. If you feel like mixing some SF tropes into your fantasy in the future (as in Burroughs' Mars books or Vance's The Dying Earth), "Dungeon Planet" is a great supplement.

    FATE is also a very good system. It's a little heavier weight than Dungeon World but not too much. As a generic system it's not going to have as much setting material, so you'd have to make up more things yourself like monsters and magic items.

    I played Burning Wheel (the first edition, not Gold) for about six months a few years ago, and found it too heavyweight for me. It has some very nice features, but there's a lot of bookkeeping for characters to keep track of (especially around experience) and the combat system is pretty complicted, to such a degree that after a few sessions the group agreed to drop it and go with simpler skill tests for fighting.

    Some other lightweight fantasy systems you should take a look at are Old School Hack and On Mighty Thews. The first is similar in complexity to Dungeon World but with a bit more crunch to its combat system, and the latter is fun in that it gives the players a whole lot of power to add to the game world by making "lore rolls" that let them declare facts.

    \n I don't know much about burning wheel but I have learned a lesson about introducing people to Storygames based on games they already love.

    In short, I will NEVER do this again.

    Using Monsterhearts as an entry point for a bunch of White-Wolf gamers was an absolute disaster. In my opinion, this had rather a lot to do with expectations on their part based on my telling them that some of the themes were similar (they play a custom version of WW where they are all kids).

    If I got to do this again, I would use something from a completely different genre to the one they are used to and I'd keep it super simple.

    Having played it now, I really really wish I'd used Lady Blackbird instead of Monsterhearts as their first game.

    Monsterhearts is a great game and would have been fine for other groups but IMO, (and realising that n=1 here) you need to make a really clean break from genre for best effect with experienced gamers. . I'm not sure that using an epic fantasy game when one of the players has played a traditional epic fantasy is wise. YMMV of course.

    \nI think the important bit is to realize that this is a potential problem. I think, being aware that you're setting both thematic and mechanical expectations, you should make your calls based on that. Realize that when you say "this is like White Wolf games", they're going to bring in a bunch of previous experiences.

    In this case, I think I would've advocated the more traditional Monsterhearts pitch: "this is the drama side of Buffy". When building a genre expectation, it makes more sense to use non-RPG fiction (especially because said fiction is usually superior), so that you don't bring in system expectations from a system that's totally differently oriented.

    In this case, Burning Wheel offers a few different influences, including LOTR and Wizard of Earthsea. Pinpoint some of those.

    \n On bit of advice for BW character burning: either "tell it like a story" to do it in the order of birth to current, letting the Settings lead where they will; or "burn backwards" by finding the final Lifepath you want to achieve, and work backwards through Settings and Leads until you're "Born ___". Lots of beginners try to make a given starting Lifepath lead to a given final Lifepath and spend HOURS grinding through possible intermediary Lifepaths, trying to get the right Prereqs, Leads, Resources, and Traits.

    On the flip side: one learns a LOT about the various Settings, doing it the long way.

    Another bit of beginning BW advice: minimize PvP squabbling (e.g., Elf v. Dwarf v. Orc) or conflicting Beliefs or Instincts. Save that for your next campaign, when you want to try to push hard at each other's BITs. Trust me: there's enough to do in play and with "normal" conflict (PvE, PvM, PvNPC) to have tons of fun without also trying to manage butthurt between players over setting-implied bullshit.

    I'd even go so far as to say "pick one race for the whole party," but no one ever listens to that advice (and, invariably, each chimes in a few weeks later about how "broken" Elves are... or Sorcery... or Faith... or Dwarven arms... etc.).

    Burning. Wheel. Is. Not. Balanced. And. Never. Will. Be.

    Final advice: Don't make your players pay for shoes if they buy any form of "outfit" or "armor". Seriously--that rule is bullshit. If someone WANTS to put 1 RP into "shoes" then I say they have SHOES, and LOTS of 'em--it's a Thing for them; a shoe for every occasion (and extra pairs for occasions that can damage shoes!).
