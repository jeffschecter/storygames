    \n One of my first RPG experiences was a Rhodesian Bush War campaign played at a local game store back in the UK. Each player took a single "soldier" from a Rhodesian government backed merc squad and we played through an episodic "Tour of Duty" style series of games, mostly revolving around the various engagements, patrols and ambushes, although there were a couple of low combat missions that were actually really tense since we were expecting the hammer to fall.
    \n
    \nEach player got victory points for specific actions (mission objectives, kills, captures etc) as well as general experience awards for taking part in missions. Very quickly, everyone had character backgrounds, cliched mannerisms/characterizations and specialities as the characters developed. I played the squads machine gunner for eight games before I failed to spot a sniper who could actually shoot...... Deaths tended to be fairly traumatic as it meant a loss of experienced squad mates and a randomly generated replacement for the unit. Some of the best scenes/missions/battles were recoveries of wounded personnel. Actually a very cool game and something I would like to recreate again, although probably in a different and less contentious conflict.

    \n I put up a thread at the end of March call Acutal Play: Once upon a time in Bosnia that describes a game I ran at the Seven Years War Association Convention. While the period is weird, the methods work for any story/miniatures game.

    The trouble I've encountered running story games with war games is that the war over powers the story or the story ignores the war. Unless the story is a war movie that focuses on battle miniatures rules get in the way. I used to be primarily a war gamer but have grow bored by the flow of play in war games. One side attacks and either wins or loses - often games end as soon as the tide turns so the collapse and route are not played. So much good story is lost because of this.

    In case the link doesn't work, the game I ran was a Matrix game. A turn consists of one player adding something to the story. The other players may challenge it one at a time to replace it. The two players roll 2d6, the high roller wins, their action happens. If there are no challenges the addition automatically happens. After that is resolved everyone gets to move the game pieces they champion. While everyone has a faction they move around, the game focuses on one main character. Players decide up front if they want this character to do well or not. The good players make things happen that help that character, the bad players try to make the hero's life a living hell. Along the way they get to move minor figures in and out of the action. The scene the players are working on can change at the end of any turn. A player just points to a new spot on the table and says what is happening there. If is very free form and loose but it works. All it takes is for one player to act as host and keep the action focused. I do that by asking questions or making small suggestions.

    Chris Engle

    \n Hey rafael,

    I haven't run an ongoing game like that, but I have run some related one shot type stuff. Here are a few things i've found or considered, or both.

    Folks don't get as attached to characters, at least not right away

    I know a few folks around here in threads about using minis have really gone out of their way to really stress the fact that they don't feel nearly as much first person attachment to their characters when using minis.I believe them, having heard it more than enough. OTOH, I kinda look at it as if this is a different form of imaginative play, and not one where first-person attachment to characters is as important. Instead I recommend going into this kind of play as someone playing it or running it with foreknowledge that this is a likely feeling. It's okay though, because it does allow for a more removed stance where players can say things about characters and even do some of that stuff that is a no-no in other contexts, like advocating for the characters to be important to the story and have interesting things happen to them, rather than advocating as a first person champion of the character. And that's fun all on it's own.

    Dump as much written anything as possible

    Seriously. Dump rule books, cheat sheets, character sheets, all that junk. Get it as far away from the table as possible. if you can't dump it entirely, move it away from the table. Only bring it out when absolutely necessary. if you're doing post game write ups or blue booking, put it online and keep the comuters away from the table too. It encourages people to get up, move around and concentrate one what they're seeing and to engage with the tactile element of using minis.

    If you don't want it to be a wargame, in the nitty gritty tactical sense, stop using wargaming type rules.

    Weird but run with this for a second. Are you familiar with Fiasco? You could very easily hack Fiasco for use a s a core engine for minis play. The set up stuff would be trickier, but the core in play part where people either choose to set up a scene and let their friends resolve it (good or bad), or the reverse, could work just as well with miniatures play as with normal non-minis Fiasco play. In fact, lots of stuff from non-tactical, non-map using play can be used with minis in slightly modified forms and give a much different sort of game than the usual minis using game firefight feel.

    Edit: Heck, it would even be simpler to hack Archipelago II for use with minis than Fiasco. Here's the hack for it: Instead of drawing the initial map, use your minis collection to layout a table. make characters inspired by some of the available minis. During play, free-play with the minis in addition to all the usual sorts of play. So, y'know, show and tell the events of the story. Other than that, use Archipelago II as-is.

    If you're going to go hardcore with minis use, go hardcore and make it a focus

    What I mean is this: Lay out a big damn table of stuff, and keep it mostly as is throughout play. Don't try to re-arrange too often, don't try to let too much imaginative content in for the session that can't be pretty well represented by the toys, and do try to match up the minis to the imaginative stuff as best able. So yes, backstory, personalities, dialogue- all that kind of stuff is fair game to add in. It doesn't really matter to the minis side of things.

    Having a mini of a baker being used as a knight? Poor form, unless that knight is mostly a baker,or in disguise, or baking is somehow that knight's real interest in life and the player running the character brings that up regularly in an interesting way. Likewise, don't use a telephone booth as a spaceship/time-machine. Unless you want to, but you see what I mean.

    Hope some of those suggestions help.

    \n FWIW, I really, reaallly like using dice too.

    I find that a lot of times, depending on the players, they can be a lot more open minded than committed hobbyist players about loosey-goosey rules.

    A few things I've looked at are:
    Itras By cards, as suggested by Matthijs along with stock phrases from Archipelago. I figure those should cover most things.

    Simple single throw quality of success rolls, as suggested by Graham in Cthulhu Dark. Any participant can call for the simple d6 roll, and the roll tells how well or poorly someone succeeded. high rolls are good and basically go off without a hitch. Very low rolls add complications to the success which may be immediate or may lay seeds for future complications.

    Simple opposed rolls, as per some forms of Matrix Games when two players have different ideas of what could happen. Might be directly opposed, in fiction stuff,or could relate to higher level, almost player level stuff. everyone really needs to agree to roll with which way the die-roll competition comes off and not attempt to negate without some sort of further in-fiction groundwork being laid down.

    Could vary the above by having possibility of different sizes of die used to indicate relative strengths of player positions/proposed outcomes.

    If you have Mythic GM Emulator handy, consider using it for sparking ideas for scenes or ongoing threads.You might want to dump the concept of PCs and NPCs in favor of ome other approach, like Main characters and secondary characters.

    Somebody will have used minis before, probably in the tactical sense. have few ideas ready on how to very simply handle those kinds of actiony events on the fly. imple roll combat, even lumpy combat, maybe a few ideas on who goes firts and so on. How far does somebody move, for example compared to someone else in a slo-mo scene. Don't get overly technical here. Kids don't when they play with their action figures. you don't really need to here either. Thinks about usinf David berg's action/scene arc thingie from Delve as another tool to speed up or slow down actions based on player interest.

    Depending on how you want to go with things Rafael, it might be worth it to just plain tell your players you have a cool idea for a "game", you're experimenting, and you want their help seeing how it works out. That's kinda where I'm at in my thinking.

    One of these days, I'm going to try to hash out some concepts about this kinda thnking, the stuff i'm calling Imaginative Miniatures Play (IMP). It's all cocked up, borrows from a bunch of different games and so forth. Really, it's just meant as facilitated ( that's you or me or whover is putting the event day together acting as facilitator) toy play informed by games ( whether minis games, RPGs or non-minis RPGs and story games). If your players are at all like my players, they don't bother to learn rules anyway prior to play, and then sometimes never really do. And that is okay for this. It isn't really meant to be competitive, it is meant more to be casual play and to wallow in source material inspired by the minis collection and the kernel of a set up idea brought by the person hosting things.
