(Blue Rose) How to Design What Doesn't Matter?

So I'm starting a separate thread, branching off from Vaxalon's "Design What Doesn't Matter". This started with Joshua BishopRoby's blog post "RPGs, Pulleys, and Microwave Ovens".

I'd like to talk more concretely about this, because I think there's a difference in perception over how game designs work. So I'd like to suggest a specific discussion of Steve Kenson's Blue Rose, as a game cited by both Brand Robins and Joshua BishopRoby. I'm not a huge fan of Blue Rose, but I liked it enough upon reading it to eventually try out a one-shot game. I wrote an RPGnet Review of it before my test run. After my test run, I was less impressed with the combat system and balance. For a reference on the system, I have a rough SRD of the Blue Rose core rules for this system online, though password-protected. (Go to http://www.darkshire.net/~jhkim/rpg/srd/srd_true_romantic/ using username "guest" and password "guest".)

Incidentally, Joshua BR started an earlier thread regarding Blue Rose, namely "Setting/System Disconnects, and How To Fix Them".

Brand_Robins wrote:
Heroquest will push your play, it will force things to move around its resolution system. Ditto the Pool. OTOH, Blue Rose (for example) will not have a similar effect upon play. Blue Rose has romantic fantasy at its heart, but very few rules that push for romantic fantasy in structure or color. Blue Rose doesn't structure play the way Heroquest, the Pool, and PTA do. It just sits there waiting for you to use it to run combat, determine sorcery power levels, and maybe do a Diplomacy roll or three -- while you structure the narrative, figure out the player's conflicts without flags, and use your players not-in-the-book system to make it work or not.
I have mixed feelings about Blue Rose. Blue Rose I think is actually a well designed DWDM game, I just happen to not like it as much because these days I want games that DWM, or at least design to give game structure.

However, I have lots of friends that LOVE Blue Rose and LOVE GURPS, and so I'm trying to talk from their experiences and the things they've told me about why they like those systems. I have a hard time with games that are both not focused and not designed to what matters (so GURPS is in my "right out" list) -- and so my pull for the one side is pulling for a team I'm not on, I guess.
I would like to analyze more what is going on here. As I understand it, you believe your friends that they have reasons to like Blue Rose, but you don't have a good understanding of why. Now, two points about understanding:

1) Just because they love it doesn't mean that the mechanics must have some intrinsic good quality. They might not be critical in their thinking regarding it. However, it's also possible that you're not seeing past your own blinders here.

2) Loving a design is relative. Just because they love it doesn't mean that everything about it is perfect. It just might be better for what they want then the competition that they're familiar with. So, for example, it might be much better than both D&D3 and Primetime Adventures for what they want. That doesn't mean that there aren't gaping flaws in it, just that it's better than the alternatives for what they want.

I'd like to examine your suggestion that Blue Rose won't push your play. I suspect that there are two things happening here. First, the group is selectively ignoring many of the rules in play. This isn't peculiar to games like Blue Rose -- I've seen the same thing happen using D&D, GURPS, and even Primetime Adventures. It depends on the mindset of the players, and to some degree on the quality of the rules (sucky rules tend to be ignored more). Second, I think you regard designs like GURPS or Blue Rose as the "default", because it's what the group did when you first encountered them. Because it was the previous norm, you imagine that it doesn't push play. However, I suspect that if they had instead gotten started on significantly different games (like Tunnels and Trolls, or Toon, or Amber), and then they switched to Blue Rose, that you would either see it push them or see them ignore the rules.

So let's dig into what influence the Blue Rose rules have on the narrative. Blue Rose doesn't have a "Core Story" -- to use Mike Mearls term. There is no simple blueprint for play like making a dungeon in D&D, or a Master in MLWM, or a Town in DitV. Thus, it's true that the group has to invent the larger situation more on their own. On the other hand, the same can be said of HeroQuest and The Pool.

As for flagging, Blue Rose has two types. First, players define strengths for their characters via their abilities, skills, and feats. They want to use these to their advantage. Second, players define a Calling, Light Nature, and Shadow Nature. These in principle define what they will be striving for, and mechanically how they recover Conviction Points. (For those not familiar, these are based on tarot cards. You pick a Calling (represented by a Major Arcana card) and a Light Nature and a Shadow Nature (each represented by a Minor Arcana card). When you act to pursue your Calling in accordance with either of your Light or Shadow natures, you gain one Conviction Point. I don't think this works very well as a reward system because it's too vague and it is disconnected from the other mechanics of play. But it seems reasonable for flagging. (I had pregenerated characters for my one-shot, so I didn't use this aspect much.)

As for its support of the genre, I tend to agree that it misses key points. I'll compare some notes: To me, the key was that there is no encouragement to make the PCs social outcasts at the start, and no mechanism for them to earn acceptance. There are Reputation rules, but they are just linear with level. Level progression I think is for the most part fine. Extended series will generally have an escalation of challenge. However, social acceptance shouldn't be tied to it. I would say there should be a Social Status trait like Wealth which can vary over time.

The resolution system also has an influence on the emphasis of challenges. Blue Rose has an involved system for combat. That doesn't mean that combat will be common, but if it occurs it will tend to take a while and involve a large degree of focus. By contrast, the system has a very quick mechanic for social influence challenges, which are always a single roll. Other challenges tend to be somewhere in between for involvement. This pushes what is emphasized in play.

In my experience of Romantic Fantasy, the quick influence roll works reasonably -- influence and social conflict don't seem emphasized -- but the True20 combats seem overlong to me. Also, it doesn't do well with static and/or environmental challenges, which are common in the literature but are generally hard to make interesting in games (in my experience).

Anyhow, for those who believe the idea of "Design What Doesn't Matter" as good design -- what here is good? How would you improve this design using the "Design What Doesn't Matter" philosophy? Personally, I would try to de-emphasize combat some, and emphasize at least some static challenges (perhaps by some sort of wagering and reward system).

    First, I nitpick your terms. It's traditional. Blue Rose is a book, a product. It presents a text that bears a design of rules intended for play. When real people sit down at a table and use those rules, they create a game. It's counter-intuitive to say that Blue Rose isn't a game, but we need to be really precise with what exactly we're talking about when we say "Blue Rose does this."

    Blue Rose gives you rules for: detailed combat, detailed magic, moderately detailed social conflict, moderately detailed commerce (buying things, building wealth), and a, yes, abyssmal reputation tracking system. It has relatively sketchy monsters, a sample adventure/situation, an elaborate (and mostly irrelevant) history, and a geography in broad strokes. Its character creation system allows you to spend points to buy attributes and skills, and select feats. (Am I missing any rules?)

    Blue Rose tells you how each of these systems works. Blue Rose does not tell you how to put them together.

    In my solo game with my wife, we set to work using some of the provided bits to create something that we cared about. We used the bits of the provided geography that suited us (Aldins, Kernites, and Islanders) and added in what mattered to us -- a network of towns that actually had relations between each other that could be strained, strains on the meritocratic system of government, a rough economy, a ruined city and history. Of note are those strains on government and the ruined city -- both used the provided material as a starting-off point and was then elaborated to our purposes (creating conflict in government and an ancient romance, specifically).

    In actual play, we used perhaps half of the combat rules, most of the magic rules, used the social conflict rules for more than that subsystem was probably designed to bear, and didn't even touch wealth and reputation. The game featured a young girl protecting her home region of Aldis from threats too great for normals but too overlookable for the big guns in the Knights of the Blue Rose, and got her into a secret society, a couple romances, and lasting friendships.

    The Stuff That Mattered are those last three -- the secret society, the romance, and the friendships, and maybe a little bit of the protecting of the weak. With the exception of the last, we didn't use any rules support for any of these things. It was all "just roleplay" and relied heavily on our real-world relationship and history of gaming together. Of special note was the situation, which I created knowing what my wife likes to play and without any system support from the book.

    I did, however, use a couple monsters from the book because I couldn't give a flying fuck how many hit points a zombie has. Combat was pretty simplistic, which was fine and good cause it was mostly window dressing. The setting was a nice backdrop, but we could have played the same situation and almost the same exact game in any generic fantasy setting. None of this stuff Mattered, even though we used it, even though we spent time in the game manipulating it, because it was all support for the secrets+romance+friendship thing we really cared about.

    Any clearer, John?

    Another issue with Blue Rose, as a sort of addendum to what Josh said above.

    The reward system of Blue Rose is a very interesting case in "what matters." I talked with a friend recently about Blue Rose, and one of the things he liked most was the way the reward system worked. He hated the way D&D did it, and couldn't get his head around Keys from TSOY (which I'd been trying to shove down his throat for weeks). He liked Blue Rose's system because "it does it the way it already works in most of the games I play in, its just more direct about it."

    In D&D you gain a level when you get enough XP. You gain XP by overcoming challanges, with the amount set by how challanging the encounter is. Thus you want to face tough challanges, step on up, and get your reward.

    In TSOY you set your own goals and issues. You gain XP by bringing them into play, confronting them, commenting on them through play, and so on. Thus you want to drive the game towards your own issues as often as possible, get story now, and get your reward.

    Both of these games tell you, through their reward system, what matters and what drives the structure of the game.

    In Blue Rose, you know how you level up? The GM decides it based on what he feels. The exact text is, "Essentially, after an adventure or two, whenever you feel the heroes have reached a point of development in their story, you can award them an additional level" (BR, 174).

    This does, I suppose, tell us what matters in Blue Rose in one sense -- convincing the GM you've come to a point of development in your story. However, even that is undercut by the rest of the paragraph, "Generally, heroes should earn one level every one or two adventures, meaning they will go from 1st level to 20th level in twenty to fourty adventures total." So what matters in Blue Rose, really, is playing. Just playing. Not driving anywhere, not stepping on up, not story now. In a real way it isn't even about following the GM's preplaned arc -- though you need to be warry if you don't do that as it could slightly slow down your progression.

    Now, in terms of what Ron has said about reward cycles and how they influence play, this is a pretty big bug-a-boo. What matters in the game besides just playing to play to play to be playing? Blue Rose doesn't tell you. Like, at all.

    And yet people like it, because it fits with what they already do. By this I suspect they mean that either they do see the reward of playing being playing to play to be playing, or that they have their own system for determining who levels up and gets advanced, and they find the Blue Rose system loose enough that it doesn't get in the way of what they're doing. They like it because it doesn't give negative preasure against their established mode of play.

    In a very real way it seems that BR's reward system was designed not to matter, and because of that it lets folks plug in their own reward systems (or lack there of) in a way that lets them do what they'd do anyway.

    System does matter.

    It's just a matter of which part of the system matters.

    A lot of gamers don't want a lot of positive preasure from their games -- they don't want the game to tell them how to play. They also don't want a lot of negative preasure from their games -- they don't want the game to be full of fiddly bits that they have to work to remove from the game.

    The first reason is why a lot of established players have problems with games like HeroQuest or Dogs. Both games preasure (forward) how you play and structure play and use your reward cycle and all of the rest. The second is the reason why a lot of established players bitched about D&D. It had a lot of gamist elements that weren't doing well for their high-concept Sim games.

    Blue Rose, otoh, gave them what they were looking for because it didn't really do either of the above. Or, that is to say, it did so in a way that fit with the methods and principled decisions they already used to run game. Rather then putting preasure on their mode of play, it lay down and asked them to walk upon it.

    When you go to play Dogs, you don't get optional rules. All the rules of Dogs are pretty set in stone, and the thing that folks get told off for when things go wrong most is not following the rules. You had that problem? Did you use the town creation rules? You had this problem? Did you set stakes before you started rolling? You had the other problem? Did you make small, givable stakes? That's because in Dogs the system matters like this:



    When you go to play Blue Rose, you get lots of optional rules around a core system that is much like the other core system that you've (probably) already been playing. When things go wrong you look to your own group and your play skills, because you know that's where the issue is. Did you have that problem? Well your GM isn't listening to you. You had this problem? Well the players need to be more mature. That's because in Blue Rose the system matters like this:



    Now, with that in mind, better quality tools in the toolkit are always a good thing. The question you have to ask, however, when you start making this tool is: Will this put preasure on people to play in a way other than the way they are already playing, or will it better support the things they are already doing? If the answer is yes, then it won't work for DWDM, because it violates the mandate of "make systems that support what people are already doing/going to do anyway."

    So better reptutation mechanics in Blue Rose would probably be a good thing. As they are they're pretty useless. And having some better ones in there would probably not preasure play too much one way or the other -- especially if they're fully modular and can be used or ignored freely. OTOH, a wagering and reward system for social engagement RP would probably not work, because it would put significant preasure on the way people already deal with social RP. If it was fully optional it could work, I suppose, but even then I can see it being a bug-a-boo because an unhappy player (even if they are only unhappy at the moment because they lost, not unhappy because of ongoing dysfunction) could start putting preasure on the group to use a system that will force them to change the way that they play.

    You can, and probably should, build better tools even in a DWDM game. However you have to do so without making those tools neccesary, and without letting them force change onto the way people already play/want to play. If you support things they want to do anyway but need tools for, that's good. If you give them tools that make them change (like, say, Universalis or My Life With Master) that's not -- because then you've started deciding what matters rather than letting them do it.

    Note: "Good" in the above means "good by the logic of this type of design." It DOES NOT MEAN "what Brand thinks is good."

    System Doesn't Matter is MY soapbox, not JoshuaBR's. :)
    I'm not sure if Joshua's definitions and mine are eye-to-eye, but he hasn't objected so far. Let me give it another shot (I note that Brand has stated some very similar points in his post):

    DWM = designing for the game. It's MY game, and if anyone else is going to play it, THIS is how. You don't have a choice. If you don't play it this way, you're not playing my game.
    The designer may be magnanimous/indifferent as to where customers take his game in reality, but from a design perspective, DWM is pretty focused.

    DWDM = designing for the customer/player. It's YOUR game. I will help you play the game you want to play, but I don't tell you how to do it. I'll give you the bits you need to support the style of game you want to play. It could be generic rules, or a toolkit, or supplemental information, or a "shell" covering material you (the player) simply don't want to deal with.
    This may look like a toolkit option nearly all the time, but it simply an enabling mechanism to help customers reach the point where they're getting something out of the game.

    DDWM = not designing the game. Uh, you'll need this to play if you want to play my way, but I'm lazy/disinterested/distracted and I won't do it for you. So, here's the idea -- run off and play however you like.
    This is pretty close to DWDM, except that the designer posts no rules at all, but rather just a general idea of the necessity for rules.

    I believe many posters are equating System Does Matter with Design What Matters, which is not correct.
    I am a proponent of Design What Matters.
    I have never been a whole-hearted proponent of System Does Matter.

    The jumping-up-and-down part of "System Does Matter" is that System is not the Book. System is however you choose to manipulate the Fiction.

    I am not, by any means, saying that System Doesn't Matter. I'm saying that you get your System from different places, and some parts of System are the things that you play the game for and other parts of the System aren't.

    If I'm writing a book and providing rules that form the part of the System that you are expected to care about, I'm going Design What Matters.

    If I'm writing a book and providing rules that complement your own System that deals with the things that you care about, I'm going Design What Doesn't Matter.

    Note that I can certainly write with a DWM assumption and then you rip it to pieces to do what you wanted. If I build a chair and you use it as a doorstop, I still built a chair.

    Brand_Robins wrote:

    The Dogs system is a negative feedback system. It rewards taking fallout -- especially d4 fallout since those maximize your chance of 1s which give the most dice. Note that there is no explicit theme here. Dogs does not give you points for making moral judgements because that's "what matters". Nor does the fact that Dogs rewards d4 fallout mean that the game is "about" having setbacks in arguments.

    The fallout rewards help the game flow by encouraging more back-and-forth in conflicts rather than just always pushing highest dice (i.e. block, block, block, block, blow, win); and moreover they encourage using the conflict system in general.

    Narratively, they reward Taking the Blow -- which means setbacks in conflict. This does set a tone for the game which works well for the theme -- i.e. Dogs are grizzled and scarred. I'd contrast this to, say, the James Bond 007 reward system where Hero Points are gained from rolling critical successes. That system rewards rolling on your highest skills -- i.e. showing off. Both the JB007 and DitV reward systems support the themes of the games, but don't define them.

    In practice, Dogs players don't make their choices over what will get them the most fallout rewards. However, there may be times when a player is encouraged to get into an argument to power up by Taking The Blow -- just as sometimes in D&D the players are encouraged to attack something for the XP.

    The Blue Rose advancement system is a deliberate and strong choice. Compared to a game like Dogs (which rewards fallout) or D&D (which rewards fights), it removes that chase after XP. Instead, you're only rewarding for pursuing your Calling, and by taking actions that you think will result in success.

    If I can inject my own observation-analogy here for a mo':

    Design What Matters is a sandcastle.
    Design What Doesn't Matter is a sandbox.
    Don't Design What Matters is a sandbox filled with gravel.

    Design What Matters (Focus): "You want a sandcastle? Here's a sandcastle. Now, if you knock this bit off here and maybe build up the other side and then fill up this bit over here with water - you can make yourself a pirate hideout. Just be aware that you've got some work to do."

    The design has a 'fixed form', but that fixed form is, in fact more plastic that it might at first seem, so long as you don't muck about too much or demand the impossible, like a 20' spire of sand.

    Design What Doesn't Matter (Multi-Focus): "You want a sandcastle? Well, here's a box full of sand, a shovel, a trowel, a rake, a pail, the hose is over there - there's some twigs and stuff laying around. Oh, and we've got these dandy molds that you can put wet sand in and make the walls with. If you want a pirate cove, well, I already showed you want we've got here."

    The design has a looser form, with lots of opportunity to craft the kind of sandcastle or sandcastlesque object that you want. You're probably not going to get the professional crafted thing that you see in the brochures, but you'll probably dig it because you've done this kind of thing before. You've been building sandcastles for a while already, and you know what you like, after all.

    Don't Design What Matters (Unfocused): "Sandcastle, huh? *sigh* Well, I think there's a shovel in the garage, if you want to go get it. You'll probably need some kinda mortar to hold it together ... I suppose you can dig up some of the yard and wattle-and-daub it."

    The further extreme of DWDM, really. You can get the thing that you want, but you're going to have to carry most of the water for the system, so to speak. And even then, you might not wind up with the kind of thing that you want.

    So DWDM is really a middle ground - the meeting plane between the focused and the unfocused games. Compromises are made for the sake of commerical interest towards the unfocued crowd because, let's face it, there are more of them than there are of the focused crowd.

You rename "Mistaken" to "Barbarism", but you keep the fact that the player who holds that position is instructed to act to thwart the Heart character. You rename "Zeal" to "Romanitas" but you keep the fact that when the player who holds that position does something that goes against the values of Roman civilization, the "Barbarism" player can call for an experience check which can reduce the player's "Romanitas" and be used to increase other values which are functionally the same as those in Polaris. If you create an isomorphism in this fashion, you're still going to have a game that's about the same thing as Polaris. (I've read that Ben said Polaris is "about" activism, and with only a slight effort at interpretation, I see what he means and how any game which uses the same structure is going to be about the same thing.)

    It's true that D&D doesn't have guidelines to explicitly generate theme or other story elements the way your preconceptions tell you that role-playing should be done. You know what? That is Design What Matters. It is telling you to run the game differently than what you would by default.
    Well, actually it does. On p. 8 of the 3rd Ed DMG, there's a list of 'styles of play' - which range from 'Kick in the Door' to 'Deep-Immersion Storytelling'. Actually, those are your two options, according to the DMG, those are your options, followed by 'Something in Between'.

    The DMG tells you how to build encounters, how to populate dungeons. The sections on adventures tell you how to string those encounters together to create an adventure, and then the next chapter tells you how to string together adventures to make a campaign. However, I submit that this is more 'stuff' - it's just that the DMG is telling you how to make your own Stuff instead of buying another book to get more Stuff.

    All of this, and all of the rest of the edifice that is 3.5 Edition, is Stuff - but it's stuff that's been designed to work together, to make it as easy as possible for someone to hang it together in whatever form they want. The greatest percentage of any D&D publication, including Dragon Magazine, which I just finished a year's subscription to, is just more and more Stuff - options and ports and raw material, from which the game coalesces in the hands of the players.

    What is missing from it is any instruction on WHY to put it all together - what the purpose, the telos, for playing the game is. That is left for the groups to decide - but usually not so explicitly. Let's look at the title headings for the first chapter of the book - 'Dungeon Mastering': 'Providing Adventures', 'Teaching the Game', 'Providing the World', 'Determining Style of Play', 'Adjudicating', 'Propelling the Game Ever Forward', 'Keeping Game Balance', 'Changing the Rules' and 'Running the Game Session'. That is the whole of the chapter entitled 'Dungeon Mastering' - and none of it - none of it - answer why. Are you playing D&D to Provide a World? Are you playing D&D to Teach the Game? Are you playing D&D to Provide Adventures?

    Most of those questions are tautological - you're playing the game to play the game.

    I would submit that there are variant - some of the Stuff - like the Rilly Evil book and the Rilly Rilly Good book, which provide some guidance in this area - I wouldn't know directly, because I don't actually own either one. But I own a good percentage of the rest of the core, and none of it tells you why - it tells you how, and it gives you an awful lot of very good tools for making your game new or different or to shape it in a way that pleases you - but none of it tells you why - nor does it even ask the question.

    Which is why D&D is a sandbox - it's a great shining pool of resources - raw materials - that you can use to build a roleplaying experience - any experience you want (as long as it's fantasy, mind) to get out of it - but you have to irst know what you want and how to make it. You have to know you want a sandcastle and you have to know how to build it.

    Let's contrast that with Polaris, which on p. 33, the very first page of the rules, you have the very first two paragraphs, which are:
    Polaris is a game, but it is more like a game that you would play with a small child and her toys than a competitive game like monopoly [sic], chess or soccer. The goals of playing Polaris are no more and no less than the goals of a child's play-pretend - to create a good story, to explore an imaginary world, and to have fun doing it..

    But Polaris also differs from pretend play in a few key ways. We are adults now, and the stories that drew us as chldren will not necessarily hold the same joy for us now. Adult stories demand more structure, more action, and more compelling quandries for the protagonists. To this end, Polaris has a set background and a set of rules to help you structure the story that you'll be developing, to make sure that it is satisfying, interesting and fun.
    Right there. Black and friggin' white. This is what this game was written to accomplish, and your goals for playing it should be likewise, or abandon hope, all ye who enter here. Polaris tells you, right from the get go, why you're playing this game, and then, after it does that, it assures you that it will tell you how to make this happen if your why and the game's why intersect.

    THAT is the difference between a sandcastle and a sandox.
    If you approach D&D with the preconception that you want to play a story game with an explicit theme about the dying light of civilization -- then you're going to find it broken and/or incomplete. You'll throw your hands up in the air and say "Where's the rules for what a protagonists Issue is?" Your conclusion is that this game doesn't really provide what is necessary to play, and thus it is design what doesn't matter.
    Nowhere does D&D declare what kind of game you're going to get out of it. As a matter of fact, it tells you in exactly six paragraphs that you can either play a mini-pushing, door-kicking hackfest, or 'deeply immersive play'.
    Suppose you picked up Dogs in the Vineyard and wanted to play a game about the last outpost of the Kivor empire against the coming hordes and the dying of the light of civilization. You'd equally well conclude that Dogs in the Vineyard doesn't support that.
    Um, it does. Replace the religion with Pax Kivorana (and what it Means to be a Roman/Kivoran), and change Sin to Barbarism. You're still upholding a nebuolous cultural ideal as stone-cold fact in the face of spreading 'corruption'. Heck, throw in supernatural overtones that may/may not be the underlying cause.

    Done.

    Kuma: except that What Matters, from a system perspective, tends to be determined by, at root, what you're explicitly rewarded for. In Dogs, you're rewarded for going up to the edge, but not over it, for "making conflicts interesting". In PTA, you're rewarded for amusing the other players or otherwise getting their interest. In an inarguably DDWM game like GURPS, you're rewarded for, well, showing up, and maybe for amusing other players.

    But in D&D, you're rewarded for winning challenges. The key to getting system rewards in D&D is to tind the biggest challenge you can beat, take it on, and do so. And the system's way of handling conflict really encourages this -- without those rewards, you're going to have trouble holding your own -- or even surviving. To move the game away from this -- -regardless- of where you are on the "kick open a door" Immersive scale, you have to ignore or change the rules, which isn't tough...but the need to drop rules to change styles isn't a sign of a DDWM game (remember, the two terms are synonymous!); but is, in fact, a direct indicator of DWM.

      Based on your description of the game, I think the rules could be made to matter less by:

      *Making level a mere characteristic of characters in the same way as attributes and skills; there might be notes indicating that level can change to reflect changes in characters, but no clear guidelines for when this should happen.

      *Not having Conviction Points (neither a way to get them nor a way to use them). By the way, did you say how they get used?
