    \n Cheapest way of doing minis, any size you want 'em:

    -Get random images of player characters. Player's can also choose their own. Scale them down to the appropiate size and print, preferrably in thick paper or cardboard. Cut them out.
    -Cut out paper or cardboard small circles, about 2cm in diameter, one for each mini you want to do.
    -Buy a tube of silicone sealant, pour big drops on the cardboard circles, stick the cut out character images vertically on top of the drops.
    -Enjoy your customized minis. You can even put them on fire for dramatic purposes if you want, however I'm not sure about the side-effects of inhaling the smoke of burned silicone.

    \n About the map, you can have apprentices being born in the town/ region they are now protecting. Then I'd have players describe the house their characters grow in, inmediate neighbours and place them in the map first. Then, the 3 places they visited the most when they were kids (like where did they play, where did they had to go for errands and where were they sent to learn things, either lore or a skill.) and people they knew in those places. Essentially, let players draw the map, so they get familiar / invest emotions or creative input in the place their characters are going to protect. That way you can have players actually care about the fiction.

    \n As WarriorMonk says, for fast production of figs, don't use minis, use tokens. Everything is easier when your figures don't have to stand up on their own.

    To give your tokens extra heft, tape a coin or poker chip or washer or something heavy to their underside; it makes them much easier to handle and less prone to fly away.

    Also, google image search is your very best friend for sourcing art. I find that simple cartoony line art -- like you see on these V&V counters -- is the best for these.

    I don't like the D&D4E tokens you get in their monster box sets right now, the artwork is too busy and so you really have to squint at the token to see what it is.

    Be sure to print a unique text label on each token so you can distinguish Goblin A from Goblin Q.

    If you want to put a little bit more work in and get some very nice-looking cardstock figures, have a look at Patrick Cruciau's cardboard figures. I've run countless sessions using Patrick's art. He is a hero of the light.

    Also, it's excellent to see that you're pre-making your maps. It's super irritating to have to pause the game for a stretch while the DM draws the map.

    \n A neat trick I saw used at a convention recently for quick minis.

    The GM used wooden Lincoln Logs. The small 1 notch pieces. He took a piece of found art from the Internet and taped it around the log in a tube.

    For NPCs, he had a few remaining logs that he put numbers on.

    He could have also prepped a few more of them with another 20-30 minutes of prep time.

    They made fun little figs. And having something that stands up like that means you can visually show prone and down type positions as well.
