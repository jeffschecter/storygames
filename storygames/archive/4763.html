    \n The GIN forums are sort of a Mos Eisley, so I'm of mixed minds recommending that folks spend time there, honestly. It's not bad, exactly, but the software the board's running on makes baby Buddha cry.

    Blue -- glad to hear Alley Cat Games is already getting some local support, I just helped get their retailer account set up recently.

    I hate responding to these sorts of things by tooting my own horn, but Spirit of the Century is a good "foot in the door" game for almost any store looking to carry IPR's stuff. I talked with both Endgame and Black Diamond Games out in California when I was out there recently and the in-store behavior definitely shores that assertion up. Gary at Black Diamond pointed at three games -- Spirit of the Century, Dogs in the Vineyard, and Burning Wheel Revised and said basically "the sales I get on these games make the risks I take on all the other games on this indie shelf affordable". So I'd encourage Alley Cat to build their success around that set -- I'd definitely add some of the other big recognizables to that list, such as Prime Time Adventures, AGON, maybe a couple others.

    If you're looking merely for a product selection, IPR's already gotten Chris from Endgame to weigh in on that front, with a buyer's guide:

    http://www.indiepressrevolution.com/xcart/home.php?cat=297

    Burning Wheel Revised isn't on that list (but Burning Empires is) and, regardless, it's an excellent core.

    As far as what you, the consumer, can do to support that store, it's this:

    - So long as you're OK with the idea that your money's going more to the store than the publisher, shift your buying patterns so you start getting your IPR products through special orders at your local store.

    - Don't just buy the games there. Play the games there. Work with the store to do in-store game events. Run demos there of the ones you like, and let the store know you're going to be running them there so they can try to have stuff in stock. Great sales can occur when someone plays a game, likes a game, and then goes over to get their own copy of the game. Your part in this is called "creating a local culture of play" and it's what Endgame does *so* excellently, and is why their IPR-based sales as a whole are highly competitive vis a vis their RPG section as a whole.
