A new approach to a World War Two RPG

Mike Montesa
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games
This is my first post here so I hope this is in the right place.

Over on my LiveJournal, http://mike-montesa.livejournal.com/ I moan and groan about WW2 RPGs. You can look at that entry but I'll repeat it here:

As far as I can recall, between the release of Behind Enemy Lines in 1983 and 1998, when Saving Private Ryan got people interested in WW2 "again," there were no other WW2 RPGs, and it wasn't until 2001 that a wave of WW2 RPGs hit the beaches. GURPS World War Two, Godlike, and Weird Wars all hit at roughly the same time.

There have been some WW2 products released for D20, but you'd be hard pressed to find anyone who even knows about them, or even played them.

Now of course, if things go well, this year will see the release of my own Savage Worlds: Weird War Two, which adapts the original PEG WW2 setting for their Savage Worlds game.

Now that's all well and good. I love WW2 RPGs and buy 'em whenever I see them. But one thing they all have in common, which I think limits the possibilities for play in some cases is, at their heart, they are wargames, and the only thing missing from most of them that removes them from playing Advanced Squad Leader is a hex map (which they may very well use anyway!)

Now, I have played Advanced Squad Leader and I like it. When I play it I can concentrate soley on tactics and the interaction and capabilities of weapon systems, and utterly remove myself from any emotional investment in the cardboard lives I'm flinging into the meatgrinder. But when I play RPGs, I really like to go for a "you are there" sort of experience. With many WW2 RPGs, the moment you slap down a map or set up for a firefight, face it, you are playing a wargame, and if I'm gonna do that to be quite honest, I'd rather play ASL.

Just look at the best WW2 movies out there. They focus on the characters and what the war does to them. They focus on the stresses of leadership. The best of them allow the audience to connect with the characters and experience their struggles, and although it's impossible to actually understand what battle is like without actually being in one, give you and idea of what it's like. They aren't paeons to equipment.

But I wonder if this can be done. A lot of people want to compare the penetration values of the U.S. 76mm gun vs. the German 75L40. They want to see how the Zero could twist around like a cat in a sack. If this is so, any one of the many true wargames dedicated to that subject will do a much better job of giving them that experience than any WW2 RPG out there.

What I want is a WW2 RPG that takes the emphasis off machinery and moving it around, and puts it squarely on the individual soldiers and how they deal with the stress, chaos, and horror of battle. I also want something that brings the chain of command and the responsibilities of leadership into play. In the end, this is what it is all about. Your equipment may be shit hot, but in the end, it always comes down to who is using it and how they use it.

Now, I started a similar thread on RPG, and checked out the game Unsung that was recommended to me there, which almost gave me what I wanted. It was missing something, though I'm still not sure what.

Anyway, I'd just like to hear what people want out of a WW2 RPG - a simpler version of ASL for roleplayers? An examination of what it means to be in command?

Also looking for some encouragement to try and develop my ideas for this a bit further. ;-)

    Here's a little mechanic I came up with last week. I didn't think I would have a use for it, but this seems to be the perfect thread.

    - During battle, soldiers accumulate Horror.

    - During interlude scenes (downtime, travel, leave) a soldier can get rid of Horror points by talking with his comrades. Each question you ask (and get an answer to) turns one Horror point into one Trust point with that soldier.

    - For every battle a soldier survives, his comrades earn Duty points equal to the Trust they have invested in him. You can spend Duty to get bonus dice during conflicts.

    - When a soldier dies, everyone with Trust points with that soldier takes Horror equal to Trust x 2.

    - When your Horror total crosses certain thresholds, you suffer. One of the first problems is that you start refusing to talk to your comrades, denying them the chance to unload Horror by bonding with you.

    That's all I have so far, but I think it has potential. It encourages the soldiers to chat about themselves and bond together, to try to keep each other alive, and to shut down and clam up when the horror becomes too great.
