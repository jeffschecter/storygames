\n Danielle's Kagematsu ashcan for Gen Con needed cover art, so I spent last weekend teaching myself to draw publishable cover art. I'd been searching flickr, iStockPhoto, Google images, and books from the local library for references for a couple of weeks. I did pencils and then inks, in black, and then scanned and color toned it, emailing several scans at each stage to Ed Heil (who did the cover art for Bacchanal) for feedback. I floundered for some time on the hands until I took digital photos of my hands and Danielle's hands, posed how I needed them, as references.

It was an incredible amount of work. More than sixteen hours, not including the research. And though I can see some flaws, I'm pretty damned happy with it and feel quite a powerful sense of accomplishment.

And y'know, I've hated Keith Senkowski for years without really understanding why until now. Doing this really brought home a realization that I've always wanted to be an auteur game creator. So now I'm seriously thinking of doing all my own illustrations for some project-to-be-determined. The sense of accomplishment would be intense, I think, to work that far at the edge of my abilities.

Check it out here in the files section of Danielle's new Kagematsu Google group:

http://kagematsu-rpg.googlegroups.com/web/kagematsu_cover_art_colored.png

Paul

p.s. I hate Vincent Baker too.
p.p.s. Jennifer Rodgers please don't tell me how quickly you produce artwork of similar complexity.

    \nWhite space is good. It gives your composition focus. Very zen.

    I'd estimate around 8 hours for myself on an illo like this, but for a first shot, 16 hours is amazing and impressive for the quality of work you've put out. For a professional-level illustration (as far as I know...I do my illos for free or glory) 20-40 hours is reasonable for cover art, including preliminary sketches and research.

    Hands are tough for everybody (Picasso famously screwed up the hands in his portrait of Gertrude Stein about 20 times), and I can tell you had issues with them here.     \n That's out of the park for a first effort, Paul. I think it's going to really set the ashcan off to a T.

    My own advice from experience is: practice, practice, practice! I used to spend nights sketching my left hand using my right. (And putting my hand at all sorts of strange angles and moving the fingers into different positions.) I found some of my sketches from 1991 a while back when going through old gaming stuff. It was very strange as I remembered sitting in my father's old leather armchair doing it. They weren't completed, just practice efforts one after the other filling a page.

    Timewise, I did all the 3:16 interior art during the month of June (apart from three pieces that I re-used from the 24-hr version). So, it was about 70 separate illustrations ranging from the very small (a black diamond shape for a rank) to a couple of full-page comic-style things with panels. I found once I got in a groove I could do a few a day, but it's very tiring!

    It also helps if you can grow into drawing in your own style. I would say most of the art I did wasn't drawn from reference photos, but straight on to the page from my head. I sketched quickly and then just attacked the page with a brush and ink, before using a pen to finish the piece off.

    And I wholeheartedly agree about the auteur thing. There is a real sense of accomplishment in making the book, illustrations and all, and seeing it completed. I also find a strange detachment too, like I'm marvelling over someone else's work since it surely is not mine.

    For covers, I've spent money on really nice stuff from professionals though (Paul Bourne and Jon Hodgson). I highly recommend that for final publication. MLwM is one of those books with a cover that I just adore.
