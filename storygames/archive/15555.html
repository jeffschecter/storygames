    \n I game best when i'm in a safe space (perform best in general, i think), but it's not like 100% necessary. But when i can get it, it would include:

    No one's gonna come out with any ooc, oog homophobic/transphobic/sexist/racist/etc bullshit.
    No one's gonna shit on anything someone else comes up with.
    No one's gonna be super self-flagellating.
    No one's gonna shit on someone else's needs. (like music volume, temperature, touchy topics, etc)

    Instead, people are all gonna be fucking stoked as all hell to be inventing together, and be grateful for it.
    I think gratitude is a good path toward a general safe space.
    A feeling of entitlement can wreck it.

    & one of the best & most recognizable forms of safe-space creation is The Buddy System. Like, if i'm at a con with a friend and we're playing in a game with three other people i don't know, i can still take emotional risks, because i have someone there watching my back, someone who (if shit hits the fan) will listen to me vent about it later, or back me up if i have to stand up for myself right there.
    ...& it's the feeling of having someone in my corner that creates the feeling of safety, not the ability to call on immediate back-up.

    \n FWIW, I wonder about some of the boundaries of shitting on ideas and self-flagellation over one's own ideas. To my mind, a safe space doesn't necessarily mean that better ideas get put out first, but that more ideas get put out, so that we can find the good ones even if they're declared second or third or fifth or whatever.

    A place feels safe, to me, when I can set an idea on the table and say, "I think I'd like to pursue this kind of action or dialogue, but I don't have it honed or ready yet." And then other players say, "What if?" or "You could also" or "That's great, go with it" until the idea is refined and ready to be plugged into the fiction.

    There's great collaboration in there, when my character can seem tough or witty or quick or savvy or whatever because she's drawing on the best ideas of the whole group.

    A place where any idea is considered sacrosanct because it was said out loud might feel, I don't know, supportive but it can feel artificially so to me. It doesn't feel more safe, 'cause I'll just assume that people are judging a weak idea inside themselves. For me, putting out an idea and then saying, "Except that's sort of shit, isn't it? What can we do that's better than my current idea?" is a behavior made possible by a safe space... but I'd worry that it wouldn't be allowed under a self-flagellation limit.

    To me, a safe space doesn't mean everyone's protected from all bumps and scrapes, it means that no one holds a grudge and that we keep a first-aid kit handy. YMMV, obviously.

    Cheers,
    Will

    \n Graham just made me think... there should be like, different types of safe spaces. You need a safe space for horror centered games, where humor shouldn't go too far. You need a different one in games with tons of drama, lika gray ranks. You need a different one for games that ask a lot of creativity from the players. I'm not talking about the ambience and how you get there, but the first moments of the game after the genre is proposed and before preparations are made. Perhaps you will need different techniques to create safe spaces.

    If you need to create a degree of trust with stranges or even among old friends I'd reccomend the oldest evolutive trick available: non-threathening, absolutely-not-sexual, brief personal contact. If you make it a sort of ritual prior to the game, better. Why you say? well:

    -This is gonna be probably the most awkward moment of the game. In fact, you can start the game stating "we're doing this because it's gonna be the most awkward thing we're gonna make today. Everything from here is going to be downhill"
    -Once people are beyond that point, all other defensive shells they had or presumptions about the game will be gone. Perhaps they will even relax and laugh a bit, if the ritual turns out to be ridiculous enough.
    -Non-threatening human contact is the most reassuring thing ever invented. It has evolved into an universal tool for expressing levels of trust. Think handshaking and hugs.

    I'm not even thinking going to the point of handshaking or hugs, even that can be too awkward between total strangers (though those people that give hugs for free can prove me wrong) What I'm thinking is just this:

    1) all party members stand in a circle, barely at arms reach of each other.
    2) Now they put their palms on the shoulders of the people at their right and left, arms fully extended, sorta football-team thing, except you don't hugh each other, just put your hands on the shoulders of the people beside you.
    3) Look at each other's eyes and say the credo of the game. Like "I'm going to make you awesome" or "we play together" or even "we bleed together", whatever helps you get not only into the safe space but in the mood of the game if you want.


    Ok, just a rough idea and writing it feels as awkward as reading it... well it makes a little more sense in spanish, at least for me, but perhaps it can be adapted to your group of players in a less scary way.

    Posted By: GrahamI wonder if we're hiding behind vague expressions: ... They prevent us getting deeper into the issue.
    I do not believe we are hiding behind anything; we are searching for something that is not very tangible. Being vague is part of that search.

    By trust I mean that we believe in each other; I have confidence that you will do your best to be a good man, a "mentsh", in our game, wether the game itself is deep or shallow. By giving me your attention in the initial stages of the game, you signal that you will consider my point of view, and that you are intent on playing this game together with me, not against me. And so do I, towards you. Once this good intent is established and communicated, mutually, we are ready to play any game.

    That leaves me with the question:
    - How do a designer help a group of players, possibly strangers, to establish and communicate good intent?
    - How do the game help them build the necessary trust?

    \n From my perspective (white, female, cis, immigrant, playing in a language that is not my primary, yadda yadda):

    1. buddy system WORKS.

    2. I can shake hands, I can share snacks, I can join a game in a separate room, I can sit in a tightly squished space where everybody pokes everybody else with elbows because there's not much space to sit in. But if you tried to do the trust building exercise above, I wouldn't let you touch me and most probably I'd excuse myself from the room and then run like hell. All trust exercises set off an alarm in my head because they read like forcing me to trust someone.

    3. choose a game that doesn't have prejudices and pvp hardcoded in it. If it does, there needs to be preexisting trust. I only played things like Dogs in the Vineyard, Witch, Love in the Time of Seidh etc when I had a "buddy" and/or knew the GM (same thing really).     4. in general, I'd say to choose a game that doesn't have pvp in it, but this might be because of my dislike for small group everyone-against-everyone pvp that I discovered this Indiecon.

    5. incidentally, handouts with rule summaries are nice because they remind you of the game terminology which facilitates speaking up.

    6. another nice thing is when the GM/facilitator/organiser/whatnot gives you a "lines/veils" talk and then establishes some of them him/herself right off the bat. This makes sure the game doesn't turn into a game of chicken, where you agree at the start that yeah, everyone can say "stop"... but then you're actually afraid to voice your desire to stop, because you might spoil other's fun/get laughed at/not treated seriously/whatnot.

    \n Jackson, I was sparked by your "shitting on" and "self-flagellation" comments, but I wasn't writing a response to them, per se. I was offering my position, which is that there's a spectrum there. As you say, it's not binary. So we seem to agree there, which is great.

    As for what constitutes a better idea, I could have said more about that, you're right. (I was trying to keep my post short enough to be read, rather than skipped over, so I cut a lot.) My presumption is that either the group reaches consensus about great ideas (it happens sometimes!) or the controlling player has veto power and final say over what his/her character does\u2014including when he/she has solicited a list of options cooked up by the rest of the players. I'd hope that all other players respect that individual authority and I strive to make it happen.

    Go back, maybe, and note how, in my example, the initial player opens up the discussion about new ideas for his character's actions. No one's stomping on anyone else, no one's being domineering. If it sounded like I was pro-stomping or pro-domineering, then I somehow got across exactly the wrong message. (Again, my attempt to write in the brief, show-don't-tell style of other Story Gamers I admire seems to have bitten me.)

    Everything else I've tried to write in response to your post is too defensive, on my part, so I've cut it.

    To make this post of mine more contributive to the thread, though, let me build out two questions and a thesis, which this exchange has me thinking about now:

    Question: What is respect at the game table and how do you show it?

    Question: How does respect interact with trust?

    Thesis: Clarity is important but the assumption of clarity is dangerous. Converse to verify. Ask questions.*

    *(I tend to call this "talking shop" at the table, because it's collaborators talking about their work. I like to verify and clarify, to ask about intent, to get players talking like actors, designers, writers. I dread the statement "But that's not what you said!")

    \n Trust and Power are intimately related, so much so that one does not have meaning without the other.

    Often removing Power removes the need for Trust, but that makes a boring and meaningless game. Nobody needs to Trust anyone when nobody has the Power to DO anything.

    Trust is about giving UP Power and seeing where things take you. So to create a safe place where Trust can flourish, you need to create a framework where it is 'safe' to give up Power.

    I'm still refining the concept for my own use, so sorry for the somewhat useless generality.

    \n I haven't found a technique yet that makes a space equally safe for self-expression and trigger avoidance.

    By default, I favor trigger avoidance.

    I've observed that people sometimes don't speak up for themselves and would rather shut down than voice discomfort. I've had a lot of success providing physical cues to indicate discomfort as a compromise between shutting down and directly voicing that discomfort.

    I draw a large red X on an index card. I show the card to everyone and then put it where anyone can reach. I tell everyone: "If something makes you uncomfortable, lift up this card and make sure I see it. We'll then edit out any uncomfortable content from the game, no questions asked, no explanations needed, and simply move on." Sometimes we'll write the uncomfortable content on the card. We once wrote "no hurting animals" (usually in a different color than the large red X).

    The X card isn't just for the players but also the GM (if there is one). I probably use it more than anyone else!

    If the X card is used more than 3 times by the same person, I'll call for a break and talk to them privately and re-asses the social dynamic. Generally I'll ask neutral questions to minimize influencing their answers as well as sitting beside them (as opposed to directly opposite) and a bit lower than their eye level with my body gesture open to make them feel more comfortable (adjusting to individuals as needed). I've found speaking first as a group to be problematic. When a person who is feeling uneasy has 5 pairs of eyes staring at them while they voice their discomfort, it can make them feel defensive and anxious.

    Just having the X card on the table, even if never used, can help alleviate tension.

    This has worked better than listing lines and veils before game begins. I've had a person say, "I don't want rape in the game" and then to my utter shock, that same person raped an NPC (what they really meant is I don't want my character to be raped but I don't have problem with rape). What we say isn't always what we do or mean so I prefer more real time mechanisms for dealing with problems as they occur. That said, I have a few techniques that have improved the odds of success of pre-game lines and veil conversations.

    Although I favor trigger avoidance over self-expression, I've found that all of the above can help make someone feel more comfortable with self-expression because they know that if their contributions bother someone else, the other person has a clear method to communicate those issues immediately. So they don't have to worry as much about what they say, and instead focus on self-expression. Where in situations where it is unclear what will offend who, or unclear if you've already offended someone, that can cause people to overly restrain their self-expression.

    I'm eager to experiment with more techniques and to learn as much as possible. Especially because I love playing with a wide array of people and I enjoy the challenge of difficult subjects.

    \n Here's a Creating Safe Space Toolkit for GLBTQ Youth that my friend who's a counselor recommends: PDF Download

    It's GLBTQ specific but there is a lot of general useful information as well.

    In addition to safe space techniques, conflict resolution skills are very useful to help maintain safe spaces. But beyond techniques, it starts with you. Changing how you think will affect how you communicate and affect how people react to you.

    Simple thing like being direct but gentle and respectful. Lean towards empathy and warmth. Have integrity and be consistent. Align what your thinking, saying, and physically communicating to build trust. Don't assume you know the the truth, what is true for you may not be true for others. Don't strive for people to agree with you, accept disagreement or alternate perspectives. Don't invalidate other people's experiences because they don't match your own. Take responsibility for your actions. Keep in mind your culture isn't universal. And before speaking, listen first, ask for clarification if anything is unclear, confirm you understand what is being said, and then voice your opinions.

    It's tricky! But all things I deeply care about.

    \n My experience: I facilitate a lot of games that benefit from high-trust and that can go to dark places, often with strangers. I used to have a very pointed lines and veils discussion, during which I'd try to set the tone by offering my own, before play. Most of the time no one would offer anything, and all of the time it seemed to spook people in an unproductive way.

    What this evolved into is a much looser approach. What I generally do now is say "If this were a movie, what would we rate it?" Usually it is an R. Then: "If we drift into territory you aren't cool with, for any reason, feel free to call for a break. We can talk about it or not, sort it out or black it out, retcon, whatever." This more casual engagement with safety seems to work well - I have had people (including myself) call for a time out to adjust the game. Interestingly, the "take a break" piece is often omitted - given permission and agency people usually just say "no, wait a minute, that's not cool" and we adjust on the fly.
