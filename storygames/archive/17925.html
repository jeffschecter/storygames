\n I am surprised there's not already a February edition of this thread. Here goes!

What stuff should we watch, read, play?

1.Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b.Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.

Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.

    \n First up, check out Tony Dowler's new project:

    The Seattle Doomsday Map is a huge painting of downtown Seattle devastated. Buildings have collapsed. Cars lie abandoned on I-5. Neighborhoods have been consumed by fire, water, and returning nature. Civilization has dwindled to a few scattered enclaves, but in those refuges, life carries on with full vigor. This vision haunts me constantly, but it also gets my imagination going, which is why I was compelled to create this image.

    And what better way to share it than as a beautiful full-color roadmap you can carry in your backpack, stash in the glove box of your car, or hang on the wall in your compound.

    I ran a zombie apocalypse adventure years ago that was followed some survivors from a doomed Alaskan cruise ship through Seattle, and I would have loved to have this map back then.

    http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/tonydowler/seattle-doomsday-map

    In the middle of a catastrophic war, one group of women has taken control of a ravaged city and declared "No more war."     \n So I'm going to be doing a SUPER DUPER introductory Google Hangouts class on probability for game designers.

    If you're already in my Gaming circle on Google+, here is the post.

    If you're not, and you're interested, PM me here.

    Here's the blurb:

    I've decided I'm going to use my mathematics degree to help game designers understand probability better, because I am really tired of reading a new amazing game, then sitting down and doing the math and throwing up my hands because the math is terrible. Probability is EXTREMELY FUN to learn, too. It is one of the most fun things to learn in math. Soooo. Are you a game designer or game-thinker who is looking to learn about probability?

    We will be looking at common systems (rolling a die, adding some stuff and beating a target number, die pools that you add up, die pools where you look for successes, etc.) and specifically at games that exemplify these systems.

    You will need the internet connective capacity to connect to a Google Hangout, the ability to speak English, and to multiply two fractions. If you have all that, you can participate in the class.

    I want to emphasize this is going to be SUPER DUPER intro level, if you did any probability in college you are not likely going to get a lot out of this except a refresher.

    After this I'll do one on game theory. Game theory is THE most fun thing to learn in math. hands on hips, glares at geometers in a daring fashion

    I intend to hold the classes (probably) every two weeks on Google Hangouts starting (probably) in April or May. I'm guessing they will each last about 45 minutes and there will be 4-5 of them. Post on this update if you're interested and want me to put you in the circle. Also, I will need a moderator for the class, and if anyone wants to donate PDFs or other fun things to be given out as prizes for correct answers/homework, I'd be down for that.

    Also, feel free to share this with your game-thinking friends and communities.

    \n [D&D Reprints #2]

    I was all "whoa, is that really a wooden box?" \u2026 then came the needle-scratching-on-record noise as I noticed the D10. OSR dudes, is that not an anachronism? As a lad I played D&D from circa '79\u2013'82, and I do not remember ever seeing a D10. We rocked strictly Platonic regular polyhedra; if we needed percentile dice we used D20s that were numbered 0\u20139 twice. I first saw a D10 in the '90s and was seriously bemused by it.

    [Edit: According to Wikipedia, the D10 was first introduced (by whom?) at GenCon in 1980. So while older than I thought, it's still an anachronism alongside these books, the last of which apparently came out in 1976.]

    \n Hey guys. The Golden Sky Stories Kickstarter isn't quite ready yet, but it's getting close. In the meantime another tabletop game project of mine is having a Kickstarter. Channel A is a party card game I created where you assemble anime titles from words on cards and try to pitch the series to the other players. It's a very silly game kind of in the vein of [i]Apples to Apples[/i], except with a strong element of creativity. The game is meant to be equally accessible to people who are deep into anime fandom and people who just want to make fun of anime. I have a no-frills print-and-play "Manga Edition" available for free, and the Kickstarter will launch the full professional version with fancy logo designs for each title card, at $25 for the basic set. Clay Gardner (who did the layout for Golden Sky Stories, and graphic design work for a ton of stuff for Minion Games) is revamping the look of the game too, so it's going to look pretty great.



    Where I'll be publishing GSS myself, for Channel A I sought out an existing publisher, and was lucky enough to get in touch with Chris Cieslik of Asmadi Games, probably best known for We Didn't Playtest This At All. Chris is running the Kickstarter, and will be publishing the game and working with Foam Brain Games to offer it at conventions. Also, he'll be running demos at a bunch of east coast cons during the funding drive; see the KS page for more details.

    http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/1889906682/channel-a
