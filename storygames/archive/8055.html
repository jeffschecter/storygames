\n I've got a game listed on Board Game Geek (here). I know I'm not the only Story Gamer with a game up there. There's Zombie Cinema and Mechaton, for example.

    \n BGG is the primary online community for many boardgamers, but for many others it's first and foremost a dictionary of boardgame information. I myself, for instance, belong in the portion of users who don't have an account and basically use BGG only as a more comprehensive and reliable replacement for Wikipedia when it comes to the boardgame specialty. For that sort of audience the only way to leverage BGG for promotion is to link to the BGG entry when appropriate - it gives your game a certain frisson of boardgamerish credibility if you do. Of course, if the game's entry is mostly empty, that's a bit sad. Thinking back on how I myself use BGG entries, a review is often the first thing I'm looking for concerning a game I look up - last week I looked up Cthulhu Rising, for instance, and benefited well for the review material on the site. Another thing many people look for are images of the game components, being how that provides a lot of information about the structure and production quality of the game.

    I don't know of the user community itself, how they work socially or whether certain sorts of BGG entries have hooks that could be leveraged in marketing. I know that the community has an element of self-inforcement in that many users have a strong inclination for collecting plays or copies of games on the various top lists generated by the community out of user evaluations - some of my boardgamer friends here in Finland have made a point of playing through the current top 20, top 50 or some such lists often enough, for example. As for how Zombie Cinema got on the site, Yoki Erdman from Sweden was good enough to add it - he's got an user account and obviously enough knows his way around the community there. I should've provided him with some pictures for the game, but I'm only now getting around to putting some up on our own site, and I see he's already procured some very snazzy shots of his own.

    I've done a bit of promoting my game as a boardgame here in Finland (putting it in my sig. on the Finnish Boardgame Society boards and such), but I'm rather low-profile about it; ultimately the great majority of boardgamers are pretty lukewarm about a story game that stresses personal creativity, so there's little sense in shouting about it and bothering everybody with chaff that goes out of the way of the play preferences of the majority. The parts that are mostly interesting for the boardgaming scene are the production logistics, really, as they're relevant for boardgame indies - I've already recommended our card printer, Guild of Blades, to many people who're looking for affordable small-run card printing.

    \n I'm fairly active there, as a lot of my game reviews are board and card games. It's like any game community, you have your cliques and factions. Most people are pretty cool, but most of the regulars are there for specific kinds of games, and they will turn their nose up at games outside of that realm. That said, there are a LOT of old skool D&D players, and you will see those games crop up on some of the geeklists and discussions.

    Crossover games are a strange creature. I actually will have a review of a hybrid style game called Aunt Millie's Millions that will go in this weekend's paper and ultimately turn up on the Geek. You claim cards with different dollar values as you squable over Millie's estate, but you are encouraged to come up with a back story for your "character" and if two people claim the same card, they plead their case in-character to a third player, who then awards the item to the one with the best story/argument. That game has seen some love among traditional gamers, even though there is a huge story-game element.

    \n It's stupid because BGG is not a community of people for or against anything. It's just a wiki for games. The people who like Euro games read the entries on Euro games and ignore everything else. The people who like historical boardgames read those - and the people who only like medium complexity operational level Eastern Front historical games only like those- and so on in smaller and smaller circles

    Unhappy because someone rated your game a "3" or something? Get used to it - but no one pays any attention to ratings except in relative terms. (But if your game is rated lower than those in the same category, then you need to examine why). And if you promote your presence there, guess what? The people who like your games give them good ratings, write good reviews, upload pics of games in progress, ask questions, answer questions, and generally provide a support community. (The one thing BGG did better than anyone else is create a good software platform.)

    So it is worse than stupid to take an opportunity with no downside and turn it an ego trip.

    \n And I know insular and petty and egotistical. I've been a miniatures gamer and boardgamer for 35 years - and started roleplaying with first edition D&D. I like all periods and subgenres of all three - and that makes me feel like an outcast sometimes in just about every group.

    As for BGG, I just ordered three historical board games. I made my choice from a larger list of similar games based on the information I found there. All the games I looked at were covered favorably at BGG - but these broke down into essentially ones that were playable in shorter periods of time and with less investment versus ones that are more simulationist. (Again, I like both, but BGG helped me decide which ones to get now, when I have limited funds.) The manufacturers sites varied from moderately helpful to no help at all. Only BGG gave me enough information to make a decision.

    As for my contributions: I just posted a suggestion for handling multiple players in Combat Commander (a two-player card-driven game) that I need for the board-gaming group I'm in - and a way to get rid of the tactical combat chits in GMT's American Revolution system - making it both much quicker and suitable for solitaire play (for me, using Vassal).
