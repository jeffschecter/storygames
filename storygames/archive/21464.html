    \n I am going to critique this video like a grumpy old man. Please don't take this as me dumping on your thread because I thought the video was funny, but I do take a few lessons from it.

    The parody highlights the incongruity between task resolution in a narrative-oriented game. Or, basically, modern D&D. The use of task resolution in this scene involves eight rolls, several of which were redundant, and it simultaneously made demands on the fiction while failing to address it.

    "What is your character doing?" the DM asks, but then demands rolls for things the Arya's player doesn't say she does. She enters Littlefinger's room but never describes how and where she's searching, and instead the DM merely calls for an Investigation check to summarize her efforts (and then the obligatory Wisdom check to get the plot back on track).

    In the OSR approach, Arya's player would go into detail about where she's searching in the room, opening cupboards and closets, and she would most likely be rewarded with an automatic success when she thinks of the mattress.

    In a more narrative approach, Arya's player would describe her goals (to discover what Littlefinger is scheming) and the conflict would be resolved with appropriate consequences (that Littlefinger discovers she was rooting around his room).

    Modern D&D straddles the fence between the two, pushing for task resolution in a manner that resolves conflicts. The results are a mixed bag. If Arya's player had said "I search the mattress," how would the DM have reacted?

    \n Having just played a short campaign of D&D5 with some people who are not part of the "story game scene, I'd say that the style of play shown here is fairly representative of how most people approach D&D (even though it's intended as a parody).

    If you look at some of the play on the Critical Role show, you'll similar hallmarks, as well: rolls are made with fairly little context and for every little thing, then used as a prompt for the GM to tell the players what happens.

    GM: [describes a scene or situation]

    Player: "Hmmmm. Can I find out any more?"

    GM: "Ok, roll [ability]."

    Player rolls.

    GM: "Ok, now I will describe what your character does and the outcomes, often including how smart or creative or intimidating your character is." (For example, "You search the room and you almost miss it, but then you have a brilliant idea! You search the mattress and there it is!")

    Players all cheer.
