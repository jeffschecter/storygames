\n Between high school, college, and after college, I worked nearly a decade in large bookstores (for two different chains, and a couple of different brands), and one thing that I took away from that experience when it comes to helping people find what they're looking for in a bookstore is that when you narrow down the subeject to a title, and you track down where it should be, you shoudl always walk the customer over to the book and put it in their hands. I don't know who invented this idea, but we were told that statistically the odds of a person purchasing a book go up significantly when they're actually holding it and looking through it.

The rest of my gaming group comes from what I guess you would call a traditional gaming background, meaning they played D&D in college, maybe tried a session of a Vampire LARP once or twice, but weren't interested, and a couple of players have tried a few different games over the years, but for the most part we all agree on something basic, like Seventh Sea.

I would like to introduce them to the possibilities of story games, to concepts like narrative control, stakes, escalation, aspects, fallout, trust mechanics, keys, all that good stuff. My introduction to story games came from podcasts, in particular Paul Tevis' ENnie Award-winning "Have Games Will Travel", where he manages to lay down in a succinct and enthusiastic fashion what's cool about all kinds of games.

Most of the folks in my gaming group do not listen to podcasts, much less to story game heavy podcast review shows.

So, my thought was what if you could excerpt the reviews of specific games you wanted to get your players hip to? "Have Games Will Travel" operates under a Creative Commons Attribution-Non-commercial Share Alike 2.5 License, which allows for a lot of leeway, particularly if you're not selling anything and you link to the source of the material.

In an attempt to get the attention of my players, I burned a copy to CD of the following excerpted (and properly attributed) reviews of games that I thought they might find interesting.

[I've posted them to Rapidshare if you're interested in checking them out]:

Cold City Review (from HGWT #73, 8:55, 16.3 MB) [Download here].

Spirit of the Century Review (from HGWT #82, 17:50, 32.6) [Download here].

Dogs in the Vineyard Review (from HGWT #6, 20:32, 37.5 MB) [Download here].

The Shadows of Yesterday Review (from HGWT #20, 3:04, 5.6 MB) [Download here].

Weapon of the Gods Review (from HGWT #75, 5:52, 10.7 MB) [Download here].

I just wonder what other people have done to try to share the gaming goodness with folks who aren't in a regular position to find it on their own. Is there a better way, short of buying copies of the book and handing it to the players? I actually did this with "Spirit of the Century", and my players are all looking forward to our first session.

To be fair, I think some of them have listened to the reviews on the CD, others haven't, but it's still better than waiting for them to discover it on their own.

    \n I wouldn't say one thing about SOTC or TSOY or any other so-called "story game" being different then their "traditional" games.

    D&D is different than Runequest. Those are both "traditional" games. When players come to a new game, they expect new rules. If those rules happen to put more narrative and co-gm mechanics in the players hands... so be it. TSOY can be run fairly "traditionally", covers a lot of the same ground, but recovering pools and getting experience is a bit different and aimed at being "fun".

    I would emphasis the fun. Or the narrow focus of the indie game. Dogs in the Vineyard is a very specific focus... 1850 Utah, you (the PCs) are Dogs with a ton of leeway to deal with small town's demons. Not my favorite setting, by the way, but I enjoyed playing it quite a bit. I don't care for heist movies, but our recent Criminal Element playtest was a blast.

    My point being is that the person introducing the game should be passionate about the game, and how it could be fun for everyone. Not in, "oooooh... TSOY has a neat experience mechanic.." but rather... "ooohh... TSOY is a good, yummy sword and sorcery vibe that is just making me itch to play".

    Unless the target audience is totally a mechanic grognard (and I know a few, and might even resemble one myself)... then launching into the mechanics of the game might go a long way.

    \n I know I lost a player by introducing a storygame. He was a hardcore tactical rpg player, mostly D&D 3.5, and he came to game with me and another group one weekend. I pulled out Best Friends, and four of the five of us loved it. He hated it, ended up playing the sick card and skipped out early. I couldn't get him to come to the next two sessions, even though we were playing stuff like T&J and SotC. I find out through other people he was pissed because he thought he was going to play "a kick ass RPG" and instead played being a teen girl.
    \n
    \nNow again, my other three players LOVED Best Friends and really got into it. Several of them are what I call traditional gamers, and one of them now won't touch a game like D&D (even though that was what he was playing when he and I met) and only wants to play story games.
    \n
    \nSo it really does depend on the person or persons. I think with story games you actually have a better chance of capturing someone who doesn't play games at all than a person who is only familiar with D&D and Warhammer and such.

    Posted By: EldirPosted By: merb101I find out through other people he was pissed because he thought he was going to play "a kick ass RPG" and instead played being a teen girl.
    \nPersonally? I wouldn't count him a great loss. Heck, maybe I should start using games like Best Friends and Panty Explosion to weed out the close-minded.

    Well, that certainly does sound like a great way to shut doors. I used to hate sushi. I'm sure glad when I was first trying it, I didn't have any friends who tried to weed me out by giving me something really spicy and covered in tentacles.

    In the spirit of the thread title, I would suggest actually finding common ground with gamers instead of trying to turn people away. If you start with something that bridges the gap more, like Spirit of the Century or Burning Wheel, then you could find out that someone who used to think D&D is the only "kick ass RPG" and end up being open to play a teenage girl.

    If you're really interested in opening other people's minds to new possibilities, it's best to bring them over slowly instead of throwing them into the deep end. Maybe a potential friend is nothing more than a passive-agressive jerk, maybe he's a cool guy who just happens to prefer tactical RPGs and you just won't mesh in play styles but he's cool to hang out with outside of gaming, or maybe he's a story gamer in the waiting and just needs to be shown the possibilities. The only way to find out is to try and be inclusive, because we all know that these games don't advertise themselves. Neither Jake Richmond or Gregor Hutton are pouring millions into TV ads to show someone there's other avenues than a new Xbox game if you're getting a little tired of D&D. Perhaps you'll consider the route of inclusion, instead of actively trying to freak the normals, so you can feel comfortable in your tower of open-mindedness.
