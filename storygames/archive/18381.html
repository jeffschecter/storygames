\n So there's been a ton of discussion on this forum lately regarding AW hacks. The large number of them suggests either the game is so popular that everybody wants to mould other games in its image, and/or it's incredibly easy to hack. I'd like to address the second possibility, and widen out the question: is it a lot easier to hack than other games, and, if so, why? The only system that seems to come anywhere near, AFAIK- in terms of volume of output on forums about hacking it- is Savage. But I have another question? What makes folk want to hack a game at all, rather than stick to the original? IOW why hack AW for game X, instead of just running either AW or Game X?

    \n I only have a moment to post, but I really like the questions starting this thread, so I'll quickly add something before I scurry off.

    Is AW easier to hack: The fewer/simpler rules a game has, the easier it is to hack. I have really enjoyed hacking Castle Falkenstein over the years because the rules are amazingly simple and put a lot of control in players' hands. AW is very similar in both of these ways.

    Why hack a game rather than playing the original: Genre appeal meets rules friendliness, I imagine. I have more friends interested in playing D&D-style fantasy than post-apocalyptic drama, so I ran Dungeon World for them as a sort of gateway drug into Apocalypse World. I'd really much rather run a game with AW-style rules than a game that requires a D&D Insider subscription just to manage character sheets, though.

    \n For the second question, my answer is that most of the time I'm the GM in games and the vast bulk of people I've played with over the years enjoy roleplaying, but simply don't care much about system at all.

    Only a rare few take time to read much of anything in terms of mechanics, and the only time I saw lots of players reading a good bit of the fluff was back in ye olden dayse of oWoD's popularity.

    The upshot is that it's usually easier on the players to play a variant of the game they already have some inkling of mechanically ( learned pretty much exclusively through play), than it is to teach them any new mechanical system.

    What makes folk want to hack a game at all, rarther than stick to the original? IOW why hack AW for game X, instead of just running either AW or Game X?
    \nI can't address Apocalypse World arts of the question (because I can't understand the appeal of Apocalypse World). But I can explain why I hack things.

    Sometimes when game designing, I find a dynamic in another game that does exactly the thing that I want. That is, two or more mechanics interact in a way that creates the desired player behavior. And if I take that dynamic out of its existing context and lug it into a different context, it can serve a novel, useful purpose. So like if I want to make a game about inevitable downward spirals into self destruction, I could try to make my own game and possibly have the mechanics fail to interact properly or I could grab Graham Walmsley's Cthulhu Dark and reskin it to be about whatever I want my game to be about. Graham already figured out how to make the mechanics do what I want, so the initial bit of game design is already taken care of.

    \nI can give an easy answer to this, because I experienced (and gotten feedback about) the same thing with my game.

    \n There are a bunch of reasons to hack a game:
    - speed or ease (quicker and easier than creating/learning something new from scratch)
    - not much needs to be done (obvious solution if you want something kinda close to an existing thing)
    - you're already fluent in the design language (you've spent a lot of time mastering a mode of design)
    - you want to build on an existing body of work (to be in dialog with other games, or push things in a new direction)
    - it already does some of the things you want your game to do (so there's no need to reinvent the wheel)
    - it's been hugely influential in how you think about games (so it gels with your thoughts and instincts anyway)
    - you have to start somewhere (and it might as well be here, with a game that you or other folks like)

    AW hits one or more of these for a bunch of folks now. Before, it was other games. Soon, it will be new games. I mean, people are already designing games inspired by DW, without much knowledge or experience with AW. This, Simba, is the great circle of life.

    \n When it comes to hacking things, for me, it tends to be two things:
    1. Can I run this game without wearing myself out on the system? (For example, I love a lot of White Wolf game worlds, but Exalted 10 step attack resolution is really not my idea of cinematic action, and the way that power is managed in Scion is too rigidly defined, too incomparable and exponentially unmanageable for me. A houseruling to suit my personal tastes would be a drastic errata to the books, so it would probably be a better idea to hack another system entirely if I chose to run it.)
    2. Is there not a system that does what I want yet? (Sometimes I want to run a game with a specific set of genre expectations and specific game world elements that I can't find synthesised together. Avatar is one, for example, where I love the world of the show but couldn't really find anything that did quite what I wanted, until @horn_head_o started work on his Avatar World hack and I thought 'cool, this does just what I want!')

    \n Artists develope their skills first by copying, musicians make covers, gamers hack games before creating anyting. It's 10% paying homage to the authors for their inspiring work, 20% the desire of doing something as nice as this thing those weirdos did, 30% of some mysterious little voice in our minds that keeps saying something here can be done better or this same tool can be used to make something else better and 40% messing with something we like to have more of it. Percentages can change for every person, of course.

    On top of that, for me it's the possibilities. I can see them, sense them, and that enough for me to rip this or that mechanic or setting element or procedure out of a game and fit it into another, connect it to a lightning rod under a storm and yell IT'S ALIVEEE!!. I save the megalomaniatic laughter for later, when players start rolling the dice and curse their luck.

    \n GURPS is one end of a spectrum (actually Universalis and HERO are further out on that spectrum), where you're not just making a campaign, you're explicitly required to create a full approach, list skills, etc. etc.. Then as you get closer to normal RPGs like Apocalypse World or AD&D, possible approaches to play are highly varied and differentiated - you might buy a Planescape or you might buy a Monsterhearts, or you might just house rule stuff into a big binder (this is normal for normal people who aren't game designers but who don't feel like doing exactly what the designer wanted at every given second, or aren't sure what it is, or are wrong about what it is).

    Eventually you get to games like Lady Blackbird at the other end of the spectrum, which are called "modules".
