    \n I used it for an alternate plane of existence in a D&D game I was GMing. It worked very well for me, and I hope to use the game for similar things again sometime soon.

    In retrospect, I kept too much of what was in the original Otherkind PDF, and should have narrowed it down to something more like how it is described in this blog post. Strip out the core system and ignore Radiance and other complications.

    One good rule I used was that in any situation where dice are rolled, I the GM came up with one bad consequence that could happen, and the player came up with a different bad thing that could happen. Then we rolled 3d6 (plus possible bonus dice) as described in the blog post. But getting the player to describe how things could go wrong meant they were engaged in the creative process, came up with stuff I the GM wouldn't have and had some interest in at least one of the bad things if they rolled like crap.

    \n So,
    \n I played the pdf version of otherkind "by the book" last night.
    \n I only got to play for about 30 minutes, but it was enlightening.
    \n
    \n Mechanics I am considering:
    \nHaving players have a Stat (e.g., Destiny) and replace the "Success" TN from the default of 5 to the stat of the character you are opposing. So, for instance, you if you were against a character with a 3 Destiny, you might only have to roll a 3 or higher in order to Narrate the actions of that character in the scene.
    \nInstead of a single pool (Radiance), let the players spend points of that Stat to alter the die roll (spo you get a temporary boost, but it makes you more vulnerable to other players moving forward. But, I don't know how I want this to refresh yet.
    \n
    \n Also, I noticed that bonus dice are much more effective than I had originally expected. And Iron seemd too powerful and Radiance seemed too scarce. I'll have to play some more to get the full picture though.
    \n What do you guys think?
