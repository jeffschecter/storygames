\n Variable difficulties of task resolution systems come from establishing circumstances surrounding a task, setting the difficulty, then having players roll against it. What if instead this idea were turned about? What if the difficulty of a task were set, then the circumstances adjudicated to match the difficulty? In particular, the idea of the GM rolling dice to determine difficulty, then narrating the circumstances that cause a task to match the difficulty.

For example, let us say the players wish to climb a mountain for whatever reason. The GM would normally say, "It's a DC 20 climb check because the path is icy and there's driving wind." To reverse this methodology: the GM rolls a die or dice, tallies them, and the result is DC 20. The GM then says, "The climb will be unusually difficult for you because the paths are icy and there's driving wind." A post-hoc justification for the difficulty.

Ultimately, I see this less satisfying than the traditional methodology, but this could certainly have some applications. Randomizing NPC moods, randomizing weather conditions, randomizing the individual strength of monsters, randomizing the impact of certain traits (Joe's a drunkard, let's roll to find out the DC of talking him into helping you, and we'll see if he's drunk, feeling a bit tipsy, hungover, etc.).

    \n That's how it works in many progressive (as in, non-traditional) games. I like to call it "formalism" when a game derives fictional details from mechanical structures, rather than the other way around, which I like to call "organic" coupling. Vincent Baker's well-known boxes and arrows thing discusses the same phenomenon. It's nowhere near a hard rule, but generally we've seen more formalism in game design over the last 15 years than was the norm in the preceding 15.

    One perhaps cannot apply formalistic procedures nilly-willy to the kind of adventure gaming you're envisioning, but there are many games with a more narrow remit that function practically entirely in this way, potentially with no organic element whatsoever.

    For adventure gaming specifically, many preparatory and abstracting processes are traditionally formalistic - whenever we don't want to bother with tracking and measuring circumstances to derive probabilities from the fiction, an arbitrary formalism works to streamline play. Two big, fat examples from Dungeons & Dragons would be random encounter checks and NPC reaction rolls: both of those apply a simple random distribution to derive an outcome case-by-case, and then ask the GM to justify the event in fictional terms.

    A perhaps more subtle foundational example is the saving throw: there is a formal target number that disregards the circumstances pretty much entirely, and once we've found out whether the character saves or not, we justify the result in narration by adding necessary detail.

    In other words: you're right, and the procedure you suggest is used pretty much as you describe it.

    \n If I'm understanding the premise correctly, the approach I advocate in a fully-GM'd game of DayTrippers is an example of this. In service to building tension in a dramatic narrative arc, the GM is advised to let the difficulty of obstacles and conflicts be guided by the party's current position in the narrative, rather than by the "organic" properties of the obstacle/combatant itself. The relative ease or difficulty of any given encounter can easily be modulated, and that modulation can be justified impromptu with declaratives of fictional positioning, number of combatants, surprise, convenient or inconvenient circumstance, etc.

    Thus, in a typical session arc with three challenges (which we might consider "plot beats"), the first might be adjudicated to about 1/2 of the party's Total Character Value, the second at 3/4, and the third at the party's full TCV. Note that this is *regardless* of what exactly the encounter is, or in what order the potential obstacles are encountered. Even the very same obstacle can be modulated and remodulated, making it more difficult the second or third time.

    The intended result is a ramping up of tension as the emergent story unfolds, increasing the challenges faced by the players in much the same way as a traditional fiction writer increases the challenges faced by the main character in a short story or TV episode, until reaching the climactic scene which tests the hero to the full extent of their mettle.

    \nNo, this terminological framework does not address the aims or purposes of the game mechanics - it's strictly a description of how the fiction communicated by the players relates to the game mechanics they are using. Here's a simple run-down of the terminology as I like to use it:

    A formal game element is something that exists as an artificial structure defined by formal declaration (rules, in other words - the players agree together that this thing exists). Examples of formal elements include things like procedural rules, character stats and any other elements of the game state that are, well, defined formally. Formal elements are often symbolized and tracked with physical knick-knacks like playing pieces or character sheets or whatnot, but let's not get confused by that - the real game-thing is the abstract idea of a Strength score, not the penciled-in number in a piece of paper.

    A real game element is something that exists independent of formal definition. Some games have negligible real elements, but in rpgs they interestingly play a rather important role: in general the things the players actually say to each other, and the shared fiction they imagine, are all real in this sense. Other real yet nevertheless existing things are feelings, etiquette, strategic notions, etc.

    (I'll note here that yes, calling something "real" this way may come to sound insane as a terminological choice, considering that we're actually mostly working with explicitly fictional things. I have bad rpg theorist justifications for this, but the reality is probably that it would be much less confusing if I just called these concepts "mechanics" and "fiction"; we're basically discussing the way mechanics and fiction of a roleplaying game relate to each other.)

    A roleplaying game fundamentally acts as a formal framework that enforces player evaluation of real ideas to produce formal ideas, and vice versa. For example, a game where the GM decides when your character is dead requires the GM to act as an interpreter between the real idea of death in a narrative, and the formal, official status of being dead in mechanical terms. It may be considered quite interesting that there is apparently no way to transfer real game-state into formal game-state, or vice versa, without having a player act as the medium of interpretation: somebody needs to decide what it looks like in the fiction when the hit points fall to zero, and somebody needs to decide what it means in the mechanics when your character falls into a pool of lava. It's sort of the is-ought problem of rpg ontology, if you will [grin].

    Moving on, we can characterize different roleplaying game procedures in terms of what kind of activity they induce in the formal and real elements of the game. This is an useful perspective on certain types of game design, just as we're seeing in how Valyrian underwent a perspective shift regarding task resolution: it is indeed possible to run task resolution procedures "in either direction" (from fiction to mechanics, or vice versa), and it's often very useful to be aware of both possibilities.

    When we categorize the different types of game actions in terms of the formal/real divide, it becomes possible to distinguish and label four fundamentally different activities:
    * Abstract game procedures are those that function strictly in the space of formal activity. Rolling the dice and comparing them to a score on the character sheet is an abstract activity, for example, because at no point of the procedure are you required to consider the fictional elements of the game to do it.
    * Narrative game procedures are the opposite, they are things that do not formally "do anything" to the game state. For example, when the player of an adventure game describes their character as having a big fancy hat, that's often a merely narrative action (assuming that they do not need to make note of the hat on the character sheet, of course, or pay for it in formally distinct currency).
    * Formal or oracular game procedures are ones that take elements of the formal game state and impact the real (by which we mean fictional - remember the brain disease) state. For example, looking at a chart and concluding that the next skill challenge should be "Hard", and therefore describing an imposing wall that would be challenging to climb, is an oracular activity: the player is mandated to impact the shared fiction by a formal rule.
    * Organic or realistic procedures are the opposite of formal ones, as they instead require a player to "read" the real game state (the fiction of the roleplaying game, most often) to gauge and deliver formal facts. For example, a GM who declares a notionally pre-existing monster to be "Large" as a formal size category in D&D is acting organically.

    Formal and organic game procedures are very important to roleplaying games due to the nature of the medium, and particularly because they're generally speaking the difficult and interesting things in a game: abstract procedures are just boardgaming, when all's said and done, and narrative procedures are just playing cops and robbers - both are legit activities, but there are reasons for why rules-based roleplaying is such a dominant paradigm among people who actually make a hobby of it.

    It may be of interest that when I worked on this stuff sometime in 2006 or so, I was inspired by a disillusionment with "oracular" storytelling games - that is, games that feature weak or no organic procedures at all. This terminology enabled me to verbalize my issues with that kind of game, and construct a bit of a theory on how a powerful roleplaying game needs to form a significant iterative feedback loop between the formal and real elements via a well-considered symphony of formal and organic mechanics. No matter how formal you want to go, a legit rpg must have those crucial moments where the players actually judge their fiction in organic terms; you can reduce those moments in number and clarify them - think My Life with Master as a case study - but ultimately that just makes the remaining moments of organic judgement that much more important.

    As I indicated earlier, this is all just a different nomenclature for Lumpley's well-known cloud-box-arrow thing from years back (even his conclusions were similar, I think); the only reason for my using a different terminology is that we were working on this stuff independent of each other at the time, and I sort of got stuck in my own terminological rabbit hole as a consequence [grin].

    \n I think leaving "How hard is this?" to the dice rather than the GM is often a good call.

    In some games, coming up with a reason for the difficulty after the roll establishes it might be just right! In games where that isn't right, you can instead roll for the reasons.

    Example: Climbing a mountain? Roll d10 terrain + d10 weather. A 1 and a 9 mean it's an easy hike, but through a hellacious hailstorm. Flip those and we get a perfect day to scale perilous cliffs. In both cases, DC 10.

    I've thought of doing exactly this sort of "roll for the state of things" in situations where it seems best to retain uncertainty, but only regarding the outcome of the PCs' endeavor, not the performance of their task. Example: The player characters request a favor from an NPC whose family they saved. There should not be a roll to see whether he's willing to help. There should be rolls for what he can give. So, rather than the GM inventing this guy's position in the world right this second, and then trying to find some uncertainty to map to a roll (or worse, rolling with a default that's inappropriate here, like "maybe he doesn't want to help"), we roll to see whether his recent fortunes have left him in possession of what the PCs need.

    \n My game Tactical Ops works like this.
    It's a team-focused mission-based game. Obviously gmLess
    The Players pick or roll the initial expected Difficulty level.
    Each Obstacle they will face will have that level of difficulty, and be described accordingly.

    So if we are on a "High Difficulty" mission and we encounter a single guard as an Obstacle we have to figure out in the fiction why this one guard represents a high level of challenge: so maybe it is very muscular, has cybernetic eyes, is equipped with a weird-looking gun, etc.

    Mechanical effects can then unexpectedly increase (for just one Obstacle, or for the overall Mission) the level of Difficulty.

    \n I think there are a handful of different ways this can be a useful or interesting approach.

    Why do we need to "set a DC" (that's easy to say, so I'll stick with that language) or determine a mechanical level of difficulty?

    I can see a few possible reasons:

    1. We want mechanical challenge to reflect fictional positioning. (So that fictional description and player choices all have an impact on outcomes.)

    I don't see how randomizing a DC is helpful here. (But maybe there's something I haven't considered.)

    2. We want success to be uncertain - there to be randomness in which actions succeed and which actions fail.

    In most games with task resolution, we are already rolling for success randomly, so adding *another* roll to establish "DC" does not give us any added value. The procedure is already random, after all.

    The GM setting DCs randomly is basically an opposed roll, which doesn't change the math significantly. (You can just redesign the task resolution check to be more highly random, if that's the desired outcome.)

    (I suppose there might be an exception if the game you're using includes many opportunities to retry tasks and you occasionally want some to be impossible - outside the range of possible skill checks, for example - but these are oddball side cases.)

    Generally you're better off with a static DC for all rolls if this is your goal.

    3. We want to leverage some random or "oracular" tools to decide things about the fiction, or to help us decide things or help us narrate details.

    This is what David is doing in his posted examples, and I would say that he's doing so in a better way than a straight up random roll.

    If I want fictional inspiration, it's much more interesting to roll for whether it's snowing that day than just roll to set a random DC. Skip the middle man, in other words.

    This is kind of how Reaction Rolls work in old school D&D.

    Here, the roll is providing us with inspiration to narrate things a certain way and helping us create fiction. (Notably, before resolution - if we want inspiration for how things go, we already have the resolution check, after all.)

    4. We want difficulties to ramp up or down or otherwise create some kind of progression.

    This is usually not well served by randomness, although there could still be hybrid cases (for instance, write down seven different DCs on cards, and then draw them in order as you play; then you're guaranteed to have one particular difficult task per session or whatever).

    This one is tricky, though; what is the intended goal?

    If it's to create a dramatic arc, this might not be the right approach. In stories, we feel a rising level of challenge/tension throughout, but I see designers misinterpreting what this means regularly.

    However, the actual common outcome in such a story is the opposite: the protagonists struggle through early challenges but almos inevitably succeed in the end. This means that, if anything, DCs should drop as the game progresses. (I don't know if this the case in your game, Tod!)

    On the other hand, if the desired situation is that getting to the end of the road successfully is difficult and rarely succeeds, a series of identical challenges which must all succeed (like a long string of coin flips) is better and simpler.

    There are many different angles here, and all worth considering,

    \n Great points, Paul. It's true that by increasing the actual DL as the climax approaches in DayTrippers, I am mechanically making success less likely. In other words, it is not the "success arc" that I'm modeling, but rather the tension and risk experienced by the players. Another way of putting this is that I'm modeling the subjective experience of the protagonists, rather than the outcome of the traditional narrative arc. This is a "Simulationist" approach - not to fictional "reality", but to the protagonists' sense of the narrative construct as viewed from within.

    There are a couple factors at play here, part illusion and part woo. Firstly, as you may know from my statements elsewhere, the DayTrippers CORE system depends a lot on "psychic content" and one of its guiding values is Bleed. I find that the propensity for outbound Bleed tends to increase as tension and stakes increase, creating a feedback loop between diegetic and non-diegetic actions and effects.

    Secondly and related to the above, I am a firm believer in dice magic, having seen it work so well for so many years. I can't explain how it works scientifically, but it does seem to play out that increased tension somehow increases the likelihood of surprisingly advantageous real-world effects (such as the outcomes of dice rolls).

    Finally (and this is where the "illusion" comes in), I hedge my bets by constructing the probability arc in such a way that while earlier challenges are easier to overcome - notice that the curve described above is 1:2, 3:4, 1:1, meaning that the final challenge actually has about a 50/50 chance of success (i.e. "balanced") - but the relative ease of the earlier challenges can be easily masked by fictional positioning and description. Again, here I am prioritizing subjective effects over mechanical odds. There are a few techniques that can be used to pull off this illusion. It's a bit long to quote here, so instead I'll link to an excerpt from the DayTrippers GameMasters Guide:
    http://daytrippersrpg.com/sites/default/files/tension.pdf

    Let me make clear: I don't expect (nor advocate) that this approach would be equally useful in every RPG. It works for DayTrippers because of the design goals and values of this particular game. It's kinda like Hunter S. Thompson said, "I hate to advocate drugs, alcohol, violence or insanity to anyone, but they've always worked for me."

    \n Yeah, I'm also not going to get behind a "magical dice" theory, nor endorse it for game design. However, we don't need to get into a debate about magical thinking: what's interesting about this particular example is that it's equally relevant to randomized difficulty in RPG design.

    Hypothetically speaking - at least to a scientifically-minded person - a typical "adventure" in Tod's described "three-tier difficulty" model should conclude in failure about 75.25% of the time (or 50% of the time, if only the final roll matters).

    My guess is that, for most tables, this is far from desirable. So, how can you have high odds of failure and still have above-average success? This is where it gets interesting from a design standpoint, as Guy points out. That second bullet point in Guy's reply is a common technique, and quite effective.

    What are other possibilities?

    * False "difficulty". This is often seen in boardgames: the level of "difficulty" keeps rising, but so does some kind of positive - the resources or abilities of the players. Perhaps, if you're smart, you can collect special items or "bonus points" all through the game, and then cash them in on that final roll, for example. This still creates the *feeling* of tension and difficulty, but, in practice, your odds may be *better* than they were earlier in the game. In video games this might mean getting better weapons, or even just getting better at playing the game (e.g. hand-eye coordination). In D&D it might be a question of saving most of your hit points for the final fight, and now finally being willing to put them on the line.

    * "What are you willing to risk?" Somewhat related to the hit point example: in many "narrativist" designs, the player and/or character has the ability to accept compromises or sacrifice things the character values to increase the odds of success. Here, the real "cost" of a difficult challenge is to make the characters suffer, to ask them what they're willing to give up or wager in order to get a high probability of success after all. Maybe sacrificing your left arm doubles your odds of success, and putting your lover's life on the line gives you another +10; you pile these up until you get your success.

    * Looking for clever tricks and optimizing your odds. In this game, there are many ways to get bonuses to your attempt - find a way to roll a different skill/stat, get circumstance bonuses, or create a situation where many people can try. As difficulty rises, then, the players have to get more and more inventive or clever; eventually, their clever ideas overwhelm the apparent difficulty and they achieve high odds of success after all. For example, when an enemy's combat statistics become too hard to handle, the players have to conspire to poison him first, and then fight him in a specially prepared ambush location.

    So, if and when you have randomized difficulty, as the OP posits, what is its purpose? Does it relate to any of these things? Of the categories we've mentioned:

    * The players will make multiple attempts, until one succeeds (as per Guy's post). The challenge will take longer to play out, as tension mounts.
    * The players must accumulate resources during earlier play in order to face the challenge.
    * The player/character must make greater sacrifices or face greater risks in order to have a chance at success.
    * The players must use more and more wits/inventiveness/strategy to overcome a higher challenge.

    If you like for challenges to vary in these ways, then changing the difficulty from situation to situation is a good thing. Now, *when* do you want it to vary? If it's at very specific times and places (e.g. the final encounter/scene), randomness will not serve you well. But if you like the idea that some situations require more or less of the above, then randomness could be a great idea.

    I think these are also fruitful ideas to ponder as a designer.

    \n In all honesty, the die sets the difficulty, not the skill levels or DC.

    This is more apparent in games like RuneQuest, where you roll against your skill. If rolling d20 under your skill, you succeed.

    Normally, each player rolls a die. If the player got a skill of 12, and the die shows 14, then the die tells the player "This is too hard for your character. You needed a skill level of 14 or more".

    If we expand on this, at any time several people would try to accomplish the same thing, the game master could roll a die. That would set the difficulty for the task. If any character has skill levels equal or above, then they would succeed.

    ---

    In my first published game Matin\xe9, the player could describe whatever (with some restrictions), but unless the roll succeeded, the game master could give a counter description that nullified that explanation. The same thing applied in the opposite direction, and I used this a lot as a game master. Here are two examples I fondly remember from when we playtested the game.

    Me: "The guy throws you out of window in the tower". [Rolled failed]
    Player: "I grab hold of one of the vines on the tower wall, and swings back in again."

    Me: "The dragon fills the room with fire." [Rolled failed]
    Player: "I fall down on my knees and pray, and the fire splits in front of me."

    The huge gain from playing like this was that the interaction between player-game master became more fluent, even during combat, where each action normally are chopped up and doesn't related to each other. When we played, it all felt like one continuous flow.

    And Matin\xe9 were all about the characters being heroes. They never failed because they were bad. Instead, unexpected circumstances came up that made them fail, and new situations were often created from this. The thief didn't fail to lock pick, but guards arrived instead. A new situation to overcome.

    Mouse Guard got the same thing with complications(?). Failed rolls that leads into something else.
