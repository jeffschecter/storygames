\n I am trying to tweak a game to be played in GMless mode.
\n
\nI soon came to realize that scene framing is one of the most powerful tools to drive narrative and players can use it to get what they want more easily than by getting their characters in danger. The more I think how to do it right, the more I make it complex and unplayable. If I keep it very simple, it seems to rely more on players' goodwill and consensus than on mechanics and I miss some of the IAWA-like competitiveness I would like to see in the game.
\n
\nWhat games do you think do it right and are robust in supporting player vs player competition?

    \n 1001 Nights by Meg Baker.

    Good scene framing, especially in a competative situation, requires incentives to be engaging to other players and for them to be engaged. Otherwise you need to deal with folks breaking competition for the narrative or breaking narrative for unengaging but brutal frames (bang - does your character die from being shot or falling off the roof?).

    \n Fiasco handles this with a very simple, very clever trick: if you frame a scene, you don't get to decide how it ends. If you choose to decide how the scene ends, you don't get to decide how it starts. This means that even if you try to frame scenes very favorably for yourself, you're giving up other control to do so, and your friends get a chance to retaliate.

    \n In 1001 Nights, there is an inner tale, told to the sultan which the characters take turns. Mechanically while telling the tale, and casting other characters within it, the tale teller is trying to get gems (the game's main resource). Players wager a gem by asking a question about the story - which when resolved gives both the asked and the storyteller a chance for the gem. This dual interest in the tale, where the teller must engage the audience and the audience must engage teller leads to balancing out the competative forces that would disrupt the use of scene framing.

    In comparison, Fiasco has a great scene framing approach, but it falters if things get too competative - because there is no incentive to not disengage the other player with fictional positioning. Scene framing, as a technique really only works if it offers interesting decisions to others, but sharp competition is most interested in offering no meaningful decisions to an opponent.

    As for getting 1001 Nights, http://www.nightskygames.com/ is Meg's page. I don't know what the store availability would be, but I've not seen it outside of conventions.

    - Mendel

    \n Showdown, Seth Ben-Ezra's game that will, eventually, be out. It has deeply helpful rules for scene framing, including the ability for your opponent to call a foul on you and make you do it differently if you go over one of the game's Boundaries. I keep talking about this game, and I wish everyone could read it and see what I've seen. It offers a lot of structure, sets players up for fluency, is the best play-by-email/post/IM game I've ever played, and is all-around excellent.
