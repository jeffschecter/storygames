A Player's Relationship To Character Failure

Jesse
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
Hello,

Last night something briefly came up at our PtA game and I've occasionally seen a similar idea floated through various threads elsewhere. The idea is a player prefering to see their character fail in certain situations. I'm going to have to admit that this is a totally bizzare an alien thing to me but I think I'm going to have to clearify a lot to communicate where I'm coming from.

First of all I do not equate character success with winning. To me character success and failure is purely a property of the fiction. Either my character fullfills his agenda or he doesn't. Winning and Losing is a property of the real people at the table. Those concepts are an ego assertion about an individual's real-world skill. Either I played the game well and I won or I played the game poorly and I lost. I do not give a damn about winning and losing. I care boatloads about seeing my character succeed.

I'd also like to clearify that this has nothing to do with "Immersion" either. In the pursuit of fulling my character's agenda I want to account for all the meta-game concerns along the way. In a Sorcerer game I consider the Humanity ramifications of my character's agenda. I consider carefully my character's agenda and how I wish to have him pursue it impacts the other player's characters. And so forth.

This also doesn't mean that I don't enjoy the consequnces of failure. In fact I hate cheating opportunities for failures. I've been in games where I've felt like certain sections were rushed or too much was resolved in a single go and that although all of that worked out in my character's favor, I felt like we were skipping opportunities for my character to confront adversity.

The bottom line is, I've realized that I greatly dislike being complicit in my character's failure (Not the same, by the way, as being given narrative rights over the consequences of failure ala Trollbabe. I'm fine with that). I want to grapple with adversity and opposition genuinely, honestly and with gusto and I can't do that if I'm partially invested in that opposition. Hell, to me, that's WHY there's a randomized element to an RPG so that in the zeal of my character's pursuits the game will slap me down and say, "Actually, no that doesn't quite work out the way you want." I enjoy this so that when my character DOES fail, I'm shocked into having to re-evaluate that character, to have him change and grow. I can't remember who or what my co-worker was quoting when he said, "I enjoy gambling not for the thrill of winning but for how alive I feel when I lose," but that pretty much sums up my relationship with my characters.

So, what is your relationship to your character like?

Jesse

    Funny, I think I agree with all of your baseline reasons (wanting to face real adversity without fighting with one hand tied behind your back, particularly) but not your conclusion.

    It's possible to face real adversity, and lose fair and square, while still wanting your character to fail at what they're trying. It just requires (as you rightly point out) that you decouple the idea of you winning from the idea of your character succeeding.

    Most simple possible example: How about if you, the player, are fighting to make your character fail (using all of his bad traits) and your opposition is fighting to make him succeed? I mean ... it's not like you have the right to declare that he fails. You might have to fight for it. You might lose, and be forced to watch your character succeed. I've been in that situation, though it's not common.

    Other examples are easier to see come up in real play, even if they're a little more difficult to understand: Sometimes what you, the player, want to have happen is completely independent of your character's intent. Like, your character wants to track down a secret council of conspirators, but what you the player want is for him to learn a lesson in humility. There are at least four possible outcomes there: He learns a lesson in humility that helps him to find the conspirators, he learns a lesson in humility by utterly failing to find the conspirators, his arrogance reins unchecked as he finds the conspirators or his unchecked arrogance prevents him from finding the conspirators. You could totally want him to fail to find the conspirators, because it would give you some advantage (either tactical in the system or just in the nature of the narration) toward winning your goal of teaching him humility. I see that kind of thing happen all the time.

    And ... that's what my relationship with my character is like, I guess.

    Well, I'd like to reiterate that I do enjoy seeing my characters fail. Failure is an opportunity, not a stop point. I think what I'm against is trying to engineer large chunks of theme in one go, rather than let theme be the sum of individual conflicts. For example, I might very well come to think my character is a bastard and deserves some humility. But I still prefer to play him with the intent of him succeeding in his agenda. Deciding what the character wants and how the character plans to go about achieving it is subjected to a wide variety of factors from meta-game system considerations to my personal view of the character to whatever.     I think Brand is wrong. Even if Jesse thinks he's right.

    Oh, I think that Jesse and Brand, and I all hate to pre-determine how play will go. But JBR doesn't, and that's not the point.

    Because one could pre-determine success, and that would be the same problem as predetermining failure. What we're talking about here is the role of advocate for one sort of story outcome vs. another.

    I've talked about this subject a lot, because it's important to Hero Quest and how it works. So I think I've got it.

    Jesse likes to be in the player role of advocate for the character's success. Like the reader watching Frodo struggle with getting the ring across Gorgoroth, he wants to both root for Frodo, but love his failures when they happen. Yeah, he also doesn't want to know if Frodo makes it in the end or not, and to have it "play out." But that's beside the point. As a player of a RPG with the ability to affect the equation of whether or not Frodo wins, we want to be in the role of the person who is causing the success part, and we want the GM to be in the role of the person who causes the failures.

    Because, "Immersion" aside, we do identify with the character. If we didn't at all, we wouldn't be interested in the events at all. The player's characters are, on some level, protagonists. So we have to sympathize with them.

    Now, some players see their role as more "author" than "audience" to their character (questions of "actor" aside). And these players may, at times, take a more active hand in forging their characters failures. But for others, they want to stick on the side of "protagonist success promotion." This is understandable.

    And it's not equating it to "winning." Tony, you're not believing Jesse, but it's the truth. You can want to have only part of the role of advocacy for your character's story, and only want it to be the positive side, and still not think of it as "winning" if he does succeed, but just as doing your part in fulfiling the character's destiny.

    Now, at the risk of sounding like a shill for HQ, I'll demonstrate how, in theory, the game system promotes this. Actually it's not 100% perfect in this, but it does a pretty good job. Basically, the player's job in a contest is to discover what abilities the character has that he can augment with. He's coming up with the "plusses" that show us the cool facets of the character, and how he interacts with the contest.

    The narrator's job is to establish the resistance and other bonuses and penalties. He is, as a "less-closely-involved" player in the character's destiny, allowed to adjust up or down, etc, but always advocates generally for plausibility to make the story good because it's believable.

    So you have these opposed roles for these players in the game: the player provides the impetus to win, and the GM provides the impetus for danger, conflict, and plausibility.

    Then there's a bit of tension (this is where things become a little less clear). Does the player try to find every augment he can? Does he go "trolling" his character sheet looking to find every little augment he can come up with, so his character can win? No, in theory he's only interested in adding abilities to the extent that he's showing the interesting facets of the character, and keeping the events plausible as well.

    What forces keep the player in line here? Well, for one the narrator can disallow or penalize augments and oddly used abilities. In this way the narrator can signal the player if he seems to be trolling to win that he's no longer playing the role of protagonist success force, and has slipped into playing to win for himself as player. Second, it's simply too easy to augment a lot usually. That is there's no player challenge to going down the entire list of abilities and complaining until you get them as augments. Players realize this immediately, it seems to me. The answer to "well, why shouldn't I troll?" is seen right off as, "Well, because it's easy and dull. And the narrator will tell me not to."

    As I say, this isn't 100% the most solid system to promote this.

    Hero Points are another example of the tension on a player - does he alwyas bump, if it means making the character do better? Well, sure, in theory. But in practice players don't bump. Not because they're engineering failures, precisely. But because they have a limited number of HP, and they have an alternative use for the HP (developing the character), that's just as good a use of them in promoting character success over the long haul.

    So you don't have to feel "guilty" if you don't bump that you're being a part of the negative side of the equation. You can say, "I'm saving them for something more important later, or to add abilties to the character to make him even cooler." You're simply deciding in which way to use the resources to make the character a success and cool.

    So that's my theory on why HQ seems to promote this sort of play so well. You can unabashedly be on the side of the character, and always promote him, but yet still be part of the process of forging his story, and not simply trying to "play to win."

    Mike

    Mike,

    I've seen lots of games (not rules, games) in which players can chose to have their characters fail rather than roll a contest.

    GM: You have to make a melee roll with an 18 if you want to beat him...
    Player: No, that's okay, he can kick my ass and take me hostage.
    GM:... Um... okay, so he does....

    In this case what you're dealing with is not using mechanical resolution for any situation where the player wants the character to fail. This is different than using mechanical resolution and hoping that your character fails. However, the two do often get conflated together. I've had a lot of players ask me, when I told them to roll anyway, why they should have to if they want to lose. In their mindset you're engaging in mechanical resolution to win -- the goal is still the old sim/trad based one of "getting the goal of the character by earning it with success." So if they don't want that, they don't see why they should need to engage. This has something to do with the winning a losing issue, but is about the structure of it rather than the feeling of it (two other things that sometimes get conflated).

    My example with Ben was intended to point out how some systems specifically derail this thought. As the Mistaken in Polaris you are supposed to be opposition to the player, not the character. So if the Heart wants to screw the Heart Character your job is to make sure the Heart character doesn't get screwed. You fight for the character to win, because thats where the tension and emergent fiction comes from.

    After that there are games in which you can use mechanical resolution with greater or lesser degrees of control over the loss of your character. You cite HeroQuest and D&D, and do a decent job of describing how they handle it. They're both very heavy on fortune and have a high degree of randomization in resolution, and so fit the model you describe. However, to get a broader perspective take a quick look at Dogs and With Great Power.

    In WGP one of Michael's stated objectives was to allow players to (mostly) be able to throw conflicts without having to abandon mechanical resolution. Thus the use of a hand of cards to play from, rather than a fully random dice roll or card draw. By having the ability to chose a really low card you have more ability to make your character lose in any given situation. Play a deuce in an enrichment scene and it's unlikely that the GM can force you to win. If you want to lose, chances are decent you can make your character lose. It isn't, however, guaranteed. I've heard AP reports where players get stuck with a mitt full of 10s when they're wanting to toss and have to win even when they really want the character to bite it.

    Now, in Dogs if you go into a conflict wanting your character to lose, it can be really difficult to stop you from doing just that. You can be sitting on the world's hugest heap of 10s in your pool and say, "I give." Just like that, your character has lost the conflict. The only thing the GM (or opposing PC) can do to stop you is to give first. (Its funny to watch two players obviously intending to do this, but also wanting to rack up fallout first trying to decide if they should take more fallout or give....) Now, this may be viewed by some groups (maybe by Jesse, for example) as short-circuiting the system. If you're in a conflict its assumed you wanted to be in it, right? But what if you don't? What if you just want to lose and tell everyone from the get go? Well then, why roll when all you want is failure (unless you also want fallout)? You're back to the situation I described back up at the top.

    So the truth is that some games do let you lose on purpose and have it still let story be emergent, but others don't, and not all games do it in the same way. And everyone has to decide what they do when the call a conflict against a characters interest and the player just says, "Screw it, I want my character to lose." And, as Jesse points out, this may not be just an issue of dealing with that players single request -- but can help decide CA for the whole table.

    All fairness to Jason, I think my issues with the Roach are local to myself and the table I played with.

    Presented with a system that asks to be treated like a game - and in the Roach it says "do this and you win, ha ha!" except in a much more deliciously urbane tone - I (and I don't think I'm completely alone here) latch on to the game-ness of the system. I can't help it. No matter how hard I try, I cannot let the game of a system alone. In traditional RPGs, I'm used to a disjoint between building a character for interest and for effectiveness. My abiding love for The Shadow of Yesterday comes from the fact that the one motive serves the other's purpose and vice versa - my most interesting TSoY characters are by the same virtue the most effective.

    Now, a lot of indie games have something like that characteristic. Their game is meant to encourage the desired play, or at least that's what I come to expect. It's mostly true of Prime Time Adventures - except for the flaw that bit Josh. You want fan mail to buy cards to swing events that you care about. So you want to get fan mail, which means delighting your fellow players. But even a colossal amount of fan mail won't guarantee anything - so you do your best to strategize, but you can get let down hard in the end. And worse than a boardgame with an ultimately random component, an RPG encourages you to invest emotionally in the results of resolution.

    So it can be hard, if you're of a strategic bent, to avoid wedding success to winning, which is an aesthetic thing, you know? And it seems like Jesse's original idea here is that his preference is for the two to be independent.

    You are so very much not alone. In our (delicious and wonderful!) TSoY game, people were averaging between ten and fifteen advances every three hour session. That's 50 - 75 experience points per session. Each.

    Why? Because we figured that the system existed to be gamed, and so we all racked up five meaty Keys immediately and started pounding the living hell out of them ... even (especially!) when it was the stupidest possible thing for our characters to do. Because that's who they were.

    TSoY (as you rightly point out) rewards that sort of aggressive attack.

    PTA (to my mind) rewards a different strategy: When we played, I (and by cooperation the others) were often trying to actually permanently remove from the system as many of the original budget points as possible. So we would max out our ability to give fan-mail and spend fan-mail, and insist that the producer be spending as much opposition as he was allowed, because we wanted the money to be moving as often as possible in the down-the-drain economic spiral. That turned out to make pretty good games, in a way that (apparently) driving the fan-mail economy toward your personal benefit doesn't do. I don't really have the PTA experience to say whether that's a real pattern or we're just an outlier.
