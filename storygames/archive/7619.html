\n
\n

\n\nGraham \n\n\n

\n
\n
\n
\n
\n While reading Houses of the Blooded, I'm struck that John Wick describes it as the "anti-D&D". To me, Houses of the Blooded is quite like D&D, in various ways, but the idea of writing a game in reaction to D&D obviously spurred him on.

I'm generally fascinated by games written in reaction to other games. The product isn't the opposite of the original game, but it's obviously a reaction to it. And your reaction to a game will be different from my reaction.

For example, I feel that bits of The Shadow Of Yesterday are a direct reaction to D&D: particularly the way players award themselves XP. But, if The Shadow Of Yesterday is a reaction to D&D, then Houses of The Blooded is a completely different reaction.

I playtested Galactic, recently, and both Steve and I reacted to it by writing the sci-fi game we wanted to play. My game is called Bleakworlds: in my head, it's a cross between Blake's Seven and Firefly.

Then, recently, I showed Bleakworlds to someone else. She's reacting by writing the Firefly game she wants to play.

It probably all goes on forever.

Graham

    \n Brand, yes. Often, I only work out what I'm saying halfway through the thread, I'm afraid. I do mean games which are a reaction against other games.

    There's a thought process which goes: "I like something about this! But I hate this thing about it! I'm going to do this game the way it should be done!". But, interestingly, your "way the game should be done" is different from someone else's way.

    So Tunnels and Trolls, Donjon and Shadow of Yesterday are both reactions against D&D (perhaps), but they fix different things, and are very different games. Trail Of Cthulhu and UnSpeakable, similarly. (I'm running out of convincing examples, now, of course. The ones I had upthread were better.)

    I've heard the story about Annalise being a reaction against Misspent Youth. Sounds right to me.

    Posted By: Graham W
    So ... Donjon and Shadow of Yesterday are both reactions against D&D (perhaps), but they fix different things, and are very different games.
    \nHmm. I've never thought of it that way. They're definitely reactions to D&D, and I'm pretty explicit in both about where the ideas came from, but I view them as positive reactions to D&D, not reactions against. The TSOY XP system is inspired mainly by AD&D 2nd edition, which gave each class specific XP awards for doing what they were supposed to do. (And, of course, by The Riddle of Steel, which can, for most purposes, be considered the real inspiration for TSOY.) Donjon started as a reaction to D&D - and the way we played it, which was more fun than the original - but ended up being a homage.

    Anyway, that sounds nit-picky, but I think there's a real distinction between reactions against and reactions to. There's games that actively try to be not-something or better-than-something, and then games that try to be something + a little more.

    \n I don\u2019t always think that reactionary games are a reaction against another game. An interesting phenomenon that I observed in the Setting Design contest that Thou Art But A Warrior grew out of was that there were four people who hacked Polaris, and yet each one of them focused on a different aspect of Polaris. I honed in on the TRAGEDY! part of Polaris, but someone else decided the push/pull between the Heart and Mistaken was what they thought was central to Polaris gameplay.

    Not that \u2018I hate this game, I could do it better\u2019 isn\u2019t a valid phenomenon. It obviously is alive and well. But I think that \u2018a game about [Subject X] is awesome, but I\u2019d rather focus on [Theme Y]\u2019 also happens quite a bit.

    \n Mage Blade (my oldest unfinished game) was a reaction to D&D's class-based system where a warrior couldn't learn to cast spells, and where a wizard couldn't use a sword.

    3.x fixed it somewhat with the way it handled multi-classing, and there were the various hybrid classes, but the idea always bothered me. When I started playing Ultima Online, I saw that mages with halberds in full-plate were the flavor of the year; They couldn't cast spells for very long, but it also didn't take long for them to kill you. All you had to do to be one such was grind spellcasting for stupid amounts of time and gold, then you were TEH UBER ROXXOR, so Mage Blade was also a reaction to that.

    Basically, unless you specifically chose otherwise, you were able to be equally adept at spellcasting and sword-play, and that was the way of the world. The concept has evolved a lot since then, and (I think) grown a lot more, but that's where it started.

    My other (likewise unfinished) games aren't reactions to/against anything, at least consciously. They borrow ideas (especially mechanical ideas) shamelessly from various other games, but I wouldn't think of them as reactionary.
