    \nA Flower for Mara opens and closes with a brief reading from the burial service from The Book of Common Prayer. Everyone stands around the grave, while the Director reads:\n
    Unto Almighty God we commend the soul of our sister Mara departed, and we commit her body to the ground; earth to earth, ashes to ashes, dust to dust; in sure and certain hope of the Resurrection unto eternal life, through our Lord Jesus Christ, at whose coming in glorious majesty to judge the world, the earth and the sea shall give up their dead; and the corruptible bodies of those who sleep in him shall be changed, and made like unto His own glorious body; according to the mighty working whereby He is able to subdue all things unto Himself.
    \nI call it Raising the Curtain and then Lowering the Curtain at the end.

    This works out, especially at the beginning, because it becomes the moment when you take your first step into your character. After all, the story opens with the burial of Mara. So Raising the Curtain is both an out-of-character ritual act and an in-character act.

    That's probably why Radio Lightning works out so well, Jason. It's not just a ritual act; it's also something that exists within the fiction.

    Seth Ben-Ezra
    Great Wolf

    \n Non-rules ritual that has existed in my gaming:

    When running Vampire: the Masquerade, I used to light candles and describe the sun setting over the city, and the awakening of the night life to open the session of play. When play was done for the night, I'd describe the rising of the sun, then I'd blow out the candles.

    To this day, whenever playing a game that may have "heroes", I tend to use some variation on the phrase "When last we left our insipid heroes..." and briefly describe the end-state of the last session, or ask someone else to describe it. It didn't really work well with my last group, as I would allow myself to tangent and ask if the characters had risen to intrepid yet.

    Alex Cherry began the "shenk" tradition in our old group. Whenever he would transition suddenly from one character's scene to another (suddenly as in in the middle of the action, leaving the scene on a cliff-hanger) he would do so by saying "shenk!", and pantomiming something suggestive of turning a camera from the active player to the next active player. The sound was supposed to suggest a quick mechanical movement, I guess.

    The most effective of these uses of ritual was the first. Looking back, I remember very little frustration due to out-of-game tangenting, or side-conversations. This was especially impressive considering that I hadn't yet developed the ability to say "No" to new people wanting to join, and at one point had some 12 players sporadically showing up to play, with a regular core of 5.

    \nAlso, in many games with stakes, conflicts are started with "Not only don't you X, but also, Y happens". Stolen from With Great Power ... .

    \nSimilarly, in many games without stakes, when the GM tries to do something and a character tries to stop it, the player reaches for the dice and says "We shall see what comes of it."
