\n Hello. This is my first post to Story Games.
\n
\nI\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve been thinking about a possible game I want to write lately. I figured I'd throw out what (little) I have so far for thoughts/opinions.
\n
\nThe game is tentatively called Grand Tour, and it is a game about Travel: the pre-trip hype, the excitement/trepidation of the actual tour, and the bummer of coming back home.
\n
\nThe players take the role of Travelers, the people going on the trip. These can be of background/age/race/religion/etc. just like the travelers one meets on the road. Certain groups make some sense (high school/university graduates on a post-graduation trip, young and seasoned hostel veterans, retiree adventurers) especially once one figures in the factor of the Tour (see below).
\n
\nPlayer characters would have a combination of hard and loose stats. For example, hard states include things like Endurance measures a Traveler\xe2\u20ac\u2122s energy levels and fatigue as the trip goes on; Resources measures a Traveler\xe2\u20ac\u2122s cash (probably pre-set at Tour creation?); and Stress measures both the initial amount of stress a Traveler begins the Tour with (you know, why one is taking a vacation for in the first place) and also the amount one accumulates as a party travels together, getting on each other\xe2\u20ac\u2122s nerves. Stress could be a sliding scale with Relaxation, with scores changing during play. Loose stats include player-defined Traits that define the Traveler (sort of like Aspects, if you will) and include things like who is the party clown, the stickler for schedule, the bar hopper, etc.
\n
\nThe skeleton upon which Grand Tour is played is, well, the Tour, and this gets constructed at the table much like a communal character. A Tour has stats as well, starting with Name: as simple as Cosmos Italy or Rick Steves Best of Europe, or imaginative like The Transfiguration Tour or The Hellhoot Adventure. (BTW, these are the names of 2 of the 4 trips I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve taken to Europe with family/friends.) The Name kind of defines also if the Tour is Fixed/Pre-packaged, or if it is Custom/Free-form, with this decision affecting some of the stats below.
\n
\nThen we have hard stats like Time (how long is the tour), Schedule (the trip itinerary which the Travelers will either strive to follow or blow to the four winds), Mobility (a measure of how much one has to move to hit all the locations in the schedule - interacts with a Traveler\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Stress and Resources), and Rigidity/Flexibility (maybe a sliding scale - how fixed is the itinerary, with pre-packaged tours being very much towards the rigid side, and free-form ones being more towards the flexible side, though there can be combinations).
\n
\nThe game would be played in a series of Phases, each part broken into parts and scenes. Phase 1 is the Pre-Trip phase, which includes Traveler and Tour creation, as well as defining all the variables for the particular tour and the establishment of the shared story of the Travelers.
\n
\nPhase 2 is the trip itself, with each location in the schedule being a part, and sub-locations being scenes. So for example, if you are doing a Tour of Paris (6 days), and your schedule includes the Louvre, Versailles (hey, a side trip!) and the Eiffel Tower, Paris in general would be a Part and the Louvre would be a scene, Versailles would be a scene, etc. This is still kind of loose, because there are sub-locations you can combo up (see Napoleon\xe2\u20ac\u2122s Tomb and the Eiffel Tower the same day) and others which could take a whole day if not more (the Louvre ), thus needed more than one scene for the sub-location.
\n
\nPhase 3 is the Post-Trip phase, in which the Travelers return home and may have to deal with the effects of post-tour funk or Bummer (this depends on the individual Traveler - some people comparmentalize their vacations and don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t let it affect their lives, while others take some time to gather back what they left behind, like me), or even if they returned at all!
\n
\nAs you can see, this still needs lots of work and thought, but I figured I\xe2\u20ac\u2122d put it out there to make it real, to stake a claim that I am doing this (mainly for myself). And to fish for thoughts. ;-)
\n
\nHave at it.

    \n This is interesting. I would suggest you consider writing it with an eye to enabling a number of different kinds of settings (travels in spaaaaaaace, fantasy travels, etc.). I'm thinking here of what I have heard one of the draws for Emily Care Boss's games, where for example you can play superheroes on a date, or a date between Jedi trainees, or whatever.

    I am also uncomfortable with the notion that coming home must necessarily be a bummer. The Joseph-Campbell-like arc of the archetypal travel story would have the return home a bittersweet affair, or even a longed-for one.

    I think you could also have fun with giving incentives for players to voluntarily introduce complications for reward. A big thing about trips is that all the things that horribly go wrong and make you miserable will be the things you remember fondly.

    \n What's QFT?

    I like the idea of a Memorability stat or additive pool (Nostalgia?) quite a bit. Perhaps building up enough of this will let you "level up" by increasing your stats for future trips. Great memories of past trips build your enthusiasm for future trips. If you work it right you might be able to get a blackjack-like thing going where if you get too bummed out during the trip you won't be as enthusiastic to go again, but if you don't have enough stuff go wrong, you won't get bonuses later.

    I like the idea of self-introduced challenge, but perhaps stuff introduced by other players is even better. There's a design precept somewhere that says that the same person should never be the author of and resolution to adversity. In fact I think this is a game that could work very well without a GM (or, to be more accurate, with distributed GM responsibilities).

    \n Got it. No problem. By the way I think I fubared the quote. It's actually, IIRC, players shouldn't be the author of their own adversity.

    I think this could present some interesting player-vs-player tension. Sometimes having players introduce one another's roadblocks causes player-to-player tension, but if the rules make it very clear that doing this gives the character additional resources, then you're literally helping your friend as you give him trouble.

    There's another neat thing which is how the people you're traveling with can get so much on your nerves and your interpersonal clashes can be part of what's memorable, too.

    I'm curious if you're trying to model the experience of being on a trip, or fiction (movies, books) about travel. You might want to decide that, as that could affect several design decisions.

    Oh! Another thing I wanted to suggest before, it might be fun to narrate what happens as flashbacks. I've seen stuff like this in Russell Collins's Contract Work, and Jared Sorensen's Inspectres. E.g., "So we started out on the train, and let me tell you, it was the smelliest train ride I'd ever taken. And it didn't help that Katy didn't see fit to shower the night before. . . ."

    The entire game could be in flashback, or you could have this as scene-framing introductions. The person whose turn it is to start a scene might say, "Oh my god do you remember that smelly train ride?" and then go into "live" roleplaying. You might also use Polaris's ideas of ritual phrases, so start a scene with "Remember when?" and end with some kind of button about what that meant to you(r character).

    \n Daniel,

    Your travel game idea sounds very entertaining, and I could see taking it in a variety of directions. If you're interested in some mechanical inspiration, you might take a look at an old draft of my upcoming pulp-inspired game "The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries." The premise of play is recounting the tale of an expedition to some exotic location from which the characters have just returned. It's got distributed GM authority (GMless) and is all told in flashback.

    The slide show angle to kick off flashbacks is keen, and I like the idea of giving attributes to the tour itself. For tours of actual places, it would be neat if you could integrate the use of travel guides into the rules - perhaps allowing players to derive bonuses for pulling out colorful facts, or have the order in which locations are laid out in the book affect the progression of the tour. Having a resource on hand would really help players who want to play a tour of Italy but have never been there. Kind of gives the game an educational element, too.

    I like the summary of play you bring in here, because that could be a very workable skeleton for Grand Tour: Player 1 frames the scene, maybe pulling everyone in, or some, or just herself; then Player 2 can jump in (whether randomly or by pre-determined order) with a new fact about the trip, using some sort of player currency to "pay" for their contribution, at which point Player 1 could take it and pass the baton, or someone else in the party (say Player 4) could throw in a new story element to either cement the just-offered new fact from Player 2 (in a sense pooling currency, possibly for a boon to be called in later on), or throw in a complication on Player 1 or 2 or both, at which point the conflict would resolve (the affected players would accept or reject the proposed facts/complications), and narrative rights pass to the next player.

    Two things jump out at me from that example:\n

    2. The issue then becomes, what does this currency represent, and how is it used? We know that it allows a player the chance to affect the story by offering a currency token to either insert a new fact or a complication; and that it allows a player to reject a proposed fact/complication by paying a token. How else could these tokens affect gameplay in a way that both encourages people to use them actively?
    \nIn #2 I'm thinking of Fate points in SotC, where in some AP thread and anecdotes, the problem of hoarding has crept up. There is always the option of specifying up-front, "Folks, this currency is meant to be spent, so do it!" but I'd rather find a mechanical way to stimulate their use in the game; in fact, the more the better!

    \n I like what I've heard/read about Mispent Youth, Robert.

    A use-it-or-lose-it mechanism could work, but I think there would have to be some sort of reward attached to the number of tokens you have at the time of the refresh in order to make it significant. Perhaps whatever score these tokens end up tracking could get an extra +1 per token spent in a story-enhancing way, be it as a new fact or complication. This would drive people to use the tokens, and accepting a token would mean you later have more tokens to affect someone else's narrative.

    Actually, now that I think about it, maybe it is by spending tokens that you gain control of the next narrative scene. This would drive people to throw facts/complications at the narrating player, and others to try to (in effect) bid over the other players.

    So, for example, Player 1 frames the intial scene, Player 2 throws in a fact, then Player 4 decides to bid over Player 2 with a complication (maybe paying one token per player bidding before him, so 2 in this case). Just as Player 1 is about to accept Player 4's complication (thus getting 2 tokens), Player 3 chimes in with a new fact, bidding over Players 2 and 4, thus paying 3 tokens; this makes it very attractive to Player 1. Player 2 decides to bow out and Player 4, as much as he'd like to, just doesn't have enough tokens to top Player 3's bid. Player 1 accepts Player 3's new fact, takes his 3 tokens, and now Player 3 has narrative control for the next scene.

    I see your point about competition and ill-feelings. My gut reaction is that, given the lighthearted nature of the game, it would take a very grumpy person to get to this point, but I understand there are people out there like that. I also understand I cannot control every player's reaction, so right my only thought is to address that with a very explicit, "Don't be a dick, you're all playing this game to have fun."

    \n I put together this summary to explain the game to people and it surprised me in that a pretty-clear structure of play emerged as I wrote it. I'd appreciate your thoughts on it:\n
    Grand Tour: A Game About Traveling is meant to be a light-hearted, fun game of shared storytelling focused on creating the experience of traveling on a tour, whether it is pre-packaged or custom-made, real or imaginary. Players take the role of Travelers, and together they create Tour, engaging in a group dynamic where one player narrates an event in the tour and other have the option of accepting the event, adding a fact to it, or throwing a complication. The object of the game is to go through the tour, racking up Memorability points without getting Burnt Out or having more Stress than you started with.
    \nAside from this, this morning I had this idea about adding a stat to the Traveler's sheet: aside from Traits that define the character's personality/idiosyncracies, I thought about adding a stat called Passion. This is a one-word Aspect-like trait that defines what is your main interest during the trip. At the moment I am thinking of making a defined list of 5 or 6 traits, things like History, Culture, Party, Rest. By choosing one, you are declaring what is your main interest during the trip, what you most hope to see/do.

    Passions also engender conflict, both in terms of complications and PvP; e.g. say a Party PC wants to go bar-hopping at night, while a Culture PC wants to check out a small exhibition down the street - this could simply split the party up or force these two Travelers into a conflict of wills to see who sways the rest of the group. This kind of thing could be tied up to how much Stress you have/generate as well, providing a mechanical way to simulate those moments when people who have been traveling together for a while, regardless of how good friends they are, bash heads and possibly come to blows. This is very off-the-top-of-my-head, but I wanted to throw it out there for later reference.

    Posted By: Highmoontwo Travelers into a conflict of wills to see who sways the rest of the group.
    \nHave you watched the Amazing Race, much? You see the "traveling companions butting heads" dynamic constantly. My only concern about the Culture, History and Rest passions is that they might not be as immediately interesting to play out as a party scene would be. Here's my idea of how to solve this:

    Once the group decides an activity, you play it out for a little bit until complications are introduced by the player whose traveler wanted to do an alternative activity.

    So let's say Bob's character Professor Bottomsworth wants to see the fascinating dancing rituals of a local tribe. (A culture activity) But Alice's character Natasha would rather just go to the local discotheque and party the night away. (A party activity) They ask for a vote from the remaining members of the group, the professor wins. The group goes to watch the local ritual.

    Now, because Natasha wanted to do something different, Alice can introduce complications to the activity. These don't necessarily need to be Natasha's fault, but they do affect the entire group, Natasha included. Such complications can include:

    "A member of the group looks exactly like a statue of this tribe's deity."
    "We've deeply offended the tribe's chief somehow."
    "You've fallen in love with a woman who's about to sacrifice herself."

    At first blush, this might just sound like a recipe for hostile sour grapes, but because the complications affect the entire group, I'd imagine there'd be less "Man, I wanted to party instead of this boring crap!" and more "Ooh! Now we can have hijinks!" As a matter of fact, if everyone agrees on one activity, that means no one could introduce complications, so you'd glaze over that scene with a single sentence like. "The dance ritual passes uneventfully." and then you go on to the next leg of the journey.

    Anyhoo, this is a very cool concept for a game.

    Grand Tour: The Big Three

    So I finally got around to answering these! Next step, first draft.\n
      \n
    1. What is your game about?
      Grand Tour is a game about travel: getting a group together, planning a tour, taking the trip and reminiscing about it afterwards.
      2. \n
    2. How is your game about that?
      Grand Tour includes phases representing the stages of a trip as described above. Character generation results in the Travelers, who then cooperate to create a Tour, who then engage in shared narrative to describe the events of the trip itself. During this phase, players can narrate, add facts or throw in complications, seeking to rack up Memorability while avoiding Burnout and/or arguments that could make it a trip to forget.
      3. \n
    3. What behaviors will your game reward?
      Grand Tour rewards a shared narrative experience where different players all combine to tell one story by awarding Memorability points to a scene based on the initial narration, and any added facts and/or complications thrown in. Players are encouraged to work together towards the creation of a memorable trip, which always include some good and not-so-good times, and penalizes players who try to hog the spotlight, or set out to create a narration where everything goes wrong, by awarding Burnout points.
      4. \n
    \nI'm not entirely convinced with what I wrote, but it does give me a clearer vision of what I want to achieve. Have at it.
