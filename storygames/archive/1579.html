    1) SIS ("Shared Imaginary Space")

    2) It implies that you're making a thing, a "space" where there are "objects". Really, what you're doing is making fiction. Part of that fiction is the setting and imaginary props, but we're not all imagining the same thing (ask around the table if you don't agree â€” what color is my character's clothing? What's the color of the princess' hair?), and the important thing about it is that we're all contributing to the parts we do share: the details relevant to the parts of the situation we care about. It's the fictional situation that matters, not an imagined, neutrally-interested "space.

    3) "Fiction".

    1. The Impossible Thing Before Breakfast

    2. It places responsibility for on-going bad experiences on a concept that 1) I find legitimate as a useful metaphor and 2) the text for which may not actually appear in the game that gets blamed for it.

    a. It's originally described as the absurd foundation for (some) incoherent games. Whether or not the named text actually appears (i.e. V:tM may be full of railroady advice--but a the phrase has been used to describe many, many RPGs that don't necessiarly contain railroady advice (GURPS)).
    b. While the "concept as a basis" need not be include the actual text, the actual text itself is invariably attacked but is, IMO, a legitimate, if extremely high level, description of the GM/Player responsibility divide for setting up games as varried as D&D and DitV (the GM does some things that are pretty darn author-like in setting up the game, the PCs are, yes, the main characters).
    c. When invoked it is often used to place the responsibility for some long-term stretch of bad gaming on the text of the book itself and not the people that actually returned again and again to the bad gaming. This is because the one-sentence description at fault must be read absurdly literally to make it paradoxical (although, for example the "cops and robbers with dice and rules" short-form description of RPGs doesn't get hammered because there exist games where PCs don't play cops).
    d. If you point out that the one-sentence description must be read absurdly literally (i.e. the reader must have no idea what RPG play is actually like with lots of people talking and such--but must, instead, assume that the GM writes the entire story before hand, using the PCs as main characters--but the players sit there [i]mutely[/i] while someone presumably reads the story--something that even the most dysfunctional groups very, very rarely do) they are told they have simply "re-interperted" the text rather than reading it "correctly."

    3. "Bad GMing Advice [of this type, i.e. railroady/illusionist/etc.] for the book's text. Or maybe: "Power-struggle issues I had/have with gaming because of the idea that [explanation, i.e. 'that I am expected to conform to a pre-planed plot even though that plot is never made clear to me.']

    -Marco

    One hundred thousand cheers for Joshua. Screw "SIS." There is a word in English, already, with the right connotations even: "Fiction."

    And for me personally:

    1) Narrativism

    2) It's a neologism, to start with. It has no obvious meaning to a reasonably well educated English speaker.
    Second, it's a hideous neologism. Say it out loud: It sounds like a bad cough trailing off into pleghm gurgles at the end: "nar-ra-ti-vism." Even "Simulationism" doesn't have that stuttering a-a-i-i vowel pattern.
    Third, its closest approach to standard English is to the word "narrative," which is precisely what the theory is not talking about: A "narrative" is the description of a sequence of events that does not have to have any particular unifying theme, and which certainly does not have to be any kind of "story" in the "Nar" sense of "someone makes an important choice, and there are real consequences."

    3) I don't know, honestly.
    Vincent Baker's "thematic play" is significantly clearer, but still not very clear.
    Ron Edward's "Story Now!" is a lot punchier, and even a lot clearer (okay, we want to make a story, and we don't just want to waffle about saying stuff, we want something arresting, now!). But it's a slogan, to be accompanied by fists upraised, and it feels silly to use in a theory discussion.
    In my own developing understanding, I'm toying with the term "dilemma games" to describe both "Narrativism" and "Gamism" in their common emphasis on the real people playing having to make non-obvious decisions with clear consequences (in contrast to "Simulationism," which is about group consensus in celebration of the material and therefore wants each decision to be as smooth and one-clear-answer as possible), with Gamism being "tactical dilemma games" and Narrativism being "moral dilemma games." But that's a term I just made up, with the obvious disadvantages.

    October 2006
    1) Fun.

    2) "Fun" is something that's promoted as the central reward of roleplaying, the end result, the stuff why people play. "Fun" also indicates something that you enjoy, that you have fun, that is cool and exciting and all that. "Fun" is a misleading term, promoting light-hearted, funny stuff and misses a whole lot of darker, more emotional and in a sense, intense stuff. "Fun" can be used as a safety net for not going where the disturbing, psychological, emotional issues lay, or at least keep them at arm's length; players are having "fun" while their characters are not. It's not "fun" to be angry, jealous, to hate something, cry, cheat, or to have someone you care to die, but it's still very meaningful and very rewarding. "Fun" is a double misnomer because some games look into these issues and still talk about "fun".

    "Fun" is also something connected to "everybody participates", "everyone gets the his place in the limelight", "everyone has an equal say what happens" which is then a whole another discussion because people think what's "fun" for them is "fun" for everyone, and no other kind of "fun" can exist.

    3) Experience. You can experience something that can't be considered "fun" and get a lot of it. Also, Experience is a good term is a sense of rewards; just leave the "points" out from the end of it, and you have a reward mechanism.

    OK, I've got one: Exploration.

    Why? Well, for starters, it may be a bit of hard feelings; when I first discovered the Forge and the Theory Essays, I was emailing Ron with questions, and said something about "exploring moral issues," in reference to Narrativism. And Ron came back and said "you can't use "explore" that way in the Big Model. And the poetic, creatively-restate-for-asthetics'-sake part of my brain went, "whaddyamean I can't use 'explore' that way?!" I eventually came to see how a rigorous and consistent vocabulary can help discourse, but I bring it up simply to highlight what I think is a pretty common way in which people become jargon-irritable ("Jargon-intolerant"? "Irritable jargon syndrome"?).

    The bigger reason I don't like it, though, is that it's kind of unclear what is being "explored," in what way it is "explored," etc. Exploration implies discovery, but the discovery of a Premise-decision, like "Oh, that's hoe much he cares about duty/friendship/whatever," can't be called Exploration, as Ron told me back in that old Email. Exploration as the stuff that the whole model floats on is easily confusable with Exploration as a Sim priority, in which the revealing, fleshing-out, oberervation-in-action, etc. of the fictional world are valued in themselves, as opposed to Gam or Nar in which that stuff is only a springboard for Saying Something, or Stepping on Up. The fact that Exploration-as-Foundation and Exploration-as-Priority are so conflatable has led to thearories like Zilchplay and the Beeg Horseshoe, and an ongoing controversy about those conceptions.

    Solution: "Imagining." We roleplay by collaboratively Imagining. All the agendas are supported by a foundation of Imagining. I think it works. We can even keep Exploration as a Sim descriptor: in Nar the priority is thematic statement, in Gam the priority is cooperative or competitive achievement, and in Sim the priority is exploring the collective imagining. Hmm, I think this works. In fact, I think this may even be a more profound revelation than I thought when I started. It's certainly got ME thinking differently and deeper about Sim. Whaddyaknow?

      Posted By: Mike Holmes3. "Use of GM authority to remove player ability to engage in their prefered creative agenda."
      Hmm, I dunno. I would prefer a definition of 'railroading' that solely depended on the GM's actions, with the followup that this is 'bad' if it blocks the player's pursuit of their preferred creative agenda. Or, to be less wordy, it's bad if it prevents player fun satisfaction.

      So, something like:

      Railroading: Use of GM authority to take the story through a series of pre-planned scenes or scenarios, often in pursuit of a pre-planned climax. This can lead to gaming dysfunction when player satisfaction hinges on the creation of scenes, scenarios, and/or climaxes of their own devising that are incompatible with those planned by the GM. If the GM furthermore actively supresses the creation of non-pre-planned scenes, the dysfunction can be more severe. Examples of techniques that can be used to facilitate functional play when using railroading include, for the GM, using player contributions as the connections between the pre-planned scenes or including peripheral scenes created via player input, and, for the player, creation of 'peripheral' scenes, embellishment of the pre-planned scenes or simple appreciation of the scenes themselves.

      ... how are you going to talk about them if you remove your words for them?

      Ah, you see this is where I fundamenetally disagree with a lot of people. I don't think that terminology is always a usesful thing in and of itself. To lapse into a scientific analogy, I feel that assigning specific names to people's creative agendas (and for what it's worth I have no problem with the phrase "creative agenda") is like citing your result to too many significant figures in a physics problem. You're implying a degree of knowledge that you do not in fact have.

      Something Ron frequently says when people criticise his terminology is "the phenomenon came first..." this is disingenuous. What in fact comes first is a set of similar looking phenomena, and we have no way of deciding whether they are really the same thing or not. If somebody then slaps a piece of terminology over that set of phenomena it creates the false impression that they are all the same sort of thing.

      You know what? The Creative Agendas: mutually exclusive, or merely heirarchical? discussion is convincing me that "Creative Agenda" is a pretty bad term. At least the specific "currently recognized" CA's are, in exactly the way that Joel originally suggested: it's problematic to clear communication. They're the terms I most often seen used to tell people that they don't know what their own play is, or how to have fun (I'm consciously refusing to use a overblown phrase like "achieve satisfaction"), and are perennially misunderstood. Partially because the Provisional Glossary definition is vague (and refers to 3 other lengthy articles). Partially because the intent of the players in implied in the distinction. Partially because it's the easiest thing to build camps of role-playing around and thus becomes an identifier (along with the pressure to conform and eject.) And quite possibly because the distinctions are questionable in the first place.

      I'm going to break the thread rules:

      1.) I hate any jargon which requires pre-existing knowledge of its definition and/or history.
      Creative Agenda, GNS, stance, etc.
      [person]'s nth [synonym of rule] of gameplay.
      Clinton's 4th axiom of gameplay.
      Ron's 45,283 terminology of gameplay.

      2.) Jargon which is specific to the RPG community, and which depends on you having read other "historically important" threads in the past, is exclusionary.

      If my goal is to reach out to new people, and help empower them to self-publish (which I KNOW is part of Ron's goal) then saying "Maybe narrativist goals are achieved better through conflict resolution than task resolution, considering that SIS is..." is entirely counter-productive.

      3.) My solution:
      If you are going to use a term, rule, jargon, etc... then you, by law, must link the reader to the definition you are using.

      So if I want to say "SIS" I have to provide a link or footnote which says "SIS = Shared Imagined Space. This is..."

      That way, people would be forced to either (a.) provide people with the tools necessary to undestand what they are saying, or (b.) actually describe their thoughts instead of just buzzword-coding them.

      That's my utopic vision.
