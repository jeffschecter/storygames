I'm excited to be doing it, but I'm also just as excited to see what the response is from the Strategicon attendees. Every Dungeon World session we've run in the previous couple of cons has gone down really well.

    \n DW GMs, please post your schedule ahead of time either here, on Hamish's blog, on twitter, etc. so those of us who run games will know when not to schedule their own stuff.     P.S. You'll have no problem filling your games but how do we market this to the traditionalist rpgers at the con (and here I'm talking about the Pathfinder/4e/D&D forever folks). I mean, DW is essentially OSR done right so how do we get those other folks interested in OSR interested in attending DW. As one of the people who used to run the con and a long time supporter of the local con scene I'm looking at your DW experience to help grow the rpg side. I know there's a certain amount of f 'em attitude towards the traditionalists (less charitably, the grognards) but I really do think there's a contingent of good local gamers who want a cool dungeon-crawl experience and are willing to experiment (and may not be yet braving indie-story games overall.)

    Posted By: skinnyghostOne thing that I find really fun and awesome for that exact crowd - the D&D / Pathfinder folks - is taking a classic adventure (I've run Village of Hommlett, Against the Giants and Keep on the Borderlands) and playing it out using DW.
    \nI ran Dwellers of the Forbidden City using DW at Strategicon in September, and it was great. Living Dungeon World will definitely have its share of references to classic D&D names, places, and so forth. For example, the first adventure I'm running is probably going to take place mostly in a ruined moathouse on the outskirts of a village, a la Hommlett. And while the second probably won't reference a specific module as overtly as that, I can pretty much guarantee it'll at least name-check some Greyhawk stuff. Not that any of this takes place in the World of Greyhawk, of course -- it's all a big pastiche. A lot of the specifics, like the names of deities, knightly orders, powerful kingdoms, etc., will emerge during play, and provide something for us to build on next time.

    Also, without going into any details, the Big Bad at the end will certainly be familiar to anyone with even a modicum of familiarity with D&D, especially the old-school stuff.

      Posted By: John HarperKeep saying things about this.
      \nThe problem is that there's so much to say. (And also that we're all beat, I think.) It was the equivalent of cramming four or five weeks' worth of two gaming groups' play sessions into three days of gaming. I think the only way we can really cover it with any sense is if each GM recaps his sessions individually, then we put those together into a single rambling narrative.

      I will say, though, that we had two very cool Last Breath bargains. In one, the being offering him a second chance at life, Graz'zt, was the Demon Prince indirectly responsible for the slaughter of his entire village, and the terms of the bargain were that the PC would become his servitor. What was awesome about it to me was that the player really gave it some serious thought before turning Graz'zt down. It was the right thing to do, IMO, and it was just great to see him stick to his character's principles -- he had been all about avenging his village and family -- instead of metagaming it. He chose to not play at all instead of playing a compromised version of the character he'd made two days before. That was awesome.

      In the other, the entity offering the dying PC the deal was Blackrazor. I think I need say no more about that.

      \n Basically, what we did was write down a brief note every time someone added something to the fiction and put these notes on the board. Mike organised it nicely one evening (I think between Friday and Saturday).

      The board was in the same room as the two tables, so we could step over and take a look whenever we needed to, and we could look at it between games for inspiration for the next game.

      We had a list of between-game moves (mostly created by Colin/Steerpike) that players could make to build strongholds, collect followers, supplicate the gods, and engage in personal business or magical research. Mike made a bunch of magic item cards, some of which had corresponding GM threat cards.

      I had big plans of writing up a debrief post, but I have a bit of a writing backlog at the moment.

      \n Also we had a pre-planned "spine" for the game. We knew the game was about killing Orcus and then we broke that down.
      \n
      \nPrologue - Villager Game, 1 Slot - Something Bad Is Happening With Undead
      \nChapter 1 - First "Real" Characters 2 Slots - Holy Shit Orcus!
      \nChapter 2 - 2 Slots - Finding The Wand of Orcus (Skull & Rod)
      \nChapter 3 - 2 Slots - Destroying The Wand of Orcus (Skull & Rod)
      \nChapter 4 - 2 Slots - Kill Orcus & His Earthly Avatar.
      \n
      \nSo each GM had a specific focus for each of his slots. From there it was a matter of making sure you know who went before you and who came after so you could co-ordinate. For example the PCs actually failed to retrieved the Rod in the "Find The Rod" scenario so the "Destroy The Rod" GM had to work around it. I ran the "Find The Skull" scenario and the interesting tid bit was that the demi-lich whose skull it was ended up trapped inside, so the Destroy the Skull GM had a bit of fun with that.

      Posted By: tony dowlerAmazing! How did you do it? How do I run something like Living Dungeon World at my own local con? What things did you do right/wrong that you'd pass on to someone else trying to do the same thing?
      \nBig question! You're talking about the whole event, right?

      A couple months before the con (BTW, guys, Gamex is, like, two months away...), all of us had a meeting where we worked out what the overarching story would be and who was going to run which sessions. This was invaluable. We could've continued to talk via email, but man, that shit took forever. In a few hours of face-time we managed to get everything worked out.

      The idea was that there'd be two tracks of games, more or less. Players didn't have to stick with one or the other, and there was a certain amount of relevance between them, but one was a little more consistently focused from the start on the Big Story than the other. Colin had always wanted to have a let's-kill-Orcus game, so that was the big goal of the weekend. It wasn't revealed right away, but I'd say by Saturday afternoon everyone was dealing with Orcus one way or another. The final two sessions occurred simultaneously in-game, with one table fighting Orcus' avatar on the Prime Material, and the other going after Orcus himself in his palace in the Abyss.

      Setting up the basic focus of each session was key, I think. Nobody dictated to any GM what was going to happen in their game, though. It was just a solid idea of what each session would be about, like "This is where they find the Rod of Orcus" or "This is where they destroy the skull." (We even had one that was basically "This is just a classic fightin'-goblins D&D adventure with no direct connection to the big story.") If things didn't go down as planned, like if the rod wasn't found or the skull wasn't destroyed, that was okay -- we all conferred between sessions to make needed adjustments to whatever we'd planned.

      We had five GMs who ran a total of ten sessions (nine planned, one "breakout" game to accommodate some alternates). I'd originally wanted this crazy, pie-in-the-sky thing where we ran three games in every slot for the whole convention, but my fellow conspirators wisely talked me down from that. However, the demand ended up being so great we probably could've handled another four-player game in each of our chosen time slots and/or expanded into additional time slots. And I imagine the demand will only be higher next time.

      Speaking of demand, we didn't do a ton of advance marketing, and frankly the convention itself let us down a little in that regard (by failing to mention LDW in any of the emails sent out to the mailing list in advance of the con). We mentioned it on blogs, discussion fora, and on Twitter, but I guess we didn't get hooked in with Strategicon itself soon enough to utilize them effectively. We had a half-dozen or so one-page fliers to post up around the RPG and registration areas, but I'm pretty sure only one of them saw the light of day. Which, as it turned out, was apparently enough.

      I'm trying to think of anything else (and I know I'm cross-posting this with Hamish and Jesse, so there's some overlap), because I'm pretty sure I'm leaving out something big and obvious, but oh well.
