\n I got the bug for roleplaying on a school adventure trip. The location was on an Island off the coast of Plymouth called Drakes Island. Pic
\nD&D; with its fantastic imagery on the box Box Lid
\nThe books and rules for creating characters and fantastical stories held an aura like a magical tomb.
\nThese books just needed to be read or understood correctly to go adventuring in this promised land.
\n
\nThe reality has been fun, with meeting lots of like minded people but,
\n
\nOne thing that stands out is the need to make these stories more. Instead of stories like The Emperor's New Clothes
\nWe dress up stories around a frame work of rules and sometimes it works!
\nSome times it doesn't and we see through our invisible worlds into the naked truth.
\n
\nThe dressing up is difficult it doesn't take long before it can feel all made up, and for someone to say so.
\nCompelling stories off the cuff ? on the wing! need something to make them fly.
\n
\nIf you look at D&D; it fly s, but its a predictable flight path.
\n
\nYou have your captain and passengers, why would the passengers want to fly the plane its scary right!
\n
\nHow do you begin to design a game or fictional story game which involves all.
\n
\nCan it be done? what prevents it, e,g, knowing the plot in advance.

    \n I'm not an authority, but I'm pretty sure this had been done, though it depends on what you mean by "story".

    Many games like this feature no specific GM, with all players being equal, though some have a GM, but give the players some GM powers (constantly or occasionally).

    I had an amazing experience with "Remember Tomorrow", by which we produced a very interesting experience of near-future, cyberpunk drama with AIs, replicants, street-gangs and government conspiracies, and it never felt in danger of exposing itself, because, I think, "Remember Tomorrow" is almost all "clothes", all the way to the center.
