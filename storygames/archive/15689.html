\n I want a LARP where all the mechanics are embedded inside QR Codes we wear as buttons (or stickers or badges).



Note: QR Codes can be in different colors, skewed, and rotated and still work. So they don't have to look boring. Generating QR Codes is very easy.

The URL you scan can have variables attached to it. So when I scan your button, it might link me to:

http://www.yourAwesomeLARP.com?armor=5

Then simply have the website grab the variable, run it through your game's system, and tell me what to do next. It could even ask you questions before giving you the final result. I scan the button, it asks me how tired / insane / hurt / powered up I am, and then it tells me what awful or amazing thing happens to me. It could even play an audio file, a video, or have your phone call the GM, an NPC, another player, or a monster! Coding this isn't too difficult. You can learn to code right now!

You can even create your own virtual worlds by using custom Google Maps! Playing in a park, city, or camp ground? Look up the location on Google Maps and fill it with virtual markers representing all fictional places to see in your game. Here's how.

    \n Yeah, I've augmented my larps with QR codes and it is awesome. For a science fiction larp, we had a Doctor Who analog who used a smartphone as a combination sonic screwdriver/tricorder. You could scan any character or larp prop and get a readout about the item. You could also implement QR codes as a wizard's detect magic spell or any other magical senses you need. (There's an old thread on this forum covering the game in more detail.)

    QR codes are supereasy to implement with online generators and such.


    Having the entire run by QR code is feasible, provided you have as many smartphones as players.

    I'm not much of a LARPer, but I've definitely thought about applying QR codes to tabletop RPG sheets.

    Imagine a slightly larger sheet, with significant parts of the sheet (that is, significantly important or perhaps hard to grasp) having QR codes next to them. Scan it in, you're taken to a Youtube video of the designer either:

    1) Straight up telling you the rules of whatever section you may be looking at, or
    2) (given that the rules are written elsewhere) the designer tells you the in-game nuances or emergent behaviors of that section.
