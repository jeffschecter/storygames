    \n I've had the good fortune to be able to answer this question a few times recently, so I have some thoughts at the ready.

    The first and most important thing you need is a body of enthusiastic volunteers to run games. Once you have a core group of people you can structure the rest of your event around that, and then draw in more volunteers as you go. This group should include one or more dedicated organizers to pull everything together and a team of hosts who are essential for greeting players and funneling them into games.

    The next thing you need is the cooperation of the convention staff\u2014usually the gaming event organizer. At the very least, you need for them to provide you with a gaming space (tables, chairs, a host table, etc.). Depending on the convention, they may offer free badges for running games, and possibly other perks (free hotel rooms, etc.). Anything you can get them to offer will help you recruit volunteers.

    You'll want to think about the focus of your event\u2014the usual GoD event is tabletop RPG focused, with a strong emphasis on small press and independent games. Outside of that, the games that are run are determined by what the GMs want to offer, and what the players opt to play from that selection.

    You may want to find out which retailers and/or publishers will be selling those games at the show and see about coordinating a connection between their efforts and your own. I like to be able to point excited players to the booths where they can buy the games, and likewise have the retailers tell them where GoD is so they can try the games out.

    This last bit points at the final issue, which is letting people know where you are and what you're doing. We've begun to build some momentum at shows like Gen Con, Origins, and PAX, which means that returning attendees know to look for us. At the start, however, we tried to get the word out by creating flyers and distributing them to the retailers and convention staff. We've also used volunteer contributions to print up banners so that players can find and identify us in the sea of tables.

    I hope that's helpful. Let me know if you have other more specific questions!

    One problem is that UK Games Expo is more of a boardgame than an RPG one-meaning that it\u2019s not on a lot of indie RPers\u2019 radars- and the emphasis has traditionally been on trad games like CoC and Traveller etc.
    \nIn that case, I'd suggest having your GMs choose a few games that will be easy hooks for that crowd- if they're looking for Cthulhu, hit them with Cthulhu Dark, or a story in that vein using another light system. Games that have a strong visual presence on the table (Dread, Fiasco, others) will catch the attention of board and card game players.

    Another tip is to be conservative about your goals and the number of tables you can fill. It's better to have a few, full tables and extra GMs working as hosts (or even playing in games) than to have lots of tables left empty, or worse, being taken over by squatters.

    Good luck!
