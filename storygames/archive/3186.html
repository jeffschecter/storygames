Are people ENTITLED to be cool?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nTonyLB \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2007 \n edited May 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I can make people cool. First, I get myself in the mindset to expect that their contributions will contain nuggets of raw awesome coolness. Then, in that mindset, I dig around mentally until I find those nuggets, pull them out, polish them and applaud them. I come at things from an angle that will make it easier to do that. And so on, and so forth.
\n
\nI consider this set of skills, at this point, pretty fundamental. I believe they are among the most important skills that an RPG player can learn.
\n
\nBut recently, I find myself questioning: Should you always be using these skills? Do you have a moral obligation to make somebody cool if you can, no matter the effort involved?
\n
\nI know the benefits. I know how much it improves the game, I know how much it enhances the camaraderie at the table. It is a large, but not infinite amount. It would take a really serious cost in effort and spent time in order to make me think that it's not worth it.
\n
\nBut I've also encountered players (mostly, it must be confessed, at conventions) who I think, in retrospect, were not worth the effort. I spent time making them cool that I could have been spending more productively on making other players cool, or on being cool myself. From the viewpoint of purely "How good would the game have been?" I made an objective mistake.
\n
\nSo there's my question, really: If I have a choice between:
\n(a) saying (more politely, but still) "No dude, you suck, and your imagination sucks, and until you prove otherwise I'm not going to bother trying to make you cool" and having a NOTABLY BETTER GAME (even after including them feeling like the suck) vs.
\n(b) spending my effort to make a desperately boring person as marginally cool as they can be, and having a NOTABLY WORSE GAME (even after including all the factors about how much better they feel, and how much happier people are at the table)
\n
\n... am I morally obligated to choose (a)? Or is this some Geek Fallacy, writ large?

    \n Ah, that's interesting. I've seen this question in improv: experienced improvisers ask themselves "Should they improvise with beginners?".

    I think this:\n
    I can make people cool. First, I get myself in the mindset to expect that their contributions will contain nuggets of raw awesome coolness.
    \n...is a good mental attitude to have, even though we know it's not always true.

    Generally, the answer in improv (slightly adapted for RPGs) is...

    1, You've got every right to play with who you want and
    2. Often, you don't want to play with people, because they suck but
    3. Sometimes people who you think suck don't actually suck, they're just inexperienced and
    4. Because they're unfamiliar with how to do it, they're often surprising and exhilirating to play with and
    5. You might also enjoy educating them into doing it right but
    6. Sometimes you can't be bothered, you just want to play with people who don't suck, and that's OK too.

    Graham

    \n I make a big, big distinction between my regular gaming and convention gaming, and this is one of the big differences. When I play at cons, and especially when I run games at cons, I am there to play with people I don't usually play with. The focus is not really on the game, it's on the players. So at convention, yeah, I'm all about digging around to get that nugget of awesome, even when building up the single player's contribution means a net loss of quality for the game as a whole.

    In my regular gaming, though, I am really fucking picky on players and rather mercenary on what gets the most attention. There, the game is the thing, and generally speaking the players don't need the extensive digging around to get to the gold: they're selected beforehand to not need a bloody intervention in order to bring the awesome.

    So for me at least, it boils down to what kind of experience I'm after at that specific game. Sometimes it's worth making everybody at the table awesome; sometimes everybody at the table is there to make the game awesome.

    \n One of the true pleasures of gaming for me is in being surprised at what I create. For me, this most often manifests as unexpected synergy between players, a moment where you jointly produce something far better than either of you could have planned. I think one of the best things you can do to make this happen is to look for it. This isn't necessarily a great effort. Sometimes it is just a matter of expecting to see it, adopting the attitude Tony expresses.

    I don't find there to be anything inherently wrong about being selective in where you look, but I do think it should be balanced out by the realization that a lot of the cool in gaming comes when you least expect it.

    As an aside, I had to laugh a bit at this topic. As someone who games with Tony, I have on (considerably) more than one occasion inadvertantly done something that triggers Tony to run at full speed, like I was in some sort of relay race I didn't even know existed. Sometimes I don't even know what I did *after* the fact. Then when Tony, with his typical generosity of spirit, credits me with all the cool, I'm left to stammer "Um. Er. Yeah....Go me!"

    \n I think this is the "play with people who you can be social with" principle at work. That is, I don't think it's at all about the quality of the creation that people make. Not one iota. I think it's all to do with whether or not you care about the person making the creative effort. Because, in the end, creation of theme is all about revealing a little about ourselves. If you like the person even a little, you'll like their revelations, which makes that process that you have to make those inclusions interesting.
    \n
    \nAm I off-base here?
    \n
    \nEverybody is an expert role-player in my opinion... I enjoy playing with anyone I like.
