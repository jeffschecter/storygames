\n Disclaimer: The subject of this post is far more inflammatory than its content. I learned my rhetorical style from those college posters that do something like "Beer (or Boobs)! Now that I have your attention. . . .")

I was reading a thread on rpg.net about how some people consider art to be a waste of money and unnecessary in an RPG. I disagree with this. I don't know why I do. I think, "knowing what art does will help me when I decide how to put it into my game." I think, "I'd better ask people about this."

So here I am. What purpose does art serve in an RPG? When one is commissioning art for one's game, how does one decide what the art will be, where to put it, how much of it to have, how big, etc.

Incidentally, I'm not very interested in 'how much should I spend?" discussions. I'm more looking for a utilitarian take on this.

    \n As a consumer, I find that art helps to contextualize the words on the page. When I picked up and started flipping through Rifts: Atlantis, for example, the jaw-dropping Metzla illustrations by Newton Ewell really sold me on the concept. Frankly, a text description (accompanied by my attempt at a verbal description in-game) wouldn't really have done the concept justice. The images really drove the world (and its creatures) home. The same is true for most of the RPGs that I've really enjoyed.

    As a designer, I rely on art to do the same thing for the people that buy my books. Just describing some of the angels in Malice wouldn't really get the point across, I'm afraid. The artwork takes text on a page and brings it to life. It helps to give context to the world that's been created.

    When I commission artwork, I focus on large, full-page illustrations that get the attention of the players. I try to set it up so that the image is matched by the text on the following page, so as to augment and complement the material.

    I try to focus on art that conveys the more impossible elements of the setting. Rather than show ordinary men and women, I hire artists to draw demons and angels and otherworldly situations. Personally, I'm only interested in that kind of material when I buy games -- after all, I know what a guy with a sword looks like. Unless the picture is just amazing, it's more effective to show something unreal or imaginary. In my estimation.

    Some people consider art to be a waste of money. They probably won't like my games. Some people think that art adds to a game. They may or may not like my games.

    \n Art can provide a concreteness to the world behind the game: the tone of the illustrations can help here, but also, the artefacts portrayed in the illustrations. That's what a Belurian Herder looks like? Cool! That's what she wears; that's what her spears look like; that pouch is how she carries her pemmican and her chewing tobacco; that's how she wears her lineage scars and her captured feather trophies.

    Illustrations can also give you a strong idea of what the climate looks like, what the buildings look like, and the urban planning.

    For an excellent example of what I'm talking about, I'd suggest looking at Artesia. Smylie has a really solid grip on what his world looks and feels like; it helps that he's a talented professional illustrator, and it also helps that his entire rulebook uses illustrations from one unified voice. For another example, I'd suggest Cadillacs and Dinosaurs in this regard: without Mark Schultz' original comic, and the liberal use of his drawings in GDW's licensed game, would anyone really be able to get the vibe that's vital to this setting? Certainly not from the rather dusty GDW house-system rules...

    Given these two examples, I'd say that, as a small-press publisher, one thing that you can do that will help your game immensely is, if at all possible, use art from only a small number of illustrators who you know get your game. One illustrator, if possible. I'd say a unified voice is much more important than having lots and lots of art, so if you have illos from only one person, you can get by with less (in which case, use them well): look at Mortal Coil for example.

    \n One more comment, different from my points above.

    I think art can serve two fundamental purposes in a game book -- as flavour, or as functional reference. Good games use both.

    As an excellent example of the latter, I point to three games. Artesia, for some of the "homeland" description sections has little thumbnail sketches of what denizens from that land look like. This is quite useful.

    But my favourite use of functional art comes from the James Bond RPG and from some old Call Of Cthulhu supplements: for adventures with NPCs, or lists of NPCs, these games often used little "headshot" thumbnails so you could see what he face of the character looked like. I thought this was an awesome use of functional reference illustration. It helped add spice to an otherwise dull page design (list of stuff) and it really helped you fix the NPCs in your mind as the referee.

    One thing I am not keen on in this arena is yet another stylized bunch of weaponry illustration. Unless these weapons are somehow culturally placed in a world background and different to what we have in our own world, I really don't see much point at all behind this.

    \n Beautiful art is always nice, but it doesn't necessarily do anything for the game.

    Here's what I do think is useful:
    --Art can capture a certain feeling, a certain relationship between people and their environment. It's hard to put into words, but it can be really inspiring. if the game actually helps you create something of that feeling in play, all the better.
    --Art can give a little window into the setting of the game. Every fantasy RPG seems to have a picture of [typical PC group] in the market square of [typical fantasy city], but some really give a distinct flavor to the setting, show you how all those setting elements come together.
    --Art can portray memorable characters that give people ideas -- the "I want to play this guy" effect Judd mentioned. This effect is heightened if the memorable character is in a memorable situation -- want to find out what happens next? Play and find out!

    Here are a few covers that stand out in my mind as doing at least two of those things: Sorcerer, Dogs in the Vineyard, The Mountain Witch. The last one is subtle, because I think it's a PC's-eye-view of the Witch's fortress, suggesting the dread and uncertainty that should be felt at that point.

    (Covers don't need to have art, I'm just referring to them because I'm not at home and can't refer to internal artwork by page.)

    \n Ultimately you want to get your game into as many hands as possible, right? Some people respond to visuals, some don't. Those who don't, probably don't give a shit either way if there's art in the book or not, but those who do, could very well overlook or even be instinctively turned off by a book of pure text. At the meanest level, the addition of artwork is a marketing tactic, and that's where you should start thinking about it. Is it worth me saving some money now to lose potential sales in the future? All the other stuff, about making a beautiful product, conveying the feel of the game, etc. come afterwards.

    \n When I buy a Book

    Art is the thing that catches my eye as I pull it down from the shelf, usually in the form of the cover. If the cover suggests something interesting (RIFTS) or is just eye-catching (Mu, third Edition L5R), it is more likely to accomplish this. This does not demand anythign specific, and indeed, the cues change over time. When D&D 3.0 came out, the hyper-stylized book covers excited and interested me, and a number of the variations they've done have similarly caught my eye, but as they genericized it, it became much less compelling. I have yet to resist pulling a nWOD core hardcover down from the shelf because I love their covers. Now, this is not the only reason I'll pull a book from the shelf - I'm usually already looking for something - but it can definitely play in.

    More powerfully, I pull a book down, flip it open, and see what jumps out at me. That initial impression comes from the layout of the book, which includes the artwork, albeit in specific ways. If the layout is very boring, I'm going to automatically assume the book is going to be a chore to read, and a clean layout, nice headers and margin-art and appropriate use of color can all offset that. Sooner or later I'll hit a piece of art, and its impact is going to be determined by it's _purpose_. If it's a diagram, I'll zoom in on the rule it's illustrating, because it's caught my interest. If it's a crappy piece of art, I'll start flipping through the book to see if that's typical. If it's a good piece of art, I'll check the nearby text to see if it speaks to the game being that cool, but even if it does not, it's caught my attention and made me think "Neat, can I be _that_ guy?" Most often, if it's enh, I flip past it and forget about it.

    At this point, I am most sensitive to a bait and switch. If the artwork and appearance of the book tell me one thing, specifically something reinforced by the blurb, I will be upset if the game then does something different. This can be true even in a game with phenomenal artwork, and I'm going to point a finger at Mortal Coil here, because my love for it is both deep and obvious. Jennifer Rodger's art is fantastic, but is also very dark. It is eye catching, since the color contrasts with the rest of the book, and if I were to _just_ look at the art, I would expect a moody horror game rather than what is in actuality a fantastic but reasonably generic engine (and that said, the cover is much closer to the actual game). Thankfully, both art and content really rocked, so I was not actually upset, but that kind of disconnect can be a real problem.

    After I've Bought The Game
    It goes on a bookshelf, and I'll faintly remember that I have it, but unless I intend to run it, there's a decent chance it gets set aside. At that point, the game will benefit from a mnemonic hook, so when I'm considering what to run next, it makes the list. A visual impression is not necessary for this, but it helps, especially depending upon exactly how visually a person thinks.

    Eventually, I will decide that maybe I want to run it, at which point I will pitch it to my friends. Art can be a useful tool to me at this point, if I can show someone a picture and say *this* is the game I'm going to run. Again, I don't need it, but it's a handy tool. Similarly, depending upon how visual the friend is, art may be more or less central to the pitch.

    Once I Begin Play
    Yeah, art's a page reference. Sometimes it's a utility - Character illustrations in a strongly inter-personal game for example can be of some use. The headshots from old Vampire and the Trumps in the Amber DRPG are great examples of this. In a crunchier game, maps, diagrams and illustrations can also be useful, but ultimately those needs tend to be very game-specific. Mostly if I'm hitting the book at this point, it's for reference, and I care less.

    The one exception to this is that if the book has some nice visual style I can emulate in my handouts, that's a big plus, but I recognize that as a deeply specialized need.

    Bottom Line
    I join the chorus - a game book should be well designed, and art is a tool of design, but like any other tool, there are ways to accomplish good design with any amount of it, from virtually none all the way up to a game presented entirely in graphic novel form. There are books that I have potent internal visual images of (Nobilis, PTA, Burning Wheel) which I would be hard pressed to remember any of the interior art, and games that I have left by the wayside but whose art still sticks with me (Rifts, 7th Sea) because those games used their art assets to good effect. Art is powerful, but without design, there's no telling if that power is going to work for or against you.

    -Rob D.

    PS - I do not object to painted illustrations for covers, but for every one that I find genuinely striking (Rifts, Thirty, Grimm) there are literally dozens that are such painful Elmore wannabees as to hurt my mind. That said, illustration can make for a great cover, but there are more artistic styles out there than just oil painting that are well worth looking at.

    \n Hey, something JD and I agree on! I'm vaguely in the art is unimportant camp, but I'll explicate more.
    \n
    \nFirst off, art definitely can sell books. But I'm really ambivalent on this matter. Yeah, it means I'll probably put it in my books purely because I have a goal to move books and get people playing my games. But I wish I didn't have to do so. Largely I feel I have to in order to make the book have a chance to be picked up and looked at. Because of threats like Kevin makes above.
    \n
    \nWorse, while art can often be evocative of what a game is about, just as often I find that it is, in fact, deceptive. Or, rather, there is no way to know if the written material will stand up to the art. There's no correlation. For instance, Rafael mentions the Metzla illos in Rifts: Atlantis. I agree, they're stunning. Which makes it only that much more dissappointing that the material behind it is Rifts. Here's where I will cop to actually having succumbed to the art and have actually bought this particular supplement, because of the art. My rationale was the typical gamer rationale... "I'll use it for inspiration in some other RPG." Have I ever? No.
    \n
    \nIt's not so much that I lament having spent the money. But that I actually feel bad that there's this visual image in my head of what these things are like, a cool image, and I've never found a way to get it into actual play of any game. Why is that? Well, because I've got about a million other such images in there, and I invent new ones as needed. Basically there's a massive surplus of such ideas.
    \n
    \nWhat there isn't, is a description of the Metzla culture that makes it meritable as an inclusion into play of an actual game. They're a "monster" race, and so are given the typical D&D-level treatment that such get. In this particular case, in fact, I recall that their culture actually makes no sense at all. It's just not useable material in the book, unless I'm running a hack-n-slash Rifts game. Which I'm not.
    \n
    \nSo the art has sold me something that I cannot use. Even when I knew better.
    \n
    \nAs for art evoking a mood... generally I don't need this. I find that I can introduce mood myself just fine, and that I'd prefer the mechanics to be of help here. The art isn't going to make it into actual play often. Heck, I often don't carry the book of the game I intend to play.
    \n
    \nYes, where art does provide a shortcut to communicating an idea, I'm all for it. Art can surpass words in many cases. For instance, a picture of a monster is much better than reading the text, to get across what that monster is like in terms of it's appearance. I would rather use a picture for something like that. That said, if the game statistics don't match the picture, I'll be irate. As an example, playing WOW, I'm sick to death of the picture of the creature in the game giving little to no data that's useful in gaguing it's actual power level. Oh, sure, a Trog that's larger than another will tend to be more powerful than the last. But this is only a tendency. Even size isn't a garuntee of power.
    \n
    \nAnd this is just rudimentary stuff.
    \n
    \nSo, sure, if your art is "accurate" in terms of getting ideas across to your players, I'm for it. But if it's not, then I'd rather have no art than misleading art. I give my favorite example of this from an old Rolemaster adventure for Shadow World entitled Vale of the Cyclops. The cyclops in question is somewhat unique in that it's not a giant with a single eye. The text describes the cyclops in question, Rarg, as being a giant who has had both eyes gouged out, and who has a mediallion with a big gem in it or somesuch that serves magically as an eye.
    \n
    \nPretty nifty. There's even a good picture of him on the cover.
    \n
    \nBut then, in the text, there's a Paul Jaquays picture with Paul's typical flair, under which is a caption, "Rarg." But then the picture has Rarg with one eye, center of head, no amulet. This is an art typo.
    \n
    \nBasically I find that art is so often used to "advertise" that I despise it in RPGs. By "advertise" here I mean "lie to get you to buy in." The product doesn't live up to the claim made by the art. And that pisses me off.
    \n
    \nGraphic design, per se, is another deal. Aligning text, art, and other design elements in order to make a text more readable is a thoroughly laudable goal. If, in fact, art is being used to somehow accomplish this, that's great. But, again, I tend to find art to be used distractingly, rather than in a way that makes the text flow better.
    \n
    \nMaybe we're just not particularly good at art in RPGs. But I think that it's fraught with such problems that I'm generally against art. Sure, where done well, and used well, it's probably a great tool. I'm just afraid that this is rarely the case.
    \n
    \nLastly, I've never missed art, where it's absent. In Jared's original InSpectres folio, the lack of any art, except for the corporate logo on the cover (if that can be called art), was perfect.
    \n
    \nLet's contemplate this... why do novels not have art? I mean, if art is so important in evoking mood and such, then why don't we see art in every single novel ever written? I'd posit that a narrative format, like both novels and RPGs, do not need art. Sure, an occasionall illustration or diagram where words would be difficult, sure. But more than that seems to me to be either distracting, or sheer advertising.
    \n
    \nHow about this... if the thousand words that the picture is saying, are ones that you wouldn't write in tbe book? ... don't include the picture.
    \n
    \nIn gaming, I think the best handling of art that I've ever seen, has to be from Games Workshop. These guys know real art, and real graphic design, and, most importantly, you play the art. That picture of an Ghazkull the Ork Chieftain in the book? Looks the same as the miniature, and the system has rules for exactly what effect all of his gear has on play. The fact that he has a power claw on the fig and in the pic reminds you that he gets the special rules for power claws.
    \n
    \nThat's effective.
    \n
    \nI'm not saying it can't be done well. Just that it's much, much harder than people think, I believe. And that, if you're not certain you can incorporate art well, then less is much, much, much more.

But, I have a hard time seeing how it can't fall into the same problems and benefit from the same successes that text does. Good text and good game design help the game. Bad text hurts it. Good art can help the game. Bad art hurts. And so on.

      The problem here is setting up art as something so distinct and special that it becomes expendable (which Mike, maybe, and Alexander definitely would advocate). Anything in a game text is expendable if it does a bad job of whatever it is the game text sets out to do.

      I advocate a more holistic approach that sizes up whether art has a place in the product, what purpose is serves (and how well it does so), just like I would do so for, say, back cover text, the right font, whether to include sidebars, or including Color text (fiction, character vignettes, etc.).

      I am not saying a game product equires art, by the way. I'm saying it's much more useful to look at all of the potential pieces of information to convey to a reader/user/player and figure out whether those things have a place. If art has a place, great (it often does)! If not, fine.

      \n WHOOPS! Would edit that first contradictory sentence from:
      Art director is someone other than the artist if the artist can't draw.

      To:
      Art director is someone other than the artist if the *art director* can't draw what he/she needs (or doesn't have time for).

      Now, as to your recent question, Robert. Yes, being your own art director has considerable advantages of time and costs, and more importantly avoids any miscommunication between creator (say, the writer) and the art director because the vision is in one person's head. But, it's not something every single person can do. I know, having done a layout for Alexander, that he is unable to do his own art direction. Literally cannot do it. That's ok. Other people -- say, Matt Wilson -- can do it. If you can, do it! The advantages are great. But, if it's your first time, ask someone for advice, or to review your product when it's in some stage near completion.

      Teaching you how to direct art is a pretty long topic. And, teaching it (rather than doing it) is probably something I don't do well. I get frustrated and just say things like "Go with your gut on how a pic looks" or something similarly useless to someone who doesn't know what the hell I'm talking about.

      Here's one concrete tip I'll share that I think is very useful. When you request/direct art from an illustrator, I find extremely useful to a game product to get artwork that has situational dynamism. That is, it isn't posed bad-assness (see: Brom). It is actual stuff going on that makes you think and learn about what the game is about. Don't see illustrations of a posing babarian chick bad ass. Get one of her standing in front of a cowering man as she stares down three thugs. Pictures that say something is going on are best. Pictures that just put something cool on the page are, for me, less useful. There are some good examples of that in Sorcerer -- look at those pics carefully. Dust Devils also has pics like that -- there's a picture of gun, which is fine. But, there are names on the bullets which is AWESOME. There are lots of other examples in texts, good and bad.

      The shorter way to think of this advice is: Get "action" shots where something is happening, rather than posed shots where there really is no action.

      What uses do I think art has? Many. Here are a few that pop into my brain off-hand.

      * Marketing.
      * Re-inforcing Color ("atmosphere" or whatever you want to call it).
      * Creating mental bookmarks in a text so people know where to turn ("Oh! Character creation is on the page with the big ass sword! Just flip to it!").
      * Showing, rather than telling situation ("See? The guy with the dagger under the table! THAT'S what I'm talking about! Awesome."
      * Making references to other media cleverly so people begin to associate things in their minds with your product. It's not just marketing and cool by association, it's also inspiration! ("Oh! I get it! You mean like Firefly! I'm totally going to make a smuggler now.")

      Oh! Art direction is not synonymous with layout, but they overlap an awful lot.
