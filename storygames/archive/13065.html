\n In another thread about 4e, Ralph had some ideas about lasersharking Action Points in 4e a bit. It struck a chord with me. Action Points have always bothered me. They are cool in principle but something always seemed a little off about them. They are pretty limited as written in the rules and keeping track of them is kind of a pain. So I took some of Ralph's ideas and have decided to incorporate them into my 4e game. Here's the rules I've instituted.
------------------------------
Action Points Uses:

1. A free Standard Action.
2. Take a Second Wind.
3. An additional 1d8 of damage.
4. A +2 to your defenses until the end of your next turn.
5. Refresh a Daily Power or Encounter Power.

A player can spend 1 AP per turn at Heroic levels, 2 per turn at Paragon levels and 3 per turn at Epic levels.

A character no longer gets 1 Second Wind per encounter. He simply spends an AP to get the Second Wind.

Getting Action Points:

1. You get an Action Point every time you fail a roll during an encounter.
2. Action Points reset to 1 at the end of each encounter.
3. You may carry over AP\u2019s equal to the number of Healing Surges you have used.

Example:

Grog, the Halfling Barbarian, has 8 healing surges. At the beginning of Encounter #1 he has 1 AP. He fails 2 rolls for +2 AP during the encounter, uses no healing surges and leaves the encounter without using any AP\u2019s. His AP total resets to 1 so he enters Encounter #2 with 1 AP. In Encounter #2 he whiffs 3 rolls for +3 AP, spends 2 healing surges and spends 1 AP. At the end of Encounter #2 he loses 1 AP because he can only carry over 2 AP since he has used 2 healing surges. In Encounter #3 Grog fails 7 rolls for +7 AP, uses 2 AP and uses 6 healing surges. At the end of Encounter #3 Grog can carry over 6 AP to the next Encounter. However, after Encounter #3 the party decided to take an extended rest. Everyone\u2019s healing surges are refreshed so Grog loses all but 1 of his Action Points. Yay for Grog!
--------------------------------------------------

Has anyone else tried anything like this? How did it turn out? Anyone see any glaring holes in these rules? Any way to tweek them that would make them cooler?

    \n Do you get all of those effects when you spend an AP? Or just pick one from the list? Because "Take a standard action" already covers the second wind option. And the second wind option is way better than +2 to defenses, because second wind already gives you +2 to defenses. (It's also redundant, as the standard action option already covers this, in that Total Defense exists as a standard action that you can take.)

    The 1d8 damage doesn't scale well as you go up in level. Of course, the complaints that you describe only really apply in Heroic tier anyway: by Paragon you'll have interesting stuff to do with APs.

    Do you need to spend the AP for extra damage before you roll? Or is it "add damage to an attack that already hit"? If you spend before the roll, it sucks as an option (better to get another standard action). If it is afterward, can I spend more than one? Can I blow all my APs to gain +10d8?

    Refreshing encounter powers is a superstrong ability. 4e avoids ever refreshing daily powers, and only has Daily options for refreshing encounter powers. If you combine "refresh a daily" with "gain an AP on a miss" you basically have made all powers Reliable, and will have PCs doing 2-3x as much damage as they are supposed to do. There's no way you could rely on existing monsters to challenge a party using these rules.

    Giving away APs every other turn seems ludicrous. It means that people will accrue APs and spend them like water. This plus infinite second winds means that Leaders are obsolete (at-wills are also obsolete).

    Characters who make more attack rolls (wizards and rangers and the like) will be getting more APs than other classes under this system. I don't think that that is intentional.

    You will tend to get fights that are blockier: since I'll have more APs, I'll spend them in chunks. So instead of it being me, you, me you, it's me you me me me me you.

    If you're annoyed at the amount of bookkeeping required now, I don't know why you'd want a system that requires more bookkeeping and more frequent bookkeeping.

    Those are the issues that I see off the top of my head. With as radical a change as this would be, you probably also mess up other stuff I'm not seeing right now.

    Oh one more: humans become really nice for all their AP feats and Action Surge totally incredible.

    \n I did work out an alternate action point mechanic for my Maho Shoujo 4E variant, but enough other elements changed as to make it difficult at best to relate it to D&D 4E. Some simple ideas were to split Action points in half, into Reserve points - which let you spent variable numbers for different results: 1 reserve point gives you advantage or lets you stunt to use a power you have in a situation which it would not normally apply, 2 gives you an extra action. Even larger expenditures let you freeform a new power right there - although I'm back to the drawing board on the exact mechanisms for this.

    I'd have to go back to my notes to remember if there was a refresh rate, but the default story (similar to daily for D&D folks - except the story ends when it ends, not at some arbitrary time interval) quantity was based on a wisdom like ability score and your class (in a similar way to how constitution affects your number of surges).

    I was aiming for making a default 3 person Maho Shoujo group equivalent to a 5 person D&D group, so increasing the availability of extra actions was very consciously a power boost. But my primary goal was to give players a direct way to take control of page 42 style stunting so that it was less of an add on. Which is something I'm still working on.

    \n There are two basic economies in 4e D&D (neither involves gold). One is the hit point/healing surge economy. The other is the action economy. Messing with either will radically alter the entire game. You can deal with changes, but you need to expect everything to be broken until shown otherwise.

    This change messes with the action economy fiercely, and to a lesser extent with HPs via the second wind rules. The game may still be playable, but it won't be the same any more.

    \n Sorry it took me a while to get back to this post. I was very busy yesterday afternoon and evening. Thanks for all the insightful comments. I agree with John. I probably need to spell out what it is I'm attempting to accomplish.

    1.) One of the things I dislike about D&D of any edition is the whiff factor involved. As a player you wait 10 minutes for your turn then you roll 1 die and whiff on the attack. Now you get to wait again and hope you don't whiff next time.

    2.) Daily Powers should never be totally ineffectual. They are your big guns and they should do something. I'm okay with some of the Wizard Powers that do something a little less impressive if you fail but I really hate the Daily Powers that just fizzle because the d20 rolled too low. Encounter and At-will Powers that do this don't bother me. Failure in general don't always bother me but the complete failure of a Daily Power is an un-fun failure.

    3.) Action Points as they are written are fairly lame. In fact, most of my players completely forget about them unless I remind them. If you had more AP's and they did more (and more interesting) stuff than just get an extra Standard Action, then maybe they would be worth keeping track of.

    Okay.... On to the comments.

    I completely forgot about some character classes having a bunch of AOE powers that would require multiple attack rolls per round. I definitely wouldn't want to give out AP for every single miss. I also didn't consider players just firing off Minor Actions with the intent of failing in order to get AP. I will have to rethink that a lot.

    I'm not really worried about the players getting too many actions. They can still only spend 1 AP per turn. That means they get 1 extra Standard Action. If they use their AP for the Standard Action, then they can't use their AP for anything else that turn, including a Second Wind. This is why I'm not worried about someone dumping 10 AP to get a +10d8 to damage. You can only spend 1 AP per turn (at Heroic Tier anyways).

    I am a little concerned about affecting the Leader characters abilities. Here's the tradeoff. If I spend an AP to get a Second Wind then I can't do anything else that turn. Remember, I can't take the AP to get an extra Standard Action and then take a Second Wind. I can only spend 1 AP per round. If the Leader character takes an action that give me the use of a healing surge, I still have my regular action and the use of my AP for something else during the round. I'm just not certain how that tradeoff will work out in actual play. Will the lure of using AP for something else be enough to keep the Leader character's abilities relevant or not?

    Thanks again for the responses.

    \nIf you're the DM and you want to make action points cooler, try not to think of them as just an extra attack. Think of them as extra move actions instead, or perhaps an extra in-combat skill roll, and look for opportunities to provoke their use in that way.

    Try to spread the characters out and then look to create crises or opportunities in the encounter, where an extra action is critical to success. Death saves are the best example -- I think the most exciting play in D&D is when a player spends an action point on a double move and a Heal roll-to-stabilize, or (even better) a triple-move and a healing minor.

    Ticking-bomb skill challenges are another good opportunity to provoke AP usage. Give the characters some things to do in the encounter besides kill the monsters, and suddenly action points can become very important indeed.

    That said, our table does use the Elven Accuracy hack, allowing action points to be used for straight rerolls.

    Looking at your proposed hacks -- I would be hesitant to allow extra healing opportunities into the game. Extra player healing = DM grind. I might just be paranoid, though, because our party has a full leader (Warlord), a hybrid leader (Bard) and a multiclass leader (Warlord) and I have a hell of a time knocking anyone in our party down.

    \n I think the appropriate principle here is 'Do not roll for something if you don't want one of the potential outcomes'. I understand whiffing is frusterating (believe me, do I understand - a monk I recently started has a great encounter power, which hasn't hit yet - 1,2,5,6 - I'm hoping there's some kind of progression there), but I'm very skeptical of approaches which ameliorate this by fiddling with game mechanics. The possibility of missing is an essential part of the tactical properties of the game. If you get rid of it, what would you do for Avengers - whose striker ability is based on having higher accuracy than everyone else?
    \n
    \nWhile D&D is certainly not perfectly balanced, I think it would be a big mistake to assume that it wasn't a lot of work to get it as balanced as it is. A lot of the suggested AP uses don't seem to understand this:
    \n1) Standard Action
    \n What APs do now - giving players an extra standard action every time they whiff would significantly increase their damage output. You can compensate for that partially by increasing monster hps, or increasing the number of monsters, but that will reduce the size of the sweet spot between trivial fights and party kills.
    \n2) Second Wind
    \n Second wind is largely only used by Dwarves anyway. Changing it to an AP cost would reduce the strength of Dwarves a bit, but that's not necessarily a bad idea - Dwarves are very strong as it is. Other than dwarves, I don't see this option being widely used.
    \n3) d8 damage
    \n If your character can't do quite a bit more expected damage on a standard action than d8, then it is significantly underperforming. (And rather impressively so - I believe you would need to deliberately design a poor character before an additional d8 damage would be a better option). More generally, you can't use a single value here because it will need to increase as players average damage increases. Assuming you did find some point at which it was approximately as useful as a second action, you would make this option overpowered for critfishers, and incredibly overpowered for coup-de-grace (There's a 9th level rogue daily that knocks the target out - a crit specializer (RRoT build, maybe) would be easily able to one-shot monsters using this option)
    \n4) +2 defenses
    \n Very weak - and also redundant, considering that I could just use an extra standard action to do the Total Defense action . (I have never seen anyone use Total Defense)
    \n5) Refresh Daily
    \n Ridiculously overpowered. Dailies are good. They are literally fight-changing. If players are able to use them like encounter powers by spending action points, then you will need to hugely increase the difficulty levels of your encounters (to the point that a slightly challenging encounter for a party which can spend APs for dailies would be a guaranteed TPK for a party which can't). Party composition would have a big effect here too - daillies which are intended to heal the party can have a stronger effect than dailies which are intended to do damage. Here's just a sample of the things you might have to deal with when dailies can be refreshed like that:
    \n Everyone in the party has resist 5 to everything, all the time.
    \n Every enemy starting it's turn next to the fighter takes d12+4 damage, automatically
    \n The cleric's healing powers always heal an additional 5 damage
    \n The entire party receives a +4 to hit the big bad guy for the entire encounter
    \n There is a 5x5 cloud of poison that the wizard controls that automatically damages everything starting in it
    \n (Those are all level 1-5, cause I'm most familiar with heroic tier - just for fun, here's some level 29:
    \n The fighter deals +1W damage on all at-wills and encounters, and automatically attacks every enemy that starts it's turn adjacent
    \n The warlock can control at least the standard action (and possibly all the actions) of one of the enemies
    \n Everyone has +4 to all defenses, and resist 5 to all damage
    \nThere are 2 problems with this increase in power - firstly, it makes it harder to balance fights, since you also need to increase the monsters power to match. Secondly, it's even harder to balance fights because you can't count on the party having those particular powers (strikers especially, tend to have dailies which are more devoted to damage, which are less susceptible to abuse in this way. The rules as written have this reasonably balanced with other dailies, but once you increase the number of uses available, the balance gets broken).
    \n

    \n1) Whiffing is frustrating, but it's a fundamental part of the system.

    \n 2a) One of it's features is the ability to create relatively balanced encounters very easily
    \n3) Trying to remove the whiff factor by a minor change to the rules is practically guaranteed to fail, either by breaking the balance, or by not
    \n actually removing the whiff factor - removing the whiff factor would take a lot of work.
    \n4) This is not a weakness of D&D, it's just how they chose to design the system.

    \nIf whiffing in D&D is a big enough problem for you, you should probably look for a different system. If you stick within the system, there are some alternatives for you which tend to reduce the whiff factor. Play an avenger (make 2 rolls for attacks against one target you chose), an elf (reroll 1 attack once per encounter) or use an inescapable magic weapon (every time you miss you get a cumulative +1 to hit). It would probably not be unbalanced to make the elf ability available as a feat as well.

Everyone in the party is guaranteed to be making a significant contribution to the success of the party.

    \n Emphasis on the uninteresting part...or deprotagonizing to use a 50 cent word. When my choices lead to suck...that's drama, that's tragedy. When random dice lead to suck...that's lame. Even for a tactical game and not a story game. When my choices lead to suck that's bad play, that's getting schooled. When random dice lead to suck...that's lame.
    \n
    \nWhen you have a roll and there is a binary outcome and 1 outcome is "Something Cool Happens" and the other outcome is "nothing happens at all" that is a design that is inherently vulnerable to whiffage.
    \nWhen that roll is mechanized such that there is a high degree of unpredictability inherent in the outcome (such as the linear probability scale along the wide range of a d20 vs. the bell curve of GURPS or FUDGE) that mechanic is inherently vulnerable to whiffage.
    \nWhen you call for a die roll repeatedly during the game because you're essentially recreating the probability curve of a die pool by rolling a series of individual dice, that mechanic is inherently vulnerable to whiffage.
    \n
    \nWhen you put all three of those things together in a game...whifftasticness commences. Basic Role Playing (BRP) suffers all of these same problems substituteing the linear curve of an even wider range %ile for the d20.
    \n
    \n
    \nMissing a roll is not itself whiffing. Missing for a completely random reason (regardless of how skillfully you played to optimize your odds) that results in nothing interesting happening usually is. As evidence witness the typical D&D; game where a missed roll is responded to with nothing more interesting than "That sucks, who's next in the Initiative"...
    \n
    \nThe granting of an AP on a miss doesn't magically transform a miss into something interesting, that would take a much larger reengineering to accomplish. What it does is give you a rain check. Rather than "that sucks, nothing interesting happens on your turn" you get "ok, nothing interesting happened on your turn, but here's a rain check that increases the likelihood of something even more interesting happening on a future turn, sorry for the inconvenience thanks for shopping at Dungeons 'R Us". Not quite the same thing, but easy to implement without a complete overhaul.
