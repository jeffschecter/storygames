Posted By: DeliveratorI think GM's say "no, that just doesn't work" for three different reasons, broadly speaking.

1) The action is simply beyond the threshold of plausibility: a relatively normal human trying to leap a 100' chasm.

2) The action would normally work, but there is a legitimate in-world reason why it doesn't in this case, although that reason is hidden from the players at the moment: the house is owned by a dude who's secretly a powerful wizard, so simply using knock to unlock the front door doesn't work.

3) The GM is simply blocking / doesn't want to "lose control" of "the story": the players realize who the bad guy is "too early" and go to confront him, but nothing they do to try to find him works.
\nLula points out a fourth category: "Because it would be socially disruptive / break the group's social contract / it's just not your turn even if the idea is cool."

Thoughts? Is this list comprehensive?

Please bear in mind, "instances where the GM calls for a roll" are not what this is about. This is only in reference to times of an absolute "no".

Matt

    Without knowing if there's a specific objective here, here are my two cents:\n
    Lula points out a fourth category: "Because it would be socially disruptive / break the group's social contract / it's just not your turn even if the idea is cool."
    That's not a "nope, that doesn't work" comment about the fiction, that's a "no, you can't do that" comment to the player. There are LOTS of reasons why people at any RPG table might do THAT. Like, if an announced action:

    A) wouldn't logically work, based on the "what"s and "how"s of the fiction
    wouldn't be suitable to the game's emotional tenor
    C) wouldn't be suitable to the game's stylistic idiom
    D) would make a participant uncomfortable (lines/veils)
    E) wouldn't be conducive to future play for reasons of boring situation
    F) wouldn't be conducive to future play for reasons of plot derailment
    G) is less cool than something else they thought of
    H) violates the system for playing the game (e.g. breaks a rule -- good call, Corley)

    Any of these could be asserting a shared value for the group or an individual value upon the group.

    "No, that just doesn't work" clearly claims that A is the case (your #1 and #2 are subsets of that). If the GM instead really means "I won't let you do that", then any of B-H could be the case (with your #3 a subset of F).

    \n I've occasionally done, "Okay, here's a Fate point in return for my saying No because it would wreck the plot / mean no other player had a chance to do anything".

    The first part, "wreck the plot", well... I will do it, be utterly open about why I'm doing it, and, as we're using Fate, there's a mechanism for me to do it, but it really is better if I don't do it.

    One reason I will do it is, "Er, guys, I actually didn't come up with an answer to that question." In one case, one of my players actually came up with a great answer. But basically, that kind of No is "No, I fucked up. Have a Fate point, and I'll try not to do that again."

    The second is the one that's trickier for me, but again, going bluntly meta and saying, "Okay, I want the other players to be able to do something, so I'm going to rule you can't solve all the problems" is usually acceptable. I've got a tolerant group of players.

    Those are cases, though, where I think needing to say "No" means something's gone wrong. Not in a big way, just... okay, someone fucked up, usually me.

    The times when I think, "It would have been better if I'd just said No, rather than Yes, but..." or "Yes, and..." default to #1 or #2. They're rare, but they have happened.

    \n I discuss this issue explicitly in Solar System. In that game (conflict resolution, GM's task is mostly about dramatic coordination) I call the standards the GM uses in modulating conflict stakes the triple principle of "leverage, propriety and scope". This is slightly different from Matt's list up top: leverage is the situation where the character is not in position to accomplish what he seeks (jumping to the moon or whatever), propriety is when the action is out of genre for the campaign (simply wishing somebody back to life because you're so much in love with them, to pick a positive example), and scope is when what the player is trying to accomplish is basically achievable, but we don't want to resolve it just yet, with just this one simple action, as it's really going to be more complex and take more attention and processing from us as a group.

    I'm pretty happy with the above in the context of TSoY, as I feel that it captures the task of declaring stakes in a pretty analytic manner. Basically, if one of the above standards doesn't conflict with a player's declaration of character intent, then the GM should simply allow it to happen, or go to the dice about it.

    Posted By: Zak S
    "Without good players and a good DM, your D&D game will necessarily suck"

    "Well isn't that a design flaw in D&D?"
    \nWeirdly, I think the disconnect there is in what is meant by the term "good." There really are two very different definitions in operation. The indie gamer's definition, in this context, is "unusually skilled"\u2014that is, that unless you have players and (especially) a DM who are actually quite extraordinary, the game will necessary suck. As in, you don't just have to be engaged and have reasonable social skills and have made a reasonable attempt to understand the game's rules. In order to have a good experience, you must be able to do (much) more than simply apply the rules and principles in the text to the game.

    I doubt that's what you meant, of course, Zak. I think you meant more like, "reasonably competent." GM is well-prepared and understands the game and is ready to teach it to new players (or parcel out such duties appropriately), players are basically cooperative and good-humored, don't spotlight hog, etc.

    The pressure to be a "good" (read: amazing! awesome! stupendous!) GM among many trad groups can actually work against becoming a "good" (can competently run a fun game consistently) GM. This was and to an extent still is certainly an issue with, for example, the group I gamed with in college. The "GM-as-auteur" model was very much the dominant paradigm, so when I started running games there (D&D 3.0), I tried really hard to put in all this "deep" stuff about metaphysics, thematic choices, and other such grandiose and pretentious bullshit. Whereas if I had been culturally able to ignore all that and simply focus on the nuts and bolts, both I and the people playing in my game would have had a much better time!

    And it's *that* pressure that many of us self-identified "indie gamers" stand against. And, of course, the OSR also demands competent (but not insanely brilliant!) GMing, so we're all good as far as that goes. But there are legions of gamers who grew up in the era when the ideals of "being a true Storyteller" and other such hogwash* were regularly promoted in the GMing advice sections of all published RPGs.

    Matt

    *It's not complete hogwash. I do know one or two people who can do it, but even they have off-days.

    \n Last night, I remember saying no in two different cases.     Regarding "How does this shit happen?": How the shit happens varies. Often, it involves things snowballing over time until the situation is so tangled up that no one knows what to do. Often, it involves one person being an unabashed jerk and other people caught off balance by this. If you're off balance and unable to react correctly, fast, it's hard to figure out what to do after the situation ends, but the problem continues. Think of any dysfunctional situation -- at work, in a relationship, whatever -- and translate it to gaming.

    From the outside, it is baffling. After the fact, it may be baffling to the people involved. As it is happening, if you're not very centered and sure of yourself (and, let us presume, more or less in the right, just to keep things simple), you can't freeze time and get your head together. You're not necessarily making the most logical and optimal move. It may or may not be clear what that move is from outside, presuming it exists, but from inside, a lot of people, including Yours Truly, just won't see it.
