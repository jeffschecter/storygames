    This is all for a guy who says "bah" to Vincents (superb) mech designs in the book.

    Go buy a bunch of the Exo-Force Lego models. They're not terribly expensive, readily available at Target and Wal-Mart and Toys-R-us and the like. (Oh, and they're REALLY neat.)

    The "main" Exo-Force robots are about $15. They are scaled much larger than Mechaton models. Build 'em anyway, because you'll see how they're put together and get ideas for how you might do some yourself on a smaller scale.

    Get some of the smaller robots, too. They sell for around $8 I think. They have the pieces and modelling that will most inspire your Mechatonners.

    The Exo-Force stuff will provide you with all kinds of functional and decorative pieces. You'll be up to your neck in weapons and missile parts. You'll also get lots of small, functionally movable pieces (hinges and the like) perfect for Mechaton 'bots.

    I say all of this having spent some fun time building Mechaton-scale mechs, and never once having played. (shrug) I get a real kick out of making original models and figuring out how they'll "dice out" for the game. Get creative!

    Oh! I have a pretty good suspicion that any of the Star Wars sets will also provides some nice pieces you can use for inventive mechs. Plus, I mean, Star Wars kits are fun. I have only one or two, but they provide some good ammo (i.e. pieces!).

    I'm playing Mechaton rather a lot.

    Clyde, what you're talking about might be OK for a little training game, but it'll get boring fast. There's all sorts of fun to be had by playing asymmetrically. It was one of the design specifications I put my foot down about while Vincent was developing it.

    If anyone wants to play at Dreamation, by the way, I'm taking all comers.

    Here's my Paktali army for the Battle for Tarkut. These guys are made out of pieces that existed when I was a kid 20 years ago, though they've each got a couple of pieces that were pretty rare.

    Here are my "alien" mecha from a previous, latter playtesting series of games. They're made almost entirely of common plates, clips, and tube plates.

    Clyde's right: get bags of Lego at yard sales, Salvation Army, whatever. Then, once you've started getting some technique, buy a bunch of pieces you need from Bricklink.

    Also, check out MOCPages' mecha pages for inspiration. And check out whose page is (mysteriously) on top. Ignore references to a defunct game system there; Mechaton gives me the giant robot love I need now.

    Edit: We're using the measuring tool from the Mechaton kits. It's a lot more fun than the hex map, frankly. Very rarely there's a situation where more precision is required. Usually, the favor in the dispute goes to whoever's losing.

    Rrrow!

    ...

    I mean, well, let's see. I assume you want them axially. "Tubes" are the same diameter as antennae (not the thin ones), ship masts, tool handles, &c. The inside diameter of a cross axle (that is, a Technic wheel/gear axle) is also the same, so they go where there's a little cross hole, too. So you could stick it inside the back of a "headlight" brick, which has a hollow hole on one side.

    I should note that spheremaking is the way that really excellent builders show off skills. I've never done it. I suggest you keep trying to do it by building lots of other stuff and it will come to you.

      how would you rule some kind of obsuring weapon? like a smoke screen or mercury fog.

      That's a blue die. It's used for your own defense. There are no rules right now for defending your comrades, and that's for the best; the game is tilted (if only slightly) toward offense. The single best defense is to get behind one of your enemies. That way, they take the hits, irrespective of their defense number.

      how about a movement canceling attack, like an oil slick or stream of a plasma?

      I'd think that a stream of plasma would be an everything canceling attack. But no, the best way to keep someone from moving is by making an avenue of movement inhospitable. It's a tactical solution, and it's a big part of the game. Also, anything where you leave stuff around the table to see if anyone hits it isn't much fun. It's just bookkeeping and passive attack. The game is about action, and mines, oil slicks, and area effect weapons decrease that by making it a game where you just get ground down all the time.

    • James_Nostack

      Andy, I went to Home Depot and got a plastic sorty-box, for like screws and nuts and bolts. It holds about 500 pieces pretty easily, probably as many as a thousand if I really wanted to fill it up. I've got it sorted by "function"--plates, slopes, bricks, rods, hinges, and specialty pieces. Sorting to find something takes probably 10 seconds.

      If you've got a monstrous pile like what you're describing--several thousand pieces--your best bet is probably a dozen or more little bins. (You don't want the bins to get TOO big, or else you'll have to paw through that stuff each time, and that defeats the purpose of sorting.) I say go with a dozen to start, and see where that gets you. If you don't use them all, bins are good for lots of other things.

      I use Plano tackle boxes to sort my parts. They rock the house. They're sturdy, they stay locked and closed when you want them to, they're inexpensive, and they come in a billion different sizes. My favorites are the 3600 and 3700. Tackle Warehouse is a good place to get them if your local hardware is sold out for some reason (cough cough). Amazon carries them, too.



      The good thing about these is they're very portable, and you can easily carry a good selection of parts (for impromptu building before a battle) without having to lug a giant tub around. I currently have my parts sorted by color scheme, so I have all the parts I need to build good mechs of a given scheme in one box. The parts in each box are sub-divided the same way (roughly) so I know where to find a 1x2 hinge-plate (for example) in any box. It's working for me so far.

      Now, Jonathan's questions:
      - I often get inspired just by fiddling around with bits. Making good leg parts is a challenge (for me) so I sometimes just stick stuff together at random until something leg-like happens, then build the rest of the mech around that.

      - I've played around with the CAD programs. They're really fun, but now that I have a good selection of parts on hand, it's easier to just go straight to building. I find BrickShelf and Flickr to be hugely inspirational, too. Seeing a nicely built mech always gives me ideas for my own designs.

      - YES. Whenever I place a BrickLink order, I usually grab a handful of odd parts that I have no plans for and then see how they work out. If I find a good use for them, it's easy to order some more. My current drone army uses a piece that I got this way.

      - I redesign a bit, but almost never for tactical reasons. It's pretty easy to rename an attachment from "concussion rifle" to "particle accelerator" and switch it from direct fire to artillery or whatever. That's something I like about Mechaton. I do sometimes redesign to add or remove flight components like wings or jets, though.

      And this guy's storage is terrifying.
      Those parts drawers are nice. Except that they only work for a certain size collection. I have one of those drawer units, for the small but very frequently used pieces.

      Having a good sized LEGO collection makes one start to realize the difficulties in organizing one's collection. What works well for sorting doesn't necessarily work well for building, and ease of sorting and building both require more space than if you want to reduce the amount of space the storage takes.

      And it depends on what you're building at any one time. If you're building a huge black castle, you want your black bricks and plates all available, and in big bins. If you're building a bunch of small things in different colors, you need a wide assortment of pieces, but you don't want that gallon sized tub of black 1x2 bricks.
