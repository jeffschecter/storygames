\n Second session last night. There wasn't any one particular scene I felt compelled to highlight in the bite-sized AP thread, but I've been thinking a lot about this game both before and after the session and wanted to jot down some things about it somewhere.

I really like the highlighted stats mechanic. The GM and another player can think about the Fronts that are likely to unfold and what might be interesting if my character approached those Fronts a certain way. In many games I'm not always sure what I want to do next and I find the starting point of "Sharp" and "Hot", for example, helpful to me in brainstorming what my Brainer might pursue for a scene.

This is the first game I've played in some years where I actually care about character advancement/XP. Like, usually I can't even be bothered to track it no matter how simple it is. You guys level up, I'm cool. Or I'll do it, but I'm not really all that excited about it. That includes Burning Wheel, TSoY, and 4E. Usually I see/recognize designer carrots, smile to myself, and am just as happy to walk the other way in terms of those carrots affecting my mode of play. I'm not exactly sure why it is that I care about it for AW so far. Something to do with scarcity and ugly choices? Maybe it's because my character is pretty vulnerable and I don't want her to lose big.

I like the tension that is present every time the dice are rolled. Like I could choose to squeeze in a read the situation/person roll here to get an improvement circle but I better be damn sure I care about it because if I roll low, Christian is going to make a Move that my character will not like. It's like the part of Stakes that I really like without having to go through all the actual Stakes-setting before you roll/draw, that part that sometimes makes me feel like I'm playing before I play. All I need to know is that on one side I get what I want, and on the other is something I seriously do not want (but that will likely make the game more fun), and in the middle is some GM-fuckery that's supported by the rules/genre. Perfect.

I see that Colin just did a bite-size on the session. I'll add another bite to justify an AP category.

David's Savvyhead Gitch incurs what is likely to be the everlasting enmity of the Mongrels chopper gang. He knocks one of them off their bike, commandeers it and then shoots out the front tire on two other bikes (my Brainer's brother on one of them). He leads the rest of the gang on a merry chase, stranding them at the toxic tar pits. This to save some pilgrim cultists the gang was intent on raiding, some cultists he doesn't even know. "Bad karma, man." is the proffered reason.

Cut to a couple scenes later. He's trying to talk H out of staying away from her pimp, Princey. He's sussed out that Princey does not have her best interests in mind when it comes to the frightening Doghead. So he (and all of we as players), know he's got to convince her or it's bad news for her. Maybe a death sentence. He lands in the 7-9 zone so she wants a promise that it'll be him and her forever. He cares about her and says she can stay in his underground trailer as long as she wants but that he can't promise her that. Note that AW's rules would have allowed him to promise that (lie) but not bind him if he didn't want that. Stupid principles...

I also enjoyed cracking my fellow players up by having Brace earnestly invite almost everyone she came into contact with to her upcoming "free movie night" on Friday, no matter how out of context the invitation. Our hardhold, Greenland, is coming apart at the seams. I seriously doubt we make it to Casablanca\u2026

    \n After you go into battle with your vehicles, guns, or gear, roll+sharp.
    On a 10+, you conserved your resources. Good job. On a 7-9, choose 1:
    - You're running dangerously low on fuel
    - You're running dangerously low on ammo
    - Your gear needs maintenance and repair
    On a miss, the GM makes a hard move as usual (or says it's all three, or says you're flat out of one thing).

    When you scavenge in the ruins, roll+sharp.
    On a 10+, choose two and find an oddment worth 1-barter. On a 7-9, choose one and find an oddment worth 1-barter.
    - You find it quickly.
    - You find it with relatively little trouble.
    - You find an item that is +valuable.
    - You find an item that is +hi-tech.

    When you declare retroactively that you've already set something up, roll+Barter spent. On a 10+, it's just as you say. On a 7-9, you set it up, yes, but here at the crucial moment the MC can introduce some hitch or delay. On a miss, you set it up, yes, but since then things you don't know about have seriously changed.

    \n I resented the fact that the game told me I could highlight whatever stat I wanted to highlight, and even suggested I highlight the character's lowest stat (or weird), and then allowed players to dispose of that stat, because there was only one move attached to it. Although, if the rules told me I couldn't highlight a stat until I introduced a custom move that used it, I'd be cool with that.

    John, our discussion of stealth in AW was mainly about my dissatisfaction with it not being systematized. The move I wrote is an "infiltrate or steal" kind of stealth move, for when you use stealth to obtain something. You can get what you want, and you can get away clean. 10+ you get both, 7-9 you get one (you choose), 6 or less, you get none. When you don't "get what you want" that means you fail to obtain the item or information you were after, i.e. you can't steal the diamond or witness the cult ceremony. The GM will say what stops you. If you don't "get away clean" that means you are caught at the scene, you suffer harm, or you leave something behind, GM's choice.

    You can, of course, make stealth rolls every time you want to sneak past guards while you explore the enemy camp, with "what you want" being "to sneak past the guards." And if there's something fucking awesome just past the, it's all cool, but if there isn't, then you are playing "explore the map" and not AW. You could, of course, make one roll with the "what you want" being "I get to explore the whole camp." And then you get to sneak around until something special happens (like you blow up the munitions shed or stumble upon the super-brainer psychic child).

    The other reason for stealth is to ambush. The point here is either a) to actually get into a fight with somebody who otherwise won't let you (because they have a gun and you don't, or because they are inside a secure compound, etc), or b) to get a free hit on somebody instead of fighting with them. In which case, you can pretty much use the same system, but there's no guarantee of getting out again after the fight in a), and "getting away clean" in b) means your enemy can't retaliate.

    \n John: I don't know AW well yet, but I intend to. Here's the current highlight text from the player perspective:

    At the end, find the character with the highest Hx on your sheet. Ask that player which of your stats is most interesting, and highlight it. The MC will have you highlight a second stat too.

    Whenever you roll a highlighted stat, and whenever you reset your Hx with someone, mark an improvement circle. When you mark the 5th, improve and erase.

    That's it. I don't always know what people are going to view as meta, but off the top of my head I think that's about as meta as AW gets. I fully intend to pitch the game to a pretty traditional group so I can get the opportunity to MoC it.

    If you like the town creation in Dogs you are going to flip over Fronts.

    You'll just have to run it to see if it's for you. For myself, there is exactly one game I'd give a ten to in all the "Story Games" that have come out to date, and that's Mouse Guard. I'm not a particular fan of the game in terms of it being for me, and there are a pile of "nines" that I'll jump to play in front of Mouse Guard. (for the record I'd give Burning Wheel a five) It's too early for me to tell, but I suspect Apocalypse World will be the first RPG that I'd give give a ten that is also for me.

    \nIt's been awhile since I looked at the text, but yes. Or actually, 'designed around' is pretty misleading -- that's the kind of trap that I think Jonathan was talking about, above. It suggests an intent towards the characters that is not really necessary (and could just get in the way.) More accurately, the Fronts are descriptive of the fiction, and the fiction is centered around the PCs. The first session starts with the GM kind of following the PCs around, asking lots of questions about their lives and the people around them, looking for problems. Some of these questions are put to the players, some of them are kind of the GM asking themselves 'oh, what about this?' and writing those questions down. All of these answers and the NPCs that appear in this initial walkabout are what the GM uses to create the initial Fronts. (The GM is not passive, here, but the GM is taking actions in response to their general apocalyptic vision of the world.)

    This is what I meant by them being descriptive (as opposed to prescriptive) -- the Front comes after something in the fiction happens to make the GM go 'oh hey, that looks like it could be part of a larger problem'. The GM will then flesh that out on their own, but it starts from something that is already there. Fronts that are created later on follow the same pattern -- they arise out of interactions and events that are already in the fiction. There should never be a point where the GM is like 'oh damn, there's nothing going on, I better create an introduce a new Front' -- if the GM needs more Fronts she should go looking for them in the fiction, just like in the first session. Creating a Front in a vaccuum is a bad idea; the reverse of that is since all the Fronts are created out of the fiction, they all must necessarily relate to the PCs. Because the fiction is about these characters. The whole game is just following these characters around, seeing what they do (and don't do), and how the world reacts to what they do (and don't do.)

    There is nothing comparable to assigning relationship dice -- a clear, outside-the-fiction mechanical marker of interest -- though I suppose the player could also be like 'oh hey, my character knows that guy'. But more likely the AW equivalent would simply be for the PC to start getting involved in that person's life, by doing stuff. The thing about Dogs is that getting involved in somebody's business is the default -- it's part of what it means to be a Dog -- so that Relationship Die is more important, because it's like 'I'm not just doing this because it's part of the town'. In AW the default action is to let people suffer and deal with their own damn problems -- so if your character starts dealing with other people's problems, that's a pretty strong indicator that there is some interest.

    \n Johnskot, I was talking to Harper (trying to keep my Johns distinct) about that this afternoon, more or less.

    One of the parts that's going to be hard for me is that AW really tells the GM to basically stop paying super close attention to the players and what they want. That's metagame bullshit, as far as AW is concerned, at least most of the time. If you, as GM, are paying attention to the players, rather than the characters and what the players say the characters actually do, you're gonna fuck it all up. You can't do that magical GM trick where you give the players what they want but didn't know or weren't allowed to ask for. You can't challenge the players the way they like to be challenged. None of that. Fuck what the players want. If they really want something, they should have their characters do something about it; then you, as the GM, can respond. You can't respond to things that haven't happened yet, fictionally, because the fiction drives everything.

    There aren't flags in the normal sense, then, at least not as a major thing. Instead what happens is something a bit like the GM spending danger in Danger Patrol, if you've played that. In Danger Patrol, the GM only gets to attack the players when they roll danger, which the GM spends to hurt them or make bad things happen as a result of their roll. AW does something similar, where the GM gets to make moves when players fail rolls... which means that the GM's move is always directly related to something a character was trying to do. This leads to a similar alignment of GM/player actions, as if the GM was hitting particular flags, but without the GM having to do all that meta tracking of player desires. The GM also gets to make a move in a few other critical cases: when the players create the perfect opportunity, when the players allow the GM's plans to come together unchallenged, and a few others. But the GM is basically reacting to what the players do, at least until there's a pause in the game and everybody's looking for the GM to say something (which is also one of the instances in which you're allowed to make a GM move).

    The GM also doesn't call for rolls. The players decide when they are making a particular move and then roll. Those are the only circumstances under which you roll dice (though maybe the new Harm rules add a qualifier). I keep having discussions about this with Harper, because it's somewhat strange to wrap your head around, in the same way as "Say Yes" in the beginning. Imagine this example, from Dead Weight:

    HARPER: "You're running from the zombies in the downpour and make it to the rain-slicked roof of the old warehouse."
    (He's following the fiction here, making the world real, and explicitly NOT setting up a conflict.)

    JAYWALT: "Uh, shit, I skate across it in my sneakers, trying to maintain my momentum without totally skidding off the edge."
    (I'm following the fiction too, but I've -- perhaps inadvertently -- invoked some of the mechanics here by describing the roof as an obstacle I'm attempting to overcome, something I didn't necessarily have to do.)

    HARPER: "So, you're 'Acting Under Fire'?"
    (He thinks I've declared a move without necessarily realizing it; he's NOT telling me to roll dice, necessarily; the question is a sincere one, making sure we're on the same page about what the fictional action I've just described means mechanically.)

    JAYWALT: "Crap, hmm, maybe. It sounds like it, doesn't it?"
    (Here we are building consensus about whether the fiction triggers a specific mechanic.)

    HARPER: "That's what I thought I heard you say."
    (One of the GM's jobs is to listen for when a player is making a move, which is a pretty different task than telling them to roll or setting up conflicts. Basically, you listen for situations when something the player has already said their character is doing is one of the "when X" conditions that require a roll. You do this rather than specifically making those situations happen, instead letting them arise 'naturally' from the fictional events.)

    JAYWALT: "Hmm, I'm gonna spend my remaining advance and take the 'Steady Feet' special move which allows me to easily cross any precarious surface."
    (I choose another action, this time a mechanical one but it could be fictional as well -- whipping out a grappling hook, for example -- that doesn't require a roll.)

    HARPER: "Awesome. Yeah, so you totally slide right across the roof, barely loosing any speed."
    (Not rolling is totally fine with John; this isn't a conflict, he has no investment in any particular outcome, and so I can totally choose to do something else.)

    \n John, here's the list of GM moves. They are all totally fictional things with no mechanics for doing them, though Vincent does describe them a bit better. You just narrate them into the fiction and the players have to do things -- which sometimes requires making moves -- in order to respond. That's how you get more reactive players to do things, by making hard moves directed at them or the things they care about. Eventually, some of them may step up and decide to take a more proactive stance to do something about it.

    - Separate them
    - Put someone in a spot
    - Offer an opportunity, with or without a cost
    - Tell them the possible consequences and ask
    - Inflict harm (as established)
    - Trade harm for harm
    - Announce future badness
    - Announce off-screen badness
    - Take away their stuff
    - Invoke scarcity and want
    - Activate their stuff's downside
    - Make a custom threat move [these come from your Fronts]

    Sledge's booming voice threatening a character is probably "announce future badness." He's about to come through the door and hit you with a hammer. What do you do? If the GM says he comes through the door swinging, without announcing his arrival, that could be "put someone in a spot."       \n Adam, I have no idea how you played that long and had so little happen in the fiction, since -- in my experience -- the fiction drives everything else in the game. With the exception of the 5-player session we played recently, we easily get 2x or 3x our "normal" dose of fiction when playing AW. Did you have 4 or 5 players?

      John, players can't create NPCs or objects instantly, because one of the core themes of the game is scarcity. There's a very tightly restricted amount of stuff (though always plenty of bullets) and there's actually, as far as I know, a very strict list of names from which the GM can create and name NPCs, basically implying -- without outright saying it -- that there's a limited number of people -- or, at least, named, specific NPCs -- in the world. Sure, the player can say, "I go find a local medic/science dude." And the GM can say, "Great, the best person you can think of is... (scans list)... Torq (crosses name off). You've known her for about six months, ever since she rolled in with that chopper gang. You're gonna go see her? She definitely knows a LOT of things about poisoning people." Or if the players want stuff they don't have, they have to go find people who have them and barter or work for them.

      Fictional descriptions in AW don't make you better or worse at things, but they can change what move it is that you're doing, which effectively does the same thing. Since the moves you make derive from what's happening in the fiction, the details of the fiction are super important. You're gonna have better stats in some areas and therefore prefer to make certain kinds of moves if you want to succeed. However, there will also be some instances where you will feel inclined to Act Under Fire or Manipulate people even if you're bad at that. Sometimes you will want to do a specific move, like "I Go Aggro on him!" and its the GM's job to ask "Okay, how do you do that?" because that's not something in the fiction yet. And you may say "I beat the snot out of him with an axe handle for challenging my authority!" But the GM could respond: "So are you focused on causing him pain and harm or on making sure your still have the authority in this gang?" So it may turn out that, according to what you want to do in the fiction, what you want isn't really to Go Aggro, it's to Seize With Force (the authority).

      Attacking 1 person and attacking a group (say, a "small gang," since that's a mechanically measured unit of people) is the exact same roll with the exact same difficulty. But a small gang is a lot tougher than a single person (they can take more Harm) and also can inflict a lot more Harm on you than a person can, unless that person is armed with a grenade launcher or has some crazy special move, like the already infamous NOT TO BE FUCKED WITH (which is apparently always written in all caps and makes a character the equivalent of a small gang).

      \n Things that aren't measured mechanically, like in the Harm they can do, are measured by their fictional consequences (a nuke probably doesn't even deal Harm, for example, it probably just has the fictional consequence of.. well, everything that happens when you set off a nuke: instant death, fallout, giant cloud, etc.).

      YOU: I'm gonna build a nuke.
      GM: Great. How do you do that?
      YOU: Well, I'm gonna need some uranium.
      GM: Cool. Where are you gonna get that?
      YOU: Can I buy it off somebody?
      GM: Do you know anybody who has uranium?
      YOU: What about those crazy mutant guys who live in the old leaky reactor?
      GM: You're gonna go talk to them?
      YOU: Yeah, sure. What could possibly go wrong?

      And so begins an entire adventure full of hijinks. And at the end, sure, maybe your character builds a nuke. That's cool. Let's see what they do with it. That'll be totally awesome. There's no mechanical or GM-force-style resistance to making specific things happen in the fiction because they're unfair or unbalancing or whatever. If the players actually do it in the fiction, it just happens.

      \n I can answer this!

      By plain oversight, I didn't include the rules for changing your highlighted stats in the playtest doc, or even in the preview book. They're in the final book, of course.

      The rules are:

      At the end of any session, anybody can say "hey, let's change highlighted stats." Do. Just go again through the same stat-highlighting process you started with -- for every PC, the high-Hx player chooses 1 and the MC chooses 1.

      When you're highlighting stats, highlight one that you genuinely think will be interesting. If the character never rolls, or can't roll, a stat, it's obviously not going to be interesting to highlight it. As the player in question, you should always point out the stats you don't roll.

      You're also allowed to say "but I want something other than cool highlighted!" or "hey would one or the other of you highlight my weird?" or "okay, but I like my highlights fine the way they are." It's their choice, but you're allowed to say what you want.

      These are all totally fine and appropriate interactions, presuming that everybody's already following the above rules:

      Keeler: Marie, I can't decide whether to highlight your sharp or your weird.
      Marie: My sharp please!
      Keeler: Sure.

      Keeler: Marie, I can't decide whether to highlight your sharp or your weird!
      MC: Don't sweat it, whichever you choose, I'll highlight the other one.

      Marie: Keeler! Please highlight my sharp!
      Keeler: Okay, but I don't know if I want to.
      MC: I'll get her sharp, Keeler. Highlight what you want.

      Marie: Keeler highlighted my weird but I really want my sharp highlighted. MC, would you?
      MC: Sure, no prob, but next time we change highlights I'm choosing your hard, so be prepared for that.

      Since highlights change all the time, getting it wrong isn't costly, and neither is accommodating someone else's wants for a session or two.

      \n Let's talk about maps.

      Vx says, "Make maps like crazy," and he's right. We make all kinds in our AW games. Relationship maps, too.

      Here's a thing: that first map you make, GM, is key. If the sheet of paper is covered by the PCs hardhold, that's their world. When they decide where to go next and what to do, they'll be all, "I go over to Dremmer's shack, over here behind the chemical shed."

      If the sheet of paper shows the whole coastline and the four settlements along it, plus the train tracks and the oil rig out at sea, then that's their world. When they decide where to go next and what to do, they'll be all, "I take the train to Salt River to talk to Cricket about those murders he needs done."

      Think about the scale and the size of your Apocalypse World. And when to add another sheet of paper's worth to it.

      \n The mapping was a part of AW that I really enjoyed. We had maps that, even though we never used them in any traditionally "useful" way they gave us a pretty good idea of how the world fell together. As we needed a place, we added it to the map. Since it was a collaborative effort (rather than something like D&D where the DM makes his map and the players make theirs and, in the end, you see that you all had a really different idea of where the bugbears lived) we all got to invest something in the map.

      I had a map of Iris' house (an empty garage) and it was almost like my characters' inventory. We knew that Iris had a broken radio because we could point to the map and say "there it is, on the milk crate next to the decomposing futon".

      The maps really did scale out, too. It started as "Here is Willis' hold - it's where Kim and Iris live. Here's a few houses and the dog-kennel and that place where Iris and Balls got in a scuffle" and then slowly spread out, like a stain - we added in Chang's (that motherfucker) Farm and the place where Willis' son got killed. By the end, we had a folio of maps that painted a really interesting picture of the world our game had been written into.
