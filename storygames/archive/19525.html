\nHow do you become a better GM?
Become one with the system.

Some people think the GM is a player, just of a different kind than the other players. This is not true. Certainly as a human being with her own sensibilities, the GM experiences many of the same subjective delights that the players do, and gets to be an audience for them just as they are for her. But the GM is not portraying or identifying with a person who faces obstacles in order to attain successes. It would be pointless to do so, because of the tremendous power she wields relative to the players.

Even in games where the stated object is for the player characters to get creamed, go insane, or otherwise "fail", that in-character failure is an OOC player success, exactly because it was a stated object of the game. The GM's successes, on the other hand, are all OOC. Even when enacted through an NPC. They are successes of artistic performance, and they happen in the real world.

To put it another way: If you actually do want your NPCs to succeed and your players not to, you're doing it wrong. In fact, you're an asshole and you shouldn't be trusted with the contents of other people's imaginations.

The GM is not playing a game. The GM is, in fact, part of the game's system. She is the part that cannot yet be automated.

The function of the GM - whether performed by a single person or distributed throughout the group - is to adjudicate all aspects of uncertainty and convey all aspects of existence within the gameworld (including color, atmosphere and the behavior of NPCs) which cannot be handled by the rules and mechanics at the level of detail, verisimilitude and originality demanded by the aesthetic expectations of a modern audience vis-a-vis a given genre and story type. The GM is like a delivery platform: that part of the system which helps the players jump the gap from Projection to Immersion.

Even in PbtA games, where the MC has "moves" and rules that control when they may be made; even though the MC decides which of those moves to apply at any given time, her every utterance is guided by the "Always Say" rules, which command her to always say "what the rules demand" and "what honesty demands". This is a directive to remove ego from the equation. This is an injunction to become one with the system.

    \n I feel like we're dragging each other a little bit more off topic with each post, @Oren! This is the thread about GM & System, not the one about players and uncertainty mechanics. But that said, here I go... ;-)

    There are actually two games going on in Fiasco (and many other storygames). We might go with convention and associate them with "Actor Stance" and "Author Stance" (even though I don't love those terms.)

    Obstacles of different types occur on these two levels. In the gameworld ("Actor Stance"), fictional obstacles are faced by your character in the act of pursuing something, and if you identify your character's success with your own (as is true in typical RPGs), you wish to overcome them. If you don't identify with your character's success, you (hopefully) act more like a GM because you are more concerned with the quality of the story as a whole. In other words, the system, rather than a set of fictional desires, guides your decisions. (Which is how it should be for GMs, as asserted in the OP.)

    Meanwhile up here in our world ("Author Stance"), narrative imperatives and limitations are faced by you as a "writer" trying to make the best story you can, while other people are shifting the whole thing all the time. Both the fictional obstacles and the narrative imperatives are elective difficulties you have bought into, i.e., they are both games, but very different ones.

    You're right that simply throwing a die over and over is not a game; that's because it doesn't present any obstacle. Mere uncertainty does not make a game. Willful engagement with unnecessary difficulty, real or simulated, makes a game. Fiasco has two levels of game going on, although neither is heavily "Gamist". That distinction has more to do with one's approach than with the game itself.

    To that end, I am not a slave of the system. The system is a symbolic abstraction, a set of shared expectations which help the people at the table come together in similar understanding, and at their best provide a symbolic framework to communicate ideas that otherwise would be quite difficult to communicate in either of a quantified or scalar way.       \nBut likewise arguably a much, much higher chance of someone at the table having the talent to be the hero that pulls the group out of that particular failing. Let's not fool ourselves; if the argument is that there are innumerable ways to fail -- that's true. But each of them are different, it's not just one "failure," and if there are innumerable ways to fail, there are innumerable ways to break that failure as well. If you want to increase the liklihood of someone at the table being able to turn failures in experience into successes, you need more people with the capability to.

      That, in my mind, reflects one of the biggest weaknesses of the traditional gaming model. One person at the table is responsible for bringing the conflict, for maintaining the community, for building the story, for avoiding the potholes, and for keeping the car out of the ditches. There are a lot of ditches. That, in a nutshell, is why for myself, I hate the traditional GMing model. It's aggravated by the fact that everybody at the table recognizes that form of social contract and if most of them are experienced gamers, that's their "default operating mode," building a base of assumption from which to actually play the game.

      I did that for 20 years. I was (and in many ways still am) the omni-GM of my social group. I'm the neophile and I'm the one fascinated by design in general. That means that I get stuck not only being Facilitator but before I could essentially find it in myself to put my foot down and say, "this far and no further" and effectively refusing to run more traditionally architected GM-full designs, being stuck as and only as a GM.

      When a game fails in a traditionally architected GM-centric format, we put it on the head of the GM. It's his responsibility. It's his failure. We talk about dysfunctional groups all the time, and how they can be dysfunctional, but when the failure comes the GM takes the rap. That's the nature of the architecture, because the GM is the only one with both the socially contracted responsibility and the mechanically contracted power to rescue any particular failure mode. The non-GM players don't actually have it as their job to make the car go. (One of the potential failure modes and categorically dysfunctional group dynamics is for one or more of the non-GM players to take it on as their personal crusade to introduce some sort of failure mode. Every GM has a list of places where they have hidden the bodies of such people.)

      Now, back to where I came in on this, compare this to group collaborative games where responsibility and power are distributed across the group by some means. Yes, you increase the possibility that one of those people will have a "fatal tic" that introduces the potential for everyone to have less fun than they might otherwise \u2013 to greater or lesser degree a "failure". But you commensurately, compared to a traditional GM-full architecture, increase vastly the probability and allow for the possibility that someone else at the table has the knowledge, talent, or chutzpah to turn that fatal tic into a non-event at worst and possibly even into an experience that nobody would've had otherwise \u2013 in a positive sense.

      In my heart, I am still an engineer \u2013 my first calling, my first training, and my first career. In that time I learned one of the most important things in developing and making anything: fail early, and fail often. The earlier you fail the less bad you feel about it because you're not heavily invested into whatever it was. The more often you fail, the more you know that you're trying something at the edge of your capability and you become inured to the idea that failure is possible, likely if you're extremely experimental, and okay to accept \u2013 it allows you to move on to something else that very well might work without carrying some sort of crushing burden.

      This is equally applicable to gaming in the context of finding something with your group that works. One of the big differences I've observed between traditional GM-full game design and collaborative GM-less design is that the latter \u2013 if it's truly not a fit for the group \u2013 fails out earlier and with less emotional overhead. Traditional GM-full designs can go on quite a while before the fatal tic, whatever it is, strikes home, and that leaves a lot of people in emotional turmoil, because they've invested a lot of their time and personal effort in playing this game. As a GM they're frustrated because they feel responsible and the mechanics emphasize their responsibility. As a player they're frustrated because someone else is failing them. All kinds of strife falls out, and that is the opposite of fun. If your group is absolutely not going to enjoy a collaborative, GM-less game \u2013 and those groups exist, those people exist; there is not One True Way, despite what people will tell you \u2013 better for it to fail on you early than for you to get six months into a campaign and suddenly realize that the GM is failing you, or that the GM's interpretation of the rules is failing you, or that the rules are failing you in the GM is faithfully executing them, or that the social contract is failing you and you feel disempowered to do anything about it.

      In my opinion, anyway.

      In my experience, the actual frequency of GM-less games failing is roughly equal to those of more traditionally GM-full designs. When they work for a group, they really work for group and they are very happy. When they don't work for a group, that failure happens early, then move on to something else, and they very well might come back to it later when they've shifted some perspective. So from a na\xefve position, just looking at "new game starts," that fail early trait can make it look like they have a much higher fail often quality, but that's not actually the case.

      We have to be careful about the implications of saying things like "there are too many cooks that the game has to rely on," because the ultimate expression of that is a recognition that the best gameplay you'll ever have, consistently and ongoingly, is by yourself \u2013 making sure that the taint of other people can never enter the temple of your holy gaming. Now, I don't know about you, but even though most of my personal romantic relationships have ended in fire and screams, I'm not quite ready to give up sex in favor of masturbation. Just putting that on the table.

      We need to be aware and actively engaged with the fact that the social contract, the structure of expectations at the table, is both incredibly important and largely outside the domain of what the game itself as a product and as an artifact is responsible for. If we want different social contracts we have to be willing and interested to experiment with radically different game designs that distribute responsibility, that distribute power, that distribute narrative in very different ways than what has been traditional. Have to be very careful to avoid philosophies which cut away the space of possibility of new exploration and of, by extension, theoretical new social contracts which could grow out of those explorations.

      The only thing I don't agree with is the same thing as what @Hasimir brought up, and I really would like to see an answer to his question.
      An initial answer can be found at the bottom of this post here. But sure, I'll elucidate. As I understand it, Hasimir's question was "How can I say this approach is more reliable in terms of generating a meaningful and artistic experience for the players?" This is the question I'll be answering below. But before I do that, some notes on terminology are in order.

      What is GMing vs What is Not GMing
      Firstly, because for some reason it isn't patently obvious to some people, I need to make clear that throughout this entire thread, I've been talking only about RPGs which involve GMs. (If you can think of another medium in which the words "GM" and "Player" are used, let me know, because this seemed pretty obvious to me.) It would be slightly impossible to talk about GMing in games where no GMs are involved, as far as I can tell. The OP and several posts since have mentioned "distributed GMs" (which is not the same thing as "No GMs"), and I don't exclude those games from consideration here. But it seems that the locally-sanctioned use of these well-known words is slippery compared to the common understanding. We may need to invent some more precise terms, if only to prevent members of the community from getting obnoxiously pedagogical or attacking others for using these words as they are typically understood. Personal attacks and insulting rebuttals do in fact occur over this stuff, even among otherwise civil company, and this either implies that the community tacitly supports dickish pedantry, or that our words are failing us. That's why I say "GM-less" (or "GM-Full") games really ought to be called something else entirely, while @Paul_T suggests that it's the GM'd games that might need another name. So far the typical solution has just been to stretch the definition of the word "GM" to near-meaninglessness, guaranteeing further confusion, discord and argument. Fuck that. I am sticking with the original functional definition, regardless of how many people perform those functions.

      Reliable, Meaningful, Artistic
      It's important to keep all three of these values in mind, because they are all equally important performance goals for me. It's possible to create systems that are "reliable" only to the extent that the content they generate isn't completely nonsensical, but I aim higher than that. This not my pastime but my Artform, and I take it very seriously. Because of this, players who become regular members of my groups are those who appreciate those values themselves and approach the Art with a similar amount of creative seriousness.

      Ok. Now that the disclaimers are out of the way, let's get on to the exegesis.

      Discipline and Experience
      The simplest answer to @Hasimir's question is that unlike the players, I spend a very large percentage of my time reading, writing, and thinking about narrative and interactive structures in all media including but not limited to RPGs, with the deliberate intention of seeking out systems and elements that could be put to good use in interactive fiction, either mechanically, structurally, or as content. Add to this thousands of hours spent studying works from related fields, such as literary arts, performing arts, systems theory, game theory, psychology, sociology, anthropology, history, computer programming, interface design, etc., not to mention the time spent literally practicing my discipline directly (i.e., running games). I spend all this time doing all these things - including reading and posting here, btw - because I just happen to be the type of person who is driven to design interactive experiences, and therefore I see these studies as valuable additions to both my designer's and GM's toolkits. If you're reading this, chances are you're the same way. This is not surprising: it's why @Rickard has his "We Know So Little" thread, for instance. It's why I'm working on a manuscript called "The History of Interactive Fiction". It's why many of us write blogs, to pursue and share knowledge - both direct and allegorical - that only people like us care about. But to expect or require a similar amount of thought or prepwork to be done by any player just to get into a game - that would be unreasonable as well as unreliable, and would filter out almost every potential player who isn't also a GM.

      \n It's possible that the above doesn't sound like a real answer to you, but rather a simple explanation of personality types. So for those who are interested, I can go into much more detail about my actual processes. Here we go.

      What I'm Doing
      My default approach to GMing begins with a trad setup, where the rules adjudicate things like action resolution, encounter generation, moves and transactions, while I serve as an "data input operator" who plugs data into the rules and determines/communicates the system's response. This is the "slave to the rules" thing I've been accused of (Rule 0 notwithstanding), although it's merely the most mechanical part of the cyber-GM's functions. No mind-reading is necessary to do this. Please note: If this was a full explanation of my position (as some yet persist in assuming), there would have been no need for me to have written the OP at all.

      The other part of the cyber-GM is the human part, whose goal is Art and whose method is Meaning. Meaning is created by paying attention to psychologically-charged details or signifiers arising from the players themselves, drawing connections between them, and injecting them back into play in dramatic ways at opportune moments. I call this player-generated stuff "psychic content" (I can already hear someone charging me with "mysticism" - so before you waste your time, look up the meaning of the word "psychic" - you will find that the primary definition has nothing to do with ESP, and I shall keep my ideas about ESP entirely out of this). To obtain psychic content, I prompt and question my players a lot, I engage in NPC dialog a lot, I feed their own words and symbols back to them a lot and I carefully watch what happens. I am not just an output unit for the rules' determinations, but a dedicated processor for human stuff: meaningful symbolism, unconscious motivations (either character level or IRL, preferably both), and logical but unseen connections between the things players say and the rest of the gameworld. Once psychic content has been attached to a story element in this way, it becomes what we might call a "Bleed Hook". I like that phrase. A Bleed Hook gives me a handle on some ephemeral detail which is likely to bring about strong emotional responses from one or more players when re-introduced.

      With me so far?

      What The Group Is Doing
      When I'm GMing, the group works rather like a team of TV writers creating a soap opera, where each writer is predominantly responsible for a particular character's storyline (this is the way many soap operas and ensemble dramas are written). But simultaneously, every TV series also has a "Lead Writer" who acts mainly in service to overall continuity (this is often the person who "invented" the concept of the show and wrote the pilot episode), and every series has a "bible" (which is basically the TV industry word for a sourcebook).

      Together the rules and I play a role which is more like a narrative support system than an executive director. The role of the cyber-GM in relation to the group certainly has some "directorly" functions in it, but also includes continuity maintenance functions, lead writer functions, fact consultant functions, "bible" functions, stage crew functions, supporting actor functions and more. My players have a huge amount of narrative freedom and are able at any time to step up and perform any of these functions by simply making a suggestion. In play, however, most of them don't actually spend a lot of time performing these functions (which is a matter of preference and personality). This kind of "GM-ly" input simply pops up randomly whenever a player has a GM-ly idea and steps up with it. But as for the system and I, we don't get off so easily. We need to keep things flowing whether a player steps up or not, or the whole experience will lag. We accept that responsibility. That's what we came onboard to do.

      Because my players have the ability to inject "GM-ly" ideas but are under no particular pressure to do so, we get very few of the lame, obvious, or silly suggestions made by headlight-impaled deer such as you often get in "GM-less" games. My approach allows each player to focus as exclusively as they wish upon developing their own story arcs with full authorial agency and directionality up to any point where a resolution is required (a move, an interaction with an NPC, or the triggering of some "world-based" event such as a trap, weather, etc). Their agency halts there temporarily, because it's both pointless and boring to let people set up their own problems AND knock them down (i.e., the Czege Principle). So instead, when a player states something that requires a resolution, the system and I go into "resolution mode". Our job in this mode is to work together to help answer the question of HOW (or how well) the described action happens or fails to happen. If there is no rule covering the situation, I rely on the "spirit" of the rules to create a custom set of likely responses which operates in harmony with both the existing mechanics and the psychic content currently being explored. This is the technique I call "What would the system say?" If the attempted action is successful, the plot moves forward in the way the player was intending. If it fails, the plot moves forward but in some other way, generally a complication, depending on the spirit of the system and colored by all the psychic content I've gleaned from all my questions and prompts. Either way, plot moves forward, meaningless content is avoided, and a large amount of psychic content is produced, lending itself to subsequent meaningful interactions.

      That's why I say "reliable".
