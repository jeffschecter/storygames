\n I ran into this quote today in an article about "magical" free will vs. "boring" compatibilism.\n
...the magician Lee Siegel: \u201cI'm writing a book on magic, I explain, and I'm asked, Real magic? By real magic people mean miracles, thaumaturgical acts, and supernatural powers. No, I answer, conjuring tricks, not real magic. Real magic, in other words, refers to the magic that is not real, while the magic that is real, that can actually be done, is not real magic."
\nPeople find magical explanations more "real" than physical explanations. I myself am fascinated with spirits living in rivers, rocks, storms, and computers. It's not that I believe they do exist; rather, I believe they *should* exist. The world is less without them.

But why do I, and others, feel this way? I suspect we apply a generalized Czege Principle. A magical explanation feels Other in a way that a physical explanation does not. Any physical system we can identify with, make a tool, embody, etc. The more we understand it, the more it becomes just a part of ourselves. Magic, on the other hand, is always something we can't fully command or subsume. It is separate by definition, and so any interaction with magic is a dance with a partner. A magical explanation is a GM; a physical explanation is a system.

***

Now, I'm not up on how theory has progressed since the Czege Principle was popular. I know there have been good arguments made for solo play, but I've never an updated theory that accounts for the newly acquired nuance. But my hypothesis is that whatever nuances have been applied to the Czege Principle could, though analogy, apply as well to questions of when to appeal to magic in fictional content (or reality, for that matter) vs. when to appeal to more physical explanations.

    \n Well, the last part is more where I'm reaching for help. If it is true that we can apply the crude version of the Czege Principle in a more generalized fashion, then it is likely also true that its limitations extend as well. Well-designed solo play can be fun, so there's a challenger to the Czege Principle. That implies constraints, and constraints in a complex system suggest the opportunity for emergent understandings. What's the greater principle at work, how might it apply to magical thinking vs. system thinking, and how can we apply that in turn to the division of ideas between fictional content and system?

    \n (For reference for those who aren't aware, the Czege Principle is "creating your own adversity and its resolution is boring.")

    The Czege Principle is something I've been thinking about lately. Partially because I'm trying to design a GM-light game where players moderate for each other. The players frequently create their own problems, and I'm having a harder time than I thought getting them to not solve their own problems. I'm definitely interested in whether this discussion can give me any insight into getting beyond this conundrum.

    As for your specific question, I can only speculate at this point. At first blush, the idea that we need to ascribe the agency to something outside ourselves is probably important. I'm not sure yet if it's the only answer.

    What's fun about solo play? What are some good examples of it? What tools are there that might help manage the adversity/resolution problem?

    The few times I've done solo play, the overriding adversity was that the story had to follow a narrative logic; the goal was to try and see what would happen while not invalidating my sense of simulation and making sense. I don't know if this lines up with anyone else's solo play.

    Though experiment: There are times when we can have systems that provide enough adversity. Blackjack and Roulette are entirely driven by rules and physics, respectively. Co-op board games like Ghost Stories or Pandemic are entirely driven by the interactions between the rules and the cards. Now, these systems have a goal set for you, so its not quite the same thing as creating your own adversity. Can we have a story game system that provides similar constraints for the resolution while allowing you to set your own adversity? What tools does solo play use to deal with this? Can they be used in group play?

    Hope these thoughts help.

    \n For me it's easier to get into that magical thinking space with a strong premise than it has been to treat the actual tools of solo play as an other. They provide some pushback, but I can't deceive myself as to what is happening. occulus and de Profundis sort of get me halfway there, but I find that I'm more engaged if I go a bit meta.

    Ie, for oculus, I may roleplay someone who is using oculus as a talisman that uses dice, rather than play myself as the person looking in. So, in my mind's eye I may see someone else sitting down at a desk playing with the Oracle, and then dive in once more to see what they're seeing.

    For de profundis, doing something along those lines would help me as well, so I might Houserule that whenever I think something in the real world would make a cool happening for the character writing letters, the character sees it in his world. Maybe that's more like letting the real world bleed into the imaginary one than the other way around.

    The more we understand it, the more it becomes just a part of ourselves. Magic, on the other hand, is always something we can't fully command or subsume. It is separate by definition, and so any interaction with magic is a dance with a partner.
    \nWell said! I agree. I think this distinction separates magical-feeling RPG magic from scientific-feeling RPG magic.\n
    A magical explanation is a GM; a physical explanation is a system.
    \nIn a game where everything comprehensible was handled solely by a system, I would agree. As is, though, in games where GMs arbitrate and communicate not only the unknown but also the known, I think that distinction is lost.

    It might be fun to have two GMs:
    - One for the mundane operations of the setting, who always needs to be able to explain themselves if asked.
    - One for the magical events of play, who isn't even allowed to explain themselves if asked.

    Two different types of player interaction for two different types of character experience.

    For all the various clever magic systems out thee, I have yet to see one where the arbiter of spell effects is a hidden partner. It might be really cool to have a GM secretly create a character who is Magic, and then arbitrate all spells by the wishes and abilities of that character. The player characters would never meet Magic, but over the course of repeated interactions, they might learn things about it.

    Not that this has anything to do with solo play or the limits of making your own adversity; just running with what the opening post inspired me to brainstorm.
