    \nScene & Structure: How to construct fiction with scene-by-scene flow, logic and readability by Jack M. Bickham.

    I read it back in 2000 or so. It breaks scenes down into logical components and provides a language for talking about how they work and why. I found it incredibly useful. The way the scene economy in Burning Empires works owes a lot to that book. I think it's useful to anyone who wants to dig more deeply into the concept of scene framing.

    \n Hey Rob,

    You want the same tired old complication-->climax-->denoument structural stuff you heard in college? My experience is that this stuff, based on analysis, is not so helpful if what you're wanting to learn is how to craft great stories.

    Or you want a discussion of what makes a character a protagonist? If so, check out the Dramatica Theory Book. It has interesting ideas about types of characters you see in stories, and the roles they play.

    Or you want stuff about various story techniques? If so, read Theatrix. The sections on cut scenes and flashbacks are worth your time. And then play it. You'll appreciate how the sub-plots mechanically drive the central plot better if you do.

    Georges Polti's 36 Dramatic Situations is also worth a look.

    Paul

    \n Rob, you could try Joseph Campbell's story structure. It's outlined well in Christopher Vogler's The Writer's Journey: embark on a journey, meet a mentor figure, meet a gatekeeper to the next part of the journey, and so forth.

    I personally prefer the story guidelines used in improvisational theatre: build a platform, add a tilt, reincorporate. These are outlined well in Keith Johnstone's Impro.

    (I should also quietly mention that I talk a lot about story structure in Play Unsafe. But you've probably read that.)

    Graham

    \n Paul said "Dramatica" before I did. I endorse it! I've studied McKee, Hero's Journey, the Truby method, and many others. Almost all of these are stuck looking at structure as an audience would. Dramatica looks at things from inside, from the author's point of view. Very exciting.

    Once you have an introduction to the theory, I highly recommend the very first Dramatica in the Real World podcast at http://www.dramatica.com/podcasts/index.htm

    You can also download audio readings of the book chapter by chapter here.

    - Alan

    \nI also recommend Scene & Structure, it does a good job of breaking down a number of story "types" (action, thriller, adventure, ...) into useful concepts. I'm not sure if it is quite as relevant for drama and romance, but it's clearly very good for what it aims towards.
    \nMy understanding of drama was substantially increased by reading Lajos Egri's The Art of Dramatic Writing. It starts with the basics, has good examples and dialogue-like Q&A; sections, and then goes on to some more subtle ideas. Probably most interesting to roleplayers is that Egri derives plot from well defined characters tuned in a particular way to interact with each other, not the other way around (Aristotle for example believed that characters are secondary to plot).
    \n
    \nI've tried reading Christopher Booker's The Seven Basic Plots: Why We Tell Stories, which isn't too bad. There's some nice analysis of each of the plots he recognizes (for example: The Journey (Gulliver's Travels), Tragedy, Comedy, etc.), but sometimes it gets a bit long. I haven't started the second part which seems to be inspired by Carl Jung's concept of archetype and ventures into psychoanalytic theories about why the heck humans are interested in stories.
