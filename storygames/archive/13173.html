    \n These are contests I thought of but which I either never acted on or never really went anywhere:

    * Design on the Road: Design a game solely within the confines of your trip to some gaming event (like a convention). Make it a game that can be played on the road. Bonus if you can pull it off on public transportation without being an asshole or getting tased.

    * Freegan Design: The constraint on this one is in production rather than in themes. Use only freely-available software, tools, and (if you're really hard-core) computers. Super-mega points for being able to sell something without spending any money.

    \n One disadvantage of "assign person A to person B" (like Reverse Engineer) is the high dropout rate. I think it is best to make each stage/contribution/whatever independent to some degree. So a Reverse Engineer challenge could be structured like this: Interested people design character sheets, then another cohort of interested people choose from among them to make games. You could offer a special prize for the most-used character sheet.

    \n Almost five years ago I issued six challenges for truly boundary breaking RPG design:

    Without Characters, Without Words, Without Sharing, and RPG as Society, RPG as Work, RPG as Ceremony.

    My hope was that by the end of this century most of these design challenges would be solved. But you could always try to push for things by making one of them a target for a design competition.

    - Mendel

    \n Primary (stronger version):
    What about a roleplaying game without roleplaying? You still use the roleplaying format of paper and pencils and people sitting around the table - you just don't play characters. Instead, you do something else. However, the mechanics still allow you to form a narrative and change and grow, just not as a person. Perhaps its civilization building? Perhaps its running a company. Perhaps you're all galactic outposts/space stations/settlements? Plenty of boardgames have tried to co-opt roleplaying games, now its time for roleplaying games to co-opt board games!

    Alternate (weaker version):
    There need to be more games where you play groups of people rather than a single character. Perhaps its a liniage, perhaps its a clan, perhaps a tribe, a corporation, or a cult. There just need to be many of you working for your common goals and beliefs. Each scene you may choose a character to play but ultimately your character sheet is a group of characters and only your collective advances.

    \n Given that game design is moving toward production of artifacts (particularly mainstream game design, but you are starting to see glimmers of it in indie games as well), I've been thinking about running some contests with artifacts in mind. Such as:

    Build a story game entirely contained on a custom "GM Screen", rules and all.

    Build a story game which makes use of Icehouse pyramids in a fundamental way.

    Build a story game out of the incoherent ramblings of my horrible Little Game Chef entry Valence or some other concept based around using molecular models for gaming.

    I should probably just offer bounties for some of these ideas.

    \n Create a game that uses oracles/playsets. After you print them out, you fold them up on the dotted lines without looking at them. As the game progresses, you unfold the paper to resolve conflicts or set the next scene. Sometimes you write on the sheet. As the paper unfolds, some things are visible, others are not. Think playsets + origami.

    \nI like the idea of games that make use of physical playing pieces to make their rules more tactile and simplify the more complex procedures, but I can't say I'm too enthused with those pyramids 'cause I don't know where you'd get them in this country (the UK). However I do think a design contest majoring on creating custom accessories for use at the gaming table is a good one, I just think that people ought to be able to choose their own accessories and how to make them. Cardstock pieces for instance solves the problem of not being able to find the stuff, since anyone with a printer can make their own.
