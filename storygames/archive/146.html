    My reaction to the thread was that I was hoping to see the bit that really echoed with me when Ron and I talked about it last week, the real world example. Thusfar, I'm not seeing it said word-for-word again, so I'll summarize it here:

    ==============================

    We gamers say: "This game (Deadlands, RIFTS, Burning Wheel, The Shadow of yesterday***, whatever) is a game fundamentally about telling stories. What is the FIRST THING we need to do to create our story experience? Why, we have to make up Characters".

    So we make up characters and then try to fit them into a Situation. Usually the GM puts them in a situation. All the players enjoy their game. That's nice. But it's something different than storytelling.

    Now, OTHER people say (and I'm not specifically saying "Players or Polaris" or Sorcerer or whatever, but I'm talking about average joe people, people who read books, watch TV shows like LOST or whatever, and have been influenced all their lives by stories), when they want to create a story: "OK, we'll begin with a Situation. Here's the world; here's what's going on. Everyone on board? OK, NOW let's define the world a little deeper, putting in Characters that have to interact in this Situation" (or they characters are simply built INTO the situation).

    ===============================

    That was the part that was most eye-opening for me: Gamers begin with making characers first (it's usually the first chapter or two of any RPG, after the "wishy washy grand-scale background" parts are laid out). All other humans, who inherently know how to tell stories, begin with situation/main story elements first.

    That's what really opened my eyes, and lead to the following personal convictions:

    1) When I write my games, one of my goals is basically write so that, say, one gamer could introduce the game to 4 non-gamer friends and have it be instantly understood by "everyone". "Everyone" already has the tools and knowledge of what stories are. If I make my game more like a traditional storytelling experience, and put situation right up front, and rethink other elements that are standard fare for RPers, then my goal of having "everyone" able to pick up the game and quickly play will be closer realized.

    2) And if I want to write a game for gamers, because those games are fun as hell too when done right, then I can consciously leave situation out, or tie it into the rules like Polaris, or create situation for the game in other ways, like the game All Flesh Must Be Eaten (where in the back there are 18 or so "example settings", which are all situations of some sort, but I would have put that in the front of the book, not the back). In other words, I can still have a goal of writing games for gamers, but make subtle changes to realign the game towards a more understandable experience.

    3) Finally, I remember my first RPG: Star Frontiers. My dad and I picked it up when I was like 7-9 or so, and went through the basic rulebook, and made characters and played, using that giant map-thing that came with the book. We literally had NO experience with D&D or other RPGs, we just picked it up because we liked SF and the commercial for the game (an RPG Commercial!!! On TV!!!!) looked cool. We ended up playing the game like a board game, having our little cardboard pieces meander around from one place to another. It was sorta fun for a little while, but we stopped after 2-3 sessions. I could see by the depth of rules and background that there was "something deeper" going on, I just couldn't put my finger on what. There was all this racial background, some setting info, rules for breaking and fixing shit, etc.

    About a year after we bought the game (though I read the books a lot, brought them on car trips, etc just to read the cool crunchy background), I remember I had an epiphany after reading the "adventure module" for like the 18th time: I'm supposed to use this game to MAKE UP AND TELL STORIES. It was such an utter flooring revelation, that I remember exactly where I was (after church, sitting in the back seat of a volvo, and I was wearing a white shirt) when I had it. A couple weeks later, I was making up my own stories to have my father and I play through.

    ....But isn't that a little fucking ridiculous, when you think about it? Why did it take me and my dad over a year to realize that we were supposed to use the game to make stories? Because it wasn't written anywhere in the book. It may have said "You will have adventures", but it didn't jive with any of our previous experiences with fiction, and we were both avid SF buffs, tearing through a book or more a week. I had to twist and turn and dig before I realized what was supposed to happen with the game. I think most of the problem lies with the fact that most of these games were predicated upon an understanding of previous RPGs, or having been "brought into the fold" from others, etc.

    4) I actually had an 6 month TSOY game stumble a bit (I had to reboot) because, after 6 MONTHS, I realized that there was no situation; we were sort of meandering from event to event with these "situation ripe" characters, not seating them in any situation. Through ignorance or whatever, we were just using TSOY to lpay D&D. I "rebooted" the setting a little when we jumped to Revised, and together we created a really focused situation with the other players: It still was a little backwards, but it was better than pulling the plug completely: Sure enough, people were hitting their Keys, getting deeper into it, etc. I know that I had a lot more fun then. At the time, I wasn't sure what the deal was: I thought the problem was that I had the characters wander about from place to place "D&D Style" instead of setting them in one location where they could get their keys firing with background NPCs. But no, that wasn't quite it: I just blew threw the small section at the beginning of the book which laid out ideas for starting up a Situation with the players.

    Anyway, those were my reactions to that specific conversation. Personally, on that thread, I'm most in line with Levi's sentiments on the matter (as when we think of "brain damage" we almost immediately think "unrepairable") but still. Personally, I like Ron's metaphor more of "Wearing a better and better prosthetic lower leg; then looking down to see that your own lower leg was always there, just bent back, tied back to your upper leg". I took away a lot from that conversation.

    --------------------

    ***Here's the fucking freaky part. I wanted to drop "Polaris", "Conspiracy of Shadows", "The Roach" or "Trollbabe" into that category, showing that I meant "all games", not "traditional ones". Here's the problem with that, though: Polaris has Situation tied into the background and rules of the game: It is IMPOSSIBLE to read the book, play as written, and have no idea what to do with the progression of the story. Trollbabe is, too, to a different degree: Not as much story-progression going on (like how to wrap them up, etc), but definitely has Situation on the goddamned character sheet. Other games, like Conspiracy of Shadows, Nine Worlds, and tons of other have been made with that "prosthetic" in mind where before chargen they clearly spell out that everyone should be onboard the situation creation: Create and agree on situation, then populate with characters. Though most of us just blow past that anyway. :-)

    So yeah, in the last two years we've seen some games make loose, VERY loose ties with traditional story elements and the like. But 2006 I think is the "line in the sand": From this point on, I'm expecting to see really cool games with zany settings and action, but that are written aimed squarely towards those storytelling parts of the brain, with better explanations of what to do and how to do it, etc. Or we'll see games manufactured towards traditional RPers, but even more juicy with an Understanding of how to make a story. Even if it's not used in the rules and game taext, a game written with that understanding will be easier to whip out and play than a game without.

    Also, that conversation led me to "reboot" the game I'm currently working on a little. I had as a goal to make it understandable for newbie gamers. Now, having that above conversation in my toolbox, I'm backing up, and making sure that it's more ripe with situation-creation instructions and the like before character creation. I already even had some of those bits on the character sheet, but now I'm turning it up a notch: Not too much, but just a little.

Big chunks of habit aren't bad of themselves, by any stretch - I mean, potty-training is a block of habits, too, and it's served me very well. This particular chunk of stuff is composed of things that do work, and often things that don't work, and workarounds for the things that don't.

    Now, let me mention that bit again, because I think it's important. I think there are often things that don't work in that block of habit, and workarounds for them. I think those things are there because we've been sold on them, and sold ourselves on them, and then had to work around them.

    Now, we can put away those habits, open up, and just naturally tell stories. Some of us don't, of course, just as some fan-ficcers choose to see the real stories they fic about through the lens of that fiction. This doesn't mean that we can't.

    We can try to filter down through that block of habit, and rebuild it. That's hard; it's not impossible at all, but it takes a lot of slogging.

    We can ignore the block entirely, and play something else - deliberately leaving the whole mass off to one side. If what we play actually turns out to generate stories in roleplaying, without involving that block of habit, the habits rapidly start to break apart; finishing the job may still be hard, but starting is easy.

    Regarding natural storytelling ability:

    I have three kids (well, two stepkids I've been raising for the past six years and one toddler). The older two are 9 and 11. In short, they don't possess a natural ability to filter out the "core story."

    Now, you might say: Yes, but they're kids! True. But how are they going to get to the point where they can understand and relate the core of a story? They need to learn it. In fact, since we're homeschooling, Lisa and I need to teach them.

    That's one anecdotal piece of evidence. Now, the other pieces in my anecdotal chest come from being an aspiring author who has been to workshops and writing groups--and from my own learning process regarding stories. I can now, finally, distill a premise from a story, but I'm still not highly proficient in it. And most aspiring writers I've met are terrible at that and at most other aspects of writing a good story.

    Now, that's not a bad thing. In fact, I frequently have to argue with assholes who think that writing is somehow a "talent" and you either have it or you don't (like Darren did on the Forge, and in response to which I wrote him a private message). That's utter bullshit. Storytelling is a craft; in fact, it's a larger field with several sub-crafts. It can be learned by just about anyone, but it's always hard work. Even for people who seem "gifted", they need to practice and edit and spend a lot of time on making a good story.

    Now, maybe that's not the level of proficiency we're talking about here. But even the basics, when people are told and go "ahh yes, conflict and characters and resolution and change", are not that easy to translate into actively creating a story.

    That all said: while I disagree that most people have a natural talent for storytelling, I agree that so-called storytelling games have tended to muddy the waters, give people bad habits, and overall created what I like to non-confrontationally refer to as "preconceived or conditioned notions" about how (storytelling) play should go. Getting those out of people's brains is hard work. Empowering them to actually focus on the story instead of those built-up artifacts will take time and energy, but most of all, probably more of a cooperative rather than an incinerating approach.

      I have a game where each player will, at times, take on a role where their task is to provide fictional opportunities for the spotlight character to get what they want. I've discovered that calling this role "GMing" is a bad idea, because many players bring in their GMing behaviors that are appropriate for other games but not for this one. Simply introducing it as "GMing but not like GMing in other games" from the get-go has made a world of difference.

      A more difficult scenario is introducing deadly dungeon crawls with GM warnings to players who are used to deadly dungeon crawls without GM warnings.
