\n Hi! there has been a good share of threads on touch these last weeks. Here's another one, a bit different. I hope you find this a good idea. Trying to be very constructive here.

The idea is to use this thread in a very practical way. To post very simple and nonthreatening tools to be used in RPGs, involving touch in some way.

I will start:

The handshake
Opening a roleplaying game, I recommend GMs to take each player in the hand, as old custom dictates, and give a greeting (say your name if you are strangers) while meeting their eyes. The handshake makes a "one-on-one bubble" around the two of you. The contact achieved in this "bubble" is a very effective foundation for the intercourse you and your players are engaging in, in a roleplaying game. It will make the players trust your ability to see them. As a leader you may build on this trust to further your game.

Do the handshake with each and every player at the start of each gamesession.

    \n Filip: to give thanks for the game is very nice, at the end of the game session. To do so by a handshake is good.

    As for "boring anti touching people": I believe it is far more unsettling to talk about touch than to actually do it. A hug or a handshake is an ordinary thing. To talk about hugs and so on is a bit more ... touchy.

    The friendship double handshake

    If in the course of a game two characters become friends it is manifested by a double handshake. You take the hands of each others, hold them between you and say: "I am your friend!" Both say this, one at a time. You may choose to use the character name while saying it, like this: "Zopher is your friend!" It's a simple ritual to celebrate the friendship, and to strengthen the emotional content of it. This may be done between players, and between GM and players.

    \n Here's one I've seen used in everything from flirting to political debate:

    The touch of attention

    Look another player in the eye, lean forward and touch their thigh lightly with your fingers. Keep them there while talking. This will create a strong link between the two of you, and you'll have their undivided attention for a moment. Use this to convey a message of importance, perhaps as part of creating a relational link. Very effective in romantic games.

    \n That's a fairly common one, yes, in flirt. I find touching the thigh of someone to be a bit too sensual for political use. That's me, of course. I would be a dreadfully dull politician!

    In friendship and intrigues I believe the following one is far more common.

    The touch of trust

    Look another player in the eye, lean forward and touch his wrist while talking. This will create a strong link between the two of you. You'll have his undivided attention for a moment, and will convey a message of unflinching trust. This tool is effective in positive relationships, but it may also be used to deceive. It can compel a player (and his character) to trust an antagonist. By winning one player, you win them all, and make them ripe for betrayal. It is a game, after all, so of course you may use touch to support the villains too.

    \nThe finger touch of attention
    \n
    \nLet's say your characters meet an NPC of authority. In situations like this, make the players stand. Be the character of authority and walk among the players. When an important point is made, point your finger at one of them and then thump it a bit on their chest to drive the point home. I have done this and found that usually the player you thump misses the point that was trying to be made but all the other players remember it just fine.
    \n
    \nThe hand slap
    \n
    \nUse a standard "give me 5" hand slap here to give kudos to a player. It brightens their mood and makes them feel good. Get them used to it then move to the missing hand.
    \n
    \nThe missing hand slap (lack of touch)
    \n
    \nThis is the same as above but pull your hand away or just put it out and then give the player a look of "nahh" then pull it back before they slap. After this is done the player feels incomplete and out of place. If they are expecting a hand slap and have been getting one the missing hand will mess with them a bit. As a GM, try not to get the urge to say "ah, ok" then give them the slap. I find that I use this technique on players who may have finished a combat etc but missed something important. Do not overuse.

    This is really just organizing normal ways of touching, to make them into more available and conscious tools for use in RPGs. I believe a lot of GMs, and possibly a few gamesmiths too, will have great use for this.

    I'd like to note that the psychology of touch is one of relation-building and happiness. By doing a lot of touch in your games you are really preparing the field for good gaming, good relationships and happy players. It is beneficial to the creative process of human interaction, creating an atmosphere of acceptance and joy.

    \nthe touch of the parent

    As you speak with another player, reach out your hand and give their cheek a light rub, as if removing a bit of dirt, or run it over thei scalp as if to smooth out a muss in their hair. Do so affably and naturally, preferably at a point where they are speaking within the conversation.

    This technique is useful when portraying a figure who considers themself to know the PC well, and know what is good for them. This may be a true parent, aunt, family retainer or village elder... Or someone with pretensions to such a role.

    The effect this may have is twofold: to disconcert the player and set up a status dynamic between the two characters on the one hand, and to indicate an attitude that is familiar and fundamentally affectionate on the other. The intent is to support character relationships that are complex in a natural manner, containing irritation and attachment alike.

    \n We often more or less subconsciously touch those we feel close to or drawn to, so when playing a potential love intrest why not try:

    The forearm stroke

    To send a signal of affection, place your hand near the players elbow and let your hand follow the forearm down to the wrist. This is not supposed to be a caress so don't emphasize it, just casually let it fall and talk about whatever at the same time. Smile and be positive and the player will definitely start thinking about what that touch meant.

    (A girl did this to me two moths ago while saying "So, I hope I see you later?". She doesn't remember doing it but it stuck in my mind. We started dating a week later and now I'm happy to call her my girlfriend so... there you go. ^^)

    \n Oh, sweet sex, it is hard to simulate in gameplay, and a game on the life of an adult would be quite bleak without it. We have to face it: most PCs are heroes, people who have an almost magical drive to be laid with whatever is out there. This is a simple tool, not to simulate sex, but to symbolize that the PC have had it.

    The sex-handhold

    A player may decide that his character have had sex. To symbolize this he may take the hand of either one of his co-players, or his GM. Holding hands with a co-player means that your characters have had good sex (so good it will show on the characters, maybe even as a small mechanical bonus: "In the flow +1"). Holding hands with the GM means that your character have had good sex with an NPC.

    It's not necessary to do this every time you've had sex. Use it when appropriate, when it will help give the game some detail and depth. Do it in a silent way, or in a jubilant way, according to the personality of your character and his/her partner in love. Silent: take the hand and make nothing more out of it. Jubilant: take the hand and lift it as a winner of a boxing match, or smile broadly, or make jests about last night.

    Either way: make it a small nugget in gameplay, to deepen your immersion in the character and/or to strengthen interaction within the group.

    \nCircle of trust

    A simple way of opening the game-session. When all is ready you, the games master, ask all to take hands around the table. Then you start the game by saying some words on trust. You may say it your self, in a calm voice, and you may (if you wish) have the players repeat your words.
    Say something like this:
    I will give myself to the game.
    I trust you to take my gifts with grace.
    Lets play this game with open minds.

    \nConquering the elbow

    A tool for the GM this, and to be used with a very calm aura. If you need an NPC to suppress or lead a PC for some reason, you may take the elbow of the player while stating the actions/speech of the NPC. A firm grip will underline the actions/words of the NPC, and will produce a reaction from the player that will surely support his immersion in the situation.

    \nThe grip of dominance

    By standing up and placing yourself behind a player, taking a light grip on his neck, you may dominate his character totally. Do this very calmly, as it is a very strong symbol of dominance. If the player wrenches free, let him go. Do not insist on the grip. You may state that the NPC keeps his grip on the character though, if that is in tune with the scene.

    Such a grip is best followed up with some evocative description of how a NPC dominate the character physically or socially (it may be used to symbolize a social dominance, as in lord and servant). If you keep the grip throughout the scene, it will give the player a very special moment for sure.

    \nThe possession

    By standing behind a player, and holding his head in your hands (one hand on each ear, steering the direction of his head), you may symbolize that his character is under possession. You may do this physically with no verbal explanation, to save the explanation for the moment the other characters start to wonder how it is his actions gets overruled by you, or they start to investigate if there is something wrong with his character.

    The player in hand (!) will have the benefit of hearing badly, thus being left a bit "outside" the game for he duration of your hold. And he will have the benefit of reacting to your gentle steering of his head, feeling a bit overruled both physically and mentally by this grip. Let go when the possession is discovered, or whenever it is appropriate in the scene, but make sure to keep the grip long enough to make the player a "puppet" in your hand (wait for him to relax, and if he don't: tell him he must relax to make you let go).

    \n These last three tools are made for the GM to intimidate and dominate players, as an effect and/or support of what is going on in the fiction, to help them understand how the character may struggle to break free from domination, suppression or possession.

    To dominate characters is very hard in classical RPGs, at least in a way that makes the players really identify with it. The player tend to defend himself, and his character, by using his own freedom of action. So he will shake of dominance as if it's a fly on his shoulder. As you grip the player in a dominating way, his freedom is restricted, and an easy break of dominance becomes less of an option for him. By using tools like this you give the player a taste of the quite interesting frame of mind usually existing in a servant or slave.

    These tools may lift a scene to new heights, especially if the player connects to the feeling created by them, and play on it.
