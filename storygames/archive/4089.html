\n I mentioned this as a joke, last night, then couldn't stop thinking about it. I nearly stayed up until the small hours writing it. But I'm glad I didn't.

It'd go something like this, I think. (I'm using italics to mark the differences from the original text. Also, I'm addicted to italics.)

It's set in late summer in some woods you know well, which are host to The Teddy Bears' Picnic*.

You play a small lost animal. Decide who has accused you of doing something naughty and what you did. Also decide a Companion.

Then...

Satyrs become Teddies. (The Teddies are naughty).
Bacchus becomes The Teddy King.
Pluto becomes Mr Farmer.
Soldiers become Big Stupid Dogs.
Minerva becomes Big Sister.
Wine becomes Ginger Beer*.
Venus becomes The White Rabbit (from Alice In Wonderland).
The Accuser and Companion stay the same.

Then...bearing in mind all the stuff with dice is the same...

On two tied Ginger Beer dice, you narrate a surge in thrills and excitement as before.
On another flavour of tie, you narrate how a lack of fun and thrills precipitates a departure, as before.
If Pluto is the high die, you describe how the scene resolves itself with someone doing something naughty.
If it's Venus, you're led to your Companion, as before.
If Ginger Beer is the high die, describe an escalation of pranks and laughter.
If a Teddy is your high die, describe a change of scene, and naughtiness at the new location.
If The Teddy King is the high die, describe an escalation of naughtiness.
If it's Accuser, you're confronted, as before.
If it's a Big Stupid Dog, describe being caught, as before.
If it's Companion, describe finding your way out of the woods.
If it's Big Sister, describe your companion, as before: you've had a dream.
And you can gift Big Sister for excessive naughtiness.

Something like that? I haven't quite separated naughtiness and pranks and laughter to my satisfaction.

Graham

* These are very English, so I'd welcome suggestions from Americans about how to make them more American. And a more American title, too.

    \n Interesting. OK.

    See, in the original Bacchanal, there are three things that get narrated (usually "increases of"):

    1. Thrills and excitement
    2. Debauchery
    3. Crimes

    So the first one sounds fun, the second sounds fun but dangerous, and the third sounds bad. And, in my head, there's a sort of escalation, or a sense that they're all tied together.

    What I'm trying to do is a similar escalation:

    1. Thrills and excitement
    2. Pranks and laughter
    3. Naughtiness

    Where the first sounds fun, again; the second is fun but dangerous; and the third is bad.

    So, although I like the childhood/adult split too, I'm not quite aiming for the opposition between the two. Instead, I'd like an idea that they all tie together.

    Does that make sense?

    Oh, and I'd still like some ideas about American equivalents for "Ginger beer" and "Back in time for tea".

    Graham
