So, since the working title for the game we're working on is, ahem, taken*, and we're searching for a new title, it made me wonder: what goes into the title-choosing process for you? I feel like there are two general approaches: cool moderately-short phrase, or punchy one-word title.

* I am a total free culture wonk at heart, and the idea that a one-syllable anglo-saxon word can be copyrighted bothers me.

    \n I'm not a lawyer, but my understanding of copyright law is that titles and short phrases can't be copyrighted. They can be trademarked, but that is an entirely different matter.


    More importantly: titles are hard. You need to encompass the whole idea of a game in the minimum number of words. Single word titles are pretty unlikely to get the job done. They convey less info/tone and are harder to use for search engine optimization efforts. (Fiasco and blowback are exceptions here because the single word magnificently captures the proper tone of each game.) You're likely served better by a more descriptive title anyway.

    \n For me it's a war between "what's catchy" and "what's unique, so I can do a google search and hit my thing first." (What Rich said.) All the catchy names are taken.

    My process is brainstorming and google searches on the results until I find one that kinda satisfies both criteria.

    And working titles tend to stick. We kept thinking about another title for Schizoid because schizoid personality disorder isn't quite the right mental disorder for the game and because a search for Schizoid would always return the personality disorder first...but never came up with one we liked better.

    \n I've had to battle this a few times, even though all I really do are hacks and house-rules. I still like to give some sort of moniker to the works I produce.
    Most of the time, when thinking of a name, I try to do something that evokes the feel of the game, without stating outright what it is. For example, my stormtrooper game is called "Screw You Rebel Scum!," because it evokes what the characters generally think, without being a title like "Stormtroopers Killing Rebels."

    More difficult is with the hack of Dungeonslayers that I've been working on for many, many months. Originally I was calling it "DungeonCrashers," which was originally going to be a comic I was sketching out when living in Japan, and it just kinda fit for the game. But someone mentioned somewhere (I think it was on the Praxis page) that they were going to be naming their game DungeonCrashers...so I decided to drop my hold on the name (just cuz).
    Currently thinking of "Adventurers & Artifacts," though the "name + name" thing has been done to death.

    But anyway, it helps to get out a huge-ass Thesaurus (avoid online ones, since I find them to be lacking in depth), and look up some words that you think best describe your game. Write down some interesting or surprising results from your search. And look up even more words. Use these as inspiration.

    Posted By: RichDHow likely your game will be found on Google is also a concern when naming. It is one of the reasons why Daniel Solis gave Do a subtitle.
    \nI think that this is less of an issue that people think. Most of the time adding one extra search criteria in Google will fix that, in this case "RPG" or "Game." This works on single word games like Fiasco (getting the right result first or second in the Google search).

    There are certain words / titles that this wouldn't work for, Do being one of them. You would have to own the top result for the word "game" or "RPG" with the word "do" on it, for it to show up as a top result. That isn't going to happen. I've been considering the name C0DE for a game I'm working on and running into the same problem. People who search "code + rpg" or "code + game" will never get my game as a top result.

    However, Loom shouldn't have that problem. It should be as easy to find as Fiasco (after a reasonable amount of time, and a little online promotion).

    \n I love coming up with titles, and I think I'm pretty good at it. Are you looking for help with what to use as a title, or are you just hoping for a discussion about how people choose titles?

    If the latter, I'm not sure what I can add. I feel like coming up with titles for things is highly situational. I know that, generally, I look for things that are euphonic (that is, sounds good), that have an enigmatic element while still being very clear, as well as something that googles well. Ideally, also, the title will work simultaneously on several levels.

    You asked about long vs. short, but I don't think that's a very useful breakdown. Among the things to consider, I'd think this is among the last things to consider.
