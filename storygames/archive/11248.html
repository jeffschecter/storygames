    \n I'm pretty sure this is in the book somewhere, but I've heard other MG players fumble this, so:

    Say what's in front of them, then ask them what they do, then say what the test is.

    For some reason, some folks got it into their heads that the GM was supposed to skip the 'ask what they do' part: "You're following the trail to Sprucetuck and you come across a giant fallen log that completely blocks the path. Pathfinder check!"

    Don't do that. The obstacle is based on how the guard deals with the problem, not decided by the GM in advance. I know that's dirt-simple advice, but I figured I'd say it just in case.

    \n One of the things I really dig about Mouse Guard is that it's really robust and flexible, which makes it harder to play "wrong" than many other games, especially since it firmly in the "progressive-and-yet-solidly-traditional" school of design, that takes the best stuff that indie games have developed and grafts it onto a familiar chassis.

    Generally, most of the old GM tricks you know from a lifetime of roleplaying are still applicable in Mouse Guard, as are most of the newer indie tricks, so you don't have to forget all of your old or new skills. And that rocks.

    As John kinda gets at, trust your instincts and Mouse Guard will take you there. Don't think the game is trying to make you do stuff that is stupid or not right for your group. The game is smarter than that. Trust yourselves first and then trust that the game will support you in the game experience you're trying to create. I mean, you know IN YOUR HEAD that just telling the players to roll Pathfinder is lame, right?

    Assuming you already know how to have fun playing with a group of folks, Mouse Guard will support you in doing that, and in ways that you'll like.

    The only problems I've seen is when people try to follow the mechanics and structural guidelines really mechanically, without letting things flow together. Like "we just finished the Weather Obstacle 3, so now better roll out my Mouse Obstacle 4." I think that comes from discomfort and trying to figure out what Secret Master Procedures the text is telling you to do, as if it was something alien and unusual. Do all the planning that the game recommends and definitely use the procedures it describes, but, once play begins, listen to your players, pay attention to what's happening at the table, not what's written in the book, and you'll be gold.

    \n Pay attention to the check economy. If the players are new to the game, point out opportunities to earn checks by nerfing themselves. That's when things get really interesting.

    Be an asshole about player checks. Obey the economy strictly. If players want to earn the right to direct the narrative, they have to earn checks. There's this wonderful MG dynamic where all the players are trying to nerf themselves during the GM turn, which makes the group dysfunctional as all hell. Our GM WIlhelm was very eager to inflict 'angry' conditions on our mice when we nerfed ourselves into clear fail territory.

    Then, during the player turn everyone gets to go do the stuff they really want to do ... write beautiful poetry, woo sexxxy civilian-mice, intrigue the patrol management, whatever. So we had this beautiful cycle of mission play and subplot with lots of intraparty grouchiness to flavor it. That is the essence of Mouse Guard to me.

    Challenge the hell out of helper dice; don't allow lame ones. Wil is pretty strict about these and it really makes the game much more flavorful.

    Get a really good grip on the extended challenge system. It's not very simple and will take some practice for the players to come up to speed on it. Maybe facilitate the learning curve by frontloading some low-ob high-disposition challenges.

    \nI love 'Carebear optimization' as a term of art. Does that come from somewhere?

    Kevin, one of the things you can do to mitigate the carebear optimization impulse is to ensure that the game proceeds along fun lines regardless of the outcomes of the rolls. I think this is in the RAW, but make sure you don't call for any kind of random engagement if you don't have fun in mind for all possible outcomes. This might be common wisdom now but I wanted to mention it.

    The receipt of an advancement fail-dot for a bad skill roll is another thing that deadens the Opitimize-A-Lot bear impulse.

    Also, if the patrol is unable to rally in a single direction, look to the leader to put some direction to it. Our table bought into the leader idea, so when our leader told us to do something, we snapped to it. And our leader did a superb job, only really pushing us when we needed that push to keep the game from stalling.

    \n Here's one of the things I try to keep in mind about Mouse Guard: The Environment is a character and he's the worst, cruelest more horrible villain you can imagine.

    Practically don't play Wilderness/Weather Obstacles/Twists "realistically" play them with cunning intelligence as if they had it out for the Mouse Guard personally. When the guard mouse fails to climb that vine he hasn't just fallen off a "rope", he's just stepped into the world's cleverly and carefully laid trap and now that trap is sprung!

    Hi Aaron. Basically, as you note, what the text says is that the GM must provide an Obstacle and the means of tackling that obstacle; the GM may also provide an alternative and the players may also make suggestions.

    Imagine I'm the GM and your patrol has to retrieve the prisoners from a weasel slave raid. I describe your patrol's approach to the cave where the prisoners are being held and the bored weasel guards keeping an eye on it. I tell you that it's going to be a versus Scout test to sneak into the cave without alerting the weasels.

    At that point, you can say, "Screw sneaking. I ready my halberd and walk right up to them and tell them in a low, gravelly voice that they better get out of here now, or I'm going drag their skulls back to Lockhaven to use as planters. Can I use Deceiver to intimidate them into running away?"

    As the GM, I can then agree to your suggestion and let you proceed with the Deceiver test. Or I can decide the test is inappropriate; there's no way these picked guards are going to run from you. It's going to be a Fight Conflict instead.

    All of that is perfectly kosher with the rules as written.

    \n Single most important piece of information for new MG GMs: Use caution when assigning Obstacles to rolls. The table in the book that says what obstacles are challenging and which are easy...is skewed to the "make it hard" side. The obstacle setting advice in the skill list (counting up the factors that apply) is even harder...you'll wind up with REALLY hard obs...like even harder than than table suggests they should be.
    \n
    \nThe dice system is pretty easy to guestimate odds...with average luck you should be able to score successes equal to 1/2 the dice you're rolling. With a small number of dice going 2-3 Ob above this rapidly gets in the "most likely to fail" category. This then can lead into players frantically trying to finangle every single extra die they can...such as everyone giving aid dice for every single roll based on increasingly preposterous ideas..."I help fight the weasel with my Geology-wise by throwing a rock at it" This also means that players can be extremely reluctant to hose themselves for Checks because losing a die or two can turn a roll from "not likely" to "forget about it".
    \n
    \nMy advice:
    \n
    \n1) Really take advantage of the "Win anyway but suffer this Condition" effect.
    \n2) Really take advantage of the "Fail but here's a Twist that even's more fun than winning would have been" effect.
    \n-- These two are the key drivers of the resolution system. Without it...whiff-tastic.
    \n3) Make sure you demonstrate the above to your players so that they know they don't need to play "weasel out every die I can get" games constantly...which can rapidly become unfun for everyone.
    \n4) This may be heretical, but you won't go too wrong in your first session shaving an Ob point off of most tests.
    \n-- IMO in the quest to ensure there's enough failure to drive the Conditions and Twists system, and enough failure to make players feel that they are mice...weak and small even if they are heroic...the rules err on the side of "slightly too hard" mathematically. I would rather reduce all the Obs by 1 across the board permanently and then ruthlessly weed out all but the most meaningful and justifiable helping dice. As it is it can start to feel rather gloomy when your mouse is only good at one thing, and even at that one thing he fails far more often than he succeeds.
    \n5) Related to all this, make sure that the things you're asking for rolls for...are suitably meaningful. MG is not a game where you should ever ask for casual tests.

    Huh. I found the ridiculously high Ob values actually make earning Checks easier. The player would look at the odds and say, "Hmm. I"m not going to win this anyway, might as well get something out of it." and take the Checks in exchange for failing by a large margin shot.

    Also I've also observed a kind of "Failing into Conflict" effect. The numbers are much more kind inside of a Conflict than in simple tests. So, that's where the real work gets done. Until then it's Fail-Twist-Fail-Twist-Fail-Twist and each twist basically keeps raising the stakes until you end up with something worth having a Conflict over.

    And yes, Conditions are your friend.

    That said, yes, be prepared for the incredibly high rate of failure. With Twists and Conditions it's not exactly failure in terms of the developing fiction but the real-world effect of having 4 successes come up when you needed 6 can still wear on you if you're not prepared for it.
