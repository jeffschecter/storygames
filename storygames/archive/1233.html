Possible Futures: What Indie Roleplaying is Becoming

J_Walton
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
So, instead of necessarily offering predictions and arguing about the multiple directions in which indie roleplaying is heading (and I'm speaking mostly about the social community that surrounds it less than the actual games themselves), I thought it might be beneficial to start by offering brief visions of possible futures. These can be apocalyptic or utopian or any mixture in between. They could be warnings of possible dangers or signposts pointed in interesting directions. And you don't necessarily have to say which yours is, because one person's apocalypse might be another's utopia. I'll start:

INDIE ROLEPLAYING MIGHT BECOME...

...something you do mostly with other indie game designers and enthusiasts. Large urban communities and some rural enclaves (like the folks out in Western Mass) have groups of indie game designers, other indie folks, and their friends, spouses, children, bf/gf, etc. At least a couple people in the group try to keep up with what's being published and talked about and the group tries to play a diversity of neat indie games, partially because they like playing them, partially because they inspire their own designs, and partially because they are friends (online, in person, etc.) with the designers who created them. Sometimes game designers or indie games fans from other communities will pass through or move into town, so there is some intermixing (and there's always big hoedowns at regional or national conventions), but mostly you just play with other local indie games people. Indie roleplaying remains a small and somewhat independent subcommunity of roleplaying in general.

  • Michael Curry
    September 2006
    ...a wasteland of bickering factions.

    During the early days of the revolution, it was easy to maintain a cohesive community of outsiders, fighting together to advance RPGs past the status quo that was maintained by the major publishers.

    Later though, when the indie games community began to gain in terms of both size and success, the schisms began. Things were said in haste or anger, feelings were hurt, friendships were damaged, and many of the various circles created by the diaspora gave up on the idea of one shared community of designers, instead choosing to deal only with a small group of like-minded friends. A minority of these decided that it was important not only to advance their own theories, but to attack those who didn't support their idea of the One True Way. More and more online venues came to be dominated by venomous words rather than thoughtful discussions, and what remained of a thriving indie RPG community became increasingly factionalized and insular.

I would say don't be those guys, don't evangelise, people can smell it and generally don't like it. People can also smell honest fun, and they do like that.

    A while back someone started a thread on rpg.net which was designed to lead eventually to some particular indie nostrum, I forget which one. But, stupidly, he dressed it up originally as a disinterested query and then tried to guide the discussion to the outcome. When it came out he had an agenda in mind from the outset the thread died, not nastily, there weren't flames, but people felt used and stopped bothering to respond. Had he set out saying "hey, I do this indie thing and it's working really well for me" I think he'd have fared a hell of a lot better.

    I think a sizeable chunk of the hostility the Forge sometimes gets is due to misguided attempts to evangelise, which comes across as patronising, instead of simply and honestly describing why stuff is fun. This game is better than what you're used to is kind of insulting, this game rocks is not. The latter is better.
