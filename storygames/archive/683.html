    Jason Morningstar wrote:
    I don't understand the one game rule but I'm sure there is a good reason for it. Somebody fill me in.

    Okay, I'll try.

    So you're thinking about GenCon, and all the benefits it can bring you. The benefit of being in the booth, having shelf-space, having your poster up on the walls, all that jazz.

    That's dreck. Yeah, it's good, but it's not the main thing. It is utterly trivial compared to the main thing.

    Here's the main thing: The other people at the booth. They're the resource you have at your disposal, more than any other factor. They are ingenious, moving, talking, engaging advertisements. They go out and give passers-by an interactive presentation on your product, complete with customized information and presentation.

    And you're worried about shelf-space? Feh.

    Your absolute first, second, third and last priority should be making the best possible use of the people at the booth. You want to give them every reason and opportunity to learn to pitch and demo your product. Here are the things you can do:

    • Have one product ... if I ask you "Okay, how do I sell your game?" and you tell me "Okay, let's start with Roach, then we can talk about how to pitch Drowning and Falling" I am already looking for somebody else to help instead. Because there are folks out there who I can help more efficiently by learning to pitch their one game.
    • Show up on wednesday. I know from experience that if you show up on thursday, people have already gelled around what they're going to do for the rest of the weekend. That was a crucial, stupid mistake that I made last year. I'm still kicking myself.
    • Pitch other people's games more than your own. This isn't about being selfless, or persuading people that you're virtuous: It is a simple matter of leaving people the room to pitch your game for you, and thereby learn to do it. Again, learn from my mistakes of last year: I was constantly at the booth, always ready to pitch my own game. When people wanted to have Capes pitched they would bring customers to me. The end result? Very few of the people who attended GenCon learned to pitch or demo Capes. Gah. I'm an idjit.

    So, honest to God, your optimal selfish strategy is to bring one game, and not obsess over it. Wierd, huh?

    Well, from being an observant participant for two years at the Forge Booth, here's what I see:

    1) Games that came out LAST YEAR at GenCon: Everyone at the Forge Booth pimps them, because they know and have played them. PTA sold "ok" the first year, but the second year they were flying off the shelves. Dogs in the Vineyard sold out at GenCon of a limited print run (was it 50, Vincent? 100? I forget), but no one knew shit about the game. All we knew was it was some kind of "Mormon Gunslinger Game", although people that later got a chance to play it with Vincent were able to sell it better... but again, it wasn't until after GenCon, and the NEXT GenCon, that it was out on everyone else's radar, and sold well.

    2) Games that came out THAT YEAR at GenCon, usually published weeks to days before the event: Don't get sold all that well. No one is familiar with them. No one has played them. No one knows much about them other than the tagline and the author's enthusiasm. Some, the "funky" ones (Polaris) rise to the top. Most get kicked into the back seat or ignored altogether. James' Death's Door, Keith's Conspiracy of Shadows (the first year it came out), Fastlane, and a bunch of others were in this camp.

    Guesses: I think that Stranger Things may sell well this year, beucase it's been on people's radar for a while. It is pretty. It has cool, enigmatic characters. It's a blend of cool stuff. And again, people have been aware of it for a while, so even if they haven't played it they know at least a few things about it.

    I think The Roach may sell well this year, because it was published in February and NOT Two Weeks Before GenCon.

    I think there's a lot to be said about getting your game out when it's time to be released, and not giving a fuck about when GenCon is. By the time the next Con rolls around, your game will have enough press, following, or interest that it will help sell it. If you aim to publish For GenCon (again, usually a few days or weeks before), it's a big fucking gamble, and unless your game is:

    * Very Pretty (TMW)

    * Off the Wall (Polaris, F*CK THAT!, etc)

    It's not goign to sell well.

    Again, this is from someone who didn't release his own game, but tried to plug others' games.

    -Andy

    Here's where I stand at this point:

    So Ron & Co. created the Forge Booth several years back, making a great space for indie games at GenCon that didn't exist before, at least not in a significant way or in a way where the economics really made sense. Before then, I imagine most indie games companies had experiences much like what Tony describes, and this includes the guys selling the pornographic baseball cards.

    However, because the Forge Booth worked so well and because Ron & Co. opened the booth up to any indie creators who wanted to participate, it's gotten bigger every year (and will continue to get bigger) and managing a bigger booth means more work and better coordination. And this means clear guidelines for people who want to participate in the booth and getting someone with an intense passion to make this work (and Luke is definitely the right guy for the job) working to herd everyone in the right direction.

    However, you have a bunch of things clashing here:

    - what's best for the booth, to make it function better and be profitable
    - what's best for indie roleplaying as a community
    - what's best for individual creators, allowing them the freedom to do what they think's best for their games

    These, of course, are closely interconnected. If individual creators are successful, that's good for the community. If the Forge Booth is successful, that's good for the community. And the Forge Booth has been a great resource and gathering point for individual creators. Still, these are not really the same thing.

    Luke, I don't think you should be surprised that the independent spirit that leads people to publish their own games makes some people bristle at the idea of giving up some of their freedom to work the Forge Booth. You're a "middle finger to the Man" guy. The Forge Booth is more grassroots organizing than the Man, of course. But as any small organization gets larger, there are growing pains, and people have to decide if they're not better off striking out on their own, especially if they don't feel like the larger organization shares the concerns that are most important to them. There are two ways to make your concerns central: join the Forge Booth and push for them, or start a seperate group with those concerns taken into consideration from the beginning.

    Personally, while I'm totally supportive of the Forge Booth and whatever policies you have to implement there to get it to work, I tend to think the only long-term solution to the problem of growth (which is ultimately about having too many games at the booth , such that there's no display space or people working there are only familiar with half the games) is to set up a bunch of seperate booths with a handful of creators at each one that work hard to really understand, pitch, and demo each other's games. I think Luke and Ben's concerns that they'll spread us all over GenCon, hidden among crappy booth selling crappy games, is one that we can definitely overcome. That's not good for GenCon or us. Most significant comic conventions now have seperate sections for indie creators, and I think that's something we could definitely organize and push for at GenCon.

    Here's a quote from HeroesCon in Charlotte:

      In an effort to promote the best guest list possible, we've created Indie Island, a well over 5000 square foot section at the very center of our Artists Alley, plus tons of booths and tables spreading out to both walls of the convention hall--filled with the leading lights of the comics industry's indie & small press, both creators and publishers! Our first ever Indie Island, in 2005, was a great big fat hit, so we're pulling out all the stops in 2006--not only are we looking to double the guest list, but we're improving the layout and expanding our Indie Island-themed panels and special events.
    So I guess I'm trying to consider the larger picture, which includes not just the rapid expansion of the Forge Booth, but other possible alternatives too. But yeah, all that's on the burner for next year, though.

    And Jess, I'm sorry if you don't feel like you can join the Forge Booth. It would be badass to have you there. Despite Luke's aggressive tone, he really wants you there too. It may not be an ideal community (as you can see from Jared's comments, which have some truth in them, while obviously being biased), but it does a great job of helping people promote and sell their games, as well as inroducing you to a lot of smart people who have a lot of experience doing this type of thing.

    Yes, the idealist in me is interested in more than that. I want to change the fucking world. And I don't especially care if I have to spend a nice chunk of my own money to do it. But it would suck to spend a nice chunk of cash and have no one care or notice. That would be soul killing. So yeah, it's gonna take at least another year to do this right. And, for me, that means learning as much as I can from the Forge Booth this year.
