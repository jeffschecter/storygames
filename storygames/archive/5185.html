\n Okay, I got a note the other day from one of my coworkers, asking me if I could run a game of Vampire: the Masquerade.
\n
\nI hesitated. I like her, and her boyfriend. I'm not sure about the other people who would be playing, but I assume they're good people. But.. Vampire: the Masquerade? Not exactly my cup of tea, system-wise.
\n
\nI'm down with clan politics, internal struggle, shady denizens of the night and paranormal intrigue. It's just the Attributes/Skills and 1,000+ splatbooks that have me leering.
\n
\nSo I proposed running something V:tM-esque, but in a system that would give me traction and allow for more indie goodness. I'm here now to ask for your help in determining that system. I talked to one of the possible players, who is the central figure in arranging the game, and he said that the following is what he likes in Vampire:
\n
\n*Character Motivations. He mentioned this one might be a leap for some of the D&D-honed players, but what is really awesome about V:tM for him is the focus on personal drives.
\n
\n*Clan Histories. He used the phrase "rich cultural tapestry" at one point. I inquired more, and found out that the idea of a rich history was more important than a well-documented one. (ie, it's fine if we re-write the cultural tapestry around the table.)
\n
\n*Disciplines. He was big on this one. Now, I've never played V:tM, so correct me if I'm wrong... but Disciplines are powers that are derived from your lineage/bloodline. They're pre-dispositions towards certain types of badass mojo.
\n
\n*Clan Politics and Social Intrigue. Any game will work with this to a degree, because I like using R-maps... but the game should foster or at least not block big social interplay.
\n
\n
\nThat all said, here's what matters to me in a game: big player input potential, not clunky or unecessarily crunchy, story-focused, low prep, ability to create and introduce new setting stuff.
\n
\nSo, here are my current thoughts on systems to run a V:tM story with:
\n
\n1.) The Shadow of Yesterday. Secrets could parallel Disciplines maybe. It's still got ranked skills, so it's a good mid-point between indie funk and trad tangibility. Keys help make that personal motivation stuff really concrete and immediate for players more used to "get the shiny" gameplay. It's simple, and I've never had a bad session of it.
\n
\n2.) Covenant. Drift it a little bit, by changing "cell" to "clan", and having every clan create a different clan sheet (different purpose, different conventions, different motifs). Maybe have all Vampires share the first Convention/Motif, and then the last two be Clan-specific.
\n
\n3.) Some hybrid of tSoY and Covenant, which tacks Conventions/Motifs (clan-specific) onto the shadow of yesterday's system.
\n
\n4.) Conspiracy of Shadows? I've never played it, nor do I own it. Can someone sell/unsell me on this one?
\n
\n5.) Something else? Pitch me something!

    \n I'm thinking TSOY has the following strengths:
    \n
    \n-Pools/Skills is quasi-familiar to the non-indie-gamers who I'm going to be pitching to, but Stakes and Keys make it about the shit I care about, as well as tying into the things AJ wanted from the game (AJ being the guy who is trying to pull this group together).
    \n
    \n-Secrets are a simple way of including Kewl Powers and they are typically grouped under different cultural backgrounds already.
    \n
    \n-The fact that Bringing Down the Pain exists is really useful in making TSOY a good bridge game for traditional gamers. It says "Just like other games, this one has simple one-roll conflicts and large, extended conflicts. The difference is that instead of making X the extended conflict, TSOY makes what you care about the extended conflict." It takes something familiar and redefines it in a really interesting and empowering way. Rock.
    \n
    \n-Secrets/Keys are easily customized/adapted/created. Keys are formulaic, and many Secrets are too. This means that it's possible to customize all these cogs to match a Vampire: the Masquerade setting and tone easily and have it all flow smoothly. There's no "hacking" needed, just "customizing". That's enticing.
    \n
    \n-Advancement + a focus on relationships means that the game can smoothly and easily flow for multiple sessions.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \nI'm thinking that Covenant has the following strengths:
    \n
    \n*That relationship map that's built right into the middle of the character sheet. 'Nuff said. Like Polaris', it makes understanding relationships and NPCs simple and engaging and somewhat tactile.
    \n
    \n*Conventions/Motifs allows for a simple way of describing Clans and possibly Disciplines.
    \n
    \n*Freeform Edges means that I don't have to gut and replace lengthy skill lists, etc, etc.
    \n
    \n*Covenant is already based in modern secret societies, so it's just an easier mental jump in ways.
    \n
    \n*Order/Faction/Personal is an easy divide which makes for immediate and emotional conflicts, which map out across a bigger political picture. Important for Vampire. Also, having Conventions/Motifs for each clan would mean that there was potential for lots of clan focus.
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \nThings I'd do if I used TSOY: Replace many Secrets/Keys. Possibly remove Gift Dice, or modify them to be like Clan Dice or something. Figure out a way to inject more clan politics into the game (Is Keys enough?)
    \n
    \nThings I'd do if I used Covenant: Remove Truisms, possibly replacing them with some form of advancement thing (Goals?).

    \n Keep gift dice. Gift dice aren't pulpy, random or superheroic! Gift dice are how the people round the table encourage each other. They can be used as highly personalised motifs (like Covenant), for attention to genre or theme, for reincorporation of setting, or for humor.     \n Hey Joe

    Its not that hard to get rid of the "bad stuff" from Vampire, actually. A few of the tweaks they made for Vampire: The Requiem and new WoD help in this regard.

    You don't need fiddly combat, for one thing. It's just contested dice pools, pick a stat, pick a skill, roll versus. Same as any other conflict - don't use the Dificulty Rating so much, actually roll dice for whatever opposition there is.

    *Disciplines: give em more than the 3 dots to start. Starting Vampire characters are really fucking boring to play unless you've never played before. Disciplines are fuckng cool! Give them lots!

    *Clans and vampire histories: Don't use the metaplot - it marginalizes the PCs and kills fun. Use only the clans the players find interesting. If somebody wants to make one up, use that. Any bits of history floating around in the books you like, take it and use it in your game, with whatever clan or NPC vampire you want. Focus on the "rich tapestry of history" and not on WW canon.

    *Social stuff: Remove things that make it easier to kill vampires, like aggravated damage. Make it much easier to kill humans - like give them half as many health boxes. So if the players want to blow shit up and go crazy, they can easily kill a bunch of mooks, but if they can get at least part of what they want by talking to vampires instead of killing (although a little punching/wrestling match can work good, too), they will be more inclined towards social maneuvering. Also if NPCs are interesting (thats universal advice tho).

    *Humanity - This is the big problem with Vampire, I have found. Humanity and the Paths are a punishment system. You do something "bad" and the GM punishes you. They don't actually encourage a player to DO anything - can't do this, can't do that, can't do the other thing. If you could instead get the players to define their character's motivation behind being a vampire, or their justification or purpose or what they do, then you could instead punish them for straying from a player-defined path, instead of a list you find in a book.


    Man, I played the shit out of Vampire back in the day. I can give you better advice in person.


    [Matt's suggestion for Covenant is pretty cool, though. Disciplines and powers might not be quite as cool, though - you'd have to watch out for that.]

    \n My first thought is that if the table majority wants V:tM then by God, you should run V:tM instead of something only you are interested in.

    My second thought is that, since you have many systems to choose from, you should be able to introduce elements from them organically. That means feedback after each session, discussions on where to take it and appropriate modifiers for the next time.

    The good thing about this approach is that it's not only responsive to the players' needs, but ends up with a finely honed result -- a game that is the best at providing exactly what the group wants, instead of a game you must talk the group into.

    It's dumb to increase the Discipline spread simply by virtue of teh Aw3s0me!!111!! for several reasons. First of all, fewer dots tends to supercharge creativity, because the tools are not perfectly suited for the job. Getting your money's worth out of Command is pretty damn nifty. A second reason is that clan ties are what get people into learning out of clan Disciplines, so if people have extra dots to pick those up it weakens motivations to get involved with the city network. A third reason is pacing, as disciplines allow many aspects if the plot to get resolved fairly rapidly by cutting through the need to deal with networks of people and vampires that are the default environment for play.

    If you want meaningful changes in power, it's smarter is to hand out Experience in big chunks. Advancement as a game of inches works well in some long-term games, but to get the feel of a character who's transformed by experiences, big leaps are better. I've used this method for 8+ years now in WoD games, where XP comes in big chunks occasionally (though in short games, make that every session) and it's excellent.

    Make use of Conscience, Self-Control and Courage for more than their typical functions. I find they work well as add ons to Attributes when no other skill applies. You might also use them to provide situational advantages.

    Posted By: Clinton R. NixonFor Pete's sake,pleasedo not use the Shadow of Yesterday. It is intentionally built to deal with humans, not monsters of the night. Seriously.
    \nBringing Down the Pain is a perfect alternative way to portray the Beast, right down to turning conflict from a general, chaotic, human affair to the kind of cold-blooded detail that only a psychopath could really walk through step by step, especially since BdtP is the only way to murder someone.

    In fact, I could see this working in the following way:

    * Use the V:tM engine but resolve conflicts in a general way with simple opposed rolls. You can't kill anyone at this stage.
    * Multiply Humanity by 10. Spend a point whenever you want to behave like a psychopath and Bring Down the Pain. The Beast gives you a predator's clarity about the situation so you can coherent plot doing very bad things to people, unalloyed by the vague generalizations you normally use to justify your actions post hoc.
    * Other Humanity losses cost you d10 points. You can regain them by being a nice bloodsucker from time to time.
    * You can engage in BdtP defensively -- you give up your right to seriously harm anyone -- by spending a Willpower point per scene. It's expensive in the short term and limited, but you'll stay human longer.

    \n Speaking of GM as stakes-setter, let's read the good stuff:\n
    Vincent Baker said:Practical Conflict Resolution Advice

    You won't have any trouble at all, and in fact your group will wonder how you got along before, if you find the magic words. I don't know what your group's magic words are but here are some I've used:
    "The danger is that..."
    "What's at stake is..."
    "What you're risking is..."
    "So what you hope to accomplish is..."

    Say the magic words every single time, when the dice are in their hands but before they roll 'em.

    At first, you'll need to finish the sentence every time yourself, with a period, like:
    "The danger is that you'll set off the trap instead of disarming it."
    "What's at stake is, do you make it to the ferry in time or do you have to go the long way around?"
    "What you're risking is being overheard by the goblins on the rooftop."
    "So what you hope to accomplish is to get through the doorway, whether this ogre lives or dies."

    But after you've said it three or four or ten times, you'll be able to trail off with a question mark when you want their input:
    "What you're risking is...?"

    And then, once the dice are on the table, always always always make it like this:
    - If they succeed, they win what's at stake. They accomplish their accomplishment or they avoid the danger.
    - If they fail, they lose what's at stake - and you IMMEDIATELY introduce something new at stake. It might be another chance, it might be a consequence, but what matters is that it's more serious that the former.

    "The danger is that you'll set off the trap ... and you do! A dart thocks into your shoulder. The danger now is that you'll succumb to its poison!"
    "You reach the dock as the ferry's pulling away. Do you want to jump for it?"
    "The goblins overhear you and start dropping in through the skylight. They scramble all over you, biting and screeching. The danger is that they'll get you off your feet!"
    "Not only does the ogre keep you away from the doorway, it's pushing you back toward the chasm..."

    In combat, you'll probably want to have an overall what's at stake for the fight, and little tactical what's at stakes for each exchange. When you describe the setup, mention two or three features of the environment, like hanging tapestries or a swaying bridge or broken cobblestones, plus an apparent weakness of the foe, like worn armor straps or a pus-filled left eye, and then when you say what's at stake for an exchange, incorporate one of those: "the danger is that he'll push you back onto the broken cobblestones" or "so what you're hoping to do is to further strain his armor straps." This is on top of hitting and damage and whatever, just add it straight in.

    It's especially effective if you always give a small bonus or penalty for the exchange before. What's it in D&D now, +2/-2? Give it every single exchange, linked to whether they won or lost the what's at stake of the previous exchange. "The broken cobblestones mess up your footing, so take a -2." "He has to shrug and shift to adjust his sagging armor, so take a +2."

    \n Joe, I don't know if this will help you or not, but here's how I "hippie-fied" Mage: The Ascension a little over 5 years ago (I might do it somewhat differently now, but this is how I did it then):

    * Kept the basic set-up of "people who will reality to do different things," but threw out all of the default setting. I made up a few factions, made up more on the fly, and if the players came up with anything, I incorporated that into the setting.

    * Kept the basic dice pools, but used them for basic conflict resolution, rather than task resolution (based on Christopher Kubasik's "Interactive Toolkit" essays, but not Vincent's hardcore stuff, since Vincent hadn't written it yet).

    * Had every player come up with a Sorcerer-esque Kicker for their PC. There was no mechanical benefit (like there is in Sorcerer), it was more of a "I'm not running a game with your character unless you provide a Kicker" thing. I explained that I was mostly doing this so that they had a real say in how the story started and what direction it took.

    * Had a bunch of passionate and driven NPCs with agendas and relationships, and then let the PCs bounce around in the sandbox, throwing Bangs at them when I felt they were needed. Based on the NPCs and their relationships to each other and the PCs and a list of cool stuff I wanted to throw in, I pretty much winged it, doing my best to only use stuff that engaged the players. It seemed to work, and everybody had a really good time. One PC started as something of a comic relief character (a sleazy, two-bit gambler and conman with a penchant for barely legal females) and ended up as one of the most dramatically-engaging and moral characters in the game.
