People will change the game!

\n
\n Oh, I know, this is nothing new, but it was spawned by a comment on the DMG thread and I wanted to talk a little about it.

People - mostly, game masters - will change the rules of the game without asking anyone's permission. They will drop rules they don't understand, change the scenario to make it more fun for their group, add monsters and tasks... you name it.

How much play experience do they need to do this? About three minutes. Let me give you an example.

A few weeks ago, I ran a game for eight teachers. After that, they ran the game for their students. I know for a fact that most of them had no previous experience. They were, in fact, pretty scared by the whole thing, and didn't know whether they could handle it.

While they were running the game, I went from classroom to classroom observing what happened. In one class the teacher decided she'd rather use her own notes than the book we'd given her; she explained the game in her own terms, adding and subtracting stuff on the fly. It almost turned into a LARP there - she was dressing up; at the end of the day, she gave her students an assignment: "Bring clothes that fit your character, and you'll score some points!" One of the girls came back the next day with a dress she'd made herself... wow.

Another teacher decided we hadn't made the monsters strong enough, so he doubled their dice to give his students more of a challenge.

A third one added a sub-plot about the queen's courtship with a foreign noble, and made the students help her write love letters in English.

Now, teachers are, of course, teachers. They'll take control and change things, because they're kings and queens of their classrooms, and that's how it should be. But still: These were people with no gaming experience, and they started rewriting the book from the beginning - scenario, game system, tasks, you name it. This leads me to think that many people don't need to be told they have the freedom to change the game - they take that freedom for granted. It's just us inhibited and pedantic rules lawyer types that sometimes have a hard time saying "yeah, whatever" and just doing what feels right.

    \n I think, people will change the game, if they know what they want from it or recognise where their enjoyment comes from.

    As long as I don't know, I'll follow rules, suggestions and conventions until I do find something I want/enjoy or give up and do something else. And the more I think about it, the less convinced I am that spelling out which parts of the game are fun helps people recognise what they want.

    Or in the words of Laurence Fishburne: "Unfortunately, no one can be told what the Matrix fun is. You have to see it for yourself. "

    \n I agree that everyone changes the games they play to suit them and their group (sometimes totally by mistake, often with discussion, sometimes just without mentioning it), but when you get a stranger that knows the system too (e.g. at a convention, when a new player arrives, when a new group starts) it's important to have those touchstone written rules to relate too, and those changes can sometimes become very awkward, communication barriers between what people have read about and what they actually do.
    \n
    \nI know that when I run a game with friends over a long term we change things we don't like, add rules, ignore stuff, and so forth, and that's great.
    \n
    \nBut I also know that when I join another person's game in a familiar system and find myself faced with a massive list of house rules, I find it confusing, offputting, and annoying. I wasn't part of the evolution of those rules changes, and they are a barrier to playing.

    \n Matthijs, remember that teachers are already used to adapting teaching materials for their own needs. Textbooks, outlines, techniques that other teachers use, and all sorts of resources are just tools to help them with their real goal - having their students learn. It sounds like these teachers of yours saw roleplaying as exactly that - another tool for the classroom. They already have their own techniques, and they know their students, so they naturally adapt rpg for their own uses.

    "Playing by the rules" is a maxim from the board game end of the gaming spectrum. When most people sit down to play Clue or Hungry Hippos, they agree to play by the rules as written. Players from the "play pretend," improv or storytelling end of the spectrum are more likely to fiddle with the rules at will.

    Matthijs, thanks for the report. I love stuff like this. I love seeing nongamers "get it" and take it and make it even better.

    I think there's a difference -- perhaps only a subtle one, I dunno -- between changes people make to make a game playable at all and changes people make to make a game even better. The first kind of change is like buying a car with a broken engine or no tires. You expect you'll have to put some time and sweat and grease into the thing to make it work at all. The second kind of change is like buying a car that works fine, but then boring out the engine, adding on a supercharger and a nitrous system, upgrading with ceramic breaks and a new suspension system, and customizing the body with fins and stripes. It's a much better car for you now, cuz you wanted a racer. Some other person might have bought the same car and had the interior ripped out and replaced with leather seats and a high end audio package.
