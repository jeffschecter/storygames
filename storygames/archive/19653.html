    \n It\u2019s not a blanket permission to fail forward. It\u2019s one specific ability that the sage has. The sage was in Basic, so this isn\u2019t new, either. I like the backgrounds and their abilities.

    To me, \u201cfailing forward\u201d ended up problematic in practice. I want success and failure to lead to drastically different places. The word choice \u201cfail forward\u201d implies to me that \u201cHmm, there\u2019s a little detour but you still get to see the big reveal I\u2019ve prepped\u201d.

    In fact, the reason I search out both story games and OSR games was that my impro-heavy gaming was always leading the narrative relentlessly forward. Even though it was improvised, it came across as a railroad.

    That said, with my perspective, adding failure \u2014 or even rolling \u2014 to knowledge skills is problematic. That\u2019s why I\u2019m happy that it the feature is phrased as \u201cif you don\u2019t know the information\u201d rather than \u201cif you fail a roll\u201d, because then it suits even those GMs who don\u2019t roll all that often for knowledge stuff.
    So it\u2019s not even failing forward. It\u2019s just \u201cYou know where to find out stuff that you don\u2019t know\u201d.

    \n Well, I'm also reading a bit into what @2097 is saying, but I *think* I get where she (?) is coming from with her comment.

    Imagine that the PCs are being railroaded towards some old castle. They fail an Orienteering test, and the GM decides that failing forward means they get to the castle anyway, but are, I don't know, spotted by the guards as they approach.

    In the hypothesized scenario of railroading, such an approach to a failed roll might only further the deprotagonization going on\u2014"We can't even fail to reach the fucking castle? This is bullshit!" So it's a valid critique of Fail Forward! However, in a proper (to my mind) adventure setup, the PCs have authored the goal of going to the castle in some way, or are otherwise invested in getting there, and so they won't object to the Fail Forward.

    It's also important that the Failure, however "Forward" it may be, also hurt. It absolutely should *not* feel like "handing the PCs what they wanted anyway." Getting the technique right takes some finesse and practice!

    \n To my mind, Failing Forward is a "No, but". Yes the Failure must hurt. The Forward should not entirely compensate, but it definitely needs to add a new narrative possibility (or "rail") out of the scene.

    Example: "You cannot tell which direction the castle is, as a matter of fact, you have no fucking idea where you are. After wandering for hours and running out of food, you suddenly realize that you're being watched by lots of eyes - they are the wood elves who live in this region."

    This is for the GM's sake, actually. Because Failure typically "collapses" an outbound rail removing it from consideration, and because there are only so many outbound rails to a scene, the "Forward" is there to give the GM a new option. A new outbound rail suddenly appears, replacing the collapsed one, without diminishing the number of potential ways forward.
