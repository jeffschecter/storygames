\n The game I'm working on doesn't have "NPCs" in the classic sense, but it has "supporting characters" which are, apart from who controls them, basically indistinguishable from NPCs to the naked eye. So:

I can think of a few different roles that NPCs fill - would welcome any others from the many experienced GMs here.

The Villain, obviously, and The Villain's Henchpersons.
The Obstacles - not villains as such, just difficulties in the way of the PCs' agenda/tests on the way to the Great Test.
The Guide.
The Helper - doesn't just guide and give information but gives some kind of logistical support, without being actually a main character with a clear separate agenda.
The Mission Briefer, who sets up the scenario.
The Walk-on Incidental Infodump.
The Comic Relief.
The Lust Interest.
The Foil, who points up some aspect of one or more PCs by comparison or contrast.
The McGuffin, who might as well be a Maltese falcon - their only role is to get rescued.
The Nameless Victims, who engage sympathy with their suffering.
The Named Victims, who were valued by the PCs and provoke them to vengeance.
The Means to an End - someone who knows something, has something, can do something or has authority to command something that the PCs need.
The Furniture (the kind who get credits like "Man in street" or "Woman #2").

I'm not sure what I'm going to do with this; it may or may not end up in the game text. At the moment it's just part of my random brainstorming to show me possibilities.

There are probably lists of standard story roles somewhere - links to such are welcome too.

    \n So I'd say, we've got a character (1), a situation (2), the character acts on the situation (3) and get some result (4).

    So I'd say an NPC would be a part of one of those things, or maybe more than one!

    1. Part of a character: not literally, of course, but if we have "a loyal character," then we've got to establish the character is loyal, etc. NPC to the rescue!
    2. Part of a situation: Are the opposition? Are they there to establish something about the situation?
    3. Part of the action: Again, opposition? Establishment of action (say, if your action is to give away a million dollars, it's nice to have a NPC just to give the million dollars too? Enabler?
    4. Part of the result: someone to demonstrate the effect of the character's action.

    Basically, I think NPCs make things human, and thus apply to humans.

    \n Sure. Supporting characters are "directed" by players and (generally) "played" by other players (so if you have a supporting character that's particularly important to you, you aren't talking to yourself when you interact with him/her). NPCs in the classic sense are directed and played by the GM.

    By "directed" I mean pretty much the stage or film sense of "direction": you tell the actors, in general terms, what you want them to do.

    Mule/Eric, thanks for the insight, which totally fits with the direction I'm taking my design in: Supporting characters are there to provide handles on the setting and situation, so that the PCs can interact with (and change) them via a human interaction rather than something more abstract. Not "I'm opposing Lunar Asterism" but "I'm having a conflict with this Lunar Asterist priest right here, right now."

    Thinking about this some more on the way home last night, I also realized that you can group these roles together into higher-level functions. NPCs provide:

    - Information
    - Motivation ("Help me, Obi-Wan Kenobi!")
    - Reflection (this is the holds-up-a-mirror thing)
    - Resources and means (which could include information)
    - Challenge
    - Colour

    Anything that doesn't fit into those general categories that you can think of?

    (Of course, the same NPC can provide more than one of these. A D&D NPC, for example, provides challenge while you're killing him, and resources when you take his stuff.)
