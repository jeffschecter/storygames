Back in the '80s (when everything was cooler), I started reading a series of novels by Barry Sadler called Casca: The Eternal Mercenary. Basically they were action porn set across a variety of historical settings and featuring the main protagonist Casca Longinus, the Roman soldier who speared Christ's side, cursed to walk the earth until the Second Coming. Since Casca knows nothing better than war, he spends his years fighting in one army or the other from ancient times to present day. Sadler wrote something on the order of twenty books in the series before his death in 1989 and another ten have been written posthumously by other authors. I will confess I haven't read them all and now they're kind of hard to come by.

I was thinking that Casca makes for a pretty good roleplaying character: skilled in combat, at the center of every story in terms of creating action and (best of all for the player) unkillable.

It's that last thing that made me start thinking about roleplaying in this context. Death is the sticky wicket of roleplaying games. Nobody likes to have their character die, but when the average character sees as much combat as Casca Longinus does... well, somebody's going to get killed. The dice don't always fall in favor of the player character.

Some folks really dig the random nature of death in roleplaying games. The risk involved makes it seem a little more "real," even as the action itself achieves mythic dimensions. But I'd wager that most players are decidedly not fans of life-or-death dice rolls. They want to live and live and live regardless of the odds against them. And, frankly, I don't see anything wrong with that.

I'm going to go on record saying that player characters shouldn't die. Unless \u2014 and this is an important distinction to make \u2014 death is part of the story. For example, it would be foolish to have a game featuring a grueling wartime setting and making the characters invincible. Dying horribly is part and parcel of the war experience and ought to be preserved as an outcome. But most gaming is heroic-level gaming and dying from a stray bullet or a lucky sword thrust simply isn't part of the story.

One could argue that removing death as a penalty means players will undertake more and riskier actions with their characters, but I ask this: so what? If players are having fun doing things they'd never dream of doing in real life, isn't that kind of the point of roleplay? After all, if I want to wear a safety helmet, elbow- and knee-pads and look both ways before crossing the street, I can do that in my everyday life.

In the second Casca novel, Casca: God of Death, our hero is sacrificed on a Central American altar. He has his heart carved from his chest and survives. I don't know about you, but that would make for the mother of all roleplaying war stories. Why would you want to miss such a thing by enforcing "reality" at every turn? Not all games can accommodate that level of over-the-topness, but I'd sure like to play in one that does.
