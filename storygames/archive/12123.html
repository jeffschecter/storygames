\n Hello Story Games,
\nA while back, we were talking about playtests and someone asked me to post a playtest questionnaire and report. I recently I had a handful of groups do some independent playtesting on the con scenario I've been running with for the last two years. The scenario seemed solid to me, but I needed to get some external feedback to be sure. Thus I posted a request for testers and then sent along the following email to those who responded:
\n
\n
original email wrote:
\n
Hello Burners!
\nYour playtest group is to run Trouble in Hochen
\nYour playtest group must provide an after-play (AP) report.
\nScenarios should not run for more than one session -- 2 to 5 hours. If your scenario goes longer than one session, do not continue it. Report it in your AP.
\n
\nYour APs are due May 23rd. People who complete early will get another -- much more fun -- assignment as a bonus.
\n
\nDo not send me recordings of your sessions. I do not have time to listen to them. Please provide written feedback as described below. It might be useful to assign a non-GM player the role of secretary in order to note down the play data as it happens.
\n
\nYour AP for each scenario must include:
\nNumber of players.
\nGeneral RPG experience of players.
\nGeneral BW experience of players.
\nSession length.
\nCharacters used.
\n
\nTest Log: ability tested, intent/task, obstacle, pass or fail. (Yes, I want a list of every test in the session, if possible.)
\nDuel of Wits Statements and Comprises.
\nFight/RnC results: number of exchanges, injuries sustained
\n
\nA brief overview of the fictional results of the session. In other words, what happened? This should be no more than a paragraph. Don't give me a blow by blow.
\n
\nPoints of Confusion: As the GM, was there a point in the scenario in which you were lost and didn't know what to do? Describe it for me, please.
\nPoints of Zen: As the GM, was there a point in the scenario in which you made the right intuitive decision and play flowed along? Describe it briefly for me, please.
\n
\nMissing Elements: What is the scenario missing?
\n
\nPlayer Observation: Was there a point at which the players became bored or disengaged? Describe it.
\n
\nPlayer Feedback: Please pass on any comments from the players about the scenario.
\n
\nThanks so much for helping us out. I look forward to hearing about your games!
\n
\n
\nSome of you may remember this scenario. I ran it a number of times in the past two or three years. I thought this playtest report was particularly useful. There's a lot of raw data plus good negative and positive feedback. (Posted with the playtester's permission. Names omitted to protect the guilty.)
\n

    \n The "around ten users" comes from research Jakob Nielsen and others did in the 1990s regarding the usability of electronic content. They found that using a methodologically sound instrument, you'll expose 95% of usability problems after a dozen users or less (Nielsen argues for five in some circumstances). Some people take this as gospel and others don't, but in my experience with usability testing of Web interfaces it seems to bear out pretty well. The first time I conducted a quick-and-dirty usability test I had a 20 user sample and I noted the results after ten. It would have been enough for our needs and after that I cut my users in half without any apparent ill effect.

    To extrapolate this for tabletop playtesting, it means you are probably in the sweet spot if you are getting a dozen thoughtful returns and you are asking the right questions. The latter is pretty important, obviously. Talking about instrument design would be really cool - I'd love to get some social scientists discussing that. All my usability work has been pretty specific to Web interfaces, so I'm not sure I'd have much to contribute.

    \n Pretty much with Jason said. In my classes it's been framed thusly:

    Assuming that (a) the thing in question is ready to be tested (fully conceptualized, tested in-house, modeled well, etc) and (b) the users that are testing it are representative samples of your target user base, you will probably get all of the useful feedback you need in order to validate and revise the project from as few as 7 users, and you'll probably top out around 12 or so in terms of learning something new.

    That said, you could have a pool of 100 users, and if only 10 of them are representative samples of the target audience, you may have to wade through a ton of useless and non-informative feedback. This is why you want to figure out your user group as specifically as you can before you invest in testing.

    So, for BWHQ, I'm willing to bet that almost every group that Luke is in touch with is a representative sample of his target user group, AND I'm sure that the material he's sending it is as close to perfect as he can get it, so him getting 7-12 playtest reports is probably going to give him all the substantive feedback that he needs. Contrast this with people sending unfinished, or poorly written manuscripts to people that may or may not actually be the target audience for the game - even if you get 20 playtest reports that way, chances are that it won't be as useful a body of feedback.

    \n Figuring out your target audience is largely dependent on the intention and thrust of the project. A lot of people have a target audience in mind from the get-go, so that makes it easier. But I would say that the first question to ask is "why am I making X"? For the sake of this discussion, X is "a new, original tabletop roleplaying game" Here's some fake examples!

    - If you are making the game because you are unsatisfied with a current game (or selection of similar games), then your target audience is probably going to be people who have experience with that game and may be looking for the same kind of "fix"

    - If you are making the game because you want to explore new ground and push boundaries, then your target audience is probably going to be people interested in experimental and fringe games, and may very well include folks in adjacent "zones" (like larpers or casual digital game players or something)

    - If you are making the game because you had a great idea that excites you, then your target audience is probably going to be people who get excited by the same things that you do, and you can probably find them in the social groups you're already involved in

    - If you are making the game in order to make a statement, then your target audience is probably going to be a mix of people who are interested in statement-making in general, and the people that the statement is aimed towards.

    And so on.

    In terms of finding those people, one way may be to make a list of games that occupy the same "zone" as your game, and try to find fans of those games. Another may be to make a list of non-game-specific demographic and lifestyle attributes, and find ways to get your word out into those adjacent but maybe overlapping interest zones (like, taking out an ad in a BMX online newsletter if your game is about the rad world of BMX, that kind of thing).

    A really effective way, as we see around here all the time, is post content from your game (or a free ready-to-play version) and see who jumps on it. Then you can potentially leverage those initial users social connections to find other like-minded users.

    A big reminder: just because you identify a "typical" or "perfect" user DOES NOT MEAN that you are shutting out other kinds of people. People will find ways to get into all kinds of things you'd never imagine them to be interested in from demographics. But concentrating on finding your ideal user is a good exercise for you in terms of making decisions about your design, and will certainly pay off in terms of playtesting and marketing.
