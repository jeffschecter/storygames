\n My wife, Ami, has been doing real genealogy for her and my family and it got me thinking about gaming (like everything does).

It's always interesting to me how a person got from there to here, and what roads they took along the way. I'm curious what gaming path everyone took to get to where they are today. The rivers more than the tributaries.

I was talking to James Jeffers the other day and he managed to avoid the White Wolf games altogether, and that fascinated me, because they were important to me and my group for almost a decade.

So, for me:

I started playing D&D in 1982. I played Erol Otus basic and expert until the new boxed edition came out and then played that until the Master boxed set came out. Then, due to Xtian hysteria I got rid of my D&D stuff and actually played Dragon Raid for a year or so. When I was ready for real gaming again I went ahead and got AD&D and played that until 1988 when a dysfunctional game caused me to give up on D&D and curse its name for over a decade, until 3e came out and made me appreciate it for what it is again.

My second important game was Star Frontiers, in about 1983, but the cool 17-year-old kid who worked at the game store told me that Traveller was where it was at, so I immediately dropped Star Frontiers and played Traveller for years after that, but I never became cool. There was a little bit of Star Wars played sometime in there too after it came out.

Next was MERP and then Stormbringer, which we played for years, and since that was BRP it led us directly into Cthulhu, which is still my favorite all-time game and something I'm always willing to play. After that it was Shadowrun and into Vampire (where I met Ami) and a little Amber. Then back to Cthulhu exclusively for years with all the cool Pagan Publishing and Delta Green stuff.

A few years ago, I ordered Dogs in the Vineyard because it looked interesting, and that opened up a whole new realm of games to play (not that I've actually played that much Dogs).

So, where I am now is with too many games and too little time. I can't say that anything I mentioned wouldn't be fun to play again. I think it's a great time to be a gamer.

So, if you are so inclined, tell me how you got to where you are now.

    Posted By: JuddMarvel Super-Heroes -> AD&D -> (this whole area after AD&D is hazy and I feel like I'm missing pieces) -> D&D 2nd Edition -> Champions -> GURPS -> Vampire: The Masquerade -> Werewolf: The Apocolypse -> Ars Magica -> L5R -> Deadlands -> D&D 3.0 -> Unknown Armies -> Dust Devils -> Sorcerer -> The Riddle of Steel
    \nFrom Marvel Super-Heroes -> GURPS was the height of my system doesn't matter stage but we all thought Champions was the bees' knees. A few of my friends and I became obsessed with how one would stat something out with the HERO system from action movie heroes to Transformers to the contents of the DC Who's Who or the Guidebooks to the Marvel Universe. I think Champions was the first game to really grab my attention.

    From Vampire -> L5R could very well be called -broken promises- if I was to name the era. It was a time when I was really frustrated with my games and my gaming. Ars Magica's vague guidelines for troupe play as each edition moved farther and farther away from what interested me in mythic Europe and Vampire's Humanity stat, Werewolf's Rage and L5R's Honor and Glory all left us wondering why these things just didn't work for us at the table.

    Oddly, I think Deadlands was the most useable of the bunch but man, that original game system was busy with dice and chips and cards.

    D&D 3.0 and Unknown Armies were neat tweener games and worked really well out of the box. It wasn't 3.0's fault that I was looking for Burning Wheel Revised and that wasn't it. 3.0 was the first game that I remember showing how the game worked behind the curtain. I remember a portion of the DMG, I think, where they walk you through making your own class and what balance issues to keep in mind. Neat stuff. UA was a game I had a ton of fun playing and GMing at home. I loved how the stress meters brought about character change whether you liked it or not. It was the first game to show a mental side-effect for violence.

    Dust Devils, Sorcerer and TRoS all changed things for me. Suddenly, games could work just out of the box and were meant to be played as written. Fun times.

    I feel like another big corner was turned with Dogs in the Vineyard and Burning Wheel Revised.

    \nRed box D&D, and all through the colors to Immortal (although we only really played the first three). That led me to Dragon magazine, that led me to Palladium, specificially Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles. Then I found more people to play with besides me and my best friend, and they were playing 1st edition AD&D (or actually somewhere in between 1st and 2nd, drifting quickly to 2nd edition with us newcomers who only had 2nd edition books, though I don't know if we were a factor, I didn't even know the different editions had anything different outside of the art). And discovering so many games so fast kept me in that mode, I never settled down.     Then a friend discovered Vampire (at first I dismissed it - "Blood Points instead of Hit Points? That's a sad rip off of D&D!" - due to misinformation) and we eventually played it. And the whole storytelling ethos professed everything I was looking to do, so that replaced a lot of games and dominated play. Then as the shine wore off years later, I started branching out again, Men In Black, Big Eyes, Small Mouth, Unknown Armies, and a bunch more I'm sure (along with a hell of a lot of White Wolf games, editions, and supplements), until I hit upon Sorcerer and my whole world changed, and that's pretty much were I am. Except I still don't own Sorcerer, but I've dived pretty deep into the indie games, and now have found a nice middle ground where I like to mix indie games and traditional games and play them honestly, not trying to push them to do something that they aren't designed to do.

    \n Really started from Dragon Warrior, Might & Magic, and other such games on computers and NES.

    Started by picking up 2nd edition D&D modules (Storm Riders) and not knowing there was this "Player's Handbook" thing that you actually needed. Thought that CHA stood for "Challenge," and couldn't understand why some people had really crappy scores when they were clearly badass.

    Enter LOTS of homebrewing. Finally picked up PHB, DMG, and the awesome three-ring Monstrous Manual (and its many friends).

    The next big deal was GURPS, especially Supers. I loved tinkering with the system. Red box came in somewhere, as well as my beloved Rules Cyclopedia. A whole game in one book! How could I resist?

    Battlelords of the 23rd Century, RIFTS, Paranoia, Marvel and DC's games, sci-fi homebrewing that eventually resulted in Valence. Planescape. Magic: the Gathering sucked my money away for quite some time.

    Werewolf was my next big impact, but it really paled when Mage showed up. I didn't 100% get the ideas behind the game for a long time, but even the surface was a hell of a lot of fun. Very deep game.

    From there things sort of exploded when I got to college, not because I met other gamers, but just because this whole "Internet" thing showed up in force and I could read about all kinds of games. Found Amber, played that for years. Palladium Fantasy, In Nomine, Ars Magica, Vampire, BESM, L5R, Deadlands, lots of other stuff. Lots.

    D&D 3rd was a nice renaissance.

    At some point I instituted a policy: never buy a game I could write myself. Was good policy. Has worked well so far.

    The first indie game I actually saw was Sorcerer, but the first one I played was... maybe Donjon? It was quite a while before I actually played one.

    And that's where I am right now. There's a lot more richness to this that escapes my feeble brain at this time.

    \n Almost my entire gaming history has been with homemade games, but here goes:
    \n
    \nMiddle School
    \nMy first RPG (aside from a partial session of Boot Hill when I was like 8) was one by my friend Rowdy called "Q": the Great Gathering that, if memory serves, was about an ancient mystical society that had a diaspora a long time ago, and they're now regrouping in modern day because their leader has reincarnated, but the individual factions have grown so different from eachother over time that now they can't get along. You didn't roll to hit; you rolled to see what you had the opportunity to do (I didn't recognize it as such at the time). We played the hell out of it.
    \n
    \nI also played one session of V:tM, which turned me off to White Wolf entirely (and probably unfairly) because the game was completely overrun by social jockeying to see who could be the kewlest, darkest, most gothinest guy in the group--and such alpha-struggle behavior was REALLY unusual with that group of friends, so it was unpleasant.
    \nMe, reading through the clans: "Hm. Maybe I'll be a Malkavian"
    \nFriend: "A Malkavian? Whaddya wanna be a fuckin' clown for?"
    \nMe: "Oh. Um, I guess I'll be a Toreador."
    \nFriend: "A fuckin' flower-sniffer? Really?"
    \nMe: "Oh. Well, what are you?"
    \nFriend, proudly: "Gangrel!"
    \n
    \nI tried my hand at writing several games (that whole circle of friends was doing it for a while), and the one that stuck and actually got played was called Misadventures in Nowhere. It was a tactics-dominated thing about outlaws and mercenaries, with swords & sorcery and also guns & cars.
    \n
    \nHigh School
    \nI played nothing but Misadventures, and it gradually evolved into a grimmer, more Sim-focused thing.
    \n
    \nCollege
    \nPlayed some TOON!, and a short campaign of D&D; (I have no clue which one). I didn't find TOON! satisfying, and I never liked D&D;'s color after I was like 12 (I kinda don't like high fantasy in general these days). Misadventures spiralled into maximum grimness and was renamed The Rustbelt (and the focus shifted from outlaws to scavengerly adventurers), full-on Purist for System bloatedness and saw some mediocre play in that milieu (due to the fact that no one else cared about simulating the world) and also some rare but riveting play that I would later learn was Narrativism.
    \n
    \nThen I stopped gaming altogether for quite some time.
    \n
    \nThen I found the Forge, and my head exploded.
    \nWrote a crazy experiment that threw out everything I thought I knew about RPGs and called it Super Action Now!, miraculously found people interested in playing in this small town where I live, and played the CRAP out of SAN!. Also started developing a sortof supernatural Men-in-Black in the Old West called Witch Trails, been playing moderately. Played InSpectres a few times and failed miserably as a GM, but was able to contribute well as a player. Played kill puppies for satan once and had a revealing experience. Also re-tooled The Rustbelt into a Nar-focused thing, played it a few times with steadily increasing success. And that puts us up to speed.

    \n I'll do the "major investments of time" thing, not the every single thing I've ever played, which is massive, or everything I own, which is actually even larger.

    The chronology is rough.

    1. Mish-mash of Erol Otus covered basic/expert D&D and the immediate precursor (some of us had one edition, some the other)
    2. AD&D1
    3. Villains & Vigilantes
    4. Traveller
    5. Gamma World 2nd ed.
    6. Champions
    7. Paranoia
    8. GURPS
    9. DC Heroes
    10. WEG Star Wars
    11. BRP-derived homebrews (weird fantasy mishmash of Elric! & Pendragon, for example, and a very fun improv "Lost World/Planetary Romance" game using CoC & Elric!/Stormbringer as it's base, with bits of RQIII thrown in (particularly Land of Ninja)). It made Spaceship Zero's setting feel a bit familiar -- we had a Farmer, a Secret Service agent, and a US Senator as PCs....

    Of that list, though, D&D variations, Traveller versions, Gamma World 2ed, Champions, and V&V probably got the most focus, at various times. Really, the first
    six games on the list. Which I never noticed before.

    Well, and GURPS, actually, which I played a lot of in college & in the post-college years.

    The Great Collapse of gaming groups, but an increase in disposable income, post-college, led to the purchase of a lot of stuff that hasn't necessarily gotten more than one or two plays, or in some cases not even that.

    Sigh.

    I discovered the Forge via Sorcerer, which I discovered via Ken Hite reviewing it, and it + the indie games in general have reinvigorated my interest in gaming more than anything else I can remember. Almost feels like back at the beginning.

    \nGrade School
    A single game of blue-book Basic D&D where my pal Hassan and I romped through B2: Keep on the Borderlands. We spontaneously invented both
    multiclassing and GM-less play. "Hey," I would say as I read through the book, "we should go over here where the minotaur is!"

    Middle School
    AD&D1e was the game of choice. Our school had a "minicourse" in D&D, i.e., 45 minutes a day and a room in which to play. The supervising teacher would
    even give you crap if you weren't playing. "Either play some D&D or else you have to go to study hall."

    Then we discovered Champions, which was soon replaced with V&V, since we could actually figure out how to play V&V. Likewise, a lot of us owned
    Traveller, but Star Frontiers made more sense.

    High School
    More AD&D1e, now enhanced by Unearthed Arcana. I owned a bajillion RPGs at this point, but AD&D was all we were playing.

    College
    I gave up RPGs entirely... Until I broke down one day Sophomore year and joined my roommate's Rolemaster game. We went on to play a lot of Call of
    Cthulhu    , and a little bit of Champions 4th edition. I still didn't really grok the latter, despite GM'ing it. All gaming stopped again Junior year,
    though.

    The Big Break
    The next nine years or so were pretty much no RPGs at all. I got into Magic for a little bit, and bought a copy of CORPS. Mostly, though, I sold the majority of my games and was occupied with other things. I hung out on various RPG newsgroups, but like a lot of people in the '90s, I talked about RPGs despite not playing them.

    D&D 3.0
    I stumbled upon an article about a new edition of D&D coming out. The described changes so set fire to my brain that I went out and bought the
    books. The new d20 engine just blew me away. I got some friends together to play D&D for the first time in forever, and when that fell through, I did
    something unprecedented. I went out onto the Web, posted on some player-finder sites, and hooked up with total strangers in order to game.
    And then dId it again so I had a second D&D group to play with.

    That I was not alone in doing this, even within the groups I joined, makes me consider D&D 3.0 one of the most important RPG releases ever. Heck, I
    organize Enworld Gamedays now, and that would never have happened if not for 3.0 (and ENWorld).

    HERO
    3.0 got me buying RPGs regularly, and among them was the new 5th edition of HERO. I fell in love (again), and found yet more total strangers to
    regularly play HERO with.

    Yup, three gaming groups, total.

    Deep in the Game
    I was tangentially aware of the Forge during this time, but I never really connected with what it was doing. Then, somehow, I found Chris Chinn's blog,
    Deep in the Game. I sat and read his entire archive... twice. That led me to other "Forge diaspora" blogs, which then led me back to the Forge itself. I then read Ron's GNS essays, and my world was officially rocked.

    Today
    I'm back to one regular group, and we primarily play D&D, though we're on a little Star Wars Saga Edition detour as we wait for 4e. Otherwise, my time and money is spent on games from IPR, my main loves being Burning Wheel and FATE. The indies and WotC are the only people who continue to impress me. HERO and various other trad RPGs have been kicked to the curb without regret.
