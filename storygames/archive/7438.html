    \n Always make at least two versions in the package you're selling: one for screen-reading, the other for printing. The screen-reading version should be pretty and have the full gamut of on-a-computer conveniences, like hyperlinks and bookmarks (it is possible to hyperlink your entire index, for instance). The print version should have low-res, grayscaled images (perhaps even brightened to use up less black ink), and it should print conveniently on 8.5x11 paper.

    \n I'll be honest, I read my pdfs, I don't print them. So for me what I want the most is that my eight year old computer won't choke on the pages.

    If I can view an entire page at once and read the text, I don't care if it's double column (Agon is like this and reads easy) or single column (My Life With Master is like this and reads easy). However, if the text is of such a size that I can't view pages at 100%, then I far prefer single column. In those cases I leave the mouse pointer on the bottom of the side scroll bar so that I can just click it whenever I'm ready for more new text, two columns means I have to move the mouse and click. I find that annoying.

    I love images that load instantly (I hate clicking next page and having any load time). Any feature that would increase the load time from instant to something not instant would really turn me off. On this one as well I find MLWM to be perfect.

    EDIT: Also, bookmarks, I like.

    EDIT: Also, there are font types that are easier to read online than others. I've always been partial to Verdana, but any common sans-serif should do.

    For font sizes, I didn't say it, but I prefer if I can fit the entire page on my screen. Meaning the font has to be large enough that I can comfortably read that. My vision is about average, or slightly below.

    \n To expand on Justin: you can do nearly anything in PDF that you can do on a web page:
    * Character sheets as forms, complete with hidden math functions (ex: figured stats).
    * Comment forms, with a (hidden? haven't tried) email send to you, the author (or, hmmm... as a forum post?).
    * Custom dice generator (card drawer, whatever), built in.
    * Hmmm... all monster's stats editable, with a Reset button--no more need to take paper notes of hits or charges or ammo during a fight. Heck, while you're at it, deliver a copyable, simpler PDF so every player can have a cheat sheet and an editable, savable (and, ultimately, uploadable) character sheet, for play by email or post (or just campaign notes online, for in-person play).

    As for a PDF-for-home-print, just be sure you have a gutter (wider inside edge margin than outside edge) for binding. Considering that hole-punching will likely be the "deepest" possible binding that your end-users will use, I'd say about 3/4" gutter--plus whatever your standard left/right margin is--should do it.

    \n What should your pdf have? A second pdf. As mentioned previously, what works for print is not what works for screen, so making two versions shows that you're really serious about giving your audience what they want. Yes, it's more work. Things worth doing often are.
    \n
    \nScreen version: hyperlinks, columns, have all been covered, but here's one that hasn't: pages that are sized and oriented to fit on a screen, with complete disregard to printing paper sizes. Pick a font that reads well on screen (sans serif fonts are more popular for screen, and they should be bigger, too). Full colour pictures are great.
    \n
    \nPrint version: Pictures should either be b&w;, or have sufficient contrast that they're still clear in b&w; printing. Pick a font that's good for print (serif fonts are more popular in print), and make sure you do all the picky tuning that can make print look that little bit nicer (increase the leading slightly, adjust the kerning on titles, etc.). If things are meant to be printed in colour, then make sure any titles done in black (or other spots that need an intense black) are 100% black and 10% cyan. Index, or even a glossary/index (like Privateer Press does). Put the page numbers in the top-right (most of your audience will likely hit print, so save numbering for double-sided for if you do a book version).
    \n
    \nFor both: chapter titles that make sense, appearing at the top of each page for quick reference. A truly amazing-looking "cover" page.

    \n It also depends on the size of the pdf. People will print a 25-page pdf on their inkjet or home laser. But if it's bigger than that, it's going be cheaper and easier to take it to Kinkos and have them do it - in which case, it really doesn't matter if it's designed to economize on ink. Just make sure that it's designed to print nicely as gray scale because color is still outrageously expensive (unless you have your own color laser). And make sure that the pagination works correctly for printing double sided.

    There are a lot of free and inexpensive PDF tools around - or you can use Adobe's online service.
