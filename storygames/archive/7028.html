\n I ran my Game Chef game Dulse for the first time yesterday and it went pretty well. Dulse is a meditation on what I'm learning from Jeepform and similar traditions. There are almost no mechanical constraints. There's no rules-oriented way to resolve conflicts - the conflicts are front-loaded and scenes don't end until they are resolved, so it is up to the players to sort it out. It's rigidly constrained in terms of theme, scenes (and related conflicts, really questions that must be answered), and characters, but wide open in every other aspect. Scene framing is completely loosey-goosey, scenes can occur in any order, players build their own relationship map, and outcomes are achieved by consensus. There's no GM; it's got the lightest touch of system. Given the default settings (a sort of fairy tale setting, a clash of love and honor), it played out like high fantasy. A very satisfying and cohesive story emerged, involving a murdered infant, an heir to a curse of war and the sorceress who frustrates his blood-thirst, an enemy kingdom on the march and a princess with the power to stop it all. It was pretty cool to see in action. It reminded me of a storytelling exercise studded with gentle reminders to keep it on-task and awesome. "Storytelling exercise" is often used as a shorthand perjorative for weak and systemless games (or parlor narration) but, you know, whatever. I wasn't at all sure it would hang together, but it really does.

    \n Hey Jamey,

    We played it as written - I just observed. So there were four players, and we agreed to use the default setting and ideals, fleshing things out a bit as we built the relationship map. "The Killing" started out pretty straightforward - Calla and Arras were both gunning for each other, Arras gloated about fooling her (the whole game hinged on some prophecy that his son would lead the nation to eternal war, and Calla was an evil pacifist witch) by murdering his own heir. She laughed at him and told him he was wrong, his plans had failed, and he was already dead - she'd poisoned his meal. Arras, a tough dude to the last, tried to take his own life before her poison could fell him. In the epilogue we discovered that he'd botched the job and died ignominiously after all.

    In the end it was Arras and Dulse with 6 love and 4 honor, and (obviously) Baston and Calla with 6 honor and 4 love. So everybody had pretty miserable outcomes. I didn't stress the tactical nature of awarding love or honor, which might have made a difference. We used playing cards (red = love, black = honor), and that was super simple. Tokens, as I originally wrote, would be a little burdensome. They would be a good option for a highly phsyical, LARP-y game, though. It would support that mode just fine. You could even structure it as a performance, I think - give the token power to the audience.

    One thing I was pleased with was that the spread of love and honor mapped well to the fiction - Arras and Dulse were more kind-hearted and less honor driven. The paired final spreads were a little anticlimactic, but it wasn't a huge deal. Another thing I observed - the relationship map got used more heavily or more lightly, depending - Arras and Dulse shared "envy", and that didn't get much play. Dulse and Calla shared "fear", and that was pretty central. Again, I think that's just fine, as long as something interesting emerges for each character.

    \n I always hate reading these actual plays that go on and on about player dynamics but never bloody well tell anyone what actually happened in the game, so let's see if I can remember how things played out.

    Scene 1 - the birth - Arras and Baston - Arras is mistrustful of Baston because he believes all men should reach out and take for themselves, and Baston has always been loyal and never greedy. We learn that Arras is the father of the child.

    Scene 2 - the betrayal - Calla and Dulse - Three months earlier, Dulse tells Calla that she refuses to have the child. Calla tells Dulse that it's too late for an abortion. Dulse abandons responsibility of the child and the child's future to the sorceress.

    Scene 3 - the secret - Baston and Dulse - Shortly after the child's birth, Baston has waged war on a country across the sea and brought back a chest. The sorceress' prophecy involves some fate of war, if the child is given what is in the chest on his 18th birthday. Dulse tells Baston to take the chest back across the sea. Baston opens the chest and finds a gauntlet, then sails off and dumps it into the ocean.

    Scene 4 - the murder - Arras and Calla - 16 years later. We learn Calla has been plotting something for 15 generations of Arras' forefathers which will culminate with the child. Arras reveals that he killed the child the day it was born and replaced it with another, who will be the legacy he leaves to the world. Calla counters that her prophecy relies on a child of heart, not of blood. Arras moves to run her through. She reveals that she has poisoned the grapes he has been eating. He stabs himself through the gut rather than die by her hand.

    Scene 5 - the wedding - Everyone - The 18th birthday of the child, Duke Arras II. At this point, with Duke Arras I dead, the new duke became a player character. The boy has conquered a pirate who had been building an empire with a treasure dug up from the bottom of the sea, taken it for himself, and returned home triumphant to name his bride. Dulse, who has remarried to Baston, wants the boy to wed the sorceress, which will make him infertile and end Calla's machinations. Instead, he gives a speech to the people renouncing his father's hedonism, and takes a milk maid as his bride.

    Epilogues: Arras II spreads war throughout the world and conquers many nations, but ends up dying old and fat like his father, exactly as Arras I wished him not to die. Baston leaves Dulse for a younger woman, and dies loveless. Calla, young and beautiful for 15 generations, finally withers to an old crone and dies. Dulse takes her place, plotting and whispering cold-hearted in the ears of rulers centuries down the line.

    \n Here's what I saw the system doing:

    \n Hi Paul,

    I wanted to mix it up a little, because I figured active, "do this" verbs would appeal to some people and inspire them. They also provide contrast to one another pretty nicely. That was the theory, anyway - in the playtest my impression was that these were among the weaker, less-utilized relationship elements. Deceive got some play, but frustrate, support, and envy got hit on lightly if at all. That might be a fluke, or maybe they are not really appropriate or juicy.

    I thought the verb list would end up being the universal anchor, but now long term I'd like to refine the verb list and also create different "builds" for different sorts of games - a romance set, a blood and guts set, and so forth. Imagine nine verbs that all focused on the nuance of love relationships. That might be fun, and it'd certainly be a different sort of game.

    Oh, and to answer your direct question - I think each group will find its own way, but I imagined pointing deceive at someone means you are actively lying to them and it should come out in play at some point. Once it does, that relationship verb probably won't be hit again, which is fine. Unless you frame a flashback or something.

    Posted By: Jason Morningstarthe fact that you're expected to make a value judgment
    \nThat's something I haven't seen elsewhere. The thing about it that most excites me (aside from keeping people engaged) is that it crosses over the internal/external barrier to get inside people's heads. Internal space has traditionally been sort of off limits, or at least, has been given a less than thorough examination (perhaps for reasons that have to do with theory...) I see Dulse, in terms of the internal/external dichotomy, on the player and character level, as sort of an opposite or counterpoint to IAWA. I'm finding the comparison illuminating.

    I'll be interested to see if this value judgment technology gets picked up in future games and becomes one of the design memes (e.g. relationship maps, stakes setting, etc.) that we see a lot of. I'm all for it, and looking forward to seeing what derivatives people might come up with.
