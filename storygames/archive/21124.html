\n
\n
\n

\n
\n In the last years during (story/RPG) game design, the most important tool for me has been a thesaurus. Personally, I mostly use the thesaurus that comes with the macOS dictionary. There are some useful online dictionaries with a thesaurus, too.

So, a thesaurus is the most indispensable tool for me when I'm tweaking the basic terminology of a new system. I go over many iterations of changing basic words like "bonus, trait, flow, technique, mechanic, flaw, vice, conquence, result, relationship, connection, suspense, volatility, power, strength, asset..." etc until I find just the right word.

In my experience, the right terminology makes a huge difference in a games' consistency and accessibility. So I wanted to share this experience and ask in return if and how you value a thesaurus in your own design process?

    \n I like to joke (in that it's a true thing that I say in a way that's meant to defy expectations for the sake of amusement) that when I'm translating I spend far more time with my head buried in a thesaurus than I do in a J-E dictionary. I think they're pretty invaluable for any kind of writing, especially where voice and tone are important.

    That's probably even more true for the technical side of game writing, where making game-relevant terms stand out by using marked language can be a significant factor in making the game rules more easy to understand. Often you're not just creating a game system, but you're also responsible for constructing a kind of dialect specifically for people to use while playing the game.

    \n I must admit that I think too many games opt for flavorful descriptors that just complicate learning the game.

    When I try to wrap my head around the game I have to memorize new terms with labels which could have been more straightforward. I don't think it's helpful. Ofttimes it's used to differentiate from similar (or even equal) mechanics in other games.

    I'd prefer designers prioritize ease/clarity over differentiating (whenever possible).

    There are no hard and fast judgements to be made, some nuances can negate everything I've just written, obviously. But I think it's something to keep in mind when making design decisions.

    \n BeePeeGee, one of the things I like about Directions Storyplaying System is that the terms you use to discribe your system are very precise.

    I actually like games to have their own precise language, because games are different from one another; they have their own agendas and aims\u2014especially, indie games. System makes a huge difference, and sometimes using the old terminology makes people think a new game is just the same old game they understand and have always played.

    There have been many times I\u2019ve facilitated Collaborative Story Games at Story Games SLC and the group has just ignored my discription of the game\u2019s unique rules and system features and has just played it the same way they\u2019ve become accustomed to playing. Why play a new game everyweek if we aren\u2019t going to actually play the game the way it was designed to be played?

    People sometimes say, they don\u2019t like being told how to play a game. I say, if you don\u2019t like a designer to tell you how to play their game, don\u2019t play their game. But don\u2019t play a game in a way it wasn\u2019t designed to be played and then blame the game when things don\u2019t work out. I think using the unique language at least gets the reader to pause and hopefully notice that games are different and that they need to play a game as it is designed, on its own terms, to really experience it.

    That said, if a game mechanic is essentially carbon copied from game to game, and there is a precise, well known term for it, another term doesn\u2019t seem very useful. I think this is much more the case in mainstream games than in indie ones though.

    Jeff_Slater said:

    That said, if a game mechanic is essentially carbon copied from game to game, and there is a precise, well known term for it, another term doesn\u2019t seem very useful. I think this is much more the case in mainstream games than in indie ones though.

    \nThat's true of indie games, too!
    I mean, if players take turns framing a scene in your game, by all means call it "players take turns framing a scene", not "Game-Directors introduce Action-Segments in accordance with the Wheel of Karma" - unless something is actually different from just taking turns framing scenes.
    Also, if you lift an entire subsystem from another game - say, "fanmail" from Primetime Adventures - consider whether you can keep calling it "fanmail" and attribute it to Primetime Adventures. That has become pretty much a standard for a few specific things, such as the X-card (and Lines and Veils before that).

    \n Yeah, the aural is important.

    I wrote a game once where each character had a stat (well, a die pool) called Anima. It looked great on paper, but it was very hard to pronounce in play, mostly because everyone felt it was important to emphasize that they weren't using the word "enemy", or, worse, "enema".

    I haven't played that game since (for unrelated reasons), but, if I do, I think I'll have to rename that, which is unfortunate, because it was exactly the right word for what I wanted.
