\n Hey mathletes, rules nerds, and assorted ne'er-do-wells, I need your help! I want to see if there's either an obvious flaw or a glaringly optimal path related to card-draw odds in this scheme:\n
You have eleven points to spend to create your hero.

There are four possible Approaches to solving problems. Approaches cost three points each, and your hero needs at least one. The Approaches are: With Grace (hearts), with Luck (diamonds), with Courage (clubs), and with Skill (spades). That's just color at this point, don't worry about it.

Any points you don\u2019t spend on Approaches are converted, one for one, into points of Resolve. So, your hero can have three Approaches and two points of Resolve, or two Approaches and five points of Resolve, or one Approach and eight points of Resolve.

The game will be punctuated with challenges, which are resolved by drawing playing cards. Challenges can usually be overcome using any Approach you\u2019ve chosen for your hero. Difficult challenges might render a specific Approach impossible, but most of the time any Approach your hero possesses will be fair game to use. Figure half the challenges will rule out a specific Approach - for example, "A deadly dart trap!" might explicitly rule out Approaching it with Courage, because it's a sneak attack. No challenge will rule out more than one Approach, ever.

When you encounter a challenge, draw a card from the deck. If it matches any of the Approaches your hero possesses and is allowed to use, you succeed. If it does not match, your hero immediately loses a point of Resolve. You can choose to give up and fail the challenge, or you can draw again, hoping for a match and success. However, if subsequent draws are also failures, your hero continues to lose a point of Resolve for each.

When Resolve reaches zero, your hero's journey ends in failure!
\nSo ... I'd value your thoughts on whether one of the three builds is optimal and so forth. If one is screamingly better - and I honestly can't tell this morning - I may just make it the default. Also, if tuning the ratio of extreme challenges that forbid an Approach would change things, I can do that, too - it could be one in four instead of half, for example. Oh! You'll always be operating alone - just your dude vs. the challenge. Thanks in advance, smart weird guys!

    \n With one approach, you fail 75% of the time- your expected loss is -.75. Thus, you lose after eleven conflicts.

    With two approaches, you fail 50% of the time - your expected loss is -.50. Thus, you lose after ten conflicts.

    With three approaches, you fail 25% of the time- your expected loss is -.25. Thus, you lose after eight conflicts.

    (If a conflict is difficult and negates one of the hero's approaches, then the expected losses are higher (-1, -.75, and -.50), with less suvivability- eight conflicts for one approach, seven conflicts for two approaches, and four conflicts for one approach.)

    Surprisingly, one approach is the optimal character build. But it doesn't seem very interesting.

    \n Let's assume for starters that no Approach is forbidden, ok?

    If you have one Approach and 8 resolve, there's 25% the card you draw will match your Approach, you need 3 cards to get over 50% of success, that's 57.8125% of success, that's exactly 4 challenges where you win most of the time.

    Two Approaches and 5 Resolve, 50% the card you draw will match one of your approaches, You need 2 cards to get over 50% of success, but that's 75%, that's 5 challenges where you win most of the time, but your chances are MUCH better.

    Three approaches and 2 resolve. 75% the card you draw will match one of your approaches. You need 1 card to get over 50% of success, and that's already your beginning position. Suppose 1 in 4 times you'll fail, that's roughly 11 challenges you play and win.

    Challenges that forbid certain Approaches change your chances of winning a specific challenge, but not your relative chance compared to the other options, and totally rules out taking only one approach, since you need to win on your own, and you're automatically going to end your journey in 0.25*percentage of challenges that forbid an approach, no matter what.

    I'll take the three Approaches route every time, if that's the only use of Resolve.

    \n [EDIT] After reading up on Geometric Series, I've adjusted some numbers to their correct values! [/EDIT]

    Build A: 3 approaches, 2 resolve
    Build B: 2 approaches, 5 resolve
    Build C: 1 approach, 8 resolve

    Challenges where no approaches are ruled out:
    Build A: 3/4 success - costs 0.33 Resolve to succeed on average - character resolve of 2 "buys" 6 successes
    Build B: 2/4 success - costs 1 Resolve to succeed on average - character resolve of 5 "buys" 5 successes
    Build C: 1/4 success - costs 3 Resolve to succeed on average - character resolve of 7 "buys" 2.33 successes

    Challenges where one approach is ruled out:
    Build A:
    3/4 chance it's one of yours - 2/4 success
    1/4 chance it's not - 3/4 success
    In all: 9/16 success - cost 0.77 - "buys" 2.57 successes
    Build B:
    2/4 chance it's one of yours - 1/4 success
    2/4 chance it's not - 2/4 success
    In all: 6/16 success - cost 1.66 - "buys" 3 successes
    Build C:
    1/4 chance it's one of yours - 0/4 success
    3/4 chance it's not - 1/4 success
    In all: 3/16 success - cost 4.33 - "buys" 1.84 successes

    If you average the cost for the two types of challenge (since about half the challenges rule out an approach, and half don't), I believe you end up with this:
    Build A: Average cost 0.55, Resolve 2 "buys" 3.63 successes
    Build B: Average cost 1.33, Resolve 5 "buys" 3.75 successes
    Build C: Average cost 3.66, Resolve 8 "buys" 2.18 successes

    So it seems like A and B are fairly balanced, and C is teh suck.

    \n I've calculated the "cost" to succeed like this:

    If you've got 3/4 chance of success, you've got 1/4 chance of failure. If you hit that 1/4, it costs you 1 Resolve, and you've got another 3/4 chance of success and 1/4 of failure. Etc.

    So you've got 1/4 chance of losing 1 resolve, and another 1/16 of losing 1 more, and another 1/64 of losing 1 more, etc etc. You lose the sum of (1/4)^1 + (1/4)^2 + (1/4)^3 + (1/4)^4... etc etc. Which adds up to 0.33.

    If you've got 2/4 chance of success, your cost to succeed is 2/4 + 4/16 + 8/64 + 16/256 etc etc etc. Which is 1.

    And so on.

    \n I did an analysis like Matthijs' and got slightly different figures. Mine say that basically you have a good balance between three approaches giving lots of success, but low endurance, and one approach giving low successes, but longer endurance.

    \nWhich is what you were after, no? I think that's a fine balance. In fact you chose the level of pool and costs that seem to balance them pretty darn closely, without fiddling a lot with it. That is, with the model you have, modifying up or down on either pool total, or approach cost throws the balance far, far off from what you have.
    \n
    \nNice intuitive work there.
    \n
    \nThe one concern I have - which might be assuaged if I knew the context better - is that 2 to 3 successes on average before the end of the adventure seems like very few. That is, you're going to have very short numbers of contests that you can throw at a player, and they're going to either not make it all the way, or win very few of them. That is, either you whiff, or you fail the mission entirely.
    \n
    \nSounds a tad "deprotagonizing." But, again, I don't know the context. Might be perfect for what you want.
    \n
    \nIf, however, you had some sort of redraw mechanic that did not require the use of Resolve, this would fix a lot of problems (escalate to one level higher to redraw?) That is, the odds of victory over time would go way up, and their lifespan would increase at the same time. Might require rebalancing the points to ensure that an optimal strategy wouldn't emerge from the three options given, sure. But it might be worth looking into.
    \n
    \nOr maybe something to do with suit color matching? If you draw a card of the same color as your approach, but not your approach, draw again? That sort of thing. Probably lots of ways you could do it.

    \nKeep this in mind... the contests that limit you to not using one approach limit the Three Approach character much more severely than the one approach character. Three times as much. So having that around means that the actual average over time of success is only 66% or so. The most talented character still loses one third of the time. And the least talented (one approach) loses approaching 80% of the time. You don't need something to mitigate that?

    \n I pared it way back - the complexity is not appropriate for my application, although it can be added as an option, I suppose. Anyway:\n
    Draw five cards and keep them face down. This is your Resolve pool \u2013 you may gain or lose more as the game goes on.

    When you reach a challenge, reveal one card from the deck.

    If it is red, you succeed. If it is black, you fail.

    If you draw a black card and want to succeed, you may turn over a card from your Resolve pool. If it is red, you succeed instead of fail, and both cards are discarded. If it is black, you still fail. You may use as many Resolve cards as you wish.

    If you run out of Resolve cards and then fail a challenge, you die.
    \nThis is probably anticlimactic to those of you who put some thought into this for me, and your efforts are not unappreciated.
