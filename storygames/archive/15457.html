\n So I have been thinking about player count and wanted to discuss it a bit. Board games all stress player counts in their design, you even see mass market publishers putting this information on the front of their boxes. so it must be a pretty important right.

Most games with a Players & GM structure are at their core 2 player games. the GM plays one side and the players side is a team effort.

What do you think are the effects of raising or lowering the number of player's in games like this?

The questions get a little harder with the more complicated player structures in modern rpgs. What are some games you feel work best with a particular number of players?

What is a good number of player's for fiasco? why?

How do you feel about games like GxB that have built in player counts and do not work with more or less?

    \n I did that analysis with The Trouble with Rose. Three turned out to be the effective minimum due to the audience voting mechanic. With 3-5 players you get five scenes each and that works out to two to three hours with a prepared play set. With 6-7 players, I drop that to 4 scenes each. Part of that was for game duration, but also because the use of dominoes is a natural constraint on the maximum number of total scenes (28 max.)

    While you could go with 8 players with three scenes each, I don't think that would work. The story would be all over the place and no one would have enough spotlight time to develop their character and affect the plot. With that many, I'd rather they break into two groups of four players.

    \n If "player" represents more than one person whose characters share the same goal, increasing from one to two or more "players" sounds really interesting. You get some kind of competitive teams thing happening. You probably need much more elaborate ways to adjudicate.

    The sweet spot for Fiasco is four players, which results in 16 scenes, which, on average, resolve themselves in under three hours. Four scenes per player means that everyone has a chance to found, build, complicate and conclude a simple narrative arc for their dude. Four players means that you start the game with one dude completely unconnected to your own and, ideally, a pair of Needs on the table. Because number of scenes rises and falls in tandem to number of players, three and five are practical upper and lower bounds.

    Games with fixed or constrained player counts are more brittle and less usable, so consider the tradeoffs carefully. In board games my favorite counter-example is Castle Panic, which scales effortlessly, on the fly, from one to six players, and is limited only by the number of cards they give you.

    \n My experience has been that pretty much every story game (with a few exceptions) works well for a certain number of players, and falls apart when you stray too far from that.

    Sometimes it's for basic, mechanical reasons (for example, Dogs in the Vineyard is really hard to run for a large group if the Dogs are hard-nosed about sticking together and don't disagree on issues, because of the way the dice work, or maybe you just don't have enough dice for a large group!), sometimes it's for more human reasons (e.g. for this game, you need one sheet of paper per player and five relationships, so having more than four players means you run out of table space and can't remember who all the characters).

    So I say, "Yes!"

    I always try to put a "player count" on games I design.

    \n One of the things I found particularly interesting about D&D 4E was that they lowered the sweet spot for player count considerably.

    With old campaigns from the 70s and 80s, you constantly hear about groups of 8 or more people playing. Reading through some of the early modules, it seems pretty clear to me that they were written for pretty big groups. I've seen occasions in modules where a fight is supposed to start with 8 enemies, and 6 or 8 more are supposed to show up in a round or two from adjoining rooms. And 3 or 4 of those are leveled up lieutenants and such. So it would take a pretty big group to have a good chance of success there.

    With 3rd edition, it seemed like the sweet spot was about 5 or 6 players. Most of the modules seemed to be written with that sort of party size assumed.

    With 4th edition, the game is very specifically written for a 4 player party, with one each of the standard "roles" covered. If the party varies from that, you adjust the encounters accordingly. But the default is to write them all for that balanced 4 player party.

    I found that interesting, and I think it's one of the many examples of ways the game has been cleaned up to make it easier for new people to start playing it.

    So the giant elephant game is certainly right there with you in terms of thinking that identifying a sweet spot of player count is a useful and important thing to do.

    \n D&D 4e presumes a default party size of 5. There are definitely the 4 roles, but all the published adventures and design guidelines presume 5 players (with the party typically doubling up on Defenders or Strikers) and a DM. It scales to 4 or 6 pretty reasonably (and published adventures typically feature advice about how to do so), but breaks pretty quickly outside that range.

    \n I don't mind fixed player counts. OTOH I usually play in drop-in one-shots and similar, so I start by counting the participants and then pitch a game suitable for that number.

    If there's 2 of us
    Ouch, tricky. Would you like to play a board game instead? No?
    * Breaking the Ice
    * Perfect

    If there's 3 of us
    Let's play!
    * While the World Ends in GM:ed mode
    * Polaris with the one moon hack.

    If there's 4 of us
    Ah, a good number, how about these games?
    * While the World Ends in GM:less mode
    * The Phenomenon / Polaris
    * Montsegur 1244
    * Panty Explosion Perfect

    If there's 5 of us
    A solid group, look at all these games!
    * The Daughters of Verona
    * A Taste for Murder
    * Montsegur 1244
    * Zombie Cinema
    * Witch Quest
    * Love in the Time of Seid
    * Panty Explosion Perfect

    If there's 6 of us
    You brought four friends? No problem, I have games we can play.
    * Montsegur 1244
    * Zombie Cinema
    * Panty Explosion Perfect

    Posted By: Colin_FredericksThis discussion makes me want to write a game that works better the more people you have in it. I don't know how yet, but I still want to.
    \nWorld course/fest larps are about the only format I can imagine that actually improves the more people you have in it, up to the point of pretty rediculous numbers. There are a few in Europe that get up to about 5000 participants.

    The biggest ones all focus on organizing around factions and having those factions pitched against each other, allowing individual plot to arise organically as you interact with the big picture.

    In a lot of ways, they are designed like a 9 player parlor larp with prescripted characters. Except that each of those 9 characters is actually a faction with, say, 100-500 people in it. (Yes, they do a lot more than just whip out 9 faction writeups, but that is a big part of the core.)

    But I'm guessing you aren't actually thinking about that kind of scale.

    The way a game works and how a player interacts with it and what expectations they can reasonably have about it changes MASSIVELY as you scale up from a small intimate tabletop game with 2-3 people to a game with 50 participants, to a game with 500+ participants. There are benchmark points where what works from one scale to the next changes dramatically.

    I have an essay on simultaneous action in larp that touches on this, if any are interested in the subject.
    http://www.mortalisrpg.com/forum/downloads/2010SummitBook.pdf
