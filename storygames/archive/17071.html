\n I've got a group of friends that are interested in running a DW campaign centered around being a group of vampire hunters. I thought an interesting way to start was to use the "love letters" idea from AW, one centered around each possible class (including the new Druid from the Kickstarter campaign), and I could really use some help.

I looked through the example letters in AW and most of them seem to follow the pattern of "[Situation] [Request for detail from player] [ROLL+STAT] [Choose]"

I've got some basic ideas for each class: the Fighter's been developing a vampire-effective fight style, the Wizard's been experimenting with vampire blood, the Thief managed to pull off (or be scammed in) a big heist with the MacGuffin being something vampire-related, etc.

It's the actual composition on the letters that's throwing me. I'm trying to keep in mind John Harper's advice (http://mightyatom.blogspot.com/2010/10/apocalypse-world-crossing-line.html) and not cross the line with regards to world-building, but I could definitely use some help/advice.

Following the pattern, I'm thinking they'd look something like:

Dear Fighter,

We hear you've been working on a new fighting style that's particularly effective against vamps and you've got a couple of followers interested in learning more. Tell us a little about it.

Then, roll+WIS: On a 10+ choose 2, on a 7-9 choose 1:

* Your style's weakness isn't currently known by one of the nearby clans.
* Your students are completely loyal.
* The child of one of the major vamps your technique took out isn't currently seeking revenge.

Xoxo,
Your GM

The idea is to have lots of vampire connected hooks from the beginning, even if they won't immediately make themselves known.

My question is: would having 4-5 characters with this much background from the start be the antithesis of AW/DW's "play to find out"?

Also - any ideas or help coming up with good hooks would be greatly appreciated!

Thanks.

    \n What if the assumption is that all the players know each other already and a vampire has claimed one of their mutual companions. In the letters, you get them to flesh out details about and their relationship with this NPC companion. That way you're group building [only] an NPC while soliciting group buy-in to hunting down the vampire that killed/abducted/turned him/her. You're also setting them up for fleshing out their bonds with each other and building the world out from around this central conflict.

    \n My advice is to save the love-letters for downtime between sessions/adventures.

    There so much going on in the first session/adventure (mechanically and fictionally) that adding to that is a bad idea. When they're creating their characters, tell them that they'll be fighting vamps.

    Do the situation/world building during that first session, not beforehand. It's too much, and you'll find that writing them comes really easily when you have concrete things to tie them to in the setting.

    \n You lost somebody precious to you to the vampires, once. When did you see them last, as an undead terror of the night? Did they escape you, or did you escape them?

    You believe vampires can be cured! You hope it, anyway. What have you tried so far that hasn't worked? What do you hope to try the next time you have an opportunity?

    An elder vampire haunts your dreams, calling to you. In what way do you resemble their love lost so many centuries ago?

    Name three groups in this city that you believe under vampiric control, and why you suspect this.

    You and a master vampire have been hunting eachother, cat and mouse, although who is cat and who is mouse is prone to change. Who currently has the advantage, and why is that in danger of shifting?

    You and a master vampire have been hunting eachother, employing any means necessary to try to strike down the other. Who was the last innocent hurt by your shadow war?

    You and a master vampire have been hunting eachother, but your battle is a mask for a deeper passion. How did this come about? Which of your teammates suspects the truth?

    \n I dunno, I like the idea of playbook specific cues, especially if the group is all jazzed about the whole Vampire Hunter thang.

    Perhaps you could tack onto the Class Specific choices in chargen and knuckle down how that relates to vampires...
    Fighter, what is it about your signature weapon that allows you to dispatch vampires? How do they go down?
    Cleric, how does your God's petition relate to the hunting of vampires?
    Thief, which one of your poisons works on vampires? How does it effect them?
    Ranger, which of your companion animal instincts relate to vampires?
    Paladin, your Quest involves vampires yes? Tell us how.

    That sort of thing.

    \n Hmmm. I think I'd want toothier questions (yes, yes, pun intended, you caught me) for class-specifics:
