\n In this month's issue of Analog Game Studies, Jason Morningstar reflects on the many iterations of the Night Witches character sheet before it reached its final form.\n
"As tangible links between the social and diagetic worlds, characters sheets are often central to the roleplaying experience. For something so important, it is surprising how little thought sometimes goes into their design."

    \n Yes. The PC Sheet is a two-way psychic osmotic membrane. On the player-facing side it needs to lend itself to discursive projection while suggesting the possible options afforded by the system. On the GM-facing side it needs to lend itself to reductive empiricism while permitting value judgments based on ephemera. Quite a task, and most are very far from perfect.

    \n Though I love well designed sheets, I almost never see them. I usually use the provided sheets for a few months, then create my own that seem to work better for us. That's when we even bother with sheets...quite honestly at least half (if not three quarters) of our games are played on notebook paper (or even 3x5s).

    Maybe part of it is that some want art in their sheets, while some want minimalist, and others want lists/databases, etc. It can also matter a lot what type of playstyles or classes are being used. Storytelling groups may keep journals, while hack n slashers might want ledgers. In D&D what a fighter needs on a sheet is very different from what a magic user needs on a sheet.
