\n I love quality in a game. By that I mean I love it when the designer and publisher (who may be the same person) have gone to great lengths to make the game look as good as possible, and be as easy and interesting to read as possible.

I was talking to a friend (Magnus Jakobsson, the designer of "Until we sink") on a park bench yesterday. I was talking about how I've been so irritated lately, mostly due to the weather. He asked me what I would get irritated about. I said that the thing that pissed me off the most these days - what I get actively angry about - is when people don't make their games live up to their potential. When they don't playtest their own games; when they don't proofread their text; when there hasn't been an editor involved at any point in the process. When all they focus on is their central ideas - be it mechanical balance or interesting techniques - but fail to make those ideas accessible and attractive.

Then I went down to the local gaming store to use a gift voucher I got for my birthday, thinking "now I can pick up something that I normally wouldn't shell out money for - something expensive". I first looked at the Swedish game Noir, which has an excellent GM section (R-maps and Bangs, all in Swedish), but realized I'd never use any other part of the book.

Then I picked up Burning Empires, and I never put it back. I read the intro and the World Burner on the train home and sent an SMS to another friend about how cool the game was.

Why? Because it tells you what the game is about. It tells you what the rules are, how to use them, what their effects are. It tells you everything in order, when you need it. It does so in an easy, structured fashion. It has a little flavor text, and only just enough setting material.

You can tell that someone's been thinking about how to do this right. Someone's been working hard. Many people have been polishing this text and the rules in different ways. This is not a rush job for Gencon; this is not something the author and his friends played four times before publication.

This is what I want my games to be like. Burning Empires sets a standard for me to follow when it comes to rules presentation. It stands in sharp contrast to a lot of games I've bought and played lately. It is, in fact, in a whole different league.

    \n There are sevearl things going on with new games. First are the half baked ideas, trying to gain a foot hold in the market place of ideas. And second there are the games trying to gain a loyal following and maybe make a little money.
    \n
    \nBecause of the ease of PDF and POD half baked ideas can get to the market place. As little as ten years ago this was not the case. Half baked ideas lived in magazine articles and as house or convention games. The cost of entering the game selling field was very high. I remember - I was stumped by it. We've encouraged half baked games to go to press in recent years which has meant a lot of undercooked ideas have made it out of the kitchen to soon. Like premature babies they finish their development out of the womb. But to do that they have to survive.
    \n
    \nGames that are trying to gain a larger following have to be better - prettier - etc. in order to survive. Competition for people's attention is high. There are a lot of skills that most people don't have that they need to compete. Game design and play testing are just the start. Graphic design, editing, production, at least some knowledge of accounting, marketing and sales have to be learned. Without them there is only so far a business can go before some flashier, better capitalized animal comes along and takes their audience away (there goes the food/cash).
    \n
    \nI don't want to be discouraging. I personally like what I've learned. I can't think of what else I would have done with my life that would have been better. The self knowledge alone has made it worthwhile. I now make something that is pretty good looking - the frustration is trying to figure out how to bring it to a bigger audience with the limited resources I have.
    \n
    \nIt is worth while to "do it right". even if it means delays to market. I hope. I've delayed until I knew I could sustain effort and quality. Now the work really picks up. No rest for the wicked.
    \n
    \nChris Engle

    Posted By: Ben LehmanIs this actually true? Are games released today any less playtested than the first Dust Devils or the first Fastlane?
    \nFastlane was conceived in August '03, and published in April '04, a gestation time of 9 months. The third trimester was almost entirely layout and publication. During that time, the following playtests were done:

    1. A single player created a character. This never went anywhere, but was still a good dry-run of character creation.
    2. There was did a single-session playtest in #indierpgs, an IRC channel. This became fodder for the example of play
    3. There was a 2-3 session playtest in #indierpgs, with some overlap with the group in #2.
    4. There was a 7-8 session playtest in real life, with zero overlap with the group in #3 other than me (the GM)
    5. I had another friend of mine, whose experience with rpgs was nil, read the Fastlane text over several times during the editing and revising process. I edited the text until she was able to understand it. Also there were several dry-mechanic runs with the people in groups 2-4 outside of the sessions proper.

    Appendix: A lot of the game was designed in public, so there was an audience reading and responding to a lot of the posts. I miss the Indie RPG Design forum for that very reason; I can't design future games on the Forge the way I was able to do Fastlane, and Fastlane came out very well.

    Anyway. I'm sure there are quite a few games that were designed as quickly as Fastlane was, maybe even some quicker. But speed is not the only indicator of quality, and I don't know how many games designed on that timescale were playtested as much as Fastlane was, with the variety that any game needs (i.e. with multiple distinct, discrete groups).

    Truthfully, Fastlane almost fails my standards as they exist today, as it was not playtested in at least one group that did not include the designer (and back then I tried, but couldn't find anyone willing and gave in to my impatience). But it is a good game, and one that communicates itself well.

    And after this past GenCon, there are quite a few games out there that fail to meet the standards of Fastlane's first edition (which aren't that high). After bringing my stash from GenCon 2006 home and reading through them, I have lost my faith in the indie community to bring rigorously designed and written games to the Forge booth. Before this year, I had faith that the game I sat down to play a demo of would be the game that I purchased. Now I'll be checking books much more closely. Maybe that's only a tragedy to me.
