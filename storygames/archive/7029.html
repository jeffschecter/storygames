\n DexCon 11 will see the release of the Seven Sins Edition of Annalise. Annalise is a game of gothic Vampire stories, told from the perspective of the victims, servants and hunters of the Vampire. This is a game about pain, hunger and redemption. Pain, because the Vampire is only attracted to the vulnerable, those people who have experienced pain, suffering or loss in their lives. Hunger, because the Vampire hungers for that pain. Redemption, because there is always the possibility of redeeming your actions and desires - if you wish it.

I will have seven custom-make, handbound copies of the game available at DexCon. Each book is themed to a sin. These sins are expressions of the vulnerabilities that attract the Vampire to its victims: Shame, Fear, Hunger, Violation, Resentment, Want, and Avarice.

The next edition of the game will be available at Gen Con.

For more info about the game, find Annalise. I'm also happy to talk about it in this thread, of course.

    Posted By: Adam Dray
    Wikipedia, the authority on all such matters*, seems confused about the novel's genre and calls it "Horror novel," "Gothic novel," and "epistolary novel." What's your understanding of it?
    \n
    The gothic period of literature is between 1760 and 1820 and is a subset of the romantic movement. The first gothic novel is considered to be "The Castle of Otronto" and the generally recognized last "true" gothic novel is "Melmoth the Wanderer." Dracula misses this period by half a century. Frankenstein however IS included.

    Dracula is sometimes referred to as a Victorian Neo-Gothic or a work of Victorian Supernaturalism a movement which is the direct descendant of the gothic but never-the-less is not a *true* gothic novel. Today we have our own contemporary neo-gothic movement which includes authors such as Patrick McGrath and Joyce Carol Oates.

    I was pleasantly surprised to discover that GURPS Screampunk actually got this history right. I also highly recommend The Oxford Book of Gothic tales which traces the gothic tradition from its roots to the contemporary. Note: I think some readers will be surprised at how downplayed the supernatural really is in actual gothic works.

    \n So, to me, "gothic" is a codeword for a style of story with a certain kind of imagery, and the people who I think will be interested in the game are people to whom that word means the same thing. If you want to be totally accurate, the game is about vampire fiction from the late 1800s through the mid 20th century, fiction that stars vulnerable people who attract and eventually confront a Vampire, and perhaps address their issues in so doing. The Vampire can turn out to be a metaphor, some other kind of creature (I've played games where the "Vampire" turned out to be a witch, a winter zombie, and a bio/AI conciousness that fed on the electricity generated by the human mind, respectively).

    \n I set up a custom radio station via Pandora. Direct Link. Pandora is super neat, and you should check it out if you don't know about it - basically, user-controlled radio.

    My understanding is that each person listens to their own "version" of the station, starting from the choices I've made for it, but then if you thumb things up or down it'll modify the station you're listening too without affecting the one I've set up. So, like, if you're way more into the late-90s metal stuff, you can modify the station more towards that and away from the quirky female singer rock stuff (or vice versa!)

    It's interesting, because the station is equal parts stuff I listen to while working on the game, and stuff Annalise would listen to. So there's moments where I'm all "this is a shitty song but it totally belongs on the station" or "this is awesome, but not for this station!:
