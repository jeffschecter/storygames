\n OK, this is kind of pie in the sky right now, as I don't have the money to buy anything gaming related anyway, but hey, what's a message board for if not pointless talk?

Anyway, for some reason I'm liking a lot of D&D 4E (even though I've never had a positive experience playing D&D before, it's probably because the game is so honest and up front about what it's about). Some of the rules issues bug me, but they seem to be well handled for the most part in the errata. The art looks ugly to me, but I never buy a gaming book for the art anyway. The one thing that is a real problem though is the use of minis and grids. It seems like such a huge investment of time and money to set up and get running, not to mention requiring a heck of a lot of table space.

So, since taking this out of the game seems like a much more major hack than I'd be willing to do, how does one play with minis without it being a huge chore and money sink, where you're playing on a dining room table that is more normal in size, instead of some huge thing?

    \n 1) Do you like minis in and of themself?
    Yes?
    E-Bay is your friend. Also recognize that character-players can be co-opted to buy some of the minis as well. In effect, this is much like asking players in a number of indie-rpgs to provide setting or situation info. The types and numbers of minis bought will come to define the types of critters encountered.

    Also, don't stick to just official minis for the RPG. Heroscape and Mage Knight minis are available cheap ( especially used), there tend to be tons of old metal minis folks dump off as they exit the hobby ( many of which will already be painted), any number of cheap toys work as monsters ( hey, this method gave us the roper, the rust monster, and the displacer beast back-in-the-day), and a company called Caesar Miniatures produces lines of fantasy figures in 1/72 scale ( about 40-50 minis for $9).

    No?
    Go 2-d. There are earlier threads here about using a button punch and pics of critter and heroes to make somevery cool discs that fit on the grids.

    Or go with paper stand-ups. There are tons of these out there, cheap or free. Drivethrurpg has ones for sale, and a Googlesearch should bring up plenty of free, downloadable ones.

    As for space, D&D 4e seems to work on a by-encounter basis, with a combat still being fairly lengthy. I'd only use minis for a clear bash-up, and use skill challenges for avoidable or minor fights.

    \n I hit an art store (Michael's, I don't know if they exist in the US) and bought a bunch of 1" and .75" and 1.5" wooden discs. I then picked up some different-color magic markers, so I have red 1-20, blue 1-20, green 1-20 of each size. I also did green A-Z, Blue A-Z, red A-Z for the 1", and used cyan and orange for large, something else for small. The smartest thing I did was "paint" the edges of the discs with the marker, so they're easy to spot. It was maybe $50 all told.

    \n Other ideas: you know those travel metal chessboards that fold up, with little magnets for chess pieces?
    There you are: palm sized encounter map complete with 32 magnetic tokens. Just check if you can find a sharpie that you can erase before drawing in walls and whatnot

    If you are not using minis but tokens, the size needs not be in the 1inch scale: just use smaller tokens, and your table space will be easily reclaimed. This way you can use a squared dry erase board (the kind you write todo lists and shopping lists on) without redrawing the squares. Just use small tokens.

Adjust the size as necessary to fit your tokens, of course.

    I'm only half-joking. You've given me a bit of a lead in to something that I think gets badly overlooked about minis use.

    Minis are a source of inspiration.

    Have you folks ( especially zenten) ever use the Everway Cards? Not the Tarot-like resolution ones, but the big deck, the one with all the re-used fantasy art and the 3 questions on the back? Anything similar?

    Minis work like that, too. You can use them as an idea generator, especially when you have build-it rather than random rolled character creation.

    See, using thought out minis rather than any old bit of gamepiece is a bit like taking the step up from having Melf the Elf. It's kinda like tripping down the path from funny voices to characteristic gestures to going to full blown personalities for your character.

    It's by no means required to play an rpg, but it adds a lot in it's own way when you jump in with both feet.

    To get back to zenten's original post:
    If you literally want to play with minis with a minimum of fuss'n'muss, there's a method to the madness of building up the stuff that goes with it. It does cost money, butthere is a way to mitigate, those costs by spreading purchases over time and across individuals.

    I wrote a series of two or three essays about just that subject and they were up at Levi Kornelson's old GameCraft site. If you contact him, maybe he can get you an archived copy.
