    \n Grab yourself a copy of PagePlus (low-cost route) or InDesign (high-cost route) and start practising.

    I won't be as harsh as Ben, but it is definitely a skillset - both learning the software and developing the "eye". If you need something that looks good in a short amount of time you will need to bring in help. If you can take a while and would rather have the experience of doing it yourself and are OK if your first thing only comes out average-looking in the end, do that. It can be fun (in a hard work sort of way).

    But be honest - if you're not wired up for this sort of thing, better to leave it to others. Do you understand that presentation is important in documents? (Some people don't.) If you look at books whose layout you like more or less, can you start to work out why?

    \n I agree with Ben and Tim, with the caveat that your expectations for the book make a big difference. If you want this to be a beautiful, well-designed book that will sell, you should probably rely on a designer's expertise. If you simply want to publish your own book for fun and as a learning exercise, by all means do some research and dig into it yourself. It's not terribly difficult to lay out and publish your own book, but it is a very different thing to do it well.

    Also, you asked us to assume the text has been done, but I would stress that having someone else (i.e. someone who is not the writer) edit the content is essential to producing a decent publication of any sort.

    \n Keith: Use Scribus, Open Office, or Kile (if you find yourself adept with mark-up like HTML) and concentrate on basic presentation through your writing, headings, and side bars. Do not expect to be a designer--rather, just try to make some existing template (even if "Normal" or "Default" or whatever) work for your content. Then iterate: if you use Styles (as you SHOULD!) then you can play around with global type and paragraph tweaks; try different simple graphics in your page templates; mess around with generated content (table of contents, subTOCs, indices, glossary, table of tables/figures, etc, etc).

    You will not become a "good designer." You *might* learn a few Best Practices for doing desktop publishing for print. It will take you (probably) ten to twenty times as long to achieve a given "look" as it would a pro (regardless of tools); and so you have to ask yourself if your time is worth 5% of a professional's. (Hint: I charge total strangers $60 - $75 an hour to do layout; so do you earn less than $3 - $4 an hour? If so, you are ahead of the game. If not, then you are violating the Givens Principle (I am assuming you don't want to get into book design as a recreation or for relaxation). You're better off going to work for those hours you would try to layout your book, and then hiring someone who can achieve your vision in a fraction of the time it would take you.

    Just being honest, man: I don't try to rebuild my motorcycle engine because I don't earn three times what a mechanic earns, and it seems to take me around 3x the book hours for major jobs, PLUS I don't often have specialty tools that make it faster to do a given job. See the parallels to trying to do "pro layout" with no experience, no application(s), and a limited budget?

    \n Read every book you can find about page layout and typography, especially the ones written in the 50s and 60s.

    Get a copy of Robert Bringhurst's "The Elements of Typographic Style." Digest it.

    Post examples of your attempts and let folks like me, John Harper, and Daniel Solis rip them apart and tell you how to fix them.

    Hang out at typography and design forums.

    Learn the difference between style and craftsmanship and where the two overlap.

    \n - What would the down-sides be to using an educational copy of which doesn't allow for commercial use (would anyone care)?

    As for breaking the law, you might as well be using a pirated copy. Typically the government relies on whistleblowers to catch commercial piracy. However, Adobe or whoever may have a tiny code implanted in any design you make and they might privately track you. As a small RPG developer, this is unlikely since you don't have any money and a lawsuit would be expensive and yield no profits.

    Basically, if you don't have a commercial copy, you're taking a risk. It seems to me that it would be cheaper, more efficient and produce better results if you just hired an RPG layout guru.

    \n Keith,

    Layout is a strange one, because both the following are true:\n
    • It is easy, and you can do it yourself, in the sense that anyone can whack the text on a page and it will be readable.
      • \n
    • It is difficult, and you need an expert, in the sense that, to make it look professional, you need someone who does it professionally. Trained layout people will make your text look much, much better than you can.
    \nSo there are different routes you can take. For the first, you don't need to learn anything. Just write your game in Word, make it look vaguely nice and upload the result to Lulu. Then, your game will be published, and people can buy it. Don't knock this route. It gets your game out there.

    For the second route, you learn just enough to lay out your game in a barely adequate fashion. Get a copy of Scribus or Serif Page Plus. Read some online tutorials. Find a book you like, which is simply laid out, and emulate it. Keep putting your layout away for a few days and looking back at it, changing the things that bother you. This is a very rewarding thing to do and you'll learn a lot. You'll end up with something you'll be proud of, although some people will spot it's not a professional layout.

    For the third route, you work with a designer. Find someone you like and drop them a line. People are friendly and will talk you through various options.

    As a personal opinion, I think we put too many barriers in the way of people who want to publish RPGs. We tell people to hire layout artists, editors, proofreaders and artists. But you can do most of this yourself: not as well, but adequately. For your first RPG, doing it yourself is a good option, since you probably don't want to spend too much money. Once you've done it yourself, you'll see exactly how much better professional layouts look, and you'll consider hiring a professional next time.

    Graham

    Photoshop, unfortunately, is of pretty limited use in designing a book. Some of the tools are present in InDesign, but I bet they're not the tools you're used to using.

    \n

    The educational version is a different version of the license, not the software. If you're not in the US, you'll have to figure out what that means according to the license Adobe uses in your country.

    \n

    Here's the biggest piece of advice I can give, though:

    \n

    Start small and simple.

    \n

    Lay out a couple of pages, go to professionals who know how to give critique, then refine. Then do a chapter. Then do a couple of chapters. That way, you don't find yourself with an enormous project all done wrongly.

    \n

    The Game Design Studio is a forum I open more or less monthly for people to give each other critique. The rules are strict and so far it's been very, very profitable to everyone who's participated. We talk about marketing, typesetting, illustration and cover design, and sometimes rules.
