\n [Long winded post, sorry]
So, I've got a weird system on my hands. It is highly probably I created something way too bizarre to be functional. The dice mechanic I'm using is as follows:
Players roll 2d6. 1d6 is for their Presence, the other d6 is for the Power they apply to the situation. The outcome schematic looks as follows:
Presence roll:
1-2 = Manifest your presence. All eyes on you.
3-4 = Minimize your presence. There is evidence of your tampering.
5-6 = Mask your presence.

Power roll:
1-2 = Your power is not felt/observed.
3-4 = Your power is noted; it's effects fall short of your expectations.
5-6 = Your power overwhelms and is acknowledged by all.

The idea is, truly stealthy actions require Finesse: very minimal presence and precise application of just the right amount of power. i.e. 5-6 on Presence and 1-2 on Power.
Similarly, truly overt actions are more concerned with presenting Force: Very high power with a maximized presence to cow the opposition into submission. i.e. 5-6 on Power and 1-2 on Presence.
Given this outcome scheme, I'm wondering if I can bypass the entire success/failure paradigm and simply stick to the narrative imposed by the dice. Since each die gives you a narrative "look" for how the action proceeds, I've found it is possible to know exactly what aspect of the task the character was able/unable to accomplish.

Consider the following example:
James wants to bluster and cajole the customs agent to convince them not to search his vessel full of stolen goods. This is an application of power over subtlety. Thus, to truly succeed, he wants to verbally demonstrate that he is a force to reckon with (high power) and also use his body language to display arrogant impatience and practiced annoyance (high presence).
James rolls a 5 for Power and a 5 for Presence. Now, his words have the cutting edge and technical jargon needed to cow the customs agent on an intellectual level. But his presence is lacking. He doesn't display the body language of physical dominance he'd like to; masked presence: the gesticulation he employed didn't convey the raw physicality he would have liked or the practiced manner of someone bored of dealing with bureaucratic functionaries.

See, this is not necessarily success or failure in the standard sense of: his action went through or it did not. To some extent, it was successful, he knew all the right jargon and phrases necessary to cajole and fluster the customs agent. At the same time, his outward appearance is much calmer and more reserved than what the customs agent would expect from a person of the station he's pretending to be. I'd call this a neutral result. The agent will stall for time, but no real resolution has been achieved, the scene carries on.

At any rate, all this rambling is to say, do we really need success and failure as the end all be all bench-mark? I know other systems have expanded this definition to include partial failures/successes. But I'm wondering if the whole system can be circumvented to only have: "This is your new narrative, act accordingly." I think the success/failure paradigm is implicit in that kind of outcome method. After all, if you didn't roll up the narrative outcome you were looking for it is a failure of sorts. Still at that point, am I not just trying to call a spade a shovel? I mean, sure they are very similar in function, but one of em looks different. From a practical application point of view, they both handle the same activity (more or less), so why try and get all fancy when you don't need to. Honestly, by any definition, the above example is very rightly categorized as a partial success or a partial failure. Not so extreme in either direction to end the scene, but certainly enough to add further complication to the issue at hand. I just don't know. Any thoughts on if a dice mechanic like this could work? I'm envisioning something that only directs the flow of narrative, but doesn't make any hard value statements of success or failure. Of course, without the possibility of failure, who gives a damn. Sigh.

    \n I think that what you're discussing here is a task resolution system with organic conflict resolution embedded in the narration in the form of oracular automatic writing. It is possible and functional, and has a proud history in roleplaying. Understanding how it works is central to understanding how a traditional roleplaying game works, I'd say.

    You're still resolving success, it's just that you're pushing the resolution up from the dice and into the hands of whoever narrates the outcomes of action. This method is one of the staples of railroading play ("you fail your climb check, but manage to grab the ledge before you fall"), but it can be used for different purposes as well. If you provide the narrator with some guidelines for how to resolve things, they can do the conflict resolution for you.

    A simpler example of a similar set-up, without the oracular element: when a character tries to do something, you roll [skill]+d6 against a difficulty number determined by how difficult the action is. This mandates whether the character succeeds in a given action. However, in addition to this the GM has a secret plot track that tells him whether the party is currently in a "down" or "up" phase of the story. If they are in a "down" phase, successes rolled for characters in task resolution ultimately come to impact the situation only in minor ways as new complications occur or the chosen concrete actions prove unnecessary or even harmful to their cause. In an "up" phase this is reversed; failed actions fail in ways that show new opportunities, or there's time to retry them.

    The key to understanding here is that task resolution (does the character succeed in doing a thing they are trying to do) and conflict resolution (does the character get the outcome they wanted) are independent factors of design. Conflating them has traditionally caused much confusion, because pretending that conflict resolution does not exist means that the impact of successful or failed action is up for grabs - whoever gets to contextualize the successful action gets to decide whether that action ultimately matters at all.

    Thus, my answer to your question: it is possible to resolve tasks and conflicts in various ways, not all of them involving dice. I am less certain as to whether it is possible to "skip" either entirely if the game purports to produce intelligible narratives about people doing things. Somebody at some point decides whether the climb check succeeds, and somebody at some point decides whether that success or failure matters.

    The system you describe avoids absolute determination of success, but somebody is still deciding whether the outcome was appropriate to the situation. That person is effectively using the dice as an oracular aid in composing their narrative. It is a very different feel for a game, so I'm not trying to say that it's trivial - but it is still resolving success.

    To further illustrate the point, consider a system where we draw a Tarot card every time a character tries something, and the GM then interprets the card to narrate how successful the character was. You're doing the same thing here, except you're using a couple of tables and dice instead of a card, and your oracle is more focused and less symbolic in nature.

    \n Fiasco's scene rules also don't care about your success or failure at a task, just how the scene winds up benefiting you or harming you. You can easily have situations where your success leads to worse results than failure. Like, if you try to kill the town mayor, a black die might mean that you succeed at your task, but now you're a wanted criminal who has to dispose of a body. Or perhaps a white die means that you fail because the mayor isn't there, but you uncover stacks of bearer bonds that you can steal. The system doesn't care if you succeed at your task or not, only whether or not the scene works out well for you. (Usually, these wind up working in parallel, but not always.)

    \n Houses of the Blooded/Blood and Honor/World of Dew (All basically the same game) essentially forego having a success resolution method (with the semi-exception of combat). It plays like a "standard" game - gather dice, maybe set some aside, roll, try to beat a target number, but then it pulls a switcheroo on you - you're not rolling to determine if you succeed or fail, you're rolling to determine whether you or the GM get to narrate (and how much). So you can succeed at the roll and narrate your failure, or you can fail at the roll and the GM might still narrate your success.

    \n@Luzelli, here's how I'm reading your idea:

    "Instead of letting the dice tell us directly whether our efforts in a certain situation succeed, can we have the dice instead tell us how our efforts manifest, leaving us to then judge whether that manifestation would be successful?"

    I think the answer is "yes, you can". I am not clear on exactly why you want to do this, but I can imagine plenty of good reasons, so I'll move on to your rules idea.

    The important bits I see are these:

    - Success isn't binary yes/no. The range of outcomes is as broad as interpretations of the fiction. As is, a lot of responsibility lies with the interpreter, and narrating the outcomes of middling rolls could feel like GM fiat. With stronger guidelines about how to interpret rolls, the fiat feeling would diminish, but the feeling of "regular old roll for success, just with degrees" would increase.

    - The outcome-narrator has two axes of performance by which to compare what the character did to both their goal and the situation. Nice! This should provide inspiration and prevent "drawing a blank".

    - Whatever happens is a result of character performance, not opponent performance or other in-fiction factors. "Roll to see how well you perform" is not new, but it is distinct from other types of success rolls. The specificity of the two-axis roll also means that we know something about your performance, even if you never roleplay it out.

    Is this what you're going for? If so, then well done! if not, then what are you going for?\n
    I'm wondering if the whole system can be circumvented to only have: "This is your new narrative, act accordingly."
    If that's what you want, then your trigger for rolling needs to be something other than "does this character achieve what they're attempting right now". Roll every time a player speaks, or at the beginnings of scenes, or every five minutes, or when play slows down, or something. And then, when a character tries to do something important that might or might not work, you'll need to deal with that in some way that doesn't highlight "so does it work?" above the rest of play. Do you actually want that?

    For the sake of people following this thread, I decided to focus the results by defining a few variables controlled by both Power and Presence.
    Presence determines your:

    Decibel of Action (Loudness and Raw Power), Verbal Quality (Subtle Language vs. Domineering Language), and Accuracy.

    Presence:
    1-2 = High Visibility, Easily Reproducible, Unfocused, Overt Body Language.
    3-4 = Low Visibility, Difficult to Reproduce, Distracted, Notable Body Language.
    5-6 = Minimal Visibility, Cannot Reproduce, High Focus, Subtle Body Language.

    Power:
    1-2 = Low Decibel, Subtle Language, High Accuracy.
    3-4 = Medium Decibel, Hesitating/Halting Language, Variable Accuracy.
    5-6 = High Decibel, Impressive/Domineering Language, Low Accuracy.

    Two thoughts occur to me:

    1) An unpublished system called PIE (Player Interpretation of Expectation) rolled 3 dice. The total dictated success or failure (which you could easily replace with a spectrum), the white die reflected the player character's performance, the black die reflected the opponent's performance, and the red die reflected the environment's contribution. I think narration was left up to the rolling player, but I can also see how it could be split up (player white, GM black, anyone else red).

    2) Break situations into a few types, so not every roll has to include covering every base. Five different rolls with three dice each is probably better than one type of roll with 15 dice (well, not that there wouldn't be overlap, but you get my point).

    Brainstorm:

    Seduction/Manipulation dice: transparency, charm, impression of value
    Intimidation dice: power, urgency, savagery
    Assessment dice: senses, intuition, reason
    Combat dice: power, speed, training
    Stealth dice: agility, timing, notice

    I feel like I'd know what to do with high and low values on those.

    Edited to add: Not that Presence and Power aren't cool! I do like those. But if you wind up wanting roll outputs beyond Visibility, Focus/Concentration, Reproducibility of Action, Body Language, Decibel of Action, Verbal Quality, and Accuracy (or if you don't want to tie them to each other -- e.g. if you want "Highly Visible + Highly Focused" to be possible), these might help.

    \nI feel like by using "overcoming a Danger" as a trigger point, you're already contextualizing the roll as a roll for success/failure. Notice how your description above still relies on success/failure to frame a roll--if failure isn't meaningful, there's no roll. What if you completely disregarded success/failure altogether for your trigger? What sorts of situations emerge then?

    For example, maybe the trigger is "When you prepare to draw your power into the world, roll the dice". The player can then trigger it when appropriate. You'd also probably have to have some way to adjucate what happens in situations that don't use the character's power. Ultimately, that seems like a sort of game that's all about watching a more-than-human character in a world of cardboard.

    So, this veers from your design a bit, but here's a rough draft of what that could look like.

    Steps of Immortals
    You are superhuman beings, immortals infused with great power.

    Character Creation: choose your True Name, the Mortal Name you go by, your Domain (how your power manifests, it can be concrete or abstract, such as "Dreams", "Love", "Animals", or "Technology"), and finally the Immortal Question: "Why do you not claimed the mortal world as your possession?"

    Mechanics: for the time being, I'll skim over most of the gameplay bits, they could use multiple models. But the important part is when the mechanics trigger.

    When you bring your power to bear on the mortal world, roll two dice and assign them to Power and Presence. You are bound by the results; tell everyone else what happens as a consequence.

    Presence:
    1-2 = Manifest your presence. All eyes on you.
    3-4 = Minimize your presence. There is evidence of your tampering.
    5-6 = Mask your presence.

    Power:
    1-2 = Your power is not felt/observed.
    3-4 = Your power is noted; it's effects fall short of your expectations.
    5-6 = Your power overwhelms and is acknowledged by all.

    \n There are lots of ways to structure failure and success in games.

    Here's one I've been interested in for a while, but haven't baked into any particular game yet:

    * When you and I enter a conflict, we each name a possible fate.

    E.g.: "One of us will lose an eye in this fight."

    Or: "One of us will leave with the Golden Idol." "One of us will be humiliated in front all onlookers."

    * Then we bid points or roll dice, and the winner gets to pick their fate first.

    (If there are more than two parties involved, we go in order or highest bid/best roll/whatever.)

    This has some interesting features as a design paradigm, as well.

    @Luzelli: Well, there's another option. If you want to mess with the success/failure paradigm, maybe the game presumes that success/failure happens when the players want it to.     \nThe task resolution in UED (English edition coming soon) posits the options as "succeed with/without cost", noting that sometimes the cost will be so high you might as well have failed.

    An example given in the text has the PC falling off a moving truck, taking a level of damage to his environment suit. The text makes point of the idea that the facts of this cost (a level of damage) should be the same regardless of what's happening in the fiction, leaving the broader outcomes to flow from the existing circumstances (for the PC in the example, it's a relatively minor inconvenience; if his suit had already been at a critical damage level, he'd be dead).

    So success or failure then comes down to the players' evaluation of the possible costs, not the die roll. I should note that managing dwindling resources is a key concern in UED.

    Now, this is arguably just a reframing of pass/fail + fail forward, but I think it's still an interesting recasting of what the task roll means to the players.

    \n Ok, I'll try to make this short.
    I've worked up a second version that splits the difference between rolling for narrative control and rolling for success.
    Players roll their die. If they hit the required outcome, they get to use 4 of the 7 variables to dictate either A) the Success/Failure of the action or Any complication that may ensue.
    The GM then uses the remaining 3 variables to describe success/failure or complication, whichever one the Player chose not to assert narrative control over.

    If they don't hit the required outcome, the GM gets to use 4 of the 7 variables and gets first choice at narrating either Success/Failure or Complications that may ensue. The players use the remaining 3 variables to describe whatever part of that axis was left out.

    Finally, every two increments on the die modify these variables. So a 1-2 on Presence would say Maximum Visibility (everyone can see you) while a 3-4 would say Low visibility (A couple people might spot you). Depending on the numbers rolled, the players would pick from variables that have this high, medium, low qualifier and use them to describe either success/failure or complication.
