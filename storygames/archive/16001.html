\n I have the chance to start a fantasy game with all new players (to me), one of who has only played D&D once. One of them is an experienced D&D & Call of Cthulhu player, the other two are both experienced video game players (one plays EVE, the other plays Skyrim)... and here is the best part, every single one of us is an artist. 3 of us are professionals. I am always the only artist. This is so cool.

So at our working Thursday lunch (we all hang out and bring artwork to work on at a coffee shop), I brought up tabletop RPGing and if anyone was interested. Everyone was. I threw out a couple of non-fantasy pitches knowing that S., the experienced one, liked mysteries , but what it morphed into is wanting a gonzo, floating cities, weird magics, alien cultures, something to inspire us as artists. D&D tropes were to be avoided by table consent (and I didn't even bring it up)... so cliche'd dungeons are out. All three of them were interested in roleplaying more. They WANT characters and interactions with the world. They have not been exposed to "Indie games". Maybe it is Exalted, but without that system. I don't want to run Exalted. That way lies Exhaustion.

So my first instinct is FATE, I already own Legends of Anglerre. I think Aspects are gold to be mined, especially with players who feel straightjacketed by D&D not allowing (easily) for a lot of social conflict stuff or interesting uses of powers and magics or broad applications of stats and skills without handwaving.

But I think LoA is a bit cookie cutter with its magic. Its not evocative enough or weird enough for the vibe I want to get to. And I don't want to do a lot of heavy lifting writing up/hacking magic rules

I think Burning Wheel is too complex for a transition from D&D for new players, too grounded in "reality".

The Grocery List-- what this campaign should have in terms of elements...
* Something faster that 4th Ed D&D. Something mechanically elegant
* Some kind of player narrative control (Fate pts w/ Aspects, Artha in BW etc).
* Powerful player characters
* Cool, weird, evocative magic
* Easy to run
* System lends itself to flavor

So, any system out there y'all would suggest?

I am looking at Other Worlds as a possibility. Gotta peruse the magic section...

    \n You want an adventure game with undefined length? If not, then Polaris, Mountain Witch and similar games are flavourful, elegant and in general do a good job in this regard.

    In the dramatic/narrative adventure game niche my go-to recommendations are Burning Wheel, FATE, TSoY and Heroquest (and Apocalypse World nowadays, too - it belongs in this family). These all except Heroquest share a degree of complexity, but both FATE and TSoY can be shorn down into the Heroquest range if you want that. FATE and Heroquest lean more towards static characters the players predefine carefully and then depict in play, while BW and TSoY are more towards the dramatic end where play is pretty much about character discovery and the impact your choices have on your character. I myself find the methodology of play to be sufficiently similar between these that as long as you're clear on your creative agenda (whether you're going to be challenging or affirming the players' character concepts, pretty much), there is no significant difference in quality between these games: just pick the one with the mechanical aesthetics you like best, and use the common methodical toolset they share to GM well.

    Other Worlds is, I understand, very close to Heroquest. Unless something important has been changed, it belongs in the above category of dramatic adventure games that share a rough toolbox of good GMing practices. I haven't read it, but unless it's very different from its antecedents it should be a fine choice for anybody who grogs its specific dice mechanics etc. and likes the idea of working with them.

    \n Your mention of having a table full of artists reminds me of something I did as GM once, with a table not full of artists.

    I ran a few sessions of play where our concept was City of Heroes + Mystery Men. The team was a bunch of underpowered wimpy self-appointed guardians of a distant bedroom community suburb of Paragon City. Plus, in the fiction, they developed their adventures into a self-published indie comic book that they made themselves.

    So about 3 sessions in, I told them that one of the problems they had to solve for the day was the fact that they hadn't finished the last page or cover of their next installment yet, and needed to do it right now. I put out paper and some art supplies and said "Go". They had a lot of fun making a page and a cover together, talking/arguing in character the whole time.

    With a table full of artists, I think it would be awesome if you would take advantage of those strengths.

    Like maybe one aspect of the customized magic system would be that if they draw a picture of their target, the spell works better.

    Or perhaps, by creating appropriate sketches, the players can generate the equivilant of fan mail points that they can give to other players to help them with their rolls.


    Also...

    Another thing to consider - you don't need to jump right in to a campaign straight off.

    I think it might be a good idea to try out a few different things first. Take a wack at some games that are one-shot or micro-campaign oriented. Things like Mountain Witch. Or Dogs in the Vineyard (which, yes, can be a campaign, but which works exceptionally well for one shots as well).

    Get your feet wet. Try some stuff. See what they like.

    As artists, you're all familiar with the concept that great works have underlying fundamentals - color, shading, perspective, brush techniques, etc. Roleplaying is a similar sort of creative act, but with fundamentals that many of your players are not yet familiar with. Playing various one shot or three shot games can help you build an array of fundamental skills that you can then bring to bear on a larger work - a campaign.

    \n I wuz wondering about Dungeon World.... but I don't own it. Haven't played it. Seems to me from the outside that player's "books" of moves are very much aimed at nostalgia, "dungeon" experiences. Am I wrong? But it seems to me that Dungeon World's design goals is to do D&D simpler and better than D&D itself. It also seems to plug into that 'zero to hero' overall arc for PCs. So, PCs start out relatively competent, but not necessarily a Conan or Grey Mouser or an Elric, Corum, Hawkmoon.

    So I am not sure it can handle starting at larger than life characters from the get-go.

    Barbarians is a fine game. I've never played it, but I've read it. I'm just not sure it is a good fit. But kind of like Jaws, the magic is really aimed at being in the hands of the "bad guys" and magic is subtle and very much Conan Sword and Sorcery vibe. From our discussion the other day, I think magic is going to be in every fibre of the societies the PCs encounter. It is going to be woven through the entire narrative. And it might be controlled by one or all three PCs.

    I realize, I've done something rather stupid in this thread.

    I didn't tell you all one of the major tent poles of my fantasy idea. The Tolkien trope is that magic is ebbing away, the elves are leaving, the day of man has arrived, blah, blah, blah. I'm SOOOO tired of that paradigm. This game is: Magic is at it's height. This is Atlantis, Shangri-La, 12 Kingdoms, where magic is so integrated into life. The players want gonzo. I want to give them gonzo. Flying cities of brass, Universities of Magic sending out and sponsoring expeditions, warrior-monks armed with Staves of Pain that shoot out ruby rays of repulsion. A village of summoned, but then discarded homonculli, elementals and demons trying to make a go at a "normal" life. Magic potions are sold by street vendors and a surprising number of them actually work. Gremlins are on strike because working on the printing presses for the Gnomes is not paying enough and are threatening to unionize. Dimensional Gates are used by the rich and powerful as routine as we fly. Woods that thrive on souls of unwary travellers. A river god who demands tribute from every boat, canoe and barge that travels its domain. It is almost superheroes meets sword and planet. But it is also chaos, things creep out of the spaces from in-between dimensions. Sorcerer Storms touch down and teleport whole villages to somewhere else, enchant things and people at random or teleport something there. Gods are born. Gods die. Fairly often.

    Taking one thing from 4th Ed, I think the Points of Light idea can kind of work here. There are pockets of stability, City-States, maybe 1 or 3 nation-states, where societies can function. But the spaces inbetween? Oh, it is a dangerous world(s) out there. PCs are those folks who are tough, brave, smart or stupid enough to try. I guess it is a little bit like Eberron too, the idea of sorta "modernizing" magic societies... but I want to cherry pick. Eberron has always struck me as being to far deep into D&D's pockets, trying to fit and accomodate Halflings, Drow and all the other D&D tropes into a world instead of bringing a lot of its own new flavours. So, it is Eberron if Eberron was written by the daughter of Moorcock and Vance and who's godfather was Leiber.

    Here are some elements that I've jotted down....

    High Fantasy Elements
    * Vale of the Crimson Crystals- where the shy Quartzmen make their home.
    * Gryphon Parcel-- pony express analog, but on Gryphons.
    * Aboria- Forested nation-state, led by an immortal sorceress who\u2019s health is tied directly into the land and her peoples.
    * Sorcery Storms-- Maelstroms of magic, like tornadoes, can hit without warning, often teleporting people and things, often enchanting places, peoples or items and other random events without rhyme or reason.
    * Galvanus- a city -state on a floating island, has a yearly flight path cycle.
    * White Necropolis- a thriving city-state of Necromancy that is quite orderly and successful, built on the \u201cslave labor\u201d of the undead servants. But using Nercromancy for murder or military power is strictly forbidden and will result in death by the Council of Necromancy. The People of White Necropolis believe that putting their dead to work is revering their memories.

    And that is the extent of what I'm going to do as world-building prior to play. Let the rest come from the players and chargen.

    But I appreciate people trying to point me to systems that might work. It has made me clarify a few things to myself as I struggle to clarify to others.

    At the risk of not being "indie," what about Fantasy Hero (Hero System)?
    * Something faster that 4th Ed D&D. Something mechanically elegant
    If you restrict Speed, Multipowers, and Variable [anything], Hero is very fast. It is elegant if one wants some crunch (maneuvers, special attack types) without getting mired in charts and graphs and modifiers and resolutions (I'm looking at YOU, Burning Wheel).

    * Some kind of player narrative control (Fate pts w/ Aspects, Artha in BW etc).
    I'm surprised that more folks don't recognize that about half of the Disadvantages are player agency in world creation. Maybe because (old-skool, trad) GMs can't stand having players make their own enemies (no, this isn't Lumpley-Principle-violating, as the GM still generates the type of adversity resulting from Hunted, etc.).
    And don't get me started on creative use of Transform....

    * Powerful player characters
    250 Base pts + 100 Disad pts and you'll be JUST fine on that!
    (Especially without folks dumping a shit-ton of points into Speed arms races.)

    * Cool, weird, evocative magic
    I can't think of a system more-suited to creativity and style, for "magic" (or whatever you decide to flavor it: faith, sorcery, psi). If you ignore my advice on bullet 1, and let in Multipowers and Variable Power Pools, you got "Mage-like" magic, too. (Elemental pools are good, too, for something more like Last-Airbender-style magic).

    * Easy to run
    Define "easy." And "run."

    * System lends itself to flavor
    I'd say that the Advantages and Limitations bits of the system reek of flavor (i.e., Color), but it's only as stinky as you make it. It doesn't have "baked-in Color" like Mouse Guard or DitV; but it gives you every tool for which you could ask, to shape Setting (not only through "special effect" Color but also with Active Cost Limits and similar, to let you relatively scale various effect types or abilities).

    ...

    Damn... did I just explain Hero System to Storn...? O_o
    :P

    Anyway... use Hero and take time to actually fill out a COMPLETE Campaign Rules Sheet (or whatever it's called this edition: the thing that lets you set Power Level, ACLs for specific powers, and/or forbid specific powers). And I'd say go with Heroic settings, unless you don't want to fool with equipment and stuff.
