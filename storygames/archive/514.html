Decisionless Games

Josh Roby
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games
This is another pick-your-brains thread. Disregard at your leisure.

In the game Candyland, the players do not make any decisions. When it's your turn, you draw a card, and you go to the next space of the matching color. If you land at the begining of a shortcut, you move up the shortcut rather than the main path. Whoever gets to the end first wins.

In structural terms, Candyland sucks.

However, it's a well-loved icon of board games. It doesn't hurt that it's made for children and a good chunk of nostalgia has built up around it.

I'm not denigrating Candyland (which was designed to help develop color-matching skills, and acheives that goal); I'm simply recognizing the reality of how it works.

Here's my question: what are some other examples of games in which the players make no decisions? They might be children's games or they might not. I want to build up a short list of different games of this stripe for a backburner project of mine. I know there are games out there that people are familiar with that I'm barely cognizant of, and I'd love to hear about them.

Thanks!

    Snap. War. Several children's card games.

    Snatch, Chain, and other "community trust" games designed for younger kids often have no real decisions in them.

    Snatch and Chain are played like this, you divide a group of kids in two and have them face each other in two lines. They then sit down and hold hands, forming a chain across the room. At one side you put a set of keys or something easily grabbed up, and at the other side you go and flip a coin. One chain is the heads chain, the other is the tails chain. The anchor at the end of the chain near you watches the coin, and when the coin comes up the direction of their team they squeeze the hand of the next kid in line. The squeeze gets passed down the line to the "snatcher" at the far end of the line. The snatcher then snatches up the keys. If they do it in three seconds then their team gets a point. If you snatch up the keys when the coin wasn't on your side, then you lose a point. After any snatch everyone gets up and moves down one place, anchor moves to the end and becomes snatcher.

    So really, the only decision in the game is "If coin = heads then squeeze hand. If hand is squeezed, squeeze next hand. If end of line and hand is squeezed, pick up keys quickly."

    But kids love the hell out of that game, especially when there is candy on the line. And teachers love the game because it teaches about focus, group cooperation, and makes the kids sit next to each other and hold hands and pay attention.

    However, it's a dull as fuck game to play with grownups.

    So really, I think it's about matching the level of decision/difficulty to the audience. If'n you have people who are hard pressed to chose between 3 options, giving them a game with 3 is a good design. If you have people who can freely mix between 300 freefloating variables, otoh, then 3 is not a good number of options.

    Contrariwise, you might be well served to google up "Plucky Little England," which appears in a Greg Costikyan essay about what makes games fun. His premise is that while we think that "decision" is what makes games fun, decisions _by themselves_ do not make for a fun game. I've always liked "Plucky Little England," and so I now paraphrase it for the delight of the unintiated and the bemusement of those who might remember it better than I:

    **Plucky Little England**
    A WWII inspirational game for one player

    The Player takes the role of England, circa 1946. Germany is kicking the shit out of you, bombing London, and all that. You have a choice: surrender to the FÃ¼hrer, becoming part of the glory of the Third Reich, or fight on! until out last man, our last breath, our last drop of blood! Never shall Dame Britania kneel before the foreign invader!


    Surrender:
    Centuries of subjugation to the German Nazi's. German spoken universally, except for the damned Yanks who stayed out of it. Enduring shame as "surrender monkeys." _You lose._
    Resist!:
    Noble valor overcomes the reaching grasp of evil! Hitler eventually defeated, Britain returns as a world power and respected nation forever! _You win!_


    _Paraphrased from the original, much better text by Greg Costikyan_

    Point being, you get a decision, but the game is still no better (arguably much worse) than Candyland. And I believe his ultimate point was something about the decisions having to be meaningful or significant, or some such rot.

    The way I see it, the reason Shoots and Ladder is still fun is because it's all about the Gamble, albeit an extended Gamble.
