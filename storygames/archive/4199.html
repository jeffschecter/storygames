\n This popped up in a session during the week, but I've seen it happen a lot. Something really bugs me about it and I'm not sure why. Help?

The game system was a homebrew and was a bit like DiTV. The player's character had a 'cowardly' trait. In a final battle, the player didnt know if his character would get involved, and so wanted to roll a dice to see. 50:50 chance of 'get involved' versus 'not'.

This irritated me for at least two reasons.

Firstly, this was not a mechanic in the game. There was a system for conflict and stakes. I wasn't happy with inventing a new mechanic.

Secondly, this *seemed* to indicate the player wasn't truly invested in the fiction. Facing up to cowardice is interesting. Running away in cowardice is interesting. Reducing those choices to a flat 50:50 chance seems to demean the fiction and the participant's place in creating that fiction.

Can y'all help me work out what was going on here?

Joe.

    \n I occasionally flip a coin to decide what to do next. I can't tell you why your player did it, but my protocol for deciding what a PC should do is usually something like (subject to social constraints):

    1. If I have a gut, intuitive feeling about what my character should do, I go with.
    2. If nothing intuitive comes to me I ask "what logically would my character do?"
    3. If 1 and 2 don't work I ask "what would be cool for the story?"
    4. If I can't think of anything for 1,2, or 3, I then use a randomizer - either flip a coin, or (more recently) look at a Tarot card.

    Now, if I have a character that I have thought alot about, it's more likely I'll stop at step one or two. If I've been handed a brand new character with negligible background, I'm more likely to slip to stage 3 or 4 (usually stopping at 3). But in both cases there's always a chance I slip to stage 4. Hmm upon reflection, I'll slip to stage 4 about once every 20 hours of game play when I've had at least 4+ hours of prep time for a character, and once about every 4 hours of gameplay with zero preparation and background.

    It's an interesting question of whether a mechanic other than high-low or Tarot-read could be used to refine the process more... but at least for me by the time I start looking for a mechanic, I've already pretty much decided that I don't actually care. Maybe some of this is mechanics are for when either two-players disagree on what should happen, or one player is taking on the responsibility of adding complications/costs to actions. But if a player with default authority over a character doesn't care what should happen, then there's no player-player conflict, and there's no price or setback she'd be willing to play.

    PC: um, I guess I act bravely
    GM: But only if your act of bravery puts your friends at risk. Is it worth it to you?
    PC: Oh, then, um, I guess I don't care. I guess I'm cowardly

    \n I've used a randomizer to decide things in real life when I couldn't decide for myself. Usually it's when neither choice sounds good but I have to decide something. I don't see a problem at all with doing it for a game situation where you can't decide what your character would do.

    \n Good lord! Thanks for all the comments. I'll try and provide some background, and then some thoughts on the session.

    There's four of us in the group, with an interesting dynamic. I've been gaming the longest, and have introduced the group to indie games. There's Calum, (who typically took a story-orientated GM role in previous games), his girlfriend Jenny (great sense of genre, sometimes shy) and Alasdair (Calum's younger brother). Alasdair's the one who bugged me. All in all, we've had about two dozen game sessions. This was our second session of this game.

    The system is a descendant of TSOY, with no GM. Players frame their scenes, pick someone else to be their conflict-wallah, negotiate towards a conflict and stakes, and roll (freeform traits)+(broad pools)+(setting motifs)+(gift dice from the rest of the table).

    We'd had a few moments during the two sessions where I wasn't convinced Alasdair was taking the responsibilities seriously. I didnt think he understood how responsible he was for his own fun, there being no GM (though wed done some GM-less stuff before).

    For example, we built the setting as a group in the first session. We wanted steampunk. Al suggested we play in a non-Euro setting. Calum suggested Western. We all liked the idea. We settled on elements of the setting as a group then went to character generation. Al wanted to play a member of the (British) House of Lords in India. Wtf?

    In a battle scene, Jenny described a cool bit where two ninja masters met, speaking one word to each other. And everyone around hushed... and Al piped up with an inappropriate comment from his character. We discussed it, and he very much wanted his character to blunder about. We discussed this as a group and the comment stood. I wasn't happy with it, as it seemed to mess with Jenny's scene setting and establishment of mood.

    Overall, I felt that Alasdair did have a sense of mood and genre, but it wasn't shared entirely. He framed one scene, describing at length the tablecloth in the dining car of a train (for 15 minutes) before describing his character's action. There were some eyes rolled round the table.

    (I'm more of a loudmouth than the other two players, and tend to push for meatier conflicts and tighter scene frames. So I do get frustrated at what felt like waffling)

    The final scene was an epic, group-wide showdown with an immortal ninja guy. Alasdair took his turn. He wanted to make two rolls - one to see if his character got involved, or simply hid. One to see how the fight went if he did get involved. Instead of a normal conflict roll for the first choice, he wanted to basically 'flip a coin' - a 50:50 chance on a d10.

    I stopped him and explained that breaking the scene into two rolls felt like prolonging the inevitable. He was vacillating. And it bugged me that the first roll was wasn't a statement. It felt arbitrary. And it felt distant from the character and the story.

    As Per says above, "I can get my head around the "player didnt know if his character would get involved" bit". I still don't understand it.

    Joe.
