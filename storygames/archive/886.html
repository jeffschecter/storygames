'Chaotic Play', this is where we talk about it

Thomas Robertson
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Play Advice
Seeing the number of comments on my post on chaotic play that were clogging up Andy's "Reveal Your Current Game Ideas!" Thread: Summer, I figured I'd take it here. Andy's not asked us to yet, but if I gave into the temptation to start discussing it there, well... Anyway, I've got some thoughts on the subject, I'm sure you do too. Say what you want.

For those who somehow didn't read Andy's thread, and yet are still reading my own, here's a recap:
Sort of a cross between LARP and tabletop. Play groups can grow larger than a single table (into the dozens), and play can take place where any two players happen to have a chance to get together. (Almost?) no one knows everything that's going on, but everyone still contributes to shared context somewhere.
Here's the deal. This is, as I said in the original thread, prompted by some of Tony's stuff on Misery Bubblegum (which is a way cooler name, so I'm going to use it; if you actually want to find the threads in question, you'll probably have to search for the lame new name Dulcimer Hall) and by some ideas spurred by Joshua BishopRoby's Agora. Oh, and a huge heaping-helping of recent reading of online freeform archives (and some academic texts on fandom and fanfiction).

Over in that thread, Jeph says:
This could be incredibly cool... I think the only way it could work is that, during any given instance of play, the only things that could be affected are things controlled by those present. 'Cuz otherwise you get shit like, "Oh, you guys hired Leo to take out the half-owlbear mage? But Jess and I totally bound Leo's soul to a pentacle and incinerated his body last week!"

Unless you've got some way to deal with contradictions like that? 'Cuz that would rock.
It's an interesting conundrum, and one that I'm considering seriously. I've got more than one model in the works, they may or may not end up combined.

1. Using online play as a model, it turns out that only using the stuff you control can be ridiculously powerful, so I'm not sure this counts as a problem. You just have to schedule a time for you and the people you need in whatever planned scene you want to do to get together. It might take you a couple of weeks, but hopefully you can manage it. (Note, this solution assumes relative ease of contact. You have to be able to get together with everyone else -- or at least the main people involved in your story arcs -- without huge amounts of work.)

2. Inspired by Agora, everything is recorded on cards, cards you can keep in your pocket (or somewhere on your person). Whatever you're carrying, that's the stuff you control. You write in changes as they happen, and you (hopefully) pass some of the cards at regular intervals. Alternatively you utilize an online repository (similar to the one I'm using for my online Agora playtest), from which you can draw fictional 'items' as needed, and which can track changes to said items.

3. Inspired (again) by online freeform play, I may just end up working up something for pure online play. Period. At the moment I think a lot of the online techniques can be adapted to more 'traditional' face-to-face play, but I'm not entirely certain that this is the case. There are some serious differences between the two mediums (article forthcoming this week, tell Jason Morningstar I haven't forgotten), and it's possible that those differences will simply make it impossible to take this as I'm currently envisioning it to our world. That said, there is a lot here that we should be learning from, I'm just not sure what's applicable... yet.

So, thoughts? Concerns? This applies to everyone, but this is especially an appeal to people who play online freeform already...

Thomas

    Probably because you have to have a GM there to let your stuff into the game. (Feel free to correct me on either of these, if this isn't generally true in LARPs...)

    This really isn't generally true in LARP, infact in the major fest style LARPs in the UK it is most definitely not the case.

    Play is quite freeform, with few references to rules necessary in normal play. If the players do something, then it happens even if there is no ref around. Infact most of the game is conducted without the explicit presence or necessity of a ref. Refs are only called for special instances or when rules arbitration is necessary. And rules arbitration is rarely necessary as the system in these kind of games is designed to be as light and as easily enforced as possible. So refs are rarely needed. I could sum up the core of one system as follows:

    "You pretend to be someone else by physically representing what they do. Between the hours of 10am and 2am, for the duration of the weekend, anything you say and do is what your character says and does. Your character will die after 2 minutes if someone hits you the player once in the head or chest with a weapon. Don't do anything which would endanger the safety of another player, don't drop out of character and don't take the piss"

    Essentially the role of the ref is to adjudicate rules questions on special cases, and to guard against cheating (primarily not taking hits, which is actually rarer than you'd think)

    The ref doesn't really control what "enters the game" in any real way. They don't act as "gate-keepers to play" because play is whatever happens between the players. If you the player say it, your character says it. If you do it, your character does it. The SIS is almost entirely equivolent to the physical reality of the players.

    Now, there are obviously systems which function like you describe, but they are by no means universal, or even the norm. Personally I don't enjoy them so much as it is very restrictive of play to have to continually need a ref to validate what has happened.

    Let's break that second one open a bit more, and see if there's anything interesting there. First of all, at least at this stage, I'm not talking about exhaustive listing. So if Frank, Janice and I are in a group, and Frank misses a session, we're not playing a different 'game' since it's just me and Janice. But, what if when Frank, Janice and I play, we play with the same ideas and characters and stuff no matter what system we play. Maybe I play these three or four guys, and Janice has these other characters, and Frank has still another set. And sometimes we use Universalis and play in the modern world, and sometimes we break out Capes and reinterpret our characters as super-powered people, and then to change things up we take our super-heroed characters and port them into With Great Power.

    And all that stuff, we call it 'our game'. We don't think of it in terms of 'when we played Universalis and then when we played some similar characters in Capes', instead we consider all of it one unit of play. I don't even know exactly what this would look like, much less what implications it would offer, but I'm betting there's some interesting things to be found out there on the edges of how we classify units of play.

    All that said, the big anyone-plays-whenever thing doesn't have to be that radical. I'm sure it's going to have to be at least somewhat radical, since it has't been done before, but I suspect that the first couple of attempts will be remarkably traditional in a sense. And there's nothing wrong with that. I just enjoy thinking about and speculating on some of this really crazy stuff...
