\n You know the standard way, in RPGs, that using two weapons works? Both weapons do the original damage, but there's some penalty for using them: usually a To Hit penalty.

And then there's a skill called two-weapon fighting, which you take, and it almost or completely negates the penalty.

Do any two weapon fighting styles actually work like this?

For example, I know, from learning stage combat, that there's a sword-and-dagger fighting style: your left hand holds a dagger, your right a sword. But they don't add in any sense.

Instead, the dagger is a defensive weapon. Your sword is still the main weapon, but you keep the dagger up to parry strikes from your non-sword side.

Now, OK, sometimes you'll get a lucky thrust in with the dagger. But there's no sense of having twice as many attacks. Or of the two weapons being equal weapons.

So, what other two weapon styles are there? Is there ever a case where you use two weapons together and their effect is additive?

Graham

    \nEscrima uses two weapons (short sticks or knives) offensively. Both weapons are used to attack and "defend," though most defensive moves in this style are actually attacks on the opponent's weapon or hands. Kali and Arnis (the styles that Escrima derives from) are similar.

    In general with weapon fighting, "blocking" doesn't really happen the way it does in stage combat. You don't hold your weapon out for it to get hit (and probably damaged). Your weapon is always moving and threatening your opponent. It's the same if you have one weapon or two.

    \n There is little-to-no similarity between fighting with one weapon and fighting with that weapon and an additional weapon. The stance, tactics, and maneuvers are all different. The difference between fighting with a rapier and fighting with a rapier-and-main-gauche is just as great as the difference between rapier and, say, big ol' broadswords. In a 'realistic' RPG implementation, Rapier, Broadsword, and Rapier-and-Main-Gauche should be three different skills. Rapier and Main Gauche aren't additive, they're just materials that can be used by different skills.

    \n I've got a level or two in Escrima as part of my Wun Hop Kuen Do and Kajukenbo training. Having two weapons has certain advantages, but in the case of stick fighting, there are disadvantages as well, because it negates your ability to grab the other person or their weapon (unless you're quick enough to drop your second stick in the right moment, which advanced fighters do).

    It doesn't mean you always get twice the hits in, but it does allow you to keep your opponent threatened directly after a strike, and it allows for flurries/combinations. It certainly does NOT make it harder to strike, and in fact, combinations are almost always more likely to hit and to inflict damage than single attacks, which is why all modern martial arts work with combinations.

    It's actually more difficult to defend against two weapons because you have to keep both in focus.

    \n I have a couple of comments from my days of Dark Age (nowadays "early medieval") re-enactment.

    Technically, fighting with sword and shield *is* fighting with two weapons. The RPG notion of a shield as a sort of optional defensive extra that makes no difference to fighting style, etc., is nonsense.

    I used to fight quite a lot with a short sword in my right hand and a short axe in my left. The main advantage of this style is that if you parry successfully with the axe, you can often lock the opposing fighter's weapon, at least for a short time. And sometimes you can parry with the sword and attack with the axe instead, for variety. But generally you're only attacking with one weapon at once. The disadvantage of fighting with two weapons is that you have to be a lot more accurate with your parries, and it's difficult to defend against more than one opponent at once. In a defensive line, facing a shield wall, you either break through or die quickly. Probably both.

    \n There is a pretty good portrayal of this in the Codex RPG (you don't know it probably). There are two kinds of two-weapon fight. If the there is a first and second weapon, than the second weapon will only raise your defense, but you still use the original skill for the first weapon. (It's probably not exactly like this, but almost. I think you can choose to defend with it or to attack, I can't remember right now.) But the important thing is: the second weapon is not handled as a seperate weapon with its own attack-per-round, but only gives bonus for your values. It doesn't works like: you have two weapons, you attack twice as much, wich I think is netto bullshit, considering one "attack" is not one slash, but something more complex.

    And there is another way, wich is more interesting: if the two weapons are equal, then you have to learn a seperate skill wich is harder to learn than the one-weapon skill. A pair of weapons is just like as it would be a completely different weapon.

    It makes sense, because it's not the weapon you learn, but the skill to use a weapon. If the techniqe is totally different, you need to have a totally different skill.
