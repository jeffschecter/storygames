\n Looking for help here. I have a couple players in my group that are convinced that dice are necessary for an RPG to be "fair" or "balanced", variously (which it is seems to vary from discussion to discussion), and are therefore blind to the fact that, as near as i can tell, the dice are spoiling their fun *way* more than they're contributing to their fun. So, for our run club i'd like to try something randomless on them, and see if they can see that, i suspect, they'd have more fun being able to choose their successes and failures, and that this can still be "balanced"--i.e., not necessarily lots of "i fail to make tea so i can succeed at slaying the dragon".

But i'm sorta blanking an games that actually fit the bill. I can only think of Amber, Theatrix, Nobilis, Marvel Universe, Mortal Coil, and maybe Universalis (i'm too lazy to go get it off teh shelf and check). Of those, i think Theatrix would feel too much like GM's fiat to these players, and Amber and Nobilis would feel too much like no rules to these players. What i'd love would be something with a crunch level approaching Ars Magica, or even Iron Heroes, but without a randomizer. Presumably resource-management, like Marvel Universe and Mortal Coil, but not necessarily.

So, what other randomless RPGs am i forgetting, and do you have any specific suggestions for players who like crunch, are mature enough to choose to fail important tasks at times, but would like to be able to choose which important tasks they fail at?

    \n IMHO, past a certain point of randomization, dice effectively pad the game and reduce crunch. You could simply use the argument that whereas a string of actions in a crunchy, diceless game such as Chess actually matter to the crunch, dice play on gambles with measurable chance. To some extent, being able to balance something akin to resource allocation with the chance of success one is comfortable with can be very fun. But I think that many (especially popular) systems use a kind of system-illusionism here, which pads both good and bad decisions, reducing crunch to almost nil, and losing my interest. Player decisions need to matter.

    One issue with System is that players often have strong opinions on what they should decide for themselves and what should be left up to mechanics. Where you draw this line defines the game in much the same way Stance (author vs. actor) does. In this instance, your players may not feel comfortable, depending on the details alternate systems you've offered up (and honestly having much to do with the way you present them!), with their own decisions mattering more than a randomizer. How do you get players to realize that this discomfort may be preventing them from enjoying good systems that see things another way? Good question! Since I don't know of any good examples on the market, I can't really point you in the right direction.

    I actually started a forge thread recently on a diceless, collaborative inner-conflict resolution mechanic, which is intended to add crunch in the grand scheme. One particular point with this mechanic is that, aside from a table which aids in quantifying what the players are conflicted over, resolution at this level is left to the players, not the dice. However, it has clearly delineated outcomes based on rules, which I think is a prerequisite for good crunch. My problem (perhaps one that you're experiencing) is the absence of an existant RPG that already does a good job at this. However, in the light of non-story games which have done a good job at diceless crunch, I'm sure that we can all agree that it's possible such an RPG does exist.

    -JT

    \n Heh. You want to playtest Contract Work?

    Resolution system in a nutshell: Bid values against each other. Divide between Attack and Defense. Reveal and compare.

    There are situations you can win with sheer number crunching, others where you have to read the other player's situation (kind of like poker) and times when you need to force his hand. Tokens to enforce a limit on bids for one side or boost the values of others. Resource management was my motivation in designing the game. A good hitman can't leave things to chance. You organize the job, pick your battles and execute the plan.

    I made it for hitman missions, but someone is already eager to hack it into a Shadowrun game.

    \n Well I can't properly suggest Solipsist, since it's not out yet (though if you want to playtest please do get in touch), but the principle it works by I think fits the bill. When you take an action the GM calculates a difficulty number (this is fixed, but he can pay tokens from a limited pool to increase it), this is then modified by your own traits, which can raise and lower it. If it ends up at or below 0 you succeed, if not you fail, but you can pay your own tokens to move it closer to 0. 0 results give you perfect successes where you narrate what happens, results below 0 succeed but you don't narrate, but you do get more tokens for future attempts.
