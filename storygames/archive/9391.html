\n

\n
\n
\n
\n You're playing one of 3 characters in an adventuring party. You enter a big cavern with a pit in the middle and a bunch of pillars running from floor to ceiling. There are a handful of orcs there, ready to attack you.

The GM draws a map of the room so he can keep track of who's where during the fight. You'd like to see the map in order to coordinate your attack strategy with your 2 fellows. However, in this game, experiencing things from your character's POV is a top priority, every bit as important as winning orc fights.

Do you ask to see the map? Why or why not?

    \n Here's how I see it:

    Helps immersion:
    Spend less time asking the GM questions. Have no risk of forgetting his answers. All relevant info available at once, rather than getting one detail at a time. Decreases time and energy spent getting, retaining, processing, and reasoning from environmental info. Forms strong basis for players to generate their own mental pictures of what their characters are seeing during the fight.

    Hinders immersion:
    Your focus is on a piece of graph paper, and manipulating the dots and lines drawn on it. This is a distraction from imagining yourself somewhere other than teh game table. Being able to track everything so easily does not resemble the experience of dodging swords and ducking around pillars in a dim room.

    My experience with the paper obsession has led me to GM with hidden maps... up until the second or third time when someone struggles to remember/visualize something their character would obviously know, at which point I show it to them.

    \n I don't know that it's really ABOUT immersion or not...
    It's about helping the GAME part of RPGs flow a bit better. So many people tend to forget the "game" in role-playing games. Games NEED structure to hang the fun on. That can be in the form of the rules, the referee (GM), turns, the dice, the playing-board (map), etc...

    I have three reasons for my thought that rely on one another:
    1. player decision making ability
    2. structure for imagination
    3. shortcut to fun

    Imagination is a wonderful thing, the only problem is - my imagination (as GM) sometimes differs from what the players imagine. Many-times my descriptions, no matter how well given, miss something that's vitally important (to the player) for the decision making abilities of the CHARACTERS - because their players then don't have "correct" or fair information to "give" the character to react with... if the players don't have complete information, and make a decision they would normally NEVER make, then the IMMERSION for the characters completely breaks.
    As opposed - giving the players a little MORE than the characters should have (ie. the overhead map) reduces the chance to have that imagination from description wrongly and unfairly influence the in-game decisions of the characters.

    I'd rather my players have a bit more and reduce chances that lead to problems due to differing imagination of the scene. In many cases I've described a situation and had players get frustrated when THEY FEEL the GM "changes" the description on them when they make a tactical move... or they consider an idea and then get to their turn to "learn" it's 20' NOT 15' away as they imagined.
    The lack of HARD information leads to incorrect assumptions and often-times bad feelings on game calls.


    THAT fact leads me to say that giving a map is LESS immersion breaking than not giving a map. Because the problems that result (many times) is MORE immersion breaking than not having a clear framework in which to set the rest of the scene descriptions.
    Afterall - what you're giving (when you give a map) is a framework... a guideline... a structure to HANG all the descriptions of the scene on. Seeing the SHAPE and SIZE of the "box" doesn't give away the prize, it just builds the anticipation and imagination.


    Lastly - many of us don't have the TIME to spend on detailed descriptions, so that everyone has the exact mental picture. Alternatively, some of us don't have the descriptive capability to build a detailed description narrative as others might... For both/either of these points - having a simple map is a great tool to shortcut to a general image of where the scene is set for the players to take (individually) or to add ideas or ask questions to clarify (as a group) on the scene description. A picture is worth 1000 words afterall, and that TIME shortcut allows the game scene and the FUN of the action/drama to continue with a (correctly) shared image of the scene...


    That's my feeling.
    -kev-

    \n Maps can break immersion when changes to the situation -- especially behavioral descriptions of character-environment interactions -- are inconsistent (but not necessarily contradictory) with the representation provided by the map. Static maps of static situations don't generally have that problem. A map of a city tends to be consistent with later descriptions. Even if the city is burned down, you can usually still recall the map for an image consistent with how the characters are interacting with the ruins. This makes such a map very useful as a framework for building and updating mental models of the situation. Once that framework is in place, the map is benignly redundant and so the player's attention will go straight from the mental model to the situation, exactly what is wanted for immersion. Similarly, a scene map or tactical map might aid immersion if it is presented only in regards to a duration of time in which the scene is relatively static, and then taken away before the scene is described as changing. Including characters in the map, even for that short duration of time, probably breaks immersion since they will be imagined in terms of inferred behaviors, and so they are never truly static.

    \n I didn't even think of world maps when I first read the OP. So, hmm....

    * World Maps - I consider these to be at least as useful as setting fiction, NPC character sketches, and player handouts with journal entries, weird puzzles, or whatever. I'm not going to fool with "immersion," except to say that the group's sharing of the imagined space takes precedent over one player's need to be solely responsible for his or her internal vision of that space. Which means:

    * Tactical Maps - As all above have said, they keep things accurate in crunch games. D&D: a must; BW: a hindrance, though i find the Reach/Range Sliders Chart I made for BWHQ to be very good for keeping track of the relevant modifiers and positioning test tweaks; DitV: irrelevant. Yep, (*sigh*) it's all relative to the game being played. I could go even further, though:

    * Miniature Settings - Why stop with hating a hand-drawn map and minis on it? Let's hate on the whole modeling hobby, who bring (what I once thought were) cool-as-hell blown-out buildings, snap-together dungeon tiles with 3D doors and chest, rolling hills and scattered trees and gullies for hiding in. But, wait! As I recall from actual play, such settings actually make me MORE immersed in the flow of the game: I may have a unique ability in that I can look at a flock-modeled tree and see, instead, its branches swaying in the wind, teasing smoke with its leaves, its roots stretching across the ground to trip the living or embrace the fallen. I can look at a 3D dungeon tile and see the water-slicked, mossy walls, hear the scrabble of rats and drips of ichor from the floor above, see the dim, flickering torchlight. And, yep, when I see a bog-standard miniature from (say) MageKnight or D&D, I can *still* imagine all the personalizing details its player tells me about attire or wild eyes or bushy beards.

    In short: bupkiss to the haters. If your "immersion" is threatened by a visual aide, then your imagination needs more exercise--I haven't seen kids play with action figures be totally constrained to the figures range of motion and scale (and I almost NEVER see kids just sitting and telling stories, without dolls or toys or figures or drawings).

    And if you have any notion of actually *sharing* the imagined space--which includes accuracy in equal parts with individual imaginative vibrancy--then a visual aide is about the ONLY way to be 100% certain that everyone "sees" the same thing in their minds' eyes. So then the *degree* to which one uses a visual aide should reflect the need for coherence in the SIS. D&D needs a lot, because every 10' counts. DitV needs almost none, as tactics take a back seat to narrative flow and the dice mechanics.

    \n I think Kev's got a really important observation about the importance of sharing info the characters would have. When that doesn't happen, you wind up with clashing visions and undoing actions/events... which of course is toxic to any attempt to "lose yourself" in the imagined sequence.

    I think we all agree here that maps are one way to share such info. I guess one of the most pertinent questions is, are there better ways? Ways that carry fewer immersive downsides? As for those downsides:\n
    Posted By: David ArtmanIf your "immersion" is threatened by a visual aide, then your imagination needs more exercise
    This is a point I've heard a lot, and although I agree with it, I don't think it really covers what I've experienced in play. Let's imagine two types of players trying to immerse:

    Player A:
    This player doesn't want to put any effort into immersing. He just wants the GM to manage everything, so he can just think in-character 100% of the time. The second the real-world experience of playing a game with other people intrudes on his consciousness, he gets pissy. "Something stopped me from thinking in-character; my immersion is ruined. This sucks."

    Player B:
    This player is committed to imagining himself in the fiction as an active endeavor. He strives to accommodate the practical realities of collaborative play; to deal with what must be dealt with, to minimize that which can safely be minimized, and to not get all bent out of shape in any case. When his immersion is interrupted, he tries to quicly rediscover the groove. He has some practice playing this way, and while he is successful overall, he doesn't have perfect control over his reactions, nor perfect ability to instantly re-immerse after interruptions.

    So, let's not talk about Player A. He's a waste of our effort. The question is whether or not Player B might find some downsides to maps. In my experience, the answer is yes. It's one more thing in the real world that calls attention to itself during time that could otherwise be spent seeing through the character's eyes -- much like dice, character sheets, drinks, fellow players, hunger, etc., etc. Perhaps more satisfying play, from an immersion standpoint, could be achieved using some procedure or ritual instead of a physical map? Or perhaps a map can be best used for initial orientation, to establish some facts, but then put away, denying the players short-hand reference and instead forcing them to engage their imaginations when making character decisions (a la Burr's suggestion)?

    I think there are two ways we can view this real-world interactivity issue of maps:
    1) distraction
    2) path of least resistance

    Regarding simply denying the players an easy way to do something, forcing them instead to do it in a slower and more demanding manner that furthers some tangential goal (e.g. immersion), I'm torn. Maybe the simple answer is, "If you really want immersion, avoid tactical combats." But I'd like to have my cake and eat it too.

    \n Some random thoughts:

    1. Maps that feature a lot of Color (textures, hand-drawn, signed by the mapmaker, pretty) could help with immersion, if they communicate something about the feeling of a place.

    2. Maps that are just simple graphics used to illustrate a point ("The dot here is you, right? The tree on your left is about... here... and the orc is somewhere over here. Got it?") are a useful tool for communication.

    3. What I hate is something that is kind of in-between. Like when you take a nice, colorful map from your Warhammer game and use it to represent the battlefield in a low-fantasy D&D game. And it's got bullet-holes punched into it, and a burning tank in the left corner. The Color of the map distracts from what I'm trying to imagine.

    If the map is not just a schematic, it'd better be EXACTLY RIGHT, or I have to work hard to ignore parts of it, even though I'm supposed to refer to it all the time in play.

    4. How do you present the map? If you just plop it down, like "the cavern looks like this!", I'd say the overall effect is to reduce immersion, in a sense. Because you're NOT going to describe things as much. The players aren't going to ask the GM to describe as much, either.

    Some highly imaginative players might still "immerse" in their own imagination, but it's not "Shared", is it? They're generating some image of their own based on the map, and imagining that as hard as they can. But we don't know what that is.

    I think for immersion, at least of a certain type, it's good to have as much talk and description as possible--lots of "sensory input" for the characters. My feeling is that, generally, when the GM pulls outs a map, a) the GM is likely to speak less, and just let people look at the map, and b) the players are less likely to ask questions ("what's the wall on the left side look like/feel life?"), as their attention is now focused on the piece of paper on the table.

    If you have a map that conveys the mood or feeling or whatever you want, go for it. Like those nice hand-drawn maps, or maps that are "within the fiction" (i.e. look just like whatever they represent in the gameworld). If not, my preference is to use description, and go to simple drawing/sketches as a tool for explanation, only when necessary. When the players start going, "wait, how far am I from the door, again?" then I draw a few dots and/or lines on the paper, but spare enough that they don't create any imagery on their own.

    That's my take on it. I think. It's probably different for everybody.

    But "immersive, tactical combat"? When I hear that, I think, "we make a careful plan beforehand" (i.e. use a map/sketch here), and, "when the fight breaks out, everything goes to shit" (describe everything in visceral, vague, terms, like through a blurry lens).

    I'm not seeing "immersive, tactical combat" with a battlemat and hexes and all that.
