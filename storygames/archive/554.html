    The caste system is pretty heavily emphasized in Musa, where you have the nobility (who are the military officers), the peasant-soldiers (who have to work their way up through the ranks), and then a slave class. Lots of yummy conflict potential there.

    Also seems like, historically, the Koreans (or the people of Goryeo or Joseon or whatever) were always fighting either 1) Japanese invaders, 2) the northern "barbarian" tribes (Mongols, Khitan, Jurchen, etc.), or, occasionally, 3) the Chinese, who wanted to make them a tributary state.

    Also, the legendary founders of ancient Korea, supposedly had between 1-3 heavenly objects that gave them supernatural powers, as well as being signs of the mandate to govern, which sounds like good mcguffins to base play off of.

    Yeah, and the people-who-used-to-be-animals thing. Very cool.

    The conflict that stands out most for me in Korean history is Admiral Yi Sun-sin's fighting off of the Japanese with ironclad Turtle ships in the Seven-Year War starting in 1592. Related to that would be fighting Japanese pirates with similar ships, particular in the century before then. That's certainly one of the most celebrated fights in Korean history.

    You might also look at 668 A.D. -- the defeat of Baekje and Goguryeo by the alliance of the Korean Silla kingdom with the Tang Chinese. The fall of Goguryeo is considered a tragic turning point in the history of Korea -- uniting the country for the first time, but at the price of surrendering much of Manchuria over to China.

    I once presented an elven culture inspired by many elements of Korean culture in my own FRPG campaign to highlight their "exotic" nature.

    I used these features in great effect:
    focusing on archery (Koreans are still sweeping golds in major archery games)
    incorporating song and dance in everyday life
    white linen clothes with their distinguishing designs
    exotic fermented vegetables (white kimchi: kimchi was not spicy until peppers were imported in 16th century)
    fiercely independent nature (they have been persistently protecting their independence throughout the history. Unfortunately, it developed into sticking to "racial purity", making them very xenophobic people. Hines Ward syndrome seems to change their thoughts now)
    unique, systematic phonetic alphabet system (I highly recommend you to learn the Hangul characters even if you don't learn the language itself. It is simply a great system)
    other cultural bits, such as the first metal moveable type technology in the world

    Some features of Korean culture directly opposes to those of "typical elven culture", like its almost Totalitarian nature. As an agricultural society (and heavily influenced by Confucianism), hierarchy, conformity and close relationship with families and neighbors have been strongly emphasized. The fervent Red Devils in which practically every Korean participated during 2002 World Cup could be an example of showing their collective-forming energy (perhaps in a positive way).

    Andy, I'd suggest checking out a couple of medieval Korean costume dramas. I'm a big fan of "The Age of Warriors," though I don't understand anything about the historical context or who anyone is.

    It is an unbelievably awesome show. A typical scene involves a dozen dudes you've never seen before, sitting around a table in their compound saying, "Wow, the Emperor/Warlord/Archfiend (there's an awesome character called "Archfiend," everyone's like, "Good day, Archfiend!")/rival clan is totally out of control. We need to rein them in!"

    And then someone says, "Ahhhhhh." And strokes their chin thoughtfully.

    And someone else says, "We should strike immediately. I have this great plan (details plan) (you sit there and say, "yes, yes, have a scene just like that, immediately!")

    And someone else says, "Hmmmmmm." And strokes their chin thoughtfully.

    And then the leader of the group says, "That would reveal our hand too quickly. Let's sit on it, and wait. Right now, let's do nothing." (And you sit there and are like, "aggggh, that's what they always say!")

    And everyone nods. And that's the end of the scene, and you never see those dudes again!

    --And then cut to a new scene, where it's 12 more people you've never seen before, and will never see again! And they have the exact same conversation-- "Let's do something cool!" "No, let's wait and see."

    So--to answer the question--based on watching a zillion episodes of "Age of Warriors," it looks like a HUGE part of Korean medieval aristocratic life was spent sitting around arguing about staging coups, counter-coups, and counter-counter-coups. But mostly people just scheme all the time and never do anything.

    And you should totally have a character called "Archfiend" by everyone, including his wives and children. His interests should include having pretty wives, wielding battle axes, and indulging in furious "now you must die" fits of laughter.

    (I speak as someone who is a huge fan of this show, yet remain in utter ignorance about anything else, and beg the indulgence of those who know better.)
