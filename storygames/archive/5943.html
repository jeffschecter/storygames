    Posted By: OgremarcoJohn Harper and I were talking about Judds gamer skills post, and we came up with what we felt were three strong pieces of advice that digest all of the social contract advice given there and elsewhere.

    1. Pay attention.
    2. Don't be a dick.

    The part I really like is how one follows the other in a big circle ad infinitum.
    \n#3 feels wrong to me. I'm going to stick with bad metaphors for a moment. Stay with me.

    When you are dancing with someone who is really good, you don't have to tell them that you want to be spun. Sometimes, your hips just tell them things or the song tells them. But it'd be pretty damned boring if you walked up to your partner and said, "I'm going to spin you and dip you and then dip you again." Please don't pick at the metaphor too intensely.

    With gaming, I want to put flags up on my sheet and not really know what my GM is going to do with them exactly. I can have a Belief about wanting to kill my brother and find out through play that my brother, actually, is an alright guy and my PC was just a prick whose head was on wrong. That'd be cool if done well.

    If done well...there's the fucking rub. If done well...

    Reading flags and responding in a way that makes sense to the other players but still surprises and still stays within genre is something that I tend to do pretty well and I have no idea how or why or what I do to make it happen. But I don't give the players exactly what they want, that'd be dull. I open it up to the table and we all come out with something we never expected.

    So, wait, #3 feels fine to me but the answer to #3 isn't to listen to your players and give them exactly what they want. The skillset that comes with #3 is listening to what they want and coming up with something within genre expectations but beyond the expectations of the character sheet or even the campaign.

    Sorry, Clint, I was disagreeing on instinct, because this is, after all, the internet.

    Posted By: RedonePosted By: Judd

    My goals with this stuff is to work it to a point where we can have exercises or games or whatever that help us get better at the bits of gaming that make good games happen.
    \nOh my, exercises to improve gaming? That seems a little odd to me. I mean, playing is the only good exercise, the real game. And if you talk a lot about things that were good or bad during tha game, then you will probably succeed. The only way to feel the game and to understand what is good and what is not is just playing. But that's only my opinion.

    Playing is the best exercise, without a doubt.

    But I think there are things that we do and do well that couldbe cultivated.

    Scene framing is a huge one. People do it so often that it is like breathing. But that doesn't mean that everyone breaths well. So many scenes and parts of games go well because we know when to take our time with a journey and set up a scene along the road and we know when to just start the scene at the crossed spears of the emperor's guards in front of the palace gate.

    Even just thinking about some of these things in isolation can be a big help.

    So, I agree and disagree. The best way to get good at this stuff is to get out there and play.

    But I think there are little parts of our gaming, techniques that can be thought about in a way as to improve them.

    \n I might argued that before you even know what to ask for what you want, you need to be involved, be it in the planning or actual play. My #3 would simply read "Be Involved" (which I think perhaps goes a bit further in depth than "Pay Attention". That doesn't mean talking over everyone--it means being an active listener, responding when need be, finding out what others' wants and goals are, figuring out what YOU want, and paying attention to your game/group. In fact, I could condense #3 and #1 together, so mine might read:

    #1: Be Involved.
    #2: Don't Be A Dick.

    Perhaps too brief/non-descriptive?
