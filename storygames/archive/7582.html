    \n English is a bit of a mutt language, being formed from bits of Germanic, Norman French, Latin and Greek, all of which have had centuries of wear and tear to drift them from the original sounds. And to top it off there was a major vowel shift just after printing was invented which meant that all of the spellings that had been more-or-less standardized were now misleading.

    And then how some things are pronounced depends where you come from, which is fairly normal for most languages, but because English is spoken so widely and has been widespread for, what, at least three centuries, and people from different language areas colonized different places, and some of them preserved old forms (like "gotten") that were superseded back in the motherland, and then there were a lot of new things invented and different places invented different names for them, and there were more dialect and pronunciation shifts in the widely separated regions...

    It's a wonder we can understand each other at all, frankly. And I have to say, I'm glad I'm a native speaker, because the damn language makes no sense at all and it must be a pig to learn deliberately by rational processes rather than by the natural language acquisition abilities of a small child.

    (My degree's in English language. I could rant on for ages, but I won't.)

      \n Dialects are different languages in Italy. And don't have written form, generally speaking. And when they have it, it's different from the italian spelling.

      It's not a matter of pronouncing a written word differently. It's a matter of the written word having an official pronunciation. Sure, someone with a strong local accent can pronounce a word differently, but they know it's not the right way, and to know the right way they just have to read it from the italian spelling.

      Since the dialectal word has a different pronunciation, it will be written differently -> not the same written word.

      More to the point: the written word in italian (and other phonetic languages like spanish and japanese katakana IIRC) is a representation of the sound of that word. If you change the sound, you change the written word.

      \n Ah, but phonetic languages have official spellings and official pronunciations: you don't write down the dialectal form, 'cause that's not (say) italian.
      OTOH, this tends to iron out (historically) the differences in pronunciation.

      Nowadays it would not be viable for English to 'move' to phonetic[*], at all, because its history has led it to have different spoken forms (wildly different, sometimes... british english and, say, southern american don't sound like the same language to my foreign ears): to move to phonetic someone would need to choose the "official" form, thus rendering all the others "subpar" dialects. Not a very viable option, right?

      You could also argue that this process of "standardization" of pronunciation means that the official language is a pretty artificial one... and you'd be correct: Italian for example is an artificial, literary language that was later officialized as "the correct italian language", but has really no more pedigree than any of the dialects (which in some case, like sardinian, are recognized as independent neo-latin languages), other than having a definite, formalized, written grammar and syntax.
