\n So, I currently live in Vancouver, where there are like 15+ game stores.
Several of which are really awesome (Strategies, Drexol, Connections, etc...)

I'm moving to a small town (pop: 10,000, and that much again within 50km) where there are 0 game stores.
You can buy a selection of children's educational games and classic board games at the toy store (which is hella cool, itself).
You can buy some outdated Warhammer and D&D stuff from the cigar shop in the mall, where they know nothing about the products.

There isn't a store that people would go to ask about Settlers of Catan, to the best of my knowledge.

And so I'm intrigued by the possibility of setting up a game store in this town.

It's fairly removed (4 hours to a city of notable size), but not isolated (several other 10,000 person towns within an hour's drive).
None of the nearby towns have a board game + card game + roleplaying game store.
This is in Canada, which implies a bunch of things, re: customs. In other words, mail order from the US is a hassle for the average consumer.

I have retail experience and good people skills.
I'm knowledgeable.

Give me your thoughts!
Would it be worthwhile to explore this idea further?
What factors should I be considering in my "market research", that might not otherwise be obvious?
What kinds of things would I want to look at stocking?
Store owners: advice for the rookie?

I'm picturing, in the best of all worlds:
a place where I (/employees) dress snappy, are expertly knowledgeable about games, carry a boutique selection, happily mail-order things for our customers, hold weekly board game nights, stock lots of the new classics of board games (Settlers of Catan, Al-hambra, Ticket to Ride, Smallworld, etc), stock roleplaying games (both trad and indie), stock fun cheap gateway games (Gloom, Citadels, Munchkin), and perhaps Magic.

A place that says "this is a place where kids and grown-ups can comfortably shop together".

    \n A store is a business, first and foremost. Sad but true. Read up on entrepreneurial literature. Is there a local small business organization you can get in touch with? (we have local chambers of commerce and the Better Business Bureau here in 'Merica that have lots of resources for small businesses). Make a business plan with hard goals and measurable metrics. Do the research ahead of time so you know how much capital you'll need to sink up front. Prepare to fail (most small businesses do). Prepare to learn from failure. Get help to do things you're not good at (like, if you're not good at money, get an accountant. It'll be worth it).

    And yah, ping game store owners, for sure. I think the idea of being a Canadian mail-order center for imported books and games is a really good idea - find out if anyone else in Canada is doing it, and how they manage it. I imagine it means you'll be absorbing the customs costs, right, so figure out what else you'll be doing that will enable you to afford doing that. Figure out what that would mean for taxes.

    \n 10 000 people is not that much, it seems to me. Maybe it is different in Canada. As a comparison, the city of Iisalmi, which is the closest city from where I live, has a bit over 20 000 people. The surrounding region has another 20 000 people for perhaps a total of 40 000 people who might be expected to do their specialty shopping in Iisalmi. There are no game stores in Iisalmi, unless you count one dedicated toy store that mostly sells puzzles, action figures and dolls, with a small sideline in Warhammer and boardgames. My first reaction to the idea of having a game store in Iisalmi is sceptical - I could just imagine pulling it off with a wide net approach relying heavily on videogame business, but it would be far from a sure thing; I'd have to be able to consistently beat the large supermarkets as a destination for gift shopping and family-oriented custom. That'd in turn require a good location and a clever shop front that'd invite the mainstream customer to check out the store.

    Game stores in Finland seem to exist in cities that service over 100 000 people in their region. Is the market that different in Canada? I could totally believe that the market is underdeveloped in Finland, especially concerning video games, but then it seems to me that the average supermarket offers wicked competition when it comes to anything remotely mainstream in gaming - a specialty store couldn't sell the latest Playstation or XBox hit game any better than the supermarkets do, for instance. And if we discount the video game market (or just specialize with a boutique selection of rare offers), that'll also cut most of the turnover from the equation, again indicating a larger population base. The same goes for any other remotely mainstream type of gaming product - CCGs are sold at small corner stores, popular boardgames like Catan are sold by those supermarkets.

    Hmm... perhaps the Finnish game retail market is not so much underdeveloped but rather co-opted and hypersaturated by mainstream retail outlets. What's the situation in Canada, and your target town specifically? Can the average teenager get their Magic or Pokemon boosters from the corner store, or could something like that be your bread and butter product? That might be a fruitful approach in thinking about this - are there any specific gaming products that are actually proven sellers that are not available in your target area from other, competitive outlets? Of course you won't find roleplaying games in any local stores, I imagine, but then those aren't going to make your bottom-line, either.

    \n I think the key to doing this would be to consider the store to be merely a side effect of your real business: import and distribution by mail. If Canadian customs really are that much of a pain for the average user, then you could easily buy in bulk ("bulk" being relative in the RPG world) and cut down on that aggravation, particularly once the customs guys get used to you. In this situation:

    Canadian consumers win because its cheaper to pay you a share of dealing with customs in bulk than handle it all themselves.
    You win because consumers will pay you money that would otherwise go elsewhere.
    Customs wins because they deal with one large order rather than many small ones.
    Publishers win because it becomes easier to get product to Canadian consumers.

    One drawback is initial capital. You might be able to get around that by running your service sort of like a commune. For example, solicit orders for a certain book, with everyone knowing that they are buying into a communal order that goes out once, say, twenty people are buying the same book. You are just managing the pool, so to speak.

    RPGs are a decent product for something like this because demand would not likely get insane, yet, when you were ready, you could extend the "buying pool" idea to other products.

    \n Investigate the possibility of running an after-school program for kids at the local school. Hire at least one employee who's a licensed EEC to run it.

    Also (this is important) realize that running a retail operation is a LOT different from hosting a gaming group. Some of your clients will view you as a benevolent, avuncular figure who provides them with a home-away-from-home. But a sizable minority will view you as the Evil Dude who's standing between them and a lot of cool stuff that they badly want, but can't afford. Your business will only work if you make it a cheerful, friendly atmosphere, but in order to do so you'll have to resist the demoralizing effects of being persistently exposed to this type of customer. And if you're standing behind the register, there's nowhere to run and nowhere to hide.

    Sorry to be a Negative Nancy, but forewarned is forearmed.

    \n Case studies. For market research, get into touch with the different types of people you might imagine as your customers. Talk with them about your plans and ask them about their buying habits. Don't make it to be about some social support - of course they think it'll be a good idea and hope that you'll succeed, but what you want to know is how much money you can talk out of them on a per-month basis by catering to their needs. Try to get a dozen solid cases where you know everything you need to know about the person. Preferably people out of all of your target market segments, whatever they are. That'll give you much data that is not available to you by stochastic methods (and a lot of stuff that wouldn't be available to a larger company paying for market research, incidentally; case study is rarely used in this sort of thing, I understand, due to how the decision-makers prefer simplified numbers on a chart).

    - Don't hire people or move to a bigger place just because you've had a good year. Next year might be really bad, and then you'll go bust.
    - Don't order stuff you like. Order stuff other people want to buy, even if you think it's stupid or irrelevant.
    - Don't do this because you love games. Do it because you love stores. If you'd be just as happy running a hardware or grocery store, you're the right person for the job.

    \n Joe, for what it's worth, I totally support you in doing this, and I would love to visit your store sometime. It's just one of those sober-doses-of-reality things - make sure all your bases are covered, and you're still gonna make mistakes. That's just how things work.

    So, I worked at a game store in high school (owned by the folks who used to do Wargames West, which they transferred to after shutting down the mail order operation), and was really actively engaged in it for a couple years. Some perspective from that experience:

    What I Liked

    - Provided free gaming space. You could just sit down whenever. There were scheduled events that took priority, obviously, but generally you wouldn't get the evil eye for using the tables even if you didn't buy anything.

    - Lots of board games. We were in the same city as Rio Grande Games, so we had almost their entire line, plus all the usual suspects. Also, we carried chess and checker sets, some of them really nice, and their was a 3D chess display out for the entire time I worked there.

    - Peripherals. There were two racks of nerd-related bumper stickers that actually sold really well. They carried shirts for a while but I think they didn't sell well enough to justify re-ordering. Dice, of course. All the little WoD accessories (clan pins, etc). Coke machine.

    - Events. The store did a good job of hosting all the sanctioned Magic tournaments it could, as well as L5R and a couple others. They also had occasional D&D things. This was in addition to store-sponsored weekly tournaments, etc. This meant that there were almost always people in the store, which is good. Even if they're not shopping, having people in your store makes it less intimidating for people to just walk in and shop.

    - Proximity to food. There were three different places you could get good, cheap food within 10 minutes walk, and the store was cool with you having food.

    - Friendly atmosphere. I mean, there were directives to always greet everyone who came in the door, learn peoples names, and ask if they needed help. Obviously that can be forced and unappealing, but the idea of being friendly (as opposed to some game stores I have known, where the person behind the counter has literally just stared at me until I try to give them money) is for the win.

    What I Didn't Like

    - The RPG selection became more limited over time, finally coming down to pretty much whatever White Wolf and Wizards had put out in the last year. You could special order stuff, of course, but even by the time I left people would rather order from Amazon than special order through the store, because it was cheaper and faster. And the main revenue streams were card games (mostly Magic and Yu-Gi-Oh), Warhammer minis and board games.

    - Nerd Entitlement. There were always people who came in just to tell you about their character. As an employee, I couldn't really do anything about it unless there was another customer who clearly was ready to buy something. But there was definitely a core of dudes who had been coming to this store (in one form or another) for years and years, so they felt like they could hang around, not playing or buying anything. They annoyed me. Dunno if there's much you can do about that.

    - Didn't keep up. The store found a model that works (magic/yu-gi-oh + warhammer + board games, lots of events, membership program with discounts), and I don't think ever evolved beyond it. That is, I don't have the sense that the management really kept up with developments in the gaming world beyond what distributors are carrying at any given time - going back there is like going back to the early 2000's for me, and even then it wasn't that different from the late 90s store that I first started going to. They're still in business, but it's not a very...exciting place.

    So, some more thoughts.

    \n The world of business, as I have hyperbolically said, is alien to me, so most of this thread is gibberish to me, but hopefully this will help. I talked to a business guy one time, and he said that the best advice he ever got when launching a new enterprise was to write down, physically, the conditions under which he would fold the business and pack his tent. That way if, like most business enterprises, it was an abject yawning abyss of crushing failure, he had some objective measure of how much he was willing to tolerate before he said "nope, not working" and moved on. This kept him from falling into the entrepreneur's hell, shackled to a business which does worse and worse with every passing moment, but constantly hoping that it will turn around. Hopefully you never reach the conditions you write down, but if you decide on them in advance, you have a good sense of how much utter failure and crippling depression you're willing to endure, and aren't pushing yourself to suffer more for the sake of something that will never give you what you want.

      \n Actually, a convention is not a bad way to start a game store, thinking about it.

      You could work the field for your store by starting a convention, which in turn will help people network and cohere as a hobby scene. It'll also be major marketing for your plans if you'll make it clear from the start that you're planning to open a store if the local scene seems like it could work. Then, after three conventions a couple of months apart, when you finally open your store, you can move the convention into an in-store event and rake in the networking and marketing benefits of having a thriving local convention that brings out all the local hobbyists into one place.

      \n So in reading your statement \u201cthis is a place where kids and grown-ups can comfortably shop together\u201d \u2013 I can recommend you talk to Scott and Brian at Seattle\u2019s Blue Highway Games, a boardgame/D&D store just dipping their toes into story games with a Mouse Guard summer camp. In a short time with a very clear focus, IMHO they have created one of the, if not the best, all around, community game stores in Seattle. This is what I see is their customer satisfaction formula (talk to them for the business formula): \n
        \n
      • Family-friendly space \u2013 Clean, bright and warm with a clean bathroom. Sounds obvious, but I\u2019m sure I\u2019m not the only one who\u2019s been in some seriously dark, industrial, dirty, cluttered game stores. No parent wants to encourage their kids or spend their dollars on what looks like the road to becoming a social outcast.
        • \n
      • Community, Community, Community \u2013 afterschool/library programs, summer camps, tournaments targeted at every age bracket and type of game they sell. They go into the community, not just expect everyone to come to them.
        • \n
      • Actively strive to lower barriers to fun. They have an extensive free game library anyone can use, late night open gaming on weekends until 11pm but frequent special events to draw people in that way too. They appear to train their staff to be attentive and to teach the games to the customers as needed. They say all you have to do is show up and you'll have a game to play. As a result of all these things, Fri and Sat nights are consistently packed.
        • \n
      I toured Endgame in Oakland recently, an amazing store with the best indie game selection I\u2019ve ever seen, and they seem to have related philosophies. Both Endgame and Blue Highway have enough real estate for separate retail and play spaces, with the play space in back or above so as not to interfere and distract from the retail.
