\n So I have been working on hacking AW to accomodate roleplaying in Westeros for quite a while but couldn't figure out how to best capture the political, emotional and physical leveraging and manipulation we see in the series, but last week it dawned on me that the answer lies in Monsterhearts. By combining vanilla AW with several ideas from MH I think I may have come up with a solid system to do just that. But perhaps you'll be the better judge?

I took Strings, several moves including "shut someone down" and "turn someone on", and plenty of playbook and sex moves from Monsterhearts and combined them with standard AW moves and rules. I think it reads really well and I am excited to playtest this soon.

I have changed "gangs" to now be "forces" and changed the sizing as follows; Diminutive 15-30 troops, Tiny 50-80 troops, Small 100-140 troops, Medium 200-250 troops, Large 400-500 troops, Huge 1000+ troops Gargantuan 2000+ troops, etc (just keep doubling numbers). I have no idea how this will work out in play, but one more thing to figure out.

I also changed the stat names to be a little more...generic I guess?
Cool= Focus, Hard=Edge, Sharp=Sense, Hot=Grace, and Weird=Wise

The playbooks I have written up so far are;

The Lord - Hardholder type. You get a domain to customize and rule over and a garrision of hundreds of troops to defend your realm. I worry a little about giving a PC that sort of power, but it's also ALOT of responsibility too so I hope it evens out. Inspirations are Eddard Stark and Tywin Lannister

The Courtier - Meant to be of Noble birth, they are a master of manipulation and seduction, the Courtier has a lot of moves to support this. They have options to take a domain like a Lord. Inspirations are Cersei Lannister and Tyrion Lannister

The Man-at-arms - A direct and deadly warrior. Loads of weapons and moves to support killing. Simple and effective. Inspirations include Bronn and Ser Sandor Clegane

The Knight - A mounted warrior with a troop of warriors at his command. A Chopper parallel, the Knight has loads of effective gear, a "gang" of men from 50-100 men-at-arms to throw around. Inspirations are Jamie Lannister and Ser Gregor Clegane.

The Vagabond - An operative parallel. The Vagabond always has some jobs on the go and a crew of men and women to help him get it done. Versatility and freedom give the Vagabond the edge to get by. Inspirations are Jaqen H'ghar, Varys, and Jon Snow (to a lesser extent).

The Maester - Master healer and advisor to Lords and Ladies. The Maester is the best person to keep you alive *when* you are injured or sickly. They also have a move to poison victims for when the need arises. Inspirations are Maester Lewin and every other Maester alive.

The Player - A master of the courtly intrigures, the players move people around like chess pieces. Inspirations include Little-finger, Tyrion and Varys.

The Mystic - This one is a little off. I basically assembled this playbook to allow other playbooks to take a move or two from it to accommodate any supernatural abilities they might want to take. It includes things like prophecy, skinchanging, etc. I doubt I will allow this as a playable class and instead a playbook to borrow from as I said. Inspirations include Mellisandre, Bran Stark, and Jojen Reed.

The Artisan - An artist or craftsmen. The Artisan is all about being the common man who is uniquely skilled in his or her trade. They get a Workspace to ply their trade, some fun commoner moves and the ability to showcase their wares to impress crowds. I am a big fan of how this playbook worked out. Inspirations include Donal Noye and Gendry.

The Scout - Excels in the wilds where most men fail. They have a special move for boosting their moves in the wild. They are super useful to people they escort. Inspirations include to a lesser extent Osha and Arya, but also Jon Snow and most Night's Watch Rangers.

The Blessed - A priest and leader of the common man. The Shepherd has a group of followers who cling to his every preaching word. Rather than restrict the various priesthoods to individal playbooks I combined them all into this one. Shepherds who want more of a supernatual edge can take some moves from The Mystic to really compliment their abilities. Inspirations include any Septons we've seen, The Damphair and Mellisandre.

The Knight - much like The Driver from AW, the knight gains a signifigant bonus when on horseback. A master of the knightly fighting styles, horsemanship and single combat, the Knight is all about defeating enemies in single combat. Inspirations are Ser Barristan Selmy and Jamie Lannister.

I would also like to thank Joe Mcdaldno and Vincent Baker for their amazing games which has directly given me the tools I've needed to make this hack. I hope they both appreciate my passion for this project and that I have their blessings in displaying it here.

    \n Looking over the basic moves you have going, a few other thing stand out:

    The go aggro/seize by force dichotomy works well for some people, but if internet forums are any indication it trips people up a lot too. I think that unless you're going for an exact replica of Vincent's understanding of violence for AW, you might be better served by just one violence move? I feel like there's probably a solution that works well for everybody who might want to use AW for ASoIaF here.

    I thought having both defy danger and hold steady was also redundant at first, but thinking about how many times Martin goes on about "the human heart in conflict with itself" this actually kinda makes sense.

    But turn somebody on does seem like a version of manipulate. Only some of the characters seduce people or turn them on, and when they do it seems like they are directly manipulating them. Or, if they're not, then the lust directed at them isn't actually a benefit. Although that could just be the fictional representation of becoming vulnerable in order to collect strings, a la the Mortal in MH. still, it kinda seems to me like every character has a different style of manipulation or influencing other people, and while the results are pretty much the same, the circumstances, methods, and victims are different.

    If I think about the books as a whole, and specifically the POV characters, I see basic moves being something like this (and this is just me, mind you):

    -- figure shit out
    -- hatch a plan
    -- do something dangerous
    -- fight some dudes*
    -- talk somebody into something
    and
    -- experience Faulknerian inner conflict

    Figure shit out and hatch a plan could both be roll+cunning. Do you need separate moves for reading people and situations? Do you need a list of questions? I dunno, I think maybe you could get by without. Plans, though. They are all over the books! Then again, that could be relegated to a special move for Tyrion or something, maybe, but my gut says no.

    Do something dangerous and fight some dudes could both be roll+dangerous. Although, come to think of it, you don't really need a separate fighting move. It's just doing something dangerous, right? Are there characters who can't fight for shit but who do all sorts of dangerous things? Maybe, or maybe the fighters all have special moves they can use in fights? Maybe instead you have do something dangerous and recover from harm. With a recover move, you can get knocked down and mauled and then be able to heroically pull yourself back up again. Or you roll crap like Tyrion and Sandor did.

    Like I said above, it seems to me like different characters have very different manners or styles of talking people into things. So maybe instead of a third stat, you have several different stats and you choose one, like say: Beautiful, Commanding, and Convincing. Cersei is beautiful, she manipulates people by using that. Otherwise, she doesn't get a bonus to her roll. Ned is commanding, he inspires loyalty in his men and they obey him. He doesn't sleep around like Robert did when he was young and good-looking, though. And Tyrion is convincing. When he has leverage, he moves people around like chess pieces. That leverage isn't anything inherent to himself, though (he's not beautiful, nor commanding), he needs to find it elsewhere. Maybe there should be one for magic stuff? Or maybe that's just convince and when you need leverage you pull out some magic.

    Maybe some characters could get two of those stats? Or maybe once you get a +1 in one of them, you're stuck with it? Dunno.

    But... hmm. You know somebody will want to play the Hound, though, yeah? He's not any of those. He's fearsome, though. Maybe if you take fearsome as your "style" you get -1 to talk somebody into it, but you can roll+dangerous to intimidate people, which puts them at a disadvantage or gives you an advantage against them, going forward. It's like a peripheral move.

    And finally, roll+Faulkner. Is the inner turmoil of characters something that needs to be put to the roll of the dice? You can hold steady in the face of fear and whatnot, but the things that each character agonizes over are different. Jon Snow is never scared to fight--duty's what messes his head up--and only the Hound is afraid of fire. All the characters have different issues... so why not make that a game element? You don't even need to have a stat or roll for it, you could just have a central dilemma as part of your character that's right there for the GM to build off of and make difficult choices out of for you to deal with. If the GM can look at your issue and give you a choice between two equally unpalatable outcomes, great! If the GM can look down at your issue and then introduce stuff that emphasizes it, great!       \n That one's pretty easy, actually, IMHO. I suspect that at least one of the other players are somehow involved in the plot - maybe Littlefinger is a PC - but it's not a necessity.

      I see it as a countdown with Cersei Lannister as the main Threat (Joffrey also being a Threat in that Front, but I'd argue not the main one). Early in the countdown, Robert dies. Later in the countdown, Janos Slynt's Gold Cloaks are bribed and change allegiance, making Ned's presence in King's Landing fraught with danger at any opportunity.

      Ned's player, driven by honour and justice, unwittingly manoeuvres into exactly the position most dangerous to him, one which he has no way out of. A handful of MC moves that definitely apply:

      Capture someone
      Put someone in a spot
      Inflict harm as established
      Turn their move back on them

      Cersei would probably represent a Brute: Family threat (impulse: to close ranks, protect their own) and as such would also provide access to the following suitable MC Moves:

      Make a coordinated attack with a coherent objective
      Tell stories (truth, lies, [allegories, homilies])
      [Rigidly follow or] Defy authority

      Remember everything follows from the fiction. The moment Ned is surrounded by Gold Cloaks in the throne room and his own guard was killed, that was it for him. Everything leading up to that point was countdown and maybe some PC interference.
