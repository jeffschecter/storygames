Some thoughts, tips and ressources on make your work more open using open licences, resusable formats, useful markup and versioning systems.

\n

In a recent SG thread, some of us started dreaming of a game design scene that embraces and adapts various tools coming from the open source software world to the area of game design and game publishing.

\n

In the thread, WarriorMonk asked for a thread to act as a guide and ressource for people who want to try this, so here\u2019s my first try at this. In the course of this \u201cguide\u201d, I\u2019ll mostly follow the levels outlined in my earlier post, ie. going roughly from least-additional-effort to most-value-added.

\n

This guide probably won\u2019t go into questions like why somebody would want to do that, beyond \u201cSome people want to make it easier for other people to use their work in their own. Here\u2019s one way to do that.\u201d.

\n

I'll try my best to give an introduction, but I'd love for people to correct me where I make mistakes and add their own guide and advice below. Let's collect as many guides and best-pratices as possible!

\n

1. Explicit, permissive license

\n

(Disclaimer: IANAL)

\n

The first step here is basially to give explicit, understandable and probably-legal permission for other people to use your work. While in most cases, like \u201cSomebody wants to use my work in their home game\u201d, just publishing your game for free on the internet might be enough, this would still prohibit people from (legally) republishing your stuff or reusing it in their own published work.

\n

So to allow that and give others a sense of legal security in their reuse, an explicit open license is probably the best way to do that. For the kind of work we\u2019re mostly talking about in game design, there are two licences currently in use to consider:

\n
    \n

  • Open Gaming License

    • \n

  • Creative Commons

    • \n
\n

Which licence to use is ... a complicated issue, somewhat depending on personal preferences, wether you yourself are reusing work published under one of those licences, your intended or suspected audience, and so on...

\n

The OGL is (almost) exclusively used in the gaming scene, especially in the subscene of d20/D&D-descedant games. Its often described as complicated and opaque and as such is probably not the easiest to use correctly.

\n

The Creative Commons licences are a family of open licences intended to be used for creative work like text, images, music and so on. It\u2019s widely used both in and outside of gaming, has lots of material published under it and has been translated to a bunch of different languages.

\n

Though I should say \u201cthem\u201d, since there are several seperate licences, allowing for different types of reuse.

\n

All CC licences allow the free distribution of the oroginal, unedited work and from then on vary what other uses they allow and under what conditions. (For more information on how CC licences work, considerations before using them and a tool to help you choose a license check out the Creative Commons website. )

\n

If you\u2019re aiming for maximum freedom-to-reuse, consider publishing your work under a CC-BY license or CC-0 license.

\n

2. Easily reusable/editable file formats

\n

Personally, this one is an even bigger deal than the open licensing: Most rpg publishing today happens in print and/or using pdfs as the digital publidhing format. Both of those make it pretty hard to resuse content, as getting the necessary information out can be a lot of work. Scanning and running OCR software in a printed book is a lot of work, but extracting large portions of text from a pdf file isn\u2019t all that much easier. (Especially with the complicated multi-column layout so common in rpgs.)

\n

So if you want other people to reuse your work easily, publish it using other formats. For text, it\u2019s pretty hard to beat a plaintext file for usability, but html files and its derivates (Like epub.) are nice as well.

\n

If your work relies on image files in addition to text, publish the image files along with the plaintext/html files and/or zip it together for ease of download. Your image files should preferably use a high-enough to allow for print publishing and use some form of losslessly compressable image format like png, raw, bmp, lossless JPEG2000, etc.

\n

For audio ... I have no idea what besides wav and flac one oughta use here, but I guess the people who consider publishing their rpg audio/video in a free/open way know what formats to use best. XD

    3. Added Markup

    \n

    Publishing your stuff as plaintext is great, but chances are it won\u2019t look all that nice and be kinda hard to read. In print/pdf publishing, we use layout to make our work pretty, well-structured and easily readable, but to do so in plaintext world, we need to add some kind of structure or markup.

    \n

    When doing this, we need to consider how our work might be used down the line:

    \n
      \n
    • Will it be used to make print files?
      • \n
    • Incorporated in other work?
      • \n
    • Published on the web?
      • \n
    • Read in the plaintext version?
      • \n
    \n

    To cover as many possible usecases as possible, it\u2019s best to use established and (mostly) standardized markup formats like Markdown or HTML. Both are widely used, so there are lots of tools to import them into layout programs, convert them into other, more specialized formats like LaTeX, XML or Mediawiki Markup or just publish them looking nicely.

    \n

    (To go into more detail, I will now focus on Markdown, as it\u2019s pretty powerful, widely used and easy to write.)

    \n

    To quote Wikipedia:

    \n
    \n

    Markdown is a lightweight markup language with plain text formatting syntax. It is designed so that it can be converted to HTML and many other formats using a tool by the same name. Markdown is often used to format readme files, for writing messages in online discussion forums, and to create rich text using a plain text editor.

    \n
    \n

    Markdown has been in development for a while now and while there are a couple of different implementations of it out there, the core of its syntax has IMO been pretty stable now, with the various versions just extending that core.

    \n

    The basics:

    \n

    Markdown was designed as an easy Markup that converts to (x)html, as such it thinks in similar concepts as html. It\u2019s plain text with bits of syntax added which add meaning to the text and mark it up to signify information like \u201cthis is a link\u201d or \u201cthis is a heading\u201d. (Here\u2019s a link to the original syntax specification.)

    \n

    If you write some plain text, it will be valid markdown, probably a bunch of paragraphs.

    \n
    This is one paragraph.

    This is another.

    This is a third,  
    Which consists of two lines.
    \n

    Chances are you want to structure your text, for example by added headings. Here we once again see that Markdown is thinking kinda like html in that it has six levels of heading:

    \n
    # This is a top level heading

    This is some text under that.

    ## Level 2 Header

    ### Level 3 Header

    ### You can of course have multiple headers of each level

    ##### Or skip some.
    \n

    Markdown also supports a different style for the first two levels which some might prefer:

    \n
    First Level
    ===========

    Some Text.


    Second Level
    ------------
    \n

    Sometimes you might want to emphasize something or make a bold statement. You can do that in Markdown as well.

    \n
    I want to _emphasize_ that people are making **bold choices**!
    \n

    You can do Blockquotes, email-style:

    \n
    Some text:

    > This is a Quote!
    >
    > > This is a nested Quote.
    > > WITH TO LINES EVEN!!!
    \n

    You can list things:

    \n
    Reasons I love lists:

    * Its a reason to use asterisks.
    * Enumeration is fun!
    * Also I\u2019m a deeply forgetful person who relies on todo lists.
    \n

    And of course you can also use links and images. After all, we\u2019re thinking like HTML.

    \n
    I sure love [Story Games](http://story-games.com/forum/)!

    Especially it\u2019s frontpage:

    ![Story Games Frontpage Image, a city drawn in child-book style](http://story-games.com/story1.jpg)
    \n

    I think these cover the basics, you can also try this Markdown Tutorial or check out the Markdown source I originally wrote this guide in.

    \n

    If you have something written in Markdown and you want to use it, there are tools to convert it to other stuff! One of the post versatile seems to be Pandoc, which can understand the most common Markdown dialects (As well as whole bunch of other stuff.) and turn it into html, pdf, epub, LaTeX, wiki syntax like mediawiki, dokuwiki or zim, plaintext, etc.

    \n
    \n

    So now have, potentially, a work, published under an open license, in an easily editable format and know how to add some markup to that to make it look pretty!

    \n

    Next step would be to put it in a versioning system like git and publish it on github or similar sites, but that\u2019s a guide for another day.

    \n The Source Rules of DayTrippers were created this way, right here on this forum. The discussion thread served as a repository and "writers room".

    Another thing to consider is making a distinction between "core rules" (necessary for the system to work) and "optional rules" - a few handfuls of them are included in the DT source documents - which can be selected a la carte by the user (GM/group) and which augment the core rules. You might consider them like "modules" or "plugins" (software analogy). The inclusion of optional rules allows for variant rules which need not be universally compatible with each other, although individually, all are compatible with the core rules.

    \n Ok, I think the git guide will have to wait until I have some more spoons, but I do have some nifty tutorials to try out in the meantime:

    Githubs "Git in 15 Minutes" is a nifty into in the basic commands of creating a repo, tracking files, making commits and undoing them again. (It also has a more advanced follow-up, but I haven't tried that one yet.)

    Git - The Simple Guide is a bit barebones, but again a useful introduction/overview of basic git functionalities.

    Working with git on Windows is just what is says: A Windows-focused look at working with git. Being Linux-only I can't really vouch for this one, but it's still a good contrast, since most of the other guides kinda-sorta assume some bash-like commandline environment.

    Once familiar with the basics, here are two other things to try:

    Git Game is a set of two git-based "riddle" games. You start by cloning a specific repo and then jump from branch to branch following riddles and clues you get by using specific git commands. Relies a bit on programming/compsci knowledge in a place or two, but that's googlable.

    Learn git Branching is a js-based webtutorial focused specifically on branching, merging and moving between commits. This is is IMO one of gits more powerful but pretty opaque aspects, but this tutorial manages to visualize gits graph basis quite nicely. Not necessary at the beginning, but you might want to check it out at some point.

    \n I saw that you forked it (Yay!) but as it said: A fork is your own repo, it doesn't give you access to mine. (It's a spinoff, not the original, if that makes any sense somewhere outside my brain.)

    That said, forking or cloning a repo is the prerequisite for Pull Requests, which is why you collaborate with somebody without them giving you push access to their repo. A pull request is basically a commit/a series of commits which you can send to the maintainer and basically say "Hey, here are some changes I made, please incorporate ("pull") them into your repo."

    Thus: Pull Request.

    If I see it correctly you already tried to create a pull request, but in your own repo. This is, amusingly, possible, but not the way people generall do it. Normally, you interact with your own (forked or otherwise) repo by normal add/commit/push/pull processes, while pull requests are for moving changes between repos.

    So, unless I'm mistaken (Somebody with more Github experience correct me here) the next steps would be:

    (0. Accept your own pull request, so your changes are in your master branch. This is technically optional, since you can have whatever in your master, but it makes things more intuitive, IMO.)

    1. Go to my repo and create a new pull request for the changes in your fork. I will then be poked by Github and proceed to...

    2. Pull your changes, check them out and (most likely) merge them into the repo.

    (If I'm not mistaken I could also just add your repo as a remote to my local repo and pull changes that way, but that's largely invisible, less explicit and probably a bad idea.)

    OK, so I am loving the name Open Design for this!

    \n

    I\u2019m going to contribute a small guide to setting up your own repo for those who have their own web servers. (For those who don\u2019t, don\u2019t worry; use one of the many git services as suggested upthread.)

    \n

    On the server:

    \n

    Create a directory with the name as your repository, let\u2019s say \u201crepo_name\u201d, in your web server root. For me, that root is /var/www but there are a couple of different standards for that.

    \n

    Then, on the remote, do git --git-dir=/var/www/repo_name init --bare (that\u2019s to say; \u2013git-dir should have the full path to your repository directory.)

    \n

    Then back home on your work station, do

    \n
    git remote add origin server_host:/var/www/repo_name
    git push --all origin
    \n

    You can put any one name instead of origin but I think origin is a standard, right? But intsead of server_host you need to put the address to your web server. In my case, idiomdrottning.org. Then after a : you put the full path to your repository directory.

    \n

    Then up on the server again, do

    \n
    cd /var/www/repo_name/hooks
    mv post-update.sample post-update
    cd ..
    git update-server-info
    \n

    Here\u2019s an example. I wrote a novel back in 2006 (the old truism about the \u201cnovel-writing GM\u201d), it\u2019s in Swedish though.

    \n

    https://idiomdrottning.org/magi/ is the webpage for it. But, it\u2019s also the git repo url for it. Someone can do git clone https://idiomdrottning.org/magi/

    \n

    (I wrote it in my own ad-hoc mark up language since I wrote it before markdown came out but include shell scripts that convert it to markdown, both kramdown and pandoc flavors.)

    \n

    Here\u2019s how I set it up:

    \n

    On my server:

    \n
    sudo mkdir /var/www/magi
    sudo chown sandra /var/www/magi
    git --git-dir=/var/www/magi init --bare
    \n

    On my workstation:

    \n
    git remote add origin idiomdrottning.org:/var/www/magi
    git push --all origin
    \n

    On my server again:

    \n
    cd /var/www/magi/hooks
    mv post-update.sample post-update
    cd ..
    git update-server-info
    \n

    Again, the name \u201cmagi\u201d is my repository name, which is going to be something else for you, \u201cidiomdrottning.org\u201d is my server name, which is going to be something else for you, \u201c/var/www\u201d is my web root, which might be the same, might be different for you, and \u201corigin\u201d is arbitrarily chosen but I think it\u2019s a standard name, if I understand correctly, and I recommend also putting in \u201corigin\u201d there.

    \n

    Then, people can import it to gitlab, github or bitbucket or the other git services.

    \n

    That\u2019s why this platform is good, it\u2019s not dependent on any one company.

    \n
    Lord_Minx said:


    If you\u2019re aiming for maximum freedom-to-reuse, consider publishing your work under a CC-BY license or CC-0 license.

    \n

    I\u2019m using CC-BY-SA primarily, with OGL when it makes sense for compatibility. For example, my images are CC-BY-SA 4.0.

    \n

    NC means non-commercial, which sounds cool initially but it can trip you up down the road. ND means non-derivs, but I do want derivs; people can build on it and maybe make it better. SA means share-alike. This is what\u2019s known as \u201ccopyleft\u201d; people can use it, as long as they don\u2019t make it non-open.

    \n

    If you don\u2019t do SA, someone can build on your work and then make something that\u2019s not part of the Open Design. This can happen with OGL too (if they overuse Product Identity, like Dungeonesque did, putting the entire book under Product Identity (not sure that\u2019s legal but that\u2019s what they did)) though.

    \n

    Copyleft vs permissive is a long standing debate and I admit there\u2019s a lot of drawbacks to copyleft. There\u2019s often a tangle of compatibility issues etc. I do fall on the pro-copyleft side of this and I\u2019ve done so for a long time but it\u2019s definitely not a clear-cut question where one side is obviously or strictly better.

    If you\u2019re aiming for maximum freedom-to-reuse, consider publishing your work under a CC-BY license or CC-0 license.

    \n

    I\u2019m using CC-BY-SA primarily, with OGL when it makes sense for compatibility. For example, my images are CC-BY-SA 4.0.

    \n

    NC means non-commercial, which sounds cool initially but it can trip you up down the road. ND means non-derivs, but I do want derivs; people can build on it and maybe make it better. SA means share-alike. This is what\u2019s known as \u201ccopyleft\u201d; people can use it, as long as they don\u2019t make it non-open.

    \n

    If you don\u2019t do SA, someone can build on your work and then make something that\u2019s not part of the Open Design. This can happen with OGL too (if they overuse Product Identity, like Dungeonesque did, putting the entire book under Product Identity (not sure that\u2019s legal but that\u2019s what they did)) though.

    \n

    Copyleft vs permissive is a long standing debate and I admit there\u2019s a lot of drawbacks to copyleft. There\u2019s often a tangle of compatibility issues etc. I do fall on the pro-copyleft side of this and I\u2019ve done so for a long time but it\u2019s definitely not a clear-cut question where one side is obviously or strictly better.

    \n+1 to all that. I also tend to use CC BY-SA and apparently we are of the same mind about the other options.

    To elaborate further:\n
    • choosing CC BY or CC BY-SA means people can always reuse your work without asking; they do have to quote you as the source, of course.
      • \n
    • Adding NC (non-commercial), on the other hand, means people will have to check with you whether you deem their particular use of the material to be "commercial", as that lacks a strict legal definition.
      • \n
    • Legally speaking, you retain the copyright to your work. If somebody asks you directly, you can always arrange something different that what the license allowed for in the first place. You can waive SA for that special friend you trust, say, or waive NC for the team who wants to run a crowdfunding for their derivative game and promises to give you a cut of the money (in fact, I suppose that's what people hope for when they choose NC; unfortunately, that's an entirely theoretical example).
      • \n
    • In practice, I believe CC BY-NC for a role-playing game will end up working the same as Vincent Baker's informal non-license: "ask me whether you can use the AW engine; I'll probably say yes" - though perhaps if you went for NC in the first place you'd OK different things than Vx routinely does (that's your right and privilege).
      • \n
    • An aside: part of my original reasoning for first choosing SA was as an effective alternative to NC. My reasoning back then: the kind of people who would try and make money from my work but not throw a penny my way are, psychologically, the kind of people who wouldn't touch SA with a 10' pole, for fear of the same happening to them.
      • \n
    • If you're really not worried about the above, CC BY (or perhaps CC0, see below) most likely offers the greatest compatibility and widest range or reusability than any license. The actual reasons for using CC BY-SA (including my own insisting on it) are political: sacrificing some potential compatibility for the sake of participating in an activist movement. Truth is, people who have opposing political views might give up reusing your work specifically on the ground of its licensing - any copyleft activist is willing to take this risk, the trade-off being an opportunity to bring in more people.
      • \n
    • @Lord_Minx also mentioned CC0, which isn't a license, exactly, but a legal device to make your work effectively public domain (inasmuch as that's possible). That's a complex topic of its own. For our purpose, however, going CC0 is a bit like waiving the "BY" from CC BY: you want people to be able to reuse your work as they please even without attributing it to you. CC0 is a bit like signing a statement that you won't, indeed, sue anybody for doing so.
