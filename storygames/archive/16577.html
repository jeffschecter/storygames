\n We've talked about forum roleplaying before - about how there are zillions of people playing Harry Potter or horses on the internet, writing long posts and keeping games running for months. "Freeform"ish games.

I don't play those, and I'm not sure why. If you don't play them, either, let's find out why!

I think, for me, there are two things going on:
1. Fixed canon. I like Star Wars, for instance, but not enough to want to just recreate it. I want new stuff.
2. I don't see any experimenting with the form. This is probably 100% due to ignorance, but to me, forum roleplaying looks like it's pretty much just posting stuff in character and nothing else happens.

(I've played other forum games - like Lexicon, which was GREAT, but took forever).

    \n I used to spend a lot of time role-playing on message boards and chat rooms.

    The biggest reason I don't do play by post much anymore is time. Interactions between characters take a long time, particularly compared to playing face to face, by skype or in a chat room. And since players are often on different schedules, threads can take days or weeks or months to play out. Many times this leads people to abandon or forget about a thread before the story has been resolved. In fact, I think I've only played one play-by-post storyline all the way to a satisfying conclusion, ever.

    And then, of course, there are the times when you're the distracted one, and you return to the thread after a day or two of absence to find that two or three players made four pages of posts and the storyline is in a totally different place than where you left it.

    \n I really love PbF but I can tell you why I don't do it more. I can only really keep 2 games going at once. More than that and I just don't remember where I left off in which game and they compete with each other for my break time.

    That said, I've really enjoyed play by forum games. Any back and forth with dice (for example D&D combat) or mother-may-I with the GM makes games really unsuited for PbF.

    \n I had a few really fun forum games way back, but the following problems kept frustrating me:
    \n
    \n1) People losing interest. People are enthusiastic in the beginning but very nearly every forum game slowly dies away before the story is finished. I only ever finished one, where I was GM'ing and holding it together with a personal effort.
    \n
    \n2) People not paying fricking attention. Too many contradictions with what others have written before. (In the games I GM'ed I played watchdog and there was a LOT of OT discussion and editing going on, but it's tiresome.)
    \n
    \n3) People rushing ahead with chat-style posts and finishing entire scenes without other players whose characters were also present getting a single post in. (Again, in the games I GM'ed I played watchdog.)
    \n
    \n4) People losing momentum, writing only one small detail or one dialogue line per post, which is fine every once in a while but if they go on like that for the entire scene, it just takes forever.
    \n
    \n5) People not contributing to the story, not taking the game anywhere, just tugging along (actually not a problem particular to forum games).
    \n
    \n6) Flawed formats. For example, the open forum game where there is one thread per location, so each location has its own timeline and it's really very hard to keep track. Or the one where players have no narrative control whatsoever and need to run everything past the GM as a "can I" question that slows the game down incredibly.
    \n
    \nAll that said, I had some great times in forum games and have never quite given up on the idea of making it work next time.

    \n What about playing PbF games that have a strong turn structure like Fiasco, Panty Explosion Perfect, Ocean, and (of course) The Trouble with Rose. Since play in these games always runs around the table and there is a lot of scene framing, it would seem like a good match. And, given three of these have very finite in-game resources (dice for Fiasco and Ocean, dominoes for TTwR) this would lead to games that have a pre-determined ending condition.

    Lacuna worked without the turn structure because it also has limited resources (heart rate) and a strong mission oriented goal.

    I'm of the mindset that going into a PbF game with no end in sight is almost a promise to fail. That's not to say it can't be fun (I'm in an open ended Wushu game that has been a ton of fun even if we lose players or tempo), but you have to do some expectation management up front.

    \n I tried Play-by-Wave Fiasco and, actually, you forget that dialog is a big deal. After initial framing, I find dialog (and table talk leading to new events in a scene) to be the bulk of "play" in Fiasco. As such, it suffers in asynchronous play. I don't think we got through more than a couple of scenes; but, as with several anecdotes above, that's probably mostly due to lack of commitment, not fatigue/boredom.

    I tried straight-up freeform play-by-post on The Lattice and it fell flat for me, but mainly because it felt a LOT like a bunch of individuals writing individual stories, with little real interaction and almost no roleplaying (meaning "acting in character"). Not my bag.

    I suspect that the PbP/PbF world is coming to an end (at least as much as playing Roguelike games or text adventures has): The ubiquity of Skype, Hangouts, video chat, and virtual tabletops is going to put paid to the "best we can do" solutions those older techniques offered, for 90-odd% of folks drawn to such play environments.

    \n I think Rafu has it right when he points out the parallels to fan fiction. Many forum games I've known, in particular the "open" GM-less ones, were actually just an excuse to write fan fiction that other people would actually (sometimes) read. There was a lot of navel-gazing and mary-sue-ism going on, and not so much collaborative storytelling really. The good ones are more interactive but still, it's all about having fun writing prose. Forum games are not really trying to substitute table-top gaming and face-to-face interaction, at least not the ones I've known. Sure, Google hangouts can do that much better.
    \n
    \nBut! Forum games, if done well, allow hobby writers to flex their writing muscle and share some prose, and to collaborate with others in a fun way. The problem seems to be that most participants don't care so much about others. And also, in most forum games and especially the large ones, many participants are very poor writers indeed.

    \n I think text is a fantastic role-play media. In the same way, and for the reasons some stories make better books then movies. And vise versa.

    Some forms/aspects of roleplay is better expressed in text then verbally or trough body language. Subtle use of language, easy shift between other actions an the character inner life you can do that with monologues, but it often done more neatly and subtly in text, you can sneak in thought in a phrase, word choice or in half a sentence, without the reader getting confused. You can get to see all the different angles of the same scene at the same times, trough different pair of eyes.

    You can have the luxury of time, in in-person role play media, you can't keep other people waiting to long to receive a reply. In text roleplay you can have the luxery of time. To go and dwell on what you character would do for hours, and then take the time ro write a perfect reply. Or you can play as fast as you would like.
