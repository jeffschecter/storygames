    \n I've been running HEX for three or four years now at conventions. The system has some definite advantages. Chief among them, it's extremely easy to teach and stays largely in the background. There are a very small number of quirks that I'm not a fan of, but most of them involve the rules for character creation. I provide characters for my convention games, so my players are insulated from those quirks.

    The other really big advantage of the system -- and this isn't really mentioned in any of the rule books -- is that the grittiness of the system is adjustable. The system is billed as a pulp engine, but intrinsically, it's really not. The thing that makes it pulpy is the Style Points that the GM awards to players for doing neat stuff. If the GM is generous with Style Points, the players can spend them to have their characters do all sorts of larger-than-life things. But if the GM is less liberal with Style Points, the game can become downright gritty.

    The first adventure I ran with the Ubiquity system was a nonpulp, post-apocalyptic scenario. (I had designed the scenario first, and then afterward selected the game system I'd use to run it. Not exactly the textbook way of doing things.) The system supported that theme very well, because I kept the Style Points in relatively short supply. The HEX adventure I'm running this year, on the other hand, is intended to be way over the top. The system supports that style of play equally well, as long as I remember to award Style Points frequently.

    So you have a flexible system that doesn't have a lot of crunch and that stays in the background. Good stuff. Just be sure to get all your players on the same page by letting them know where you want your adventure to fall on the scale from gritty to high action.

    The Hollow Earth setting, on the other hand, is pure pulp and is just freakin' cool. You'll find all of Exile Studios' settings to be wonderfully evocative. Perfect for heart-pounding action and over-the-top stunts.

    - Todd
