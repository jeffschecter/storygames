\n
\n
\n
\n So what I had in mind for the thing on friday: a platoon of American infantry (4th div, 8th infantry regiment, first ground troops on the beach on d-day make their way through the Normandy campaign from the beach to Cherbourg.
\n
\nSystem: The characters start with nothing more than their names, ranks, and hometown listed on their sheets. They get one die to represent their training. All dramatic moments will be decided by rolling dice against a target number determined by the gm (me).
\n
\nDuring act 1 (beach landing) the players can call for a flashback when a conflict has been initiated. This flashback should provide the character with something that can help him survive amidst the chaos (establishing the character with a skill, knack, reason to live, etc.) and will provide a bonus die for the roll. if the character wins the conflict another die will go into Unit Morale (a bowl on the table between the players) to be used by anyone at any time. If the character loses the conflict the player can either choose another character or make up the margin of loss with bad personality traits.
\n
\nexample: Pvt Tom Randall ditches out of the landing craft over the side to avoid machine gun fire, his gear starts to weigh him down in the cold, and much deeper than expected, water. The gm declares this life threatening situation to be a seven. Joe's player cannot make an eight on a d6, so he calls for a flashback where he remembers a happier time in his life training for a swim meet. Joe now rolls two d6. He gets a 2 and a 4 for a total of six, not enough, Joe's player could let poor Joe go to his tragic watery death and take another character, but his player decides to take a bad personality trait, complainer, to make it to 7 and let Joe continue on in the story.
\n
\nBad personality traits will later tempt the characters into bad behavior that will drain Unit Morale. The gm will come up with situations that will bring the bad personality traits to the forefront and the characters will have to pass a self control test to not act on these impulses. Acting on these impulses will allow the gm to remove a die from Unit morale and put it into Hard Luck (a bowl in front of the players).
\n
\nJust as the players may always take dice from the Unit morale to increase their chance of success (before, or after the initial roll). so may the gm take dice from Hard luck to increase standing difficulty numbers.
\n
\nSelfless actions and other good guy type behavior after the first scene will put dice into Unit Morale.
\n
\nAct 1 :The Beach Landing (combat)
\nAct 2:Regrouping (interstitial)
\nAct 3: Releiving St. Mere Englese (combat)
\nAct 4: Downtime (interstitial)
\nAct 5: Hedgerows (combat)
\nAct 6: Before the streetsfight (interstitial)
\nAct 7: Streets of Cherbourg (combat/finale)
\n
\nThanks to John Wick for the concept of Unit Morale (The Thirty) and Fred hicks for the currency across the table thing (Don't Rest Your Head) and Mike and Cam from the Godzilla Gaming podcast for inspiring me to run some military stuff again.

    \n Here's what we came up with at the first playtest.

    I set a static difficulty for each combat scene. 9 for the beach landing, 7 for St. Mere Englese, 8 for the hedgerows, and 10 for the street fighting in Cherbourg. This worked pretty well.

    Assigning the negative morale traits with the difference of failure was broken. It meant that some people had them and some didn't which meant the interstitial acts were broken too. I'm going to have the players assign a negative morale trait right off with a difficulty of three across the board.

    We also had passing a negative morale trait test reducing the value of the trait. Broken. it meant that i had nearly no hard luck to work with.

    I set the scenes in the combats acts and the players set the scenes in the interstitial acts. This worked.

    About midway through the game the players figured out that they could call scenes in the interstitials that were morale builders as well as ones that revolved around the negatives. This sang and filled the morale bowl with dice. Nice.

    All in all, it was a good time, very productive and a few of the player's first experience with narrative structure. They really liked it.

    I am pleased and have quite a lot to work on. Mostly i'm mulling over ideas that get the hard luck bowl about half as full as the morale bowl.

    \n I sat down to write out a draft today, and I realized that I was just not ready. I think it needs a few more playtests.

    As it stands now The act structure works.

    In act 1: Beach Landing the players grab guys from the pool or make guys themselves, this hasn't been formalized yet, but a combination of both is working well, it permits player authorship, but aslo has some back up in case people need a quick replacement.

    Then I begin with some set-up narration. I talk about the choppiness of the channel, the seasickness, and the shells splashing into the water all around. As an interesting emergent behavior, players tend to starts pantomiming checking gear, crossing themselves, recite prayers, Sergeants holler orders that everyone knows by heart by now. I like that.

    The ramp goes down and all hell breaks loose. I pick a player and say something like "machinegun fire is killing everyone." Sometimes I get a flashback right then, sometimes they say something like "I go over the side." and I tell them they're drowning (and they rightly call for a flashback. Lots of swim meets) and sometimes they say something like "I help people over the side." For this I toss some dice into the morale bowl. "Congratulations, you started the ball rolling, you might just make this."

    Once there are dice in the morale bowl players can use them. Once there are dice in the bowl they also need to start watching out for shitty (character) behavior, because i'll pull dice into the hard luck bowl for it.

    Everyone gets a flashback during this act, so the morale bowl can really fill up if players get the idea.

    I'm gonna go do some housework and i'll come back and talk about Act 2 later.

    \n
    \n So in Act 2: Regrouping, I put the survivors into a squad together, let them call scenes with each other, I usually call a scene with an officer and i let a d6 decide if he's an ass (low roll), or a decent guy(high roll). I'm pretty sure this is going to get put in the rules. Really, how the characters deal with the officer can move some dice either way. Bitching and moaning after a decent officer tries his best can take dice from morale and move them to hard luck, and a well placed Bronx cheer after a dickheaded officer moves on can put some dice into morale, so don't let the calibre of officer fool you.

    The gm needs to be really alert during this act as all the scenes are free flow and narrated mostly by the players. Watching for where dice go is the most important part of this act for the gm, massaging the narration into a whole is secondary. If anything the narration duties of this scene for the gm are to make sure everyone gets enough camera time.

    A rough guide for morale and hard luck dice is if something makes everyone crack up in character, toss in a die. If a player fishes for brotherhood by saying he has no smokes and someone gives him some, toss die in the morale die. If no one gives him some smokes, take a die from morale and put it into hard luck.

    I end the scene by briefing them on the situation of the 101st Airborne at St. Mere Englese. I listen for pep talks and whining for final movements of the dice before the next act.

    Just to be absolutely clear, the Bad personality Traits have been scrapped. They didn't work. Rolls in the first Act are truly pass fail. expect some harsh churn if there's no morale flying around.

    Oh, and I've been using Hard Luck dice to add difficulty to individual rolls during combat acts. Don't think of it as punishment, more of a way to cull the morale bowl when it's become unwieldly, but then again if they're a cantankerous group of jerky characters, bad shit will fall like rain.

    \n Here's an overview of acts one and two.

    Act One- set difficulty number (I suggest 9), set the act then call individual combat scenes. The players call their flashback as a smash cut during this scene, and watch to see if dice move to the morale and hardluck pools. Remember that hard luck can only come out of morale.

    Players start with one die that they'll always have to represent their training. they get another die from the flashback.

    Write the title of the flashback on the character sheet. This way it can be called for a variety of reasons instead of one specific one. As an example: for a swim meet flashback, title it "Swim Meet" instead of swimming. That way the players can say "My sweetheart was there, I gotta get home to see her." Another example is "Helping Dad in the Butcher Shop" instead of Used to Blood.

    Act 2: Allow the players to interact free form, there's no rolling here, just freeform that influences the movement of dice in and out of the pools. You can put more than one die at a time into morale, and may move more than one die into Hard luck, but I've found it's ok to be a little conservative, one or two dice per player, but then again, i need to see what happens when there's more.

    Please feel free to ask any questions that come to mind.

    \n This sounds kinda like what I'm doing in Geiger Counter, where I took a lot of inspiration from cooperative board games like Shadows Over Camelot, Lord of the Rings, and Betrayal at House on the Hill. You have an external threat that the group has to gradually build up the strength to overcome, by learning more about themselves and gaining new traits, but with comrades getting killed along the way. I'd definitely suggest taking a look at Knizia's Lord of the Rings, if you haven't already, since it has this sort of serial structure -- Bree, Moria, etc. -- and a varying level of threat and resources for the player group to draw on. Personally, I'm really excited to see another roleplaying game with (at least what looks like) cooperative board game influences. I think there's a lot of potential in this kind of thing. I've been wanting to try to run Carry using Geiger Counter, but this looks like it might work even better...
