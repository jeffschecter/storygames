\n What stuff should we watch, read, play?

1. Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b. Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.

Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page.

Cool?

Cool!

    \n Not new news, but we finally got to play a few rounds of Fabula recently, and it's a lot of fun.

    (There's essentially an old school GM, reading the purple text from the scenario book. Strange Deja Vu feelings.)

    The game includes several beautiful prompts (like Dixit cards, but 9"x7" and thicker) and there's a storyteller who reads a corresponding intro.
    Then there are three "Chapters" where a challenge is described, and each of the players tries to overcome it with the item cards available in the middle of the table.

    And there's an emphasis on doing things in character. So the story-teller asks how the assembled characters will help, and they respond in turns, describing their strategy.

    I chose the Wolf last time, and tried to think "Wolf" thoughts, which didn't earn too many points in the end. (That's okay, still had a good time.) Other characters include Fairy Godmother, Fox, Baby Dragon, Goblin, Knight, etc.

    The two players with the most points at the end of the three Chapters take turns using the remaining cards to sum up an ending, and that's that. Shuffle, repeat. We even played with a 6-year old, who pretty much crushed us.

    \nThe Warren Comics Archive is available online!

    Warren Publishing was an American magazine company founded by James Warren, who published his first magazines in 1957 and continued in the business for decades. Magazines published by Warren include After Hours, Creepy, Eerie, Famous Monsters of Filmland, Help!, and Vampirella. Initially based in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, the company relocated by 1965 to New York City, New York.
    Begun by James Warren, Warren Publishing's initial publications were the horror-fantasy-science fiction movie magazine Famous Monsters of Filmland and Monster World, both edited by Forrest J Ackerman. Warren soon published Spacemen magazine and in 1960 Help! magazine, with the first employee of the magazine being Gloria Steinem. After first introducing what he called "Monster Comics" in Monster World, Warren expanded in 1964 with horror-comics stories in the sister magazines Creepy and Eerie \u2014 black-and-white publications in a standard magazine format, rather that comic-book size, and selling for 35 cents as opposed to the standard comic-book price of 12 cents. Such a format, Warren explained, averted the restrictions of the Comics Code Authority, the comic-book industry's self-censorship body:

    The Comics Code saved the industry from turmoil, but at the same time, it had a cleansing kind of effect on comics, making them 'clean, proper and family-oriented'. [...] We would overcome this by saying to the Code Authority, the industry, the printers, and the distributors: 'We are not a comic book; we are a magazine. Creepy is magazine-sized and will be sold on magazine racks, not comic book racks'. Creepy's manifesto was brief and direct: First, it was to be a magazine format, 8\xbd" \xd7 11", going to an older audience not subject to the Code Authority."

    By publishing graphic stories in a magazine format to which the Code did not apply, Warren paved the way for such later graphic-story magazines as the American version of Heavy Metal; Marvel Comics' Epic Illustrated; Psycho and other "horror-mood" series from Skywald Publications; and Warren's own line of magazines.

    \n Looks like no one's mentioned Peter Borah's "Unsung Tales" yet; I just found it via an ad he placed in the new issue of "Worlds Without Master".\n
    Unsung Tales is a card-based roleplaying game, in which you and your friends will work together to tell stories of mystery and adventure in a Sword and Sorcery world. It's designed to be easy to learn and quick to play, while also having a robust set of rules that ensure none of you knows what's coming next.

    For those of you who care about such things, it's got some interesting features. It stars a single protagonist, and has a unique set of player roles that don't leave any one person in charge of creating the world.

    The game is currently in open beta. This means that I think it's pretty good, but I'm not quite ready to ask for money yet. You can download the PDFs for free, and it's all Creative Commons licensed. The game is really better with a physical set of cards, though, so I'm also giving away free copies of the game, as explained more below.

    \nRisus 2.0, the "20th Anniversary Edition", is out!

    S. John Ross adds some commentary, on the Risus mailing list:\n
    It won't take long to see that Risus 2.0 isn't a huge leap from 1.53 ... it's more _permissive_ here and there (a couple of things that used to be no-nos are now yeah-okays), and it addresses a couple of the most common misconceptions (those that I could address without derailing the flow, basically). Some things that were too literal in earlier versions (like the half-dice stuff) have been amended, etc. The game no longer even implicitly protests serious-game use (though the game's own tone remains basically identical).

    The stick figures are two-toned now, which is so sexy it might physically injure some of you, as it has me.

    The webpage has changed more than the game, really. Dan Suptic over at Risusiverse has taken over maintenance of all those hyperlinks, because I was very very bad at maintaining them and he's proven very very good at this sort of thing ... so, give any Dan Suptics you see a hug of thanks, and there's a good chance it'll be the right Dan Suptic, and if you accidentally hug the wrong Dan Suptic well, that's okay. The world needs more hugs in general.

    Funky Dice isn't gone; it's on the webpage, now. There's a new character sheet, etc. Other things to poke for. There are three deliberate Easter-eggs to be found (because what's Christmas without Easter eggs?), and apparently the game itself may count as a fourth

    Oh, and that mug with all the rules on it is updated, even. So is Ring of Thieves (even the Lulu version), and such.
    \nThere are already edition wars breaking out, about how the new edition is too board-gamey and the Viking class is overpowered, and now we have to buy lots of expensive new book(let)s and custom dice... ;-)

    \n [ASMR 1]

    (oops, ASMR; thanks @Adam_Dray )

    Uggghghhhhhhhh. Wow.

    See, I get AMSR really, really bad. It's mostly triggered by babies(!!!). My friend Matt's baby twins are basically a killer duo of explosive ASMR arm/neck sensation. But totally by accident, nothing like "built up/purposeful" about it.

    I find ASMR videos really, really creepy. It's mostly stuff that doesn't do anything for me. I mean, I don't mean "perverted" or anything (some folks think they are, but that's not what it's about), it's just that most of them involve a girl with a soft voice being super soft, which doesn't set off my ASMR, but gives me the willies. Like she's about to pull out a knife and attack!

    That said, though (and that got kinda bloggy), this is kind of amazing. I knew that there were already some "role-play-y" scenario videos out there, like "nurse", "teacher", etc (and again, not "sexy" or whatever, just a scenario to trigger ASMR sensations in a different way)... but this kinda takes the cake for creative scenarios.

    I now imagine an ASMR scenario where an expressionless elf maiden adjusts your Templar armor; or where the expressionless creepy-calm dungeonmaster softly, calmly walks you through the Tomb of Horrors.

    Holy shit, now I kinda want to make that latter one.
