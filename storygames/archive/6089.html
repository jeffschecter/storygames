    \n I like Paizo as a company but I am very biased. They were probably prepared for this path (the 3.75 PDF must have taken at least 2 months to put together) but ultimately I don't think they had much of a choice. Wizards still hasn't released their GSL license and 4E materials. If Paizo kept waiting, it would stall their product line and seeing how so much of their products are subscription based, that wasn't an option. They could have pushed forward, business as usual but the fact that they are going the extra mile and releasing 3.75 as a free document and an open playtest is pretty awesome!
    \n
    \nThere is quite a bit I like about 4E. But I don't think 4E will support the type of play Paizo's audience expects and the type of scenarios their modules support. From the reaction I've read online and the people I've spoken to offline, Paizo is garnering quite a bit of support. And a lot of the negative reaction is coming from people who aren't their customers to begin with. My guess is that this will be very much financially lucrative for them up to the 4E launch all the way to November 08. Past that I have no idea how things will swing. The fact that they hired Nick Logue to run their organized play efforts only solidifies my faith in their approach. There quite a few RPGA members that are pissed at wizards hitting the reset button.

    Posted By: Saint&SinnerWhat's to stop Paizo from lifting the rules they like from D&D4ed and dropping them in 3.75? You can only really protect setting based stuff right?
    \nThere are no absolutes (contracts and various forms of protection are more like bonuses or penalties to your law roll... but you can always roll a 1!) but it depends on if the 4E mechanics are patented. Which is an annoying process. Copyright, which is easy, only protects the words that express the mechanics. Not the mechanics themselves. Paizo could have mechanics similar to 4E but if they ever got into trouble, they should be prepared to prove that their rules are not derivative work. But it is likely that most of the rules in 4E are derivative themselves. It's tricky. Proceed with caution :-)

    Posted By: buzzPosted By: jenskotBut I don't think 4E will support the type of play Paizo's audience expects and the type of scenarios their modules support.
    \nI've heard other people say this (Paizo included) but I don't get it. How are the rule changes in 4e going to prevent Paizo from writing the same adventures they always have? 4e does all the same D&D stuff that 3.5 did.

    Mostly based on hearsay (which can all turn out to be false once 4E is released), the following factors likely affected Paizo's decision:

    - No backwards compatibility.
    - A huge portion of their audience has spent hundreds if not thousands of dollars on 3.5 supplements, they will likely be slow to upgrade.
    - I know at least in NY, many RPGA members feel frustrated that Wizards hit the reset button. Given that many of them subscribe to Pathfinder and that Paizo is launching some sort of organized play effort, there is likely a lot of money in appealing to this segment of their audience.
    - I'm curious what official 4E modules will look like but given what I've heard about the rules, a module would need to visually detail every single room referenced since using a battle map is now 100% required.
    - Paizo loves fully fleshed out NPCs. NPCs in 4E are now threats and more modular (which I like) but also more generic. I've also heard mixed information regarding how customizable NPCs are.
    - Power levels are completely different. Players are much, much harder to kill. Resource management is completely different.
    - Paizo also loves fluff and color. Mechanically, most of the powers and spells I've seen in 4E are now all 100% combat based. Seemingly, anything with interactivity that can not be replicated via their virtual online environment have been removed. Although I've heard mixed things here. Not sure how true this is yet.

    But most important of all, as I mentioned above, most of this is hearsay. And it is also hearsay from Paizo's perspective as well. They have no real idea what 4E will look like. Wizards hasn't released their licensing information yet nor revealed what the rules look like to their third party partners. It will likely take months for Paizo to adapt to the 4E rules let alone support it with quality material. Many of their sales are subscription based. They can't just stop production and hope Wizards will help them out.

    That all being said, although I think 4E will be a very different game, and not what I necessarily want from D&D, I am excited about what I have read and do plan on buying and playing it.

    \n Monte said as much in his blog yesterday:\n

    Paizo announced today that I'm working with them on their new Pathfinder game. I think it's going to be a lot of fun and I'm looking forward to the finished product. I think the lead designer, Jason Bulmahn, and the whole Paizo crew are doing an amazing job.

    My role is "rules consultant," and I'll be really up-front with you regarding what that means (and doesn't mean). The way it's been working for the last couple of weeks is, Jason emails me with the occasional question or bounces an idea off of me, and I tell him what I think. I also review the new material he comes up with and give my 2 cents. And that's really about it. Jason's not under any obligation to take any advice I might give. It's his baby. He makes the final decisions. I'm not a designer on this project, I'm a resource. Don't, for example, expect to see a ton of stuff from the Book of Experimental Might books showing up in Pathfinder (although there might be a little). Pathfinder and the BoXM books have very different goals. Also, because that's the way my role works, don't come to me with your own ideas or feedback regarding Pathfinder--I'm not going to be able to do anything with them. The Paizo folks, however, are eager to get any and all feedback that people provide, which I think is supremely cool and part of the reason I'm glad to be a part of this.

    Part of me wishes I could be involved more deeply, but to be very honest, I just don't have the time with the other (non-P&P game related) projects I have going on. I feel fortunate and honored that they value my opinions enough to let me be involved at all.

    Jason and Erik have so far been great to work with. Jason and I met for an afternoon a couple of weeks ago and discussed a lot of the stuff that ended up in Alpha Release 2.
