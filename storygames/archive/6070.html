\n So A DS game about the Holocaust (thanks for the pointer, Judd).

From the screenshots it looks like a standard platformer with a lot of didactic educational content in paragraph form, which seems like a missed opportunity. I hope they retool the content and redirect the focus - body counts and macabre spectacles don't really communicate the story of the Holocaust in a meaningful way. I think there's a ton of room for adding pedagogy to games in engaging ways, maybe subtle ways, and I'll be following this closely. What do you think?

    \n Jason, sure, that's one angle to see it from, one possible benefit.
    \n
    \nFor me, I think that it's more purely explorative, however. I like playing these games often for the same reason I liked reading Assimov and Clarke, the sense of "What if?" It's important to me as an investigation into values, and the meaning of our existence. You've probably heard me liken RPGs to the activity performed by story-tellers in "living myth" societies. As ritual, I'm certain that it is cathartic, yes. But the social purpose is to communicate values, and investigate things like, "What would we do if we were slaves?"
    \n
    \nI really know next to nothing about Steal Away Jordan, so I can't comment cogently on it. But if there's a value to the fantasy elements, it's sorta "meta," no? Ever seen the movie "In The Mouth Of Madness?" I mean, not only can we potentially look at what fantasy means to the characters, but also what it means to us. In fact it's unavoidable, in that every character investigation reveals something about ourselves as players.
    \n
    \nMaybe it's no surprise that fantasy worlds are so prevalent and compelling in RPG play. I'll go out on a limb and posit that it's not a random act of fate that resulted in the first RPG being based on a world like Tolkien's. That, in fact, RPGs are self-referential to some extent. To discuss people like ourselves through a fiction like an RPG, we have to discuss it through a world that is, explicitly, a fantasy. No, not always, but it makes sense.
    \n
    \nCheck this out... actual Myths differ from RPG in a particular significant fashion. Myths are about "Heroes" meaning characters who do things on a grand scale that have particular meanings for us. Shreyas has always been obsessed with finding play that's like this. Instead he runs into games like Hero Quest. Which claim to be mythic. But the difference in HQ is that you don't actually play these Heroes. You play people who exist in a "living myth" society who believe in these heroes, and you emulate them. Almost all fantasy play is this way, as are most fantasy novels (I'd argue that Tolkien is a strange mix).
    \n
    \nThe "fantasy" is not that we're the heroes, but that we're living in a world where we believe in heroes, and the supernatural nature of their acts.

    \n Man, this is one of those projects that gets my imagination churning at hearing the broad outline. Seeing the details though, I'm feeling the same way, that the blunt instrument of big text blocks makes it look like it's going to be Super Mario Bros. 15: EAT YOUR VITAMINS. What a shame. There are so many viable angles to come at this from, many of them without even mentioning the holocaust directly, and probably could avoid the censorship the developer is foreseeing, if came at from the right angle.

    Jason, I work for an e-learning company and how to thread educational materials within a game and make it flow smoothly and naturally within that context is something we've been wrestling with. For another bad example, check out Pretty and lame made by the BBC and the Samorost people. It's gorgeous and looks like fun, but after some cursory mouse-hunting interactvities, we're confronted with some blandly formatted multiple choice questions. And it's clear that the multiple choice questions are the whole reason for the course's existence. Hooray! It's like putting whipped cream, sprinkles, hot fudge and a cherry on top of a mouldy brussel sprout. There's no integration of learning and gameplay going on there, but I bet they think they're being clever.

    A lot of games teach something, even if it's not the point. Sim City wasn't primarily designed as an educational tool, but it taught me some broad concepts about how cities work, as a kid, knowledge that's been useful. Said knowledge wasn't even imparted via text blocks. That there are residential, commercial and industrial sectors was a new concept for me and you learned how they worked through experimentation. My hunch is that learning happens more often in the games where education is a byproduct of gameplay rather than the other way around.

    The question of fantasy is a whole other kettle of fish. I'd say it's the sugar on the pill to make it go down easier for kids. This is sort of assuming that kids need games to be fun, engaging or at least not a total bummer. Then again, kids are assigned The Diary of Anne Frank as reading material in schools. A lot of kids find reading assigned literature onerous, but they do it and probably get something out of it if they pay the slightest bit of attention. Could a game be assigned the same way? Could you make a game about the stark realities of the Holocaust without some sort of false veneer laid on top work? Not yet, but maybe some day, I would guess.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarGood stuff, Mikael. How do we translate the good examples to tabletop?
    \nWith an effective, streamlined system customized to the situation / issue you want to explore. But I don't need to tell you that, Mr. Murub.

    Answering that question made me realize why systems that only simulate physics are so poor at providing that pedagogical effect. Even if the group is engaged in collaborative storytelling, it is transparent when an issue is addressed by individual narrative rather than system. When one of your peers raise a possible answer to a question, it is no more effective than if she had raised it while the two of you were engaged in a debate. If the system raises a possible answer, it will be considered less as opinion and more as truth.

    Further, you can test the theory by attempting to reproduce the effect, or manipulate it in other ways, and achieve expanded insight into the subject. This is harder to do with a person presenting a narrative, as even if you ask "What If..." questions they will attempt to stick to their opinion. A system has no such defenses, and will gladly adjust to allow you to explore alternate solutions, which lends further credibility to the message the system implicitly relays to the player.

    As a simple example, the competitive nature and thematic elements in "The Shab-Al-Hiri Roach" seems to drive players towards visiting atrocities upon each other even before the evil elder God starts asserting its influence. This teaches a lesson in human moral behaviour which has made at least one of my friends reluctant to play it with me.

To recreat something like this in a game, you simply develop a bunch of systems that reward the player in one way, and carry a certain risk - to be accurate or educational, you'd want the reward and risk to match the source in a general sense. Adding horomones lets you move cows from the feed lot to restaurant faster, using less food. Benefits are you don't have to dedicate as much towards soy fields and you can keep your restaurant stocked. Downside is that it slightly increases the risk of obesity criticism from the public.

    The ReDistricting Game presents it even more matter of factly, with small controlled scenarios. The first one is a really simple population balacing, then you have to worry about pleasing the majority party, then you have to please both parties, then you have to please both sides and introduce a ethnic representative. Just from the first couple you see how the system is being manipulated. In the final scenario, you see the proposed reform measure and how it balances things out. But the message is pretty clear the whole time that reform is what the game is pushing. And yet it does so by presenting the apparent facts in a straightforward manner.

    In the first case, it's not necessarily something everyone will agree with. Maybe it's speaking to an audience (since I'm in that audience, it's hard to say how effective it is) but, well, a lot of indie games do that already, so speaking to an audience with a message they are going to be receptive to isn't a huge step.

    In the latter case, it's much more populist, and the gameplay really just focuses on how the system works and lets the system be self evident, with only minimal manipulation, on how it is broken and needs reform.

    But regardless of the method, I think the lesson is clear. Whether you're pushing a specific message or simply trying to expand knowledge of a subject, gameplay should contain the message, in a way that offers some flexibility to try multiple paths so you can choose to experiment with changing the system, or dive into it and push the elements even further.

    Edit: I should add that the McVideoGame, unlike the districting game, doesn't really have any method for changing/improving the situation. Like you can't turn your McDonalds into a completely healthy place, or even a little thing like increasing wages - the only way to make employees happy is by firing them and replacing them (which is easy to do, as long as you don't fire too rapidly). But because the other elements are really good, I didn't really notice it until just now. So I think, harnessing the power of table top, it seems best to provide the flexibility of being able to experiment with changing the situation. It brings to mind SimSocieties, at least in theory*, where you can experiment with different social models.

    *The reviews pretty much panned it, so I'm not recommending it as an example.

    \nI got the impression the McVideoGame essentially allowed you to choose between running a fast-food chain that ranged between mostly harmless (and having to ignore your share holders) and freakin' Godzilla (and having to ignore the public's screams of outrage). I assumed that not allowing the player to reach that state of harmonious bliss where everyone's happy was a very conscious choice. This has its own set of pros and cons. It is relatively easy to see through the illusion that this is a perfect simulation and read the message inherent in the design, which lessens the potential impact of that message. On the other hand, if the simulation was approaching perfection, the game would not make its point as readily, as players would see it as their goal to elevate their company to divine status rather than explore the hellish impact of running the company as the corporation the game is modeled after.

    Perhaps the best alternative would be to aspire to a perfect simulation, but somehow rig the system to ensure the player would naturally attempt - at first - that wide path that leads to easy money and eventual disaster, rather than the narrow, virtuous path. Tricky balancing act, that.

    Also, very good point about these games preaching to the choir. Who's going to play the McVideoGame? People who take their Hummer through the drive-thru a couple times a day? Hardly. People hovering around the poverty line, who apparently constitute a considerable part of McD's market? Unlikely. Wannabe-hippie middle class office workers who plays it on their "extended lunch break" at their cushy unionized job after chewing down their home-made granola and carefully saving the remainders for composting at home? Yep, more likely. And what impact does that actually have?

    Yet, thinking about games like "Steal Away Jordan" (which I have yet to play, sadly) - sure, none of us actually think slavery's a good idea, but I can't help but think a game like that could still teach us (predominantly white males) a thing or two about the world. I guess it comes down to nuance. Make sure you have a message, make sure players receive it during play, but don't shove it in their faces.
