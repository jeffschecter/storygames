    \n Melingor, Per - Mostly handling time and the greater likelihood of talking back and forth about the dice before getting back to the imaginary stuff. Here's my taxonomy of clunkiness.

    So the least clunky would be: Roll 1d10, did you get 1-5? You win.

    Next is: The GM says a number. Roll 1d10, did you meet or beat it? You win.

    Trivial addition: The GM says a number. Roll 1d10, did you meet or beat it? You win. If you rolled a 10, you win BIG. If you rolled a 1, you lose BAD.

    Next: The GM says a number, roll 1d10 and add a number from your character sheet. Did you meet or beat it? You win. If you rolled a 10, you win BIG. If you rolled a 1, you lose BAD.

    Next: The GM says a number. Roll Xd10 where X is a number on your character sheet. How many dice came back 1-5? Is that more than the GM's number? You win!

    Next: The GM says two numbers. Roll Xd10 where X is a number on your character sheet. How many dice came back bigger than the GM's first number? Is that more than the GM's second number? You win!

    Next: The GM rolls Xd10 based on his GMing sheet. This gives him two numbers. Roll Xd10 where X is a number on your character sheet. How many dice came back bigger than the GM's first number? Is that more than the GM's second number? You win!

    Next: The GM rolls Xd10 based on his GMing sheet, plus dice for a few other things that come up. This gives him two numbers. Roll Xd10 where X is a number on your character sheet, but add some extra dice based on circumstances that the GM tells you about. How many dice came back bigger than the GM's first number? Is that more than the GM's second number? You win!

    Next: The GM rolls Xd10 based on his GMing sheet, plus dice for a few other things that come up. This doesn't give him anything, because he needs to see your roll first. Roll Xd10 where X is a number on your character sheet, but add some extra dice based on circumstances that the GM tells you about. Who had the highest single roll? How many dice came back bigger than the other side's highest roll? They win!

    Next: The GM rolls Xd10 based on his GMing sheet, plus dice for a few other things. This doesn't give him anything, because he needs to see your roll first. Roll Xd10 where X is a number on your character sheet, but add some extra dice based on circumstances that the GM tells you about. Don't count ties for the highest roll. Who had the highest single roll? How many dice came back bigger than the other side's highest roll? They win! Oh, unless you forgot about a modifier, in which case it might screw the whole thing up, because you roll that, get the highest number now, and it turns around. Wait, should we start again?

    \nI think this is the problem with "rules light" games. If you play a game where the core dice mechanics are boring and uninteresting than you are motivated to use them as little as possible and wind up spending most play in free form talky silliness. Games should have mechanics where the mechanics themselves are interesting enough to captivate attention and be something desireable to use in their own right...not as a distraction from play...but as a fun thing to actually do in and of themselves. Much of the demand for light rules is really a demand for interesting rules worth using.
    \n
    \nThe problem with interesting rules is that they have a learning curve to them. Master the learning curve and the clunkiness aspect goes away and what you are left with are rules that are actually fun to use that you'll want to use frequently because they are fun in and of themselves.
    \n
    \nIn my view clunkiness as a term should refer to rules that have a multitude of steps but in the end produce no more interesting interaction or results than a simpler system. The term should not be used for rules that have an extraordinary depth to them but which the players just haven't taken the time to master the learning curve.

    2d6+X vs. Y

    GMs state Y, players roll the 2d6+X.

    \nThere are a few situations and special cases where it may not be instantly obvious how to interpret a result, because there is a good bit going on when the dice hit the table, and those are the situations that generate questions on the Sorcerer forum. Interpreting the frequency of those questions as indicating a high level of complexity, really is inaccurate. Most of the time its an incredibly quick and simple means to resove complex goals. Then you resolve those actions in the order of the rolls. Almost any combination of results is possible.

    \n Hey, Ryan:

    To answer your question about 2d6+X vs. Y, you'll have to do some number-crunching to see whether you can get a similar range of results (and how will you handle bonus/penalty dice? Add too many and the underdog can't win anymore).

    However, it's been my experience that, for many players, adding three numbers (your two dice plus your modifier--and there might be several of those, right?) is actually MUCH SLOWER than simply having to call out your best result. Depends how fast you are on the math, of course, but, as I said, "for many players".

    Sometimes, "pick up an extra die" is much easier than "add X to your existing three numbers in your head, and be sure to remember which was which, etc". Consider, for instance, that you have to remember all those modifiers when the fifth player's turn comes up in a hot and sticky fight, as opposed to just picking up and rerolling the dice. Instead, you have to remember all the relevant modifiers, bonuses and penalties based on previous rolls, etc. In addition, you can't just look at someone else's dice and see what their highest die is--you have to know what the "+X" parts are before you can do that, which means more mental arithmetic as opposed to just looking at a number.

    So, to answer your own question, you might need to try both with a varied group of players and see how they take to one method or another.

    \n Ah, we're criticizing the Sorcerer dice mechanics in general. Let me go on record saying that Sorcerer has one of the most elegant, flexible and powerful base dicing mechanics that I've ever seen. The math is impeccable, the execution is simple and the possibilities for variation endless. Many of my own designs, including Zombie Cinema, basically just swipe the Sorcerer mechanics, and I'm not exactly alone in this.

    But, to get back on topic: if you can't stand how Sorcerer does things, you just need to find another mechanic that gives you proportional resolution (the odds of value X beating value Y are X:Y) and a range of success that gets proportionally lower with larger ability scores. You could get pretty close by rolling a d20 against a table like so:\n
    result < X+1Ability X wins
    X < result < X+Y+1Ability Y wins
    X+Y < resultReroll and get it right
    (For small values of X and Y, roll a smaller die - as long as the die size is larger than X+Y, this'll work.) Then, after you've figured out which player is going to win, roll a pool of d10 equal to the winning ability value and pick the lowest single value - that's the margin of victory.

    The above is, of course, not nearly as elegant as the actual system, and it's only a rough approximation as is. But it's pretty difficult to make a system that does the same things the Sorcerer mechanic does, but simpler.

    \n Also, it's important to point out that the Sorcerer dice mechanics can't really be analyzed without looking at the larger context.
    \n
    \nThe game gives you a significant mechanical advantage for putting stress on other players & NPC's, in the form of 2 bonus dice. This is very important when you're in a do-or-die crisis: when things start to suck for one player, they very rapidly start to suck for everyone else.
    \n
    \nAlso, because of the rollover-dice trick, players are encouraged to create little preparatory scenes for the big do-or-die moment. Oh, I need to punch Satan in the face? Well, I guess first I need to convince the Pope to let me bathe my hands in the blood of the Holy Grail. And in order to convince the Pope to do that, I need to win some other conflict over here first.
    \n
    \nBetween these two rules, the Sorcerer dice mechanic is also a player-driven pacing mechanic.
    \n
    \nAlso, because Sorcerer allows you to roll any score's dice against any other score's, it's possible to have these internal monologue flashbacks (Humanity vs. Stamina, for example) which generate a bonus to a Stamina roll later on - you also have this strong incentive to show the character's inner life and past in the game: stressful conflicts, by necessitating rollovers, can bring out these hidden details and inner struggles of the character as well. So it's also a player-driven characterization mechanic.
    \n
    \nNow: it's true that Sorcerer's text is thick as mud. And it's true that some system might do all of these things as well or better. But to do all of it with a single throw of the dice isn't how I normally interpret the word "clunky." (I'm not saying one shouldn't try to improve on this system! Just: be aware of what the system is really doing. Simply deciding who wins or who loses in a conflict isn't the most important thing the dice do: it's setting up fractures and stress points for the emerging fiction.)

    \n I. . .I guess if you say have what you want, Ryan, then Godspeed and goodnight. Personally, I don't see much advantage in Alexandre's proposal over the original rules. I guess if having to compare the two rolls is the sticking point, it does eliminate that, but then, it adds the step of comparing successes afterward, where Sorcerer has the comparison all bound up in the original "accounting" of the roll.

    I certainly would use Alexandre's hack before I'd try any of these other weird proposals with the roll plus modifiers or the D20 table or what have you. The Alexandre method is something of an approximation of the real mechanic, and as he points out, you don't have to change the surrounding rules. It doesn't seem to address any of your fears about forgetting to add in bonus dice and whatnot, but I've already explained how I don't think that's much of a worry (Ralph's point about the Free and Clear is also relevant there). I would caution that the 4-6 Success method technically allows you to generate "successes" outside of actual opposition in the fiction (i.e. an opposed roll), which is meaningless in the original dice rules. If you do go with the alternate, I'd be real careful about letting that concept creep leak into the game--a Sorcerer roll should always be an opposed roll.
