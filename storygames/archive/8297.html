\n I see this all the time. I do this all the time, too.

Someone asks a question, and someone else answers with a reference to this great game. It's focused, or does this great new thing, or is just intensely fun - and it's made by their friend.

The game probably is great, too! And in the current deluge of great role-playing game designs, no wonder that nobody's heard of it. Still, though, as a recommendation, it's only one step up from "My game is great!" To me, it says "in this tiny bubble of gaming culture that is our personal circle of gamers, between 2 and 10 people are enjoying this game." If I'm not part of that bubble, the recommendation doesn't help me all that much.

Not saying people should stop recommending their friends' games. I'm going to continue doing it, as long as those games have merit (you can check out my past posts on Story Games to see what I write about Itras By, Until we Sink and Muu, for example). But just wanted to post this as a general observation.

Any opinions? Do you care who recommends a game? Does it matter whether they're a friend of the designer or not?

    \n It's a good point, sort of. You certainly see circular back-patting and pimping from groups like the Collective Endeavour and the Play Collective, pseudo-business entities with some quantitative interest in mutual success. Of course they are also all friends, and they are also generating quality games, too. Nobody is trying to fool anybody.

    I do this too, and I'm far more likely to take notice of, play, and praise a game written by someone I know, trust, and like. Who isn't? And people you like probably like you too, and the mutual admiration society is born. I'm keenly aware of this and understand to some degree my reputation rests on my practice of not saying things I don't mean. If I say "Anna Kreider's Polaris hack Thou Art But A Warrior is fantastic", I mean it - even though Anna and I are also best pals and she's said the same thing about my games.

    So, shrug. If you're worried that marketing is getting mixed in with honest enthusiasm, don't buy games you haven't played and the problem is solved. If you can't do that, ignore the advice of people you don't know, trust, and like.

I figure in a crowded market with limited play time, investing in a game by playing it is probably a good indication that the game has something to it and is worth sharing.
    \n

I'm not a fan of this game can do everything because it is my favorite game!
    \n

Being friends with the creator, having playtested, and any business relationships (for money or volunteer which is none at the moment except for the playstorming crew). They're my community and we support each other like local bands support each other, like I support my local brewery. Not just because they're local, but because their games, music and beer are spectacular and I don't need to keep it to myself. I never recommend something that I am not genuinely enthusiastic about

    \n

    Disregard my recommendations of games by Vincent Baker, Emily Care Boss, Rob Bohl, Clinton R. Nixon, Malcolm Craig, Judd Karlman, and yes, Jason "I'm so tortured about my good reputation" Morningstar et al. to your own detriment.

    \n

    That doesn't mean I won't recommend Agon, even though I don't know John that well, when competitive beheadings are what's called for. Not one bit. But this is a community. When someone's made something I like, I go meet them to see what it's all about. Agon looked neat, so I met John. We live far away, so we're not that close. I'm still going to recommend his game. Likewise, Eero lives in Finland, but I'm still enamored of Zombie Cinema and I'll tell people all about it. I don't know James Wallis, but The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron M\xfcnchhausen is a ball and a half of a game to play, and I'll let people know.

    \n

    I don't recommend games for a number of reasons. "Not someone I know" is not one of them. There are some games I don't understand, some games I don't enjoy, some that I think are broken. If we're talking about things that don't work, we'll discuss that, but it's not where I expend most of my energy.

    \n

    There's no separation between "creators" and "people you know." This is the 21st century.

    \n

    The day that people stop recommending their friends' games is the day when I am fucking done with this community. That's the day it stops being a community and just becomes a bunch of hobby fora where people talk about "publishers" and "designers" like they're a distant category of creator-aliens.

    \n

    Talk about your friends' games. Never lie. Talk about the things that excite you. Tell about games you've played. That's the core of this community.

    \n There are two sides to this particular issue.
    \n
    \nThere are those who recommend their friends games, and those who read those recommendations.
    \n
    \nOn the one side, I'm not good friends with any of the designers in this community with the sole exception of Alexander Cherry, whose Fastlane I recommend whenever appropriate because I think it is a great game, and because I feel it doesn't get the attention it deserves. I do not recommend Snowball, Ensemble or any of his other projects because they do not interest me, or because I have not played them. I have no basis for recommending them or calling them great.
    \n
    \nOn the other side, I don't know that I care if you're friends with the designer. I generally only take seriously the advice of people I respect. I don't have to know you, but I, like everyone, am constantly observing and judging. If someone constantly acquits themselves well in public forum, writing well-written and thought-out posts, not being an asshole when things get a little high-tension, then eventually I'll form the opinion that they're a pretty squared away person. When they recommend something, I'm much more likely to listen. Especially if they express opinions about games that I know that I happen to agree with.
    \n
    \nSo.. yeah. This is a non-issue with me, on both sides. I'm not going to give a disclaimer when I recommend a friend's game. If I've acquitted myself well, you'll respect my opinion, or you won't. I won't expect a disclaimer when you recommend your friends' games, because my opinion about your recommendation will not be based on your relationship with the designer.

    \n I don't care about the relationship between whoever is recommending a game and whoever wrote it; honestly, if that has any tiny effect whatsoever on how I receive that recommendation, it is thoroughly drowned out by the effect of my relationship to whoever is making the recommendation.

    If my closest friends tell me something's awesome, I believe them implicitly and anticipate sharing their enthusiasm. If a casual acquaintance tells me something's awesome, I listen charitably but don't necessarily think I'm going to end up agreeing with them. If it's just someone I know from the internet saying something is awesome, pfft, whatever; they have to win me over with reasons and evidence, because I tend to just ignore their enthusiasm outright. Maybe on some level I just figure that what they think is cool is likely to be nothing like anything I think is cool...I know it's got nothing to do with their reliability or objectivity as individuals, because in general, I'm sure that people on the internet are just as reliable and objective as anyone I know in real life. No, it's purely a matter of me only giving weight to opinions that come from sources I know very well and who in return know me very well.

    So I don't mind if someone wants to tell me that their friend's game is great. They don't even have to tell me that it's their friend's game, unless they really want to.

    \n When someone recommends a game to me, it's usually as a response to something I said I wanted.

    \n I don't care about the relationship between the recommender and the designer, I care why they are making the recommendation.

    If you recommend your friend's game because it is great, or because it fits the topic, or because it perfectly fits my need, then that's great. If you recommend your friend's game because you want to pump his reputation and get his game mentioned, appropriate or not, that's not great. The same applies if you substitute the words "club", "group", "gaming organization", "company", or "hero" for friend.

    It's the same reason that people tend to distrust a designer's recommendation of their own game, because there is a suspicion they may be doing so out of bias. Which can be a real pain when the designer's game is the perfect game for the question.
