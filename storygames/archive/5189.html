\n This idea is bothering me. Help me kick it around.

So it's a Colditz roleplaying game. One player plays the Commandant, Colonel Schmidt; the others the Prisoners. The Commandant player leaves the room, while the Prisoners hatch an escape plan. Then the Commandant re-enters.

Over the course of the game, the Prisoners and Commandant play out life in the castle. The Commandant plays the guards. The Prisoners go to the library, have breakfast, help at the theatre, have showers and so on.

While this happens, the Commandant allocates Guard Dice to each other player. These represent how closely the other prisoners are observed: they represent the Commandant's suspicion that a Prisoner of War is not, say, just building theatre sets but is planning to steal a hammer. Or is not just in the library, but planning to dig a tunnel.

When the Allied players want to do something to break the rules of the camp (stealing equipment, digging a tunnel, absconding), they reveal it publicly and roll dice of some kind. The Commandant rolls the Guard Dice allocated to that player. If they win, they win (they might scale a wall or get some equipment). If not, they don't.

It's a rough idea. How far does this get us? Anything to add? Obviously equipment needs to be a part of it. So do roll-calls. So do escape attempts.

Graham

P.S. In my head, I am writing this game for Jason Morningstar, because it's all historical and shit. Hence the reason the Commandant has a name and it's playable by three players.

    \n Yeah, my first thought echoes Darcy's. There need to be objectives, either distributed or in sequence, that end in escape. When the guards bust you, the plan gets pushed back, delayed, or completely scrubbed. maybe degree of failure/discovery dictates that. Maybe there's a loose template that the escape plan fills in:

    Get the THING to make the OTHER THING that allows you to get past the PERSON and arrive at the LOCATION.
    Convince the PERSON to give access to the LOCATON to get the THING that will get you the OTHER THING.

    That sort of thing.

    I love the idea of a competitive game that relies of player vs. player secrecy and suspicion - the Commandant player is actually watching his friends as their characters go about routine events, judging which ones are busy trying to escape. "Another die for you, Steve!"

    Also, Prawitt would be a better Commandant to model.
