This month's Player's Handbook 3 debut content introduces skill powers. These are utility powers that characters qualify for based not on their class but by virtue of their training in a particular skill. Skill powers are available to every character in the game, whether it's a fighter built using only the Player's Handbook or a genasi hybrid invoker/wizard using content from a wide range of resources. All of this content comes directly from the pages of next year's Player's Handbook 3, and you get to see it months before everyone else: just another of the great benefits of being a D&D; Insider!
\n
\nA skill power is a way for a player to make a skill important in whatever situation he wants. If you want your high Diplomacy character to feel like a charismatic leader in battle, just pick out a combat-useful Diplomacy power. With a skill power, you extend a skill into whatever situation that power applies to.

    \n One example is Acrobatics:
    Stand Up
    At Will Utility 2 Minor Action
    You stand up.

    This means it is no longer a move action for someone is trained in Acrobatics to stand up from being prone.

    I think it isn't core concepts being expressed, but more adept expressions of core concepts if you chose to focus on them.

    Another:

    Arcane Mutterings
    Encounter
    Substitute Arcana for a Bluff or Diplomacy check.

    \n As a head's up to folks who can't see them, the skill powers are (for the most part) typical utility powers which focus on the themes of a particular skill and require training in that skill to have - rather than being class dependent.

    For example, Cry For Mercy - a Diplomacy Utility, which as a Close Burst 5 lets you bolster one bloodied ally (or yourself) by making movement not provoke and giving a bonus to defenses for a turn.

    Another example, Improvisational Arcana - a Bluff Utility where you fake a ritual using your Bluff skill and half the components (and explicitly doesn't apply to Brew Potion or Enchant Magic Item).

    I'm of several minds on these. On one hand, I feel that 4E seriously needs more ways to link the combat-verse and the skill-verse, which these begin to do. But I don't think they go far enough.
