    \n If you like Fellowship FOLLOW, you'll also like its slightly more complex "elder brother", Kingdom (also by Ben Robbins). I suspect Dog Eat Dog (by Liam Liwanag Burke) might be of your liking too.

    Microscope (Ben Robbins) and The Quiet Year (Avery Alder) don't have you play any single named character, but rather history or a community as a whole.

    In House of Reeds (Sam Kabo Ashwell) players portray all of the people dwelling in a house - possibly, successive generations of people. In Misericord(e) (Emily Care Boss) the Guilds of the eponymous town are in a sense the main characters and each player champions a Guild.

    In The Drifter's Escape (Ben Lehman) one player is the Drifter and plays a single character, but another is the Devil and plays all evil or evil-controlled characters, while the remaining players collectively play as The Man. In Amazons (Vincent Baker) all but two players are World Players and collectively run the world the two Amazons encounter.

    There are also games with two "GMs" who are responsible for different parts or aspects of the game world: The Dreaming Crucible by Joli St. Patrick (the Light Fairy and the Dark Fairy), How We Came to Live Here by Brennan Taylor (an "inside" GM and an "outside" GM), They Became Flesh by Elizabeth Sampat (Humankind player and God player).*

    * = gosh, this last one is impossible to find a link to... I have a PDF from supporting the 2011 Kickstarter campaign, but I have no idea how one could manage to buy a copy today).

    \nHere I am, at last. So, her recipe for 3-player Mars Colony is: one player is the Governor of Opposition scenes, one is the Governor of Personal scenes and one is the Savior. They all take turns framing scenes of their type, in a regular round-robin fashion: one Governor goes, then the other and then the Savior. The Savior player still has the option of choosing whether to frame a Personal or a Progress scene - for a convention environment or other time-limit context, though, it's recommended the Savior chooses a Progress scene at every opportunity - while having a Governor in charge of that ensures there's still plenty of Personal scenes in a game nonetheless.

    In practice, the line between Personal and Opposition can easily become blurred - that's OK. In regular 2-player play it's obviously a non-issue, since both scene types are governed by the same rules. In this variant it may look like an issue, because different individuals should be framing the scene in each case... Just don't sweat it: start each scene as legit and appropriate to its kind, but then, just allow it morph into whatever kind of scene it happens to be.
