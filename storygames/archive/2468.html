Has anyone seen indie or "story-game" style rpgs on the shelves of a brick-and-mortar store? If so, what kind of store was it?

    Jon's pretty much right, in that an educated and enthusiastic staff will sell more books than when they just sit on the shelf. That's a general thing.

    I've worked at a full-on games store, and I've been spending a good deal of time in another one over the last couple of months, so here's some observations from that perspective.

    I think what we see in "traditional" game stores is that part of the customer base is fairly ossified - there's a solid core of customers, who tend to be in the older gaming demographic (late-30s through late 40s, maybe?), and who know what they want. They come in to buy the latest WotC or WW release, or fill out a Warhammer unit, and they may not even browse what else is on the shelf.

    The other big part of the customer base is younger (sometimes very young) kids, like elementary and middle-school age, who play whatever CCG is big at the time. They come in with their parents, who in general are not interested in gaming, by booster packs, and many of them play in instore tournament events. My sense (from when I sold a lot of Yu-Gi-Oh), is that there's a lot of individual turnover, but the general sales level for the product overall is fairly constant over the medium-term.

    So, again talking about a "traditional" games store (which is a preface to what I actually want to talk about, I'm getting there!), the customer base for Indie games tends to be people going to the store to get those games, people who set up events at the store in order to raise awareness and their audience, "hardcore" customers companions who browse and see something interesting, and some "hardcore" customers who have something catch their eye, and are either curious in general, or are burned out on their game of choice (I think Burning Wheel does a good job with this demographic).

    All of which is to say that the "traditional" games store is not, I think, a good model for supporting indie games sales. Now, it sounds like your friend is interested in developing a community of play around her store. This is exactly how indie games would fit into that business model. Many of them can be played out to satisfaction in one night, and there is such a breadth of genre and game focus that an educated sales staff could tailor game events to the mix of people who show up to them.

    Personally, I would imagine that having an organized actual play system in place, run by either store employees or dedicated volunteers who are familiar with and educated (and passionate!) about the games, would be the best and most sustainable way to include indie games in the kind of store you outline above. Think about having "theme" nights that link board games and story games by genre or theme, where the store offers (say) Shadow Over Camelot and Paladin; or Arkham Horror and Conspiracy of Shadows; or the Lord of the Rings board game and a Burning Wheel LotR scenario.

    Whew. I hope there's some food for thought in there....

    I sell about half as many games through stores as online. I have it on good authority that Polaris and The Mountain Witch sell about the same as online. They're a year older than Shock: and were selling about like Shock: does a year ago.

    What that means for me is that a certain number of books go out that otherwise wouldn't have that still get me a percentage. It's therefore useful when selling though IPR to have games in stores. Distributors, near as I can tell, will fuck you sideways six way to Tuesday and then tell stores that they're out of stock.

    So, given that this kind of thing is a viable business model at the retail end, I can see it being a viable outlet for IPR sales. Building actual culture around our games is what sells them. Nothing is a substitute for real enthusiasm, and having a place to be enthusiastic about something right there on the shelf that I can buy right now is probably an experiment worth pursuing.

    I say this from my own, totally biased standpoint, that I want more people to play my games, and it benefits every mutualist one of us when someone buys one, because they'll buy another one, too. But my biased view doesn't include a perspective as a retailer. I don't have any idea how (or even if) retail works.
