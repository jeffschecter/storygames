\n The setting is generally acknowledged as a central element of the games we play. Some games make without it, or; they let the players make it. But most games are sold with a setting. The setting normally works as a framework for the identity and actions of the characters, from which the fiction (narrative, drama) spring forth.

The above is only to give some basis for a discussion on how we make such settings functional.

Here's my questions:

- How much setting is too much? How little will fail to support the players?

- What are the tricks and tools available to us as designers? How do we write our settings? How do we organize the elements of the setting? What are the different kinds of setting-elements we have at our disposal?

- How do we get players to use our setting? How do we present it? What is the relationship between setting and method (system, rules, mecanics)? How do the characters fit into the picture?


A lot of big questions, I know, but still; I hope it can be a sound basis for a talk on this side of role-playing games. Lets try to keep the talk open, guys, so we may freely take the insights presented to heart, and give back in kind. ;-)

    \n I like making setting material. I enjoy researching, making maps and documents, and breathing life into it. I've learned that this sort of foundational pre-play can be valuable, but requires a gentle touch - everything you create is fair game for other players to re-contextualize, re-interpret, and ignore completely. So I'd say "getting players to use your setting" is a little off-kilter; it is their setting as well, and they'll use it however they like.

    I know there's a wide range of preference in the volume, detail, and application of setting material. Here's a good example - I recently wrote a Trail of Cthulhu adventure, and a playtester was unhappy with it because I didn't provide a map of a hotel the investigators are likely to stay in for a night. At my table, that level of detail is unnecessary; I'd just whip up a freehand sketch if it became relevant, which it probably wouldn't. But at that guy's table, the layout of the hotel was important and its absence was felt.

    \n
    \nI've slowly began to think that having a great premise and some cool details can really allow players to explore the possibilities of a setting through their imaginations. It's as though we provide a lens through which they look at the world and the experiences they wish to create.
    \n
    \nAs of late, one of my favorite techniques for creating a setting has been emulating Asimov's encyclopedia entries in the "Foundation" novels. For those unfamiliar with these, they are basically subtle hints at how the story unfolds from the perspective of a researcher/writer from the future. Of course, this can be problematic if you don't want to script an ending before you start, so I tend to emulate the writing style more than the foreshadowing element. Just enough to hint at a more lush, well thought out setting than I actually wrote down. I'll keep the minute details in my head, that is for certain, but unless it is critical to the way the game unfolds, there is no need to make the setting too rigid by actually writing it down for EVERYONE to see (it's usually enough if I keep it hidden in my notebooks!).

    \n I think any discussion of setting needs to first start with scale and a firm understanding of why you're designing a setting at that scale.

    There's nothing more annoying and more wasteful than designing a continent spanning setting filled with thousands of years of history and several pantheons of gods and then expecting characters to start off as the proverbial level 1 farm boy.

    IMO the scale of your setting should match the scale of your characters. If your setting is filled with dynastic history, sweeping wars, and pantheons of gods...then your starting characters should be dukes and kings and bishops and popes. If your starting character is a farm boy dreaming about life outside of his village, then your setting should be his farm stead, his village, the neighboring village, the near by market town, the castle of the local knight, and maybe rumors of what the closest big city is like.

    Star Wars, I think hit the right scale here. We don't need to know what Tatooine was like, just the parts Luke was from and important to their travels. Each planet they encountered was boiled down to its requisite role in the overall story. They didn't interact with the entire planet of Endor...just the part around the shield generator base and the particular terrain and people who lived there. I think later material got carried away with the need to fill everything in, but IMO the only parts of the setting that need to be defined in a setting book are the pieces that your starting characters would have a reason to know. Which means most setting books contain too many details of the entirely unnecessary variety, and not enough of the actually pertinent variety.

    NPCs are some of the most important aspect of setting. But again, NPCs at the appropriate scale of people the characters are likely to know.

    My answers...

    - How much setting is too much?

    Too much setting is setting that is:

    1. Hard to quickly reference in play.
    2. The basics can't be communicated by the GM to the players in less than 10 minutes.
    3. The players have to do extensive homework outside of game to understand what is going on.

    - How little will fail to support the players?

    Too little setting will fail the players if:

    1. There isn't enough material to get everyone on the same page and imagining similar locations, situations, and characters.
    2. Aren't inspired.

    - What are the tricks and tools available to us as designers? How do we write our settings? How do we organize the elements of the setting? What are the different kinds of setting-elements we have at our disposal? How do we get players to use our setting? How do we present it? What is the relationship between setting and method (system, rules, mecanics)? How do the characters fit into the picture?

    One size does not fit all. I would like multiple options to present the setting to GM and players, for example:

    - A few evocative paragraphs the GM can read aloud.
    - World creation questions that allow players to focus in on what parts of the setting they want to explore.
    - List of survey questions where players can specify what they want and don't in the game.
    - Character classes, lifepaths, skills, stats, traits, gear, example names, relationships, affiliations, reputations, factions, politics, and culture that all hook in and evoke the setting.
    - Lists of what is different and special about the setting that give players opportunities to hook in. Many lists of hooks. Lots of lists.
    - Example player characters.
    - Examples of what players are expected or tend to do, common goals, common obstacles.
    - Short fiction evoking the setting.
    - Setting timeline.
    - Maps with relevant points of interest highlighted.
    - Wikipedia dedicated to setting.
    - Intro scenarios that introduces the setting and sets expectations.

    \n I think the question "How much setting is too much?" needs to be broken down into two parts: the amount of setting in the book, and the amount of setting at the table.

    In the book, I don't think there's necessarily such a thing as "too much," provided that it's presented clearly, can be referenced easily, and (most importantly!) can be edited to suit the needs of your gaming group. This, to me, is why big sprawling settings like the Forgotten Realms and the World of Darkness got so much traction: you could pick and choose how much of all of that stuff you wanted to actually play with, and could let the rest of it sit on the shelf. If somehow it became important to know something about Waterdeep or the Tzimisce or whatever, you could pull those elements down and put them into your game; if not, you didn't have to, and you wouldn't bother.

    Because at the table, there's definitely such a thing as too much setting, especially at the beginning of the game. These days I'm happiest with settings whose basics can be summed up and presented to everyone on a single sheet of paper, and resent/dislike settings that require more than two sheets to describe. I want settings where we can hit the ground running in the first session, and add in/explore more elements of it as the game moves forward. If we start off with everyone understanding the very basics (wizards are rare and scary, it is a time of galactic civil war, cosmic rays have given some people superpowers, and so forth), that's enough to get things rolling. More complicated concepts, the ones that actually relate directly to the game we're playing, can be introduced and explained as they become relevant.

    I'm also a big fan of conveying setting information in multiple ways. One of the reasons I like settings that are similar to movies and TV shows is that they convey a lot of information in a very accessible way: you sit down with your friends and spend a few entertaining hours watching it together, and at the end of it you all have a shared understanding of what that setting looks like and sounds like and so on. Say "post-apocalyptic outer-space exploration" and you might get a dozen or more different impressions from a gaming group; say "The Road Warrior meets Star Trek" and it gets everyone closer to a consensus.

    \nOh yeah, definitely. Setting that doesn't connect us to good in-game situations is just dead weight, dragging the whole campaign down.     Posted By: TomasHVMThat is a very interesting notion, Troy! Why is it so, do you think?
    \nWell, everyone's pointing at the importance of Situation. Situation is made up of Characters acting in the Setting. If both the characters (PCs and NPCs) and the Setting are very well defined, then there's no room for action to take place. No matter where you go or what you do, the game has already provided all the possible information the players could create. You end up with something like Forgotten Realms or Dragonlance. The players can certainly do things but it won't end up really affecting the Situation because so rigidly set already. It ends up that the only real options open to the players to meaningfully change the Situation are irony or deconstruction. And those get old.

    If both the Characters and Setting are vague, then there's nothing for the players to really latch on to. They just wander aimlessly around looking for something to do but not really finding much. Rolemaster would be a good example. The game feels empty and undefined. On the surface it looks like there's a lot of cool stuff to do, but once play starts the player-characters are just sitting in the Tavern scratching their heads going, "So where do we go from here?"

    If one is vague and the other is well defined, it gives a route for action to flow. The players start with something concrete from which to draw inspiration prior to play and then an area of vagueness they get to define with their actions during play. Games with this setup are Sorcerer, Dogs in the Vineyard, The Pool, TSOY, Hero Wars, The Mountain Witch, My Life With Master, etc. In my experience, these types of games tend to produce more consistently fun play without requiring huge amounts of work from the GM or players just to fix the text to the point where they can use it.

    \n I think settings provide different things for different audiences and it is critical to differentiate between them. I will primarily speak of games with a central GM role, as GM-less games tend to differ.

    The GM can benefit from more detailed reference information including some historical events, some important places and some themes that are of import. The GM is looking for data and story hooks in most games so as to drive the action forward in a direction that suits the player characters. The key here is that, assuming no other reference text, all of the necessary data should be here. This doesn't have to be in the form of 200 pages of fiction, but I think that providing some info can be useful to many GM's.

    By contrast, players benefit from brief texts which impart the mood of the setting, the basic setting assumptions and a good view of the bigger picture. A few bullet points worth of key facts, some key character concepts, examples of typical social groups and a few of the key conflicts in the setting are ideal for the player's use. The player just needs enough inspiration to spur them in a general direction in the creation of their character, while the GM should have a rough idea of how much is in that direction.

    I think that, for me, the essential purpose of the setting is to focus the character concepts and ensure that all of the PC\u2019s are playing the same essential game. Once that is done, the GM can drive the game based on the character concepts. If you have completely divergent characters without setting-fostered thematic links, it will be very hard to encourage a strong, compelling and inclusive narrative in my experience.

    The amount of setting presented varies as well based on the length of play. A one-shot game can focus entirely on a single situation, such as \u201coverthrowing your Evil Master\u201d and need not include 30 pages of detail of eastern European politics and folklore. Conversely, if you are running a long-term (30+ session) game consisting of the ascension of downtrodden peasants to eastern European warlords you may benefit greatly from that quantity of setting information.

    Sorry, I am a bit of a setting junkie and I am very happy to yammer on at length about setting creation and documentation.

      \n Oh, also, a counterpoint to "the setting is just a situation battery": the characters can be situation batteries plugged into certain setting details, leaving the rest of the setting free to be anything. If I'm the leader of an argumentative cult, I've got some fun stuff to do, regardless of whether we're in Rome or on a starship. The choice of Rome vs starship in that case is pure color.

      As for choosing color, I always weigh familiarity vs difference.

      Difference grabs more attention, pulls you further out of reality, tempts you to re-wire your brain, but leaves you frequently out of synch. ("Wait, would your character know that?" "Oops.")

      Familiarity enables assumption, streamlines consensus, and loads context quickly, but doesn't offer the entoxicating allure of an exotic alternate reality.

      Thus, while a lot of folks seem to hate "12th Century Europe where everyone thinks and talks like 2011 Americans," I've come to find such compromises have a lot going for them.

      Access is an important part of a setting. Call it an entry point, call it the new-player experience, call it something else, the vector by which we enter a setting is an important part of setting design.       Introducing a setting through stages or carefully crafted steps is one wonderful method. For Harry Potter's world this is especially fitting because you start off with our muggle world and then explore deeper and deeper into the wizarding world.

      The Matrix lets us in on some of the new rules of the fictional setting early on, not just by showing us what Trinity is capable of but by showing us the reactions of the police officers, who say, "That's impossible..." Now we have a sense of both what's considered normal and what's actually possible. More rules are revealed over time.

      In a way, even Star Wars does this, first by orienting the fictional world as a fairy tale, relative to us in the audience ("A long time ago..."), and then by easing us in with the intro text, and then by dropping us into a telling visual with an immediate conflict.

      Sometimes we see the steps into the world juggled into different orders, in which we start inside the fictional world, with some kind of action sequence already in progress, and then back out to the familiar. See again The Matrix, in which we are given this little snapshot of what's possible in the world, and then back out to the normalcy of Mr. Anderson's life, and then gradually submerge into the truth and premise of the larger setting.

      \n Felt like it was time for me to go back to the original post and look at the questions there.

      - How much setting is too much? How little will fail to support the players?

      Setting is too much when it fails to inspire the players. There's a fine line between creative constraints and hemmed in by too much setting.

      Too little setting gives players nothing to riff off of, feeling adrift and very often with intense pressure to create something cool without any kind of foundation.

      - What are the tricks and tools available to us as designers? How do we write our settings? How do we organize the elements of the setting? What are the different kinds of setting-elements we have at our disposal?

      Tricks and tools, like: embedding the setting into the mechanics and character creation (thinking of BW here), giving an outline of what the setting looks and feels like so that the GM can create bits of the setting on their own (thinking of Dogs in the Vineyard here), putting the setting together as an in-game document with incomplete snippets from the game world so that the players only need to read an entry or two to get the basic idea of the setting (thinking of Dictionary of Mu here, tooting my own tome).

      - How do we get players to use our setting? How do we present it? What is the relationship between setting and method (system, rules, mecanics)? How do the characters fit into the picture?

      Hopefully, we don't get them to use the setting but in its presentation and through play it is just unavoidable. The bits presented get the players hungry and inspired to dwell there for a while and make up their own shit to add to it.       Posted By: Will HindmarchSo let me ask you:As a setting is populated over time by stories and characters, does that setting become less apt for RPG play?
      Interesting question. How do you "reboot" a setting at the start of a new campaign? I've played again and again in the same settings, and have found three solutions to this. I expect them to be quite general.

      1 - You start anew, ignoring old campaigns
      - Good: you can use all the setting-material as is
      - Bad: the players have left no mark on "the world" of the setting

      2 - You incorporate old campaigns in the setting, before starting a new one
      - Good: players love to explore the consequences left by former characters
      - Bad: making adjustments to the setting is demanding work

      3 - You use bits from old sessions which are easy to incorporate, and toss the rest
      - Good: easy to start, players may find their own "footmarks"
      - Bad: players may find it weird that some stuff never happened, when other stuff clearly did

      \nSee, for me, the issue isn't about the level of detail which exists for a setting - it's about how much of that the players need to know before they can play. So the real world is a really simple choice for a campaign - I can say to my group (as I once did) that the next campaign will be set in 17C Jamaica during the Golden Age of Piracy, and that's as much information as they need to play (because everyone's seen at least one pirate movie and so can get going). Similarly, FR and DL are close enough to "vanilla fantasy" that you can describe "an orc", "a tavern" or "a castle" without having to worry about whether everyone's picturing it right (and yes, I'm aware of the error in my examples with respect to one of those two settings).

      The settings which are more problematic are those where the players need a lot of knowledge to get going. So things like Transhuman Space, Cthulutech, or Skyrealms of Jorune. You're making the buy-in harder for the players, which will be an issue to a greater or lesser degree depending on your group.

      There are a number of ways around it of course. As I mentioned previously Tekumel makes this a feature, with the default first campaign being "you're a barbarian who knows nothing about Tekumel". Another example would be Vampire: The Masquerade. That's got a great clear pitch you can sell to the players - in fact the title of the game is practically a condensed version of the pitch - but it also has a lot of assumed knowledge you need to learn before play, in terms of vampire clans and politics etc. The genius of VTM, however, is that it makes all of the additional assumed knowledge part of char gen, so that by the time you're ready to sit down to play you're all up to speed.
