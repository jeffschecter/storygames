\n Archipelago's "Try A Different Way" is really interesting to me. It seems to fly in the face of the improv "always say Yes; try and build" thing - but I've played so many games where I later think "That was great, but..." that I'm pining for that additional editorial control.

I've only played Archipelago with my daughter - I hesitate to bring it or Seid out at story game night because I worry that TADW might get abused.

Has anyone seen the use of "Try A Different Way" go overboard - kill the group's momentum by making someone feel like their idea got shot down - or otherwise mess things up?

And - a question for Jason Morningstar - since you're a fan of Archipelago, how much unofficial "Try A Different Way" sorts of negotiations happen when you play Fiasco and other games that don't explicitly have it?

    \n In Dogs in the Vineyard, a Trait can't be used if anyone playing says so.

    I haven't often seen this rule used but it does come up maybe once every 2-3 games. It's often a non-issue, and probably not used enough. It's only ever been a problem when 1 player says NO to a trait, then the target chooses a different trait, and the same person says NO again. In general, I would like there to be a limit. If you say, "try something different" and they do, and there is still a problem... unless it is a major problem, I would let it go.

    I like these rules but I would only play with them or play the games that require them with specific people (which is not a bad thing, there are different people I would play withe when it comes to competitive games vs cooperative games or free form games vs tactical games). I'm not talking strangers vs friends. It's just some people can express criticism in a positive way and others can't.

    \n "Try a different way" isn't an "I call bullshit" or community veto rule exactly. More often it means "we can do better than this". From Love In The Time of Sei\xf0:\n
    "Do It Differently!" ...tells the player that something feels like it\u2019s jarring. Are things suddenly going Monty Python? Or is the scene going nowhere? Rewind and try again. You\u2019re doing everyone a favor by being quality control, making sure that uninspiring stuff doesn\u2019t enter the fiction you\u2019re sharing. And you\u2019re giving the other player a chance to really shine, instead of having to stick to the half-baked thing they just produced.

    \u201cIn the sauna, everyone is naked. The king\u2019s big, hairy testicles are old and shriveled. As he walks, they bounce up and down before the Earl\u2019s face. He\u2019s jealous of their weight...\u201d

    \u201cDo it differently! Don\u2019t play it for laughs.\u201d

    At the risk of sounding like the old guy in The Name of the Rose, I think that too many silly descriptions and jokes tend to derail the story. They\u2019re often used to relieve tension at exactly the moment when the story needs tension. Don\u2019t let that happen.

    \u201cAfter the Earl gives his command, his soldiers just stand there. They don\u2019t pay him any attention.\u201d

    \u201cDo it differently! We\u2019ve already established that they\u2019re blindly loyal and efficient.\u201d

    A good way to enforce consistency of character and setting. Don\u2019t let things change for no reason.

    \u201cDo it differently\u201d can, of course, be phrased in whatever way your group is comfortable with - it isn\u2019t meant to be critical or confrontational, but rather to enforce the consensus on the game\u2019s tone. You can use phrases to push back \u2013 if the Event guide instructs you to play a certain way, you can say \u201cdo it differently! My character would never do that\u201d. Do it differently is hugely important. If you\u2019ve established a healthy dynamic around that phrase you can push as hard as you want to. You can endow a character with motivations because they will tell you when you go too far.

    \n The first time I played Archipelago, with the original rules, we used lots of "Do it Differently." It never really seemed problematic; every time I received it I took it as a sign that my thing wasn't really pushing the other player's aesthetic and/or dramatic buttons, and so tweaked accordingly. It never came up for a contribution I was highly invested in, though. Just some opening scene-setting stuff, where it was easy to adjust.

    Interestingly, we didn't play with the caveat that you have to explain how you'd like it to be different. I dunno if that was a lack in the original rules, or if we just didn't pick up on it. That aspect was a bit disconcerting, like I had to play a guessing game over what woulod satisfy the player. I decided to take it as a creative challenge, but it was rather harsh-feeling. I like the new rule better. On a similar note, I very much prefer "Try a different way" over "Do it differently!" I don't think I'd want to soften it any further, though, with "maybe" or "perhaps, if it's not too much trouble?" As long as we know it's open to negotiation, I'd rather make a clear, though soft, statement.



    The other phrase I've found to be problematic at times is "Draw a Card/That might not be so easy!" In that first Archipelago game, folks started calling 'Draw a Card" like over and over again; I think it was a reflexive gamer instinct: "Oh, this the 'roll dice to see if you succeed' mechanic. Ok, let's roll!" And I believe in the original Archipelago, it was a simple playing card that gave success or failure, not the ultra-cool selection of "Yes, but..." "No, but..." "Yes, and..." options the game has currently. Also, the new rules patch this nicely, only allowing one "that might not be so easy!" per scene, so you can't spam it.

    I played a game just the other day, though, where that very scarcity prompted some "That Might Not Be So Easy" abuse. The game had taken an adversarial turn, and I found myself frantically busting out "TMNBSE" to try to prevent the other character from getting his way all the damn time. Bad form on my part, I'll readily admit. And it turned out I wasted the card draw on a relatively trivial action, because I was suddenly SURE the guy was going to go "and then I proceed to solve all my problems and win everything forever" and I panicked. It would have been far better to save the draw for the actual climax of the story, which came soon after.

    I think it's significant that in that game, "Try a Different Way" was extremely under-used. Instead of course-correcting early on and communicating expectations to each other, we let the fiction roll on getting more and more disparate and uneven in tone, until it became a power struggle over both where the story went and what story we were even telling. Granted, we were only sort of half-invested in playing that evening to begin with, and started off rather silly. But "Try a Different Way" could still have helped us calibrate the story and get more on the same page.

    One fascinating aspect of that whole experience: I would actually postulate that "Do It Differently" could have helped PREVENT blocking. A lot of my frustration in the game stemmed from other players contributing bits that sort of squelched or dampened elements that I had just introduced myself. If I had thought to say, "Try a different way--without invalidating what I just established," it could've done a world of good.

    Peace,
    -Joel

    \n Here's one thing I like about that rule, by the way. There's an improv game in which you ring a bell every time to get people to change what they've just done.

    "This steak is undercooked."
    [Bell rings]
    "This steak is overdone."
    [Bell rings]
    "This isn't a steak at all."
    [Bell rings]
    "I am a steak-pervert. Bring me seventeen juicy..."
    [Bell rings]
    "My steak just talked to me."
    [Bell doesn't ring, scene continues.]

    Note what happens here. You begin with a dull statement. The bell rings to make it better. The second option reverses the statement, but that's dull too. The third and fourth options are desperate attempts to be creative: they are also dull. The fifth is pleasingly weird. It's a statement produced under pressure, blurted out, sprung from the depths of the unconscious. And that's what the game is for. It produces something nobody would ever have thought of saying.

    So, if Try A Different Way does that, I really like it. I don't think it does, actually. But it would be cool if it did. Perhaps I'm derailing.

    \n I think the question about unofficial forms of TADW in other games that don't explicitly have it is the most interesting thing here. Maybe I and many of the folks I play with just have S-G brain damage, but most the games I play in have this phrase in the System mix in some form. Most of the time it takes the shape of someone getting excited, leaning forward and exclaiming, "Oh! No Wait! I know! How about something more like this?" They're most likely to do it when the original narration has been offered with some mixture of hesitancy or a tone that suggests that this-is-good-but-I'm-not-100%-set-on-it, but sometimes even when that's not the case. In a group with some reasonable degree of trust, these offers are generally made and received with generosity.

    And we were doing it with GM-less games and games like PTA long before TADW came along. So maybe TADW is a little stronger or has more foot-stepping potential than then previous forms but I and others employ the phrase more in the spirit of the earlier non-explicit forms.

    There were about fifteen people in a room late Saturday night riffing for a time on passive aggressive forms of TADW. It was pretty amusing.
