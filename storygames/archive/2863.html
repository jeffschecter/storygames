\n So I have been reading Esoterrorists, Robin Laws game about supernatural investigators. GUMSHOE is going to be the engine for Ken Hite's newest version of Call of Cthulhu. I like what Robin does here but I see a lot of companies going in the same direction. My impression is that many companies are going in the direction of "give GMs tools to run games and lead their players through THEIR plots." I think it's a great step in a traditional framework and I admit I am a solid convert to hippy games but I do wonder if the direction even further AWAY from player input is a pointed decision to distance themselves from many of the games I play (tMW, PTA, even BW/BE) or a move closer to the "GM is god" model. I know Mr. Laws is familiar with player driven games and more spontaneous storytelling. I have considered making a comment on his livejournal to find out his take on it all.

Has anyone else read Esoterrorists or GUMSHOE. After putting myself into a traditional mindset I decided that I really did like the approach and the system (and Esoterrorists is a interesting and direct setting).

    \n Gumshoe is about facilitating successful actual play with mysteries, investigation led games.

    There are two ways in which to approach mystery rpgs, one is to have a moveable solution based on what players come up with in play. Inspectres typifies this approach. That's fine, but for many folk looking to play mysteries fundamentally misses the point as they want an actual mystery with a real solution capable of unravelling.

    The second approach is to have an actual mystery with a real solution capable of unravelling. CoC traditionally typifies this approach.

    That second approach necessitates a clear GM/Player split. It's integral to the existence of a mystery which is unravelled in play that someone creates and knows the truth to that mystery and others discover it. I'm not saying that's better or worse, but it is what this play is about. Co-creation is directly incompatible with discovery of the mystery.

    Historically however, techniques for facilitating this play have not been good. One common problem, particularly in published CoC scenarios, is the one roll blocks the investigation issue. PCs have to discover a single clue and if they don't the investigation stalls, the mystery is not solved, the players do not find out what was happening through their own efforts.

    There are a number of ways round that, but one way is the method used in Gumshoe. Gumshoe is about structuring a system explicitly enabling GMs to successfully run games in which there is an actual mystery with a real solution capable of unravelling while avoiding the possibility of a sudden blockage destroying the game.

    It's not really about GM as god or about new ways of doing gaming, it's about providing tools to faciliate a particular type of rpg experience. It is quite explicitly not about railroading, the fact the GM creates the underlying truth of the mystery in no way requires that the GM tell the players a story in which they are along for the ride and Esoterrorists tries to provide tools to prevent that occurring, though the example scenario in the book I think fails in that regard.

    \n Well, Max, I could imagine a number of solutions. The first is to basically do something like the game Clue does (I believe that they call it Cluedo on your side of the pond?). Where the solution is determined randomly, and some mechanical method for determining it exists.
    \n
    \nOn an entirely different tangent, I think that there's a problem with the dichotomy that you're setting up, in that there are probably a lot of other ways to approach mysteries. That is, the COC/Essoterrorist method is not only to have a mystery with a solution to it pre-existing. But, and I think this is very important, the path to the solution of the mystery is pre-determined. One can also imagine, for instance, having the mystery's solution pre-determined... we know whodunnit... but not how the players are going to find out.
    \n
    \nThe "problem" being solved by Essoterrorists (I've read it), is the one you identify - not being able to get to the end of the mystery without throwing out most of the mechanics anyhow. I think it's found a way to succeed at that, largely. But even there, and to me quite ironically, it allows the "quest" to fail on combat. That is, for some reason it's OK to allow automatic success on getting from scene to scene... unless you get killed in the process. I'm pretty sure that's an accurate reading of the rules. In fact, it goes so far to explain that this is neccessary in order to have some sort of tension.
    \n
    \nThis is fascinating. Apparently figuring out how to get from scene to scene can be done with automatic success, and is still interesting, but getting through a combat with automatic success is not. It seems that some traditions are inviolate...
    \n
    \nThat aside, again, there's another model that would work here, which would be to allow the players to indicate the path in collaboration with the GM. That is, they come up with what the scenes are, but the narrator figures out how to ensure that some clue shows up that leads to the solution of the mystery. I think Laws even mentions this as a possibility for super-GMs. I think, with the right mechanical rules, it would be possible for any GM.
    \n
    \nIn any case, do I see this method as superior? Well, the "problem" with COC scenarios, as I see it, is not only that they tend to not get to the end of the mystery. That's one problem, certainly. But the other issue is that in stringing the players along from scene to scene, the players tend to have very little input. They are not solving the mystery, they are just following the script already set for the characters to solve the mystey. In Gumshoe this is, if anything, more true. With COC, at least you had those occasions where the players would fail, and the intrepid GM would take some cue from the players on where to go next, and figure out how to bring them back on track. Yeah that's a lot of work without support, and you get illusionism problems, perhaps. But at least the players get to actually participate in creating the plot through the declared actions of their characters.
    \n
    \nAs I'm fond of saying, in looking at what makes a game fun for the players, determine what decisions it is that they have to make. In almost all RPGs it's going to be possible for them to at least create "window dressing" in terms of their narrations, and such. But what Steve is calling structure can, for some players, tends to not be enough. They want to have an effect on whet he's calling the framework. If, in fact, they're being lead by the nose from scene to scene, then they feel that they aren't really controlling anything important. Certainly not their character's destinies in any appreciable fashion.
    \n
    \nAt least in D&D; you could decide to go left or right at the T-shaped intersection.
    \n
    \nOr maybe this is my own proclivities speaking. I'm sure that some people will be delighted by the solutions that Gumshoe provides. It won't, for me however, solve all the problems of COC. I think that Don's concern is quite a valid one.

    \n Gumshoe is not supposed to have a predetermined path, the idea is that there are clues in existence but that the PCs can determine how they approach things and which avenues they follow. The example adventure is railroady, but that's the example adventure, the game is pretty clear that the path is not laid down in advance.

    Personally I think the problem it seeks to address is a non-problem anyway, or more accurately a problem of shitty GMing and scenario design, in that I routinely run mystery/investigation games in which I in no way prejudge what the PCs will do and in which there is never a single roll that blocks the game. I see game blocking rolls all the time in published CoC scenarios, but not in my own actual play.

    The combat bit, sure, but the game isn't seeking to avoid failure, the game is seeking to address precisely one issue - an investigation stalling because a clue is necessary to progress and is dependent on a single roll. That's it, the game is addressing that point and no other. I don't think it's a point that needs a mechanical fix, but that's why it doesn't avoid possible failure via combat or by some other means (running out of time for example). Guaranteed success is not a goal of the game.

    But led by the nose from scene to scene? That's not something necessarily present in Esoterrorists, I believe the text (I'm selling my copy so it's in a box right now and I can't check) explicitly speaks to that point and does not recommend forcing players down one path, and there is absolutely nothing in an investigation game that requires that kind of channelling.

    Anyway, that's the key point here, Esoterrorists and Gumshoe do not in fact assume a predetermined investigation path, that's not actually correct. They only assume a predetermined answer, how you get there is open.

    \n Okay, let me clarify my point here cause I got lost

    1. As noted above Esoterrorist takes a step forward in giving a "better means for moving players through predetermined hoops." It IS a better way to handle it in that games like CoC don't address it at all. The Basic Roleplay system is about task resolution and going crazy and says little to nothing about scenes and the ongoing plot. Esoterrorists/GUMSHOE gives players many paths to move the story along by fixing a common problem in other task resolution systems - the game stops because the players don't learn what they need to to move forward. Is GUMSHOE the end-all-and-be-all to mystery games? No, but I found it a refreshing step and something I'd be willing to run, especially with those extra design notes mentioned earlier.

    2. That being said, it still seems to further one goal - "making better GMs (among GM-centric games)." The focus is really on "how do you run a game and give the players the illusion that they have free will." His design notes even mention that often the best way to do this is to show the players openly that you're making decisions on the fly (all the while you really have a plan). This was the main thesis/query of my original post (hopefully laid out better here). I see this a lot. While many of us are thinking about "how do I empower us all to work together to tell a good story equally" I see the big dogs handing out (read: selling) GMing advice I had been using for literally decades. The shame of it is that the core constituent either finds validation in such products, showing them that they "have it right" or the material comes off as being fresh and new to people tired of comparing their cocks at the game table. In my indie-game myopia it seems to me that the companies ARE trying to adapt and progress but simply widen the differences between the mainstream hobby and the independant games (as was discussed in the Delancy thread a short while back).

    \n Well this sounds tautological. If by "Mystery Game" you mean one in which there's a challenge to solve the mystery, then, yeah, they're all Gamist. I often run mysteries, telling the players the solution from the start, in order that we discover things about the players through the course of the investigation.     \n
    \nFor players who understand that the outcome of the scenario is railroaded, however, the game is probably more sim. That is, if they know that the result is that they're going to get to the end, there's no gamism. Just a "What's an investigation of this sort like?" That is, the idea is that you're trying to find the bad-guys, and destroy them, and your abilities as a player are being tested in the process. Worse, there's usually no player tactics allowed in the vast majority of the situations. So mysteries can in principle support things like personal horror as the PCs descend into madness on the basis of what they find as they solve the mystery.     The thing about the GUMSHOE ad line is that it's not actually an innovation to have a clue that you don't have to roll dice to find. There are lots of mystery scenarios written over decades which have these. There is a basic set of information that is automatically available and which is sufficient to move ahead -- but there are extra details that the players can roll to find.

    I often enjoy mysteries, but I did not like GUMSHOE's style of jumping through hoops. I think good mystery scenarios can be done without a predetermined path or tree of scenes. Instead, the GM knows the backstory and evidence, and the players try to solve it by coming up with their own plan for investigation -- which allows a lot of room for flexibility. (Incidentally, I'd highly recommend Chris Lehrich's notes on Mysteries: Step by Step Instructions.)

The rest of that material was awesome stuff: But it was all for the GMs eyes only, unless the PCs specifically roll high enough or had those skills to find out that info (like the reasons and motives behind everyone, the secret info for why the aliens are doing what they're doing, or the ties between the bank ledger and the cult, etc). Running pre-gen CoC scenarios tends to be a grand affair, since they tend to be written extremely well, have cool plot elements, awesome NPCs, are usually really well thought out, lots of hand-out material, etc. But I realize that from The Corbett House (THE first CoC adventure everyone plays) all the way to the Hastaur Adventures in DG: Countdown, they all fall prey to this "Only the GM gets to know all the really awesome stuff most of the time, unless the dice are on fire and the players are having their characters 'click everywhere' " syndrome.

    With Esoterrorists, I can run through any of those awesome Delta Green adventures, and actually in the course of the adventure have a chance to present all this cool information to the players.

    That alone made the purchase worthwhile. Note, though, I only planned to run through the Nazi Zombies DG adventure in the next few months (plus an "Esoterrorists of Waterdeep" game set in forgotten realms), I haven't actually had a play experience with it yet. Though I would not turn one down, cause I want to see it as a player as well.

    -Andy
