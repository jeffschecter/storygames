    This is hard to comment on without the context of the game into which the text will go. But, for instance, looking at the sample sentence, instead write something like:

    "Be sure to describe the events surrounding what's happening to the character incorporating these elements into that description."

    But, again, that might not work right for some game. Does the game have some section that defines what narration is like or who does what part of the narration? Is there a sample of play? Do you have a glossary where you define jargon that the game must already have?


    On a tangent, personally I think that clauses like "it is expected" and my similar "be sure to" are actually weak phrasing. They make it seem like there are exceptions to these rules, or that you're only supposed to try. Maybe that's what you intend. But, personally, I prefer rules that are written like law.

    "The player must incorporate these elements into his description of the action, or he gets no bonus for them."

    Along with a clause somewhere else in the text that says something like, "If there is a question of whether some rule has been satisfied or not, the GM should rule quickly on this, and move play on." Doesn't have to be that rule, but some rule as to who decides when there is a judgment call. I see too many games where the text is simply suggesting technique, doesn't make these things a rule, and relies on hopefulness that the players will never have a difference of opinion as to the interpretation of the rule, instead of having a rule that gives players a way to decide these issues and move on.

    That's the "hippie-est" part of these games to me, actually, when the implied rule is "why can't we all just get along?"


    To get back on topic, I think that clauses like the one being discussed are much clearer when they're not couched in "soft" language. If you do use some jargon defined elsewhere in the text, or simply explain well what you mean in a rule on the spot, it'll work better if you simply say "do this, do that" not "try to do X" or "If you can, do Y."

    Mike

    When there's a judgment call, somebody at the table has to be assigned to decide what counts and what does not. Often the GM, but there are other ways to do it.

    I've said this before, but this is actually pretty definitive of tabletop RPGs, that, at some point some participant has to make this sort of judgment call. In other RPGs, it's often "When do we roll?" In others it's "What gives a modifier?" But it's precisely this judgment that leads to the potentially infinite number of situations in a TT RPG, which is something you don't find in many other forms of play. This is what separates them from CRPGs, for instance, where the computer can't make subjective calls, but always has a set of criteria to look at.

    People need to embrace this, and learn where the judgement calls are in their games, and ensure that there are ways to make sure that everyone agrees with the judgments. Even if it's just "GM decides." It's by such a method that the group can develop a community standard for these things. Yes, the standard will be different for each group. But sans the parallel of a court system to establish precedence, and make final rulings, it's only by luck that you'll end up with anything like a community standard.

    Mike
