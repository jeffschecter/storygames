    \n I don't think you'll find consensus on this point. There are many here who find the idea very applicable and useful, and others who think it's fairly asinine.

    I find it very useful, myself, although more as a general concept (knowing a specific game's Creative Agenda - "We try to impress the GM while trying to outdo each other in our depictions of Early 20's gangsters, with extra points if they are morally grey in way which is appropriate to the period" - and then building all elements of play towards supporting it) than as an exhaustive list of only three possible play goals. I think this is in line with Ron Edwards' conceptualization of this stuff: it's three general families of Creative Agenda, and we may find that there are more (or fewer) than three, as we learn more about roleplaying and expand its boundaries. Like all families, some members fit the mold and others hardly seem to belong there.

    Note, however, that "GNS" is not, as you put it, a "player model". It is not intended to describe players and their priorities. It is, rather, a simplification of creative goals found by a group in a specific instance of playing a certain game. That's an important distinction. (Look at it as a "player model", and I'll quickly switch to the "fairly asinine" camp.)

    Much like, say, pop-psych personality types, or studying historical periods ("The Dark Ages") or artistic movements ("Postmodern Hip-Hop"), this kind of thing can be useful as a shorthand when talking about things at a certain distance. Get "into the trenches" with your actual source material, and you quickly find that, more often than not, the full picture is much more nuanced and mixed-up than your model would presume. In the same way, with the Big Model, I find it useful from a certain removed perspective, but sometimes lacking when it comes to dealing with real people playing a real game: there are all kinds of details and ephemera which break expectations and conventions, and those come to the fore. Much like "historical eras" or artistic movements, the terms are far from meaningless and can make for useful discussion as well as a quick shorthand, but learn enough about the material and you see how the terms miss the real crux of the matter.

    (Much like how learning that so-and-so was an Existentialist is useful when studying philosophers, but actually getting into that person's intimate letters to family may yield surprising information which breaks out of the scope of this constructed "box" the term "Existentialist" is supposed to describe.)

I don't think it's really important, or even possible, to neatly categorize all possible CAs - what's important is understanding what a CA is, and (for a game-designer especially) having at least some firsthand experience of playing with different, explicitly negotiated CAs.

    \n Creative Agenda is a very useful concept - having a shared sense of what fits and doesn't in the midst of play is a powerful way to be creative with each other.

    GNS is intensely problematic:
    1) It encourages people to think there are "Gamer Players, Story Players, and Sim Players." Which the Big Model consistently discounts.
    2) Even if you apply it just to "this group, playing that game, at a particular time", it still ends of being a claim that they are almost certainly behaving closer to X than Y or Z - a claim that is perfectly valid regardless of what X, Y, and Z are in terms of Creative Agenda archetypes.
    3) Even if you get everyone in a group to closely hew to valuing Story, Game, or Sim, it doesn't actually tell them which of the many different ways to value those things. Is your Star Wars Sim agenda focused on the mythic resonance, consistent technology, or extended universe minutiae? In your competitive game, what constitutes clever play versus bad sportsmanship? In your story game, what themes and tropes are too trite or overused and which are old favorites? This is the actual creative agenda of play, something you can dig into if you scrape off the GNS labels.

    \n One of the most interesting things to come from GNS, from my perspective, is a counterpoint to this whole idea of "player types". The concept of Creative Agenda can create a paradigm shift in the way you look at playing a game. (And Ron's "bass player" analogy is a good one in this respect.)

    A "traditional" (perhaps?) way of thinking about RPGs:

    "This player is Type A, so they need X, while that one is Type B, so they need Y. The GM needs to provide jollies of Type X and Y, in a balanced recipe, to entertain the players and keep them happy."

    (Noteworthy is the omission of "GM types": player types generally have to do with the non-GM players, whose demands/needs/wants must be "satisfied" by the GM.)

    The Big Model's approach, rather, has nothing to do with player "types":

    "This game is type A (having a specific Creative Agenda). Therefore, all players should strive to fulfill their roles in a way which augments and reinforces A (the Creative Agenda); making decisions in-play to stay focused on this particular game's creative goals."

    Speaking purely for myself, I find the latter point of view to be much more fun and much more effective. I've enjoyed gaming a great deal more - and with much greater variety - once I started looking at it this way.

    A musical analogy is apt here: in the first case, the musician joins a band with the attitude, "Hey, I really enjoy music which is loud!" And then it's the responsibility of someone in charge to give them what they like. In the second case, the musician considers the group and the piece or song being played, and tries to add something which enhances the whole.

    For me, this attitude (especially when all the players in the game embrace it) has led to much more fun gaming (and more consistently). Whether I'm the GM or player, I'm looking to build upon what's happening at the table and make moves which enhance (or challenge, if it's that kind of game) what the other players are doing.

    It's about figuring out what makes a particular group and game really shine, and focusing in on that. When everyone at a table does that... magic happens.

    This is quite a contrast to the more "traditional" mindset, which often held the assumption that there was one monolithic game or activity which was "roleplaying", and it had to fulfill all needs and desires.

    It's certainly not illogical to seek a single game and a single group which fulfills all the needs of each player. It's maybe even a worthy goal.

    I'm only talking of my personal experience when I say, however, that the latter approach did not contribute to my enjoyment of gaming as much as the former. We don't seek one movie to fulfill all our film-watching desires: rather, we enjoy a variety of comedies, tragedies, dramas, action films, and documentaries. We don't expect one musical group to suit all occasions or all moods - rather, we enjoy a variety of bands, styles, and musical traditions.

    As far as I know, Hollywood doesn't try to put together "the world's best musical group" and "world's best film composer" and then use them to record the soundtrack to every film they make. (Which would probably be much more efficient than contracting each soundtrack separately.) Rather, they try to match each soundtrack to each film as appropriately as possible, to highlight the qualities of that particular picture. Sometimes you need a string orchestra and sometimes some Top 40's dance hits will be a better match.

    Would it make for better films if Hollywood producers used this kind of logic? "Hey, our last action flick didn't resonate well with the American stay-at-home-mom crowd. We'd better add Johnny Depp and Oprah to the next one... but also make sure to include lots of raunchy humour for our younger viewers, and a bit of historical commentary for the older crowd interested in learning about period events. There, now our movies will be much better!"

    Certainly you could argue that movies should be made this way. Wouldn't the ultimate film be equally interesting for everyone? I suspect there's a reason pleasing every possible audience within the scope of any particular film isn't the way things are done, however...

    I see no reason for gaming to be different. Sometimes you want immersive wish-fulfillment fantasy with a group of players who are capable of creating Tolkienesque epics, and sometimes you like to play a tactical science fiction battle with a bunch of rowdy, hilarious punks.

    But to each his own! This mindset was promoted there and has now become a way to approach gaming for a lot of people, who seem to have benefited from it quite a lot in terms of being able to find and enjoy games which fulfill them.

    I think that's a big part of how the "GNS" (or, rather, the concept of Creative Agenda, which can be useful even if you don't agree that G, N, and S are sensible categories) has influenced gamers and the gaming scene.

    \n I mostly agree with the Big Model take on Creative Agenda, but with time & practice I came to the (very personal) conclusion that the GNS distinction is actually irrelevant.
    I did not "get" this from chatting about it with Ron while scratching my perplexed head, nor from raging on forums where 600 users had 600 different conflicting opinions.
    I got it from actually designing games, observing what helped me, and what did not.

    What I mean is:
    it is HUGELY important and useful to understand that rules are not neutral, that they will influence Player's behaviour, and that such influence depends on the total overall holistic structure of your game (the sum of all rules interacting together).
    This influence can be perceived by the designer as a sort of "direction", where rules push and pull the game experience towards some direction...
    The important / desirable bit is to have all rules contribute to the same overall direction. This is what makes for a coherent game.

    (I often visualize Vampire Masquerade, for example, with me in the middle and a horse tied to my limbs and each one pulling in a different direction ... ouch!)

    What is NOT important, and is instead the core of 90% of the flames around CA, is to specifically identify and label this "direction thing".
    It doesn't matter if your game idea is going North or South, or if it is Nar or Sim or Glam or P0rn or whatever... it matters that you LOOK for a direction and then you work to have everything go more or less in that same nameless direction.

    Of course it helps to know what that direction is, but instead of an abstract label or category that never quite fits what you had in mind, I find INFINITELY MORE useful to actually describe in practical at-the-table terms how the game experience you have in mind should look and feel like.

    So the concept of CA in crucial for good, coherent, design. While the actual identification and labelling and horoscoping of specific CAs is largely irrelevant, and in my experience actually harmful to both the design of games and the discussion around them :P

    Does anybody else feel this way?

    \n I agree with Hasimir regarding game design. GNS theory agrees as well, insofar as it lays no claim to "horoscoping" (I like that simile) having any special value per se. If you're designing with a cohesive creative agenda, everything is going well, and it does not actually matter for the success of your creative goals as to which agenda mode your game and creative agenda might ultimately eventuate.

    Putting aside people's limitless curiousity towards labeling things and nit-picking the definitions of those labels, GNS theory basically puts forward just one substantial, falsifiable and actionable claim about gaming: given a group of players trying to play together, if the creative agendas they are trying to fulfill fall into different agenda modes, the game will incohere creatively. I guess the emphasis on the underbrush of terminological definition arises out of difficulties with understanding and applying the agenda modes; if you don't understand what Sim is, then you won't recognize it when you see it, and that'll certainly make it somewhat difficult to do GNS analysis on given practical play experiences.

    Still, for game design purposes the above concerns are irrelevant. Historically GNS has been relevant to game designers because of the majestic picture it paints of the variety of possibilities in roleplaying; you design differently when you realize that your first task is to nail down your creative agenda, support it and communicate it effectively to the reader of your game text, rather than blithely assuming that you should support each and every impulse that's ever crossed the game table. Besides inspiration, though, nobody designs by first deciding what agenda mode their game is supposed to support, and then doing, I don't know, copy-paste out of the standard Gamism manual for the bare bones of their game or something - that's simply not something we do. GNS does not talk to design, even if the Big Model does.

      Of course this observation raises the question as to how one goes about discovering what the theory actually says. For my druthers this is a misleading question rooted in a flawed epistemology, as it assumes that there is a coherent theory with an authoritative text to be queried where this is not necessarily the case. The GNS theory has evolved overwhelmingly in the form of dialogues with varied participation, and there is to my knowledge no "final" text for it that would be underwritten by anybody. Ron is something of a GNS pope (it being originally his theory), but even he hasn't put down anything very comprehensive or final on the matter.       \n I don't particularly want to derail the interesting conversation, but I consistently see people trying to understand or critique the Creative Agenda aspect of the Big Model misunderstanding the three "currently recognized" (as Ron puts it) CA modes.

      I'm not necessarily laying blame at their feet: clearly, if so many people get it wrong, there must be something wrong with the way the theory is explained, and the nomenclature of G, N, and S overlapping with a similar-but-different Threefold definition of the terms doesn't help.

      That said, a lot of people (including several of you/us/people in this thread) are operating from a very different understanding of G/N/S than the more "modern" Big Model suggests.

      On the internet and in casual discussions with gamers, I usually see this kind of understanding emerge (and then that model is criticized, even though this isn't how the Big Model defines the CA modes at all):\n

      G stands for Gamism.

      Gamism means competing with your other fellow players by trying to take advantage of the system of the game: making the most powerful character builds, picking the best powers, and doing everything you can to win encounters and level up as fast as possible.

      N stands for Narrativism.

      Narrativism is all about stepping away from the mechanical aspect of the game (which the Gamist loves) and making play all about telling stories. We ignore the rules when they get in the way of story, and all we want to do is focus on the fictional events. Not rolling the dice too often is part of this, as are rules which allow players to narrate anything they like during the game: we want to be free to tell exactly the story we want! (Or to tell exactly the story that the GM wants, in some cases, like a pre-scripted adventure.)

      S stands for Simulationism.

      Simulationism is all about modeling a fictional universe as accurately as possible, and playing to "find out" what would happen in certain hypothetical situations. We play to explore possible situations and then use a set of rules as the "physics of the gameworld" to determine what would happen next. We ignore the rules when they present "unrealistic" outcomes, or things that don't fit what we're trying to imagine.
      \nWhile there's a bit of overlap, these definitions are very definitely NOT what the Big Model says (for which Eero's primer, recently posted, is much better).

      Arguably they're clearer than the Big Model (which is harder to grasp, being more abstract), but they are NOT what the Big Model says. If you think they are, you should be aware of that before you get into a Big Model/GNS debate, because otherwise there will be major miscommunications or misunderstandings.

      \n I'd much prefer to make things more accessible rather than go to another forum. So, to address a recent post, \n
      Coming dangerously close to a semantics trap, sometimes the "how" can be the "why". I want to play Full Myth because I am interested in exploring Full Myth experiences and in finding out how they compare to No Myth experiences. That's a TA-level "why".

      I'm coming around to "Full Myth" as a term, concept, and goal. It should be understandable enough to Big Model folks.
      I mean by it a prepared and populated place, not a prepared sequence of events.
      Where "No Myth" is of course the familiar "laying the tracks just ahead of the train as the train speeds on" type of improvised play.
      \nAs a summary,

      "No Myth" play is more like acting improv games, where nothing is defined in advance, and the group draws the map as they go along. "Full Myth" play is where the game-world is fully mapped and for every event, the GM consults his notes for what is present - such as a predefined "sandbox" that the GM has prepared where the players can do whatever they want within the bounds.

      GNS theory divides things into

      1) GNS Creative Agenda - such as Gamist, Narrativist, and Simulationist - that it posits as more fundamental motivations that constitute why people play.

      2) Technical agenda (that 2097 calls "TA-level why") is supposedly preferences for how players approach the fundamental motivations of play, such as a preference for science fiction over fantasy, or no myth improv over full myth sandbox.

      I agree that this how and why aren't clearly differentiated. In particular, I don't think that creative agenda is a real motivation for people. People are motivated by real world results, not fiction. As I suggested in another thread, this could be things like stress relief, forming social bonds, emotional catharsis, and so forth.

      \n I disagree that Creative Agenda is "why people play." All of Ron's Big Model stuff draws heavily from his behaviorist science training. The theory talks about observable things and events. When push comes to shove, in the case of CA, it's talking about /the things that are to be done/ (AGENDA, in the Latin sense), not some idealized notion of what a player wants. More than that, the way I saw Ron use it over and over, was as a general gist of the /shared priority/ of an entire group of players over a session or more of play.

      So to say CA is "why people play" might be a useful shorthand to help people get to an understanding of the original intent, but I don't think it's terribly accurate or useful.

      The definition I tend to use is something like this:\n

      Creative Agenda is a group's primary priority of what's cool or desirable in play, reinforced and validated by the group over a reasonable length of actual play that touches on all the major parts of the system.
      \nTechnical Agenda (in my mind, but not necessarily anyone else's) is the set of Techniques to make that play happen. I think it's useful to use in the same sense as CA, looking back at play for diagnosis, and less useful (maybe even dangerous, in the "will cause countless arguments" sense) to use it as a player preference thing.

      Even "We want to learn about Korean culture" isn't a creative agenda. It's just a goal, maybe in the Social Contract layer of the Model, and a group of players might go at that goal with a Right to Dream agenda, or a Story Now agenda, or whatever.

      To be a creative agenda, it has to be something that guides a player's creative input into the fiction (into the "shared imagined space," in Big Model parlance). I don't think "we want to learn about Korean culture" is a primary guide for creative input during play.

      I don't think it's useful to tangle Creative Agenda and Techniques in this way, to say that a CA can be a Technique. CA /by definition/ is a spear that touches every layer of the Model, so if your definition of CA lets it be a Technique, then it's not the same definition as the Big Model's. Which is fine, but it's its own thing then.

      This is all just a long way of saying, there are goals that are in the Social Contract layer that are not creative agenda goals, and I think your example showcases this.

      \n I think it's really important when talking about Creative Agenda to remember that the "unit of observation" (i.e., the thing that it applies to) is the play group. Adam said this above, but it's easy to lose sight of, particularly because we can see specific games as facilitating, and individual players as preferring a particular GNS mode (even as they also hold preferences for particular genres, techniques, and players over others). But the Creative Agenda of a group is something that only becomes visible within that group over a long enough time, as the group members demonstrate appreciation for and thereby reinforce particular individual contributions to play. This sometimes requires some rather extensive debriefing in order to decipher (see the old Forge thread started by Levi Kornelson in 2006, Frostfolk and GNS Aggravation, for example).

      \n What Bill said. More specifically, I believe the unit of observation is the play group during a given instance of play. A play group might have one CA on Tuesday and a different one on Wednesday, for the same Champions campaign (but probably not).

      And thus, this:\n
      1) My interpretation that the Creative Agenda is a goal in itself.
      \ndoesn't work within that definition. A group of people can all share a priority or they can not share a priority. I think "goal" is a little different than "priority," which I prefer. You can have two goals, but only one of them can be a priority.

      I'll add that Ron's 2003 Story Now essay does say that individuals have Creative Agendas:

      Narrativism: Story Now, by Ron Edwards:\n
      Creative Agenda is the blanket term for people's demonstrated goals and desired feedback during play. In the past, I called it "GNS." Since all of this is enclosed in Social Contract, GNS-stuff is not only "what I want" but also "what I want from role-playing with this group of people." Since Exploration necessarily includes System, that means, as soon as we start talking about Creative Agenda, real play has begun.

      On paper, I draw this term as an arrow, because this "step" or "level" in my model shifts out of the abstract and solidly into this group, playing this game, this way, at this time. The model instantly ceases to be a broad overview and becomes a diagnostic or description of a real play-experience among real people. Unless you are thinking of such a case, you will be left flailing at this point in the discussion.
      \nThings to take from that:

      1. CA is a goal, but it's a /demonstrated/ goal.
      2. CA doesn't make sense out of the context of "role-playing with this group of people."
      3. CA doesn't make sense out of the context of real, actual play.

      Thus,

      4. CA isn't about a person's /abstract/ preferences about role-playing.

      \n Oh, here's the bit I wanted to talk about:

      Ron Edwards in "Frostfolk and GNS Aggravation":\n
      PART TWO: AVOID THIS RED HERRING, PLEASE

      I am uninterested in personal motivations. That soloist might be a raving egotist who only tolerates the other band members insofar as they give him a chance to solo, or he might be an aspiring and humble youngster, grateful for the chance, or whatever.

      Relative to the point I am making, and about Creative Agenda in general, the psychologies of the participants are not part of the analysis.       \n For what it's worth, I agree with Sandra there (yeah, I'm not thoroughly orthodox on everything that Ron's ever said) - I think that discounting psychology as the originating explanatory framework for Creative Agenda is tricky. I think that you can affirm the existence of broad nature of Creative Agenda by external observation of the social cues (you can see from a person when they're interested and excited, or you can ask them, for instance), but I likewise fail to understand how the Creative Agenda modes of GNS can be derived or postulated without a model of the mind and some pretty heavy assumptions about what people value and how.

      After all, the key tenets of GNS concern cohesion and incohesion of Creative Agenda at the game table, and if we discount psychology as an explanation for that, then we have nothing whatsoever to explain why or how this should be the case. I guess we could just take it as a given that transcendent insight has provided us with the idea of the GNS modes, and we can still test them as social behaviours to some degree, but as I said, it's all quite tricky if one refuses to acknowledge or explain the underlying psychology of creativity even a little bit. We're stuck with a theory that predicts events based on antecedents that are undefined (maybe this player will perform a Gamist agenda at the table, but we can't know until he does), and does not explain the predictions in any way. It's like doing astrology without seeing the sky: we can say that clearly those people having marital troubles is because their star signs are in opposition this month, and we know that their star signs are in opposition because they are having marital troubles

      Disagreeing with Ron, I should say, never was that uncommon at the Forge. We wouldn't have much to discuss if we didn't disagree with each other on this or that, after all.

      \n Games can clash with groups (or to be more precise - players at the table can clash with themselves or others due to instructions they have been reading from a game text), and players can clash with players. I think that you shouldn't read too much into the notion that CA is expressed in group action - what Ron means by that is that we cannot discern CA (he believes) unless we look at what people are doing in a social circumstance. That does not mean that CA only exists in coherent group action, it means that we can only see people exercise a CA in the context of group action. Thus, a "CA conflict" or "CA clash" is when a group does not have a coherent Creative Agenda because individual players are acting upon conflicting CAs.

      As far as I understand Ron's thinking, I think that he agrees that individual players can hold a Creative Agenda, we just cannot truly perceive what it is until we see that player actually engage or fail to engage in play behaviour with their gaming group. This is a pretty practical opinion as far as it goes, there are certainly all sorts of pitfalls in self-reporting and such, people sure can deceive themselves in all sorts of ways. An individual player having a Creative Agenda is pretty fundamental to the theory (as are the psychological anteriors of how that agenda comes about and why, if you ask me), as that's where CA conflict basically comes from: individual players with their own life experiences and interests join together to play a game, and they discover that the other players are playing "wrong". If we say that the CA somehow just comes into existence at the moment that the play starts, we're basically just complexifying the terminology for no real gain: we still need to consider the personal creative interests, except for some reason we're no longer calling them "Creative Agenda", but something else apparently.

      But even this, if it's about group behavior, how can one player clash?
      Here are my various understandings of CA clash:

      Why we care that it happens:
      CA clash happens when two or more players want such fundamentally different things from play that they consistently don't see the value in each other's contributions.

      Why we care about the cause:
      "Basic point of play" has often proven to be a non-obvious place for people to look for issues of disagreement.

      An canonical example:
      CA clash happens when someone who would like to be playing this game Gamist and someone who would like to be playing this game Narrativist are playing this game together.

      A less canonical example:
      CA clash happens when one player wants to test their rules mastery skills and another player wants to test their spatial strategic position skills via the fiction. Two would-be Gamists, two very different agendas. Some might refer to this as a "mere" clash of technique preferences, but to me that's a meaningless distinction, as the result is the same: complete disinterest in each other's play.
