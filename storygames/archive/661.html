    I think there's a whole slew of "boys' hobbies" that are slowly trying to become mediums that interest adults of both genders and people from a more diverse variey of backgrounds. They are also struggling for respect in academia. The major ones are probably:

    - Roleplaying
    - Comics
    - Video Games
    - Cartoons

    They currently share a lot of the same traits. The indie roleplaying movement is a lot like the indie comics movement, and I imagine we'll soon see an indie-style publishing arm grafted onto a company like White Wolf, just like DC Comics starting Vertigo.

    American comics, video games, and cartoons have all been given a push towards "respectability" by the high social position they are given in Japanese culture. Manga, anime, and artsy Japanese video games have done a lot to help these "boys' media" gain attention in the US. Unless Andy gets Tenra Bansho out and it rocks the world, I don't see that happening for roleplaying, though.

    So yeah, these aren't related to roleplaying in the sense of being related practices, but, culturally, I think they're pretty close. Also, people who did one of these mediums are likely to be open to becoming a fun of the others, if they aren't already.

    Because I'm me, I, naturally, have two cents.

    I think that we could easily talk about what people in our groups happen to do, in addition to role-playing. (Mine would include language study/linguistics, martial arts, music composition, illustration, clubbing, and lots more.) But that doesn't really tell us much except about the people we're already playing with, does it?

    I do think it's useful to think about what hobbies are culturally allied with role-playing. No, I don't think that role-playing and comics are necessarily close to one another (although both are forms of multi-modal storytelling), but they're seen as part of a constellation of activities which are socially grouped together. To most people, comics and role-playing feel similar, and that's worth knowing.

    I'm not sure it's useful to throw up our hands and say, "Well, hey, everything is related to role-playing to some degree, because we're all people and these hobbies are all things that people do." There are some things that are more role-playing-like than others, and if we want to figure out how to learn from/reach out to/be curious about those other communities, it would be good to identify which ones they are.

    I see five main threads of commonality that we might be able to tug on. Or, if we want to turn around and say, "How can we talk to people who don't understand these things," that's cool too.

    I think that we have strong connections to hobbies that are:
    - narrative
    - improvisational
    - collaborative
    - creative/performative
    - game-like

    Some hobbies that we're related to are going to be just one of those things (i.e. comics readers, where we've got the whole story thing going on). Some are going to be related along several dimensions, like the SCA. Some hobbies that many role-players happen to do aren't going to appear under this analysis at all - because this part of how I'm thinking about the question has to do with structural factors, not with cultural/social ones. The nice thing about thinking about things this way, though, is that we have some idea of how we can connect to people in these other communities, not just that we can do so.

    By the way, there's free mind-mapping software available here, with a list of other (free and paid) solutions if you don't like the Java version.
