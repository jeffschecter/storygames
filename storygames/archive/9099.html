\n I'm re-starting work on one of my old RPG projects, and it's one that's heavily inspired by several CCGs (You know, like Magic: the Gathering, Yu-gi-oh, Vs. System, that sort of thing). Originally D&D was inspired by wargames, but I think that card games could be equally as inspiring. Thinking of CCGs in terms of RPGs brings to mind high levels of fiddly rules at first, but it doesn't necessarily have to be that way. I know some games use cards (In a Wicked Age's Oracles come to mind, but that reminds me more of a tarot deck than a CCG). I think that D&D 4e sort of moved in the direction of CCGs with the way its powers work. Hell, some people even make cards with their powers on them. Still though, it plays more like a war-game than a CCG.

Anyways, I was wondering what people thought about the relationship between CCGs and RPGs. What lessons could be learned from CCGs? Has anyone made (or is making) any games inspired by CCGs?

Personally, I think that the playing of cards representing characters or events, with more powerful or significant things being played as the game progresses to be a dynamic just waiting to be exploited by an RPG, but I'm interested in hearing what Storygames thoughts on the subject might be. Not sure if many of you guys are current or former CCG players, but feel free to chime in anyways if you have anything you want to add.

    \n I think the advantage in card-based games is that they can have relatively simple main rules, but then the cards can have most of the 'real' rules on them. The cards cover all the exceptions, the special cases, it's the common rule-book phrase "unless you have a card that says otherwise".

    The lesson you probably don't want to take is that of having to learn what all the cards are, so you know what can counter the cards you have. This effect is inevitable, but minimizing it will help new players from getting annoyed by it.

    Posted By: whiteknifeWhat lessons could be learned from CCGs?
    \nMultiple resources. MtG has at least 5 different resources, and even more depending on the card type ("eats lands", "eats player creature/artifact in order to cause effect").

    RPG: Have something more than just HP and attack power (rolling a die, die bonus to roll, etc). I dunno, "Agility/Skill points", "Terrain mastery points", etc which can be used together for various effects.

    These resources are "made to bloom" by the player: In other words, a user could forgo red/black/white mana and focus only on green and blue, and make a green/blue deck in turn.

    RPG: Thief could focus on a stealth and terrain resource, while a fighter could focus on a strength/weapon and damage/healing resource.

    \n timonkey has hit the nail on the head. Cards allow you to put relevant rules right in front of the player's eyeballs.

    Dragon Storm is an RPG played entirely (I think) through collectible cards. There was another RPG played using Magic cards, though the name escapes me.

    It seems to me that D&D 4e is strongly influenced by CCGs. Note the precision in their terminology. Effects are classified across multiple axes. The system is designed for extensibility and to minimize rules disputes, which come out of 15 years of experience with Magic: The Gathering and other CCGs.

    I used cards in my own game for rule reference and for the draft. Of course, repeating a rule vastly increases the possiblity of contradicting it. Get all the way through the development process and nail the rules down before summarizing them.

    \n One factor of CCGs that has yet to be mentioned is the concept of random resources. That is, I can tailor my deck to my liking, but I'm still limited by what cards happen to be in my hand at any particular time. Perhaps this feeling could be factored into an RPG somehow, without using an actual deck of cards. You create a character from a set of abilities or aspects, and then your roll (or otherwise randomize) to see which aspects are available to you for a particular scene? Not sure how fun it would be, but it would interesting to toy with.

    \n Cards have a lot of traits about them that I like, people have already identified some:

    * You can put rules for things right on your cards!
    * You can scramble them up and never be sure what is going to come out!

    Here are some more:

    * There are a finite number of cards, if I have the King of Spades, you don't, and only 3 other Kings are out there.
    * If I have a hand of cards that I'm playing from, I know what cards I have at my disposal, do I want to play something cool or save it back? It is a mystery.
    * Cards are tactile and you can lay them down, turn them over, turn them sideways, put them on top of each other or underneath each other, lay them on spots on a board, create little pathways with them...

    CCGs add:

    * Stacking the deck is a good thing! Loading up a deck with the kind of crap you want to pull, within parameters, is really great.

    Another (to me mostly negative aspect) of CCG's is power creep through subsequent releases of cards (if that would be done in the RPG setting, this approaches being an issue as each edition of D&D matures, for example).     \nMagic's power creep is pretty interesting, though. The very first set had what remain some of the most powerful cards ever, because Richard Garfield underestimated the game's popularity. Magic R&D has done a great job exploring horizontal design space and not just printing cards that are strictly better than what has come before.
