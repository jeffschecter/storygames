      \nWhile chatting with a player after a round of Technoir, I mentioned that I was worried about running AW in a 2-hour slot, and he laid some science on me, which he claimed he found here on SG. I'll see if I can dig up his source later. Here's the basic structure.

      Ask: "So, who's in charge?" Somebody will chime in, or get volunteered once the players realize you mean it.
      Ask: "So, why are you in charge? Is it because you run the cult? ...toss out Hocus... Is it because you've got the biggest, baddest gang in town? ...Chopper... Because you run the joint at the center of it all? ...Maestro'D... Or just cause you're the leader?" ...Hardholder...
      A picks one of those four playbooks, take the rest out of play.

      Ask: "Who wishes they were running the place instead?" Toss out Battlebabe, Brainer, Skinner, briefly explain them.

      \n Yeah, I think this conversation has been really great at illustrating the tensions that exist between different tasks that Games on Demand might be used for (in general, I think these are productive tensions, rather than negative ones). These tasks include, at least:

      - showcase indie games to as many people as possible
      - showcase as many different indie games as possible
      - showcase a wide variety of different types of indie games
      - sell as many copies of indie games as possible
      - allow people to try out games they might not otherwise get to play
      - playtest new games in a convention setting, with random people
      - bring in a new audience to indie games by hooking them with genres they already enjoy
      - play a bunch of really fun games with cool people
      - play a bunch of really fun games with old friends
      - play a bunch of really fun games with their designers
      - try out new gaming experiences that are more likely to happen in convention settings
      - create a sense of community and shared endeavor around indie games
      - have new experimental play experiences
      - etc.

      Negotiating between these and hitting as many of them as possible (or as people want) seems like the responsibility of both whoever's organized Games on Demand at a particular convention and also the people there running games. Not every convention or meetup is going to hit all of them every time, and it's not surprising that people have different priorities based on their interests and what they think can be most easily done at a particular convention.

      \n Just for the record: I'm very much in favor of setting up a "real" organization and doing a Kickstarter to fund GenCon 2013. I do a lot of organizing in my real life, and I don't often see this kind of energy and excitement around events. It's rare to see so many people give of their time, energy, and funds so freely, which tells me that we have a lot of really passionate and motivated people.

      At the moment, though, there isn't really a structure that ensures that GoD stays strong over time. We have some amazing leaders, but our resources are diffuse and personal; a few key people dropping out could severely impact the organization. It's unlikely those people are going to bail, but I think it's worth noting that a GoD Foundation would have a few people who were officially in charge of safeguarding the project over time.

      It seems to me that the organizers want to avoid the obligations that outside forces might put on the org. But I think the org already has obligations, silent and unspoken ties, that do restrain and channel our behavior already. Taking sponsorships might violate some of the neutrality and diversity that we think is central to our success, but a Kickstarter, allowing us to reach our fans, the people who are passionate as players, would really only bind the community closer together.

      I don't want to be naive about this: I know that setting up an org and running the Kickstarter would be an enormous effort. But I think we've got some serious momentum going right now that would make all those efforts well worth it.

      \n I am actually pretty strongly opposed to institutionalizing GoD in a formal way. I view collective indie endeavors like this much the way I view internet forums: when there stops being momentum and energy behind GoD, it's time will have passed and we'll organize what we do in some other way. That's already happened with the Forge Booth and several other things. We're still doing most of the same stuff, but leaving it as this freewheeling thing driven by individual passions means that it's adaptable and can take completely different forms if needed in the future. Nobody should feel like they have to carry the GoD banner if something else begins to make more sense.

      It may be a lot of work having these same discussions every year about how to change it and make it better, but if somebody took ownership of Games on Demand and asserted definitively what it was... it's easy for me to imagine a lot of indie folks going their own way or starting their own thing. When things get too big and established, indie folks either have to change the way they do things or start back again building up from the bottom. The grassroots of indie games convention organizing are local meetups and house cons, and folks can always go back to zero there.

      I am actually pretty strongly opposed to institutionalizing GoD in a formal way. I view collective indie endeavors like this much the way I view internet forums: when there stops being momentum and energy behind GoD, it's time will have passed and we'll organize what we do in some other way.
