How can toolkit games help players communicate expectations to each other?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nRy \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2008 \n edited September 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n This applies to any game with solid replay value - any game that can be played more than one way. Universalis, PTA, Solar System, and Mortal Coil are on my mind as I write this, but I bet Shock fits in here too.

Lots of games have "setup phases" that are at least partially optional, or partially done in advance away from the table. This can easily lead to miscommunications about expectations, despite the designer's efforts to explain it in flavor text.

Also, it's easy to drift off of or misremember the agreement after a few weeks go by.

But, this isn't outside the realm of the designer, because:
  • Elements of setup that have a mechanical impact on character creation can't be skipped.
  • Elements that empower other members of the group can't be done away from the table.
  • Elements of setup that can have a continuous impact in play are harder to forget about.
What else can games do, or do better, to help the players communicate their expectations - and then keep those expectations salient?

    \n One of the issues with a flags-based approach is that the flagging mechanism itself may limit what sorts of things you can flag. Say the setup phase allows you to choose which relationships are most important to you, or to create pre-existing relationships to use in play. That's cool, but it carries the assumption that relationships are an important part of play for all players.

    In tightly focused games, that's not a problem. If you don't want to play a game that's about relationships, you don't play that game. With a toolbox game though, this is important, because the game is intended to be drifted. It might or might not be about relationships, depending on the group and their tastes. If you have relationship flags, it becomes harder to drift away from relationship based play, especially if those flags are tied into the mechanics of play.

    The same could be said for thematic flags, setting flags, situation flags, and really any other kind of flag you invent. As an example of the way that having different goals impacts how flags might be used: Consider a group that exhibits both D&D-style Strategist tendencies and story game Radical tendencies (to pick a particular flavor of each.) The first might be much more interested in which parts of the world they get to see, and thus want to have ways to flag bits of the setting. The second might be much more interested in who they get to interact with, and therefore want to be able to flag either other characters or relationships with those characters. It's the difference between "I want to explore some dwarven ruins!" and "I want to figure out what makes the royal cartographer tick, so that I can convince him to divulge the location of the ruins!" For a toolbox style game, you'd ideally want whatever sort of flagging mechanism you come up to be able to handle both of those and a number of other cases as well.

    The trade-off though is that, with any sort of flag, the boundaries or constraints created by connecting them to "during play" mechanics will necessarily limit the amount you can drift them. Or, to put it another way, the more general you make the flags the harder it becomes to tie them into the procedures of play. Yet tying pre-game flags into play-time mechanics is one way, maybe the best way, to keep them relevant.

    It's complicated, and I'm still thinking about it pretty hard.

    \n Use a Coolmap.

    That is, map out beforehand what elements (setting, thematic, whatever) people are interested in seeing. Vote for them so you know which ones are most interesting to the greatest number of people. Then, whoever introduces them into a scene gets a number of points equal to the votes to spend on Scene Stuff.

    Not a total solution, of course, since you can still have the "Vampires!" "Yes, totally!" thing going on where the two players are thinking about entirely different kinds of vampires. But given that part of the Coolmap process involves connecting up the elements to each other in a network, Verge-style, it is at least reasonably likely to come out that one person is looking for "creepy vampire ultraviolence" and the other is looking for "romantic vampire angst".

    \n Re: Joshua's post

    I want to clarify the implications around In A Wicked Age:

    1) Best interests have no mechanical effect.
    Best interests still are part of setting up conflict for the Owe list, which is the key to the whole game (this is clear in the text). So they don't have a direct mechanical effect but they provide the most important lineup for mechanical effects later in the game.

    2) Best interests are something you'll drift off of in the later weeks of the game.
    Each new session has new best interests set up - you don't have to carry best interests from one game to the next. Your play proceeds to the next stage, with new best interests. But the important distinction here is that best interests are NOT done at the end of the session to set up the next chapter; they're done at the beginning. That makes them salient for this session and not prone to drifting. Any drifting that's happening in between sessions is then either nipped, made explicit, or is irrelevant because there's a new setup phase each session.

    Other stuff:

    3) The Trash example sounds like it's making my point; Shock is supporting the game featuring asked-for content and it's being taken somewhere cool by the players, but those asked-for and agreed-on elements aren't being lost in the "somewhere cool" because Shock is helping them stay in. But if you actually wanted to flag "relationships on the internet vs. real life" would you know to choose Drug Addiction and Swappable bodies as what you're flagging?

    4) If you want to support the players in flagging a theme or style you need to have something that's going to recur in play. But I think what Joshua's getting at is that flagging this stuff can't just be a one-time thing, and I'm on board with that.

    Posted By: Ryan StoughtonGetting games to get groups to communicate about tone and style, and stick with it, is the holy grail.
    \nIt has long been my contention that there are four important aspects (or quadrants) to think about when you analyze a topic. Those four are behaviors, experiences, cultures, and systems. There's a cycle, since individual's experiences shape their values, which shape the shared cultural values, which shape the systems in place, which shape the behaviors people engage in, which complete the loop by shaping what sort of experience one has. (It runs the other direction too, but that's less relevant here.) For roleplaying, you can roughly equate culture= shared preferences/interests, systems=design, and behaviors=play.

    To situate what you're saying in this thread within that context, flags are a way to feed preferences/interests (culture) into game designs in such a way that the rules and structures of the games (systems) promote certain kinds of play (behaviors) that maintain interest, engagement, and investment (experiences.) Because there is a feedback loop, the way that play evolves (like Joshua was talking about) can shift what people are most interested in right now. What just happened affects what you want to happen next. This sort of feedback process is present whether or not it's acknowledged by the participants, or the game they're playing.

    IAWA handles the feedback cycle in a pretty genius way, by iterating the setup phase, yet carrying over elements from one session to the next. And it boosts the cycle every time around by offering up some random pieces to build on, yet it lets you choose which ones you want to invest the most energy in -- an implicit flag.

    \n Agreed on both counts. I don't think it will too controversial, though, to say that some games fit themselves into that feedback cycle better than others -- some are more cognizant of what's happening than others, in a way. This ties in to the ongoing discussion about how much of the load has to be carried by social agreements outside of the stated procedures of a game, and inversely, how well the game creates the certain sorts of social agreements that it needs to function properly.

    Connecting that more explicitly to this thread, you can play, say, L5R, which has no explicit flagging, and pick up that slack by agreeing what your game is going to be about. You're basically supplementing the effects that the game has on the feedback cycle with additional support structures, in order to create the sort of game experience you want. (To coin an unfortunate term, you're filling the Un-Fruitful Void.) With other games, you don't need those extra agreements about what the game is about, because the structure of the game itself provokes you to make agreements using flags, and then ties those into the ongoing process of play by making them mechanically relevant. And some games do that better than others, as well.
