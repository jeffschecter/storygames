How many fiddly bits can a gamer be expected to fiddle with?

\n
\n

\n This paraphrases the line in "Amadeus" where the Archduke says to Mozart, "You put in too many notes. There are only so many notes the royal ear can hear in an evening." (To which Mozart replies, "Which notes should I cut out?")

So how many bits in a game can a gamer be expected to keep in their head at once? How many angles / stances / Power Pools / Factions / Influences / Powers / Abilities / Compels / whatever?

In Spirit of the Century you've got 10 aspects, 5 stunts, and however many skills to keep track of, your Fate Points, plus the bits of business that evolve during play like Tagging the Aspect of a scene (it's foggy) or compeling the Aspect of a villain (Dr. Volcano sleeps with his teddy bear!).

In Mortal Coil, you've got your Action chips, your Power chips, your Passion chips, your Magic chips. You've got however many Magic rules have been created, your Passions, your Faculties, your Aptitudes.

In Burning Wheel....you've got a lot. Too much for me to remember off the top of my head. Artha (different flavors of it), your character's lifepaths, your Instincts, etc., etc.

In Werewolf: the Apocalypse you had your Tribe, your Auspice, your Breed, your Gifts, your Rage, Gnosis and Willpower, Attributes, and Abilities (Skills/Talents/Knowledges).


I'm not criticizing these games. I like them. I just wonder. So how much can we expect our players to hold in their "temporary memory"? I know character sheets are for when people forget. But I think a game really "gets going" when people know their character and its abilities well enough to play without much reference to the sheet.

Let me know what you think?

    \n I think that what you keep track of in your head at once is not necessarily the important thing. Once you commit certain things to long-term memory, you can access much more of it at once. For instance, in D&D, I know most of my character's powers because he's been using them for several levels and I've thrown them around a lot. I remember a lot of monster stats because I've run into them quite a bit and relatively recently, though I sometimes remember stats for older-edition monster better.

    It's how many new things come up in a particular scene that causes me to get confused. If I always have +2 to hit, no problem. If I have +2, and then the bard's +1, and then the +2 from Bull's Strength, and then -2 from circumstance, and a 50% miss chance, I become more likely to forget to confirm a critical hit.

    Posted By: OgremarcoI found that "added layer of complication" to be a design strength in GURPS. I liked being able to hammer the game into shape for what I wanted to do with it. Granted I played much longer games then, so there was a lot more time between choices.
    \nThere's a bigger problem inherent in that idea than just the amount of time the GM has on hand. The implication of the GM modifying the game to make it work, often in rather undocumented ways, is that power distribution in the game is unbalanced between GM and players. That is, the GM now knows the algorithms behind the universe, while the players are hapless victims of rules they cannot hope to comprehend.

    I'm not saying that GMs are evil. I'm saying that with such power distribution, the GM might as well be evil. To put it another way, whether God exists but is out for your throat, or whether God doesn't exist and your suffering is caused by ignorance and random chance, are synonymous.

    This is obviously very conducive to extreme Sim play, where the players can simply state what their characters do and the GM/rules-complex (in such a game, the two are not separate entities) acts as a black box, feeding them back a plausible result. I don't have a problem with this if that's what players (GM inclusive) want.

    But I do have a problem with the statement that ultra-complex smorgasbord rules systems intended for GM customization are somehow generally useful, and that such design principles are generally a good idea.

I just have never, ever, in decades of gaming with several dozens of gamers in two different countries, seen that actually happen with systems like GURPS or HERO. What I have seen is GMs using the rules they feel are interesting and important, ignoring others, applying yet others in some occasions and not at other occasions, and assuming that total knowledge of the rules is their job and their job alone.     This probably looks like nothing but an annoying tangent in the thread, but it's actually very central to it. In a game where the rules are not intended to simulate physical reality, it's imperative for players to know the rules - maybe not inside and out, but well enough that they can reliably use them to serve their creative goals. For most players who aren't rules junkies, that means Keep It Simple Stupid. Complex smorgasbords like GURPS and HERO work well for what they do, but they sure as hell aren't "generic" in the "totality of available role-playing experiences" sense.

    By "our players" I assume you mean "the players of our games, including GM". Maybe you mean "the other players of the game I'm GMing". Please specify.

    For most games, you can expect a different level of investment from GMs and other players. This is especially true in games where the GM drives the story, since they tend to be heavily invested into that story. The level of investment drives the mental energy people are willing to expend in learning and remembering the fiddly bits of a game.     This is why BW/BE requires a lot of player investment, compared both to GURPS and to D&D - there's a lot of fiddly bits and they apply equally to GM and players.

    This is why players can't be expected to remember all the house rules, exceptions and expansions a GM may opt to use (regardless of the fact that they never actually own all those books and can peruse them at their leisure).

    \nHell, yes. This makes perfect sense, and checks out against some other knowledge I've taken from the past. When I was running games for my kids I was always pleasantly surprised the amount of detail they recalled. Absorption of lore, facts, organized systems of information was something they did as well as they breathed. I believe this was in part due to the fact that they were invested / engaged. They certainly didn't learn their multiplication tables with the same vigor.\n
