\nI played a game at Gen Con where 4 out of 7 players cried.
\n
\nThe GM started the game by saying, "this isn't a game\u2026 this is a movie, and we're all directors, actors, and audience." The game was as emotional as any movie I have ever seen.
\n
\nThis was NOT a perfect storm of elements coming together to create a rare experience. My friends have played several games with this GM where multiple people cried repeatedly. The experience was crafted (part GM skill, part game design), not random.
\n
\nHe used a combination of GM and game design techniques to achieve this. Many of which I would have never expected to be so emotional. The game was like jeepform\u2026 but less intense (which made it more emotional). For example, one technique he used was boredom! There were long stretches of the game that were "boring" from a modern movie perspective. Some of his techniques are detailed in Graham's new book, Stealing Cthulhu. The game was a perfect execution of page 38.
\n
\nI don't have a lot of time right now to go into details but I will later.
\n
\nFor now, have you ever played a game where you or another player cried*?
\n
\nWhat caused this reaction?
\n
\nAs a complete aside, I feel really lucky. I've had the privilege and fortune to game with hundreds of different people all over the world, many with completely different styles, many out of my initial comfort zone, and I've learned a lot. I work as a creative director professionally and it's definitely improved my craft.
\n
\n*Cry in a good way. There are dysfunctional ways to make someone cry in a game. That's not what I'm talking about. I mean the kind of crying where someone watching a movie is so emotionally engaged in what's happening that they get caught up in the moment.

    \n
    \nGAME MECHANIC DETAILS:
    \n
    \nThe game was a hack of Unknown Armies.
    \n
    \nSucceed at tasks if you roll 2d10 percentage dice under a stat or skill. Stats ranged from 40-60%. Skills were 30% or less. If you roll, you will most likely lose. This fact was emphasized. You can tap your traits (Obsession, Rage Stimulus, Fear Stimulus, Noble Stimulus) a limited number of times to manipulate the dice to increase your chance of success. But the more stress you overcome, the more hardened you become, the less emotional you become, the less you can tap your traits to help you. In play, we rarely roll skills till the very end of the game. But we frequently, unrelentlessly, almost immediately roll against stress. Our characters started the game with hardened and failed boxes checked off.
    \n
    \nWhen faced with stress, you roll 2d10 under your Mind stat. On average you have a 50% chance of success but you roll frequently (I rolled stress 11 to 13 times). When a stress roll is called, it is against 1 of 5 categories of stress: Violence, Unnatural, Helplessness, Isolation, and Self. And each stress test has a severity associated with it rated from 1 (lowest) to 10 (highest). When you succeed a stress roll, you mark 1 of the 10 Hardness boxes next to that specific category of stress (say Helplessness). You are then in control of your character to act as you wish. The more hardened you become, they less you are able to use your traits but the less stressed you become when faced with the same source of stress. For example, if you are asked to make say a stress roll against Violence, severity 2 but you have 2 boxes of Hardness checked next to Violence\u2026 you don't have to make the test! Failing a stress roll is when the craziness begins\u2026
    \n
    \nFailing stress rolls greatly differs from the default Unknown Armies rules. If you fail 5 stress rolls in any category, you become broken. This never happened so I don't know the specific consequences of reaching this state but it was always a threat since the GM can ask you to make stress roll after stress roll in succession, you usually have a 50% chance, and there is very little you can do to improve your odds of success. You are never safe! But the effects of failing a single stress roll was unlike what I've experience in most other games.
    \n
    \nFailing a stress roll means losing control of your character. You choose 3 options: fight, flee, or freeze. You have 3 seconds to choose one or the GM chooses. Then the GM tells you what happens. If you choose fight, the GM could say you snap, try to punch a police person, and then when you snap out of it you are in jail. If you choose flee, the GM could say you run into oncoming traffic. If you choose freeze\u2026 you don't stand in place, instead your mind shuts down and the GM can do anything they want. And to be very clear, in most cases, when you failed a roll, you were plunged into the worst possible situations imaginable. The GM tells you what you do, and the results of your action. If you choose fight, the GM says who you fight, if you win or lose, describes the immediate consequences, and hands you back control of your character at the worst possible time. You often are in a situation where you are apologizing for something your character did, but it is also something you as a player did not do. And nothing was held back!
    \n
    \n
    \nSOCIAL CONTRACT:
    \n
    \nNOTE: The GM was intelligent, kind, usually soft spoken, often complementary, patient but strict. When he makes statements, he doesn't ask for approval or consensus. This is ultimately his game. He clearly without hesitation states in a parental / authoritative way what he wants and doesn't want. Many of the players later commented that the GM at times felt like their father was speaking to them (even before the GM played their character's fathers in the game).
    \n
    \nLoosely paraphrasing the GM's introduction:
    \n
    \n"This is not a game, this is a movie. A movie like The Ring, The Others, or The Exorcist. I like to run what I call, Twilight Zone adventures. I am the director of this movie, you are the actors in this movie. I pay for this movie and film is expensive. In fact, I pay for it inch by inch so I will be very strict about cutting scenes, saying when and where scenes start, and keeping everything moving."
    \n
    \n"There are times that I will hand the director chair over to you. When you are the director, you say what happens. Don't ask me if there is a window behind you. If you say there is, then I paid for someone in my prop department to put a window there. You are in control. Describe everything from the perspective of what the audience sees. Tell us what the camera sees, how the camera moves. Tell us where and when we are. Tell us who is there. Tell us what everyone is doing (he made it clear later that we could even tell other players what their characters were doing when it was our scene, although very few people felt comfortable doing this). But sometimes, because film is expensive, I will tell you when the scene cuts and when we need to move on. If you say something that is untrue (I can't remember the example, but say you said World War 2 ended in 1949 instead of 1945), for all intents and purposes it is true in our movie. Just do not contradict something we have already established and do not break the movie's tone. And be mindful of the people around us, we are not alone in this room. You may say awful things but if for example, you see a child in the room, censor yourself as needed."
    \n
    \n"You don't have to always speak in character but always describe what the audience sees. I will not tolerate making this movie into a joke. And I will not tolerate breaking the tone of the movie. Do not make inside jokes or turn this movie into a parody of other movies. There will not be any explicit sex in this movie on camera but sex can happen behind closed doors outside of what the camera and the audience sees. If you need to use your phone, to talk or text, you may, but do not do it at this table. But feel free to leave the room and come back when you are ready. There will not be any specific bathroom breaks, if you need to go to the bathroom or go get water, feel free to go at any time (and then he gave us very clear, very friendly instructions about where the bathroom and water fountains are)."
    \n
    \n"Keep the details of your character handout secret but we all see what the camera sees. If someone reveals a secret that the audience sees but our characters don't have any knowledge of, you may ask the person who revealed the secret if there is anyway your character can find out what happened."
    \n
    \n"I should warn you, I've run this game 13 times now and in 10 of the games at least 1 person, often multiple people cried. This movie deals with sensitive material that often cuts very close to home for many people."
    \n
    \nAlthough there was a warning about many things, there wasn't a discussion about racism, sexism, abuse, different kinds of violence, or any other content that someone might object to or feel uncomfortable with in the game. The GM never said it, but it seemed implicit that he would push us but not too far and some of these uncomfortable topics were used explicitly to make the game feel more "real". He also gave off the feeling, "you can trust me." But there was at least 2 scenes where a few of us were worried, "are we really going there" (I'm not going to go into more detail than that) but the GM never went too far. It felt like Jeepform in this way. Everything about the game actually screamed Jeepform to me.

    \n
    \n
    \nCHARACTERS:
    \n
    \nEvery player had a pre-generated character with a handout filled with backstory written from the character's perspective. The writing was excellent. Every character had their unique voice, concerns, and perspective. The handouts detailed how we're connected to everyone else in just enough detail. Every character had clear motivations, fears, and reasons for those motivations and fears.
    \n
    \nEvery player hand a giant standing tent card that had our character's picture, name, and outwardly obvious traits written on them on both sides of the card so everyone could see who everyone was playing.
    \n
    \nWe also had smaller tent cards for "fight, flee, freeze" that we could place on top of our character tent cards when we failed a stress roll so everyone knows who was stressing out and how.
    \n
    \nWe were playing teenagers, all in high school, all living with their parents.
    \n
    \n
    \nGAME PLAY:
    \n
    \nThe GM was soft spoken most of the time in a way that made you lean forward to listen closely to what he was saying. But he would grow very loud when it was appropriate and the contrast was at times startling in a good way
    \n
    \nI don't know to what extent, but the game felt very scripted except for the end (where you had a lot of freedom). Although "feeling" scripted and "being" scripted are 2 different things. I know for example, most of the GM's notes were bullet points. It felt like we were supposed to do specific things, sometimes in specific orders (there were 2 instances where the GM asked us to do things in different orders because it would be better for the movie). This tight scripting allowed the GM to foreshadow in overt and covert ways almost everything that was going to happen (which greatly added to the tension). The game was full of symbolism and feedback.
    \n
    \nThe game always felt real. The GM was a good actor. Everyone stayed in character 90% of the time.
    \n
    \nThe GM described everything in terms of a movie. When important, he described where the camera was focused and how the camera moved. Everything was described as a scene. He even described title and end credits. And the abstract introduction collage perfectly foreshadowed what was going to happen in the movie.
    \n
    \nThe GM was your biggest fan. In general, your character was made to look positive in some way. The GM was very supporting and complimentary. Additionally, because making skill rolls almost always meant failure, he often would allow you to succeed at anything you wanted to do. You never roll to spot, listen, or research. And in a few cases where a skill was called for, if you roleplayed in a convincing and persuasive manner, you would gain a bonus or he would change his mind and no skill roll was needed.
    \n
    \nIn a lot of modern horror, you often build up tension, then release it. Build up tension more, then release again. You keep ebbing and flowing upward till you reached the climax. In this game, there is very little release. The game is often very quiet. Sometimes even boring (except the GM is strict about you staying mostly in character, not playing with your phones, or doing anything that would alleviate this boredom). The game just slowly, slowly ratcheted up the tension to a boiling point over 4 hours.
    \n
    \nSPOILERS, SPOILERS, SPOILERS ---> The game was also an emotional thrill ride with twist after twist. You thought you did something terrible that meant you were all going to die BUT it turns out you were safe BUT your loved ones were the ones who were going to die BUT it turns out your loved one aren't who you thought they were! <---SPOILERS, SPOILERS, SPOILERS
    \n
    \nBecause we were playing real people with real problems, in a few cases characters had to deal with problems that players or people they know and care about had to deal with in their own lives. Everything was close to home. Very much like Jeepform.
    \n
    \nSPOILERS, SPOILERS, SPOILERS ---> There was a moment where the big truth was revealed in the form of an NPC telling us a story that was pre-written on 2.5 pages. Instead of the NPC talking, the GM decided that it should shown to the audience as a flash back and we should each take turns reading 1 paragraph from the story. Reading it this way was quite impactful, didn\u2019t feel monotonous (having 1 person speak for that long), and was especially chilling because it changed who are characters thought we were. This was the moment where a few people cried. <---SPOILERS, SPOILERS, SPOILERS
    \n
    \nSPOILERS, SPOILERS, SPOILERS ---> The second moment where people cried was when they had to face their loved ones after finding out the truth. Life and death was on the line in a moment where you could feel your teenage characters "growing up" for the first time as they faced the people they loved who were simultaneously not the people you thought they were BUT still the people you always loved. And give than the GM naturally had a "parental" way of speaking to you the entire game, having him play your parents in a few cases was intense. <---SPOILERS, SPOILERS, SPOILERS
    \n
    \nThe way control worked in the game was a rollercoaster ride. You had a lot of control then no control then a lot of control then no control. You could frame a scene and then all of a sudden you had no control. There were times you roll skills, many times you automatically succeeded, and many times you automatically failed. You had power then no power.
    \n
    \nThere were many personal dark secrets slowly revealed.
    \n
    \nAnd there were no cartoon characters or stereotypes. You had plenty of "bad" people doing good things for bad reasons AND "good" people doing bad things for good reasons.

    \n OK, so I've flat-out cried at a game once. It happened this march, when I played my game The Dreaming Crucible at the Gomestorm convention with one old friend, and one new one. The other players didn't cry, but were very supportive and present with me in that moment.

    What caused this reaction? Well, I blogged about it, like I do, but in brief:

    So, as the Dark Faerie player it's my duty to play harsh influences on a troubled teenager, both in the real world and in Faerie. Lisa, the Hero player, chose a Seed from the book about an angry father chewing out a sullen son, and it was job to frame that scene. I fished around mentally for a "hook" for playing the dad, and grabbed onto some mannerisms of my own angry father as a basis. As we played that scene, I ended up playing him more and more as my Dad, and when the boy Trevor came back home from his Faerie quest, the strength, bravery and forgiveness he showed his father caused me to just break. We called scene, I explained what I'd been dealing with, and started crying. Hugs all around.

    I didn't set out to engineer some sort of catharsis for myself. I was just saying what I saw in the moment, what I felt was true (this is a rule of the game, in fact). And the truth was that this dad was my dad. I knew exactly how to play him, because I knew him. And the simple and pure way that Lisa described Trevor's forgiveness resonated with me, because things with my dad had seemed so complex and he died with so much unsaid between us. It felt heart-wrenching and wonderful to dredge all those feelings up and giving a new level of closure to them before putting them to bed again.

    Supportive and thoughtful players were definitely key. Even though I'd just met one of them, they both bought into the "I Will Not Abandon You" foundation of the game in a way that told me it was safe to go there, even before I knew what "there" we were going to.

    * * *

    I've had a few "almost-cries" in games; times where events moved me but I didn't quite break into tears, just misted up. I think this is far more common for me, not because I'm afraid to cry or be vulnerable or what have you, but because I'm trying to control myself for the sake of the roleplaying. Especially in a game where the material is moving and meaningful, I don't want to break the spell by having to bawl, recover myself, and dive back in. So I'm going to restrain my outward reaction at least enough to continue playing, because we've just gotten to the good stuff!\n

One was the unfolding story of the boy Amiel, and how we who were playing the soldiers were taking him under our wing, and inadvertently crushing his child's soul in molding him into a soldier. By the end of the game the boy Amiel was gone, replaced by a hollow, self-denying martyr. It was agonizing.

      And the other, closer to home theme that wrenched my heart was the Captain Pierre Roger (my primary character) and his dead, lifeless marriage. Right from the start the creeping separation between him and his wife Philippa (played by Susan, I think) was apparent, and it only grew. he was shut out of her life entirely, and their newborn son, finding comfort in the arms of Arsende the Harlot, but longing for intimacy with his family.     \n As far as John's mystery GM goes, I'm intrigued and would like to try this game, but I will admit I'm a bit off-put by his particular brand of intentionality, as you've described (I read most of the description, but not the SPOILERS! parts). I would never say I'm suspicious--you're reporting that the situation was non-abusive, and that's enough for me. But I find it difficult to engage with emotional vulnerability when someone's holding themselves apart from me rather than being right there with me putting their emotions on the line.

    There's a significant strain of classical GM advice that's all about "making" the players feel this and that. "I'm running a horror campaign, how can I make the players really feel scared?" "The PCs keep killing off all my NPCs! How can I make them care about them?" "I've got this tragic arc in mind for the game's climax; how can I make the players really invest in how sad it is?"

    There's a functional question at the core of these kinds of inquiries, which is "how can we foster emotional investment at the table?" But it's encrusted with so many assumptions, about power dynamics, GM-as-performer, etc. that it's nearly impossible, in my experience, to really address the issue of functional, mutual techniques. It tends to be more about tricking the (other) players to feel emotions, just like you trick them into following a plot hook and so forth.

    When I asked on my blog for people to share character trajectories that personally move them, this guy Kevin responded, "On rare occasions I have actually brought tears to the eyes of my players when I endangered a NPC that they\u2019ve come to enjoy during a Game. I consider it the highest complement to my story line when I can manage it but save the attempts for important twists in the gaming."

    I asked him if he'd ever had his own emotions engaged that way as a player and he was like, "uh, no." It definitely seemed like something that GMs do TO players.

    Sounds like this guy did well at putting the assumptions and techniques out on the table. That's great! Far better than trying to slip the emotions in without the players noticing or whatever. My own instinct would be to explain expectations and warn about emotional subject matter, but stop short of saying "most people cry at this game," since that would seem to weight the results toward that. But whatever! Transparent social contract is good!

    I still feel a little weird about the whole thing because, as I say, I want all the players, including the GM, to be right there with me in vulnerability-ville, not not holding back and orchestrating the experience. The vulnerability of a GM comes from a different place than the vulnerability of an ordinary player--that of having to put pressure on characters we care about, rather than responding to pressure--but it's still vulnerability. In my Dreaming Crucible examples above, I was playing the role of adversity-provider, but I was very emotionally involved; providing that adversity was agonizing, and I had to fight to not flinch from it.

    So, I dunno. It sounds like his setup requires a very specific manner and method, and he's got it down pat. I wouldn't ask him to alter that. I just wonder how I'd feel playing under those conditions.

      \n With this post, I\u2019m going to try to address both David\u2019s question (how do the players interact with the story) and Jason's question (about player dynamics). Here's how a session runs, from my perspective.

      As you can tell from John\u2019s earlier descriptions, my player briefing makes it clear that I want the players to participate and contribute. Focus on making our movie. Always keep the audience engaged. But don't become the audience: Don't start talking about how this scene reminds you of an episode of Seinfeld or telling us about the fantastic steak you had last night.

      Then I give the players their character hand-out. One of the things I love about Unknown Armies is that when you finish reading the hand-out, you know your character. You know what he cares about and what he hates and who he\u2019s best friends with. So you\u2019ve got a character you understand and can sympathize with.

      After I teach the rules, I describe the opening credits and the scene that runs behind the opening credits. Then I frame the opening scene and say, \u201cOK, you\u2019re all in the parking lot, waiting for the drop-off. I need the movie audience to see the tension. And this is where you make your first impression on the audience. What do you want them to learn about you right away? We\u2019ll start with you -- tell me exactly what the movie audience sees.\u201d I\u2019m very active in the first scene, until the players start to get comfortable with their characters. Then I can pull back.

      Because the game mechanics are both lightweight and infrequently invoked, the players can on focus making a good movie rather than making a good die roll.

      The rest of the movie continues the pattern. I keep the camera moving from player to player, so that everyone\u2019s engaged. I\u2019ve designed the scenario and the characters in tandem, so that I can ensure each has hooks into the other. Sometimes the player characters push the story forward; other times, the story (or nonplayer characters) push the PCs forward.

      None of the scenes have a predetermined ending, but every scene has a purpose (find the clue, talk with Bob, foreshadow the storm, whatever), because I hate scenes where the players have no idea what to do. There\u2019s something or someone that will interact with the characters in each scene. When I feel the audience has seen enough, I cut to the next scene.

      One thing we haven\u2019t discussed yet: I believe that everyone understands movies. Even if you don\u2019t know what a jump cut is, everybody knows, through constant exposure to the medium, how movies are supposed to work. Once they get started, players quickly figure out how to tell the story in terms of \u201cwhat the audience sees\u201d rather than \u201cwhat I do.\u201d And we all know that movies invite you to become emotionally involved with them. I live the movie analogy in my games, and I think that\u2019s a big part of what makes my games accessible.

      So, to summarize, the things that I think keep the players involved are:\n
        \n
      • Sympathetic characters that feel like real people, with real relationships and real problems.
      • An engaging story that\u2019s internally consistent (because plot holes drive me nuts).
      • Every scene has a purpose and something that will interact with the characters or that the characters can interact with.
      • Aggressive control of pacing.
      • Milking the movie analogy for all that it\u2019s worth.

      • Camera tricks. I once started an adventure with a cut scene of the main villain doing something evil. But in the entire cut scene, the villain had his back to the camera. So when the PCs finally met him later in the adventure, they didn\u2019t know who he was, even though their players could visualize exactly what he had done at the start of the movie. Players are encouraged to use similar tricks when they narrate a scene.
      • Suspense. Your character looks into the box and the audience sees the look of awe on her face. She closes the box without showing the camera what\u2019s inside. The audience wants to know what is it?
      \nFocus! Don\u2019t use my expensive film on something the audience doesn\u2019t have to see. \n
      • Understand what can be done off camera. The audience doesn't need to see everything.
      • Every frame of the movie should work toward conveying something important. Sure, there can be a scene of your character and my character just talking. But what do you want that conversation to convey the audience?
      \nGive the audience a reason to keep watching.\n
      • In drama, the audience should cringe when your character is presented with difficult choices.
      • In pulp, the audience should gasp when your character is in danger.
      • In cyberpunk, the audience should wonder who you can trust.

      \n Latecomer in the thread here. I don't think I've cried before during any tabletop rpg, but I rarely cry during movies, and the few times I have have been right after break ups. Big fights or break ups with people I love are pretty much the only time I cry, really. I have however cried from online roleplaying but I think that was largely due to some problems I've had with that medium. The times I've been brought to tears all involved always IC muck style rpgs where something in the story either created a huge rift between a character my pc was close to or just plain killed one of us off. Part of what hit me hard was the tragedy in the story but another big part would be realizing that my only connection to this person I enjoyed playing with was through the game, so by losing the in character relationship our actual relationship was effectively cut off, making it difficult to seperate my character's feelings of loss from my own. I've been hit pretty hard by finding out someone I wanted to play with but didn't know yet had their character killed off or otherwise removed from play due to a traumatic event too. I think the frustration of trying to keep up with people who were online a lot more than me was sometimes part of it, where between two of my sessions my character's best friend or whatever went through a whole story arc that led to cutting them off from whatever plot or character development I had been working towards with them. Another part of it must also be that I have privacy in an online situation where in person I don't want to make everyone else uncomfortable, but those added elements coming together seemed essential to pushing me far enough to cry.

      By the same token similar situations hit me hard in tabletop games, but usually only in longform games where I've had time to grow attatched to characters and at worse I tear up. A random and meaningless, nonconsentual PC character death often bothers me a lot too, as a similar combination of the in game tragedy, not being able to continue to play scenes between my character and theirs anymore, and the player of the character who died arbitrarily (unfairly in my mind) losing their agency to impact the fiction. Probably another reason that bothers me is my first experience with roleplaying, where the GM quickly killed me and my friends characters off within a few minutes after I failed a dex check to not spill my beer on an overpowered NPC.

      Part of me is jealous of people who talk about crying in tabletop games, partly because I wish I was that good of a method actor, and partly because I wish I had the courage to allow myself to express emotions that openly. The other part of me is glad that it's easy for me to seperate my feelings from the fiction for the most part.
