\n I continue to encounter these types of situations where it's one player trying to hide, or sneak up and ambush the next, in games like TSoY and IAWA, where part of the resolution steers you toward presuming that you "sense" the other player-- which sort of defeats the point.
\n
\nI know that IAWA suggests you resolve that question at the first roll, but somehow that just feels funny/wrong.
\n
\nHow do you resolve Sneak/Hide issues in your more freeform games?

    \n In TSoY you can make it a contest between relevant skills. We had an epic sneaking contest that ended in PC death in one game. Pain was Brought Down. A guy in search of his true mother was out-stalked by his deadly foe inside a carnival tent, and died at her feet. Total PVP.

    In a looser system, say Archipelago II, it'd go like this:

    JASON: I sneak up and stab your dude in the heart. The king must die.

    RUSTIN: I don't think that will be quite so easy.

    JASON: Joel, will you draw a resolution card?

    (JOEL draws "Yes, but...")

    RUSTIN: Damn you Morningstar!

    JASON: I stab him in the heart! He dies at my feet! With my brother dead I am now king!

    RUSTIN: But a ray of dawn light pierces the cathedral, revealing your foul act to the assembled bishops!

    JASON: Nooooooooooooo!

    \n Maybe I misunderstood - in this case it was framed as a duel. Two guys who both knew the other was after them in the dark.

    In TSoY I'd deal with a sneak-attack ambush type deal as an uncontested roll and riff off degree of success for the initial surprise, unless the victim had some way of being forewarned. Maybe negotiate with the player to establish what various degree sof success meant in advance. I've also played in settings where there was a "Sense Danger" ability, in which case this sort of thing could be contested.

    \n After some thought, I think I missed the core issue.
    \n
    \nI am starting to think players new to indie/hippy games which require engagement in a scene-- have a default avoidance reaction, and that reaction becomes a "sneak/hide" in the fiction.
    \n
    \nSo, where the GM frames a tight scene, with people in conflict with each other-- calling out for a player move their player into the fray. And then I get, "I hide and make sure they don't see me," reactions from players.
    \n
    \n
    \nThe fictional component of being "hidden" but it is mirroring their own desire to hide from the game itself--
    \n
    \nIs this a poor scene framing skill on my part? Poor communication of expectations?
    \n
    \nAny suggestions/observations would be helpful. Thanks.
    \n(95% of the time I'm GMing these games for folks completely new to the system).

    \n I was trying to ease them into the setting (game of IAWA, just recently).
    \nI asked them "where would your PC would be in a typical day."
    \nThen I pushed a NPC that had a Best interested against them into the setting they described. In the most recent case I put another PC that had conflicting best interests in the scene. Both characters then shifted to the "hide." So I had to move my NPC to take concrete action.
    \n
    \nI did eventually get the scene going-- once we resolved the hide/sneak portion, the conflict went ok--not great.
    \n
    \nThat first part of "hiding" just seemed wonky to me. I had to go to the dice to resolve it. But the way the dice resolved it seemed strange. Players were narrating "sensing" something there-- so at that point the other player was, technically, no longer hidden.

    \n Okay, that's interesting.

    But in some way, it makes some sense.

    Say I aim to find the Jewel of Ochra (whatever). You ask me what scene I want, I say I want a scene in a temple, praying the gods for good fortune. Total color scene. Or, possibly, refreshment scene, but lets say its purely color.

    Then you send in rival npc. Well, maybe be *I* wouldn't hide, but, if you think about it, why wouldn't I? What is there to gain in a conflict at this point?

    If that's the kind of thing that happens, I suggest you ask them something about their goal :

    -Hey Jason, what are you going to do to further your quest to find the Jewel of Ochra?
    -Hum... I dunno... I mean I don't even know where it is!
    -Well, Rival NPC is also looking, maybe *he* knows...
    -But he hates my gut! He won't tell me...
    -Not with that attitude he won't...

    or...

    -So, where do you want your starting scene this session, Jason.
    -Well, I'm at the temple, I guess, praying the gods to guide me on my quest for the Jewel of Ochra!
    -Okay, nice! You're just praying? You don't want to use any abilities or secrets? Otherwise, it's just a color scene, which is fine, you know, but we only got a few hours, we might as well start rolling...
    -Okay, but what can I use at the temple?
    -Well, you know, maybe is the high priest a famous scholar. He might know the whereabouts of the jewel. You could go ask him?
    -Sure!
    -Except Nefarious NPC rival is already in the antichamber with the high priest, he's got a knife, and looks menacing.
    -I hide!
    -Okay, if you want, but it's getting violent.
    -Can I hear what he's saying?
    -Sure! "Now that you've told me all I wanted to know, I should make sure you won't tell anyone else, dontcha think?

    Of course, it's more fun when players are more proactive. And when they don't welcome your contribution to the story.

    -I'm in a store.
    -Bad guy comes in.
    -I hide!
    -Okay, bad guy leaves. He didn't see you.
    -Good. Back to shopping!
    -Indeed. Thank god we've avoided a potentially interesting scene with your nemesis!

    But it's understandable that they would be prudent, especially if they are used to more deadly games. Once they figure out character death can be avoided easily in this game, they might be more willing to take risks. I suggest, above all, you have a talk with them.
