"Half-myth" (why Burning Wheel is a no myth game)

2097
June 3 in Story Games

So a “no myth” game was originally defined as a game where the game state wasn’t fixed until it entered the shared imagined space.

That means that “no myth” is a property of the ontology of the game. Something exists if it’s put into play and doesn’t exist if it hasn’t. (“Put into play” can also include things like “OK, before we start, I want to say that this game is about Darth Vader in a shopping mall speaking french so those three things definitely exist”.)

The GM in a no-myth game can think “I really want to put this blue chair into play, I’m gonna do it to the first normally furnished room the players enter, I feel such a strong attachment to this particular quantum blue chair. It’s gonna get in for sure. Oh blue chair my favorite” The fact that the GM is really into this blue chair does not make the blue chair half myth or the ontological state of the game “half myth”.

This means, logically, that the phrase “half myth” can’t meaningfully be defined as “some specific offscreen entities are canon, some are not”. Because that’s already what “no myth” means.

Instead, for a meaningful definition of “half myth”, how about: “some categories of offscreen entities are canon, some are not.”

“Hi guys, I’ll run everything in Duckburg fully prepped, but things over in Gooseburg is more ‘no myth’.”

“Hi guys, everything related to combat is improvised and ran ‘no myth’, I’ll just throw orcs atcha according to my own whim, but I have prepped all the entities and rules regarding the big fashion show in Veluna because I know that that’s the aspect of play that we’re the most invested in. Clothes, designers, audience reactions—all accounted for & implemented. Only with a steady whimsical stream of orc with murderous intent.”

What does this mean for Burning Wheel? It’s more of a no-myth game innit? There is no category of entities in the game that can be consistently expected to be solid.

    I have been trying to avoid using the word since it is (intentionally?) very unclear. @Silmenume suggested 'Klockwerk' as an alternative, which I like. Here's my sense of what we're talking about (which @2097 is calling blorb):
    -Games where as much as possible (location details, adversity, stakes, etc) is prepped in detail ahead of time.
    -What can't be prepped (or wasn't) is decided by an totally transparent fortune system (random tables etc).
    -System that alter the game state dynamically (time, inventory, resources, hunger, etc) are tracked transparently and consistently.
    -Players have a single character, and their input into the game is limited to the plausible actions of that character, questions about the perceptions of that character, and manipulation of the games systems (buying/selling, following consistent combat resolution rules.

    The above add up (to me) to a feeling like the game is running largely on its own logic, to which GM and player alike are bound. That's why I like the term Klockwerk: this is a style where the cogs move on their own (as much as possible) and the players adapt.

      Ah, well, that's pretty simple, then. Games like AW are specifically for people who want to play out a story about sexy guys in jeans in the post apocalypse, while expressing themselves aesthetically, and do it from a first-person character perspective.

      Consider, for instance, that as a Hardholder, you're going to be facing "hunger" (or whatever other problem) in your hardhold because the dice said so, on a regular basis. If you go and solve the problem that's causing the hunger, a new one will just pop up the next time you roll badly (but note that you are the one choosing "hunger" when that happens, unless it's a full miss).

      There's no "blorb" there. Rather, the game says that, hey, this is the kind of problem that comes up regularly in your hardhold. And the player says, "Yeah, that's what I want to spend my time playing out!"

      In other words, Vincent decided that the player's desire to "play a Hardholder dealing with hunger problems in her hardhold" is the thing we want, and we can get it far more easily this way than by going through all the hassle of thorough prep, gloracular outcomes, and so forth (and that could still fail to happen, even despite all that work). Instead, we have a game structure where you get your wish - of playing the hardholder dealing with hunger problems among her population - in an easier and more reliable way.

      If you require your gaming to always have "full blorb", then you're definitionally excluding most of these games: most/all PbtA games, Burning Wheel, The Shadow of Yesterday, Sorcerer, My Life with Master, etc, etc, etc - basically anything that's not director stance story gaming or OSR-style "explore the dungeon" play.

      I think the key is that people want to participate in a cool story, but their Technical Agenda is to do so purely through the lens of their character. (Kind of like how you want to win, yes, but you want to win as [Character X], with all that implies.)

      Lots of tools in the game keep this from turning into that kind of "90s play" you're complaining about, starting with MC principles and agenda and so forth (they're explicit and overt, so players can and will and should call out an MC who doesn't follow them), to rules that create these dynamics (like the rule which tells you when the people are hungry, the instructions to make everything a threat, the rules for spending barter or going hungry yourself, and so on), to rules which create consistent and reliable resolutions when your character applies their pressure to these situations. All of that creates reliably emergent, unpredictable fiction which satisfies all of that criteria, at least when you play it right (I don't think AW is as bulletproof as some people think, but the play culture has really caught on and become a thing that works great for people).

      In other words, in a 90s style game, you're at the mercy of the GM. In these games, your choices (of Beliefs, of playbooks, of moves, etc) "set the table" for the kinds of conflicts and situations you want to play out and be involved in, instead. The GM can't force them on you (not without breaking Principles, anyway). And then the way the Agenda, Principles, and moves work together gives us emergent, unpredictable outcomes which are exciting for everyone involved. If you want to go shoot some guy or sleep with some guy, the rules guarantee you the possibility of success, and the MC's prep sets interesting stakes for when you fail. It's not at all like klokwerk but it has its own gloracle-like process, which creates unexpected and new material at the same (or higher) level of unpredictability. We've all committed to these processes and following them faithfully, just like how the klokwerk GM and players commit to certain principles, and that's what makes the game great (and completely different from a 90s style game).

      But the ability of all the participants to make decisions along the way is basically what makes it fun, artistically. You get to say, "I want to play a hardholder, and my holding has problems with raiders and a water shortage", because that excites you. You don't, instead, roll a bunch of random situations and see if you like them after the fact.

      Does that get you any closer to understanding half-Myth play?
