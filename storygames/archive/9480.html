\n I few weeks ago I was reading about a new ebook reader that will be coming out later this year. The Plastic Logic read shows 8.5x11 pdfs graphics and all. Cool I thought - an Ereader I might actually buy. In fact it sounds like a reader that could push printed trade paperbacks out of the market. Even though I do printing and bookbinding I was not saddened by this. Instead I see it as an opening for game books and boardgames to become art objects.
\n
\nPDF sales should be greatly advantaged by a good reader so that business model will continue and improve. I attended a panel at Marcon (a sci fi con in Columbus Ohio) and found people also not sad to see books go. I thought there would be more emotion against it from the "books are my friends" crowd but there wasn't. So I think the audience is ready for this. Which begs the question of what happens to paper books.
\n
\nBecause I never got another company to make my games I set up a workshop in my garage to do printing and book binding. The skills heavily overlap with board game making so now I make boardgames. I can make 100 games a month which way exceeds what I can sell right now. I don't think other people need to buy machines like I have. A simple art studio could do the trick. The point is after sheets come off the computer (which remains the printing press in my eyes) how they process and package it is where the art comes in.
\n
\nThe hard part of any business model is how to sell after an item is made. Art can be sold on line just as easily as a pdf - the only difference is you have to make something and mail it. If you like getting your hands dirty this should not be a problem. If you're like me and can make 100 games a month then you have enough to sell to stores if you want. It will mean calling them and building up relationships but this is what artists do with galleries so it really is the art business.
\n
\nI believe that the art talent in this community could really make this approach work and would increase the prestige of game making because of the skill and workmanship put into it. Think of the beautiful handbound books that could be made with rich bindings!
\n
\nI'm planning on trying out this model and will let you all know how it turns out. I'd love to hear your comments and questions now.

    \n Hi Chris, I saw your games at Gen Con last year and loved them.

    I have a datapoint that may be of interest. Last year my wife began to sew custom hand-made dolls inspired by the curriculum at my daughter's school. This is a waldorf school, where there's a strong emphasis on hand-made items. Recently, she opened a shop on Etsy.com. Etsy is a marketplace specificlly for hand-made objects: nothing mass-produced. It's gotten strong adoption from a number of disparate communities, including the Waldorf community and the Maker community. She's basically been selling dolls as rapidly as she can make them.

    Maybe what home-made games need is its own online channel. This might be Etsy or some other service. Browsing around Etsy I find people's hand-painted Warhammer 40k miniatures, felt "Mario Brothers" dioramas, goth ktisch, as well as lot of more traditional hand crafts. There's a huge potential there. I've been kicking around the idea of selling hand-crafted maps and models there myself.

    \n I'm with you David. I think Etsy is 100% the right place for hand-crafted board games. What it needs is a bit of a grassroots effort to get people to realize that 1) there is such a thing as a hand-crafted board game (and why they are cool) and 2) an understanding that Etsy is where you find them. To this end, you'd probably want to get some Etsy sellers together and form an Etsy group. Then figure out a common tag to share so people know how to find crafted games (and game paraphenalia) in general. Etsy has a bunch of useful tools to help you build a store, a community, and a following. I'm a fan.\n

    \nI understand these concerns, but I think they're mostly non-concerns. At least I hope they are. The "maker/craftsman" community has a very similar ethic to the story game community. They're about making cool stuff and sharing it with other people. There are always people who tray to charge a fortune for a piece of crap, but it's the exception. If you look at Chris' stuff, you'll see he doesn't fall into any of the categories you're worried about.

    To me, handcrafted game stuff boils down to this: if you buy a fantasy flight game, you're paying for a certain amount of tone-setting fancy bling coolness to enhance your gaming experience. If you go buy one of Chris' games, you're also getting something really beautiful to enhance your gaming experience, except it's not factory-produced. It has it's own brand of cool-ness to offer. Thats my personal take on the whole handcrafted thing.
