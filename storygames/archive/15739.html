    \n Hey Ethan, thanks for asking!

    Monsterhearts engages the topic of queer desire and identity on three levels - in the game's allegories, in the game's mechanics, in the game's text.

    The Queer Allegory
    In Monsterhearts, you play someone who is different. You're hiding that difference, and you feel like it makes you a monster in the eyes of the rest of the world. If the wrong people were to learn your secret, it could lead to a lot of pain and danger for you. That secret is inextricably tied up in your body and your sexuality. Being a flesh-eating creature of the night is equal parts literal premise and allegorical premise. It's an exploration of what it means to be non-normative.

    The Queer Mechanics
    In Monsterhearts (like in the real world!), you don't get to control what you find sexy. You get to control how you react to that information. The turn someone on move (as well as other mechanical bits throughout) are structured to make sexuality a messy and unpredictable force in the game, very specifically allowing the possibility of queer desire. Not every session played has queer characters or queer arousal, but importantly the mechanics create that space and highlight its potential.\n
    When you turn someone on, roll with hot. On a 10 up, take a String against them. \u2022 On a 7-9, they choose one: give themselves to you, promise something they think you want, give you a String against them.

    When you use this move, you have the opportunity to step outside of your character role, and speak like an author would. Describe your character\u2019s pouty lips, how the sweat rolls off their strong brow, and how they look silhouetted against the pale moon. Unlike other basic moves, this one can be triggered without a specific action being taken. If the player describes how arousing the scene is, without their PC actually doing anything other than standing around and looking sexy, the move can still be triggered.

    This move implies something about sexuality, and particularly teenage sexuality. We don\u2019t get to decide what turns us on. When you make a move to turn someone on (with a character action or with scene description), the other player doesn\u2019t get to exclaim, \u201cWait, my character is straight! There\u2019s no way that\u2019d turn them on.\u201d That\u2019s a decision that we as players can\u2019t make for our characters. The dice are going to be the ultimate referees of what is and isn\u2019t sexy for these characters. Their own sexuality will confuse them and surprise them; it\u2019ll show up in situations they didn\u2019t expect to find it in. Regardless of the results of the roll, however, each player still gets to decide how their character reacts. Being turned on by someone doesn\u2019t imply or demand a reaction.
    \nThe Queer Text
    I was vigilant in ensuring that queer examples and mentions of the kind of alienation that queer youth face is sown throughout the text in little ways. My goal here was simply to normalize the possibility of queer characters, and to remind people to step outside their expectations.

    There's also a section about why & how to go about introducing queer content into your game. I wrote it with a great deal of support and critique from a queer, trans, poly friend of mine.\n
    Queer Content

    This isn\u2019t really a game about monsters. It\u2019s a game about the confusion that arises when your body and your social world start changing without your permission.

    Your story will be more interesting and real if it includes queer content. It\u2019s another dimension of isolation and anxiety to explore through your characters. It\u2019s another way to keep the story feral, by breaking down our own expectations of heteronormativity. It\u2019s another way to introduce messy, unrequited love interests into the picture.

    Remember that the turn someone on move can create statements about a character\u2019s sexuality. If Lukas rolls to turn on Jackson, and succeeds, then we know that Jackson sometimes gets turned on by other guys, at least this once. It doesn\u2019t replace what was previously true about Jackson, necessarily, but it does add a new dimension to the character. Maybe Jackson\u2019s still straight, maybe his relationship with his girlfriend is still as stable as it\u2019s ever been, but we also know about these other feelings he's experienced. And given that Monsterhearts is a game about messy, sexy melodrama, chances are it\u2019ll push Jackson into new and uncomfortable territory.

    When I suggest that you introduce queer content, here are some examples of what I mean:
    \u2022 Be willing to be surprised about who your character turns out to be and what turns them on.
    \u2022 If you\u2019re a vampire, ask yourself: does feeding on boys feel the same as feeding on girls? Do your feeding preferences line up with your sexual preferences? In what ways is vampiric feeding a sexual thing?
    \u2022 If you\u2019re a mortal, take a couple as your true love.
    \u2022 Explore what it means to be betrayed by your body, whether it\u2019s becoming a flesh-eating monster that stalks the night, or being trans and experiencing the wrong puberty, or both.
    \u2022 If you\u2019re the MC, introduce queer characters, and make them fundamentally different from one another. Let their sexuality and gender be incidental in some cases, and let it be the chaotic thing that drives the story forward in others.

    Use the game to explore and challenge your own ideas about gender, sexuality, relationships, what\u2019s normal and what\u2019s monstrous.

    \nJames,

    Yes! A number of people have questioned it or expressed concern with it. My default answer is, "Well, try playing the game as written, and see how it turns out for you." I feel like the entire Monsterhearts package is really alienating to some folks, and those people have probably already decided that the game isn't for them by the time they encounter this move.

    The move's explanatory text was worded differently at one point, and it carried connotations that I hadn't meant for it to carry... and on that subject, a playtester unpacked at great length how it made him uncomfortable and was problematic. But that's part of what playtesting is about, I think. Words and ideas mean different things to each of us, and playtesting helps us learn about our own assumptions. It happens to be that I'm playing with volatile words and ideas in the design and writing of this game. The playtester's concerns were about a few careless sentences that made a sweeping and unintentional statement about "how much control you get over your character's reaction." So I changed them, and now that problem isn't alive.

    The important thing about the turn someone on move is that it doesn't dictate or demand a response. Rick turns Mitt on, with his beautiful green eyes and his half-smirk. Rick gets a String on Mitt. It's now a totally valid response for Mitt's player to say, "Yeah, so maybe I'm turned on, but I don't show it. I go back to working on my science project." That's not bad roleplaying or blocking; it's a totally normal way to process weird information - by just ignoring it. Rick still gets what he was after, that String over Mitt.

    \n Thanks for all the lovely discussion, people.

    I'd just like to call out two things:

    (1) This is a thread where I was asked to highlight how the game handles queer content, sexuality, desire, and the like. And so I really went under-the-hood with my description, and brought a lot of my own personal politics along with me. But in play, this stuff is often as simple/unexamined as, "So, my sexy vampire totally gives you a sultry look as you brush shoulders in the hall. I'm rolling to turn you on. 10. Sweet, I totally get a String on you." MC: "So, Vicky, Raleigh bumps shoulders and gives you this look, right? What do you do next?" I don't want to downplay the fact that it's a game about brooding hotties, because that's a huge part of the game... but I want to stress that it's not as... charged at the table as it might appear in this thread.

    (2) This is a game I've played at several cons with strangers. It's not for everyone, definitely, but I've felt totally comfortable playing this with strangers, and strangers have felt totally comfortable playing this with me. The moves and mechanics and "vying for most Strings held" all work together to ease people into the idea of playing characters whose sexuality matters. And just like in any other game, player discomfort can be addressed at a social contract level: lines, veils, excusing oneself from the session to go play Munchkin instead, etc. But most people, I find, quickly slide into a comfort zone that they didn't expect.
