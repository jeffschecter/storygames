\n I like to think that role-playing games produce some astounding fiction. If I were to choose, I would go for role-playing over literature nine times out of ten.

However: one thing I've learned to appreciate in some novels, is the epic spanning several generations, revealing how consequences may "pile up" over the generations, to make a family or township or government go in the wrong direction.

- How do you plan for such an epic in a role-playing game?
- Are there games out there aimed at exactly this kind of drama?
- Is it possible to make a game that places the characters/players in the middle of it, to benefit of the "inside view" of how things go wrong?

- Or is this a field best covered by the novel?

    Posted By: TomasHVMWhat kind of process makes such shifts in a nation (or group) possible...
    \n1- The day to day conditions of ordinary people
    2- The outcomes of battles, skirmishes, assassination attempts, street violence, riots, et cetera
    3- Procedural tricks and chicanery in any existing political bodies
    4- The intimidation, disappearance, murder, or, conversely, arrival/actualization of important new leaders in political and social movements

    Basically, the interaction of:
    - everyday people in large numbers
    and
    - systems and the leaders in/of such systems
    interacting with
    - death, promotion, good and bad luck, timing (personal and more mass-scale), and other factors

    It's not magic. It's just got a huge host of unknowns, both in-the-moment unknowns (ignorance/misinformation changes things: ex. Hannibal's army stays in the Alps for a decade instead of attacking Rome. The city is saved!) and historical unknowns (the reason for an event itself is unknown, or cannot be known: ex. the reason for Kennedy's assassination).
    To be more precise, there are incidents in which the ignorance OF some actor in the event is the source of the twist. There are also hidden motives - the actor in the event is well aware of why they do a thing, but that reason is lost to history.
    Those, to me, are two of the biggest sources of anomalies in the fates of nations: acting on incomplete information, and incomplete historical records.

    As far as games and design related to this, it might be cool to flag particular characters with tentative Fates or Destinies, and then watch the players charge forward to support or tear down such characters. We're the audience - we see everything - so why not act with full knowledge and soak up the dramatic irony?
