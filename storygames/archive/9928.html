\nIn Defiance you and your friends will create stories about insurrectionists fighting against the corrupt authorities ruling their city. You'll take on the role of saboteurs, firebrands, renegades, infiltrators and heretics attempting to show the oppressed population that they can fight back, that they can rise up and claim their freedom. But your characters motives for taking on this struggle threatens them from within. Will they give in to their resentment and anger or will they use them to fuel their heroic indignation? With its peerless allotment based system, Defiance may change the way you think about narrative gaming forever.

    \n Why has this not recieved any love? This looks really cool, Marcus! The text is very clear and the game looks interesting.

    One question: How are influence points used? I'm a bit skeptical of "Earn a point to get yourself in trouble" mechanics, as they often just lead to "Spend a point to get yourself out of it", making it a tiny feedback loop of Czege principle violations.

    I really love the virtues, and the open ended questions followed by "impressions" are pure genius. It'd be interesting to see how the fate points work out in play. Are there any AP reports of this game to look at?

    \n Influence points are used to draw more beads during challenges. Players typically draw three beads to determine the result of a challenge. Players can use influence to draw up to five beads and then choose the three beads they want to use. While play testing I noticed that players easily separated their own interests from their characters interests, and I've witnessed on more than one occasion players earning influence by getting their characters into trouble and then spending influence to either keep them in trouble or make matters worse.
    \n
    \nI see virtues as a way of seeing why a character does something rather than how they do it. I'm also interested in the idea of people who are seething with anger, bitterness and resentment being selfless and heroic precisely because they were so angry, bitter and resentful. They kind of have to be insurrectionists because of who they are as people.
    \n
    \nFate worked better than I hoped. Players loved being in control of how and when their insurrectionists got removed from the story. And knowing that it was an inevitable part of the story created a sense of tragedy that helped the players get something more immediate from the relationship they had with their insurrectionists. I think players often have the feeling that death probably won't happen to their characters, that they'll roll well enough, someone else will save them, or the GM won't allow it. But when character death (or elimination) becomes an inevitable outcome rather than a possible outcome, players tend to invest more time into really getting to know their characters much sooner.
    \n

    \n I've been thinking about compels and I wonder if something could be done where a player narrator is given influence points if they are compelled to roleplay their insurrectionist in a way suggested by another player narrator. The compel would have to fit in with their insurrectionists attitude statements. Also the player narrator offering the compel would gain influence points if the compel fits in with their insurrectionists impression of the other player narrator's insurrectionist.
