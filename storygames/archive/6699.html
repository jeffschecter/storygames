\n So here's my thought. Today, we have numerous groups of hackers distributed about the country, all acquiring software, stripping the protection, and releasing it for free distribution in illegal channels.

In Shadowrun, not only are you mysteriously unable to hax0r nuyen and credsticks, it seems that there are no sources anywhere that offer software for free download. This is an on-again off-again conversation I've had with an SR GM. My position is that in a world SATURATED with hackers all software should be effectively free at chargen, possibly excepting the razor-sharp highest dice pool stuff. His position is "that may be so, but you still have to pay".

Your thoughts!

    Posted By: VeavIn Shadowrun, not only are you mysteriously unable to hax0r nuyen and credsticks
    \nThere's a great short story in Greg Egan's Luminous (or was it Distress?) where an agent basically negotiates with this other dude for money: They exchange like 3 types of electronic currency: Tracable govt-issued exchange (like dollars), generic but backed currency that's untracable (and thus more valuable for shady deals), but imminently more stealable (sounds like its pocketed on a USB key-like device), etc.

    I like the idea of multiple levels of electronic currency, each with ties to different markets, backing, and trace ratings. Getting a job for a fat pile of tracable cash might be easy, but if you then want to buy guns with it, that's impossible; instead you may have to cut a deal for a (much?) smaller amount of untracable cash in order to later use for illegal gear.\n
    Posted By: VeavMy position is that in a world SATURATED with hackers all software should be effectively free at chargen, possibly excepting the razor-sharp highest dice pool stuff.
    \nI agree with the GM. The fun thing will be thinking about what that money/resource pool for buying software represents. It might become an abstraction like a quotient of Time and Money.

    Like, "Black Ice 4" might be a program which you effectively wrote yourself from scratch, because you're a fucking genius. The amount you "pay" is the amount that you "would have gotten paid by a legitimate company if you just took that nice job that your mom wanted you to take". Or in a world with hackers and equally effective AI anti-hacking software, it might represent the time sink that it takes to download a free version of some Attack Software, and then go in and re-rig it using again your genius brain in a way so it doesn't resemble the original version anymore; because as soon as the original version hit the streets, ProtecSource released an update to AI firewall/ice routers to block its ports, unravel its code, ignore it, etc on high exec systems (but you might be able to still use it out of the box to hack a mom and pop shop or a university). So you have the benefit of the 2 million skillful lines of hacker code that tunnels around ice in new ways, but you have to spend a lot of time (represented abstractly by Program Cost) recoding it so that it doesn't get auto-rejected by any device you connect with. And even then, you probably have to retool it every few weeks to avoid tracking.\n

    \n From the 4th ed Rules:\n
    Source Code and Piracy

    Most legally-purchased programs are sold with built-in copy protection, so that the program cannot easily be duplicated without an access code. Hackers have invented many ways of circumventing these methods, however, to the point where piracy is the rule rather than the exception.

    If a character wishes to duplicate a program that is copy-protected, he needs to break the protection. This requires an Extended Software + Logic Test with a threshold between 10 and 20 (gamemaster\u2019s discretion) and an interval of 1 hour.
    \nSo they at least acknowledged it.

    But, really, software in cyberpunk is about getting the big score. The protagonists never write the killer apps themselves, they always lifted them from some shady megacorp or get given them by the employer as payment.

    \n Software hacks today work by leveraging flaws in computer systems. It might be the same in the future of the 80s. Today, some hackers sell their non-published exploits to the highest bidder instead of improving the world by sharing their knowledge. The cost for highly effective ICE or ICE breaking software might just be the cost of getting that information from people. Of course, knowledge like this becomes worthless eventually, when the flaws are fixed; I don't know if this kind of attrition is built into recent netrunning/matrix rules.
