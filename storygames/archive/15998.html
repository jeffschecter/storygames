    \n Are you thinking more of the level of editing or the underlying style here? If it's the former, then I have to say that I don't care for it. I've appreciated heteropractical literary style in the past (kill puppies for satan comes to mind), but the typos and whatnot here make the text more difficult to read than it needs to be. If it's intentional, it should be toned down and made more uniform, so it's a stylistic flavour on the text and not a dominant thing. Clear chaptering and a logical order of text flow are paramount to clarity.

    As for the underlying style, it's not a problem - that sort of thing can be good if done well. I'd say that the strength of conversational style is that it's more entertaining to read, and it encourages the reader to discard excess legalism and try to understand the procedures described. It works well for readers who are already experienced roleplayers, and for games that are not intended as reference sources, but as something you read and learn in advance of play.

    Vincent Baker is a strikingly compelling game text author who writes in this mode most of the time. Dogs in the Vineyard, for example, owes much of its popularity to the clarity of the text, which is mostly gained by informal, effortless style and frank discussion of the procedures of play. His game In a Wicked Age is a good example of the pitfalls of the style: it's notoriously confusing in the way it jumps between rules text and play description that actually conceals a part of the procedures within.

    Hmm, I managed to cite three of Vincent's games there. Another work that comes to mind concerning informal game text writing is Vornheim, it has that same "I am a specific human being writing to you, not a paid scribe with a word quota to fill" going on. Works really well for me as a reader, I enjoy reading that sort of thing.

    All in all, I guess that I'm pretty much in favour of this. My own style is usually rather informal as well. If I had to pinpoint a danger to it aside from the hazard of confusing organization, I'd have to say that you can't be a poseur with this style. It's a death to the feel of the work if the reader starts thinking that you're being forced about your style. You see this sometimes with game texts that have a real hard-on about say punk culture or other such colorful things. The author lavishes his text with the subculture, so that it's not informal, but merely stilted in a weird direction. The sample text we have here suffers from this slightly, a good editor would tone it down so it doesn't seem that the author is adding excess adjectives just for the sake of emphasizing how informal he's being.

    \n Hey, neat!

    At first, I was really put off by the quality of the grammar, typos, etc. However, much to my surprise, I could pretty much understand everything. Poor grammar and editing is only really a problem in this context if it makes it hard to understand what's written, and I didn't have any trouble with that. I do like the "introduction": it's evocative and very quick to get to the point. I could see using that first paragraph or two as a very dense sourcebook of various play material. Kind of like an "oracle" (in the sense used in In a Wicked Age...).

    For example, I can pick a random snippet: "the school mates not returning to class and noone notices". There's a great idea for a "scenario", or at least how it begins, and there's lots of really great ones in that paragraph (or whatever you want to call it: the first block of text). So I like the introduction. Lots of good stuff there!

    What I really *don't* like is the editing/formatting. It's really, really hard to look through the text and see where the information is. Some headings, or at least consistency in spacing and where ALL CAPS is used, would really help me read this without getting a headache.

    The actual presentation of the game is good in principle: it reminds me very much of Apocalypse World. You've got a brief evocative introduction (with very little actual information, so you fill it in yourself), and then the rest is basically character creation material. Some of the "moves" and powers and what-not are very cool, some not so interesting to me, but overall it seems like it would be easy to find something you can get excited about (like the "freezy beams!").

    The rules are very hard to understand (for example, I have absolutely no idea how damage works after having read this text, but I *do* get the impression that damage is really, really important in this game, because it's mentioned all the time). However, since this seems to be a "hack" of Feng Shui, rather than its own game, I have to assume that maybe all that would make sense if I was familiar with the game.

    My main reaction to the text other than that is that a lot of the cool-sounding stuff I read in the introduction (like the mermaid returning your call and the black castle hanging above Tokyo, and stealing boyfriends and all that good stuff) doesn't seem represented in the rules. Most of the text seems to be talking about guns and fire blasts and super armor and how much damage they do (with a very few exceptions). So, I would know how to play this game with lots of fighting and killing and power blasts and detachable heads, but what I really found interesting about the game was the "and everyone goes to your concert and the teacher doesn't flunk you" bits, because those seem like they would create a really neat kind of contrast to all the blood and bullets and weird plasma magic and freezy beams.

    So I'd be worried about it just being a fighting game without much focus. But if the game is more about the contrast between the weird fighty stuff and the slightly creepy real-world stuff, then I'm quite interested!

    Overall, I see this text as very similar to Apocalypse World in style (as with AW, I find myself wanting to write in that same style after reading through it), except that it:

    a) Clearly lacks polish (I could be convinced that the bad grammar and spelling is an intentional effect--nothing wrong with that, necessarily--but other things about the text make me suspect that this is just a first draft and not a carefully put-together piece of work, such as the organization of the contents), and
    b) doesn't actually tell you how to play the game. (The most unfortunate part, for me.)

    There's some great imagery in there, though, maybe enough to convince me to give playing in a genre-that's-more-or-less-the-opposite-of-what-I-usually-like a shot!

    Example 1. they're at work and brain eating lizards start invading earth. However, they were just flirting with their cute coworker, and a lizard invasion can really put crimp in that! So the player spends a fate point to de-escalate and declares that the question isn't if they can prevent the invasion or not, but if they can do it without freaking the coworker out.

    Example 2. they're in their favorite cafe, and oops, out of cash when it comes time to settle the bill. They also know ghosts of unborn babies have been restless recently, and are wondering if they should be doing something about it. The player spends a fate point to escalate and says that with all those babyghosts around they're liable to possess someone in the cafe -- rendering the stupid bill moot.

    If I was doing this, I'd make those points precious as hell, though. Maybe give each player two to start with, and be pretty pinchy about giving more of them.

    (I'm sure others here will have suggestions involving more fast-flowing point economies, and I've nothing against them, but whenever I've done something like this I've found that scarce points are easier to give real oomph to, and interfere less with the simmy engine.)

    \nI like this idea a lot. Use some changes in the way the rules work to reinforce the difference between the everyday/serious and the mayhem and insanity. Maybe when you're fighting monsters, you get to use your highly competent skills, but when you want to ask that boy out for the school dance, suddenly your abilities are useless, your rolls are only one-half as effective, etc.

    Maybe in "mayhem" moments, unusual results (like doubles on the dice) bring about bizarre complications, or cause people to trip out and hallucinate?

    Or maybe all this varies character-by-character. You can choose to have a character who is competent in the "real world", tends to get their way, can hold down a good job, whatever, but is completely useless when it comes to dealing with the baby-eating ghouls, because it freaks her out... or you can have the monster-hunter character who has amazing magical abilities and is terrifying when she fights bizarre creatures, but everything in "real life" tends to fall apart, fall completely out of control.

    \n The closest thing to this I've run was "Escape from Arcadia": teenage fairies who'd screwed up and been thrown to Titania's dungeon escaped, and ended up on Earth, on Al Amarja (a gonzo island from the game Over The Edge.) It wasn't *anything* like what you're outlining, really, but had some broad similarities in way of weird shit --> gonzo action --> mundane stuff --> gonzo action --> relationships --> gonzo action...

    So, notes based on that. Not sure how applicable they are:

    - I ran a SoTC-style character creation, where everyone specified a previous adventure (in Arcadia!) they had been involved in, and then their role in two other characters prior adventures. This worked great to establish both the common ground for characters, and a common understanding of what sort of things their characters would consider normal.

    - We had a ton of fun with characters relationship to pop culture. Our Arcadia got plenty of magazines and books from Earth, and the occasional live performer, but no technology could cross the barrier. So characters loved Buffy and Angel *books* but sort of thought thy were actual people. While much of the fun was in their confusion, I'm pretty sure just the character <--> pop culture connection was golden in itself. I'm very much going to try to do something similar in another game.

    - Because I wanted to keep the weirdness and mundanity sort of connected, every single NPC in the game had a "weird" hook. It might not come up, but it allowed me to introduce weirdness through them, and it allowed me insert them at least peripherally into the gonzo action much more easily. Stuff like "Next door neighbor is a retired mecha pilot from parallel dimension."

    - Characters had really wild fairy magics that I let them do almost anything with, but they were heavily constrained by the amount of glamour they had. Glamour was gained by acting like a fairy of their type should. Players could award glamour to each other, but they often forgot, so in the end I kept handing it out.

    - I totally gimped the characters sometimes, except I didn't. "You're captured in a Burmese Fairy Trap! You can't get out!" The trap was a wooden mask, but as soon as playing just a mask that talks got boring (muffled noise coming from a briefcase...) I let him transform into a small wooden animal: the character was a shapeshifter.

    - Lots and lots of coincidences and small mysteries they could engage with, or shrug at and ignore. Who broke into their apartment and stole their Angel DVDs, but left Buffy, the TV, and DVD player, etc? Then when they're engaged with X, let Y intrude by force.

    - Having an idea of all the background stuff, but very much listening to characters speculate -- and changing stuff to match their interpretation at least some of the time. Otherwise the stuff that's wild enough is just too strange to figure out unless you're a mind reader...
