    Review of Tragedy, by Eva Schiffer

    In Tragedy, we have a good beta version of a game ripe for playtest and further development. The basic concept is to reboot and roleplay a Shakespearean tragedy, with each player working towards one or more tragic ends. It relies heavily on freeform roleplaying techniques, and weighs in on the "guided story collaboration" end of the roleplaying spectrum.

    Playability of the game - 7/10
    I have no doubt that this game could be played as-is and produce an enjoyable evening perhaps 70% of the time. A good solid playtest or three could iron out the kinks in currency and responsibilities, but more importantly the game lacks a strong sense of what the players should be doing at any given point in time. I suspect that the strong core of the game exists in Eva's head; the game text just needs some focusing the bring it more into line with her plans and expectations.
    The development of characters as they proceed towards their inevitably tragic ends is clever and intriguing, but the actual procedures to guide the players there is obscure at best. It's all there, but could be explained in better detail and format. The flowcharts are not necessary and were something of a turn off for me. The rules are simple enough that they can be described in the main text just fine.

    Usefulness of the character sheet - 9/10
    Eva got one of the most "interesting" character sheets in the challenge, Shreyas' creation which cut the sheet into pieces and then used arrows to connect different elements on three lists. The interactive elements of the sheet were put to brilliant use, and the game's progression engages the sheet directly. Equating each list with a different Act was clever; turning the pathways created between items into character traits was an inspired leap of design.
    My only hesitation is in player control over the pathways created. The control is not absolute, which isn't necessarily a bad thing, but what control a player has and when is not adequately explained. It also appears that the more prominent your character becomes and the more you play towards your "shift theme" the more likely it is that you will be forced to shift your list up or down. Playing well seems to result in a loss of control. Either I have not understood the intended effect of the rules or the benefits of this feedback cycle is not clearly explained; either way the rules text could be clearer on how the sheet is used.

    Wow, thatâ€™s cool factor. - 6/10
    Rewriting Shakespearean plays in real time at the table? Awesome. Unfortunately, while this is patently the game's genesis, the game has strayed from that original kernel. This design would have been a lot stronger if it had stayed with its inspiration rather than trying to make the game universal. This late-stage conceptual retooling is most evident in the Writing Playbills section; the suggestions to use eighties high school movies or science fiction operas rings false, more appropriate to a sidebar option than a central procedure in the main text. Writing Playbills would be considerably simplified if the procedure was simply to take the Dramatis Personae from any of the eleven Shakespearean tragedies and go from there.
    Lastly, while the characters' ends are almost certainly tragic, the game as it stands misses the most important aspect of the tragedy, the acheivement of catharsis. Tragic characters fall, yes, but in doing so they make their world a better place or lead the audience to understanding something about the human character. The tragic character is a sacrifice for the betterment of everyone else. While players intent on creating this can certainly attain that through the game, there is nothing that will guide other players to succeed in emulating the game's namesake.

    Total Score: 22/30
    I like this game, and I'd love to see it developed further. The central kernel is intriguing, and the use of the sheet is inspired. Tragedy does not have far to go to be an excellent game.

    Specimen for the Resurrection, by Paul Czege

    Playability 6/10
    In the most simple terms I can muster, Paul has created a game that is deeply affecting, demanding of your emotions, and entirely tragic. The game in no uncertain terms asks you, the player, to judge humanities worth in the face of extinction.

    See, the basis of this game is that humanity has long since gone the way of the dinosaurs and cosmic beings with the power to resurrect humanity has sent probes (artificial humans) back into time to live ordinary lives, to sin, to grieve, to forgive, to repent, to laugh, to be human. At the end of the session, these probes are brought forth to the cosmic beings and made to judge whether humanity should live again. I found myself quite emotional while reading this short game, and am convinced no one could do this game justice.

    While the rules seem solid, they are a bit confusing as it require the players to use a set of six-sided dice with a special numbering sequence not commonly available: Sicherman Dice. Additionally, while examples are given of how situations are resolved, no solid idea of what the players are suppose to be doing is presented. I got the idea that the intention is to present a series of vignettes, slices of life, but I could be wrong.

    With such a strong concept that demands the most of the players abilities, I simply felt the rules to back it up were a little short and awkward.

    Integration of the Character Sheet 9/10
    Paul had a handful with Selene Tan's character sheet. I'm happy to say that Paul made the most of it's rather intimidating design and every little detail of the character is used in the game, although what the game requires a person to write in a given space is often much longer than the space allows, but it is a small factor.

    What impresses me the most is not only how the rules use the sheet, but how Paul constructed the game in such a way that if I had not known about the Rev/Eng challenge, I would swear Paul created the sheet specifically for the game. Selene's sheet invokes a certain feel, an intensity, that is matched by the game.


    Wow, That's Cool 9/10
    Upon reading Specimen for the Resurrection, I literally thought to myself, "Wow...that's cool. I could play 12 Monkeys with this game." And so I could. Specimen for the Resurrection treads into those areas of gaming that are sensitive for most gamers: Introspection. It asks the players to make tough decisions about life, to examine the human condition, and then to sit in judgement. My friends....that is most certainly cool.

    Total: 24/30
    Specimen for the Resurrection is a challenging little game, that I'm very pleased to have reviewed. All I can say is, I would love to see the concept expanded, and detailed. Fine tune the rules, and perhaps find another dice mechanic, because as it stands, the funky dice situation probably would prevent many people from playing a game that quite franky, needs to be played.

    The Best Thing, by Mark Vallianatos


    The Best Thing takes the players to a fictional empire with some Chinese flavour. While the Emperor is sick and going to die without proper medical treatment, the players take the role of wise healers, mystics and/or charlatans who want to get famous by making the Emperor an Immortal.

    The game has a very strict mechanic structure. The play progresses in Seasons, and each season each player can decide whether his or her character travels to the Lands Beyond to gain more Knowledge or remain in the capital to get more contacts at court. The characters need both to get an appointment with the Emperor and cure him successfully.

    During play can choose whether they want to cooperate or hinder each other. I think this is meant to be what Vincent calls the Fruitful Void. If they hinder each other, they might get the fame for themselves, but if they do it too much, the Emperor might die. The problem I see here is that the helping/hindering mechanic is uses the same resource players need to improve their own characters. This reminds me of the Drama Dice in 7th Sea where players have to choose either doing cool stuff or getting a better character, too. In 7th Sea that is a very unfortunate connection, and it might turn out the same way in The Best Thing. (Of course it may come out different in actual play.)



    Playability 8/10
    As for the mechanics the game takes the term "funky dice tricks" to a whole new level. The central mechanic requires rolling a number equal to the Knowledge used and then assembling the dice in the shape represented on the character sheet. Then the player can activate and narrate the use of Assets in order to attach more dice to shape. Also other players can now spent the aforementioned Tokens to add or substract dice and narrate their help or sabotage.

    The the active player will take "Sweeps" over the assembled dice as indicated by the type of obstacle, Season and Region adding the dice he or she comes across in order to beat a difficulty number. That means there is quite a bit of tactics in choosing each season the right Knowledge, right obstacles and right Region. It would have been nice, though, to have rules for using additional Knowledges in a season like, say, Trollbabe and Galactic use additional abilities. This way there is only one Knowledge per season.

    Things I did not understand are exactly how to Sweep and the rules for the Emperors diseases are inconsistent. The text mentions that in the beginning there are only rumours about the maladies of his majesty, but later in the Treatment chapter they all seem to be real. Still the game should be playable.


    Integration of the Character Sheet 9/10
    Simply great. Better than I would have done it, and I made that sheet. I find it particularly interesting that Mark used more then half of the elements for pacing and structure, although I would have used them to describe the character. Mark's attempt is probably better.

    Only the Influence section does not really describe influence but contacts at court, but this is a slight cosmetic problem at most.


    Wow, That's Cool 7/10
    Fantasy with Eastern elements is cool all by itself, and the text offers some nice ideas for using them. Also the theme of cooperation vs. sabotage reminds me of the prisonerâ€™s dilemma, which is cool.


    Total: 24/30
    With some slight changes to the rules I see big potential for a nice little game.

    Angels of Tangut is, as admitted by Jack, not a complete game, but what is presented makes for a somewhat disjointed promise, a glimmer, of something very remarkable. Characters are angels (however you conceive them) that roam a Tibetan landscape created by the players collectively, solving conflicts and problems with the locals.     Children of the Sun, Children of the Moon
    Game: Russell Collins
    Sheet: Nathan Paoletta

    I like this game a lot. It's packed with good ideas: the idea of two characters combining to become a true Traveller is golden, the starlands sound fantastic and the mechanics around passing poker chips are superbly ingenious. It's a great use of the character sheet and I hope it goes on to be developed. That said, I'm worried about that central mechanic and how it'll impact playability.

    I'd like to see the rules revised, too, for a bit more clarity. For example, I think the three sorts of chips are different colours, but I can't find where it says that: did I miss that? Oh, and there's two Ls in Traveller, of course, but lots of Americans make that mistake.

    1. Playability 5

    It's a shame, really, that I'm marking this game on playability. If we were marking on potential, I'd give a much higher mark. I think these rules are ingenious and, with tweaking, will be a fantastic ruleset. Right now, though, I'm worried about the playability.

    Firstly, I'm worried about the description of the way the game proceeds: "Travellers move freely through the star lands, interacting with its peoples and observing the scenery until the Guide presents a foe". It makes the game sound like merely a series of foes: and, indeed, the example adventure does read like that.

    What I think Russell's aiming for is "Say yes or roll the dice", along the lines of DitV. But DitV presents a series of moral dilemmas that the players must cope with and that's a rather different thing to a series of foes. Also, note that DitV lets the players launch conflicts: in this game, it's always, I think, the GM who initiates a foe. Stop me if I've got that wrong.

    Secondly, I'm worried about the way the GM chooses the type of foe. For example, the GM chooses a Love obstacle, which the players must combat with Love. I can imagine the GM presenting a Love obstacle and the players wanting to overcome it with, say, Void: for example, in the conflict example in the text, I really want to hit that Vizier. But I couldn't, because I've got to overcome him with Love.

    And, because you can only beat him with Love, the conflicts are likely to consist mainly of narration about emotions and Love. It would be nice to have some variety: a Love gambit, say, followed by an attempt at Void to defeat the obstacle, then another try at Love. Note how, in the play example, the whole narration is an emotional argument, with points for and against. I'd much rather one player was trying to convince the Vizier while another was crawling through the rafters ready to fire a crossbow at him if he refused. With the current ruleset, I think that's unlikely to happen.

    And, thirdly, I'm unclear as to how narration interacts with the poker chips. It looks, from the play example, as though you only narrate when you play a chip of the same type as the obstacle (Love, in the example), and then you narrate something appropriate to that type (in the example, arguments). If that's so, it'll make for a rather one-tracked narration.

    All that sounds rather harsh, but I think the ruleset could be fantastic: the spinning of the coin to choose the Sphere is great; so is the passing of the chips; so's the ideas behind Love and Moon. But I think it needs tweaking.

    2. Integration of Character Sheet 9

    The character sheet is beautiful and it's well integrated. Russell has taken all the various elements and formed a story around them, and it's a great story. Even the barrier down the middle of the character sheet makes sense.

    It's not quite perfect: the term "Void" doesn't quite seem to correspond to "Physical Force" and Refreshes aren't really refreshes. But it's really good.

    3. Wow, That's Cool 7

    The mechanics are really, really cool. They'll make a great game (after, I think, some adjustments).

    The setting is cool too. The starlands are a beautiful idea for a setting and the backstory's quite good too. And much of the backstory - for example, the true Travellers being open to corruption - is actually reflected in the mechanics, in a very cool way.

    So, that score of 7, for me, means: I like this game and, if I saw it was being played at a convention, I'd definitely want to play. An 8 would have meant "I really like this game and I'd play it like a shot" and a 10 "I'm completely obsessed by this game and would kill to get a copy".

    So, although I really like it, it's not pushing all my buttons. I've said before that, when I read Dogs In The Vineyard, I feel as though I can smell the air and see the waterfalls in the mountains. I'm not getting that feeling with the star lands. And I'm not, quite, getting the feeling that I want to solve the corruption in the star lands: whereas, with DitV, I want to get stuck right in.

    But it's a nice game. I'd like to see the mechanics changed, a little, and then I'd love to play it.

    Chimaera
    Game: Fred Hicks (or possibly Rob Donoghue, it's hard to tell)
    Character Sheet: Adam Dray

    Yeah, forget that thing I said about marking low. This is great.

    1. Playability 9

    The game looks almost completely playable. The world / game creation system looks great. The conflicts look great.

    If I have one doubt, it's about the two types of scene and the allocation of characters. By defining a conflict scene as a "Conflict Scene", you instantly launch into, well, conflict. It might be nicer if the conflict grew out of a scene about something else.

    Also, the allocation of characters. In the play example for the conflict scene, the GM has automatically taken a general sort of Robots-And-Evil-Laboratory-opposition role. Could this be explained in more detail? Could the players (and GM) be guided into what sort of roles they should take? (For example: "The GM should usually take a general opposition role. If it's a team-fights-evil scene, the players should take their own characters and battle the GM, who should play a general "force of evil". If it's a one-character-against-the-world scene, one player should play his own character, while another player could play the evil villain, leaving the GM to play general mook opposition." Something like that.).

    But it's great and looks as though it could be played as it is.

    2. Use of character sheet 10

    It's an amazing character sheet and it's been used brilliantly. The use of the flaps, with different positions indicating different states, is inspired. The glass bead thing is very cool.

    If this game were developed, I think the glass-bead-sliding would be more interesting if some of the web links weren't present. That would mean, for example, that to get to "Body" you'd have to go via your "Mind" trait (say).

    3. Wow, That's Cool 8

    It really is a very cool game. I'm not usually into Godzilla-esque games, but this one makes it sound fun: and adds some emotional depth, too. I'd love to try the world creation system.

    This is great stuff: I do hope it gets developed.

    FateClock explores "ideas of leadership, history, conflict, and humanity" and threatens to "make history more interesting than it ever was." Please, no! (just kidding!)

    The players take on roles of powerful godlike entities (the "riders") who visit various periods in time, possess people there (the "ridden"), and have their way with them. The purpose of these characters is to experience their lives. The purpose of the players is to toy with history. The game encourages players to throw real history aside and use it only as a jumping point for playing a wild game of "what if?" Having just written a game where magical beings travel through time, possessing people there, in order to rewrite history, I have to say I'm enamoured of the concept.

    Overall Playability: 7
    The character generation rules look solid enough but the drawing and assigning of cards for Rider/Ridden should be explained in the earlier section, not in the conflict resolution section; that was very confusing.

    Conflict resolution is simple and elegant. You draw a card at random, add the total single-card value of your Rider or Ridden (or both, if they apply), and add your Fate Burden, which starts at -9. It's player-vs-player, so one of the other players does the same thing. The player with the highest result wins and narrates whatever he or she likes. The rules do not give much guidance on what is fair and unfair narration or how it has to tie in with the original conflict situation but it's assumed that the winner just narrates how he gets what he wanted in the first place, before another player opposed his action. A player can receive aid from another player; this lets the player draw an additional card but none of the aider's traits help.

    As you complete conflicts, your Fate Burden changes if your opponent deems it so. It slowly slides from -9 towards 0 and when you reach 0 you are overcome with evil and the world ends if two players signify that desire by crossing their arms. I totally do not understand why an opponent would ever "award" a Fate Burden shift (it's 1 or 2 points each time) since this makes one's opponents more powerful (less Fate Burden) and also brings the end of the game closer.

    Overall, I think the game is pretty playable though I have doubts about the reward system.

    Integration of the Sheet: 7
    My first test for this category is one of expectation. I read the game rules then look at the sheet. Is the sheet what I expected? In this case, it is not. Did Tomas create a cool game from Meg's sheet? You bet. Did he do a good job of reverse engineering the sheet? Not really. He does an okay job and he uses most or all of the sheet's aspects.

    Here's a deeper look at Meg's sheet.
    1. It has four unfinished statements at the top: I am... I feel... I see... I want...
    2. At the bottom is the unfinished statement, ____ knows my name.
    3. The main part of the sheet contains twelve boxes situated around...
    4. a lavender oval "Fate Clock" disc.
    5. These twelve boxes each have a line off to one side for writing something.
    6. The left six are under the banner, "Rider," and the right six are under the banner, "Ridden."
    7. The left side is in a slightly lighter color than the right. This seems an intentional mechanical, not artistic, choice.
    8. Also, most peculiar is the way the boxes are half bisected diagonally.
    9. Over the Fate Clock is a 10-pointed star. This is probably just an artistic flair.
    10. The upper right corner contains three concentric circles arranged like gears within gears or some conjunction of planets. Could be a logo. Doesn't look like it's mechanically-oriented.
    Tomas' rules use most of the sheet. The players fill in the statements (#1) as expected. They choose a player who knows their true evil nature (#2). The 12 boxes get filled in with card values (#3) and the Fate Burden gets written inside the Fate Clock disc (#4). The players writes names of Rider/Ridden on the lines by the boxes (#5, #6).

    The "clock nature" of the 12 boxes around the Fate Clock is not exploited. The fact that the left side is darker than the right isn't used (#7), nor is the funny line inside the boxes (#8). I would hope to see the artistic flair of the 10-pointed star and the "logo" of three concentric circles appear within the thematic content somehow, but they do not. I see missed opportunities here.

    Overall, I think the game does a better than average job of integrating the sheet.

    General Coolness: 5
    I'm a sucker for time-traveling history-making games. The idea of this godlike creature toying with history sounds like a great deal of fun. But the "cool" is in the play. Without a reward mechanism, this just seems like a toy, not a game, and it falls flat for me.

    Comments:
    The little punch-out boxes with designer notes are jarring. The tone of the writing within them is jovial and irreverent and self-mocking. It doesn't fit my expectations after the declaration of such a strong theme of rewriting history, only more interesting. The designer notes are apologetic at times and tell me not to take the content seriously. I think this detracts from the game as a whole. I'd get rid of these. A couple of the notes contained real play advice. Move these into the main text and lose the attitude. I almost scored down a point for this but in the end decided it was a cosmetic concern that didn't make the game any less cool.

    Total Score: 19

    Bullseye
    Game: Judson Lester
    Sheet: Jason Sims

    Bullseye is a fascinating game: it uses the whole "chase genre", which I love. And it's an experiment in some very interesting mechanics: defining characters gradually over the course of the game; letting other players reward characterisations that interest them; GM-less stuff.

    What I'm unsure of, though, is whether these mechanics will produce a good story. It's another game where I'd rather give a "potential" mark than a "playability" mark.

    And, by the way, I feel slightly shitty about posting this review. Bullseye has got a rave review above so, by scoring it slightly lower, I may well take down the average score. But I'm working on the principle that it's better to post a review than no review, so here we go.

    1. Playability 5

    The mechanics have real potential, and they're fascinating, but I'm not sure they'll make a good story.

    Firstly, the "reaching out" phase. There's six flags, which you persuade other players to give to you, by telling them a plan to accomplish something (I'm paraphrasing). Now, I like the idea of other players rewarding you for your narration, but how would it play? I'm seeing something like this:

    Player 1: [Hands flag to Player 2] "I call my ex-wife, break down in tears as I protest my innocence."
    Player 2: [Puts flag on "Loved ones"] "Works for me."
    Player 4: [Hands flag to Player 1] "I hack into the Police computer, try to find the evidence that paints me as the murderer."
    Player 1: [Puts flag on "Friends"] "OK, let's put that on Friends: you find some of the police think you're innocent."
    Player 3: [Hands flag to Player 2] "I phone my brother, get him to come and rescue me."
    Player 2: [Puts flag back on table] "Yeah, I'm not buying that."

    ...and, if we repeat that twice more, we get to the end of the reaching out phase. But I'm worried that it's just disparate bits, often of different stories: I'm not sure it'll come together to form a narrative.

    Then we roll the dice. Those dice become a Fortune-In-The-Beginning thing (almost Bacchanal-style), in that you roll the dice, find out the result and then roleplay a scene based on the result, fleshing out the character a bit more as you do so.

    This is kind of cool, actually: whoever is the target that round gets to flesh out their character a bit more, by playing scenes to do with their character's friends, relatives, loved ones, possessions, etc, etc.

    And, as I understand it, that's the game. There's Reaching Out sections, where each character tries things and succeeds or fails, and the Shooter phase, where we flesh out the characters.

    It's rather cool, and I love the fact that we're defining the characters as we go along, using scenes about people connected with them. I think it'd probably play OK (hence the "5" score). But I'm not 100% sure it's going to make for a great story, unless there's some very good roleplayers who know who to make all those scenes fit together.

    As usual, stop me if I misunderstood anything.

    2. Use of character sheet 10

    Hard to fault this, really. It's a gorgeous sheet. It's used really well: not only does Judson use every bit of the sheet, in a way which makes a coherent game, but he uses very cool things like putting Evidence Flags on the bullseye. It's great.

    3. Wow, That's Cool 7

    It's an experimental game and I think that's what competitions like this should be about. Specifically, it's experimenting with a GM-less structure where players reward each other for good narration; and it's got some interesting Fortune-At-The-Beginning stuff in it. So that's great.

    As it stands at the moment, I'm not champing at the bit, desperate to play this game. I love the chase genre, but I'd like a chase story to be fast-paced and exciting, with the chasee alone against the world. This game has several people being chased and it's often seen through the eyes of the people who the chasee left behind. I'm not so into that.

    But it's a cool game, with cool mechanics, and hence the high "cool" score. By the way, I'm fascinated by the mechanics, so if Judson or anyone wants to start a thread and talk about them, I'd be interested. Especially if I've misunderstood them.

    Total: 22

    Graham

I'm going to try to shuffle all my subjective opinions about the game into the 'general cool factor', on the theory that I'm judging subjective coolness, not objective coolness. Hopefully I won't be only one of two people rating the game, and it'll find its audience somewhere else.

    The game did seem basically playable. One dominating factor of the mechanics is that they push for two clear 'optimal' strategies: if you want to play as an optimist, you max 'Penance' and 'Passion', pushing points into 'Identity' when they fail. If you want to play as a pessimist, your strategy is less clear, but maxing 'Sin' and 'Facade' seem reasonable, perhaps pushing a few points into 'Penance' or 'Passion' as the game nears its end. For a slightly more mechanically interesting game, having people start with the same number of points (2 or 3?) in each of the five slots might be the way to go.

    It also seemed dreadfully easy to 'game' your final influence over the alien verdict, since you write what you claim is what you want after you know whether or not the aliens will go with or against that purported desire. So, if you have more Illusions than Histories, you apply a little bit of reverse psychology and you're good to go.

    Maybe this would end up with an interesting story anyway if the 'point' of the game is more narrativist than (ahem) gamist. But I better appreciate games where character desires are congruent to player desires, so as the player games the system as best he can, the character is likewise 'gaming' their reality to produce their own desired outcomes.

    The character sheet was fairly well integrated into the game: all the named components have a role to play in the game itself. The arrows on the sheet (and since when do character sheets have arrows? This contest seemed to have a surfeit of them, but I don't ever remember seeing an arrow on a sheet before) were incorporated decently with the 'shift points around' mechanic. (It would probably be easier to play this game with stones instead of written numbers. And I'm not just saying that because I used stones you move around in my own game; I think there's probably too much erasing in the game as it stands.) One aspect of the sheet wasn't used at all (the relative sizes of the circles), and I didn't quite buy the 'I did' sentences in the unlabeled circles. This was partially because these and other lines get filled in after the game is done instead of before the game begins or organically during play. I do like the general idea of having parts you fill in afterwards (I've never seen it before, myself), but I felt the game relied a bit too heavily on it.

    In general, I felt that if someone took only the game and made a character sheet from it, it would look a bit like Selene's sheet, but not quite.

    So, upshot: the basic premise didn't appeal to me, but it seemed the game could be played by those who were engaged by it--but they'd get more out of it if they ignored the obvious strategies and instead immersed themselves in their characters.

    Fatalism
    Fred Lenza, from sheet by Matthijs Holter

    Playability of the Game: 4
    Integration of Character Sheet: 8
    General Cool Factor: 5
    Overall Score: 17

    Fatalism is 'Final Destination: The RPG', played competitively. I'm not entirely sure it would work, but it's a fun take on a very minimalist sheet.

    The reason I'm not sure it would work is the combination of competitiveness with a lack of 'crunch' to referee the competition (and no GM to referee, either). This is a bigger issue in this particular game, too, because the genre it emulates is based on a series of Rube Goldberg-esque improbable events. I can envision arguments that would either break out or be suppressed 'for the good of the game' about whether a character had indeed taken adequate measures to prepare themselves against the crazy death-trap that just blew up. Since there's no in-game mechanism to resolve these disputes, it falls back to the group itself to resolve things, and that worries me. The fun of the game seems to be the thinking up of crazy death-traps by one player, and their clever avoidance by another. Being clever is where the enjoyment lies. But resolution comes down to 'who was more clever', and suddenly there's a potential for conflict that wasn't there before. In my ComedySportz improv days, there was a great built-in mechanism for resolving these types of disputes: the audience would vote. They're impartial; the points don't matter; everybody goes home happy. The built-in mechanism for resolving these types of disputes in a RPG is usually dice, which are also impartial, and again the points don't matter in the end. But here it's just straight cleverness vs. cleverness, and things just... play out, I guess. So I would have preferred some sort of built-in neutral arbitration so that everyone could get on with just being creative without worrying about being creative enough.

    Now, Fred might be aware of this and not care--the person who goes first is explicitly the person whose opening story everyone likes the least, after all, which is the same sort of thing. But I didn't like the method of choosing who goes first, either, so at least we're both consistent.

    The good news is that I believe something appropriate could be added fairly seamlessly, making this a more inherently functional game. IMO, of course.

    As far as sheet integration... well, there were a grand total of two elements on the sheet, and Fred's used them both. He seems to require that you print it on something transparent, which is a bit weird but I can live with, and you have to extend the beginning of the arrow to the corner of the sheet, which is fine but not perfect in terms of the contest (though it does make for a better game). But he did use the fact that it is a sheet of paper for what looks like an interesting sub-game of sheet placement and connections. So overall very good, but not perfect.

    The cool factor? Here's where I get to be arbitrary and bring in my personal preferences, so I'll reveal that I'm not a big horror movie fan, and though I haven't seen the Final Destination movies, the previews and the premise seemed kinda dumb. I'm also not a big fan of player-vs-player RPG conflict (or at least not in the form presented here). But Fred's writing made the game seem at least intriguing if not highly compelling even to a non-fan like me, so I have to give him props for that.

      Growing Pains - by Andrew Kenrick

      Before I start, I'd just like to say that this is by far the most professionally produced and entertaining of the three games I've looked at so far. I do like this game quite a bit, so if I sound a little negative in the review that follows it's just because I'm being thorough.

      Playability of the Game
      First and foremost it has to be said that Growing Pains has been put together in a neat, professional manner that is an absolute joy to read. It's simple, well-illustrated with examples (something sadly and seriously lacking in the last game I reviewed) and has at its base a concept with equal capacity for satire and serious use. The players characters are teenagers who can transform into powerful... er... things (what those things are is left up to the group, but the game has a very strong 'Power Rangers' vibe) and also have to deal with... angst. Argh. Now I hate angst in a game, but (a) this is purely a subjective view on my part and (b) it's a subject that's positively ripe for satire, so that's certainly not a points-losing situation.

      Speaking of Angst, the concept is worked directly into the mechanics. It starts off low and, as the character succeeds at tasks and so forth, slowly creeps up. Seems a bit contrary to real life to be honest, I usually find that doing well at stuff relieves angst, but hey! This isn't meant to be real life. Anyway, your Angst level acts as the difficulty of performing future actions. The better you do at stuff, the more likely you are to fail in the future. The GM can also set an independent difficulty number if the character attempts to do something that ought to be more difficult than their current Angst level, but the difficulty is never LESS than Angst. This is an interesting concept. It encourages the players to start off with accomplishing small challenges and work their way up to the big stuff as their Angst increases. However it also means that as the game progresses they find the simplest tasks more and more difficult to accomplish. Because of Angst. It's a fascinating concept, and I can see it working from time to time. It presents the player with an interesting problem. HIGH Angst means you fail more challenges and have to take the consequences, but failing challenges reduces your Transformation and makes it easier to change into your other form (whatever that might be). LOW Angst means that challenges are easier, but you can't use your super powers until it reaches 4, and of course if you succeed at all your challenges you'll find it difficult to transform. Fortunately you can also reduce your angst by requesting an Angst scene.

      The dice mechanics are simple enough. Nothing uber-specially-different, but that's not the point. They are quick, intuitive and fulfil their purpose. I am not entirely sold on the Angst-management aspect of the game myself, but it is at least interesting and in-theme for the subject matter.

      One thing that I would like to see clarified is the way in which scenes are handled. While the game has a traditional GM figure (the Nemesis) it is the players who define the type of scene they want to face. This is either a Challenge (increase Angst or decrease Transformation) an Angst scene (combines healing and Angst management) or a montage (recover exhausted powers, develop new ones and/or decrease Transformation). However the game's examples and descriptions take as a case in point only one character. I am not entirely certain how the Nemesis ought to deal with a game involving multiple characters. Should they all be involved in the same scene, even if they have nothing to do with the subject? Should they each get a scene of their own in turn? Growing Pains has a very relaxed atmosphere to it, so I'm guessing that players just take part in scenes as and when they feel it appropriate, and discuss who gets their own scenes where necessary. It would be nice to get a little more guidance on this matter though, and while the examples included are easily adequate to explain most of the rules, some multiple-player examples would be nice.

      Overall though, I'm mostly nitpicking. Growing Pains has a very neatly laid-out set of rules and I'm confident that the vast majority of gaming groups would be able to pick it up in a short space of time and enjoy it.

      Playability: 8
      Overall impressions are excellent, but a little more assistance with how to run the game would be greatly appreciated. Also I'm not totally sold on the Angst mechanics, but since they are thematically appropriate and pretty well-developed I'm letting it ride.

      This Place
      Joe Prince, from sheet by Mark Vallianatos

      Playability of the Game: 4
      Integration of Character Sheet: 5
      General Cool Factor: 2
      Overall Score: 11

      'This Place' is a game about figuring out who someone is and where they are. It doesn't really add much to the sheet as it came from Mark, because you look at the sheet, and think, "Hmm, I wonder who this person is and where they are?" And Joe says, "Yes, that's the question, isn't it?" In the right context it could be clever... once. But the single rule the game adds is that you sort of haphazardly randomly determine which player's view of reality is correct, and then you're done. It didn't strike me as a very good way of facilitating creativity, one of the main reasons I like to roleplay.

      It occurs to me that what we're seeing here writ large, and what I've seen in fits and spurts in some of the other games I've reviewed so far is the RPG Designer's version of the White Room Syndrome. To quote the Turkey City Lexicon:
      White Room Syndrome
      A clear and common sign of the failure of the author's imagination, most often seen at the beginning of a story, before the setting, background, or characters have gelled. "She awoke in a white room." The 'white room' is a featureless set for which details have yet to be invented -- a failure of invention by the author. The character 'wakes' in order to begin a fresh train of thought -- again, just like the author. This 'white room' opening is generally followed by much earnest pondering of circumstances and useless exposition; all of which can be cut, painlessly.
      In an RPG context, this failure of imagination is foisted off onto the players, who are forced to start in the white room, handed paintbrushes, and then abandoned. In 'This Place' the players are handed a pencil with which to create a few doodles, and a black paint roller with which to blot out other people's doodles.

      Now, RPGs are inherently dependent on creative input from the players. To extend the metaphor a bit too far, the game designer must leave white sections of the room for the players to fill in, and give them paintbrushes, pencils, pens, or chalk as is appropriate. The trick is to fill in the right amount, and leave unfilled the right amount. I thought 'You Got Ta Be Startin Sumthin' got this right--the players are told to determine the cause of the apocalypse, and the nature of the 'Zombies', but the game itself fills in the results of the apocalypse and complete rules for dealing with the Zombies in whatever form they manifest themselves. I took issue with 'Artifact Retrieval Corps' 'rule' of "Fill in Body, Mind, and Spirit however you like," because it felt more like a failure of imagination on David's part than it did a deliberate choice that sprang from the game itself.

      Anyway, that's quite the digression. A 'White Room' game could be done well (just as a white room short story could be done well) but it would require a thorough understanding of the form, and probably take quite a bit of work. 'This Place' is not that game, though it possibly could be developed into one given enough time and effort.

      Is it playable? Sort of. There are gaps even in the minimal ruleset (how exactly does one change order? Why would you ever simply accept someone's answers? What if you're playing with more or less than four people? What if you're last to go and your section is the only one unchallenged?), but more seriously, I think it violates the Siskel Criteria of RPGs. The Siskel Criteria for movies is, "Is this movie more interesting than watching the same set of actors have lunch?" The Siskel Criteria for RPGs is therefore, "Is playing this RPG more interesting than having lunch with your friends?" The fact that there's never any synthesis of ideas here, merely bland acceptance or rejection, makes the game much less interesting than it could have been.

      The character sheet itself is integrated into the game only by default. Almost nothing is added to integrate, meaning the game itself sort of limps along with the provided character sheet as a crutch. It doesn't do much actual reverse engineering, and instead just points.

      As for the cool factor, the game didn't really grab me, as is probably clear by now. I am somewhat intrigued by the idea of a 'White Room Game', and if Joe decided to develop this idea, it might turn into the ideal example of the trope, which would be great, both because it could be a very unique and fun experience, and also because then nobody else would have to write it again ;-)

      Title: Utopolis: Policing the Retrofuture
      Character Sheet Designer: Thom Foster
      RPG Designer: Eric J. Boyd

      General Comments:
      A RPG of crime solving in a future inspired by 1950's futurists, Utopolis has significant potential despite its rough spots. Particularly compelling, the PCs are former criminals themselves, who have had their memories wiped and are serving time as police detectives. All that remains is a muse describing what their original crime was, and the dramatic recurrence of the fear of committing the crime again and the flaw that lead down that road in the first place.

      Less compelling, but still of interest is that each player also takes on the role of another player's robotic partner, with a handful of tokens to affect the other player's scenes. It is said that the robot is to keep the partner in line, but there isn't much motivation for them. This seems more present to accommodate an additional part of the character sheet, although it has definite untapped potential.

      The overall structure of the game is to solve one crime after another, occasionally confronting a character's hidden memories in fear/flaw scene (which are triggered by pushing, so players always have a choice to cause or avoid one). These scenes can eventually lead to breakdowns, or baring such characters will eventually leave the force and retire as upright citizens who fully forgets her or his crime. The suggestion of a broken character being discovered as the culprit of the crime under investigation is an excellent one, and fully justifies letting those characters keep on until the current crime is solved.

      As far as solving crimes, Utopolis borrows significantly from Troy Costisick's Holmes and Watson Committee from this past Game Chef competition, inheriting some of the problems in that design, though fixing a few as well. Chief among the borrowings is the central, though somewhat nebulous, accumulation of clues and the promotion of those clues into evidence.

      Since evidence is never pinned downed mechanically or in a fictional legal sense, it becomes an implicit communal pacing mechanic - as players choose to make some clues evidence to speed the case along. The acquisition of clues is also somewhat limited, being reserved solely for winning conflicts between characters, leading to the odd effect that it "takes two to make a clue."

      In addition, clues that might be found within a conflict are determined by a question die, where the clue helps answer the who, what where, etc. of the crime. The difficulty being that it is unclear when a clue is interpreted (made to help answer this question). In genre this happens sometime after the clue is discovered, but this mechanic suggests that the clue's meaning is narrated in its discovery.

      Another major concern is the conflict resolution mechanic, which is a somewhat tricky poker variant based on hands drawn by conflict participants. Comparing the hands comes down to four different ranks and intermediate comparisons within these, to break ties and to award cinema points (which are a general character resource for drawing more cards and inserting narration). The cinema award is particularly unclear, I believe it should be gain an additional cinema point equal to how much larger your largest X of a kind is versus the largest your opponent's have, but I cannot be sure.

      On the whole, the resolution mechanic could be much simpler, without losing anything in terms of game effect. I suggest making clear distinctions (possibly, but not necessarily mechanically) between leads (given when the GM wins a conflict), clues, and evidence. Also, I think the game would benefit from interleaving crimes and generally more guidance for GMs in setting up an interesting crime or crimes. Lastly, the character by character end game results in either breakdown or blissful retirement, you cannot presently have a character learn to live with the knowledge of their crime. This seems to be a rich vein of potential left untapped.

      Estimated Playability: 6

      Definitely a playable game, the only issues here are whether or not it plays the way intended, and how many omissions need to be filled in by the players and the GM. Some things help, like fear/flaw tests, other things get in the way, like the direct link between conflicts and clues.

      Incorporation of Character Sheet: 8

      The character sheet looks and feels right for the game, although some elements feel more strained. Certainly the sheet has not been abandoned by the designer, and he has brought out aspects that might otherwise have been forgettable.

      Coolness Factor: 6

      I find the idea of the game very compelling. The execution has many problems, some of which detract. But ultimately I want this game to be better than it currently is, and that if anything else, is coolness factor.

      Total Score: 20

      Title: Motes in the Eye of the Emperor
      Character Sheet Designer: Jeff Moore
      RPG Designer: Alexander Cherry

      General Comments:
      Imagine you are a virtuous bureaucrat taken from your earthly domain and brought to manage the technological heaven beyond your world. What would you do? That is the question that never quite gets answered in this RPG. More clearly, Motes is incomplete, and avowedly stated so by the author.

      Things start out well enough, futuristic colony of Chinese descent, and emperor in some refined space station watching and maintaining order, above and below. Complex protocols incomprehensible to the players, and an evocative beginning scene where players introduce characters, and then go around choosing whether to be that character. After that, the momentum appears to be lost.

      The main difficulty is the lack of motivation. The reward mechanic gives you experience when you roll doubles on a percentile roll, regardless of success. This reduces your aspects, and gives you experience. Experience may do something, but what it might be is omitted. Regardless though each turn players go around and attempt actions, gaining experience a tenth of the time they roll. What aspect is rolled under is determined by the key aspect, which, reflecting the opacity of the centuries of protocols, define how something must be attempted, or if it cannot be done auspiciously.

      But ultimately, none of these provide a sense for what characters actually do, or why they do them. It is the Emperor's job to figure that out, by framing a scene for each player in turn. Characters also gain abilities, which allow them to "do the impossible" and hold some special post within the bureaucracy. But since no example aspects are show, it is extremely unclear what this means for a player. After all in a sci-fi setting, what actually qualifies as impossible, and which of those should be impossible even with an ability.

      While Motes is only a fragment of a RPG, several major problems are already creeping into the design. Specifically, the resolution mechanic, while functional is overemphasized and ill-suited to the themes of the game. At the same time play dynamics and motivations are largely ignored. It has potential, but needs a lot of work to bring out the "awesome" as the author puts it.

      Estimated Playability: 2
      As a fragment it is definitely not playable, but the set-up of the first scene especially shows that some parts are eminently playable.

      Game: Etherium
      Designer: Thom Foster
      Sheet by: Kirk Mitchell

      Premise:
      Those who sleep and those whose spirits will not rest meet in the realm of dreams. A mostly drama-resolution, freeform-ish, evocative game that, against my hopes, fails to fulfill its promise.

      Playability: 4
      Etherium walks a delicate path. Its creator wants it to be playable by anyone, even people who have never played a roleplaying game before. But at the same time, the game is deeply evocative instead of structured and clear.

      At its core, Etherium features drama resolution with a die only to be used to determine who gets to narrate the setup and who gets to resolve conflicts. That's it for rules. Everything else in the game has no mechanical value and is meant to be used for inspiration for scenes, conflicts, etc. The evocative writing helps experienced players to set up neat characters, but it does not help at all with situation creation, what to do in the game, how to help pace or structure the game, etc. Anyone without any roleplaying experience is likely to fall flat on their faces trying to come up with a good game session. Sure, they could probably narrate a thing or two, but the lack of direction--in parts stemming from the refusal to say things without the dream-like confusing tone of the game text--will leave most people hanging.

      Now, it is possible to make a drama-resolution-based, coherent, playable game out of this that could actually be played as a "first game," but it would need a lot of work.

      Sheet Integration: 3
      Many of this game's main character aspects (Phobias, Themela, symbols) aren't even on the sheet. Sure, Thom somewhat incorporated the sheet into the game, but overall it looks like there was a game here that was superimposed on the sheet, and the result is that the core information of the characters is nowhere written down. Take away the sheet and its parts out of the game and 95% of the game would still be the same.

      Coolness Factor: 8
      I enjoyed reading the game and loved the evocative flavor. But I'm also someone with 11+ years of freeform play experience who could easily play this as written using my experience and techniques I've learned. That said, the mechanics don't add anything that I couldn't play out in freeform, character-ownership games. What the game really does is provide you with inspiring little snippets to develop interesting characters. So while the evocative aspects of the game are really cool--especially all the poetic touches and symmetries throughout (such as poems that are mirrored like hour glasses)--there isn't much "game" here.
