We have a problem to solve.

Many gamers, especially online ones, are caught up in a debate that doesn't actually exist on all the levels they think it does. Many of us (yes, I include myself), when looking at theory, get caught up in images of people, and of "what they're really saying underneath".

And then we debate it. Pointlessly. Often with varied strains of utterly useless intellectualism and anti-elitism thrown straight into the pot - often, the anti-elitists fire first and hardest, as a defense. And when others come to tell them that this isn't necessary, the defenders know each other, and speak the same language.

Sooner or later, somebody gets dogpiled.

Somebody waves around a name or two; someone accuses another person of telling them that they're having the wrong kind of fun, or someone accuses another of being anti-intellectual.

This is a thing I want to help stop and, aggravatingly, I fall prey to it myself often enough to piss off people on both sides a bit, now and then.

But I think we need to stop it, and I've been trying.

No names. Just ideas. What else can we do?

    There are (to my mind) two (no, make that three) reasons to respond to somebody who's clearly wound around the axle by their own emotional take on what's being said:
    To convey your point, so that other people (who are not so confused) can respond to itTo try to untangle their emotional problems over the internetTo get them further wound up because you like watching them squirm and flail
    The second of those seems a bit quixotic, though I've felt the urge many times. The third of them seems a bit mean-spirited ... though I've felt the urge many times. The first one, though, really does work.

    As someone who has fallen into his share of scuffles, but has steered clear for the most part, I'm afraid you'll be unpleasantly surprised. There is a great deal of wilfull misunderstanding and uncharitable reading.

    While some people really are communicating past each other and could come to an equitable understanding, there are a number of usual subjects who are actively trying to shut discussion down. Mark knows.

    Judd's advice really is the best. Talk about your actual play and the practical application of your theoretical ideas.

    When you're talking theory in the absence of application or evidence, it is too easy to simply dismiss your ideas and get into a tautologous argument. If you're talking about your actual play, they're in a position where they either have to discount your play or actually enter into a productive discussion by explaining what they think is happening.

    It has the added benefit that well-adjusted people who are not interested in that style of play can back out without feeling that it's an attack on the way they like to play, while people who are open to new things get an honest-to-goodness example that really expresses what you're talking about. It's win-win.

    I believe this is a big part of the reason that Ron and Clinton have chosen to channel all theory discussion through the Actual Play forum at The Forge, by the way.
