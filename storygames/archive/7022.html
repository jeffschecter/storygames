\n I'm a big fan of the Watchmen comic.

One of the things I loved most about it was how we knew nothing about the characters at the start of the story and how their past was revealed to us in bits and pieces... and that the past is what is driving the contemporary story line.
I loved learning about their secret inter-connectivity.

I've always wanted to replicate this with a game.

So what system/game would you use to recreate the "Revelatory Backstory" effect?

    \n My first thought was Shock:. Then when I saw what you were actually looking for, Sign in Stranger popped into my head.

    In Shock: Watchmen, the Shock would be costumed crimefighters, and the issues would be whatever social or political hotbuttons you wanted to examine relevant to masked vigilantes' impact on a culture. And every protag would have their own Story Goal that they're pursuing as they navigate the tawdry maze of urban intrigue. Pretty straightforward, really.

    but it doesn't really do much with "Relevatory Backstory," at least not formally. Whereas with Sign in Stranger, you would get two things:

    1) a cast of characters exploring their bewildering environment, confronted by a series of problems that require multiple steps of revealed acts about the setting before they can be solved.

    2) Flashbacks as an integral part of the game, with the directed purpose of revealing aspects of a character's nature and history specifically relevant to the situation in the present.

    At least that's what I remember. I read the playtest doc sometime ago, but haven't got to play it.

    \n I have a few alternate ideas, if you're still hunting system, Jordan.

    In a Watched Age - The chapter structure of the Watchmen--including the new articles and such--is very conducive to IaWA game play, with its chapter structure. It might take a bit of finagling the We Owe List, should events in the story cause a principle character to become ineligible for subsequent chapters. But flashback chapters are a no-brainer--part and parcel of IaWA standard play, per the RAW.

    Universalis - If your group is really all-about-story, then I'd go Uni. Again, flashback scenes are par for the course; and there's really no "balance" issue between Doc v. Rorschach, because "Can See Future" is only one Trait, equal in effectiveness in Challenges as "Scary, flowing-ink mask." Doc shrinking and growing is only color; creating a castle of sand on Mars is only a scene change and some Fact instantiation; and ruminating upon the past and his certain-to-fail marriage is, at best, an interstitial--a scene of Color to establish tone and mood (or even theme, with a good player in charge of that scene).

    But that said... Andrew beat me to it: Doctor Manhattan is a plot device for the GM (i.e. an NPC), not a player. As Is Ozy, IMO, unless you're doing some kind of competitive, team-versus-team (or -itself)--he's the main villain. So with IaWA, Doc and Ozy are NPCs run by the GM; and with Uni, they are just characters with as many Traits as anyone else, ready to be controlled by whomever, for whatever functional role they must serve in the story.

    In short: if your players want to have "My Guy," do it with IaWA and be sure they understand how "their guy" needs to be on the We Owe List each chapter end, to continue to a new past or future scene. Note that the character can be dead, but on the We Owe, and can thus easily get into a past scene; but it might need some deux ex, to get into a future scene. If your players don't necessarily care about one-player-one-PC, then Uni offers the most flexibility to build all sorts of time-disjointed stories about supers... or anything else.

    HTH--have fun with it!
    David
