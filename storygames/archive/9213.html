\n Swine flu is being tracked using the WHO Pandemic Alert Phases:

Phase 1: No viruses circulating among animals causing infections in humans
Phase 2: Animal influenza virus causes infection in humans, and is considered potential pandemic threat
Phase 3: Influenza causes sporadic cases in people, but no significant human-to-human transmission
Phase 4: Verified human-to-human transmission able to cause community-level outbreaks. Significant increase in risk of a pandemic
Phase 5: Human-to-human transmission in at least two countries. Strong signal pandemic imminent
Phase 6: Virus spreads to another country in a different region. Global pandemic under way

Now, is it just me or are these perfectly designed for an RPG? The progression of stages, the fact that there's six of them, so you can, in some way, determine them with a d6. They're quite like Zombie Cinema.

How would this RPG work? What sort of pandemic would it be? What would the mechanics be?

Graham

    \n It's a relationship based game, focussing on a small community, somewhere around the level of It's Complicated.

    The game proceeds by scene with some resolution mechanic and fallout, that is what happens as a result of the someone losing the scene resolution. The WHO Pandemic Alert Phases are used to inform the level of fallout from a scene so as things get more desperate on the global scale, they also become more extreme on the local scale. Perhaps one rolls a number of d6 equal to the WHO PAP, each one above 4 indicates some amount of injury inflicted on the loser, whether it be physically, mentally or perhaps even to their property.

    It's what Ballard would have wanted.

    \n We'll call the phase list the Rank.
    \nThe Rank determines the Scale and the Difficulty.
    \n
    \nYou play medics/scientists/etc trying to stop the pandemic.
    \n
    \nYou need to roll equal to or above the Difficulty to succeed. The higher the Scale, the more people get hurt when you fail. Every session starts the game in a different area of the world - roll 1d6 + number of sessions to determine the starting Rank for the session.
    \n
    \nThe game ends at session 5, at which point all of the world would be at Rank 6. Consider overall total successes of the PC's. If they've done particularly well, they stop the virus (or at least slow it).
    \n
    \nPossibly include a way to reduce the effective session count, thus allowing for longer play as the PC's find solutions (which the pandemic adapts to, eventually) before the final session?

    \n Start w/ # of diseases equal to # of players. Each disease starts in a separate location. All diseases at Rank 2. Player characters are a party that goes on missions to eradicate diseases. Each mission counts as one "turn". Each turn, all diseases advance by 1 rank.

    If characters fail in a mission, and the disease they were fighting goes up to Rank 4 or higher, they become carriers for that disease, spreading it wherever they go until a true antidote can be found.

    If the characters infect 2 sites, their boss will realize they're carriers, terminate their operation, and quarantine them, thus ending the game in failure (option to become hunted refugees instead while still pursuing cures sans resources?). The GM will then play out the apocalypse depending on the state of things when the characters were removed from play.

    During each mission, the players must deploy their characters' talents and resources to get bonuses to the mission's die roll. Each disease is different; the GM chooses from a dozen templates, each one with its own strengths, weaknesses, telltales, and color. When the players have done all they can, the die is rolled.
    Results range:
    1) Cure Found, Eradication Imminent - this disease is vanquished, though possibly with a delay pending endgame conditions
    2) Treatments & Containment Instated - this disease is reduced to Rank 1; each turn, it can be kept at Rank 1 by sacrificing small resources (i.e., choose slight penalty on other missions), otherwise it resumes normal progression
    3) Buying Time - this disease does not progress in rank during this turn or the next
    4) Failure - you accomplish nothing; this disease marches on

    Roleplaying through a specific mission involves mostly investigation, looking at stuff, talking to people, and gathering info so as to best strategize use of resources. Tests are made along the way, with success granting bonus to final mission die roll and failure granting penalties. Tests are mostly based on character skills, with resources as modifiers. All skills are character-specific (no overlap), and all measured resources are finite.

    All this can be high-color as the GM riffs off the disease template and injects the fiction with sympathetic, stricken NPCs; or low-color, as the players quickly assemble facts and make decisions.

    That's my brainstorm. Honestly, this is a bit boardgamey for my own RPG tastes... I'd probably use it as a framework, and inject mission scenes with a lot of character-specific and overarching-plot stuff.

    \n I don't think I would use a d6. Suggests too much of being able to jump around. Zombie Cinema would be a neat hack, but to come up with something more base (or maybe Serial Homicide Unit, drifted for CDC rather than homicide detectives)...

    A game about people reacting to the different phases comes to mind. In fact, I would look at stealing from Pandemic (the board game) and make this about people who over time are dealing with how the pandemic intersects with their lives. The early scenes are more about them to establish a baseline, and the mechanic (from the board game) involves a deck of cards, specifically:

    * A deck of forty A-10 cards split into five after shuffling, so eight cards in each pile.
    * In each sub-deck, put a face card in, and shuffle that.
    * Put all the decks together, so that somewhere within each layer of nine is a face card, but you don't know where.

    Then as you play, people draw a card to resolve, uh, something. I don't care about that right now. The number or suit or bother determines crap -- still don't care. When the face card is drawn, the scene is interrupted with how the escalation happens. You start at phase 1, then with the first card you're at phase 2, and so on. With each phase, the chance that the illness interferes with your life is greater -- perhaps the number on the card determines that, and the suit merely determines who gets whatever in the conflict that I still don't care about.

    In any case, that's how I would envision it, mainly because Graham titled the thread as so.
