\n Like many other folks, I've been thinking about the ongoing unrest in Iran and, having experienced first-hand how roleplaying can serve as a medium for creating a more personal investment in real global conflicts (thanks, Emily and Jason!), I've been thinking about writing a "serious game" hack about the current conflict in Iran, kinda like the Dogs in the Vineyard hack I wrote about the spring 2008 unrest in Tibet.

Is anyone else potentially interested in playing something like this or helping put something together? I think the "Dogs but only some people have guns" approach I took to the Tibet hack might work here too, or maybe something like Shock:, Microscope, or Emily's A Day in the War hack of Zombie Cinema that takes a look at a crisis from multiple different angles. My purpose would be making a game that's both "fun" to play -- in the sense of providing both a narrative and mechanics that are engaging, not necessarily in that it's any happier than Grey Ranks or most sessions of Dogs -- while also providing players with an opportunity to learn more about Iran, the current conflict, and the complexities of a struggle that is currently spiraling into more and more violence.

If some folks find the very idea of this distasteful, that's totally reasonable. Personally, I feel like attempting to put yourself in someone else's shoes and understand why people make the choices they do, whether it's standing up to armed security forces or firing on unarmed civilians, can only serve to make the world a better place. But the decision to pursue that goal through the structure of roleplaying games is my choice and I'm not suggesting that anyone else make that choice unless they want to.

That said, how do we start? Personally, for something like this to be really effective, I feel like it's important to include characters from very different walks of life, to show the diversity of perspectives at play. So maybe we could start by listing a bunch of different character concepts and then figure out how to fit them into different games? I'm totally fine with developing hacks for a number of different systems, if there's enough interest and people are pushing in different directions.

Some initial character concepts:

- a refugee from the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, now finding themselves amidst a new civil conflict in Iran
- a non-Muslim Jewish or Zoroastrian citizen, or maybe a Sunni such as a Kurd from the northeast of Iran
- a foreign trained scientist (MIT?) working on Iran's nuclear program
- one of the 400 women who finally joined the National Police Force in 2003
- a member of VEVAK, the Iranian ministry of intelligence, or Basij, the volunteer paramilitary organization (like the National Guard or state militias)

    \n I think this is very worthwhile but, for a variety of reasons, pretty shaky ground. It'd be extraordinarily easy to do it wrong, basically. "Playing" current events raises all sorts of issues (see Kuma/War).

    One thing that I think would be important is that the game provide the tools for understanding the context of what you are playing, so that it is a learning tool.

    I think Zombie Cinema/A Day in the War is a good place to start. I'd also look at Ron's game-in-development, Shahida, which touches on current events (or can). My gut feeling is that playing extraordinary people (like those on your list) isn't the best approach - I think I'd make the playable character set very ordinary Iranians, with departures from a mundane baseline just far enough to illustrate the diversity of circumstance within, say, a particular neighborhood in Tehran. I might take a hard look at what "playable" means, and drift away from character monogamy toward some sort of ensemble thing.

    \nMore substantively, I think what he means by extraordinary v. ordinary is more representative of the statistical composite of the mean Iranian, as it were (a soldier drafted from a rural area near Merv, an unemployed textile worker in Isfahan, a housewife in Tabriz) versus more distant from the statistical composite along some very notable dimension (being from a small religious/ethnic minority, a foreigner/professional smartypants, an intelligence agent). I think of the examples you gave, a member of the Basij might be closer to Jason's idea of "ordinary," bearing in mind that they are expected to represent and believe in a particularly strong version of the prevailing ideology of the Islamic Republic, so a bit like playing a blackshirt in Mussolini's Italy. Obviously, Jason, if I'm totally missing your point, do let me know.
    \n
    \nI'm sort of more sympathetic to this idea; I think that what is happening now is much more interesting when viewed from the perspective of someone who was until recently very much inside of the system, rather than someone who is largely an outsider. Outsiders, I feel, can more readily imagine how circumstances might be very different from what they are, or at least, not necessarily fear a major upheaval (since they're not in positions of privilege as is), whereas someone closer to the composite of someone within the system has a much more fraught set of feelings on average about what might happen, pro-Ahmadi or anti-Ahmadi.

    \nI'd suggest that focusing on the extraordinary (which Joshua Riley pretty much nails; I use the word literally) is less likely to lead to empathy, to understanding, to personal investment. It's more likely to lead to action and excitement and conflict as lots of very different people are thrown together as though it were a Raymond Carver novel or something, which is great for a roleplaying game. Roleplaying seems like only a piece of this idea, though, a means to an end.

    You may be totally right about paralysis. I approach these things with reverence, since I might have to look someone in the eye who has lived it (in this case is living it). There's a point of successful balance you can find, and maybe that varies from person to person, and maybe getting it wrong is inevitable to some degree because of that.

    \n Joshua, I was going by this map, which indicates there are Kurds in both the northeast and northwest. My background in Iranian affairs is not very strong, though, which is partially why I wanted to start this thread and get some feedback from others.



    I get what you and Jason are saying about playing members of the common, everyday majority. Honestly, I didn't mention them in my original post partially because they're the first people that come to mind and I wanted to make sure to remember that they don't necessarily represent the diversity of interests that exists. But you're right that it's important to show people who are invested in the status quo or are interested in making less radical changes that don't threaten their own livelihood. I was also thinking, Shock: style, that it might be interesting to show characters who are at the crossroads of different interests, such as female police officers.

    On the reverence issue, Jason, I guess I feel that a one-shot roleplaying session (or even a few sessions), inherently ephemeral and experienced solely by the people involved, is less likely to deeply wound folks directly involved in the actual events than a movie or novel or article on the same subject, which potentially stands for all eternity and it publicly available. If I was going to publish a game about something like this, as you did with Grey Ranks, I would definitely do a lot more research and talk to a bunch of people, but I worry that moving cautiously on something like this risks loosing the immediacy. Sure, we could play my Tibet game today (and indeed, I wasn't actually able to play it last spring), now that the outcome of that conflict is known (at least for now), but it wouldn't really be the same, yeah?
