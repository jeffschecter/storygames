A Good Playtest

wyrmwood
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
So this weekend I had an indepth playtest with a mix of players, via a local gaming club for Drift. I'd estimate it was 60-40 fun to drudgery, including learning the rules and making characters. With only two concerns (reworking difficulty numbers and making impetus costlier to spend), the players had only positive feedback. One of them even asked hopefully when Drift would be for sale. Then I went through and listed nearly a dozen possible changes suggested by the playtest, getting feedback on each. I'm largely convinced the core mechanic needs a complete overhaul, although it seems I was the only one leaving the playtest with that opinion. All in all a good playtest.

The hard part will be ensuring Drift doesn't just become Dogs in space...

But anyway, this seems to be a trend in later steps of my game designs. A fun playtest which hits on 8 out of 10 cylinders compels me to make significant changes, and so on. Pure Shoujo is an excellent example, a game which when it gets going is pure gold, its a matter of ramping up to the action. Because it still requires about 1/2 an hour and at least one socially confrontational player - or bitting the bullet and having a GM.

So what is your idea of a good playtest? And how close should your design be to your goals before you go on to publish it?

    Here are some of the questions I would ask, just to get things started:

    1. How many playtest groups do you have? More than one is best, with no overlap.

    2. How many playtest groups have played without your presence?

    2A. Are they able to play the game the way you play the game, without you being there to guide them?

    2B. Is EVERYTHING you do to make a fun game actually written in the text?

    3. Have they played through full reward/currency cycles of the game? If your game is intended to provide more than just one shots, single-session playtests aren't really going to be sufficient.
