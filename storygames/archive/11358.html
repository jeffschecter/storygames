    \n For a long time, the GM's "character sheet" was his GM screen plus his notepad. I think you could make a generic sheet where a GM could keep his notes most pertinent to both the campaign and the game at hand. But, like some people above I am also convinced this would only blossom when coupled with specific mechanics.

    Note: the character sheet is not a place where players note down their in-character diary or something, it's basically a rules and numbers quick-reference. It's a memetic tool.

    I think the games that would most benefit from a "GM sheet" are those that have very direct, focused rules for the GM to follow. In a game where I'm supposed to fiat and improvise it all, why would I need a specific sheet? I can just scrible onto any piece of paper I find, if needed.
    Now, games with strong story or scene structure, act progression, and intense GM-procedures to follow, yes, in those cases a sheet would be great I believe.

    What would I put on a GM sheet for
    *FATE? List of random names and bangs to drive the game forward, list of players with their characters and aspects...that's it.
    *D&D? Rules summaries, references, initiative track, little monster boxes with vital combat information...
    *Mouse Guard? Now we're getting meaty, Season progression, Weather twists, GM-turn-Player-turn notes, Checks, Beliefs...
    *Apocalypse World (which I've recently read through) - this game already has something like it incorporated - you've got Fronts and Clocks and whatnot.

    Nameless brings up the WHFRP3 Party Sheet, which is, I think another example how a game can make good use of "Sheets" of any kind, when they're incorporated into the mechanics. I have been thinking about putting stuff like this into my next game, but I'm not that far along to say for sure if it will make it.

    Bottom line is, playsheets are aides, and maybe games should figure them in their whole design a little more (much like WHFRP3 did). We can have Setting Sheets, Cheat Sheets, Character Sheets, Theme Documents, GM Sheets, Party/Team Sheets, Adventure Sheets, Story Sheets...

    If you consider this possibility when designing your game, you can end up with three or four or five sheets of paper on the table, that contain everything you need to play. No need to crack open the book during the game. The sheets can be evocative, nicely designed, they are tangible elements that can be manipulated and used much like dice, they can remind the players of the vital elements, of what the game is about etc.

    \n The sheet you are proposing seems like it might be useful for a traditional or indie/traditional type game - but I worry for other RPGs it'll be more problematic that it is helpful.

    Perhaps it would be a good idea to have one side as a session worksheet (to speed prep), including variant versions for structures other than three act, and the other side as a more stationary persistent reference including in and out of game elements (including the social contract elements). That way games which don't require prep or have other more unusual ways of using a GM can still make use of the reference side. And in any case, the GM can swap out the session side without needing to rewrite the persistent side.

    In any case, this seems like one possible fix for RPGs which haven't adequately designed the role of the GM. If they have, then it seems that there would already be an implicit or explicit GM sheet for the game, or that none would be necessary.

    \n My first few thoughts for example problematic games are ones I have in playtest (specifically ones that have their own GM sheets of sorts), but I realized that those would be less helpful as you wouldn't have heard of them. But what to my mind makes a sheet as described in the opening post wholly useful or not is whether the 3 act structure is being tacked on unnecessarily or whether it provides a much needed assist to the GM. From that perspective there are several classes of games, those that come to mind:

    1) RPGs with strong or moderate GM authority and no overriding story structure - these are fairly traditional and will lend themselves well to a sheet which provides the GM with organization and story structure. By design many games (traditional and some indie) fall into this category - for example if I run GURPS Transhuman Space, Nobilis, or D&D.

    2) RPGs with strong or moderate GM authority and overriding story structure - the story structure here is a big issue, if it clashes with your sheet then you will find its helpfulness greatly diminished. Examples here include category (1) with story structure implanted by group consensus, or designed RPGs (usually indie games) like Dogs in the Vineyard or Prime Time Adventures.

    3) RPGs with weak GM authority - the story is as much in the hands of the players as the GM. In this case the planning part of the GM sheet will likely serve as a frustration, encouraging the GM to over plan the session and leaving little of the narrative described to occur. Examples here include some social contract versions of (2) and games like Inspectres.

    4) RPGs with no GMs - it is unlikely you expect your GM sheet to help in these cases.

    5) RPGs with multiple GMs - I think about this category because I'm currently designing in this space and some fascinating things arise here, especially in the case where the GMs are not fully coordinated, which results in a situation much like (3).

    So, while I can imagine many games where this could work (if customized for a system), how much would this sheet need to be changed to be of comparable help to the GM of an Inspectres game?

    - Mendel

    \n Cool idea. Thanks for posting your sheets, dude.

    FYI - Moon Designs has a GM aide pdf download for Heroquest. It has the usual run of GM-screen tables, but what got my attention were the blank sections where you can track the try-fail history for each character and write down character-pertinent notes.     My only critique of the very cool sheets posted here is that they strike me as dense, and I've found during a game that having a lot of tightly packed information spread in front of me ends up being more distracting than helpful. I wouldn't find it necessary to write down the social contract and system, for instance--or much of the header info on p.1. Some areas look pretty jammed. I'm less concerned about it being a comprehensive capture of everything about the players and the story.

    The very last sheet on story details is the one I like the best, though it still seems overdense. I'd prefer to break out one act at a time and provide a space on the right for notes taken in play. But again, that's tailoring the sheet to someone with my particular set of preferences. I often find myself jotting down quick notes in response to player decisions or events in-game, and linking a note section to portions of a streamlined cheat-sheet would work better for GMs like me.
