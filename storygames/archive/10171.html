    \n I was a great big fan of Sons of Kryos when it was still an ongoing concern. A number of things got me interested in indie/story RPGs a few years back, but I'd credit this podcast as the biggest one.

    The things that stand out as being the awesomest were:

    * The dynamic between Judd and Jeff, and the way the expressed their opinions and personalities. They seemed like such nice, friendly, reasonable fellows, with a personal history and friendship and a connection to their local gaming scene.

    * The two of them seemed to be interested in different gaming styles/traditions, with Judd championing less-traditional games, and Jeff doing the opposite ... but with lots of overlap, so that a) they could always enjoy games the other enjoyed, and b) if they were discussing a new, maybe not-so-traditional game, there were always references to more familiar territory to provide context.

    * The show had a very obvious focus on Actual Play, which is a big deal, and meant that whatever they were saying was about their gaming experiences, and not nebulous theorizing.

    \n I've chosen to take the approach of naming qualities that I affirmatively like, and qualities that I passionately dislike.
    \n
    \n(Some of these are a wee bit ranty, since I've just listened to like 20 different podcasts and hardly any of them have made the cut. Sigh. So much crap...)
    \n
    \nMy own favorite gaming podcasts...
    \n
    \n- are hosted by *interesting* people with a lot of gaming experience, ideally people with a little disagreement
    \n- are hosted by people with good social skills, so it's not like listening to an alpha gamer and his sidekick simply happy to be a "podcaster" now
    \n- understand the difference between entertainment and content, and focus on content
    \n- are generally 15-35 minutes in duration
    \n- don't waste my time with long music intros and outtros and fucking bumpers (the Durham 3 is awesome!)
    \n- don't waste my time by taking 5 minutes to tell me what they're about to tell me before the show *really* starts
    \n- avoid over-production (and instead focus just on information)
    \n- if the show has multiple sections, offer chapterized .aac versions so listeners can move around
    \n- have short but descriptive episode titles: ep # + a few words about the main topic
    \n- have good, but still brief, episode descriptions
    \n- have hosts who are confident enough to not talk about themselves, engage in histrionics, or are using this as a self-promotion vehicle for Internet Fame
    \n- don't sink into discussing their game fiction, as if I could possibly know or care
    \n- have websites with searchable episode contents
    \n- do not need forums; discussing at any of the regular gaming forums is fine
    \n- publish on iTunes and offer direct download from their websites
    \n- don't rehash the same Luke Crane or Fred Hicks interview ad nauseum (much as I enjoy these guys, if I hear one more person ask either of them how they got into gaming, or what their favorite character was, I'm going to throw my iPhone through the window)
    \n- stay on topic... STAY ON TOPIC!
    \n- have equipment to support reasonably good quality audio (doesn't need to be pro, but better than phones and both people equally loud, please?)
    \n- don't feel compelled to offer convention interviews from the show floor... these are often hectic and full of invective; instead, favor sit-down, thoughtful interviews (like Clyde's)
    \n- avoid inside jokes or stupid nerd humor, followed by 60 seconds in which the hosts are laughing at their jokes, and trying to one-up each other
    \n- only release episodes when they have something thoughtful to say, or actual content to release
    \n- have hosts who are at least somewhat articulate, and have reasons for their views besides "I just like|hate it"
    \n- are driven hard by hosts who know exactly what their show is about
    \n- are more than just recorded table chatter
    \n- have hosts who know better than to say "we'll put a link in the show notes"
    \n- and in fact do NOT need elaborate show notes; just a brief accounting of the topics. for the rest, we have google
    \n- when interviewing someone, the hosts don't drift into a conversation with each other and ignore their guests
    \n- don't participate in the gaming podcaster echo chamber, trying to pass off podcasters talking to podcasters is content or something
    \n- do discuss current events and interesting threads on web forums and conventions
    \n- have discussions grounded in ACTUAL personal experience instead of what everyone says about that game
    \n- avoid the most annoying verbal tics of our hobby, like: making sweeping generalizations, overuse of the word "basically", using the word "um" to end a sentence, or laughing at your own joke to end a sentence, etc. grr! :-)
    \n- stick to RPGs as their topic (mostly)
    \n- are not afraid to generate longer or different content, but don't include it in the main feed... or if they do, mark it as bonus content, not an actual episode
    \n- unless targeted at a young audience, do not bleep profanity

    \n
    \nOutside of gaming:
    \n- Science Fiction Book Review - The reviewer has an interesting background (Brit living in Berlin, professional juggler), enthusiastically speaks his mind.
    \n- LensWork - A very short form podcast by a fine art photographer and publisher about art and creativity (and sometimes photography)... there are > 550 episodes in the archives right now
    \n- This American Life - For the often compelling stories. Great storytelling.
    \n- Craft Beer Radio - Mostly; Greg is a lot stronger than Jeff. Listen for the sensory discussion and comparisons.
    \n- Ante Up Poker Podcast - These two guys have gone on to make a more polished and snazzy podcast at a new feed, but this is very much two guys who work for a newspaper who like poker -- and are ok but not great -- talking about the game and their experiences at the table, and trying to learn how to get better. Very enthusiastic, some great stories. Watch for episodes with Mike Fasso, an occasional guest who's a real character. The early episodes where they recorded in the newspaper break room (with noises in the background) are oddly fascinating.
    \n- Unfettered Mind - About practical, pragmatic Buddhism by an American who studied in Tibet for years.
    \n- Tennis Connected - Commentary on recent professioanal tennis by people who have played the game
    \n- Python 411 - About the Python programming language and community... the host is an enthusiastic hobbyist, which makes up for a lot, but this is not a show for professional programmers. :-)
    \n- Lake Wobegon podcast - The newer episodes are generally not as good as those from 20 years ago, but I still like them.

    \n Let me give you some feedback about gaming podcasts, that may help you understand them a little better.
    \n
    \n(You in all cases is in the abstract, I'm not singling people out just approaching the statements voiced here as if I was given them anonymously)
    \n
    \nIn the general podcast world the 15-20 minute podcast is king. Tight and short being the best. In gaming podcasts the most demand is for the 45-60 minute podcast. So, you may not want longer podcasts but the vast majority of gaming podcast listeners do.
    \n
    \nAlso in gaming podcasts roughly half of the audience listens to you for personality and the other half for content. And increasing your content does not tend to net you any more listeners, it simply costs you listeners.
    \n
    \nYou (and I for that matter) may not like 3+ hour actual play podcasts, but they are extremely popular. Much more popular than "content" shows.
    \n
    \nYou may not like "in" jokes. Lot of people like them, Good use of in jokes is an extremely potent drive for "super fans." Super fans are themselves one of the best drives for popularity because they promote your show.
    \n
    \nYou may want podcasters to use external forums, but forums dislike outsiders. And they (the gestalt of the forum) dislike people who have authority not derived from their community.
    \n
    \nAbove all if you are interested in gaming podcasts go to rpgpodcasts.com for a complete list. And if you have any questions ask a podcaster. Now the bad news is that the particular type of person who is a podcaster is often a huge dick. But the secret is that they are also all incredibly vain, so just lie to them and claim you love their show.
