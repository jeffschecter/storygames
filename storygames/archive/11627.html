\n My last game, "Lykke" (Happiness), is written with the goal of having the players creating and experiencing happiness in the game, through their characters. It succeeded in doing so, in the first play-test.

I have made a couple of other games too, with similar goals (specific human conditions). That is a natural design-venue for me.

Now I wonder;
- do you think about rpg\xb4s in these terms?
- do you work with similar goals in your design, and how do you do it?

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI'd love to see how you approach it.
    Through the game-methods I write (the "method" meaning the whole bandwidth of storyteller-and system-techniques in a game).

    My first game with this feature as a major part was Muu (1989), a traditional rpg with GM and players, but with some very original language- and dialogue-rules which helped the players establish a harmonious muuish\xb4ness. Central in this game is the GM\xb4s use of soft voice and poetic-naiive terms in her descriptions, and the continuous focus on and communication of the emotions of the characters. These elements works together with the rest of the game, inducing the players with the harmony of muu, and thus creating harmony in the players too.\n
    Posted By: Jason Morningstargames should, on some level, always be about creating joy in any of its possible facets.
    I see what you mean, but no. No-no-no! "Joy" is the dogma of "entertainment" in disguise. It is putting a clowns costume on an art-form which is equally suited to deal with both comedy and tragedy, and any dramatic genre between them. I believe the reason so many gamesmiths find it difficult to break out of the limited scope of the traditional rpg, is "the tyranny of entertainment" (they are scared brainless by the prospect of not entertaining the average gamer).

    Joy is ok, but not as the sole measure for success in a game. It is a very limited design-tool.

    Working with other human conditions than joy, it is obvious that you need to have other measures of success. And to work efficiently with such conditions, you need to engage more than you entertain. Focus on how you engage your players, and give them the tools to explore the human condition of your game.

    Games should always engage the players, and may enslave or empower them. Roleplaying games should transform the players into sacred vehicles for their characters.\n
    Posted By: Jason Morningstarif you engineer your procedures specifically to evoke an emotional state, how does that color the result?
    In a lot of ways. Look to Muu; the game creates a very strong fictional universe, and force the players to accept the ramificastions of this universe through the use of a strong method. As the players dive into the fictional universe, it will start to color them, and thus they find themselves looking upon their fictional surroundings with the eyes of a muu. The strangeness of being a muu in this way, evokes certain feelings in the players, and they share in these feelings; curiosity, frustration, joy, contentment, anxiety, relief, extasy ... all of these emotions may be experienced by a single muu during a game-session, and will be part of the player-experience of the game, and the player will communicate them all to the other players (a part of the game-method). Thus the game creates a strong emotional co-existence between the muu-characters of the players.

    The strong emotional co-existence of the muu\xb4s in the game, leaves the players with a strong sense of harmony when the game is over. It has the same effect as a successful meditation.

    Hope this answers some of your questions.

    \n As I said, Jason; I understood what you where aiming at. I chose to answer to the chosen word still, as I believe it is most often read as a simile with "entertainment". That one out of the way: all great games gives the players some reward for playing. My favorites are sense of wonder and self-discovery.

    All games are, by nature, manipulative; they depend on the players acting out fictional conflicts, engaging in them and using all possible means to win them, as if they where real. A lot of players will actually cheat to win such a fictional conflict.

    And a lot of games actually aims at creating certain emotional states in the players; "manipulating" them into feeling fear (CoC) or battle-frenzy (D&D) or paranoia (Paranoia) or what-not.

    To induce players with positive feelings is a bit original, I see, but I would believe it to be quite as harmless as inducing them with fear or anger. ;-)

    But then; 999 of 1000 people (or even more) do know the difference between fiction and reality, so they will stop short of cheating and will, however engaging the game is, continue to consider the conflict a fictional one. I have worked with games such as these since 1989, and have touched upon a lot of different emotions, without ever having to call for medical help or the police. ;-)

    I would say that a historically based game/scenario from, lets say; Poland in WWII, would be in greater danger of hitting on a sore spot of a player, and thus creating some undesired effect (crying, nervous breakdown, psychotic episode, etc.). ;-)

    No, I really do not find it very constructive to discuss possible dangers linked to the playing of dialogue based rpg\xb4s such as ours. There is no evidence that such a game has ever damaged anyone.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarIt seems like dangerous ground, and runs the risk of becoming manipulative.
    I seem to get myself into this discussion all the time. People respond to my ideas by talking of "dangers" and "manipulation".

    I\xb4ve pondered why, as I\xb4ve never considered myself manipulative, and have never seen any dangerous states being created in my games. Not a whiff of it, actually.

    This morning I finally solved the 4-piece puzzle of this conundrum;

    - 1 -
    I am talking about my design as a gamesmith. I am into highly conscious design, with "how a game affect the players" as a strong principle. I\xb4ve made a lot of game-experiments to hone my skills in this field, and have so far succeeded reasonably. So I am able to design games that really makes the players transport themselves into strong feelings and/or moods.

    - 2 -
    Emotions are emotional stuff, of course, so people read my posts on such subjects with a great deal of skepticism. And as long as I fail to underline that it is the players that creates the emotions, it is easy to read me as "manipulative".

    - 3 -
    At the same time I take it for granted that you all understand that my games, as well as other games, are tools by which the players make themselves happy, or sad, or entertained. The players transport themselves to the green fields of emotions, and explore them, using my game. I do not lead them there, and I do not steer them while they are there.

    - 4 -
    The players agree to play a game, for example my game Lykke, and when they agree to do so they also agree to reach for the goal of the game; some form of happiness. There is nothing "manipulative" in it, only an agreement to share in the creation of a certain experience.\n
    Posted By: MatthijsI don't feel comfortable having someone consciously try to make me sad or happy over the course of several hours - in game form
    You are making yourself happy in such a game, Matthijs. So I believe you may lay back and relax in your resistance towards others making you something. :-)

    I hope this clarifies some issues around such a design-practise. It is not some hazardous mumbo-jumbo, but a way to make games that people may use to create great experiences.

    So, I hope we can discuss this topic in a more constructive way now:

    - Do you work with player-moods/emotions as a design-goal, and how do you do it?

    - What kind of moods/emotions would you like to investigate in a game, and why?

    - Can you imagine, or have you seen, game-tools which are efficient in games aiming at moods/emotions?
