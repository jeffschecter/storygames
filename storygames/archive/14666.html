\n Since nobody else has started a thread yet, I am!

Friday early afternoon, Matt Gagan and I arrived in Seattle, then met up with Andy K., Remi Treuer, Jeremy Tidwell, Eric Boyd, and Matt SB. After some lounging and chatting at a cafe, we spontaneously busted out Dungeon World. We played schoolmates at something like an adventurer's college. We used all of the classes except for the wizard.

(Possible little spoiler for Purple Worm's Graveyard coming up!)

Remi played the evil halfling thief Bug, and I was the evil fighter with con 8 (so we bonded over the whole backstabbing approach). We were running theft and fencing cons at the college. Matt G. was the bard, and through the bard's bonds, he decided he was habitually mocking Bug. In fact, when Jeremy's ranger read the tracks inside the Purple Worm Graveyard dungeon and told us there were disfigured goblins shuffling around, Matt's character was like, "Just to get a good image of them, are they more or less disfigured than Bug?"

So when Bug found a trapped door that when closed would lock the heroes in a room and unleash monsters, he was like, "it's all clear, go ahead!" So the ranger, cleric and paladin went right in, but Bug stopped the bard, slammed the door shut, and jammed his dagger into her gut. "THAT'S FOR ALL THOSE SONGS!" Maybe there was minor resentment over the mockery.

Short game told even shorter, the three heroes that were locked in lost the paladin to the unleashed monsters (they didn't have a fighter to help them out), and I, sworn to protect Bug, had to run down the hightailing bard with my sinister mace because I couldn't leave any witnesses against Bug alive. Then Bug trapped the entrance to the dungeon and we went home, whistling joyfully.

THE END!

PS: Dungeon World is a lot of fun, even when you're not getting to slay the party bard! The bonds are awesome relationship starters.

    \nPlayed: Microscope, Innovation, Capes, Dungeon World, Lady Blackbird.

    Microscope: Jason, Sylvia, and I explored The Rise And Fall of a Puppet Empire, which started out kind of focused on this guy Wolfkiller who was unpuppetable, but once we realized that the early humans were feeding themselves via "krakening" it quickly became apparent that the kraken were more than just a foodstuff, and the puppet masters never quite as in control as they would like. Outcome: I seek out Ben and buy a copy of Microscope.

    Innovation: Outcome: Steven teaches me that it's hard to pick up all your arms with both teeth broken.

    Capes: Sgt. Maj. Bustem and Captain Taffy worked to thwart Urban Shaman and his father, Mousetrap, in their "bare essentials" plan to telepathically control the Boy Scouts to build and install devices in their parents cars that would result in a massive pileup of collisions in the retail district just loud and precise enough to shatter all the windows of the jewelry stores so their henchmen could walk in and liberate the jewels. Didn't actually thwart much, but Captain Taffy managed to prevent theft of the Crown Jewel by ... posing as the Crown Jewel. Outcome: Steven learns humility ... is for people who are not geniuses.

    Lady Blackbird: Played Kale, the ship's resident engineer kleptomaniac. Helped Lady B with a situation involving some lost keys and an important trunk. Had a discussion with Lady B's bodyguard about property rights. Outcome: I learn the merits of cuttlefish as a munition.

    Dungeon World: I played one game not run by Sage, one game run by Sage, and one game run by me. Loved all three. Played two utterly different clerics, one a salt-of-the-earth servant of the god of hard labor, the other a kindly, helpful, bookish, and deeply evil servant of Nyarlat'hotep (in retrospect this should have been Thoth, probably). In the game I ran, the characters set out to recover a jewel one of the two thieves had lost, and wound up disrupting some kind of archeological mining operation adjacent to a massive underground dungeon. Bjorn the cleric died standing in the doorway of the dungeon. The rest of the characters proceeded into the depths. Outcome: Even after the events of the opening battle, the other players are mystifyingly still convinced that Chris Bennet's character is on their side.

    \nPlayed: Fiasco, Dungeon World x 2, Danger Patrol Gamma, Regiment, The Believers, Seance, It Wasn't Me!, The Shab-al-Hiri Roach


    Highlights
    Getting to be a part of two tables playing my Dallas 1963 Fiasco playset on Friday evening. During the Tilt, both tables exchanged pieces of content and incorporated it into the second half of their games. In my JFK assassination research, I had not been aware until now that a pair of second-rate thieves had tried to rip off a reel-to-reel tape of the Beatles new record 'A Hard Day's Night' from the trainyard just beyond the Grassy Knoll. My character certainly deserved a better end than being handcuffed to Lee Harvey Oswald and being accidentally shot by Jack Ruby on live TV.

    Playing Dungeon World twice in two days. First time was GM'd by Tony Dowler using one of his cool microdungeons, and Sage (one of the designers) was at the table. Second time was by a relatively new GM to the ruleset. And as I had a crappy time running AW last year, this gave me a good vantage point on where the game design is now. Apocalypse World just didn't do it for me, and running OD&D for two years had led to plenty of hacks to smooth off the rough edges. I feel like Sage and co. had basically codified how I really wanted to run old-school dungeon crawls into a ruleset. Props!

    Finally playing Danger Patrol, which I had first seen at GPNW 2009. But this was Brendan's "I tried to fake John Harper into giving me the Gamma rules that actually don't exist so I had to hack them together in a day" ruleset. And I think we can safely say that his ersatz rules fucking ROCKED. I got to play Quan Li, Mystic Explorer. I felt like the game really riffed on the love that was Lady Blackbird, and extended it with smoother rules for skills and tags. I didn't see any evidence of XP and Keys (which aren't really necessary for a one-shot) so I'd love to see how the game would play out over several sessions. And I loved how Brendan took a throwaway scene of mine and turned it into the basis for the final battle with the Queen and her sticky-gross spider minions.

    The Regiment still needs some work, but I enjoyed the "WW2 never quite ended, it just dragged on for 40 years" premise and what John and Paul (and I'm sure George and Ringo) are doing to extend the AW ruleset. Some real promise here and I'd like to dig in more.

    The Believers (play-in-15-mins roleplaying poem) was quite weird. Part of the way through the arrival of the aliens, people were shooting at them and battles were going on. But when things calmed down, either the world was convinced that the aliens meant well, or (more likely) they were brainwashed. Three of the four players chose to go with the aliens.

    Susan played a great medium in Seance (another roleplaying poem of mine). I love how this game of friends and relatives communing with the dead always turns into internal quabbles. Much fun was had.

    It Wasn't Me! was our "GM-less murder mystery in about an hour". Turns out Lady Gaga was killed by a kid's all-day sucker in the throat after a bizarre trebuchet accident that was setup by her manager. Simply horrible.

    And how could I forget our now-yearly Roach game with an amazing group of players? Johnzo wrote a truly disturbing hack and took place at a Writers Convention full of aspiring authors desperate for a book deal. I will just say "Spongebob Squarepants G-string" and move on here...


    Amazing time. Is it next year yet???

    \n\nJuly 2011 \n

    \n Yay, GoPlayNW.

    Stuff I played, in order:

    Friday night, Tenra Bansho Zero. I signed up for this mostly because omg it's that guy from the Internet, and because I had heard the name of the game a million times due to it being mentioned around here. This was probably a mistake, in retrospect -- I did not have much fun playing this game. Or rather, I had fun playing the game, but very little play occured due to the large number of players -- something I contributed to by insisting on creating my own PC rather than share with Phil -- and the general demo-game approach Andy took towards the material. Also a whole lot of talking about things rather than demonstrating them.

    I did enjoy creating my 11-year old armour-pilot, a devoted sister of a powerful noble family who lived in her elder brother's shadow until he was killed (in the first scene, at my request) while under her protection. The entertaining random social mechanics meant that I fell in love-at-first-sight with a cocky, cynical mercenary armour-hunter, which given the characters' relative ages was more or less destined to be a terrible idea. And in general, I think we had a fairly cool collection of PCs, but getting through only one scene each made it difficult to take that anywhere interesting. The boss fight at the end was as bored as I got all weekend.

    Feedback to Andy, since I imagine he will be demoing this game a lot once it comes out: show, don't tell! Don't let 7 people into the game if you only want 4 PCs! More story scenes would have been better than a boring boss fight, though probably would not have fit in the time we had either. More positively, great job getting everyone using the fan mail, and the Zero Scenes were all lots of fun.

    Most of the Con, Grudge Mansion. This was Brendan's 'assassin-like' meta-con-game, my experience of which is detailed in this GoPlayNW Forum thread. Suffice to say that in the end I was murdered, but at least I made my murderer drop his banana chasing me down.

    Saturday morning, Silver & Light. I doubt Jackson could make a game I wouldn't enjoy playing, especially with him, Jonathan Walton & Tori Brewster at the table with me, but this game feels practically custom-made. Bored suburban teenagers? A nearly-existential mystery? Yearning for an impossible, beautiful escape? Narration-sharing mechanics that shift around the table whenever your characters touch? Hell, yes. (But it's still no Love Nothing, damnit.) There's definitely ways this could get even better, though, and I'm looking forward to playing future versions.

    We also decided to move a table outside to play this game -- I don't know what was going on with the AC, but it was extra-stuffy inside that morning. As we set up, I mentioned to my fellow-players that by that afternoon half the con would be out there with us -- which turned out to be unsurprisingly-prophetic.

    Saturday early-afternoon, The Quiet Year. I think there were three or four different groups running Joe McDaldno's British post-apocalypse game; we had to play in the main room to avoid Tony Dowler's murderous intentions towards yours-truly, which meant that we were mostly left to our own devices with the playtest rules & a deck of cards. Handling time was high as we got started, with some rules hard to find, but once we got going it was a lot of fun.

    Our Quiet Year was for a group of survivors who had moved into an abandoned high school in a mostly-overgrown gated community. We had fresh water in abundance, but lacked skilled labour, security, and mobility. My faction immediately began to catalogue the remains of the school's library in the hopes of starting up a rigorous training program, while other community members built a fence to protect us from the predatory animals that roamed the nearby forest. A lucky find of gas-station-remains in the forest solved our immobility problem before it got out of hand, fueling our dilapidated fleet of two school buses, but the other scarcity-driven threats proved much more difficult to address.

    Meanwhile, Kynin's faction displayed a somewhat questionable understanding of priorities, devoting significant resources to digging up the mass grave we found under the school fields in order to process the bodies into bonemeal to fertilize their farming expansion -- which earned several tokens worth of Contempt from what remained of the library faction. Scouts located a nearby sporting goods store, which was promptly raided for just enough guns and ammo to cause trouble.

    A fellow-player: "Okay, so I see the opportunity here, but what's the risk?"
    Me: "What's the risk? They're guns, that's the risk."

    In the end it was only the noble sacrifice of two of my faction's three leaders that secured the community when the forest wolves, pushed to near starvation by the lack of prey and our well-built fence, stormed the school. Only Bartholomew (?), the architect of the library-restoration, survived to carry forward our mission.

    Longer-term, the lack of skilled labour -- specifically, medical expertise -- continued to undermine the community's best efforts at reconstruction. There was a breakthrough, however, due to the arrival of a straggling group of refugees at our gates -- which prompted the one and only Discussion among our community leaders. Despite some major difficulties integrating the refugees into our society, we eventually managed to built an infirmary for the doctor, but not before at least one community leader lost their life after a wound from a farming accident was left untreated.

    Another faction lost several leaders as the result of a cult that sprung up in the now-safe forest, related to some mysterious lights that appeared only at night. The cult, largely left unaddressed, became increasingly meddlesome, sabotaging several projects and plotting to steal the school buses. Unfortunately, our game was cut short by time just before the advent of Fall, which would have surely brought even more troubles and opportunities to our doorstep. Which was a shame, since I had only just begun efforts to recruit the (apparently quite sexy) doctor to my faction to replace their decimated leadership.

    Saturday later-afternoon, Ribbon Drive. I must facilitate this game every con, and so it was again, with myself, Joe, Morgan, Jamie & Wilson setting up outdoors with the assistance of a very long extension cord -- this required a death-defying dash through The Room Where I Could Be Killed in order to plug it in, but thankfully Mr. Dowler was nowhere to be seen. I distributed extra mixes for those who had not had a chance to prepare, and in the end it was Shore Leave that was chosen.

    Our road trip turned out to be four Navy men on the way to a wedding -- the groom being one of them, and his gay lover being another -- joined by a childhood friend of the groom who had come to pick them up and who had also been meanwhile sleeping with the bride. As always, the game delivered, though much credit also to my fellow-players -- Morgan's character, Billy, was the highlight for me. Never before has a trait about doing push-ups been put to such effective use, and his cog-with-no-machine putdown of my outsider, Francis, had the harsh ring of truth about it. Jamie's uniform-wielding stonewalling of the bouncer at some middle-of-nowhere strip club -- "I'm going to have to ask you to apologize" -- was great.

    Unfortunately, the shorter time slot proved to be a bit of a hindrance -- this game definitely would have shone a little bit brighter with more room to breathe, and we had to rush towards the wedding at a somewhat breakneck speed. The final showdown/shouting match at the family restaurant, with Jamie's poor seaman trapped between two separate arguments, was a nice wrap-up, though in the end no clear protagonist emerged, and we rounded the session off with a wedding-day montage.

    Saturday evening (off-site), Bacchanal. After some daytime plotting, a group of us withdrew to Tori's house to drink wine and play Bacchanal, Paul Czege's game of debauchery & sexually-explicit narration. It was interesting, and made me somewhat less uncomfortable than I had expected -- though still a little uncomfortable, ifyouknowwhatImean.

    Sunday morning, Sleep In So I Can Drive Home To Vancouver Tonight Without Killing My Passengers. By far the smoothest game of the weekend for me, thanks to my extra-drowsy anti-histamine/cat pills.

    Sunday afternoon, Don't Rest Your Head. I was feeling pretty cliquey by the time I arrived at Sunday afternoon without having participated in a single Game Donut, so I hied me hence and, due to a lack of other facilitators, ended up stepping forward to run DRYH for a group of enthusiastic players.

    This session was simultaneously a lot of fun and, in retrospect, the source of some regret. I love to GM the game, but my approach to doing so in a con environment might leave something to be desired -- we had probably-one-too-many PCs and between that and my failing to encourage character-crossover, the result was several very fun (for me at least) but also very disconnected scenes. In the end only half the players got to move their story very far past those first few moments of 'WTF is going on?' and into the part of the game where they start getting to be a little more proactive about their troubles. That said, I got to make use of my collection of printed-out character portraits, and even used up one of my random-DRYH-cue index cards: A card game played in the back of a city bus.
