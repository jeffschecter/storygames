Veins of Gold (Or 'What is your thing as a designer")

Iso
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
A couple or many things have been building up in my mind. First, there is this thread that Jonathan started. Then there is Victor's post over on his blog about a 'culture of criticism.' Then there are a swath of connections too difficult to disentangle but include in no particular order: Vincent's talk of designing in cells, big questions coming up about the goals of indie game community/communities, the discussion of what 'your thing' is as a player... Enough background, though. Questions:

Anyone ever looked at Julia Cameron's Vein of Gold? It is all about helping creators (especially, but not only, creative writers) figure out what themes are most powerful for them, on the premise that working with themes you find powerful and moving makes it a lot easier to write.

Anyone ever thought about doing something like that to get your game design juices flowing? Even in small doses, her ideas are suggestive.

Anyone notice certain designers with a vein of gold? If you are a designer, do you feel like you have a 'vein of gold?'

This is the part that hooks back up to the thread/post mentioned above. It strikes me that seeking out the 'vein of gold' in a work is a very positive and powerful way to sharpen game review practice, something that already seems to be at work in a lot of Forge-driven design praxis (power 19, big three, etc.). Jonathan opened up his thread mentioning he would like to learn from Jason's historical sense and it hit me. If I were to suggest a vein of gold for Jason, it would be somewhere in that mixture of historical setting, risk, and constraint. Now that's simplistic, but I bet there is something to that which could be meaningfully developed.

This is, yes, very author-centric and there are reasons why you don't want to get stuck in that mode of approaching a product, but it always strikes me as a good place to visit, to look around.

    I totally know what this is for me. When I'm making a role-playing game, I frequently have trouble with making very alien settings and then just letting it get too out of hand. I've found that I am most likely to complete an RPG if I have an allegorical backbone that is personally and emotionally relevant to me. This even translates into my board games.

    When I'm making a board game, I'm remembering when I was very young, living with my mom in not-a-good-place in Queens, NY. Back then, I would constantly make my own toys by recycling whatever loose scraps I could find around the house. Socks became dolls, cereal boxes became buildings, yada yada. Today, that frugal nature is what spurs my desire to create games that use the fewest, cheapest, most ubiquitous components possible and to recontextualize non-gameish components. It's entirely by accident that this also sometimes leads to an elegant abstract game.

    Human psychology is something that comes up again and again in my games. Also, walking the line between player and character and using the real emotional, visceral experiences that the people playing a game have as the meat of the game itself, without falling into total bleed-over. The themes I'm drawn to are about self-discovery and subversion: about gender and romance with Breaking the Ice, about character and competition in Shooting the Moon, and about identity and assimilation in Sign in Stranger. The next slot of games I've got coming down the pike just get nastier and more dangerous: sex, infidelity, polyamory and core insecurities in Under My Skin, and slavery and revolution in Rise Up.

    Player collaboration, even in competitive venues, is central to design for me, and I'm right there with Jonathan Walton and Meg about decentralizing game play. Another aspect of design that sings for me is utilizing the players' unconscious in the process: the color association in Breaking the Ice and the mad-libs world development system in Sign in Stranger are all about that. Play can be richer than we might consciously let ourselves have it be if we tap into our subconscious via free-association and spring-board creativity. I personally find role playing challenging yet most fulfilling when you have to come up with stuff off the fly, so I try to design to make that easy, like falling off a log to come up with interesting stuff. And having all the players work together and have their ideas interlock makes it possible for everyone to get to feel like a rockstar, by getting further insight and improvisation happen off of what they came up with.

    If I hit a tenth of that in my games, I'll feel like a lucky girl.

    I don't know if it is my "gold vein" or not, but my foci in design right now are meeting the social needs and constraints of people like my wife and myself.

    This includes but is not limited to:
    * Easily available random generators (six-sided dice, playing cards) - though this is shifting as my wife buys more and more polyhedrals for herself.
    * Minimal prep time for everyone. My time outside of the game is limited, so if I'm running it, it needs to be easy to prep. Similarly, my wife is unlikely to read a game book cover-to-cover and even less likely to want to mini-max details, so char gen needs to be quick and intuitive, and to a lesser extent balanced.
    * Quickly understood settings - or settings that can quickly crafted/adjusted by the game group. See the reading game books thing above. The game that has worked best for our current group has been Prime Time Adventures (with a close second of Adventure!). With PTA we literally sat down and said it's a show about college-age superheroes and we are basing them on heroes everyone knows rather than forcing everyone to learn a new mythology.

    Typing this up, I guess my current thing is "audience". Or more specifically, designing for a specific audience.

    September 2006
    Ah! Duh. My vein of gold is the distribution of creative authority. I've democratized narration! Sweet.

    To date, my games have been about dismantling any notion that groups of human beings should invest in one-and-only-one of their group with the arbitrary power of determinig the entire group's entertainment. I can't easily imagine me publishing a good game with such assumptions.

    So, how can I make this work for me? It makes me realize that my strength as a designer is creating systems that use currency, techniques and other controls that empower everyone at the table. Cool. When I design a system that does not do that, I'd better self-examine to make sure it's "right."

    And, this is also helpful in shaping up some marketing approaches. For example, I could pitch something like "Chimera Creative Games: What Everyone Says Matters." Ok, that's terrible, but you get the idea. It actually helps found a baseline for figuring out how to shape pitches to con-goers, create ads and messages, talk about it in forums, and so on.
