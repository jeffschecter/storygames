Agora - playtest and review

Caledonian
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
Having been a Sid Meier's Alpha Centauri fan for many years, I was very excited when a friend suggested that we playtest Agora. The thematic material in the game was very interesting, if just a little unpolished because it's still being worked out, and I couldn't wait to try a game where my characters' philosophical positions change as a consequence of play.

So we ran through a Descent, and experienced the system directly. I played a Green Uprising Ecological Engineer who had hijacked the entertainment/arcology section of a dying spacecraft and needed to somehow bring it down to the surface of the planet with its life intact. I was opposed by a decaying orbit, a massive atmospheric storm, and the possibility that it would become necessary to sacrifice some of my subordinates in the process of survival. My resources were optimism, creative design, a nanoforge, and a fusion core; my lieutenants were the Fabricator of Dreams and an engineering Apprentice, with a handful of followers. Due to a few lucky rolls, the Descent went very rapidly, although some of the contested rolls took a long time to resolve.

Agora's mechanics seem to have two major goals: to provide a story-generation system, and to provide an entertaining game system. Unfortunately, I don't think it fulfills either of these goals.

The rules don't lend themselves to interesting gameplay. If one side happens to roll well, as happened in my Descent when I rolled 2, 1, 2, there's little the other side can do to turn this around. As the lucky roller, I had no reason to reroll these numbers, and my opponent was left futilely trying for an Epiphany or two. Even in later rolls, there were really very few tactical or strategic options, and it was extremely difficult to perceive any meaning in the choices I made. The extreme parsity of options at all points didn't lend itself to thoughtful or interesting gameplay at all.

As a story-generation system, the rules are much more complex than the nature of the results they produce. Mechanically, they determine which of two competing sides gets to decide what happens, and as a side benefit whether specific resources (whose properties are otherwise undefined) can be included in the narrative. Except for Epiphanies, which determine when faction philosophies must be changed, there's little that couldn't be accomplished by flipping a coin. The rules don't seem to inform or be informed by the narrative at all. Obviously the quality of any player-generated story is going to depend heavily on the players, but this system did little more than identify who got to determine how part of the story turned out, and everything about the story had to be determined by the players' own imaginations. The narrative is an overlay placed atop the rule system but not really connected to it, and we really don't need a dice-rolling mechanic to exercise our imaginations in communal storytelling. The nature of our resources could be flexible or limited, specialized or universally-applicable, and their mechanical representations would behave exactly the same way. The process of resolution takes far too much time and effort for the quality of resolution it provides.

In short, I like the idea of the game very much. I believe the mechanics need to be almost completely reworked or scrapped altogether, though. I would be very interested in hearing from anyone who has tried the game and has suggestions for productive change.

    Mendel, if you have two kinds of dice and only one Position to invest them in, you only use one kind of dice. The others are lost.

    When one side gets lucky, then the other sides just have to keep rolling hoping that they will get equally or even more lucky, all the while their chances of doing so are diminishing.

    This is actually by design -- you're overlooking an option, though. When one side gets lucky, the other side has to choose whether to push things by continually rolling (and risking) dice in the hopes of getting lucky, or they can surrender the stakes, take their +1 die to an ideal, and try for an epiphany for later. A great deal of the game revolves around "How much do I want those stakes?" and pulling out of a conflict when it's no longer worth staying in it.

    In Alpha Centauri, sometimes you get into wars with the other factions, and you go in thinking you can win or take X number of cities or whatever, but as time goes on and you're losing units, that becomes more and more unlikely, and at some point you've just got to call the other faction leader and propose a peace treaty. That's what I'm going for here.

    Sometimes pulling out is your best option. Pulling out rewards you with an ideal die (which never goes away, unlike resource dice) and if you lost a lot of resources to burnout, probably an epiphany that can help you whomp on them in a later scene.

    That make any sense? Players will pull out of conflicts, especially against Obstacles that rolled high for their die pools, all the time. It's not a crushing defeat, it's just a setback, and one that gives them some ammunition for the rematch.

    When you're in a conflict, there are two things you can do:
    * roll in new dice from a trait (and bringing this trait into your narration)
    * reroll some dice on your side

    The first does seem to inform the story fine, more or less as in Dogs, where a raise is informed by the resources you've just called in, if any.

    The second, however, has no fictional component to it; it's merely something you do.

    Another important thing is that initial success on your dice is never reduced by simply staying in the conflict, but only by great success on the part of the opposition, and a enough success can buffer against the success of the opposition. (Contrast with Dogs: I can make a big raise that costs you a lot of dice, but after that, we've both used up some resources, and I can't just sit on the result I'd gotten.)

    Mendel --
    Lemme reframe a bit. Instead of "What are you willing to gamble to get your way?" think of it as "Is this thing that I wanted worth gambling the people and culture that I lead and protect?" To cast it in a modern light, is spreading democracy to Iraq worth gambling the safety of American troops or the prosperity of the oil-addicted populace? Every single decision in this game is a value judgement -- is this more important than that, is this worth that, if I sacrifice this what am I left with?

    Caledonian --
    Two of your comments, "It really doesn't matter what names I gave to my groups of dice" and "a fight that no one can afford to lose" tell me a lot about your game. It sounds like you set up some very black-and-white stakes, one of which you needed and one of which you couldn't bear. Since you absolutely had to win, all of the resources on your sheet became equal, not just in terms of power to win the conflict, but in terms of what they meant to you as a player. Since you absolutely had to win, it didn't matter what your dice were assigned to, because their only value was to help you win the conflict that you couldn't afford to lose.

    Assuming Mendel correctly characterized your experience, it's no wonder that the individual decisions that you made did not seem to matter or engage in the fiction. They didn't. All that mattered was throwing dice at your opposition so you didn't have to take the intolerable counterstakes.

    (Also, did you name your lieutenants, or simply give them positions? Once you name people, they tend to develop personalities that you care about, instead of remaining warm bodies in hierarchical positions there to do your bidding. I should probably make sure the rules text directs players to give their lieutenants names rather than just positions.)

    Now, if you wanted to give the game another try (which you very well may not, and I'd certainly understand), I'd suggest you very carefully select stakes, not so much in terms of what stuff your guy needs to acquire in order to survive and thrive, but the means by which you the player would like to see your character survive and thrive. Counterstakes then would shuttle him in a different direction -- not that he gets shut down and fails to acquire the resources he wanted, but that he gets shunted into a different situation that still affords him some interesting, if different, options.

    As an example: your eco-engineer is on the surface of the planet with his terrarium of plant and animal life. A group of refugees comes to seek shelter. Your stakes might be that you bring them into the arcology and teach them how to live in balance with the ecosystem within; the counterstakes could be that they can't hack living in balance, and settle outside surrounding the arcology, still part of your group but not within the arcology like your original followers. Since you're not dealing with life-or-death stakes, the situation that evolves becomes far more nuanced, allows the question of whether risking a lieutenant's patience and skill is worth educating some ignorant refugees. At that point, you're addressing "how shall we live?" rather than "will we live?"

    That make any sense?
