\n Hey friends,

Well, I just backed Dungeon World for my first "Kickstarter" ever! My gaming friends and I played a bit with the ideas from both Dungeon World and World of Dungeons, and all this rpg'ing got me thinking...

Having lurked in the shadows behind the whole "world" phenomena, I want to know a couple things about the dice mechanic where things are split into:

6: No success
7-9: Partial success
10+: Full success

I would love to hack around with this idea, maybe even modify it to help me fight the nasty impasse in design I've fallen into, but again, I need to know a couple things before I toy around!!!

a.) Is there a formal name for this mechanic?
b.) Who was the first person (people) to use it/what is its origin (I want to give someone credit if I end up doing something with it!!!)

Basically, we've already hacked a bit, but I want to create something a bit more formal and freely available. (something where I can put my burgeoning design skills to use so everyone can have fun with the ideas floating around my brain).

    \n To expand on Brett's suggestion, Apocalypse World contains a breakdown of this die mechanic and the use of moves, and gives good advice about how to write new ones. Rafu's recommendation of Joe's Simple World is also an excellent starting point for hacking this mechanic.

    Here is a short essay of little or no importance on the subject of ancient history:

    The basic 2d6 pass/partial/fail mechanic has been present in role-playing games since the very beginning, appearing in the original Dungeons & Dragons, book 1, on page 12. It appears as follows;
    2 Attacks immediately
    3-5 Hostile reaction
    6-8 Uncertain, make another offer, roll again
    9-11 Accepts offer, friendly
    12 Enthusiastic, volunteers help

    As you can see, it is slightly more complicated than even 6-/7-9/10-11/12+, but the principle is the same. This reaction table was repeated in the Holmes edition of D&D (1977, page 11), the Moldvay edition of Basic D&D (1981, page B21), and the Mentzer Red Box edition of Basic D&D (1983, Dungeon Master's Rulebook page 20).

    When it appeared in the Rules Cyclopedia (1991, page 93), it had changed slightly:
    2-3 Monster attacks
    4-6 Monster is aggressive
    7-9 Monster is cautious
    10-11 Monster is neutral
    12 Monster is friendly

    This reaction roll table did NOT make its way into Advanced Dungeons & Dragons, but the die mechanic did, in the form of the 7th-level spell "Vision." Vision is an Illusionist spell in 1st ed AD&D (1978, Players Handbook page 100) and a Magic-user spell in 2e (1989, Players Handbook, page 187). This spell has almost the same effect as the Apocalypse World move "When you open your brain to the psychic maelstrom," is divided into 6 or less, 7-9, and 10-12 results, and includes provisions for getting a bonus of up to +3 (just like stats in AW or DW).

    As John Harper noted, the 2d6+modifier vs target number is also quite old, but Traveller (1977) is entirely a pass/fail system. There is no middle result.

    Apocalypse World is probably (?) the first rpg to use this die mechanic for basic task resolution, instead of a peripheral mechanic like it is in D&D. The similarities between AW and D&D are coincidental, and to me it looks more like a compression of the kinds of results generated by Otherkind Dice, which are more interesting than a typical pass/fail system.

    An old thread about Otherkind Dice vs. AW Moves can be found here.

    \n (The Coda-system RPGs from Decipher\u2014Star Trek and Lord of the Rings, early 2000s\u2014use exploding 2d6 + stat versus a target number, with stepped degrees of success, as base task resolution. Every 5 points above the target number, you get a better success. It's more of a [fail/pass/excel/excel better] mechanism than AW's three-degree scale, but I mention it for completeness and because the math in those games has influenced a fair amount of my play going forward.)

    \n Good point. The new World of Darkness (nWoD) uses a substantially different dice mechanic but arranges its results into four categories of greater failure, regular failure, regular success, and greater success (generally), based on fixed thresholds, with an emphasis on regular success, statistically. (I was the developer of nWoD Vampire and other books for a few years.)

    AW's different dice, probabilities, and emphasis on the mixed 7-9 result in notably different, but not incomparable, dynamics, in my opinion. Even these subtle distinctions in dice, category definitions, and explicit versus implicit consequential implications make for big differences in emergent play, inspired hacking, in-play interpretations, and so forth. As I imagine is obvious, I'm just saying.

    \n The other game in this history worth noting is Talislanta (1984). It used a d20 + modifiers vs. target number, based on a table which resulted in:
    - Mishap
    - Failure
    - Partial Success
    - Full Success
    - Critical Success

    I mention it because the system is where I first encountered tiered success like that and because Vincent is a big Tal fan from way back, and credits it in the Apocalypse World text.
