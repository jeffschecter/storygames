    Okay, let's do mine again...

    Phillip K. Dick. I'd recommend pretty much everything he's written because all of it is excellent. Here's what I like from the first couple of pages of Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?. Dick opens up with thie interplay about chemical emotion control. The ability to choose your mood and then have a system adjust your brain chemistry to feel that mood. (There is, hilariously, a setting that puts you in the mood to want to use the machine.) What's great about this? Dick takes this awesome idea... and never uses it again for the rest of the book. I've seen people take entire novels discussing stuff this rich, and Dick just throws it in as background. It's just part of how the world works. This sort of extremely-awesome-yet-undeveloped stuff shows up throughout his work and it gives a real sense of 'This isn't the world we live in'.

    Spider Robinson. Callahan's Crosstime Saloon is a short story collection. Perhaps my favorite short story is about an assassin who wears bright purple sneakers. Everyone notices his sneakers, no one remembers what he looks like (except that he had bright purple sneakers). Good stuff. He also has this great short story on time travel that made me think of time travel itself in new ways.

    Frank Herbert. Dune just had such a cool world. It's feudal space empires, and full of potential. I think one of my favorite bits is that due to the terrible wars with machines in the ancient past, no one builds computers anymore. Instead they have an entire class of specialists who are trained to be human computers. It's just cool.

    Robert Heinlein. I like a lot of his stuff, but Stranger in a Strange Land is interesting because it puts all of Heinlein's other stuff in perspective (or it did for me). It's basically a Messianic story, and the perfect religion that Heinlein envisions provides interesting context for the rest of his stuff.

    Most of the ones I was going to mention are on Brand's list (not surprisingly). Though I agree about the "not necessarily good" disclaimer (and personally I can't abide Gene Wolf - many other people's mileage evidently varies).

    I would recommend almost any Terry Pratchett except The Colour of Magic, which was his first, most parodic, and (IMO) least good by quite a lot. You could do a lot worse than start with Guards, Guards! (the first of the Guards sub-series), Wyrd Sisters (effectively the first of the Witches sub-series, though one of the characters appears in an earlier book), or Mort (the first of the Death sub-series, though Death appears in all the other books at one time or another).
    I like them because they're humourous without being lame or contrived (almost all of the time), but at the exact same time as the humour the best ones have very thought-provoking themes about power and responsiblity and what it means to be human and how imperfect people who really care about doing the right thing can change the world for the better.

    And for all the same reasons I recommend Lois McMaster Bujold's Vorkosigan series, starting with Cordelia's Honour. The scene in which she half-drowns her social worker in the fish tank is alone worth the price of admission.

    For something both less serious and less humourous, but still with some of each, try Julian May's Galactic Milieu series, starting with Intervention. Epic scope! Likeable aliens! Kewl psychic powerz! Dynastic politics affecting the whole galaxy! And so forth.

    Sherri Tepper's stuff is good if you like a bit of Gaia with your SF. I tend to enjoy her earliest books the most (The True Game series, for example); recently she's got a bit preachy. The Gate to Women's Country is very thought-provoking on gender politics, and Raising the Stones is one of the best pieces of SF I have read that deals with religion and how it can go wrong.

    Roger Zelazny's Amber is phantasmagoric and colourful and amusing.

    C.S. Lewis's Space Trilogy is philosophical and phantasmagoric.

    G.K. Chesterton's The Man Who Was Thursday is really strange and extremely thought-provoking about anarchism, faith and "what is reality?".

    And Connie Willis's stuff (any of it) is haunting, moving, and manages to display the fruits of massive research without giving the reader pages of "idiot lecture". To Say Nothing of the Dog, which I think is one of her latest, is also a hilarious romp, like a P.G. Wodehouse novel written by J.B. Priestley.

    Basically, whenever this topic comes up, I've got to throw up horns for John Brunner's more serious work (especially his 4 distopic novels, and especially The Shockwave Rider) and then launch into panting fervor for Ian McDonald.

    Graham, you may actually be able to find McDonald in the UK. He's completely dropped off of mainstream shelves here. I'd strongly recommend his most recent River of Gods, actually. And I've got a special fondness for Out on Blue Six and Desolation Road. McDonald is unique in my experience as writing actually very sophisticated prose in decidedly science fiction novels. His novels tend to throwaway a lot of novel (and established) science fiction tropes as mere background. His early work does magical pastiches of favorite scenes from the canon. He's progressively more in touch with modern politics, starting with an almost aggressive consciousness of religious and political strife in Ireland, but his more recent work has treated 1st world/3rd world issues, starting with Terminal Cafe. Most importantly, where the bulk of science fiction arrives by presenting a neat new idea and playing with it, McDonald takes those concepts and looks at what they'll mean to the characters in his novels; the ideas support that characters, rather than the other way around.

    cracks knuckles, okay here we go.

    Camp Concentration, by Thomas M. Disch -- Disch is an under-appreciated author, and I read this one for class a couple years back. Inmates given a variant disease that makes them into geniouses, then kills them. Very literary, very interesting, commentary/parallels to Tuskeegee, concentration camps, cold war.

    Light, by M. John Harrison -- Harrison is a well-respected veteran of the New Wave SF era, now associating himself with the New Weird (see China Mieville and Jeff VanderMeer, among others). This book is set in three frames, with intriguing characters, smart investigations of the implication of technology, and a great way of tying all the characters together. Be forewarned, one of the characters is a serial killer, however.

    I have to second reccomendations for A Canticle For Liebowitz, Stranger in a Strange Land, and Neuromancer.

    In addition to Neuromancer, I really liked Gibson's Pattern Recognition. Less over-the-top SF-ish, it's very polished and a good indicator of what SF is doing these days, more with less.

    Night Watch, by Sergei Lukyanenko -- The first book in the trilogy which has spawned the all-time-highest-selling Russian film trilogy. Contemporary Russia with 'Others,' supernaturally-powered humans who fall into 'Light' and 'Dark' camps, and have established 'Watches' to police one another. Palpatably different than British or American SF.

    Air, by Geoff Ryman -- A very solid and personal investigation of the effects of the proliferation of high-speed electronic communication seen through the life and times of a woman in a tiny village in the fictional nation of Karzistan.

    Lies of Locke Lamora, by Scott Lynch -- A first-novel offering from a former RPG.net member, this is a fantasy-style (though technically SF) Ocean's Eleven/Robin Hood. Great pacing, plot twists, and a marvelously realized world.

    Blood Music, by Greg Bear -- The definitive nanotechnology novel, IHMO. It's a bit dated, but it stands up really well. Highly reccomended as foundational for writers who came after.

    Accelerando, by Charles Stross -- This is actually a collection of short stories, all based on/influenced by Vernor Vinge's idea of the 'Technological Singularity.' Stross has the texts of all of those stories avaliable online for free.

    Dawn, by Octavia Butler -- The first in her Xenogenesis series, this follows a black woman saved from an earthly apocalypse by an alien species that wants to mix their genome with humanity to learn and advance. The best alien contact narrative I've read, with good attention to gender, sexuality, and race. Her short story collection 'Bloodchild and other stories' is also highly reccomended, especially "Speech Sounds"

    Stories of Your Life and other stories, by Ted Chaing -- This short story collection comes from Hugo-winning Chiang, who is brilliant byt not very prolific. The stories are some of the best of their type, the kind of science fiction that takes real questions about science, logically extrapolates, and then investigates the social and personal implications. If nothing else, read "Liking What You See: A Documentary"

    I like the typical authors on the lists. I see little mention of Clarke, which I find odd. He's great. But I put one on the list above the others, and that's Assimov.

    I love reading his work and knowing as I'm getting on in it that he's going to do it again to me. That is, he's going to turn the tables on me. Not capriciously. If I were some sort of mega-genius, maybe I could figure out what's coming. But it's not even so much the challenge of figuring out what's going to happen. But understanding that, when it does, my head will be twisted around 180 degrees, and I'll start to see the whole universe from a different perspective.

    The fecundity with which this happens with his work, especially given the volume of his work, is amazing to me. I leave almost every one of his books not being able to look at the world the same way again. It's not neccessarily that the new perspective is informative...just that I now have to think again about everything I come across.

    The moment that this happens in reading his work is...heady. That's the best word I can think of. I actually get sorta dizzy as the brain chemicals start scrambling for cover, knowing that they're going to suddenly be working like little madmen for at least a day.

    Interestingly, the majority of his 400 plus books are non-fiction. And what's also amazing is how his non-fiction can turn your head about in the same way.

    For a lesser known Assimov fiction title, try "The Gods Themselves"


    Oh, and one more note, I like to recommend a short story by Harlan Ellison called "I Have No Mouth, But I Must Scream." If you get a chance to read it, I'd love to hear your thoughts.

    A little surprised that nobody's mentioned Margaret Atwood. The Blind Assassin is the only book I found as heavy as BotNS, figuratively and literally. I won't even link you to the blog entry where I talked about that one, because it's embarrassing to see how much it affected me. It's a book within a book within a book; the outermost is a letter an old woman is writing to her estranged granddaughter, the middle is her autobiography, and the innermost is a pure pulp sci-fi novella. I usually cite Ender's Game as the book that had the most influence on me in adolescence, but Assassin is the most powerful book I've read as an adult.

    For something a little less weighty (if no lighter in tone), the best sci-fi book I've read this year is Richard Paul Russo's Ship of Fools. The titular ship is a colony vessel, built around a huge cathedral, and it's been voyaging so long that it no longer remembers its destination; when it finds a habitable planet and another colony ship abandoned nearby, the deformed advisor Bartolomeo must play careful politics with the ship's aristocracy to keep their whole society from collapsing. The book really deserves to be an RPG.

    Well the Chiang book is a bunch of short stories that address pretty different things. A thread common to many is taking contemporary scientific ideas and extrapolating or twisting them in some way - stories deal with prosopagnosia, intelligence and syntax, and theres another dealing with the whole enterprise of mathmatics. The reason I bring this up is that often I would hate this kind of writing.

    I'm a scientist by trade but not a 'science fan', if you get what I mean by that, so I don't get geeked-out on e.g. hearing how FTL space travel is possible if you assume x and y and z. Frankly, I find science for science's sake pretty dry! I also get bugged when writing has a veneer of scientific seriousness and then bungles it - give me dilythium crystals and mekon deathrays rather than waste my time on that crap.

    But Chiang gets it just right. I know my neuropsychological science, and "Liking What You See: A Documentary" gets it just so right, and explains it succintly and cleanly. Atop the scientific foundation, it builds human stories that are as thought provoking as sci-fi should be (would I choose to screen human beauty out of my consciousness?) but he manages to ground it all in unexceptional but real people, resisting both talking-head everyman figures and "christ, I'm quirky" characters.       Nobody has mentioned Kim Stanley Robinson's Mars trilogy (Red Mars, Green Mars, Blue Mars) but it's worth reading attentively. It's postmodern hard sf, epic in scope and utopian in vision but intriguingly reflexive and ironically self-referential (meaning that it's full of big ideas, but it doesn't take itself too seriously as fiction). I just finished re-reading the 3rd volume, which I'd thought was the weakest; I was surprised at how moved I was by the end of the story.

      In brief: In Red Mars, 100 colonists arrive on Mars as part of an international science team and begin to explore and exploit the new landscape, coming into conflict with each other and eventually with other new arrivals and the powers-that-be back on Earth. Political tension over the exploitation of Mars escalates and becomes violent, ending with the bloody suppression of the dissenters. In Green Mars, the survivors regroup and begin the process again, succeeding eventually in liberating Mars. In Blue Mars, an independent Mars is the cornerstone and showcase of a vast expansion of humanity throughout the Solar System.

      I've added this to the Wiki as well:

      Salt, by Adam Roberts.

      By coincidence, I read this book just after a friend had shown me Tillich's discussion of three kinds of morality in his History of Christian Thought: heteronomy (the law of the other), autonomy (the law of the self) and theonomy (the law of God). Although both groups in this novel, the anarchist Alsists and the military dictatorship of Senaar, consider themselves, in some sense, ruled by God, their different flawed conceptions of God mean that they are actually autonomists and heteronomists, respectively.

      The Senaarians can't be themselves, they can only be constituted by their society; but the Alsists hardly have a society, because nobody takes responsibility for anyone but themselves. In neither case do they have much in the way of compassion. The Senaarian dictator, for example, when talking about immigration from another city where there has been internal strife, assumes that the immigrants will have to be self-supporting economically; "there would be no point in them coming here to starve in the streets." Charity never crosses his mind. Economics (not free market economics, exactly, but something developed from many of its premises) governs the Senaarians as much as their militarism, and in this way they have a shadow side which is like the self-centred Alsists. The Alsists, on the other hand, work if they want to, but when asked, "What if someone never wants to?" - i.e., what happens to "free riders" - the Alsist narrator says that eventually their friends will get sick of it and beat them up. In other words, the enforcement of social conformity by the threat of violence which the Senaarians use is the shadow side of the Alsists.

      Inevitably, therefore, they have a hideous and unnecessary war over their irreconcilable misunderstandings of each other, which each side justifies to itself in its own terms, and everything they can't admit to in their own society is projected onto the other.

      The great tragedy is that the story is so realistic.
