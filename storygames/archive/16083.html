\n I guess due to the opening of John Carter we are obliged to have at least one "sword and planet" thread, so this will have to do.

First things first: I love this sub-genre. I have all the Barsoom novels and Burroughs' Venus novels, as well. And I have enjoyed other authors' forays into sword and planet territory. Big stories, dashing heroes, high stakes, beautiful damsels... all good.

Clearly I'm not alone among folks in the roleplaying community in adoring this stuff, as there have been numerous attempts to make gaming in the sword and planet sub-genre a reality. But one thing I notice, from the brilliant little mini-game Iron Lords of Jupiter to the Savage Worlds-powered Mars, is that we have a lot of games that give us tools for mechanical action, but don't address the story end of things. To me that's the difference between "traditional" and "story" games: in the case of the latter the rules are as much concerned with the tale being told as they are with how actions are accomplished. You may disagree.

Anyway, do we have a story game that will render up the Burroughsian sword and planet action I so adore? No dedicated game appears to have been written for it, but surely there's something that can be bent to this purpose. I have a feeling someone's going to say Spirit of the Century, though I know very little about it, and from my own perspective it seems like it might be possible to do something interesting with In a Wicked Age..., but what say you? And if you have a suggestion, please tell us how it would be used, as the implementation might not be immediately obvious to everyone. I'm willing to elaborate on my IAWA idea if asked.

    Posted By: Sam!Anyway, it looks like we have two systems that are recommended more than the rest. We have multiple recommendations forSolar System, followed byOn Mighty Thews. Both are very reasonably priced, so picking them up won't be a hardship.
    \nKnow that you can also read the Solar System online here.

    Since the two games are very different - but this may not be blatantly apparent - pick the Solar System if you want:\n
      \n
    • continuous "campaign" play, probably over a handful or a dozen of sessions
      • \n
    • "weaveplay", that is, player characters involved in separate but interconnecting plots, and the focus moving around the table
      • \n
    • the ability to accommodate 4 or more players, plus a Story Guide (but you can play a satisfactory game with as few as 2+1)
      • \n
    • to make use of a detailed world/campaign setting (though you'll only focus on a portion of it, and your table's Barsoom won't be exactly like anybody else's Barsoom)
      • \n
    • to play to find out what happens to the characters
      • \n
    • to play to find out what the characters actually turn out to be like
      • \n
    • larger-than-life but ultimately human characters who often change parts of their identity, dramatically, and make changes to the world in the process
      • \n
    • focus on character advancement as a tool of plot-development and thematic exploration
      • \n
    • mechanics with lots of moving parts, but which you can customize toward how much crunchy or plain you ultimately want the game to be
      • \n
    • to tinker a little with the system to customize it for your setting/world before play
      • \n
    • as a Story Guide, to "drive with bangs", putting the relationships and value statements of the PCs under stress
      • \n
    • as a Story Guide, to make some (if minimal) prep in-between sessions
      • \n
    • to use the Sword & Planet backdrop as a springboard for confronting universal human themes through the main characters
      • \n
    \nPick On Mighty Thews if you instead want:\n
      \n
    • one-shot games with possible sequels, or prequels, like loosely connected short-stories
      • \n
    • the possibility of "solo" play (1 GM & 1 PC player), though the "sweet spot" for the game is probably 1+2 players
      • \n
    • to collectively ad-lib your world/campaign setting with only a vague shared aesthetic as the starting point
      • \n
    • to play to find out what the world surrounding the characters is like
      • \n
    • to play to celebrate how cool and competent the characters are
      • \n
    • larger than life, iconic characters who reliably keep true to your personal vision of them (including the fundamental identity conflict each one embeds)
      • \n
    • no actual "character advancement" mechanics, but you can freely revise the characters or change the cast in-between games
      • \n
    • a compact and integrated system which will work right out of the box
      • \n
    • that all players by default posses the ability to define bits of the world besides their own characters
      • \n
    • "no myth" play
      • \n
    • as a GM, to set up scenes against colorful scenery, populate those with weird NPCs and be ready to provide more and more action, but ultimately play reactively to the PC players' loud lead guitars
      • \n
    • zero-prep
      • \n
    • to celebrate the Sword & Planet aesthetic as the ultimate point of play
      • \n

    Posted By: Sam!I honestly didn't think there'd be an audience for Barsoomian miniatures gaming. I don't think I've ever seen a lead Thark in my life.
    \nWant some links?

    There's someone copying old Heritage models and selling them regularly on E Bay.

    Bronze Age miniatures has their Dead Earth line of Not-Carter stuff (which is beautuful by the way and I really need to paint my two unassembled Tharks from them. Err... "Wasteland Mutants", I mean).

    There's another company putting out a small line as well, whose name I forget but I'm sure I could dig up after a quick 5 minute visit to theminiaturespage.com.

    Parroom Station has some related stuff, although they're mostly more Space 1889 ish in direction.

    Classically, most of the Space 1889 Canal Martin stuff from RAFM could be used as Red Men without any real modification, beyond a switcheroo of paint schemes.

    Even the prepainted D&D collectible minis putout some four-armed gorilla figures, although I forget what they're called. You could track them down as singles.

    In a similar vein, Mage Knight put out three different 4-Armed green guys, their Not-Tharks. i forget what they called them, but folks have been using them for a substitute for some time. i have a few somewhere in a box. i'll see if I can find them and post what they're called ( Darkfen Blade and Shyft fiend are the two types i have. The third is a wizard/shaman type guy).

    Finally, some folks have used Warhammer 40K Kroot, doubled up the torsoes by way of bitz selling services and made Tharks that way.

    That's the 25/28mm stuff.

    Then there is Black hat miniatures which puts out 15mm stuff more for army-type gaming rather than roleplaying/skirmish gaming.
