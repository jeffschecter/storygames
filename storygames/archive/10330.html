\n Yay, more of the newest, bestest SG tradition!

Last night, we did setting discussion and made characters for my new post-Ragnar\xf6k techno-Valkyries game, Broken Rainbow, for which we're only slightly tweaking Mouse Guard. The power of the gods was shattered in the apocalypse and the players are new Valkyries, daughters of that philanderer Wotan (Odin), who ride sky cycles and fight giant wolves and the fighter jets of various competing warlords. Valhalla crashed to earth during Ragnar\xf6k, but Wotan has adopted Kirbytech to make it fly again, a bunch of shattered fragments of the old palace linked together by bridges, Final Fantasy style. The characters are sweet. Alexis' Valkyrie was trained by a ex-Valkyrie in the city that grew up among the shattered pieces of Valhalla that remain on the ground and became a target of Das Schwarze Auge (The Dark Eye), a criminal brotherhood named after the venerable German RPG. David's Valkyrie runs a pirate radio station. They have each been given a shard of the broken Bifrost Bridge by Wotan, but it's not clear exactly what they're supposed to do with them yet.

Oh yeah, and a Valkyrie's Nature is: flying (sky cycles, planes, etc.), dealing with the dead (talking to ghosts, stealing people's souls for Valhalla), and killing (they are supposed to be bloodthirsty battle maidens). Can't wait.

    \n I played Seth's forthcoming two-player game Showdown last night, actually face to face rather than over the internet. My friend Chuck, who had never played a story game, was my opponent. We were Viking warriors who attacked each others' masculinity between raids on the Irish coast, until finally his guy had had enough of mine, and we had it out on an Irish beach while our crewmates watched. The usual over-the-top theatrics of Showdown duels ensued, with slashed femoral arteries and open-fractured jaws and a bit-off nose and dying in a pool of vomit, mucus, and mingled blood.

    An interesting thing came up in our game: Chuck never won the Flash of Insight during play, although he started with it, and thus never Revealed one of my Qualities to be an embarrassing lie. Instead, I won the Flash five times in a row, the last time allowing me to Injure his last remaining pair of dice and bring the game to its conclusion. For those unfamiliar with Showdown, this means that I was able to take every element of his character's self-image and reveal the gap between it and the sad reality, while carefully preserving my character's own ego (although he was revealed within the fiction as a slaver and a sociopath, violent, abusive and utterly lacking in empathy, but all in a way consonant with his picture of himself as a Viking warrior). The descent of Chuck's character into pathos was perfect, and it made for a very memorable arc as he became a loser in the eyes of our crew and our village, with no way to confront my character other than sudden violence.

    It was a really good time, a game that both Chuck and I will play again. Highly recommended. And for players in a hurry, note that we played out the whole game in a Puppetlandesque 55 minutes!

    \n I had day-long flight travel from SF-NYC-France recently. And I spent much of the time playing Classic Traveller solo on the plane.

    It actually worked out really well, and the crew of the Free Trader Rocinante was a Merchant Captain who was barely making ends meet, a Navy Lt. engineer who wasn't afraid to get his hands dirty, a Scout pilot who BS'd his way into the job, and Marine Colonel who also happened to be a Baron and the black ship of his influential family. The Baron quickly ended up being a very interesting character who started off as a high passenger and ended up as the "Security/Liaison/Cargo Officer" due to his unique skills.

    In a short time, the crew talked their way around some "discrepancies" in their cargo manifest, found themselves embroiled in a secret war between a group of suicidal cultists and the secret police who were after them, and escaping from a laser-blasting patrol cutter and into jump space.

    One technique of making the story happen in solo play with multiple characters was to write a "crewman's log" for every character between systems. This gave me a real chance to explore the story in their own words, and the personalities of the crew as well as their differing relationships really started to come out. And when I was on vacation, taking days in-between gaming sessions helped provide a fresh perspective when I sat down to play again without having to make split decisions all the time.

    In solo play you really need to look at every piece of content giving to you and find a way to run with it. I'm looking forward to doing more of it, actually!

In the end, Mexico becomes a much greater power in the world, with clockwork gatling guns...and the crew are now on the run from Integrity Marines for their crimes against the timeline.

    Quincunx (A game about reality TV supernatural hunters)

    I'll cut this down to the best scene:

    In the same location on the map are two things, 1) the central distribution centre for mail in Sydney, Australia, 2) Rookwood Cemetery, the largest cemetery in the southern hemisphere with over 1,000,000 bodies buried.

    A player uncovers the fact that there is something mystical within the area, and we allow players to set their own scene framing.

      \n We kicked off our Houses of the Blooded campaign last night. I ended up tailoring the night's content pretty well to our "abbreviated" play footprint; in my head, we're playing through a half-hour political drama teevee show, not a full hour, which fits our available three-and-a-half hours pretty well.

      My three players were instantly at each others' throats, even without the earthquake destabilizing the neighboring barony.

      The unmarried Moryandal Thorne extended hospitality to her unlucky neighbor Bail Uvan and his two unmarried sons and sent their destroyed castle a relief caravan. Of course, said caravan was harboring a spy that contacted the unmarried baron on the other side of Bail's land, with a combined mission of scoping out marriage prospects and coordinating conquest. She didn't tell either of the other PCs about caravan or spy (although another PC did find out...).

      Rajh Yvarai spent most of the story avoiding his wife, the niece of Bail Uvan, while at the same time sending the beleaguered visiting ven whores to make sure they were comfortable. He oversaw the contract negotiations with the neighbors, winning rights to explore their ruins and a hefty payment for all three PCs in exchange for a few season actions on their part. By the terms of the contract, the PCs decide when and where everything goes down, so they can string poor Bail along indefinitely.

      Meanwhile, Niassa Burghe moved her men into her absent liege's castle, to "protect" it while the Countess was away. When the staff started getting suspicious, she gave them a massive feast, which only served to erode their loyalties to the Countess further. When the PC barons sat down to negotiate with Bail Uvan, she exposed Moryandal's spy hiding in the relief caravan.

      Moryandal exploded in emotion, convincing Bail that the spy was actually proof that she was interested in marrying one of his sons.

      It was lovely.
