    \n I find this case interesting due to how the different play cultures within D&D seem to be clashing in that GitP thread. It's easy to forget that even if you've yourself made your peace with the fact that D&D 3rd edition is not an universal story game, there are still humongous amounts of people who expect to play it in this way, and quite often do successfully. It's no wonder that issues like rules lawyering are so very current and near to the hearts of D&D players.\n
    Posted By: UserClone-Have you had a similarly aggravating experience/person where you felt you had to leave a long-standing group?
    \nWhen I GMed 3rd edition D&D early last decade for several years, we had our rough spots in the group. Some people left the game due to pretty similar differences about creative agenda, while others left simply because they didn't with with the group socially. My original intent for the game had been to make it encompassing and welcoming to different styles of play (I even added a heavy layer of Forge-based state of the art homebrew elements to the game to enable dramatic and narrativist play better), but in practice this standard mostly acted as a winnowing-ground for players, leaving us with those who were most interested in doing what D&D does best; the more story-oriented, rules-light and challenge-averse players tended to drop off, while the hard core we developed continues to this day to get along famously, playing everything D&D from 4th edition to OSR.\n
    -Have you thought about leaving D&D behind to become nothing more than a fond, nostalgic reverie? Have you ACTUALLY done so?
    \nWell, D&D never was the big thing for us here in my local Finnish play culture that it is in the USA. I played the game seriously for the first time in 2002 or so, well over a decade after I started roleplaying. Until the TSR collapse and WotC buyoff I'd largely lived with the impression that D&D was a permanent footnote in roleplaying history.

    Closest I get to this experience of abandonment is when I decided to end our 3rd edition campaign in 2005-ish in favour of focusing more on Forge-style proge games. It wasn't the soul-shattering creative leap that giving up D&D is for those who came to the hobby with it and have done most of their roleplaying with D&D, but it was my most successful campaign to date, and the game was working swimmingly, so it was the first time I ever walked away from a successful roleplaying game because I wanted to up my creative performance even further. This was probably the hardest part for me, as it meant arranging for the group to continue with a different GM while I looked for new people to play different games with. Fortunately we're all sane and adult people, so all this went off pretty much without a hitch, and now that I've been doing more D&D once more (this time in an OSR context), we've played more together with the old crew as well. Besides, everybody is growing wiser with age, and even the hardcore D&D guys have been enjoying forays to TSoY and other such games over the years, so it's not like I lost any friends over it.\n
    -Anyone interested in a fuckton of 3.5 material (okay, maybe not a METRIC fuckton)?
    \nNah, I've got my own collection. I recommend you hold onto yours as well - it might not seem that way now, but odds are you'll enjoy these gaming materials anew in 15 years or so when the 3rd edition revival rolls up. Then again, it'll likely be even more trivial to get this stuff in electronic format then.\n
    -Do you think I was being unfair?
    \nIf you really need to know, a bit. Of course I don't know you guys, but Douglas doesn't seem like an "inane pedant" in that thread to me. He's just playing 3rd edition D&D hardcore, which is a fine and respectable way of playing the game. In fact, I wouldn't personally care for anything else when it comes to 3rd edition, as the other way pretty much devolves into trivial freeforming and arbitrary GM calls that amount to following the GM's aesthetics over any joint play procedure, which is so much easier without bringing the massive 3rd edition rules construction with you in the first place. I'm the sort of guy for whom the procedure is the game, so if we're not going to play by the rules, then by all means lets switch to some rules we're willing to play by. I just find it sad when so many people insist on playing D&D (especially 3rd edition) when all they want is light-weight fantasy adventure story game experience. 3rd edition D&D is a challenge-based skirmish wargame rules-set, so I have sympathy for Douglas wanting to play by the rules and make any changes in a democratic and transparent manner instead of having the GM make arbitrary judgement calls.

    To give you an idea of how this seems to me, I should say that I'd probably be entertained by playing with Douglas myself, based on what I saw there. He argues gogently about how the rules work, but seems perfectly willing to go through houseruling procedures as long as that happens in an informed manner instead of having the GM do snap judgments in the middle of a fight. Framing this as "rules lawyering" is not useful, as that sort of language demonizes what some people think of as the core of 3rd edition D&D gameplay experience. I definitely admit that it's not always easy to bridge these play culture and creative agenda issues and communicate clearly, but what you have in that thread doesn't seem problematic at all. The GM seems a bit defensive about his perfectly acceptable decision to not have standard races in his world (something about how his elves don't have sleep magic immunity), but that's just superficial communication friction, nothing to lose sleep over.

    On the other hand, I think that your decision to not continue playing the game where you find the contents of play inane is a very fine thing. I mean, even people who should have nothing invested in it (like me) often find that rules manipulation is one of 3rd edition's central facets, so why try to make it do something else? The world is full of games that do other things better. Besides, Adaptive Style totally does reneve all maneuvers, so if you can't handle it, better GTFO

    \n Wow, thanks for the really thoughtful responses, Eero! I think that I may have miscommunicated both here and there on at least one point, though: I enjoy pedantry to a point. I actually like figuring out how rules go together, but do it with an eye towards keeping things as RAW as possible, insofar as RAW isn't crazy. On the other hand, Douglas likes to twist the RAW to gain whatever advantage for the player he possibly can.
    \n
    \nExample: The rules text in Tome of Battle for Warblades says that in order to refresh all of their maneuvers, spend a swift action followed by either a melee attack or spending a standard action doing nothing (representing a fancy flourish of one's weapon).
    \n
    \nSo I read that and think "Cool, so I spend a swift action, then either make a(1) melee attack or waste my standard action if no bad guys are near enough to make one."
    \n
    \nDouglas reads the same words and thinks "...Hm, 'a melee attack,' eh? Well it doesn't specify that it can't be followed up with a full-attack action, because you are making at least one attack." Now, you have to understand, that even if you don't find this particular one to be a big deal, he reads Every. Single. Phrase. this way.
    \n
    \nTo me, this type of rules-lawyering is the disruptive type I don't care for, which tends to subvert the DM's ability to keep everyone on the same page, and forces him and myself (the rules-savvy people within the game other than douglas) to spend more time and energy debating rules than on playing the game. To me, that's not cool.
    \n
    \nAlso, the guy is just rude. The DM asks us all if we're cool with his niece and nephew (late high school-age and college-age, respectively) joining the game, and we were all like "Welcome aboard!" Additionally, we've had a long-standing rule that says if you don't post within 3 days, the DM will co-opt your character to keep things moving, playing it as faithfully to the way you usually play as possible.
    \n
    \nAlmost immediately douglas was calling for their expulsion, because on a few occasions, the "rule of 3" was invoked. Remember, this was the guy whom we were nice enough to keep around after he copypasted and deleted a post seven times in order to ace a saving throw roll, which we had all felt was cheating and unfair.

    \n Don't play Dungeon World. Not yet. The genre is too familiar and you'll end up drifting it and playing it like it's D&D, even if you try not to.

    Play something more different. Play some one-shots or miniseries games that are super different. Play Dogs. Play Fiasco. Play a short 40s pulp story using Savage Worlds.

    Play something that's more different than the difference between D&D and DW.

    At least a few times.

    I'm not saying that you need to commit to a 3 year campaign or anything. Just try something different.

    Ooh. Here's one. Play The Mountain Witch. It uses the "mismatched band of dudes on an adventure to defeat the big baddie" format, but it changes so many things that it'll be easy not to drift it into old habits. And the mechanics are totally different - they're laser-focused on teamwork and trust and betrayal. And it plays well in a single session of about 6 hours.

    Do it.

    And yeah, don't dump your books. Just shelve em. I have a whole small bookshelf full of game books, 2/3 of which are unplayed. Random inspirations come in handy sometimes. Eventually one day when you want to play Dungeon World, you'll wish you had some of those books still for a few cool ideas that you vaguely remember were in there but wish you could go back and reread.

    -Have you had a similarly aggravating experience/person where you felt you had to leave a long-standing group?
    \nHonestly, I've never been into DnD, partly because I see this type of rule lawyering (there's a difference between keeping things in line with the rules and spending 3/4 of your night arguing rules instead of playing a game) ruin games for people on a regular basis. Even rules with lighter/simpler rules or less necessary rules can have this issue, though. I would up seeing a two year running LARP (Changeling the Lost) tear apart because the players were trying to have fun and the person running the game was being a rules nazi. This would be fine if he was following the book, but every night saw a new house rule off the cuff just so the GM could have his NPCs win every conflict. I've always been a believer that the game (well, WoD/CtL in particular, not every game is meant to do this) is about building a communal narrative, not about either the GM or players could "win." In the end, the GMs desire to win wound up with all of the good players moving on, and the people that love to bring drama and make everyone around them miserable stayed. Worked out nicely if you ask me, they go what they wanted, we got what we wanted.\n

    -Do you think I was being unfair?
    \nEero raises a lot of interesting points concerning your fairness and the way DnD is played in general. There's one thing he says that I think is particularly worth looking at (though it starts to move a little tangential to your question...\n
    Posted By: Eero TuovinenHe argues gogently about how the rules work, but seems perfectly willing to go through houseruling procedures as long as that happens in an informed manner instead of having the GM do snap judgments in the middle of a fight. Framing this as "rules lawyering" is not useful, as that sort of language demonizes what some people think of as the core of 3rd edition D&D gameplay experience.
    \nDouglas' obsession with the rules is a way that many people choose to (and enjoy) play DnD. Honestly, the game is essentially designed to be played in a way that is rules-centric and narrative-light. As Eero said, it's really a "challenge-based skirmish wargame rules-set," and Douglas is more interested in that aspect of it than the narrative that results out of it. The trouble is, I (much like you, I'm guessing), find that the actual heart DnD is my least favourite part of it, and the narrative is the real winner for me. That's why my friends and I have always played other games, DnD played as it's meant to is really just math in a fantasy setting and it doesn't do it for us. I'm not saying there's anything wrong with it, it's just not our cup of tea.

    Back to your actual fairness question, I can illustrate again with something that my old group did. Every game we played, there was always a solid understanding before we started about how important (or not) the formal rules would be. Any time we brought in new people, we made it clear that we felt the rules were to facilitate our fun, and weren't the actual fun itself. The point being: if you all had an understanding that the rules were not tantamount and narrative was king, how you feel is completely fair. If there was no such understanding, it's not really fair to begrudge Douglas for playing the game as he sees it being meant to be played. Personally, I think you should talk to Douglas (or have the GM do it) and see what he's trying to get out of the game. Even if you still walk away, it would be good to understand what he's in it for, because he's not the last person you'll meet like that in the land of RPGs.
