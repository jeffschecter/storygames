    \n "Freeform" is wat we call it when we're adults. We used to just call it Play Pretend as kids.

    I don't think rules are crutches at all. They're tools for building up and sometimes rebuilding our muscles for Play Pretend that have atrophied.

    They can help us focus and inform our Play Pretend, and sometimes they're a fun creative constraint all on their own.

    I don't think we ever really ever move past them, even when we seemingly dispense with them. Instead we internalize them and use them to make our Play Pretend richer.

    Edit to add:
    If you feel you are having problems playing games bordering more on the freeform end of the spectrum, take some time out and go read a copy of Matthijs Holter's game Archipelago, and particularly pay attention to the phrases that are core to that game. Then take them with you mentally into whatever loosey-goosey game you're playing and use them.

    \n Here's my general take on freeform. I hope it's applicable here.

    Which parts of freeform are natural? I think it depends on your background as a storyteller, actor, author, party-goer, diplomat, manager, debater, and more. The same stuff that affects any game or creative or social activity comes into play; the only difference with freeform is, if that stuff doesn't line up right, there's no game.

    Games with structure give us concrete things to do, so even if it's not easy or natural or fun, we can in fact proceed. So then maybe it becomes fun. Or maybe we derive satisfaction purely from system interaction. There are a lot of options, a lot of outs!

    Freeform has no outs. You either make it work or it utterly dies. Learning to make it work can give you some mad skills, though! And once you have those skills, it may become your preferred way to play.

    If you come to roleplaying to act and talk and imagine and subsume into virtual experience, then being able to do that without dice or cards or character sheets is kind of sweet.

    \n "Freeform" is an oxymoron, if ever I saw one. :-)

    Important:
    Posted By: ivanThe lack of rules to handle it made me panic, whereas Johnstone and Daniel (Emperor) just negotiated up to a compromise..
    Negotiating is key; setting down the form, where no form is. There is no game that works well with players being fully free, so you have to negotiate what kind of form to have. It may be a one sided negotiation (I, the GM, decide) or it may be consensus driven (we, the players, decide), but you have to reach a mutual understanding of how to play within a very short time from opening the game. And you need to be prepared for re-negotiating, in case of challenges to the agreed form. The basis for this (constant) negotiation is the mindset of players; try to facilitate an open and positive mindset, ensuring that everyone will participate in negotiations with serious expediency; We want to reach a quick and simple agreement, so play may continue!

    When J and D manages this negotiation, it's due to them being more experienced, and thus feeling more comfortable in the interactive-creative context. And they know something about what makes a game work, so they are able to negotiate fast, so that the game don't die on them.

    As long as you know what to do (negotiate), and have some inkling what methodic tools (simple ones) will support a gang of grown ups playing, you should be able to fix this with some ease too.

    One important thing to remember, is that we call it "freeform" due to our lust for unfettered role-playing. So you need to start with no rules, and then negotiate simple and light rules, to have the players keep a feeling that their game-play is full and flowing freeform (form made up on the spot, with ease).

    In my view "freeform" is mostly about how you perceive the game. To keep the positive flow such a game often starts up with (but sadly; often loose within the first debacle of rules-negotiation), the players need to keep the perception of it as something light, nimble and unfettered. As long as you do, it will stay that way, even when delving into deeper themes (which such games, sadly, seldom do).\n
    Posted By: ivanuntil you get the level of flow to go fully freeform..
    Keep an open mindset, know your negotiation, and how far you can go with rules (light, simple), and the game is yours. The flow is something that comes by itself, so don't worry about it.

    \n How can you play a game without knowing how to play it? At some point of our life we have both readed and watched other people play RPGs, both trad and freeform and learned the basic outlines of the rules/social contract applied, and so we came to understand the game. Perhaps in a few cases you just needed some basic rule and came up with the rest of the game idea yourself, and then found out there were more games like the one you created.

    I'd say the effortless way you mean actually refers to how quickly you come to understand/invent/came up with a mindset to confront the issue in front of you. For some people its easier to create content and drive other people to create content in the same way. Some people have a harder time at this but can easily calculate tons of modifiers and bonuses multiplied by seven different rolls, and remember even the most obscure rules from forgotten suplements. So we have different tools to confront different problems.

    For example, I haven't been able yet to play games that go more into the narrative freeform than into the strategical use of the rules. However I've done a ton of brainstorming for writing fiction with some pals, even talking in character and all and having fun at it, though for some reason we didin't considered it roleplaying but a fun way of writing. Even in this way of working, we started using a couple of tricks from the Cave of Plato that help a lot to keep the fiction on trails.

    Now, is learning to play hard won with experience? I'd say it depends on the way you like to play. Perhaps you can just relax and let the fiction flow, or like me you need at least a little bit of control, or perhaps you can't relax if you don't go like a total control freak on it. Either way has it's ups and downs and the only way it can become harder is if you try to play using a different mindset that the one you're used to.

    \n My hunch:

    Playing freeform is neither "natural and effortless" or "difficult and skill-based", because it depends on what you're doing and who you're playing with.

    Maybe playing out a conversation in-character with your friend Maria comes naturally and easily. But doing the same thing with Jim is painful and awkward. However, with Jim you can create complex worlds from scratch is easy. And Julia knows just how to offer you a challenge in the form of a puzzle. Etc.

    I think "doing freeform" isn't so much a thing. "Playing the way we do in our group when we decide to do THIS," that's a thing.

    That's not to say, of course, that you can't develop your skills in any of these situations. Improv "rules" and principles are really useful ideas in this sense. The other skills, it seems to me, are not all that different from regular old social skills and paying close attention to those around you. So, I suppose "listening" would be the primary skill here.

    Vincent's thoughts on the limitations of freeform are very, very interesting, however! I tend to agree with everything he says on that subject.

    \n One could say that freeform is the most natural state of play. Children do it without any instruction at all (in most, if not all, social animal species). We don't need to learn shit to freeform because freeform is about learning. We learn not to do it as we grow up. You can get back to it pretty easily, though you may need some practice and a safe space to do it (such as a time set side with others interested in doing it as well).

    There is a therapeutic practice called Developmental Transformation (DVT) that has a sole goal of forming a "play space" that allows a place for the freeform play we did naturally as children. The idea is that this teaches us more about who we are (and helps us explore personal issues in a much more accessible way) and it's very challenging to get comfortable with outside of childhood.

    But we don't really mean that when we talk about freeform.

    I think the truth is few of us what that learning, challenging, unfettered play. We want improv and acting techniques to help ensure that it's "good" play. We want prompts and dice rolls to give us permission to go in new directions. We want rules to set boundaries on what is and isn't acceptable. We want all these things because we want to get something out of it. Maybe fun. Maybe compelling stories. Maybe a strong theme. Freeform as typically discussed for a roleplaying style very much fits into here. And restrictions are something that has to be learned. It's all through experience.

    But that experience can simply be words on a page. You don't need to go through 10 years of roleplaying to "hard win" that experience. Someone can just tell you the boundaries and give you room to play. You may hit those boundaries wrong sometimes when starting out, and it hurts, but it's not mountain climbing. It's natural and part of the process. But the truth is those restrictions are what's unnatural. We need to learn those. And that's what takes some playing around with. Whether those rules are freeform play or hard-core HERO games super heroes, they're not natural, they're tools we've made. Still, the best way to learn them is to play with them.

    \n Paul,

    Yeah, I liked Vincent's breakdowns too. They start here, but get developed more here and here. The end result looks something like:

    In RPG play, you can change people's normal social system with:

    1. Content ("We're on Tatooine and I'm a storm trooper!")
    2. Explicit principles and instructions ("Immersion is most important; whenever your immersion is broken, call a time out.")
    3. Fictional structure ("After this scene, the game says the Nazis pull out of the city and we have one scene left.")
    4. Constructed interactions without cues ("There's a GM and it's me.")
    5. Procedural cues ("Roll dice for narration rights.")
    6. Fiction-mediating cues ("Roll dice for which character wins.")

    I imagine most freeform roleplay includes a bunch of these. An explicit 1 & 4 plus an implicit 2, for example.

    \n So I wrote a really smart response to this, but it got eaten. Here's the summary:

    1. Play Silver & White and see if it has enough instructions and guidance for you. I don't think it's trying to be "GMless" or "freeform" as much as it wants to be something else; something that makes it seem obvious and natural to play games this way.

    2. Games like Polaris and Fiasco and current versions of Geiger Counter still show signs of the GM having been removed. This is also true of some freeform games where certain mechanics have been obviously removed, such that you can still see where they might have been. In my opinion, this is less ideal than moving towards a different model, where you never even consider a GM or mechanics. Even Silver & White's descriptive roles are kinda GM-like and the touching takes the place where you would normally expect mechanical resolution, so it falls into this category at present, but I think it hints at something more.

    3. It's really hard to talk about GMless or freeform games in the abstract, because they're even more different (in my experience) than different games that both have GMs. The ways you can portion out responsibility and share creative control are endless.

    Posted By: ivanHow would you do THAT?! Seriously, how would you go about it?
    \nHmm, I'm not sure it's easier to talk about than to actually do. Here's an example of how you could do it, based on one game I'm working on:

    1. When it's your turn (in the AW sense, not like turn-taking), say what your character says, does, feels, thinks, or looks like.

    2. When it's the GM's turn, they describe what NPCs say, do, or look like, or what the environment sounds, feels, smells, tastes, or looks like.

    3. To press your advantage against an NPC or the environment, tell the GM that you are using one of your "trumps." The basic trumps that everyone has are:
    -- threats or promises
    -- violence
    -- expert knowledge
    -- practiced skill
    -- desparation

    Each class of character also begins with an additional trump. Wizards get "arcane power" for example. Assassins might get "quick and deadly."

    When you invoke a trump, the other side has to choose:
    -- fall victim to your prowess
    -- resist, at great cost to themselves
    -- defy all fairness, fate, and the will of the gods, escalating the situation

    Some NPCs and environmental features (traps) have their own trumps. For example, "poisoned tentacles" or "long drop onto sharpened sticks."

    If a PC, NPC, or environmental feature has multiple trumps invoked against them at once, they have to choose different options. Thus, if you have three trumps invoked against you, you have to choose all three options.

    4. When you resist at great cost or fall victim to someone or something that means you harm, you suffer the fictionally-established consequences: shame, pain, desire, death, etc. Someone should probably write these consequences down, so they are not forgotten. In appropriate situations, other players or the GM can invoke these "conditions" against you as if they were trumps. So, if you have a broken leg but are trying to leap out of the way of a falling pillar, I can invoke your injury as a trump and make you pick (succomb, resist, defy).

    5. You can recover from a given "condition" (i.e. set of fictional consequences) once A) it has been invoked against you and you've met any fictional requirements for overcoming it, such as recieving medical treatment, having a curse lifted, finding new reasons not to trust someone, etc.

    Boom, there's a freeform-y (depending on your definition) dungeon-y game, based just on advantages and choices. It's still a bit game-like, and may not be quite as mechanic-free as Tomas is thinking, but it's something more in that direction. You could probably make one that was more like Montsegur 1244, more about character relationships and even less about resolving what happens.

    \n David, we're approaching the point of no return here, where existing terminology breaks down because it was never intended to describe this stuff, but I certainly see your point. The thing is, any set of freeform methods, if used over time, develop into those kinds of fictional triggers for specific actions. Like, when I was running an online freeform group back in the early 2000s, the rule was that you couldn't narrate stuff about other people's characters without their permission. In other words, "When you want to narrate something about another PC, ask their player first," which is a fictional trigger if I ever heard one. There were others too. Like, "When two players narrate incompatible things happening, the group moderator should step in and help resolve them."

    In my mind, what the ruleset above -- and other similar kinds of "structured freeform" systems -- is doing is mostly just formalizing the informal or shared-but-not-regularly-spoken-aloud principles of freeform play and then extending them to structure play a bit more strongly than traditional freeform. Yes, it looks and feels different than just winging it, but it also lets the group jump ahead a bit, as if you'd already been playing together for a while and reached an understanding as to how the game will be played. If you want the exploratory nature of starting a new freeform game and gradually developing a shared understanding with your fellow players, being surprised along the way, this may not give you exactly the same experience, but the methods being used here aren't alien or incompatible with freeform play as it's traditionally understood, I don't think. In my mind, it's a natural extension. I mean, how else do you further develop freeform play techniques and systems, right? You kinda have to formalize them in order to be able to talk about them and be intentional about using certain ones and not others.

    \n Agreed that formalizing has some virtues, but I think it can have some drawbacks*, depending on how the formalized version is used in play.

      \n Uhm... I like JWalton's fictional moves formalization as a set of guidelines rather than rules.
      I see it as a method to find out what can logically derive from the fiction.

      Most outcomes can probably readily be agreed upon by anyone, based on the level of skill of the characters and the other parties involved.

      When there is an impasse (is my mage stronger than yours? can my warrior hit your mage before he casts the spell?) then the conflict becomes about "how far are you willing to go to win?" Are you draining all your strength? Are you making a pact with a demon? Do you risk serious consequences?

      When outcomes cannot be overcome, then you try to set up the environment to give you a chance.. and when you are on a level playing field you might still have to make sacrifices.

      I partially use this for In a Wicked Age as well: suppose that you beat me using violence, even though you have no weapon and I do. You will be able to do this, but you will have to justify a fictional outcome that makes it possible, maybe even involving someone that helped you.
