    \n Actually, Push 1 is now available for free.

    http://plays-well.com/push1.pdf

    I scorn and despise those who put up pdf's of products without permission but since plays-well is the site for Push's folks, I assume they authorize this.

    It is indeed a good book, as I wrote on my blog a few months back when noting the pdf was available


    The games in it that seem strange, think about them, look behind them.

    Waiting for the Queen/Tea at Midnight

    Fascinating and engaging character backgrounds and then....

    Pick up the bucket, put down the bucket ~ what I see in this is that game actions are boring, it's what you make of them, how you play them, what you add to them, these create an interesting game.

    That is every tactical-heritage RPG in a nutshell. Once shiny wears off the crunch, without Game Master and Players bringing something to it, it's all just buckets being moved around after some backstory is written.

    Why indie games with less crunch and more narrative focus are good to play even if you go back to/stay mainly with traditional games - they will teach you to be more narratively interesting and descriptive, to look beyond mechanics of what character can do in the fiction.

    \n Glad folks are still interested in Push. I really need to revise the Push section of the Bleeding Play site, to more prominently feature links to the PDF, but I've just had other priorities lately. Once I do that, Push 1 will actually be back up on Lulu, so that folks can buy print copies at cost, so it's totally cool that you went ahead and did that, Hans.

    Push 2 may still happen, probably just including the material that's already been written. But that means I have to actually get around to editing it and so far I've been a total flake, much to the detriment of everyone who's written stuff for it (apologies!). I have the bad habit of committing to far more than I can actually accomplish, which has left more than a few projects lying by the wayside, but my resolution for the New Year involves resolving more of my unfinished business.

    After Push 2, I'm not sure if Push has a definite future, but we'll see. I still feel that there is a definite need to be served there, but, as Matt figured out with Daedalus, running a journal or zine takes a lot more work than one would expect. Honestly, I think moving to an electronic, HTML format (as opposed to print or PDF) -- more like a group blog -- might make that easier and limit the amount of editing / layout that was necessary, streamlining the goal of getting content to an audience. But I'm just speculating at this point. Maybe folks could guest-contribute articles to Bleeding Play? Not sure. Any brilliant suggestions out there?

    \n Well, to me it seems like your efforts with Push have logically been moving towards a communal blog-with-editor. Perhaps simply having a blog and then asking interesting people to write blog posts for it on an individual basis would serve your style better. Something like Wordpress easily allows you to do a revision cycle as well as keeping yourself as the main editor. This approach would cost in terms of media profile visibility, but I don't know that you'd care about that so much - and it'd certainly be a more lightweight structure compared to how you've been going about it 'till now. And it's not difficult to compile blog material for a higher-profile publication such as a pdf 'zine later if you feel like doing something of the sort.

    Ultimately, though, much of the interest for Push has been fed by the choice of medium, so I don't know how many people would even notice the same or similar content when offered in a low-profile medium like a blog. So it's a choice between visibility and convenience. Considering that you already chose to make the project free, it'd seem logical to complete the move towards low-barrier, low-profile publishing by opting for the blog. Or heck, you could opt for "blogs" - instead of having your own blog and editing other people's writing for it, tell people to write interesting articles at their own blogs like it was your business to do so

    \n Ha. Well, I could see Push working as a, uh, blog aggregator with power. That is, I could just go about my business, reading blog articles and whatnot, and then, when I found something I thought was really cool, I could see if the author wanted to revise and expand that idea into something a bit more formal, which could then be combined with other similar articles, commented on in the manner of the marginal commentary in Push 1, and then thrown up on Lulu to sell for cost. That would cut out the draft phase entirely, because it would be based on already written material. And I would probably cut the amount of editing I did way down, letting the author's work stand on its own more, for better or worse.
