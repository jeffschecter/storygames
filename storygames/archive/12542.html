\n In Dungeons and Dragons the default die for randomization of difficult activities is a d20. At low levels the the impact of the randomizer is much greater than the impact of any skill the character may have. As the levels decrease that changes somewhat, but it remains a substantial fraction of the total value of a check. That feels out of whack to me. I think that skill should play the bigger part in success.

What systems have a balance between skill and randomizer where skill dominates, but the randomizer still matters a bit?
What percent of the total do you think should come from skill and what percent should come from the randomizer?

    \n There's a lot of factors that play into how randomization impacts resolution.

    One factor is the number of rolls per resolution. Sure, D&D's D20 is a big swing, however, each resolution requires several D20 rolls.

    Another factor is the shape of the randomizer curve. A bell curve, usually generated by rolling several dice and adding them, open ended dice mechanisms are another bell curve mechanism, can focus results on a narrow range. Note that D&Ds many rolls to resolve combat basically turn the single D20 roll into a bell curve. The nice thing about a bell curve is that it also allows for some outlier results.

    \n I term I coined a while ago was a 'certainty'. The idea is, what difference in skill (between skill and target) makes the outcome certain, or as near as makes little difference.
    \n
    \nIn D20, the eponymous die sums up the 'certainty' for a single roll. For a fight, however, the difference is probably much lower.
    \nIn Feng Shui, a certainty is about 5 points of AV.
    \n
    \nI found it handy for thinking about what range of ability is allowed from the bottom to the top of a system, and how the balance between randomness and attributes is truck.
    \n
    \nThe problem with your questions is that there's no correct answer to either. It all depends on what feel you want for a game. If it's madcap, or cinematic, the 'ideal' balance will skew towards randomness. In a game with a lot of careful planning or investigation, it skews the other way. Too little randomness can risk making everything seem rote, and you might as well ditch the randomiser altogether. Too much, and the defining characteristics of a character might not seem to matter, or promote buckshot play where you keep trying things until something sticks. Even in a given milieu, it's highly subjective.

    \nThe One Role Engine's approach to this is sort of interesting. The way the basic "set" mechanics work, each increase in skill matters less than the previous one. That is, there is a diminishing return on raising your skill. But... as you raise your skill, the more you can do at once (that is, more skill means more opportunity for multiple sets, which ORE uses for multiple actions, sort of). So, skill always matters... and luck always matters. They just matter for different reasons as skill increases.

    Calculating probability in the ORE is a pain in the ass, though.

    \n Robin, yes. You need to look at the random factor as a percentage of the range of differences in difficulties.

    The cumulative nature of D&D style combat is also very important. For Cold Iron, a college friends game which has a mechanic that enables use of the real normal distribution curve (the "ideal" bell curve), my friend developed a computerized combat simulator, that would take two opponents and have them slug it out until one went down, and ran the same combat repeatedly. Despite the standard deviation of the randomizer being about 7 (actually 20/3), a single point of difference in combat rating resulted in the higher rated combatant winning I think 60% of the time. This is actually a HUGE swing. So a pretty large randomizer (it's actually sort of similar to a D20 in that there's about a 5% chance of at least a +10, so if the scale was changed from -10 to +10 to 1 to 20, a 1 and a 20 would have about the same 5% chance of occurrence that they have on a D20 - sort of), combats are actually fairly predictable. Yet, there is always the chance for a 1st level character to kill a 10th level character in a single blow.

    Now if instead, the D20 roll was a single roll to resolve a conflict, then the predictability would be much lower, even if our ratings are such that on a 16+ I win and on a 15- you win, there's still a 25% chance I win despite our ratings being 5 difference (take a look at RuneQuest/Basic Role Playings resistance chart for this example - even though it uses D100, but in 5% increments that basically mimic a D20).

    \n I think my favourite games are ones where both skill and random input matter a whole lot, but what they do is create difficult choices for the players, and it's those choices that really matter.

    Dogs in the Vineyard is a good example: because of the number of dice you're rolling, it's arguable that there is a huge bell curve effect going on. However, it's really each player's choices that matter: what kinds of Raises will you make? When are willing to Take the Blow? And when (and how often) are you willing to escalate?

    The dice just lay out the landscape within which those choices are made.
