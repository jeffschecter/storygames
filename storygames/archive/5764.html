Why the indie publishing model produces unreadable books

\n
\n
\n
\n\nMatthijs \n\n\n
\n
\n\nFebruary 2008 \n edited February 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Yeah, I wrote that just to get your attention. Now let me explain what I mean.

I've written many articles in many fora. They're very rarely edited.

See, when I send stuff to RPG.net, they basically publish what I write. I had a review sent back once, with a paragraph explaining it wasn't up to their standards; I rewrote the whole thing, sent it back, got it published. Other than that, I've never heard from an editor. When I write for fanzines, well, you know how that works.

However, I recently submitted an article to the Solmukohta (also known as Knutepunkt) book for 2008. It was half-baked; now it's fully baked. What happened was this - short version:

Me: "Here's my article, go publish!"
They: "We're not sure this should be in the book."
Me: "WHAT??? It TOTALLY should! Can't you see that (such and such)?"
They: "Well, maybe. Write that in the article and we'll look at it again."
Me: Grumble grumble... "Here. Now is it good enough for you?"
They: "Oh sure, it's very promising! If you rewrite this half, add something about this, fix these references, explain what you mean here, say more about this, cut some of this crap, it's probably something we can publish!"
Me: Oh well. "Here. How about this?"
They: "Oh yes, we'll definitely publish this! After you make these changes here and here and here."

The thing is that by the third exchange, I was so heavily invested that I'd rewrite it almost any number of times to make it good enough. I had something to live up to. There was someone who A) was in a position to say "Not Good Enough. Do it again", and cared enough to do it five times in a row.

THAT's what editing is. That's the major advantage of somebody else publishing your article, or book, or game. They have the power to say "no".

The disadvantage of indie publishing is that nobody has the power to say "no" to you. Nobody can tell you "we're not publishing this unless you work on it some more". And if you want a writer to perform at their very best, you need somebody with that power.

    \n I think there are a couple of things that can help with this. I suffer from all of them:
    \n
    \n1. Have some pride in your work, edged with humility
    \n
    \nNow this might be quite a strange one because I think everyone would be proud of their game. However its the sprinkles of humility that really help out. I'm totally in shock everytime someone has the kindness to send me a comment about my game in playtest ... God only knows what my reaction will be if someone plops down some cold hard cash to pay for the bloody thing! My game is an extension of me that someone has bought into and it goddamn better be the very best showing of what I am about that it can be. The very last thing I would want to do is waste anyone's time or money on some tat.
    \n
    \n2. Never ... Ever ...EEEVVVEEERRR assume that your view of your product will suffice
    \n
    \nAgain, drawning from my experience, many moons ago I was all puffed up and happy and all finished with Duty & Honour. I have free access to print facilities so I bound up a slack handful of copies and I was ready to rumble. They are all in the bin. They are embarassing. I had totally convinced myself that the game was 'done' when it was only really starting. It was a classic schoolboy error of the highest magnitude. I concluded that I would never ever consider the game finished until at least a few people had seen it and gone 'Uhmm...yup, thats cool.'
    \n
    \n3. Find yourself some bastards friends
    \n
    \nI am very lucky. I have some friends who are ... academics. They exist in a world of accuracy and pendantary that beggars belief. They spend a lot of their time marking and reviewing texts. They are ruthless assassins when it comes to game text. Whats more is that they are both by bestest buddies so I know that their barbed dispatch of my text is filled with love. They want me to succeed, they don't want me to embarass myself, so they drive me to make the best thing I can. Find yourselves some utter utter UTTER bastards like these and let them go at your text. Its illuminating!
    \n
    \nNeil

I see some games that I suspect will never be finished, because the authors are striving after that illusion of perfection.

    Posted By: MatthijsThe vast majority of those aren't going to see your text before publication.
    \nThat's a good point. However, I have learned of a number of independent game designers whose game text is made available for free online. Admittedly, in many cases, these games are available through retail channels, so a potential customer might run across such a game on a store shelf and make the purchase, never knowing that such a preview existed on the web. However, in other cases, these games are only available through the designer's web site, ensuring that the purchaser will at least be informed enough to choose whether to read the text first before clicking the buy button.

    This minor point aside, I agree with what you're saying. It's the risk faced by other small-press publications, such as vanity-press novels. These books are not scrutinized or edited by major (or minor) publishers, and the resulting gold-to-dross ratio can be disappointing. In the case of RPGs, few such publishers exist (it's not like you can submit your game to White Wolf or WOTC and wait for an acceptance letter, after all), so the water is muddied by the fact that most of us, regardless of budget, are vanity publishers in a way (insomuch as we finance all printing costs on our own, rather than simply creating text and forwarding it to a publishing entity for approval).

    Neil, damn good point. I agree that if you have access to anyone with red-ink skills, turning them loose on your manuscript can save you a lot of grief down the line.

    \n Hi Matthijs,

    I can understand your point but I don't realy know what you want from this discussion : other testimonies of similar experiences, opposing views,...

    My only experience of a relationship with an editor comes from a very different world. I once draw a children book as both author and artist. The projet came from the editor, not me, but the constraints were both strict and very loose : this size, this number of page, no words, only images, children this age with that, do what you want.

    It was my first book (and still my only one to this day) and I was very intimidated. So, I made some sketches, showed them to the editor. Then we had a discussion, he advised me of some weak points, we spoke about children, art, films, books, etc... I redraw some pictures and that was all.

    I my case, meeting this man was very important. He is a man with a vast culture, able to see a potential even in a work he's not familiar with and very open. Yet he also had a vision of the kind of books he wanted to publish. This vision was kind of an artistic or esthetic vision of its own even if I was the artist on this precise book.
    This was a great experience, a great meeting. I learned a lot.

    BUT

    I thought that the point of indie publishing (in a given support or expression) was precisely NOT to have this last vision, the one of the editor, come from a different person than the author of the words, pictures, games, music whatever... Even to point of risking to be too personnal.
    And quality is not even at stake because you can have peer reviews, public reviews, editors, etc... even in the indie model as people said.

    I don't object to your point. I just find it great that parallel to the traditional model of edition, a very personnal and unadulterated vision could come to bear fruit and become a game, a comics, etc... Without that we could never have something as Acts of evil from Paul Czege, Epileptic from David B. and such great works.

    \n I think someone playing this role is vital to the production of a good game. I think NOT having that someone be an authority with true control is vital to the very concept of being an indie-publisher. However, resolving this paradox requires nothing more complicated than the designer being willing and...i don't know...strong? enough to grant someone they know and respect (and who has taste and talent in this regard) that kind of position and then voluntarily abide by it.

    Right now I'm playtesting Blood Red Sands with a group that includes game designer and publisher Keith Sears, game designer and publisher Seth Ben-Ezra and Seth's gamer wife (whose also doing the layout) and sister. These are people who know what a good playable solid design looks and plays like. There's no doubt in my mind that if I say "Awesome, lets get this ready for Gen-Con" and some combination of them give me the frowny look and are like "no way this is ready for GenCon" or "hey, this has been fun, but I wouldn't spend money on it"...then it ain't going to GenCon ('cept maybe to the Ashcan Front).

    I mean, I know that sounds simplistic, but I think if the group you playtest with understands that part of the job of a playtester is to serve as that "not-ready-for-prime-time" gate-keeper, AND the designer voluntarily keeps the gate closed...it can be just that simple.

    But then I'm the guy whose still working on Robots & Rapiers after years, because 6 months before I was scheduled to release it playtesting showed "this game ain't fun to play". Thankfully, now it is and I just have to find time to finish it up and get the rest of it to my editor.

    \n Right...that's why I said strong* enough.

    If you're not strong enough to keep to your diet, your diet will fail.
    If you're not strong enough to keep to your work out routine, you won't get the desired results.
    If you're not strong enough to keep to your schedule, you won't get projects complete on time.

    If you're not strong enough to listen to your Quality Control people when they tell you your game isn't ready for sale, then you'll put out product with quality issues.

    To me, that's just part of the responsibility of being an adult human, to actually carry through on what you've said you're going to do.

    Which is not to detract from your initial point. Its definitely easier to have someone with authority to make you do it...but I think the important conclusion is that the Quality Control check needs to be done. As an Indie-Publisher that means having to find an alternative way of doing it.

    \nA-fuckin-men.

    What some people might not really think about is that the whole editing process is one complex, exhausting negotiation process. It isn't just dictating demands or marking up text. I know that experienced folks like Thor and Luke know this, but I thought I'd state it for everyone who is just starting out.

    \n So I dunno, in my experience a good editor (which is a rare thing, maybe 1 editor in 10) doesn't make a book less an author's, she makes it more the authors.

    When Ken Cliffe edited my copy for Second Sight, there were six different places in which his only comment was a variation on "What is this shit? You are better then this, don't be so lazy." By the time we finished what went to press was more what I had wanted in the first place than what I'd written down my first time through. My big complaint about it is that because we were under crunch, we didn't get enough iterations of it. If Ken and I had gone through it half a dozen more times the damn thing might have been perfect.

    Ken Cliffe was a good editor. I'd work with him again, even though I know he's gonna call me a lazy get.

    I love a good editor, because they let me write what I actually wanted to write but didn't.

    The key role here is not the copy editor, who just ensures the basic mechanics of writing are correct*, or the managing editor, who is reviewing the project as a whole. It's the production editor, the person like Thor and Ryan who understands how to write well and who works with the author to improve the writing quality of the work. That's what Matthijs is talking about, as well as Neil, Brand, and Tony - at least as far as the writing itself is concerned. (Quality control on the game design is important too, but isn't necessarily something the (writing) production editor would or should handle; in terms of a specific role, that's more of a managing/line editor task.)

    (* The spelling and grammar check features of Microsoft Word are a nice starting point, but they are not reliable - particularly the grammar checker. You do need someone to act as a copy editor and proofread your text for the basics. But a text can meet all the requirements of spelling and grammar, and still be an unreadable, unclear mess. That's what a production editor is for, to help you shape your unreadable and unclear mess into a clear and concise explanation of your game.)
