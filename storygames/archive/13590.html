\n I got thinking this morning about how complicated all this game making stuff is. There is so much to know and the vast majority of us do not know much of it. PDFs and POD make the means of production more accessible to us than in the past but now there are iphone apps - and how the hell do you make those?!? The means of production is alienated from me again!

The complex system of game making boils down into four parts.

The means of creativity - Writing and having something to write about.

The means of production - Being able to make something economically that is precieved as valuable.

The means of distribution - Basically this means sales. You can put your game out for free as a PDF but if it isn't dstributed and seen it really hasn't been distributed.

The means of coordination - In a word management and accounting.



So I've been doing the creative part for 25 years. I know how to write and have something to write about. I've learned how to make games in a variety of ways in the last ten years (but not the new and cool apps!) My distribution sucks because that is really a full time job and I've already got a day job. I need an organization to get this done - on a shoe string budget. Fortunately I understand accounting and basic management so I think I can do that part - when I have an organization.

Sooooo

I think we all like to write - see all the messages we type.

PDF and POD make production easy for everyone - though I've personally moved on from that.

Sales! We all hate selling things. I like working a booth at Gen Con but feel completely out of my element in making sales calls. It's an emotional thing.

And it took me years, many years, to get comfortable with accounting and management.

So how alienating is this process - game making? How can we help the people who come after us move through learning it more easily (less painfully)? Are there parts of the process that I'm missing? I know it sounds real Marxist and materialistic but it seems like a good way of getting my mind around this whole system.

Anyway - these are just some random musings that could go off in any number of directions. I give it to you to see where you take it.

Chris Engle

    \n Traditionally that's where the publishing system comes in. Freelancer does the creativity part, publisher handles all the rest (or contracts out to wholesaler's etc). Traditionally this is done under a model where the publisher is the center of the business and hires the Freelancer.

    What I'm excited about seeing coming out now is the opposite arrangement. Where the creative person is the center and hires the publisher as a subcontractor to handle the other three...or a combination of other entities to handle different parts.

    This model is way more consistant with the idea of being independent, because you're hiring a publisher to work for you rather than you working for them.

    I think embracing, encouraging, using, and promoting the new companies coming out now who are fulfilling those roles is a great way of making it more painless for others.

    Posted By: Matt SheridanSo, how are people feeling about print on demand, these days?
    \nThe advantages of true PoD (printed only when a customer orders one, like Lulu) are that you can keep a title perpetually available in print, and you don't have to manage inventory at all. These are significant advantages for some people! The margins are, indeed, pretty thin.

    Short-run digital printing (of 100-250 books at a time) is where I do the majority of my print runs. It takes more time on the phone with printers, and you have to manage inventory and shipping, but the margins are much better. In addition, short-run printers will work with you to do things that aren't possible with true PoD (which, so far as I've experienced, have set book templates that you pick and choose from, so if you have an odd trim size or something you can't use them). Annalise, for example, uses two kinds of paper stock and page-specific color, neither of which I could do with Lulu. carry, which I could print PoD, is over 3$ cheaper per book to print in quantities of 100+ than it would be to do PoD.

    My understanding is that its possible to get offset print runs in quantity of 1000 or 1500 these days, which is an even better margin (generally), but the up-front capital investment is still pretty high compared to the digital printing of 100, and you have to be confident that you'll sell through 1/3 to 1/2 of the run to pay for it. I've never looked into it for my publishing.

    So those are my data points!

    \n Sorry for being unclear. When I use the word alienated I'm not meaning it in the angry teenage meaning where I'm upset by not knowing this new skill. I'm meaning it in the old economic meaning of being separated from something - in this case a means of production.

    I recognize that programing and publishing are very different skill sets. There is a lot of computer knowledge needed to do the production part of game making which I've learned but there is also a lot of other skills as well (knowledge of materials, lay out, writing, proof reading etc.) One can not quickly jump from one discipline to another.

    As a programmer you probably don't know what I know about publishing (alienation by lack of knowledge) and you probably don't have the machines I have (alienation from capital equipment). I'm not able to program (alienation by lack of knowledge). But with POD game makers have been able to get into publishing easily without spending the time to learn how it is done on a nuts and bolts level. Technology closed the alienation from means of production in printing.

    I don't know if iphone apps are going to be the next thing that endures but they are certainly hot now. If they do come one and become the way people like getting their information products (because in the end what else is a game) then I missed the bus. I'm not crying about this - just recognizing it. It is an artifact of age, we are bound by our life experiences.

    With this threat I'm also looking at other areas we are frequently alienated from by lack of knowledge (or in the case of sales calls that feeling of fear that many have). Thoughts and ideas on this are welcome but as I said above it is sort of a brain fart idea.

    Cheers!

    \nHere's an interesting layman's diary about app development.\n
    Posted By: ValamirIf you want a Matrix Games I-phone App...why isn't that just as easy as finding an App-guy to program one for you? Same as a pen and paper designer can find a POD-guy to print and bind for them?
    \nIt isn't as easy because these two things are not the same.

    You can believe me on this because I have been both a comics publisher and a software architect. I have written print orders and software specs. I have managed software outsourcing projects.

    A print order, nowadays, is a matter of a few paragraphs. Or mouse clicks. POD-guy takes this print order and your mechs, prints your order, and ships it. You verify the print run is good. You're done. Maybe there's a couple of cycles of proofing in there too.

    App-guy, on the other hand, needs a spec that will describe, with as few ambiguities and contradictions as possible, how your software will operate. This is a very complicated thing to write. I have written specs that are dozens of pages long. I have implemented specs of over a hundred pages. Every incompleteness or ambiguity in that spec will cost you, the client, $$.

    Plus, App-guy's work is never done. They will be with you for the entire lifetime of App, because software is a living thing. "Completed" software still requires testing, updating, enhancing, porting, and bugfixing. App-guy gets paid for all of those activities.
