So, even though it's been months and months since I last played Exalted, and I don't have forty bucks to drop on a game that takes a lot of prep time, I still started reading through 2nd Edition in the bookstore.

Holy Shit! It has Keys! And Relationships!

Or rather "Motivations" and "Intimacies". Motivations are the replacement for the Nature mechanic - for those not in the know, it was WW's standard characterization enforcement thing, that (in my opinion) suffered from 1) actually being half of a mechanic (nature/demeanor) that only really fit in Vampire. 2) being too vauge, and 3)having to be chosen from a list. Motivations are epic, specific, and player authored. Intimacies only really matter in the social combat system, apparently, but it's still awesome just that they're in there. I think the book said, "The important thing is to be able to say 'My character loves his sword, his horse, and his country.'" Coolness. Now if only it was connected to the experience engine, instead of Willpower.

It also seems like the book really expects you to be doing epic stuff. Skimming the Charm trees (kool p0werz), I noticed that they took all the boring ones out, put them at the begining of the chapter and said "here, take one of these first, then have your fun". So all the crazy cool shit that you couldn't do unless you min/maxed your character or played for three months is now the second or third thing in each tree. They have rules for army vs army stuff too. The game is supporting what it claims to support.

So now all I need is time and money.

    First, it's important to note Intimacies actually do nothing in terms of reward:

    Normally, Intimacies don't mean all that much. Serving them does not gain the character Willpower or Essence, for example. Instead they are a way to protect certaian things the character care sabout from socially adept characters ... while providing those same socialites with handles they can use to manipulate the character....     And, Motivation only scores back a single Willpower point for a whole scene acting on it, normally. You can get more points by a) sleeping, b) stunting, or c) limit breaking. So in this sense, players are more rewarded to have their Exalted either rest or freak out in action, with the motivation being merely sprinkles of reward to the action sundae they're encouraging.

    The experience system appears to be designed with the specific idea of not letting the different characters grow too far apart in abilities in the group rather than any actual reward in mind. Basically, the awesomest roleplayer and the wallflower should only see a difference in rewards after 20-30 sessions of play... and even then, it's going to amount to a couple of extra powers, and that's it.

    My overall feeling is that these ideas were designed after hearing about how a lot of input mechanics work for indie games, but not actually playing them and seeing what it is that they really do...

  • shreyas
    April 2006

    Here's an easy modification that'll bite up social combat a bit:

    Clinch-analogue. (See physical combat rules.) When 'clinched,' you have imposed a new Motivation on your target(s). A character can only support as many Motivations as his max(Temperance,Essence). The rest get pushed down into a stack, submerged by more recent Motivations. (This allows monastic religious types and heroes to have complicated motivations, while still dominating mortals efficiently.)

    A social "crush" is a normal social attack performed while clinched. Social dodge is impossible. Basically, the idea here is that you impose a Motivation compatible to your goal, and then issue orders that align with that Motivation.

    Charm interfaces for this include mucking with the Motivation stack and messing with the reward structures for Motivations; consider a Motivation reward like "get (2*Essence) motes each turn you act directly for your Motivation" or "get motes when you ignore danger because of your Motivation" or "get motes when you abandon opportunities to work for your M." and so on...

  • shreyas
    April 2006 edited April 2006

    Matt,

    All Charms are extensions of a numerically-rated Trait. Depending on your magical heritage, it might be an Ability, an Attribute, or a Virtue, or something else.

    Generally a Charm either permanently improves your effectiveness in some way (one class of Charms provides extra Health Levels), or you spend resources to activate it and improve your effectiveness temporarily. The resources available are Willpower, Health Levels, and the most frequently-used one, motes of Essence, which are a fairly small unit. Most characters have a maximum reserve around 30+. Motes of Essence replenish very rapidly.

    In the new edition, there is a general template for Charms that provide more dice or successes, but the bulk of any heritage's Charms section is Trait-specific Charms with special, specific, rule-breaking effects. (For instance, in Ride there is a Charm that gives your mount the ability to fly and the confidence to do so.)

  • bankuei
    April 2006
    Charms are the kewl powerz of Exalted. Often they cost "Essence" which is the power points resource of the game, and occassionally they may cost Willpower as well, which is a much more limited resource. A few Charms are just "always on" and cost nothing at all once you possess them.

    There's several categories of Charms, which determines whether you can activate them only on your action, or in reaction defensively etc. Basically, think of how Magic the Gathering has "Instants/Interrupts/etc." and Exalted has an equivalent.

    You're only supposed to be allowed to activate one charm until your next action, but you can also use "Combos" that let you activate any number of them -in- the combo . (and yes, there's rules for what can go in a Combo, what can't etc.)

    Effectively, the tactical decisions of Exalted are about the resource management of Essence, Willpower, and timing/use of Charms.
