\n So I'm knee-deep in making 64 example characters when I whine to Ryan about how hard it is. I am doing this because I am OCD about this kind of thing. There are eight Origin archetypes that describe in broad terms the type of life your character had when she was growing up. Then there are eight archetypes that describe the circumstances in which she left home and went to the temple. 8 times 8 equals creative exhaustion for yours truly, even though I've got plenty of playtest characters to choose from.

Ryan then tries to smack some sense into me. This would be like if D&D came with example characters of every possible combination of species and class. It'd be huge! From the Evil Hat side of things, Spirit of the Century only had three example characters despite a lot more archetypes. So, it'd be better if I stuck to a small group of signature, inspiring characters and let everything else be open for players to interpret without my baggage.

That's all well and good, but I still don't feel like Do has the advantaged position that either of those games has. In D&D's case, they're using signature characters to show optimal character builds for monster fightin'. Do is a different sort of game, so there really is no "optimal" Aide/Runaway or Dreamer/Orphan combo.

Both D&D and Spirit of the Century also have the benefit of strong roots in a well-established genre, "fantasy hack n' slash" for the former and "two-fisted pulp adventures" for the latter. You can afford to just show a Rocketeer and Rosie the Riveter to get the gist of a pulp adventure game, but "coming-of-age road trip" isn't quite as iconic.

So, the trick is thinking of some iconic signature characters, both as a way to demonstrate a variety of possible characters you can play and to really nail down the baseline of this genre.

Oh, and more broadly, how do you feel about signature characters in general?

    \n Shreyas is right. Signature characters blow, if you mean in the White Wolf sense where they're actually canonical and you shape the setting for the chaacters and characters for the setting. I was talking with Elizabeth recently about how Nobilis' example of play opens with a giant fight scene (because R. Sean heard players were having trouble running fights in Nobilis) even though Nobilis isn't really about fights, so it gives players a weird picture of the game. There's this essential tension in all examples, basically, between being demonstrative, showing the players how the game works, and being representative, showing players what playing the game is actually like. WW signature characters, IMO, generally mix these up all over the place and uber-fail. They're not functional characters that any player would actually play, especially not as a starting character, so they basically only demonstrate the themes of the setting and stuff, which makes them super boring. They're not characters so much as giant walking stereotypes and are therefore bad example characters for people picking up the game.

    A better question to ask, rather than how many, is what purpose(s) do you want these example characters to serve?

    \n Signature characters are awesome!

    Unlike a lot of games that simply say "OK, make your dude" (but lacking any characters to show you how its done), signature characters (when stats are provided) show you the various characters that can be created. Each character is an example, and there is no such thing in RPGs as "too many examples". Also, they could be used in in-game text to be an anchor, which the reader can follow through the book as they read rules examples, setting text, and so on.

    With 64 signature characters (or, well, 8) you could demonstrate what a "normal" character would look like, a normal character with a different build, a normal character with 1-2 "weird points", a totally weird/out there character, and so on. Plus, they become a stable of NPCs that can be used in play. Or, if you provide the stats, they could also be used as PCs for quick play.

    The only thing where signature characters become problematic is:
    * When the signature characters do things that are impossible for the PCs to do (see: Some WoD books, and many console games; "Cut Scene vs When the player has control of the character").
    * When the game talk on forums and the like focuses more on the coolness of the background characters than actual play (I see this in various Exalted threads in RPGNet... however, they're balanced with triple the number of AP threads, so it's not "bad" or anything. Though clearly, some folks think more about the awesome signature characters and their adventures than they do their own characters).

    -Andy

    \n The only RPG I can think of that does signature characters quite the way I want is Yuuyake Koyake, which of course most of you can't read. YK introduces a signature character for each of the six animal types (the cover shows each of them in their human and animal forms), and over the course of the book you get to know them through seeing them in both gameplay examples and artwork that shows them in a great variety of situations. To me this is a better way to give the reader a feel for the overall experience of the game than having different characters in each illustration. Not only could I make a character like Suzune, but my character will get to live in a world where she might chat with a spider goddess, attract the curiosity of adventurous young boys, or sit with a tanuki friend eating soba noodles. That draws my interest a lot more than a bunch of generic pictures of characters would.

    I've played YK twice, and I've yet to see a player make a character that was derivative of the sample ones. OTOH, while the book gives you a very clear view of the personalities of these characters, it doesn't actually have stats for most of them.

    \n Daniel, here's my thoughts.

    Using a character as an example in the character creation rules to show how the steps are actually done with a real character...Useful.

    Unless there's some really strange character creation option that you really need to illustrate, having more than one such example...wasted space.

    Using that same character in your later rules examples, so you don't have to explain why the character in the example is trying to substitute skill X for skill Y because they don't have skill Y (or whatever) because the reader knows from the earlier example what this character can do...Useful.

    Having that character actually be some important figure in the canon...opinions will vary but I tend to dislike that (although perhaps not as stridently as Shreyas).

    Having lots of generic example characters built as NPCs in the back for when the GM needs Generic Kung Fu Palace Guard #17...Useful.

    Having multiple named NPC characters to serve as NPCs in the back...IMO...Useful only for a specific "scenario" / "module"...wasted space as part of the core rules (unless, like a Harper design, the core rules ARE a specific scenario).

    I may have found a compromise: A small group of characters, maybe four, but each with a sidebar noting possible variations on their background. Let's use Aang as an example:\n
    Pilgrim Aang the Last Airbender
    Spiritual/Runaway: Aang is a young monk at the peaceful Air Temple with a strong talent for airbending. He is 13 years old when he finds out that he is the current incarnation of the Avatar, the spirit who keeps all the worlds elemental forces in balance. Intimidated by the cosmic responsibility now resting on his shoulders, he runs away from the Air Temple and his destiny.

    Sidebar: What if Aang were a Warrior/Runaway?
    Sensing that the balance of power is tilting towards war, Aang tries to convince the monks of the air temple to take pre-emptive action. Being pacifists, they see no need to resort to violence even in self-defense. Frustrated with their lack of foresight, Aang runs away to find some one who will help him fight.

    Sidebar: What if Aang were a Spiritual/Orphan?
    Just as Aang is told that he is the Avatar, fires erupt all around the temple. Mechanized armies march up the sacred mountains, crushing holy buildings. Aang loses sight of the other monks in the ensuing chaos and barely escapes with his life.
    \nThe goal being that the "what if" questions help explain what those archetypes mean, but also ensure that the characters not set-in-stone "canon" characters in the WW/D&D style.
