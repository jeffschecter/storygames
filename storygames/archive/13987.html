\n I'm not sure if this is a new thing or just something I've been more keenly aware of lately, but I feel like when I have combat in RPGs it's either uninteresting, or is interesting as a sub-game that doesn't involve much role-playing.

So, how do you make combat not suck?

I'm interested in answers to this question from basically any perspective: players, GMs, and designers alike, since they're all part of how I experience RPGs.

    \n
    \n Design each encounter with unique goals. Never (or, at least, rarely) make the point of combat about one side annihilating the other side.

    In a game where you kill dragons by reducing their hit points to 0, there's only one way to kill a dragon.
    But there's several ways to get the princess back into the safety of the keep, when you've got a retinue of 12 guardsmen.

    Anima Prime (Christian Griffen's game) does this really well. Conflicts often revolve around Achievements, special goals that you work towards achieving. You can just try to take out your enemies, but it's often smarter (and more interesting) to use your maneuvers to succeed at those Achievements. Some grant you a victory condition for the conflict, others boost your attacks or lower the enemy's defenses, and some unlock interesting story avenues.

    \n I think it also helps to be able to read the group: what will excite and interest these particular players about this particular fight at this particular moment?

    Because really, there's no one magic formula for making a combat scene work. Depending on the circumstances, the people around the table might get the most fun out of a grueling battle where their utter defeat is likely, or they might just want to cut loose and show off how awesome their characters are by beating fifteen different kinds of snot out of some hopelessly outclassed cannon fodder. They might want to have to choose between several different goals, or they might want a climactic final showdown. They might want a fast-paced fight where a lot happens in a very short span of real time, or they might prefer to slow down and do the whole thing in "bullet time."

    Basically, if you're the one who's generating the opposition and setting the scene for these fights, everyone will benefit when you correctly identify what kind of combat is going to be the most appealing right now, and can quickly adapt to it. Sometimes you can get a sense of this from how everyone is playing before the fight starts, or from how they start a fight, or even (horrors!) by asking them questions about what they like and don't like.


    But I do agree with a lot of what's been said so far, and think that most of the advice given is so broadly applicable as to be nearly universal. Especially Mcdaldno's "design each encounter with unique goals" and JDCorley's "Location, location, location!" because those right there are wonderful ways to make every fight memorable and meaningful, instead of just being another goddamn generic 10x10 room with 2d6 kobolds. I think it's fun to have more choices available than "who are you attacking?", so anything that expands the possibilities of the combat scene (do you try to close the gates? do you set off the sprinkler systems to try and short out their laser guns? do you challenge their leader to single combat?) works for me.

    \n This is a vexing question. There are so many ways to approach it -- most contradictory!

    I might say: Don't design "combat encounters." For a certain game and situation, that would be great advice.

    I might say: Add cool terrain features to your combat encounters. Good advice for a different game.

    I might say: Emphasize the shock, blood, and horror of it. That'll work for several games.

    Each of those is terrible advice for some other games. It's hard to fathom how to start answering this question in the abstract, aside from the stock answer: Play lots of games to see different styles and approaches in action. Keep those tricks in your kit bag. Apply as needed.

    "How to make combat not suck in game X" (or game style X) is a different story. That's enough context to work with to provide practical answers.

    \n I agree with John as well. There's no easy answer to your question.

    However, I recently came to a realization about my own personal preferences concerning combat that might mirror your's, Ewen. I discovered that I need a certain amount of story every few minutes or I get bored with the mechanisms of the game. That is, I need to see the situation change in an interesting way. If thirty minutes pass and the only thing that's happened is that the monster is halfway down on HP and I'm low on arrows, that's not enough for me. And that means that nearly every gritty, time-consuming combat system out there won't work for me anymore. The only exception that I can think of is DitV, where resolution might take 20 or 30 minutes, but the situation changes so often that I remain entertained.

    So, now I make combat not suck by either playing games that don't have long processing times in their resolution, by playing games that offer dramatic situation changes as part of their resolution, or by hacking the shit out of the game I'm playing until it meets one of the previous two criteria.

    \n Speaking from a purely dramatic - may not work for every game - perspective\u2026

    - Opposition that's a credible threat. You know they can and will hurt you.

    - Fighting over something you care deeply about. More than just survival. Show us what is at stake.

    - Time pressure. Your back is to the wall and you must act now!

    - Interactive locations that can work in your favor or against you.

    - Ways to end the combat early if it drags, other than reducing hit points to zero.

    - The situation changes so that every round feels different.

    - Failure doesn't mean nothing happens. Failure means consequences.

    - Momentum shifts! It's darkest before the comeback. Fail forward towards success. The underdog always has a chance to pull off a victory at the very last second.

    - If it's clear who will win, skip to the end or change the situation so it isn't predictable.

    - Sell every action. It's not a loss of hit points\u2026 it's broken bones. Collateral damage is a plus!

    - Background music!

    Posted By: stefoidMy theory is the combat will suck in direct proportion to how irrelevant it is to your character's goals.
    I think you could modify that to "the combat will suck in direct proportion to how irrelevant it is to your goals as a player and your character's goals" for maximum accuracy.

    After all, the fun in gaming is had by the people playing -- it's a good idea to think about your own goals first, and to then structure your character's goals so that they will flow neatly into play situations where your goals as a player can be satisfied. That, to me, is the critical part in making non-sucky combat scenes: to figure out what (if anything) you find enjoyable about playing out a fight scene, and then adjust whatever needs to be adjusted (setup, mechanics, pacing, whatever) in order to get more enjoyable stuff and less sucky stuff.

    \n I used to play a lot of wargames and there came a point when I lost interest in war. I found the games too predictable. I realized that combat was a dance with very predictable steps.

    One side attacks
    The other side either stands or runs away immediately
    They clash
    One side breaks and runs away
    The winner pursues or doesn't
    If not pursued, the routing side rallies and counter-attacks
    Repeat ab infinitum

    You could practically run though this critical event to critical event rather than minute to minute.

    Anymore my games don't even include combat rules. I tell players to make up scenes. Combat can be something you make up in a scene. They just need to say who wins and what damage occures. So I've effectively cut out combat as a major part of the game. I'm more interested in other things.

    Chris Engle

    \nI think the easiest way to do that is to change the assumed ending conditions, which you can do as a near-invisible hack to most (but not all) systems. You just say that when characters are at 0 hit points or incapacitated or whatever, it means they are hurt but not dead. Sure, Riddle of Steel doesn't really work that way, but most games do.

    Then, after the fight is over, you have a bunch of groaning, injured folk on the losing side. And now you have to deal with them in some way.

    If you want, that can be turned on and off as the situation indicates. Basically, if either the GM or a player wants to have further interactions with the defeated, then they are available for that.

    The nice thing about having NPCs that persist after being defeated is that it lets you defeat your players without killing them. The PCs can be scared off or captured or whatever, without being killed.

    I'll make a comparison to LARP again, as I often do. In many live combat larps, characters that are defeated in combat end up unconscious and bleeding - if they bleed for 5 minutes without being tended to, they die. This makes it very difficult to defeat the players without killing them off. Other systems avoid this problem by saying that if you are defeated, you are incapacitated for 5 minutes, after which you regain your last hit point and you're OK again. This means that you can beat up the PCs pretty good without unintentionally murdering your entire campaign with a badly statted encounter. It doesn't mean that the players are immune to death - while incapacitated they can be murdered relatively easily. But giving an NPC license to finish someone off is separate from just defeating them in a fight. If you want, you can have NPCs try to capture a couple PCs. Or take some of their stuff. Or other things that add more to the story than killing people off does.
