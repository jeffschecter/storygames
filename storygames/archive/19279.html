I'm struggling to think of examples, but maybe Eclipse Phase or Into the Odd would fit? Can anyone add to the list?

What systems or practices make for a good sensation of the surreal in a sci-fi setting? Whether that's entering "weird space" like in old Star Trek episodes or PCs exploring more metaphysical locations as surreal entities themselves, or some other method.

Is random generation of events and generally obscuring the nature of the game-world from the players an essential part of preserving the mystery of the surreal or can we be more open and definite and say things like "So this is how the Howling plains of Nggul-Tur work" with the expectation that players don't meta-game away the challenge the plains present? Does anyone have any experience of playing in either way?

In terms of system or mechanical considerations. Is surrealness something that is added as "fluff" around a more grounded/trad play procedure, or does anyone have experience of rules that invoke or encourage the weird?

Not terribly certain how I want this discussion to progress, but hopefully we can go over the terminology, get some examples and chat about surrealness in gaming generally.

    Don't Rest Your Head? I mean it kind of depends what the 'sci-fi' part of 'surreal sci-fi' means. The madness talents of DRYH are generally quite surreal, and if you ignore the book setting and instead construct the world around the PCs and their abilities, you usually end up with a coherently surreal experience. That said, the system is geared towards horror/action rather than the more contemplative strangeness that I associate with (for example) Moebius' art.

    I do think there are specific challenges for surreality in roleplaying, because the fiction is already largely or wholly invented; part of the impact of surrealism is the sudden, unexpected juxtaposition of ideas and objects in a way that transforms their context, and points to a concealed meaning beyond the immediate, obvious one. The problem is that in order for there to be a meaning beyond the immediate, obvious one, there first has to be an immediate, obvious meaning that has general consensus. You can't have surreality without reality, and RPGs are already working overtime to construct that first baseline level of reality and invest it with enough meaning and coherence to resonate with players. I think almost anyone who has tried to pursue this kind of roleplaying has experienced what happens when you put too much focus on the sur- and not enough on the -real -- players just start stacking more and more ridiculous things on top of each other, without any tether to a baseline that would give them context. (This is often referred to as 'gonzo' gaming, and it happens in lots of other genres of gaming too.)

    This is why surreal sci-fi seems doubly-challenging to me, because the usual solution to this problem is to use a contemporary, familiar setting as a baseline -- Don't Rest Your Head, along with most urban fantasy, works best when you start as close to home as possible. But surrealism in the context of a futuristic society, or an alien planet, becomes more complicated -- unless, again, the PCs are arriving there from somewhere closer to real, so that the sci-fi elements ARE the surreal elements in question.

Both have crossovers, genre-wise, but have distinctly separate implications in terms of interacting with a setting.     \n Revisiting my A/B/M notion, but leaving out the total randomness... For any given NPC (and maybe locations as well), I see three levels into which the surrealism can be injected: Appearance, Behaviour and Meaning. Appearance refers to observable physical details, Behaviour refers to mechanical functions (skills, dice rolls, etc), and Meaning refers to narrative purpose. I place them in this order because when I lay them out, I feel like the depth of the interpretation increases as we go from A to B to M. For instance, let's examine blending "policeman" and "dog":

    Appearance is symbolic - It behaves as a policeman (it has policeman skills and says things policemen say) and it means the same thing narratively as a policeman (the eyes of the state are upon you) but it looks like a dog. Straight-up anthropomorphism is trad fare for fantasy fiction, cartoons and childrens' literature - they are full of such analogs. It is simply accepting of the fact that "this dog is a policeman". Notwithstanding the fact that he looks like a dog, you may (and should) respond to him as you would a policeman.

    Behaviour is symbolic - It looks like a policeman (wears a uniform, carries a weapon) and it means the same thing narratively as a policeman (the eyes of the state are upon you) but it behaves like a dog (barks, growls, and sniffs your butt). This is a deeper statement because it seems to be literally likening policemen to dogs. Satirical fiction and surrealistic social commentary are full of such tropes (Pink Floyd's "Animals" or Orwell's "Animal Farm"), which push a casual metaphor into an ontological reality of the fictional world.

    Meaning is symbolic - It looks like a policeman (wears a uniform, carries a weapon) and behaves as a policeman (it has policeman skills and says things policemen say) but it means the same thing narratively as a dog (it's kept as a pet, it begs for treats, it guards your house, etc). This seems to be the most surreal of all three possibilities, because we cannot trust our first impressions (which tend to be based on appearance or behaviour) to give us a reasonable impression of what the actual Meaning is.

    If you displace 2 of these 3 traits, the thing gets much harder to interpret. If you displace only a partial trait (e.g., the policeman's hands are dog paws), the interpretation gets more subtle or refined (and also somehow creepier).

    All of the above is assuming that the "surreal" elements do in fact have some symbolic meaning, rather than being just a totally random stack of mixed-up details. But you didn't actually stipulate that, so I'll stop here for now.

      \n Fair enough, Paul. The OP features a beautiful image by Moebius (the late Jean Giraud), brilliant illustrator and author of "The Airtight Garage of Jerry Cornelius" and many other things, to whom Mike alluded in the OP. Those of you who remember Heavy Metal magazine in the 80s will certainly remember this strip, it was his magnum opus. In the strip, Jerry Cornelius (name later changed due a perceived snub by Michael Moorcock) adventures within a massive asteroid housing a pocket universe which contains hundreds of worlds, all with different forms of life, types of people, and laws of physics, some of them still forming. It gets super weird, in beautiful and funny ways.

      Before diving back into mechanical/game design issues again I'll back up a little bit. You asked about big-S Surrealism. I think Daniel got that into a small nutshell very well:\n
      ...the mechanical creation of surreal images was... what the Surrealists spent most of their time doing. Automatic writing, question-and-answer games, exquisite corpse, and the like [...] The key theme of all these exercises was the removal of conscious agency and choice; precisely to avoid someone (say, the GM) deciding which elements 'should' go together, or would be 'most weird' or whatever. The suggestion (or fervent belief, whichever) of the Surrealists being that this bypassing-of-consciousness would allow the subconscious reality of the mind to show through.
      \nOne example many people are familiar with, even if they didn't know the name, is "Exquisite Corpses". In this game, you all take turns adding a word, sentence or paragraph to an ongoing text (the size of the desired text is predetermined before play begins), but you aren't allowed to look at what was written before (or you are only allowed to see the last bit of it). It can also be played with drawings: You draw a head (without showing it to anyone), then fold the paper over so the next person can only see the two little lines where the neck ends... then they draw the torso, fold the paper so only the hip-lines are showing, and the third person draws the legs. Maybe not the best example of a Surrealistic mechanic, but it's a good enough starting-point: basically we're talking about bypassing conscious intent in order to access subconscious "truth" and generate dream-like symbolism that is both surprising and evocative, even for its own creators.

      A possible tangent that occurs to me is the difference between "removal of conscious agency" and "subconscious reality showing through" - they are not necessarily the same thing. For example: We all remove our own conscious agency whenever we reach for the dice, but only some of us believe that our unconscious minds can influence the result of that roll.

      So what Daniel was pointing out earlier is that Big-S Surrealism could really only arise from a mechanic that was radically and consciously blind. Such "blindness" may be suitable for some types of collaborative/freeform storygames, but it would be much trickier to do it within a typical RPG mechanic. On the other hand, we have "small-s surrealism" ("Twin Peaks" might be a good example) which over time tends to verge into either (a) direct allegory, or (b) a weird world but a totally stable one ("You can use a snail as a radio? Ok then, I use my snail to call for help"). Once you get used to it, it's no longer surreal for you. It's just fiction. That's why I kept prattling on about "flux" and randomizing mechanisms, although both Mike and Daniel pointed out (in different ways) that these techniques would have to be carefully designed and managed, lest they devolve into meaninglessness, or cause more problems than they solve.

      So part of the confusion thus far has been in separating those two meanings of the S word. This has been paralleled by a discussion about the "scope" of surreality that might work in various game types, and some brainstorming on mechanics. You can ignore the stuff about Foucault and Nabokov.

      Yeah, I don't think it's that important. Let's get back to the more general topic.

      My favourite suggestion so far is to make use of player-generated images and ideas to guide GM-curated/introduced elements. Repurposing the players' ideas in unfamiliar ways is a great way to evoke surreal reactions, and mimic a certain quality of dreams -- that they seem like they are some part of you, talking to you from outside yourself.

      The game that this brings to mind most clearly is A Penny For My Thoughts, which IMO is the closest thing we currently have to a straight up surrealist sci-fi game. But the thing is that it is not superficially* surreal, in that usually the moment-to-moment narratives that are described tend to be fairly realistic. But the underlying mode of play -- where you 'remember' things for the first time, and then are forced to give up agency at the very moment where your actions are most decisive -- is extremely surreal. And of course the basic premise is also squarely in the sci-fi/surreality camp.

      And, tying things back in, it also makes use of exactly the technique mentioned above -- the seeds for all the memories are player-generated sensations/images/phrases that they write down in advance, with absolutely no context for their usage in the game. The players are simply told to write down something short and evocative. Now whenever I play Penny, I almost invariably write down a striking sensation from my own personal experience -- whether my actual history/memory, or just something that is vivid to me in that moment of play -- and equally invariably this adds a layer of surreality to the scenes where that sensation is re-introduced and made into the basis of somebody else's life.

      So I think anyone who is interested in producing evocative surrealist play should at a minimum read/play Penny and think about how/why it works. And I think a big part of why it works is the combination of a) soliciting player-generated, immediate (i.e. if not subconscious then at least not premeditated) sensations and then b) introducing those elements in a context of deeply-personal identity-altering moments, whose underlying mechanics are actively undermining the players'/characters' feelings of control over the situation at hand. Combining loss of control with deep investment in identity means that the players are motivated to invest the results with meaning, even though the meaning is coming from unexpected and exterior sources.

      If there's no investment, there's no reason to try and figure out what the surreal situation means; it can just mean nothing, it can just be a crazy melting clock. Penny creates the investment with a super-grabby premise -- the whole game is about figuring out who you are, and every scene is clearly presented as crucially important to answering the question.

      I think we actually have two sets of questions going on here, which shouldn't be surprising since the whole field has this fuzzy dichotomy between "RPGs" and "storygames" (scarequotes to indicate I'm being pithy for rhetorical purposes). Like a variation on that famous supreme court definition of pornography, the line is something that we can't define (because everywhere we look we see examples of games that step blatantly across it). But we also can't deny that the field of interactive/fictional/narrative/collaboration/immersion design possesses two core bodies of thought and approach, which are situated in what seems like diametrical opposition to each other. So we have:

      1. Questions of the application of surreality to "storygames" in the sense of collaborative emergent fiction with roleplaying in it. Here, as in the "Penny For My Thoughts" example above, a sense of surreality can be brought about directly by the way players interact with the rules (independently of whether the fiction itself seems surreal).

      2. Questions of the application of surreality to "RPGs" in the sense of simulated fictional worlds (or universes, or multiverses) with roleplaying in them. In the OP, this was referred to obliquely as "a more grounded/trad play procedure", in which we might expect to feel the sense of surreality arising more indirectly, i.e. coming more from the fiction itself.

      I expect that the answers to these two sets of questions will be very different, even though we all blur that line on a daily basis. It's kinda like racism. It's not there, but it's there.

      \n Very interesting discussion here! And actually, seeing this discussion is what gave me the push to finally join Story Games. Hope it's okay I'm jumping in here.

      When considering systems or rules that might simulate the surreal, pacing mechanisms immediately come to mind. In many Philip K. Dick stories, reality gradually unravels to reveal (sort of a tongue twister: reality, unravel, reveal) something that was previously concealed. What is real and what isn't? Glimpses of the surreal, or clues to an alternate reality/existence, often begin as minor things. These minor things could even be confused with a character's paranoia. Over time, clues or glimpses into the surreal become more intense, until they seamlessly blend with what the character knows to be real, and the entire fabric of reality begins to be questioned.

      In this sense, surreal doesn't have to be melting clocks and crazy visuals. It can also be feelings and a gradual decay of reality. The most common pacing mechanism, hit points, could reflect a growing detachment to reality and thus an increase in surreal happenings. Or, it could mean that, amidst surreal happenings/environments, the character is gradually becoming unhinged and integrated with that reality, and thus distant from a stable starting point. Again, I think the most interesting aspects of surreal settings are the way they make characters feel, and not necessarily what they see.

      I'm interested in keeping the human element attached to the surreal. Whether the surreal be a product of one's imagination or paranoia, it can be a powerful and engaging agent if rules are attached to an environment -- but not static. Surrealism isn't necessarily random. It does have basis in the human experience, and is dependent on the thoughts and ideas of those who experience it. And the idea of surreal environments having their own set of pacing tools (sort of making them into fluid NPCs) is appealing.

      \nNoyce. I am reminded of the extradimensional journeys in William Gibson's story "Hinterlands" (in Burning Chrome). In that story, the other realm was so incomprehensible to the human mind that the majority of travelers who came back were left traumatized, in a state of catatonia. Others returned dead. So maybe that's what can happen at worst, eh?

      Let's see... For each Daytrip, we might need to...
      - Determine axes of slippage
      - Determine degree of slippage along each axis
      - Determine the resulting "reality type"
      - Determine specific details, hardships, and maguffins
      - Create Locations/NPCs/Objects/Events

      When I say "Determine", I'm talking about something that could be a random process with a solo GM (roll on charts, draw cards, etc), or it could be a collaborative group process, or some combination of the two (structured brainstorming, etc). For a more narrativistic approach, you might leave out the creation of specifics and just "play to find out" (taking input from the players, perhaps intermingled with your own determinations, etc). I think either approach could produce an interesting game (and the backstory definitely allows enough room for tons of cool stories to arise). But unless some funky content-processing mechanics are implemented, I'm pretty sure that the more input players have into these decisions, the less surprising (and therefore the less "surreal") the resulting experiences will seem to them subjectively.

      \n For possible use in DayTrippers, I'm brainstorming on simple rules for eidetic projection and surreal visions. Sorta like Oblique Strategies for GMs running surrealist games. Here are a few I've come up with...

      TWEAKING THE NATURAL WORLD:
      Think of a natural law or process, or look at the natural laws and processes which are at play in the scene and choose one. Then\u2026
      \u2022\tReverse it or degrade it
      \u2022\tMake it work on different elements or objects
      \u2022\tHave it be controlled by a person/some people
      \u2022\tHave it be controlled by a machine or a system
      \u2022\tThink of an impossible way to exploit it and do it
      \u2022\tMake it produce the wrong things
      \u2022\tMake it produce inorganic things

      TWEAKING THE CONSTRUCTED WORLD:
      Think of a type of common machine or device, or look at the machines and devices which are at play in the scene and choose one. Then\u2026
      \u2022\tGive it different input or from a difference source
      \u2022\tMake it create a different kind of output
      \u2022\tMake it run on an impossible fuel source
      \u2022\tMake it produce organic things
      \u2022\tMake it intelligent, emotional, or communicative
      \u2022\tHave it be controlled by a natural law or process
      \u2022\tMake it ridiculously large or small

      TWEAKING CHARACTERS:
      Think of a person, archetype, or character, or look at the characters in the scene and choose one. Then\u2026
      \u2022\tAlter their age
      \u2022\tAlter their gender
      \u2022\tGive them an animal part
      \u2022\tGive them a machine or tool part
      \u2022\tPut a machine inside them
      \u2022\tGive them a purely symbolic part
      \u2022\tFuse them with another person or role
      \u2022\tTake away their greatest asset
      \u2022\tTurn their emotions up to 11 or down to 0
      \u2022\tEnhance one of their skills to legendary levels
      \u2022\tSwap their function with another person or type
      \u2022\tInvert, replace or conflict their core motivation
      \u2022\tPut them inside something symbolic or alive
      \u2022\tRemove something important from them

      \n Reading this thread had got me thinking about incorporating magical realism (not surrealism, but close). Specifically, I'm thinking about something Murakami does a few times where two worlds exist in parallel, one the everyday world and the other dreamlike. Perhaps the characters are transposed between the two at random or by GM fiat. In fact, maybe it's not even immediately obvious that they've moved across. Depending on the specific flavor of unreality of the parallel world, they might not notice until they tried to touch something or reached into their pockets for something that's no longer there. The parallel world gives them a different way to approach their problems (it prevents some actions, but allows others that are impossible in the normal world). And the parallel world is just as dangerous, perhaps more so, than the normal one.

      \n I don't have much to add in the gaming universe, everyone else has already stated my big go-tos. However, I have three things to mention for inspiration.

      Bizarro fiction, specifically books like Carlton Mellick III writes. Seems right up the alley of bizarre cult like surrealism. It's very NSFW and pornographic at points but why not, no worse than Metabarons. http://carltonmellick.com/books/

      Which leads to Alejandro Jodorowsky. His comic series Metabarons might as well be considered surreal sci fi, it's way off the map. As well as his movies like Holy Mountain, though it's not sci fi it is surreal.

      Lastly, an amazing comic book from the Heavy Metal books is Requiem Chevalier Vampire. His artwork and stories have a very painting like surreal quality to them. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Requiem_Chevalier_Vampire

      And that's about all I have. If you are not aware of the above, then happy You day! If you were already aware, then others can learn. Enjoy and let us know how the surreal gaming goes.
