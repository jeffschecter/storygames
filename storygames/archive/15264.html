\n ... are professor M.A.R. Barker's (of Tekumel fame) so-called "Perfected" rules.

Starting at least as early as OD&D, Mr. Barker formulated rules that go like this:

1) We both roll dice.
2) If you roll high, your view of reality prevails.
3) If I roll high, my view of reality prevails.
4) If we're close, we negotiate.

(I've whipped up a nice version of them here: http://analogkonsole.wordpress.com/2011/10/17/perfekt-perfected)

Just imagine, folks! Some 30+ years ago, we had it all: narration rights, conch shell mechanics. I GM'ed old-school Shadowrun yesterday, using the prof's rules -- and it was a very, very, very good session. Just as my players and I like it, the mechanics took a backseat, but subtly determined who got to narrate. Great stuff. And I thought octaNe was great -- but Perfected is way better

    \n Hans:
    I've been emailing with Jeff Berry, one of Mr. Barker's long-time players. He told me that Mr. Barker used these rules with the Tekumel groups he trusted. He had tried the "official", very D&D-like "Empire of the Petal Throne" rules, but didn't like them too much. So he used a system of d100 rolls, with low=positive effect, and high=negative effect. Then, after he had gained some insight into the players and their characters, he'd switch to Perfected. Those rules were never published, but are pure M.A.R. Barker and thus, pure Tekumel.

Personally, I think these questions and the thinking behind it are the remnants of old-time D&D play where you had to roll certain numbers or better to succeed. If you trust your co-players and the process, you'll either find you don't need more rules than "Perfected", or you'll add stuff that's specifically yours.

    \n I don't think it's really an issue of social jockeying, although that is an area which can trip it up. For me the inherent incompleteness stems from guidance for such basic things as who the players represent.

    From the way the rule set is presented in the original post it would seem that we are meant to play the game at a purely meta level...whose view of reality prevails surely isn't a character level concern outside of Amber/Nobilis esque play. And yet the presentation is so succinct that it leaves open the possibility that the RAW is merely shorthand and not meant to imply meta level play at all.

    One of the problems with a meta level system that has no real teeth comes when no one is actually bringing real adversity. When everyone is dedicated to making the other players' awesome and all are operating from appreciation of the same tropes it's easy to all just know what cool thing needs to happen next. So I win and I just know that X is the great next twist, and you win and you just know that X is the great next twist and so essentially we're group writing a novel.

    Contrast this paradigm with one of active character advocacy. Where my job as a player when I win isn't to do what I think is cool or best for the "story" but to drive hard to fulfill the goals and ambitions of my character while you are driving hard to fulfill the goals and ambitions for your character.

    Anyone whose spent any time at all thinking about such things knows that both paradigms can work but are utterly incompatible if different players are operating on different assumptions in the same game. So which way is it?

    That doesn't even begin to touch on whether the "version of reality" includes author/director stance powers or is strictly actor.

    There are literally dozens of questions like that (who gets to say what when, IIEE, veto powers, on and on) that have to be answered before functional play can happen. These are the real rules of the game, the real system being used. I'm reasonably certain these are the sorts of things Bob's been trying to tease out. Now clearly the answers are meant tobe filled in however your particular group wishes. For me it's the absence of such details, or even any indication that such answers are needed that justifies references to this rule as a "toy"rather than a "game".

      \n OK... some bits from our application of Perfected:

      Character creation

      We apply different kinds of character creation.

      a) When we have newbies in the group, I usually let them roll 3d6 in order, for Courage, Intelligence, Agility, Charisma and Strength, respectively. When we're playing old school fantasy, every character gets 30 "hit points" and an "Attack" and a "Defense" value. Those numbers are only there to help me gauge a character's "efficiency" in the game (strictly in a SIM sense: how good is he at doing something). This also helps a new player to get a better feel for his character.

      b) When everyone at the table has at least a bit of experience in roleplaying, I ask them to write up some "Good stuff" ("things" -- skills, attributes, friendships, items -- that are good for the character) and "Bad stuff" (things that are bad for the character). No numbers, though. When I need to know how good or how bad a character is at/in something, I ask the player to specifiy.

      c) Sometimes, we don't define characters in any structured way. Players just write down the character's name and the rough concept.

      Equipment is either bought (old school fantasy) or simply written down when it fits the character concept.


      I hope this helps?

      To me it seems like the opposite of Go. Go's rules produce the things you are talking about -- by being very specific and covering 100% of all possible game cases.       Why even bother with the dice? Why not just start telling a story and agree by popular acclaim whose version of reality should prevail?
      \nYou could also play it without dice but with a game mechanic. Lets say you all start with ten tokens in a GM less game. Every time two participants have a conflict, follow these steps:

      1) The "instigator" of the conflict bet any number of that person's tokens.
      2) The "defender" may call with the same number of tokens to narrate the outcome. The instigator gets the pot.
      3) If the defender doesn't call, the instigator will loose what it bet to the defender, but gets to narrate the outcome.
      4) If the bet in combination with the outcome of the conflict is unfair, we negotiate.

      With these rules, the players have a lot of decision to make in comparison to a dice roll. How important is the conflict? What consequence will it bring to loose to many tokens? That sort of things.

      \n I guess one thing I would have wanted to emphasize if I had been in on this discussion is that in a game like this, the world itself is part of the 'system.' It is not an accident, I think, that a lot of the 'old school' games I played in that pushed in a mechanics light/'talking adventure'/freeform direction, or toward certain more recently named techniques, were ones in which there was a very detailed setting, either a 'literary' one such as Middle Earth or Tekumel, or one crafted in play through a long 'campaign' of adventures.
