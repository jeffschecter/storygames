\n I feel that Christopher Kubasik's point got lost in that massive thread. I have no idea how what I'm about to say relates to that thread but it was definitely inspired by that thread. I want to talk about games that require practice. To some extent ALL games probably take practice. The degree to which a game already speaks to your past history is probably the degree to which it takes less practice. Dogs in the Vineyard for example is pretty much everything I ever tried to achieve with the game Chill so I can play that game in my sleep. There are four games right now in my collection that I look at and instantly feel like they require practice (to me).

Sorcerer
Spione
Dirty Secrets
Capes

Some of you know that I have this project called Sorcerer Unbound which is supposed to be my "How To" manual for Sorcerer. If you take a look at my hard drive you'll notice about 20 documents with that title and various dates appended on to it. That's because this project is my Great White Whale and I've tried every approach known to man from a high level theory perspective to a linear step-by-step approach and I'm never happy with it. About a month or so ago I hit upon the core confounding question of this project, "Why is it so dirt simple to explain Sorcerer in the specifics of actual play, but so fucking hard to explain it in the abstract?" And like Ron's Band metaphor I hit upon the answer in a musical analogy.

The core Sorcerer book is like the fingering chart for a flute. It tells you where to put your fingers to play a C note, and A note, etc. I think to some extent it fails to tell you how to read music but I think that has to do with Ron's target audience. What it does not tell you how to do is compose music. So when you come to me with a specific piece of music it's very easy to tell you where to put your fingers, how to transition, whether to blow hard or soft. But if you come to me and ask me how one composes a piece of music I'm going to look at you rather blankly.

Quite frankly Sorcerer requires a bit of artistry with regard to mastering what is basically this simple process 1) identifying conflicts -> 2) interpreting the outcome of conflicts and -> 3) how to apply victory dice. Four groups can more or less be faced with the same fictional set of circumstances and apply the rules in four different ways all of which would be "legal" and the differences would largely be to due to where each of the four groups emotional and creative investment was. This is why trying to translate established fiction into game mechanics is nonsensical. Quite frankly I can take any piece of completed fiction and come up with four different ways the Sorcerer mechanics could have produced that fiction as output.

Now I'm going to anticipate an objection. The objection I think I might hear is, "But Jesse, isn't this EXACTLY what we were trying to get away from? Rules that require GM/Player fiat to get working? If you can apply the Sorcerer rules in multiple ways to the same situation then aren't we just back to Rule 0/The Golden Rule?"

NO.

There is vast difference between Rule 0/The Golden Rule and a self-consistent rule base that still requires artistry to apply. Rule 0 actually undercuts what could otherwise be a rather functional social dynamic. Most people I talk to agree that I don't need rules to defend myself from dick weed players. I have a front door, a lock and an invitation to leave to defend myself from dick weed players. I'm assuming a socially and creatively synergistic group of people as given. When you take that for granted you realize that stuff like the IIEE parts of Forge theory are actually WAY more important than Creative Agenda.

So if you HAVE a socially and creatively synergistic group what do rules matter? Such is the core criticism of System Does Matter. However, some how that wonderfully functional social and creative synergy has to be given form and function and that's what games of any sort do. What Rule 0 does is undercut the form and function. The game says here is the process and structure of play up until someone (usually the GM) says it isn't. At best this undercuts any value of having any kind of organization at all, and at worst it opens the group up to even well meaning power plays for creative recognition.

Compare that to say Maneuvers in Spione. During Maneuvers in Spione if it's your turn what you say is, when it's not your turn what you say is a suggestion to the person whose turn it is. That very simple rule which can be applied consistently without change suddenly cuts out 98% of the problems you get in a game where say three people all shout out what their character is "doing" and someone has to manually organize it all into a coherent sequence before it can be accepted into the fiction. Add in that the text says the purpose of Maneuvers is to place the principle spies into The Cold (a very specific kind of conflict as explained in the text) and now the creative dialog among our socially and creatively synergistic group has form and function and in play this flows rather smoothly.

Notice however that the content might still be crap. We might not be very good at putting spies into the cold. Our turns might still be full of sound and fury signifying nothing. Even though we have a consistently applicable rule base with no hint of rule 0 we might still suck. Quality Spione play takes practice.

Sorcerer has more fiddly bits but the principle applies. Same with Dirty Secrets. Doubly so for Capes.

Now in some sense I feel like I'm time traveling. I feel like I'm saying something we all agreed with back in 2002. But maybe some lessons are worth repeating because I also feel like there's been this over seizing of System Does Matter to the point of System Should Do The Work For You. Spione does not produce espionage fiction through cold chess like applications of its rules. Dirty Secrets does not produce quality hardboiled detective fiction through an arms length mechanistic application of its procedures. What these games do is channel your already in place, pre-play commitment to producing quality espionage or hardboiled detective fiction into a functional group process. They require emotional engagement and creative and artistic applications of their procedures. They require passionate play. They require practice.

I think some of the weaker designs out there try and create rules that either substitute for having a socially and creatively synergistic group OR try to protect you from sucky contributions and thereby trying to guarantee that every game produces a quality experience regardless of the quality of input. I think some of the weaker TEXTS have wonderfully functional games that fail to tell you key social elements that are required for play. Spione would be a mess if it didn't explain the concept of The Cold to you. Dirty Secrets would be a mess if the entire second half of the book didn't exist.

To tie this back to Christopher's post I think what he was suggesting is that we as designers would have a lot to learn by practicing our existing games until we are good of them. Had a bad time the first time you played Capes? Re-read the book, read AP posts, go back try again. Just cause you can't play a symphony the first time you take the flute out of the box doesn't mean the instrument is broken.

Play Sorcerer until you are good at it.

Play Spione until you are good at it.

Play Dirty Secrets until you are good at it.

Play Capes until you are good at it.

Play any game you think takes practice until you are good at it.

Then take what you've learned and design from there.

Jesse

    \n Well, when I say that a game requires practice I don't necessarily mean that your first attempt will be bad. I've played Dirty Secrets and Spione twice each and both games were fun and awesome but... I want to be better. I want my Spione games to be more coherent. I want my Dirty Secrets games to have a little more "action" in them.

    Action isn't quite the right word. So one of the best pieces of advice in the book is that players should have a working theory of the case and just play to that. One of the skills to master when playing Dirty Secrets is to learn how to act without motivation and speculate on motivation in retrospect. I notice players hesitate to really seize a character and have them do something proactive because they don't know what the character is up to. The skill to cultivate is to learn how to just decide in the short term what you *think* the character is up to, play to that with gusto, but still be willing to revise what you think if another player takes what you did in another direction.

    So far my Dirty Secrets games haven't had a lot of motion. They almost feel like point-n-click adventure games where you always find the same characters in the same set pieces and all that's changed is the dialog options that have opened up since the investigator was last here. Which sometimes hardboiled detective novels look like but they aren't. Characters move around and do stuff while the investigator isn't looking and then the investigator stumbles into the consequences. I want more of that in my Dirty Secrets play.

    \n If your point is that some games improve more with practice than others, I agree. Or that every game becomes better with practice. That's non-controversial.
    \n
    \nBut I think that a game that requires practice to be fun is not a good design. Since so many games are fun right out of the box. Oh, I could imagine a game that marketed itself as "advanced" and gave you exercises to ramp up to where you need to be to play it or something. But the expectation is, quite rightly, that the game should work pretty well in the first session of play.
    \n
    \nIf "System Matters" then it should do what it claims to. Just as D&D produces reasonably fun gamism the first time you try it.
    \n
    \nFor instance, let's take Sorcerer. I think that it's not hard. Of course, this will vary for different people. I'm probably like Ron in enough ways that it was a pretty easy game for me to pick up and get into (my recent cricisms of it aside). It meets my criteria, therefore, because it does what it claims to very well, right out of the gate. No practice needed.
    \n
    \nNow, if you needed practice to become good at it... well not all games are created equal for all players. It may have required some practice and advice to get it to work for you. But that's not the same as saying that this is true for all.
    \n
    \nTo this extent, I'd go so far as to say that it's going to be very hard to generalize games that require more work to become expert with than those that do not. For instance, I had no trouble at all with Rolemaster, being a math geek and all. Others find it incredibly hard to deal with because of their distaste for all of the math, and probably had to work very hard to get it to be as functional as it could be for them. On the other hand, there have been systems that others have taken to like ducks to water, that I have had to struggle with. This is going to be a very individual thing, I think.

    Posted By: WillH
    Can you give some examples of this?
    \nI can. But with this caveat: I'm not saying that that the first or second session of these games are no-fun or totally suck. I'm saying that for the game to give out its maximum payout takes a few sessions.

    Sorcerer. This is a big one. The first session of a Sorcerer game can be rough on a GM if you're not expecting it. You will walk away feeling like a totally failure because "nothing happened." You will find many posts on The Forge about this first session Sorcerer GM malaise. However, if you talk to the players you will find that they are usually pretty jazzed about the *potential* energy that first session created. They have ideas percolating about where they want to go with the material. And if you just trust that and use that potential energy you'll find that by session three the game will kick into high gear. The Humanity issues need to boil a bit before they really start coming into play in full force.

    Primetime Adventures - A single session gives you a taste. Playing out a full 5 episode season arc pays out in amazing ways. When people know just how many episodes away their Spotlight Episode is and where it falls in relation to everyone else's Spotlight Episode they play to it and creates a very specific multi-session creative dynamic.

    Dogs in the Vineyard - This one is kind of cheat since a single town can start the awesome factor pretty high. Let me just say that the fifth town in a campaign is that awesome squared.

    Trollbabe - Similar to Dogs in the Vineyard in that it has a deceptively fun and engaging single session unit of play but it's designed to have a cumulative multi-session world spanning Epic scope over the long run that you don't see in the single unit.

    My Life with Master - The frantic chaos of a single session game just doesn't have the kind of heart breaking emotional payoff that a slower boil multi-session game does.

    Note: I argue that all my examples above are fundamentally different then say, just the accumulated experiences of playing ANY game for more than a single session. That the games are fundamentally different experiences, by design, over the long haul as opposed to just relying on the natural emotional continuity of the players.

    \n Thing is that narrativism is not gamism. That is the rules in games that promote narrativism aren't some challenging minefield through which to be maneuvered. The rules in gamism facilitate challenge. The rules in a game meant to promote narrativism should make it relatively easy.
    \n
    \nThe challenge with narrativism is in coming up with the most entertaining themes we can given the tools present. The tools should facilitate this. Just as good gamism rules should facilitate the creation of player challenge.
    \n
    \nWhich is not to say that I'm against challenging narrativism games, either. Simply that the rules, and using them effectively, should not be wherein the challenge lay.
    \n
    \n
    \nRalph and I used to get all astounded at what we found to be a "learning curve" for Universalis. And others note it too, when playing - they figure out the emergent techniques as they play. But what's interesting is that, in fact, this "curve" is pretty quick. Most people have come up with a lot of techniques by the end of their first session. What did we, or others, expect? Instant understanding of every possible technique that could come out of what is a game that has a lot of emergent qualities?
    \n
    \nI mean we could have written about our discoveries in the book, but we felt that it was better to let people discover these properties on their own. Because we could have written forever - there are potentially infinite numbers of possible techniques that could emerge.
    \n
    \nInstead we wrote the game that we felt people could have fun with just understanding what we'd written, and they'd learn everything they needed to know about it quick enough.
    \n
    \n
    \nThese are RPGs, and even in a complex wargame, you can't explain all of the emergent properties of play. In RPGs it's ten times worse, as the precise subjects of play are so potentially diverse, even in the most limited of games. So you have to give the basic tools that people need, and let them go off and discover the play on their own.
    \n
    \nYes, this takes time...practice if you will. But that path of discovery is what RPGs are all about. This is just exploration of system we're talking about here.

    \n
    \nThor, you're right, but let me explain it like this.
    \n
    \nThere are two levels to play.
    \n
    \n - Understanding the Rules
    \n - Understanding how best to use the Rules
    \n
    \nLearning the rules of chess is dead easy. The "practice" comes in mastering the emergent complexities. The same is true with good gamism RPGs. Game sessions "suck" often because we simply don't understand the procedures of play. We expect to have no trouble with this part.
    \n
    \nNarrativism is the same, you are right. In that it's only problematic if we're not sure what we're supposed to be doing, and play falls apart as an activity. Understanding how best to use the rules, mastery of them in terms of creating theme, should be challenging, yes. We should be applauded for showing how well we do it.
    \n
    \n
    \nThe difference is that in Gamism, the rules themselves often exist on both levels. That is, it's not just emergent properties, but the basic properties of the rules that we have to understand in order to excel at playing in a gamism mode. Does this feat work well in conjunction with this other feat? The rules themselves serve as the test. And it's a gamism test.
    \n
    \nIn narrativism, once you've got a hold of the rules, and you're creating theme, it's largely on the player to come up with a theme that's entertaining to the group. The ability rewarded is not in manipluating the rules in some clever way in order to create the theme, but simply in how well they construct it from what they control in the SIS. I'd posit that a narrativism game creates this challenge by, say, the situation or characters. I can't think of a single game that attempts to do this by system. I tend to think that, if you did, it would cause the game to tend to promote gamism.
    \n
    \nI sometimes feel this way about MLWM. Even though it's very simple to use.
    \n
    \nAny better?
    \n
    \nI'm not saying that mastery should neccessarily come easy (though some people like that). I'm saying that the rules in a narrativism promoting game will tend not to provide that challenge in a mechanical way, so much as in a creative way.

    \n Mike, I think we're in agreement now.\n
    Posted By: Mike HolmesPut another way, if, in fact, there were an easier way to have BE do what it does... you wouldn't change that? Difficulty is good for difficulty's sake? Doesn't it have to pertain to the creative agenda in some way, proving you've met the challenge?
    \nHonestly, I don't know. I'm really happy with the game as is.

    It's supposed to be a challenging game to play. Making thematic decisions is the primary goal: The center of play is the constant struggle between fighting for what you believe and sacrificing that belief for the greater good. When you have to choose between having it out with someone over what you believe (conflict scene) or walking away to allow another player to have a conflict scene that advances humanity's efforts against the Vaylen, you are engaging in the thematic struggle by confronting a limitation placed in your way by the mechanics.

    So the challenge presented by effective use of the rules are a major element in driving thematic play in the game. In turn, it makes those moments when you choose to play non-optimally in order to pursue what your character believes really potent. Now, it can make the rules look like they support a gamist agenda. But I don't think the game would be very satisfying played in that way. There isn't quite enough game outside the context of the thematic struggle to make it really satisfying in that mode.

    As far as My Life with Master goes, in some ways it is a game that supports a narrativist agenda but without guardrails. In other words, there's nothing to prevent you from approaching the game without a narrativist agenda. You can play it to win and without caring about the Innocents. But it won't be a very satisfying experience. The same is true of Dogs in the Vineyard. You can play the game to shoot the crap out of people without caring about any of the NPCs or their problems, but it will be a dissatisfying experience. On the other hand, if you DO care about the NPCs and want them to overcome their problems, if you DO accept them as friends and family, the game provides a very different and much more interesting experience.
