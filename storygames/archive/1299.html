    (Oops. I wrote the post below and then found out it wasn't exactly what you asked for. Well, perhaps it'll be useful anyway, for you or someone else).

    I used to run a gaming & comics store some ten years ago in Norway. I don't think they operate very differently in the U. S. - but I could be wrong.

    We used to order only from major distributors, to save on shipping costs, handling time etc. We had one contact person at each distributor. We weren't approached by any independent publishers; if we had been, we would most certainly have considered selling their games - if they were A) novelty items, or of obvious quality.

    Approaches that would have worked with us:

    - Coming by the store and showing us some games.
    - Leaving a copy of a game for us to look at.
    - Stating the key points that make the game different - and making sure they're really _different_.
    - Being ready to answer questions concisely.
    - Being a nice and cool person.
    - Offering to run an in-store demo for customers at a later date. (Good promotion for the store).

    Approaches that wouldn't:

    - Insisting that we make an instant decision.
    - Trying a hard sell. (Shopkeepers sell stuff for a living; attempts at manipulation will piss them off).
    - Asking for an hour of our time to show us how the game works.
    - Talking way too much about the product. (Shopkeepers have to hear people ranting about games all day).
    - Being pushy, a fake, or bothering the customers in any way.

  • Matthijs
    September 2006
    Getting products from any source other than your standard, major distributor is a pain in the ass. You have to spend time & thought on the process, and the payoff will be minimal. If you usually spend three hours each week ordering stock, spending an additional hour just to make 10-20 bucks isn't worth it.

    So you should make the process of buying/ordering your game extremely easy - pop by the store to check if they still have your game in stock; if not, ask if they want some more, and if they do, give it to them right then and there (along with an invoice). The first few times, give them a money-back guarantee: If the game takes up shelf space for a month, the store doesn't have to buy it from you. (I think Dave Sim had a deal something like this for Cerebus).

    Tony, demoing in a store is a good idea; make sure it involves _no_ extra hassle for the owner. Print your own posters, put up a "sign-up-here" list with your contact info. The ideal deal for the store is that all they have to do is say OK, and you make something cool happen in their store for their customers.

    Tip on contacting store personnel: Pop by once or twice and talk to people there, so you know who the RPG person is, who the manager is etc. Ask them about their RPG section. Then, when you're sure you're talking to the right person (someone who you've got a positive conversation with, and who has decision-making power or influence), ask if they're interested in selling your game.

    They want to know:

    - that you're sane
    - that you've got a solid product
    - and that it's no hassle for them to stock it.

    Don't do any of these:

    - Step up to the first person you see and ask if they'll sell your game.
    - Bother sales people who are very busy with customers.
    - Let a junior employee make a decision.

    Hey y'all,

    Sorry for the slow reply. Yesterday ended up by being a family day, so I ran out of time. So better late than never, I suppose. So here goes.

    Little background. EndGame has been stocking Story Games for about the past year. We have had great success with them, focused Mini-Cons around them, and done as much as we can to market them to our local customer base. Featuring them at our 3 local conventions this year seemed to really help. I have had to buy product from both IPR (who is a pleasure to work with), and directly from the authors. I do what I have to to get product. More on that later...
    Slightly more background. EndGame is 4,200sq. feet. I have a 2,500 sq. ft. show room, and a 1,700sq. ft. play space. Managing both with just three full time employees takes a lot of time with the growth we are trying to achieve. I can safely say we have one of the strongest brands in the SF Bay area, and I think that is growing beyond those boundaries. How does any of that apply to you as a small publisher? Honestly, by the approach.

    In a pretty lame way, I was just trying to frame how busy my day is. I am in the shop for 10 hours a day, and I would say most of my day is filled at least a week in advance with all the promotions I am working on, events I am juggling..and then there is that whole pesky customers thing. What helps me want to buy something from you as a potential vendor is respecting my time. I am not trying to be a drama queen here, I just really have my time booked out during the week, and may not have any for you if you just wander in to sell me something. So my number 1 recommendation is call ahead, ask for the buyer handling (fill in the blank, in this case RPGS), and set up an appointment. Some game stores aren't run like mine, and that might not be necessary...but man will it come off well even if it isn't. I promise I will make time for you if you call ahead.

    Number 2, and this is where it may get harder for some of you. If I can get you through distribution (including IPR) I am more likely to order. Why? Because it's easy. I have $175,000 worth of stock in my store. I and my partners spend about 20 hours a week ordering and receiving goods. As hard as we try, we forget the "little" guys. We are human, and suck occasionally. All that said, if I meet with you, have a good impression, I always try harder if I need to go direct. As mentioned we still do it for some small press titles.

    Number 3 is where we come to terms. (Pun intended) In my specific geographic location, if I don't get at least 36% off something, I lose money on the transaction. This will vary widely around the US and rest of the world. I can only talk about my needs here. So, if you want to sell me your book for 30%, I am more than likely to give a pass. 40% I am getting used to, and will suck it up and buy...but many stores won't. They are used to getting 45-50%, and will turn you away unless they get it. Our fixed costs are just like yours, so there isn't a lot of wiggle room there. There is one clever way around this, but it involves more work for both parties: Consignment. If you and the store owner can agree to just stock your product on his shelf, and have you get paid as they sell, you may be able to take a higher cut. You are assuming all the risk. That's on a case by case basis, and in truth, i don't personally do it. I just buy the product for 40-50% assume the risk, and begin throwing it in my wide net of marketing.

    So, what are some of the benefits of going through all this work? Well, again, that depends on your local game store. I am 100% aware that there are a large and vast number of stores run by trogs who just expect the product to sell itself. They don't bother to learn what they are selling, don't spend any marketing dollars, well...you get the picture. In truth, there isn't a lot of benefit from that type of store other than getting eyes on your product. If you can partner with a store that is actively working to sell your product, that's SO much better. Maybe you can offer to help them organize a small mini con (if they have play space). Offer to help them with the flyers, and to find the gms. Remember, YOU are more than likely the expert in this case. Help them, and they will help you.

    I know there is a segment of the community who feels that retail isn't necessary for success, and I am going to agree depending on expectations and needs for sales. At some point I will try and remember to post my sales per title here to give you an idea of the volume that can move through a retail store. If memory serves, i am pretty sure we have gone though about 30 copies of DiV, and that's with several droughts to supply.

    I think I have covered the basics. But I am sure I have missed a lot. Ask questions if this forced any. I will do my best to answer.

    Chris Hanrahan
    EndGame

    Having gone store-to-store in the past, I can say that for me it basically wasn't worth it. Two reasons:

    1. Lots of effort on my part, and little to none on the part of the people at the store. The books often went right into the shelves with no time on the display racks.
    2. They want 50% of the cover price, which means my game is either priced too high for anyone to even consider, or I make about $1 per copy.
    3. Some of them just fucking scam you. Hey, Wizard's Tower in Nashua New Hampshire: Fuck you.

    Even for those stores that were easier to work with, and who put my games on display rather than into a bookcase, (Modern Myths, Phoenix Games, thank you guys), it was more worth my time to spend an hour or two improving my online sales than driving around. I *never* made my printing costs back.

    Point #2 may be better these days with lower-cost printing. Point #1 and #3 basically boil down to a store-by-store basis, and even really nice folks at good stores often just don't care. Why should they? Chris' points apply completely, and some people aren't as nice as he is, as competent, or as interested in promoting indie games.

    This maybe a little late, hopefully this will be worthwhile: I'll post my experiences as a buyer of camping/climbing/paddlesport for a 22 store chain. Different industry, but retail is retail. And reps are reps. When selling your own product, you are basically a rep. What Chris Hanrahan posted above rings very true for me even though I sold different products. In addition, I've switched industries to photography and have to approach stores to sell my fine art prints, so I had to figure out what I liked as a buyer and practice that.

    Briefly, this is what I'd recommend for approaching a store:

    Initial Contact

    1. Call. Ask who the RPG buyer is?
    2. If he or she is in, then ask to speak to him or her.
    3. Briefly introduce yourself (quick) and ask him or her if they have time to set something up for you to come into the store and talk or offer to send some promotional materials. (Did I say quick!!! Try and keep this call under five minutes unless the buyer is lengthening the call.)
    4. Offer to send a copy of the game or promotional materials. I always wanted to see promos, catalogues, price lists before I'd send my time meeting with someone.
    5. Thank them for their time.

    Follow Up
    1. Call a week later.
    2. Ask if they received the promo.
    3. Ask if they are interested in meeting.
    4. Set up a brief meeting.

    In the Store
    Before: Make a price list. Have terms, shipping costs, etc... all set before you get there. (Email, and I'll send you a copy of my Photography Wholesale Price Sheet for you to see if you want to see how I do it.)
    1. Be early, but not too early. As a buyer I was always busy, but hated it when someone was waiting for me a long time, because they got there too early.
    2. Introduce yourself and shake hands.
    3. Be quick. Finish your presentation early. If you asked for an hour, finish in 40 minutes. If you asked for 30 minutes, finish in 20. If you asked for 20 minutes finish in 15.
    4. Be nice. I liked laid back reps, but other buyers may vary.
    5. Don't be pushy. As a buyer, I'd make my own decisions based on how I thought the product would sell for us. If I didn't think it would sell, then a pushy rep would leave a bad taste in my mouth for next years product line.
    6. Take the extra time to either ask if they want to try a quick demo of the game if you have the right vibe.
    7. Or take the extra time to learn about how their purchasing works, so you can accommodate the store better. A good question, "So, do you usually buy a product after meeting with a rep, or do you like to think about it and call in the order later?" Or something similar based on your comfort.
    8. Take an order or give them a faxable order sheet.
    9. Thank them for their time and leave before the time allotted is over.

    Smile!!!! Don't be afraid. From what I've read on the Internet here and at the Forge, you guys are really nice guys, and most buyers are nice guys to. Just try to get along and build a friendship and relationship. I liked the reps who built relationships with me, and even when I didn't think their product line would work, I'd give them a chance to convince me otherwise, and sometimes, based on that relationship, I'd try something new in one of the stores to see if it would work. Sometimes it did and sometimes it didn't, but those relationships often become fruitful in the future.

    Hope this helps.
    Bryan
