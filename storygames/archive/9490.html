\n So, I've been running a one-on-one game of 4E D&D lately with my roommate, and in order to beef up the PC in the face of threats, he basically has two character classes, which means even in the world of D&D where PC classes are like superpowers, he is particularly mighty.

But the more we play, the more it's driven home that D&D isn't the game for the kind of fantasy I like to read, particularly the pulp fantasy of the early-mid 20th century and its descendants. You know what I mean: Howard, Lieber, Burroughs, Moorcock. So I'm looking for something that is a better fit.

Based on a (very) brief overview I'm seeing a few options:

Jaws of the Six Serpents (don't own, some familiarity with PDQ)
Sorcerer/Sorcerer and Sword (don't own, no familiarity)
In a Wicked Age (own, have not run/play)
Burning Wheel (own, have played briefly, but not run)

So, what I'm looking for is ideas for play, other systems to consider, strengths and weaknesses of different systems for this kind of play, and particularly, strength and weaknesses of different systems for one-on-one play, since getting my group together is something of a challenge.

Thanks in advance,

    \n IAWA is like sweet peppered honey to me, and I have had a good time running it for small groups of me + 2 or 3 players. That said, I think a lot of the goodness of IAWA comes from player-on-player conflict, so a one-on-one game is suboptimal in my view. Whatever game you do wind up playing, though, I'd see if you could import that IAWA oracles into it. They, more than anything else, give IAWA its sexy sword-and-sorcery feel.

    I have heard about lots of people having fun with one-on-one Burning Wheel, since BW is all about generating characters who are layered and interesting and worthy of a story, and it's cool not having to split spotlight time between more than one character like that. In fact, the one game of BW I played had three fascinating characters who barely interacted for the first few sessions.

    (and everyone was cool with that, tho I felt bad for the poor GM who had to interleave three completely independent storylines...)

    Since you've up-powered your D&D guy, and since characters like Conan and Fafhrd are highly capable, I'm guessing you want a pretty epic character power level. I think BW has got you covered there as well. The edition I played had tiers of power (I remember them named after colors, but I'm not sure) that affected your per-die target number.

    Good luck!

    \n Given what you'd posted I'd favor Sorcerer & Sword slightly over In A Wicked Age...

    However, there's an important gameplay distinction between those two. In Sorcerer & Sword the source of adversity is clearly centralized in the GM. The GM is expected to play the demons as a source of pressure, the world as a source of pressure, and so on. As such it requires a GM who enjoys hitting the PCs are in an uncompromising way and willing to let the consequences stand without flinching or back peddling. If you don't like being an aggressive (which not the same as "winning" or an asshole) then I don't think Sorcerer & Sword is a good pick.

    In A Wicked Age... however tends to spread things out a bit more. The GMs job in that game is play the characters which the players don't choose. Those choices may or may not include the primary source of adversity. It might very well be the case that one of the players steps up to fulfill that fictional role. The GM need only worry about adversity when there's a notable absence of it.

    \n IAWA is a very fun game but it's not tactical except in a very broad sense of chosing your battles wisely and the resolution is not very detailed.

    BW is the opposite, in terms of tactics. Very detailed, Mouse Guard is a lighter version of the system but still retains much crunch although the focus is less worldy than the fiction you mention.

    Sorcerer makes your character mighty, especially as regards other mere mortals but the magic system is very focussed on demon summoning and it's also not really a tactical game. That said, Dictionary of Mu is an excellent background and has had much success.
