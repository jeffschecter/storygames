    \n I'd expect trust issues to come into it as well, and automation of course. Would people trust you enough to just send you random donations via Paypal with no guarantee that you wouldn't just take the money? Maybe Kickstarter could be abused in this way too, but since Kickstarter is designed specifically for the purpose I suspect there may be better failsafes. I also suspect it would require much less paperwork than doing the same thing with Paypal. Furthermore, with Kickstarter, the money isn't taken until the term is up. That means that if the target isn't met you've spent nothing. I'm pretty sure that's how it works; I know my donation to 'Happy Birthday Robot' didn't leave until after the final date. It's also more fashionable at the moment, and I daresay that helps a little too.

    So while I can't confirm any of these things since I've not funded a project through Kickstarter, I'd take an educated guess and say that it's a better platform for this kind of venture than Paypal.

    You might also google the Random Model and see what comes up there.

    \n As I understand it, there are two key parts of the Ransom Model as established by Greg Stolze:

    1) The product is already finished, ready to be distributed. The creator is simply holding it "hostage."
    2) The product is free to everyone once the ransom is met. In other words, the creator sets it free.

    With these two stipulations, the product is usually a PDF or other digital file with low or no-cost distribution.

    Kickstarter is more like traditional fundraising, except it's all online and donations are in smaller amounts than the sort of fundraising that goes on at fancy galas or cocktail parties. You can hold ransoms through Kickstarter, as many people do, but the service is more versatile than just that model alone.

    \nI think people get there via a specific project. But then, a lot of them end up staying and exploring.
    It's common to see users that have backed 9 different projects.

    I surf it like I've seen others surf Myspace or Facebook.

    Someone's making a movie about Waldo (of Where's Waldo? fame) returning home after a book tour, to visit his younger and less worldly brother.

    Someone else is making an indie-folk musical about Hansel and Gretel returning to their childhood home, and confronting the more confusing faces of memory, and fear, and the past.

    Someone else is making a map book of a city that doesn't exist, that started showing up on Google Maps as an error (Argleton).

    There's a morbid children's puppet show where all the puppets are made up of salvaged couches & weird odds and ends, several community gardens, gourmet snow-cone businesses, people writing amazing children's books in the vein of classic Golden Books, An 8-bit musical tribute to Miles Davis' Kind of Blue (called Kind of Bloop), a "cooking sushi with kids from the south, to teach essential skills and play with culture fusion" project, bizarre public art instillations...

    I spend a silly amount of time scouring Kickstarter. It's one of the most inspiring and intriguing catalogues of human activity ever.

    \n I love the idea of kickstart in general. However, I don't think folks should expect too much from it.

    For example, my buddy Jerry did the HELLAS supplement kickstart. I'm a fan of the game (and him) so I promoted it heavily online and locally, threw a big wad of cash at it, etc. However, it was set at a pretty high bar. If there is a future supplement, and a supplement after that, all at the same price point, it will be very hard to keep enthusiasm ("funds") flowing.

    The community of kickstart projects and donators is VERY interesting, though.
