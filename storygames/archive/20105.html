\n So I've been mulling over a bunch of magic systems in RPG's. I loved the idea of magic, but I've always been somewhat disapointed in how magic is performed in the typical RPG. It usually boils down to a dice roll, and some sort of effect. I wanted to know what games really put some spin on their magic system to make it more evocative or make the mechanics feel like "magic"?

    \nMy game poem. <=)

    In stories, magic is limited arbitrarily by the author or by folklore, and it's generally rare. Rarity helps obviate the need for a set of rules for it. Only common magic needs detailed rules.

    RPGs have lots of rules for magic for game balance reasons. All magic always has a cost. Free magic would be terrifyingly nasty for most games. So game designers carefully spell out (ha) the costs and effects of different magical formulae, and all the wonder fizzles out.

    I have a backburner game design that is built around wondrous magic (with a little more crunch than the game poem there). If I end up doing anything with it, I'll post about it here.

    \nMortal Coil has a pretty interesting take on Magic: when you spend a resource token to perform a new magic in the game, you get to define the effect, but another player defines the cost or other drawback of that magic.

    Conspiracy X had a sub-system for psi powers (which might as well be magic) that used Zener cards: I can't recall the exact mechanics, but you had to predict a card in order to use the power and the higher your power rating, the more chances you got to make your prediction.

    In the Orrorsh setting for Torg, there was a ritual magic system that worked around describing your ritual, e.g. "I will research herb lore; this herb lore will identify magical plants; I will gather these plants and ferment them..." and so on. Basically, each step in the ritual earned you a cumulative bonus to your magic roll, but you had to actually carry them out and I think you got bigger bonuses for harder steps, e.g. if you had to rely on a Solar eclipse or use the rope that had hanged someone who had killed 13 people, etc.

    \n Okay, so my backburner game idea for magic works like this:

    Throughout play, the GM uses random tables to generate ingredients and components that the wizard finds. The wizard also gets to make things, like wands, powders, potions, permanent engraved circles, standing stones, and the like. All of these things have magical properties, worked out between the wizard and the GM. Probably, the wizard just gets to define the property, and a skill check confirms what the player says the PC thinks ("I find sagegrass? That's good for... uh... it's good for dental pain, right?" "Make an Herbal Lore check.").

    The player records all of these components and their properties.

    When the player wants to work magic, she crafts a spell. She can scan through her list of components and properties for stuff she thinks should help, and make some kind of spell craft roll (Alchemy, Binding, Circles, etc.) to add the component to the spell. Success indicates adding dice or bonuses to the final spell check.

    At last, the spell is built and a roll determines its success and cost.

    The spell can be recorded (with all components and the roll peculiarities) and recast later, without all the combination skill checks, unless there's skill required to transform components somehow (and transformation and purification would be useful things to do, to reduce costs).

    Oh, and the wizard can always "discover" new properties through some kind of roll during spell construction. ("Oh, sagegrass mixed with dragonscale powder is a fire suppressant, I think." "Roll Alchemy, difficult check.")

    \n I wanted a freeform magic system and read those that I could find on the market. From Ars Magica to Gemini. (Sorcerer did get away though.) None felt like it did what it wanted. Both the mentioned game even had spells(!).

    I made my own for my game Matin\xe9 where you chose a theme and can do anything within that theme. The system only focuses on the effect it has on the world, if you use it in combat, to modify the world based on the theme, or to create something from nothing, and how many that will be affected or how long the spell lasted. The player could describe the effect in any way, but that didn't really matter. They could throw fireballs without having to include MOVEMENT + FIRE to get that effect. Just fire as the theme was needed.

    Spells drained the wizard, like strong winds tores a sail, unless s/he could control the spell fully. Too much drain and the magic skill would increase. If a wizard got up to 15 in his or her magic skill (skills were between 1-20), the theme would affect how the magician perceived the world. A mage with fire as theme would become a pyromaniac, and the illusionist would start to live in a world of illusions. These things were something the game master should use to subjectively describe the world, were a fire could make a wizard get a homey feeling, or the illusionist could talk to people that the rest of the group would see as shadows.

    ---

    Apart from that, I really like the feeling of Deadlands, where the wizards were hucksters that conjured poker hand to turn them into magical influences on the world. The game used a poker deck as a dice resolution mechanic.

    \n It's simple, but the diceless stuff in Golden Sky Stories actually winds up being very evocative, I think. Everything in that game is diceless, and one of your resources (Feelings) gets mostly used to augment your attributes (i.e., you spend it to temporarily improve an ability score for a single check), and the other resource (Wonder) gets spent to use your magical powers.

    Of course, your powers all do particular things which don't have specific uses, so characters wind up using them very creatively.

    Oh, and a strongly evocative part of it is where you get Wonder and Feelings from: augmenting your character's connections to others. I don't remember which is which, but one of the resources comes from your connections to others, and the other one comes from connections to you...so you literally get more resources by connecting with other characters.

    \n I can think of tons of magic systems that I like, but none from published games that really feel "magical" to me. The only two "magical" ones that come to mind are:

    Hex Rangers (fka Witch Trails), where spells are heavily charged by immediate emotional and other circumstances and never work the same way twice. I don't know if Marshall ever finished this, but he spells out his intent beautifully here, and some brainstorming on implementation follows.

    Delve, my game, where "how magic works" must be discovered by the characters during play. The answer is part grammar-based spell-casting (judged by the rules for that, which the GM knows) and part nudging the spell in your desired direction by invoking what relevant connections and associations you can (judged by the GM, based on knowledge of said connections and associations).

    \n I liked Anima summoning, where you had one skill for summoning creatures, another for controlling them, one for bonding them to your service more permanently and one for banishing them. When you summoned a creature you had to roll for each (except for bonding, unless you wanted to). You needed to roll for banishing at the end of the combat or if you wanted to banish another summoner's creature. You could also wrestre for control of another summoner's creature, except if it was bonded.

    Anyway, the core book didn't have too many creatures and the guidelines for building one were way too complex for me and my group, so I ended making a list of difficulty target numbers for creatures per level, along witht the HP they got when summoned. The ruling for summoners was that they could give their creature any shape they wanted, let me know of which element was going to be and what level they intended to summon before rolling the dice. Results went from practical and interesting to incredibly hilarious but awesome. Like the time a PC summoned a gigantic holy sh*t as a practically suicidal last resort to stop the main villain. It happened on an island, and the skill roll went so high that the creature was seen from the shores of the continents beyond the sea.

    Another of the things I loved from the mechanic was that a fumble on each skill meant a different thing. A fumble while summoning the creature meant that the creature ended up summoning you to their world (which happened on the first campaign and ended up serving as the origin an introduction for the main villain for the next 4 campaigns)

    \n Just remembered: I should have mentioned Barbarians of Lemuria. With a creative player and GM, this game's magic system is fantastic. The rules link (1) meaningful tiers of fictional costs and demands, (2) power expenditures at different refresh rates, and (3) general levels of potency & permanence.

    You can pretty much make up any effect you want, and then see how long you'll have to prep, how long you'll be without magic thereafter, whether you'll have to maintain physical contact or sacrifice a special item, etc. Sudden improv spellcasting is weak but flexible and cheap, while month-long rituals are powerful but costly.

    On an unrelated note, I also love magic in On Mighty Thews, where a "sorcerer's" schtick is that they're good at knowledge rolls, and knowledge rolls define facts about the world - or, more properly, add important details to the framing of the current scene. Details which you can then leverage to your own advantage.

    \n I didn't see any mention here of Burning Wheel, which I thought looked like a really interesting magic system. As I remember, each spell is defined by a number of parameters (element, target, duration, effect, etc), and a spell failure shifted these parameters by a random number of steps along a complex wheel diagram, so that you end up casting a different unique spell.

    Okay, so my backburner game idea for magic works like this:
    \nNifty! What would also be fun (different kind) would be a system where even the effects tied components are randomized. The skill check in that case would simply determine whether a GM tells you what the effect is or not (and upon critical failure tells you a false result).

    Something like that could make a fun house-rule to a wacky game like Roll For Shoes so that you can have a game focused on wielding the power of magic, specifically.

    Not sure how to make something like that "feel like magic" though. With R4S maybe you do something where your description of the new magic skills you pick up have some implication against the resulting spell effects. Like maybe each new skill(spell) has to be named as its rule: "Spells that can only be performed on the first Sunday of the month (3)"

    And each number you increase it lets you add a new spell.

    And everyone would need the "meta" skills, of course. The primary(only?) one being "Spell Research (1)" or "Witchcraft (1)" or whatever you want to call it.
