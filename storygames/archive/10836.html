\n I've just been invited to a Call of Cthulhu game, for which the Keeper said that we'd spend the first session creating characters and talking about background. (If you're reading this, Robert, hello, and I'm posting this for interest rather than complaining about it.)

Now, I totally don't get character creation sessions. I get twitchy and want to start the game. The idea of going to someone's house for a character creation session is, to be honest, rather unappealing.

People who do enjoy character creation sessions: what do you get out of them? Should I learn to enjoy them? Am I missing something?

    \n I enjoy them a lot when either:

    A.) I am meeting a new (to me) group of players, because I get a chance to sort of 'feel them out' and see how we might game compatibly.

    B.) When everyone is really jazzed about the game, and willing to craft PCs that actually relate to one another. It can be pretty rewarding to bounce ideas off one another in person and communicate everyone's expectation for the game before sitting down to actually play it. However, if these sessions last more than a few hours, I quickly grow impatient and also just want to sit down and play.

    \n Graham,

    When I've enjoyed these sessions, it's been the joy of brainstorming and co-creation and being clever and laying the groundwork for future fun. We swap ideas ("oh, you could have him be an ex-Marine, too, which would..."), give references ("I'm imagining the city with a Blade Runner sort of vibe, with maybe a touch of Minority Report"), brainstorm ("Hm... perhaps he's a secret agent? con man? Ex-cop pulled back in? Ex-con pulled back in?"), lay the groundwork ("I'm imagining that a big part of this character's arc will be testing his resolve, in the light of all this family stuff that's arising"), planting flags ("I'm going to say that he's also incredibly wary of government officials").

    It's the joy of laying traps, rather than springing them.
    To use another terrible analogy: It's a similar joy to decorating for a surprise party.

    When I've not enjoyed these sessions, I felt like we were having to deal with all these simulationist stop-checks before we could begin ("Well, how do the orcs feed themselves, if their nation is all tar and soot? That doesn't make sense, from a socioeconomic standpoint."), fill in a bunch of useless mechanical information ("Now, has everyone filled in their Half-Reflex Wound Threshold? Charlie, you haven't! Take your Reflex Modifier, divide by three, add two, times by two."), cycling through a catalogue of potential ideas while searching for something unknown ("No, samurai-noir won't work for me. Yeah, neo-noir won't either. Wuxia? No, I'm not feeling it. Supers/Wuxia doesn't work for me either. Supers-noir is sooooo overdone. Cop procedural? Way too boring. There's gotta be something....").

    \n We use character creation sessions and find them quite valuable.

    We discuss the setting -- what should everyone expect, and are there any changes to a written setting that need to be made clear? Is there some media that we can watch together that will help people get a handle on the setting?

    We discuss the characters as a group -- what do the PCs do together?

    We discuss the characters' backgrounds -- how do the PCs know each other, what shared experiences do they have, what shared ideals do they have, what fertile ground is there for in-character disputes or reminiscences or relationships? Are there any toxic relationships between PCs that need to be ironed out before the game starts (a PC who is mortally afraid of all things reptile in a group with two reptile-people, etc.)?

    We discuss the characters' mechanics -- does every PC have a spotlight? Do PCs that share a spotlight share it well? Does anyone have a spotlight that will never, ever come up (i.e., the ace pilot in a game where no one will ever see an airplane)?

    We sometimes do things like "describe the enemy of the PC of the player to your left" or "what was the worst job the PC to your right ever had?"

    We pass around our usually-limited set of books and players who have made characters before (or who are just faster at it than others) help out new players/slower players.

    We typically have a GM, so when everything is said and done, the GM (who also participates in the entire process, of course) gets to take some notes on the PCs and let that inform his prep for the first actual play session next week.


    Any one or two of those things would be worth a Saturday before starting the game just by themselves. Put 'em all together and it's just stupid for our group to not take the time to do a dedicated character creation session.

    \n For some games (Burning Wheel, Spirit of the Century, D&D 3E, etc) the actual process of creating characters takes a fair amount of time and effort. So you're already gonna spend a couple hours makin' your dude.

    If everyone does this together, it makes for lots of useful feedback that will result in a better game ("Wait, so that shadowy organization that's chasing your guy? That's totally gonna be the people who took my daughter. What are they called, again?" ... "Is your ranger an archer-type? If so, I'll make my rogue more of a sword guy." ... and so on).

    Without a specific character creation session, you don't get that kind of thing. I've been in games where that sort of stuff is attempted remotely (usually via email or wiki) and it is lame and fruitless.

    \n Thanks, everyone.

    I'm coming to the conclusion that character creation sessions just aren't for me. I'm reading the various posts thinking "Man, I'm glad I'm not at that session". No offence, you understand.

    I'm wary when games (such as Diaspora) or people (such as Mike) suggest that character creation sessions are generally good. I mean, I understand it works for their group, and that's cool. But I don't assume our group's stuff will work for everybody else. I don't write games that say "You must have a meal in the middle of your game! It will make your game better!".

    It may be my improv training. Every time someone suggests you can work out relationships / background / etc, I think: why don't we just work it out in the game? It'll all change, anyway.

    I like the idea of watching Raging Bull. I'd happily do that. I do also enjoy world creation, much more than character creation, and I can imagine a preliminary session of Ganakagok or Sign In Stranger.

    And, oh, man, the idea that we could socialise or play Guitar Hero, instead of roleplaying, is anathema to me. I mean, we'd agreed to roleplay. It's like turning up, in full football kit, ready to play, only for someone to say "Hey, shall we go to the pub instead? It's all social time, right?". That sort of thing really bugs me (although I am delighted it works for you).

    \n Brian: Hah! That's almost exactly the conversation I had with my wife after I came back from our Mouse Guard session zero.

    I'm a proponent of it because it gets us on the same page, and it's a valuable brainstorming session. Particularly with a new group of people, or those who are relatively new to each other, it's good to hang out and chat while doing the chargen. For a oneshot, of course I don't do this. I go with pregens or we're playing a game like The Mountain Witch or 3:16 or something that has quick chargen. But when I sit down for something I expect to go fairly long term, I consider this essential.

    My Mouse Guard group is a good example. While I've played with everyone there, they have not all played with each other (though they have all met each other at various times). So for this group anyway, it's as much a "get to know you" session as it is something where we attend to the business of the game. I can tell you that we had a good time kicking ideas around, building certain things up, putting things together. We were playing, even if we weren't creating in-game fiction (we were making pre-game fiction, if you want to think of it that way).

    I can understand Graham not liking it or getting it, but well, it works for me and it works well, and I hardly see it as a waste of time - I hung out with friends, we talked about the game and got ourselves on the same page creatively. It's valuable to me so I do it.

    \n Well, Graham, it looks like character creation sessions aren't for you. That's fine. Everyone's different.

    Having run the Diaspora Cluster/Character creation session twice in the last week for two different groups, I thought that I would share some of my thoughts on that. (I intend to put out a detailed write up of this soon, once I finish transcribing my notes.)

    First of all, I hadn't originally intended to run the cluster creation, thinking I would just scale back to do something set in our solar system (albeit in the future). But at the first creation session, one player was late, so we started it.

    We made a 6-system cluster then, and everyone started swapping ideas. The ideas for systems fed into the ideas for characters. Very few players made characters from the systems they "owned." Most interestingly, after they saw how the cluster was arranged, they decided that they would rather run a slipknot station (a la Babylon 5) and let the adventure come to them. We had no idea starting out that they would get a station. It just emerged from our cluster/character creation.

    The second group was smaller (four players, counting myself), so we each made 2 systems, for a cluster of 8. This time, the rolls greatly influenced us. You never saw a cluster with so many barren, resource-lite worlds. We quickly agreed that the who Cluster should get the "Used Up" aspect. And our sole Tech+3 system, after we looked at the cluster as a whole, picked up the aspect "Screw You Guys, We're Outta Here." They instantly became the bad guys of the Cluster, stealing resources from other systems to build a generational ark to leave the cluster for somewhere else. And this became part of the backgrounds of each character. In the end, we decided that a run in with these guys had left our characters' ship crashed on an ancient, ruined world filled with "Man-Eating Mutants."

    So, I think Character/Setting creation sessions are a good place for emergent play... we wandered down a few dead ends, but in the end we had a good feel for what had happened in our characters' lives to that point. Perhaps they're not for everyone, but at the same time, it can be a valuable resource for a GM.

    Posted By: GrahamPeople who do enjoy character creation sessions: what do you get out of them? Should I learn to enjoy them? Am I missing something?
    \nI find them to be a necessary evil in traditional games where the system does not allow for collaborative and player contribution. A group character creation session lets everyone talk about what sorts of characters, and thereby story, everyone wants and create characters that are already connected before the game starts. Since these games also tend to require a couple of hours to fully make a character, this can be done as well. Although usually, I find that a couple of people actually make their character during the session. Most people go home and make their character after the group basics have been worked out.

    Although really, you can do this online as long as there's someone to pester people who have trouble contributing. All it takes is a little bit of structure, talking about high level themes and drilling down into character specifics, making sure no one moves too fast so they don't have Sir Fred the Battlescarred and his Family History while another person is still debating the highest level details (or at least, make sure that they are okay with others moving ahead if they are having a hard time deciding). Personally, I prefer the excuse to get together and socialize. Even if I'm not already friends with these people involved - no, especially if I'm not already friends with them - I want the chance to have a light evening where the game isn't happening to see how I might get along with them. A character creation session is a wonderful space to be able to go "You know, this isn't going to work for me" without as much of the guilt that occurs with a game that's already underway.
