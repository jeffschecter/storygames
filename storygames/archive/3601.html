    \n Tower Creek Branch (from the book) is always the first town when I introduce Dogs to a group. It's a messy situation, with no clear "right" solution. It goes all the way up to Hate and Murder, and it's easy to show the players that the Town won't be able to survive a rash of miscarriages and stillborn children. Oh, and I tweak it so that the mother with the stillborn child is the sister of one of the Dogs, and have her come up to the Dogs as the first scene.

    \nWhy I Like Tower Creek, which I've run a dozen times or so. I definitely recommend making the mother related to one of the Dogs: sister is good. Every Dog should be related to at least one person in every town. For one-shots I'll explain about relationships and then tell the players a little about who will be in town and suggest they take relationships there, and it works really well. For a campaign its fine if the Dogs take relationships to people who don't show up in the first town; they'll get to them in time.

    Judd Karlman's New Gidea (linked to above) is also excellent, and I've run it a bunch of times with excellent results, although I have the wife badly injured, not dead, since if she's dead the Dogs seem to have an easier time judging. But try it both ways. One of the great things about running the same town multiple times is that with different Dogs/Players, you can get very different outcomes with the same situation.

    Fort Lemon scares me; it's a great town too although I've never run it, because
    I mostly run one-shots at cons and game stores and have to fit rules teaching, character generation and the whole town into four hours. It's a bit big and juicy for that, I suspect.

    Something I haven't done but mean to try someday is to make the first town the Dogs' hometown. It's no surprise that first time players often have Dogs that have serious issues with their family, but rather than push those off into a future game that's never going to happen in a one-shot, why not have them ride into their own home town as the Hands of God?

    And the best single bit of advice I can offer: don't pull your punches in the accomplishments, although the temptation will be mighty. This is where a first-time player gets a taste of what it is to be a Dog, and it sets the tone for the town to follow. Since it's a given that the Dog doesn't die in Accomplishment, it can be lots of fun to see if they'll take a bullet for their Faith. Fallout is fun!
