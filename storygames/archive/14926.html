Posted By: David BergAs for the future of Story Games, I think a lot depends on how well future generations can learn from past generations.

I think that in turn depends on accessibility. Searching through forums and picking through a zillion threads for nuggets of wisdom doesn't count. Nor does being given 300 different "must play" game recommendations from 100 different aficionados.

We need a more user-friendly trove of knowledge. I've made some efforts to nudge the Forge Glossary toward being that, but that effort didn't go as I'd hoped.

So, big picture-wise, I'm guessing we're in part of the "post-WoD* baby steps" phase. 99% of what current story gamers have innovated will be forgotten and reinvented later. The 1% that will survive makes me happy, though.

*the mid-'90s explosion of World of Darkness games being the last "phase" of large-scale relevance that I can identify
\n
Posted By: Jocelyn RobitaillePosted By: David BergWe need a more user-friendly trove of knowledge.
\nThis.

I think the corpus of story games out there has grown to be so diverse that the single label of "it's a story game" doesn't say much.

For instance, I'vehatedmy play experience with some games and really really liked some others. And some of them I just thought were kind of meh. I know it's not a matter of how well designed the games are, because some of those game that I didn't enjoy are getting accolades left and right. By now, I've played enough games that I kind of have an instinctual inkling of what kind of game I like and what kind of game I don't like. But I still can't put words on these types (and I have this gut feeling that I have basically one or two types I really like).

The way I see this, if I came into this subset of the gaming hobby right now and tried a single story game to see if I would keep on digging the body of games out there, it would basically be a coin toss. Pick a game I like, and I keep digging. But if I pick a game I don't, I'm done with story games with no inkling that this game has jackshit to do with that other game I was thinking of trying.

Someone needs to come up with a way to recommend games in a way that fit a person's tastes real fast, or I'm under the impression that story games will end up losing fans on that coin toss that they could've brought on board with a better "referral."\n
Posted By: Keith FyansThe same is true of Roleplay games, Boardgames, Wargames and Card games. Some you will like, some you won't; the only way to tell is by trial and experience. I fail to see why Story Games would be any different in this respect.
\nSure, let's take boardgames for instance. I like a bit of everything, but my girlfriend hates worker placement and resource management games, and she likes tile placement, route/network building, set collection, and area control games. Without any kind of trial or experience with new game X, I can know that it's not a good purchase to play with my girlfriend if it features worker placement, or that it's likely to be a hit if it's about route building. Not only can I know that, but I have an established vocabulary to express my preferences or to seek information about a game I'm looking to buy.

This is the difference between story games and board games - story games lack that vocabulary, yet have grown diverse enough that having one would be really helpful to people with specific taste.

    Posted By: David BergWe need a more user-friendly trove of knowledge.
    \nHere is how not to build such a trove: build a system that has some kind of labels for styles of games, then pigeonhole games into those labels and present those labels as choices for "recommendations". Such a process has too much of the wrong kind of bias, both in the labels chosen and in what games they are applied to.

    Instead, let data guide you. Example:

    Suppose the existence of a web site containing a list of all games. People create an account. For each game, they say:

    how often they've played the game (never, made characters but never used them, once, a few times, etc.)
    in what format they own the game (none, book, PDF, etc.)
    how enjoyable their typical play experience was, for some deliberately vague definition of "enjoyable" (hated it, meh, good, great, etc.)

    Then, assuming everyone has rated every game they've played (which, of course, will clearly happen, since everyone loves data entry), you make recommendations to people based on how others with a similar rating profile to the user voted on a game.

    Such a system contains quite a bit of knowledge, but it never requires any judgement about why the knowledge is that way, or even what the knowledge actually is. It doesn't know why anyone likes anything, it just assumes that if person A liked the same collection of games as person B, and A liked game X too, then it must have some nebulous quality that person B will probably like as well. It has no idea at all what that nebulous quality is.

    Once such a system had a lot of rating data, you might be able to mine it all to figure out what these nebulous qualities are, but that's the end point, not the starting point.

    This system has several kinds of bias, too. One is exactly the kind of bias you want: people's opinions on games.

    Another type stems from the bias created by lack of data: the recommendations are only as good as the data it has. In real life, this would kill such a system, as it is extremely unlikely that a niche hobby like roleplaying would be able to generate a large enough data set.

    \n I was thinking about that. Certainly, it is a start. I suspect that RPG Geek is asking the wrong questions, though. Their rating system could really mean anything. Does Eoris get 7 out of 10 because people have fun playing it, or because it has great art and a funky setting? How many people who voted have even played it?     \n Random idea for a way to encourage at least some data collection in a system like this:

    When you sign up, you are presented with a plea that says some variation of "this site only works if it collects lots of data" and then offers an opt-in "nagging" feature (which is fully explained and disclosed):

    Once a day, everyone who has this feature on is asked (by e-mail, or any time they log in, or whatever) to rate the same game. They follow a link. This page shows them the "game of the day", plus up to three previous games of the day that they didn't vote on (maybe they were busy that day). They vote quickly and move on.

    Importantly, if you have already voted for the game of the day, you are not nagged. (Minor incentive to vote for other games on your own.)

    Assuming altruistic members, this sort of setup offers some advantages:

    you get a lot of ratings for the same game at the same time.
    exposes members to games they may not know about.
    allows whoever runs the site to pick games that will best enhance the data set.

    \n Building a database to make fuzzy connections based on user data only really works when you have nearly-passive input by users.

    Netflix and Amazon have great systems for making recommendations for books and movies. Because they have thousands and thousands of users. And each of those thousands of users makes dozens or hundreds of transactions through their system. So they have assloads of data to work with.

    And they still give me stuff that I say "Nope. Absolutely not. Not interested." on a regular basis. But they also make a lot of really good suggestions.

    DriveThruRPG does a little of this. They track things that their users look at at the same time and make suggestions based on that to later users. And when I am browsing through their site for something, I see a lot of good, related suggestions that I often check out.

    But mostly that helps with things like "I'm looking for paper terrain models and I want to see several by various companies before I pick ones I want." or "I'm going to run a Pathfinder game in an urban setting and I want to see lots of different idea books about that before I pick out a few to buy and read." It helps you find things that are very very similar like that.

    But here's the thing. We're talking about indie games. Games that are distributed by many different methods. Many of them are only sold through individual websites. They don't all use the same distribution channel, so you can't use a shopping-based data collection model to data mine for them. And, to be honest, there's not enough reason for users to submit data if you're not talking about a shopping-based model.

    If you got them all to use the same distribution channel, then you could do it. But then they wouldn't really be indie games anymore, would they?

He's the only other one who has read the Savage Worlds rules that we're using a hack of. He used to do some freelance gamebook writing, but all of his experience has been with trad style games. He would read through the 2 sentences. And then reread them a few times to parse them, because they are quite dense. Then he would say "Huh. I've never played a game like that. Sounds neat. What's a relationship map? It sounds like it would be fun to try." And then he'd mostly forget about it and go back to thinking about the stuff he's more familiar with.

    Aya. Mid 20s. Larp friend. I think the majority of her roleplay experience has been through freeform forum play. She's very dramatic. She's the type of person who will play in the same game for years and never really "get" the rules, she'll just get enough of them to passibly function. Cause she's not there for systems or structures or rules or procedures or any of that. She's there to be in character and to socialize. She'd read the 2 sentences and say "Yeah. Neat. I have no idea what that means." In fact, I can hear her voice saying exactly that.

    --

    So out of the 8 of us (myself included) only 2 would be able to get through that description unaided without either giving up, not understanding, not caring, or needing lots of help clarifying and giving examples.

    The average Story-Games.com poster is FAR FAR FAR from the average gamer, and I think many of the people here would need to reread those 2 sentences slowly and chew on them a bit in order to get at all the information densely packed in there. I know I did.

    No, a non-gamer does not get "using some random inputs" when you put it like that. If you say "you use dice or playing card draws sometimes to randomize some parts of the game, which in other ways is quite heavily structured and static" then, yeah, a non-gamer would get it at that point. So some of it is, as I said, jargony looking because of how succinct it is.

    Some of the stuff looks like jargon because it's the parts that challenge common assumption that people with only super trad-style experience would hold.

    Like "predetermined outcomes". I consider that jargony because it's just so different. The only thing that is even remotely similar for the "average gamer" is the notion of "railroading", which is BAD. So why would anyone want to play a game that marked that as a feature? The concept needs to be unpacked and described with examples before it can be comprehended.

    When you're describing something that people can relate to, you can be super succinct and you're fine.

    When you're describing something that goes almost completely opposite of what they would normally assume to be good, then you need to spend a lot more time on your description in order to encourage comprehension.

    In other words, story-games.com readers can read those 2 sentences easily because we've spent time thinking about those ideas before and considering them as at least possible options that some people might like.

    Other folks who have spent over a decade getting used to vastly different assumptions that they've never really challenged before have a hard time comprehending something that runs so counter, even if you're using terminology that they should be able to work with.

    I'm certainly not ragging on such folks. Nor am I trying to place myself as superior. I'm just anticipating comprehension difficulties based on my experiences.
