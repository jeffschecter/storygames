    You are a fighter in a fantasy setting. You are in a tavern, sitting alone. The tavern you are sitting in is the kind of tavern you'd get in, say, 14th-century Yorkshire, except the timber is darker in color and the fireplace is a little different. You wait around for about twenty minutes, before you decide to order a drink. You order an ale from a serving girl of 17 and a half summers or so, who looks like your cousin, but not quite, her complexion is a little more tanned. Some time later, the ale comes, and it tastes like your typical ale, except maybe a little more sour than what you are accustomed to. A while later, you feel a bit hungry, so you order sausages from another serving-girl, who looks like the first except that her hair is darker and her eyebrows arch in a slightly different way. 5 minutes later you think, hang on, I'm not that hungry, maybe I'll get her to serve me a loaf of rye bread instead. You try to get the attention of one of the serving girls, but they're busy at the back, doing god-knows-what, so you decide to wait a bit instead of making a fuss. When one of the serving girls come back, you start telling her to change your order, except that you are a bit hungry, so you say, "Never mind." She looks a bit annoyed at you, and then walks off to the kitchen. You then think, hold on, maybe rye bread would be good with sausages, and besides, you could use a bit more ale, so you try and get their attention again, but they kind of ignore you or maybe they don't hear, whatever. When they do bring you your sausages, you order the bread and more ale. You wait a bit while they bring you those things, and then you notice someone else in the room, someone you know from long ago. It's your cousin from the next county, he's come to buy some wool at the market, except he came in a bit late and the market's already closed so he's staying in the inn until the market opens the next day. So the both of you do a bit a small talk, and he asks you how it's like being a fighter. You say, oh, it's not too bad, business has been slow. He then asks you about cousin Mildred, who's given birth to her third child, a son. Oh, you didn't know that she had given birth already, she moved to Oakthorpe last winter, didn't you hear? Apparently not, according to your cousin. He then asks about your dad, and you shrug and make evasive remarks, so the conversation kind of dies there and then. The serving girl comes back with the rye bread and ale, and you say, hang on, the sausages are cold, I'd like one that isn't cold. The girl grumbles a bit and takes your meal, and then comes back maybe 10 minutes later.

    You are a fighter in a fantasy setting. You are in a tavern, sitting alone. Sitting alone is an acceptable cultural practice in this area, and not stigmatized. While the majority of people don't travel alone (though neither is there either any strict injunction against it, nor are the roads so dangerous that practical concerns would require travelling in pairs, or even in larger numbers, as some more imperilled regions, but not this one, occasionally require) it is not uncommon for one member of a pair to decide that they are more interested in getting some sleep than in immediately eating, or for two people to choose to both eat, but to eat at different times in the evening. This, together with the people who do travel alone (in the absence of practical bars, as I cited above) creates a reasonable proportion of people who choose to sit alone. Absent any correlation between that subsection of people and any traits viewed as undesirable (either for legitimate reasons, such as lone brigands travelling the roadways and entering respectable establishments incognito to secure food and supplies (a common tactic, as reports of the social separation between criminal elements and the common, lawful man in medieval society have been greatly exaggerated) or for religious or cultural stigma) this population has created an understandable perception in the local society of sitting alone as a normative, and therefore unthreatening, activity. In short, no judgment accrues, save the obvious ... a judgment that you are, at least for the moment, without companionship (as such alternate possibilities as miniscule companions (pixies, etc.) or invisible ones occur in such a vanishingly small percentage of cases (well outside of four standard deviations, and therefore beyond the likely expectations of any observer) as to make it unlikely that local observers would even entertain the notions, much less give them credence, unless they are particularly superstitious and paranoid individuals, and I wouldn't list the perceptions of such people as part of the overall judgment of a society (unless such traits were wide-spread) but rather would describe them as individual judgments, along with a description of the person involved, but no such individuals are present, or at least if they are they aren't making their judgments visible in such a way as to merit description, though you can roll a spot check if you'd like).

    The tavern is a large building, as befits its purpose in housing large numbers of people. The room you are in is particularly large, in the context of the building itself, though not unreasonably or unexpectedly large given its purpose, which is to serve as a common area for serving food and for other general purposes. In keeping with that function, it is, as I've mentioned, fairly large. Most of the room's construction is of wood, that being a local building material due to the presence nearby of significant stands of timber in the form of forests. In some areas, even near forests, other building materials such as brick or stone are used more heavily, either due to their greater availability (particularly in the case of fieldstone construction, where stones must be de-facto "quarried" from fields as part of plowing the fields (as, naturally, the plow catches on the stones and agricultural progress cannot proceed (at least with the typical furrow-based practices of the time) without the removal of the stone to another place)) or due to taboos against the cutting of wood, either non-rational (or, as is often the case in religion, of no IMMEDIATE rational backing, though indirect reasons and patterns may prove to be very compelling when analyzed fully) or rational (such as the presence of treants or dryads in nearby woods, which would make the indirect cost of logging much higher than the opportunity cost involved in dedicating labor (usually not a scarce resource in medieval societies) to the more time-intensive processes of extracting other building materials). However, in this case, those factors must not apply (at least to their fullest extent, unless they do apply but have not yet been fully understood, or perhaps the use of wood in this building is a deliberate social signal playing off of opposition to those known restrictions, either to increase the status of the owner or that of the community) because the construction does appear to be of wood. However, the hearth area, where a fire provides both warmth and light for the room (though that light is supplemented by tapers, typical for this region and purpose, as candles (though providing a clearer and more consistent light) are sufficiently resource intensive to recommend the lowly taper (of cloth treated or impregnated with parrafin or other combustible material, appropriately doped so as neither to burn too quickly nor too hotly for the receptacles into which it will be placed) as a reasonable economic substitute, one which the tavern owners (necessarily rational actors, as both the technologies involved and the trade routes that control supply and cost have been largely static for long enough that economic darwinism would have gradually weeded out those who apply irrational value judgments to these questions) would obviously embrace) as well as heat for cooking is made of stone. This is in keeping both with local manufacture and resources, and with its purpose in the room.

    Your sword leans against your chair. Carrying a sword in public is NOT normative cultural practice, though it is within the standard set of social archetypes to which a typical member of the local populace would have been exposed. The fact that it elicits no explicit comment at this time and place (which is not to say that there are not implicit comments inherent in people's placement and body language, only that such comments are not sufficiently out of the ordinary to warrant comment at this time) is due to the social context of the tavern itself, as a place for wayfarers. The same sword, naturally, would be a different social artifact in, e.g., a farmer's living room (though, truth be told, economics in this area as well as demographics would indicate that most farmers would not have a living room worthy of the name, since giving a specific role-title to the room indicates that it would be distinct to that role, whereas for many (though not all, but certainly sufficient to be considered the plurality, and therefore sufficient to use as a short-hand "typical" case, lest our discussion become unwieldy) farmers the "living room" would in fact be nothing more than "the room", in which all functions of the household (except for those which, due to their very nature, are unsuitable for mixing with the living area of the house) are conducted). However, in the social context of the tavern, the sword is an expected feature, being correlated both with wealthy outsiders and with travellers concerned for their own safety. Since both of those populations are disproportionately represented in the context of the tavern (due to the self-selection of people who choose to frequent the tavern (which is not to say that locals AVOID the tavern, indeed they flock to it, but nonetheless the tendency of outsiders to visit ONLY the tavern creates a substantial self-selection bias despite the local population's tendencies)) the presence of the sword is a not-atypical feature of the modified social population that could be expected in this context. As such, it is unlikely to draw comment unless someone were specifically seeking out your services in the role which that sword typically represents, e.g. a merchant who might wish to hire you to guard (for example) a caravan (as, though brigandage and other threats to travellers are not sufficient to merit extreme measures in the case of most (whose resources to effect those measures are limited in any event) they are a sufficient concern that those specifically setting out to transport valuables a long distance would be rationally motivated to invest somewhat in the protection of those resources. Such brigandage and threat, by the way, is not unusual for this period of history, and indeed their absence (a complete safety upon the roads) would be more of a powerful indicator of information about the local area, as it would (in the hypothetical) indicate either strongly organized criminal elements (perhaps in league with local political figures) or a strong effort in the past to purge crime and to secure the roadways (often a prelude to imperialist expansion). However, the threat on the roadways appears (at least from the perception and patterns of behavior of local people) to be normal, such that it would not indicate any such situation.) And, though it would be hypothetically possible (and not abnormal at all) for a merchant to approach you in order to guard a caravan, that is not such a common occurrence that its absence should be noted. Indeed, there is no merchant, and so your sword goes without comment.

    \n You are a fighter in a tavern. It is late afternoon as you sit down on the rough grained wooden bar stool. You can see the sun beginning to set through the window behind the bar. After weeks of walking long hours each day only to set up camp to sleep on hard and rocky pavement you could almost be excited to be among people again where you can sleep in a real bed and converse with actual people. You stool creaks slightly as you shift your weight for a more comfortable seat. A glance at the other stools to your left and right shows them to be extremely dry and well worn, suggesting the tavern has been here for a good number of years.
    \n
    \nAs is typical of your way and your profession, you take time to note everything around you to the smallest detail. Observing the bar you see it is made of a rich cherry wood of high quality and are quite sure when it had been new it had cost the bar keep a pretty penny. Though these days the bar itself is as well worn as the bar stools sitting in front of it, smooth and worn by years of use every night by patrons. You mentally note that the bar, despite its age, remains very sturdy and in the event of a hostile crowd in the bar you know you could hop over the bar and hold off a dozen men fighting from behind it. Though an initial glance at the few patrons actually in the tavern at this hour tells you you have no need to worry about a bat fight breaking out.
    \n
    \nYour attention shifts to the short plump blond bair maid walking your way. Your best estimate places the girl around age fifteen or sixteen. She smiles shyly at you. If it weren't for the unwashed state of her hair, old and worn state of her light brown woll dress and stocky, heavy set posture, you think her smile could almost make her seem pretty. Then on second thought, in fact, you simply realize that you have been travelling among the barren wastes too long and probably consider any women not utterly smeared with dirst and grim to be attractive. "Would you like something to drink good Sir?" she asks.
    \n
    \nGiven the passive nature of the patrons you consider her question a moment and decide to splurge a bit and order a nice bottle of red wine. Under more usual circumstances you would normally just ask for a watered down ale, showing a good sensible degree of caution against having too much drink in the presence of strangers. A second glance around the room assures you that tonight caution is not so necessary. There are only three other patrons in the tavern at present. Two middle aged men in their late twenties sitting together having simple bowls of stew and drinking ale and one elderly lady sitting on the other side of the room knitting a sweater. The two men, based on their dingy old clothing and dirty bare feet are obviously farmers and have come in for a simple dinner after working the fields all day. Most likely single men with no families of their own yet and here to hopefully socialize with some single ladies as, presumably, a few more patrons come in. Quite possibly one of the men might even fancy the young bar maid here, which would explain their willingness to splurge on a tavern bought meal. On another day you might watch them a bit more to verify that assumption, but you are tired tonight and could really care less.
    \n

The old women seems to be wearing a shoddy old dress that might have been somewhat nice ten or twenty years ago. These days it is little more than rags. Lacking those it seems highly unlikely the old lady could be carrying any magical dusts or powders or any of the spell components for making magic. After delivering your drink the bar made walks over to the table where the old lady continues to knit her sweater and sits down. The old lady takes no notice nor do the two begin talking. You decide the old lady and the bar made are probably both belong to the family that runs the tavern. Possibly grandmother and grand daughter.

    \n I yawn, and I look outside and see that it's still dark outside. There's not much to see, but I gaze casually at the ground that fully surrounds the tavern. I count to a thousand, and it is still dark. Sooner or later, maybe hours from now, the sun will rise and that will mean I will have only wait here for six more days before the soup of the day is mutton stew. Yesterday was chicken broth, which is alright. Oh, but that means it will be five days till mutton stew. I try counting to two thousand this time, and roll several dice to see how far I get. Thirty-seven. So I start counting from there and get to five hundred seventy-three before giving up and checking outside again. Still dark. I look back inside. There's a couple whores dozing at a corner table. I don't care to spend my money. Instead, I climb up the stairs and look at my room. It is empty, except for a bed. The window makes it obvious that it's still dark outside. I try to determine the shade of darkness, but it looks pretty normal. Slightly more dark than my room. I don't step inside; no reason to. I linger within the doorway and consider the difference between a hallway and a room. One is more squarish. It is a short hallway (I roll to determine how short: 8.6 feet), but the room is more squarish all told. I don't feel like sleeping, so it's a waste of a silver, really; but it is no matter. If the night were halfway through, then maybe mutton stew is only four days away. Except it isn't served till midday. Do church bells sound at midnight? I can't recall ever hearing them at midday, but it's easy to get used to such noises. There is no church. I was thinking of the tavern at Braxely. It also had squarish rooms. I wonder if it is light yet at Braxely. They never served mutton stew. I could travel there if I ever got bored here. The door frame offers no opinion. I walk back down to the bar. One of the regular customers has gone home, but I can't remember who all was here before. I roll to remember and get a 6. I remember, but it was actually the entertainment who left. There was entertainment? Too bad I hadn't noticed them. They may have passed the time. At least until leaving. What time do they normally leave? I ask the bartender what time it is. He looks outside and tells me it's still dark. I tip him a copper and sigh and sit back down at the bar. I look over the selection of water-beers. I don't like to get drunk when I'm waiting.

      \n So, it's another Zen theory thing I wanted to test out: the less you do, the more you do.

      If you try to be boring, you're often quite interesting. If you try to be interesting, you're often boring. Like all these things, it's not a hard and fast rule, but there's some sense in it.

      Burr's post, above, is perfect. In an effort to be boring, he's describing lots of mundane details and they're actually rather interesting. Lots of others are also fascinating, at least in parts.

      You can actually be boring, of course, by repeating yourself, using a convoluted style of writing, describing things that aren't relevant, stuff like that.

      Thank you. I really enjoyed the boring posts.

      Posted By: Graham W... describing things that aren't relevant ...
      This is the one I zeroed in on. Particularly, it is possible (indeed, easy, once you get the knack) to describe stuff as part of your explanation of why it would never be relevant. "These are five interesting things that could be happening, which I'm going to describe in excruciating detail in order to explain to you why they aren't happening."

      This little exercise has pushed me a little further away from using hypotheticals in my world-conveying. My takeaway lesson: Don't tell people about how they might encounter gorillas in the jungle ... have a gorilla drop angry on the hood of their jeep, and then explain the local fauna.

      Thanks, Graham, for suggesting the exercise. It was an interesting one (paradoxically enough).
