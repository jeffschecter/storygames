\nHook: You have what it takes to be a roleplaying game designer. But do you have what it takes to be a FAST roleplaying game designer? Do you love games but hate spending time making them?! Do you want to earn the adoration of people on this board who enjoy games and game-related ideas?!
Do you have six minutes and five seconds to spare?

The Contest: Produce a finished RPG in 365 seconds (six minutes, five seconds)

The Requirements: During the contest period you must produce a concept and a design. You may not use any previous writing, but go ahead and use ideas you\u2019ve been kicking around. If you have time for editing, layout, or play-testing, you may have super-speed, and should use it to become a superhero.

The Deadline: You only have SIX MINUTES AND FIVE SECONDS from the second you start. Start as soon as you finish reading this post, or any other time you want, but don\u2019t cheat by mulling over ideas first.

The Prize: Your prize is an incomplete game! However, it will probably have some awesome ideas and you\u2019ll have made something! In addition, you'll earn the approval and admiration of some people, probably. I\u2019ll think you\u2019re cool, at least.
Best of all, should you complete the contest and meet your deadline, you will be awarded a pile of self respect and a feeling of accomplishment so great, so massive and enormous in its magnitude, that all those viewing it and by extension you will be forced to utter: man, that is one awesome person!

Meet The Judges: Look at your screen. There you will see names. Those names are people who might read your game. There\u2019s no formal judging but we\u2019ll all know who the winner will be.

Entry Fee: Six minutes and five seconds of your time.

*Note: This is only sort of an April Fools. We all want to see what you can come up with. It's only a few minutes, go ahead and participate!

    \n Umbrella. Thank you!

    Character Creation:
    You have 8 sections of your umbrella. Decide how many sections are of each color.

    Red: Physical Attack
    Blue: Defense
    Yellow: Flight
    Green: Magic

    Dice pool. Take as many dice of each color as your umbrella has colored sections. Take 2 more from each color represented in your umbrella.

    Setting. You are the masters of the land. Lower humans work the soil. You fly above them as their betters. What will you use your position of power for?

    Conflict. Each side sets stakes. Describe your approach and roll as many dice as you want from your pool from associated colors. Compare highest dice, moving on down until there is no tie. High die wins and gets stakes. Loser(s) get to take 3 dice of any colors from the pool rolled. Dice not in your umbrella must be traded away to others during a interstitial scene.

    \nGUN THIEF

    Gun Thief is about three things: using a gun, taking things from others, and having amazingly raw sex with beautiful but jagged women.

    It might be about stealing guns, but that isn't necessitated by the rules. The title should thus be interpreted as "a thief who uses guns," and not "a thief of guns," though take it either way you want, I guess.

    There are three players in Gun Thief: The Gun Thief, The Law and The Jagged Beautiful Women.

    The Gun Thief

    Right, pick five of the following: gun, gun, gun, blunt instrument, buddy, buddy, woman, camero, chopper, mask, stash of drugs, money, money.
    Affix one of the following tags to each: trustworthy, one ugly motherfucker of a, brokedown, brokedown, brokedown, tainted, tainted, last-chance, glorious, devil.
    Pick a badass name, or a hardened-over-the-years name, or an ugly name.

    Example: I took a trustworthy gun, one ugly motherfucker of a gun, a tainted buddy, a last-chance woman and a devil camero. The name's Aspen.

    The Law

    Make things awful, right out of the gate. In media res the shit out of 'im, or just stick him with an ugly predicament.
    Say that he's fifty in debt to a fella named Horse, for example.
    Say that Jenna's going cowgirl on him while Indie's taking all his stuff, but he's too fucked on bad coke to sort it all out, for example.

    The Gun Thief

    You get three of these things: keeping all your items, gaining a new item, get the girl, get what you came for, get out unharmed.
    You can alternately pick only one, but have it be a permanent gain, lose everything else, and ride off into the sunset.

    The Jagged Beautiful Women

    There are going to be jagged beautiful women everywhere.
    Breathe life into them.
    Make it hard not to pick "get the girl" in every sticky situation the gun thief wanders into.

    When he gets the girl, give her up.
    Frame a scene, or a moment, and make it ooze with sweat and rush and make him powerful.

    And then, as soon as possible, take that jagged beautiful woman back away from him.
    Never let him keep the things that make him happy.
    Make him fuck up his life to get a second taste.
    Or make the second taste pathetically hollow, so that he goes looking elsewhere for his tastes.

    Oh, and if he takes a woman as one of his five starting things, work like hell to break that relationship down.
    If he has a lady, and calls that lady his own, constantly be offering up sexy desire magnets, constantly be getting under that woman's skin, constantly be trying to derail his life so that you can get back in.

    The Law

    So, like, the moment that shit's in line, offer more problems.
    Involve police, and gangsters, and guys who think they're heroes, and drug lords, and ugly cowboys.
    Make it impossible for the gun thief to be a good person.

    It's fine to say "so, now you're robbing a convenience store, and the guy pulls a gun on you", even if the Gun Thief didn't say he had plans to rob a convenience store.
    The law dictates the problems, the gun thief chooses 3 of 5 things, and the jagged beautiful women come to life and try to lure him into lusty, dangerous places.

    The End

    \nIn Transit

    This game is inspired by those awesome BMW short films ("The Hire") featuring the Driver (Clive Owen) and the general concept behind The Transporter films, though not the sub-par quality of them.

    One player is the Driver and picks a Hotwheels car to represent their vehicle.

    Another player plays the GM.

    Together, the player and GM negotiate how many playing cards to lay out to represent the journey to the destination where "the package" needs to be delivered. A short, quick game chase could be played with a dozen (or even fewer) cards. A longer game might be 30 cards or string multiple chases or deliveries together.

    Decide what the package and destination are. Roleplay out the hire and the Driver starting up his car and beginning the journey.

    Place the agreed upon number of cards in a curving path across the table, representing the road from here to the destination. These cards should be played face up.

    The Driver places his Hotwheels car on the first card and moves it forward one card at a time.

    - Cards with values of 5 or below the Driver can just drive right over. The GM can describe the open road.
    - Cards with values higher than 7 require the Driver to draw a card from the deck and attempt to overcome them. The GM should describe the difficulty.
    - If the Driver's draw exceeds the value of the challenge, the Driver describes how he easily maneuvers around the difficulty
    - If the Driver's draw does not equal or exceed the value of the challenge, the GM describes the failure but the Driver continues on, with the car taking the brunt of the damage. The GM describes how everything is going to shit.
    - If the Driver hits a face card, he cannot go through the challenge but has to go around it. Begin offroading.

    To offroad, begin playing cards face-down off the side of the path, maneuvering around the face card until you can get back on the road. Flip over the challenges for off-roading one card at a time, treating them as normal. Yes, you sometimes have to offroad from your offroad.

    For enhanced fun, add other cars (played by other players?) who are trying to catch the Driver. I'm not sure how.

    With more/better rules and playtesting this game could be a lot better.

    MAGNETIC ANIMALS
    For this game you're going to need:
    -a GM plus 1-5 players
    -a working, running clock on which you can easily change the time it shows
    -a bag with three white and three black stones or three coins or three dice or yarrow sticks (I-Ching)

    PLAYERS
    Write down your name.

    You have 77 minutes to save the world, but all you have with you is:
    (pick one from each list, two players can't pick the same object)

    Left pocket:
    torn pages from a library copy of Magic Without Tears by Alistair Crowley, photocopies of spells in the AD&D spellbook, a notepad with dozens of clumsily copied sigils from Simon's Necronomicon, a children's Bible with lots of pictures, a tattered paperback of The Western Lands by William S. Burroughs, a black and white photo of your deceased grandmother, a cheap pack of tarot cards with a few major arcana missing, a backwards misprint of Alice through the Looking Glass, a scratched CD of West African music

    Right pocket:
    a few pellets of mescaline, a cigarette spiked with hashish oil, a square of LSD, a quart of vodka mixed with strong black coffee, a smiley pill, a tube of glue, peyote in a plastic bottle, a bag of sugar cubes, a bottle of ether, some benzedrine, cheap coke, snuff, drain cleaning liquid

    You're either scrawny or fat or if you're not then you're sick: either way you can't fight for shit.

    GM
    Explain the mechanics, then let them make characters. After the characters are made, set the clock to 22: 43, then start a scene with all the characters. They all know the world is ending, but nobody will believe them.

    All the time:
    -keep and adjust the doomsday clock
    -describe what they perceive through their five senses
    -answer their questions honestly, but not beyond what their characters would reliably know

    When you have a reason:
    -call the police on them
    -call their family on them
    -take their stuff
    -lock them away
    -wash your hands and let them fuck up themselves

    MECHANICS
    Each player says, in order: what happens, how it happens and why it happens, then rolls, flips or draws three, in order for each statement:
    White stones, even numbers on dice, heads on coins or full lines on sticks are YES
    Black stones, odd numbers, tails and broken lines are NO
    On a NO, the player's statement was false, the GM embellishes. It's not flat-out denial, don't just say \u201cNo.\u201d, rather say \u201cNo, but...\u201d or \u201cNo, and...\u201d or even \u201cYes, but...\u201d.

    The player can state things about the game table, too. Seating order, rules, the time the clock is showing...all's fair.

    \nBLACK IMPALA
    A game about fucked up demon hunters

    Monsters are real. Demons, vampires, werewolves, all that shit.

    You hunt monsters. Hunting monsters is awesome because you get to drive around in a cool car with a bunch of guns in the trunk and fight evil. It sucks because you're basically homeless, you've seen friends and family killed in horrible ways, and no matter how many monsters you kill, there's always more. So you're kind of fucked up.

    You need a bunch of index cards and a handful of change. Pregame setup is writing, say, 10 useful facts about monsters (like "sunlight kills vampires" or whatever) on the index cards. All of these things are indisputable parts of the game's lore.

    Your stats are Monster Killing, Lore Research, Street Smarts, and Smooth Moves. Divide 9 points among your stats.
    Pick one thing you're really good at, other than killing monsters.
    Write down the one thing you miss the most about your normal, pre-monster-killing life. You don't do anything with this, it just sits there in the middle of your character sheet reminding you of what you've had to give up.
    Your Fucked Up score starts at 1. Decide what you're fucked up about.

    To do stuff, toss coins equal to your stat. Heads are good, tails are bad. You succeed if you have more good than bad. You can take away bad coins by raising your Fucked Up score.

    When you throw Killing Monsters, you get to add one good coin for every established monster fact you work into your description of your monster killing scene.

    When you throw Lore Research, you get to make up one more monster fact for every good coin.

    The GM will hit you with life challenges, like "you're out of money for gas and ammo". You throw Street Smarts to overcome those.

    The more Fucked Up you are, the more the GM gets to screw with your character.

    I don't know what Smooth Moves do yet.

    My cigarette's done, so I'm calling that 6 minutes and some change.

    \nNEW EARTH DEFENSE LEAGUE
    A game about psychic sailors saving whales

    It's 1987 and you're on a shabby boat in the middle of the Arctic ocean as a Soviet whaler bears down on a pod of whales. Their psychic screams are drowning out your thoughts.

    Characters

    Pick one of the following: telekinesis, precognition, mind control, astral projection, death touch, kung-fu. This is the special power you think you have.

    Pick one of the following acronyms: UCLA, WWF, CIA, KGB, HPM, OD, UN, DLC. This organisation has some hold on your life.

    Challenges

    Every few minutes, the GM will present you with a situation of some sort. Soviet whalers firing harpoons, storm-wracked seas, dying whale pods.

    Roll a d10. If you get a 10, you have a psychic power and you can use it to overcome the challenge. If you don't, you have to let one of the organisations noted above fuck up your life in some way to overcome it.

    ~end~

    Okay, that's pretty shit. Wasn't really helped by someone coming and interrupting me in the middle of the process, neither.

    \nReal Soldiers Never Die

    You are one of an elite corps of the Real Soldiers, the last line of defense stopping the forces of Entropy that seek to destroy reality.

    Soldiers can: Fight, Convince, Repair. Real Soldiers can travel through the bounds of time and space via the technology developed by the Corps.

    Forces of Entropy can: Fight, Terrify, Destroy. FoE are limited to either a time or a space, but they are hard to destroy.

    \nAlone in a room

    Player one sits on a chair in a room. He/she gets no instructions before the game. The room has a few objects in it, for example: A bowl of candy. A piece of string. A chair. A pencil and some paper. A toy gun. A needle.

    On the wall there should be a big poster saying "This is not a game". On the other side, not showing, it says "this is a game".

    Player two enters the room. He/she issues commands. Player one is given no further explanation. There is no consequence if player one doesn't follow the commands, but this won't be explained.

    Player three enters the room. He/she starts opposing player two, and tries to make player one rebel. At this stage, player two should issue commands player one wants to follow.

    Player four enters the room. He/she takes notes along the way. He/she sometimes whispers things to player two and three; it doesn't matter what, and it will have no effect on their actions, but player one doesn't know that.

    At some point, player four should start whispering to player one. The whispers should be about how player one should feel. Things like: "You are angry at these two for fighting". Or "You really want that bowl of candy."

    Player four ends the game by turning the poster around so it says "this is a game". Everyone leaves the room in reverse order, in silence. Then, they can talk about the game.

    The Restaurant

    This is a game about a restaurant on a weekend night, full of lovey-dovey couples, drunkards, inexperienced youngsters and other people. The Finnish small town restaurant is not a night club, it's clientele is a snapshot of society, normal people. The height of iniquity in these circles is drinking too much and too consistently, dying of a failed liver later on.

    Each player character has Abilities for Cooking, Patter, Bouncing and Attention. You use Cooking to make foods (and complex drinks), Patter to service people (including at the bar), Bouncing to keep the crowd happy and in line, and Attention to do any niggly attention-requiring tasks, such as cleaning the place up after the night. All Abilities are dice pools, something like ten dice between them. Having a zero in something means that you're the sort of wreck who works in these places, completely unable to do the thing indicated.

    One player plays the Crowd. He gets a bunch of dice, each representing a customer. Whenever he rolls a bunch of dice on the table, leave them wherever they are: those customers just got into the restaurant. The customers want stuff; you can point to individual dice and describe what the people do. Also move the dice around to depict action. To succeed in servicing a customer or repairing something they cause, win the die value with a dice roll. Reroll any customers who are beaten, now they want something else. A customer leaves once he's drunk or has paired with another customer of different gender. If a customer leaves but has left something behind, keep the die somewhere the track the target number for fixing whatever it was.

    Each won customer-service brings one chip to the restaurant. A chip can be spent to improve a dice result by one point.

    The game is won if the restaurant is still standing and profitable at the end of the night, after clean-up.

    \n OK, so the first rule is this: Don't say anything if someone else is saying something. Not even if you think they're almost done and you've just GOT to get this word in before anyone else and you're JUST overlapping your first syllable with their last. Just DON'T.

    Second rule: Limit the time that you say something. Other people are waiting, DESPERATELY, perhaps, to say something in return. Let them. Say one or two sentences, and that's IT. If your character is speaking dialogue, then you get THREE sentences.

    Third rule: If it's said, it happened. The story proceeds, and it waits for no one ti mull over their options, or think of the best way to say it, or to go back and revise because they didn't say it right. If it comes out your mouth, it happened. Deal with it.

    So, the exceptions: you get two totems: a 3-minute hourglass timer, and a pencil. One of each for the whole table. If you want to talk for longer than 1-2 sentences, pick up the timer and turn it over. You get that long to speak uninterrupted. You CAN'T USE THE TIMER AGAIN UNTIL SOMEONE ELSE HAS TAKEN A TURN WITH IT.

    If you wanna say something not part of the story, hold up the pencil. You don't have to limit to just -12 sentences in this case, but don't go on forever. You're breaking game flow, so save it for something important. If someone else wants to get on with things, they can pick up the timer and turn it over. This does NOT count as their use of the timer. You CAN'T USE THE PENCIL AGAIN UNTIL SOMEONE ELSE HAS TAKEN A TURN WITH IT.

    Who do you want dead?

    Which of the other PCs do you trust the least?

    Bastard Memoir

    You are a man of greatness. Destined to live a daring life of adventure, fame or power. Charismatic, decisive, brilliant, you stride the land like a giant, and it is only fitting that such exploits should be chronicled by...YOU.

    After all, who else could?

    Everyone else plays his aides, assistants, family members, and other people in his orbit.

    The Bastard sets a scene, declaring location, location, what he is trying to accomplish, and who is there with him. The others all give short descriptions of what they are doing to assist (or not) the Bastard.

    The Bastard then narrates, in High Autobiographical Style, the beginning of the scene, including the actions of others based on their opening descriptions. The Bastard is callous, cruel, selfish, petulant, pompous, and abusive, but spins his actions in the most sympathetic and morally superior light possible.

    When you, the Bastard, have reached a point where all the others' descriptions have been brought into play (and the Bastard's reactions to them), stop. Let the others describe new actions based on what's happened and how the Bastard has treated them.     Then play out a denoument scene. The final disposition and fate of the Bastard and the Others will be revealed. This plays like all scenes, except that any Other who has renounced or abandoned the Bastard will have their own Denoument scene without any Bastard input. Multiple Others who qualify for separate Denoument can combine their Denouments if they wish.

    THE END.

    \nHISTORY HAD IT COMING

    At the end of the universe, one person is supposed to oversee the operation and kick-start the creation of the next one. There was a cock-up, and multiple people turned up. Fortunately, a necessary requirement for starting the new universe was access to time-travel technology, thus providing the means of clearing the matter up.

    Basics

    Each player takes the role of one of the candidates. Character prep consists of writing, on an index card, how that character came to invent or come into possession of time travel technology (TTT).

    At the start of the game, reveal these cards and place them in front of you.

    The Game

    Take it in turns to start a scene by describing when and where you are going. Your goal is to scrub one or more of the other characters by preventing them from gaining access to TTT. Every character is in every scene- naturally.

    Describe your actions, shouting across one another, etc, to reflect the real chaos that would ensue. Resolution is by means of 'spoken endurance'- that is, how long someone can argue their point without having to stop for breath.

    When the scene is resolved, the winner writes up what happened on an index card and places it in the middle of the table.

    Getting Scrubbed
    All player characters have 2 states- scrubbed and non-scrubbed. If you are scrubbed, you can start a scene to restore TTT to yourself. There is no penalty for currently being in a state of 'scrub'- TTT, remember? There are no paradoxes, just things that haven't already have happened yet.

    Recursion
    Inevitably, play may suffer recursion. To represent different version of the same character being present, use hand puppets and funny voices.

    Game End
    When only 1 character remains unscrubbed, he is declared the winner.

    \nKung Fu Mook

    In a world where action movie-like situations are commonplace, you are one of the guys who gets anonymously beaten down by the hero time after time. You die. Often. But it doesn't matter. You just keep coming back, over and over again, often against the exact same hero 20 minutes later. Your life, such as it is, is a living hell. But there is a way out. All you have to do is anonymously kill the hero, any motherfucking hero, and eternal rest is yours.

    To kill a Hero, you must:
    • Remain anonymous.
    • You must roll in your Kill Range, which defaults to 12+.
    • The Hero must roll in his fail range, which defaults to 1.
    \nThe Mook

    You have three things going for you:

    Numbers: You fight with 2d12 comrades (rolled separately for each Fight Scene), all identical to you.

    Anonymity: People rarely remember you. To Recognize you, they need to roll your Anonymity score or better on a d12. At the start of each movie, your Anonymity score starts at 12.

    Disguise: Your face is usually concealed by something, and that something gives you an edge. In any Fight Scene, you choose one of the following:\n
    • Visored Helmet: the helmet makes your harder to hurt, subtracting one from the Hero's roll.
    • Ninja hood: the hood makes you better able to sneak up on the Hero, adding one to your roll.
    • Mutated face: with your face transformed into a horrible creature, you are more dangerous and so, your kill range becomes 10+; however, the Hero is allowed to use more gratuitous violence against you, so adds 2 to his roll.
    • Natural face: with your natural face, you can Emote; however, the Hero has a chance to Recognize you.
    \nThe Hero

    The hero has this going for him:

    Destiny: The Hero is supposed to win. The longer the movie last, the more likely it is he or she will. The Hero adds their Destiny score to their roll. Destiny starts at 5.

    Movies

    A movie is a series of up five to Fight Scenes against the same Hero. Each time you fight, both you and the hero get a little better.

    Fight Scene

    If you are wearing your natural face, the Hero rolls a d12. If this roll equals or exceeds your Anonymity, you are Recognized. If you are Recognized, no matter what else happens in this scene, you cannot kill the Hero.

    Both you and the Hero roll a d12. These rolls are modified as follows:
      \n
    • Your disguise changes the rolls as described.
    • If you Emote, you describe how your facial expression makes it more likely you kill the Hero, and you add 5 to your roll.
    • The Hero adds his Destiny to his roll.
    • For every three of your fellow mooks, reduce the Hero's roll by 1.
    \nIf you kill the hero, the game is over. Otherwise...

    If the Hero has the higher roll, all the mooks die, including you, but the'll be back the next scene. Reduce your Anonymity score by 1. If you were Recognized in this scene, reduce it by an additional 2 (three total). The Hero's Destiny increases by 1.

    If you have the higher roll, all the mooks die, including you, but the'll be back the next scene. You, however, made an impression on the Hero. Reduce your Anonymity by 2. If you were Recognized in this scene, reduce it by an additional 2 (four total). The Hero is a bit shaken, so does not gain any Destiny.

    If the rolls are tied, the result is as if you won the roll, but the Hero's Destiny increases by 1.

    I went over the time limit. By like 20 minutes. And the end result is a dice game.

    You Are Badass
    Chris Sakkas

    Humanity is fucked and you are badass.

    This game does not measure how fucked humanity is or how badass you are.

    Rules
    When you try to do something, roll a die. 1-2, you fail \u2013 badly. 3-4, you fail but \u2026 or you succeed but \u2026. 5-6, you succeed.

    Don't like your result? Like your result a lot? Explain how something that matters to you a lot is on the line, and roll another die. Now, you can distribute those dice between your goal and the thing that matters.

    Thing that matters: 1-2, lost forever. 3-4, lost for now. 5-6, secure for now.

    Or, put BOTH dice on the one thing. If those are two fives or 2 sixes, the thing is succeeded mightily or secure forever.

    Yes, you can keep adding things that matter to the roll.

    Things That Matter
    Write down five things that matter to you, or ten or twenty if you like.

    Badass Level
    You begin as a badass. See Appendix 1: List of Badasses.

    If you lose everything that matters to you or you secure everything that matters to you, you become a motherfucker or you retire your character and make a motherfucker. Come up with another list of things that matter, this time things that matter to humanity.

    See Appendix 2: List of Motherfuckers.

    Playstyles
    This game can be played in two ways, either straight or ironically.

    Straight: If you are ironic, you lose your character. If you are ironic again, you are kicked out of the session. If you are ironic again, you are not allowed to play You Are Badass again.
    Ironically: This game cannot be played ironically.

    Appendix 1: List of Badasses
    Cops, spies, agents, Freemasons, psychics, magicians, sorcerers, PIs, etc.

    Appendix 2: List of Motherfuckers
    Mecha, monsters, people who can turn into monsters, people who can turn into mecha, demigods, things from the stars, etc.

      DEATH BY NUMBERS

      This is a highly narrative role-playing game that takes place within 5 minutes. 5 minutes and 5 seconds, to be precise; set your watches NOW (reading this is included in the game). By the way: this game was conceived and written in less than that time.

      4-9 players. Each player a pencil and a paper.

      The 1-2-3 Method

      1 - Make your characters:
      - first; a fictional name of our world
      - and then; 3 traits of our world (at least one of the traits must be murderous in some way).
      - First player to finish his character starts narrating, and will have his character as the sole survivor at the end of the game. It's not important that other players finish their characters completely, as they will all die anyway ...

      2 - Set up situations rapidly, letting the first character meet the others one by one, or in twos. Let there be some conflict, and let it end by murder.

      Try to make the scenes feed into eachother; like if the first scene has a murder (it has, of course), then the next scene is about how the murderer (the first charcter) spots a witness (another character), hunts it down, and kills it ... and the next scene can be about the detective ... etc.

      3 - end it by 5 minutes and 5 seconds. An alarm would be nice.

      Have a happy narration!

      Orcs

      When you lose a conflict you get as many scars as you have opponents. Everyone has a rank within the pack, if you have a conflict with someone and the one with lower rank wins trade ranks. Add your scars to your stat in conflict resolution. Pass the pigs seems fine (add scars to total score). 2D6, snake eyes = lose all score, a 1 = this turn you get 0. Target number orcs agree on. You can assist someone, it's super good for winning bullying but doesn't get YOU anything so hope asshole holds his part of the deal or beat him up later.

      The orc with the lowest rank finds items from time to time. Player decides what the item is and what it does. Taking an item from another orc is a conflict. Low level orcs find and fight over food and bones to gnaw on and high level orcs fight over kingdoms and black fortresses.

      You are always in a war.

      Example item:

      Piece of food: eat before a conflict to gain +2
      Evil sword: get +1 in all conflicts.

      \n ADVENTURE TIME PRIME

      A universal story game

      SETUP
      1. Collect 3-6 players, some index cards, writing materials, coins, and a timer. Sit at a table.
      2. Decide, by consensus, on a flavor for a game. This could be as general as "Lovecraftian horror" or as specific as "Das Boot". Don't get married to it yet, though.
      3. Each player gets two index cards. On each index card, write a name and three things that are true about a person that has that name. The game host collects these cards ("character cards") face up.
      4. Each player gets three index cards. On each index card, write a danger that you think could happen to a character in that flavor of game. The host collects these cards too ("danger cards") face down and shuffles them.
      5. Starting with the host and moving left, each player chooses a character card. You'll be primarily responsible for that character, but as you'll see, there's some sharing.
      6. Once everyone has a character, they add one fact to the card.
      7. The host deals a danger card to each player. Count to three and turn them over. Take five minutes, exactly, to figure out the initial situation, in which all the facts on the character cards, plus all the dangers, are true. Write down the initial situation, then take a short break. It is required to stand up and take at least one step away from the table at this time. Once everyone sits back down, play continues.

      PLAY:
      During this part, one player will have the timer in front of him/her. That player is the "dealer" (the timer is the "button").
      1. Set the timer for one hour and put it in front of the host, making the host the first dealer. The host begins narrating from the initial situation.
      2. The dealer narrates until a crisis is reached - that is, a point at which a danger card currently in front of some player could be overcome. Anyone else can speak during the narration too, and if no one disagrees, whatever they say is true. If any player thinks the dealer has reached a crisis - a point at which a danger could be removed from a player - he/she should say "Are you sure?" and point to the card that can be removed. At that point, the crisis is deemed reached.
      3. CRISIS: The player to the dealer's left chooses a new danger card from those not currently in front of any player, and not removed from the game. The dealer flips three different coins. The one with the lowest value is the success coin. If it comes up heads, the danger central to the crisis is resolved and removed from play entirely. The middle value coin is the special coin. If one of the dealer's character card facts is relevant to the situation, as determined by majority vote, then the dealer can use that coin instead of the success coin or the failure coin - or both. The largest coin is the failure coin. If it comes up heads, the dealer gives the new danger card to any player, himself included, and then wraps up by explaining how that danger is now true.
      4. Pass the button left, and continue from 2, until the timer expires. When that happens, wrap up the current turn and then stop. Take a break, walking away from the table, continuing when everyone returns.
      5. Play for one more hour, starting with the player whose turn it was next. But now, the game can also end by consensus, when you're out of dangers, when every character is unplayable, or when no player has a danger in front of him/her.

      CRAP. Over by thirty seconds. Still OK?

      \nEverybody write 3 words on the palm of both hands then play rock paper scissors.

      The winner matches winning rock, paper, or scissors with the 1st 2nd or 3rd word, respectively.

      Winner then crosses out that word and 'uses' the word in play.

      If, in a subsequent round, a Rock Paper Scissors win corresponds to a word that's already crossed-out,
      that reverses the win into a big Lose. So remember not to repeat.



      Time ran out while I contemplated whether big Lose = kicked in the balls.

      Now I'll just keep the clock running on this entry, and use it for Luke's contest, too.

      Playtesters: this could be a long year. do you have the balls?

      \n Second attempt:


      I dig in the trash and craft from trash.
      Kai uses only the finest materials to craft.

      "Finest materials", however, are only defined by the current regime of culture and class.
      Trash is eternal, beyond today's fleeting fads.

      The trash craftsman will perhaps attain the highest pure immortality,
      whereas Kai can only pander to mortal approval.

      Then again, trash may only result in trash and obscurity.

      Our brotherly competition is fierce, everything is at stake.
      It is easy to be disheartened, but the prize is worth it;
      if I can remember the prize is worth it.

      Resolution:
      Of course I roll trash and try to beat compare against his finery.

      \n ORCS IN A ROOM

      A game for 4-5 players

      You are orcs in a room. In a few minutes' time, adventurers will burst through the door and most likely kill all of you.

      Set a timer for 15, 20 or 30 minutes. One which ticks and rings loudly is best. Hide it. When it rings, the adventurers arrive.

      The tallest person in the lead orc in the room is the lead orc. The lead orc player places a six-sided die face up in front of him, with the six face up.

      The lead orc tells the other orcs the pecking order and they place the die with the appropriate number face up. The runt orc (the lowest) always has a one on the die.

      Players must hold playing cards as if they are playing an orcish card game.

      Place (number of players minus three) cups on the table - the rotgut.

      Place (number of players times five) tokens on the table - the loot.

      Play proceeds, with players laying cards and making up orcish hands.

      Eventually, a player says "I win" and stands up.

      Any player can challenge

      \n Here's my own, somewhat disjointed entry.

      365 SALVATION

      There's a new, easily made drug on the market: 365 rush.

      Take it an you're unstoppable at everything for 365 seconds. Afterwords, you die.
      This has rocked the world. As the balance of power shifts, what will be the fallout?

      Play is not linear: each scene is a different event showing an effect the drug is having on the world in microcosm.

      Example Scenes: Suicide bombing via unstoppable rampage, assassination attempts, revolts, murder-suicides, heroic martyrdom, meetings of heads of state, meetings of other groups in power, people at home on the home-front, people at home in a war-zone or area in a revolution. Choose dark themes, for these are dark times.

      System: Describe a series of events, other players add to this. Roll to determine the outcome:
      1: Fuck up.
      2-3: Complications
      4+: Just as Planned

      Those "rushing" roll 1d6, all others roll 1d4.
