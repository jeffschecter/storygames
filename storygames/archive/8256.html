\n A few days ago I posted this, then a little later someone posted this, so I'm willing to declare twice is enough to peg it a trend, and call for a general outpouring of clever play-enhancing uses of index cards.

How about random char-gen from a conceptual perspective? Write a bunch of cards with origins, another bunch with shticks and another stack with goals and blind-pick one of each. So you could end up with "a albino galley-slave taken from home at early age" who is "a master of sea-snake style kung-fu" and is "on a quest to kill his own mother".

Or draw little sketches of streets, alleys and other locales. When you need to generate a chase sequence, just shuffle and lay 'em out to make the map.

What else you got?

    \n In [i]Super Action Now![/i] we use index cards to keep track of the stats of the ever-increasing numbers of "characters" (which, the game's rules is anything with stats and an agenda -- so if you Endow the fridge with "The fridge hates Steve 2d10" it's a character now).

    I'm working on another game (the Castlevania one) where you write out the effects of your combat maneuvers on index cards (simple stuff like "Snap stance: Pow +2 / Def -1 / Spd +5" and a grid diagram of the area of effect relative to your position), and each round consists of a match-and-show of maneuvers from everyone involved. It's sort of like chess meets rock-paper-scissors, except you customize your the moves.

    \n To get things back on track:

    As a general tool, index cards are great for being clear physical boundries to certain kinds of input. Lots of games offer strictures such as "describe it in 50 words" but I dislike taking time to count them out, even in estimate (neither do I like arguing if "thusly" is enough of an article to count against the limit or not). Much easier just to hand a player a 4x6 card and tell them "your character background has to fit on that, written legibly."

    \n The very fact that Eco publishes books and articles in english is probably a sign that he has 'discovered' index cards just like I did, by exposure tu American culture... but hey, it's worth checking. Also, apologies again to E. T. Smith for derailing this thread, I did not mean it.

    To mitigate this, I'll throw in some other uses for Index Cards:

    Shock: ICs can be used to track minutiae and keep them handy for later games, just rubber band the ICs together and keep them with your *Tagonists sheets and Shock/Issue grid.

    1001 nights: ICs seem to be a natural choice to jot down the Player's wagers/story ideas, with the Gems on them to roll.

    SotC: as mentioned, for Scene, Campaign and other Aspects. Not only to write them down, but also to visually cue when they are tagged: "tap" them, or flip them over, or place a token on them.


    General uses:

    Minions and Villains: if it can't fit on an IC it's probably overdone anyway (in systems that let you write all the mechanical stuff in very little space, that is). This way you can keep track of who's in scene, mark off "casualties" on the edge of the card, and maybe even let players rip them in pieces when defeated (for that cathartic experience).

    Abstract Map Pieces: I kinda remember some Hipster PDA templates for mind mapping that would totally work for this, also: useful for SotC/Fate Zones, but also for RedBoxHack, and all those games that don't really require a grid-map, but will benefit from actually knowing where is something, on a "tactical" level. Each card can be a "place", with arrows connecting and pointing to other cards. The map will build naturally, and if you are careful you can rebuild it for the next session. On each of them, mark the most important stuff of that zone/place: people, features, mechanical modifiers.


    There, that was off the top of my head, I'm sure there's more.

    \n Ok, to make amends for joining in thread derailing, my contribution:

    For playstorming / playtesting

    Write possible player actions on index cards. You can also describe some specifics or mechanical requirements and/or effects. One action per card. You can shuffle through the cards when thinking how to act and quickly modify the set of actions in each playtest by including the cards you like to test.

    \n How about a brainstorm session of NPC and plot generation? Take one stack of cards and have everyone write character descriptions on them. Then take an equal stack and everyone writes motivations and open-ended personal connections (hates the red card, suspects the blue card of eloping). Shuffle each stack, then randomly match one motive card to each character card. The GM collects the results and then the situation is played.

    I have this cool trick I learned a few years back. It is called a Deck of Secrets.

    I take one card and write one interesting/adventure-seeding secret about any important part of my game (a NPC, a villain, a place in the setting, an event). Then, next time I am thinking about what I'd use for tonight's game I shuffle those cards and randomly draw one or two. Then I somehow integrate them in the adventure. That way I get to tie the cool stuff with new stuff, in a way that allows me to organically work without ever needing to "script" everything. And the cool thing is since I'm only giving some clues, I get to use the same secret several times. That is, unless I decide to make my deck grow with every session.
