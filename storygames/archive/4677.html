\n So I just got this smokin' new computer at work. I want to get myself a new computer game to celebrate. Here are my requirements.

1) Fantasy is preferred but not essential.
2) Gameplay shouldn't be too complicated nor too trivially easy. (See #5 below)
3) It should lend itself to periodic short-session play, e.g., hour a day every few days.
4) Solo play only--no internet play.
5) Diablo/Diablo2 is my paradigm. That's exaclty the kind of game I'm looking for. I recently played Dungeon Siege II, which is very close to what I'm looking for but just a little too laden with quests. I like my stuff very linear with only a few quests at any given time.

    \n Thanks for the replies. Titan Quest looks like just the ticket. I have to say, I'm intrigued by Oblivion. I've heard good things about it before, but I'm wary of its utter open-endedness. Since I'm playing at work, it will basically be a half-hour to an hour a day every other day or so. And there will probably be some times when I'll go a week without playing. I need to be able to pick it up again without worrying about forgetting what I'm supposed to do next. That was part of the problem with Dungeon Siege II and having 5+ quests open at any time. If I had to shut the game down for a week, I wouldn't know where to start after that. It got frustrating, resulting in a stall about two-thirds of the way through.

    So, do you think my limitations would make Oblivion a bad choice? Should I stick with Titan Quest?

    (Relatedly, one of the concerns I have with Titan Quest is that you start over at the refresh pool not at the point-of-save. I may have stretches where I can't push on to the next pool resulting in some repetitive play.)

    Posted By: shreyasYou know, I never understood the appeal of roguelike games. Is it, like, just nostalgia? They just don't seem to have anything built-in going for them.
    \nPartly, I suspect. But the thing is that there's this crazy replayability angle, with the RNG making everything. You're delving into the earth, and you have no idea what's down there, you don't know what's good to drink and what'll kill you, and all you've got is one life and all the loot you can grab. It's as pure an old-skool RPG experience you can get, without having to deal with dice rolling, paperwork and the tables.

    Mind you, Incursion won me over when I realized that I could climb up to the ceiling and bypass obstacles like chasms and rivers of magma. I haven't really experienced anything like hanging on to the ceiling of a kilometre-long magma river in a computer game before, so it was kind of nice.
