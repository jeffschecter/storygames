\n I'm trying to bring some direction to this game I've had kicking around in my head for almost fifteen years. Any two cents are valued--constructive criticism only, please.







The core conceit is that each player has a bag filled with beads--same shape and size, different colors. (I use pony beads, cheap and plentiful.) The core mechanic is the draw, analogous to a die roll in most games: the player pulls 3-5 beads from the bag, either comparing them to an established target, or interpreting them in some less quantified manner.

Here's some background, by way of showing what I don't want: back in 2005 or so, I was using the beads like Fate dice, where each color simply provided a stat modifier (+0s and +1s were common, +4s and +5s were rare). Experience points could add certain colors to the bag, thereby tweaking your character's actual success probabilities, or fate. This felt nifty enough in 2005, but feels like last week's leftovers now.

My current goal is to make filling the bag of beads the hot tech of character creation. This could occur in turns around the table, or players could choose an archetype with a certain allotment of starting beads and then purchase additional ones at the cost of black (consequence) beads also being added... there are several ways this could work.

Here's my current frustration: what could these beads stand for, both in character creation, and when drawn? I'm using 9 colors right now: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Violet, White, Clear, and Black. With 6 colors and 3... not colors(?), it's tempting to go with 6 stats and 3 narrative triggers, like this:

-------------------------
Red - Careful
Orange - Clever
Yellow - Flashy
Green - Forceful
Blue - Quick
Violet - Sneaky
-------------------------
White - Wild (any color)
Clear - Narrative Privilege
Black - Consequence
-------------------------

No, I don't want to use the FAE approaches particularly, but they're holding place for now. You could easily do STR/DEX/CON/INT/WIS/CHA.

So, let's say you rank your 6 stats from 1-6, and get an appropriate number of starting beads:

--------------------------------
Red - Careful - 30
Orange - Clever - 25
Yellow - Flashy - 20
Green - Forceful - 15
Blue - Quick - 10
Violet - Sneaky - 5
--------------------------------
White - Wild (any color) - 2
Clear - Narrative Privilege - 2
Black - Consequence - 10

Then you can spend some of those, or take on additional black (consequence) beads, to purchase whites and clears, which cost 2 for colors, only 1 for blacks.

--------------------------------
Red - Careful - 29
Orange - Clever - 24
Yellow - Flashy - 19
Green - Forceful - 14
Blue - Quick - 9
Violet - Sneaky - 0
--------------------------------
White - Wild (any color) - 8
Clear - Narrative Privilege - 8
Black - Consequence - 17
--------------------------------

Okay, so how do tasks and conflicts work? Let's start with tasks: you pick your approach (going with the FAE model for now), or work out with the GM which stat will be most relevant, and make a 5-bead draw, trying to show 1, 2, or 3 of that color, depending on the difficulty/complexity of the task. With the numbers above, this is pretty tough, except for two things: the wild beads can be any color, and you can spend beads to achieve success. So, if you need 3 reds to carefully trail a suspect, but you only show 1 in your draw, you can spend it, scoring the necessary 3, but you never get it back. Perhaps you could even spend off-color beads to achieve lower levels or different types of success than you were aiming for.

* * * * * * * * * * *

Well, that's enough for now. Let me try to list what I'm struggling with:

* How to do character creation: archetypes, or...?
* What do the beads signify? Must they be stats, identical across all characters, or could they be generated by each player individually? If the latter, how would task resolution work, i.e., how could the GM set difficulties when all the character traits are different? Could it just be subjective resolution? "I hope to give a good impression to the stranger, so I draw 5 and try to hit X number of Y color, which is my Outstanding Personality."
* Is any of this fun?

    \n Spitballing here...

    What about colours=emotions? So creating your character means defining their emotional beats, e.g:
    Red (Anger) = 30
    Orange (Happiness) = 25
    Yellow (Fear) = 20
    Green (Envy) = 15
    Blue (Sadness) = 10
    Violet (Desire) = 5

    You could further define each emotion by assigning it a unique trait for your character, so I might have Anger (Fight) but you might have Anger (Intimidate) or some such. Performing an action is then about pulling beads from the bag that match the emotional state of your character, with extra beads being drawn when using the trait associated with that emotion and the Black, White and Clear beads being wild as before.

    You could also do this with far fewer beads per bag, if you just need one bead of the right colour to succeed; there could even be a 'colour wheel' option, that allows you to shift your emotional state one step clockwise or anti-clockwise to rescue a partial success from what would otherwise be a failure. For example, I try to pull a Red bead for Anger (Fight) but if I only get an Orange bead, then I can switch to Happiness (Make a bargain) instead and take a lesser level of success instead of a total failure.

    \n I'm down with the idea of beads representing whatever aspects/characteristics/traits are fundamental to your game's theme...

    Regarding character creation, what about a series of questions that subtly reveal the character's moral fiber (or whatever)? Each one could have, say, three different answers, and each answer corresponds to a certain number/type of beads that are added to the bag. You could potentially tailor questions to specific roles that are meant to appear in the game, or make them random for everyone (or have random subsets for each role, possibly with various questions overlapping multiple roles).

    Instead of having the game's task resolutions call for a specific attribute, the player could draw first and then see what she can do with what she pulls. This might create the undesirable (for you as a designer, or for the player, whatever) situation of forcing a character to take an approach that will lead to "failure" (from a material perspective, at least) because her goals are overridden by her virtues, or something like that; I don't know if that's the kind of game you're going for or not. Maybe some sort of system where choosing to override your detrimental pulls means discarding beads that correspond to a certain attribute, which means you can't call on it later on when you need it, and may even result into a kind of spiral of moral depravity as the character discards her ideological underpinnings in the pursuit of a goal.

    \n If you have different "moves" or actions you can make in your game, you can associate those with the colours, too. Ideally you have a choice of one or two colours whenever you draw, however.

    My favourite part is your "Consequence" beads. It's really interesting that the number you draw influences the outcome - perhaps good or bad, it makes the outcome more weighty.

    I'd like to see more of that kind of thing:

    The draw gives us narrative constraints on how to resolve the action. Was it consequential? Did someone get hurt? Will it cost more than you expected? Will there be unforeseen benefits?

    For character creation, start with a small pool and then add and/or take out beads as things progress. (Some of this could happen in character creation or "preludes" like Dogs in the Vineyard's "initiation scenes".)

    That would allow you to make character creation and character advancement seamless. Perhaps one bead colour is "advancement" or "Experience", and whenever you draw that colour you have some options (adding new beads, giving away some beads, exchanging beads). This way, the collection of beads in your bag is always changing as you play, transforming your character.

    As a simple example:

    Any time you draw a "Learning" bead, you can choose to downgrade your success, and hand it in to exchange for a bead of another colour.

    ...

    Another crazy idea:

    Borrow from chemistry and make combinations of colours/beads to create effects. Two blue and one red means a [Fickle molecule], which allows you to tag certain effects, but not others.

    That's probably too complex, but could be interesting if it suits the theme of your game somehow.

    \n I love the idea! The title immediately makes me think of the game from the Hermann Hesse book:

    https://sites.google.com/site/abimepublications/home

    ...but I'm not proposing adapting that as a story game!

    1) Including negative consequences in the bag gives each draw a Dread-type feel. Sure, you'll probably succeed but...there is a risk every time you exert yourself. And without replacement, the risk could grow with each draw.

    The consequence beads could just be negatives in a mechanical sense (-1), but I like the idea that they scale up in potency if they clump up. So when you draw a fistfull of beads, and count up the negatives:

    1 black bead: nothing bad (but nothing good...just a lost bead)
    2 black beads: minor consequence (scene complication)
    3 black beads: major consequence (session complication)
    ...

    You could give players discretion in how "hard" they try (how many beads they draw). So trying harder might raise your chances of success at the given task, but it also raises your chances of disaster. So how desperate are you?

    2) How often is the bag refreshed? Do you add beads back every draw, or is it only replenished after a narrative action such as rest?

    I like the idea of mapping fatigue onto the bag. If only rest replenishes the bag, then at some point you'll run empty and won't be able to perform pulls without rest. And if you're adding consequences as you go and not replenishing with other beads, the probability of consequence keeps going up.

    3) What adds negative consequences into the bag? Mechanically, you could have them represent character stress from over-exertion (running, combat, magic). Perhaps the player makes a draw and comes up short, but has the option of drawing additional beads...at the cost of adding a consequence bead.

    But are consequences permanent (go back into bag) or just experienced once? I was assuming that they never reenter the bag, at which point they aren't that bad unless they start clumping up.

    Of course, you could use two colors of beads for consequences: permanent (black?) and singleton (grey?). A permanent consequence bead would be pretty painful.

    4) What adds positive beads? Presumably this is an XP kind of reward system. And if the colors mark approaches or traits, then adding a particular color is similar to advancing that ability or skill. But do players just buy them with XP, or are they rewarded for using an approach or ability well by getting more of that ability? Or there could be Lady Blackbird-style character keys, or moral consequences for taking an easy but immoral choice.

    5) I agree with Jeph that "Try to draw at least n beads of $COLOR" isn't much different than a dice roll. But instead of declaring your approach and then drawing against that, what if you drew first and then had to work out your actions with whatever approach beads you drew?

    This removes some player agency and forces some improv, but could give an interesting "molded by circumstances" flavor to character development. Maybe the player designed his character as a big lug (many Forceful beads, few Quick), but at the crucial moment of the session somehow draws a fistfull of Quick? The player had something else in mind, but now he has to roll with what he's given. If the player pulls it off, maybe the character learned something about himself and should be rewarded with more Quick.
