For some reason, organizing it this way makes the advice easier for me to grok. I'm sure we could spend a lot of time talking about how certain parts fall under more than one heading, but this way works for me:

\n

The Master of Ceremonies

\n

That\u2019s you, the MC, Apocalypse World\u2019s GM.

\n

There are a million ways to GM games; this is the way to GM Apocalypse World:

\n

Guidance

\n
    \n
  • Make Apocalypse World seem real, so...
    \n
    \n
      \n
    • Barf Forth Apocalyptica
      • \n
    • Say what your prep demands
      • \n
    • Say what honesty demands
      • \n
    • Address yourself to the characters, not the players.
      • \n
    • Make your move, but misdirect.
      • \n
    • Make your move, but never speak its name.
      • \n
    • Name everyone, make everyone human.
      • \n
    • Think offscreen too.
      • \n
    • Sometimes, disclaim decision-making.
      • \n
    • Respect the line between the players jobs and the MC's job.
      • \n
    • \n
  • Make the players\u2019 characters\u2019 lives not boring, so...
    \n
    \n
      \n
    • Say what the principles demand
      • \n
    • Look through crosshairs.
      • \n
    • Ask provocative questions and build on the answers.
      • \n
    • Respond with fuckery and intermittent rewards.
      • \n
    • \n
  • Play to find out what happens, so...
    \n
    \n
      \n
    • Say what the rules demand
      • \n
    • Be a fan of the players\u2019 characters.
      • \n
    • \n

    \n I do think there are some interesting/provocative (to me!) ideas hiding in looking for unlikely matchups:

    Saying what my prep demands to make my characters' lives not-boring: Sure as shit implies I'd better not be prepping boring stuff!

    Saying what honesty demands to make my characters' lives not-boring: I think this means facing some very hard honest things. I know you don't like that your character is caught inside an abandoned car body, surrounded by angry cannibals, with no way out. But I told you -- I TOLD YOU -- there was nowhere to hide. I TOLD YOU they weren't gonna just give up. I'm your character's fan, but jeebus you've gone out of your way to make enemies and stupid choices. You're the architect of your own demise here. He's going down and he brought all this on himself. Script immunity is gonna feel contrived and we both know it.

    Never speak your move's name to find out what happens: I like this matchup a lot. It means keeping the action really grounded in the fiction, rather than trying to run the math on your optimal play. If you're not sure if I've just announced future badness or put you in a spot, I think that's a useful bit of obfuscation.

    Barf forth apocalyptica to find out what happens: Also interesting I think. What's the logical conclusion of a hardhold that's built on a sinking tanker, with just a couple crufty motorboats but a pair of brothers, both dying of cancer, vying to become the hardholder? Dunno! A straight matchup between two gangs is one thing, but what if one of them is hooked on a drug that lets them see five minutes ahead in the maelstrom?

    Be a fan of your characters to make the world seem real, I think, is one of the few happy moments/implications in the game. The MC likes you, she really really likes you, so the regular fuckovers mean something other than just a nasty, indifferent universe grinding you down. It's not The Road.

    Just kind of riffing.

    Here's another one:

    "Think offscreen, too" and "be a fan of the players' characters".

    This is suggesting, to me, to think of possible repercussions on people and places offscreen; remember that the characters are badasses whose presence and whose action has impact on the wider world, and then be sure to celebrate that together.
