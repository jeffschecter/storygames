Universal Roleplaying Tools

Josh Roby
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
So in a few other threads which I'm too lazy to link to right now, folks have been talking about tools and techniques that are applicable to most if not all roleplaying games. The carpentry metaphor, comparing Ikea flatpack bookshelves against making your own bookshelves, comes up often. We speculate after the existence of "the rotary saw of roleplaying" by which we mean a tool that could be used in multiple games just as a rotary saw can be used in multiple carpentry projects.

So what I'd like to do is brainstorm with you folks on what those (semi-)universal tools and techniques might be. I'm not so interested in concepts (like flags) so much as actual things that players can do at or away from the table, before or during the game. Here's an example:

Character Background, the Jigsaw of Roleplaying
How It's Used: Write up a relatively brief synopsis of your character's history prior to the start of the game. Who were their parents, where did they grow up, and how did they discover that they had super powers / a great destiny / a certain roguish charm that made them become player characters? The technique allows you to shape your character -- hence "jigsaw."
Benefits: The character background gives players context for their own characters, a place to start from and some specifics to ground their roleplaying in. Actual play will often develop past the details of the character background, but especially for the first session the background gives a sense of where the character is coming from. Additionally, GMs can mine the character backgrounds for material to include in the running game.
Limitations: Depending on player expectations, the character background can be "ignored" or "exploited" by the GM; without guidelines as to how the background should be incorporated into play, there is always the too much or too little problem. Additionally, when the GM mines the background for material, it can be difficult identifying what is a flag for player interest and what is expository context uninteresting to the player. If the character backgrounds are written in isolation, they can make threading characters together more difficult, since the characters become firmly entrenched in incompatible positions.

So tell me, Story Games: what's in your toolbox?

    Pre-loaded Character Color
    How It's used: Look at your characters abilities, spells, skills, whatever - the things he or she can do in game. For any of these, you can come up with cool descriptions or phrases to use ahead of time. So instead of just saying 'I'm shooting him' it can be 'I flip the safety off my Kalishnikov and bring the stock to my shoulder'. Or 'My eyes glaze over in concentration as I focus my telepathy on him'. You can write these on index cards or post-its or something for easy finding when you're in game.
    Benefits: It adds variety, and gives you a chance to shine quickly at the game table. Plus, it can be done away from the table for fun when your group's not around. Good for both players and GMs. Gives players a chance and a reason to contribute color in-game.
    Limitations: It can certainly be overdone - to the point where its not clear what you're actually doing mechanically or to the point where its annoying to your group.

    3x5 Cards of Doom
    How it's Used: This is a GM tool. The GM carries around a few 3x5 cards wherever he goes. Every new idea the GM gets is put on a 3x5 card, and filed. Ideas start off in an "inspiration" folder, and get moved up to an "on deck" folder when they're fully statted-up and prepared for play. After they get used, they get moved to an "in play" folder, and if they can no longer be used, they get moved to a "graveyard" folder. Notes are made on the cards when their status changes. Artistic GM's can sketch pictures on the back. Cards that are slated to be used in a particular play session can be pulled out and clipped together to simplify play.
    Benefits: The GM has a ready resource to review all of the characters, locations, plots, etc. that are currently active in the game, or are ready to be brought into the game, that doesn't require power or an internet connection.
    Limitations: Fiddling with cards adds handling time, moreso as the number of cards increases.

    I've used this method many times and I'm a firm believer, especially when running DnD.

    The Character Map

    How It's Used: This is a sheet of paper depicting the network structure in which characters are enmeshed, linking them in terms of whatever relational ties are significant--kinship and sex only for the pure "relationship map," a la Sorceror, broader in other uses.

    Simple Example: Snape <--- teacher/student (dislikes) ---> Harry <---student/teacher (likes) ---> Dumbledore (with an additional link between Snape and Dumbledore).

    Benefits: The patterns of interdependence among characters are easy to see, providing situation and potential lines of action for a GM; if put in front of the players the map itself becomes a medium of play, in that players begin to orient their actions so that the map changes in their favor.
    Limitations: With numerous characters in multiplex relations, they can be quite complex to draw, since the number of potential ties grows geometrically with each additional character added to the map. They are probably most useful in cases where one has a relatively small and stable cast of characters.

    The Bag of Rumors

    I've used this for traditional GM-driven con games and short-shots where I have some setting details that are critical to the scenario, but I don't want to lecture on it.

    How it's used: It's like a the random rumor table in a bag. Get some index cards, write short rumors and factoids on them, along with some information about the NPC that gave them the info and maybe the location that they heard it in. Do one for each character. Put them in the bag. At the start of the session, pass the bag around and have 'em pick.

    Benefits: You've just seriously parallelized your information-sharing. If you've got (n) players, you now have (n) pieces of data and (n) NPCs and (n) locations to work with, very quickly. Engaged players will be eager to communicate your information.

    Drawbacks: Too much information may paralyze a large group that is prone to overanalysis. (Interesting that parallelize and paralyze sound so similar.)

    I have it in my head to try a more player-centric variant of this, where the players write the rumor cards, and a player is rewarded when they play to the material on the rumor card they've been dealt. Probably do this as a bolt-on to Heroquest or Over the Edge or a similar game with powerful game currency and slender rules.

Imagine a town where stuff went down like this:

    At the weapons shop:

    "Oh ho, I see you are wearing armor that bears the craftmark of my despicable rival, and so though you have spent good money on it I will not sell you this keen and unbreakable sword that you very obviously desire unless you take off the armor and smash it with a hammer."

    Buying potions:

    "Aren't you the one who smashed the armor my brother sold to you? Sorry, we're out of stock. Come back later."

    Now I don't know that that's particularly fun, but it's enabled by knowing who's connected to whom how. If anything, that's a sim technique.

    Facial expressions and voice

    How it's used: Practise different facial expression that your character often use, and try to find the voice of the character. Also find some "catch phrases" the character often use.
    Benefits: It will give you a much stronger feeling of the character, and hopefully it will also give the other players a better understanding of the character.
    Limitations: If it is overdone it will become silly.

    On the other hand, this sentence makes me think I'm in the wrong discussion. I think that that flagging is exactly this kind of tool. Whether or a not a game makes it's flags explicit, they're still an incredible communication tool. Even before the concept was identified, DMs everywhere have looked at a party with CHA 4 across the board and re-thought the courtly intrigue plot they'd been planning.
