\n A while ago I posted about the idea of dungeons as a form of conflict resolution. I've been thinking about it a lot recently, and the post about Ryan's D&D hacks really cemented things for me. Here's the first draft of my D&D hack "Say Yes or Face the Dungeon!"

This is a hack for D&D which turns dungeons (or any other closed series of encounters) into a conflict resolution system. In this hack, the players say what their characters are doing, and the DM either agrees (says yes), or makes them "Face the Dungeon". Facing the dungeon works just like conflict resolution in any other game. The GM and the players set stakes together, then the characters (or only some of the characters) face the dungeon. I\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll talk more about what \xe2\u20ac\u0153facing the dungeon\xe2\u20ac\x9d means below. If the characters beat the dungeon, they win their stakes. If they don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t, they lose the stakes. There are two complications:

First, in normal D&D, you hardly ever lose. Sure, you get beat up, sometimes a character dies, but a total failure to beat a dungeon is a pretty rare thing. If you do fail, it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s also problematic, because usually this means that all the PCs are dead. Boring. So, how to change this? Ryan\xe2\u20ac\u2122s \xe2\u20ac\u0153Raising the Stakes\xe2\u20ac\x9d hack provides the answer. The problem with \xe2\u20ac\u0153Raising the Stakes\xe2\u20ac\x9d is that, absent any other factor, running out of hit points doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t really have any negative consequences, or if it does, they are entirely at the hands of the DM. In \xe2\u20ac\u0153Say Yes or Face the Dungeon!\xe2\u20ac\x9d the consequences of everyone running out of hit points are outlined during stakes setting. For example, if the conflict was \xe2\u20ac\u0153Convince this hamlet to ally with the nearby Elves\xe2\u20ac\x9d, and the stakes were \xe2\u20ac\u0153they ally with the Elves or else they ally with the Orcs\xe2\u20ac\u0153, and the characters faced a dungeon in which they all ran out of hit points, even though the characters are still alive, the consequences of failure happen, the hamlet allies with the Orcs. To really give this teeth, the DM should ramp up the difficulty of all encounters. I\xe2\u20ac\u2122d suggest increasing the numbers of enemies faced, rather than the CR. Four times as many enemies in general seems a good rule. Opponents even one or two CRs higher than the party are a very big threat.

Second problem: What can the PCs achieve? You can\xe2\u20ac\u2122t declare a conflict \xe2\u20ac\u0153I take over the world\xe2\u20ac\x9d, and expect your first level character to succeed. What\xe2\u20ac\u2122s needed is a simple chart that outlines the base CR for a given conflict, to give the DM a guideline. Outlining twenty separate difficulty levels is a pain though. Once again, Ryan comes to the rescue, with E6. Six levels of challenge is much more manageable, and I think it fits better with the aims of this hack as well. The idea that the most world-sweeping changes to the world are achievable by a sixth level character makes total sense. Here\xe2\u20ac\u2122s the prototype chart, showing the level of the challenge and what can be achieved:

1: Influence a single person of moderate power (a town mayor, the priest of a small church), or a small group of insignificant power (an entire hamlet, part of a town).
2: Influence several people of moderate power (a noble family, a clique of courtiers, a council of elders), or a large group of weak power (a whole town, a minor city gang, a local militia). Create a relationship with a single person of moderate power that allows for significant favours. Gain titles, land, or wealth that grants minor status in a small region.
3: Influence a single person of significant power (a baron or duke, a high-ranking member of a major religion, or a small group of moderate power (another adventuring party of equivalent level, a political faction, a gang of bandits). Gain titles, land, or wealth that grants great status in a small region, or else some recognition that is globally renowned.
4: Influence a single person of great power (a king or queen, the leader of a major religion), or large groups of moderate power (cities, clans, faiths). Gain titles, land, or wealth that grants great status in a large region.
5:
6: Create a lasting relationship with a person of great power. Gain control of vital resources for large areas.
7: Influence a single person of immense power (a mighty wizard or dragon, celestials and demons), or a large group of great power (armies, minor races) Become a King or Queen.
8:
9:
10: Influence a deity. Sway nations, or entire races.


That\xe2\u20ac\u2122s super rough though. I think this is a really tricky thing to get right, and I\xe2\u20ac\u2122m wondering if there\xe2\u20ac\u2122s a better way to do it. Perhaps a simple extension of stake setting? So, if the DM feels the player is asking for too much, they can simply say \xe2\u20ac\u0153Ok, but it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s CR+1\xe2\u20ac\x9d or whatever. Similarly, the players could argue for a reduced CR. That might lead to boring play though.

The DM should have at hand three or four pre-prepared dungeons (or other string of encounters) which comprise three encounters of a CR equal to or near the level of the party. The DM should feel free to mix up this formula a little, with several weak encounters, or one encounter of a slightly higher CR than the party. Bear in mind that \xe2\u20ac\u0153appropriate CR\xe2\u20ac\x9d for this hack means about four times as many opponents as usual. When the characters must \xe2\u20ac\u0153face the dungeon\xe2\u20ac\x9d, the DM should pull out one of these prepared dungeons (of appropriate CR), and give a little into to it, like \xe2\u20ac\u0153it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s a dungeon full of orcs\xe2\u20ac\x9d or \xe2\u20ac\u0153it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s fighting Gnolls in a forest\xe2\u20ac\x9d. Then all the players should work out how this dungeon can be used to resolve the current conflict. All players are free to improvise facts about the world to help this process. \xe2\u20ac\u0153Maybe the village hates the Elves because they stole a holy symbol, but it\xe2\u20ac\u2122s really at the bottom of a dungeon full of Orcs!\xe2\u20ac\x9d \xe2\u20ac\u0153Maybe the Orc ambassador is holed up in a dungeon near here, and if we can kill them all they won\xe2\u20ac\u2122t have a chance to talk to the village!\xe2\u20ac\x9d

Single Encounters:
I think there should be a place or single encounters in this scheme. Perhaps if a conflict feels like it doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t deserve a full dungeon, if the character wants something pretty minor, or most of all, if an NPC is provoking a conflict, perhaps a single encounter is called for. This could cover things that the DM wants to have happen in play, but doesn\xe2\u20ac\u2122t want to cause by fiat. For example \xe2\u20ac\u0153all your stuff gets stolen\xe2\u20ac\x9d, \xe2\u20ac\u0153the village is burned to the ground\xe2\u20ac\x9d or whatever. I\xe2\u20ac\u2122m not sure if that\xe2\u20ac\u2122s a good idea though, or how often these should happen.

    \n Good call, Mike.

    I guess my main concern was more that for a DM, it might be easier to have a guideline for what sort of challenge to provide for what level of conflict. In practice though, I think you're right that it's not needed, and may actually cause more problems than it solves. I wanted to avoid situations where the DM had to say no to the players without some concrete rule to point to, but I think that the way you suggest, with the GM pitching dungeons and the players bidding on them, is much cooler. Do you think there needs to be a formal process for that? It also kind of changes the focus from "Say Yes or Face the Dungeon" to "Find a Dungeon then come up with a reason". I'm not sure which would be more satisfying in play. What's your feeling?

    John, "40 Goblin" encounters are a hassle, you're right. Actually, the "x4" is a mistake. It should be "x(the number of members in the party)", which might help or hinder the issue. I think that by the time you're fighting forty goblins, which is (off the top of my head) about a CR 4, there are a lot of other tougher things you can throw at the party. The hack removes the need to have opponents that fit the situation at all, so that if you find goblin encounters (or any other horde of low-level foes) there's no need to have them. Also, D&D provides a lot of options for levelling up NPCs to make them tougher. The other way to increase the difficulty without increasing the CR of the monsters (which gets pretty unfair) is to increase the number of encounters without rest. I was reluctant to do this because I didn't want a single dungeon to take up too much time, so that the conflict dominated play when some of the players weren't interested in it.

    How do people feel about the "Single Encounter" issue? I kind of think it's a good idea to break up the flow of play, and also one dungeon and one single encounter makes for a good evening of play, with regards to time. So what would you do with these?

    \nIn terms of the process, I think it's still goal first, dungeon selection second. And I think it needs to be formalized, yes, simply because I think everything is better when formal (so take that with a grain of salt). But as an example:
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Hmmm. My character needs to get into the Duke's good graces. I'm calling for a dungeon [there's your formality]. How about he is near the caves of chaos from Keep on the Borderlands, and needs those cleared out?"
    \n
    \nDM: "Are you kidding me? Maybe if he was some lightweight local official, but the Duke? He's important. How about he needs you to get an idol from the Shrine of the Kua-Toa?"
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Hmm. What's that one like?"
    \n
    \nDM: "It's this huge temple complex crawling with Kua-toans."
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Hmmm. If it's huge and crawling, that sounds way too tough. How about we know where the idol is in the temple?"
    \n
    \nDM: "OK, it's on the altar of Blibdoolpoolp in the central hall."
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Guarded by their best, I suppose?"
    \n
    \nDM: "Maybe... I'm not telling, that's for you to discover as you go in, if you accept the mission."
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Fine, let's lock in [formality the second part] the Shrine with the idol on the altar."
    \n
    \nDM: [Third part of the formalities] "Dungeon locked. What about negative stakes?"
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Hmm. How about if we just can't get the idol and are forced to run?"
    \n
    \nDM: "Nope, I think you should get caught, if you fail, and then get traded on down to these folks called the Drow."
    \n
    \nPlayer: "How about we get caught, but get a chance to escape before being traded down?"
    \n
    \nDM: [Formality]OK, let's lock those stakes.
    \n
    \nPlayer: [Completing formalities] Yep, locked.
    \n
    \nDM: OK you can do the standard prep, and then we're off to the Shrine!
    \n
    \n
    \nNote that I still think that it's cool as part of this process to ensure that there's an explanation of how success in the dungeon explains the gaining of the stakes.
    \n
    \nI still love the single encounter option as a bid:
    \nPlayer: "Hmm, I want my character to get a wand of magic missiles. I'm calling for an encounter. We meet five ogres on the road, and they have the wand on them, not knowing how to use it?"
    \n
    \nDM: "How about one of them is an ogre-mage, and DOES know how to use it?"
    \n
    \nPlayer: "One less ogre, and you have yourself a deal."
    \n
    \nDM: "Fine, lock?
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Lock."
    \n
    \nDM: "If you lose, you're just wounded and flee?"
    \n
    \nPlayer: "Fine, lock it."
    \n
    \nDM: "OK, that's a lock. You find yourself in a wooded area, suddenly beset by several ogres, one dressed in garrish and strange attire...."

    \n Joel, you raise a good point. What sparked this idea was actually a thread at the forge where a guy was wondering what to do about all the wasted prep he was doing, while not having anything prepared for what the PCs actually ended up doing. It got me thinking about how to make a D&D game that guaranteed no wasted prep. The idea has moved away from that a little, but that's what's at its core.

    See, I like prepping dungeons. I like rummaging through charts and rolling up treasure and thinking up devious tactical twists to make a boring fight into an interesting challenge. I imagine I'm not alone. What I hate though is never getting to use all that work. I hate it when I have to bin a dungeon because it's not where the PCs wanted to go, and I hate even worse the temptation to shuffle things around so that they end up going ther anyway. So I tried to have it both ways.

    I think the way it works now there's still going to be some wasted prep, but I'm working on ways to do away with that. What you suggest, tools to make up dungeons on the fly, would certainly work. But it's not going to get me my dungeon making fix, and I think sometimes it can lead to kinda stale dungeons. Certainly I think it could be part of the toolkit of a DM, so that you can be extra prepared, but I always want to be able to spend a half-hour drawing up a really nasty dungeon for the characters to face off against.

    What I'm coming to is the idea that there should be two kinds of encounter. Small scale, single encounters, which can be made up on the fly, or prepped really easily, and then great big set-piece dungeons. The small scale encounters can be used to resolve conflicts as they come up. A PC wants to find a magic item, wants to attract romantic attention, wants to befriend a local mayor, or whatever. The big dungeons can be used more formally. The players know that they're going to be facing one big dungeon, so they should come up with a major task that they want to achieve from it. They're welcome to come up with multiple tasks, but that's going to reduce the amount they can achieve with the dungeon overall. In both cases, it works just like bidding.

    GM "Today we have the Caves of Calamity, a level six dungeon, with lots of encounters"
    Player One: "Ok cool! We've been bothered by those orcs for too long. Let's stop them once and for all!"
    Player Two: "That's cool, but I really want to recover my father's sword."
    GM: "Well, the dungeon's not that tough, I don't think you can do both."
    Player One: "Dude, the orcs are far more pressing than some stupid sword. We've got to focus on that"
    Player Two: "Waaaaahh"
    GM: "Hey, how about you stop the orcs, and you get a piece of your father's sword. It was so powerful that the bad guys broke it up and scattered it."
    Player Two: "*sniff* I can live with that"
    GM: "Ok, but since you're achieving quite a lot with the dungeon, I want to make the stakes pretty high. How about the orcs march on Freeport, and some asshole Paladin gets his hands on the piece of your father's sword"
    Player One: "I can live with that"
    Player Two: "Ok"
    All: "Let's go!"
    Player Three: "Wait, what?"

    \n Actual Play Update!

    We played Basic D&D (1981 Moldvay) the other day, and the DM, Tony, used a variant of this technique. It worked great!

    We come upon a barred door. Classic D&D thing, and in classic style, we start coming up with crazy ass ways to open it. We especially want to open it sneakily and surprise anyone on the other side.

    Tony has the hobgoblins on the other side call out, "Fargle? Is that you, you worthless lizard?" And we're like, "Uh... yessss, masters! It's Fargle!"

    And they're like, "Well, if you don't have that map from the spider cavern, you might as well go away. We're not letting you in without it."

    So then we have a choice: force the door and fight the hobgoblins straight up, or go find the map and then get in with sneakiness and surprise. Basically, since there was no mechanic for "trick the guards" Tony let us face the dungeon instead. "Explore the spider cavern successfully" == "Trick the hobgoblin guards." This worked wonderfully, and also made the dungeon culture come alive a bit, too.

    \n Yeah, I was totally inspired by this thread. I posted an AP for the game, but I totally left this part out. The Red Box works so awesome as long as the players stick to the limited set of options. Once they break out of them, the game starts to break down. So as a patch, I decided that the players could do non-standard options only by fulfilling a short dungeon quest of some sort.

    I'm also totally in favor of low-to-no-prep dungeoning. Like Colin, I love the prep but I loath the waste. I'm trying for a method that cuts out the parts of prep I don't like, but still lets me have fun getting ready. Moldvay works beautifuly for this. There's a simple table to roll to discover what combination of monster/treasure/trap the room contains. One of the options you can roll is "special". What the hell does that mean? It means you make it up. So I came in with a handful of "specail" rooms in reserve and pulled them out when I needed them.

    Moldvay also fudges beautifully on when to use the table and when not. He essentially tells you to map out the main monster and boss of the dungeon and fill in the gaps with the table. So off to one side I've got a few monster groups. They're not even statted, just a sentence each like "an outpost of the Gnoll kingdom under a ruthless leader" or "kobolds are bribing the owlbear with giant fungus titbits, but now they're running out". Each of those is good for 3-4 linked rooms. You can end up with a dungeon that's quick and organic, but still has things to prepped for the players to discover. That's my goal, anyway.
