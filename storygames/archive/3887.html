\n This is a little collaborative project that grew out of the Situation Book Manifesto thread, inspired by The Story Game Name Project.

The idea is to create situations and seeds that Story Gamers can pick up and adapt for their games. Send your submissions to me (tony DOT dowler AT gmail DOT com). I'll collate and edit them. We'll make the files publicly available somewhere and, if we get enough, we'll print a book and give the proceeds to charity.

This is very much in the spirit of Clinton's Fantasy Oracle, The Big List of RPG Plots, Abulafia and others. If you emulate these, you won't go wrong. There are a bunch of situation lists and generators in the manifesto thread, so feel free to check them out too.

Here are my proposed guidelines.

I envision dividing the book up into sections, roughly as follows:\n
    \n
  • Generic: stuff that's widely applicable across genres and settings
    • \n
  • Fantasy
    • \n
  • Science Fiction
    • \n
  • Contemporary
    • \n
  • Historical and Alternate-Historical
    • \n
  • Cornucopia: stuff that's cross-genre, weird, or otherwise difficult to classify
\nIn each section it would be nice to see:\n
    \n
  • Lists of setting nuggets that are pregnant with conflict and dripping with atmosphere
    • \n
  • Characters with strong interests that are bound to push conflicts and consequences
    • \n
  • Setting elements that can create threats, adversity, provoke strong reactions, or disrupt the status quo
    • \n
  • People, factions, or institutions with actionable goals that can't be ignored
\nYou may either send individual submissions that fit the genre sections, or submit themed lists of your own.

If you submit a list, please make it some randomizable multiple (i.e. 10 or 20 are good, 13 is not).

Rights will be CC attribution, non commercial, sharealike, just like the Story Games Names Project.

Don't submit copyrighted stuff you don't own.

Update: Some Examples
JBR suggested some examples. Here are a few good bits from The Fantasy Oracle, for a start:\n
    \n
  • The flight of a prince and his forbidden lover into hiding.
    • \n
  • A knowledge-mad sorceress, delving into ancient secrets.
    • \n
  • A monastery and its associated shrines, each to its own god.
    • \n
  • A wandering intelligence, intent on driving mortals mad.
    • \n
  • Instruments of torture, haunted by their long-dead victims.

    \n I'm about to disappear for a week, but first I want to post here and let everyone know (especially you Gen Con returners), that this project is very much under way. I've got a few submissions, and I should have some first draft pdfs to show off when I get back.

    In the meantime, I'd like to make a plea for oracles.

    An oracle is a collection of highly flavorful situation elements pregnant with conflict. Oracle entries are neither too vague, nor too definitive. Here are some examples from. The AG&G Oracle:

    An innkeeper who murders and robs his wealthy guests
    A copper basin, to which are enslaved spirits of the air
    A wizard jealously guarding her magical territory
    A wizard jealously guarding her magical territory
    A vast precipice plunging steeply into the valley below
    A scholar and antiquarian, unmindful of danger
    An outlying watchtower on a wooded hill
    The campsite of a travelling army, not long deserted
    A golden armlet, still on the skeletal arm of its owner
    An apothecary, squint-eyed, with many uncanny potions

    An oracle for the situation book should probably have 10-20 situations. Flavor your situations to a particular genre, setting, or game. I'd love to see oracles made by designers for their own games, or their favorite games.

    \n I know you gentlemen already have done some development of your situation book idea. But at GenCon I ran across and interesting permutation of the random situation generator in a Dust Devils game that I wanted to share.

    From this thread on the forge:
    http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forum/index.php?topic=24632.new#new\n

    Dust Devils: Western - This game continues to be my favorite convention game ever. I used the same town of Galilee as I used last year and have used in a number of other one shot situations, but shifted the core conflict generation bit around. Normally, to ensure that there is some initial conflict, I start one character down a well, amnesiac, next to the body of their dead partner. This time, I decided there would be a wedding between two of the NPC's (Kat Miller [a pure chance coincidence, btw], and Mayor O'Gallagan) in the game. I then provided pregen character sheets, each of which had the following set of four things on the bottom.

    Bride's true love or bride's worst nightmare
    In love with the bride or spurned by the bride
    The groom needs you or in the grooms way
    You need the groom or the groom is in your way

    Each player then picked one of the eight options above, and then another of the eight, with no overlap on the first choice, and no overlap on the very first item. Then, I started off the game by saying "OK, the wedding is going to take place in four hours, what do you do" and it all went from there. Everyone seemed to really groove on this, with people picking sides like crazy.
    \nI was a player in this game and this idea worked like a charm. Essentially, it took a structured situation (Location, NPCs, Event) and then allowed the PCs to firmly position themselves in ways that were most likely to lead to interesting conflicts with the NPCs and each other.

    I'm not sure if this idea would fit within the Story Games Situation Book or not (it doesn't fit the structure of the input that Tony requests). But this assignment of roles within an existing situation, whether chosen or randomly rolled, might be a powerful complimentary technique. Just a thought.
