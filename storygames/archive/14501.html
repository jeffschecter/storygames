    \n I've actually put a lot of thought into this avenue. The point I arrived at is: for me, this isn't a good match. I seriously love AW and superheros, but I don't think they match up well. Don't let my thoughts stop you, though.

    AW moves need clear, fictional triggers and effects, right? I don't think that covers supers well at all. In comics (not superhero RPGs) the same basic action can have pretty different effect, and may never occur again. One time, Superman used his solar-power storing cells to drain the heart of the anti-sun. One time, Kitty Pride phased a giant bullet through Earth.

    I don't think Superman needs a "When you absorb solar power" move, even though the way he actually beat Maggedon was by absorbing solar power. Kitty Pride's phasing might have a "when you phase something else" move but also a "when you phase yourself" and "when you phase through electronics." Fitting powers to moves, in my attempts, you end up with endless lists (so that phasing through this can be different from that) or catch-all boring moves.

    I'm not even sure comics are that fictionally consistent: AW is all about honestly portraying the world, but what flies in superhero comics... I don't know how I can honestly portray that world, because it's not very solidly defined.

    On top of that, Fronts aren't a very good fit. Fronts tend to be broad: there's this natural disaster, and these people effected by it, and this guy wants to take over your hardhold cause he thinks its the end of the world, and you're stuck in the middle of it all. What do you do? Comics tend to be much more focused. Dr. Doom is doing this thing, right now, what do you do?

    That said, I think there is a lot to learn from AW that can be applied to supers games. Variations on countdowns and MC moves are fantastic tools for Superhero gaming. I just think that shoehorning powers into moves is actually not ta very good option.

    \n I just want to echo Sage. I don't see a good reason why this would be a fit.
    And I've certainly put efforts into making odd fits with Apocalypse World.

    In Supers, the characters endure pain because of their relationships and the things they hold dear.
    In Apocalypse World, the characters endure pain because of their actions and their choices.

    Other systems that can do Supers better:
    In A Wicked Age (But maybe without using Oracles at the start?)
    Primetime Adventures
    Any Supers Game (Capes, With Great Power, Spirit of the Century)

    In A Wicked Age is great because it demands that all problems be resolved physically, even the emotional ones, which is true of supers. It allows for characters with Super-Powers to take Particular Strengths to model them. The "take damage or accept this hard bargain" effectively mirrors the kind of dilemmas that supers are asked to solve during fights.

    I just wouldn't use Oracles to set things up. With Supers, you can just have one person make a villain, one person make a hero, and then everyone else choose to make a Significant Other, a Side-Kick, another Hero, or another Villain.

    I wouldn't use AW to run supers, but I'd be very curious to hear how it went from people who try it. I have thought about an AW setting 50 years after my World of the 400 Champions campaign, but not seriously.

    \n When you channel the ghost field to manifest your power, roll+ghost. On a 10+, your power manifests fully and under your control. On a 7-9, the GM chooses one:
    - It's wild: your power manifests with fearsome effect, lashing out beyond your control.
    - It's weak: your power manifests with only little effect.
    - It's weird: your power manifests in a new and strange way with effects you've never seen before.
    - It's worse: your power manifests normally, but you suffer. Take 1-harm ap or s-harm (crippling pain), your choice.
