\n I got a raised eyebrow when I wrote about what games that I didn't like in 2012, and I feel I need to get one thing that bugs me out in the air. I'm not saying that I'm playing the game right, so please point out if there's anything wrong but don't try to defend the game. I played Fiasco three times now and I didn't like any of the sessions, even if one was praised by the others, one had two cool stories and one ... well, no one thought it was a good session.

Fiasco is a GM less game where it will go to hell with most of the characters. You got a lot of playsets to choose from, even if they are mostly sceneries that forms the mind of the participants. The really cool part is that you play out most of the scenes through dialogue and dialogue alone. But when I play I get a creative block from the game. Why?

One thing in roleplaying games that I really like is when I can contribute with ideas and help create a mood or maintain it. Once upon a time I really liked getting in the character and play the shit out of different roles. Not that much any more. It takes to much effort for what I feel that I get from the game, and I feel that I played most of the parts that I can play. Also, Fiasco forces you in how to play your role by making the others choose if it's going to be a good or a bad end in the scene in combination with having the scenes played out as dialogues. I have nothing against being restricted in how I should play my character - I love how I have to choose one of the six moods in Psychodrame, but that will always leave me a choice. Fiasco don't. So why don't I just choose how the scene should end? Because of what I wrote in the beginning of this paragraph: I also like to contribute to the scene. I noticed if another participant lets the others create a scene, I'm the one in 70-80% of the times that suggest that new scene. That's when I can shine as a player in my area of expertise. But that's pretty much the only time I can shine. So I got two choice, either being forced in how I should play out my character so the scene ends good/bad or I can sit quietly and let the other players have a fun time.

And how about when there's a scene that my character isn't in it. The only thing I can do is to sit and listen. Sure, that's fun in itself, but why am I then playing?

Fiasco forces you in another way. The end game. The end game that you roll doesn't have anything to do with what happened during the game. Someone who got just white dice can still end up in prison. I did. Things worked out for me and I wasn't even in the last scene - that ended in a shootout. Still, I ended up in prison for some reason. Like I said before: I have nothing against restrictions but I want to choose which one to use. Why can't the game just count the number of white or black dice and then give me three suggestions to choose from?

Finally the relationships. I have little choosing in how my character will end up with any kind of relations, which could be good, but in this game it's not. Relations can be sorted into three categories: active agendas, reactive relations and passive relations (traits, stuff, locations). Some of the relations in Fiasco are agendas, but a lot of the relations are passive. They don't do anything - they just are there. According to me, all personal characters should have an agenda - something to strive for, or at least have something to react to. My character last time had a vague goal to get high (agenda) that was ignored by my partner, a place (passive), a work relation (passive) and a reactive relation (desperate manager and a singer). I was the manager who looked out for my next star, so anything that happened to her was in my interest. But out of the four relations, there were only one to use: the reactive relations, which put my outside of everything that happened. This has happened two times out of three, where I more enjoy listen to the other peoples stories than my own. Sure, that's fun in itself, but why am I then playing?

Even if I don't like Fiasco, I still think it's a good game. In the next post I will address why.

I don't appear to share Fiasco's design ideals (I'm pretty much burned out by the whole collaborative storytelling premise), but it's easy to play and seems to keep its promises.

    \n Now to the fun part. I haven't read the game, so I can't say anything about how it's written.

    The idea of playsets is sheer brilliance. It gives an easy way to build out the game, much like some computer games companies that design the games so it can be modded by their fans. The playsets also provides tropes that will form how the relations should be interpreted.

    Talking about relations. A relationship map can be hard to grasp, but by extending the creation of relationships to more than just yourself, you get a collaborate creation of the map. Everybody will learn each others relations in an easy way. Yes, I did say that this was one of the things that made me dislike the game, but I would've liked it if it was handled in a different way.

    Talking about praising what I dislike, here's another thing: the outcome of the scene. First of all, the choice of letting you frame the scene or handling the outcome of the scene is a really sleek and intelligent solution of one of the troubles in collaborate storytelling games. Sometimes you have an idea of how your story should go, but other times you have no idea. This mechanic has embraced that. Also, letting the non-participants of a scene to decide the outcome of a scene is a way of creating active listening, which is always good.

    Another thing I like about the outcome if the scenes is that it's a refinement of a structure that almost all storygames have: frame a scene, play up to a waypoint, roll a die, frame a new scene. This is a really shallow way of handling things, where you always strive so each scene end up with that Really Important Die Roll. I don't understand why people rolls die if the outcome is one of two possible (fail or succeed). It also gets really repetitive, and some storygames like Mouse Guard or Lady Blackbird, tries to break this by having refreshment scenes or similar. Fiasco is more true to this by acknowledging that there are only but two outcomes of a scene (This is why I started to play D&D4: the game was more honest about what the game was about than the previous versions).

    The structure of the game is easy to understand, it breaks off very nicely and it's easy to add stuff like playsets too. If you played with one playset, you know how to play the game, instead of having to learn a bunch of new stuff. Brilliant design.

    Related to the structure, but deserves a paragraph of it's own, is the time it take to play a session. You get a full session with no preparation and everybody to participate in all phases within two hours. This means that you can play it with non-gamers during an evening, or play two different playsets with your gaming group after each other. Exemplary!

    Fiasco creates cool stories. But if I imagine myself being one of the players around that table, I wouldn't enjoy acting out any of the parts. Fiasco doesn't suit my style of gaming ... but I can still acknowledge it as a good game.

    \n This isn't even a hack so much as a more specific structure to framing scenes that I think is assumed but not outright stated (I haven't read the rulebook actually, I've just played this game a lot).     The resolution mechanics of handing out black dice and white dice to determine the outcome of a scene only make sense if there's a clear good outcome and bad outcome from the start. While the former can end up with the opening scene from Pulp Fiction by chance, the latter framing will explicitly go for that scene.

    A character's agenda does not have to be a Need in their relationships. In the example I give above there's no explicit Need being focused on, only a Location, but there is a clear agenda to the character's actions.
