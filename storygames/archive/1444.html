Hello All! (newbie here, but been in the games market for quite some time)
This is my first question that I felt had some merit here in the community.

I am develpoing, after some years of intial development followed by several major and heartbreaking setbacks, a swords & sorcery type RPG based upon character concepts and simple mechanics that mirror a realistic feel and logic as much as is possible (yes, I know ths has been done a thousand times over, but I actually believe that I can contribute something to the trite genre that would actually allow the players to have fun!).

As an aside I am a musician, an author, and a computer programmer and have had varying levels of professional success at all of them...at least to the point where some people buy my stuff and my mommy still loves me. I have also been involved in the RPG industry as a player, critic, and sometimes contributor since the late 1970's (Yes, I know how old that makes me).

So here's the question: (with lots of fluffy text thrown in because I am a long-winded self-absorbed narcissist)

After more than 2 years of develpoment (with MAJOR setbacks) and a detailed outline that has been revised and revised upon the revisions (picture a game manual without the flowing prose that merely describes how things are done) I am now in the stage of writing the detailed and florid version of the game I am working on (the actual game text). Lots and lots of play-testing (in the ALPHA stage) has led up to a group of rules and a singular system that seems to work the best for my goals. It ihas a relistic feel, follows inhgerent logic, and is esy to both grasp and play.

The three groups of Sub-Beta testers is chomping at the bit (this being two independent groups and one that I shall GM. I strongly feel that people that are not around me should playtest so I can get totally objective opinions and suggestions) and are awaiting the actual first draft of the real game manual.

I somehow settled on the idea that each section/ chapter should be forewarded by a small piece of fiction (about 1 or so pages of text, or less) that depicts the main points of the chapter. As the mechanics are explained, the prose given at the beginning of the chapter is sighted to illustrate the key points and ideas. Likewise the examples draw upon the previous prose so that the reader (whom already has a mental image of how things were going on and working out) can draw a logical line between the game mechanics, game play, and how things would be "in-game". Since I am a fiction author of some, at least miniscule, success, this at least entertains the reader while they learn the game. It also allows the reader to generate their own assumptions and/or visions of what is going on and how it realtes to the game in their own fashion and (hopefully) allows them to associate the game mechanics in terms of the story given earlier.

So my question(s) (yes!!! finally he gets to the points!) is this:

Has anyone else attempted or done this before? If so, have you had any success at it? If you haven't; as a game developer, do you think this is a good idea? Do you feel that by reading a simple story that does not involve game systems, rules, or mechanics first and then reading how the mechanics of the game apply to what you just read is good idea?


Please keep in mind that the prose is very short, just a few paragraphs. Likewise it is done as if you were were reading a novel, not an example. Also, the explanations of the rules follow the events in the text so you can easily think about what you've just read and how the game mechanics might apply.

I appreciate all comments and critiques, even if it's just to tell me that I am an idiotic windbag (please note that ALL AND ANY posts that just say "YOU ARE AN IDIOTIC WINDBAG" wil be chuckled at, but disregarded)

    I can't think of any game that's tried quite that approach before. White Wolf got into the habit of putting a vignette before each chapter, though a lot of people complained about the quality of these (and in Exalted 2e they have 2-page comics instead), and they tended to be tangential to the contents of the chapter.

    The closest I've gotten in my own work is in a game I'm working on currently called Tokyo Heroes. Since I'm trying to convey a genre that not many people know (sentai) within the book, I got sick writing noodly text and saying, "Sometimes sentai series have this. Often they have that," and decided to write a few of them in the form of a vignette.

    So, I think it definitely could work. Like everything else, it's all in the execution.

    As an idea, I'd have to see it done. Most of the background/setting material for White Wolf's Demon was in fiction form, and I almost threw the book across the room when I found that out.

    I'm not against the idea of using fiction, per se, but I think that there needs to be clear expositional passages to back it up. As I said at the time: "You can't FORCE me to read your crappy game fiction!"

    So as long as it's not the only means of understanding the rules, go for it.

    A few examples off the top of my head:

    In the conflict section (I think) of DitV, Vincent describes a cool, tense scene -- and then says something like "and now let's see how that looks with the dice", and describes the conflict again with the mechanics that are being used.

    White Wolf's 'actual play' sections (at least for some of the later games) were in the form of comic book layouts -- on the left side of the page you had the unadorned story panels, with all the in-SIS action. On the right side the panels were overlayed with the mechanics being used as well as the dialogue between players about what is going on.

    --

    I think it's an excellent approach in general. It's particularly great when the scene you describe has a lot of bite, and seems to include things that players might not expect to see dealt with by the mechanics (the Dogs example is good for this, of course.) If the piece of fiction is like "whoa, awesome!" and then you make a convincing case for it emerging from typical gameplay and mechanics, players will be hooked.

    Actually it's something I did in Dead of Night. Each section was prefaced by a couple of pages of fiction which formed a short story called Wolf Moon, and which illustrated the rules I was about to detail. I also put a couple of paragraphs of examples, describing the fiction in games terms.
