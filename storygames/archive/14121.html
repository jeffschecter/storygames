\n So I'm starting up a Dresden Files RPG campaign, and the social skill rules remind me of an issue that I have with social skill use in some games. I played Burning Wheel for a while as a social skill specialist. (I had described her as a "lean, mean Duel of Wits machine".) In that game, social skill use would typically be invoked by (1) an NPC would decide to do something that we didn't like; (2) I would deploy my social skill to make them agree; (3) if I won, they would go along.

The problem I had was that this sort of social skill use came across as obnoxious aggression. Besides how it felt at the time, it also influenced how characters acted later. Other players and even the GM would tend to role-play characters acting resentful after they were forced to agree, rather than being friendlier towards my character.

So I've been pondering about how to handle social skill use along different lines than treating it as a conflict to be won, and the opponent defeated. I don't have a complete system yet, just some scattered thoughts about an alternate system:
1) In the big picture, social skills should be used more for winning and keeping allies than for defeating enemies.
2) A group with socially skilled characters should be more effective as a whole than one without. Social skills aren't just about making other people obey you - they can help you and others work together.
3) Social skill rolls should be required for social interaction - not just an option. Characters with low social skill should be the cause of conflict, not just bystanders or weak combatants if there is conflict.
4) Social interaction isn't just about getting your way - it can also be about communication. Two people might be in conflict because of lack of social skill, because they don't realize their common ground.

As for how to implement these... In FATE, I think that working together could be handled as positive maneuvers. Emphasize using Rapport or other skills for teamwork maneuvers that help others. (Roll on the social skill rather than a physical conflict skill.) I'm not sure how to handle conflict over miscommunication, but there should be required social skill rolls for social interaction - and that could be one outcome.

    \n I think this shows one of the problems with our obsession with modeling all interactions as conflict. Conversation very rarely follows that model in real life, after all. Effective persuasion doesn't involve "beating" your opponent, it means reaching a point of consensus.

    With a FATE game like the Dresden Files, you actually have a pretty interesting way of modeling that, if you can think of zones not just as physical spaces, but potentially as emotional or ideological spaces. Mark out a map of the possible reactions\u2014maybe a spectrum from "Hostile" to "Friendly," maybe something more complex. Rather than marking off stress boxes, use social rolls to move one of the other characters to the next zone; or, roll some more internally-focused skill (Empathy, Discipline, Conviction, etc.) to see if you can find it in yourself to compromise a bit or actually listen to the other party, and move your own position closer. The "conflict" ends when you all inhabit the same zone.

    When phrased as a conflict, I can sympathize with the other players who would tend to roleplay that result as having "beaten" the NPC, and anyone who feels beaten would naturally tend towards resentment. With this, I think the zone would provide a good roleplaying prompt. It doesn't look like a defeat, so much as a meeting, and the zone you met in tells you a lot about the attitudes everyone ended up with.

    \nThis is ultimately what ALL social conflict is about. I want one thing and you want another. In order for me to change your stance I have to present you with information that you don't have, that makes my stance more acceptable. That's what persuasion is, figuring out what information you don't have and providing it to you. In the end, theres no reason to maintain a grudge because you really do feel differently than you did before considering the information at hand.     If the system allows for compromise and the results indicate it, obviously after all the information is presented no one is fully convinced but both parties have a better understanding of the others stance. And we've identified some common ground that benefits us both. And you represent that in the fiction.

    \n There's a lot of interesting things to think about here.

    One small bit: In real life, if you persuade someone to do you a favor, then in the future it generally becomes even easier to persuade them to help you more. There's a cognitive dissonance thing at work, where internally they have to justify the fact that they went out on a limb for you by convincing themselves that it was for a good reason. Otherwise, they have to admit that they were duped. This can build up until they're presented with incontrovertible evidence of the fact that they were fooled / coerced / manipulated, and even then they might reject the evidence.

    It's how con games work, and also cults. Get someone to do you a little favor, and it becomes easier to get them to do bigger favors for you later. It seems to me this is sort of the opposite of how many games model the interaction. Maybe you can't fast talk the security guard into letting you into the building. But you can, maybe, bum a cigarette off of them.     \n This is how I avoid these issues in Mouse Guard (when they are actually problems, which depends on the players)\u2026

    \n It seems to me that a lot of these issues come down to how Conflict is modelled in BW (and most other games), and how that turns out not to be very easy to apply to a lot of social maneuvering or interaction. In most circumstances where social manipulation is involved, if you ever get a point where the other guy says 'no' and you have to say 'yes', you've already failed. There certainly is a kind of socially adept person who is all about getting to that point and then still badgering/arguing his way to victory, but that will only really fly in certain circles that accept a level of argumentativeness as standard social procedure (eg. politics, academia, internet forums.)

    But there is a whole subsection of social ability that really has to do with never appearing to be in conflict with anyone, even as you get what you want; and with, in fact, arranging things socially so that everyone involved feels like they are getting what they want. A very charismatic person tends to win people over as part of their natural demeanor, not by reaching a point of stark opposition and then confronting the people with the inferiority of their position.

    So really the problem seems to be about at what point in the fiction you engage the mechanics; the players need to perceive a kind of soft-edged conflict of interests prior to it becoming a conflict that is so obvious that even the characters involved realise it is a conflict. And the system needs to deliver on ways for a proactive, socially adept character to stake out their position long before everyone else involves understands that that is what is happening. Improving someone's general impression of you to the point that they are reluctant to engage in social confrontation, for example.

    For me, the Duel of Wits has always worked in the fiction by being strictly limited to public-facing arguments; the assumption of an audience (and the inferred admission, by both parties, that this audience holds sufficient power to sway their actions) makes the interaction easier to understand. It also allows for characters to be 'convinced' to take an action that they are not actually personally convinced is the best one; they take it because they perceive the audience to be so far in favor of it that not taking it would damage their reputation, which is a worse outcome than taking the 'wrong' action.

    The fact that lots of people are perceiving this as a "it's roleplaying" problem suggests to me that Burning Wheel just doesn't model the part of social interaction that jhkim was talking about, not that it actually models it fine if you just interpolate a whole layer of roleplaying on top of the mechanics. Personally I'm a lot more interested in thinking of ways to model this than going over the many ways in which the Duel of Wits does not. I like the idea put forward of how to do this in FATE*, where you could use maneuvers to basically put conditions on people like 'I like PC X!' or 'PC X has my best interests at heart' -- these conditions could then serve as guidelines to players in terms of setting a mechanical threshhold for social opposition. As in, if you have +4 worth of Aspects of the basically-I-like-this-guy sort, then you can think 'does this social conflict matter enough that I am willing to pony up 2 FATE straight off the bat, just to get it started?' These would be guidelines for the players as to their character's mostly-subconscious social behaviours.

    Similarly, Aspects such as those outlined above could also do double duty for modelling how a socially successful leader can help groups work together, since you could also tap/activate/whatever the Aspect in circumstances where you were acting to help That Guy You Really Like, or whatever.

    Again though, this is going to rely on players cultivating a new skill or a new appreciation of when to 'go to dice' -- because you do it before a conflict, or before what we traditionally recognize as 'conflict.' It's kind of like a 'conflict mitigation' stage; and I think a lot of us have spent a lot of time trying to avoid a very similar social behaviour at our tables, where someone who is more socially adept manipulates the story through 'good roleplaying' so as to avoid any conflicts that are bad for his character. The solution to that has generally been to push to conflict, and therefore to dice, sooner -- so that this maneuvering can happen only as part of the mechanical conflict, limiting any social advantage a player may be wielding over the table -- but it may turn out that by doing so we have also cut out a significant opportunity for the characters to exercise a similar sort of in-fiction social maneuvering which is actually completely appropriate.

      \nOnce everyone has had a turn, players can spend some sort of resource, or take a penalty, or maybe just without cost propose more stakes in turn.
      \n
      \nNow you've got a bunch of compatible stakes on the table.
      \nEveryone makes a social skill check.
      \nThe winner of the check rolls one d6 per stake on the table, and assigns one to each stake. High dice to stakes they want to decide the outcome of, low dice to stakes their less concerned about.
      \nFor every point of success on the skill check, the winner can re-roll or modify one die by one.
      \nMaybe there's another round where people have a chance to make more checks to tweak the general state of the stakes now that they see how things are going.
      \n
      \nAt the end, the winner narrates how the stakes with a 4+ are resolved (either successfully or not, at their whim.) The higher the die, the better the outcome (success of failure) for that character.
      \nThe opposition (there'd need to be some mechanic for figuring out who in a 3+ way conflict.) gets to narrate any Stakes with a 3 or less. The lower the die, the better the outcome (success of failure) for that character.
      \n
      \nThis would naturally allow for a kind of negotiation during the stakes setting stage, and compromises (skewed by social skill) during resolution.

      The reason why I want social mechanics is because my game revolves around players setting and going after goals, and whether they get the goals or not should not be down to GM fiat.

      Posted By: jhkimSo I've been pondering about how to handle social skill use along different lines than treating it as a conflict to be won, and the opponent defeated. I don't have a complete system yet, just some scattered thoughts about an alternate system:
      1) In the big picture, social skills should be used more for winning and keeping allies than for defeating enemies.
      2) A group with socially skilled characters should be more effective as a whole than one without. Social skills aren't just about making other people obey you - they can help you and others work together.
      3) Social skill rolls should be required for social interaction - not just an option. Characters with low social skill should be the cause of conflict, not just bystanders or weak combatants if there is conflict.
      4) Social interaction isn't just about getting your way - it can also be about communication. Two people might be in conflict because of lack of social skill, because they don't realize their common ground.
      Your analysis is very good!

      Here is what I do in my frpg Fabula ...

      The social skill is a broad tradition-skill, and does this;
      - it tells how to make birthdays, weddings, funerals, feasts, and are used to check how characters handle such occasions
      - it includes mending your own clothes, doing daily chores, working a farm, etc.
      - it is used to behave correctly, establish contacts, get friends and finding/winning sweethearts (typically done in festive social occasions)

      It is used as a pro-active skill, not a reactive one. So it is used to inform the players how they characters do in social occasions, and then we play out the happenings accordingly. The "conflict" is not with other characters/NPCs, but with the social hopes and ambitions of the character.

      Typical play example:
      - Player Geir is the lone ranger Nimrod. He is invited in his sisters wedding.
      - Geir says he will try to behave politely, which is fairly simple (skill of 7 + D20 must reach 10 or more to succeed). Him saying this establish Nimrod as a social outcast, who has done some bad things in the past (we don't know what).
      - Geir rolls a 5, with a total of 12; success!
      - We play out the scene, or a range of small scenes at the wedding, Nimrod being a nice brother all the way; not very eloquent, but conscientiously polite. It ends with his sister thanking him warmly for coming (and doing the effort), and his new brother in law being very friendly.


      Another typical play example:
      - Player Geir is the lone ranger Nimrod. He is invited to his sisters for the new year feast, and there's a lot of people coming. His sister says several of the women are single and on the look out ...
      - Geir says Nimrod will look for a girlfriend there, which is quite hard (skill of 7 + D20 must reach 30 or more to succeed). Him saying this is sending Nimrod in a new social direction, hopefully ...
      - Geir rolls a 5, with a total of 12; a far way from success!
      - We play out a range of small scenes at the feast, Nimrod being a clumsy man around the women, stepping on toes, getting irritated, drinking too much and starting to shout his frustration. His brother in law with friends cut the shouting short, and locks Nimrod in the smithy, to calm down. It ends at the breakfast table the next morning, with his sister refusing to talk with him and his brother in law shaking his head in sadness.

      One roll, and a lot of informed and enjoyable social interaction to be had from it. ;-)

      The point is, I believe, not to focus on conflict in social interaction, but to focus on how to inform the players how to interpret their characters in social situations, based on what their ambition is and how their skills serve up. And then to let the social interplay develop in liege with that. It leaves the social interplay flexible and enjoyable, and something we are free to play out, and that is important.

Three of them are tied to the classes in society; one social skill for farmers, one for burghers, and one for nobles.       Posted By: stefoidI havent given any thought to whether thats a comfort zone -- its just what Im striving for.
      Only teasing, Steve. The tease is, however, based on an observation that anglo-american game-smiths have a tendency to put more weight on mechanics than I like. I like to think of how to use both mechanics (resolution systems) and techniques(verbal interactions) in game-design, both in modern and classical design. By using mechanical and technical tools in different variations, as your game needs them, I believe we have a greater chance of developing (over time) better role-playing games. As of now, anglo-american designers are still too lopsided in favor of mechanics. But they are coming around ...

      But back on issue: what I'm trying to say here, is that if you want social skill that isn't obnoxious aggression, you have to look at how skills are used, and what the different use of them are most likely to produce. So you have to be careful with what kind of goals you set up, and how you deal with them; striving for goals often leads to in-game conflict-resolutions. Nothing wrong in having conflict-resolutions in games, per se, but using skills to win a conflict is most likely to produce winners, and loosers. That is not very healthy in a social context.

      What is really healthy, though, is to immerse yourself, with co-players, in social interaction that recreates golden moments, moments of insecurity and release (or shame), and other kinds of moments that makes you feel alive, as your character, in the game. There is great joy to be had in that, and it is not well served by mechanics alone. Formalizing the game in too strong mechanical terms makes friendly social interaction harder to achieve. You have to think about how to feed the verbal interaction, to make it able to recreate the nuances of real life social interaction; including the basically friendly nature of it (us striving to be liked, and to understand each other). Feeding this interaction is often better done with a kind of "freedom of expression". That is; trusting the players to produce the nuances, based on their own experiences, and helping them by opening up the playing field, so that their nuances may pop out, and flourish.

      When you succeed in doing it, it's like being granted a second personality with a life of its own, a totally captivating and thrilling experience. So go for it, Steve!

      I once made a game with absolutely no conflict in it (Muu), and it worked very well (hilarious, and meditative, at the same time). You should try that; design for no conflict!

      \nI would probably distinguish between goals and methods here - although even goals can often be in the service of higher-level goals.       I'd include deception as one of many methods to accomplish these.

      \n Reading through this thread, which is a great read but nonetheless highlights a big part of my issue with most RPG design. Systems most often limit Spinners (er.. players) to a set of verbs imagined by the designer. As we've already seen, with many different interpretation of what a social list should look like in this thread, not every set of verbs suits every Spinner,or every story.

      Not to mention the intertextual frames everyone applies to the list, making such lists read very differently across cultures.

      My solution with Bhaloidam (yeah,I know I've been very "Bhaloidam, Bhaloidam" in all my comments, I've been working on it for the past 3 years and it's a bit pent up and eager to be set free), was to focus on the underlying mechanism of the social interactions--the sort of relationships are being expressed by the verbs common to RPGs, literature, film, folklore, etc. I distilled this down into six Influences--Mastery, Persistence, Design, Poise, Sleight, and Charm--knowing that each Skein would interpret them a bit differently and be free to base any action on them that suited their storyworld and understanding.
