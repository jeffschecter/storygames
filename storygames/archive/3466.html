I'm back in the nineties again.

\n
\n
\n
\n\nMatthijs \n\n\n
\n
\n\nJune 2007 \n edited June 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n About ten years ago, or a little more, I was reviewing games regularly for Imagonem (a Norwegian 'zine). I would end up saying stuff like "as usual, the system is of the type 'roll 1d10, add your skill and stat, try to get equal to above the difficulty'."

These days, I would say "as usual, the system is of the type 'frame a scene, play until conflict, roll some dice based on traits, change value of traits, next scene until endgame'".

It seems, to me, the state of the art in indie games has become the mainstream in indie games. We've reached a certain plateau.

Next plateau: 2015. See you then!

    \n Good observation, Matthijs, but do you really see a plateau? There are good ideas; they get appropriated, tweaked, advanced, repurposed, iterated. Innovation just to be different is dumb and/or pretentious. Stealing something that works well and adjusting it to do what you need is smart. I see designs building off the backs of others constantly - a plateau would seem to indicate that honest innovation has stagnated, and that nobody is inspired by what's been produced by others. I'm not seeing that myself.
