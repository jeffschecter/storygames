\n The So . . . what are these games?" thread raised some issues that are particularly interesting to me. I'm fascinated by the interaction (or frequently lack of!) between the gamer culture and, well, the rest of the people in the world.

For example, Ben Lehman says he's seen little to no negative reaction when he says "it's like D&D" to non-gamers. Meanwhile, Brendan Adkins seems to say he sees it a lot. My experience match Brendans! Which doesn't make Ben wrong, of course. But, it does make Ben less able to confront any problem because he can't see it. And, I think there are lots of Bens here, likely.

Anyway, I have a slightly weird position on this broad topic. It leads to online pissing matches more often than it does to constructive conversations. Please, please help make this one of those constructive ones.

My position is this: I find the culture of gamers personally unsatisfying. I find the activity of playing role-playing games with individual people I know and meet very satisfying.

You can probably see that my position leads to confusion, even hurt, particularly among gamers. Which leads to the pissing matches I mentioned. That's not what I want to discuss, please.

One of the extensions of my position is that I start by assuming the reactions of non-gamers to gamer-dom and its activities are natural, understandable, and human. That is, the burden's on gamers and gamer-dom to remedy the problem, not on regular people are are behaving as they should. OF COURSE this does not mean regular people never fuck up and they're always right. Of course not. But, it's a charitable position to start understanding painful, negative things like: \n
  • You are all nerds and should be outcast socially
    • \n
  • You are fat / smelly / gross
    • \n
  • You are losers who will never get laid
    • \n
  • You are in a cult
    • \n
  • You are going to murder someone
    • \n
  • You're probably on drugs
    • \n
  • You can't tell reality from fantasy and will hurt yourself somehow
    • \n
  • You live in your parents' basement, and this is a shameful thing
    • \n
  • You can't relate to regular people / girls / boys / etc.
\nThese and others should sound pretty familar to many of us. They certainly do to me. I've experienced several items here first hand from my own family, let alone friends. One of the only serious conversations I had with my mom growing up was how she was worried I could not distinguish reality from fantasy. I was something like 16 years old, and she was being a frightened, foolish mom.

The list above is the kind of thing that most often get brought up in online discussions when we "analyze the hobby" or whatever. They are real things. People do think this stuff about gamers, for good or ill. (Obviously, mostly for ill.) But, most often, they get dismissed, often defensively so. And, that's pretty understandable given the list of nasty stuff. So, the conversations don't often get far; mainly they just reinforce the circle-the-wagons mentality of gamer-dom and that whole Own-Your-Geek-As-Cool thing that I just don't "get" but seems to work for many people.

But, here are some other things that I don't think get talked about as much. \n
  • What the hell IS this activity? I don't get it. What's all the fuss?
    • \n
  • I don't have time for this. It takes too much investment.
    • \n
  • I can't understand this complex stuff. It's so intimidating and makes me feel dumb!
    • \n
  • They'll never let someone "normal" like me play. Oh well.
    • \n
  • You are too sexist / racist / elistist. Who needs THAT?!?
    • \n
  • What if I do it wrong? They'll think I suck.
    • \n
  • Oh, you mean like World of Warcraft. My girlfriend dated this guy and they broke up because of it. He was addicted.
    • \n
  • Why don't they just go out and live life! This is too boring.
\nAlso all real stuff. Again, much of which I've experienced first-hand.

When I look at that list, I start to see non-gamers much less defensively. I see them as people with doubts, fears, envy, passion, curiousity, shame, you name it. I "get" these reactions. They're totally defensible! And, unfortunately, our gamer culture doesn't have especially good answers for most of this.

So, that's what I'd love to see a discussion about. Why do negative reactions surface? What, if anything, is the gamer-culture's reaction to it? Can or should it improve? How? Do you, as a gamer, sympathize with any negative reactions like this (I do, for one).

What else comes to mind about this stuff? I'd love to hear it.

Please, please keep the shit-flinging out of the zen, cool?

    \nWhilst I don't see all of the reactions in your secondary list, I do see a lot regarding complexity, time, intimidation and confusion with CRPGs. To be fair, many RPGs are complex and time-consuming. Many gaming groups can have their own micro-culture which is extremely intimidating to penetrate. And for many people, 'roleplaying games' mean Final Fantasy and WoW.
    \n
    \nI think that one of the issues might be that we, the gamers, have a tendency to hear something vaguely negative regarding the hobby and view it as one of the first batch of jibes you mentioned, as opposed to the second. As a hobby that has rarely been accepted within the mainstream, we have a sort of siege mentality that sometimes morphs into a sort of converting zeal.
    \n
    \nLook at it this way - imagine Golf? I have some pretty negative feelings about golf (weird, expensive, elitist, time consuming, too many rules, poncy etc). However, I have never had anyone try to recruit me into playing golf, attempt to design a 'beginners' gold game to help me realise my love of the game, challenged my bias against golf or debated the issues behind my assumptions. However, when it comes to gaming, we do all of these things and more.
    \n
    \nHow do we improve this? Well, I would say 'be true to yourself.'
    \n
    \nFolks, we sit around for hours at a time, week in-week out, pretending to be elves and dwarves. Our standard games have rule books like small encyclopedias with, in some cases, literally 100s of different rules and permutations. We play games that can last years. We have a culture riddled with jargon and unique terminology. All of these things are true and its pretty hard to escape them as you become involved in the hobby.
    \n
    \nJust like everyone will not play golf, not everyone will roleplay. Just like I cannot see the attraction of following a little white ball around a trail of grass, some people cannot see the attraction of acting out the adventures of Star Captain Zack Wang. If we stop viewing these as problems - either with roleplaying or us or the person - and just accept that there are different strokes for different folks, we might come across as a little less ... desperate?
    \n
    \nAnd now my favourite Gamer-Mother story. When I first told my now sadly dead Mother that I had just scored a free trip to Wrestlemania off the back of working for a CCG company, she was very pleased. She asked "Is that like that Dungeons and Dragons?". I said that it was, sort of, but I don't really play D&D; anymore. She replied "Oh thats good, I was always a little worried about the demon worship!". Upon further investigation I discovered that she had thought that all the time I had been roleplaying, from around 12-18, I was also a devil worshipper - a one man cult in my little terraced bedroom in a mining village on the Northumberland borders. I asked her why she never stopped me? Her reply? "Well ... you looked like you were having such a good time, I didn't want to interfere!"

    \n I do sympathize with reactions like these. My experience with gamers (including myself) has shown me a lot of potential reasons why these same perceptions tend to surface frequently. A lot of wounded people are drawn to roleplaying, and their reactions to others often don't put the best face on the hobby. Is there anything we can do about that? No, and yes. No, because it's not like we can ask people to leave -- not to mention, much of the time, they is us. But also yes, because we can each deal with our own stuff, and work to react to outsiders intelligently, responsibly, empathetically.

You're free to like this hobby, or not, which is in no way a reflection of me, or my choice to pursue it." But it's a tough one to give, especially when you're feeling defensive. At some point, I realized that the way people react to me is much more a reflection of their state of mind than my actuality. So I try to let them live in whatever state they want...when I don't get too defensive.

    \n I think every non-mainstream avocation/hobby/activity struggles with many if not most of these question. When Matt says that "reactions of non-gamers to gamer-dom and its activities are natural, understandable, and human", he is in my opinion absolutely correct insofar as the natural human reaction to activities that are unfamiliar is "eeewww weird". This is natural and human because it once helped us avoid being eaten by cougars. Being a neophile requires either aberrant genetics or training.

    So for me the first step in deciding how to react is to reject the thesis that we as the community of gamers (regardless of whether gamers means in context "players of video games", "players of tabletop games", "players of tabletop role playing games", "players of tabletop role playing games with non-traditional mechanics put out by small publisher", etc), are doing anything badwrong. When you are a member of a small focused community of interest who is not familiar to the mainstream, you must simply assume that most people that learn of your avocation will be initially puzzled, and may have random associations based on their history of interactions with other people they may have perceived as being part of the community you are identified with. This is true regardless of whether the community is gaming, rat breeding, or morris dancing (to name a few).

    \n This conversation is of interest to me. Not too long ago, I attempted to put down my thoughts on these very topics here:
    http://dariuswolfe.livejournal.com/80322.html

    I think the negative reactions have less to do with the activity than they do with the perceptions of the people who partake in the activity. "Normal" people can be interested in the same sorts of stories: fantasy, sci-fi, martial arts, espionage, action. "Normal" people can like to play games of all sorts, video games, board games, card games.. Some of them pretty complex. "Normal" people can be interested in telling stories, and usually do on a regular basis, from funny jokes, things they've had happen to them or that they've done, or heard of. What it is, I think, is that the people who are typically attracted to roleplaying games are people who have social problems.. Geeks and nerds. Thus, roleplaying has come to be associated with the social outcasts who are its primary participants.

    As for why people shun these people (like me) I can't say. I wish I knew.

    What is the culture's reaction to it? See above; the majority of us have social problems, so it's pretty natural to assume that our reactions to it are going to be less than ideal. We embrace our outcast status by outcasting those who have shunned us. We paint ourselves the reviled geniuses of our age. (It doesn't help that many "geeks and nerds" are possessed of a higher than average intelligence; this only fuels our feelings of superiority) Those who dismiss us, or are perceived as dismissing us are in turn dismissed by us. The attitude is reinforced when we come together and share our grievances and how we've dealt with them.

    Can it be improved? Maybe. Our culture is growing up. It will always be a haven for the outcast and socially inept, I think, but more and more people are actually learning to overcome their social problems, and are still involved in the hobby. I happen to know a very attractive, very successful doctor who is an avid D&D gamer. One of my current group is a physically fit, probably good looking (I'm a bad judge of male attractiveness) non-geeky infantryman; This is probably the least geeky, most "manly" career choice one can have. While our hobby isn't really becoming more mainstream, members of the culture are becoming less of the outcast sort.

    Should it be? No. I like my insular, holier-than-thou culture. But I'm just one guy, and I'm only half-serious.

    Do I sympathize with the aforementioned reactions? Eh, not so much, but I'm not a very sympathetic person. I do, however, understand many of them. In the first list, every gamer knows one or more gamers who truly fits one of those statements. Virgins, slobs, basement dwellers. People who have lost touch with reality, and who might hurt someone. We, as members of our culture, have felt this way about specific members of our culture. In the second list, we've probably all felt this way at some point or another about specific games we don't currently play. As indie games get more out there, I find myself saying "I don't get it", and choosing not to invest my time in finding out. I've felt that people who are into certain games would never let someone with my preferences play with them.

    As for other hobbies/interests for which I've met the same types of reactions, yes; Reading, definitely. Interest in computers and programming, yes. Decent skill and interest in math and science, yup. Being a member of the SCA (medieval re-creation society, for them what don't know) a decided yes. At some time or another, I have met with all of the viewpoints in Matt's two lists for some hobby of mine other than gaming.

    Here's my personal experience with the negative stereotype. I don't identify "gaming" (the big, wide culture of rpgs) as the thing I like to do.
