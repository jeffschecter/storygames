    Doom mechanics: each time one of players wants to, s/he can get s perfect success as s/he had rolled all 1s. In exchange, the colony gains one point of Doom. The result is carried over from game to game (we don't know how much we can accumulate before Doom is at hand.) We had one player use it, to make sure that Haunce didn't get accused of stealing the grain and redirect suspicion (and a lynch mob) on the neighbouring Indian tribes.

    Other interesting addition from the GM: Personal Doom (lifted from Keith Senkowski's Conspiracy of Shadows). At any point a player can declare that s/he is invoking her/his Personal Doom and get perfect success. After that, the character will die.

    February 2007 edited March 2007
    Oh, I forgot to mention another mechanic the GM added: at the beginning, we can only add up to 4 details (for a maximum pool of 4 dice) in Spotlight conflict instead of the normal 6. When we discover key information about the enemy (like who it is, I guess!) we will be able to add one more detail and die, etc.

  • johnzo
    February 2007
    This is a huge group by storygames standards, with seven players. During the first session, there was a substantial amount of downtime / audience play, but no one got bored or wandered off or started chatting on unrelated things. In my experience, SGA games are usually pretty casual, and wild (and fun) off-game tangents are not uncommon. But for Roanoke, there was a laser focus on the game. I'm curious to figure out how Ed did that.

    Some guesses:

    a) the atmosphere was rigorously Roanoke, with the only light being candlelight and sound effect CDs of frogs and storms going in the background. It was pure gamespace.

    b) There was a substantial amount of pre-play on our mailing list, with people talking about their characters, building relationships, etc, so there was a much greater level of player investment than in the typical SGA game, which uses pregens.

    c) Ed required that any secrets our characters have be open secrets, which I think is a great way to drive towards author-stance play, since making effective use of those secrets to drive play is inherently authorial. From there, I think that author-stance rewards audience play -- the more you're in tune with the game, the better you'll riff off it.

    However it works, there are some serious skills on exhibit in this game and it is a joy to be included in it.

    March 2007 edited March 2007
    Actually, there were several little GM tricks that helped. Individually, there things we have all seen done or read about and thought, "Sure, that's obvious." Jointly, they really helped get in the mindset. As mentioned, players also jumped into things with both feet.
    • Pre-game promotion. The game was advertised early on the club mailing list, teasers were posted, the campaign bible was available even before the game started, characters were made in advanced and discussed, the theme, mood and atmosphere for the game were explicitly advertised and were were asked -- and promised to try -- to refrain from our usual kibitzing.
    • GM prep. The GM read source material about the setting, referred to games, books and movies with similar atmosphere, posted links and source documents, prepared ye olde bag o'rumours, planned little intro scenes tailored for each character, etc.
    • Mood enhancers and props. :-) The GM selected a play list of music and sound effects and used them very well during the games, wrote an intro and selected theme music for the credits, lit the room with candles, handed maps and props, etc.
    • Player prep and cooperation. Players also did background research, exchanged ideas on their characters and how they would interact, shared "character secrets" in the interest of creating a good story, found character pictures, wrote character background stories and journals, discussed similar TV shows and movies and how the "Roanoke TV series" would develop, gave each other ideas, and were very open about communicating the difference in their characters' expectations and their own as players.
