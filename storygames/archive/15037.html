\n I asked this on RPG.net, but I'm also curious what folks around here may answer me.
So, in Dogs in the Vineyard, the Fallout rules let the players choose between various things, some of which have a clear mechanical disadvantage (like losing a stat die or turning an existing non-d4 trait into a d4 trait), while others give a clear mechanical advantage - specifically, the options to create new d4 traits or to add an additional d4 to an existing d4 trait. (Mechanically, all traits are advantages, since you can always pretend a trait simply isn't there.)
With the infinite variety of things you can write under a trait (including stuff like 'broken arm', 'got humiliated before an entire congregation', 'a nasty scar on my face' etc), I can't see why a player should choose any other option.
Thoughts?

    \n It's perfectly interesting for me, mind. It's just that my belief is that any game mechanic should to be built in a way that encourages what it's about and that the choices it creates should be meaningful and interesting. I believe that regardless of the designer's intents, the players WILL maximise their efficiency within the frame of a given design, so I think saying 'you can do it but please don't' is inferior to designing it so that doing it wouldn't be a smart thing, mechanically. Now, I have no problem with having some of the considerations be narrative instead of purely mechanical (and this is where real munchkinism lies - when there ARE narrative drawbacks to a decision, but you simply ignore them), but this isn't the case here, since 90% of the time I think you'd be able to perfectly justify gaining a new d4 trait or relationship, or gaining another d4 in one of your existing d4 traits, as a logical and reasonable result o the conflict.
    Plus, it's hardly min/maxing when a game provides me with a clear choice between something that is entirely good for me and something that is entirely bad for me, with little to no in-world difference between them.

    P.S. It should be noted that in all other cases DitV does a brilliant job of mechanically encouraging exactly the sort of play it's all about and having the mechanical options also be interesting and meaningful in-world options. Which is why the contrast of this small aspect strikes me so.

    \n ODDin, great question.

    With Belongings, if you want to min/max, nothing is stopping you. You can describe every Belonging as Big + Powerful. What limits you is the fiction. It's cool to have a Crap gun you've never shot before. It can be incredibly cool to have a Crap coat, especially if everyone else has beautiful coats. The fiction incentivizes creative and varied options.

    BUT this isn't quite the case with the D4 Trait / Relationship Fallout options. As a GM, I much more enjoy when a players takes a new D4 Trait or Relationship because they get to write something cool on their character sheet that reminds us what happened in the game. And the D4 Trait / Relationship is mechanically useful. And it can be just as relevant to the fiction in most situations as any other Fallout option. If my Uncle betrayed me in a Conflict, lowering my Uncle's Relationship dice is as fictionally valid as taking a new Trait, "Even blood can betray you." I'd argue that the new Trait is way more interesting.

    Often when I see people not taking the D4 Fallout option, it is out of guilt. They feel cheesy... "this is too good, what am I doing wrong." Or sometimes they do it just to show the rest of the group that they aren't a "power gamer", which can be seen as another example of guilt.

    There are exceptions. Sometimes lowering a Trait just feels right. It's not rational. I've seen plenty of people do it without hesitation.

    Why is Dogs designed this way? I don't know. Maybe it's to tell the player, "I trust you, do what you think makes the most sense."

    \n Oh! I have some advice about "stay in the conflict until you've got only your d4s left, then give, as though you hadn't rolled those d4s at all." That's fine but it's not the optimal mechanical play.

    Mechanically, the best thing you can do for your character is take the blow pretty often when you're just talking, and regularly but not always when it's physical, not fighting. A mix of d4 and d6 fallout, heavy on the d4s, is how you get the most and biggest new dice on your character sheet.

    So if you're rolling d4s, the best thing to do with them isn't give before you use them, but use them early (supposing the conflict starts with an argument). Collect your fallout before the guns come out, and while you're doing it save some of your big dice for when the fallout can do you real harm.
