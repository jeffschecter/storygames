    \n Just taking a quick glance around the corebook section of rpgnow reveals many.Just the free ones (and disregarding quickstarts, which you really shouldn't because many are amazing):

    44, 6d6 UltraLite, Atomic Highway (I think), Back Streets, Beyond the Beyond, d6 Adventure, Fantasy or Space (this actually used to be considered ridiculously rules-lite, though I think it's pretty moderate now), Disgenesia, Doublecross (actually NUELOW games in general are pretty solid for this), EABALite and EABAnywhere, FreeFATE, FUBAR, Mythos, PDQ# (gonna be a close call on system heaviness there), Pitch, Sword's Edge (mmmaybe), USR, and XPress (mmmmaybe).

    Toon and Ghostbusters both have this covered for comedy, too, though possibly Ghostbusters doesn't count as indie because it's A) a license and not Luke Crane, and Toon was for-hire for Steve Jackson Games.

    Also, Very Early D&D plus the words "nobody can play a spellcaster".

    Basically, like indie roleplaying games, lite roleplaying games have been insanely common throughout the history of the hobby and are available more easily now than ever before. I think all game designers have said "I think I'll do a lite game!" at some point in their careers.

    \n I thought this was a thread for games that is at most 4-6 pages long, but when people suggest things like InSpectres with 70-something pages, then I start to wonder. Yeah, sure. InSpectres can be summarized on a single page, but was that really what this thread was about? You still need to read 70+ pages.

    Cthulhu Dark (3 pages): You don't need more rules than this to play Call of Cthulhu.

    The Coyotes of Chicago (12 pages): a really cool way of writing to play improvised adventures. Third time in two days that I name drop this game. This suggestion is a stretch when it comes to the number of pages, because even if you shouldn't read the last five pages, it's still a seven page read. The game is about Alex that is about the found out the truth behind the coyotes in Chicago.

    1001 nights (pages unknown): I got it while it was still free and it looks like it's around 5-6 pages long. The game is about courtiers telling stories with the other courtiers involved in their stories. A really nice little game with ambitions and reprimands.

    Sea Dracula (2 pages): a perfect party game where you play animal lawyers under water. To prove you point, you need your skills on the dance floor.

    The Wildlings (3 pages): John Harper's introduction kit to roleplaying games for kids. Your journey is towards a ruin with a foul beast that has robbed the village of precious items and a kid.

    Fortune Cookies and Nuclear War (a business card): a two player game about Russia and USA that needs to come to an agreement.

    I wish I had finished my This Is Pulp, because it's 2,5 A4 pages long at the moment. A half page to long for my taste.

    Lots of short form stuff in the Norwegian Style book, though it's laid out differently b/c it's collected into a book. Matthij's Fuck Youth is a particular favorite.

    Jackson Tegu's small games are brilliant, and absolutely fit the bill. I am remiss for not mentioning them earlier, and glad he spotted this thread.

    Superhero is a simple game that you learn as you play. It scales gracefully from 2-8 participants, is an outlet for returning to the frenetic glee that you thought you'd lost as you departed youth, and will guaranteed result in a room doubled over in laughter for the duration of play. It is tied in my mind for "best party + story game ever," along with Sea Dracula. (The games it comes sold with are solid too.)

    Temper is an interesting meditation on what games are, and what games can be used for. I think about it often. It's packaged with another game that's cool too.

    Cthulhu Dark (3 pages): You don't need more rules than this to play Call of Cthulhu.
    \nI ran this for the first time a few weeks ago and it worked great. Though it does focus play a lot more on the traditional circuit of description* than a lot of games "with 'indie' mechanics" (as Sophia put it) do. But it also works well if you've got a bunch of skills from other games like scene framing or stake setting. Though since Graham designed it as a means of testing scenarios for more traditional Cthulhu gaming, it's probably designed to be played in the circuit of description approach* rather than with other techniques.

    * Keeper describes situation, player describes character's action, Keeper describes new situation, player describes character's action and on and on.
