Players have a lot of input towards the setting, but they're still surprised by the result. I love it!

    Like some other posters, I've got some issues with the implementation of the Apocalypse engine. Having the Keeper Hold 1 on a player Miss seems so awkward to me. A friend of mine suggested that it was a pacing mechanic, to better emulate the horror genre. I can totally see that! But what then happens when the player fails a roll and the Keeper decides to Hold 1 instead of making a Move? Does the player still fail in the fiction, and have consequences? If so, aren't they getting double consequences for a single Miss? And if not, how does the fiction respond to a Miss? Does nothing happen? It's just...a bit strange to me.

    My other issue is with the stats; it's not a big deal but I wish they'd rename them. As it is, the stats sort of imply that they're quantified measurements of the character's abilities, for example: Might and Reason. These sound like they tell me how strong and intelligent my character is. Why, then, do I roll +Reason to dodge a punch? Reason is the catch-all Act Under Pressure stat and I don't understand why that is, when Dungeon World already laid down a pretty solid paradigm for swapping stats with Defy Danger. Someone in another forum has already hacked his game, changing Might to Aggression and Reason to Will. This made my all my issues disappear. So much of it is just presentation. Which brings me to the last thing...

    I find it kind of awkward the way they present Moves as literal Things: "the MC uses up one of their hard moves and deals damage to the player" is an actual example they use in the book. My interpretation of hard and soft moves is more descriptive: "when the player does something, the world responds appropriately." Turning hard moves into something you can hoard and dish out at will, as opposed to REACTIONS within the fiction, just feels... I don't know, weird to me.\ufeff

      I'm enarmoured with making the whole Keeper takes one hold / Token thing too. So this is my plan:
      * Get the players to choose their elements of Colour and Lore and write them on the margins of the map and have one of the players read out the Player's intro blurb for that particular playset whilst I fill out the town worksheet and look up the corresponding framework elements.
      * Throw the Playbooks out there and let the players decide on who they want to play, all the while asking provocative questions a la AW/MH/DW chargen, tying the characters to the setting / hazards / and NPCs listed under the playset.

      Next I'll follow Graham's advice on playing investigative con games:\n

      * For the first hour, let them explore a world and interact in character. Exploring is fun. Interacting is fun.
      * For the second hour, let them investigate. Now, we're switching to the more traditional sort of fun, which is the players uncovering the GM's mystery.
      * In the third hour, hit them with the full horror and let them react to it. Now, we're going back to character interaction. Let party tensions come to the fore. Ideally, they should be turning on each other, restraining each other, pulling guns on each other.

      This requires two things: tensions between player characters and a mystery that will knock the character sideways.
      \nThe tensions between the PC's I will focus on through well versed AW play: Setting up triangles and looking for where they are not in control and pushing there. The mystery will emerge from the elements they are interested in and obvious tragic ends that speak from the hazards and threads developed between them. As clues emerge from play, we write them on the map, infer scenes and further investigations, uncovering the mystery we as a group are most interested / frightened of discovering out.

      The novel thing I'm going to do is have the classic Lovecraftian Endings stolen (as Graham suggests p.21) and written on aged Index Cards in a fancy Handwritten font placed on the table where all can see. Namely:
      The Descent
      The Final Horror
      The Escape (often followed by the Confrontation)
      The Realisation

      Now here's the cool thing. I don't now as Keeper which of these classic endings I'm going to go with, they all could be wonderfully satisfying, almost as if they are stake questions to be answered. So as the player's make moves and uncover clues and I start asking more provocative questions and turn their moves back on them, or foreshadow future trouble, it will become clearer which direction the mystery is heading. Every time I 'save' a move after a failed Poke Around or Help / Hinder I will take a token and place it on one of the Ending Cards I'm most interested in seeing happen. Conversely, the Players can spend one of their Lore tokens to do exactly the same thing.

      That way the play builds collaboratively in a fiasco like way. We know the ending is going to be lovecraftian, but only through the emergent play can we establish as a group the way our mystery will end.

      \n Sooooo, I just got back from 4 hours of Tremulus the first session with one old hand at role playing and two newbies. It was a great session and I'll do a full write up after I get some sleep, but I just wanted to get some thoughts down before I go to bed.

      Without Graham's Stealing Cthulhu the game would have been half as good. His advice on pacing, aggressive scene framing, reincorporation, beginnings (and endings) and relating the clues to the horror (creature) and increasing the horror need to be paraphrased for the Tremulus ruleset.

      The Playset is good, and I tweaked it a little to match our home town of Katoomba, but I found running Hazards listed on the fly a little page-flippy, even after taking 5 to fill out the Town worksheet, the Hazard moves are hard to reference and there needs to be an object category as well as location and hazards. I managed to re-incorporate a book that our salesman had stashed in his 'Caravan of Dreams', that became the nexus of the whole mystery, but still. For a quick Durance style hook, the mystery went down well.

      We muddled through the Trust / Hx section with lots of provocative questions, but I think the choices from lists like in DW Bonds, or AW Hx works far better. Folks always elaborate on these lists anyways.

      The wealth abstract is a resource without scarcity. Despite using up their wealth as often as possible on missed rolls, it seemed irrelevant to the the greater mystery at hand of elder gods or or contemptuous cult leaders. Just another track to keep.

      I found the proliferation of mechanical bonuses and penalties painful when making moves. I pushed for a narrative reason, but it just became too laborious. I quickly fell back into my AW MC style and things made more sense. I didn't ever save hard moves, I simply made them on a miss. There was lots of "yes and...' going on. We simply rolled moves as stated with attribute bonuses, incorporating the playbook moves as narrative justification rather than just +1 this or that. Act under pressure became the go-to move with variations based on different attributes.

      Lore was hard to come by, despite players wanting to make Lore moves and attempting to collect it. It was simply another resource to track and not that common a track anyways, plus it was a resource I couldn't easily threaten as a Keeper. So I did away with it as written. The players could make a lore move if they missed a basic move, sort of like a fresh chance.

      WE had fun! The props I prepared worked great, as did the framework suggested in the playbook on the player's town choices. I'll post an AP soon, but the players are keen to play next week and see where the mystery leads.
