    Posted By: jenskotAdded wrinkle. You don't know them. It's a convention. Your committed to running the game. They paid to play. What do you do?
    \nThat's some wrinkle -- making it about someone I don't know, have no expectation of seeing again anytime soon, and am only going to be playing with for 3-5 hours at the absolute most means that I'm not inclined to waste time playing twenty questions with them. If I ask what's up and get "Nothing" as an answer, I won't keep pushing it at the expense of all the other non-annoyed people who also paid to play this game.

    I might try to salvage things indirectly within the game, by throwing different kinds of stuff at the annoyed guy to see if he responds to any of it; if he does, problem solved, he's back to playing. If he doesn't respond to any of it, I might look to see if he's pissed off at someone else at the table, and if I can put pressure on that person to stop doing whatever's causing the problem. (Assuming that I agree that this other person is being rude; I might not, in which case the annoyed guy is out of luck.)

    And that's about where I'd leave it. If these were my friends and this was an ongoing game, I would be willing to go to the wall to make things work out, but for a con game? No way.

    And hey, maybe someone who is that passive-aggressive and that touchy just shouldn't be going to cons; he should grow a thicker skin or learn how to solve his own problems before spending his cash on something that's practically guaranteed to annoy him. Or at least learn how to fucking say what his goddamn problem is instead of mumbling "Nothing" when someone asks him point blank what's wrong.

    \n My experience with people who do this isn't in a convention. It's not a passive agressive play for attention either. I've occasionally had players get worked up over something, get upset over how something turned out, and then disengage from the campaign, and shut down any attempt to talk about it. Often, it's because they;ve understood something in a different way to me - something looks worse to them than it looks to me. Any attempt to discuss it tends to make things even worse. (Which may say something about my conflict management skills... Or it may be to paraphrase someone else, whatever happened in the player's head is none of my business.)

This has worked for me every time - the player soon gets back into it and whatever happened is left behind.

    "I want to explain this in a way that makes sense in a very roundabout
    fashion so you understand why the answer that a woman ends up with is
    "Nothing" when you ask that particular question.

    Think of a woman as a robot who has been programmed by society but
    somewhere underneath it all, possesses a genuine intelligence. (I can
    make this metaphor because it's really true to some extent of guys AND
    girls, metaphorically. I'm not putting down my gender or anything.)

    The woman has feelings. Sometimes she gets upset. Her sensory intake
    is so vast that frequently her emotions can jolt into high gear
    remarkably faster than her brain can get the sensory message and
    inform her conscious mind of why she's upset. Once she begins to
    experience a feeling, her sensory input multiplies her feelings before
    she's even consciously aware of them, because they're working so fast.

    When she does become consciously aware of them, it's already
    telegraphed in the rest of her body. This generally the point at which
    a guy asks "What's wrong?" She has only just become aware of her
    feelings via her hardware.

    At this point her software (social expectations) kick in. This is why
    some women can immediately say what's wrong where many others will say
    "Nothing." etc. Some women have great software.

    If they have not-so-great software, their intelligence attempts to
    interpret their sensory information. If they can't yet interpret it,
    the default answer is "Nothing" as nothing has yet been established as
    "wrong."

    In social situations/in front of other people, the software classifies
    what's wrong as "nothing" temporarily, as the software frequently has
    a caveat that it never admits to having a problem in public, because
    if in public, nothing is wrong. Wrong is defined as a state of being
    that occurs only in private.

    The most insidious version of this - that happens before a woman can
    even think about it - is her software has been programmed to not
    recognize the problem. So in a sense, it is erring without knowing its
    erring. When a woman encounters a situation in which she feels upset
    and knows why, but has been programmed to identify the reason as not
    an appropriate cause of being upset (and in some cases if she knows
    YOU don't think it's a problem that can/should cause her to be upset),
    then technically nothing is wrong. She is feeling upset, but her
    software refuses to allow her to identify the cause, because its
    already identified as a non-cause.

    The circular logic (at the speed of light) goes like this: What is
    wrong? (blank) is wrong. (blank) cannot be wrong. Nothing must be
    wrong.

    And then before she's even had the chance to circumvent this,
    "Nothing" comes out of her mouth.

    Of course, later, you have to deal with her intelligence that wanders
    around her psyche fixing things and saying, well, SOMETHING must be
    causing this error, let's try redefining this as a potential problem
    source and experimentally fix it. This is why four hours later, you'll
    have to have a conversation about what's wrong, even though she told
    you nothing was wrong.

    I think it would help if you considered the "Nothing" as "EMOTIONS DO
    NOT COMPUTE! ERROR! ERROR!" Because I'm pretty sure that's mostly what
    it means."
    \nSo, yeah, one more vote against the notion that "nothing" = "passive aggressive bullshit."

    \n I think it depends on how one says "Nothing."

    You can say it casually, like, "it's my problem; nothing is wrong that you need to be concerned with; sorry that it became obvious at the table." That's not passive-aggressive bullshit.

    You can also say it with varying degrees of bile and causticity, in which case you're probably being passive-aggressive. Don't say the word "nothing" and all the while telegraph the opposite meaning with the tone of your voice. That's bullshit.

    A couple words with the person should be able to clear up which case it is. "Hey, is there anything you need me to do to make this game more fun for you? Want to step away from the table with me for a minute and we can hash this out?" If they decline, they've basically taken responsibility for solving the problem without you.

    \nHere\u2019s my take away:
    \n
    \nDon\u2019t assume you know what\u2019s wrong. If it\u2019s not disruptive, ignore it, give them their turn and opportunities to participate but no more than anyone else.
    \n
    \nDon\u2019t call them out. Doing so might be more disruptive to everyone else. And putting someone on the spot can put them on the defensive and illicit more emotion than rational problem solving.
    \n
    \nIf it\u2019s mildly disruptive, wait for a break, and speak to them privately. If they don\u2019t want to talk, don\u2019t push them. If the problem can be solved quickly, do so. If the problem seems like it could grow, give them an easy out of the game.
    \n
    \nIf they say \u201cnothing is wrong\u201d when it is obvious something is, don\u2019t take it personally. They may be doing you a favor. They might know there is an issue but don\u2019t want to be disruptive. Or there may be an issue but they don\u2019t know what it is or don\u2019t understand it yet. Either way, it\u2019s not your responsibility to solve the problem.
    \n
    \nIf it is huge disruption that is affecting multiple people, deal with it quickly. Call for a break. Talk it out. Give them 1 chance. Otherwise ask them to leave.
