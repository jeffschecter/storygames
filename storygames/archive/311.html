    In answer to the original question, Stuff may be a strategic reward, but it is also one of the most basic contextualizing elements. All the following should be qualified with "in some games..."

    That +2 longsword you found in the hoard isn't just a resource. It comes with a story: I found this thing there after doing that. It becomes part of the character: I carry this thing. You might say this is part of the process of bricolage, or of accretion of meaning.

    Continuity is an expression of the idea that a person is formed through his or her experiences.

  • Nir Shiffer

    Reverse the "get stuff --> have stuff" order:

    Players have resources - whether they started the game with them, drew them randomly from a deck or just given them during play for reasons unknown (but not because they just "earned" them, because that would just get us where we started). Let's say for this example that these resources (sword, bank accout, magicla doohicky) are written on cards.

    Every time a player gets a card (alternative: wants to bring a card into play from his "hand") he has to integrate it into his character's history. He has, right there, to tell us what is this thing, how he got it and what it means to him.

    This needs a lot of refining, but I imagine it would create a game full of flashbacks and nonlinear storytelling.

    How'd that happen?

    It happened because the characters are (1) inherently interesting and (2) repetition and variation strengthens themes.

    But without some sort of explanation, you're not going to get away with it in this day and age. Sitcoms have turned increasingly into soap operas.     Even in things like Calvin and Hobbes there is continuity -- its just that it is an internal continuity of character rather than continuity of event. Calvin is always Calvin, and his actions, choices, and emotional responses will constantly reinforce that fact. That he reacts this time to playing Calvinball the way he reacted last time to Hobbes messing up his Adventure of Spaceman Spiff is the continuity.

    Let's ditto the Conan stories. When Howard wrote them there wasn't a lot of continnuity between the Conan stories. They occured in a pretty random order, didn't usually refere to each other, and even changed facts and locations about the world between stories. And yet, if you read the Howard canon of Conan you get a very strong sense of continuity both in character and in world. It's because Conan makes the same choices, reinforces the same themes, and lives in a world that has the same tone over and over. His continuity isn't "last time on Conan," it is "Barbarism will always triumph."

    In that I think some of the ideas in games like Trollbabe could be useful. What you're testing for isn't to see if you can get stuff, its to see if you can establish character through choices. I tend to play Trollbabe a lot like Dogs without the mechanical change to the character -- each episode is a testing of "you said that last episode, do you still mean it if THIS?" Even when the episodes aren't in any kind of chronological order and don't have any reference in the game to what happened last episode, the reference is still there in our minds, and the sense of character revelation (as opposed to character development, which doesn't work as well in this type of game, IME) grows game to game. It works even without Dog's traits from fallout.

    Sorcerer and Sword has some more words on this idea, especially in the context of Conan.

    I wonder how surreal you could get with Dogs. The fallout system implies some continuity -- but it occurs to me that you could get some wicked Dark Tower surrealist action if between games you said that unspecifiable time and layers of reality passed between towns and let players freely reasign their traits and relations based on themes they want to hit. So in one town you have young brother Hiram who has a weakness for girls and old brother Zeke who can kill anything that walks or flies or crawls, and in the next town you have young brother Zeke the pacifist and old brother Hiram the chaste....
