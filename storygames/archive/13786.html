\n Well ...
- the other day I talked with two young role-players, and they asked me what I saw in the future of role-playing games ...
- "Little", I said, first, and then:
- "A lot!"

And I gave this Grand Prediction:
As the earth gets steadily more overheated and flooded, whole nations are crumbling, systems we knew is breaking down, and we, the human race, would be cast into a dark age of misery and no hope ... if it was not for the dialogue based role-playing games! Where no electricity is, there is no computer and console-games. Where no functional factories are maintained, there is no paper and printing. Simple role-playing games rule the world of entertainment. People scratch themselves their characters in the dirt, squatting with bone-dice in their hands, narrating grand contemporary (for their time) dystopias and quite believable and realistic sounding armageddons.

And their game-smiths make epic games on the folly of generations before them, mocking the age of enlightenment and industrialization, laughing their heads of, and crying a bit, over "the post-modern era", which they have aptly renamed the last modern era. Doing silly comedies set to that era, with characters of grand ineptness trying to save the earth, while refusing to change anything that leads to the catastrophe.

Role-playing games will be an effective tool for building tradition, propagate learning and developing sustainable philosophies. They will teach us love, happiness and good use of the environment, laying the foundations of an extraordinary surge in spiritual development amongst mankind. Mankind will gradually find itself in a paradise of real and imagined low-tech co-existence. All will be well, for the earth will regain its balance seven million years after the environmental catastrophe, with humans still roaming the continents, and having fun with their handprinted and highly sophisticated role-playing games.

Huzzah!

    Grand prediction: Within twenty years, RPGs will no longer be sold as physical books anywhere. The big companies who make their bones off selling physical objects (e.g., whoever owns D&D at that point) will switch to making games whose rules can be printed on cards, tiles, and/or figurines, perhaps with a supplemental pamphlet. Smaller companies will just decide that paying publishing costs isn't worth it (especially with the near-ubiquity of eReaders and similar devices at that point) and will switch over to strictly digital distribution. This will likely murder Friendly Local Game Stores in all but the very largest cities, so whatever social functions those serve for gamers will end up shifting somewhere else; gaming clubs or mini-conventions something of that ilk, I imagine.

    Also, someone will finally, finally take the PDF format out into the woods and shoot it in the head. They will invent a better way of reading text + pictures on a screen, one that doesn't suck.

    \n Tomas, you get a fanmail from me for your idea! That's a grand prediction indeed.

    Accounting for Taste: what you say is quite possible considering the printing costs and limited printing numbers. However, I sincerely hope not. No electronic means of creating a book can ever be as cool as, say, the ashcan of Acts of Evil. And how about the paper with which In A Wicked Age is printed?
    Lots of people recently speak about the disappearance of books in general, but that's not gonna happen. If your ebook reader breaks, you risk losing everything you had in it. A book can fall from a skyscraper and still be perfectly readable. Also, it's a physical medium that needs no support to be used. On the other hand, you can't read a PDF (or other file) without at least a screen.
    That leads to the growing frailty of the conservation of communications, and the dependancy from delicate technology for something which hasn't been requiring it for thousands of years, since writing was invented. So I hope your vision won't become true. It's the bleakest of all.

Smaller companies will just decide that paying publishing costs isn't worth it (especially with the near-ubiquity of eReaders and similar devices at that point) and will switch over to strictly digital distribution. This will likely murder Friendly Local Game Stores in all but the very largest cities, so whatever social functions those serve for gamers will end up shifting somewhere else; gaming clubs or mini-conventions something of that ilk, I imagine.

    Also, someone will finally, finally take the PDF format out into the woods and shoot it in the head. They will invent a better way of reading text + pictures on a screen, one that doesn't suck.
    \nTangent: Rules and physical bits have been produced by seperate companies for almost the entire history of the minis-gaming hobby's existence. Companies like GW who produce both the minis and the rules as viable for-sale products are extremely rare and almost worthy of consideration as unique situations.

    I don't think smaller producers will ever go entirely over to digital, although having both physical and digital available certainly makes sense. What you might see is the disappearance of the coffee-table book rule book, with the exception of ones for proven designs. 1e AD+D hardbacks set up a nasty little tarpit by their mere existence, causing all kinds of badthink where horses and carts are involved.

    Here's my prediction:
    The greying of the RPG hobby ends up following some trends I've seen about the even greyer minis wargaming hobby: Grandparents and grandkids playing together causes a small retro-resurgence of the hobby and supports the few remaining and/or new companies still producing the stuff for the hobby.
