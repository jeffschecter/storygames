\n So I'm working on this dungeon idea, right, and I realize suddenly that I haven't ever actually played a 'classic' dungeoncrawl like I'm trying to emulate. I mean, back in the day, we played Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles and GURPS: Fantasy. So we strayed closer to crime-fighting or continent-spanning epics. So I went online and found: Tomb of Horrors and White Plume Mountain, which are available as free PDFs.

And I read them.

So, um... are all D&D dungeons this lame? I mean, this shit makes no sense whatsoever! Tomb of Horrors ends with a complete anticlimax after the GM basically tortures the players for not having read the adventure before playing. White Plume Mountain has chamber after chamber filled with random monsters and guards who are guarding dead ends. What the hell?

    \n Tomb of Horrors is a bit of an exception. It was designed as a player killer for those who had already seen it all, so to speak. I'm not familiar with the other one. You might want to look up Temple of Elemental Evil if you can. That one is much better.

    But yes, over all that's what a Dungeon Crawl is. It's about two things a) exploration and b) overcoming the next encounter. If the GM has gone the extra mile then there's some context to the encounter that at least makes it appear some what logical like the basement is flooded and therefore some water serpent has made it's home there. But the disconnection of encounters is a D&D staple. The "point" of the adventure might be to defeat the wizard whose built his lab on the third floor but that has little to do with the Yellow Mold growing on the dishes in the forgotten wing of the castle or the wererats that have taken up residence in the basement or the giant bats living in the chapel bell tower.

    If the GM has gone the EXTRA EXTRA mile then some encounters are linked. Like if you've killed the wererats then nothing happens when you trip the alarm in the bedroom. But if you HAVEN'T killed the wererats then they show up if you trip the alarm.

    \n Oh, I could tell you some stories.

    So I will!

    One time, I designed and ran a dungeon where an entire level was X shaped sections of tunnel mounted on tuntables that turned 90 degrees any time someone activated a trigger plate. I actually ran that for almost an hour, while the players scrupulously mapped these corridors that seemed to run straight but were actually leading them in circles. Hilarious!

    I can't believe what an ass I was.

    A good dungeon leads players through a series of interesting set-piece encounters (interesting terrain, split-level fights, ambushes from secret tunnels), presents a few tactical or logistical decisions (press on or turn back, clear out side tunnels or go for the main goal, take this secret passage or stick to the known routes), and imparts something of the mood or setting (it's an old dwarven mine, it's a long buried space ship, it's what remains of a earlier, more civilised age). Most of all, a good dungeon is a pacing device. It lets you build up to the main encounter. It's a railroad without the illusionism, because the tracks are 10' wide corridors through solid stone.

    I love a good dungeon.

    \n I think three is my magic number. You can throw in a few little things in between, like traps, forking tunnels, non-hostiles, and things, but I like to stick to about three "big fights", including the big finale. Between them should go the interesting choices and stuff. For D&D 3.5, four is the magic number. Four good challenges is what it takes before a party needs to rest and recuperate, barring good or bad luck. In a dungeon, you can put in a few more, like five or six, if you want it to be a two-parter, or if there are ways of avoiding some of the encounters.

    EDIT: I play what most people seem to think of as short sessions - less than two hours. You might want to double up, with an opportunity for rest in the middle, if you're planning a long session. Resting inside a dungeon is a weird D&Dism, but it can be kinda cool. Otherwise, leaving the dungeon presents a cool opportunity for ambushes, or for things to have changed when they come back. One time I ran a dungeon where the mass murder of so many orcs led to the overthrow of the chief by his sorcerer adviser. They found the chief's body hacked to pieces at the mouth of the dungeon.

    \n I love dungeons (or "site-based adventures" in general, regardless of whether they take place in an actual dungeon or not), but I'm no so fond of the dungeoncrawl -- thirty levels of rooms full of monsters and treasures with no reason for the PCs to be there other than killing monsters and collecting treasures gets pretty tedious after a while.

    I like my dungeon adventures to take from one to no more than three gaming sessions (which for me are generally four to six hours), and the dungeon itself needs to exist within the context of some kind of larger story. This story might be a full fledged campaign incorporating several dungeon episodes, or it could just a simple "introduction - dungeon - conclusion".

    The Five Room Dungeon is a nice outline for designing short dungeon adventures.

    \n I think if you want to get a better idea of what dungeons are, you should play a few. Reading some samples is going to give you a very 1 dimensional idea of what a dungeon is. Tomb of Horrors and White Plume Mountain both occupy one extreme end of what a dungeon is. Both are very good examples of that style (though very old ones).

    For reading, I suggest you read Keep on the Borderlands. It's a very fine example of a different style of dungeon. It's also meant specifically to introduce both game master and players to dungeons. It will probably make a lot more "sense" to you. Tomb and White Plume both make a lot of assumptions about what comprises a dungeon and what style of play you're aiming for.

    Finally, get a good DM to run basic D&D for you a few times. It doesn't matter where they get their dungeon from. This will show you the dungeon as a factor of actual play. That's what a dungeon really amounts to. Reading written dungeons will give you a warped view.

    \nI knew that from your other post there was something wrong. Some disconnect. And this is it. You just don't understand what a "dungeon" is, or how they got started. Oh, I'm sure you've heard the historiography, but let's recap:
    \n
    \n - Gygax makes Miniatures rules that will some day become Chainmail.
    \n - Arneson creates the original one-player-to-one-figure dungeon *scenario*. His inspiration is obviously Moria.
    \n - D&D is born, as the titles say on the covers of the books, as a miniatures wargame. (If you don't believe me about the cover, see here: http://www.pen-paper.net/rpgdb.php?op=showbook&bookid=2469)
    \n - Gygax starts thinking that there could be a semi-realisticly presented world that exists between scenarios. That you can "play" the characters between scenarii. His publications start heading this way. But won't get there until AD&D.
    \n
    \nDo you play miniatures at all? Or other wargames? I play Warhammer 40K regularly. When we set up to play, we decide on how many points to play, and which teams each side will use, and what the victory conditions are. Playing Apocalypse recently, the victory conditions are to have the majority control of six positions on the game table.
    \n
    \nDo you think that we even bother to describe where the fight is occuring? Or what the victory positions represent? Or even why the fight is happening? No, it's WH40K, and somebody is fighting a fight like this somewhere for some reason, and we simply don't care.
    \n
    \nThat's because it's a game, not what we think of now as RPG. Almost all early D&D play was wargame. If you read the Basic D&D text, for instance, you'll find that there's this concept of "town" which is the place where characters go between dungeons to rest and get new equipment. Basically there's zero indication that you are to play out town, it's just a hit point reset, and an opportunity to spend treasure on new equipment. And "Town" is an evolution from Original D&D, in which you just healed and got equipment between sessions with no explanation. Pass GO, collect 200$.
    \n
    \nDungeons, as scenarii, quite simply had little to no rationale put forth. The rationales in the modules you mention are actually quite sophisticated, compared to most play where folks didn't even bother to have ANY rationale. The characters are going to a dungeon - why? Because the game is Dungeons & Dragons. It's like asking why does the little shoe go around the board in Monopoly... because that's how you play the game. "Character Motivations?" The characters have personality? Why? What's that for? They're just a pile of numbers on a sheet that tell you how good the character is at hacking up the monsters in the dungeon.
    \n
    \nIf you want a real taste, check out Module B1 "Into the Unknown." It is absolutely the clasic model for dungeons from to which all others can be geneologically traced. It's "premise" is that it was built by a warrior and a mage named Rogan and Zelgar (or sumptin), who have since disappeared, only to be replaced by monsters. Enter the characters to obtain the various treasures of the place. Note this carefully: each room has a brief description, and then has two openings on it for the GM to fill in that look like this...
    \n
    \nMonster:
    \n
    \n
    \nTreasure:
    \n
    \n
    \nYep, they don't even bother putting in the creatures or treasure. Why? Replayability, of course! How can you use it repeatedly, if the monsters are the same each time, and you always know where the best treasure is located (hmmmm, World of Warcraft... hmmm)? No, the monsters change each time, and the treasure, with no explanation of why or how this could happen.
    \n
    \nBecause we don't need an explanation. Its the dungeon. It's the place in which the wargaming scenarios occur. It doesn't have to make any sense. It just has to give us an opportunity to hack monsters, and cast spells.
    \n
    \nRead that copy of "Keep on the Borderlands." Now realize that the rules there for "wilderness adventuring" did not exist prior to this module. Because nobody ever went anywhere but "town" and the dungeon. See the evolution of thought?
    \n
    \n
    \nThe fact that Gygax very slowly morphed D&D into something else over the next 10 years, is an interesting part of the history. But you have to remember, then, that he was adjusting his own wargaming canalization, and having to deal with a culture built by him and Arneson from 1974 to 1979. Instead of presenting a whole new game, he was "converting" the wargamers over. Which meant is was all incremental. Like...
    \n
    \n"Hey, you should have the economics of the world work sensibly... like if a lot of money comes out of a dungeon, this will affect prices of equipment."
    \n
    \nIt's still a wargaming mentality, just adjusted for economics. Yes, by 1980, there's this budding idea in the heads of many GMs, fostered by some articles from the Dragon magazine, that, in fact, players should actually "roleplay" their characters, as in having the characters act not as pawns of the player, but as thought they had their own motivations.

    \n
    \nNow, all along, and definitely exploding in the mid 80s, there were groups that were starting up using newer games that began with the "roleplaying" ethic. The idea that the world was a full place, and not just put there to support dungeons. Oh, somehow they each had a rationale for having scenarii, called "Adventures." But it was heading to what we know today.
    \n
    \nBut lots and lots of players never left that Basic D&D mode of play. In fact I know a lot of them. Probably more of them than I know Story Gamers. Oh, they're probably in the minority now. But the majority is probably made up of groups where "Adventures" don't have to have a lot of rationale, though we also play the characters as though they had real motivations.
    \n
    \nI'd posit that this is a very difficult sort of play, but that's an argument for a different thread. But the point is that, even if they are "roleplaying" as we think of it, they do so with extremely heavy-handed arrangement of play where everybody knows that every night it's going to be back into some hole in the ground. Or the like.
    \n
    \nI don't know if you're having trouble getting your head around this ideal of play, but it's what a lot of people consider to be RPG play. For every one of us who Poo-poos their lack of "Real" roleplaying, there's one of them that is bemoaning how many folks have gone over to the "Fruity" sort of play that we do. They're out just playing a game, killing stuff. We actually play out the emotions of our characters? What the fuck? How is that even a game?
    \n
    \nWe exhort: Stop playing games!
    \nAnd they retort: Stop gaming plays!
    \n
    \nExpecting that the materials that they produce to make any sense for our sort of play... just won't work. It's a very different sort of activity on a very fundamental level.
    \n
    \nThat's not to say that you're Don Quixote in my book, trying to find a rationale for dungeons for your game. But you won't find it in early D&D modules. Not by a longshot. I'd suggest staying away from the later "good" stuff, too. Temple of Elemental Evil, and Underworld, etc - while way more "middle ground" than the early modules you cite - are still vastly different than what you have come to expect from RPG materials. They still assume that the characters are in a "party" and that the players should work to keep the party together, so as to keep the action going. Action before roleplay. Roleplay is something you do during downtime, don't let it get in the way of actually playing the game.
    \n
    \nBasically the best you'll get out of those materials is that everyone should simply agree to the central conciet of the dungeon before hand, and go with it. The Archduke needs you to go into the Temple? OK, in you go.
    \n
    \nNot at all what you're looking for.
    \n
    \nSo... yes... to answer your question. All dungeons are this sucky. For this purpose (for some they're the awesome). Any exceptions that exist, only serve to prove the rule.

They're not designed to make a lot of sense but, rather, to showcase, in a limited window of time, what the authors considered to be key selling points of a then (largely) new entertainment medium and the AD&D system in particular. Much like convention scenarios for most other RPGs.

    Holding up these specialized scenarios as representative of the milieu (or D&D) is unfair and skews things more than a bit based on oversight of the information that I impart above. Doing so is an old, tired, and well known troll in online D&D communities. I'm not certain that you're utilizing it in that manner but, given the recent-ish need of several Indie game fans and designers to celebrate the passing of Gary Gygax by unfairly criticizing D&D in his absence, I'm exercising caution.

    If you're simply about criticizing D&D, there are literally hundreds of valid faults with the system -- please choose one of those to vent your frustrations on. If you genuinely have no exposure to D&D and want to know if two old-ish convention modules are representative of the typical D&D dungeon crawl. . . well. . . I think I've already answered that question (to be clear, though, no -- such modules are not representative of the typical D&D dungeon crawl).

    [Edit: Incidentally, if you're genuinely this unfamiliar with the classic dungeon crawl milieu, I respectfully suggest that you may want to become familiar with it before you attempt to emulate it.]

      \nYes, those are tournament modules, but... if you're going to suggest that somehow the Village of Omlette is somehow supportive of roleplaying, all I can do is to give you the recapitulation of what my players did with it. They killed everyone in the Omlette. Why? Because, well, while the module didn't tell you what the NPCs were like at all, other than their class and level and hits, it did tell you those things, and, it told you how much treasure each NPC had. So... it's an outdoor dungeon, right? We just kill everyrthing like any other dungeon, right?
      \n
      \nNo, really. OK, we're playing AD&D, and the characters have alignments like lawful good. The first time they attack a villager, I get a sinking feeling in the pit of my stomach... this isn't how this is supposed to go, is it? I stop and ask the players... isn't it kinda evil to do this? They look at me with blank stares. And then decide to do it anyhow.
      \n
      \nWhy? Because they had absolutely no other idea of how to interact with the game world. Talk to an NPC? Characters can talk? Where does it say that? Huh. Do you get EXP for talking to them?
      \n
      \nYes, I'd played wargames before this... got my start in "adventure gaming" with Panzerblitz, in fact, at age 6. But, again, I as GM knew that there was something wrong. The other players, most of them had NEVER played a wargame. They were simply responding to looking at their character abilities, how they had played D&D before (if at all), and made the assumption that the only way a town would be any fun is if they just looked at it as just another module. In fact, it's in one of them there moduly folders. Must be a dungeon.
      \n
      \nLet me put it this way: all of the content of the module is wargaming notes, except for a few that speak about who knows where the "real dungeon" is outside of town and slim facts like that. Why do I need to know Ostler Grundigoot's level? What's the purpose of it being in the notes? Well, it's there "in case" he ends up in a fight somehow with somebody. I mean we have to cover that potential possibility, right? That's what the D&D mechanics do...
      \n
      \nForget the Hamlet of Hommlet. Let's flash forward to the 90s. I purchase an Al Quadim module, actually part of a boxed set. Ah... Arabian Nights... look at the beautiful and evocative artwork. Surely within this box is setting information that's sure to be good as the start of stories. Right? The "Module" within has a classic map layout, like Hommlet. That is, you learn about the place by looking at the map, wondering what that building is, and reading the numbred entry. In which you get the combat stats.
      \n
      \nOh, but wait, there's more! This has "roleplaying" in it. That is, obviously we're not meant to kill of the encounters. Well, sure, thre's the random demon here and there sprinkled in just so that we won't go too long without a combat, but most of the numbered encounters are listed like,
      \n
      \n1. PCs talk to dude. He sends them over to other dude at 2.
      \n2. This dude tells them that he needs them to guard caravan. If the PCs say yes, then he directs them to location 3 to get equipped. If they say no, then screw the players for being uncooperative bastards.
      \n
      \nOh, yes, we're no longer in the dungeon. But it's just as scripted as any tournament module.
      \n
      \nAre there exceptions? I'm sure there are. But, again, they just prove the rule. There is no comparison between an Al Quadim module and, say, the Delta Green sourcebook. The former gives you a script to play through. The latter gives you ideas from which you can form play in any way that you like.
      \n
      \nDungeons are easy - following a script makes it rather foolproof. But that doesn't mean that it's good for other forms of RPG play.
      \n
      \n
      \nBrand... that's poetic. My play started going south with D&D in 1980. All my fault, really, I just could no longer stomach another "Massacre at Hommlet." I wasn't able to get the idea across of how I thought play was supposed to go. I'm sure folks will blame it on the fact that I was only 12 years old at the time, and playing with other kids. But I knew what the problem was, it was the dang system. I tried to fix it. Didn't work. I tried using other systems. Didn't work. I created my first homebrew system at age 14. That didn't solve the problems. I finally decided that it must be the fault of the players. They just didn't get it. Nothing I do with the system can make it realistic enough to make the players have their characters act realistically.
      \n
      \nYep, that's what I thought the solution was. Rolemaster. That's so realistic and dangerous, that players can't do things like leap off of cliffs knowing it won't do enough damage to kill them (they could roll a high crit and die!). If the dangers are real, then they'll FEEL their character's plights, and, well, have the chracters act like it.
      \n
      \nNope. I recall one situation in which the players encountered a new PC, and immediately adopted him. "Why?" I asked. "Because he's got three dimensions. He's obviously not an NPC." This is 1994 now. I call them to task for playing with player knowledge that the characters do not have. So the next time they encounter another PC, what do they do? They kill him. "Why?" I ask. "Because he was acting suspiciously. It was the realistic thing to do."
      \n
      \nSo I almost gave up on RPGs. I blamed myself. I am a bad GM. I do not make the world realistic enough, so the players don't get their characters. And I blamed them... they should try harder!
      \n
      \nAnd then I found out that, know what? Modules inform play a lot! If you present "dungeons" to players, and killing is what play is about, then they'll play to win, and kill everything.
      \n
      \nBut wait!... if you play "situations..." Ones that center around the characters in play?

That's not me saying people shouldn't add things to RPGs, and play the way they want to play, they should. It's just that I can't credit a module for what you add to play that it does not show you how to add to play. I credit you.
      \nI am talking as a creative DM in here. By allowing you a lot of room for change and adaptation, I get to use that dungeon for whatever story I have in mind. I might use the map and the baddies, but I don't have to break the pacing of my own story to include it in my game. I do not change the system a bit. I add to the system (the roleplaying and storytelling factors). And the rules give me room for doing just that.

      I differentiate games like monopoly from games like D&D. Sure you could add roleplaying aspects to monopoly. But you'd have to change the way rules work. (I'm talking about freedom in here. As in "what if my character would prefer to walk the opposite way around?".) And in D&D some rules might not exist, but you are allowed to add them, and they don't change the system a bit. They only change the way you play it.

      I prefer limits to prohibitions. "Yes, but..." to "No way, you can't". Sure my guy can jump from the highest tower. I am allowed to. I would probably get critically wounded and probably die by doing so, but there is no "you can't jump" rule. (BTW, that is why I prefer D&D 3.x to AD&D 2nd.)

      Now, I am not saying that empty modules where the DM has to do all the work are good. I'm just saying that I personally prefer those. (I did use the word "personally".)

      \nAs someone who started out with blue-book basic in 1981, here's my take on this stuff.
      \n
      \nMy brother and I and what became our extended gaming group came to D&D; with The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings (and maybe Riddlemaster of Hed and/or King Arthur books) under our belts. We were around 10.
      \n
      \nCharacters were obviously people, from the beginning. But the interesting parts of their lives were the Adventure. Think the kid at the beginning of The Princess Bride. "Is this a kissing book?" "Killed by Pirates is good!"
      \n
      \nD&D; of that era was an incomplete framework. There is the game as it is written, and there is the game as it was played. These are not the same things for most gamers of that era -- some people played the letter of the rules, most did not. Some groups started exclusively with "alright, you start at a set of steps, leading down", while others started with you all the way back in town at The Inn(tm). Some people had their dungeons in a world -- but they didn't start there, unless they were joining a pre-existing group.
      \n
      \nNo, what happened was you started with the rulebook. And an intro adventure/module/dungeon.
      \n
      \nThen, you had played it. You could make your own (if, say, your dad was a Civil Engineer and had graph paper handy), because it was easy. You draw a map. Then you put monsters in it. Then you start playing.
      \n
      \nNow, once you'd made your own adventures, if you got a new module, it got tacked on. But, somewhere in here, you bought an issue of Dragon. Or White Dwarf. Or you got the Greyhawk module. Oh, look. You draw a *bigger* map and put all the dungeons onto that map.
      \n
      \nAnd we knew about those maps, too, from LotR. So that was easy, too. We could do that.
      \n
      \nWell, maps had towns.
      \n
      \nIf you joined a group somewhere on this trajectory, you learned about towns and cities and everything, and then were surprised when you read the rules and didn't see all that.
      \n
      \nIf you were starting with a group all of whom were 'virgin' to gaming -- which is what happened to me -- you went through some or all of this evolution collectively, slowly accreting technique and ideas, with occasional injections from the wider culture (ie, magazines & modules) or from other gamers you met -- new kids in school who moved up from the big city, or whatever.
      \n
      \nAnd, in the early 80s, there were already other games. RuneQuest. Traveller. Tunnels & Trolls. Champions. Gamma World. All with slightly different ideas of what a character was, what an "adventure module" looked like, and with products which contained all those slightly different ideas.
      \n
      \nPlus we started devouring fantasy books, if we weren't already. Plus we went and saw The Dark Crystal or Time Bandits or whatever, and found more raw material.
      \n
      \nAll of those different sources of ideas cross-polinated with D&D;, at least in my group.
      \n
      \nThe game, the modules, and the idea of dungeon crawling itself, were all sort of frameworks on which to get together and make stuff up. Everybody in the group DMed. We'd rotate. "Ooo, I had a great idea for a dungeon," and off you'd go to map it.
      \n
      \nIn my experience, most gaming groups of that early "second gen" era were a mix of this sort of slow cross-polination and internal creativity, and the internal group collective idea of what the game was trumped what the rules said it was in a curious way.
      \n
      \nWe started with the game as written, but slowly drifted off, finding our own collective manner of play, using bits of skeletons from whatever we had available that appealed to us.
      \n
      \nShrug.
      \n
      \nThe dungeon module, in my gaming group, was there to supplement the group, not to define the experience. IMHO.

      \n I think that Traveller is interesting when looking at how we moved beyond dungeons. Traveller is interesting because as a SF game it would seem to have less affinity initially to the idea of the dungeon: where was our Moria for SF to use as inspiration. But go and look at some of the earliest Traveller modules out there like Shadows/Annic Nova and you will see that they are dungeons. The dungeon format is preserved through the expedient of the exploration of alien ruins. Later modules are often little more than the descriptions of starships, The Kinunir, and bases, Research Station Gamma and Twilight's Peak, and reasons for the player's to go there. You might see this as the Alien mode of play (where both movies are effectively SF dungeon crawls). A lot of play focused on this 'explorer' trope.

      Twilight's Peak was interesting because it picked up on the tramp freighter mode of play. You have a ship but meeting the payments is not easy so you need to take on other jobs to supplement the money you get from shipping passengers and goods. For me this changed with 76 patrons. This supplement was filled with proto-bangs. A situation, devoid of place and time, that could be thrust at the players. The dying man that thrusts the note into your hands, the kidnapping on the steps of your hotel. Most had one or two suggested plots behind the events but the play style was all about improvisation for many groups. I guess that these 'patron encounters' became the equivalent of D&D's 'town' play often taking place in Starttown, the ubiquitous Mos Eisley style settlement, surrounding a starport.

      A lot of these games saw the players on very gray sides of the law as mercenaries or smugglers. This is what I would call the Firefly era of Traveller games, because play tended to mirror an episode of that series, with attempts to make ends meet constantly jeopardized by double-cross or bad intelligence. Other strands were straight military adventures as mercenaries for the wargamers;a whole strand of Traveller play revolved around mercs. For many groups though the star mercs route was often just one type of job that you might pick up to help pay the bills.

      \n Sorry, I wasn't clear. GURPS gives you tools to build everything, and tells you that you should or can build everything for your game. The D&D stuff I've seen, and Dana's description, do not present tools for building stuff, and do not even tell you that you can or should build stuff -- but players, it seems, end up 'building' stuff with house rules to cover the gaps.

      D&D: Here is a monster and a sword. Have an adventure!
      GURPS: Here's how to build a monster, here's how to build a sword, here's how to build an adventure, here's how to build a planet, here's how to build vehicles... (and so on) ... Have an adventure!

      Note I'm not at all trying to say one is better than the other; just different approaches.
