  • sben
    April 2006
    * Everybody starts with a stack of coins of the same size.

    * In turn, each person is asked to narrate a ridiculous story. ("Tell me, Baron, of the time you escaped from the Russian Army, and how you ended up with both the Tsar's daughter and his bedpan.")

    * Anyone can interrupt the story with a challenge; to do so, they place one of their coins in the pot. ("But Baron, I thought the Tsar's daughter was kept locked in an impregnable part of the Kremlin.") The storyteller can either work that into the story ("Of course" or "Now that you mention it, I do recall" or the like), taking the coin, or can reject it ("You must have misremembered"), bidding a coin of his own. If the storyteller eventually accepts, he takes the coin(s); if the challenger backs down, they go to the challenger.

    * Challenges cannot result in the storyteller being dead, as that is obviously false, nor (I think) can anyone be accused of lying, as that is not genteel.

    * If I remember correctly, at the end, people give their coins as they see fit to the best storyteller. The one with the most coins is then the winner, and must buy the next round of drinks.

    I've played it only once. Bad thing: One of the players kind of "froze up", thinking too hard about making the story plausible, and that made it less fun than it should've been. (The rest of us didn't do a good job of getting him through his mental block, either. The game suggests throwing bread rolls at the storyteller. Seriously.) When the storytellers (including the one) were in a groove, though, it was pure fun.

    There's much more to it, and it's absolutely worth tracking down a copy. (Edit: "Much more to it" meaning ancillary fun; I think I've got most of the essence of the game above. Things like chargen (write your name on the paper; the rest is for taking down the names of attractive members of your preferred gender) and challenges if you feel you've been insulted (with sub-rulesets "Duelling" and "Duelling for Cowards").)

    A few more things...

    You get the initial phrases ("Baron, tell us about the time you...") out of a book (such as "...used a hot air ballon to fix the canons at Gibralter.") If I remember right, somebody else picks the phrase you have to use.

    Also, at the end of the game, everyone votes for who was the best storyteller---somebody other than yourself---and gives that person all the coins you acquired during the game. Whoever has the most after the voting "wins."
