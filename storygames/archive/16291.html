\n I'm still recovering from PAX East, exhausted but excited about all the great stuff that was going on. Most of my time was spent in Games on Demand, which was packed from about mid-day on Friday through Saturday night. I missed Sunday, but I'm assuming it was just as popular that day as well. The Tabletop area was an airplane hangar full of gaming tables, 95% of which were covered in card and board games. The PAX crowd is hungry for fun and generally completely inexperienced with tabletop RPGs, a tragic wrong we tried to right.

So that was all I saw, with just a brief tour through the exhibit hall. I hear there were some great panels or shows. What was your PAX East experience like?

    \n So much to say...

    ...but I need time to recover!


    I GMed for 60 people!

    Terry hosted Games on Demand all of PAX and spoke to easily over 300+ attendees.

    I spoke on the "How to Start Playing D&D" panel which had 500+ attendees. It was an amazing panel and all of PAX people kept stopping me to tell me how much they enjoyed it!


    Lots of ups and downs but mostly ups.


    My top favorite parts of PAX:

    1. Meeting and hanging out with Will Hindmarch.
    2. Spending more time with Steve Segedy.
    3. The "How to Start Playing D&D" panel.
    4. Running Mouse Guard games for mostly female players.
    5. Running Marvel for 8-11 year old boys and girls.
    6. The diverse crowds. People from Russia, Canada, Korea, Japan.
    7. PAX attendees kept thanking us for running Games on Demand. We heard everything from, "I don't know what this is but I want it" to "how are you guys doing this, this is so amazing" to "we were playing magic but you guys were laughing and smiling and jumping around... we had to know why you were having so much fun" to "this year's PAX is better than last year because of you guys".


    There were also downs.

    Anime Boston running at the same time as PAX took away a significant younger crowd. PAX attendance wasn't hurt and seemed bigger than last year but I felt the audience was a little older. Marvel overwhelmed GoD. It felt like we were turned into the official "learn how to play Marvel" destination which strained our GM, table, and personal financial resources (at some point I had to go buy more dice and make more copies of the materials). On Saturday we had 9 concurrent games going, all tables were filled up, and we had to turn people away. And because we were doing this out of love, we had very limited financial resources to meet the overwhelming demand. Attendees complained we didn't advertise the event, have fancy signs and banners, or provide more materials for games but meanwhile many of us were spending our own personal money to make this all happen. But on the flipside, when many attendees realized we were doing this for them, they were shocked how giving and how much work we were doing to entertain them. We even had D&D DMs tell us they wanted to DM for us next year because of how friendly and close we all were. I do wish we ran more indie games. It seemed like we had to change the model from "tell us what you want to play" to "were running Dread anytime between 2-6pm, come if you are curious, even just to watch" to make room for less known games.

    Last year, I didn't have a good time at PAX. This year, I had a good time.

    But a lot of that good time was because people like Steve, Brennan, Terry, Will, Jim, and many others worked their asses off to make GoD happen. The weekend before PAX Terry was at a Vegetarian Convention in Austin Texas and as part of her day job she ran seminars for hundreds of people, made Tamale food samples for 200+ people, and yet\u2026 she worked harder at GoD!

    I think right now there is a bit of a disconnect. It seems like PAX expects retailers and large companies to provide RPG entertainment in the Table Top area. Where it's GMs who bring the entertainment. Although to be fair, this is my complaint at many cons and why I try to treat the GMs at our local events as nicely as possible (and we're a non-profit). Games on Demand is starting to change this at PAX but it's because of wonderful people like Luke and Steve.

    \n The 14 hours I put in on Saturday seemed like good prep for running GoD at Origins this year. : )

    I also ponied up some money to print out a lot of materials and buy extra dice to have them available to use. I didn't get to actually run 3:16, for instance, but since a game of it got going as I was leaving, I left behind my rulebook, all my sheets and trooper standees and they got used. Will and Steve buying me drinks on Saturday evened that out.

    I think it's safe to say Fiasco was actually our most-requested and most-run game, with Marvel a reasonably close second. Steve speaks the truth: we just put up whatever anyone said they were willing to run, so we got a full range of stuff including more 'trad' stuff like Marvel, CthulhuTech, and Eclipse Phase, to the definitively-Indie; my 2.5 hour demo of Apocalypse World went off on Sunday to an enthusiastic response.

    I think that it's worth remembering that PAX is a bit of a different beast than GneCon or Origins: we did not emphasize 'Indie' Games on Demand, and I think that's the right way to go for that crowd. At GenCon or Origins, it makes sense to aggressively brand what we're doing as cutting-edge and outside the normal range of what people expect. At PAX, everything is new to most everyone, and we also needed every GM we could get once things started heating up, so as John says, we can emphasize certain games, but I think it'd make little sense to turn away effective ambassadors for gaming because they're not passing the Indie Litmus Test. The devil's advocate argument I'd make is that if people are really just interested in Marvel, it's better that one of us teaches it to them, so that if they have fun at our games, maybe they come back next year and go "We had fun in that guy's game, what's he running this year?" and we can ease them into some of the more adventurous Story Game content.

    On a personal note, it was great to see everyone, and I met a bunch of people I hope to get a chance to play with in the future. Will and Steve and John and Terry and everyone else, thanks!

    (And, if anyone reading all of this is jealous and wants in, it's never too late to stop by and see us at Origins this year, even if just to say hello!)

    Posted By: jenskotOn Sunday morning a 10 year old (with his parents behind him) asked to play a non-violent game. In a pinch we didn't know which game to run, we figured Fiasco might work. Instead\u2026 somehow\u2026 one of our GMs co-designed two RPGs with and for them!
    \nThis was definitely a highlight for me! The 10-year old interrupted my admittedly not-very-smooth pitch for "soft" Fiasco and told me "one of us" should have made a game about Sun Tzu (as he had just finished reading "The Art of War"). He then started to explain to me exactly what that game would look like, at which point I suggested we all sit down at one of our tables and try to design the game. It was fun! He very quickly decided that you needed to be able to play generals from ALL eras ("Caesar, Charlemagne, and Patton!) and was very admamant that the game be as realistic as possible. He ended up deciding it should really be two games, though: an ancient version and a modern version that would be sold separately, but that would be based on the same system so that Patton could go back in time and fight Caesar. (Among the rules he was sure his game needed: "you get +2 to your strength if you have the high ground".)

    My PAX East, in general:

    This was my first time at PAX. I spent most of my time running stuff at Games on Demand (much thanks to Steve, John, and Terry for helping make this all happen). I was mostly overwhelmed by my few forays into the video game expo area. But I was psyched by everything that was going on in the tabletop hall. It was just great to see so much enthusiasm for playing tons of different games with tons of different people. I'm more used to the vibe at comic book conventions, which feel a lot less social and much more focused on commerce. (Even the retail booths in the tabletop area at PAX seemed as much focused on selling/providing/supporting a social experience as they did on selling a product.) By the end of the weekend I was simultaneously (a) a bit worn out and loopy from all the games I had run/played and (b) wanting more time to play more games.

    I was glad to be there with a purpose/job/etc., because without it I might have ended up overwhelmed and wandering around aimlessly, but I do wish I had been able to go to some of the panels. Still, I met lots of cool people with whom I had lots of fun talking about all sorts of games. It was especially meaningful to me that I got to introduce a bunch of people to RPGs/story-games, and was told (a bunch of times) that they didn't know RPGs were this much fun. A couple of times a new player would start the game telling me that they weren't good at telling stories or that they could never act stuff out in character, but by the end they were completely caught up in it. Overall, a great weekend!

    \n Bob, my sense is that GoD started as an initiative of the indie games community (Kathryn Miller originally organized it, yeah?), as a way to promote and run our games at the con in a longer-than-demo format. We've never had to be super restructive about what games people ran, I don't think, because (1) it was mostly indie stuff, (2) there's typically enough space, and (3) defining "indie" is tough and often stupid. Mouse Guard is just as troublesome as Marvel in that regard, right? I agree that it's one of the best formats for running ANY game, and somebody should definitely run pick-up D&D that way for folks new to the hobby, for sure. But if GoD stops being the go-to place for running and playing indie games, because they're overwhelmed by other stuff, then indie games will have to do something else. That's fine, things adapt and change, and we're nothing if not flexible, but it's good to be aware and intentional about such changes, as much as possible. That's my only concern here. Obviously people having fun playing games is never a bad thing.
