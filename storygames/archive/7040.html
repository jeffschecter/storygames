\n In this episode I talk with Ben Lehman of These Are Our Games (aka TAO games). We touch on his game about the lives of microcelebrity extreme athletes, XXXXtreme Street Luge, while conducting a wider-ranging discussion about microcelebrity of another kind, playtesting, ashcanning, and "what's wrong with the indie game design community, and what's always been wrong with the indie game design community." Warning, this one gets a mite prickly.

    \n Great interview. This is now my favorite podcast. Made the commute to work pleasurable. In my dream world, Rob would have a new episode daily!

    Ben, thanks for being so blunt and honest.

    Regarding playstorming, my favorite approach is to:

    - describe the kind of fiction we want to create or immerse in
    - hand out a few examples of that fiction
    - ask people what kinds of things they see themselves doing with that fiction, if they were to watch a movie based on the game, what would it be like?
    - build rules to make those things happen

    It's definitely a starting point. Very much like an alpha playtest.

    \n I found the discussion of playstorming baffling. That is, I don't get why you'd publish the results of such a thing - you've just set up for a campaign for your group, as opposed to writing a new game, from my perspective. Playtest is rules-as-written, to me; it's frustrating largely because I know people are going to mod the fuck out of it, but by doing it that way, I reduce their workload.

    Basically, playstorming strikes me as play. Or really early and ugly design. But not as testing, in a meaningful sense. I may be confused about what it entails, though.

    ...

    The microfame bits were cool. I don't totally 100% agree, but the perspective was educational. Some small clicky bits. Therefore, I like.

    \n With Red Sky AM, I was doing what Ben described - playtesting my designed game, taking my lumps, not defending myself, then going away and working through the feedback critically for myself.

    Significantly, playstorming includes this last step. You still have to work through the feedback - well, it's not feedback, it's a mass of design ideas - but you still have to work through it critically for yourself. Discipline in the face of design peer-pressure is just as important to have if you're a playstormer as if you're me or Ben.

    Here's how I've used playstorming.

    \n

    I have this idea for a game, Xenon: Alien Science Fiction. It's been rattling around my head. I want it to be like Wayne Barlowe's Expedition or After Man. I want it to be an aesthetic experience, creating this place with geology and wildlife and cultures, and I want the pieces of it to be compatible with Shock:

    \n

    So I sit down with these fine folks and I say what I'm trying to do, and I set up some stuff that I hadn't put into words before, and we play until it sucks. Then we find a way to address the suck. In the end, what I've got is a rough but functioning sketch of the game. Now I can get down to serious designing. I can figure out the things that the game doesn't do that it ought to, I can trim the fat, I can look at the assumptions I was making. Now I can make a first playtest draft, then play it and subject it to critique.

    \n

    It's a lot more fun than just, you know, guessing. But Ben's right: you have to know how your mechanics work. If something happens one time in a hundred, you're probably not going to find it by playtesting, but you're going to get crabby emails all the time if you publish like that. Know the statistics of your randomness. Look for systems with positive feedback loops that make winners win more or losers lose more and make sure those are the things you want. But when you want to figure out how you want the experience to go in the first place, Playstorming is a great technique.

    \n

    Also, it doesn't require sharing, just regular critique listening. The desired outcome is not fun play, but valuable feedback that tells you where the fun is and isn't. It shows you where to look and what to work on.
