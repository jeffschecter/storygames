\n Has anyone else noticed this? A lot of story games are really paper heavy: you need to keep marking things off, or checking what kind of scene you can play, or moving tokens around, or playing cards, or even have little scraps of paper defining everyone's relationships with each other.

I didn't think much about it when I was mostly playing these kinds of games at cons, which are always around a table; but my long term group is taking a break between campaigns (*) by playing things like Monster of the Week and Fiasco, and I've noticed that it really changes the vibe of the game from people lounging on couches and armchairs and keeping the game mostly in our heads, to needing to have your character sheet and a pencil with you, or needing to be sitting around a table (in our case, small and crowded). It's interesting.

* (The system we're mostly using for campaigns is a very stripped down version of Fate: characters start life mostly as a name and a few key aspects, and dice rolls generally work out as high, so-so or bad. Fate points... we have an interesting attitude to fate points. We both think that they're really important, but also don't bother tracking them. So our GM would try to bribe us into behaviour that would get us into trouble, or we'd influence a roll, but only when we really cared about it.)

    Posted By: KayfallBy the way, can you say more about your Fate variant? How can fate points be important if you're not tracking them? Do you mean that you just try to remember them in your mind without writing them down or counting chips or something? in other words, tracking them with thoughts rather than paper or other physical things?
    It's mostly that the group of people we have are quite likely to do things regardless of our character's best interests, because that's what the character's drives are - our GM hasn't needed to invoke aspects against us very often. And fate points ended up more as a... signifier, I guess? If I'm willing to spend a fate point on a roll, then it's because it's very important to me, but I'm not writing down how many fate points I've got, because I can't be bothered with the paperwork, and I trust the GM and the rest of the group not to take the piss about it. And when fate points come in at all, it's mostly because someone wants to reinvoke an aspect, rather than bumping up a dice roll directly. I think we kind of drifted into it, rather than having a conscious discussion of rules, although it means when we're looking for replacement players we need to be a bit careful about how their personality will fit in.
