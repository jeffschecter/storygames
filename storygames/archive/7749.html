\n Hey Story Games!

We're selling through the first edition of Grey Ranks and getting ready for a reprint. In the tradition of Matt Wilson, I'd like to know what ought to be changed, so I'm asking. I already know we're changing:

* The cover
* The map errors that cause paroxysms of mirth for our customers in central Europe
* Some of the situation elements that never get picked (who thought those up?)
* Some verbiage about the whole big die = failure, small die = success thing, which throws people all the time
* Some verbiage about playing the first few chapters
* One or two typographical errors and one layout error

What else? What needs better explanation, or more discussion, or more precision, or whatever? What's missing? What would be helpful, and what should be neatly excised and never mentioned again? Lay it on me, and thanks!

    Some verbiage as to what kind of material is appropriate for a Mission Scene vs. a Personal Scene. Particularly in regards to what makes a good goal in a Personal Scene and what Success/Failure in a Mission Scene refers to.

    I say this because something our group did is something that I don't think is actually in the text. When we framed a Grey Rank into a Mission Scene we also gave them an explicit objective that was a submission. So if the whole Mission was about sabotaging a power plant then when we framed mission scene we'd say something like, "Okay, Grom you have to go get us the blue prints to the power plant." And then the die contributed determined the success or failure of that specific sub-mission.

    But again I don't think that's in the text. The text is a bit murky and says "other players frame mission scenes" and the die contributed determines if the character will succeed or fail.... but I'm not sure it ever says, "at what."

    \n One piece of advice that you may want to put in the book: the person who frames a scene doesn't define the conflict.

    We found this out with the personal scenes in the GR game we're playing at the moment. Quite a few of the personal scenes were falling flat and the players were very happy to fail the scenes to avoid a corner square on the grid. A bit of discussion and we quickly realised that we were falling victim to the Czege Principle: the player was framing the scene, specifying the conflict, and then resolving it. We changed our procedures of play so that while a player frames their own personal scene, they don't say anything about the conflict; we discover what the conflict is during play in that scene.

    We quickly generated a bunch of scenes that were much more compelling and tense.

    Oh, another thing! As part of chargen, you say that each player should describe another NPC and write them down on a card. It would be a helpful prod to suggest that people do the same thing with all the other NPCs that get generated in play: write down their name, description, and Reputation on a card. It helps reincorporation during the game.

    Neil.
