    \n From experience, it's VERY important to use maps when you're doing a story game online - and I mean the kind of maps you wouldn't be using if you gamed in person. We create shared spaces with body language so when we don't have body language to fall back on it can get confusing quickly. Since the environments we use are so low-definition you can get a lot of mileage out of a map even if it's only vaguely suitable.

    I also recommend using GOOD maps, not crummy ones. A good map with detail and style gives everyone something to work with in a scene. It also gives an interesting visual for people who aren't in the scene at the moment.

    For example, this map:



    is a good set. It gives everyone something to work with. We did 4 scenes in our game using that map and I honestly got a bit of a sense of "shakespeare in the park" - because the set had just enough detail to provide 4 different "stage areas" that supported the game very nicely.

    \n Well, as an example from last night, I had a scene in the wintry keep - a PC wizardess was at a small fire in front of the idol there and an NPC sorcerer entered the scene from the outside. The sorcerer was clearly up to no good, started talking about how she had been weak and foolish, and as I was talking I moved the sorcerer bit by bit closer to the wizardess. My volume also increased at the same time.

    Another example was a character who was running away from a pack of dog-like demons. He was knocked to the ground, so I moved his mini a couple of squares and circled him with the pack. Tension raised.
