\n Judging by the Xbox 360 gamertag thread, there's a fair amount of people here with 360's, and given the recent thread on Spore, I thought I'd bring up Braid, a game that in some ways come closer to story games then any other I've played on a console.

On the pro side: the mechanics actually communicate ideas and meaning

On the con side: the player's agency is just to move through the game, you have no way of effecting any significant outcomes

The thing that most struck me (beyond the beautiful presentation) was the way the mechanics were as integral to the theme as the story. Since playing it, I've found myself more then a few times when thinking about the past or memories Braid has popped into my head with it's themes as a way of looking at how people interact with their past (or something like that).

So who else has played it? What did you think?

    \n I played a little of Braid, but by the second stage I wasn't having fun due to the level of platforming skill involved. The problem with using the ability to rewind time as a puzzle mechanic means that it's no longer available to buffer less-skilled players against their mistakes.

    I did watch my husband play some of it, helping out with the puzzles and sometimes dispensing clues after looking up a walkthrough. The text vignettes I found okay, but not great.

    The stars are a super-sekrit thing where if you get all of them, the ending of the game changes. After finding out that one of them is impossible to get if you complete the game normally, my husband and I went online and read the text of the revealed ending.

    \n My main peeve is that (like many other console games*) it doesn't frakking tell you what all the controls are/do. My couple puzzles that I was stuck on up above? I saw a reference to speeding up the rate of time reversal, and I was all "what the hell?" and experimented with the controller until I could make that happen. Suddenly, those impossible puzzles were easy, and now I'm stuck again farther along, and wondering what other buttons I haven't been told about.

    Games that do this piss me off. It's like "you're not really good at the game until you find all the little secret tricks I'm not telling you!" Fuck that. You need to practice driving a whole lot before you're a good driver, but no one in their right mind pulls crap like not telling you what all the controls do.

    Give me all the controls and let the learning curve be inside the gameplay, not outside the box with hidden button combos.


    James


    *fighty games especially. AABBX-down-left-down-AA-up-down-up killer combo crap.

    As for the controls, after the initial stage that points out the basics (jump, climb), everything else has contextual triggers. When you die, it shows you the X button so you can explore it's function. If you hold X long enough, it brings up contextual information for the left and right bumpers.
