\n There's been some challenging threads here lately, initiated by people not "in the know" of how to think and talk on Story Games. They've had a mixed reception, a lot of the people here barking at high post-speed about how wrong the new posters are.

There might be a strong convention of ideas developing here, that makes it very hard for people to enter with different ideas, or different points of view. Some new posters have reacted very strongly to the way we have treated their shared thoughts.

I have reacted myself, to the way a lot of debaters have worked themselves up, insisting on reading me wrong, even when they have been corrected. It is silly, and moot, to discuss any serious issue with people not able to keep a clear head, and not willing to discuss the real meanings of what we present here. Not all have done this, but I've seen reasonable voices go silent in some threads, and suspect that they may have shut up due to others actively misinterpreting my meanings, making them quite difficult to swallow. I hate it when a barking pack of hounds take over a thread.

I know that I have my share of "peculiar" ideas, with my own philosophy and a particular terminology, on role-playing games. Being such a brave knight of my own philosophy, and so open with my experiences, I have come to expect some heated discussions and silly misunderstandings in my threads. What I do not expect, is for me to be blamed with the misunderstandings of others, and to be held responsible for their lack of manners.

To have my ideas called "toxic", on the basis of assumptions made by the poster, as happened in a debate recently, really test my patience. I managed to keep my tongue. But to see that the poster gets away with such language, seemingly with no repercussions, feels wrong for me. It sends a message to other posters, that this kind of language is permitted, and this level of discussion is ok. It is not.

If I were to invent meanings, putting words in the mouth of others, and then go on to be seriously offended by them, shouting my head off, I would surely become a very engaged, and utterly silly, man. Pray I don't do so, ever.

I do believe that this forum, as most others dedicated to open discussions, would be better off welcoming alternate points of view with more open curiosity and constructive criticism. There has been a long tradition of doing exactly that in this forum, and that is one of the reasons I've stayed here so long. I have had much use of the discussions here, and would be sad to leave. But recent discussions seems to indicate that this forum is sliding towards bad practice, leaving me and others in a soggy field of time-consuming nonsense.

So, in earnest, I ask you this;
- is this forum in the process of developing a culture for bad discussion techniques?
- if it is; how can we turn the trend? Start anew? Get over it?

    \nAs Hans says, this seems to be a normal part of how discussion cultures develop. Something to do with prisoner's dilemmas and short vs. long term advantages, I'd hazard; individuals gain short term advantages such as satisfaction from inconsiderate and uncivilized behaviors, and pave the way for further deterioration at the same time by showing a bad example to others.\n
    - if it is; how can we turn the trend? Start anew? Get over it?
    \nThe only reliable solution I've seen is ethos-based: when a widely consensual ethos is enforced by eternal vigilance, deterioration in social standards cannot proceed. Act the way you wish others to act, don't hesitate to speak against wrongs, that sort of thing. Old-fashioned leadership, in other words: if others respect your ethos and decide to follow it, you can maintain an entire community that way. Force-based authority doesn't really fundamentally help you be a better person, despite the amount of energy people put into fiddling with moderation rules of web communities. In this regard the Story Games moderation is pretty ideal, but maybe the community lacks in shared ethos a bit now and then. This is not surprising, considering how the Story Games community is relatively plural and egalitarian compared to eg. The Forge, one of my favourite web communities.

    \n Let's talk about procedures to avoid more roadkill.

    There is the ethos police (official or unofficial): speaking out (privately or publicly) when the ethos is violated. I have seen this in practice and while it works it feels very unwelcoming to new members.

    Then there is deflection: you just leave the thread, stop replying and maybe start a separate one. This can be done silently or with some protocol that shows why you did it. As a protocol, it must be accepted graciously by both parties. People will decide on which thread keep commenting.

    Personally, if I feel attacked on an internet forum I just stop replying, as I do not want my name to be associated with any form of trolling or public misbehaviour. I will just cut my losses and let the others have their fight. Really, there are no good reasons to fight to be right on the internet.

    Posted By: TomasHVM- is this forum in the process of developing a culture for bad discussion techniques?
    - if it is; how can we turn the trend? Start anew? Get over it?
    \nHere are techniques that can help combat a bad discussion before it starts\u2026

    - Be as clear as possible. What do you want? What don't you want?

    - Be as transparent as possible. Say your reasons for posting.

    - Be careful your subject doesn't undermine your intentions. It may seem counterintuitive, but many people react to subjects more than posts. Or a strong subject can cause people to misread posts. This is tricky because a strong provocative subject can cause more people to read a post. But at the same time, provocative can also derail the post itself. Try to be provocative without being personal.

    - Define loaded terms. If "immersion", "narrativism", "realism", "game", "toy", "freeform", "rules", "gns" and other loaded terms are important to your description, be clear what you mean by these terms. It can be frustrating. Why don't people simply infer my meaning via context? Some will. Many won't. Better to be safe. Tell us what you mean when you use these words. Better yet, avoid these types of words if you can. If you can't, define them.

    - Guide your thread. If someone takes your thread in a direction that distracts from the point of the thread, politely restate the goal of the thread and politely ask people to start new threads to discuss any tangential conversation.

    - Be polite. Use neutral language when discussing material that has a high percentage chance or history of being offensive.

    - Don't assume, ask questions. Don't put words in someone else's mouth. If you aren't sure what someone is saying or why, ask them.

    - Be brief or summarize. If you post is long, summarize it at the beginning and end.

    - Read Story Games' rules before posting.

    - Use the rules. If someone is causing problems in your thread after you asked them to stop, contact a moderator to intervene.

    - Re-read your post before posting. Roleplay it out! Imagine you are a community member reading your post. How will they react? Is there anything that is unclear? Is there anything people may react badly to? What can you rewrite to solve problems before they even start? Re-read. Edit. Then post.

    - Don't post out of anger. If you are angry, write down your post and save it. But don't post it. Give it a day. Then re-read your post. Are you still angry? If not, re-write it. Then post.

    - Don't feed the trolls. Some people who cause problems want attention. Don't give them attention.

    - Most trolls aren't trolls. Most problems are not because someone has a bad intent towards you. It is because there is some sort of misunderstanding. Which is why these techniques are so important. Work to reach mutual understanding.

    - Update your first post. Was there something in your first post that caused problems in the thread? Go back, edit the first post, and update it so that new people coming to the thread won't repeat the same mistakes. But state that your post has been edited, how it has been edited, and why.

    - Are you trying to win? Don't post. Forums are for discussions. When you want to start a new thread, you click "start a new discussion." If you don't actually want to have a discussion, don't start a thread. Instead write a blog post, facebook status update, google plus update, or twitter tweet. Here is my rule of thumb. If my goal is to convince you of something without learning anything myself, then it is probably not a great post for discussion.

    - Assume you are part of the problem first. If multiple people are reacting negatively to something you said, odds are, something you said is causing the reaction. That doesn't mean you are wrong, it doesn't mean you have a bad intent, it doesn't mean you are a bad person, and it doesn't mean no one else is at fault. Fault doesn't matter. Just fixing problems. What can you do differently?

    - It starts with you. Many of these techniques require that you do extra work. That can seem inefficient. Maybe it is inefficient. But your percentage chance of influencing your own actions is far greater than influencing someone else's actions. Let alone 100 other people's actions. Let alone strangers. Let alone strangers from different cultures with vastly different life experiences who speak different languages. It's more work, but if you focus on what you can do differently rather than what others can do, your chance of success is much higher.

    - If all else fails, learn from mistakes, and try again. If a thread is beyond saving, step back and ask yourself why. What went wrong? What can you do to stop those problems from happening again? Then try again. Start the same thread but incorporate solutions to prevent the same problems from happening.

    - Perception is more important than reality. It doesn't matter what you meant. Only what people think you mean. Which is 90% of the reason for the above techniques. These techniques help align perception with reality.

    Hope that helps!

    \n As a relative newcomer to this and other rpg forums, and therefore having seen this pattern developing in more than one place (it isn't just here, oh no - but I'm sure you knew that), I observe the following. Really unpleasant threads develop when people are talking at cross-purposes or plain disagree about something, but can't let it go, even after it becomes obvious that the thread has stopped being productive. When that happens the thread becomes unpleasant not just for the people involved in the argument, but for anyone else who might be thinking of contributing. I think this might be John's "are you trying to win" point, which strikes me as a really important one that lies at the root of most flamewars.

    The other side of this is people being dickish from the outset. I think it should be obvious to everyone that you should never be dickish to someone except in jest, they won't know it's in jest unless they know you quite well and (it being the internet) even then maybe not, and even if they do, others may assume you really are being dickish. The standing advice for internet-based writing is, don't say anything if it could be construed as dickish, even if you feel very strongly that it isn't. Posting on the internet is standing on top of a hill with a loudhailer broadcasting to the entire world: someone is going to misread what you're saying.

    Anyway, thanks Tomas for starting the thread. I think it's really worth any internet community periodically reminding itself of the need to be careful in internet discussions.
