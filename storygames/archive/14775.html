    \n Not really the best, but an interesting attempt was the Nevermore setting for True20. Yes, True20. When you were in Nevermore (the dream world), you had double conviction -- the currency used for rerolls, special powers, and dramatic editing -- and it was considerably more powerful. You could shape things out of raw dream stuff and change shape a bit and call for aid from thin air using dream conviction.

    Several of the PDQ games -- Dead Inside, The Zorcerer of Zo and Vox are at least somewhat surreal.

    \n Lend out my Great White Book? Or my almost-minty copy of Continuum? BLASPHEMY. Although a blasphemy that makes me intrigued as to the object of discussion, I'll admit.

    Back to the original topic, I don't know about "best", but I will admit I've always wanted to take Mage: The Awakening's Astral Realms supplement out for a spin. There's a couple of cool things going for it, like the fact that, despite sort of being an "inside your head" psychoplane, Mind magic isn't really that much better than all the others, and gradual broadening journey from "inside your head" to "inside humanity's head" to "inside the world's head". And tons of totally stealable tidbits, like Walkers (astral spirits embodying the public's perception of a famous person, as opposed to how they actually are) and the Clavicularius (mages who summon their own personal demons, literally) and little things like that.

    \n Actually the best dreamy game I ever did was run using GURPS. No, I wouldn't do that now what with all the nifty indie tools at my disposal, but it was good. Everyone had a couple of psychic powers at feeble and extremely subtle levels when waking, and that same stuff got magnified times umpteen when they entered the collective dream-world, accessible through people's individual dreams. The dream world was a reflection of the waking world, plus literal representation of a great deal of symbolic stuff. And lots of stuff that it would take too long to recount.

    Otherwise, I think my money is on Polaris. That felt like a weird, terrible, beautiful dream in play. There is nothing explicit about it being dreamy, but it had that vibe.

    Posted By: JohnstonePosted By: ccreitzDelta Green: Countdownhas rules for Dream Travel for use in Carcosa that are closer to useful, in my experience, but even they're not all that great.
    \nCould you possibly summarize them? I haven't kept up with Delta Green after the original book. Your loss, there. Countdown is the greatest game supplement supplement ever, and on the greatest-game-supplement-ever shortlist. Even if you don't get Targets or Eyes Only, you should get Countdown. Get it!

    Anyway... When you're in dim Carcosa, you keep track of your SAN loss separately from your usual SAN loss. It works like a magical skill, allowing you to get places and find things and people in Carcosa, regardless of normal causality; your group, if they agree about where they want to go, can roll the sum of their Carcosa-related SAN loss as a skill instead. If you don't roll, or you fail, you're at the GM's mercy.
