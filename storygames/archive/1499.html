My ideal game competition

Graham
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Play Advice
In my ideal gaming competition...

1. You'd use four random words to spark the contest, as with Game Chef and the Ronnies, because it's fun and stops you planning in advance.

2. Everything would be submitted in .txt format, so there was no doubt about whether good graphic design influenced the judging. All you'd see would be the game text. And ASCII art would be banned.

3. The judging rules would be published in advance, so you knew what you were designing for. I remember, say, Jason's Owlbear Challenge. I decided, as an exercise, to write a very simple text without pretty layout, to get myself out of the habit of using pretty fonts to spice the game up. Then I found the games were being judged on Beauty and, well, got knocked on it. That's not specific to the Owlbear thing, of course, but it's nice to know what you're being judged on.

4. There'd be a playtesting phase. We talk about playtesting all the time, and how important it is, so we should really shove it into the competitions somewhere. Perhaps you'd do a design, and then the designs would be randomly assigned for playtesting, and then revised.

5. While we're at it, all the games would have their roots in Actual Play in some sense. You'd design a game around something you'd seen in a play session: so, for example, I might design a game to try and recapture that feeling I'd had playing Paranoia as a teenager; you might design a game to play at those sessions when you've only got three people because everyone else cancelled. That kind of thing.

6. Everyone would be nice at all times. All reviews would be nice. Comments like "Well, I can't say I'm too impressed", "All the designer seems to have done..." or "This is just a mess" wouldn't happen. Constructive criticism would be encouraged - especially if it went through the game, point by point, and identified flaws - but subjective judgements wouldn't be. Unless they were nicely expressed: "This isn't my thing, but it's done very well."

7. Games would be marked on how good they could potentially be (after revision), rather than playability. One thing I worry about is that game competitions reward playable games developed in limited periods of time. And who develops playable games in limited periods of time? Well, experienced game designers, of course, or newcomers who use an established game model and tweak it a bit. My problem is, here, that all the competition entries I enjoy most are the ones that aren't quite playable but have fascinating parts to them; but the ones that win are solid, playable games.

8. There's also be a mark for how innovative the mechanics were. Because the games I like are the ones that take risks, even if they don't quite hang together: and it's sad that those games don't often get far in competitions.

8a. In fact, no, sod it, there's be no marks out of ten, because it's really quite sad to find a review which scores your game 2/10. Instead, games would be rated in terms of preferences. You'd divide the games up, and ask people: between Game A and Game B, which game would you prefer to win? Then you'd use those preferences to work out finalists and a winner.

9. Oh, and there'd be a dedicated forum, like with Game Chef, because it works really well and creates a community atmosphere.

10. In that dedicated forum, everyone would be nice and happy and would give detailed, incredibly useful feedback on each other's games.

11. Examples of play would be banned, because my examples of play always make my games seem much better than they actually are.

12. After a year, the competition would run again with the same entries, to see how they'd been revised after playtesting.

Most of this is bollocks, of course, and there are various things (like marking how good games could potentially be) that I don't entirely mean.

But it has its roots in a few worries about gaming competitions: not least that, while we talk about the importance of actual play and playtesting, competitions generally reward quick design without testing. Does it spark any thoughts? Am I talking out of my arse? Is there anything that you'd definitely put into / leave out of your ideal game competition?

Graham

    Oh, and since a lot of Graham's post was about the review phase of the contest, here's a good list of how to give good feedback:

    http://groups.google.com/group/rec.arts.int-fiction/msg/3334ebf88bfd1386

    In brief:
    1) Discuss the game, not the author.
    2) Be specific.
    3) Offer suggestions on how to fix things.
    4) Phrase your criticisms politely.
    5) Don't be afraid of criticising.
    6) Don't be afraid of praising.
    7) If you disagree with another poster, let the world know.
    8) Admit to your biases.
