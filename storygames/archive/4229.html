    \n I ask myself: do I want to keep the characters in the dark or do I want to pull interesting surprises for the players? If the former, I just continue running the game as normal and everybody keeps on watching the events unfold. Sometimes, the players not present will help me by running NPCs (friendlies and enemies) or provide input in some other way.

    If I want to keep the players in the dark for their later enjoyment of some plot revelation, I physically move away with the concerned player and I make sure it doesn't last long. But this is fairly uncommon.

    I tell all my players that if they ever need their characters to do something outside the group, they need to ask themselves the same damn question: should just the other characters not be aware of what's going on or will the ignorance of other players benefit our whole group experience at some point?

    More shortly: If you're cutting someone's game time, it should be to reward him/her later. (I'm not sure this makes sense but I can't elegantly put it down in English)

    \n I handle it by cutting between groups after any significant piece of action.

    So, to use a dirt-simple example, if you have one group going to town to get info from some guy and another group exploring a dungeon, you might let the dungeon group do one room and then flip back to the town group, let them discover where the guy is, cut back and let the dungeon group do another room, etc.

    For conflicts, jump cut between any significant unit, like rounds or whatever. One round of combat, some dialogue with the guy. Another round of combat, more dialogue. Etc.

    There are a lot of good examples in film as to what constitutes a good cue for jump cutting between sets of protagonists, but I can't really think of any off the top of my head. Anyone?

    \n Hi James,

    Here's me. I understand it might not work for you.

    1) 4 or less players

    2) I don't play with parties. Each character is interesting in his or her own right, and each is building a thread in the tapestry that is the whole story.

    3) Pick games that involve thematic material and hinge on interesting character choices instead of mechanical effects and results. (Examples: PtA, Sorcerer, Shock, Grey Ranks et al...) I ran a game of PtA at a con recently, and one of the players had only played D&D before this. After the game I asked him if he was bored during the scenes of another character -- did he have that impulse to go off and read a magazine. He said, "Well, at first I thought I'd be reading the Order of the Stick book I just bought downstairs, but everything was so interesting I just wanted to keep listening."

    Human choices are interesting to human beings. it tends to hold the attention of others even if their character is not in the scene. (People who haven't experienced this don't believe it's possible, but it's true.)

    4) Many of the games above, in one way or another, let a player mechanically influence a scene that 'belongs' to someone else. Fan Mail in PtA, Minutiae in Shock. Mechanics like this work really well for this issue.

    5) Cut across scenes frequently. Build to a moment of crisis or climax, and then shoot over to another player.

    How I play obviously isn't for everybody. But since I've dumped the whole concept of "the party" I'm getting the kind of play I want.

    \n This discussion fascinates me, because we're seeing several dichotomies in play style that dictate what will or won't work for your group for this "problem." A lot of it comes down to a divide between the people who think that "story-games" are indie-forgey games, and the other contingent are a set of newer people to this site who seem to feel that more traditional games are "story-games." While it's up to Andy, if anyone, to decide the focus of this site, I'm going to assume that we're being ecumenical, and that for the moment we'll include traditional RPGs here. But the readers should note that the dichotomy does exist, and that this informs the posts above.
    \n
    \n(I really can't emphasize how different these styles of play are. It's not just a cosmetic thing, it's fundamentally different. I wouldn't go so far as to call them unrelated activities, but I'd be tempted to do so just to get traditional players to understand the difference. )
    \n
    \nWhat it comes down to, largely (and Martin gets credit for being the first to identify it), is the question of what's sometimes called the "stance" of the player with regard to their character. "Actor" stance assumes that the player is making decisions on the part of the character based only on what the character knows. Often this is desired because of some factor like "immersion." The key technique here is to keep player and character knowledge unified, so that the player doesn't have to go through the mental gymnastics of trying to forget what they as a player know that the character does not.
    \n
    \nThis is as opposed to "Author" stance, where the player can be better thought of as the author of the character's story. As authors of characters in novels do, these players use their knowledge of things that the character does not know, in order to make play more interesting. Note that there is a subset of Author stance called "Pawn" stance, where the player has the character make decisions that are implausible - where the character acts with knowledge he does not have, and that only the player has. Much as one would do with a pawn in a boardgame. Most author stance play is still plausible, but still informed by things that the character does not know.
    \n
    \nHere's where I usually give an example to make this clear. Consider a situation in which Player A's character is getting beaten up in a park, and Player B's character is having a drink in a bar, not far off. The GM indicates that the bar is closing, and asks player B what his character is doing.
    \n - Actor Stance Player: "My character goes home."
    \nThe character doesn't know about the fight involving Player A's character, so the player cannot decide to have his character go help.
    \n
    \n - Pawn Stance Player: "My character goes and helps out his buddy!"
    \nThe player ignores the fact of his character's ignorance of the fight, and has his character respond as if he did.
    \n
    \n - Author Stance Player: "My character heads home, but on the way back decides to take a stroll in the park."
    \nThe player is acting to get his character into the fight to help his friend, but doing so in a plausible fasion.
    \n
    \nNow, the dichotomy that I mention largely splits down the line where the "indie-forgey" story-game players like myself will tend to prefer Author stance play. Wheras the indications of the more traditional players here is for actor stance play, and techniques that support that.
    \n
    \n
    \nNow, since this is James' thread, and he's obviously a more traditional gamer, this is why you get Chris saying things like "this might not work for you." What he's saying is that, barring a pretty radical change in play-style, our techniques won't work for you.
    \n
    \nSo, in a way, we're being subversive here. What we're saying is that one of the nifty benefits of our style of play is, quite simply, that problems like "splitting the party" simply vanish entirely. The concept of the "party" dissapears completely, and all play is, non-problematically, a series of scenes that involve whatever characters make sense to include.
    \n
    \nTo be clear, there's no attempt to "reimburse" players for them not having had the spotlight, because:
    \nA) They'll get it when it's their turn, and
    \nB) When it's not their turn, they're as engaged in play as when it is their turn. Different, but no less interesting.
    \n
    \nThis mode of play is more about being the "Audience" to the character's stories, than "being" the character. This is what, largely, is meant when people say "Story-games" intending it to be indicative of something different than traditional RPGs.
    \n
    \nNow, we can discuss the benefits of this mode of play til the cows come home, and it won't benefit James one bit if he's not interested in trying this mode of play. So, James? Do you want feedback on this sort of play? Or do you just want comments on the problem as it pertains to the traditional mode of play?
    \n
    \nIf the latter, I can at least explicate the problem in detail. The real issue at hand with "party" play is that you have two conflicting motives for the players:
    \n1) They want to all stay together so that they don't lose time playing, since, if they split up, they want to ignore (or even be separated from) the actions going on where their character is not.
    \n2) They want to play their character as they think their character would act.
    \n
    \nThis leads to really difficult situations, with different solutions. Many players just give up, and stop asking the question "Why is my character going along again?" Basically they either come up with excuses why their character is always with the other characters, or they simply move to pawn stance, and just don't bother to justify why their character is doing what it does. And Pawn stance itself can be frustrating. Especially this sort, where the player is basically giving up on being proactive at all, and simply following some other participant's lead (often the GMs).
    \n
    \nOther players, unwilling to give up control of their characters this way, will do things like split up the party, beause it's what they feel that the character would do. In fact, in really dysfunctional cases, the player may feel that they're not enjoying play, and simply seek to avoid control, falling into what is sometimes called "My Guy" behavior. Where the player justifies everything that they have the character do based on "it's what My Guy would do."
    \n
    \nEven in non-dysfunctional cases, then we have the issue as James presents it, where, because players are being plausible with their character's actions, the group splits up, and then everybody gets less enjoyable play time (though, that said, there's an argument against this, too, that says that parties already split up screen time, even when together).
    \n
    \nSo, again, there's this constant conflict of interest - the player wants control of his character, and, yet, the player wants to stick with the party.

    \nYeah, there are some simple solutions. One of them is the "Mission" approach - the concept is that the "party" is a team of some sort (maybe they work for the queen), and they are assigned missions to perform. This gives them strong excuses to stay together. The challenge in this case is that you have to work hard to get personal issues into play for each character (see Law & Order for how), or you have to ignore that entirely.
    \n
    \nAnother simple solution is just to accept pawn stance as the standard for play. A lot of entertaining dungeon crawling play does exactly this.
    \n
    \nBut in each case, you're losing something to gain "party" unity. If that something isn't important to you, then you have your solution. But for many groups, they want to have their cake and eat it, too.
    \n
    \n
    \nLikewise, the style of play that we (Chris, JBR, John Harper, myself, and, less so, Andy) espouse could be seen as giving up a little on "immersion" of the sort that requires separation of player and character knowledge (or, at least, investment in the knowledge that the character doesn't have). But that's not something we value, hence why we've moved in this particular direction - well one reason.
    \n
    \nI hope that helps shed some light on the nature of the problem. Understanding it should help in determining in which direction you'd like to go in obtaining your personal solution to the problem.

    \n Jason, good point. Some indie RPGs do more with this, some less, certainly. And it also could be argued that it has less to do with the designs in question, and more with the traditions of this group of players 'round here.
    \n
    \nBut if you look closely at DitV, there are some really subtle things going on with the mechanics, and not just in the textual exhortations (I, too, give little credence to the efficacy of such). Is "say yes, or roll the dice" a mechanic or a technique? Eh, I'm not too concerned. Let's look at how resolution works instead. The DitV mechanics subtextually say to the player, "You can feel free to push for what you're character believes in... but he'll pay the price." But look at that "price." What's subversive is that fallout is actually positive stuff for the character in some ways.
    \n
    \nThis sort of player informing by the mechanics (and I'm sure with more time I could think of better examples), does seem to produce the sort of play that our kind wants to see. The player is told that they're supposed to be interested in their character's failure... like an author would be. There are lots of other parts of the mechanics that do this, too. The initiation mechanic, for instance, informs players that conflicts tend to be one dog at a time, and are very personal in nature - not group goal activities. In the end, what we see when playing, are players who don't have their character in the scene, but who are just as engaged in play as if they were playing their character.
    \n
    \nNow, here's the million dollar question, and one that I've asked before in the open on the Forge. Is it really the system that's at work here, or the culture? I mean, it's certainly possible that you may have had the party problem playing DitV. The claim that might get made, however, and one that might rankle, is that what's going on there is that you're applying your cultural norms of play to DitV, and not being informed by the rules. It's your "fault" for not following the spirit of the game the mechanics try to support.
    \n
    \nOf course this is BS to some extent. That is, if the mechanics were really powerful, they could actually alter your behavior. Right? You fould fire back that it's solely our culture causing the phenomenon in question, not the mechanics.
    \n

    \n Hi Mike,

    Great posts. I just want to address two points:\n
    Posted By: Mike HolmesThis mode of play is more about being the "Audience" to the character's stories, than "being" the character. This is what, largely, is meant when people say "Story-games" intending it to be indicative of something different than traditional RPGs.
    \nI want to clarify this (or, perhaps, state the way I see this issue): I've observed that "being" the character happens all the time in these wacky story-games. The way you've phrased it above suggests that the Players are someone how not engaged as or with their characters, and I just haven't seen this.

    What I have seen is players alternating between being their characters with being an Audience to the character's stories. This is what's new. In a lot of game groups, being in-character is the default position: it's where you belong and to leave it is to be playing wrong. Hence, the moment it's not your scene you are lacking any function or role in the game. Some people are sent from the room; others are encouraged, as best they can, to sort of "ignore" what's going on in another PC's scene. But no matter how you slice it, if you're only function at the table is to "be" the character and nothing else, when you aren't getting a chance to be the character in a scene, you got nothing.

    By opening up the possibility of Players not only being in-character but also happily as audience members, the Players are allowed a role at the table no matter if their PC is in a scene or not. Sometimes this is reinforced with mechanics ("Pay attention to these other scenes -- you can do things!") and sometimes it's just the fact that the game's imaginative elements draw the players imaginations together in interesting ways and the players want to pay attention (in Sorcerer, all the PCs share the notion of Humanity, and Players, I've noticed, tend to want to see what people do when Humanity choices roll down the road.)

    The proportion of in-character to audience can and will vary from player to player and group to group, of course, and how far one invests in-character is another variable.

    But I think it's fair to say that when Players are playing their characters the option to being the character is fully present. And then, when the Player wants, they can take that character off and be fully engaged as an Audience member and participant.


    Second, \n
    Posted By: Mike Holmes(I really can't emphasize how different these styles of play are. It's not just a cosmetic thing, it's fundamentally different. I wouldn't go so far as to call them unrelated activities, but I'd be tempted to do so just to get traditional players to understand the difference. )
    \nI posted this on the Gateway thread, but I'll repeat the story here:

    So, it's midnight Sunday night, and all of us who had just played Shock! with Judson facilitating have stumbled into an elevator and are riding it down to the lobby. We've all had a great time and are chatting it up.

    And as the doors open and we start to get out, Evan, one of the players, is a friend of Judson's, and Judson roped him into the game. Evan is a board gamer, and he came for the board games.

    So, Evan says, "Okay. Maybe someone can help me with something. I haven't played a role-playing game since Dungeon & Dragons fifteen years ago." He pauses. "What was that!?"

    Imagine how strange it would be to have played D&D over a decade ago, and then get dumped into a Shock game without any of the intervening steps of Dogs or whatever? That's the moment I realized how strange and different these games had become. Even the games that don't look that radically different are pretty different. Many people look at Sorcerer, see that you roll dice in conflict, and go, "Well, what's the big deal." But the fact that there's a player authored Kicker on that character sheet, the fact that there's no party, the fact that there's no mechanic for killing the PC -- these are big deals when compared to how most people assume an RPG "should" work.

    Then throw games like Shock or Primetime Adventures or Dogs at people and assumptions about mechanics, the roles of players and even what an adventure is in terms of prep goes right out the window.

    You're right. They are related. But sometimes we underestimate the differences. And to speak of these things without being clear about that -- which I sometimes do -- is to be crazy-misleading.

    \n Control pacing. All the separate groups are moving toward a goal. Cut between them frequently, and use your control of screen time to do your best to have everybody reach the dramatic climax of their thread at the same time.

    This is especially important when those dramatic climaxes are going to be complex mechanical situations. Even if you care about the outcome of a fight, sitting there with nothing to do while the mechanics are engaged isn't all that thrilling, whether the game's Dogs or D&D. Run the conflicts in parallel. (This is easier when all the conflicts are using the same basic mechanics.)

    More importantly, people will be engaged by what's happening when they're off-stage if they care about it. If the other characters' problems interest them, they'll be engaged. If they don't, no set of mechanics can make them care. The supposed indie vs. traditional dichotomy here is mostly in the fact that 'indie' games are often strongly character-focussed. More interesting characters are more likely to create the necessary feedback loop, especially when you add in collaborative world/character creation mechanics.

    You can reinforce this by snarling the characters' connections together. One character's argument with his brother isn't necessarily engaging, but if the brother is also another character's rival, that player is more interested in the scene. Similarly, you can tie the situations together.

    Even if the situations don't directly tie together, create parallels. Make the theme of the day revenge, or missing family members.
