This is a composite image of the Cartwheel Galaxy. The four images along the side are from four different orbital telescopes, each of which sees a different portion of the electromagnetic spectrum. All of this data was put together to form the large image. This is pretty much the point of NASA's Great Observatories project.

Now, what you're seeing: this is actually three galaxies. The big one is Cartwheel; the other two don't have names to my knowledge. We believe the Cartwheel galaxy used to be a spiral galaxy just like the Milky Way, with arms turning around the center and everything. The reason it has this ring structure is that one of those smaller galaxies smashed through it. The ring around the outside is the shockwave of interstellar gas that is expanding outwards at 200,000 miles an hour, triggering new star formation as it goes. Given that the Cartwheel galaxy is a bit bigger than the Milky Way, the shockwave has been expanding for 500 million years.

    \n The thing is, even at 200,000 miles per hour, the shockwave goes INCREDIBLY slowly on a human timescale. If your galactic civilization had faster-than-light travel, the net effect of the shockwave would be that there was a wall between the inside and the outside, through which you (possibly) couldn't travel, and behind which you would find burned-out cinders of planets illuminated by the harsh ultraviolet glare of newborn supergiant stars.

    This was actually one of the premises of Larry Niven's Ringworld stories; the reason that the Puppetteers had left the galaxy in their planetary rosette was because just such a shockwave had been detected coming out of the center of the Milky Way, and in order to get out of its way without going into hyperspace (which gives 'sane' Puppetteers the raving heebie-geebies) they had to get started very early.

    The whole reason the Puppetteers were financing the expedition to explore the Ringworld is that they were expecting Humanity (which had no such problems with hyperspace) to have already leapfrogged ahead to Andromeda and started building them by the time the Puppetteers arrived.

    So Andy, the answer isn't "Rifts" but "Ringworld"

    \n Larry, look up "Galactic Life Zone" for a treatise on just how unlikely life in the Galaxy might be.

    The "arms" of a galaxy are basically miniature star-formation shockwaves. The chances of a lifebearing world surviving passing through a spiral arm are slim; the number of supernovae (due to the rapid formation and destruction of supergiants) is just too high.
