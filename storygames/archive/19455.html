\n Hey, so I'm working on a mostly Marvel Heroic Cortex+ hack and looking for a few brave souls to help play test this thing.

The hack I'm working on / play testing uses MHR as the basis, drops Affiliations for Drama's Values and Relationsips, then runs that dice pool through the Action resolution mechanic.

Opportunities generate Complications. Complications cover stress.

Everything else works basically straight out of MHR.

The goal is something with a bit more interpersonal drama, and that runs a bit quicker, than bog standard MHR. Also the complications make for more interesting and unexpected twists than the Doom Pool.

I'm running a play test over pbp, if anyone's interested pm me. I thought teen superheroes would be the best way try this first.

    Maybe they will. But I honestly doubt that from my experience with MHR. I'm not exaggerating when I say that literally every single game of MHR I've played--and I've played the hell out of this game since it's release--has involved several... discussions about which Affiliation a character is really using or allowed to use in a given circumstance.     \nHuh. Our group asked a lot of questions about it the first couple of sessions we played, but eventually realized that it didn't actually matter. As long as we had some kind of general guidelines on which Affiliation was appropriate, whether or not anyone got it right on any particular roll wasn't worth worrying about. (Seriously, it's only one die in a large pool, so even if someone uses a d10 instead of a d8 or a d6, it really doesn't have that much impact in the mid-to-high power range.*)


    For whatever it's worth, our guidelines were:

    - If you're getting help from anyone else, you can't be Solo. (We loved getting help from other characters, so this was a big one.)

    - If what you're doing requires another character in the scene to be worth doing (flanking maneuvers or "softening up" a villain for someone else's attack), you should be using Buddy at a minimum. Team if it requires more than one other character.

    - If you are in fact the only good guy there, you're Solo. (Obviously.)

    - Beyond that, whatever works in your mind is probably okay with everyone else at the table. Tailor your description to whatever affiliation you want to use, it's really not a big deal.


    * I imagine that in a much lower-powered game with much smaller die pools -- like, all the characters are novice SHIELD agents or something and have no powers and very little equipment -- the affiliation die might become more important. But I'm not sure MHR really works that great at that end of the spectrum anyway, so maybe it's irrelevant?

    Affiliations. From my experience it was the push to get the biggest dice possible. Despite the narrative or that a d10 instead of a d8 in a dice pool with four, five, or six other dice wasn't that big of a bonus, players just argued to get the best they could. Didn't matter what. Biggest. Best. Because they could.
    \nI guess what I'm saying is, it's not something that actually needs to be argued about. If they take the d10 instead of the d8, it doesn't matter all that much to their roll. (In the long run, it might actually suck a little bit for them to keep doing it, because the reduced odds of getting a plot point can't really be made up for by the very tiny bump in their roll they get from always grabbing for their d10.)

    Affiliation isn't a particularly compelling thing in play; it's a neat way of describing a character, but once you start building dice pools, it's almost entirely meaningless. Which maybe means it's a good idea to swap it out for a more meaningful Smallville-style value+relationship combo, but not, I think, if your goal is to make things go faster. Figuring out how a relationship applies to some rolls can be a huge speed bump, in my experience, while it's easy to just roll your eyes condescendingly and go with it whenever someone keeps arguing that they're totally Solo when they're flying a quinjet full of Avengers into the living island's face.

    YMMV, of course.

    \n Oh yeah, I don't harbor any illusions that the Values and Relationships switch will make things quicker in and of itself, probably about a fair trade from my experience. The quicker bit is using Action (Leverage/Firefly) to resolve rolls.

    Goal One: More drama and personal stories.
    Change One: Use Values and Relationships from Smallville.

    Goal Two: Speed up game play, fewer die rolls than MHR.
    Change Two: Use the Action resolution mechanics.
