\n I've played a number of indie games with novel play mechanics and was wondering what people found to be some of the most fun and interesting ones they'd come across. I'll kick off the discussion with three that I found particularly cool.
\n
\nDread's Jenga mechanic is easily one of the more recognizable and inventive ones. Using a shared Jenga tower really brings out the suspense tension in the game.
\n
\nGodlike (One Roll Engine)'s die mechanic is fairly awesome. You roll a set of d10s and pick a set with all the same number. The number itself determines how effective you are and the number of same valued dice determines how fast you are. This leads some wonderful choices of being effective or being first, which is especially appropriate for the gritty WWII era.
\n
\nPrincipia's world building moral questions really add a lot to conflicts and over a long series give the world a player-created feel.
\n
\nWhat are some others that you all really found provoking or profound?

    \n Dread's use of Jenga is amazing - it's the kind of thing that sounds like it'd be hokey and gimmicky, but in actual play is amazingly tone-setting.

    Polaris uses ritual phrases that both set tone and mediate conflict. They create a totally different way of thinking about conflicts - not as a thing to win, but as a thing to exploit.

    While I wrote it, I am incredibly proud of Ribbon Drive's rules for creating a Protagonist - when you've abandoned all your expectations of the future, and all your demands of it, you become the protagonist. That signals the end of the game.

    Montsegur has questions built into the pre-made characters. They're not necessarily meant to be answered, but they beckon you into the quiet world of that character, and they give you a sense of both who they are and who they might become.

    \n They're maybe not indie game anymore, but I like the Declaration mechanic in Spirit of the Century/FATE. You can make a knowledge/academic check to declare a fact related to that topic. The difficulty is based on how self-serving/cool the fact would be. If you succeed, the fact is true. Regardless of whether you succeed, you believe it to be true. It's a nice small measure of player authorship.
    \n
    \nI wish another game would use Polaris's "But only if..." structure. It seems like it's got a lot of potential for ratcheting up to interesting decisions and tradeoffs. I feel like Apocalypse World's "offer a hard choice" on a partial success is akin to this. Regardless, that's also a solid mechanic.
    \n
    \nThey're not really novel at this point, but BW Beliefs, TSoY Keys, and (to a lesser extent) Fate Aspects can all do a great job of driving games towards cool stories by tying mechanical rewards to character drives.

    \n This is neither indie, nor profound - it's the to-hit and damage die mechanic from Silent Death.
    \n
    \nThe ship's pilot contributes a die based on his or her skill - d4 for newbs, up to d10 or perhaps d12 for the very skilled. Weapon systems (e.g. blatguns, paired lasers, etc.) contributed two dice of their own, and had a damage rating (Low/Med/High). Paired blasters might be 2D6 Low, for example.
    \n
    \nTo hit, you'd roll all three dice (two from the weapon, one for the pilot). If the total exceeded the target number (highest for maneuverable targets with good ECM), it was a hit. To work out damage, you'd use the lowest, middle, or highest die value.
    \n
    \nNeat way to make damage correlated with accuracy, but with just one roll - but still possible to just clip someone.
