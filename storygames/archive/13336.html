\n I've got a pretty good idea of what kind of contest I want to run, but I'd like some feedback about the details. It comes from an idea I had for a roleplaying game which uses the text of Machiavelli's The Prince as the basis for a game. Paul Czege toyed with it, but it never got any further.

Summary

First Round
Contestants should submit a new roleplaying game which is based on an out-of-copyright document. The game, at this stage, must include no more than 5000 words, excluding the document on which it's based.

All submissions must be anonymous.

All submissions must have been playtested by the designer, changed as a result, and playtested at least once more.

All designers must agree to playtest another randomly selected game from the contestants and submit a playtest report to the designer. Entrants are welcome to playtest any other entry of their choice and submit reports.

I'll also solicit playtest feedback from other non-contestants, however, only the one playtest response from another designer is necessary to continue to the next round.

Second Round
Contestants edit and resubmit their documents based on the playtest feedback. The word restriction is lifted. I might add an art/layout contest at this stage.

If necessary, I will whittle the entrants down to three somehow. Help with this stage, please!

At this point, I'll arrange for all three entries to be playtested by outside playtesters who have no contact with the game other than your text. Each group will test all three games.

I will playtest all three entries, too.

A Golden Satsuma winner will emerge as a result of the feedback, as will the Silver and Bronze Satsumas, unless none make the cut. I don't know whether to allow some additional changes based on the feedback first. Help with this stage, please!

The Prize
The prize will be good, do not worry. It will be either cash up front, on publication, or ongoing publishing bills paid. I'd like to see the game published commercially, but that's up to the designer. I don't care what the publication or ownership model is.

Details
This is not a layout contest, but the text should be clean and accessible.

All in English, people.

The base document must available for download and listed as out of copyright at either http://www.gutenberg.org/ or http://www.archive.org/details/texts
There must be no restrictions on republication of these texts, even if you would be happy to release it with such strictures. For example, on archive.org, it would be listed as NOT_IN_COPYRIGHT. The title, if you are using it for the title of your game, must not be a trademark for games in the US. If you choose not to use the title, then this is not an issue. No Lovecraftian or Mythos entries.

By "based on" I mean that the setting or system of the roleplaying game is based on, or depends on, the document.

By anonymous, I mean make all reasonable efforts to disguise your indentity, and keep it hidden throughout the process.

    \n I'm also concerned about the Playtest requirement. Quite simply put, I can't even find enough people to play a game of Dresden Files. Role-players in my area are notoriously shy of anything outside of the mainstream, and here I'd have to get them to do it at least twice? If I also ask them to submit written playtest reports... In short, I wouldn't be able to do it.

    Other things that concern me: I would be worried about picking a document that other people had already declared an interest in writing a game for. More than one game based on the same document would have less impact due to perceived similarities and direct competition from other games, rather than being able to stand on its own merits. This is especially the case in an anonymous contest since the contestants can't discuss how they're going to treat the source document and arrange to do things differently. I'd personally like to see some kind of 'lock out' where entrants have to register their interest in a given text, then nobody else can pick the same one. A bit fiddly to organise I know, but I think it would be fairer to everyone in the long run.

    All this being equal, I think it's a pretty good idea. I hope enough time will be made in the first section for people to thoroughly read their source material though.

    \n I think there are a few things to consider.

    1. Your entrants won't just be indie designers. They'll be, at least partly, from the Cthulhu side.     \n I'll chime in and say -- as Murderland and Game Chef this year have shown -- that reading all the entries and commenting substantially on them takes a LONG time, especially if you are actually trying to help people make revisions and improve their games. When I'm moving really quickly and have short games of about 5 pages, I can do about 2 in an hour. Longer games or games that need more comments (either because they need more praise or need more work) take closer to an hour each.

    Also, there's really no way of verifying how much playtesting has occurred, especially with anonymous submissions, though you can often tell if a game has not been playtested with a close reading. Three rounds of playtesting (two of your game, one of someone else's), makes it sound like this contest will take 6 months, and honestly, I'm not sure if you want a contest to dominate your life for that long.

    One of the things I want to be able to leave Game Chef with is a judging/playtesting system that is actually feasible for one or two people to manage and happens in a relatively short amount of time. We've never had that, period, and it's really hurting the contest in the long run. The playoffs this year are a step forward, but I don't think they're a real solution. It's honestly much easier to compare games against each other and decide which one is better, so next year I'm thinking the playoffs might have a bracket structure like an actual sports tournament. And, then, you can assign people to read or play two different games and decide on a winner in their stage of the bracket, before passing that on to the next round. That way, you distribute the work, and the better games get read, commented, and/or played more without having to count #s of playtests or whatever. The real trouble in such a system is the seeding of games, but that could be completely random or based on seniority or something.

    In any event, the good thing about crowdsourcing is you have to do less work. The bad thing is inconsistent quality of judging / feedback etc.

    \n Jared's Story Games design contest determined winners by who sells the most $1 games in 1 week. I liked this for breaking away from pure judge subjectivity (although I'm biased being in the winning group).

    I've judged a few design (web / graphic / interface) contests and often it came down to gaming the Judges' tastes. I try to judge outside my personal interests but it's difficult. And often the designs that win competitions aren't the designs that would do well in the wild where customers decide. It's easier to impress the 1% mega gamers than the general RPG audience.

    But Jared's method rewarded designers with marketing skills not people purely focused on game design (which was on purpose since it emulated reality, at least on an indie level). The playtesting requirement rewards people with organization & networking skills. If layout is a factor, you reward those skills. Tight deadlines reward people with more free time. No matter what it won't be purely about game design and you won't please everyone. It becomes a question of what skill sets you want to reward.

    In a pure "judge decides based on what they personally like" situation, it can help to diversify the judges. Maybe someone who loves traditional games, someone else who loves story games, a game store owner. If you diversify the judges you take away "gaming the judges" and subjectivity becomes a contest feature.

    What criteria do you want the judges to use? What's more important to you? Which games best make use of their source material? Innovation? Marketability? Personally I think innovation is overrated and would rather reward games that solve problems.

    Whatever the criteria, if there is a scoring component, I've found having categories limited to 2-3 possible ratings "bad, good, great" eliminates judges interpreting ratings differently. Even "thumbs up" and "thumbs down" is better than 0-10 ratings.

    In terms of weeding out contestants, I'm a fan of a funnel approach where requirements becomes stricter as you proceed so people effectively filter themselves out. Which is why playtesting requirements could work well. We may not be able to prove playtesting but listing playtester names and trust mostly takes care of that.

    \n So thoughts on an entry fee:

    It's a natural filter. Ashok's wrong; make 'em pay up front. Don't start if you know you won't finish, and if circumstances conspire against you, you're out five bucks. The positive and negative of this are pretty obvious. As a contest judge I'd love a way to filter out people who will take up a lot of bandwidth and then fail to turn in a game. As a contest participant I'd hate to shell out real money.

    It'd make the final prize/s a little more luxurious. It might also lead to a sense of entitlement or an expectation related to future contests.

    It'd make it feel more real, whatever that means - money on the line as well as social cachet. Conversely, it would make things that much more competitive, and not in a friendly way.

    I'm down on the idea, myself. It just seems like an idea that is more bad than good, but that's probably because I'm invested in doing things a certain way.
