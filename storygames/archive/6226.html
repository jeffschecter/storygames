    \n Hi, Chris!

    A few thoughts:

    1) I'd say that given there are Key triggers like "Gain 1 XP when you're in the same scene as X character," it's not a stretch to say you can gain XP from performing some feat without making a Contest of it. Just like you can refresh pools without a Contest, though you can certainly roll to see how well you arm-wrestled or whatever.

    2) That said, these examples are situations where you would probably want to use a helping roll to roll the successes from Dressmaking or whatever into bonus dice for an opposed "Gain the Trust of the Beggar" or "Gain the recognition of the Baron" Contest. That's where a lot of the robustness of the TSoY engine lies, and that's really where the meat of the conflict is, in both cases.

    3) I do think that your instinct to set nasty stakes for mightier obstacles is right on. Losing stakes are a key part of TSoY; making failure interesting gives maximum fun whether PCs win or lose. It doesn't have to be either/or, though: you can give a penalty die or two for a titanic struggle against Mighty Caradharas. . .and make the consequences for failure particularly brutal. I mean, if it's really that important to the drama ("the story is bracing itself for a new turn"), then we want every outcome to have sufficient gravitas.

    \nHi Chris,


    As I read your example with the tailor and the beggar, the conflict as such is not unopposed - the player wants to do two things. One is activating a Key and the second is establishing a relationship - and that's where the conflict is. Roll against the beggar to see if a relationship is created through the creation of the garment. Will the tailor see the beggar again? Will the beggar abuse the friendship? Will he refuse to receive the gift? There are plenty of outcomes and options. The roll to win boon and recognition is likewise a roll against either the Baron or his court.

    Secondly if you find the players choosing unopposed conflicts, try to consider the object of the conflict as an abstract NPC. The players wants to plunder the Dungeon, so treat the Dungeon itself as a monster. The characters are trapped in logbuilding during a blizzard and they decide to risk the weather. Then treat the blizzard as a monster (or a group of monsters that independently attack each character). The strength of TSoY is that it can handle abstract NPC's. Had the same positive experience with In a Wicked Age, where two of the most dangerous character's I've played was a Dark Wall dividing a battlefield - symbolic of the destructiveness of warfare, hence draining the PC's of their will to live - and likewise An Ancient Oak under which a pagen god lies dormant. The Oak wanted to lure people to it, bringing blood sacrifices, to awaken the sleeping god. In the same sense you could have Nightmares or Drug Abuse as NPC's.

    In this sense there are only few unopposed rolls, as most should in my opinion be directed against NPC's or the Object of the roll should be transformed into an abstract NPC.

    \n This thread is weird to me.

    TSOY doesn't say that rolls are always conflicts of interest. It has simple task resolution in the form of unopposed rolls. The example in the text is "Pieter is going to try to climb that boulder." So, clearly, the game expects you to roll for something as simple as that. The criteria in the text for an ability check is "When performing an uncertain action..."

    Don't worry about the abstract NPC business. It can be used for cool stuff sometimes (You fight the tornado!) but those are exceptional situations, not the norm. The text doesn't even talk about doing this at all.

    You should have lots and lots of unopposed ability checks in a TSOY game. They are by far the most common type of roll.

    The technique that keeps these rolls from sucking is simple: the stakes for failure are never "nothing happens." Every roll changes the situation, for better or worse.

    Also, Fudge dice being what they are, even "unopposed" rolls are a crapshoot sometimes. The dice can go negative just as easily as positive, so there's some built-in difficulty to every roll.
