\n I ran a play-test session of Contract Work on Saturday. The AP is on the Lucre Press blog, here. Comment away.

If you know nothing of this game (likely, considering my silent running of the past 6 months,) here's a summary:

For any first-time readers, Contract Work is a game of Debt and Hitmen struggling to pay that debt. You don't become a Hitman for fun, you do it for money. (And if you did come to the business for fun, it's not long before a mobster, the law, or a black ops program has a huge investment in you.)

Players are called Hitters and the GM is the Interrogator. The game is played as the Interrogator leads the Hitters by way of question and answer through the job, starting from the kill and tracing back the path to it. Cul-de-sacs and lies are just as much a part of the game as truth as Hitters narrate their successes and failures to satisfy the Interrogator. Is the Interrogator an FBI agent, ready to send the Hitters away for the rest of their lives? Their lawyer hoping to make a convincing case? The Boss wanting the seedy details of the job he ordered? Only the end of the job will let us know.

The game system is about resource management. Every aspect of the mechanics reflect money. Time is money, effort is money, growth is money, cash is money. Characters are built in a point buy system and wagers are made with poker chips to resolve Confrontations that are determined by how much the Hitters and Interrogator wagered on attacking their foes and defending themselves. There are Free Action tokens that allow them to act covertly and hold an advantage over the Interrogator and Risk tokens that let the Interrogator push back.

Hitters get some money up front for a job and try to make the Target spend all his money so that there's nothing left to pay for security that can be used to stop them. After the job is done, the rest of the pay is divvied up and the Hitters pay an upkeep for their abilities (called assets) to reflect the work they do on downtime to stay in shape.

Injury to Hitters raises their debt toward a credit limit created by the Boss's investment in you. If a Hitter exceeds that limit, they are worth more dead than alive and become a target themselves.

A campaign follows the life of the Hitters as they try to earn enough money to buy their way out of the business before they outlive their usefulness.

    \nDo you like the Shadowrun or Cyberpunk settings or style of play but dislike their rules? Contract Work would make the perfect Shadowrun game (and I've played Shadowrun for 10+ years).
    \n
    \nI love how almost everything is measured in terms of money. I love how legwork matters. I love how you can play the game in flashbacks as you are being interviewed by an interrogator (although who is the interrogator... your mob boss, the police after you screwed up, someone else? I don't know. But we will find out!!!). I love how you have money to spend on you assignments but you get to keep what you don't spend. I love how you may win, but lose as you go farther and farther into debt. I love how you gain liability traits. I love how you have to maintain your assets. I love this game!
    \n
    \nIt rocks! I am seriously excited and highly anticipating this game's release. And I'm glad Russell is taking his time to make this game just right.

    Jenskot: Yes. I am generally irritated by Cyberpunk. The lack of shared activity between players, the clunky humanity rules, and the slide into anime of the recent edition. I've tried to play Shadowrun and the "everything all the time" rules crunch broke me. Contract Work started as a Cyberpunk heartbreaker, but even that seemed too complex so I figured I'd start small, without the AIs, robot limbs and self firing guns. One day I hope to go back to that Cyberpunk game. Maybe as a supplement to Contract Work that you could co-author. Eh?

    Edits: Some of the rules are up on that same blog in much earlier entries but they have evolved a good deal since then (I may have pulled them down because of the inaccuracies. I'd check but I have to be somewhere soon.) So here is a brief overview of the resolution mechanic.\n
      \n
    • Players are created in a point-buy system with Assets. These are the tools and talents to get the job done. Those Assets tell us the value of their target and the money (resources) for both sides.

    • With only half the money of the target, the Hitters can't charge in to kill him. They must make him spend money first and wear him down. To do this, Hitters collect Preparations (I know the pass code to his security system; I know where he meets his mistress; etc.) through confrontations. In confrontations, Hitters wager money in a bid system.

    • Hitter bids amount, Interrogator must match and may raise (up to twice the original bid,) Hitter may then match again or stay. Hitter declares an Asset at risk whose value is added to the pool, Interrogator adds the generic Asset of the Target and may add a Token to boost his side by a few points.

    • The bid numbers are tallied (1 pt. per Asset value, 1 pt. per $100) and each side divides their numbers in secret on an index card between Attack and Defense (how bad you want to win vs. how cautious you are.) There are some limitations on how the Interrogator must bid if the Hitters are acting covertly.

    • Cards are revealed. H's Attack higher than I's Defense earns the Hitters a Preparation valued at the Interrogator's expenses. The reverse is damage to the Hitter's Asset at risk. Both sides could hit each other if neither defends well enough, or both could cancel each other out if they don't attack hard enough. Ties are broken with a nominal fee. Dice may be rolled to boost the Hitter's side but the Interrogator gets tokens to use later.

    • Attacking the Target is similar, except the Hitters use only the pts. of Preparations they earned instead of cash and directly attack the Target's "HP."

    • The Target can strike back with his own attacks against the Preparations of the Hitters, lowering their value if successful. Use it or lose it.
      • \n
    \nThat's it in a nutshell.
