    \n That story was pretty bleak. But it definitely wasn't an indictment of D&D. In fact, I detected the author's defense of D&D in the story when the narrator encounters another DM, who plays in a mixed-gender group pf popular kids, at school (!), and explictly calls out the crazy DM kid as being the problem. Which, of course, the story is about.

    A comment on 'The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao' (the novel, not the short story excerpt linked above). I was reading it at the same time as a young woman in my office, and we were talking about it one night. We both liked it (it's a great book), and I mentioned how much I enjoyed all the bits about the life of a high-school nerd (D&D modules, Dragonlance books, comic book characters, etc), because I remembered all those things vividly, and she said "Oh, that stuff is all real? I thought he just made it all up."

The gifted kids game is the better game of the two; the other one is the stuff of countless RPG.net threads where the right answer is always "Dude, you should quit that game". The fact that the narrator prefers it suggests that he feels like he "belongs" to that game; he thinks he deserves the punishment he gets from the Dungeon Master.

So the other game, the "good" game, is the author going out of his way to not indict or blame D&D for the fucked-upedness of these kids.
