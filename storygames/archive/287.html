Tell me how to playtest

Graham
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
I'm currently playtesting a game I've written (it's called "Heist", it's about robbing places and it's designed to be played with cards in a pub with a minimum of equipment).

Could some of you who are used to playtesting give me tips and techniques for making it effective? For example, do you play to break the game or do you try to make it a normal gaming session? Do you change any rules that don't seem to work or do you persevere? If everyone's bored, do you give up?

At the moment, I keep things very informal: we go the pub, we play the game. If the person I'm playing with looks bored, I skip over the section that's boring them and we move on. If rules don't work, we rewrite them on the fly. At the end, I ask which bits they enjoyed and which they didn't.

Don't just concentrate on my playtesting, though. If you've discovered any strategies for making playtesting work, tell me them.

Thanks

Graham

    March 2006

    In my experience, players (including the GM) who know that the rules can be changed on the fly do not put much mental energy into seeing how to apply rules in ways that aren't obvious. Likewise, players whose intent is to break the system.

    My best results come when I say "We're going to use the rules as written. We're not changing them. We're going to try to play a game, and if it doesn't work we'll just quit and play something else. Observations about how the rules could be better are incredibly valuable but will not be folded in to the playing of this session."

    That way, people will say "Y'know, it would be nice if there were a Rule Z that let me do this particular thing. I guess, since there isn't, I can use rules A, E and Q in combination to achieve the same thing," which lets you know that rules A, E and Q synergize in ways that people find useful.

    Graham, it sounds like you're doing a good job. I don't think there is any formula.

    Playing hard with the deliberate goal of breaking the game is useful. In this mode you'll be stopping and re-starting often. It takes dedicated testers because it isn't that much fun. But it is gold.

    Playing fun with the deliberate goal of having a good time is also useful. In this mode you'll be throwing stuff out and re-writing on the fly. Try to keep track of the stuff that got hand-waved or tossed out, because chances are you can ruthlessly purge it instead of re-tooling it.

    My main suggestion would be to gather as much intelligence as you can - ask your players what worked and what did not, and what they expected but did not see and vice-versa. Writing up an actual play post immediately after a session is a great way of preserving knowledge. It's so easy to forget and there's nothing worse than repeating an obvious and noted mistake in a later playtest. I actually forgot the rules of my gameat a convention last year. We played wrong and that was a session wasted, in terms of playtest value.

    Another suggestion - get your game out there. Play it with people you don't know, or let others play it without you being involved (if you can observe, that's fantastic). Actual play feedback from strangers (or distant friends) is always super useful. Take it to conventions, which are a cauldron of insane strangers who will break your game for you.

    Yeah, Tony's right. Each time you playtest, you playtest the rules as written. Then you take notes as you play. Based on your notes, you revise. Then you play the revised version as written.     Personally, it depends on what stage the game is at. When it's at bundle-of-notes-that-only-I-can-decipher stage, I tend to be much more open to just changing stuff on the fly, and then seeing how that integrates with what I actually have written. During this stage, I often find that I'll do stuff in play, then see that I never wrote that stuff down, but I should have.

    When its at send-it-out-to-other-people stage, I'm a fan of playing it as written and seeing what happens. If something glaring comes up, or there's a known issue that I just want to poke at a little more to see if it's really an issue, and I have an idea of how to change it, I'll do that in the middle of the game, but for more minor stuff I take notes and revise later. Case in point - with Carry, I was unsure about the Endgame trigger mechanics, and I got a playtest report that said that it was a problem for them, so when they became a problem for me in my playtest I went ahead and changed them.

    I'm really bad at playing-to-break, though. Really bad.

    I agree with Tony about the value of playing sessions with the rules as written. Make adjustments between sessions. Also, get folks to write down what they liked, disliked, and didn't care about. Set an example with simple lists - one entry for each category is better than nothing, and as they get into the habit, many will supply more details. And make sure that playtest sessions go on where you the creator aren't present at all.

    Actually, the Grinding/Riffing applies to development as a whole, not playtesting. Ex: Eric's (technomancer) is a Grinder when it comes to making rules, but he switches some rules on the fly when he wants to test them in a pressure environment (or one rule consistently falls down). I'm a total riffer (stealing from other ideas, running with something that someone else has done before me, etc) but I rarely switch rules on the fly when playtesting (unless something is clearly UnFun-broke, and so far I've been lucky and I haven't done any switching) because I want to get a full idea of how everything relates.

    I suspect bringing those design-scale words over here may confuse things on this scale. How about "straight-up" (play as-written) and "fly-boy" (switch rules on the fly)?

    Tony, I know that I certainly playtest to check myself against others' thoughts and perceptions. I have this tendency to elide, when I'm working with concepts I've thought about a long time. Playtesting is indispensible for catching that.

    Of course it's not always perfect. None of the playtest groups had any trouble with what turned out to be d20 Gamma World's single largest headache. It was our intention that people use the d20 creature abilities as PC and NPC mutations - we figured, look, books full of cool powers, this'll work great. And all the playtest groups went with this, using the text as written. But dear Lord in Hoboken, the heat from folks who absolutely didn't make the connection....eventually we did sort it out in a way I'm happy with, but it was the single biggest mechanics issue we had, and that despite a dozen groups of wide distribution in location and style.

    *shrug* So it goes, I guess.
