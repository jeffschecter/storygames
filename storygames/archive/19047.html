\n To start Going Agro reads this way:

When you go aggro on someone, roll+hard. On a 10+, they have to choose: force
your hand and suck it up, or cave and do what you want. On a 7\u20139, they can
instead choose 1:
\u2022 get the hell out of your way
\u2022 barricade themselves securely in
\u2022 give you something they think you want
\u2022 back off calmly, hands where you can see
\u2022 tell you what you want to know (or what you want to hear)

So to me it's like a non-directed violence threat or a directed threat of violence for purpose other than violence. Players will often be in a situation and decide there's an NPC they don't like or someone needs some pain and say, "I wanna shoot this guy... I'll roll Going Agro!" And I say, "No need to roll. You do." The game can then progress with a firefight with me returning Harm for Harm ("What do you do?") or the NPC can wallow away or die. I got hung up my first few times running AW when players would want to start a fight and roll Going Agro and I get to choose one of those options which makes for a kinda anticlimactic firefight when everyone you shoot barricades themselves in or starts throwing things they think you want at you.

I tried to use it as a threat type move but it just got in the way of Sieze by Force. I just need some good examples where this move is functional. I had a player smash someones face into a bar to get some information and rolled a 7 on Going aggro... so options on and two are out... three is valid... four is not an option since the character has hold of his hair and five is the same as three... so... it's not really an option and it's just as good as a 10+ then... so... no real tension in the roll... why did we even roll?

So... I need to know why I should let this move back in my games. From a design and GM perspective the use of Going Agro is strange to me. I prefer to let things happen as the player states when violence is concerned.

From a design standpoint Hack And Slash from Dungeon World is a nice and clear cut move. I know exactly where to place it and even though its just the same as me doing Harm for Harm with a roll to possibly prevent me from using that move it's effective and I understand it. Dungeon World isn't supposed to be evoking a fiction (like AW is apocalypse fiction or Monster Hearts is teen monster drama fiction) it's evoking D&D and players roll to attack in D&D... done. But AW is different and I don't get this move.

    \n Let's see\u2026 It's a move for one-way violence. But violence in itself is rarely an end, more often a tool that people enjoy a little too much. It's all about establishing dominance.
    One way to put it, is: Go aggro is the move for when a PC is employing violence (note: not merely the threat of violence, that's Manipulate) and they're most likely not going to get hurt in exchange, but still something could go wrong (if it's obvious they're most likely going to get hurt in exchange, it's Seize instead; if nothing could go wrong, don't even roll).
    If they really have it their way (10+) they reap the benefits of being violent: they either hurt or control their victim. If there's nothing they want out of their victim but hurt them, they hurt them. If said victim's too tough or crazy or stubborn to give up free will, they can suck it up, which more often than not means they die: you can't rob them of this chance to be a big damn tragic hero, sure, if that's what they really want. If the victim can suck it up and it costs them nothing serious, hmm, try with more serious violence next time?
    If they don't quite have it their way but sorta (7-9), they still establish themselves as the alpha. Victim's got an escape route, but whatever it is it's an all-out defeat: they're running from the violent person, metaphorically or actually.
    And there's still a chance they don't have it their way, not at all, and then it's on the MC, as usual. Shifting the sitch from one-way violence to an actual battle is only one of the options.

    My bottom line is \u2014 in my AP experience, "Go aggro" is a move which seriously shaped the social landscape of our apocalypse world: it emerged as a tough, brutal world, where people often let their guns do the talking, and violent death is always on the table as a possible outcome of most interactions. This doesn't have to be your apocalypse world, though. If going aggro, as a move, doesn't make sense to you, stick to your judgment and do what makes sense. OTOH, if it makes sense to you in principle, but don't like it in the details of execution\u2026 well, fix it! And then tell us about it. ^_^

    \n To me, "I shoot them" indicates that you're trying to kill them, and in that case, you're not going aggro--you're going for Harm. Unless you were shooting to cause pain.

    Going aggro is about getting up in someone's space so that you can get something from them; that's coded into the results. You level some sort of violence at them (physical, social, whatever) and they have to stand up to you or get out. In a firefight situation, you don't go aggro to kill someone; you go aggro to blow them back and get to the door. Hollowpoint (to diverge slightly) makes a great point about firefight situations: they're not about killing people, they're about winning ground and aggressively advancing on your objective, leveling the threat of violence as a weapon.

    When you Go Aggro, violence isn't the intended outcome, it's the tool. The intended outcome is that the other side yields. Here's the breakdown of the outcomes...

    10+: you force the NPC to take action. They can't keep stalling. They either have to take the violence that you're leveling at them, or else cave and give you what they want. There's no safe option of "I weasel out of it unharmed". That's why it's the best option; either they get hurt (and nobody wants to get hurt) or give in (which you want).
    7-9: the NPC gets an "out", but at least you made them do something. They're also probably terrified of you, depending on what they chose. Irregardless, you've fundamentally changed the situation.

    \n This quote by Vincent is from the first thread Johnstone posted is going to be the best compass to steer by:
    "Going aggro is for when you care what your enemy does, and seizing by force is for when you care what your enemy has."

    Remember, in Apocalypse World you're picking the moves based off the effect/result the PC wants to have, not on the action per se. This can be counter-intuitive for many people used to other games, where the rolls is about what your character is doing and whether he succeeds in doing the action. It's a subtle distinction, but an important one. In OD&D I roll a d20 to see if I successfully hit. I then roll a damage die to see the effect. In AW I'm going for the effect and roll for that immediately.

    I also think the name for the move Go Aggro is... well, a tricky name. It puts the focus on being aggressive. Which it isn't. The focus is about doing something (usually aggressive) to get a result.

    I think this which is why Dungeon World is a smoother fit for lots of people. Hack n' Slash is about aggressively doing harm. It takes the d20 and damage roll of D&D and slips it into AW's dice mechanic. But it's actually a very different thing than how AW kind of runs.

    Finally, in the threads above, Vincent suggests always looking at the possible outcomes for the Move rolls as a guideline for which move to use. This seems incredibly sound to me. Remember, we're guided by the fiction first in AW. If a result is offered that doesn't make sense for what is happening in the fiction, don't try to cram that result into the fiction. Either pick a different move if the possible results don't jibe with the fiction, or ignore result if the move made sense but some of the results don't fit.

    The general consensus from the linked threads seems to be: "If you want to take a guy's life from him, it's Seize by Force. What you're seizing is his life." You succeed and you can do terrible harm. Depending on how well you do you can get other effects (suffer no harm from the opponent, terrify the opposition, and so forth.)

    \n AW makes a lot more sense to me if I don't think of the moves as being prescriptions which are triggered by specific actions, but rather as tools/brushes we can use to continue the story or the flow of the game. Which one do we pick? It's an artistic decision.

    "Ok, we've got someone pulling a knife, and it's a tense situation! What do we do next?"

    The MC can simply make an MC move - sometimes, that fits best. (And "inflict harm" is one good option here! But so is "offer an opportunity and ask" or "ask a provocative question".)

    The MC can ask to roll "go aggro", or "seize by force", or some other move.

    The question to ask is: which move gives us the most interesting outcomes for this situation? Which "feels" the most right? (e.g. Seize by Force almost never feels right unless the opposition is positioned and ready to fight back, which is what John Harper gets at in his post about it being a peripheral move - not the way you want to do things in Apocalypse World!)

    I don't think the game works very well if you treat it more mechanically, and just start rolling moves and *then* try to sort it out. "Well, do these outcomes make sense, or not?"

    "Go Aggro" makes the most sense to me if I take the name of the move as literally as possible: "Going aggro." It's someone getting violent and aggressive on someone else! Don't use it for just a threat!

    Example:

    The gunlugger says he wants to slam the guy's face into the bar, unless he tells him some stuff.

    MC: "Ok, so you're being all threatening and stuff? Roll go aggro."

    Here, to me it's going to feel a little weird if we roll 7-9 or a miss, and equally weird if we roll 10+ and now the player feels like they have no choice but to "follow through", even though they haven't even taken any action yet. The character's action (grabbing the opponent and slamming their face in) hasn't even happened yet, so the 10+ result is kind of forcing the player's hand - why should the player have to decide before they even start doing anything whether they're bluffing or not?

    I prefer it like this:

    MC: "Oh, you're being all threatening and stuff? Well, he just sits there drinking, scoffs at you, but you can see his hand is shaking..."

    PC: "Fucker. I grab him by the ponytail and slam his face into the bar!"

    [Note: this is already happening now! The action has been initiated by the character, whether he manages to pull it all the way through to broken-nose-against-the-bar or not. But the character's in motion, he's taking action!]

    MC: "Ouch! That's 1-harm... actually, wait a minute. Do you just want to hurt him to make a point, or are you trying to get something out of him?"

    PC: "Oh, I want him to tell me where the girl is."

    MC: "Ok, so you're clearly going aggro, grabbing the dude and smashing him into the bar! Let's roll."

    The player rolls.

    10+ - Some shit goes down, and the NPC now has to choose whether to suck it up or cave and tell.

    Unless I can see how it's fictionally appropriate for that NOT to be the case, I'd imagine I'll being dealing harm to the NPC in either case here.

    Either way, the player's got the upper hand here and the initiative afterwards. ("Now what do you do?")

    He might even be able to repeat the move, with more severe consequences ("Now I'm strangling him!"), which usually isn't a good idea, but could really work here.

    7-9 - Shit goes down, but the NPC might lie ("tell you what he thinks you want to hear"), try to bribe the player, or slip away in the process, if that seems believable.

    Again, I'll probably be dealing harm here anyway, but I could see that not being the case in most situations.

    Either way, the intimidation worked: the player's got the upper hand and the initiative. They've definitely won the fight (at least so far!), the enemy is cowed.

    They're just not getting what they want out of it.

    6 or less - Shit goes down, and the MC makes a move.

    This could be good or bad, as far as the outcome of the violence goes.

    If the NPC is totally badass, maybe they catch the PC off-guard and knock their teeth out first, instead. (But usually, I'm with Golux - the PCs are badasses in AW, if they initiate shit from a position of strength, they'll probably make a good show of force in my Apocalypse World. It would take a pretty unusual NPC to just flip the tables like that - or, more likely, the character taking action under not-so-ideal circumstances, like being surrounded by the dude's friends.)

    "Trade harm for harm" is a good MC move here, too. ("Ok, you fight for a moment, both take 1-harm. He's totally pissed off. Now what?")

    It could be something else, too, though, like the scuffle attracts unwanted attention and now it's time to run, or the PC hits the guy too hard and kills him by accident.

    The point, though, is that at the end of it the player a) almost certainly doesn't have what they want (the information on the girl), and they don't have the initiative either: now the MC's making a move, which is probably bad news, and the player's got to react.

    "Ok, you've got the guy by the ponytail and he's screaming, but everyone's crowding around you, looking for blood. Now what?"

    "He goes limp after you slam him into the bar a couple of times, and he's not breathing. You still don't know where the girl is. Now what?"

    Etc.

    I hope that helps.

      A few years ago I altered how I GM'd Sorcerer. I stopped picking up the dice first for rolls for the NPCs. I never initiated the conflict system myself.       If I'm not mistaken, this is much the way Apocalypse World works -- but more formalized since only the Players can pick up the dice. The MC uses the MC Moves -- again and again -- nudging the PCs with fictional details and positioning. The Players counter with their own Characters doing positioning. And every once in a while the Players take an action that activates a Move. And now the dice are rolled.
