How promiscuous are you?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nThe_Magus \n\n\n
\n
\n\nNovember 2010 \n edited November 2010 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I was at Dragonmeet yesterday. The trade hall seemed livelier than last year and I spent my hard earned cash dutifully on products new and old. I got to thinking about why I do this? Some of my thoughts were a self-justificatory monologue ("I'm supporting the scene and traders in times of economic crisis"), some of it was following fashion ("I've read or heard stuff about this game and I just have to look at the rules") but I wondered was most of it a kind of promiscuity?

I seem to be searching for that 'ultimate' RPG experience that will blow my mind and that I will remain loyal to. In doing so I get into bed with a number of games only to feel a vague sense of dissatisfaction after. Not total misery but just "is that it?"

I was wondering how other people are in relation to this phenomenon.

    Posted By: The Magus
    I seem to be searching for that 'ultimate' RPG experience that will blow my mind and that I will remain loyal to. In doing so I get into bed with a number of games only to feel a vague sense of dissatisfaction after. Not total misery but just "is that it?"

    I was wondering how other people are in relation to this phenomenon.
    \nI was going to play with your metaphor a bit, and try to write some big, coy response. But I got bored, quickly.

    Instead: If we're going to analogize trying new games with casual sex, I'm happy to declare myself a proud slut. I try lots of games. And I love most of them. Part of that is that I know what I like, and I select for it. I don't show up to a table without having prepared myself for that experience.

    The notion that "one game will meet all of your needs" is silly (as silly, incidentally, as the notion that "one person will meet all of your needs"). Games are enriched by a wide spectrum of play experiences. Games become more fun when you let them be themselves, instead of trying to force all of your needs into this one little container. Danger Patrol is better when you don't try to make it a cathartic hero's drama. With Great Power is better when you don't try to make it a to-the-walls gonzo laugh-fest.

    So, uh: I'm promiscuous. And I'm proud of it. And I sort of resent phrasing that implies that I shouldn't be.

    Go Play Something New.

    \n It's complicated.

    I don't go to cons, preferring to play almost entirely within my own circle of friends. Also, I hardly buy anything at all (I have to really want it AND know for a fact that we are playing it within the next three months, plus it usually has to be available at a discount).

    But I'll play basically anything that any of my friends put in front of me, and I'll read about games (even ones I'm 90% sure I won't be playing and 99% sure I will never buy). And part of what I like about gaming is that we change games and systems every couple of months; I've heard tales of the old endless campaigns where folks spent twelve years with the same characters in the same world, and I want nothing to do with that.

    So, monogamous but freaky, I suppose.

    \nI share your pain, Graham. And I can't help agreeing with MJ on his addiction remark. But I've happily quit the traditional games' paradigm of brand loyalty some years ago, and now I can easily say that there's no point in it. You stay loyal to a brand expecting that particular system to support any creative agenda your group comes up with when you meet, but that only labels that system as incoherent. It's not supporting any creative agenda at all.
    So it depends. What do we want to play tonight? Ooo, let's play Contenders! Or Polaris, or Agon... the game experience varies so much that I really don't feel like I'm in search of a system to stick to.

    On the other hand, I'm a shopping whore when it comes to new systems. Recently I've become more cautious, but curiosity often takes the upper hand. Especially if the game is sci-fi. Usually I search for review I find plausible, and in case they don't satisfy me or I can't find any, that particular game's homepage usually has downloadable material. Well, the character sheet alone says a lot about that game, so I check that first. Thus said, I think I own some 30 or 40 indie rpgs... Haven't counted them yet...
