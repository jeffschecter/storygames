\n Down at Furnace I was talking with various people about ashcan front and pondering how succesful it had been. Now I don't mean in physical sales, from what I hear they were very good. I mean how successful it has been in getting feedback for the designers of the ashcans. I have several related questions so I will list them:

1) How well has the front done in getting feedback for designers, and I mean ALL designers, who took part? I see on the site Acts of Evil, Giants and Psi Run are getting a lot of feedback but I can't see many others on there. I seem to remeber a good number of games on the ashcan front but I am not sure of percentages.

2) This goes to all deisgners who designed an ashcan. Since releasing your ashcan have you had second thoughts, beacuase of feedback or otherwise, about releasing the full version? I ask this as the games that were meant to go to ashcan were supposed to be around 90% complete and have had some playtesting before they hit public release. I have a concern that people may be rushing games out without even playtesting them themselves.

Paul and Matt what percentage of games that were on the ashcan front do you hope to see in a full version?

3) Quality I have found is an interesting one. I have now seen some of the 'ashcans' and in particular 44, matt's game. Now I believe the idea was for:

'The book as an object has a hand assembled or copy shop aesthetic and is clearly not "store ready".'

44 is a beautiful looking thing and has obviously been professionaly printed and looks like it is store ready. Galactic is another one I have seen and I would say it sits on the border of shop ready and the 'copy shop aesthetic' mentioned above. I realise that enforcing an aesthetic would be very hard for you, but it seems odd that Matt went for such a layout when he is one of the founders. Do you think it made others feel 'bad' about the quality of their own ashcans? Do you think releasing such a professional looking book as an ashcan will harm the final product, or even cause dissapointment when it releases?

4) This next one needs a little explanation first. I have a concern that some designers will release games well before they are ready, before they reach that sort of 90% playtested stage of things, using the aschan front to make a quick buck as it were. The reason I have this concern is I have seen similar things happen in computer game s recently where designer would release games that aren't ready and then use microtransactions to patch them later, or just patch them later. Now I would hope that no one would do this but I think it remains a legitimate concern, and I worry that releasing an ashcan might be the final step for some people.

This is slightly different from question 2, which addresses my concern with people releasing before they are anywhere near ready. This is more about people using the front to make money and then not carrying through on the promises that involvement implies.

Do people think this is a legitimate concern?

Thats it for now. I obviously have misgivings about the ashcan front as I worry that designers will become detached from their designs, not engaging fully in the playtesting process, or release products that aren't even ready for the ashcan front.

Cheers

Iain

    \n My short answer on success is that it's too soon to tell. It's hard to run right home from Gen Con and start playing. Acts of Evil, for example, is on the to-play list of a group I'm in, and we just haven't got it into the rotation yet.

    As far as presentation, it was easier for me to put together a book based closely on a final layout than one that looked like an indie comic. What I eventually release will be a different trim size, perfect bound, but you know, I had all that art already, and I'd invested in that cool fond for the headers. Might as well use it.

    Games being released too soon, even in ashcan... well, the idea of making a quick buck at Gen Con via ashcan is absurd. I'll say that to let anyone know who was actually considering it. The cost-per-unit for an ashcan just isn't viable as a money maker at a show like that.

    The other issue is that determining what's 90% is pretty hard. It's awfully arbitrary.

    \n Hey, i sold copies of Sweet Agatha: Preview Edition at the ashcan front. Here's my answers/opinions in regards to the questions above:

    1. I haven't received much feedback at all since gencon. I got some very good discussion with folks AT the con, but very little afterwards. I'm not sure why exactly. The game is super easy to play, and from start to finish only takes about 45 min, and only 2 people. I figured there would be a lot of early play. Maybe its the back of peoples cue, or they are waiting for the final edition. Hard to say, really. I probably should have sold and supported the game a bit more on the front, but i was kind of in a haze all gencon, and didn't really hit on all cylinders, i could have been a better author, for sure. I have a free game coupon offer in the back of the book, you send it to me and you'll get the full game for free (shipping included) when it's done (round early december, late november). I have, to date, only received one coupon back.

    2. I pushed back my release date for Sweet Agatha 2 months, based a lot on what i saw at gencon. I wanted a really appealing package, and putting stuff under the microscope after hearing people's thoughts really helped.

    3. I went for a VERY copy shop aesthetic. But that's kind of my style anyway, and it suited the material (all xeroxed, stapled together, a spray paint stencil on the cover). That's what i WANTED, regardless of what Paul and Matt suggested for the shelf (it was nice that it meshed though). I was a little surprised by the production values of 44 and acts of evil (both very VERY cool looking books, but neither were really "copy shop"). When i think copyshop i think ugly san fran zines from the 90's. Maybe if zine culture continued as strong into this decade we would see production values more akin to 44 and Acts, but who knows. It was awesome to see books that looked THAT good. but maybe it did a disservice. I purchased Acts because it was such an awesome art object, I have a lot of interest in the game, but unless Paul takes more than a year to get a finished version out the door, i probably won't be very helpful as a playtester (sorry paul). When the game does come out, i doubt it's going to look better than this hand embossed copy i have on my shelf. I'll probably just get the PDF so i can play with the finished rules. I got my artifact.

    I also have a lot of interest in 44. It looks really cool and i think i'll have a lot of fun with it. I didn't get the ashcan. I figured that waiting for the final rules would be a good idea (again, i probably won't time out a playtest), and i know the final version is going to look at least that good. What the ashcan DID do for me... well, now when the game comes out, i'm going to buy it sight unseen. I'm comfortable knowing it's going to be well laid out and look good. No need to concern myself with that.

    I felt Jeff's "kingdom of nothing" really hit the mark for copyshop aesthetic. Glossy cardstock cover, saddle stiched xerox paper. You can leave the book open (the cover is too thick) and you can keep the book exactly shut (there's too many pages stapled together). These are the kind of layout concerns that don't matter for an ashcan but can't be ignored in a final version. The cover was exciting enough that it drew the eye, and the guts were simple enough that you got the message on how to play, but you knew there was work to be done.

    4. If you're a new designer and you think that releasing an ashcan is going to give a little boost of capitol before your real release you don't understand the economy of production, or you're ripping off your customers and you would probably steel sheep from starving peasants. Ashcan's don't make money, they cost money. I don't know if people will now rush to produce ashcans. Maybe they will. I think if that's the case, we're better off buying quarter baked games sold to us as ashcans than half baked games sold to us as final products. If i got an ashcan and it wasn't really much of a game, i probably wouldn't feel ripped off, and to be honest i would be more likely to give feedback ("ugh, you don't really explain how anything works, maybe you should be more clear on that.")

    \n Graham, I'd be pretty stupid if I didn't defend something I paid to do and believed in!

    The Ashcan Front is not immune to crticism. I'm happy to hear suggestions; so is Paul.

    That said, I consider it a pretty remarkable success to date. This is not to say it was a home run for everyone who stepped on the black & red checkered floor, nor it is to say that from this date onward all publishers will necessarily succeed.

    On to Iain's questions:
    \n
    1) How well has the front done in getting feedback for designers, and I mean ALL designers, who took part? I see on the site Acts of Evil, Giants and Psi Run are getting a lot of feedback but I can't see many others on there. I seem to remeber a good number of games on the ashcan front but I am not sure of percentages.
    \nIt's done ok. I think there are probably a couple games that aren't getting much feedback. I'm not getting much, for one. A little, but not much. However, I didn't really expect any for at least six months, honestly. I figured it'd take at least that long for someone to play and then respond with useful information.

    So, I'm not concerned about lack of feedback just yet. But, it's certainly possible that some games will fail to receieve anything substantial and useful. I'd consider that a failure for that game and that person / publisher.
    \n
    2) This goes to all deisgners who designed an ashcan. Since releasing your ashcan have you had second thoughts, beacuase of feedback or otherwise, about releasing the full version? I ask this as the games that were meant to go to ashcan were supposed to be around 90% complete and have had some playtesting before they hit public release.     \nNope, not here. Others can chime in. I'm not aware of anyone in that situation.\n
    Paul and Matt what percentage of games that were on the ashcan front do you hope to see in a full version?
    \nThat one's easy! I hope to see 100% of course. Paul and I have done our part, and will likely do more, to help people along. But, ultimately, we both realize that it's the personal responsibility of the publisher himself (unfortunately, we had no 'herselfs'). So, in all honesty, I do no expect the 100% will happen. I'd be thrilled if it did. I do expect that, say, 80%-90% of the games will be published in fuller form later.\n
    3) Quality I have found is an interesting one. I have now seen some of the 'ashcans' and in particular 44, matt's game. (SNIP) I realise that enforcing an aesthetic would be very hard for you, but it seems odd that Matt went for such a layout when he is one of the founders. Do you think it made others feel 'bad' about the quality of their own ashcans? Do you think releasing such a professional looking book as an ashcan will harm the final product, or even cause dissapointment when it releases?
    \nNo.

    Having one's feet held to the fire for being "too pretty" isn't exactly a problem. It is not one of my concerns, neither as offending other colleagues (I sensed no concerns, and sensed lots of other appreciation) nor inviting disappointment later.

    I think my book design is good, not great. It's attractive. It also demonstrates the diversity of acceptable formats for ashcans. That Iain (and others, maybe?) is suggesting it might not be acceptable is ... strange.

    I just have to shrug and suggest you to reconsider your personal definition of what the physical format of an ashcan. And, accept -- as I said repeatedly online and in podcasts since May or so -- that ashcan is more about process and design stage than it is about whether or not your game is wrapped up in a brown paper sack.

    I have other concerns, like people talking about how 44 looks more than how it plays or how it might be improved. Read it, play it, and let me know how it goes!\n
    4) This next one needs a little explanation first. I have a concern that some designers will release games well before they are ready, before they reach that sort of 90% playtested stage of things, using the aschan front to make a quick buck as it were.
    \nThis concern was raised about the Ashcan Front in particular elsewhere. I have to look at it with a seriously jaded eye. People paid at least $160 (for booth entry and badge, some paid $180) plus expenses (food, travel, and hotel, which far, far exceed $180) to be able to sell their $10 or $15 game to roughly 20-40 people. Paul and I threw down considerably more than that, but did recoup some of our costs.

    As you can see, the math is not in favor of making a quick buck. It is a losing proposition, likely. Consider it a cost of performing a unique kind of research.

    Could other designers outside the Ashcan Front (or "after" the AF) create ashcan games with an eye toward making a fast buck, perhaps online? Sure, I guess.

    But, that ignores the cause that started the Ashcan Front in the first place. I believed the phrase getting tossed around was "half-baked" games and another phrase was "stealing money from your wallet."

    I'm much more concerned about the lack of ashcans than I am concerned about hypothetical ashcan charlatanism.

    \n Some interesting posts here, and a general positive feel for the ashcan front which I can only take to be a good thing.

    I will reply to individual things that interested me below and by name or subject to clarify what I was meaning

    The fast-buck problem: I can totally understand people thinking this is a ludicrous idea for something like Gencon where the expense is high to get in. I was more thinking of the ashcan catching on in other places and smaller cons. For instance, throwing some figures around at random here, if I go to Conpulsion, in my home town, and take 10 ashcans of 'Stitch' say and sell them for \xc2\xa35-6 a pop, I will make a profit of at least \xc2\xa33 on each giving me \xc2\xa330 in pocket. Nothing major but profit nonetheless. I am not saying you could make a living of it, I just worry about unscrupulous deisgners making a crappy game, ashcanning it and using the profits to fund their beer money for the weekend rather than embracing the good intentions of the 'ashcan movement'. Hope that isn't too strong a turn of phrase.

    Matt Snyder:     Unacceptable is a strong word, more inappropriate was what i meant, reading from the material you and Paul put on the website. Your parameters, for want of a better word, was that it should have a copy shop aesthetic to it, which 44 obviously does not. I would say it was one of the better books for actually promoting debate about the game because of the side bars you added, I just felt that as one of the founders of the front, you should have adhered to the guidance you laid down. I have not seen 'Acts of Evil' so am in no position to comment on it.

    As to those guidelines I have a follow up question Matt. Matt Wilson pointed out, quite rightly, that the 90% thing is quite an arbitary kind of mark. What guidlines did you give to designers as to how playtested their games should be before they get ashcanned? To me it would seem right to have playtested it 4 or 5 times at least and gone through a few different versions of the rules, clarifying things each time until you are ready to release a public playtest version, which is basically what an ashcan is.

    As to success, I was wondering if you might be able to tell me how many games were on the ashcan front table. That would help me better judge the level of feedback being given. Any info. you can give in this regard would be appreciated.

    Spookyfanboy: sorry don't know your actual name. I see no evidence to suggest that just because someone has paid for it they are more likely to playtest it.     \n Hi, Iain --

    I believe the correct number of games at the booth was 11.

    Second, regarding the whole "pay to playtest" gripe. I agree with you that there's no actual evidence (that is, reliable data) that people who pay for the Ashcan Front games (and, ashcans in general) will be more likely to playtest. It certainly could be the case.

    That said, I don't agree. I think people who pay actually ARE more likely to playtest, and we're seeing some indication (not especially reliable, accountable information) that this tends to happen.

    We have a very different kind of trasaction going on here that this thread does not, I think, appreciate. People are not buying product. They are not consumers. But, this thread, I think, assumes that they are, that all of this is just like any other exchange of money and goods.

    Paul and I strongly disagree with that approach. We do not think this is a consumer driven issue. We believe it is a hobby issue, and enthusiast issue. It is a meaningful exchange of not only currency and product, but of strong relationships among enthusiasts. This is, we think, very different than either a consumer issue or a collector issue. (The collector issue is interesting and more murky in this case. It's worth talking about for example with Acts of Evil. I'm not going to get into it in this post, at least.)

    Here's what we think is going down:

    We have two people. These people view one another as equals. They are peers. They are not producers and consumers. They are not celebrities and fans. This is really important to the whole thing. It's fundamental to why Paul and I did this in the first place.

    One person has created an ashcan game, which is to say a game that is not fully complete, but that is playable and enjoyable. Part of the enjoyable of that game is noticing the few remaining gaps and helping figure out how to fill them and make the game complete. This first person needs to find new access to feedback from his or her peers. His or her other channels -- friends, online pals, whatever -- are exhausted. The person has already talked to all of them, probably playtested the game with them too. Now, he or she needs to reach out for new feedback and input.

    We also have a second person. The second person enjoys the process of playtesting, of examining games both critically and in actual play. It is a part of their hobby they find rewarding. This is not for everyone. Not everyone in the role-playing hobby is this person. Some are, and these some are people we want to pay money for ashcans. These "some" people genuinely get a real kick out of this process, out of interacting with the creators, out of being part of the creation itself in a less direct way. It's a process, and action that they value. They are willing to pay to do this.

    It is not a service this second person provides. It is involvement on an intimate level with a creative process they enjoy. They engage with the creator. The do not expect payment. Indeed, they are willing to pay money to be involved -- to be recognized, to exercise their brians, to have a little fun playing, and so on.

    The second person pays the first person money for his or her ashcan.

    That's what we do, and did, at the Ashcan Front. In that, it was a success. We aren't foolish enough to think that 100% of all copies sold will be this process. We want some portion of those transactions to be this. We think that happened for most, if not all, of the games sold at the booth.

    I hope you can see how this approach -- which, again, is not going to happen 100% across the board with all people and all transactions -- informs what all of the Ashcan Front participants have done.

    You might see, for example, why we do belive, contrary to your concern, that people will be likelier to playtest after they've paid for an ashcan. You might also see, for another example, why I think the quick buck problem is not really about ashcan's as things sold, but instead about a lack of us educating people that this transaction is not equal to producer-consumer transactions of other products.

    \n As a recent consumer of an ashcan product I would just like to chime in in support of Matt Snyder's analysis and go one step further -- if those producing ashcans fail to recognize this peerage then their ashcan is unlikely to generate the desired results. It's relatively easy to get praise from collectors, readers, and even players of RPGs but if you want useful criticism on a pre-production product, you'll want to take advantage of the critical nature of a peer review.

    When your game design meets players that have no pre-knowledge of the game -- that is, they haven't read it before and they haven't played it with one of the game designers or someone in constant contact with the game designer -- the issues that come up in understanding and enjoying the game are ALL relevant. They will mostly represent defects that don't exist in the game you have in your head but do exist in the rules that got printed.
