\n Back in 1989 I was part of arranging the Oslo International Storytelling Festival. One of the people we had at the festival, was an old Dakota chieftain. He made a very strong impression on me. He told the story of his people, but not the story of oppression and hurt. He was committed to preserving their heritage, to give as much as he could of it to the younger generations. So he told stories of leadership, work and pride, philosophy and culture, and great myths. He told us the Dakota and Lakota indians were people of the great plains.

- Like a Sioux, asked I.
- The indian looked at me, all serious, and said: No, not like a "sioux". The word sioux is from my language, he said, and it means "dog". That is the name the white people put on us. They took our word for dogs, and named us by it. We do not call ourselves dogs.
He said it with no malice, and I wondered how a man could say such a thing about his own life and identity, and be so calm.

That chieftain is still a hero to me. His calm answer to the ignorant question of mine, is one of the greatest lessons I've ever had.

When I googled the Dakota indians tonight, i found a site for children, answering questions about the "Dakota-Lakota Sioux Language". I was perplexed; how come this sioux-word is part of this site? I suppose it's a clouded issue for both indians and whites alike, today.

Anyway:
- my challenge is for you, the Americans amongst us here, to make role-playing games about indians.
You may, of course, choose to make a game on any indians, in any time, with whatever theme you deem to be the most interesting.
Pluck up your courage, and go forth calmly.
Show leadership.
______________________________________________________

BTW:
I wrote a suit of poems dedicated to this Dakota chieftain some years later, telling the story of how his eagle-form graced the streets of Oslo, and learned our spirits to fly. They were part of a book I published in 1996, called Mouthfullmoon. The particular suit of poems dedicated to the Dakota chieftain was called The History of Flying Technique.

This is one of the poems (my translation):

We are frail beings
fleeing the gravitation.

Dancing eagles
on snowy boardwalks,
covering the heavens
with our wings.

    \n Tomas,

    I'm not American. I'm happy therefore to be corrected, but my understanding is that in the US the term Indian is seen as offensive when used in this way. I think the preferred term is Native American. Happy to be corrected though as I say.

    More pressingly, your posts on this subject have smacked to me of exoticisation, which is a form of racist depiction. Making people an other, perhaps in some ways a better other (wiser or whatever) but an other. The approach seems each time to be about us engaging with them, which presupposes that they are not us.

    Finally, and most importantly, you ask Americans to write a game about Indians. That language strongly implies to me a dichotomy. Two separate groups. Americans and Indians. The thing is though that Native Americans are Americans. To create the artificial separation your language implies is to put them again in that position of the other, which historically didn't go so well.

    You invite Americans to make roleplaying games about Indians as if one cannot be both. Your language, to be blunt, seems racist to me.

    All that and frankly it just seems in plain bad taste. Essentially you're asking the victors of a historical struggle to make up games about the losers of that struggle. I find that distasteful. If you want to know more about Native Americans there are some excellent books on the subject, some by academics and some collating folklore and oral accounts. The key is that those texts don't objectify, which I think you are.

    Apologies if I've harshed the vibe. I don't mean to. I just find these posts troubling and felt it worth saying why.

    \n Um. There is a game, I helped illustrate it. Its called Totems of the Dead for Savage Worlds. Now, granted, it is very sword and sorcery, but the Americas it depicts is pretty solidly researched as far as I can tell. I'm no expert. But my best friend in high school is now an archaeologist at Cornell. He studies Iroquois-colonial time period at a few digs. The illustration I had to do concerning the Iroquios, I went to him for research.

    If *I* was a game designer, and I wanted to really seriously tackle an Americas RPG, I think the emphasis would be on trade between tribes. My friend found a bead in a site near Geneva, NY (top of Lake Seneca). This site was a dual colonial-iroquis site, both were living there, it was a trading town. It also has the distinction of being the first found in that time period with no defensive palisade. Both tidbits I find really interesting! Back to the bead. The bead is made of stone and it could ONLY have come from Baja California. That bead travelled!!! That is kinda cool and suggests a very robust trading network across the whole north continent. I've read some other theories in the last few years that seem to support a very sophisticated trade network in both continents.

    My last bit comes from an NPR article that I heard about 10 years ago. There was this tree researcher/expert at Michigan University. He studies trees primarily in the "ohio valley". When white colonials came to the Ohio valley, they said a squirrel could leave Maine and travel all the way to Indiana by tree limb and never touch the ground. That is how dense and primeval the forest was in the Ohio valley. I was told as a lad that my neck of the woods use to be nothing but untouched, natural, old growth trees. That the whole Ohio Valley was made of this amazing old growth forest.

    Not so fast. Our tree expert from Mich U understands that tree growth in fallow fields follows a certain, specific pattern. The ohio valley? Fits exactly that pattern. His theory; The entire Ohio Valley was cleared and was farms and agriculture from Ohio to Connecticut. Prior to white explorer's coming. When they did come, they didn't see old growth trees, they saw trees that were 200 years old.... which syncs up perfectly with the ravaging diseases that raced across the continent and depopulated the population... allowing those fields to go untended...allowing the trees to grow back in the same way they grow now when a field goes untended.

    Again, pointing to a much more vast quiltwork of tribes and civilizations than the first western explorers could have dreamed of. By the time they got there, it was all buried under trees.

    So. My take on a cool native american campaign would be to center on a hardy band of traders, travelling from tribe to tribe, exchanging stories and goods. But I have to be very honest with myself. That would be such a cool campaign in my head, but oh by all the gods of gaming, that would be a aircraft carrier amount of work and prep. And there aren't really a lot of sources. Some regions have some pretty good research into them (Pacific NW, Mesa Verde area, history after whites make contact), but others, are only just now starting to get any scrutiny at all. I seriously doubt my ability as a scholar or a gamemaster to pull off such an undertaking.
