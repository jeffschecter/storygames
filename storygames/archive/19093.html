\n Hi all, it seems like there has been some grumbling about tremulus playbooks and lore moves. I just picked up the game and think it's swell, but I appreciate that there is always room to build and improve. So that got me thinking: how hard would it be draw up new playbooks? I mean, between Graham's Stealing Cthulhu, the occupation descriptions in Trail of Cthulhu and the bazillion great Apocalypse World hacks out there that really push the limit and show what is possible, it seems to me that there is a lot of resources for figuring this out. Does anyone want to give it a shot? Or to just comment and proof what we come up with?

I am thinking the overall theme a tremulus playbook should embody is both "narrative control" and "the investigation." As the Jank Cast crew has mentioned (55 minutes, 47 seconds into the podcast), tremulus departs from Apocalypse World in that characters are not explicitly "in control" or powerful in that sense. Rather, as Graham suggests in Stealing Cthulhu, Lovecraftian protagonists are basically the vehicle that takes us on the sight-seeing tour of terrible and unspeakable realities (under the chapter "Creating Investigations"). Graham adds that to make these protagonists viable in a game, we have to make them do something\u2014actively work to uncover the mystery. Therefore, it is interesting to focus not on how the characters can control events of the game (by beating up people, overtly browbeating NPC's into doing what you want), but how their players can feed into the story with their paranoid speculations. It's more about narrative influence than active character empowerment (kind of like, for instance, Polaris).

So here is an example playbook for the Alienist. What do you think? Does it get at these two goals? Imagine that it also has the Therapy automatic move from the tremulus playbook and one of Johnstone's one-liner Lore moves as well. Note, I am using Bonds instead of Trust (I just find that a better game mechanic).

Alienist
Psychoanalyst: When you have a bond with someone, tell them what you think, whether they ask you or not, and roll +passion. On a hit, they either follow your advice or act under pressure by ignoring your uncomfortably accurate reading. On a 10+ you also gain a bond with them. On a miss, they shut you down.

Let's Be Reasonable!: You can defuse any tense situation. When you interfere in threats or violence between men, roll +reason. On a 10+, choose 3. On a 7-9, choose two.
\u2022 they back off and put their weapons away
\u2022 they don't harm others
\u2022 they don't make other demands
\u2022 you don't suffer any harm in the process

Dream-reader: When you hypnotize a willing subject, ask them a line of questioning and roll read a person. In addition to the results, you get bonus hold equal to the number of bonds you have with the subject. You may also spend hold to ask the hypnotized subject up to one question from the puzzle things out move, accessing their suppressed knowledge or suspicions. On a 10+, you uncover a truly shocking insight\u2014announce what hypnotized subject says and gain 1 Lore (the Keeper may ask you to make a Sanity Check).

Licensed Practitioner: When you flash your practitioner's license, you may gain access to any mental records and sanitarium wards normally off limits to the public.

Criminal Profiler: When you survey the scene of a crime, apply your forensic psychology training and announce the psychological profile of the criminal, including detailing the perpetrator's likely personal history, age, habits and attitudes. Additionally, when you spend any amount of time interacting with someone with whom you do not already have a bond, you may gain a bond with them as you assemble their psychological profile.

    \n I remember one of the *World designers (though I don't remember which one) saying that the last place you should start an Apocalypse World hack is with the playbooks.

    I think that's equally true when it comes to fixing Apocalypse World hacks. Tremulus playbooks are pretty weak, but that weakness reflects more fundamental flaws in tremulus. The Alienist is terrible because the sanity rules are terrible. The Heir is bad because the wealth system is bad, etc.

    The game has major problems right down to its basic moves.\xb9 Before you try to fix the playbooks, consider how to best fix the structures those playbooks are build on.

    \xb9General Rule: "Go Aggro" and "Seize by Force" are moves about how violence works in the apocalypse. When a hack uses those moves unchanged, as tremulus does, that's a big red flag.

    And some new stuff... I'm thinking playbooks will be broadly categorized as investigators that interact best with things (objects, clues etc) and investigators that interact best with people (manipulating, influencing etc). I see the Alienist as belonging to the latter. The Author is kind of a mixed bag\u2014a jack of all trades that does a little of everything.

    The Author
    Work In Progress\u2014For you, writing is both a cathartic relief from the strange investigation you have become wrapped up in as well as a method of figuring things out through your subconscious insights and intuitions. When the investigation inspires the next chapter of your work in progress, roll +passion. On 10+, hold 3. On 7-9, hold 1. On a miss, hold 1, but someone somehow finds out you are writing about them and aims to fix that (the Keeper gets a hold). For each hold you gain, explain the latest plot twist in your novel which actually relates to one of the NPC's or unsolved mysteries in the current investigation. You gain +1 forward when pursuing that line of inquiry.

    Star Power\u2014When you explain that you are an author to someone, roll +luck. On a hit, they have heard of you and are excited to meet someone of your stature, cheerfully volunteering what information they can (or doing some other small favor) with the hope of being in your next novel. On a 7-9, they later gossip about you, making your stay in the area (and everything they know about it) a well known secret. On a miss, they don't know you and they don't like the idea that you are writing a book about them and their close-knit community\u2014you enter the rumor mill, but the gossip is less than flattering.

    Odd Jobs\u2014You've had to work countless jobs between droughts of publishing. No matter, it has been a great source of research and first hand experience for your novels. You may choose one move from any other playbook.

    Bookhound\u2014As a writer, you have spent more time in dusty libraries than you care to admit. When you encounter something puzzling and strange, you could have sworn that you read about it before. The next time you are in a library, roll +reason. On a miss, you spend hours poring over dusty manuals to no avail. On a hit, you find the book you had in mind. The Keeper will give the book's account of a very similar occurrence in another place and time. On a 10+, in addition to whatever clues this book reveals, you gain +1 Forward for pursuing the mysterious encounter further.

    Author-in-Residence\u2014As per tremulous playbook.

    @arscott and Johnstone, thanks, it's awesome to have you guys on board.

    Re: Basic structure, I definitely don't have any sacred cows, so let's do it. I thought wealth and sanity rules were OK (not mind-blowing, but decent), but if you think they could speak to a core theme more strongly, please share your vision!
    Apocalypse World is about scarcity. It's about what you have and what you need. The Harm & Healing rules in AW really follow from that--your harm clock is a depleting resource, damage is heavily influenced by who has managed to get what equipment, and the healing rules are about how much medicine the angel is willing to spend on you when other people need it too. Likewise, the wealth rules are a near-direct port of the scarcity-focused Barter. I think maybe leaving a character's financial resources to the fiction and the conversation rather than codifying it in the playbooks is a solution that works well for an Ebon Eaves type environment. Maybe an optional "Credit Rating" stat for settings where wealth is going to be more important, but I think it works better as a fixed stat than a pool of currency.

    Sanity is, of course, the one place were the scarcity of a resource matters. But even then, It's poorly served with a re-named version of the AW harm rules. I have some further thoughts on that that I'll write up and post in a bit.

I'd even get rid of "threaten" as a core move--"Go Aggro" is about how violent threats work in a world of casual violence, not how they work in a sleepy new England town. I think "convince" can work for general threats, while a criminal playbook might have something along the lines of "an offer you can't refuse".

    Whether the players or the Keeper is in control of the mystery is an important distinction, but the real groundbreaking aspect of these moves is that is changes the mystery from something that's planned in advance to something that's created spontaneously on the fly. That's not necessarily a bad idea or unMythos-like. If done correctly, I think it could be amazing. But it does require a lot more work on the part of the players and the GM. So if that's the route you choose, own it! (although I will say this: mystery generation is the one thing that tremulus does really well. Ebon Eaves creates a planned-in-advance mystery that still incorporates significant player input. Moves that pivot the game toward planned-on-the-fly mystery will dilute this strength somewhat)

    \n Some Ideas for shock/sanity:

    As I said before, I don't think the shock rules (just re-skinned harm rules) are particularly good at capturing what i find compelling about sanity in Lovecraftian horror.

    Trail of Cthulhu describes sanity very well:
    Exposure to the truths of the Cthulhu Mythos shatters the core of the human psyche by stripping away all illusions of human significance, benign nature, and loving God, leaving nothing but the terrifying vistas of stark, cosmic nihilism.
    And, importantly, the moment where it all falls apart
    The key moment in any Lovecraft story \u2013 or in any drama whatsoever \u2013 is when the truth comes out and all hell breaks loose. Aristotle called it the anagnorisis; with a nod to Raymond Chandler, we call it the \u201cbig reveal.\u201d

    In the Pulp idiom, the big reveal is nothing more than the solution to the mystery, or finally getting to see the monster in a good light (or through a rifle scope). But in the Purist idiom, it is the moment that the main character pieces together the nature of the Mythos and goes to pieces \u2013 when Wilmarth sees the face and hands on the desk, when Thurston realizes the Cthulhu cult is still active and goes to his fate, when Olmstead realizes he has the Deep One taint, when young Danforth looks back and discerns that Poe and Alhazred were not writing fiction or mysticism.
    \nSo as far as the game goes, I think the best sanity system
    a) follows the narrative of something being worn away
    b) brings the character closer to truth, making them more powerful as it destroys them
    c) hits at a narratively appropriate point, not just when the character happens to see their fifth dead body

    The Heat system used in "Hood: Big Fish in a Small Pond" is close to what I want: Characters gain heat by doing things that attract notice of the authorities, and lose it by lying low. Characters advance when Heat+XP reaches five, so the more heat the more quickly a character advances. A character with 5 heat is immediately burned (out of the game), and as a move, the MC can have the character roll + heat with the potential of getting burned.

    This satisfies b and c from my list above, but doesn't really satisfy a: sanity isn't really something you can manage in the same way that characters in Hood manage heat.

    The other system I was looking at was fallout from Dogs in the Vineyard. In DitV, during a conflict, characters receive "fallout dice" depending on the type of conflict, ranging from d4s for exchanging words to d10s for exchanging bullets. At the end of the conflict, the characters roll all the dice they've accumulated during the conflict, add the two highest, and suffer the consequences accordingly. (while rolling 1 on a conflict die gives XP)

    When you're subjected to a shock, add a die to your shock pool based on the source:

    Mundane: d4 (war, murder, crime)
    Supernatural: d6 (omens, visions, witches, cultists)
    Gods & Monsters: d8 (ghosts, ghouls, deep ones, the Black Man)
    Truly Alien: d10 (shoggoths, yithians, cthulhu, Azathoth)

    When the narrators instructs you to, roll all of the dice in your shock pool. If any of the dice roll 1, gain an advance. Then take the two highest dice and add them together:

    1-7: choose one
    \u2022 take -1 ongoing
    \u2022 act under pressure to avoid freezing or fumbling
    \u2022 lose a point of trust with another character present
    8-11: choose one
    \u2022 faint
    \u2022 fight, heedless of further danger
    \u2022 flee, heedless of further danger
    12-15:gain a minor insanity, plus choose from the 1-7 list
    16-19:gain a major insanity, plus choose from the 8-11 list
    20: You go irrevocably, permanently Insane

    \n Great stuff, everyone. Thinking about the basic sanity rules, how about we divide between mundane shock (mangled bodies etc.) and supernatural/mythos realizations. So basic shock would look a lot like the regular Harm rules, maybe:

    When you encounter something terrible and traumatic, roll +reason to shake it off. On a hit, take -1 Forward to your next roll. On a miss, take -1 Ongoing until you find some solace (either confiding in another or in the bottom of the bottle). On any roll lower than a 10, choose one of the following:

    ~ You cry out, drawing attention to yourself.
    ~ You lose your footing.
    ~ You lose your grip on whatever you're holding.
    ~ You lose track of someone or something you're attending to.
    ~ You miss noticing something important.
    ~ It's too much\u2014you leave the space and refuse to return there.

    Mythos shock would use new moves entirely:

    When a clue of the true nature of the universe is revealed to you, gain +1 Mythos point.

    These are sort of a blessing and a curse. Scenario-specific moves will require you to roll against +Mythos at the end of the investigation to ward off the monstrous doom that threatens to enter the world and overcome all decency and truth. Maybe you need them to understand a blasphemous tome, or to understand a riddle in the Dreamlands, or to perform a hideous rite and seal an evil from this dimension forever. Each investigation should have a unique end-move (or moves) to match its theme and feel. Since Mythos points are largely subconscious until the big reveal, they cannon be shared between investigators, and each investigator has their own pool of points.

    +Mythos is also used to cast spells and to go insane. You can try to bleed it off earlier with coping techniques, but that is risky and in any case reduces the Mythos points that you will need to ultimately succeed.

    When you try to subdue the lying madness that lurks in your subconscious and you have some downtime, roll +reason to rationally reject it, +passion to accept it, +might to trust in own self-confidence, +luck to distract yourself from it or +affinity to confide in another. Subtract your Mythos points from your roll. On a miss, your condition worsens\u2014choose three from below. On a hit, choose one from below and take -1 Forward on your next roll. On a 7-9, lose 1 Mythos point as you banish the thoughts from your mind. On a 10+, you are able to delude yourself for now\u2014Lose 3 points of Mythos.

    ~ You begin to demonstrate the signs of a mental illness (or your previous illness worsens).
    ~ Your denial opens up new subconscious vistas. Gain +1 Mythos.
    ~ You have nagging doubts, take -1 Ongoing until you roll +Mythos again.
    ~ Your distant and delusional manner pushes others away from you. The Keeper will choose one bond for you to forfeit.

    When you are confronted directly with the lying madness and can no longer deny it, roll +Mythos. On a 10+, you are devoured. The investigator is removed from play (and possibly from the world). On a 7-9, choose three from the following list. On a miss, choose two from the following list.

    ~ -1 Ongoing until you receive professional psychiatric treatment.
    ~ You go numb with cosmic dread and any roll is treated as acting under pressure in addition to the intended move until the investigation is over.
    ~ You black out. What you do next and what happens to you is at the Keeper's discretion.
    ~ Otherworldly beings take note of you and enter the world.
    ~ The truth withers you. Reduce one of your attributes to -1 permanently and gain a permanent +1 to any roll of +Mythos as you walk down the sorcerer's path.
    ~ You go mad. Work out a relevant mental illness to the psychological damage that you have been dealt.

    Happy New Years, everyone!

    \nI think this move actually works against the premise of the game (which is: investigation). If I know there is a body in the conservatory, why would I go in and look at it? I KNOW I'm getting a -1 for it, and my next roll is probably the one to investigate the crime scene, so that will go badly too. Basically, it means actually investigating the horror stuff is gonna suck, and so discourages it. Usually, you get something when you go risking harm, here you get nothing. It doesn't matter if I'm supposed to be playing a wussy Lovecraftian victim who faints at a bad smell, if I'm supposed to be advocating for my character, then I'm going to avoid stuff if I know it's going to screw with my chances to actually do things properly. Instead, I'll go do other things so I can actually manage to do something properly. Except they won't be the mystery, and so they won't be fun.

    It's a lot simpler to remove the -1s and make it just like act under fire. Everybody knows it's horror, so a roll where the benefit is you act normally when confronted by gruesome things won't go amiss.

    Something that would actually encourage investigation might look something like this, though: On a 10+, take +1 forward as the adrenaline kicks in. On a 7-9, the GM chooses your immediate reaction (reel back, flinch, cry out, drop something, etc) but after that you're back in control. On a miss, GM's call and you don't handle it well, of course.

    Then if I already know there's a body, of course I will go in, why wouldn't I? If I roll good enough, I'll get a bonus to the investigate roll. Sweet!
