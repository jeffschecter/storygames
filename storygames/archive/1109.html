Some background so you know what you're looking at.

Errantry is a storygame of medieval quests. It is fantasy as the medievals knew it. You play a Knight, a Warrior Princess, a Brave Damsel, a Troubadour, an Enchanter/Enchantress, a Friar or a Nun. You encounter wise hermits, dark knights, perilous sorceresses and terrifying beasts as you valourously strive to complete your quest.

It's built on a chassis of the Solar System (Clinton's system for The Shadow of Yesterday), somewhat customized.

The quest takes place in episodes, self-contained encounters which advance, delay or divert you on your quest.

There are several rotating roles which together add up to a distributed GM (inspired by Jasper's City of Birds). The one I will focus on here is the "Tenan" (so named for setting-related reasons), who runs the NPCs for the scene/episode. The NPCs are referred to as "encounter characters".

You have two packets of cards, one of the 12 court cards and one of the 40 non-court cards. The Tenan draws a court card (known as the Encounter card) at the beginning of the scene. This card is associated with a particular encounter character.

If the card has come up before, the Tenan grabs the related character sheet and it's all on.

If not, or if the character has been killed or something, the Tenan draws two cards from the packet of non-court cards. These are the Tens card and the Ones card, and they together give a percentile.

The Tenan looks this up on a chart (yet to be developed) which tells him or her what kind of character has been encountered. He or she then uses the suit of the three cards and the denomination of the court card in several different ways to generate the crunch for the encounter character. The colour can be chosen freely from lists.

The overview of the process is here and an example using the Knight character type is here.

If you want more detail, the whole process of playing out an episode is here and the overview of the whole game is here.

Now, the answer to the Pointed One:

I'm looking for feedback on how manageable or otherwise the card-based chargen seems. Is it too fiddly? Too confusing? Poorly explained? Ill-conceived?

I'm also looking for alternative brilliant suggestions of how I could achieve the same thing more simply or effectively, preferably without adding any further card draws, die rolls etc.

Thanks in advance.

    OK, one thought for possible simplification. The size of the NPCs' pools doesn't really matter, they're not going to get to refresh them. The number of points in the pools does matter.

    We have 3 cards, we have 3 pools.

    A range of 0-3 would work OK for the pools. We have 4 suits.

    What I'm thinking is, on the yet-to-be-created Encounter Character Sheet, I set up a grid with some checkboxes. The rows are Vigour, Courtesie and Artes (my three pools). The columns are labelled with the symbols for Club, Diamond, Heart in that order (Spades are zero).

    Your encounter card is Vigour, Tens is Courtesie, Ones is Artes. You tick along the boxes from left to right until you reach the one with the suit of the card you hold. This shows how many points you have in your pool.

    That reduces the number of tables you are looking up by one, and should speed and simplify the process noticeably.
