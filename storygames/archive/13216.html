    \n Hey, Paul, I really like that thread (or better, the idea behind that thread), but frankly, I think it belongs here much more than on RPGnet's "design" subforum (the fact that only I replied is probably an indicator).

    If we're talking about adding mechanics to OSR games, I think that will be received as heresy by many OSR people. I think OSR has pretty much achieved what it set out to do:
    -to revive and preserve the "old" game
    -to continue publishing material for it and to support it

    So I don't think there's anything "next" for OSR other than to continue doing this, in bigger and better style (see the recent merging of efforts by Raggi and McKinney).

    On the other hand, even the DIY, the house-rules, variants, free-wheeling approach are somewhat antithetic to Gary Gygax's original vision (he often derided "house rules", even forbade them, mocked the "amateur press" and so on). It's about people's gaming, not the actual game. Many authors (specifically Raggi and McKinney, mentioned above) are pushing their own visions. The inclusion of elves, dwarves and halflings in LotFP was a necessity of the fact that it's an retro-clone, but then again, it's very much not in line with LotFP's aesthetic, imho. The demi-humans should totally be thrown out. Likewise, the systematized, list-based, pseudo-scientific Vancian magic system seems very much contrary to Raggi's presentation of magic. He argues that it doesn't matter, since magic is, well, magic, and doesn't make sense, but I think his talk of magic as something wild, strange, unique, non-repeatable, non-scientific etc. goes very much contrary to how magic actually works in LotFP. There's only so far that mere colour can go. "System" is "not limited to" rules, yes, but it is also "including".

    Therefore I can totally see that certain sensibilities of the old-school will get re-incorporated, revived, pushed forward, reinvented etc, in a different framework*. But that won't be the OSR anymore, I think.

    *In fact it's already happening big-time. Red Box Hack, Old School Hack, Dungeon World (even Apocalypse World, to a degree), Dungeon Slayers, Shadow, Sword & Spell, Storming the Wizard's Tower...all these are games that are not actually retro-clones of the boxed sets, but are leaking old-school flavour. Even the 4E Red Box and Essentials products are exhibiting some nostalgic sensibilities (Mike Mearls is a big time oD&D/AD&D gamer).

    \n Paul, I agree that The Elfish Gene is a very important glimpse of a social landscape, but I think what it's showing us is the birth of trad and the eclipse of old-school. It's what happens when a bunch of pre-teen kids take games that were made by & for mature adults to do collaborative improv, and use them as the turf for peer-group dominance hierarchy battles. I teach a D&D; afterschool class for kids age 8-11, and I'm ready to attest that kids at the upper end of that range will turn anything into a struggle for superiority (although they're particularly drawn to 4E because of all the quantitative ways it offers to show how your guy is better than someone else's guy).
    \n
    \nSeen in a certain light, all the rest of the history of RPGs follows from that moment when kids became the dominant market and, eventually, kids who grew up playing that way designed RPGs to fix the problems that came from their primal social landscape. Every occasion for creative GM adjucation becomes a bitter fight due to lack of mature consensus-building skills --> hundreds of pages of tightly defined universal mechanics covering every case. Players don't want to care about anything because it'll give a vindictive DM a handle to screw them with --> systems for explicitly defining your character's goals and beliefs and delimiting the ways they arise in play.
    \n
    \nAs I explained on Canon Puncture, I see the defining mechanics of old-school as random encounters, morale checks, reaction rolls, and tables for random generation of treasures, dungeons, courtesans, etc. Games that give a big role to these subvert a DM's ability to tell a pre-planned story, and instead encourage them to get into an open mindset where unexpected inputs from the dice or the characters' actions and reactions can be incorporated into an ongoing collaboration of awesomeness that the DM discovers & is surprised by just like the players. To my mind, it's post-AD&D; 1E editions that successively increase the DM's ability to drive their personal vision of awesome, whether that's by advising the DM to ignore wandering monster checks, fudge dice rolls, or skip the "un-fun parts", or by replacing mechanics that create unpredictability with ones that let you more reliably quantify how much of a party's resources a pre-planned encounter will drain.
    \n
    \nI wasn't there, but from my brief conversations with Arneson and Wesely I get the sense that the truly old-school, pre-Barrowcliffe social landscape was populated by adults (Gygax was 33, Arneson was 24) who'd learned how to create social cohesion and collaborative adjucation by playing wargames like Braunstein that had imaginative systems instead of rules, or like Chainmail that had a parcel of sub-systems meant to be situationally adopted, modified, or swapped out for new inventions as the players desired. While Gygax eventually went on to drive his own personal vision of awesome via AD&D;'s standardized rules and Greyhawk's solitary world-creation, all the evidence suggests that Arneson's original campaign was highly collaborative; the action was driven by player initiative in settling baronies and pursuing the individual "special interests" that turned gold into experience, and the famous villains of Blackmoor were created by players who Arneson approached to contribute their creativity.
    \n
    \nI think the most exciting work the OSR has been doing at places like Fin's OD&D; boards is to make explicit some of the assumptions and approaches of this earliest style of play. I personally don't want to see a set of mechanics regulating the social landscape - I much prefer what Trollsmyth calls the neo-classical approach, where the central activity of play (a mutual & always-surprising group creation of awesomeness) flourishes unconstrained in the space suggested by the peripheral mechanics that are defined (in this case, the kind of random encounter tables and town-stocking procedures that Ry describes upthread).
    \n
    \nIf I had to choose just one thing that new-school approaches could to contribute to the OSR, it's the practice of calling out and explicating what the existing rules are meant to do and how you can use them in play to achieve the desired effect. Ry's description of when to use wandering monsters is a good example, as is LotFP's referee rulebook, as are the Apocalypse World MC guidelines.

    \n Regarding trad vs. old-school I don't think I've ever seen it discussed in this way explicitly, but I feel it's very much implied in a lot of discussions. When you say "trad" to people, they'll probably think of Rifts and GURPS and maybe even White Wolf, while old-school stands almost exclusively for oD&D (also Traveller etc.). The distinction is there, it just hasn't been expressed.


    Re: "random" dice vs. coherent vision... I think (emphasize 'think') the real trouble started when

    1. the DM's "personal vision of awesome" was replaced by published materials. Official metaplots, published settings and the like, and...
    2. rules upon rules were added to ensure "mechanical coherence" in addition to the "coherence of vision", because the game became more and more about competition. More crunch gave players something to fall back on to when the GM's fiat was deemed "unfair" (or vice-versa, when a DM was accused of being too hard, he could point at the book and say "it says so in here).

    In both occasions, the official, sanctioned version became "safe" and reliable, whereas people seemingly lost the ability to really create something collaboratively.

    In fact, I perceive a very strong trend from oD&D to 4E that's consistently removing any really dangerous or "punitive" mechanics (such as save or die) and "balancing" encounters to the point where a single encounter in 4E is specifically intended to draw out a number of the party's resources, but not kill or defeat the party. It's very much about "playing it safe". While there are obvious benefits to this, it's obvious that the game had to turn its back on other aspects to achieve this goal.

    So, the next one I'm running the characters are rogues and adventurers in 16th Century Florence.

    Where does fiat come in? Fiat is the most powerful and beneficial tool in my arsenal as a GM. When nobody knows what will happen or what will matter in play, clearly we can't have rules for everything. For other reasons I wouldn't want that anyway. So, many things will come up for which there are no rules.

    I, as GM, will make a call. Now, I dislike the word fiat in part because it implies to me arbitrariness, that I make my call in a vacuum. I don't. If a situation arises, players may make suggestions, I may ask what people think, but after taking any inputs that are offered or asked for I'll make a call and we'll go on.

    What I don't do is override the dice. The dice fall where they may. When a player lets something go to the dice, they're saying that the conflict is important enough to them for failure to be acceptable. That's why I don't worry about PCs getting ganked by unimportant guards. Why are you in a fight to the death with an unimportant guard? If you are, you accept you might get pointlessly killed.

    So, fiat is to allow the GM to come up with consistent rulings that support the shared vision of the world (which is built on, but not constricted to, the GM's vision). GM fiat is not for overriding the dice, that way lies storygaming in the White Wolf sense.

    One last thing, for me rules getting out of the way is a positive good. It's not that I don't believe there are rules which can support a particular style of play or experience, it's that for what I want the most effective way to achieve that is rules' transparency. A very common theory error is to say "ah, that's because you haven't found the right supportive ruleset yet". Iff you find yourself thinking that, I'd strongly recommend listening more to why people want the rules to fade away. It's not because they don't matter.
