\n On the one hand having your character die is pretty shitty (at least depending on the game, for the most part I'd say that's true though), but on the other hand, it's that shitty feeling that makes things have more meaning when you escape death. So having character death be too frequent and random is a bad thing, because the shitty feeling actually comes up, but having it not be possible (for certain players and games) is just as bad a thing because then you don't have the satisfaction which comes with completing a difficult and risky task.

What are ways that have been done with systems that mechanically can have your character die, theoretically in any encounter with a string of bad results, but are set up so that in reality it doesn't happen much, without cheapening that reality to the point that you don't expect character death and lose the satisfaction of not dying, but still get that sinking feeling when it actually happens?

    \n As far as I have ever been able to discover, there's only one way around this problem. First, one has to always make sure that every character death has meaning and importance within the overall structure of the narrative. This isn't too difficulty even in luck-heavy games, and it's dead easy elsewhere. Second, one has to ensure that players get more satisfaction from the overall narrative than they do from advancing their characters. This will in most cases either be a foregone conclusion or utterly impossible, depending upon whom you' replaying with.

    The one thing that bothers me about this feature of most RPGs in which there's any such thing as "player characters," (which excludes a surprisingly large number of games, e.g. Universalis, Everyone is John) is that it requires an absolutely non-negotiable sacrifice of realism, since in the real world there are obviously plenty of deaths that are pure happenstance, and plenty of people who won't be able to derive comfort from a narrative in compensation for this fact. I've occasionally seen hints that some games (Apocalypse World, maybe?) make an aggressive attempt to be more realistic in this respect. I'd be curious to hear what others have to say about the topic, at any rate.

    \nEDIT: On reflection, it seems that two conflicting definitions of tension have been collated into one argument. First, there's the satisfaction of Narrative Tension, e.g. what's going to happen next? As a reader, viewer or player of a story, I can't be sure where that story is going and what revelations will be made or what fates will befall each character. A random, meaningless death produces the kind of shitty experience mentioned.
    \n
    \nOn the other hand though, there is Sporting Tension, e.g. will I win or lose? As a gamer, I try to optimise my strategies for the best outcome and min/max everything as far as possible, but I still can't be sure that some random bad luck will wipe me out... or secure my absolute victory. I rely on luck for both, so absolute victory and random death are consequences I accept at the start when I sit down to play.
    \n
    \nSo, if I want a satisfying narrative experience, I want guarantees that my character's death will not be random and meaningless; whereas for a satifsying sporting experience, I want the sensation of having played well and beaten the odds, which requires the risk of losing/dying. Therefore, I'll make choices at the very start as to which type of game I want to play, which will provide the type of experience I'm looking for.

    \n Some ways to make it not suck:

    Make it not matter thematically (See 3:16)

    Make it very much matter thematically (See TSOY)

    Make it irrelevant to the player's engagement (See Grey Ranks)

    Remove character monogamy (See Archipelago II)

    Make it a cool stepping stone to further play (See Hero's Banner)

    Make it a binary choice (See Montesgur 1244)

    In none of these games can death suck.

    \n Also, it helps to think about why death sucks in whatever game you're playing.

    Does it suck because the character fails at accomplishing his/her goals? (And if so, does it suck more or less than the other ways that the character might fail? Could the stakes for most conflicts actually be something besides just life or death, and would that make the few times when death is on the table seem to matter more?)

    Or does it suck because it punishes the player, by taking them out of the game until they can make a new character, get their dead character resurrected, and/or come back into the game? (If this is the case, why are you punishing the player? Could there be another way to have a character die without making it impossible for the player to continue participating in the game? Could character creation and re-introduction be streamlined? Could the dead character continue to participate in the fight at some penalty, and only actually die at the end of the scene? Could the player participate in some other way: getting to spend his remaining fate points on the actions of other players, running NPC allies, etc.?)

    Or does it suck because it ruins the story? (Does the group feel like the story is better when the original characters see it through to the end? Is there a way to make the loss of a character and/or the introduction of replacement characters something that enhances the story rather than taking away from it?)

    \n So playing Burning Empires I had a big revelation about this. What makes this work is that there has to be something else at stake in the fight OTHER than the character dying. In Burning Empires using a Firefight conflict simply to kill someone is against the rules. You have to explicitly what you tactically hope to achieve with the fire fight and life and death is a BY PRODUCT of the fight itself.

    I've now started taking note of this in fiction. I've been reading the Dresden Files books lately. The following is said with advance apologies to Mr. Butcher. In book four there's this extended multi-chapter fight in a Walmart. The bad guys have only one Agenda: Kill Dresden and his friend Murphy. Both are recurring characters and one is the star of the series. There is absolutely nothing else at stake in the scene. The only question on the table is do Dresden and Murphy get whacked by the bad guys? By the very nature of the media the only answer can be NO. It reduces the scene from one of conflict to one of exposition. Dresden discovers something important during the fight that lets him move the plot along. For me, the scene was incredibly protracted and uninteresting to read because of this.

    In book five, however, there's a scene where some bad guys show up. However, there's a lot more going on. First of all they seem interested in *capturing* not killing Dresden. There's a also a McGuffin they want. And third, there's a not-known-to-be-recurring third character in the scene who is a liability. Suddenly the scene becomes much more dynamic. We know Dresden can't die but there's A LOT more to lose in the scene than just his life. His freedom, the McGuffin and the life of the third character are all legitimately on the line.

    In an RPG context, you're generally free from the "on going series" constraint. You don't have a publisher to answer to which means your character can legitimately be on the line in such scenes. If you combine this with certain system elements that basically allow the player to yield the other things at stake in exchange for reduced chance of death then you put a lot of narrative power over death in the player's hands. Basically every moment of the fight is asking, "Is this worth dying for?"

    What that means is that if the player doesn't feel this is the "it" moment for his character then he can always stop. He can always say, "No, take the McGuffin, we'll fight again for it later." Or "No, let the NPC die, I'll avenger later." and so on. Whatever ELSE is at stake in the scene can be exchanged for the life of the PC and will therefore automatically create an interesting evolution in the developing story. If on the OTHER hand the player goes, no, this really IS worth dying for. And pushes themselves and actually DOES get himself killed well, then, that can't ever not be meaningful. We found the reason they were willing to die. After all they could have yielded and lived.

If so, be aware that both fictional attachment and particular degrees of risk aversion / attraction are variable enough to require your ideal game to heavily customize to the player, especially as reward/punishment dynamics of the "death risk" will necessarilly change a player's sweet spot.

    When talking probability in game design it is essential distinguish between the odds and the perception of the odds, they far too often have little to do with each other.

    - Mendel

    \nThere are two things going on here.

    Sky diving and bungie jumping work because they play on the reptilian brain: when you jump out of an airplane, your instinct is that OMG YOU ARE GOING TO DIE even though your rational brain knows you're relatively safe. When you take that leap, your amygdala estimates the chance of you dying at 100%, even though your cerebrum knows that unless there's equipment failure or you do something stupid, you're going to survive. You are going to have a very difficult time transferring that gut-level reaction to imaginary characters.

    And people are pattern-matchers. If you want the perception among players that recklessness plus bad luck, or merely bad luck, can kill a character, then you have to create a situation where recklessness plus bad luck, or merely bad luck, *does* kill a character. But if you want death to *also* be infrequent, you need to create a situation where recklessness and bad luck *don't* often kill characters, because characters are often reckless and players often have bad luck. If you create a situation where you say death is common and often a risk, but in play it winds up being infrequent, players will pay attention not to what you say but to what actually happens.

    You *can* have players know that character death is a frequent risk, but only if it really is.

      I'm thinking something like segmented health bars in video games. How it works is that your health bar is split into 4 sections. Each section regenerates by itself, but once it's taken out completely, it doesn't regenerate. So if each section has 100 points, if you're down to 1/100 you can survive and get back to 100/100, but if it hits 0/100, you've got until you can get healing 3/4 of your health left.       \nRandom PC death in Dogs in the Vineyard

      First, a most important feature of the game's rules: taking damage is a side effect of fighting, it's not how you resolve fights. You can take all the damage, yet win; you can dish out all the damage, yet lose.

      When you take damage, you take it in the form of damage dice, which you leave on the table unrolled. The stronger the hit, the more damage dice: 3 damage dice for the weakest hit, up to 6 damage dice for the strongest. The more dangerous the weapon, the bigger the damage dice: d6s for a punch, d8s for most weapons, d10s for a gunshot. So a basic solid punch gets you 3d6 damage dice, while a basic solid gunshot gets you 3d10, and the most serious, decisive gunshot gets you 6d10. (In fact it can go even higher than 6 dice, but I've seen that in play only maybe once or twice.)

      Because you're accumulating the damage dice unrolled, you can tell generally how badly you're hurt, but not specifically how badly you're hurt. This creates a powerful sense of risk in play. You can feel when the fight turns against you -- just look at all those big damage dice you're accumulating -- but maybe they'll roll well and you'll be okay! Or maybe they won't! It's your choice whether to stay in the fight, and you don't know how bad the consequences will really be, and you feel it right in the pit of your stomach.

      At the end of the fight, you roll all your accumulated damage dice. For purposes of living and dying, we care only about the highest two: sum the highest two and set the rest aside. If the highest two damage dice sum to 20, your character's dead or dying, and there's nothing anybody can do. If they sum to 16-19, he's dying, but immediate medical aid could still save him. If they sum to 15 or less, he's hurt, maybe badly hurt, and he might need medical aid after all, but basically he's not going to die. Worth noticing: a 20 is possible only if you've taken a bullet, but a 16 is possible if your enemy's brought out any weapon at all. This is because Dogs in the Vineyard is a western, so guns are where it's at.

      So here's you. You've seen a fight through to the bitter end. You've taken a couple of bullets but you don't know yet how bad they are -- I can say more about this if you like, some people choke on it even though it's very good sim -- and now it's time to sit down and pull open your shirt and see. You roll your big pile of damage dice, pull out the highest two, and...

      ...They sum to 20. This is, by far, the least likely result. A cool feature of the sum-the-highest-two mechanic is that no matter how many d10s you roll, a 19 is always more likely than a 20. The worse you get shot up, the more likely you are to die from it, but the more likely still that you'll cheat death by 1 point on the dice.

      When you do get that 20, it's guaranteed dramatically appropriate, because you stayed in the fight when you knew the risks. If you'd wanted to be safe, you'd've run away when the guns came out.

      ...Or else...

      ...They sum to 16-19. You've cheated death for now, but here's the cool thing about 16-19: now it's somebody else's job to save your life. The old risk -- that you've gotten yourself killed -- becomes a new risk, and you feel it in your stomach again.

      If there's no healer who can reach you in time, you die. Again, you knew it was possible when you stayed in the fight.

      If there is a healer, now the healer has to roll dice to save your life. The odds are stacked on the healer's side, so again, narrowly escaping death is the most likely outcome, but dying remains just possible enough to make you feel it.

      ...Or else...

      ...They sum to 15 or less. This is the most likely outcome if you've taken only one bullet, or if guns never came out. In fact, if it's been a knife fight or something, 15 is to 16 as 19 is to 20: the most likely outcome of a gunfight is that you'll narrowly cheat death; the most likely outcome of a knife fight is that you'll narrowly cheat the possibility of death.

      Whichever the case, you spend the whole fight acutely, viscerally aware of what you're risking, so that when the most likely thing happens and you cheat death, you feel like you've cheated death.

      I'm happy to go into more detail if you're curious about any part of it, but that's how it works in broad.

      -Vincent
