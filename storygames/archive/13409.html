\n Hopefully these are easy:

1. Has the issue of "I seize his life by force" been settled?

In other words, does "seizing someone's life" completely bypass the harm rules, or not? How so?

2. When two player characters get into a fight, what does it look like? Do we have to go to battle rules (i.e. enforce the two players taking turns to make a move)?

If you use a move on me, can I interfere with it?

If you attack me and roll a miss, does the MC inflict harm on my behalf, or what? What happens?

3. We're in a room together. You say, "I grab the door handle and run for it like crazy!" I say, "I shoot you as you run away!"

How does that work? Going aggro would always give the fleeing character the option of getting away without getting shot, it seems. Acting under fire for the fleeing character seems a little more likely, perhaps. But then it's totally out of the hands of the aggressor, which is odd given that he's really the active character in the scene, in a way. And does the shooter get to roll to interfere with the fleeing person's move, if that's what's going on?

I find this confusing. Help!

    \n On 2: you don't go to the battle rules unless it's a battle, meaning at least a few guys on either side. PC vs PC combat isn't better defined than any other combat: someone who wants to take the initiative says what they're doing, and you roll for it. Opposing someone's move with an Interfere roll can be done, as described on p. 207.
    \n
    \nOn 3: Going aggro doesn't always allow for run away, if you do the "I want you to get shot, or I'll shoot you" thing that's described in the book somewhere. If you roll 10+, they'll have to choose between getting shot or sucking it up, meaning getting shot. On 7-9 they can get the hell out of your way, so, you'll have to roll good to shoot someone who's all about running away. If anyone of them makes a roll, the other can interfere (though to do it they must do it, so if one player rolls act under fire to escape the shooter, the shooter might have to raise to actually following out the door in order to get an intereference roll). If the fleeing character is a PC and the shooter an NPC, then the latter can't roll so acting under fire it is.
    \n
    \nDon't know about 1. My personal call would be to bypass the harm rules for NPCs (since I'm looking at them through crosshairs anyway), but not for PCs. Taking definite hold of a PCs life would pretty much be to make them still be at your mercy after the first shot - say laying at your feet or still in your stranglehold, so you can pretty much roll again without doing much more. If the victim PC is still conscious however, they could get another chance at interfering. Not taking definite hold would open for the victim to roll, say, acting under fire or fuck this shit right after surviving the first seize roll.

    \n 1. I don't think this is an issue. I guess it depends on your group's preferences, but the book tells you to look through crosshairs and don't try to protect your NPCs, so if it's a NPC who had his life seized by force, and taken definite hold of it, yeah, they're dead. If it's a PC you might jockey it a bit, but both parties will probably take a lot of harm. One option, instead of just killing them would be to bring them to 12:00 and leave them at the other character's mercy. "Do you really, really kill them?"

    But in general, the answer is yes, if a life is seized by force, then it's seized. You might also want to look at this link by John Harper, about how Seizing by Force is/should be a peripheral move.

    The book also gives you examples like this for Going Aggro. If I sneak up behind you and slit your throat with a knife, I'm going aggro and what I want is for you to kneel over and die bleeding. You can either do as I say (and die) or suck it up and take damage from the knife and then we fight it out.

    2. I wouldn't say you should go to the battle rules. Just make sure everyone's got their chance to react and respond. After each move say "what do you do?" etc.

    If you can describe how you're interfering with them, sure you can. But frankly, do you just want a -1 on their roll or do you want to break out a move of your own? So a) make sure you have fictional support for your interfering and b) is it worth it?

    If he misses his attack, then the MC makes a hard move. One of which would be to turn over to you and say "what do you do?". They just blew their roll, and now it's "your turn". That's pretty hard in itself. I wouldn't inflict harm as a MC, unless it was specified by the move that there is harm involved.

    3. Whoa whoa whoa. Why are you running away? Did this actually happen in a game? What's the reason you bolted? That tells us whether you're acting under fire or whatever. Was he aiming a gun at you? What was happening?
    If not, you're doing it. You grab the door handle and are running. Then the other person shoots after you. What is he doing? Is the trying to kill you? Stop you from running? Just scaring you with the bullets. Either way, it's Go Aggro or Manipulate. If it's Go Aggro, he says what he wants you to do and rolls and you do it, suck it up or do something else, depending on the roll. If it's manipulation and he rolls a hit, you can stop and mark XP or
    you don't and it's his loss.

    \n Another big issue: mechanically, combat in Apocalypse World is just not that interesting. The interesting part is *why* people are shooting at each other or trying to kill each other, and the *why* of the situation, as well as the details, is how you choose what moves are going on.

    For issue 1: can you seize a life by force? I'd say, with NPCs, of course you can. If you take definite hold of an NPC's life, that means you get in a position to kill him, then and there -- you have their life in your hands, but they're not dead yet, and I-the-MC get to make a move now. Most of the time, I'm looking through crosshairs and being a fan of the characters, so the move I make is going to be informed by that: is it going to be cooler if the NPC just dies, or do I want to raise the stakes by offering an opportunity with a cost? For PCs, I'd probably say much the same thing, only the other PC also has the option to interfere. "Okay, so you're not quite sure where you lost control, but there was a scuffle, and Balls wound up on top of you. There's blood running from his nose and he's got a nasty cut on his arm [you inflicted 2-harm on him], but you're feeling pretty beat up too, and one of your eyes is swelling shut [he inflicted 2-harm on you]. He's got both hands on your throat, and you're having a hard time breathing. It's obvious he wants to kill you. What do you do?"

    As far as the simultaneous thing: follow the fiction. Someone is acting, and someone reacting. The person acting is making a move; the person reacting is either doing nothing, helping or hindering, or making a move once the first move is resolved. It's not really whoever speaks first, although it's likely to coincide with that a great deal. It's also not going to happen right out of the blue that one character says "I seize his life by force!" -- there's going to be a setting and a backstory, possibly some interaction (opportunities to read the situation and the other character).

    For issue 2: I think it's implicit in the rules (but unfortunately not explicit) that anyone gets to respond to a move with another move, once the first move is resolved. If Player A tries to seize Player B's life by force and fails, the MC gets to make a move in response to that, and Player B gets to make a move in response to that as well.

    Now, of course Player B can also interfere with Player A, but remember: if you do it, you do it; to do it, you have to do it. *How* is Player B interfering with Player A? Again, if you play with just the mechanics, it gets boring fast, because the mechanics are meant to support and enhance the fiction, not to replace it. "I seize his life by force." "I interfere." No - what you want is, "Before he knows it, I have my machete out, and I'm tackling him with it. I want to have him on the ground under me, at my mercy. He's armed, so that's seizing his life by force." "I have warning? I'm going to try to get my arm up near my neck, so that if he succeeds he won't be able to cut my throat, and try to punch him in the face. That's interference."
    And for example 3, there are two ways to model it.

    Player A opens the door and flees. Player B opens fire to prevent him from running away.

    Model 1: Player B goes aggro on Player A. The desired behavior is to stay there. If Player B rolls a hard success (possibly with Player A's interference), then Player A gets the choice to get shot and run away, or to stay there and not get shot. If Player B rolls a soft success, Player A gets an expanded list of options, which can be interpreted as getting away without getting shot. Them's the breaks: Player A was not as intimidating as he thought, or not hard enough to pull the trigger fast enough.

    Model 2: Player A tries to escape, and so is acting under fire, with the fire being, well, gunfire. Player B can interfere with that, of course. If Player A rolls a hard success, the character gets away. If Player A rolls a soft success, the GM gets to offer a worse outcome, a hard bargain, or an ugly choice -- "You get away, but you take 2-harm from bullets." Or, "You can get away, but *only if* you take 2-harm from bullets."

    But the question I'd ask is, fundamentally, why are two PCs trying to kill each other in the first place? And the question there is not meant to imply that they shouldn't be. But if they are, there's probably some context that's developed for it, and that would really inform a lot of the decisions I'd make if presented with this. And part of the reason the questions are so tough is that they're so abstract: when you get to the point where PCs are trying to kill each other, you should have a lot of history and context to work with.

    (If I had two characters who wanted to kill each other from the opening moments of the first session, I'd point them at page 101: "Your characters don\u2019t have to be friends, but they do have to know each other, and they should be basically allies. They might become enemies in play, but they shouldn\u2019t start out enemies." And part of that is that you need the context and the backstory to have developed by the time they actually become enemies.)

    \n My interpretation trying to stick to the rules as written (which may be wrong as I don't believe they explicit cover these situations):

    - Whomever acts first, rolls first.
    - The other player may use the Help/Interfere rules as normal.
    - Then the MC plays any Moves if applicable, and turns to the next player and says, "what do you do?"

    I don't think taking definitive hold of another PC using Seize by Force should allow you to bypass the Harm rules and automatically kill someone. PCs take Harm differently than NPCs. And this could rob the target PC from ever responding. But I would rule that if you take definitive hold of another PC, you can Go Aggro on them while restraining them instead of rolling Seize by Force again.

    I have not seen a situation where it wasn't clear who acted first. If PC 1 says I run, and then PC 2 says I shoot him as he runs, PC 1 goes first. The only reason PC 2 is shooting PC 1 is because PC 1 is running, so obviously running happened first. The opposite is true. If 1 PC says they shoot and the other says they run to avoid being shot, the person shooting goes first.

    Posted By: Paul T.You mentioned that the specifics of the story, and the history between the characters, would inform your decision.

    So, as MC, given this situation, and the two players shouting out pretty much simultaneously:
    - "I run for it!"
    - "I shoot him!"

    ...what factors would you want to know? What's an example of something that would make you go with Going Aggro, and what's an example of something that would make you rather handle it as a Acting Under Fire situation?
    \nWell, I think it's a reasonable assumption that you didn't sit down at the table and say "Okay, everybody ready to start our first session of Apocalypse World?" and then both players declared these things. What situation are the players in? What's motivating Character A to run for it? Why does he think he's in danger? What's motivating Character B to shoot? Why does she think she needs to shoot?

    If Character B is stomping around and scowling at Character A, so that character A decides to take the initiative and get out of dodge before Character B decides to shoot, then Character A is acting and Character B is reacting, so the move is Character A acting under fire.

    If Character A thinks nothing is wrong, and Character B takes the initiative to make an intimidating move, then Character B is acting and Character A is reacting, so it's Character B going aggro.

    In the fiction, one of the characters is *acting*, and the other character is *reacting*. The one that is *acting* is the one making the first move.

    \n See, Paul, now you're getting it!

    Whether or not Character A is making a move is dependent on the fiction that comes before. What exactly Character B is doing is dependent on context. You know what is going on because you made the example up (or saw it at your table), but when we have "Character A runs for the door, Character B shoots at him," that can generate a whole bunch of different scenarios depending on what sort of context those two actions are given.

    In general: whoever talks first should go first. If it's NOT a move, it just happens, let other players react to it, or let the same player continue talking. If it IS a move, maybe be a bit more strict about letting other players react, but don't be super-strict about the round-robin turn-taking. If you're in a battle, make sure everybody gets a turn each tick.

    Shooting a guy, for a PC, is nearly always a move in the final text. I think the examples make it pretty clear that only if somebody is completely helpless do you just dish out the harm with no roll. If it's an NPC shooting at the PC, do the Acting Under Fire roll for sure, though.
