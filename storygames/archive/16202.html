    \nI'm designing the ingredient list this year, but it'll be Jonathan that posts it and the theme.

    In past years, I've been frustrated by ingredient lists that were too cohesive and pointed. Last year, we had a Shakespearean theme and some very Shakespearean ingredients as well. The result was a field that was more narrow than necessary.

    The ingredients this year will be as divergent and disconnected as possible. Something along the lines of "tentacle, repair, envy, travelogue." Though, of course, having said those words you can now guarantee that none of them will be on the final list.

      Is a simple text document, with-out layout acceptable? What about no graphics, printer friendly version? Supplementary material is not counted towards the word count? Can you enter more than one game?
      \nYes to all of these.\n
      Do you just go on what the word count button says?
      \nThe wordcount is a rough guideline to keep the games focused and not sprawling messes that are too much work for peer-reviewers and judges. Consequently, yeah, I would just go by the word count on whatever writing program you're using. If that's too complicated or vague, just try to keep it to 4-5 pages, 11-point font or bigger.\n
      What is considered supplementary material?
      \nIt varies, but can include anything that's not part of explaining how to play the game. Typically these are things that the peer-reviewers and judges don't have to read or comment on in great detail, but can skim to quickly understand the purpose. Character sheets, maps, random tables, diagrams, playmats, cards, or whatever other stuff is useful for playing your game, but not part of the core rules.

      From #1: "GM has 'Puzzle Chips'. He can slap one of these down when he wants to put a puzzle between the player and his goal, which he needs to use his skills and player ingenuity to get past."

      From #3: [It] seems best if played for raw comedy, with the joke basically being on the guy character; otherwise there's no real point. [...] I greatly appreciate the uncompromising "yes, she's nuts" content, which made me laugh out loud several times during reading.

      From #4: Here you have characters that are thrust into the end of the world and they don't seem to be able to give up their previous cultural biases.

      Those ideas, coupled with "doctor," are giving me interesting ground to work with: a game about a doctor (perhaps a psychologist?) and a "crazy person" (perhaps with latent psychic powers?), in a world where those categories are starting to break down. It's the end times. Is the doctor merely "stuck in the past"? Is the crazy person merely "stuck in the future"? The old definitions of reason and sanity are eroding in the face of a new and alien world.

      And along the way, the two have... puzzles to solve? Yeah! Maybe the puzzles require, in order to resolve them, some kind of "negotiating what reality and sanity mean, in this new world" process.

      So, the game is about two wanderers in the end-times: a shrink and a purportedly crazy person. They both have to contend with newly-negotiated definitions of sanity. And solve puzzles in order to keep on keepin' on.

      Thread 18531: How do you reduce Sim/Gam prep? (2006)

      \n

      Not a bad thread - most inspirational quote for me was...

      \n
      \n

      The GM's role is to create ambiguous Situations and the players' "role" is to clarify/resolve that ambiguity when addressing said Situation. The Dream is expanded...

      \n
      \n

      Thread 28109:
      \nVictoria detectives and dice bidding (2009)

      \n

      Brief (three-post) discussion of the intriguing possibilities and inherent limitations of story-stick techniques (bidding for narrative control).

      \n
      \n

      I'd argue that success or failure MUST be tied to the fiction in a very strong way. Furthermore, game currency should be tied to the things a player has his character do. These things can't just be random. They need to be modified in strong ways by the fiction's situation.

      \n
      \n

      Thread 28649: Research: rewarding idealism (2009)

      \n

      OP is "playing with notions of reward incentives for following specific philosophies, without really caring about what the philosophies are." Many games are mentioned, notably Vulpinoid's Rajah Spiny Rat, and Vulpinoid chimes in...

      \n
      \n

      My current games are working with the notion that everyone is defined by paths that they follow. A player can choose to have a very one dimensional character who follows only a single path, or they can become as diverse as they want by following multiple paths. Such paths include everything from occupations, religions, personality types, subcultures, racial types, supernatural powers, virtues, vices, etc. If it's not a flat number and it helps you twist the narrative to your advantage, it fits into one of these paths.

      \n

      On the down side, every path has a disadvantage associated with it. If your a cop, you might get a bonus to investigation skills and you might have a decent familiarity with firearms...but on the down side, you'll be expected to behave in a certain manner at all times, and quite often you'll be assigned to tasks by your superiors whether you want to or not.

      \n

      Every time you accept the flaw of your path, you're mastery in the path increases and you get advantages.

      \n
      \n

      Thread 25890: Candycreeps -- aesthetics and mechanics (2008)

      \n

      Far-ranging discussion of a game-in-progress about goth kids that (among other things) encompasses two aesthetic traits: Cute and Creepy. There is a reference to TSoY that includes the following...

      \n
      \n

      So, if you do Cute and Creepy as pools, you could refresh them by being Cute for the sake of being Cute, and being Creepy for the sake of being Creepy.

      \n
      \n

      ...which is a pretty adorable idea.
