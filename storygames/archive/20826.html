    \n There isn't really any hard and fast rule for "How much should I make up and how much should I 'farm out' to the players?" I think the best answer is "If you have a totally awesome idea for something, you should use your totally awesome idea, and if you have no idea, you should ask someone else, and for everything in between, use your judgement." but that's not actually super helpful. Unfortunately, I think this is an art rather than a science.

    In terms of what to cut and leave in - well, Lady Blackbird doesn't have that much pre-existing stuff. It gives you a starting situation, and some characters, mostly. So where you "wrap" your session is largely up to you - escaping from the clutches of the imperials seems like a good place for that. You probably won't have time to cover the rest of the journey and meeting Lady Blackbird's betrothed or whatnot.

    In terms of general GMing advice, I recommend Adam Koebel's "Office Hours" videos, orginally on twitch but also generally archived to Youtube, but that's a lot of ground to cover before your game. So view GMing as a learning experience. And keep copious notes about what happened and what you did and why. The Angry GM suggests spending the "end of session" period where everyone is jawing about the game to write down what happened during the session, anything you need to improve on, etc. I think that's good advice.

    \nI actually think covers it pretty well!

    When you have a great idea, jump in with it.

    When you don't, approach it like a curious scientist.

    "Hey, I wonder what IS in there?"

    Next, ask yourself:

    "What kind of question could I ask to get at the information?"

    Using leading questions, make them provocative, using creative constraints.

    They open a box and... what's in there?

    "Ooh, it's something you've been hoping to find... (Aside: What have you been hoping to find, by the way? Right, ok!) ...except it's like a weird, twisted version made by a madman. Why would you be afraid to even bring this onboard?"

    \n Taking an expectation and adding a twist is always a good technique.

    "It looks like a part of the engine... but why does it look like it doesn't belong here?"

    "It's a highly valuable thing... but how has it been damaged or defaced?"

    "It's an old friend of yours... but what's disturbing and different about her?"

    "The cargo hold is pretty much as you'd expect, large and cavernous. What's the one smell you notice which doesn't belong here?"

    \n My two pieces of advice: first, scour the entire text, as John cleverly hid little details in odd places, and often by implication.

    The second is that LB has a 3 act structure (roughly, Act 1 Escape from the Hand of Sorrow; Act 2 Flight to Haven, Journey to the Scatters; Act 3 Meeting Uriah) so that may give you some ideas on how to pace things. In general I wouldn't get too hung up on the escape from the Hand of Sorrow, which can be done pretty quickly if you want. (I'll be curious if your PCs kidnap Captain Hollas, who always seems to end up hostage when I run it...)

    \n Another tip:

    Anytime things are unclear, vague, or seem to be missing "teeth", ask yourself if a flashback or refreshment scene could clear it up.

    For instance, two characters are wondering whether to do this thing, or not. It seems like they're taking opposite sides, but it's not entirely clear why they care. Have a flashback, asking one of the players to tell us how their character came to hate X, or another what it was like the last one they met one of these...
