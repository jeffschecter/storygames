\n We play and playtest a lot of games. Often a new game every 2 weeks. And we don't have time to read the rules before we get together.
\n
\nTo help, we take turns reading the rules out loud. Say 4 pages each and then pass the book around. And it usually works awesome!
\n
\nThere are limits of course. There is only so much information we can absorb this way. It helps to organize the rules so that there isn't a lot of flipping back and forth. And it's great when the rules have break points where you can start playing the game (say play out a battle with the bare bone rules), read more rules, play more, read more rules, and so on.
\n
\nI would love if RPG text were written specifically to support this! BattleLore works this way.
\n
\nRock,

    Posted By: DanielSolisIf it's too awkward to say, it's too awkward to read.
    \n>.<

    This is one of my pet peeves. Writing for reading aloud is categorically different than writing for silent reading. Among other things, text for spoken word requires shorter sentences, cannot use lists of any real length, and has to take breath into account. It significantly reduces the complexity that the text can convey. Which may be a good thing for your specific purposes, especially in this context, but is a very, very different thing than a general rule.

    (No direct accusation at you, Daniel, you just said what a lot of folks take as a given.)

    All that said, taking into account how your text/product is going to actually be used is absolutely essential. If you expect your book to be read aloud, then dude, write it accordingly. Alternately, provide 'real aloud' sidebars and boxed text that gives the bullet points of the more elaborate rules in the main text flow.

    There's also writing for sight-reading. Everything on a character sheet should require no more than a glance to read. Quick reference cards, too. Using short words, eschewing sentences, and judicious use of type styles are all very important, there.

    But yeah: always take into account how the text will be used.

    \n My last two games - Kristallnacht and Bugnende Blader - are both specifically designed to be read aloud. With Kristallnacht, after two or three playtest runs, I sat down at the keyboard and basically read the whole thing aloud to myself to see what it sounded like. I ended up changing a lot of the text.

    In my opinion/experience, things like lists, bullet points, short sentences etc work well for written games - but are horrible for read-aloud games. In read-aloud games, you can - and must, sometimes - use more words and description.

    The great thing is that reading the game aloud becomes part of the gaming ritual, and you have a wonderful opportunity for getting people into the right vibe for your game. Instead of someone leafing through a book and going "okay, let's see, I think this rule was here somewhere... Yeah, you roll 3d7 for Pumpkinicity, oh, but first we have to design the world", you have someone reading your beaaaautifully written sentences in just the right order.
