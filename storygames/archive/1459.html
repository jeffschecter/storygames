The Opposite of Flags

DanielSolis
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
Totally off the top of my head: So we have "flags" to say what we want our characters to do in a game. I've listened to many a podcast now that suggests players explicitly state what they don't feel comfortable doing in a game. Are these "counter-flags?" There's gotta be a better word for that.

Is there a way to make these work in a game without making a laundry list of sensitive subjects that may not have happened anyway?

Or maybe a game that is about pushing taboos and sensitive subjects? I don't know if anyone's able to get out of their head enough to state stuff they "don't" want to happen without, in some sense, wanting it to happen anyway. That makes it a flag, right? A counter-flag would probably be created by other players and given to your character.

Anyhoo, it's a little idea. Thought I'd toss it out to you guys.

    Actually, my sense of a counter-flag has a wonderful example in the Amber DRPG. In the ADRPG, the psyche stat can do a lot of stupid, abusive stuff. Some people love it and some people hate it, but in either case there is strong incentive to buy it high. If you like it, you want it high so you can use it, and if you hate it, you buy it high so you don't have to deal with that crap.

    Same principal is broadly applicable - sometimes a player will spike in something because they _don't_ want to get tangled up in it. A player may buy a high diplomacy skill so he can just roll the dice and move on rather than have to play out scenes that he feels awkward or uncomfortable with. Similarly, if a player hates a certain sort of opposition, say, mind control or energy drain, he may spike in things to counter that.

    It also comes back to being flags in a roundabout way. When a player makes a defensive purchase, it can be a flag for the defense _working_, but that is a very different sort of flag than a flag that says "I dig this sort of scene". As en example, if I stock up on anti mind-control stuff, that's a flag for me to have a scene where my enemy's mind control powers are useless, but is not a flag that I am interested in mind control scenes and will be interested in the opponent who subverts my defense.

    Of course, if you don't talk to the player, flags and counter-flags look the same on the character sheet, so if nothing else, it's a potent reminder about the dangers of assumptions.

    -Rob D.

    That all said, I think that lines&veils are not the opposite of flags. Flags say, "This is cool, I'd like to do as much as possible of this."

    Anti-flags would say, "This is boring, I'd like to do as little of this as possible."

    You can still do that in a game of, e.g., D&D. You can tell your DM that encumbrance is really boring to you and you'd like to deal with it as little as possible. That's not drawing a line or veil, it's a different way of focusing the game on the fun parts, and people do it all the time in an unstructured way.
