Piggy-back publishing

Jared A. Sorensen
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games
Just an idea I had:

If your goal is simply "to get published" and you have a small game that might not make it on its own, get in touch with another indie game publisher and offer it as free bonus content. It'll save you publication costs and get your name/work out there with a minimum of financial risk (and to a wider audience). For the person helping you, it's additional material/page count.

Anyone tried this?

    It's not that funny, because it raises another possibility: designers with name recognition giving tiny microgames to up-and-coming designers, to publish as bonus material in a new game book. It's similar in a way to famous writers doing forwards for novels by new writers.

    Now, my gut feeling is that the more famous name providing the bonus material works better than the other way around, but I'm really wondering whether anyone's actually tested the truth of this, in game design or elsewhere. Granted, I bought Philip Jose Farmer's Dungeon, which turned out to be a really crappy series, because of the gimmick that these unknown writers were writing about a world custom-made for them by Farmer. But how well did the series actually sell? Did it sell better than those same writers' other attempts?

    This works very, very well in genre fiction. Like, for example, I recent saw (and just now purchased) a book called Exquisite Corpse: Surrealism and the Black Dahlia Murder. Don't know how true the premise of the book is; it feels awfully conspiracy theory-ish. But then that's part of its charm: it's painting a picture of the exact subculture I was thinking of when I wrote a game about possibly insane surrealist artists. I should get on this.

    Ben indirectly raises a point: how much attention should prospective piggy-backers be paying to how their games fit together stylistically or thematically? One theory is two games in the same book should be as similar in theme or style (but probably not both) as possible, so that the published book feels like a coherent product. But another theory is that the games should be as different as possible, so that the microgame acts as a respite or counterpoint to the main game.
