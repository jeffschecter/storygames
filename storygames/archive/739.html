Character Arc Chains!

Christian_Griffen
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
Jonathan posted something about chakra keys that has some really cool implications. Some games like TSOY have goals that, when fulfilled, allow for character development; they give XP. However, those are single things; imagine what you could do if you had story arc elements that people can earn and chain together in different ways to then trade for character development!

That idea begs for examples. Say you have a list of certain character events, such as "falls in love," "gets smacked down," "shows respect to an elder," "fulfills revenge," "sacrifices a friend," "backstabs an ally," etc.

Now, during play, your character earns some of these events due to what happens and the choices they make. Not very often; maybe one or two per session. The most significant events, in other words.

There are also chains of events--let's call them arcs--that are listed in the rules or made up before play by the players, such as "falls in love"+"gets rejected"+"heartbroken rage"+"gives up love"... OR... "falls in love"+"gets rejected"+"pursues love"+"gets rejected"+"pursues love"+"wins over love".

The player can then trade in established chains for character development! The events and chains work INSTEAD OF experience points, so that your character development is all story-organic and shit. And if you want to get crunchy, shorter and longer chains give different rewards, or you could even have certain things only available through particular chains (such as a "hero" reputation, a "jaded" ability, a "ruthless" advantage, etc.). If you want the "Dark Side Powers" ability, you need to go down a chain that includes anger, suffering, betraying people, killing innocents...

Minor issues to be worked out include whether those events should all be active choices (e.g., "fulfills revenge") or include the fate the character hasn't chosen as well (e.g., "is rejected"). The first makes choices more meaningful, the second allows for rewards even when the character fails at something.

Anyway, I thought this had some potential.

    Glad people are digging it.

    I've been thinking a lot about structuring player input as a way of creating a distinct sense of character and this is part of that on-going exploration. Traditional play would have us define what characters CAN do, when this is instead looking at what characters WOULD do. You limit the kinds of choices characters can make instead of the likelihood or degree to which they can succeed at various tasks.

    So instead of having a set of abilities, you collect various options that are open to the character and so the player, by making some choices, closes certain roads and open others (which is what Waiting/Tea was trying to model on a micro-level).

    Personally, I think this has the possibility, in certain games, of not just supplementing traditional attribute systems, but completely replacing them, to the point of rendering them unnecessary if the possible character choices are structured well enough. Obviously, you could also use them like Keys or Spiritual Attributes (from Riddle of Steel) and help structure more traditional play. But that's not where my interest really lies, being a subversive design junkie

    Some other thoughts I had this morning:

    Consider computer-based adventure games, from Zelda to Final Fantasy.

    You're on a quest to reassemble the Master Sword. It's not just some crazy, open-ended thing. First you have to complete step A, then step B, then step C, and then, once you get to step H, you get the Master Sword. Of course, playing these games, I often wished things were less strictly ordered, so you could do step E and then step B and then step G, etc. And that's where you could swap various "chain links" around or, like Christian said, improvize the necessary chain out of a handful of options.

    Now, just switch this over to a character development model, instead of just developing your ability to kick ass. You're a nice innocent farm boy, like Luke Skywalker at the beginning of Star Wars or Taren in The Book of Three. Luke ends up becoming a Jedi,which means more than just kewl powerz; it means a serious shift in his character and personality. Likewise, Taren eventually becomes the High King of the whole world. Now going from farm boy to supreme leader is a long process, so you probably want to break it down into steps. Start with say, Farm Boy -> Competent Warrior. It's like multiclassing in D&D or chosing a new occupation in Warhammer Fantasy, but this is less about getting the skills or powers of a warrior and more about framing the experiences that will help turn your farm boy into a warrior. And this can be a fixed series of steps or a more general structure that's open to a lot of moving things around.

    Note too that it should be totally possible to change your goals mid-stream. You complete a few chain links and then decide you want your character to become more of a lover than a fighter. Well, ideally, you should still be able to draw on your past experience (your completed chain links) to move towards that as well. Anybody remember the Marvel Universe Roleplaying Game with its "lines of experience?" You would write down important things that you did and gain experience from them. This is like that, except that the kinds of things you can do are predetermined, so you're trying to meet various requirements instead of retroactively recalling the neat stuff you did.

    Also, consider how cool it is to have requirements involving failure. Normally, in roleplaying, failure isn't always especially fun, because it doesn't necessarily drive the story anywhere. Dogs' Fallout was one solution to this, creating baggage from failure that's sure to crop up later and create more interesting situations. Chain links involving failure, though, allow you to progress as a character by getting your ass handed to you or having fate not be on your side. Wanna become a warrior? Well, a good way to get that motivation would be having someone destroy everything you love and humiliate you at the same time. Look, now you have a desire for revenge. Isn't that handy?

    Think about most American martial arts or sports movies. The Karate Kid. Rocky. The Mighty Ducks. The main characters usually have to lose a fair amount in the beginning in order to make their comeback (after a montage sequence where they get better) even more significant and exciting. Now you can have mechanics where the characters have to fail a lot in order to finally succeed. This was my point about many of the characters in Avatar (and a lot of kids shows), who have to fail so that they can learn how to get better. Kids shows are about teaching lessons and showing kids how to learn from their mistakes. Also, they try to teach abstract thinking and problem solving, not just looking for the obvious solution. So, in Avatar the obvious solution almost never works. Instead, like Rebecca Borgstrom says in Nobilis, you gain energy through adversity.

    More later, as I continue to ponder this.

    There are two ways to play with this type of system though, right? One is Top-Down ("Here's where you want to go, so do steps A, B and C") and the other is Bottom-Up ("Here's where you are ... you can do A, D or F and they have consequences down the road ... what looks best to you right now?")

    I think Dirk's comment about the FF-X Sphere Grid is a very keen one, and one that I'd like to elaborate a bit: The topology of the links between things is going to push people in one direction or another on the Top-Down/Bottom-Up axis.

    If you have long strings with comparatively few branches ("Okay, you can do A ... and then your only sensible choices after that are B, C, D, E, F and G or else you can do H, and then your only sensible choices are I, J, K, L and M") then people will have a more structured and constrained experience (if they want to seek the benefits of the system) but fewer choices. Much as the word is overloaded, this is the basic setup of a railroad system. You pick where you're going to go, and then you ride the rails.

    By comparison, a system with short strings, many branches and constant interweaving gives people substantially more choices, at the cost of clear structure. Sure, I'm a Farm-Boy who has become a competent warrior, but where the hell do I go next? So. Many. Options!

    FF-X has a fascinating balance: strings of three to four items, connected together like spaghetti. This gives choices and structure, though both in moderation.

    Deep stuff! I like how the mathematics of how things are connected can translate into a play experience in terms of how people feel. What would the ability to dynamically create paths (as Christian suggested) do in terms of changing that experience?

    Here's the bare-bones of how I did something like this as an add-on for a fairly conventional game. I started with a set of Whimsy Cards (anybody remember those?). Cards with a variety of short, punchy dramatic elements on them, meant to spur creativity. I took some of those, wrote some of my own, and welded on a "fate point" type mechanic. Typically, the card could be played by adding something to a scene that referenced the concept on the card. You could either have it affect your own stuff (in which case you gained a point or two from the bank) or you could play the card and pay another player to have it affect something they owned.

    A typical card: "Something's Missing. Something that ought to be here isn't. It could be your sword, or a friend you were hoping to meet, or something else. Gain 1 Destiny to play this on yourself, or pay 2 Destiny to play on someone else."

    I tried added some suits and progressions to the cards, such that you could increase the reward value by chaining them, but it got too fiddly for the people I was playing with at that point. In general, the idea never really caught on with the group, and while I've been pecking at it for a while, I still haven't found a happy implementation.

    First, - this makes for a very interesting tool that can be used in both closed and open GMing models. Supposing there is a full on narrative web, you could use it in a game where all players have full visibility into the web, and can plan their development from square one as easily as you could in a game where players are more in the dark (seeing only their section of the map) and will have much strong process of discovery. In way of example, a specific sort of failure may lead to a new path. In an open game, a player may plan for this, in a closed game, a player may discover this.

    Whether you think one approach is better than the other, what's remarkable is that this provides a concrete tool that could be used equally well from the same source material.

    Second - while still on the topic of writing stuff for other people, consider how this addresses a huge flaw of published adventures: they're not written for _your_ group. It makes it very hard to hook players in without needing to do enough work to call into question why you bought the product in the first place. Supposing that you had certain recognizable patterns, you could write an adventure to those patterns, creating sort of a translation layer to create immediate hook-ins.

    To illustrate, suppose you've got a game based off console RPGs, you isolate a few chakras/patterns/whatever. The Silent Stranger, the Hero with a clouded past, the powerful innocent and so on. When I write an adventure, i don't know what the specific hoosk of the characters in yoru group are, but I know what _patterns_ you use, so I can hook into those.

    Third - This sort of thing makes me want to see someone take the existing RPG design patterns and turn it up to 11. :)

    Anyway, cool, chewy stuff, but I'd be insanely curious to see how different this looks in various people's heads.

    -Rob D.

      It strikes me now that this is a lot like the "tagging" that's become so popular in organizing information nowadays.       I've included a mechanic for the development-comes-from-suffering idea in my Agonistes game system. I call it "dark fortune cookies" and "light fortune cookies". If you roleplay a flaw in your character, this earns you a dark fortune cookie, and you can spend this by making something bad happen to your character. This can be an opportunity for character development (character learns the lesson), or the character can respond with a flaw again and earn another dark fortune cookie. Light fortune cookies are the opposite, rewards for roleplaying a character strength, and you can use them to save your butt by introducing a "lucky coincidence". The underlying idea is "fortune rewards those who act in accordance with their nature." Kind of a karma thing.
