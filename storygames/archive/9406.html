It may be entertaining to explore what it would be like to be a minority, pagan/atheist, female or gay, but if it becomes non-entertaining at any point, I stop doing it.

I think paying attention to it, making allowances for it, discussing it (even as I do it now) apologize for it, blaming it for your misfortunes (even where it is demonstrably true) causes more harm than a rather large number of other things. Cultures are much more valuable and realistic concepts. It's not about appearance, it's about self-identification and enculturation.
    \n
    \nSo I'll never design a game with "minorities" in mind. I absolutely want all people who like the same sorts of things I'm making a game about to play my games. Their racial makeup is of no consequence to me. Their religion is of no consequence. Their sexual orientation and gender are of no consequence. If I gathered a group of people together who were all black (besides me, of course) atheist lesbians, and we had enough common ground to enjoy each other's company and enough trust to play passionately, I would explore any taboo concepts that caught our collective fancy. But I wouldn't pick such a group to explore certain concepts, or explore certain concepts because of the demographic makeup of the group. The important parts come after the buzzwords. It's not black atheist lesbians you should be paying attention to. It's "common ground" "enjoy each other's company" and "trust" that are important.
    \n
    \nWhen I write a game, I am absolutely not going to go out of my way to avoid offending anyone. Ever. Not even a little bit. I also will not attempt to offend anyone on purpose. Most of the time. If I make a game about relationships, I'm not going to make allowances for same-sex relationships. I'm also not going to have a disclaimer saying that if you're a sexual deviant who practices homosexuality that you should avoid my game and get some religion before the devil gits ya. If I make a game about angels and demons, I'm not going to pull my punches and say that they're possibly the psychic constructs of human belief. No, their angels, fighting for God, and demons, fighting for the Devil. Atheists will accept this as "fiction" or they'll go play something else. I will not (who does this anymore? I mean, seriously?) make a game where blacks are big-eyed, big-lipped and lazy sidekicks who run around calling the brawny protagonist 'massah'. I'm also never going to make a roleplaying game that explores the 'black tax'.
    \n
    \nThat is how race and racism will affect my game design and game play.

    \n Couple thoughts.

    First, on design: People construct their identities around totally arbitrary things. Their hobbies, their skin color, their activism, their major, their nationality, their fandom. Whatever.

    If you take that identity-forging thing and portray it negatively or incorrectly or disrespectfully, then congratulations, you've just shrunk your potential market.

    Like Brainy Smurf. Back in the eighties, when I was a little public-school nerd, I suffered because of that little blue fuck. The Smurfs had serious influence on my Grade 4 class, and if you acted like you knew something, you earned the same treatment that Brainy earned. It got so that I didn't want to wear my glasses to school. Now that I am a big adult nerd who can kick a football some distance, I want to find the anti-intellectual turdkernel who originated Brainy and attempt field goals with their testicles.

    To summarize: it's hard to entertain people at the same time as you're shitting on them.

    Second, on play: I am DMing a 4E Greyhawk game right now. Greyhawk has lots of lightly airbrushed human cultures -- the creepy proto-Nazi Suel, wandering in search of their living space, the aboriginal Flan* who are contentedly being pushed aside by the migrating olive-skinned Aerdy imperialists, the Arabesque Baklunish who are off on their own and kind of marginalized, and the Gypsy-ish Rhenee riverboat nomads, who are insular, mistrusted, and culturally non-mainstream.

    At the risk of playing with fire, the tension between various subraces really seemed like the interesting bit about the setting. One of the PCs is in a law enforcement role, and during a skill challenge I said to him 'you're hearing complaints about the Rhennee all the time.' It seemed like an honest thing to say in the moment but following up on that with in-game specifics is gonna have to be handled with some delicacy.

    I don't want my game to Teach An Important Lesson and I don't want to lazily wallow in negative stereotypes and I've got a limited amount of table-time in which to not do all these things. But at the same time, taking on this challenge in a relatively safe space is almost irresistible. Writing the other so that they are alien and familiar and sympathetic and unsympathetic all at once -- that's something I want to achieve someday.

    Okay, back to the original topic.

    I'm not a designer of games. But I do illustrate them. And i'm white, middle aged, not religious at all. OF course I'm affected by this stuff. I don't always think of drawing enough women or minorities. Left to my own devices, I often draw caucasians.

    It is tough when you have a typical fantasy dungeon party (or say sci-fi) and you really do have actual, different SPECIES interacting with each other. If I have 5 people to get across as "the player characters" and three of the five are a dwarf, elf and an orc...the other two have to take on the entirety of humanity. I try. But I don't always succeed. Sure, I can make the dwarf look like hispanic, but elves are tough. Mess with those aquiline features and put a different skin tone? They no longer are elfin. This is one of the reasons, I think, Drow are not thought of as "people of color" very often. (Judd has a good diatribe about Drow...hee, hee. ) Most of the Drow I've seen are very caucasian features with a skin tone that can range from the unnatural colors of blue and purple to more realistic skin tones of blue-brown. Blue is not a color we see in mammals very often. So it is a wonderful color to say "holy andaran, that person is alien!!!".

    In Catspaw, my webcomic, I told my writer, "let's make the main character a woman and black".... just because we have not seen that very often in Sword and Sorcery and Catspaw is about John and my love for sword and sorcery. And we don't belabor it. We don't have characters pointing out she is black. Just present it and move on with the story. Oh. She is also bald. And I love drawing her because she is based on a friend of mine from NYU waaaay back in my early college days visually. My friend had close cropped hair, wasn't bald. But bald is interesting on a woman and basically because i just simply liked the visual. So, like I said, I am trying to be aware of the problem and do something about it. Because sword and sorcery dates to a time when ALL protagonists were white and the majority of them male.

    I'm getting the chance to do some artwork on the fantasy section for long running universal RPG system. The art director and I have exchanged several emails on when and how to change up the pelthora of white options. I know that the duo I came up with to sorta show Sword and Sorcery tropes is a female mongol-like archer (with strongly indian-asia minor features, clothing) and a Ape-ninja. I'm hoping that is a dynamic duo that at least plays with the idea of WHO can be the protagonists of stories.

    But part of what I do is illustrate tropes. Sometimes it is my job to simply do that. Not tweak or twist the trope, but to go right to the source and do it.

    I had a very interesting conversation with the then producer of Justice League International. We had a long discussion at a very slow con once about race in comics. He said that the word came down to make the Justice League Int as poly-cultural as possible. And there was great interest in having a strong asian Male superhero that was not stereotypical martial-artist. Dwayne said to me "and did you know Storn, we could not find ONE male who wasn't a martial artist or an offensive buffoon (asian sidekicks were AWFULLY prevalent in the Golden era, Chop-Chop from Blackhawks anyone?) in the 80 years of DC comics history?!?!?" They ended up going with Doctor Light (japanese woman). I think that is a pretty scary tidbit. That is an oversight. That is a challenge to some DC writer. C'mon.

    I've been writing up the game at two levels, one of which is a large scale game played on a city-wide map with teams of interacting "minions". The second game is a more intimate roleplaying game exploring the feelings of dealing with different cultures, along with the myths and legends that define those cultures.

    I had someone over on the forge tell me that my game was skating close to racism.

    It's a valid concern, and something that I've really been worried about. Are my explorations of the world's cultures merely an attempt to stereotype them?

    It would be easy to ignore if I was creating a fantasy world. I could really exaggerate certain angles, and then when people take offence I could easily say "Get a life, it's only a fucking game", but when I'm mirroring the real world it becomes a little harder to distance.

    Heretics within the Islamic extremists dealing with djinni.
    Fundamentalist Christians (or Mormons) calling on Angels to smite those they consider wicked.
    Neo-pagans calling on the powers of ancient and immortal fey.
    Voodounistas gathering the pieces of a half-dozen religions to craft some of the most potent charms in the world.
    Technofetishists trying to push the envelope through science and technology.
    All trying to shape the world in their image; while preventing other factions from gaining too much power.

    And our poor players stuck in the middle, constantly questioning their moral values while trying to determine which cultural group are the good guys in a given situation.

    It's a world of grey morality, and personally I'm at the point where this reflection of the world is reasonably accurate. I'm just trying to present the facts as I see them, offer the questions that I feel everyone needs to face once in a while, and leave the answers for the players to resolve among themselves.

    I'm also trying to open up the creative potential of the world to as many people as possible, to prevent the world from focusing too strongly on the bias of my perspective. It's not that I don't have a strong creative vision for the setting, but I'd like to set the path in motion and see where people take it. If one group of players wants to run a highly racist politically charged hatefest, then that a reflection of their characters within the setting; there are certainly enough neo-nazis in the real world to justify this type of group occurring in the game world. I probably wouldn't be interested in playing this sort of game, but the potential is there.

    If another group of players wants to run a co-operative storyline where the members of different cultures struggle to survive against the odds of a natural disaster, then that would be just as interesting to engage (if not more so). It's probably the kind of game I'd enjoy more.

    I'm trying to provide some interesting character aspects that transcend the stereotypes by allowing players to pick and choose from a variety of options that reflect the races and cultures of the world; religions, virtues, vices, typical skills, etc. A player could really develop a character that plays up all the stereotypes of a race, or they could mix and match to get something a bit more interesting.

    It's something I've been thinking long and hard about. I don't think my solution is perfect, it'll always be a work in progress, but at least I'm thinking about it.

      \n The modern conception of race emerged from colonial shipping destinations (far-east asia, the americas, africa, and the middle east changed from oriental to caucasian as their role in shipping changed). The classification by skin colour became a mental shortcut, vulnerable to all the failings therein. I see inclusion of other races as reinforcing the shortcut when what we really need are ways to get around it and latch onto more relevant details.

      How do you bring diversity into a game without falling back on lazy assumptions like race? One way is to design multiple starting categories, such as economic status, religion, rulership structure, food production, etc. Come up with a number of subgroups within each category (e.g. food production: family agricultural, forager, nomadic herding, horticulture, high-yield industrial farming). Instead of building stereotypes for each subgroup, find the intersections between subgroups from different categories and build from there. I like to merge two subgroups to design a social group, really thinking how each issue affects the other, then make individual NPCs incorporate a third. Since there are so many combinations, you get a lot of diversity, where there are both connections and differences. Don't feel you have to use every combo, but I find that 3-4 connections to each subgroup is a good number.

      There are already plenty of games out there that do this. I prefer ones that plan it out ahead of time, rather than trying to make it up during play (or even at the start of play), because I find that people more often resort back to stereotypes when pushed to think quickly.

When I first started on Mage Blade (the only of my games with an explicit setting) I went the typical fantasy route, and had elves dwarves and such to fill that gap. Since then, I've not gotten rid of the fantasy races, but I've scaled them back. Instead of having 3 nations of elves, 3 nations of dwarves and 3 nations of men, I have 1 nation of elves (from a specific origin) two nations of dwarves, and 6 nations of men. I don't want all those nations of men to be 'white fantasy' nations, not that I've ever thought about it in those terms. It just seems that there needs to be something.. distinct about them. I also don't want them to be thinly veiled cognates to real world cultures.
      \n
      \nInterestingly, thinking about that setting in light of recent conversations, the orc nation (not previously mentioned because it didn't feel immediately applicable) is/was a clumsy, unconscious attempt at racial issues. The orcs were a savage race, enslaved by humans over time as a means of attempting to control their depredations. Eventually, there was a slave revolt that sparked off a war, which eventually ended with the nations being called to the negotiation tables, and the orcs being given their own nation in reparation for generations of treatment that was against the codes of the over-arching society. Orcs weren't considered part of that society, therefore outside its protection, until the agreements. Obviously, this solution had some enemies. The orcs had, by and large, been slaves to the humans for so long that they had little cultural identity of their own, and suddenly, they were running their own nation. Some of the humans still felt the orcs deserved slavery or death. Others who might have been more sympathetic were angry when their lands were reduced to make a place for the orcish nation. Some of the orcs chose to join their savage brethren who hadn't been captured or enslaved, but mostly they knew they didn't belong there anymore. They were too civilized for the tribes, and too primitive for their supposed peer nations, most of whom were hostile toward them, or at best neutral.
      \n
      \nThe canon setting begins 5 years after the formation of the orcish nation.
      \n
      \nAre there obvious parallels to black slavery in America? Definitely. Are there also some major differences? Absolutely.
      \n
      \nAmong the human cultures, there are divisions along the lines of how a nation 'officially' feels about the orcish nation, but they are also culturally different. The people of Arien are industrious, quiet and little given to decoration, finding expression in the minute differences in utilitarian craft styles. Their music is wordless, but rich with emotion. In the island nation of Crysse, there is a deep reverence for ocean and sky, and it's almost required that one serve in a sea-related field at some point in their life. Their art usually reflects this strong relationship with the sea, and they have a marked preference for blues, greens, greys and browns in their clothing.
      \n

I figure they're scattered throughout the nations, an artifact of the globe-spanning civilization that died 500 years ago. They've had 5 centuries of a civilization that has pulled itself out of the ashes of the old. I don't figure anyone much cares what color your skin is, so much as they'd care what nation you call home, or if you're a member of one of the non-human races.
