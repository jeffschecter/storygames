\n I'm a little confused about Conditions. They fall into two categories, as I understand it:

1. Conditions that describe what people perceive, but have no effect on what the character feels. "Slut", "Crybaby", "Meanie", "Doormat", and "Teacher's Pet" all fall into this category.
2. Conditions that do have an effect on what the character feels, like "Drained" or "Terrified".

Conditions in the second category seem only to be valid when the rules specifically say something like "Get the Condition Drained". Do I have that right?

Do any of these Conditions ever cause a +1 or -1 modifier? I'd vaguely assumed that they all did all the time, but I was clearly wrong on that.

The book says, "No matter what, the highest bonus you can have on any given roll (counting both stat and forward) is 5." I note that this does not count Strings. I presume that this is deliberate. I also presume that one can burn as many Strings as one has on a character at a time. Is all of this correct? Did I miss a rule somewhere?

In theory, the Vampire feeds off other people. The Ghoul certainly needs to feed, one way or another. Neither Skin has any backstory on the order of "You've fed on someone. Take / Give X Strings". I presume that this is deliberate? Does one always presume that the Ghoul and Vampire have fed before the game starts and are feeding, or might one decide that they've never fed before the start of the game? (We noticed on a reread that these Skins are a little different than, say, the Selkie's, where someone has already stolen the pelt, or the Witch's, where the Witch has already acquired sympathetic tokens, or the Mortal, where the Mortal already has a True Love as part of the backstory.)

    \nOn conditions

    Okay, so here's the thing and it's pretty much just a minor critique or flaw in the game.

    Joe's said to me, while playing, 'Conditions shouldn't dictate an emotional state for a character.'

    Except, there are a couple of moves, namely Gaze, where you do get an emotional state sorta dictated to you: 'Drained'.

    Rather, as he writes 'It's best if it doesn't dictate an emotional state' on Page 28.

    And, when you take advantage of another PCs conditions, you get +1. That's it.

    On Strings

    You can only spend on at a time on any given roll - the max is still five from all sources.

    Remember, it's spend a (single) String, not spend X Strings. To do it, do it.

    Also, the Singleton Rule. You can't double up on XP or Strings from a single move.

    On backstory

    In regards to Vamps and Ghouls, when you're making you seating chart and town map, asking where and on whom the PCs feed are excellent questions to ask. Like with the Queen, PCs may or may not be involved with the other PCs moves at the start of the game.

    The reason that another PC isn't by default, is that it deprotagonizes that PC without much recourse.

    Asking a few leading questions about the PCs lives and relationship is a great way to start any Powered by the Apocalypse game.

    \nConditions & Emotional Autonomy

    You should never apply a Condition to someone else that dictates their emotional state. Conditions applied to other people should always dictate either public perceptions about them or physical circumstances that they're in. (page 28 goes into this.)

    A few moves hand emotional-state-type Conditions to you. That's fine. You're allowed to dictate your own emotional states and internal reactions, so there's no boundary-crossing issue here. You choose the terrified Condition and your character is terrified. It's only an issue when you try to dictate the emotions & responses of the other PCs. That's not allowed.

    By design choices I made, Monsterhearts clearly posits that arousal is a physical circumstance. It's a physical thing that can happen to you. Your emotions & responses to it are still your own to choose, though.

    When Conditions Apply

    Conditions are things that others can take advantage of. That's why Conditions give +1s to other peoples' rolls against you (page 28). Conditions don't make you more or less effective, they just make you more vulnerable.

    So everyone thinks you're a slut? That gives people an added edge when they're trying to hurt you. But it doesn't make you any less (or more) capable of putting a knife between their ribs when they try to.

    Strings and To Do It, Do It

    You can only spend one String on a given effect on a given roll. This is tied into the "to do it, do it" rule. How are you leveraging emotional hold over them in order to cause them to fuck something up? You don't get to just spend the String and not reflect it in the fiction. We need to know about the glare you shoot, or the way your lips quiver and gosh she just can't bring herself to hurt someone she once loved, etc.

    Feeding in Backstories

    Not all vampires and ghouls start the gaming having fed.
    Some are recently turned. Some are conscientious abstainers.
    Some don't even understand that they're dead, and the revelation is part of their opening scenes.
    To incorporate a feeding into their backstory mandates a lot about the characters at the table.

    Plus, as Orlando points out, it mandates that another PC start the game having been violated without recourse. There are agency & response issues there that are more problematic than interesting. Starting the game from a place of de-protagonization that doesn't allow you to draw lines/veils is not cool.

    Can you "double up" on an effect? No.

    \n Hey, a follow-up question on spending Strings:

    I can see why you wouldn't want to "double up" on String effects like dealing extra harm or offering experience. Those bits are designed to work a certain way; we don't necessarily want someone with a bunch of Strings to be able to kill someone with one roll, etc.

    But why is it important to limit String use after a roll (for a +1 per String) to one-at-a-time?

    The effect is that you can only benefit from spending Strings against another PC if you roll a 6 or a 9. For a PC with any stat in the starting range (-1 to +2), that means exactly 25% of the time. The rest of the time, you won't be able to improve your result.* Is that the intended effect?


    *: Of course, it's not quite as bad as 25%, since you'll never want to spend a String on a 10+ roll. But I can't estimate odds for that as easily, since it'll be different for each situation, depending on the stat's value. For instance, if your stat is 0, you won't want to spend a String after any 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, or 8. You will want to use a String on a 6 or 9, and you won't need to use a String on a 10, 11, or 12. That's still 58.3% of the time, so it's significant.

    \n Backstory and Strings -- do you do it before or after creating the homeroom?

    I've seen it done both ways, and I've seen the pros and cons of each.

    Before Homeroom
    Pro: Great connections between PCs!
    Con: Practically no one has a String on any NPC.

    After Homeroom
    Pro: The NPCs are tied in more strongly to the relationship map.
    Con: This can lead to weaker ties between PCs.
    Con: If the NPCs in the string web don't come up strongly in the game, the strings they have might as well not exist. (Why wouldn't these NPCs come up, given there are strings? Time crunch, especially at a convention one shot. Forgetting they exist. Enough interesting stuff going on in another corner of the game world.)

    \n The cards are usually filled out by me or the player who introduces the character when we ask questions (as a group); is this person important enough to be more than just a name? Usually if they are in the homeroom then that's a yes! The card is where we can add details about them, strings and connections, and just physically move them around for relationship maps. When I'm scene framing I'll often grab the NPC card(s) of the folks in the scene and plonk them down on the table between me and the PC(s) involved, making it immediate and obvious who we are dealing with in the scene.

    Drives (motivations) are usually as the rules say, simple and to the point. Simple is better, the players can complicate relationships all on their own.

    as Joe says:
    Make your NPCs straightforward and obvious enough that the other players know how to react to them. Give them obvious goals and obvious means. And then make sure that their goals divide the PCs, pitting them against one another.... Whatever answer you get, trust it and build upon it. Add your own details to the answers that players give, and reincorporate whatever they say into future descriptions.... Play them recklessly, and play them knowing that they aren\u2019t going to last. If you do so, you\u2019ll have constant drama, constant sex, constant violence, and constant chaos. That\u2019s ideal.
    \nOne of my favourite things is to spend an NPC string and to come out of nowhere with a hard move. The string representing the emotional context for this move and forcing me to narratively justify my choice and allowing the NPC to surprise us all with their power to effect our player characters and emergent fiction.

    What does it look like? Whatever makes sense in the story! The chapter on Villans has some good ideas for incorporating cravings and offerings and what they might look like as hard moves in the fiction. Generally I just scan my list as as you say, sometimes its as simple as having someone walk in at the wrong moment, whereas othertimes it may require a little foreshadowing or vignette of their own.
