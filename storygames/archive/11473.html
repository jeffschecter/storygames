\n So, in the other thread which is now closed, there was a comment I was responding to:

-----
There is something incredibly powerful about such themes in gaming. Perhaps, indeed, themes like this are at the heart of all roleplaying games. You roll the dice and your fate depends on fortune alone.
-----

I relayed a memorable moment in my gaming that was 100% a "dice moment" (copied below). What's your memorable moment involving dice?

-----
(answer to the above, and the dice vignette)

Hell yes. Who hasn't cheered over a critical hit on a d20, or a botched roll that we had a lot (in our minds) riding on? That feeling of luck, fate and exhilaration is one of the reasons I think that even the hippie small-press folks are so in love with using a randomization mechanic, with dice at the square center of that randomization: That rush, triumph and defeat is really exciting as a player.

Here's a story: As a side-adventure to our Long AD&D 2E Campaign in high school, the DM did a 3-session adventure set in another area of his campaign, a Mythic Scotland kind of realm. I remember very little, that I was a fighter with a two-handed sword, and that I ran naked in the snow to an altar where I killed some witches or something.

But I do remember one thing VERY clearly about that campaign, and it's the most vivid memory of those entire 3 sessions:

When I rolled my fighter character, I had an 18 strength (we used a die assignment method from one of the AD&D supplements which pretty much assured you of one "18" if you wanted it) and I had to roll percentile dice to determine what my "percentile strength" was.

I rolled a 97. I rolled it, my best friend was watching. I hollared and started writing down this great score on my sheet.

But the DM didn't see it. He didn't watch me roll it. He called BS, saying "Look, I trust you and all, but for this and hit points I need to *see* the roll. That's the way we play." And to my credit (I was 16 at the time) I thoughtfully nodded my head, agreeing "thems are the rules". 'Sides, it was only a mini-campaign, so I didn't really care too strongly. So I rolled again, in front of the DM.

I rolled a 00. One hundred. The single highest score, which has its own special damage and attack bonus categories. We all cheered, even the DM.

Again, I remember little about that campaign, but man I still remember making that die roll.
-----

So again, what's your memorable moment involving dice? Good, Bad, Weird?

-Andy

    \n I have a story just like yours, and it is pretty unbelievable. I wouldn't have believed it if I hadn't seen it. It was 30 years ago and I still remember it vividly.

    I was running Space Opera, in which you roll stats on d100 (and then check a table to get scores of 1-19).
    I'd hacked the game to split the Psionics stat into two - a strength and a skill.
    My younger brother, who I always made sport of at that age, rolled his psionics and got a 100 on both rolls in full view of me and the other two players.
    I HAD seen the rolls, but I pretended I hadn't - "oh dear, I never saw it. You'll have to reroll - that's the way we do things." He'd already got well above the other players on the rest of the stats, he didn't need to be awesome here too.
    He complained, the other two players laughed but didn't say anything.
    So he rolled again, and got 100 twice, again.

    We were all bowled over, awed in that, "that's not osmething you see every day" kind of way. He was jubilant, and I grudgingly accepted it: "You can't fight fate!"

    I'd have checked the dice to see if they were loaded, but they were my dice. Although I never got them back after that. They were his lucky dice now3 - and were used a year or so later for one memorable strike against a dragon in a rolemaster game.

    In RM, you roll percentile for your attacks, and if you roll 96-00 yoll roll again and add. Then add your attack bonus and subtract defence, check a table for damage.
    If you get high enough damage, you score a critical, and again roll percentile for that. In that old edition, if you were facing Large or Super Large creatures, you rolled again if you got 96+.

    So, the group had just roused the oldest deadliest dragon, and were fleeing as it flew towards them. My brother thought he had time for one shot before they reached the safety of the caves, and took the chance. He then learned he'd miscalculated - his pause meant he was going to be in the dragons firebreath when it acted. His attack roll got a roll up, and with that and the dragons defence, he still got only just enough for a critical roll. That roll was at a penalty, but he rolled up three times, and got the maximum possible result. So, one arrow later, the dragon plummeted from the sky, drawing comparisons with Smaug's death.

    You play an rpg long enough, you see all sorts of crazy, unlikely dice rolls.

    \n I wrote this a-ways back, but it fits the bill:\n
    \u201cGood shot MacReady!\u201d

    So about a million years ago I'm running a Star Frontiers game. It's D&D in space, about an inch away from listening at doors. The PCs uncover a spy in the computer center, but before they can grab him he dashes into the maze of computer terminals, needler in hand. Two characters catch up with him and pile on, but they can't take him out. This is not their fault, since in Star Frontiers it takes about 18-25 unarmed hits to drop a normal person. Knocking someone out is even harder \u2014 roll a 01-02 on percentile. 2% chance. If you are using a blunt weapon and you get a roll ending in 0 that also hits, that also counts. So if you need a 45 or less to hit, you have a 6% chance of a knock-out instead of 2%. Woo hoo.

    A few embarrassing rounds go by before John's character strolls up to the pig pile. John is the epitome of cool. He looks down on his fellow PCs rolling around on the floor.

    "I got him," John says casually, and then announces he will cold-cock the spy with his rifle butt.

    02. Unconscious, one hit.

    \n We were playing Call of Cthulhu two weeks ago. There's a rule that, when you're untrained in a skill, there's a 1% chance of success. Usually, the rule seems pretty pointless, and you roll without any hope of succeeding.

    But, at our game, someone actually rolled 01. It was very exciting. We sat and stared at it. Surely that must be 10, not 01? But no. He'd rolled a 1% chance when he needed it.

    Simon has a theory that dice are fun, regardless of what you're rolling for. Do you remember that silly thread, a while ago, when I encouraged people to roll a d20 and post how they felt? That stemmed from Simon's idea. The night before, he had made me roll a d20, to see how I felt about it. I got a 16. It made me irrationally happy.

    \n A couple from D&D games of long ago...

    We're about sixth level or so, taking on an evil mage and his henchmen. We get the last henchman down. It's been a long fight, and everyone's low on hit points, with our two fighters at about 18 or so. "Well," the DM says, "now that he doesn't have to worry about hitting and friends... it's fireball time!" We roll our saving throws -- and only two of us make it. The two party members with the lowest current hit points, of course. The DM cackles and we grit our teeth, expecting to die as he rolls a handful of d6 behind the screen. Then he stops and stares.

    "You guys have to see this," he says, and lifts the screen. Eight six-siders... all but two of them 1's, and those two 2's. Ten points of damage total, from an eighth-level fireball. We grinned, whooped, and proceeded to concentrate fire on the mage, taking him down in one round.

    Another game, 1st edition AD&D. I forget the circumstances now, but a PC mentions the name "Demogorgon" in character. The DM rolls dice behind his screen, stares, grins wickedly. Then he lifts the screen so we can all see the 01 he just rolled on percentiles. Demogorgon appears -- to a bunch of 4th level characters. Those of us who can run for the hills as the demon prince begins to slaughter the character who had the temerity to speak his name aloud....

    Good times. :-)

    \n It was our first time playing Dogs, so three of us had characters that were total douchebags, except for Nick's character Lazarus. Lazarus was a genuinely decent human being that all of us loved. During the last town in this, rather long, campaign of Dogs, Kit's character, Hector, has turned against the rest of the Dogs. Worse, he's become a sorceror. So the rest of us are going out to the edge of town to take Hector down.

    There's a long, horrible, emotionally fraught, totally sorcerous gun battle between Hector and the other dogs. Hector takes an unbelievable amount of fallout during the conflict - at least 8d10 and a whole pile of d8 and d6 fallout, enough that Kit can barely roll all the dice. I manage to avoid taking serious fallout - just d6s. Lazarus wasn't too bad off, just 2d10 and a few d8s. But in the end, we win the conflict and Hector is stopped. Time to roll fallout.

    "I can take it I'm not getting medical attention?" Kit quips as he picks up his dice. "Fuck you," was the general response. So I roll my fallout and turn out to be okay. "Okay, dude," I say to Nick. "Just don't roll a 20 and I can heal you."

    Nick rolls a 20. Everyone's faces fall. Then we look at Kit, who rolls his massive poo pile of dice. AND HIS HIGHEST PAIR IS AN ELEVEN. So we decided that the demons were actually twisting Hector's many, many wounds shut even as Lazarus bled out from his one gunshot. I remember being horrified that the dice would kill Lazarus, who was the best of all of our characters, and still let Hector live. Hands down, it was the most surprising die roll I've ever experienced.

    \n I GM'd a game of Usagi Yojimbo (Fuzion version) a whole bunch of years ago. One of the characters was a bodyguard, so he has the ability to interpose himself when someone is attacked. He's guarding a government agent and has unknowingly invited an assassin into the group (a blind woman who's a ninja, of course).

    The agent has figured out who she is and why she's there and confronts her, telling her to do her job now or get lost. Naturally she's ends up taking a stab at him. The bodyguard steps in, both go with all out attacks; this means the loser will take double damage and, most likely, die.

    We find ourselves just staring at each other when both rolls come up equal, it's a tie.

    My brain grinded to a halt, I had no clue what to do. I was (and am) pretty sure the rules say that both takes damage in this case which means they'll probably both go down, which of course is pretty Japanese. But then the player, almost timidly, asks if this might be a matter of pride to his character. He's playing a lion (no, actually a tiger, but we used the lion stats) so he's got an ability called Pride: if it's a matter of pride to him, he gets +3.

    Of course it's a matter of pride; he friggin' invited the assassin to the group! He also kinda fell in love with her. And now, he kills her. Suddenly it's not just pretty Japanese, it's about as Japanese as it gets.

    Sure, the system played a pretty big role in this whole thing, but damnit, if we hadn't tied or rolls and hadn't been dumbstruck by it, that scene wouldn't have been nearly as beautiful and gut-wrenching.

    \n Call of Cthulhu, some 15 years ago, I think. The GM was a real grognard - probably among the first in Norway to even play an RPG. His wife was a n00b (oh yes, that's how we thought about things in those days) who had only recently been shown the glory of gaming. So the authority distribution when it came to all things gamelike was pretty much set. Still, she'd often quibble and challenge his authority, and he'd get grumpy about it.

    In this game, we were up against some huge unmentionable horror - I don't remember which one, probably Nyarlathotep (he was in fashion in our group back then). We had no way of killing it - no weapons were good enough.

    So the wife says: "Can I make napalm?" The answer is a blunt no, and a frown. But she insists. "I even have Chemistry!" "Napalm wasn't invented back then. You can't make it!" "Why can't I invent it? Someone invented it!" He sighs, and says - obviously just to show her how silly it is, and move on - "okay, if you roll a 01".

    The dice hit the table... and, of course, it's a 01.

    I still remember the look on her face: Excited and happy, rebellious, and vengeful. "I rolled a one! See? I rolled a one!"

    I can't remember what happened to the napalm.

    \n Bayushi Dorokhan was my first Legends of the Five Rings character. He was a Scorpion Duellist, disgusted with the dishonorable ways of his family, left the Scorpion lands to find honor for himself. His father insisted that he would learn that the Scorpion ways were important, as important as any honor in the empire.

    We were loving L5R. It was so damned brutal and when you swung that six-foot razor blade that was also your grandfather's six-foot razor blade, you felt it and men fell like wheat. And you could die quickly too. We had never played in a system that brutal and we were really fascinated.

    So, it was the siege of Yasugi Castle and this big bastard of a Scorpion general was picking apart the Hare Clan's only castle. Our PC's were in the castle trying to fight for this noble Minor Clan but we knew it was doomed.

    The battle rules for L5R were really cool and the seige was just a harrowing experience. We left the first game of the seige physically tired. After the second game we were exhausted. It was evident after the third game that we weren't going to gain any kind of easy win.

    We hoped to get some of the peasants out, thirty were stuck inside the walls. The general offered to allow us to aijutsu duel three of his best and for every one we beat, ten peasants would be allowed to leave unharmed. Aijutsu dueling was no joke and it was really easy to die.

    My character was paired with the best duelist in the field, who was also his sister. She was holding my father's sword, a blade I hoped to claim if I won.

    We were rolling dice in the open.

    I lost.

    I got killed.

    My player was a real mover and a shaker. Dorokhan wasn't the leader of the party in any real sense but the other players definitely looked ot me/him for direction. And his plots in this campaign, half a dozen sessions in were just developing. The other PC's really respected him trying to find an honorable road when all of his skills and abilities were about a dishonorable one.

    And he was dead.

    Bam.

    The GM was mortified, all of the plans he had for Dorokhan were in the toilet. Honestly, the GM took it harder than me. He told me flat out that if he had been rolling behind a screen he would have fudged it because he adored Dorokhan so much.

    I loved it.

    I wanted to win the damned duel, don't think I didn't and I wanted to see Dorokhan mature and figure out the empire for himself. But this meant that his father, in a very real way, might have been correct. I made the character to answer a question and the answer wasn't what I expected.

    I see this as parallel to GM's who guide the game along with the players rather than force their own pre-scripted ending. As a player too, I have to be ready for a heroic epic to be a tragedy. That is okay.

    I made up a Crab clan Shugenja to follow up, so that he would have nothing in common with my politically motivated duelist and when we speak of the Siege of Yasugi Castle, we still have a harsh edge to our voice.

    And I wouldn't have it any other way.
