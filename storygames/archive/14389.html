\n No, this isn't a trick question. Well, maybe it is a little.

I'm working on my Cyborg game recently. I keep bouncing around from setting creation, to character creation, to game system, to theme and how I see play forming. I'm writing lots of things down, talking about them at length with people, and trying to visualize. I guess I'm still in that brainstorming phase.

I'm one of those gamers that really likes character central games (vs combat central or... world building central games). There's lots of buzz on here recently about character building, but I don't think its been addressed in quite this way.

My questions: Is character creation the most important part of the role playing game? I mean, the whole game is pretty much about the PC characters, so it should be pretty central, right? Does it extend beyond that into, maybe, having the character sheet be the most important part of the game, because its the artifact you interface the most with when you play? Is there another point I'm missing here that I should delve further into when thinking about character creation and character sheet interaction?

I'm trying to focus on this issue critically while I'm designing. I know certain games have a sort of psychology behind situation and character building... I'm trying to get at that I guess.

    \n I think the most important part is what happens right after character creation, the bridge between what you've done before play begins and how you actually use that in practice.     Your character creation can be super fun, but if it doesn't feed easily into what actually happens in play, people will make a lot of characters but maybe not play your game so much. And that would be a shame.

    * Have no characters
    * Have no character monogamy, or no character permanence, or whatever
    * Have no character sheets or records
    * Have no character information at the beginning of the game

    By "character creation" I take it that you actually refer to the character preparation? You know; the stuff we jot down on a paper to make it easy for us to create a character in play ...

    - yes, for that is where the real character is created; in play! So the game-play is the most important part of the game, obviously.

In many ways the creation of a character takes a whole life-time; the life-time of the character; from the moment you start playing it, to the moment you leave the game, never to play that character again.

    \n I think it depends on what you define as an RPG. Shannon A. wrote an interesting article in 2003, and one of the distinctions was between character development and storytelling. If the emphasis is on character development, and the focus is on what computer gamers consider to be an RPG, then character creation is pretty crucial. You want a strong base to develop from. If your focus is on storytelling though, then it's important in a different way. I'm quite fond of systems that have collaborative character building of some sort, which automatically integrates the characters together in the plot. In that sense it's still important, but it's how the characters relate to each other that's important, rather than the capabilities of the character.

    \n How I'd read the chart (adding some of the secret connections Zac may be talking about), ymmv: \n
      \n
    • From a given setting, you choose a promising situation, which determines or influences the choice of characters.
      • \n
    • Then, Characters are the players' primary (and pivotal for GM too) tools for interacting with the world and fiction. This happens by the players doing all the things you list: interacting with characters, developing the characters, narrating the fiction etc. (Used to be that the GM had more tools for these things than the players, but we've got a lot more selections of options now a days.)
      • \n
    • Character creation/evolution and interaction are subsets of Character. Drama, Relationships and Action are ways character interacts with setting or is used to establish the setting. (Relationships may fit as a subcategory of character creation or interaction?)
      • \n
    • Theme and mood underly and inform everything, and also are continuously determined by all that is done in the fiction, as well as at the table (eg music, laughter, side talk, focus etc.).
      • \n
    • Objects and Places here are elements of setting, which makes them look pretty external to character. They may be elements of character or important keys to interaction with the setting. Objects especially.
      • \n
    \n
    These are great questions to be pondering as you work on the design. One thing I noticed when I was working on Shooting the Moon was that at a certain point once we had finished character creation (which includes world creation as well), we could see all these possibilities for great scenes happening involving the elements laid out before us (eg beloved children just waiting to be poisoned in a Machiavelli era Italian setting, a huge manor house ready to be burned down in an Elizabethan comedy setting). However, in play, none of those things happened. So I aimed the mechanics at giving the players ways to accomplish those things. In certain scenes you can "sacrifice" traits including things like the kids and manor house, bingo!

    Character, as a tool, is incredibly important. It's true though, that character creation per se may not be the most important thing for the player about why that is and how it is relevant in play. Jason and John's example of using pre-determined characters points to that. Though, how successfully those characters are or can be used in play means that at some point it's important, even if it's not done by the player themself.
