      \nThere is not a learning threshold for playing Polaris, there is a learning WALL that you must scale before you can play. The Pedagogy of Play guys have some tools to make that easier, but I have not seen them available online.
      Wait what. I played Polaris with my family over Thanksgiving, at the table and in the car on an 8 hour drive. None of them play RPGs, and teaching the entirety of the rules was no harder than teaching a new boardgame. In my book the mechanics support the play better than any RPG I've ever played - teach the mechanics, mechanically follow them at first, and in my experience everyone learns right quick what to do. Here was my method, boiled down: describe the setting briefly. Relate key responsibilities: Heart's job is to make-hero her protagonist, Mistake's is to antagonize, Moons are to make the situation such that all of the Heart's good decisions will give the knight experience (cowardice, sympathy for demons, hatred of People, etc). Create characters according to book's steps. "Here's a flowchart with the phrases you can say to move from freeplay to flowchart and back", briefly describe exact effects of phrases and exhausting Aspects. Say it's tragedy so you start with Zeal and experience moves you to lower Zeal to Weariness to higher Weariness, or refreshes your Aspects, and "we'll get to Ice and Light when someone gets experience but see that keyphrase? 1/6 chance to start off with, so be careful". Then have a scene start preferably with your opposite number's knight so you're Mistaken. If the Heart ever seems stuck, ask "what would your knight want most to happen right now? okay say that happens".

      Also:

      1. Freemarket

      Merry Christmas, Wilhelm! The gist is all there. There are nuances that may not come across, and it's a constantly evolving process. But that's Willem's original outline and a good place to start.

      Also, we "Pedagogy of Play guys" like to say "Fluency Play" now.

      My own anecdotal evidence: firstly, Willem arrived at the "Pedagogy concept after trying the "just dive in the pool!" approach and floundering. Myself, I tended to be skeptical that the bit by bit approach was really necessary, until I saw for myself how much smoother it was. I also sorta sheepishly realized that the awkwardness I'd always had in trying to teach any kind of game was related to the difference in approaches.

      Second, my wife was reading Polaris when I first brought it home, and loved the poetic writing, the setting, mood, concept, the whole works, and was talking about how much she was looking forward to playing it (she doesn't play story games much but she dabbles). then she stopped reading it and stopped talking about it. I wondered why, and later found out that when she hit some key game mechanics passages the thought of trying to keep all the Key Phrases straight from the get-go was too daunting and she mentally "shelved" the game. Now that she's seen how the Fluency model works in practice she's open to playing again!
