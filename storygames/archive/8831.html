\n Traditionally, "support" for games meant "more products, like supplements". These days it usually means "internet presence, downloadable materials, etc".

So the Burning Crew are pretty well known for their excellent support of their games. These come in the form of:
Forum support at their site: Answering questions, etc.
Mini supplements every now and then.
Enthusiastic help at other sites.
Lots of downloadable material, like sheets and stuff.

What other forms can "good game support" take?

-Andy

    \n AP reports from the designer. While it's helpful to see how other fans play a game, if the designer posts an actual play report, it often sheds some light on how an ideal game (according to the designer) should flow. This can be a big help, particularly if that kind of information isn't included in the game text.

    \n Great post.

    I've been thinking about this stuff for Verge. I want to write directly in the rules, "Hey, man, if you have questions, email me and I'll help you. I suspect people are wiggy about doing that, because random strangers won't believe that I actually want to have a dialog with people playing my game (but I do). I will need to set up another, less personal web forum for chicks and dudes to chat with me.

    I think good game support can also include:
    • Presence at cons. I want to be able to go to cons and play the game, maybe even with the author.
    • Purchase availability. I want to be able to buy the damned game. This is pretty basic, but I don't think it's a "given" in the indie arena.
    • Fan base. I want to see what other people are doing with the game. I don't want to feel like I am the only one playing it. Getting help doesn't just mean talking to the designer. It can mean talking to other folks who have played the game and have solved the problems I am having, without the designer ego getting in the way.
    • Swag. Can I get bumper stickers or t-shirts for this game? Cuz that could be cool. It's a way to tell other people, "Hey, ask me about this awesome game I play."
    • Translations. Is the game available in a variety of media (print, PDF, eBook) and foreign languages? I might want to tell my Swedish friends to play it.

    \n An idea I've been playing around with lately is ''What can I play with your game?''

    For example, for Dark Days, I've been writing down how things would work if you wanted to play a campaign based on the TV series ''Carnivale''. I plan on explaining characters and events in terms of the Dark Days universe and try and analyze the series in a way that would prove helpful for someone playing a similar campaign.
    Expanding on that, you could definitely write some essays like ''How to play Hellblazer with my game'' and such.

    \n Another approach I would appreciate, when it comes to communicating how the game is supposed to be played, is video. If game authors started to do video snippets, focused tightly on some difficult aspect of the game, showing how the mechanics are working and how players can appropriate them, that would be really useful. Think of those players that don't go to cons but still wish they got a demo of the game. I think the style of the first Sons of Kryos vidcast would fit the need perfectly.

    \n I've been experimenting with fan-created support. A lot of source material for the Books of Pandemonium has been written by fans, so I make the PDFs and whatnot available on my site.

    I also publish a magazine called Crux, which is sold at cost via Lulu (or available as a free PDF download). It consists of fan-created scenarios, monsters, and additional materials (and I usually toss in a couple of items myself). The first issue filled up pretty quick, and I'm getting ready to publish a second issue.

    Mostly, I do it because it's cool. Got the whole Dragon/Rifter thing going on.

    But -- it's also provided the community with a sense of ownership, and it's great support for the games, and it's something that keeps people coming back to the forums to discuss the new material.

    \n Multiple formats, to suit buyer's preferences - Hard back, soft back, print-at-home PDF (less art, grayscale, pre-imposed, etc), on-screen PDF (hyperlinked, completable and auto-calculating forms, possibly animations or video).

    Gamer/GM networking tools - Forums and/or calendaring software and/or "Facebook-like" socnets. (This is much more relevant for LARPs, I think, particularly those seeking to run under a national chronicle with consistent rules and timelines.)

    Posted By: David ArtmanMultiple formats, to suit buyer's preferences
    \nThis reminds me, somewhat tangentially, of the excellent idea John Stavropolous of releasing multiple editions of a game in different flavors for different markets. For example, he suggested I could put out Misspent Youth in its current, punky form, as well as in an anime/Pokemon form, and in an Avatar-like form. You change the art, the layout, and the flavor of the text with each version but the basic game remains the same. The idea here is not to resell the same game to multiple people, but to reach out to markets you're not currently addressing.

    I've been too lazy to act on it, but I like the idea a lot.

    \n Lots of games have weblogs, but how many have frequently-updated weblogs?

    Point to fan-created content in the main news blog for the game. Answer game questions. Make it a major point of contact. Call out things from the forums all the time.

    Add the option to subscribe to the blog via email. Many blogs do this but not many do it the way they should, e.g. loudly and with some consideration.

    Add the option to get a well-designed (well-templated actually, otherwise it's impractical) PDF emailed to them every week, instead of getting the individual blog posts. Free PDFs on a regular basis, even if they're only two or three pages, would make a certain segment of gamers go nuts for you.
