[Get Out] Encouraging hostilities between players

DanielSolis
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Story Games
At GenCon, Chris Chin had a funny story about the first edition of Exalted. Apparently, it described a system as a way to prevent bitterness and hostility between players. The assumption that such negative feelings existed between players at all times struck us as quite hilarious. He captured it perfectly when he said, "That's like Chess being a system to prevent knife fights between the two players."

So that begs the question: What if a system were made that aggravated existing tensions between the players even if there were none before? Here's the little idea, with many thanks to Shreyas for helping me kick it up a notch.

Get Out

Begin with any number of participants in a room.

All participants try to force each other to shout "GET OUT!" The person who shouts this must refer to themselves in the third person. So, if Melanie wants to leave, she has to shout, "GET OUT, MELANIE!"

Participants can force each other to say this through whatever means necessary short of physical violence. You can lie, cheat, insult, berate, betray and otherwise persecute whoever you wish until they finally break.

When someone says "I want out" you must immediately stop berating them and allow them to leave the room. Outcasts should wait around in the same area until the game is complete.

The game continues until there is only one person left in the room, who ironically ends up being the one who is most hated by the rest of the group.

Now, I'm not actually condoning this kind of behavior, especially among friends, but I was just curious about exploring the darker side of manipulating player behavior through system. Honestly, I feel kinda dirty. :P

    Having slept on it, I feel bad for posting this idea in a forum where the opening splash page says "happy!" right there on the front, so here's a more positive (and slightly creepier) alteration:

    Instead of insulting the person you want to leave, kill them with kindness. Compliment them, tell them how much you appreciate their presence in your life, tell them all the nice things you never get a chance to say anymore. When they say "Thank you," you must stop complimenting them and let them leave the room.

    The weird twist about this is that whoever is the last person in the room is still kind of disliked by the rest of the group since they are either supremely narcissistic or can't take a compliment. :P

    Obviously these aren't really story-games, and not even really games at all, more like psychological experiments. But as Shreyas says, we use system as an interface to interact with one another. Are there any other examples of controlling player-to-player interaction either directly or through emergent properties of a system?

    Chris, I finally figured out what bothers me about this (the Exalted quote).

    Storytelling is about shared creativity, right? I could be wrong, but I think lots of us have TERRIBLE associations of bitterness and resentment about sharing our creativity with other people, dating back way before roleplaying games, to when we were kids and were slowly being taught that being artistic was only okay if you were good at it. So I don't think it's unreasonable for a game to suggest "hey, here's a game where there's a system to protect your creativity from the negative feedback of your peers," because I think there may very well be lots of people out there who do want to be creative, but feel like they aren't good enough to do so and don't want to get hurt trying -- and this isn't because roleplaying games are frequently socially messed up, but because collaborative creation is frequently socially messed up.
