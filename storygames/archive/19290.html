\n Hello again everyone.

I've got my third game through closed testing and now I'm ready for some open playtesting. If you think you'd like to get in on some of that let me know daniel@hazardgaming.com.

I imagine before committing to such an enterprise though you'd like to know what it's all about so here's the blurb from the website...


You've reached the location to request copies of the playtest documents for Hazard Gaming's latest games Nimbus and das Sonnenrad. So, welcome and thank you for checking things out.

Before continuing though I want to be absolutely clear: no reasonable human being should brook any argument excusing the abhorrent policies and actions of the Nazis. Das Sonnenrad is not a vehicle for perpetuating, even in fiction, the evil of Adolf Hitler.

Nimbus and das Sonnenrad are games about the sacrifices made on our behalf by people we will never know. Both games are set against the backdrop of World War Two but I am very aware that these types of sacrifice are not restricted to wartime, nor to soldiers. Every day people give everything for the well-being of others. I'd like to think I'd do the same but hope I never have to find out.

While it is undoubtedly easier to inhabit the mind-space of an Allied soldier risking everything for freedom, my hope is that some of you will accept the challenge of playing the "bad guys".

Das Sonnenrad opens in 1945 with all characters fully aware of the atrocities conducted by the Reich. However, they must also contend with the certainty that failure will likely lead to their loved ones falling victim to the depredations of Soviet troops advancing on Berlin. You will have many difficult decisions to make and I thank you in advance for attempting the challenge of reconciling these facts.


So there you go. Drop me a line if it sounds like something you'd be interested in taking a look at and offering a little feedback.

    \n Writing Nimbus was "easy". Both my grandfathers fought in WW2 (one with the "Forgotten Army" in Burma and the other against Rommel and the Afrika Korps) and my Great Uncle worked at Bletchley Park as a code cracker on Enigma , and I spoke with them about their experiences.

    Writing das Sonnenrad came from imagining what I would have done if I were a German in 1945, trying to reconcile hating Hitler and the Nazis for what they had done in the name of Germany with an all consuming desire to protect my loved ones from the Soviets.

    It's naive to think all Germans were monsters without the same fears for the safety of themselves and their loved ones as any British soldier during the battle of Britain.

    Das Sonnenrad is their story.

    \n So I finally have the time to do a first read.

    First the easy stuff, german language advice.
    It is "F\xfchrer" and not "Furher" which you get right once and wrong a few times on in the introductionary fiction.

    The intro fiction also pushes the characters to allready have lost their faith in nazism. Which is a good way to start I suppose but takes one source of conflict that would be true to late war operations of WW2, which would be still having fanatics around. But it is a good choice to not make it about playing Nazis and in the background scenes the loss of faith, if there ever was any can be used.
    Meeting with Hitler directly to get the task and the supernatural event puts the whole setting into pulp territory from the start, which allows to play it safe and have fun with the movie villain version of Nazis. But if the players do want to go deeper the potential is there and the working directly for Hitler part takes away the ability to deny who they really are working for. So that the characters have to face that topic.

    I am not sure if the game is the perfect tool to think about the Nazi soldiers as people and to experience their motivations. But it can work like elevated pulp films (for example Inglorious Basterds or Peckinpah's Cross of Iron) work, still having fun roleplaying while still hitting the topics. But I assume you aren't going the Misery Tourism route here anyway.


    To critique how the game works I really would have to play it. The scene structure and lists make sense from reading but it does not tell too much about how it actually plays. I fear that I will not be able to provide a german playtest quickly. But I will try and talk with a few other people about the game.
    I do think it might be an enjoyable and / or thought provoking experience but most of that would depent on the group around the table. I do not feel the game pushing for either too much.

    I hope this helps a little. If I read it again or have more thoughts I will post about that again.
