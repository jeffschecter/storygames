Yesterday a friend of mine was at KublaCon, and he picked up a copy of a Japanese game at the dealers room, which turned out to be a Japanese game company's fanservicey entries into Flying Buffalo's Lost Worlds "fantasy combat books," called "Queen's Blade." Each book represents a character, and you actually trade books with your opponent so they get a "first person" view of your character. This in turn reminded me of Cheapass Games' Button Men, where your playing piece is a button with numbers on it, that represents a character, not to mention Brawl, where each character is represented by a specific deck of cards.

I'm wondering if this approach -- with specific pre-made characters -- has been tried before with roleplaying games. People do pregens for cons and whatnot (and published Paranoia adventures usually have pregens included), but what about pre-made characters as a deliberate design choice? I can't help but think there's some game concept out there that would be served that way.

    I used premade characters for the LARP I ran at the weekend.

    Premade characters are superb. You can set them up so they play off each other: one character has an interest in investigating cults; another is a member of a cult.

    In a sense, it's odd that many games let players create their own characters. You end up with a mish-mash of characters, with nothing in common.

    Using premade characters alleviates that. You can set up your party of characters so they've all got common backgrounds, they all know secrets about each other and they all have character quirks that will set off each other's quirks.

    The use of pre-made characters is quite common in super-hero role-playing. This is especially true if playing using an existing universe (Marvel, DC), where part of the fun is not just playing any super-hero, but playing your favourite super-hero. If I remember correctly, the first edition of the Marvel Super-Heroes game had a very minimal system for designing your own characters. That might classify as a "deliberate design choice". Certainly many, if not most, of the published adventures for the Marvel and DC games were intended to be played with the stock characters.

    Also, I think that the Talislanta "archetypes" which seem to be the main path of character creation, provide enough detail that I think one could classify them as pre-gen characters. But that is simply based on an off-hand glance through the rules at one point.

    In my experience, the problem with pregenerated characters is finding the balance between so much backstory detail that the player doesn't really feel like they are contributing anything, to so little backstory detail that you might as well just have let them come up with their own characters.

    As a tie-in to Lost Worlds, Alfred Leonardi (the designer of those books), also did a series of equivalent books for Marvel super-heroes called Battle Books.

    Also, one of the scantily clad Queen's Blade characters appears to be named Echidna...something got a bit mixed up in translation there, as the character bears no resemblance whatsoever to a small, furry, egg-laying mammal. http://www.flyingbuffalo.com/echidna.htm

    Two friends of mine have started an RP company called Shifting Forest Storyworks and they specialise in Parlor Larps: larps designed to be run at the drop of a hat, in one room, with no (or extremely little) prep. Sortof like the How to Host a Murder games. The catch is that all their pre-gen character sheets also have questions on the back, allowing for personalisation of the character; the answers don't change the motives, just why they want them. And there's no pre-set ending. And it works really really well. The questions help the players get an idea of what their character is like as well as helping them get into the game-world mindset. I think it sits nicely in the gap between pre-gen and personalised characters.
