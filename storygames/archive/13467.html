\n I've been oer at another site, quizzing some players and ddesigners about how they get actual good play of a particular game from players, especially since it has some very particular constraints (it's generally played at conventions, a bit like a Braunstein, and the Bad Guy Players can't win the overall scenario in-fiction).

That got me to thinking more generally about the discussion over here regarding the "what is a RPG" thread and the responses that advocated for skipping that bigger subject and instead concentrating on describing how to play a particular game ( or style of game even).

So far, so good. But I also thought about JDCorley's responses indicating that he'd gotten quite a bit out of the various "What is an RPG?" sections over time.

Now all of that swirling around together made me think more about player level procedures that make games work (or not!). I also got thinking that in a very real way, that stuff should come before any nitty-gritty discussion of procedure, and also that it shouldn't be buried off in some "advice" section.

So here's the excersise and what I'd like a response on:

You as the designer or GM/initiating player of a game have basically five main bullet points (with only a sentence or two of follow up at most) to describe what players* need to do to get good play from a game/style of game and why. Further, this stuff is going to be front and center in either a rulebook or a convention handout, and it of necessity might be the only real thing that the players read (or have read to them) prior to jumping into play.

(*For now, I'm going to assume character-players, not any sort of GM player.)

Feel free to choose a particular game or a style of play as you will, keeping in mind that if you're really specific about an indie game that I and any other readers may actually be in the position of that hypothetical convention goer reading one of these things!

( I suppose a short explanation of the overall rub of the game, a 3-4 paragraph sentence is also allowable before the bullet points if you really don't think folks are familiar with the game).

GO!

    \n For LARP (particularly boffer/airsoft):
    * You will act as your character at all times during the game (downtime is optional); and but YOU ARE NOT YOUR CHARACTER.
    * You will engage in martial and ranged conflict safely, with controlled swings and aimed shots.
    * You will be clear about the effects you apply to others, but understanding if they can't hear you at times.
    * You will move about the venue safely and carefully, respecting out-of-bounds areas and other players' personal space.
    * You will help to entertain the others, help others portray their characters, and cooperate with organizers on both rules adjudication and venue maintenance

    I figure if every player that came to LARPs did those things, all the other confusions, mistakes, and rules blunders can be hand-waved, forgiven, or corrected when the action isn't fast and furious.

    Funny how they sound more "rule-like" because of LARP--no "advice" stuff here AT ALL, when it comes to player safety, maximizing the fun for the most folks, and the good of the LARP organization's reputation.

    \nSocially:

    * learn what the other players are psyched about, and try to get into that too

    * express what you're psyched about, and welcome the other players to do that too

    * if you get bored, make a suggestion that would make the game more fun for you

    * narrate to the whole group

    Procedurally:

    * decide what you really want before announcing intent

    * be clear on resolution methods and factors before announcing action

    My game:

    * imagine you're really there

    * be bold about trying new things

    * be cautious about trying new things that already have a body count

    * speculate; form theories

    * make it completely clear when you're speaking in- and out-of-character

    \n People seem to be missing the [this game] part of this.

    If you're playing D&D and I'm the DM:

    * When you're not rolling dice, make stuff up. I'm listening. The only thing that's sacred to me is what happens at the table.

    * Don't say "Can I?" Say "I want to...."

    * When you're rolling dice, play to win. I'm trying to win too. Make the baddest character you want; I can deal.

    * Keep the game speedy. When it's not your turn, prepare for your turn. And please roll your damage while you roll your to-hit.

    * Don't over-respect my preparation. Keep me on my toes. It's a delight when my plans are disrupted and I have to improvise.

    \n OK, Robert--these two are closely related AND speak to social contract more than procedure and rules:\n
    Posted By: David Artman* You will act as your character at all times during the game (downtime is optional);andbut YOU ARE NOT YOUR CHARACTER.
    This practice helps other players imagine the world and remain in the moment, free of distractions from out-of-game issues or responsibilities. LARP is an escape, but only if it is free of constant reminders that it's unreal and, to some extent, contrived.
    That said, remember that others are in character also, and NEVER take in-character actions personally, as a player. Another PC might hate your PC, even as its player considers you a friend, enjoys your company, and is playing to make you have more fun and get spotlight time. After all... if that player didn't want to engage with you in-game, it is easy enough to ignore your character and its agendas in favor of other PCs and plots. Be flattered that the other player is using their precious game time to play with you.\n
    * You will help to entertain the others, help others portray their characters, and cooperate with organizers on both rules adjudication and venue maintenance
    The first clause of this rule reminds players to try to be "on," because nearly all social rewards of LARP result from appreciation of your efforts by the other players. It doesn't matter how "awesome" your PC is if no one ever enjoys the evidence of that or no one finds that it enhances their own game time.
    The second clause is a large part of improvisational acting: do not block others' efforts to entertain YOU. As much as you can while staying in character, "go along" with the leads given you by other players. Maybe their acting is awkward--so what? Help them be awesome, and they will become more confident of their voice and abilities to entertain, which will have a knock-on effect of making them more fun with which to play.
    The third clause reminds players that the organizers are in-game for all the same reasons that you are: to entertain you, to help you explore your character and pursue your agendas, and to help you be awesome (which makes you even more entertaining--see the virtuous spiral in action?). So when they make a rule decision, it's not to block you or to play favorites; it's to be sure everyone has similar spotlight time and be sure each spotlight shines as brightly as the other. Go with their judgments.
    Finally, it is just plain HARD to get a venue consistently, and all come with the classic Scouting duty to "leave the site better than you found it." Clean up when asked, and volunteer to do so if not. Never litter, and pick up the litter of your fellow players (or even previous venue users!) without complaint. When a host finds their venue in immaculate condition after a game, they are far more inclined to invite your group back, or even give your group preferential scheduling!

    In a nutshell. Hell... that's going into GLASS. Thanks for asking me to unpack!

    \n John, I like your D&D list, especially since you specified that it was meant for when you were DMing.

    Here's my take on a general list for B/X D&D (clearly with my own biases showing...)

    Each game session make a point of taking some action/reaction or description that highlights at least 2-3 different elements of your character
    Your character is briefly described by four main elements: Character name (and short one-line description), Class, Alignment, and Game Stats (including HP and Gear as well). Shoot for a level of character portrayal about on par with a 1960s-1970s comic-book character or action movie character. We aren't shooting for High Art here (though it's really cool when it happens that way!)

    The D&D universe is one of both adventure and danger
    If you aren't dying or at least risking death or some other horrible fate once per session you're playing too cautiously; If you're getting your character (or other peoples' characters) killed more than twice per session you are being too bold.

    Color outside of the lines
    At least once per session try something that you think should be possible in the fictional universe which you either aren't familiar with the rules for, suspect no direct rules exist which cover it, or for which you suspect the DM doesn't know the rules which cover it. This helps everyone playing learn the rules and aids the DM in sharpening up their skills at ad-hoc decision making, as well as creating precedent for unknown rule situations.

    Why are we all together doing this again?
    Create reasons why the party is working together, even when the character concepts are in conflict. Create reasons why the characters are involved with the adventure even when it conflicts with your character concept. This is just a conceit that allows us to get playing the game so run with it.

    "My Game" evolves into "Our Game" over time
    In the first couple of sessions of adventuring, I'm going to simply give you places to explore and loot. After the first couple of sessions, you and your fellow players should begin to give me ideas about the kinds of adventures and parts of the game world you want to explore, both in and out of character.

    \n I'm going to use this thread to try this out for the design I'm working on for next year's Gen Con games.

    Title: Madness

    1. This is a game about saving the world and slowly going insane. It is played in two parts: Investigation and Adventure

    2. In Investigation you make up scenes about the wierd evil plots that are happening in your town. Roll a 7 or less on 2d6 and your scene passes the first check. Other players can add to or contradict you. If they contradict then roll again and the high roller wins.

    3. In Adventure you make up scenes about the horrible things you find in the cultist lairs (which drive you insane) make jokes (which relieve insanity) and kick cultist butt. One player is the bad guy the others are heroes. Roll for scenes as above.

    4. You get ten scenes per adventure but in the next round of investigation you only get as many scenes as you made scenes to drive yourself mad.

    5. If you don't defeat the cultists the "Catastrometer" moves one step closer to the apocalypse (six levels). You move one step up the insanity scale each time you come back from an adventure insane (six levels before you are out of the game.

    That was hard - and is not complete - but I like the idea of starting a rule book with this kind of overview. It just spells out play right up front. Everything else are variations on the theme.

    \n It's interesting to see the responses, based off the OP. I read it as (obviously!) speaking to social contract issues, not so much systemic procedures. Or, put another way, as bullets answering the question, "how to play this game when specifically NOT following a rule procedure."

    To use a D&D example, the requested "How To" bullets tell players how to portray characters, act synergistically with their team, share the spotlight, and so forth--stuff that the d20 system doesn't speak to AT ALL (except in "play advice"); one can easily play d20 as a miniatures game with absolutely no narrative hooks or "story" to explain the dungeon crawl (not even as much story or narrative justification that one find at the start of a scenario for Descent: JitD).

    \n How to play Burning Wheel as a player:

    * You will be playing a game in which internally conflicted characters endure adversity to pursue things they care about.

    * When writing Beliefs, start your character in crisis and keep him there at all times. You will be rewarded via Artha for doing this. If your Artha is low, it's because your character is out of trouble and is therefore leading a boring life. So get him in trouble ASAP. Change your Beliefs on the spot to make this happen.

    * If you don't know what to do next, look at your Beliefs. If a Belief doesn't strongly imply your next action, rewrite it until it does. Be prepared to fight hard for what you said you cared about in the game via your Beliefs. If you don't, that means you were lying when you wrote the Belief. Change it and play with principle going forward.

    * If you want something to happen, engage the system. Ask the GM if you don't know how to make something happen. Very rarely will the GM tell you no outright, but that might happen if you're angling for an outcome that's going to make it impossible for everyone to stay on the same page. This means you need to know what is possible in the system, so please learn at least the basic assumptions.

    * The game runs on bribes: do what your BITs tell you in a principled way now, and gain Artha so you can avoid complications in the future. There will be an ebb and flow between sticking to your BITs to earn this currency, and spending this currency to overcome the threat of complication. You will only very rarely be compelled to do something a BIT says.
