\n In another thread I talked a little about playing canon characters in a borrowed media property and used Blade Runner's Rick Deckard as an example. That little seed got my wheels turning and before long I was thinking seriously (yet again) about a game set in that world.

One of the things I like about Blade Runner \u2014 full disclosure: it's my favorite movie and I love it like I would an old, dear friend \u2014 is that it's so full of detail that I almost feel like I could walk away from Rick Deckard's story and just get lost in a whole other world with any one of hundreds of different stories going on. That to me is an indicator that it's a solid setting for roleplay: you don't need Deckard and his plight in order to game.

With that in mind, I'm going to fish for plot hooks and story ideas because I like to see what people come up with. And because I like to steal, of course.

The first thing that comes to mind, of course, is the blade runner unit. We see two blade runners in the movie \u2014 Deckard and Holden, natch \u2014 but there must be more. And escaped replicants must be a serious issue to warrant having a whole squad devoted to their pursuit and retirement. That alone makes for a good basic set-up and allows direct exploration of the movie's core issue of humanity and what it means to be human. But that can't be all of it or people are going to fell like they're just replaying the film with different characters.

    I consider Roy to be the only real protagonist in the story. With that in mind, I'd love to play replicants in the off-world colonies. Stories about the humanity of slaves are severely lacking in our larger cultural canon. Cuz I bet the off-world colonies suck hard for everyone. Their primary advantage is that they're better than dead Earth, where there's no longer an ecosystem.

    \n

    It occurred to me the last time I watched it (we have a similar relationship to the movie) that, with Tyrell dead, the only people capable of making replicant minds are replicants. There's some interesting gnarl in there. Maybe replicants are the future of humanity. Maybe their 4-year lifespan is a huge advantage. With generations going by that fast, they'll outpace humanity in just a couple of years. I mean, Roy is one of a line. There are more just like him, also figuring out their own biomechanics, also figuring out their own emotional responses, also figuring out their moral universe. No other ones can kill their creator, but I bet they're out there, coming up with their own solutions.

    \n

    ... and therein lies an interesting game that is both canon characters and not. You can play Roy, Priss, Zorah, Leon, or a bunch of other models that are built for particular functions. You have your own names, of course, but you're played by Rutger Hauer, Daryl Hannah, et al. No reason to believe there couldn't be multiples of each of you. A whole bunch of Prisses, taking over the brothel and generating a mother cult. An army of Leons leading a worker revolt. An infiltration team of one Roy, one Zorah, taking over the entire universe.

      • \n
    • Kills Zhora.
      • \n

      • \n

      • \n
    • Gets beat up.
      • \n
    • Gets saved by Rachael.
      • \n
    • Brings Rachael home.
      • \n

      • \n

      • \n

    \n
      \n
    • Goes to Earth to meet his maker
      • \n
    • Does actual detective work to find out who that is
      • \n
    • Kills Chu, who put him in this position, who gave him his eyes (which are symbolic of his soul)
      • \n
    • Conspires with Pris to gain access to Sebastian
      • \n
    • Befriends him
      • \n

      • \n
    • Kills Sebastian, who made his body, and Tyrell, who made him care
      • \n
    • Mourns Pris
      • \n
    • Forces Deckard to understand that he's a murderer
      • \n
    • Shows compassion that no one else in the movie does: he saves him without hurting someone else.
      • \n
    • Dies consciously, having accomplished everything conceivable in his life.

    \n Well, the bladerunner angle per se isn't the key. It's just the noir detective thing. Again, Android is a good example for this. Sure, there are artificial beings running around, but the murder (and surrounding events) isn't necessarily connected to that fact. Instead, it's about personal issues and massive conspiracies intersecting in this case. The SF setting let's you exaggerate current issues (such as corporate control, government cover-ups, or the underclass) and explore them through the crime story genre.

    Of course, I key into cybernoir heavily on color while I key into crime stories on thematics, so take all this with a grain of salt.

    Sam, Blade Runner is an example used throughout the book and is one of the reasons the game is designed the way it is. But keep in mind, I see Roy as one of very few protagonists in the story \u2014\xa0he's the only one who takes action that affects the course of the story, the only one who makes moral decisions, the only one with any integrity to test. Harrison Ford, himself, complained that there wasn't much to Deckard \u2014\xa0that the character didn't do anything \u2014 and I think the movie would have been better if there were more to him than a drunken, lonely, guilty pile of self-pity.

    \n

    Shock: is about what happens to your moral universe when the universe changes under your feet. If that's what you want to do stories about, then it's great. If you want to do really personal stories about human interactions between protagonists, you'd be better off sticking with PTA. I don't know much about Dirty World.
