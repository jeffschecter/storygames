\n I'm going to meander to the point.... So there has been a fair number of discussions (and usually are after a big con where we can air our frustrations) about players who are not engaging with our games on some level. It's work. It's not the loving story zeitgeist that generate pure awesome.

I get that when at a convention you don't have a lot of control over who comes to the table (although now a days I have pretty specific people play my games, often at direct invitation). I get we have friends that want to game with us and it's hard to exclude them. It's not impossible but it's not easy either.

So I get why we want to talk about "how can we better interact with a challenging player."

That being said:

God! Am I am bad person for not wanting to game with this type of player? No matter what my role I go to the table with these expectations: Bring the fun! Make everyone else look awesome! A good number of folks here have played with me. Am I a bully? (really, I'm asking so no ego here. Open, honest communication accepted).

Now I am not trying to make this a pity party or an introspection of "why we can't get the dog to play with Don." It's not about me specifically but I don't mind it if it's helpful and part of the equation. I'm boorish, opinionated and direct. I'm a 42 year old man. I can live with the social freight train perspective.

What I am looking for is this: What is the problem with putting the responsibility of maintaining social contract squarely on the shoulders of every player? I've always been about communicating what the game is about and what we all expect at the table. On occasion I have had to pull awesome out of people but eventually they get it or they don't come back. I just don't have a problem saying "hey, clearly you are running from conflict in the game and we said from the beginning that wasn't in the spirit of the game. It's cool if this style of play isn't your cup of tea but shit or get off the pot."

Thank for your input,
- Don

    \n Don,

    No, you are not a bad person but you might be a lazy person (I'm not calling Don or anyone lazy (yet)).

    When you say: "hey, clearly you are running from conflict in the game and we said from the beginning that wasn't in the spirit of the game. It's cool if this style of play isn't your cup of tea but shit or get off the pot."

    When you could say: "hey, clearly you are running from conflict in the game and we said from the beginning that wasn't in the spirit of the game. It's cool if this style of play isn't your cup of tea but I'm here to help if you you need. Look at your beliefs and attack one head on and I promise we'll have some fun."

    It is a subtle change. One is a confrontation and the other is supportive. Sometimes we have to realize that a game's style of play might not only be alien but might be behavior for which a player might get jeered at where they game every week. Easing them out of that mode can take just a little doing.

    That said, I'm not saying that we should be anyone's psychiatrist or occupational therapist. Sometimes, it is the wrong person at the wrong table at the wrong time. But I'd make that supportive effort and see how it flies first.

    Judd

    \n Conventions are more like dating. You spend time with people who are nearly strangers. You meet them. You get to know them. Sometimes you have magical, memorable experiences. Sometimes you're thinking about escaping out the bathroom window. Ya rolls yer dice, ya takes yer chances.

    Ongoing home games are more like marriage. It's important to start by carefully selecting your partners to increase the chance that things will work out well. But even after an excellent selection process, there will still be friction points. So it takes maintenance work and honest communication to try to work through those issues. It's worth the effort to try to be the change you want to see and to incentivize the behavior you want to encourage. But sometimes, even if all sides are honestly doing the work, it doesn't work out. And sometimes it turns out you married an axe murderer. Cest la vie.

    \n Don, the only questionable case I can see is at a con, and the solution depends on how you see your responsibilities there.

    Personally, as much as I'd love to play only with people who bring the awesome, I see RPG conventions as events where it's typical for awkward folks to pay money to be shown a good time in a welcoming social environment, and I'd like to oblige them. I consider their right to do what they want at my table to be infinite, up until the point where they're making it hard for another player to have fun. (If that's by making it impossible for me to GM, that counts too.)

    I've never played with anyone who deliberately ruined everyone's fun. I think I'd have no problem kicking them out of my circle of responsibility. "I no longer care about your good time. If you want your money back, tell the con organizer to talk to me later."

    When someone is accidentally ruining the fun, I do consider it my responsibility to do everything I can to find a solution that works for everyone. That person who's out of synch with the game and group is probably the person who most needs the friendly safe space a con may seem to offer.

    I find this difficult and taxing, and it's why I don't sign up to GM at cons lightly, and why I want free registration.

    Perhaps if everyone approached it the way I do, there wouldn't be enough GMs at cons.
