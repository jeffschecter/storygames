    \nI'm in the same game! I got to be Caller last night!

    You don't necessarily need to leave by the end of the session, but you need to be able to demonstrate that you have a clear path to the exit.

    Our session (exactly 4 hours) consisted mainly of figuring out how to easily and reliably get to and from the Tower regularly, and then we spent 90 minutes extricating ourselves from a nearly-disastrous pit trap. We encountered a single orc, whom we whipped a Sleep on before he saw us, and it was one of the most tense encounters I've had in years.

    So far, no 'funny behavior', and I suspect there won't be, due to the stronger nerd tendency to want to one-up each other; that thing John mentioned about getting xp for improving the previous version of the map is a powerful motivator not to fuck around too much. It's pretty amazing, and I am really looking forward to seeing it develop month-to-month.

    Supposedly we also get xp based on treasure we find, but only 1/10th standard for combat. So far, we need to take the treasure thing on faith, because we didn't find squat on our expedition. : P

    \n It seems odd at first but I suspect it adds to our general immersion (which is subjective and individual). Although the "do this and get XP" is overt, it's still tied to in character action. Our characters have a treasure map that we are drawing in and out of game. Not knowing the details helps us not power game (and there is nothing wrong with power gaming) and rather focus on our emotions, not our rational thinking. The unknown feels scary. Quantifying too much takes some of us out of the fiction (for this specific game).

    For example, the pit wasn't actually a huge deal once we knew how it worked mechanically. But without knowing those specific, it felt like a huge deal!

    Posted By: Paul T.Is there some reason for this, or is it just a way for the GM to leave room to be more flexible?
    \nPart is that the GM has an agenda to run a game that isn't specifically monster slaughter. In order to do that there are a number of house rules plugging into Basic Pathfinder. A bunch of these assumptions about rewards and mechanics are being tweaked from session to session. I suspect he hasn't spelled it out because it's still in flux.

    The rewards, and many of the mechanics, are usually a party based average. Individuals aren't winning xp, the party is and then you receive your share at the end of the evening. etc.

    \nThe only clash, for me, was with the way skills were being handled. Enormous amounts of pixel-bitching Perception checks and the like. I'd actually prefer fewer skill rolls and more established procedures for simply describing the way we search a room.

    But the combination of some tough monsters combined with 1/10th XP for combat really makes the game overall work very well in terms of keeping to the goals.     - 270xp for creature threats thwarted (that's NOT just the one you sleeped - he was just worth 13xp)

    Posted By: Paul T.How are the sessions and the parties organized, by the way? It is like West Marches, where you assemble a group for a dungeon "raid" and then schedule it with the GM?
    \nit's an online cattle call. there are a pool of players and a few dates are scattered over a couple of weeks with a handful of slots per session. the general understanding is that each player can attend once per wave. if there is an unpopular evening, the GM opens that date up to the general playing pool.\n
    , what happens if you can't finish a session with a way out of the dungeon? What if you *think* you know a way out, but your map is flawed?
    \nmind you, a lot of this is flying-by-your-pants house rules so much of this is developing as we go. The two main principles for the game are that they be a: not just monster farming; and b: each session be self contained. so that you're not wringing your hands about how to deal with player loss from session to session. So the exit, to this point, has been somewhat handwaved in a quick epilogue in the closing minutes of the session. For now, we're still all wrangling with the mechanics of getting to the dungeon per session so some of the crunch is still there.
