So, JBR brought up the idea of intertextuality in another thread (stories which explicitly respond to other stories). For example, Rosencrantz and Guildenstern are Dead relates to Hamlet, and The House of Mirth draws from Jane Austen's observations of the social realm.

I'd be curious to hear how and whether people here relate their games to existing stories. Hence, here are some questions for thinking and discussion!

Do you ever set out to play or design a game with the intention of retelling, modernizing or exploring a familiar story? What stories inspire you the most? How does your game continue to relate to the original material? What happens when your game, as games do, takes a left turn?

Do you ever set out to play or design a game that conflicts with, opposes, or brings into question a familiar story? Which stories? How do you incorporate the original material? How do you decide which parts to oppose, and what does it mean to you?

Do you make clear to your players what stories you're drawing from? How much do you actually use the original material, and how much is it 'inspired by'? Are there certain kinds of stories that work better with certain games? Are there certain kinds of stories that work better in general?

Do you find yourself turning to books, movies, television shows? All of the above? Do you find yourself looking to recent works or to older material? Do you go out and investigate stories just so that you can work them into your game? Do you often rely on what you know about a particular story from hearsay or cultural familiarity? Do you plan to use other works in this way, or do you wake up one morning and realize your game is Hamlet (so to speak)?

I'm sure there are more questions that one could ask, but this should get things started nicely.

--Jess

    We make new stories in relation to the stories that we have heard and told before. There ain't no way to start from a clean slate.

    I don't do this intentionally. I don't have to do it intentionally. It happens whether I intend it or not.

    I start with any event ... say "The King dies" ... and past stories crowd into my head:

    • Does the kingdom's greatest warrior succeed? Where was that warrior at the time of the king's death, anyway?
    • What about the wife? What about the son? What about the uncle?
    • And a new King arose in Mitzrayim, who did not know Joseph ...
    • Odysseus "madly" plowing his field, so as not to fulfill his civic duties ... does the proposed heir even want the job?

    I can't unthink these things, once they're thought ... I can respond to them in many ways, as you list, but I can't not be influenced.

    I do this all the damn time, but as a former literature student, it's actually pretty hard for me not to. My default frame of reference when telling stories is that they are embedded in a context of other stories, and this is at the same time a potent tool and an occasional stumbling block.

    I favor games that explicitly allow or even encourage this kind of play. I've played lots and lots (and lots) of Changeling, for instance, which basically has you take a myth/legend/fairytale and live it out in the modern world. In Tribe 8, the local goddesses are all based off of female figures of myth and history (Joan of Arc, the Black Dahlia, Baba Yaga), and coming from their tribe constitutes making your interpretation of the impact that those female figures had.

    With my usual players over my gaming history, running out and doing in-depth research for characters and plot lines is de rigeur; I've played with lots of academics who don't bat an eye at plumbing the yellowed stacks of the library to satisfy a minor point of curiosity. For a lot of us, it's also a way to recontextualize the things that are near and dear to us. So we bring our backgrounds in, say, Indian history and myth to bear on our game play (*cough*cough*Brand*cough*).

    So that's all the nice bits. On the other hand, this can crimp your style if you're not too careful. There's a difference between recontextualizing and simply re-enacting. To take a terrible example, I once played in a game that was based off of the anime title Ninja Scroll -- but the GM didn't tell us that, and the GM was the only one who had seen the movie. We faced a succession of badguys ripped directly from the movie, and no matter what we did or how we tried to overcome them, the only way to win was to do what the hero had done in the movie -- which wasn't always an obvious option (dude, you totally have to dunk the guy with a beehive on his back into the river, and then the bees will sting him as they drown -- duh!).

    Now in my older-and-wiser days, I recognize that it's all good to crib situation from other sources and letting the characters create their own addresses to that situation, but expecting play to mirror the plotline is probably just silly.

    I've been thinking about reusing things from other sources recently, and want to relate an experience of my own, from the other side of things.

    A few years ago, when I first started playing with some people who have become good friends, the GM would reuse things from various TV shows. This made me unhappy, because he'd seen the TV show, his partner had seen the TV show, and I hadn't. He'd introduce something to the game, and while I'm still asking questions and working out what the hell was going on, the other player is up and doing, because she recognised the situation, had previously thought about it, and knew what she wanted to do.     Illuminatus! Trilogy (novels)
    Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas (novel)
    Twin Peaks (TV series)
    Bossanova by Pixies (rock album)

    I have told the players that the game is based on these sources and they are all to some extent familiar with them. It's working quite well, but I'm using the sources as inspiration for types of NPC / Situation / Atmosphere rather than some kind of plot template.

    It gives me a kind of handy shordhand for creating situation: any time I need to have something I just think "Hmm what would Wilson / Thompson / Lynch / Black Francis do?"
