    \n I've sent quite a few people to prison and dealt with them when they got back.
    \n
    \nRealistically:
    \nUnless you cause major problems, majority of the day is spent doing jobs (laundry, yardwork, cleaning, ec.). Also, lots of classes: addiction, religion, GED, some college, cognitive rehabilitation.
    \nPrison gangs are real: Black gangs (Folks/Crips, Bloods, Gangster Disciples), Hispanic Gangs (Latin Kings, Mexican Mafia, MS-13), White (Aryan Brotherhood), Bikers (Hells Angels, Pagans, Outlaws).
    \n
    \nIdeas: Drug dealing, "hits" on other prisoners, sexaul assault, cooperation among "rival" factions (Crips working with AB to sell drugs), escapes, smuggling items in, contraband (making hooch in the toilet, shanks out of toothbrushes, drugs, porn)
    \n
    \nAll I can think of for now. Hit me up if you have any questions.

    \n I've treated some people who've spend extended periods of time in prison (as a social worker in mental health).

    I'm impressed by the amount of time and effort spent making alcohol and acquiring drugs. They have to hide the smell - so they put the brew in the ventilation system so the smell is everywhere.

    There is a lot of time spent playing mind games. It's all about establishing a pecking order. In the 50's the guards were solidly in charge. They still enforced the rule of silence in many ways (a 19th century rule where by the inmates were not suppose to talk to one another - BTW this tend to drive people crazy). This changed with prison reform in the 60's and teh 70's. Now the inmates establish pecking orders through gangs and other affiliations (religions, AA, classes, work). People get killed over changing the TV channel.

    I think the mind games with the guards might be the most interesting in an RPG. Cops and Robbers all the time.

    When new guys come in they are called "Rabbits" and what happens to rabbits is they get eaten. I won't ellaborate.

    Then there is solitary confinment. One guy told me that the guards are able to modify the temperature is it is like a skinner box from hell. This is the guards payback from the mind games.

    Being in prison too long tends to induce panic disorder and agoraphobia when people are discharged.

    Prison is a total institution where people have very little say over their schedule. This breeds learned helplessness.

    Oh and then there are the jail house lawyers. I think this is another version of the mind games already mentioned.

    Prison is also bad for the people who work in them. The guards take home the authoritarian habbit - which makes their kids rebel. And therapists are locked in the mind games attempting to help. It is a thankless task. Some become hard asses others don't fight and let the crazieness go by them. I can't say which is better or worse. All I know is I would never want to do that work!

    Hope this is helpful!

    Chris Engle

    \n The reason I ask is that I'm, in case it isn't obvious, considering a game about the topic. The thought that occurred to me early on was that you're constrained in what you can do, so basically there's a big list of the actions you can take and when it's your time to do something you pick it off the list. Inspired by Contenders and then later on by Apocalypse World basically, I mean I guess they're 'moves'. It's also about seeing the story emerge from people being funnelled down these particular and sometimes extreme courses of action when forced to react to something that threatens, displeases or hurts them.

    Posted By: nocluePosted By: ccreitz"Gang Leader for a Day" is actually a fairly light read and very rewarding too. Recommended.
    Yes. I was looking at this a few months ago in Borders, and it's on my to read list. He's got another book out as well, about the underground economy that runs the street scene in Chicago. Very cool as well. It's worth reading for sure, but as a literary artifact Off the Books has a more academic tone, and it's a bit of a slog. Gang Leader for a Day has a unifying narrative and a strong central cast of characters, and reads like journalism. That said, if you have any interest in the ways that a person could live off the formal economic grid, but still in a major city, you should definitely read both, and probably OtB first, as its analysis of power concentration and the lack of a medium of exchange give additional insights into the motivations of the characters in GL.
