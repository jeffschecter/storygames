\n
\n
\n
\n\nGB_Steve \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2011 \n edited August 2011 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I don't really have any theories about this but I'd like to see some kind of non-shouty discussion about the status of story gaming as a community or a project or whatever it was. The reason I ask is that I've just been to one of the happiest smiliest Gen Cons ever and there seemed to be much less of an out-there story gaming presence. That's not to say that many of those good people weren't there (although I did miss many that weren't) but they were functioning in what seemed like a different, perhaps quieter, perhaps less "we're in this against something (something not necessarily well or easily defined)"-way.

I'd like to think that the story project has in many ways succeeded in changing a lot of what happens in games and is gradually seeping into the greater conciousness, or even being welcomed there.

What do you think?

(I orginally wrote indie instead of story but thought the latter was less inflammatory. You know the kinds of games I mean. Let's not focus on that).

    \n It feels like we've unlocked a spectrum, and the notion that people can play the kind of games they like and never look back.

    There are folks who continue to push the boundaries of what can be a game, but it strikes me as no longer being about fighting to prove something. The fight's already been won. Now we're earnestly carving out new design space.

    Some folks are realizing they love old skool, and are returning to it, or recreating it. Some people are realizing they love structured freeform, and exploring different traditions in that vein (GMless Morningstar stuff, Jeepform, others). Some people are realizing they love sex and violence, and playing "to the pain," and they're pushing that envelope for themselves.

    I feel like there was a struggle, to prove that games could be more than one thing. And then there was a struggle to prove that they could be about transgressive things, about scary things, about personal things. There was a struggle to prove that games could be mundane. I feel like our time of struggling to prove shit is largely over, and now it's a time of struggling to refine our spectrum of ideas.

    \n Honestly, I feel like this is a plateau, which happens all the time in whatever endeavor you're at. A lot of the game concepts that seemed alien or really difficult to implement just 5 years ago seem much more possible and accessible now. Personally, I hit a brick wall a few years back with a few of my designs, which were just beyond my ability to really implement well, but the advances in thinking and product design and audience familiarity/willingness in the last few years have led me to return to some of them and feel like I can make progress again. It definitely feels different now, whether that is perception or reality.

    But, at the same time, this plateau is just the beginning. Fighting for "games can do / be about different things" was a worthy thing, but that should have been obvious from the get-go, right? There are so many important battles that people are beginning to chip away it -- about accessibility, about product design, about inclusivity, about handling different kinds of content, about changing people's thinking and behavior, about mixing methods, etc. Plus, once the impossible games become increasingly possible, it's time to expand our vision to things beyond what we can easily imagine.

    Can we make games that emulate documentary films? Can we make games that don't try to emulate another form of media at all, but really embrace gaming as its own medium? Can we make games that will save people's lives? Etc, etc. There's so much left to do.

    Also, "phases" like this can only really be diagnosed in retrospect. It's much easier to talk about the phase we just left than whatever phase is happening now or will happen next.

    Posted By: J. WaltonFighting for "games can do / be about different things" was a worthy thing, but that should have been obvious from the get-go, right?
    \nJonathan, none the less, it was a hell of a fight. And any fight should not be discounted just because it was won and is now in the past. What seems obvious at one time was not obvious before.

    I think we've all got stories like Jesse's -- that "before and after" picture of the transition that took place over the last eleven or so years.

    Mine was RPG.net. I remember talking about Kickers in 2001 or so -- the notion that the Players come up with what they want their Characters to be engaged with in terms of story; that the GM doesn't have plot or an adventure. Now, I'm not claiming utter novelty for the notion of Kickers in RPG play (though as a rule for play, it clearly was.)

    What I am saying I was told, literally, to shut up an stop talking about it. I was told -- who today comes to these boards and is a peer to many story game folks people in the LA game community -- to get off the RPG forum and take it to the Game Design forum, since clearly Kickers were a) not worthy of talking about in an RPG forum and b) the mere presence of the discussion is too much to bear.

    Now, one can say, "Well of course that shouldn't have been happening." But it did. All the time. Just talking about this stuff seemed to rile up the strangest responses. And now? People are happily mixing and matching and trying new games and openly talking about all sorts of games over at RPG.net. That's a big change.

    So, in my view, the phase story games are entering is "normal."

      \n As for the future of Story Games, I think a lot depends on how well future generations can learn from past generations.

      I think that in turn depends on accessibility. Searching through forums and picking through a zillion threads for nuggets of wisdom doesn't count. Nor does being given 300 different "must play" game recommendations from 100 different aficionados.

      We need a more user-friendly trove of knowledge. I've made some efforts to nudge the Forge Glossary toward being that, but that effort didn't go as I'd hoped.

      So, big picture-wise, I'm guessing we're in part of the "post-WoD* baby steps" phase. 99% of what current story gamers have innovated will be forgotten and reinvented later. The 1% that will survive makes me happy, though.

      *the mid-'90s explosion of World of Darkness games being the last "phase" of large-scale relevance that I can identify

      Posted By: David BergWe need a more user-friendly trove of knowledge.
      \nThis.

      I think the corpus of story games out there has grown to be so diverse that the single label of "it's a story game" doesn't say much.

      For instance, I've hated my play experience with some games and really really liked some others. And some of them I just thought were kind of meh. I know it's not a matter of how well designed the games are, because some of those game that I didn't enjoy are getting accolades left and right. By now, I've played enough games that I kind of have an instinctual inkling of what kind of game I like and what kind of game I don't like. But I still can't put words on these types (and I have this gut feeling that I have basically one or two types I really like).

      The way I see this, if I came into this subset of the gaming hobby right now and tried a single story game to see if I would keep on digging the body of games out there, it would basically be a coin toss. Pick a game I like, and I keep digging. But if I pick a game I don't, I'm done with story games with no inkling that this game has jackshit to do with that other game I was thinking of trying.

      Someone needs to come up with a way to recommend games in a way that fit a person's tastes real fast, or I'm under the impression that story games will end up losing fans on that coin toss that they could've brought on board with a better "referral."
