Why are Concepts always in title case?

Judson_Lester
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games
This a really little idea, actually. Nothing more than a quibble, really.

In actually writing my own games, I tend to come up with the issue that I come to a crux paragraph where everything gets related. And that Paragraph is Full of Title Case, because all the Concepts of the Gottverdammt Game are in There!

And so I begin to ask myself, why do I Title Case game concepts? I mean, there is a convention there, and it does spill out to game theory, but it's not a conventional convention. Other fields of thought, other specifications, other kinds of rules don't capitolize their concepts. They might, if you're lucky, _**bold and italicize**_ their first appearance, along with a definition. But even that is pretty patronizing.

By extention, why do we talk about Story Now, even when we're referring to a narrativist creed rather than a particular article.[1] See, I even, on purpose, called it "narrativism," with a little "n." Is that wrong? I mean, isn't narrativism a philosophical idea, like communism, rather than a organization, like the Communist Party?

fn1. Which would be "Story Now," right? Cuz it's an article, which would be published in a larger work, rather than an atomic work in it's own right, in which case it would be ??Story Now,?? right?

    All good points. But ultimately, while I'm curious about the origins of using capitalization of game terms like Aspect, which then transitions into theory terms like Stance, I'm more bemused by the convention in the first place.

    Go to any other field. Check out a computer language specification, check out a philosophical treatise published since 1900, check out a business magazine. There are lots of specilized terms, and common terms used in a specialized sense, and none of them get capitalized for reasons you wouldn't find in a Strunk's guide.

    Now, granted, there is the "beside their common counterparts" angle. And I'm tempted to blame White Wolf for that one. But, honestly, it's probably not that hard to carefully select your conceptual terms so as not to collide with common terms.

    I think this matters because none of the explainations I can come with are flattering to game designers as a whole. In terms of being able to hold my head up high where ever I am, I'd like to be able to at least justify the quirky Old English stylistic convention.

    Eh, it's not a game-specific flaw. You see it in other works that try to reach a largely non-or-anti-professional audience with technical writing. I think in particular of business management, "inspirational", and other pop-social-science fields.

    You want to use "plain language", because your audience prides itself on being "plain folks" or because that's part of your branding, but you need to communicate terms-of-art and technical concepts.     **Mark:**

    bq. Honestly, I have a hard time imagining a way to write snappy, effective game text that doesn't somehow call out terms-of-art.

Any specialized information will need to use terms-of-art (which is an excellent steal from legal jargon), and most of the time it's used without special treatment. It's assumed that the audience is intelligent enough to pick out the special meaning of the phrases from context.

    **talysman:**

    bq. Actually, old (and middle) english didn't capitalize nouns, but in the 18th century, several philosophers started capitalizing abstract nouns and the like.

    I stand corrected. Still, that's interesting to find out. I wonder where the general impression that Old English capitalizes nouns comes from... Honestly, the inference that it comes from German was pretty natural. And it seems to me that, for example, Victorian newspaper ads do use capped nouns.

    bq. Not everything was, though: mainly stuff you would see as labels on a character sheet, or headers in a table.

    I can see that. And my vague recollection is that it's **Hit Points:** on the character sheet and "hit points" in the text. At least the old red book text. And it's right and proper to capitalize a label or table header.

    Other sources of capitalization that I think got absorbed into RPGs:

    * Acronyms are capitalized. Then the capitalization gets carried back the the original phrases, hence Game Master and Player Character.

    * This residual capitalization of acronymic phrases is further reinforced by a cargo cultism of organizational acronyms. For instance NASA(National Aeronautics and Space Administration) is capitalized, so obviously, if it's an acronym, it gets capitalized, _nicht war?_

    * Stylistic imitation of medical and scientific texts. Especially in psychology, I have the (layman's) impression of significant phrases being capitalized. The reason there is that the phrases are in fact titles: Meyer's-Briggs Personality Test, for instance.

    I tend to think, and I'll certainly be experimenting with this seriously as I rewrite Greylight and start futzing with my spy game, that between choosing terms carefully, coining the occasionally neologism if necessary, and relying on context to disambiguate between common terms and terms-of-art.
