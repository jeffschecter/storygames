\n I\u2019d love to see more examples of concessions because I think it\u2019s hard to see what you can ask for, and not ask for, both in comparison with being Taken Out, and in comparison with the opposite, Not Losing.

For example, say you are fighting against some \u201cdi sma undar jordi\u201d creatures in a dungeon and it\u2019s going pretty good for them. You decide to concede.
Can you ask for getting away safely, running away? (Will that trigger a chase Contest?)
Can you ask for getting capture but not killed? What if these \u201cdi sma\u201d are super vicious?
What if they turn you into slaves\u2026 what happens?

I guess it\u2019s more that I\u2019ve got some procedural murk, I think, namely: who decides? What is the protocol?
Is it: conceder proposes (along with terms), the other can accept the concession or fight to the death?
Or does the other have to accept?
I mean, what if the PCs are fighting some NPC dork and the dork wants to concede in order to flee in their magical balloon.
Can the PCs say no, we want to keep on fighting until we get a Taken Out result, because we want the dork behind bars?

    Interrigator inflicts enough stress/consequences to bring the subject close to being taken out.
    The interrigation subject conceeds and spills the details of the super evil project/location/plot (showing the Interrigator's "Won", but narrates that he gets a bargain for cooperating and maybe some better accomodations in prison)

    Alternatively, the Interrigator "takes out" the subject
    The subject spills the beans but won't get the plea bargain they would have asked for. The Interrigator throws them in to survive with the General Population in prison and charges them with something narritively appropriate.

    \n Yeah, Core kinda skimps on the details here.

    I figure that The Dresden Files RPG, being the most mature official Fate game to come out before Core, is a worthwhile guide to clearing up stuff that seems murky in Core. The Dresden rules (Your Story, pg 206) say that the gaming group as a whole gets to say whether the proposed concession is reasonable or not. It says that the terms of the concession "have to represent a clear and decisive disadvantage" for the character conceding. It also gives some guidelines: The conceding character should have at least one moderate or worse consequence, or have some kind of significant difficulty created, or something that restricts them. Or some other outcome that seems equivalent to those. I think the discussion of costs in Fate Core (pg 190) might be a useful source of ideas.

    Dresden also suggests that the group discuss how lethal conflicts are going to be, and that in games where it's not assumed that any conflict can kill you, the GM should hint at players at the start of (or even before) the conflict when an antagonist is looking to kill. Concessions then become a mechanism for insuring that PCs only die when it's meaningful to the player.

    There's nothing in there about what happens when a concession is refused. I think the assumption in current Fate is that if a character concedes, the opponent doesn't get to refuse it. Which isn't how things worked in Spirit of the Century; there concessions could be refused, but the character refusing loses a Fate point, and the character who offered the concession gains one.

    \n IMO it all comes down to what is most interesting and what fits the theme/genre of your game. Remember, you're not (or shouldn't be at any rate) playing to "win". How I see it concessions are designed to simulate the classic example of the villian getting away. Look at most pulp media (the genre FATE seems to really be designed to emulate IMO).

    The heroes corner the villian in his evil lab after a lengthy fight, "there's no escape !". "Ha, that's where you're wrong!" And POOF the villian dissapears in a cloud of smoke!

    The villian is clearly loosing the battle and the hero makes some sort of attack with a social skill causing mentel stress. That hit is the point where the villian decides to conceed. He's been bested and he knows it. He gets away, but maybe he left some valuable info in his lair in his hasty retreat.

    Sure, the hero PC could go, "nope your reign of terror ends here!" And veto the concession... And maybe in this instance that's the dramatically appropriate thing to do. It's all up to the group at the table, GM included, to decide if this is the appropriate place to end a story arc. Maybe the players like this particular villian and want to keep tangling with him. Maybe the villian is tied to one of the PC's in some way (twin, best friend, parent? Etc). Maybe it's time for a change of antagonist for the game. It's all a judgement call for everyone involved.

    I just watched a video of Burning Wheel in play and it seemed like they had a very detailed protocol for \u201ccompromises\u201d in Battle of Wits\u2014I have revised lying around somewhere here but never read the BoW chapter.
    \nYup! In Burning Wheel (which is a phenomenal game; I have the later Gold edition, I don't know how Revised compares), both sides enter the conflict with a pre-determined goal. This has to be mutually agreeable. Goals are binary: they happen or they don't happen. This means that you usually have two orthogonal goals. If Side A wants to exile Side B, then Side B doesn't have to make their goal "I am not exiled", because if Side B wins, Side A's goal isn't gonna happen anyway.

    Let's say that Side A's goal is: "You will accept exile from this country."
    Let's say that Side B's goal is: "You will agree to release the Black Fox from the dungeons."

    Then, both sides determine their Body of Argument; it's effectively their hitpoints for the conflict. They chip away at one another, and then--when one side is dropped to 0 Body of Argument, they lose. At this point, you figure out compromises (unless one side blew the other away and sustained no damage).

    If you lost a point or two, you have to give up a minor compromise. Give up a small concession from your goal, or agree to a minor condition from the other side. E.g., if Side A loses, but chipped a little bit off of Side B's BoA, then the Black Fox is released...but perhaps with a conspicuous brand on their face. (Ouch!) That's the extra condition behind Side A's intent.

    If you lose a substantial amount, you have to grant a big concession or extra condition. E.g., Side A loses, but took a big chunk out of Side B. The Black Fox is released, but they're all exiled from the country for the span of a month, until the political situation dies down.

    If you lost almost all of your BoA, you get your goal, but it should "feel like losing". E.g., Side A loses, but only narrowly. The Black Fox is released and branded. But Side B is arrested, branded, and released a week later, to teach them a lesson about standing up to Side A.

    The compromise is discussed among all the players, and it should be honestly agreed on, within the guidelines. (And you need to be honest when you have to give a big concession! Take that hit!)

    ~~~

    Back to the specific example in Fate, though--the intent of the characters should really be made clear. If the Warden wants to kill the warlock, then that should be spelled out. That makes the Concession rules easier to apply.

    From the SRD, my emphasis in bold...\n
    Concession gives the other person what they wanted from you, or in the case of more than two combatants, removes you as a concern for the opposing side. You\u2019re out of the conflict, period.

    ...

    Yes, you lost, and the narration has to reflect that. But you can\u2019t use this privilege to undermine the opponent\u2019s victory, either\u2014what you say happens has to pass muster with the group.
    \nConceding and saying "I get away" does not give the Warden what they wanted. Only death will. In that case, I think the GM should've been more creative. Thinking on that, I would probably say "I die, but not before I accomplish X." For instance, "I die, but as a Concession, I unleash my death curse on you." or "You kill me...just as I immolate a crowd of civilians." If they really want the kill, they have to live with the consequences. (That being said, I'm sure it was a speedy judgement by the GM.)
