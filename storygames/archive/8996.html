    \n I've been running a campaign for three weeks, wherein the players desired Owlbear mounts. Essentially the owlbears just hang out, being INT 6 (INT 8 means you can't read; INT 6 in my game means you don't know you can't read, if that makes sense) bastards to the players. They all think their name is "Owlbear."

    Recently they came across a group of Owlbear slavers, who breed humans for their delicious meat. I gave the party double XP for hanging out and hanging dudes instead of fighting. Easily the best D&D night of the last year. My buddy Alex remarked, "Owlbears are pretty much the only reason I tolerate fantasy at all anymore." I'm inclined to agree.

    All hail Owlbear, saviour of Middle Earth!

    \n I remember running Dungeon mag's Age of Worms adventure path - in the first dungeon, as part of a somewhat-convoluted (and unpopular with my players) side quest to appease the ghost who controls the door they need to open to get through to the last room of the dungeon (good ol' linear dungeoneering) - the 1st- to 2nd- level PCs fight an (admittedly already wounded) owlbear. It's pretty vicious on them, but the best bit is that you find a little baby owlbear still alive when you find his lair (den-nest?).
    \n
    \nDisappointingly, my players didn't want to keep it. They made me so mad... even though the adventure says you can't train them. I'd already reasoned that because you can train owls, and because people have Russian dancing bears, you could easily train an owlbear.
