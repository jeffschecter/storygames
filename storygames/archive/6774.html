\n I've always liked minis a little bit. (da dum ching!) But, only a little bit. Through my years of gaming, we never really used 'em. We drooled at the minis in White Dwarf before it was the company marketing vehicle (and after, too). But, in practice, we almost never used them.

I dabbled in them when I played some D&D 3rd edition. I LOVED that they were pre-painted. I HATED (still do) that they are collectible. I want Wizards to release module sets where you can buy all the minis for an adventure they publish.

Even so, I'm really not that interested in buying D&D minis.

But, I do have one mini addiction. I think it was one or two GenCons ago they gave out free Axis & Allies minis. And, I really dug them. I gathered up some free booster boxes from people who didn't want 'em. Several months later I bought some basic German and American units to fill up a nice roster.

My idea was that I'd use 'em for a fun one-shot of Savage Worlds with pulp commandos in WWII.

Now, I've started buying them again. I can't get enough. I LOVE these things. I have used them for one very short "game" of Savage Worlds with my brother.

But, I want to create a heroic game (RPG? Just fun wargame? I dunno) to play with these minis. I haven't even started doing so -- too much going on in my life right now. But, someday...

Anyone else have similar minis stories? Pics? Ever wanted to design a game around them?

What about WWII games? Any fun stuff there?

    \n 1980s - Had a small stack of the D&D lead mini boxed sets, a smattering of which were painted (quite well, I felt) by my then-step-mother. Used them all the time... until we got out of D&D. Began to notice problem with heavily-themed mini sets.
    1990s - Paper tokens became easier to manage and afford. But we'd moved into game types with a lot of wild, ranging combat (think supers and super-ish heroes) which was a bitch to manage with battle boards. Usage faded.
    2000s - Mage Knight. A complete mini game on its own, with fascinating mechanics and a range of genre themes. Oh, wow... and they weren't really too big for the old battlemats. Several cases of boosters later (you wouldn't believe how many you could get a certain times on Potomac Distribution), and I really don't ever need to buy minis for other games again. If the bases become a real bitch (or, say, if I wanted to use a common as a PC in D&D4e) then a moment's careful work with an exacto knife and I can glue them onto a standard mini base, with some flocking even.

    Mage Knight is Da Bomb.

    \n I'm a huge Mage Knight fan. You also can find (pretty cheap online or at conventions) all of the WizKids MechWarrior stuff, which has great vehicles and Mechs. Old HeroClix pieces are pretty cheap, and the Indy HC has a lot of asian style characters, which I've used extensively in super-hero and other style RPGs.

    Also, if you are looking for more pulp-esque minis, the old Crimson Skies game has some good figs, though they are slightly larger than the HeroClix and Mage Knight pieces. I picked up the entire Crimson Skies line at Origins last year for $15, and the game by itself actually aint bad. The planes are very cool.

    \n I couldn't help but buy some D&D minis even if I don't run D&D anymore (in fact I started buying the minis once I stopped playing D&D 3).

    What is strange is that I never uses minis in my game, ever. But once I had those minis in my hand I just wanted to use them but without bothering with the tactics and rules of D&D.

    That's what led me to design the Bloodbath Map for Gates & Gorgons, inspired by the japanese rpg Ryuu Tama. I just wanted an excuse to put my minis on the table. And to prove my fellow players I wasn't become addicted to minis like I was to toys when I was twelve years old.

    \n Storage is a major issue. I was lucky to have a games club with a huge storage area where I kept all my stuff. The other issue is space for making the stuff. My living room was a mess for a while.

    A lot of the skill in doing it is just knowing the right techniques and finding the right materials to make the job easy. The railings on the above terrain were a nightmare until I discovered these neat polystyrene "L" beams. After that, it's just patience.
