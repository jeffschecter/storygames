\n Games in which the mechanics do not have set procedures for specific actions irk me. Fate's broad categories of actions are so nebulous that they can be applied to almost any scenario. (What's the difference between using Create an Advantage to create the Aspect of Flesh Woundand using Attack to inflict enough stress to create the Aspect of Flesh Wound? What about using a Contest to handle a fight, or what about using Overcome?) Compared to something like PbtA's moves, they're lackluster, providing little guidance or grounding within the fiction.

On the other hand, games in which the mechanics are overly detailed for specific actions likewise irk me. D&D is the prime offender in this regard, but you can apply this criticism to any game with a strong emphasis on finely-grained combat rules: Runequest, Call of Cthulhu, Dark Heresy, Shadowrun, World of Darkness, etc. I don't want combat to be a separate minigame that grinds the game to a halt.

Are there any systems that provide a middle ground between these two methodologies (not PbtA)?

    \n I love this question. I'm kind of blanking on an answer, though.

    It's easy for me to think of Primetime Adventures at one end of the spectrum and D&D3 at the other, but then the middle ground is something like... Word of Darkness and Fate.

    I think it depends on which fictional details you want to really care about (as causality, not just color) and which you don't.

    Timing of who goes when and which actions are quicker than others? Spatial positioning of who can reach whom, who's surrounded or who's best able to maneuver or flee? The strength, speed, skill and experience of the combatants? Their weapons and armor? Their strategies and chosen trade-offs?

    If yes to either of the first two, then I think you wind up with the minigame phenomenon, as you start tracking time and space more precisely than you do otherwise.

    \n There's a simple answer: you are playing the wrong game. The game designer invested rule complexity, which is a very limited resource, in certain important actions that need it because of the intended purpose of the game, but you want rule complexity distributed differently because you want your game to be about something else.

    For example, traditional D&D combat is about rushing to deal enough damage to disable enemies before they kill us, and therefore calculating damage, blow by blow, is necessary and all rules about damage amount (extra attacks, damage reduction, critical hits, damage modifiers, etc.) and damage consequences (determining and increasing hit points, stages of not-quite-dying at low or negative hit points, etc.) are important and complex. (Making characters able to deal a lot of damage is a strategic goal for players that fits perfectly in the power-playing, character advancement mindset of D&D).

    If, instead, you wanted your combat to be about putting pressure on the character's personality, like in Masks: a new generation, D&D rules would be a square peg in a round hole: no support for what you want and too much unjustified effort.

    \n A few thoughts on some of the suggestions offered:

    Sorcerer's basic action type breakdown is just as broad as Fate's, but then it factors in a bunch of specifics such as which sorcerous ability you're using. The lack of combat rounds and unlikelihood of multiple PCs in one combat results in a much simpler process than a typical World of Darkness combat.

    Dogs in the Vineyard has a minigame for every conflict, not just for combat.

    Don't Rest Your Head may give you a bit more fictional traction than Fate, as your decision of which type of power to use is very important and engenders corresponding narration. The specifics are still all up to you, though.

    \n This seems firmly like the sort of situation that's well addressed by looking at lots of different games, which of course is what you're doing here. Good going, just keep reading to form your own perspective.

    Another suggestion that might prove interesting is some old-school edition of D&D, LotFP or something of the sort. Those are less detailed than modern D&D, but they delve deeper into detail as necessary by adding circumstance modifiers and whatnot, a bit like TSoY. I think that figuring out why that sort of combat system doesn't fit your tastes would be illuminating for further exploration (or if it does, well, problem solved).

    Hey, I just realized that Runeslayers could potentially have precisely as much complexity as you're looking for in its combat system. It's very combat-focused, but simultaneously not extremely rules-heavy. Particularly the amount of options in combat is very low, even as it does detail hit locations and critical injury tables.

    One more observation: I've seen people who have sort of similar tastes find satisfaction when they've taken a little bit firmer hold of one of those traditional systems, customizing them to their tastes. Many of these games really come from a grab-bag school of game design, and that long list of different combat maneuvers is really there "just in case" more than for any real reason. I've found that something like say WFRPG or modern D&D or such games are relatively easy to strip down, and that's apparently what people who play these games usually do to them.

    \n Not exactly the same perhaps, but I've got the same irk for both lack of efficiency and lack of guidelines to order the game and achieve a particular experience. However I agree that it's mostly because I haven't found yet the game that gives me enough rules but leaves enough space for the kind of fun I'm looking for. Part of the thing for me is that you may learn how to deal with different situations without using a system, but then you will have when a game makes you use the system to deal with those parts.

    Yet this frustration is most of what makes me design games, so I'm ok with the irk I think :P
