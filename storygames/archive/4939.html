    \n I'm at the table, with my imagined world in my head. You, also, are present, with your imagined world in yours.

    There's communication between us, words and descriptions, and both of our imagined worlds are similar because of it. Absolutely the case.

    However, this doesn't mean that they are identical. There will be differences in what a description makes me imagine, and what it makes you imagine. All very obvious. These differences are often minimized in size as more description is added, or become increasingly minimal in relevance as they are left behind and we move on through play.

    So, what we're looking at are multiple overlapping imagined spaces. The parts that overlap are actual consensus; the parts that don't might be (falsely) assumed to be consensus, but aren't. The practical upshot being...

    ...If you have too rigid a hold on your own fiction, or a "crystal clear" view of it, and refuse to allow it to be corrected naturally through action, you might regularly run into mismatches where the fictions don't overlap, and feel the need to ignore or correct others. In actuality, most of us hold our fictions pretty loosely.

    ...Exerting authority ("properly" or not, consciously or not) on what is there, by means of any declaration, often assumes that something is as you picture it.

    Which means that assumption, declaration, and adjustment are going on all the time. A group that has developed a really heavily overlapping set of internal fictions has an easier time of it - so does one that hold their fiction loosely.

    Some weird questions come from that, to me...

    Are there people that primarily want (in a given game or intance, even) to have everyone hold onto their own loose fiction, and adjust a lot? What do they get out of that?

    Are there people that try (in a given game or instance, even) and encourage as strongly-overlapping a set of fictions as possible? What do they get out of that?

    \n To me, there is only value in killing the cow if it was standing in the way of seeing a better cow, or if its parts can be tastily divied up to sort out which really are sacred and which aren't.

    Value in the SIS concept likely somes from recognizing the variability of the first S- Shared. Each of those around the table have their own individual IS, where it overlaps but not between all is "partially SIS" and where all Players (including GM as Player just with different role/authority) overlap is "full SIS". (I debated "personal IS" but the acronym for that would a bit distracting/denigrating).

    Such as, from above about clashes, a good game design/good player/good GM all work to prevent individual IS and partial SIS from becoming sources of disjunction/unfun in play and to resolve clashes.

    For example, in GMing a game for years that included spending time via email and message boards catering to individual players with "1 on 1" dialogues (Partial SIS) as much as they've wanted, I've decided that I don't want that anymore. That all the effort creating 1 on 1 dialogue does not yield as much "payback" as it would if shared and encourages "play to be directed away from the group". It saps the time I as GM have for collective play activity and it directs individual Player energy into "self-satisfying" rather than "mutually satisfying" activity.

    In a recent session of more free form play, several players authored activities for others to witness that revealed bits of personality often part of the 1 on 1 between Player and I that collective play hadn't shown. In several cases this was found greatly entertaining and informing of character by others. Aspects of individual PC's that had been "Partial SIS" now moved into SIS. The creativity of each of those players yielded more enjoyment (for other players not just GM) and was riffed off between them and interwoven easily. It has meant "corrections" in Player perceptions of different characters (e.g., character seen mostly as intolerant and annoying by others is seen as self-sacrificing and enduring social difficulties stoicly when the Player's "individual IS" was shared more fully).

    I've decided I should move away from catering as GM to 1:1 activity at all precisely because it's "Partial SIS" and does create situations where one Player and I as GM are seeing, and thus reacting, to something differently than others around table.

    The concept of SIS was not pivotal in my sorting through this but it was useful.

    The assumption I arrive at is thus that SIS is useful, valid concept, and that its value is in encouraging Players (including GM) to push imaginings into SIS rather than leave them Individual or Partial. This seems a valuable concept given the frequency with which it seems there is imagining that is important, that matters to play and that is not in SIS. If others have seen better play from doing otherwise (not pushing imagining into SIS), that would be interesting to hear about, or play where the concept was irrelevant, that too would be interesting.

    I thus nominate SIS for the status of "Super Cow", but it doubtless has further ordeal to undergo before it may don its cape and tights.

    \n I wrote a game called "Through the Ansible" (playtest thread here, rules (kinda) here) as a kind of meditation on the concept of Shared Imagined Space. In the game, there's no authority over the text, so anything written is not neccesarily "true". It kind of deconstructs the notion of a shared story, instead making the game about each player constructing a story out of the text. It also kind of implies that exerting authority over the text is a kind of violence.

    I guess I think SIS is a pretty compromised, and very useful concept. The sacred cow here, I think, is the notion that the SIS is the entirety, or the "authoratative version" of what happened in play. Maybe the sacred cow is that there needs to be a consensus among players about "what really happened".

    Posted By: Malcolm SheppardThat'd be until I want to turn the cow's hide into a coat to match by black and white pants or I make any other assertion about the cow that was considered trivial until I brought it up.
    That's when that assertion becomes shared. Then it is contested, and everybody else has to accept or refuse it. Up until then, it is irrelevant to everyone except the individual. Yes, this can be a problem in play. Indeed, this is typically why we have most of the system weight we have - in order to either minimize these "refocus" moments a priori by explicitly limiting and defining what is "legal" to introduce, or to swiftly and smoothly resolve the differences in personal imagination (the "roleplaying") when they become relevant to the shared, social activity that is the "game."

    It's practically definitional for the form that this gap exists between shared/public/social creative content and internal.

    \n I don't think we have to get rid of the notion of a Shared Imagined Space, but what we do have to do is make clear exactly what it is and, more importantly, how it comes to be.

    The way I see it, all human interaction is, in some manner or other, the creation of Shared Imagined Spaces. All social activity entails the manipulation of signs and, consequently, the negotiation of meaning. This, I believe, is where we must begin our search for the ontogeny of the SIS: in the ways meaning is negotiated among people. Roleplaying is a semiotic system. Players agree upon a code which relates certain objects and events in the real world to certain fictitious objects and events \u2014 for instance, when I say "my guy stabs the orc", this is interpreted as signifying the fictive event of my guy stabbing the orc \u2014 but like all codes, it is imperfect. Just like people in their everyday use of language must continually negotiate how the code is employed \u2014 "What do you mean by saying that?", "How do you define that word?", "It's a figure of speech", "it was a rethorical question", "I didn't mean it like that" \u2014 so, too, must roleplayers continually negotiate how their code is used to signify the fictive events of the gaming narrative.

    If there is an SIS, it can only come into existence through such a process of negotiated interpretation. The problem arises, however, that we can never be sure that the imagined space is really shared, because the only way that we can get information about what goes on in the other players' heads is through the very code whose interpretation is at issue: if the negotiation fails, we might never know. Thus, if the SIS is to be considered the result of a successfully negotiated interpretation, we can never really know whether there's really an SIS \u2014 we can only be more or less certain. However, the same problem exists in everyday language use: we can never really be sure that people understand us. And yet we labor under the assumption that they do, because things would get so messy otherwise. The same goes for role-playing: just because we can never be sure that the Imagined Space is really Shared, we shouldn't get rid of the notion, because it is a useful one.

    /Kalle
