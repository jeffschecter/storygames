\n So over herethe discussion went off on a bit of a tangent about creating buzz towards your games, and Chad and Fred chimed in with some excellent advice.

I thought I'd spin it off into a separate discussion about the art of buzz. We all know that people talking about our games is how our games sell, but is there more to it than that? How can we encourage people to talk about our games and generate excitement about it without it looking like shilling?

So, what is it?
How important is it?
How do you create it (in a genuine way)?
How do you harness it?

I don't have much to add, but I'm eager to learn! I'm rubbish at generating buzz - I think it might be something to do with my natural english state of embarrassment and modesty! Dead of Night was nearly stifled by my lack of talking about it, from conducting the playtest in private forums to not posting actual play reports or barely mentioning it! So I have plenty to learn.

    \n It's true, Fred and Chad are in fact very good at creating buzz in a nice, friendly, unasssuming, non-pushy way. I think it helps tremendously not only in direct sales, but also in creating contacts that can be useful. I like their games, but I also like many other comparable games, and I sometimes feel very sorry for neglected games that deserved better but were not promoted and supported enough. I know game designers don't necessarily enjoy the promotion part (some do, some don't, some hate it) but at least being friendly to fans, offering some resources on your website, and creating at least one online location fans can use to exchange comments, play examples and questions go a long way without requiring any hard-sale spiel.

    We have so many online tools now -- blogs, forums, wikis, mailing lists, review sites, etc. -- that are there just waiting to be used... I really think a few such connections can really improve visibility in the gamer community and push a game to the forefront. The key point, I believe, is cross-linking; if all your buzz is in a single place (say, all on Story Games or all on The Forge, etc.) then you're not getting enough buzz for your effort. You're only speaking to one constituency. What you need -- or what your product needs -- is to appear at least a bit in many places so more people have a chance to notice you.

    Posted By: andrew_kenrickOk, posting bits from and talking about games is good. Where? On blogs, here, over at rpg.net? Or all of these places? How do you make it more like "here's something cool I've done" and less like "here's my shameless promotion of my game. Buy it" ? I'm terribly worried about coming across as a shameless pimp.
    \n(1) Post a design journal. Have a dedicated blog that people can comment.

    (2) If you/your games already have fans, also start a mailing list (Yahoo group, forum, etc.) where you can post announcements and allow fans to discuss the upcoming game.

    (3) Have all relevant links in your sig file on every forum you post in, but not gigantic and multi-colour. Remain tasteful! :-)

    (4) If there is opportunity, exchange blog comments with other authors on similar projects and post in forum threads, but don't manufacture excuses. My feeling is that if you have a good reason to think something about your game is cool or unique, it's perfectly legitimate to articulate it in discussion; and if you are frank and courteous, there shouldn't be anything objectionable.

    (5) Don't hesitate to give sincere compliments to other authors, but only if you mean them. Still, it buys some good will.

    (6) Have the patience of a boddhisatva and address all comments and questions courteously. Even the stupid ones. (They might be mine!)

    (7) If you have dedicated fans, encourage them to post actual play threads, reviews, wikis, Web sites, etc. Tell them that it really helps your visibility. Ask them to post these threads somewhere visible, like RPGNet, EN World, etc. I love Story Games for the conversations, but it's not the most visible forum. Yet.

    (8) Keep your Web site or blog updated, and link to all these fan-generated bits.

    (9) Include freebies and downloads on your Web site: character sheets, scenarios, additional material, maps, etc.

    \n I'm trying to promote Beast Hunters strictly by talking about things that excite me. Here's a thing that's cool, and here's another thing that's cool, etc. You're allowed to express your enthusiasm on your blog; blogs are after all billboards for advertising your life. People will in general respond to enthusiasm in kind, so long as you aren't pushing it on them.

    In all of my yap about Beast Hunters, I've done a harder sell-push once (saying the preorder is happening, and talking a bit about how small press companies live or die on direct sales more than anything -- but even that latter message is done sort of with the message of, "Hey, here's this topic I care about, and my opinion on the matter. This is why I think it should matter to you." rather than a "Buy buy buy! Now now now!") and I expect I'll top out at maybe 2 or 3 "buy this" pushes out of about one to two dozen general enthusiasm-sharings.

    The best advertising -- the best buzz -- happens when you show people enough stuff to get them interested in talking about it on their own. By talking regularly, by sharing your enthusiasm, but not pushing it onto those who don't want it, you're keeping your product visible and you're giving people reason to be excited about it. That's buzz.

    To get a little mercenary, you're also creating pockets of special knowledge. The folks who know to check your blog or look for your posts on a web forum get to adopt the special knowledge -- the new enthusiasms you're sharing -- as their own. This means they then get to turn around and be the guys and gals who are "in the know" about something new and cool. When this works right, it means that your closest fans get to become rock-stars in their own right. They get to pick up on the buzz and own a part of it; they get to have some of the cool rub off on them because they are the purveyors of the special message.

    This is a killer chain-reaction to be able to kick off; simply by sharing your enthusiasm about the thing you're working on, you create -- as a simple side-effect of being read! -- a personal set of soldiers/true-believers/lieutenants/marketeers who get to be cool by carrying your enthusiasm out to an even broader audience. And then this audience comes to you, and connects direct to you, and thus gets promoted to be a part of your army.

    After a bit of practice at it, you can start subtly encouraging this process to happen, by crafting the message of your enthusiasm such that the folks reading it are encouraged to "tell a friend!". I've done blog quizzes, viral advertising where I "tag" my blog readers to post about the game, "here's how you can help something you love" manifestos, telling people I think they rock when they say they'll post some actual play they did, etc. I do that, yes, because it IS cool and IS part of my enthusiasm, but it also has the pragmatic buzz-building effect of touching off the chain reaction I'm talking about here.

    But it all comes back to a simple core: share your excitement. You're bound to find other people who are just as excited about it as you are. Build relationships with those people -- make real and genuine friends out of fans -- and it'll all just come natural from there.
