    Sheet: Russel Collins' 'Labrynth'
    Design: none!

    caveat: Russel, this is in no way a slam against you. Consider the lack of a game a testament to my inability to step up to the task. I'm not saying that you made the task easy, but the responsibility lies squarely on my shoulders.

    Awesomeness
    Yay! a board, or something like a board. COOL.

    Board is tied to some sort of resource/ability (via numbers). Also cool.

    Some subtle introduction of colour via "thy" instead of "your" or "the" in the title place-holder. Couple that with the font selection, and the game definitely took on an 'olde-worlde' feel to me. Good guidance.

    Challenges
    Overly-constrained. Wow. It looked so touchy-feely at first blush. But man, once I got into it, it felt like I was hitting a wall at every turn. Like what you ask? It's all about reading the inferred meaning in the graphic design. Obvious stuff is the numerical link between the labrynth and the lower section of the page. The concept of a labrynth is in itself constraining. Title/Being/Home is tough to work in a meaningful (read: not just colour) fashion.

    Hidden Pitfall. That 1-16 numbering scheme is a pain in the ass. Doesn't map to a nice die mechanic, and even worse: the numbers in the Labrynth don't map to a meaningful pattern when compared with: Desires / Insights, Exertion / Resistance, Individual rows, or Numerical Columns. This is probably the biggest hurdle I tried tackling.

    Personal Creative Block. I think this entry is probably another item under "Overly Constrained", but it holds a special place in my heart. Incorporating "Identify" into everything else. I mean, man. Normally something like this spurs me to new creative heights, but I couldn't see my way out. And I did get help, and I spitballed with plenty of folks.

    Whitespace Weirdness. The complete lack of whitespace around the "Emergence" and "Entrance" really stumped me. There's no place to record anything, so they really become non-starters. Why are they on the sheet? Then, there are apparently three of each? Colour me stumped.

    Thoughts
    I guess it really amounted to me not feeling like there was any creative wiggle room -- at least, not for an RPG. I had several really good core ideas for board games, but nothing that would support a Nar, Sim, or Gam RP agenda.

    There is this one awesome thing that came out of the whole process for me: Those horizontal lines next to the Desire and Identify tags got me thinking in terms of using Continuums instead of numbers/words/etc. If each player had a colour of counter, then use those to record relative levels within each continuum. Cool!

    Sheet: Mendel Schmiedekamp's
    Design: 11th Hour

    The sheet seemed to have just the right amount of constraint. A multiplicity of ideas seemed to pour forth from it the moment I saw it, and none of them got firmly slapped down by the sheet, just redirected somewhat.

    The clock, light and dark, and "Form" lines shoved lycanthropy to the forefront, but I didn't really groove to that, and there were so many ideas to choose from.

    The "moment" that grabbed me was the idea of a clock striking midnight and terrible things happening. Now the sheet represents a countdown to that moment. Cool.

    What am I going to do about the four corners? IS it a single sheet for four people? No, because then the words should all be upside-down so as to face the nearest player properly. Should it be folded into quarters? No, becuase then the roman numerals would be upside down for two of them. Maybe I play two characters at once. One light, and one dark. And then at some point I turn it upside-down and play two different characters. One attacking, one defending? And the characters transform midway through? OK, that's all neat.

    Clock + dark attackers + countdown to midnight = haunted house.

    I'm clearly supposed to record something in each pie slice. I don't have a good idea for that one. But I like the idea of a countdown. We'll see.

    Then I got busy at Real Life for a good week or so. On revisit, I remembered how excited I was by the sheet. I love that.

    I made a list of things I wanted to encourage the players to do. I wanted an expendable resource players had to decide whether to use on attack or defense. I didn't want dice clattering around, didn't seem to fit the mood. I want the players to actively screw over each others' characters. Etc.

    Then I started messing with mechanics to steer players toward all that. The anchor was that "moment" at the end where the clock strikes midnight and we see what happens. I latched onto the notion of having a semi-unpredicable ending that rolled the suspense out. I think I nailed that. Then I worked backward from that, deciding how to reward players throughout by angling "good" play toward a strong position in the endgame.

    I didn't have any terrible sticking moments, but I think that's largely because I was given a great frame width, and I didn't get to a playtesting phase where I'd have to face sticky issues that arise when you see what actually happens to your ideas when put into practice.

    Sheet: http://www.kevinallenjr.com/reverseengineer/zachwelhouse.pdf
    Game: http://roger.carbol.com/rpg/reverse/TheClosedDoor.pdf

    Looking Back in Obligation

    My first thoughts were, cripes, if I'm going to submit something like this to some other poor game designer, I'd better be ready and willing to step up to the plate myself and write a game.

    Upon receiving the character sheet for my game, the big maze-like structure in the middle of it struck me. Hmmm, -- I should do something with that. I tried using the four big empty areas for different character portraits, but that idea kinda fizzled out.

    I think I was feeling a bit contrarian in the beginning. Yes, the sheet gives me Hunger, Burden, Threads, and a big maze. But did I take a cue from that? If anything, I recoiled from it. But the more I thought it over, the more those themes started to sink in.

    What should a game with this character sheet be about? Being trapped, or perhaps rescuing others who are trapped, or perhaps both. That idea seemed to have some promise, so I thought it over for a goodly while.

    My first inclination was to make the PCs bound demons of some sort, under the thumb of a tyrannical GM. But that felt sorta flat and derivative and generally not something I felt like writing. Best file it away for some other rainy day.

    I continued to think over the theme of being trapped, and all at once I thought, "Hey... that Sartre play, No Exit, is about being trapped." I re-read the play and a few ideas started to congeal. It would be something different, anyway.

    About a day before the deadline, I still had nothing written. I was feeling like bailing out on the whole thing. Then I figured, hey, I should feel a sense of obligation about this whole thing. It's not like I didn't have an idea of where to go with it. So I opened up a text file and just started writing.

    A year or so ago I remember thinking, "If one wrote an explicit-enough example-of-play, that would be sufficient. The rules and the game would be defined by it." So I started with these players talking about the game. They never actually got the chance to play it, but I still think it sorta served its purpose.

    I hit a bit of a wall when I got to the nitty-gritty mechanics of how the system actually works. But by then I had a pretty good idea of how I wanted it to work. And the character sheet gave me some useful structure here, too: I knew the only real stats I had were Hunger and Burden. So I mentally fiddled with how I wanted things to work out, ran a few gedankenexperiments, and eventually hacked it out.

    The rules summary pretty much wrote itself.

    In terms of what I take away from this process at this point: 1) Keep churning through ideas until you find something that catches your interest. 2) It's easier to continue writing than it is to start writing. 3) Sometimes it's best not to talk about these sorts of things too early in the process. It lets the magic out.


    Cheers,
    Roger

    Sheet: Andrew Kenrick

    Game: Cubit

    So, this was one of those really open-ended sheets, with little more than a collection of mysterious shapes. No decoration to suggest a theme or genre, no labels to suggest gameplay mechanics, except for one thing: instructions to assemble it into a cube and "roll your character sheet". Having only one guidepost, it immediately started to chafe. How dare the sheet designer tell the players what to do! Only I have that authority!

    So I subverted that instruction, interpreting "roll" as a way to move a cube around on a grid by measured steps. And, having established that you'd be moving things around on a grid, it almost inevitably became a combat-oriented miniatures game. This is almost certainly not what Mr. Kenrick was imagining would be done with his mysterious shapes, but it all kind of seemed to follow naturally at the time.

    Once I'd decided on this sort of game, I started noticing all the things that the sheet didn't have. Like places to record hit points or experience, or even the character's name. Oh, sure, I could just designate the side with the rounded rectangle on it to be you "name tag" side, but that seemed inelegant. If you're going to go to the trouble of folding your character sheet into a cube, it had better be important that it's a cube. That means that anything you write on a side is specific to that side, which means that the rules can never require keeping track of non-side-specific information. Not even a name. I took pains to make sure that everything you write on your cube is actually affected by the cube's orientation, and that everything that isn't affected by the cube's orientation was simple enough to not require writing down.

    Now, the tactical system I had come up with to use these cubes was kind of interesting, but this isn't a board-game contest. This cube-rolling had to fit into a story. In order to make it plausible as a simulation of what's going on physically in the game world, it had to be a pretty weird game world. And so it is. This is perhaps another level of "reversed engineering" in this game: the details of the game world were chosen to fit the decided-on mechanics, rather than vice versa.

    One of the joys of creating an alien environment out of nothing is that you can make it a better fit to gameplay than the real world. I know that players have lengthy strategy discussions in between combat rounds, so I provided an in-game justification for it.

    I have a vague idea of the kind of thing that I would have done if I had decided to just roll the cube like a die, the way it was obviously intended. That would have supported a much more abstract form of gameplay -- say, one where each of the six shapes represents a different kind of role in the story, and all the players roll their cubes simultaneously to find out what kind of things their characters are required to do in the next scene. That might have been a better game, but I'm glad I did it the way I did, because I'm pleased with the game world.

    Practical approach, looking at the sheet:

    "I am... I see... I feel... I want..." - is all about who "I" am in the game, the "character".

    "Rider" is something in movement, maybe something moving the "I"? Ah, a game of possession!

    "Ridden" is movement too, maybe both by the "I" and the "Rider"? Each Ridden has a corresponding Rider. Ah, the Rider is a leader, and the Ridden is his subjects to command/guide!

    "______________ knows my name" is all about the name not having a space on this sheet. So maybe it is hidden, but one of the other players know it? Ah, the name is spoken at game end!

    Oh, there's some circles in the upper right corner... forgot about them. Well, they don't have to matter much. I'll just ignore them. Let them be a space free for the players to doodle in!

    And then it was all about writing the game. Had a great time doing it! Thanks!

    Game: Foe Destroyer

    Challenges: I had a hard time coming up with a system that would support the type of play that I was envisioning. I was striving for a very open story telling type of play that could lead anywhere, like I experienced in an online game of Death Stakes, but I wanted to confine play to the relationships between characters. I wanted to wrap this in a futuristic giant robot cartoon style setting. I also wanted the character's to not exactly like each other at the start of play, have that hatred grow, but be redeemable at the end. I also wanted simple. It was very challenging to come up with a system that I thought would work for this. And I'm still not sure that the system will support the redemption enough.

    I also had very little time for the contest, because of work load during the busiest weeks of my work season. But everyone else also was very busy I'm sure.

    Over Coming The Challenge: After struggling for a couple of days, I just decided to write a script out about how I envisioned a game of Foe Destroyer would play. I used four players and wrote out character creation, scene setting, conflict resolution, and end game as dialogue between the four imaginary players. This helped me come up with a system that I hope will help somewhat to emulate this style of play.

I really enjoyed the challenge of the deadline and working hard to get things finished. Also, the cover of the game came from my brother.     It was a fun contest. Thanks Kevin and Joshua for letting me participate and giving me the character sheet to attempt to build a game from.

    I, too, had Jonathan Walton's sheet. Jack and I were the only two people to work off the same sheet, I believe, and the outcome is quite different.

    Challenges: The sheet has none of the usual character trappings, so I knew I had to think outside of the box. The diagrams provided ample opportunity to do that. However, the biggest challenge was the strange tone of the sheet. While the diagrams had one feel, "Fearful Symmetries" had another, and having "Tangut-a-Go-Go" on the sheet ultimately made it impossible for me to create a serious game. I wasted at least a week thinking of serious game premises--whether it's people laying spirits to rest or interpreting dogma or fighting back the invading horde of Ghengis Khan or walking between worlds--because they ultimately clashed with the Go-Go.

    Resolution: In the end, I decided to completely swing the other way and make an over-the-top game where you're supposed to be silly and overacting. In a way, that actually seems quite worthwhile, because so many of our recent indie games are high on the pressure of coming up with cool, serious stuff. I used the diagrams as built-in relationship and location maps.

    Joy: I think the game is actually playable. It's also silly and fun. Having people interpret the Tangut symbols as a result of play and then each person writing a "moral of the story" thing out of them is one of my favorite parts of this design. This was a challenge, and it opened my mind to new possibilities.

    Regrets: The finalization was rushed once I knew what I wanted to do. So the way Fearful Symmetries and the Glyph are used are half-baked. Also, I don't know what Jonathan was expecting, so I hope he's alright with it being a very light-hearted game.

    Further Notes: Next time I'll do it if I don't have so many other things already going on. Also, I hope that no one is taking the game with its lighthearted, over-the-top soap aspect as in any way a commentary on Chinese culture. It could just as well be Caucasian Soap and be exactly the same game. :)

    Sheet: Joe McDonald's
    Game: not/guilty

    Designing this game was a blast.

    Generally, my design process (these days) looks like this: I get a game concept, I gather some input (reference and inspiration), and I kinda let it percolate in my head for a few days; then a game text sort of bubbles out. It's all intuition-powered, and, when there's good input, effortless.

    The Awesome:
    Joe's sheet has lots of input, good input: it transparently gave me subject matter and inspirational tone-setting images, and the fill-in boxes and questions made it obvious how the heet interacted with play.

    The Lame:
    Upon making my layout decision, which involved a lot of information separating and hiding, I had to rewrite the game text under very tight structural constraints, which was challenging and not overly fun. I detest rewriting.

    Awesome Again:
    Laying out the game gave me an excuse to dig out some of the gorgeous photos of graffiti I had taken recently in Barcelona, and was in general a good time. I didn't intend the game to become the structural experiment it is now, but I think it's more fun for it.

    The Design:
    My initial thoughts about this game were vague and meandering. I said to myself, "Hm, a mysterious death? Okay..." and made it about a whole investigation. But as I was writing, I kept cutting things away, and each time I did so, the game felt sharper, cleaner. I'm really pleased with how specific it is, and how tightly structured it is. I'd love to see it played; I'm curious about the effect the naming rules have on the way people portray the characters.
