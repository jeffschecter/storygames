Talking about Rustin's AP of Afraid has gotten me all charged up about scene framing. Specifically, major scene framing mechanics like Afraid's circumstances. Something happens mechanically, which determines (in whole or in part) how the next scene must be framed.

The earliest examples I know of are being captured and having the horror revealed in My Life with Master. Polaris has one when you go from novice to veteran. Then there's Afraid. I can't think of any others.

Are there some more out there that I don't know about? Anybody have anything in the works that uses this technique? How about advice on how to use them in play?
  • Josh Roby
    August 2006
    In Full Light, Full Steam, there are little pieces of paper called scrips that are passed around the table. You pass a scrip whenever you reference another PC's thematic battery, which is sort of like a core character stat. So if I'm playing a Lady, your character opens a door for me and you hand me the scrip. I sign off on it. Once one scrip has collected everybody's signature, whoever passes the scrip can close the current scene and frame the next one. Alternately, if you pass the scrip by referencing a PC who is not in the scene, that player can close the scene and frame a new one featuring the absent PC.

    So basically, a scene becomes "done" when there's been sufficient characterization performed in the scene, or you can jump-cut to somebody not present ("Where is Masterson and that giant robot he promised us?!?")

    Fastlane has one when your character is burned out. Your next scene must be their last hurrah. This only happens once for each character. The Shadow of Yesterday is similar with its Transcendance, again in that it only happens once and generally spells the end for the character, though in TSoY it's usually a triumphant endpoint while in Fastlane it's often tragic.

    Ensemble, a game that I'm currently working on right now, is all about this. It has a mechanism called blocking that tells you exactly which protagonists will be in the Spotlight in each scene, as well as who frames it. It doesn't dictate content, but it does inform about cast. And it happens for every scene.

    I agree that there's a lot of untapped potential here. I plan on mining more of it for my dream-based game, where each scene must contain a thematic element from the previous scene, to establish a sort of light, dreamy continuity.

    In Tenra Bansho, the "Zero Edition" of which came out some 6 years ago, it has Acts and Scenes, and unlike old TORG (or other games that used that terminology but didn't really "mean it"), these Scenes pretty much correspond exactly to what you would imagine on a stage. A scene in a play; when the location changes, or time passes, New Scene. Period.

    So here's the thing; the game has a little comic showing how you should... Ah, HERE IT IS. See that top right panel with the little illustration? That helps get in in people's minds. However, I'm thinking that that's not enough.

    For FUN (not necisarily "must use", like the grid maps in D&D 3.5: Rather more like grid maps in Spycraft or COnan OGL: 'Optional but useful'), and considering I've become a big fan recently of PAPERCRAFT aka PEPAKURA, and after seeing the SEGA Sonic the Hedgehog Green Hill Zone Papercraft set, I've got it in my head that I'm going to create a relatively simple Papercraft Kabuki stage for Tenra Bansho.

    The Kabiki Stage will sit in the middle of the table, and when a new PL decides to spend a Kiai point to enter the scene, they drop their own papercraft counter (most likely stupid-simple, like those PIG printable paper miniatures or the like) onto the stage. There will be some for major NPCs (1-4, whch say "NPC" and maybe the a blank to fill in the name of the NPC) too, but otherwise they won't have to drop in "shopkeeper", "sweeping lady", "guy in corner drinking sake", etc.

    So yeah, for scene framing, the GM takes all the pieces off of the papercraft kabuki stage and drops the next scene players on; then who's in and who's out is decided from there, and play resumes in the new scene.

    -Andy
