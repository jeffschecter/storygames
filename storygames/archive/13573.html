    \n We played Gamma World (the new one) last night. It was pretty fun! The Pyrokinetic Hawkoid, Yeti Mind-Coercer, and Seismic Giant fought some badger-people and pig-men, then some carnivorous grass and a flying lion with laser eyes. The giant kind of died for a second, but he got better.

    I ran it with zero prep, so that was nice. It plays a lot faster than 4e, but could maybe benefit from a bit more 4e rules pruning. Also, it really needs totally random encounters (to go with the rest of the fun randomness), so I'm gonna bang up some tables. Neatly arranged, level-appropriate fights are pretty lame for Gamma World.

    They came this close to putting everything you need in the box, too. If they had cards for weapons, ancient junk, and character origin stuff, things would have been much easier (I have since found fan made materials). Still, much better as a quick-play game than 4e.

    \n I played The Visitor last night (richly-deserving winner of the 2010 Little Game Chef contest). It was quick-paced, and funny. If I understand correctly, its immediate influences are Sign In Stranger, Archipelago, and some sort of cargo-cult version of the TV show Alf.

    The game's killer app is a d12 chart of various categories ("Mating and Reproduction", "Eating and Excrement", "Politics and Law"", etc) upon which the alien player must roll to determine how he interprets various objects and items he encounters. For example, when faced with a jerk health-store sales manager's beloved bichon frise, he (naturally) rolled "Eating and Excrement", and immediately stowed the dog in his secondary stomach.

    \n The designer of High Valor, Tim Kirk, ran a game of it for me and my friend online. We were a duo of elves who investigated a bloody scene of massacre.

    Highlights:

    Whitebread high fantasy! Delicious!

    Character creation... Easy, fun, and your guy is instantly rooted into the fiction of setting. Like Burning Wheel, only not fiddly.

    If you play an elf, your name gets special treatment - you get to name yourself after an elemental force or a special being, and then you gain its awesome powers, for use in the game just like any other trait!

    Investigation of a small bloodbath by bandit eventually shows there is a more powerful force behind them... The buildup to it! From the bloody battlefield to babblings by its lone survivor to the tracking them in the woods to the gaping wound someone left in the secret magical elven shortcut realm, to the necklaces of people we killed turning out to be pendants of enslavement, to a wispy demon announcing its presence, to running after it wildly through the forest... Hell yeah!

    Cryptic elven banter. For some reason, it is remarkably satisfying to talk in nothing but riddle and metaphor when you get a chance. Would probably have been less fun if the party had more racial diversity to it, but just being a couple of elves allowed us to speak in nothing but stuff like "We may have found more than we were looking -- a knife in fire." "Then that knife must chip and shatter." without fear of pissing other people off. Remarkably, we also understood each other quite well throughout the process, and had a blast doing it. Having so much fun with language was in fact my biggest surprise with this game.

    \n We played Slipstream. The PCs - a Bird Man, Lion Man, Robot Man and an Earthling defeated the pirate/slavers trying to take the rocketship they took passage on, and leapt across a nearly airless void to capture the pirate's rocketship for their own. During the festivities they witness Queen Anathraxa's nefarious poisoning of the fragment's atmosphere. They take up arms and defend their hosts from soldiers intent on rounding up all available Lion Men (women and children too!) and taking them to the radium mines. Now they must break through Queen Anathraxa's orbital siege and find an antidote to the Death Clouds!

    \n Just playtested Sage's supers game, Powers For Good. It was a blast, and we managed to have a good brainstorming sessions afterwards, too.

    Our team, The Agency, consulted corporations (banks, mostly) on metahuman security issues, and also contracted as on-call emergency responders. We were:

    Prowl: ruthless ex-criminal famous for assassinating Ultra Man. A normal human with extraordinary training and gadgets. The security expert.

    Yusuf al Rashid: an immortal djinn and powerhouse executive. Primary investor in the Agency and mystical expert.

    Jennifer Clark: (aka Athena): an ambitious lawyer who left behind her family legacy of super-heroics in order to found her own company. Legal and metahuman expert. (She won't wear the tights anymore, but she still has the powers.)

    We foiled a heist at a Swiss bank (and made a new client), then tracked down the source of the robbery to one of Yusuf's ancient foes, Ramses II. We also ran afoul of Switzerland's own home-grown hero, The Neutralizer.

    The game had a great "we're super heroes!" vibe and really felt like the beginning of a comic book story arc. I look forward to our next issue.

    Keep an eye on this one as it develops.

    \n Playtested my solo game-in-development Facing the End. It works, but needs some refinement.

    Premise of the game is that you are infected with a supernatural dream disease that will kill you and your loved ones if you don't sacrifice yourself at the Stone Circle at Graystead. So, you will die, it is how that matters. Meant to explore end of life issues. The game runs on a complicated set of random encounter tables.

    Quick summary of scenes:
    Scenes
    1) Faced Yuri Ilia - Sorcerer of the Dreamcult, at Elderberry Wells, with two gypsy wagons encamped 100 yards away, eldritch shootout, no consequences
    2) Temptation by promise of continued life with Emily. Deepened Bond with my wife.
    3) Faced Vivian Linda and her Band of Thieves on the High Road at midnight, swordfight, suffered minor wound.
    4) Temptation by playing with my children Sam and Hannah, retained and strenghthened Bond with Children
    5) Faced Brother Lino Vitor - Questioner, on the playground outside of Throughbridge School, many children playing, chase and escape, no one harmed
    6) Faced Taras Radomir - Assassin, in the Cherry Hill Inn upstairs corridor, just before dawn, scuffle that woke the Inn, aggravation of previous wound.
    7) Faced Eerika Helka - Common Brigand, by the ruined cathedral in the early morning, chase and escape, loss of supplies.
    8) Temptation by running in the woods, larch needles scattered on the trail, overcome with Grief, weakened Bond with Family.
    9) Faced Vivian Linda and her Band of Thieves again, inside a windmill, with the cogs and gears rattling, the mill churning, sword fight, another wound
    10) Faced Sister Uschi Fabiola - Dream Hunter, The crowded dining hall of The Rose restaurant, Dreaming Duel, no complications.
    11) Temptation with my practice as a healer, weakened Bond to Graduation.
    12) Faced The Black Wind, a shortcut through Chester Ridge cemetery, possibly makeable before night, chase and escape, overwhelmed with the task before me.
    13) Temptation with Running with my children
    14) Faced Vivian Linda and her Band of Thieves again, in a stand of pines on Bedron\u2019s Ridge in a rainstorm, sword fight, Vivian angry, barely escape, scared of Vivian.
    15) Faced Philip Gregers - Citizen Loyal to the Barron,at Farmer Gable\u2019s Farm, just after supper time, wanted to turn me in, grapple, chase and escape
    16) Temptation with life with Emily, deepened Bond with Emily.
    17) Faced The Urdhu Band - Dream Goblins, on the rope bridge above Tinker\u2019s Creek, a stiff breeze, sunset, arrows vs magic, another wound
    18) Temptation with reading, loss of Bond
    19) Temptation with stickball competitions, weakened Bond

    \n Just wrapped up my first-ever game of Agon - we did a one-shot called the Beast of Koropoli (or something).
    Plot - basically, a king's wedding feast, thrown for his daughter, is interrupted by a talking Athena-statue, letting y'all know that the wedding had best not go forward until a certain Beast has been slain, a creature to whom the king sacrificed his *eldest* daughter. Yikes!

    We, four noble heroes, three demi-gods and a mortal, set forth on that awesome quest. My main takeaway from this is that the Glory mechanic is exactly what we needed to get social license to trash-talk, gloat, victory-dance, and all that. Nobody among us is particularly aggressive or competitive, but wow, this game unleashed something. We kept it friendly, but still! Every time I succeeded on an attack roll, I felt compelled to stand and pump my fists in the air.
    Every. Time. The secret to that is: pretty much every contest that can generate Glory (everything but Interlude contests, I think) means that beating the GM gets you a little Glory, and beating everybody at the table gets you more!
    I rolled a Cunning challenge to lead us on the right path through the labyrinth, burning Divine Favor like crazy, and I totally annihilated the GM's roll. Seven glory at a stretch - so awesome!

    We took a little while getting the hang of Interlude Scene contests - at first, we didn't know that everyone could participate in such a contest, not just the contest-caller and a challenger. We milked that Interlude for all it was worth, calling one contest per player (our maximum), and then we set off to find the beast in its lair, a labyrinth under the palace.

    Sure enough - there were minotaurs. I thought the Range Band thing for combat positioning would be lame, but it's totally not - coordinating weapon ranges, using your movement point to move yourself OR a bad guy, and protecting the archer - - all very diverting!

    One last thought: it doesn't necessarily pay to make your character well-rounded, although that is the default. Ten-siders were pretty critical to a lot of the more dramatic successes rolled by the players. Layering bonuses from your Heroic Trait on top of your best skills is pretty great, also - contributing a d10 *and* +2 to the highest die-roll in the pool is really helpful.
    Overall, it's fascinating that there can be so much friendly competition between players, all with medium-but-not-threatening stakes. Great game!

    \n Had my 12year old Autistic nephew stay for the last few weeks and have had some pretty awesome story game style WHFRP 3rd Ed going on. He's created Jacob the elven waywatcher, whose clandestine activities always seem to get him into tricky situations and difficult choices revolving around beastmen, chaos culties and hunting game.
    \n
    \nHe chose 3 location cards, one big bad he wants to encounter (a red dragon natch) and wrote a player goal of 'I'll free all the elves from beastman slavery'. Cool. Thus far the game has wound in and out of the wilds, with a few choice scenes at a chaos cultist overrun hunting lodge inspired from the core ruleset. The narrative potential of the dice has been awesome, my nephew has really taken up the fictional possibilities represented in each roll result, succeed or fail. The action cards and talent slots provide a little mechanical advantage, but mainly persist as flags and colour.

      \n Ghost/Echo, using a hack detailed here.

      The crew
      Hull, a college kid with some serious hardware and occult training
      Grip, the leader and organizer. Recovering from addiction to something called "Venom's" (as in, a drug made by Venom, whoever that may be)
      Coil, an old hand at gang-work; a little too well known among the different crews out there

      I've played something homebrewed that's very close to Ghost/Echo, but I hadn't played the real G/E deal.
      This was solid. We played it "open", with almost zero setting or character information to begin - all we really had was the .pdf and the starting situation. From there, kind of Shock:esque, whenever someone mentioned something cool about the Real World, the Ghost World, wraiths, etc., it got written down as canon. That happened a lot, especially with Loot: Paper and Stones, and it was awesome.
      The flashback stuff meant that we had a lot to say about our characters and about the setting overall. It got very dreamlike and Sandman-ish pretty fast, with summonings, Ghost World goddesses, and Freudian Ballistics tech all rubbing cheek-to-jowl.

      We got away with a stash of papers, despite Walker's gang leading us right into an ambush. We went to ground at my crash pad in the skinworld, where we started burning Papers - when you burn 'em with the essence of the Ghost World in the air, the red wax seals on 'em start turning into cherry-red clones of whatever object is mentioned on a given Paper.
      So, I huffed some Ghost World and lit a Paper labeled "Alpha Male Multiplier" - and created a bright red dog that disrupts any and all pack order and discipline on-sight. We were going up against Axis and his dogs, so it seemed appropriate.
      But then Walker's gang followed up on us, coming 'round to my place with the assumption that we were dead and in the Ghost World. We ended up trying to talk things over, guns drawn in a good old Mexican standoff, but then Coil jumped out the window.
      Walker had said something about their guns being loaded with Stones (which I guess is good; we never clarified what they are), and Coil, formerly of Walker's gang, decided to high-tail it while he could before he got plugged for being a traitor.
      Coil found the gang's van, with the Stone stash in it, and then sped off to a secret, prepared warehouse to stash 'em. Fucker skunked us, and we never saw him again.

      Due to time constraints, we ended up lingering on the standoff more than the actual "quest" we'd been put on to pay off our tab with Kilo, sexy goddess of wisdom and healing and serpents.
      When we arrived at Belltown to fight Axis, the hound-wraith master, I pulled out a Tectonic Javelin and leveled the place; he was killed in the 'quake, the dogs were unharmed, and I kind of sort of ripped a hole in reality, which a member of the Guardians of Public Order arrived to sew shut again. Not before the dogs lost their wraithliness and became neither Ghost nor Living - shadow dogs!
      Kilo decided that her servitors, the viper-wraiths, should become shadowy too, and forgave us for letting our buddy kill her poor little servitors.
      The session ended with the vipers reborn more powerful as shadow-snakes, in the midst of a New Orleans style funeral for the snakes in the middle of Heartbreak Square.
      My guy, Hull, went back to college, and our crew boss Grip set about putting a new crew together.
      A firm handshake, and we were done.

      \n Last weekend at JiffyCon I got a chance to play Swords Without Master, a fantastic narrative-driven REH/Fritz Leiber swords and sorcery adventure game.

      My PC was a thinly-veiled homage to Prince Namor, the Sub-Mariner. My sublime REH moment was harpooning one of the twin giant god-spiders and hanging on for dear life as it thrashed about its golden web, bleeding out.

      Where its blood fell, coins and gems coagulated, handily explaining why the city merchants would turn pale and close up shop when we proffered the spoils paid to us by the Cult of those same gods for an assassination we carried out at the start of the adventure.

      Lessons learned:

      Never turn your back on an armed guard.
      Our enemies will provide.
      Don't be the first person to attack the Spider God.

      The game is a blast and really tightly-designed to push towards the fun and dramatic irony that's the heart of that specific sort of fantasy adventure. I felt like I had been dropped right into the middle of a particularly good issue of Savage Sword of Conan.

      -JC
