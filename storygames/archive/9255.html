ok.





here we go:




Dowler's Delvers

a game for one player.
it will be a timed game, with the player attempting to beat the clock.

the player will control a group of delvers, going through tunnels, getting treasure, killing monsters,
you know, the usual.
the mapping techniques will make judicious use of tony's brilliant hand-as-measuring-tool idea
(which i believe he stole from the romans); that corridors will be a finger-width or a thumb-width,
maybe a hand-width.

i was thinking that the game would be playable on a commute by bus,
meaning that more than one or two dice would be crazy, and we couldn't use tony's roll-the-die-and-see-where-it-lands-on-the-page
thing, which he also got from the romans; but i am totally willing to toss that bus-friendliness out.

the timer comes in with the length of the delve. every member of the party eats one food-unit every X minutes,
where at level one X=like 15, and that goes down two minutes a level, or maybe just X=10 minutes at level 2
and 5 minutes at level three.

hmm. i just had some idea about drawing the walls in one color, and just drawing a line wherever the party goes,
so it's a matter of physically drawing it... in another color. hmm..

&, like, how about monster generation? how do we do that? how do we make treasures?

& for clarity's sake, i imagined this using a lot of paper, like going off the bottom of one sheet and
continuing on the top of another.
in its own way, this creates confusion about getting lost in the tunnels, and takes time figuring out
"where you are" as you flip to the right page.

& what about fighting? how does that work, how is that fun?

& how do you generate a party?
is the "main character" differently powered than the rest of the party?
is there a "main", or do you just load up on people when you go back to town,
regardless as to who died?

& how do you get food, and how much food and treasure can the party carry?

so far we might need:
an assload of paper
colored pencils
a digital timer, ideally one which will give like one beep every time fifteen minutes (or whatever) passes.
an eraser
probably some dice, because as i once read in a review of the dragonlance 5th age rpg,
"leaving my dicebag at home makes me want to stay home with it".

and that is not the image we are trying to achieve.


there we have it!
you all are dears and lovely creative devils,
so get your brains in gear and let's churn this out.

also, any aesthetically correct artwork would be lovely. i'm picturing black hand-drawn lines, with minimal shading,
and weirdo creatures and vaguely wimpy and mediocrely-armored heroes.

& this could wind up as a two-sheet or a little booklet, or whatev'.



get goin'.

    \n Dice! Bah, dice! Dice are easy to lose on a bus and require a solid surface. What about a deck of cards? That way you could also assign cards to your fellow busgoers ("that guy is clearly a Jack of Hearts, whereas she's probably a Four of Clubs...") and pull them in as substitutes when you weren't drawing the cards you wanted.

    Red cards are new monsters. Black cards are reserve delvers. You use the number to determine which direction the tunnel goes (and whether it splits) and an evens/odds system to determine who takes a loss in a fight. If you use up your regular deck, you reshuffle your discard pile, BUT food is consumed by pulling cards out of the bottom of the discards and putting them back in the box. When you run out of cards the game is over.

    I like Jason's idea too!

    \n A spin on Brendan's concept; if you use playing cards as the primary system mechanic, then combat could be resolved like a game of War. (Ace = 14, King = 13, Queen = 12, etc.)
    \n
    \nStart by drawing two "Delver" cards at random as well as one treasure card.
    \n
    \nUse Brendan's concept for generating the dungeon and determining when an encounter occurs. I also like concept of removing cards from the deck for food. Maybe pull X cards from the deck every Y minutes and/or pull Z cards per N delvers from the deck every encounter.
    \n
    \nWhen an encounter occurs, draw a number of monster cards equal to twice the current level of the dungeon (Level 1 being the easiest, each time you go one level further down into the bowels of the dungeon, draw two more cards for the monsters)
    \n
    \nSum the cards on each side. The side with the lowest total loses one card at random -- NPC/Monster dies. If the delvers beat the monster, the card becomes the treasure and it has value proportional to the number on the card.
    \n
    \nAfter each round, the players and monsters can choose to continue the fight or run away. If the monsters run away, the PC's get one of the monster cards at random as dropped loot. If the PC's run away, discard one of their collected treasure cards at random. If the PC's have no treasure, then nothing is lost -- don't need to add injury to insult ;). If the fight continues, repeat the process until combat is resolved.
    \n
    \nWhen the party returns to "town," the player can discard treasure cards whose total is greater than or equal to the sum of the values of the delver cards to hire an additional delver. The player can also discard a treasure card to buy "food," thus replenishing the deck by a number of cards equal to the value of the treasure card discarded.
    \n
    \nIf the party enters the dungeon with more delvers than two times the current dungeon level, they encounter the "boss." Draw a number of cards for the monsters equal to twice the current dungeon level + 1 or # of delvers - 1, which ever is greater. Combat mechanics are the same as above. If the PC's win, they proceed to the next dungeon level and the stakes go up.
    \n
    \n***
    \nOK, that was a lot of stream of conscious brainstorming... do with that as you will. I'll add that I think this concept is really cool and it would be a fun gift. I'll look forward to playing it at GPNW.

    \n Jackson: yeah, that's pretty much it. So the encounter says you need to allocate one dice to victory, two dice to injuries, 1 dice to treasure, whatever. The total of the dice tells you what you get out of that category, either on a per encounter basis or based on some general rules.

    So a possible encounter:
    Echoing Hall full of Orcs
    Victory: 1 dice
    Injuries: 2 dice
    Noise (Special): 1 dice
    A high roll in this slot means you manage to keep your skirmish quiet, even in the huge echoing cave around you. A low roll means everyone within the dungeon hears you.
    1-2: Like a bull bowling in a library. You're so loud, the monsters in the next room are able to bring the pain. As soon as this encounter is resolved, create another encounter. You don't roll again for this encounter, instead subtract one from each dice (minimum 1) and reuse them.
    3-4: What a ruckus. Everyone know's you're coming, subtract one from each dice you roll in the next encounter (minimum one).
    5-6: Quiet... for a battle. No effect
    7-8: Sneaky. You take your enemies by surprise, reroll any once dice in your next encounter.
    9-10: Very Sneaky. Reroll as many dice as you want in your next encounter.
    11-12: Like ninjas in a padded room. After rolling dice for your next encounter, you may create a new encounter to replace it AND reroll as many dice as you want when recreating.

    The effects of Victory would be defined in some general area (since all/most encounters have a Victory slot):
    Victory:
    The roll in this slot indicates the general outcome of the battle, including who wins.
    1-2: Delvers Routed - Your enemies overwhelm you, driving you back. Go back to the room you entered from and create a new encounter there.
    3-4: Escape - When things stop going your way you manage to cut your losses. Exit to any room adjacent to this one.
    5-6: Deadlock - You're too evenly matched, no one wins. Roll against this same encounter again, but you may keep any one roll you currently have.
    7-8: Victory - You win! You can leave the room on your own terms
    9-10: Decisive Victory - Ain't nothing that can stand up to your massive magical prowess/muscles/skills. You can leave the room on your own terms and add one to your total in any other slot.
    11-12: Massacre - What enemies? You mean that bloody pile on the floor? You can leave the room on your own terms and you can keep any single dice roll from this encounter and reuse it in the next as your momentum carries you forward.

    Likewise, Injury would be a common one defined in some other section:
    Injury:
    Any delver dice assigned to Injury and injured.
    d4: Adds a -1 penalty to the dice until the dice is used in a Rest slot.
    d6: Becomes a d4
    d8: Becomes a d6
    d10: Becomes a d8
    d12: Becomes a d10

    I'm imagining starting out with a party of mostly d4s and d6s. 'Low level' encounters would let you double up dice on victory and have few if any slots for bad stuff. I think Death might be a slot that removes the dice permanently, which might be a good thing for a Injured d4. Levelling would probably be increased dice size, but I'm not sure when it would happen (maybe you get 'improvement points' per encounter? Or it's a slot?). I could also see 'special abilities' like this:

    Lesser Mirror Image
    When you use this ability, you can use the dice it's associated with in two slots in the next encounter but the dice is Inured afterwards.

    Mirror Image
    When you use this ability, you can use the dice it's associated with in two slots in the next encounter but the dice is given a -1 afterwards (unless it's a d4, sometimes you're just so weak it doesn't matter).

    Mirror Image
    When you use this ability, you can use the dice it's associated with in two slots in the next encounter (and nothing bad comes of it).



    An added layer to this could be that the slots are in some way determined by the room. So, maybe you draw some shape, then based on the level of encounter you get certain obstacles to place in the room, and their placement affects the placement of others? In this case, the 'slots' could literally be where that character is fighting/casting/skilling/dying.

    I'd like to bring more roleplaying into it, but I'm not really sure how. Also notably absent is the cool entropy aspect that I'm all for. There's some bits I like to this, but it also feels more boardgame-y than RPG-ish. Granted, I'm not sure how much of an RPG it can be for one player? (I'm sure someone smarter than I can solve that problem.)
