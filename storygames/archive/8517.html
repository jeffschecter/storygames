\n Most role-playing games have built in the assumption that you will spend the entire first session of the game building characters, and discussion house-rules and the social contract.
\n
\nThis is useful, as these are things that need to be discussed, but I find that my enthusiasm for a new game wanes significantly between the organization session and the time when everyone finally sits down to play.
\n
\nLess complex systems, or ones in which character generation is considered part of the game itself (looking at you Spirit of the Century) mitigate this potential issue but do not directly address it. Even in games in which character creation is extremely simple (such as wushu), it is not uncommon to see the entire first session taken up with building character backgrounds.
\n
\nMy question is this: How much time do you think is necessary between the organization and logistical discussion of a game, and the time when you begin actual play? How long is too long? Do you believe it is necessary to begin play during the first session of a game?

    \n In a lot of traditional RPG campaigns that I've played in, the GM told everyone the "house rules" in advance, and everyone was expected to come to the first session with an already-generated character. Any discussion of the group "fitting together" was left to the players to work out among themselves outside the actual sessions.

    This worked all right with games with well-defined roles (e.g., D&D, where the players can simply tell each other, "I want to play the mage", etc.) or with groups who already know each other well. In other situations, I've seen it fall down badly.

    Personally, I prefer quick character generation and/or develop-in-play generation, so it can be done during the first session while still allowing time to play.

    I also tend to use a lot fewer house rules than I used to, and to both come to the game with a sketchier world background, and encourage the players to contribute to the world. Sometimes this makes things faster; sometimes it doesn't.

    \n This is a very hard question to answer. On first blush, I am tempted to say either that a game pitch that can't last through a setup session is a weak pitch/concept to start with, but I can't say that without thinking back to some train wrecked chargen sessions that tore the focus asunder.

    I also do not like to delay group play with chargen sessions or setup sessions. That is why I almost always get right into the play as quickly as possible after chargen. I will always run something that first session, either by having players prep ahead of time, playing game with quick chargen or templates, or just running a short James Bond-esque prologue cliffhanger that is short, but gets things rolling and has a cliffhanger to keep them thinking about the next game.

    Games that have long chargen should give a thought to insuring that chargen independent of a session is possible, or that templates and such can be made to speed up flow. Another idea is to create some basic templates in games by making a good build of a stealth-guy, a spell-guy, a warrior, a noble, etc. in your system and letting the players dress those up in color that makes them happy.

    \n I think it's very, very easy to put all the exciting stuff about the game into the setup session -- the whole "play before you play" thing. If you doodle up a cool character but his coolness is derived from his backstory and character-making process, it's safe and confined and can't me sullied by actual play. It's very hard for an RPG session/campaign to live up to the great mini-novelizations we come up with on our own.

    That said, my very favorite setup approach is to specifically disallow lengthy backstories. If someone has a great backstory idea, make the game about that (shared setting creation in BW). Or play specifically at the moment when the character(s) first get into trouble (DitV). Or play one of the games where your backstory is totally irrelevant to the future progress of the game (Lacuna, 3:16).

    Posted By: Jack the QuickMy question is this: How much time do you think is necessary between the organization and logistical discussion of a game, and the time when you begin actual play?
    \nAt least a day. Personally, a week seems to work out well: we have the discussion and character creation one week, and we're ready to play when we meet up the next week.\n
    How long is too long?
    \nThree weeks is starting to push it. A full month seems waaaaay too long. Although really, the killer for our group is time between sessions, not time between discussion and first session. Spend a month between sessions, and the game may not be dead, but it's certainly circling the drain.\n
    Do you believe it is necessary to begin play during the first session of a game?
    \nAbsolutely fucking not, no way, nunca, never. Especially not for a game that we're going to keep coming back to. I really don't like coming straight out of character creation and into playing, because that first session will always be a scattershot, half-assed parade of things that I will look back at later on and say "I wish I had done that differently." Give me a little time to soak it in, to prepare, and I have a much more enjoyable time. That week between discussing what we're going to play and us actually playing is a week during which the iron is very much in the fire for me: it's white hot when we get to the table the next Saturday, instead of being merely lukewarm and a little bit sooty.


    But hey, man, that's me and my group. I'm no evangelist, I've got zero investment in whether any of the rest of you feel the same way. Go with what works for you.

    \n I think this issue is very much a game-to-game thing. Some games need more, some need less. 3:16 barely needs it at all.

    One of my games, The Rustbelt, requires a character & situation creation meetup before the first session of actual play. But that part is actually pretty quick -- creating characters is easy, and once you've got them it's easy to create the starting situation. The reason it's set apart is because the GM needs time to think about it and get things behind the scenes ready. He's got a lot of NPCs that he has to represent as actual characters, and he's got to start thinking about the PCs, and how he can squeeze them into revealing all of their most monstrous and admirable qualities. It takes some thought and reflection (but not planning), and therefore time.

    Or maybe I just suck at it, and I need time to do it.
