    \n Search Flickr.com or Google image for something related to your game. Go into Advanced search options, and filter by license to only return Creative Commons licensed images. Also possibly filter by art style. Or download photos and run them through some simple photoshop filters (no art skill required).

    There are plenty of other sites full of free art, too. FOSsil Bank has a good section on free art and BibliOdyssey often has really nice public domain art up there. I also like using the icons from The Noun Project, which can be used to give a game a distinctive look and pretty up a document but aren't representational like most RPG artwork.


    A lot of public domain art is a century or more old, which might not work for your game specifically. But if the fighting game had, say, a supplement using the mechanics for fencing, then you'd be able to find a wealth of swordfighting manuals online that you could use for free. This can be a design consideration when you make a game. The Shab Al-Hiri Roach is set when it is so that Mr. Morningstar could use all sorts of public domain art of roaches and colleges and things.

    \n There is always the option of looking for art you like and then explaining the situation to the artist and seeing if they will let you use the art for a non-commercial project. If the artist makes art for a living, you probably won't get a reply. However, there's plenty of great art out there made by people who are thrilled that people take in interest in their projects. Always offer to give credit where credit is due and, if there's room, link to the artist's website. Always offer to send them a draft of the project so they can see how their art is being used and always send them a copy of the final piece. (This is one way I get label art for homebrews [beer])
