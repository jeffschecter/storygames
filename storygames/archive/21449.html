\n One of the dungeons I am writing for my current sandbox is an ancient, high-security vault. (Or rather, that's the lowest layer on the dungeon). My idea is that there are perhaps 6 - 10 separate high security vaults, each of which is protected by defences both physical, magical and monstrous. Without the right key, you're going to have to pick locks, burn through metal, dispel wards, and fight monstrous guardians. One or two keys may be lost forever. But the rest will be scattered throughout the rest of the sandbox world. Hopefully, the players will keep returning here, keep wondering what's behind the remaining doors, and be eagerly seeking out rumours and clues to the location of the remaining keys.

So with all that hype, I want the treasures to be really great - each one exciting in its own right. And I want them to be varied - not all coming from my single authorial voice. So: please tell me your treasure ideas!

They can cover quite a broad range of "value", as there will be different levels of security on the vaults. There may also be some low and medium security treasures - safety deposit boxes and treasure chests and the like, so if you have cool ideas for those, feel free to throw those into the pot too!

    \n Well, obviously you're going to have to have the Deck of Many Things in there.

    Other notions, let's see how many I can type up in 10 minutes:
    * Design plans and notes for frightfully advanced weaponry, with one or two working samples, plus a keg of gunpowder. Think matchlock guns or breech-loading rifles or whatever suits the setting.
    * The Death Note
    * The Lament Configuration
    * OK, no more direct joinks from elsewhere. Instead, a full suit of armor made from the skin and bones of a being whose death is still a going concern religiously, politically and/or ecologically in the world.
    * A selection of flawless precious gems, plus a machine that stores magic into flawless gems, as if writing scrolls.
    * Ancient political skeletons, worthless now. Literal skeletons, of course - they murdered the royal family and paid the dwarves to hide the bodies. There's even a receipt. Dwarves are stone cold.
    * Dwarf Eggs
    * A trapped angel
    * The manual to an otherwise lost prestige class
    * A magical hand-mirror that can be manipulated to transmit light and sound between the mirror and any flawless precious gems (not otherwise magically charged) within a mile or so. (Intended for and useful in both AV systems and spying.)
    ... and that's time.

    This is the sort of adventure location that others would like to use, too, by the way.

    Also, check out Jim Raggi's Crawling God, it's basically the same idea. You could probably use many of his treasures in your own vault.

    \n A jar with a heavy, locked lid of brass and steel. Inside is a swollen worm, about the size of an eggplant. It occasionally secretes or gives birth to small, blind larval worms, like maggots that can barely move.

    If a maggot is held against warm flesh, it will gently (and almost painlessly) burrow within, where it will lay dormant as it matures.

    The maggots are connected in the Astral Plane (or however magic works in your world), creating a link between them. If one person has a maggot burrowing under their skin, they become linked to anyone who also has one. From now on, strange magical effects like curses have a chance of affecting both parties, and one can cast any "touch" spells on another person bearing the maggot, regardless of distance.

    Why was it locked up here, instead of killed?

    What will happen if a significant population starts "carrying" these parasites?

    The mature size and form of the maggots, or how long their dormant period lasts, is not known, but is theorized to depend on some astral or thaumaturgic events or cycles.

    \n An ancient written document the mere existence of which, if known, would be a major political concern - such as substantiating or negating the legitimacy of a church, kingdom or other institution. For example, the dying will of an ancient emperor granting land to build a temple to a different religion than the one that actually did (expelling their rivals and claiming the emperor's will favored them).

    An ancient document granting the bearer a legitimate claim to rule over a still existing political entity - such as a fiefdom, kingdom or bishopric. The claim can and will be disputed, of course, but it is actionable - especially if backed with the force of arms or perhaps a quest of sorts.

    \n I now have a little more time and a larger keyboard with which to write.

    First, here's some ideas of my own:

    * The Chandelier of Truces: A elaborate gold chandelier that reveals truth when lit. When all 1,000 spaces are filled with lit, pure beeswax candles, the light that the chandelier casts reveals hidden weapons, outlines magical items with an aura revealing their school and strength, and reveals all invisible things as per true sight. In the past, important diplomatic banquets and negotiations were held by its light.

    * A severed arm, unnaturally pale and branded with sigils. When the stump is placed against skin, the arm bonds there and from then on can be controlled mentally. This mental control takes the form of commands like "defend me", "grab that", not proprioception and direct control, so it's hard to do things which require co-ordination like tie knots or juggle. The arm cannot be removed except with surgery. The arm has its own secret agenda, and if the host goes against it, the arm threatens the host, or attempts to kill them if they persist. The arm's agenda is (d6): 1. The preservation of all Dwarven lives 2. (rest of table to come later)

    * A magic carpet that only flies at enormous speed, and offers no protection against falling off.

    * A jeep with several drums of fuel in the back and the keys in the ignition (at the bottom of a dungeon with lots of staircases...)

    I would be delighted to make this available to others to use if there's interest (and I'll of course credit contributors). Thanks for the reminder about The God that Crawls @Eero_Tuovinen, I had forgotten that had interesting treasure in it. More generally, everyone please feel free to contribute favourite treasures from rulebooks and published modules. But fresh ideas are always nice.

    \n I would use this myself, yeah. Your module wouldn't need to specify where all the keys are in the world, either; just spec the location itself with its dangers and treasures, and it'd be a fit addition to rather many campaign worlds. Just lots of different keys, a whole system of them (maybe color-coded or something), so there's a lot of stuff to scatter around. And regenerating dangers so the place can still surprise on the second visit [grin].

    Just remember to put some cleared vaults in there as well, if the concept is that people sometimes find these keys and use them successfully. The premise needs a good balance of lore (not everybody can know about the place, or the keys would not be discoveries) and keys and time (too much of it, and every key's already been found and used).

    \n The greatest artists of their era in magical slumber together with samples of their work. Examples of artists: chef, stone carver, painter, architect (the vault itself might be the sample), harpist, tattooist and some art which has been completely lost to time such as opera. They will likely have useful information regarding the other vaults.

    A lost biome complete with soil, plants and animals. I might be the preserve of an exctinct magic animal (dinosaur, for riding? the black unicorn, evil to the core) or plant (extends life, expands conciousness).

    A library containing the Colossal Tome. Each page is the size of a door. Reading it creates a secondary game where the reader(s) play protagonist in an alternate world. Their demise or narration ends the story. All other books in the library are transcribed adventures from the Colossal Tome. They hint at the magical purpose of it, the stories contain repetetive elements of the protagonists watching the sky during their adventures. The fictional sky is drawn in great accuracy in the pages of the Colossal Tome and when torn out becomes a gate to the alternate world. People in this world are aware of the Colossal Tome and their power over them, they hate and fear it.
