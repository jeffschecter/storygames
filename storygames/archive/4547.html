\n so as i was reading the discussion Is Character Advancement Necessary for Campaign-Style Play? i had about trying to do a game where the stereo-typical "zero to hero" or "peasant farmboy to king" format for many D&Dish games is reversed. A character starts at the peak of his game and declines for one reason or another. I thought this sounded interesting, but it might frustrate a lot of players. So today as I was going about my business I had another thought:
what if the character wasn't in decline but was telling his story to someone or reliving it through flashbacks?

Essentially I imagine having a character who is rather old. His adventuring days are done. At the start of the game, all we know is where the character is RIGHT NOW. He's king of some powerful country, perhaps. But we, as players, don't know how he got there. So we work backwards, Memento-style, playing through a sequence of scenes or adventures that lead him to his king-ship. Perhaps he was the hero that killed the evil warlock who had raised a demon army to take over the world. Cool. But how did our hero even get there? Not just anyone could challenge the evil warlock! So we go backwards by another step . . . and another and another until we're back to the beginning.

So. Is this stupid? Has it been done before? If it isn't and hasn't, could it work? How? Could it be done with an existing system?

I think it could potentially work in a variety of genres, from weird suspense/mystery like Memento to fantasy adventure or sci-fi.     \n This is a very interesting idea! I love stories that do this, with time jumping back and forth between the young heroes and their older versions, knowing that the best friends of youth are now deadly enemies but not knowing why or how until you hit that part of the narrative. Often there's a bit of plot movement in the "older" section, too, but it's slower and doesn't make a lot of sense until you've read through all the backstory.

    Come to think of it, Watchmen uses a related technique, keeping a "present-day" story going while making extensive use of flashbacks to tell the story of two related generations of "heroes". This technique is good for more than just heroic fantasy, I think.

    \n Brian,

    I second the recommendations of Munchausen and Snowball - they both play out in a manner similar to what you're looking for. My own game, The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries, features a group of adventurers recounting the tale of an expedition they have returned from to their formidable peers. A similar storytelling focus on the twists and turns of a good yarn rather than questions of survival or ultimate success could be used to get at what you're looking for. The idea of jumping around in time and then tying events back together is a cool storytelling challenge.
