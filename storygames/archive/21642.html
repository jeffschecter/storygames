Nothing to do with weather tables, everything to do with limiting GM fiat.

    Interpreting the pithy catchphrase as some sort of universal rpg wisdom is the same trap of essentialism that roleplaying has struggled with for its entire history. If 21st century rpg theory has actually contributed anything worthwhile to the artform, it is the realization that there is very little essential to it. "Say yes or roll the dice" certainly isn't it, we could spend all day naming games where it's just bad advice - simply because the purpose of play is different, and the dice are used for different purposes.

    Your mention of how coarsely you prep demands the question: At what level of granularity do you truly stop? How many drawers, loose baseboards, and books on the shelves are there to search? If there's ever a time when a player achieved their ends only by searching the correct room, that's their expectation. If there's ever a time when a player was thwarted UNTIL they searched the tenth tiny, discrete location, they'll always search at least that much whenever they can. The only way to head that off is to do things like creating tight time restrictions or some kind of backlash for leaving traces of their presence/search that are only triggered by searching some certain (possibly secret, possibly arbitrary) amount.

    Of course all of this depends on the system (for example, a game in which stakes are announced up-front is going to work differently from a game in which outcomes are only narrated afterwards), but you can avoid making your own bed of problems with that one by saying that fictional positioning matters in a different way: *how* things are done.
    "I look in all of the obvious places - carefully, so I don't leave any sign of my presence. Do I find anything or do any less obvious places catch my eye in the process?"
    That's when you offer a tougher choice: "You don't find anything in a cursory search, but there are a lot of places something could be hidden. If you want to get more involved, it's going to eat up your afternoon and you'll definitely leave traces of your presence behind."
    Then the player has the chance to commit to that risk or alter the circumstances: "I don't have time for this, I toss the place," or, "I call in a favor to Mrs. X. One of her boys can make this look like a burglary."
    And then, the best part is that you can still have different outcomes based on the final act and how it's being carried out. Tossing the place is more thorough than a careful once-over, but is haphazard, so the player found a piece of small evidence. But calling in Mrs. X's boys finds a safe that contains something more serious.

    \nBut of course, please do. I was just exploring the implications.

    I am specifically concerned about the imponderables myself, when it comes to D&D procedure: if you don't roll the dice or engage in tediously simulating the cracks in the ceiling, how do you account for them? Does it not devolve into "say yes or roll the dice" in that sense? How does a GM achieve a moral position where they can actually say no to a player on the basis of imponderable details that nobody has really established?

    (What I mean by "imponderables" in the wargame context: those small details of the fictional situation that obviously exist yet are never established in advance because they fall below the threshold of simulation. Wind and sun direction are usually not accounted in D&D missile combat, for instance. The basic wargaming conceit is that the dice roll "covers" these details - or equivalently, that we can establish them after the fact, once we know what the roll was. "You rolled so badly, apparently the wind and the sun were not your friends on this shot.")

    I can see a GM avoid rolling the dice to determine the weight of the imponderable factors in challengeful D&D by "saying yes": you just assume that anything that has not been explicitly established poses no problem to the adventurers.

    I can also see the GM rolling for the imponderables: now that we've established that something is happening, let's roll to find out if anything goes awry. Sometimes there is no problem, other times the imponderable factors are indeed against the adventurers, and cause an otherwise well-thought-out plan to fail. Sometimes you just miss that secret stash, even if your search procedure covered it; perhaps you got smoke in your eye at the wrong moment, or somebody distracted you.

    What I can't see is the GM outright deciding that the imponderable factors cause failure; that would be arbitrary tyranny. I suppose that the D&D GM is, indeed, caught in the "say yes or roll the dice" binary!

    \nI think it's a matter of the golden mean, a very old-fashioned and omnipresent question of wargaming simulation. "How detailed do you want to go?" is always present in this type of game where you want to have real issues while obviously not being a god capable of simply creating a pocket universe to run simulations in. It is an universal fact if you're doing any sort of simulative gaming at all.

    I refer to a golden mean because forgoing the simulation entirely (with e.g. mechanical subgame like modern D&D has) side-steps the very thing we're trying to do, while insisting (falsely) that you're running a perfect simulation leaves the imponderable factors up to arbitrary GM tyranny. The best position is, I think, in the middle: strive to simulate your wargaming concerns, yet recognize that the simulation is limited and you are forced to simplify, abstract and randomize on minor and undetermined factors.

    Sometimes the simulation threshold is lower and sometimes higher, some people want to simulate some things that others prefer to leave imponderable. It is a category error to perceive this as a binary matter.

    Eero_Tuovinen said:

    you just assume that anything that has not been explicitly established poses no problem to the adventurers.

    I prefer a different approach.

    I think if the GM's job in that game is to be an impartial referee of challenges, then that dovetails nicely with the GM also acting as an impartial presenter of the setting.

    Accordingly, the GM's intuitive sense of what is correct, if informed by a strong but dispassionate connection to the setting, is a perfectly fine substitute for random rolls, and vastly superior to a logic of "every corner of the setting you haven't previously examined favors the players".

    The moral authority to say "no" stems from the social contract which grants the GM that authority in exchange for the GM providing the players with as "real"-feeling a challenge arena (and exploration sandbox) as the GM can.

    When I GM, I am careful to make this logic transparent. It's never, "I don't want there to be cracks in the ceiling," it's always, "This place appears stable and not that old, and this part of the world has never known earthquakes, so no, there are no cracks in the ceiling."

    If my intuition and knowledge of the setting are completely mute on a question, though, then I go with "say yes or roll".

    (Not that all fiction-focused challenge gaming is the same. Maybe there are plenty of reasons why this wouldn't work for other groups.)

    \n Nathan,
    The mantra "Say yes, or roll the dice" is about avoiding superfluous rolls. It is about training GMs to not call for a roll for stuff you can just say yes to. The mechanics of DitV (where this comes from) are time consuming. So, if you are not willing to say yes, roll the dice. Otherwise say yes.

    It addresses several issues:

    1) The "we played all night without rolling the dice" phenomenon. Is that a good thing, did you say yes the whole time?

    2) The "do we have to roll the dice" question? If you as a GM don't want to say, yes, roll it.

    3) The "I (the GM) don't like that outcome, so no you can't have it" issue. Too bad, rol the dice and let them decide.

    4) The "what do I do when PCs do stuff the game is not designed for" question. Say yes or make a conflict that draws the game into what it is designed for.

    5) The "roll for awareness" technique. While this is a good technique, its a bad idea in ditv where rolling takes a long time and test how far your character is willing to go to get what they want. If they PC looks around, just tell them what they see.

    I would say, I never treated it as gospel in any other games besides ditv. But the concept did bleed into my other GMing (more saying yes and more use of mechanics and less fiat).

    Not sure I have heard other GMs treat this as anything more than interesting advice.

    \n There are many rules that capture very widely applicable design principles that are nevertheless not universal. That's part of why roleplaying has been presented as a monolithic exercise so credibly, often and long: there are all these widely useful cornerstone principles that seem to pass effortlessly from game to game because they are true for a lot of games. If your play is at all parochial, you well could spend decades playing only games for which a given cornerstone maxim holds true; it would be easy to mistake part for the whole and start thinking that it's universally true for all rpgs, simply because you haven't personally witnessed ones where it's the wrong advice.

    "Say yes or roll the dice."
    "Players play their characters, GM plays the world."
    "The GM's word is law."
    "Rulings, not rules."
    "Never cheat or fudge."
    "The GM brings the plot."
    "Immerse in your character."
    "There is no wrong answer."
    "Always go for the throat."
    "Avoid cliches."
    "Embrace tropes."

    Note that roleplaying is much more varied in this regard than many other forms of game. Boardgames, for instance, have many universal maxims where roleplaying games hardly have any.
