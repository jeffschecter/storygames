Failing Conflicts vs. Whiffing Tasks

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJesse \n\n\n
\n
\n\nOctober 2008 \n edited October 2008 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In various threads about intention and conflict and tasks and all that usual stuff something was bothering me and I only just put a finger on what it is. Some people are mistaking simple failure in a conflict with "whiffing." I wanted to point out that there's a difference here.

"Whiffing" requires absolutely no change at all. When you fail a To Hit roll in a combat round of D&D (don't know about 4e) literally nothing changes. You get another To Hit roll on another round. Similarly systems along these lines talk about re-rolls. If you don't pick the lock and there is nothing pressing you can simply re-roll. In theory all that's happening is time is passing. You just try and try and try until you succeed or give up or the GM rules that so much time has passed that something happens.

That's not true in say, Sorcerer. If you're interrogating Jones and you fail. That's it. Interrogation WILL NOT WORK, period. The game assumes you gave the interrogation your all and there's nothing left. This is not a "Whiff" because the situation has changed. A whole line of action and decisions is now cut off. You do not have to change your *agenda*. You can still be fully committed to getting the information out of Jones but interrogation is no longer an option.

This is less apparent when you break things down a little bit. For example, you might threaten Jone's life and you fail. You could continue to "interrogate" but you'd have to change your approach. Threatening Jone's life won't work. The situation changes. Jones is willing to die (or at least call your bluff), period. So you try threatening his family, that would be worth another roll.

People seem to discount this "cutting off of approaches" as "a change in the situation."

Jesse

    Posted By: JesseWhen you fail a To Hit roll in a combat round of D&D (don't know about 4e) literally nothing changes. You get another To Hit roll on another round.
    \nUm, no, something changes, because the other guy now gets a chance at you that, if you had taken him out, he wouldn't have had.

    Anyway, "whiffing" vs. failure is not only about whether the situation changes, it's about expectations. If a player goes, "Hm...my character MIGHT know this...it's not her field but it's pretty close." and a GM goes "Make a Knowledge check." and the player goes "12" and the GM goes, "You don't know anything about the subject." then it is not whiffing, even though the situation has not changed at all, and indeed, the only fact we've introduced into the game world is negative - the character doesn't know something. "Whiffing" is a grouchy way of saying "frustrating failure". If the situation changes in a frustrating way, the mere fact that the situation has changed may make the "whiffing" worse rather than better.

    \n I've had games of D&D where a spectacularly lousy set of dice rolls have made 2 or even 3 (I think once 3) combat rounds in a row have absolutely nothing change. PCs swing and miss, enemies swing a miss. PCs maybe move a little, swing and miss, NPCs swing and miss.... Frustration.

    I also had a A Dirty World game last night where the PC in a scene tied with the NPC like four rolls in a row (statistically improbable, but it happened). It really was just a long series of characters bouncing off of each other with little changing. However, the difference was that in A Dirty World we knew how to integrate it into the fiction better, so that the fact that these two characters couldn't change each other became part of the fun of the game, rather than a frustration. So, in some ways, despite the fact that the system changed little to nothing, the fiction did change as a result.

    That, for me, is where the difference between failing and wiffing comes in. When failure, of any kind, becomes frustrating and blocking, it's "wiffing." When failure leads to more tension or something interesting, it isn't.

    \n Let me see if I can't tie some of these thoughts together, because they really are just describing different parts of the elephant.
    \n
    \n
    \nFor a failure to be a whiff, it first and foremost must be something your character should be good at. A first level D&D; character failing to hit a Red Dragon, isn't a whiff...it was just a stupid attack. A Pendragon Knight failing to hit a peasant 3 times in a row because BRP puts pretty low odds on success...even for things you're supposed to be good at...may well be a whiff.
    \n
    \nSecond is the roll's effect on the dramatic tension. Here I'm not talking about hippy Drama resolution stuff...I'm talking about whatever it is in your game that gets you wide eyed on the edge of your seat. If a failure increases Dramatic Tension, its not a whiff. If a failure decreases Dramatic Tension it may well be. Dramatic Tension can be decreased for a variety of reasons. The classic example is the anti climax. This can happen alot in games like D&D; when you know the enemy is defeated, and it just becomes a question of which player isn't going flub their to-hit roll and land the killing blow.
    \n
    \nTime passing is an interesting deal. Sometimes the passing of time helps ramp up Dramatic Tension. You've got 12 rounds to save the prisoner, its going to take at least 8 to execute the plan. You've got 4 rounds to spare...and you just failed your lock picking roll...a roll that your character normally makes in their sleep. That can definitely ramp up Dramatic Tension (although its been used so much in movies and TVs to be a bit cliche at this point).
    \n
    \nOn the other hand, sometimes Time Passing is not dramatic at all. Its just resource management. Resource management can be tactically interesting and a source of great fun, but only sometimes is it dramatic. I'm a skilled fighter, due to a flubbed die roll I fail to kill the last kobold. That last kobold gets a final attack which does 7 points of damage to me reducing my Hit Points to 57. Sure...one can imagine that at some point in the future, I may be regretting those 7 hitpoints. Those 7 Hitpoints might be an extra Cure Light or Potion of Healing. It might mean we only have the resources to go 3 more fights before returning to town instead of 4. So its clearly not a case of "nothing happens". But its also not in any way dramatic or tense. Its just a whiff.
    \n
    \nGames that are whiff prone then are those that provide a high chance of failure to competent characters acting within their area of expertise (like BRP, or D&D2nd;), and that don't provide any mechanical rules for using failure to ramp up tension after the fact.
    \n
    \nGames that are largely immune to whiffage are those in which it is very unlikely for a character to fail in their area of expertise, or where the player is given some ability to control when they fail or when they don't. TSOY's Bringing Down the Pain works well to combat whiffage as does SotC Compells in which players can choose to failure for a reward...and then (if memory serves) use that reward to avoid failing.
    \n
    \nAlso games which allow for random failures, but then empower the players to twist the knife in some way to make that failure dramatically interesting can dodge whiffing problems. IaWA does this with the Stick and Negotiations. Failure is painfully easy in that game...the number of times I've had overwhelming dice and still lost is embarrassing. But rare is the time I can't negotiate an outcome from the failure that is at least as entertaining as the success I'd envisioned. Polaris is another good example, where the string of But Only Ifs and Furthermores pretty much guarentee that SOMETHING interesting is going to happen (and pretty much guarentee that you'll be hard put to figure out who actually "won")

    \n There's a little detail here that is also important, and that's the word "whiff" itself. I'm willing to bet that it originates from golf, where a "whiff" is a stroke that misses the ball. Which is pretty shameful -- real golfers don't whiff.

    The way that Ron talks about whiffing in Sorcerer & Sword also suggests this to me, where he talks about how, in some games, if you fail your attack roll then your sword goes "whiff," contacting nothing but air. But if you're a badass barbarian, that shouldn't happen. Badass barbarians don't whiff. Their attacks might be parried or blocked or dodged by a centimeter, but they don't just plain-vanilla miss.

    (To be fair, when D&D says "miss" it actually means "fail to inflict damage," which is why armor makes you harder to "hit" -- but there's a lot of people who don't catch that)

    So, I think whiffiness also has to do with what failure looks like.

So if you're playing some kind of espionage game and you're breaking into some kind of facility and the GM knows where all the cameras are, and how many guards there are and what their patrol routes are and how long it takes.... then whether it takes you one roll or four rolls to pick that lock can be the difference between whether there are or aren't guards on the other side.

    That can be interesting.

    \n Isn't part of the point Brand's word, deprotagonisation?

    I see a whiff as a failure that damages character concept. If my concept is a badass barbarian dude, and I roll to hit and flatly miss, that's not very badass. It's a whiff.

    If my concept is a hapless potboy pulled in to adventures against his better judgement, that same miss is not a whiff, concept is intact, now I have the fear of having left myself open to my opponent's counterattack.

    Whiffing in large part comes about in my view not because of system, but because of a mismatch of system and player expectation. BRP assumes mortal, fallible protagonists. Players often expect a more cinematic reality than the system is designed to deliver. The gap between the game's intent and the player's expectation is where you find the whiff.
