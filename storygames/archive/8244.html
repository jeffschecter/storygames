    \n My limited experience says that there are three main reasons why I personally might not choose to distribute my product through a well known channel.

    1 : Lack of knowledge

    A simple one, sometimes the seller just doesn't know the market. I personally owe great thanks to Gregor Hutton for making sure I sold on IPR and RPGnow because I just didn't know those sites existed when I wrote my game. I wasn't a customer so I didn't use them. Sure research can cure that, but I am sure there are still other sites, which some segment of market would regard as a well known channel, that I don't know.

    2 : Ease and Cost Effectiveness

    Some channels are not easy to use. Some channels are not easy to actually get your product on even if you want to, because they do not accept all products (of course this doesn't apply to DitV , or because they are not open to residents of all countries, or because they just make it hard to do!

    Other channels are easy enough to use, but they consume so much profit in a sale, and take away possibly more profitable sales from other channels, that they really are not worth using. Just because something is the main distributor does not make it cost effective, indeed the more monolithic the service ... generally ... the worse value it is for the seller A direct PDF sale makes me a little less than twice as much as an RPGNow sale as an example.

    3 : Lack of Contact

    Unlike some game creators I value the opportunity for personal contact that comes with a direct sale. When you buy a PDF from my website you email me, and I email you, and often we will correspond and talk about the game. I may persuade you to join my mailing list, or point you at useful threads. When you buy from another channel, that doesn't happen.

    There are of course other reasons more important to other people, like the things pointed out on those Forge links

    \n Perhaps one concise summation of this topic is that game designers sometimes seem to act incomprehensibly. I understand this feeling well, I feel that way too when I see how others choose to act in regards to their game, their product, their finances, their correspondence and other social matters. But then I look at how my own actions look like from the outside, and I can easily see why things look so weird: we're overworked by our hobby, of course we're going to make all sorts of choices about even minor matters that seem incomprehensible outside. How can you explain it to somebody that you don't have the time or the energy or the will to do something he deems trivial?

    Even when you might have the energy to do something, remember that you could be spending that time doing the things that interest you. Even more importantly, by not doing what the other guy wants of you, you're making your own value statement. My game is not available from any of the aforementioned webstores because I can't be bothered to research them, and they don't superficially seem like the sort of store where my game should be available. Independence allows me to make these choices.

    But the first point is the more important one here: in the perfect world the game designers would service us all perfectly, but most of us have so much more interesting things to worry about that optimizing the sales is far from the first thing every morning. This despite the fact that we're not hurting the consumer (who presumably can get by perfectly well without us servicing him) but rather ourselves. That's tough, but we aren't going to get our distribution optimized without working on only that for quite some time, and that's all time taken away from actually designing and playing games. So in practice we're stuck with imperfect game distribution even when it seems insane from the outside to not take the five minutes / five hours / five days to utilize whatever is the individual outsider's favourite method of distribution.

    \n Kev,
    \n OK, regarding getting your game in an FLGS, here is an idea of what goes into it:
    \n1) You have to print your book in quantities to fulfill distribution orders without getting paid
    \n2) You have to market your game to FLGS's
    \n3) The FLGS's have to order it
    \n4) You have to get a Distributor to pick up your title
    \n5) You have to be willing to wait 15, 30 or more days to get paid for all that after you ahve already spent your money.
    \n
    \n On top of all that your profit per book is more than 50% less (the Distributor buys the book at 50% of the MSRP). When Vincent sells a book on the Unstore, he could, theoretically, make $12. If we only factor in the cost to print and store it (and I am sure he has other costs). If he sells it through a traditional 3-tier system, he might make $1 because he has to sell the book for $11 instead of $22.
    \n Now, if he can pull it all together and get most FLGS's to buy a couple of copies, sure, that's a decent pile of cash (we can say maybe $3,000 hypothetically), but how much is spent on marketing to get the Distributor and FLGS to order those books? And that has to be spent sight unseen with no guarantee your game will actually be ordered. And on top of that, if he were to sell to FLGS's he would have to spend the money to print 1,000 books (or whatever the order size is) and not get compensated for 15, 30 or more days.
    \n
    \n To be fair Vincent's costs are totally made up. I have no insight as to what his costs are at all. So, this is a theoretical discussion. Except for the very real fact that Distributors buy the books at 50% MSRP and don't order any books just because you offer them. And if they do order the books, they will buy them in huge quantities and want a quick turnaround.
    \n
    \n And who knows, it might payoff, but there are costs involved. And there are risks involved. And there are other considerations such as the marketing, placement and control of your product. But putting those aside, there is a cost benefit/risk management process that has to take place before you can even approach Distributors. Even if you undergo to take that risk, there is no guarantee that the Distributor will pick up your title. For all we know (and we don't), Vincent may have tried to market ditv through the 3-tier distribution system and no one has ordered his books. I find that hard to believe, but it is a real possibility. And even so, given his latest endeavor in the form of the Unstore, it would undermine his own, separate business.
    \n
    \n As to why other authors would reject the 3-tier system, it is a case of cost:benefit analysis/risk assessment. As well as possibly other philosophical considerations. Only they can tell you their motivations about why they would or wouldn't pursue 3-tier distribution.
    \n
    \n I hope that helps, sorry some people got so defensive. I think that a lot of what you are asking has been internalized by many authors/publishers and so its a little like asking "why are you wearing that shirt?" It can be taken many different ways, right?
