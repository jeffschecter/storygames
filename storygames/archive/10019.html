\n So, let's play a little social game!

10, 20, 30 years from now, you're 65 years old or so, you have all the time ind the world and you're still playing roleplaying games with your friends.

What kind of game is it? What is it about? Do you still play monster-killers and dungeon-bashers? Is it freeform, Jeepform, 8th ed D&D, rules heavy or what?

One rule to this game: if you don't think you'll be playing rpgs at the age of 65, don't play this game

All the best

Peter

    \n OK, I'll start.

    We're a group of 4 persons. We are all in our mid-60's. Some of us are still working, some are retired. We play once a month or more often. The game is an ongoing story about a family's history through the ages. The system uses ordinary playing cards and is highly improvisational and play is strictly no-prep. We're very engaged in the story and the characters, but play is also very informal and we talk about other stuff, drink beer, wine and spirits and enjoy ourselves.

    We enjoy portraying aging characters, but from time to time we also take on the roles of young people. The emphasis is not so much on conflict as on change and growth - both of characters and of story. Everyone brings his or her own expertise and knowledge to the table and we often have longer dialogues about history, technology and other fields of knowledge.

    Everyone takes turns to be GM, but that role is mostly about moving the story along - as well as arranging wine, food and housing.

    /Peter

    \n We've got a fantasy campaign we've been running for about a year. It supports ensemble play with rotating game-masters. The game has pracitcally no "mechanics" as we would understand them, yet is heavy with setting and color. The rich setting is represented through cards, each of which falls somewhere between an IAWA oracle, and John Harper's Ghost Echo game. Sometimes we draw a playing card to settle a conflict or move the plot along, but most of the time we're just talking. It looks like there's hardly any game there at all, yet the game is decptively deep.
