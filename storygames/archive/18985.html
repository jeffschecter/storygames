\n Looking for some assistance.

Much of this is similar to those who've played D&D. I'm much more of a rules-light person, so classic over advanced or newer versions--however some iterations like Castles and Crusades can work for me.

What I want to do is base the system around a character's six ability scores instead of the the ability modifiers. I don't really care about backward compatibility, so whatever gets ditched is not an issue for me.

* Classic six ability scores ranging from 3-18
* Roll high (d20) add ability score. Meet or beat target number to succeed
* Class is similar to careers in Barbarians of Lemuria (no skills, but you can have broad talents or moves)
* Six Saving Throws tied to each class for reactive checks
* Hit Points represent actual "hits" you can take before CON damage occurs
* Ability damage (scores can be temp. reduced from different types of attacks)
* Danger Die (to be explained in further detail) which ratchets up tension/excitement
* AC as either "ability challenge" in non-combat and AC as armor class in a fight

The target number in an ability challenge is your opponent's ability score + level (or HD). So you want to sneak past the guard, you've got a DEX of 10. The guard is level 5 and has a WIS of 8. So the AC is 13. Roll d20 and add your ability score, if it is a 13 or higher you succeed.

For combat, the AC is armor type (ascending 1-10) + DEX (or perhaps DEX+WIS / 2). Let's say the guard notices you and pulls a sword. The guard has 5 for amor and a DEX of 11, to the AC is a 16. Roll d20 and add your ability score, if it is a 16 or higher you succeed.

Thought for saving throws is I could use ability modifiers + level. I'd have six saving throw categories which tied to your class. If you are a 6th level Thief and have a DEX of 16. Roll a d20 and add +8 (level+mod), if your score matches or beats the ST number then you succeed. The nice thing about this is I don't need to have a sliding Saving Throw scale. Each class has a different set of numbers for Saves.

For advancement I'd go with 20th level as the top. You can increase 6 ability points over 18 levels as you progress. No ability score can be higher than an 18.

You can raise your ability score by one point upon reaching:

LVL 3
LVL 6
LVL 9
LVL 12
LVL 15
LVL 18

I also like HP represented as "hits" which is the number you can take before damage occurs. So a fighter with 7 HP can be hit seven times before damage is taken. HP covers, flesh wounds, weariness, morale etc. Once you are at zero, then damage reduces your CON score. Reach 0 CON and save vs death.

As you advance, each level gives you +1 HP. So at level two the fighter can take 8 hits. At level nine the fighter can take 15 hits.

With regard to magical weapons, a +2 sword doesn't give you a bonus to your roll, it gives you 2 extra hits if your roll is successful. So if you were fighting a Goblin with 3 HP, you would need to hit it three times before it takes damage. However with a +2 Sword you could immediately cause damage.

I'm not a big fan of skills and would probably use something along the lines of careers or talents (much more broad-based) which covers multiple class actions. However, not sure how to implement this into the mechanic. You could go with a bonus, a die step, or reduce the TN. Not sure which one works best.

The Danger Die is a risk that the player can take to increase rewards. For example, a fighter is striking at a dragon, but want to cause extra damage. He takes a d6 as his Danger Die and rolls it with his d20. The d6 number is reduced from result. If successful, the die can be used along with standard weapon to increase damage. You can save the die for later, for give it to someone else to use on their turn. If you fail however, the DM gets to keep it and use it against you.

I'd love to get some feedback from you!

    \nThere's something funky with your math here. I mean, what's your baseline success rate for an "equal" challenge?

    Your base mechanism here is rolling 1d20+ability vs. ability+"something". It's easy to simplify out the ability score and see that we're actually looking at "1d20 vs. something", with that "something" ruling the general percentage of success.

    Your to-hit rules as written imply that an "average" attacker is going to hit an "average" defender with "average" armor 75% of the times. If you want that to be 50%, though, it's very easy to fix: just increase the value of all armor types by a constant 5, so that we're looking at a 6-15 range, an unarmored human being armor=5.

    For non-combat challenges, though, your "something" there is the opponent's level. Which means that - ability scores being equal - the chance of failing at a task is (opponent's level)*5%. I'm not sure that's what you want, or is it?

    RULES SUMMARY

    When you try to overcome an obstacle and the outcome is uncertain, you roll dice. There are several types of dice that this game will use, these are:

    d4\t\td6\t\td8\t\td10\t\td12\t\td20\t

    For most rolls you\u2019ll see the equation d20+DEX, which means rolling a twenty-sided die and adding your ability score (in this case Dexterity) to the result.

    You will do this whenever you attempt something and a roll is required. If the total is the same as the task\u2019s difficulty number or higher, then you succeed. Rolling a natural 20 is always a success, while rolling a natural 1 is always a failure.

    This roll-high method is used throughout play, though there are some subtle alterations to the resolution.

    To Hit. Whenever you want to attack an opponent, roll a d20 and add either your STR or DEX (depending on the weapon) score. If the result is less than the target\u2019s Armor Class, the attack misses. AC is determined by adding DEX+WIS+Armor Type (the better the armor, the higher the number).

    To Act. When you want to attempt something risky, roll a d20 and add your appropriate ability score. The target number for your ability challenge is determined by difficulty. If you roll less than the DC, then you fail.

    To Save. When you need to react in a dangerous situation, you will need to roll a d20 and add your Level to try and save yourself. This applies to things such as spells, poison, falling, traps and death. The difficulty for each type of Saving Throw is determined by your class. If you succeed, you escape the effect.

    These rolls can be affected by Adds: numbers that are added to the d20 or the difficulty. Here are some of the things which can impact a roll.

    Ability Bonuses. For each one of your abilities, you can have a positive or negative modifier. This bonus will be applied when you need to roll a Saving Throw.

    Class Dice. You get an extra die if there is a class-related talent or move you can use. Roll the die along with the d20+Ability score and add it to the result. The better you get at a talent or move, the higher the die you get to use.

    \n It reads pretty clearly. I love your Danger Die mechanic.

    The premise of d20+ATTR is clean, but the handling time on the math is harder with larger numbers. Quick, what's 19+17?

    Can you accomplish the same thing with lower numbers? D20-based games (the die, not the OGL) tend to be swingy, and a 5% increment between successive numbers isn't very meaningful. In fact, a lot of modern d20 rules give a +2 as the stock bonus, suggesting that a d10 might have served the game better. Also, the single-die roll is linear.

    Sticking to a 3-18 range is nostalgic for a lot of gamers, so I get why you might want to do it. But consider using the modifiers as the actual score. Attributes are rated from -3 to +3. You roll 2d6+ATTR. Typical target is a 7+, hard target is a 10+. A +1 or -1 is a significant modifier.

    Your Danger Die mechanic becomes, "Take a -1 or -2 to hit, and gain a +1 or +2 to damage, respectively." Simplified and elegant. The handling time on the math is super fast.

    (Check out the game Dungeon World for examples of this working really well.)

    Now I could offset somewhat this using the advantage/disadvantage option in the D&D Next Playtest. If you have an advantage you roll 2d20 and keep the highest.

    Assume you need to roll an 11 to succeed. With a straight d20, you have a 50% chance of success. With advantage, this goes up to 75%. That\u2019s the equivalent of a +5 bonus to the roll, since you would also have a 75% chance of success if you only needed a 6 or better on a single d20.

    On the flip side for the target of 11, disadvantage means you only have a 25% chance of success, equivalent to a -5 penalty to the roll (when you need a 16 or better on a d20, you also have a 25% chance of success).

    you need a 15 on the die to succeed. With a single d20, you\u2019ll only get this 30% of the time. With advantage, you\u2019ll get it 51% of the time \u2013 about the same as you would get an 11 or better on a single d20. So advantage in this case is worth about a +4. Disadvantage, similarly, is about a -4: You only succeed 9% of the time with disadvantage, which is about the same as a single d20 with a target of 19.

    At the extremes, advantage makes the least difference. If you need a natural 20 to hit, that\u2019s only going to happen 5% of the time normally. Advantage ups your chance to 9.75% \u2013 equivalent to getting a +1. Disadvantage takes you chance down to 0.25%, or 1 in 400.

    \n I'll riff off of Hituro and recommend his route rather than using Talents or a similar mechanic to add to what would be Skills in other systems. You're already using Careers (ala Barbarians of Lemuria), make 'em pull double duty by allowing them to apply to Skill rolls directly, along with whatever cool thing is emblematic of that particular career. While I'm not all that experienced with old-school fantasy gaming (although I've GMed a few one-shot games of DCC that were absolutely amazing) my impression is that generally it tries to stay away from ranked talents of any sort.

    So what I'd recommend is going with:

    Warrior: Class level adds to hitting folks, Defense HP, and riding, watching for intruders, intimidating people etc.

    Thief: Class Level adds to defense, acrobatics, stealth, lying, stealing, sabotage, improvising magical effects etc.

    You know stuff like that. Building bonuses like what you were talking about with class talents into the advancement structure of the class reduces build time, and centers focus on how the character is built but rather on how the character is played. Which if I'm not mistaken is a central conceit of the OSR and of old-school gaming in general.

    What I found in play testing was that 3-18 gives too much of a spread. Whichever ability you choose to hit in combat - dexterity usually - a player that rolls an 18 as part of character generation in the first few minutes of sitting at the table gains an immense advantage over everyone else for months of gameplay to come. Mine is a Superhero game and I want extreme results, but this was too much.

    I now halve the 3-18 roll to get modifiers from +2 to +9 which seems to work well.

    Your point 1 and point 3 are actually the same mechanic - which makes the game more streamlined. I use d20 plus modifier for everything. You can use d20 and something to do with ability score to hit, save and use skills.

    You point 4 and 5 are actually the same mechanic - which makes the game more streamlined. You can take damage to any ability (I like the idea of charisma damage) but if you take damage to CON it's actual physical damage.

    Your "danger die" seems to be a cool idea and may well end up being the USP of your game.

    Personally I don't like opposed rolls as they slow the game down. GM and player both have to roll for every attack/interaction. I prefer a single roll against a set target - speeds things up. D20 plus ability vs. 10 plus opposing ability should do. (Or d20 plus {ability - 10} - which could be noted in a column on the character sheet - vs. opposing ability)

    RULES SUMMARY

    When you try to overcome an obstacle and the outcome is uncertain, you roll dice. For most rolls you\u2019ll see the equation d20+DEX, which means rolling a twenty-sided die and adding your ability score (in this case Dexterity) to the result.

    You will do this whenever you attempt something and a roll is required. If the total is the same as the task\u2019s difficulty number or higher, then you succeed. Rolling a natural 20 is always a success, while rolling a natural 1 is always a failure. This roll-high method is used throughout play, though there are some subtle alterations to the resolution.

    To Hit. Whenever you want to attack an opponent, roll a d20 and add either your STR or DEX (depending on the weapon) score. If the result is less than the target\u2019s Armor Class, the attack misses. AC is determined by adding DEX+WIS+Armor Type (the better the armor, the higher the number). For certain attacks, your class can give you an advantage. With this advantage you'll get to roll 2d20 and keep the highest die. Level and Adds and also boost the result.

    To Act. When you want to attempt something risky, roll a d20 and add your appropriate ability score. The target number for your ability challenge is determined by difficulty. If you roll less than the DC, then you fail. For certain actions, your class can give you an advantage. With this advantage you'll get to roll 2d20 and keep the highest die. Level and Adds and also boost the result.

    To Save. When you need to react in a dangerous situation, you will need to roll a d20 and add your ability score to try and save yourself. For certain saves your class and level can boost the result. This applies to things such as spells, poison, falling, traps and death. You'll need to roll a 20 or higher to make a successful saving throw. If you succeed, you escape the effect.

    Rolls can be affected by Adds: numbers that are added to the d20 or the difficulty.

    Level Bonuses. You'll get a bonus as you advance upward in your class. These bonuses can be used in certain situations to help increase your result.

    Class Dice. You get an extra d20 if there is a class-related talent or move you can use. Roll 2d20 and keep the highest die.

    You can ratchet up the excitement by using a Danger Die. The Danger Die is a risk that the player can take to increase rewards. For example, a fighter is striking at a dragon, but want to cause extra damage. He takes a d6 as his Danger Die and rolls it with his d20. The d6 number is reduced from result. If successful, the die can be used along with standard weapon to increase damage. You can save the die for later, for give it to someone else to use on their turn. If you fail however, the DM gets to keep it and use it against you. You can only keep one Danger Die at a time and use it once per session--so choose the situation wisely.
