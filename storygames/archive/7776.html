\n I have a problem.

I've been designing these storytelling games. And they're a blast. With the kinks ironed out, they work well, and they're fun. They do what I designed them to do. Cool.

Here's the problem: due to geography, timing, and other issues, every time I play it's with a group that consists mostly of people playing for the first time. (The good part is, the different people playing is because many, many people are interested in the games, not because people never want to play twice, so that's very cool.) Why is this a problem? Well, these storytelling games, while working to focus the story, pretty much allow anyone to narrate freely, whatever they want (like a GM in an RPG).

It seems that people playing for the first time really enjoy that freedom and tend to get really wacky and out there. They're having fun, it's cool, and I would do the same thing if it was my first time. But I am getting really sick of it, because I've really gotten the "woo! let's make a story!" thing out of my system, and want to tell meaningful, powerful, coherent stories. I've gotten the same from some of the players: "OK, I see how this works now. I think I could make a really great story with this next time..." But they've all played once or twice. I've now been through many, many playtests and want some more depth.

Note: I'm 99% sure that the system is not creating the wackiness. The support for strong, meaningful stories is there; the players just aren't willing.

Is there a way I can keep first-timers from getting all silly with it, or is that spoiling their fun? Is there anything I can do that would help them get serious about it? Or am I the one with the problem? (Maybe I just need to be patient...)

    \n Paul,

    Here's the thing I've learned the hard way. It's not the freedom of the system. It's not the newbie-ness of the players. It's the player's commitment to the fiction. Ron's games have really driven this home for me with their extensive biographies and attention to the content of the source literature. Sorcerer & Sword is intended to be played with people committed to creating awesome pulp fantasy. Spione is intended to be played by people committed to political espionage fiction.

    That's my advice. Figure out what kind of story you're craving then go find fans of that kind of story. Systems that then support that commitment will play themselves. The commitment comes first, the system spins that commitment into something awesome.

    \n Yeah, as Rob notes, this happens all the time in Universalis.
    \n
    \nIts not that the players are new to gaming, that causes it.
    \n
    \nAsk yourself this...when was the last time any of these players was asked to put their inner creativity on display for the appreciation and judgement of their peers? Unless they're actors, or DJs, or Kareoke singers...probably not very often. And even if they are comfortable with THOSE displays...because they believe they're good at it...that doesn't necessarily mean they'll be comfortable with the sorts of display an RPG requires.
    \n
    \nHere's a reverse example. I have no problem sitting down with group of people I only kinda sorta know and roleplaying...I'm good at it, I'm comfortable with it. But you won' t catch me dead on a Kareoke stage...nuh-uh, no way...welllllll, maaayyyybeee...if I was really drunk and a bunch of us were up there just screwing around and being silly.
    \n
    \nIts easier to be silly. People tell jokes all the time, they want others to laugh at the things they say. People also know that not all jokes are winners...sometimes they fall flat. So what that means is simply this. When your creativity is on display and being judged...and you're not sure you're any good with the activity in question...the safest place to be is silly-land. Going dramatic and having people laugh or think its not good, hurts. Being silly and having people laugh is the point...and even if it doesn't work, it doesn't hurt nearly as much because you weren't really trying, you were just being silly.
    \n
    \nSo how to fix that. Well, any technique that enhances people's level of comfort with their fellow players will help. If you've got one guy in a group that everyone knows just loves to mock people...that's going to make everyone less likely to risk judgement. After that time and familiarity are the best solutions.
    \n
    \nBut Jesse's also right. People are going to be more motivated to get better at an activity if their committed to what the activity represents. Give them something that's really important to them (like Ron's love affair with classic pulp sword and sorcery...or spy fiction) and they'll try harder because they want to do justice to something they love.

    \n Would I be crushing toes (specifically Jesse and Ralph's) if I were to re-emphasize the point about commitment to fiction a few centimeters and suggest that, in my view, it's not about commitment to "re-creating" a genre, but to committing to fiction itself -- which means asking the players to put on the line something they actually care about?

    The rules of a fictional setting matter -- YES! And it doesn't have to be heavy handed "Woe, Look At Me Angst!" stuff. Just the idea that good pulp stories, good action adventure movies and so on draw their strength not from getting a style of storytelling right, but from the commitment of the creator to address something he or she cares about.

    Ron's essays about the fiction, as far as I'm concerned, are valuable because he lifts the hood and says, "Look! Look at at this stuff that's going on under the color!" As creators, our work in socially creating a story goes better if we're on the stuff that's under the hood.

    If you don't do that you can get a lot of "creation" -- but it will be noisy and maybe fun, but often not with much point.

    \n I've always wanted to be a necromancer: so I'm bringing the virgins back!

    Here's the deal: I've figured out another angle on this question.

    When I get a bunch of people interested in these games, they get excited, they show up, they want to play. But many of them (even the gamers among them) are simply not in tune with the idea that we are going to participate in a process that has the potential to create a quality product. They've never done that in a "game". They are expecting silly fun.

    How can I communicate this to them better without sounding like some weird snobby elitist who really, really does not want you to join his game unless you're a cool, creative, inspired genius with impeccable taste?

    Surely you've played some story games with non-gamers before. How can you communicate to them that it can be a fun and relaxed process, but they should still be looking towards high-quality story results?

    (Note: this mainly has to do with distributed-authority games. In an RPG with a strong GM, they can, of course, just enforce their own vision.)

    For example, my game begins with a brainstorming process, from which select elements are chosen by the group. Everyone has an equal say in this process--no one can "take charge"--this is governed by the mechanics. Those chosen elements will form the basic bits of the story, so the quality of the story is dependent on the careful selection of the elements by the players. But most players don't seem to get it the first time around: they're not used to the idea that the could be playing a game for two hours, and that it's fun depends on them making choices based on what they care about.

    So, how can you communicate that they need to commit to the fiction if they want to have fun... without sounding like an arty-farty jerk?

    Do you have positive or negative experiences to recount that relate to this?

    I feel like just writing about has made a lot of crystallize in my head, but I'd like to hear about your experiences.

    Yes, I think so. It has happened in each playtest, to varying degrees. I'm just getting more and more curious about the potential of the games to create deeper, darker, more tragic stories.     \nThis is probably your problem. Everyone who has roleplayed extensively with people from different gaming cultures know that in experienced gamers the expectatios of roleplaying can be deeply imprinted. Playing with new comers can often give you possibilities in play that are otherwise hard to get (or at least takes a long time of coordination of expectations).

    But really, the most important thing is probably to have common expectations of the session at hand. From my own experience: when I'm went to a larp to play a computer programmer and sex-delinquent I prepared for that and you adjust to what you have to do. Bu then, if these kind of themes suddenly appearduring an night of ordinary dice-rolling rpg then I know I always try to sneak away from it. And I think this is the case for everyone.

    If you want heavier, more meaningful stories I first of think you have to talk with the other players about a common agenda and second, as mentioned earlier in the thread, specify a theme of the specific game. I don't believe that you need huge amounts of source or texts, but it is a proven fact that rather small amounts of heavily geared text given to the players right before play will very much influence their style.
