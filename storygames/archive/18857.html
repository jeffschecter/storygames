\n So I've been gearing up for my second kickstarter these days and have also been lamenting the ludicrous prices of international shipping from the states along with everyone else on kickstarters (and in general). I live in South Korea where printing and shipping books is really, really cheap so I was wondering if designers and publishers out there had any interest in something I was thinking of starting up. This is obviously just in the brainstorming phase but what I was thinking was something along these lines:

1. When a designer/publisher wants to set up international fulfillment for a kickstarter or some such thing, they contact me.
2. I set them up with some printers in Korea that have representatives that speak English so they can get their own quotes and do all that action. All of the stuff with the printer can still be handled by them, the designer and publisher, since their English is usually pretty good (I could help out when/if necessary). I can also send along proofs to the states when it comes, take pictures from here in Korea and give my impressions in addition.
3. When setting up where to ship the books, you just have them send it to me, via domestic shipping and I handle fulfillment after you send me the list of names and addresses to ship to, providing documentation, invoices and such for shipping materials and the cost of shipments, with costs of such thing all being given up front. I'm not really sure how I'd work this, but I'd get some form of payment for my services.

This could be something publishers do for just international orders, but I've also found lately that shipping from Korea (normal air mail, 10-14 days) to America is actually cheaper than domestic shipping in country! So it might be even easier (re:cheaper) for me to handle virtually all fulfillment, and then any surplus could be arranged to be sent to the states perhaps.


So I only have my Magicians kickstarter to base the numbers off of, but here are the numbers to see if it'd be worth it for publishers and designers to try doing it this way - printing and shipping from South Korea. I sent books to a ton of countries all over the world but I'll focus on America for obvious reason (and because it's one of the more expensive places to ship to from Korea, compared to Europe and Asia).

I got 1,200 copies of Magicians printed in Korea, which ended up costing me about $2,685 USD (Korean won = \u20a92,880,000) <6x9, black and white, 212 page softcover>
To ship one book, via printed media air mail, costs less than $6 USD (Korean won about \u20a96,000)
For packing materials, I paid about $28 USD (Korean won = \u20a930,000) for every 100 items (Safety envelopes with bubble wrap inside them).
Domestic shipping for the books, from printing house to mine cost me less than $30 USD for all 1,200 books (Korean won = \u20a930,000)


As far as I can tell, it costs about $10 to ship a book of Magicians size (6x9 softcover, 212 pages) from point A to point B within the United States, so it's actually cheaper to send a book from Korea, though you probably have to wait a bit longer.

I'd love to be able to provide fulfillment, especially international, but to whomever to get some awesome games that seem to be less and less available to us international folks. But you guys tell me! Do you think it's a viable option that you, as publishers and game designers, would be interested in and use?

Here's a spreadsheet for comparisons for cost of printing and shipping.

    \n Ordinary fulfillment relies on long-term stability, yes. However, i understood that Kyle was thinking of doing short burst projects, something where he'd take care of the crowdfunding fulfillment and then send the rest of the print run wherever for the long tail market. Theoretically you'd need to rely on Kyle for merely a couple of weeks, or a couple of months on the outside.

    Of course the liabilities involved are necessarily pretty large. I suspect that you, Kyle, would need to explicitly take a pretty large share of the risks involved in doing something like this for people to be willing to partner up; if your part in e.g. a shipping disaster or house fire that ruins half a print run is that of a mere consultant, a publisher working with you might even find themselves in a legally weakened position compared to more ordinary arrangements. Just to pick an arbitrary example, you might end up in a situation where you, Kyle, were the only party capable of litigating against a printer or shipping company, while your partner would be the party actually suffering damages; this could lead into a situation where it is not in your interests to actually pursue such litigation, which might leave your partner incapable of protecting themselves against whatever the original unfortunate event was.

    Continuing with the obvious train of thought, it's clear that the persnickety details can be figured out, given sufficient motivation. However, once that has been done, it seems to me that Kyle will have developed a legitimate intermediate business agency for foreign investment into Korean publishing industry I mean, surely there are already companies working in this field? At least here in Finland there are, indeed, companies whose entire business is about facilitating foreign business desiring to operate in Finland. And I know that I've seen Internet-dwelling companies that focus on providing printing & shipping services originating in China.

    Which is of course not to say that it's a bad idea - clearly it's a good one, considering that others are already doing it. However, it's also a pretty serious and dull one (dull as in work, I mean), I suspect, so if you were thinking of doing something fun related to roleplaying, then this might prove a challenging proposition. The key question is balancing the liability issue against the fun stuff: is it possible to do this sort of thing at a relatively informal garage-punk level (where things are still fun) without running into the sort of fuckfest that occasionally happens when indie hippies try to make things just a little bit too simple?

    Also, an observation on a higher level of abstraction: if it is cheaper to ship to the US from Korea than from the US, one would expect this to be caused by some sort of subsidy, rather than natural logistical costs. This means that your business idea is vulnerable to political changes over a longer time period. This well might be another good reason to focus on burst fulfillment over long tail warehousing.
