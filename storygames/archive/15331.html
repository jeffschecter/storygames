\n
\n
\n So let's say you start a game of Dungeon World as a bunch of undifferentiated villagers, OK? You have the basic moves but no class moves. Maybe you have stats of a uniform zero. Through play you slowly differentiate based on the choices you make and after a session or two there's a break, a training montage, and you are first level guys in your chosen classes with a deep shared history.

Would this work and, more importantly, would it be fun? It might lead to having multiple fighters or clerics or whatever - what are the implications of that?

I'm thinking maybe you build stats on the fly, and that helps define who you will be. So you start with a zero in everything, but any time you engage a stat-based move you have the option of permanently raising or lowering it. In short order you'll have strong differentiation, and the raising/lowering ends when you have a level one stat package. Then it is time for the training montage and cool class moves, and maybe the table votes on your alignment.

I just like plucky weakling villagers rising to greatness. What do you think?

    \n I did this back in AD&D 2nd Edition. All the characters were 0th level villagers, and within a session or two they chose a class and leveled up to "level one". One guy even met a cool NPC and wanted to apprentice under him, so we had write him a whole new class based on the NPC's stories of his former glory. It was fun, and it really gave that "old school" fantasy novel feel.

    Now, how you'd want to do this mechanically... lots of different options there!

    Maybe you could use existing AW mechanics, like stat highlights: after you roll a certain stat X many times, you get an advance, which you can use to switch into a playbook related to that stat. Doing it freeform, based on the fiction, could work, too, though (that's what we did).

    \n This is somewhat related.

    I'm playing in a Dungeon Crawl Classics (beta) campaign set in the Wilderlands. There's this thing called "the character funnel" where you make one or more level-0 characters and they're pretty much just vagrants, farmers, herders and barrel-makers, etc. Maybe the occasional astronomer or con-man. Background is randomly determined, just like the 3d6 rolled-in-order abilities.

    Then all these villagers go into a "dungeon" of some sort. Them what survives the appalling mortality rate get to be level one character in one of the four basic classes. Prior to that, you may very well just have a weirdo character with a high INT or PER (WIS). It's surprisingly fun for a session or two prior to level one "graduation."

    The scant details and commonplace backgrounds of our characters have proven fruitful voids of chargen-through-action.

    \n I have a few questions, and thought I'd post here instead of on the DW forums, in case Jason or Marshall are about too.
    \n
    \nDo Bonds count as a stat? The only reason I ask is that say during the fiction, you establish a bond in order to use the Aid / Interfere move... Can you assign the Bond bonus/penalty to the move? I would rule yes?
    \n
    \nWith the Goodwill Move, it says '+Goodwill spent' (not accrued).... What if the player has spent that accrued goodwill on moves involving other villagers that harm / hinder them? It gave me a chuckle imagining a halfling ner'do'well accruing Goodwill as they helped themselves (whilst helping fellow villagers) then spent that hold on manipulating / stealing / blackmailing / hack and slashing with said villagers! I guess then this guides your alignment choice on leveling up, and gives a fictionally devious slant to why the villagers gave you a 'warm' send off.
    \n
    \nDid you playtest any other iterations of the 'village'? I'm thinking other communities may be just as exciting and in line with the D&D tropes - Cities, Academies, Tribes, Slaves/prisoners. Hmmmm, the possibilities!
    \n
    \nAny advice on initial situation? Or provocative questions that yoy may have asked? Or a little zero-level specific dungeon starter?
    \n
    \nIt looks wonderful chaps, can't wait to get this onto the table!
