\n Good evening!

I'm gearing up for an OSR style sandbox / dungeon crawl with a homebrew system we came up with to suit our needs. I'm planning on making it very much in the West Marches style of campaign, with some slight modifications. I'm slowly building up my own personal campaign grimoire of sorts - starting with a broad description of the setting and where the players will begin (a decent-sized, fortified settlement in the middle of what is essentially god-forsaken wildlands and forests), then creating a basic Hex crawl setup (central hex surrounded by a day's in any direction, each of which should include some kind of encounter or interesting something or other), random encounters, a couple dungeons with history and their own gimmicks/mysteries to deal with.. and so on.

As I'm doing this, I'm encountering an interesting paradox in that while the system itself is very rules light, I'm doing more prep work for this than I have for any of my narrative-based games in years. Lots of notes, notecards, scribbles, and so on.

So that brings us to the general topic:

How do you guys manage your campaigns, D&D/OSR and otherwise? Everything from maps and story notes, to keeping track of monsters, stats, items.. relationship maps, background info. I almost feel like I need some kind of custom database to do all this, but that's obviously far beyond my programming capabilities. hah. On the other hand, there's always pen and paper, but you wind up with a three ring binder that has fifty pages of scribbles in it to thumb through.

Any tips, tricks or programs you've found really make your job easier?

    Saladdin and myself are both early backers of the Realm Works kickstarter. I can't speak for them but I am still learning its myriad features and I'm blown away by it.

    Obsidian Portal seems a solid free alternative.

    I guess what I'm wondering about is what exactly are you tracking? I run very NPC-heavy games so my notebooks tend to be one NPC per page, and I list what they're doing and what they're after.
    \nI'm starting with a pretty simple hex crawl setup - a starting hex, and then one hex in every direction, each representing a day or so's travel. Then throwing a handful of 'interesting locations' on the map.

    So insofar as what I'd need to organize:
    * Rough-notes of maps for the 'locations' when applicable
    * Random encounters / wandering critters. If not one list per hex, one list per 'region' of hexes, to populate them separately.
    * Details for all of the encounters, including monsters and the like
    * Individual entries for important NPCs, complete with motivations, stats, and agendas

    I'd like to keep adding more .. random content generation charts and things as well. Part of the emphasis of the project is actually watching what grows out of the randomness of the charts and such. Random monster-generation charts, undead charts, NPC creation charts, and so on down the line.

    And of course, keeping track of the results of those charts as they become important to the game..

    \n@Saladdin, watched the video and thought this can be what I wanted... but definitely no, when I saw the GM screen and the book size I realized it's still too complex for what I'm looking for.\n

    I developed this for my own campaign. One roll can generate a pretty robust encounter if you can whip up a table pretty quick beforehand: intothedungeondark.blogspot.com/2014/03/sandbox-encounter-table-methodology.html
    \nNice trick, thought of something like that before, but the problem there is that

    a) You're somewhat limited by the dice: you can either have a number of options equal to the dice top number or less.
    b) Rolling dice already interrupts the game flow, though the time it takes is nelligible enough so player's won't mind. However, searching/cheking the results and then interpreting them into the current situation adds seconds of interruption that increase that flow interruption too much, until players concentration start to wander outside the shared imagined space, meaning you have to re-frame the whole scene. Might be not much of a trouble for either you or the players, but why not avoid this if you can?

    Something as simple as cards with words on them solve both problems. You can have as many cards as options you have, and drawing one to read it and interpretate it takes as much time as a single roll (with no table cheking). The only thing now is how many random tables you need/can fit on the cards and how to make the mix more efficient, creative and with a bit more open interpretations.

    \n This may be too simple for what you need, but I've been lately using XMind to keep track of campaign ideas. It's a free mindmapping software where you can create nodes, connect them in different ways, write notes for each node, define simple hierarchical relationships between them, make squiggles, attach files. It doesn't do anything RPG-specific like roll on tables or draw hex grids, but for cases where a simple .txt file is too linear to represent a jumble of ideas in my head, this has been pretty cool so far. You can set a background image, so maybe you could use your region map for that background image and lay your ideas over it? YMMV, but I've been liking it more and more as I've gotten more comfortable with the interface.

    \n I use MediaWiki. It's the engine that Wikipedia runs on, so it's very powerful. It's a bit of a pain to get set up, but once it's running, it can do a lot. Being able to do imagemaps is especially handy -- I can set a large-scale map so that clicking on an area of it allows 'drilling down' to successively smaller-scale maps. It's also free and open source. And of course it's a wiki, so creating new articles, searching, adding categories, adding images, etc. are all quite easy.
