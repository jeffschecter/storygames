\n On another thread I asked Julia for some help with this:-\n
It would be really useful to me to hear some suggestions on ways that this sort of material could be handled in a way that's not (or at least less) likely to cause offense. I'm tinkering with pulp fantasy and sword and sorcery games, which bump into some of the same issues, and currently I don't feel hugely equipped to navigate them.
\nThe swords & sorceries in question are specifically and closely modelled on Howard's original Conan stories. I love the exoticism and strangeness of them, and the fact the hero spends most of his time in non-European locales. But there's all this stuff in there that is deeply uncomfortable: the whole of The Vale of Lost Women, for a start, and chunks of Shadows in Zamboula and various others.

I guess I'm after a few different strands of advice. What is it in these stories that's liekly to cause offense (sure, some of it's obvious, but I'm betting some of it's not). And what are ways to deal with it? How can the exoticism and strangeness be preserved without regurgitating old stereotypes or otherwise dehumanising people?

The original question was asked of Julia, but anyone with something to contribute, please chip in!

    \n I just re-read Vale of Lost Women this morning in order to pull oracle elements out of it for the IaWA / Conan thread. As I said in my post there, it contains what I think of as the most racist dialogue Howard ever put in Conan's mouth.

    The thing is, it was written in the '30s, when racist attitudes were common, and it's set in a fantasy version of 'ancient history' when racist attitudes were, if anything, even harsher. It's not out of character for Conan to disparage the black natives as much as he disparages the white city-dwellers, (As a Cimmerian, he probably feels the same way about the neighboring Vanir tribe back home, and they're white); it's just repugnant to my modern sensibilities, because Conan is supposed to be the character that the reader identifies with. Not that Conan is acting particularly like a 'good-guy' hero in this story -- he's breaking a treaty, considering blackmailing a woman into sex, and slaughtering a village full of people, some of them non-combatant women. He also rescues the girl from the monster, and decides not to blackmail her into sex in the end, so I guess that counts for something.

    To get around the blatant racism, when I pulled oracle elements from the story, I mentioned 'civilized' characters and 'savage' characters, without mentioning race or color at all. (I did use "brown" along with "glowing eyes" in regard to the 'women of the vale', but they are not even technically human, nor are they of any racial stock otherwise appearing in the story.) If players using those oracle entries have the knee-jerk reaction that 'civilized' characters are white and 'savage' characters aren't, I at least have plausible deniability.

    Actively combating the old stereotypes means aggressively changing them up. Seth Ben-Ezra's Dirty Secrets game uses racial and economic demographics to organize the various characters -- theres no mechanical effect, but it is a kind of open-ended test of the player's knee-jerk reactions. Will the players create stereotypical characters or won't they?

    \n I think everybody has a comfort level that balances honoring the source and acknowledging that we are real people playing a game. I played in a game set in 1913, and my guy was casually racist - a man of the times in a pulpy game that was pretty ahistorical. It was an interesting experience. It took a little trust and confidence in my friends. I thought about it and didn't fall back on "but that's how people were, so it's OK", which is weak sauce to me - I don't care how people were, I care about me and my friends, who are real people talking to each other. Making my guy an ugly paragon of his times was a choice, and it brought those times into sharp relief with our own and added some depth. In that way, it contributed to the fun as well. Chris, you asked:\n

    \n Here's a quick litmus test I use when creating characters who are ethnically different from me. So you have an idea of what this character looks like, you're drawing inspiration from your favorite movie, tv show, body of literature, etc. Ask yourself: if someone whose opinion I cared about of the same ethnicity as my character was standing right there, would I be embarrassed in my portrayal, and/or I'm playing into the stereotype, is the humor or parody obvious?

    In rpg's black=bad and white=good or foreign=bad and familliar=good are both interesting (to me) ideas to explore, whether you're in it for the fun or for deeper exploration of the concepts. I think looking into the root of the stereotypical ideas, how they evolve through time, and how they're viewed in the present tense might be helpful.

    How'zat for a start?

    \n Re: the litmus test:

    I do a similar thing at work: When one of my less-socially-conscious co-workers starts to tell an ethnic joke, I stop them and say "Assume that I, sitting here next to you, am of the ethnicity that is the butt of your joke, and that I will be just as offended by it as if that were actually true. Now, tell it if you want to."

    Works about half the time.

    \n Hi all,

    Thanks for the responses. There's really good stuff here, and I want to have a proper think about it. If I don't post here for a day or so, it's just because I'm cogitating.

    Dain - the 'civilised' and 'savage' approach, removing any reference to ethnicity or skin colour from them, is sort of the approach I'd intended to take. But I'm wondering if that's enough - if it's just a token change that, while faintly obscuring the original stereotype, does so quite transparently and weakly. Does that make sense?

    Jason - that's an approach I hadn't considered. I think it's really interesting. Initially I thought it might not be suitable in a game that's more escapist and less concerned with introspection, but now I'm not so sure. Like you say, the intention - and the willingness of everyone present to recognise it - is significant, and might work in any game.

    Julia - that's a fine start. The problem with it I have is that as someone who has never been the victim of racism, I'm not sure how good I'd be at putting myself in the perspective as someone who has. In the case of asking myself if I'd be embarrassed if in the presence of someone of the same ethnicity as my character, I might be tempted to err on the side of caution and avoid the issue altogether. Which isn't really a solution.

    Hope all that makes sense.

    \n I'm really glad you asked this question Chris. It's not a new problem, but it's a difficult one. I often have a hard time defending my love of Conan, when the awful racism and sexism is so apparent. And yet, there's almost something about the racisim and sexism that makes the stories so appealing. This is a hella risky thing to say on the internet, so please, everyone, read as charitably as you can.

    Is it possible to read something that was written with serious intent as parody now? I guess I don't subscribe to the "authorial intent" idea of criticism. What that means is that I don't care whether Howard was just repeating the dominant memes of the times. If it reads as racist, it's racist. On the other hand, it's SO racisit, it's such an obvious projection of fantasies about race, that reading it now is kinda hilarious. Like Julia was saying, it's interesting to explore the idea of a world where black or foreign = bad. Does it matter that when Howard wrote it, it was read "straight"? It's kinda like cowboys becoming gay icons, because they so embody masculinity. I think maybe stories like "Conan" can be a vehicle for criticism, or exploration of racism, because they so embody the fantasies of racists.

    Of course, this stuff kinda has to be handled like it's uranium. In the wrong hands...

    So I guess what I'm saying is that you don't need to excise the racism from Conan to make it cool to play it. But you do need to be really aware of what you're doing at the table, and be commited to that agenda. I don't know that I feel very comfortable about doing that personally, but I'd admire the group that did.

    \n Play it straight.

    If it makes sense for a villain to be of a particular race, then that's what he is. Likewise, the monsters are alien, monstrous, and in general poisonous to mankind. Heroes are still heroic, but this is pulp-fantasy; if they have to be bastards, they can and will be.

    Use it as a way to drive home just how chaotic, brutal, and superstitious the times are. Exaggerate it until it becomes something, like radiation, that soaks into your characters. It's dangerous, but it pays to remind people that the characters and the cast of these types of games simply do not know better. If the players are uncomfortable, then talk before or after about how best to dial it back without breaking the suspension of disbelief. But they should know going into it that it's not high-fantasy: the cost of being a hero as we understand one today is high, high enough that it may even make sense to not be so heroic, and to take what you can get.

    This of course assumes a level of trust and maturity that Generic You and Generic Your Group may not have. Always proceed with caution. (Why did I almost write, "Always proceed with cro\xfbtons" there?)

    \n Rob, think of it this way: Harry Dresden can save people because he's special, and Newark, NJ is a pretty diverse town. If you don't want it to be white people save black people from themselves, it doesn't have to be. Pull back the focus and it's Harry Dresden does Newark, nothing more.

    There's also the flip side of that: the magical negro/colored sidekick who swoops in and saves all the white people, especially the alleged hero, but never takes or gets the credit s/he's due.
    Consider Pirates of the Caribbean. I really love these movies. Fun mind candy, sexy scuzzy pirates, hot chick with dreadlocks and a Caribbean accent. Tia Dalma is a cool character, but they downplayed her abilities in order to let the heroes get their way--at her expense. Why didn't she sink everyone? In the end they all sucked and deserved to drown. I guess that wouldn't have been the Disney version.

    So I have an aversion to playing magical negro-types, even though they can be pretty fun to play (because who doesn't like being magical?). For example, I played the same character in Poison'd in three different games. Her name was Abyssinia, she was a Barbary Pirate, she had no magical powers and didn't believe in the magic. I didn't want her to be another Tia Dalma. In the last game I played her, there was all kinds of "magical" stuff happening. It never really came up, but I imagined her thinking to herself, "crazy Europeans with their dumbass sorcery. There are better ways to get into hell."
