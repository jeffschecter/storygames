    Well, there's the rub. There's not much in NG to say how you should run it. The GM, I would imagine, sets up the initial conflicts after a communal brainstorm on the background colour (it's suggested that the girls are all working together, at school together etc). I think you need to fix your eyes firmly on the characters' dreams and frame scenes that put them at risk. Allow the players heaps of directorial power, and be ready to follow in any direction. Some people are going to be able to do this on their heads; others will struggle.

    See, I need something more qualified there: what makes a game more fit for small groups rather than large groups? For example, I've been running a 2-player Amber game for about 10 years with my husband, and an AD&D (1stEd modified) even longer. We've had a GURPS Black Ops campaign for about as long as we've had the book... but is it because of our interpersonal dynamic, or...?

    Set-up and preparation are probably the main issues I would have in regards to running a game with a smaller group of people. After a while it's kind of like feeding an army; throwing another mouse in the pot is simple once you've got the carrots chopped and the stew spiced, but I don't use the same pot for a meal for two. (Why make an incredibly in-depth world with a hidden mountain city past the coast for a small sea-port adventure? Because we're fanatics. Oh, yeah.)
