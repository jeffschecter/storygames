What's your game like? Why?

    Posted By: RafaelHow do conflicts get resolved?
    \nA die is rolled. If the result is even, then resolution is additive: the colors of all involved in the conflict are added together as if they are light. The person playing the color closest to the result decides the outcome, but can only do so by adding complications to the story.

    If the result is odd, then resolution is subtractive: the colors of all involved in the conflict are added together as if they are paint. The person playing the color closest to the result decides the outcome, but can only do so by concluding a thread of the story, with finality.

    \n You control moods and themes and tone. There are characters, but there is no player ownership of characters: you can take control of a PC for a scene and play them to fit your mood. Then in a later scene, another player steals control of the character from you and has the character start acting differently.

    Mechanics largely relate to seizing control of a character from another player. When a character expresses regret, the blue player can choose to seize control of the character (but would have to give up the character they have now). If you (the red player) really want to see Aunt Betty flip out and get roaring angry drunk at Thanksgiving dinner, then you'll have to pay some resources or roll some dice to get control of her from cowardly Yellow over there.


    Each mood is, naturally, tied to an appropriate color. Narrating in a given color gives you resources appropriate to that color. Possibly if I describe something as golden, I get a resource and the yellow player also gets a (different?) resource.

    \n Hm, not a game where each character is a different color, but perhaps where each character perceives a different range of colors. I was listening to RadioLab do a show on color and they talked about how different species have different numbers of cones with which to see color. For example, dogs have two cones (green and blue, IIRC), (most) humans have three (green, red, and blue), butterflies have five or six and mantis shrimp have 16 (!). Dogs can't see as many colors as us, but butterflies and mantis shrimp can see more. Thus they can (theoretically) react to stimuli that we can't even process as existing. Now how do we make that into a game?

    All I know is I'm picturing the spirit realm as ultraviolet.

    \n The colors we talk about would be based on the NCS system of color. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_Color_System

    Where each color is describes as the percent of darkness/light, the percent of saturation and the hue. For example: The yellow and blue shades of the Swedish flag:
    Yellow \u2013 NCS 0580-Y10R (= 5% darkness, 80% saturation, 90% yellow + 10% red = very slightly darkish mostly saturated yellow with a slight orangish tinge)
    Blue \u2013 NCS 4055-R95B (= 40% darkness, 55% saturation, 5% red + 95% blue = somewhat dark rather unsaturated blue with a very slight purplish tinge)

    The darkness/lightness would indicate hoe dark/light you wanted the theme of the character.
    The saturation how extreme the characters emotions went. High saturation, extreme emotions, low saturation bleak emotions.
    The color would indicate that drove the character. Red for desire, Yellow for ideals, Green for Vehemence, Blue for Duty.

    \n Someone once asked for a "Tron" inspired game, where I played with the 256-bit light scheme found in VGA computer monitors and most websites (from 000000 through to FFFFFF). I used each of the colours (Red-Green-Blue) to represent a characters ability to perform a specific range of skills (data extraction, data insertion, data manipulation), the combination of the three colours defined the ribbons of light across their digital avatar's costume.
