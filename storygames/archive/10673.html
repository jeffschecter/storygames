- The Mountain Witch
- 3:16 Carnage among the stars
- Prime time adventure
- In a Wicked Age
- The Shab-al-hiri-Roach (maybe more for 5 then 6).
- Blood red sands (maybe more for 5 then 6).
- Sign in strangers

It look like that games who work well with 5 or more players encourage plavers vs players conflicts or give other players some ways to influence a scene while their characters are not present. Or simply favor group action and conflicts like 3:16 battles scenes.

    \n I've successfully GMed 1,001 Nights, The Mountain Witch, Agon, InSpectres and carry* for 5+ players.

    The Mountain Witch and carry both work because they create situations where the characters start gunning for one another, so there's a lot of inter-player back-and-forth during the game, while also having mechanical situations that require everyone at the table to be involved for at least short periods of time.

    1,001 Nights works well because it's GMless/GMful, so the energy is distributed pretty well, the rules are simple, and the game is fun if everyone is interested in the fiction. 1,001 Nights can be pretty creatively exhausting (regardless of number of players)

    InSpectres works because, once the ball is rolling, the GM just needs to throw Stress at the characters and keep a good handle on scene length. I've found a good technique for large-group InSpectres play is to let the group naturally split into two or three groups each off investigating some facet of the case, and cut back and forth between them quickly, This keeps the energy pretty high, and because a lot of the fiction is generated by the players actions, not as exhausting for the GM as it sounds. Also, you want to have a goal of, like, 30 franchise dice minimum if you want a game longer than an hour.

    Agon works cuz it's awesome. Well, really, cuz the play structure is very simple and once everyone gets the mechanics, the GM pretty much just needs to pay attention to the monsters. I would recommend having a couple of easy fights so that everyone gets the handle of things, and then keep an eye on the problem that occasionally pops up where the fight is certainly going to go in the heroes favor, but they just...can't...get...that....last...hit...in - especially on a Monster with good defense powers. I usually let that go for about a round and then have the heroes have a contest among themselves to see who gets the killing blow in.

    Hope some of that helps!

    *disclosure: carry is one of my games.

    \n There's a difference between games that simply work with 5-6 players, and games that are optimized for 5-6 players. Games that work off the (direct) interaction between players usually work better more players. The easiest way to do this is with PC vs PC conflict, but it's not a strict requirement. There are a lot of games that simply work with 5-6 players, but very few that are truly optimized for that type of play.

    \n I haven't played very many games from the last 2-3 years, but prior to that, the only 2 games that I know of that I would call "optimized" for big groups is The Mountain Witch and Elfs.

    TMW works because it's all about the relationship between characters. Elfs I haven't actually played, but I believe it works because the game feeds off a certain chaos and riffing-off-each factor that builds with more people (I think).

    That said, I'm a little skeptical of calling most of the games mentioned in this thread "optimized" for big groups. I'm sure Poison'd works great with big groups because it feeds off the same dynamic as TMW. From what (little) I know of Carry, it wouldn't surprise me to hear that it works well with big groups. PTA I would NOT call "optimized", it will work with slightly larger groups, but I think its sweet spot is really 3-4 players + GM. I can't really comment on any of the other games beyond what people have already said.

    \n We played 2 sessions of 10.000, a game designed by Kobayashi.
    The problem was that the game is more geared toward one player conflicts, so I tryed to separate the group to give each player interesting personal conflicts, but this bring a lot of players down time. And when I group two or more players together the conflicts become too easy and/or not rewarding or only rewarding for one player.

    In my last game, one player, got only one "ok" scene, so I felt bad for him, and near the end of the session, I tryed to put the spotlight on his character, but It was a group scene and in the end it was not very satisfying.

    After the session, I was wishing for a game where having 5 players dont bring annoying (workable) complications, but bring interesting situations and gameplay.

    I am thinking that it could be interesting to switch for a game that dont only work for a large group but that also ask for and "enjoy" a large group.
