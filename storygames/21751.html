I'm looking for some light, casual advice.
I'm starting to run a fairly traditional role-playing game using the system from Over the Edge.

I'm thinking play will be somewhat sandbox-y, but I'm not quite sure yet?

    \n I'd encourage you/him to give it a try. Have clear guidelines and method for the GM role in either position, and treat it as a "playtest" (i.e. you don't expect choirs of angels to come down and sing your praises as you play; it's normal and human to make mistakes and struggles while you try this thing out and see if it's any fun).

    There are very, very, very few people who are (possibly) legitimately incapable of this kind of thing, in my experience, and they're usually not interested in roleplaying games or anything of the sort.

    \n@Nathan_H Your proposed game set-up is a interesting one.

    @Paul_T At first glance, your idea seems really cool, but there is a problem if there are only two players. If player A is the GM for the Before storyline, then he/she will have (meta-)knowledge about the answers to all the historical what- and why-questions when they are a player in the After story-line. Player B doesn't really have a (pro-)active role as GM in the After story-line because a lot of the decisions have been made in (i.e. will come from) the Before storyline.

    I'm not sure how OtE plays, so maybe I am wrong. Perhaps if the After storyline started the game then it would work, but I still feel like it would leave one player/GM with not much to do. If you had more players to split the roles between, or if you split the roles like this like this for two players: player A GM Before, player B GM After, player B GM Before, player A GM After, but I still think there is still the same problem but just drawn out over more game sessions.

    I don't know how much that sense of discovery is important to Nathan's play-style so maybe this isn't an important issue, but the way the game was thumb-nailed, above, it seems like that sense of discovery is an intended part of the design.
