    Hi Shreyas. Thank you too for these questions. I'm really a terrible business planner, but I have conceived of a semi-intangible target audience. I'm selling to family gamers and craft hobbyists.
    \nActually, that description (family gamers and craft hobbyists) is just a description of me. I hadn't realised at all until I wrote it down. I mulled that over for two days, and looked at where the game has succeeded and where it has failed. This allowed me to conceive a new target audience, as you'll see if you read to the end of the post.

    When I wrote the game for Game Chef, I intended a game of storytelling, much lighter and liberal than anything I'd done before. The first playtest was a disaster, of course. We played at home with a bunch of close friends, and, although the emblem and trait creation worked nicely, the gameplay suffered. Sorry, I'm getting away from the point. After that, nearly all of the playtests happened in public places, not at home. Conventions, monthly meets, weekly meets, always at a point when no-one knew for sure what would be happening that evening.

    The only time it didn't work (didn't function) was when, after introducing the game's theme to a stranger, I decided not to run the game for him (or us). He was noticeably dubious and actively concerned about the game's playability. "It'll either play okay or it'll completely fail." This is what he told me after a 60 second intro. It could have been my spiel, but I think, since it caught the interest of others at the table, it was probably more to do with his own tastes. After getting to know him better, I started to understand that he was new to story gaming. Like me, he had gotten into it through Spirit of the Century, so I understood his reservations. Chronicles of Skin looks like a "hippy game."

    After thinking about people like this man, I came to understand that doodling and thematic storytelling are things that convention going gamers are more likely to want to do at conventions. The weirdest, off the wall, riskiest games of my life have all been played at conventions. It's the time of my gaming life when I'll have an excess of choice, so one bad game out of X isn't too much of a loss.

    Basically, at conventions, some people take risks with new games. Those people are my target audience.\n
    Are you hitting them? Do they know about your game, and do they want it?
    \nChronicles of Skin fits in your pocket, has four pages of rules and plays in under 3 hours. I think I'm hitting that convention-game-gap moment. But we all say that, or think that. don't we? So how do I find out if they want it? Shreyas, have you got any advice for me?

    \n Hi Sebastian,

    A few questions about your game:

    -- how many cards is it?
    -- who are you planning on printing it with?
    -- what's the price-per-copy of printing?
    -- how many copies are you printing?
    -- how much is shipping likely to be?
    -- what's the minimum amount of money you need to make this happen?
    -- once it's printed and you have extra copies, are you planning on using a fulfilment service like IPR?

    And a few questions about outreach so far:

    -- do you have a website?
    -- do you have a blog (can be the same as above)?
    -- are you on Twitter (if so, how many followers)?
    -- how many Kickstarter projects have you helped fund?

    All of these things help you build up a following so people know who you are and want to help fund things you're involved in.

    Other things to think about include:

    -- do you have a business bank account seperate from your personal funds?
    -- have you registered for the state and local business licenses you're going to need to legally sell your game?

    In general, it's worth thinking about whether you actually want to run a small business. Releasing your game for free online or selling a few dozen cheaply produced copies at local game conventions and meetups is really easy and much, MUCH easier than formally publishing your game and continuing to make it available to would-be purchasers. Another option, if you don't feel like you're much of a business guy and aren't interested in becoming one, is partnering up with someone you know and trust who IS a business person. I don't know who that might be, but those people are worth their weight in gold.
