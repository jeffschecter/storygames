    I had a thought when I was explaining Conflict Resolution some time ago.

    I'll give a quick recap to explain my thinking.

    "Conflict Resolution" = Resolves a conflict of interest between two characters in the fiction, right?

    To have a conflict of interest, the NPC needs an agenda of their own that could clash with player characters. I'll call these guys "named characters" to distinguish them from faceless extras (security guards and so on) which don't have agendas of their own. Extras are just furniture which can be used as tools by the named characters if needed. Of course, you can consider 'environmental antagonists' to be named characters with their own agenda as well (A cliff that wishes to throw any who climb it onto the jagged rocks below, for example).

    OK?

    So as I see it, you resolve at the smallest scale where the player's goal would interfere with any named NPC's agenda. Smaller than that, and the PC don't have anybody to conflict with. Bigger than that, and you are ignoring the agendas of some of your NPCs. I think using "how" and "why" is a great technique to get to this scale.

    Does that help any with finding a "stop" point?

    I've been thinking about this a whole lot.

    Jason, making up kooky things about a character to stonewall isn't the same as failed resolution and certainly isn't in the real of providing fit opposition across a moral line. It's just stopping the protags from proceeding. Whoop dee doo.

    Will breaking in get the protagonist something they want? Well, if they get into her desk, it does. Does getting into the desk get them what they want? Well, if evidence is found, it does.

    Finding evidence is a turning point in a story. If they find evidence, the story takes a new direction. If they break in but don't find any evidence, breaking in doesn't do anything, unless the conflict's really about whether a character can make themselves break the law or something. Then it's not about finding the evidence at all. Can we find out that the NPC's a neat freak in the course of resolution? You bet! That's good stuff! Can we find out if the protag is able to overcome their fear of breaking the law? Yeah, awesome! But we have to be able to have the crux of the conflict be about what direction the events turn. Other stuff can happen, but the resolution has to resolve the issue.

    Codifying that, though, that's hard. Prime Time Adventures has something to say about that, but I'm not sure exactly how to put it into words.

    Warren, you're onto something.
