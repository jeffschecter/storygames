    \n I ran a game of Anima Prime for Blake, Brendan and Joel. They were a family of bounty hunters, vying for a big score against their archrivals in a city of towers under siege by an army of fiends. Their characters were great: the veteran aunt who ended up riding a batasaur; the drunk uncle who charged into danger with his huge sword and bare chest; and the young nephew who inherited the pair of magical pistols, who didn't think he was worthy (until the end of the game, when he took the "not" out of his "I'm not worthy of the family legacy" trait). I greatly enjoyed it.

    Later on Saturday I played the Battlestar Galactica board game with Matthew, Brendan, Johnzo and David. Man, that was one of my favorite board game experiences of all time. The game itself is brilliant, replicating the mistrust issues of the series and the struggle to balance all of the dwindling resources against each other under constant threat of cylon attacks, with base ships bombarding the Galactica and centurions boarding it. Nobody trusted Gaius, who turned out to be a human, while Saul Tigh -- our Admiral in charge of the nukes -- was revealed to be a cylon, as well as the chief. And even though Roslin, Starbuck and Gaius fought off centurions and made a crazy jump that could have cost us half the remaining fleet, in the end, the cylons starved us to death before we could make our final getaway. It was an epic game, and there's much to be learned from this game in terms of design, pacing, competing use of resources, tough choices, etc. I sure hope I'll get to play it again soon.

    \n It doesn't/didn't really.

    About thirty minutes before people started to arrive, I got the idea to make this little gathering feel more like a "real mini-con." I made a quote board, put up little signs naming each of the gaming spaces after a different Nautilus room and threw up that bulletin board. The way it was supposed to work is that people would put what they were willing to run in each of the top slots and what they might be interested in playing in the bottom slots to try to help organize the chaos. But people mostly went off the wiki and the "pitch-your-game-right-before-the-session-starts thing."

    I've never really been a con guy. I've been to Gamestorm four times, GPNW three times and Dreamation twice. That's it. So I don't/didn't know what the hell I was doing. I don't know if I'd go more or even less organized next time but I'll definitely give it more thought and get some input from others.

    I was inspired by a birthday mini-con that Johnzo had done in the spring up in Seattle and wanted to do something similar. My concept was a Story Games version of the very early days of Alan Moon's "Gathering of Friends" (minus the part where the organizer is a brilliant, prolific game designer). At the few cons I've been to, and on the forums here, some complain they don't get to spend enough time just talking to friends. I wanted to create an intimate environment where the gaming schedule was really relaxed, people mostly knew each other (but not entirely!) and folks could spend a long time talking between games.

    I invited people from Vancouver B.C., Seattle, Olympia, Eugene, S.F. and Portland that I knew and had either gamed with a few times or that I knew and had never gotten to game with. The turnout wasn't what I hoped or had room for but I still had a wonderful time and want to try it again. I'd probably put it earlier in May if I were try it again next year. Further from the new Fabricated Realities, which I think I was competing with for regional folks free weekends, but still sufficiently distant from Gamestorm.

    \n After starting this thread, I haven't even had a chance to chime in on how awesome all the games were. I was flat-out three for three:

    City of Lepers / On Mighty Thews -- Admiral Idir & Kor-Gozar destroying the City of Lepers and reaping their vengeance (with a little help from Laten the Wise) was a fine time. I mean c'mon, who doesn't want to purge a city of arrogant, bureaucratic lepers? It's automatically on my to-do list. We spun around a bit with the rules, but roleplaying the polite but less-than heroic rogue opposite the two booming heroes was sweet.


    Capes & Cthulu / Microscope -- Saturday night I played an awesome Microscope game with Alex, Joel and Mike. We start off with the standard "superheroes undermine the rule of law" history seed. We have no idea it's going to emerge that not only did all superheroes get their powers from the same mysterious source, but that same source is the entity periodically controlling the minds of supers and making them do terrible things. Like destroying Europe.

    Oh, and that mysterious, unseen entity lurks sunken in depths of the Pacific, Cthulhu-style. Where's your due process now?

    Awesome game all around, even when Cthulhu wasn't involved. Lots and lots of great scenes and characters.


    Art for the Star / Shock -- It was a sleepy Sunday when Joel, Jackson, Alex and I sat down to play, but despite driving the con entirely over the time limit by about a zillion hours we rocked our issues and made some scathing sci fi. Our issues were Need for Faith, Legality of Art, Privilege and Plagiarism, and we came up with a Shock of humanity finding a strange star that some people could commune with, but only when in the right mental state, which turned out to be letting yourself go and making abstract art. Unfortunately this growing cult of "star-loving artists" is interfering with the master plan to study the star's energy patterns and build duplicates in other star systems. Damn hippies!

    For speed we only did two protagonists (so each player was either a protagonist or antagonist, not both) and we had some great domino bits, with one protagonist's story creating consequences for the other -- putting the artist/believers in camps, Jackson's protagonist getting burned by the luddite mob which spurred the jihad or, y'know, killing 90% of the world population with lethal solar flares.

    Joel's protagonist was about the Need for Faith, and I have to admit, I pounded on that issue like a 5 lb hammer in a metric screw world. Bitter domestic arguments over her new-found beliefs (and new-found friends), riot police cracking down on the artist-believers, the terrified survivors of the genocidal solar flares doubling-down on fanaticism and fear, spinning all the way up to a stellar jihad. Accept the love of the Blessed Star or burn. Joel rolled the dice to stop the jihad (three times actually) but no luck. Tamar had to console herself that all the evils done in the star's name didn't reflect the real love it wanted to share.

    Also, Alex as the rogue scientist Dr. Baths: lovely. So sorry he only lived for one scene, because he was a great conversationalist.
