Business Cards for your Game? (At GenCon?)

DevP
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games

(I won't have a game ready; maybe you will?)

I had the idea of making a small take-home slip of paper, perhaps about business card sized, with the logo for your game on it, obligatory description, and some neat flavor text. If someone wasn't ready to buy the game then and there, they could take the card with them. (Or, if you sold out, or your game is only in PDF format, or not on the shelf, you can still have these cards with you.) This could help a unsure buyer make the purchase later (even if they're not ready at the con). The downside is that LOTS of people will just pick up business cards with only the attention of "collecting" them... but then I guess that's neat too.

    May 2006
    I'd actually recommend against this. A business card will just get lost, and your "click-through" rate will be negligible -- for every hundred cards you give out, I expect you'd get less than one sale. If it costs you a couple bucks for a hundred cards and you make a couple bucks off a single sale... you can see where I'm going with this.

    However, there are ways around this and better ways to do this. The first is what Jason proposes -- ie, he already has these cards lying around and all it's going to cost him is some hand-stamping time. Also, handing potential customers an artifact from the game rather than an advertisement will actually work better -- we're all very practiced at ignoring advertisements, these days.

    Spending a little more money, you can create a mini game or micro game that prints up on something hand-sized that can be given out to people. If it's a functional thing, they'll be more likely to keep it around, maybe try it out -- even if they never try it, they will be thinking about how they might try it. That's why the artifacts work better than advertisements. You'll think about a game card and what else it entails about the whole product; you'll actively dismiss an advertisement from your headspace. Fireborn did this, although they went a little overboard and did a full-color poster thingy which probably cost more than was cost-effective.

    The other thing that anybody with a product at Con should be doing is using character sheets with your freaking URL printed on them. The one thing that a potential customer is most likely to take away from any gaming table is their character sheet, and if your demo is any good, that character sheet will be invested with memories of entertaining play. If it also says on that sheet where to get the game, you're golden.
