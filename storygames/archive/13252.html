    \n\nOctober 2010 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n More. 1-2 game sessions per week. Three years ago, it was about one game every month or two..

    Three reasons/games for the increase are:

    1) We managed to figure out how to get our normal (very casual) gaming group to play an actual campaign. It's been a bit of an issue to commit to long-run gaming lately (mostly because we have a sad history of campaigns "fading out"). But seems like committing to games in small pieces is easier for us than to say "sure, I'll come play in your game for an undefined time" - so we've agreed to play well-defined story arcs with limited number of game sessions each and people only promise to be aboard for the one we're playing. At the end of the arc we asses if people still have time and enthusiasm for the next one and those that do agree to participate in the next X game sessions, and we repeat.

    2) Started playing games with completely new group outside my regular one. Friends of friends and people I haven't really been hanging around with for some years. New people = more gaming

    and most importantly 3) In May I finally started my three year project to run my alternate history campaign "Century" once every 10 days.

    It'll go on for a total of 101 game sessions. Each session is set in a different year (starting from 1912, ending in 2012, next session is 1934). 2-4 players per game, chosen from a pool of (currently) 30 people, using the formula "who wants to play that day and hasn't been playing in a while" - so-far it's working like a charm. One game got postponed 4 days because I was in bedrest, but the next one was played according to the original schedule.

    Century has proven to be a very effective way to get around the "people are adults and don't have time to play like they used to" -problem - There is no pressure to commit to the game for any longer than just the one game session - each session is self-contained with a beginning, middle and an end. People are allowed to cancel at last minute if something comes up, since there is a "reserve" of players that can be called if needed so those with unpredictable lives can muster up the courage to sign up. The once-every-10-days rotation means that the day of the week the game is set keeps changing, so people with "I have choir every Monday, Thursday and Friday" types of problems can still participate in some of the games. etc. To be honest, a lot of it has been designed the way it is because three years ago, it was impossible to keep a game going because of people and their scheduling problems.
