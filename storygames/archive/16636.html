\n Ran my 7yo. daughter on her first RPG. At her request the game is Psi-Run!

End of the session, 20 year old shapechanger (and princess) Alisa has answered all her Questions after a harrowing chase taking place almost entirely on Liberty Island in New York Harbor. She knows she was kidnapped from her parents - the royal family of some obscure European country - at a young age. Her father died, her mother may still be alive somewhere. Now it's time to choose the Crossroads from the list, player gets first say.

Without hesitation, my daughter chooses 'Makes a discovery' and narrates...

"The leader of the Chasers is my brother, but he lost his memory just like me. He doesn't remember me or our parents."

We end the game with Alisa escaping on the ferry back to New York, looking back on the confounded Chasers on the dock and recognizing their leader as they recede into the distance...

    \n Soft Horizon play test
    \n
    \nWitch attacks me (Cap'n Finn, a pixie diplomat) with Domination: she's trying to control me and my platoon of human soldiers through her sorcery. My Domination sucks so rather than play in that context I use my Knowledge to shift to Warfare and have my platoon flank and capture her. I spend a lot to succeed since Knowledge is not my best skill either but I succeed, though racking up some Essence hits. My mind is foggy and some of my men are bordering on mutinous.
    \n
    \nNext round is Warfare, which I still suck at and she is okay at. But I didn't really want to stay in this context anyway -- the context I wanted was Diplomacy, it's just that the path from Domination to Diplomacy was hard for me (Subterfuge; not good at it). Anyway I have more dice now and can succeed more cheaply with what I really want, which is to use Knowledge again which is the path from Warfare to Diplomacy. Diplomacy I am good at. I win that one and my men corner her, forcing her to listen to what I have to say.
    \n
    \nThing is, Diplomacy is the witch's denial -- she can't do it at all. I was betting on that and fairly easily win the Diplomacy fight, playing my allies (powerful gods) against hers (a relatively obscure and weak god). She decides that discretion is the better part of valour and that her god would be pretty pissed if someone brought Greater Powers into this. She flees. Witch removed from kingdom sans bloodshed.\ufeff

    \n Ran an Archipelago hack called A Grail Epoch, and we did something fun with Destiny: since we had three players, each would contribute half of the third guy's destiny.

    One of my players was a Dragon-Elven Battle Mage. For his Destiny, I wrote, "... he watches as the tears in the vial turn to blood." So the other player wrote the first half of the sentence: "As meteors sent by the gods themselves rain down..." And lo, between the two of us, the third player's Destiny was written.

    During the last scene, one player described a volcanic eruption during the climactic battle. And then we all realized, at pretty much exactly the same time, that it was going to fit perfectly. Sure enough, another player narrated how the ash thickened in the sky over this wintry realm, and became huge chunks of stone that fell, like meteors, to the ground, just as the Battle Mage captured the bloody tears of the undead dragon in his vial. Destiny fulfilled. Really wonderful how it all came together.

    Running the game with dice was fun, but the minis added a whole new level of insanity: A Grail Epoch.

    \n FIASCO IN VEGAS, BABY!

    I'm 'Tippler the Intoxicating', up-and-coing magician whose shtick is a Dean Martin/rat pack/Mad Men 60's retro-chic drinking and smoking thing.

    My manager is a former exotic dancer whose managed to work her way off the pole and is getting ready for me to make my big break; there's an HBO special being talked about, and Teller is supposed to show up to watch at some point.

    The problem is that I can't seem to stay out of the clink because I hit the sauce a bit too hard sometimes and get tossed in there to cool off. There's a guy, Jimmy, I've gotten to know because he's in there for misdemeanor grift about as often as I'm in there on D&D. It's weird, because his twin brother is this hot-shit plastic surgeon whose mug we all see on billboards all over town, and the doc's got kind of a thing for my Manager.

    Long story short, losing my lucky Dean Martin cuff links sends me on a bender, the doc starts to get super-weird, Sarah has second thoughts, and its all Jimmy can do to help me with this Russian hooker who's nodded off in my bathtub. The gig goes... badly. Teller walks out, I take a tumble off the stage and end up in the hospital, where the doc finally snaps and tries to get Sarah to pose for pics at gunpoint; it turns out he's remade his former plain Jane of a wife into the spitting image of Sarah, and just needs a couple more reference shots for her to be 'perfect'.

    So, yeah, basically, it was a case of magnificent self-destruction, with a side dish of confusion, followed by pain. Sarah ended up back in a g-string and pasties, the doc was comatose when his brother drove the car into that billboard truck and walked away with his ID. For my part, taking the cash the doc stole from those drug dealers to try and bribe Sarah and spending it around town? Not my best career move, as witness my corpse, throat cut, tossed in this shallow grave about an hour drive out of town...

      \n I ran A Grail Epoch last night -- it's my dice-and-minis hack of Archipelago. If you haven't played Archipelago, it's a story-driven game in which you draw a map with various locations on it, and during play, you mark down which characters are in which locations, and you write down any in-game changes to the map as you play.

      In A Grail Epoch, you start with 12 locations, connected by 9 lines, and you place miniatures on the map's locations. When it's your turn to set a scene, you move three miniatures (one of which must be your character) up to two locations (along the drawn lines), and then you tell the other players who's playing which characters, and off you go.

      This time around, out of the twelve locations on the map, three were 'locked' -- we couldn't reach them until a character had died (one death per location). Then, we also had new connections: for each character who died after that, we could connect two previously unconnected locations.

      The scenario played out on a cruise ship in the Bermuda Triangle. A golden zeppelin hovered above the ship, and smoke billowed from a volcano on a nearby island. And all the characters had amnesia.

      Player 1 had played the game before, but Player 2 was new to it. Here's what they had to say:

      PLAYER 1

      "I had no idea how we were going to tie mysterious black totems, glowing babies, injectable goo, and zeppelins together, but it all worked out perfectly in the end."

      PLAYER 2

      "My overall favorite moment of the night was Raf's earnest inquiry about the death of an NPC.\xa0He was logically enforcing the rules of the game, but it was pretty funny in context.

      Jim:\xa0'A single beam of light erupts from the Black Totem into the space just vacated by Murphy.\xa0Murphy's fall has saved him... but Jenkins isn't so lucky.\xa0A gaping hole -- roughly the size of a basketball -- has been seared through the entire middle of her chest mass. She pauses for a brief second of wide-eyed horror before crumpling upon the deck.'

      Raf absorbs this scenario and then queries:\xa0'Does she survive?'

      Jim nearly sprays Matt with his sip of coffee.

      And THIS is what was so awesome about this game.\xa0In my mind, according to the narrative above, there was no WAY that Jenkins was surviving that blast. However, the rules of the game allowed for the possibility for her to stand up and dust herself off, along with some awesomely demented reason from Matt or Raf as to how this could have possibly occurred. Total creative freedom within a framework - awesome, awesome experience."
