\nPOST 1 OF 7
I've been aware of, and participating in, RPG discussion on the internet for about 5 years. During that time, lots of folks who care a lot about this discussion have lamented various status issues. I've never been quite sure what they're talking about. It seems to me that, on the internet, folks who've published games that other people like get taken more seriously, and folks who are funny and cause trouble get more attention. It's all kinda shrug-worthy to me, but it keeps coming up, so I feel like there must be things I'm missing.

So, I just had a thought about internet status yesterday, when Simon announced the release of his game here. I saw the thread title. I had four main reactions to it:

On Mighty Thews -- I recognize that name. I've heard it on the internet, but can't remember where. Why do I remember it? (Now I've got it! I think I looked at the cover on the glyphpress design critique forum a while back. I either gave feedback or at least thought about. Visual + interaction = remembered.)
I've already invested a little bit of caring about this game's success, by putting in the effort to stare at the cover, think about it, and try to provide constructive feedback. I want to see if the thing I tried to help succeed did in fact do so. It'd be especially cool if I saw evidence that I made a difference, though I don't expect that in this case.
"On Mighty Thews" is a funny and evocative title. Makes me think of the Promethea issue where badass Grace is talking about the drooling writers who wrote her stories in the 1920s, describing her "rippling thews".
Simon C:A) Simon's said a lot of stuff on the internet about RPGs that I found interesting, particularly about viewing non-Narr play through the lens of theme. So I'm interested in seeing his work.B) He's also been generally pleasant and constructive-leaning on the internet, so I'm interested in being supportive.C) He also isn't famous, so I probably can't just wait around to see what lots of other people think about this game from playing it; I have to go look myself.

    So I opened the thread. It doesn't take too much to get me to open a thread, but it doesn't take nothing either.

    I then read the game blurb (in bold), with the following reactions:

    Take your friends into the blood-soaked world of your own imaginations!
    Dammit, I know what a roleplaying game is! This pitch isn't aimed at me; is there one that is? Also, "blood-soaked" starts giving me an idea -- combined with the title, it has me thinking "slaughter the kobolds". I'm, uh, not really into that.

    Mighty warrior-heroes face mortal peril in a land of sword-fighting, strange cultures, monsters and magic. On Mighty Thews lets you and your friends tell bizzarre and bloody tales of swords and sorcery
    Ah. So, a lot like what tended to happen when I played D&D2 at age 13. I'm certainly not grabbed by this concept so far, as there's nothing new or challenging there, but the blurb is short enough that I'll keep reading.

    in a world of your own creation.
    I do like the world-creation part of various indie RPGs.

    Perfect for a casual and quick game, you can be playing within minutes of opening the book.
    I like trying new games, and if I can give one a worthy try in a single session, that's a boon for my group's logistics. So this has a leg up in making the long, long list of "games to try at some point". I don't pay money for that alone, though.

    Two other games that do exactly what's been described thus far also jump to mind: Swords Without Master and Final Hour of a Storied Age. So On Mighty Thews needs to distinguish itself ASAP...

    On Mighty Thews is a pulp fantasy roleplaying game that\u2019s fast and easy to play.
    Adventure Awaits!
    You already told me that.

    This is where I'd normally go do something else. Not because I have enough info to think I won't like the game, but just because there are tons of games out there that, based on what I already know about them, give me better odds. Plus there are free games.

    So, what keeps me reading, and following links? This takes me back to why I bothered in the first place (see numbered points from first post).

    1. Recognized the name. Nope, not sufficient.

    2. Invested in success. Yeah, that'd keep me around. This has in fact been the case for Final Hour of a Storied Age. For Thews, making one short comment on the cover means I haven't invested much, though.

    3. Good name. Nope, we're past that now.

    4. Simon. Yes! I'm still curious to see what this particular internet voice does with pulp fantasy roleplay.

    Having clicked into Onmightythews.com, I go, "Oh no! This site is not built to grab me with style or content!"

    Content: the only content on the home page is that same blurb I just read in the S-G thread!

    Style: the font for the game name is very well-chosen. The rest of the page is WordPress's blandest default theme. Nothing communicates the mood, atmosphere, or experience of the game. This site is purely utilitarian.

    The only color on the page is the blue text of the irrelevant right nav, so that's where my eye goes. Fortunately, I quickly recognize these as WordPress defaults, and look to the thin grey text of the top nav. Despite the tight spacing, it's easy to pick out the categories, because there's only 3 of them. Home, Links and Downloads, and News. Okay, so the only thing I'm going to get from this site is the downloads. So I click on that.

    Play Aids: This three-page pdf contains all the summaries from the main book, in a handy printable format.

    Not gonna tell me more about the game that I need to know at this stage. I know the sword & sorcery idiom well enough.

    The Tower of the Elephant: The full text of this classic sword and sorcery story is now online.

    \nPOST 5 OF 7
    The play aids look good! They are easy to read. The text hierarchies and layout are clear. Most of the text blocks are small enough to let me think that I can absorb this quickly going point by point.

    The first page, Making Characters and Starting Play seems like a logical place to start.

    Okay, characters get 6 descriptive traits assigned to various dice. They're powerful masters of their own fates. Write a name and description from there. Seems quick and easy. Nothing to get excited about yet.

    Starting the game looks similarly quick, easy, and generic. Make up some neat locations, then pick which of them to play in. "Poles of the world" is a neat concept, but I can't tell how that's being used here. Maybe I'll ask Simon later.

    Framing scenes, starting scenes, during scenes, ending scenes, tips: this is all very well conceived, articulated, and organized, but 100% of it is stuff that's "well, duh" for me. The goals, general techniques, and implied principles, are my defaults for "how to enjoy roleplaying". They aren't the only approach, but they're a fairly non-controversial one. Which has me thinking, "Cool, we're on the same page about the basics; now what are you offering me that I can't already do on my own?" I don't mean that to be vastly demanding, like, "Give me some experience I could never have otherwise!" I just mean I'm looking for what makes play unique, whether it be process, outcomes, tone, or whatever.

    I wonder, is this simply a game for people who don't yet know how to roleplay? Is it not for me?

    I move on to the next page, Dangerous and Competing Conflicts, and scan the section headers. Nothing jumps out to promise answers to my previous questions. This is where I feel the urge to give up and spend my internet time doing something easier and more fun. What stops me? Let's check in on the "why bother" list again:

    1. Recognized. Nope.

    2. Invested. Maybe. Barely. I wanted this game to be something cool. I still don't know that it isn't. But I'm losing faith that I'm going to see that is. At this point, just asking Simon a short question might be better than reading on. Or not, I dunno.

    3. Name. Nope.

    4. Simon. Yeah. Why would a guy with interesting thoughts about gaming create such a generic game? I'll read on...

    Play aids, page 2: Dangerous and Competing Conflicts.

    First section is Dangerous Conflicts. What's it say? Use when there's danger, set stakes, roll relevant dice, re-roll if you have a token you want to spend, get a fact or bonus to a future action. My first reaction is that it all sounds familiar. Solid, well-explained, but uninspiring.

    Next section is Injuring Players' Characters. I start reading and oh man is this basic, predictable stuff. Again I wonder if I'm the target audience. Now I skip past the tips to Competing Conflicts. Again, well-explained, but all "duh" material.

    My brain is starting to lose focus. I'm jumping around the page in hopes that something cool will leap out at me. The remaining sections' names look like they'll just be elaborations and special cases of what I've read.

    I'm tempted to go back and take a closer look at Dangerous Conflicts. Earning facts or re-rolls might be interesting. Was that explained? I don't think so. I should probably look again before I give up, but first I should check out the final page.

    At this point, there are two things keeping me going:

    1) Simon must have made something more interesting than this. Right? Right?! Give me a sign!

    2) Whatever I'd invested before, I have now invested more time in reading these two pages. I don't want to have gotten nothing out of this!

    That's the point at which I thought, "Hey, I wouldn't be doing this if Simon was a dick or a total stranger," and I decided to start this thread.

    Posted By: jenskotDave,

    - What were the last 3 RPGs you've bought? Why?
    \nLast few purchases:

    1) For a Polaris PDF, I exchanged a few emails with Ben, then spent a few hours culling through some AP, organizing it all into one post, and posting on two sites.

    2) For the How We Came to Live Here book, I exchanged a few emails with Brennan; organized, traveled to, and ran a one-shot of the game; wrote a review; sent a few more emails, and wrote another review.

    3) I bought the Burning Wheel core books (system + character burner) at a local retailer.

    4) I bought Dogs in the Vineyard at some con in 2008 or 2009.

    5) I bought The Shadow of Yesterday online from IPR in 2007.

    Why?

    I bought tSoY because, of all the games the Forge hyped as being brilliant, revolutionary, and complete in ways that trad games weren't, it sounded most compatible with my tastes in fiction. And it didn't sound as emo as Sorcerer. I was disappointed, and became skeptical of Forge recommendations.

    I bought DitV because the online chatter, accounts from friends, and back cover text all pointed to a laser-like focus on a certain goal for play. I think a podcast interview with Ron and Vincent gave me the sense that play was inherently, constantly filled with tough calls on violence, and I wanted to see how that could be made to be the case. This game also seemed like my best chance to find out what the hell Narrativism was.

    I bought Burning Wheel because, after playing tons of indie games that were purely made or broken by who was playing, I asked around about rules and structure that I could steal for my fantasy game, and a few folks (including one of the Evil Hat guys) suggested BW.

    Since then, I've become poor, decided that I hate the way most game texts are formatted, met more friends with games I can borrow, and realized that I already have access to more games than I'll ever play. Oh, and playtests of my stuff are more fun than most of 'em.

    I did the work for HWCTLH for many, many reasons:
    1) I knew what I was getting. I'd already played it. I knew there were parts I loved.
    2) What did I love? The color. The way the color is integrated into character creation and the way things are named. The relationship map. The 2 GMs.
    3) First indie game I ever played! As a playtest! At my first ever con! Soft spot for that.
    4) Huge invesment! I spent 10+ hours doing an editing pass over an early draft and sending my comments to Brennan. I felt that this effort went almost completely unappreciated, which left me slightly grumpy. So it's worth noting that feeling slighted by a creator isn't enough to deter me from their product (at least at the cost of work, rather than money).

    I did the work for Polaris because, after playing it, I thought maybe we'd missed out on an interesting relationship between mechanics and narration by doing it wrong. So I wanted to check. But I still haven't.

    Of these 5 games, HWCTLH is the only one I've brought to a group or played from my copy of the book. I've still never played tSoY (unless solar system hacks count).\n
    Posted By: jenskot- What are your 3 favorite published RPGs (free counts if it is published)? Why?
    \nI have no idea. Most of my best play experiences have been with unpublished games -- my games, and Land of Nodd. I can name you 70 games that I loved some part of, but 0 that I loved all parts of. I'll just go stream of consciousness here:

    1) Sign in Stranger. Narrating environmental sense data without meanings or explanations makes for truly unique experiences of (a) embodying a character, and (b) creating setting. I'm also free to focus on the fiction rather than being forced to frequently interact with formal cues (at least, that's how it used to be; I hope that hasn't changed!).

    2) Primetime Adventures. I like chatting with my groups about what we want to do, I like explicit models from other fiction, I like conflict resolution around character issues, and of all the games that do those things, this one does it with the least other crap getting in the way. I do hack it to set explicit stakes, though.

    3) FreeMarket or InSpectres. I love getting togther with friends, coming up with wacky ideas for projects, and pursuing them. I particularly love doing this in fictional realities, where I can be bold, risky and impractical. I love how both these games put me in a situation to do that. FreeMarket's intrusive challenge mechanic annoys me, while InSpectres' open situation leaves me worried about GM-reliance. I haven't played either enough to know which, if either, is a deal-breaker. This was a toss-up with Grey Ranks Montsegur 1244 Marvel Superheroes Cthulhu Stormbringer Swords Without Master Ganakagok Dogs in the Vineyard Vampire the Masquerade Apocalypse World 1001 Nights Your Mom.

      \nWhat On Mighty Thews does is not so much produce fiction that fits within the sword and sorcery genre, as it responds to fiction that does so. The rules assume a group that's already committed to making a sword and sorcery story, and then gives them a rules framework that makes that compelling and interesting - preserving the in-fiction conflicts of interest against the unity of interest of the group.

      The "d20 Trait" mechanic is a good example of this. Your character has a trait that represents a core duality within them. "Civilised" for example, or "Fatalistic". When you act according to that trait, you get a re-roll token at the end of each scene. When you act against that trait, you get to roll a d20 in a conflict. So the civilised barbarian, when pressed, can revert to savagery and gain the advantage. The doomed hero can, in the heat of battle, defy their fatalistic nature and triumph.

      Those things are possible without the d20 trait mechanic, but what the rule does is push that theme to the forefront - it makes us take notice of how the character is acting in terms of their thematic issue. The game responds to fiction that suits its core themes.

      \n I think the primer does this, but it also reminds a different group what is cool about S&S who might have forgotten.

      In thinking back on past purchases and non-purchases I think these are the questions I ask about a game. The more 'yes' answers the more likely I am to buy it.

      Niches:
      - Am I missing a game from my library that could do this?
      - Am I interested in the fiction this could create?
      - Do I like the mechanics?
      - Is there something cool or different in the mechanics?
      - Do the mechanics support the fiction?

      Status:
      - Have I liked other games by this designer?
      - Has someone I respect endorsed it?
      - Is this designer not one of the few people I think of as dicks?

      Marketing:
      - Have I heard of this before?
      - Can I find answers to the above questions easily?

      I've got examples for each of these, but a couple are:
      -\xa0I didn't think I'd like post-apocalyptic so I didn't buy AW straight away. When I saw the DW hack which filled a missing 'yes' I bought it straight away.

      This is a philosophical thought, but I was feeling philosophical when I started this thread, so I'm gonna indulge myself.

      So, judging from this thread, it seems that more people were drawn to On Mighty Thews by Swords and Sorcery than by Simon. Of the people who were drawn by Simon, most mentioned the fact that he's been pleasant and said something interesting on the internet.

      I want to emphasize interesting as opposed to smart.

      I think we are absolutely flooded with smart. We have too many smart people discussing and making games to even remember their smartness.

      Interesting, on the other hand, can be more distinctive, because it's a matter of taste. By expressing what we care about most passionately in gaming, and sharing our takes on that, and doing that over and over, we find like-minded folks.

      For example, I care about exploration-and-discovery play. I know that Gareth (contracycle on the Forge) does too. I know it because he posts about it a lot, and I've seen some of those posts, and interacted with some too. He's said enough stuff I already agreed with to show me we're on the same page, and enough stuff I hadn't thought of to make me want to hear more. If he designs a game, he's automatically got my attention. Likewise if he starts a thread on some exploratory play topic.

      I guess this all sounds pretty obvious, but in my eyes it's a good thing that doesn't get talked about much, so I just wanted to say it.
