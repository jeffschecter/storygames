\n The Sci-Fi channel just ran their mini-series which I'd wanted to see for Peter Krause, (of Six Feet Under fame) and for the Tim Powers/Unknown Armies vibe I'd gotten off of it.

And it was quite watchable. Did the whole 6 hours in two sittings, and it certainly had Krause (and I got over feeling like I was watching Nate Fischer, Police Detective) and it certainly bore a relation to Earthquake Weather and the Cardboard Palace. What it had that I didn't like so much, but that on reflection strikes me as very interesting is this: it's all about exploring the sci-fi premise of this weird pocket dimensiony thing and the consequences of it's existence.

Trying not to spoil the whole thing for others, but the idea is that there's this motel key, that will open any door with a pin-and-tumbler lock, and the door will open into a motel room, circa 1960. Upon leaving, if you fix a door in your mind, it'll open onto that door. Neat, very powerful little modern-magic item. And there's a bunch of everyday objects that originate in the room that are somehow promoted to being Objects, each with their own power and with unpredictable interactions. Oh, and there's this little feature that if you leave the room and come back, it's restored to exactly it's original state, and anything that's not an Object (like, say, your 8 year old daughter) disappears.

There's all kinds of internally consistent consequences that the miniseries expounds on. Watching it, I got this feeling I'd only ever gotten from Joss Whedon TV: that I was watching someone else's (edited) role playing transcripts, complete with planning/doing cycles, and ad hoc terminology applied once and rigorously maintained. The Lost Room even ends on this weird, shaky conclusion, like the GM was reaching to wrap things up for a particular session.

And, much as I usually hate to compare role play to other media, I'm sort of thinking that that weird feeling is the realization that The Lost Room, and Firefly (and Buffy for that matter) are simulationist shows. There's a creative agenda there, and it's about exploring the setting (in the case of Lost Room) or character (in the case of Joss Whedon's stuff.)

So I wonder, if that's a legitimate observation, can we glean anything from it towards actually supporting Sim? Or, by contrast with almost all other fiction, towards supporting Nar better?
