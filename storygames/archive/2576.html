I just thought of something. Bounced it off of gamers and nongamers I know, and many agree.

Gamism? The term makes sense. It's a game. You can win encounters. You try to win through tactical prowess. You powergame, but you are by no means means being a munchkin. You play to win, a very satisfying way of playing. Exalted is very Gamist in this way.

Simulationsim? The term makes sense. It simulates a fictional world. It tries to have depth and verisimilitude. You try not to stray far from an archtype of something in the world - samurai who follow Bushido, for example.

Narrativism? Doesn't make sense. The above two can easily create a narrative. Countless arguments about the theory stem from the old "But those are stories too!" chestnut.

The problem is not one of definition. Forge "Narrativism" at it's core is about adressing the themes characters bring up. Dogs in the Vineyard is considered Nar because it deals with the themes of faith and moral responsibility, although it is also very Gamist in the strategies you can employ to explore themes.

So, why not call this creative agenda Thematic?

Not all stories rely on theme heavily.

But all "narrativist games" are heavily Thematic.

Correct me if I'm wrong...

    After looking this stuff over, it looks to me like you are just splitting GNS CAs into more discrete groups. Which is fine, it will probably yield some fruit. I really like Ron's essays, so I use them in my designs and play reflections. But here is what I suggest if you want to break down CAs some more:

    Formerly Gamist:

    Challenging- players who prefer to explore the "Crunch" spoken of in Ron's Gamist Article (not the so called 'cunchyness' of a system)
    Chancey- players who prefer to explore the "Gamble" spoken of in Ron's Gamist Article

    Formerly Narrativist:

    Thematic- players who prefer to address or explore a premise, usually a moral question, during play

    Formerly Simulationist:

    Emulation- players who prefer to explore Setting above any other aspect of the game
    Experiential- players who prefer to explore the Situation in a game over any other aspect of the game
    Immersion- players who prefer to explore their Character in a game over any other aspect of the game
    Essentialism- players who prefer to explore the System of a game over any other aspect of the game
    Deatiling- players who prefer to explore Color (like weapons, gear, historical figures, religious pantheons etc) over any other aspect of the game

    Maybe that will help ya out

    --tropism: playing to embody and live out the tropes of an agreed genre. Corresponds to "emulation" in Troy's list.
    --gravityism: playing to explore the definitions and consequences of the natural laws of the game world as expressed in the rules. Corresponds to "essentialism" in Troy's list?
    --improvisationism: playing to create a three-dimensional character and act as them through a variety of interactions and situations, learning about them thereby. Corresponds to, uh, I dunno what. We called this improvisationism because the thought process you engage is very similar to long-form improv.

    I'm interested to hear where these fit into Troy's breakdown and if anybody can clarify what his categories actually mean in terms of behavior in play.

    \n What we're coming up with over at www.rpg.net is these:

    ACTING - play about creating and inhabiting three-dimensional characters.

    CELEBRATION - play that celebrates the tropes of a genre.

    CHALLENGE - play about overcoming difficulties through in-game resources and ingenuity.

    EXPLORATION - play about boldly going where no one has gone before. Play about exploring and interacting with an internally consistent world with minimal metagame intrusion.

    THEMATICS - play addressing moral issues through the rules of the game.

    \n I'm sure that somewhere there's somebody doing what Curly is saying is going on - so let's try to take his admonishment at face value. His other point is correct, however. Nothing wrong with taxonomies as long as you realize what a taxonomy can do, and what it can't.
    \n
    \nWhat's interesting is how Ron and most other folks into the theory got off of doing agenda taxonomies a long time ago. In fact that's the primary reason that the Forge Theory Forum got closed. People kept on wanting to rehash GNS when most of the theory had moved on to say, "the general taxonomies aren't as important as the techniques that people are using."
    \n
    \nAll of the attempts at redefining GNS are, therefore, kinda like Neitszche spending his whole life trying to disprove god - they really make the point that people can identify with the idea that there are different sorts of play, even if they think that there's something wrong with the "pigeonholing." The problem is that, as Shreyas points out, the taxonomies rarely apply perfectly as a description, and so they become of little direct use. Arguing what X taxonomy is precisely doesn't end up addressing things like the techniques being used, and how that affects the agenda (and issues of player dissonance in agenda, etc).
    \n
    \nI'm not saying that it isn't interesting or potentially useful to do the taxonomy thing. Just that it's incorrect to say that Forge theory holds that it's an end-all, when said theory moved beyond agenda taxonomies long ago.
    \n
    \n
    \nCurly, you have captured precisely why I don't just say "Thematic" but instead, "Play that primarily is intended to produce theme." Narrativism is much easier to write, but has the problems that Andy noted with it (despite, oddly, the root intentionally being chosen as narrative, and not narration). I don't suppose Thematism would stick? Or clarify that theme isn't missing from other modes?
    \n
    \nOn the subject of "ists" I think we get a bit touchy about that. I have no problem with somebody being labeled a Gamist. But what that must mean is that the player prefers gamism - not that he engages in gamism. I like play that could be ascribed to all three of the modes. If I'm engaging in gamism behavior, that doesn't make me a gamist neccessarily. But neither does it preclude the possibility that I am.
    \n
    \nThis is no different from the notion that a person who promotes Marxism is a Marxist. Just because somebody reads Marx, or even teaches his ideas doesn't make somebody a Marxist - they might also teach free-market capitalism. It's only when a person promotes or adheres only to an ism that you call them an ist.
    \n
    \nRon is a Narrativist. Oh, not as rabidly as some would paint him, perhaps - he's been known to play other ways. But he's definitely been a proponent for what he originally saw as the unsupported play style, the style he preferred.
    \n
    \nI'm somewhat Narrativist at this point as well. But my play proclivities are more about certain techniques than anything else. Explicit author stance for players, lots of "Director Stance Power" for players, but with strong GM control of events through metaplot (seems contradictory, but ask me about it some time). Non-explicit GM controlled stake-setting, except for cases that are extremely fraught with potential trouble. Etc, etc.
    \n
    \nAnd really that's just about my HQ game. In InSpectres, because of the rules, I use a different set of rules. I'm only a narrativist in that I either play games that support narrativism well, or slant less clear-cut games towards narrativism with technique.
    \n
    \nIn my own Beeg Horseshoe 2 model, I'm a medium fecundity (you should see Chris Weeks get all twisted in knots when I bring out any of the world canon), theme producing promoter. I don't think that there's anything wrong with stating your proclivities, as long as you keep an open mind about styles of play. I've also played some strictly gamism D&D; in the past year. So...
    \n
    \nSorry to go off on such a tangent, but I hope I've given some perspective to the conversation. Well, mine, at least and for what its worth.
