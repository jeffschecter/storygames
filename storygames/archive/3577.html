\nYou braved crocodiles, Nazis, necromancy, and devolved ape men to find the mythical city and the secrets it holds. You tested your skills and resolve to their limits, driven by a secret desire that motivates you to greater deeds just as it blinds you to possible pitfalls.

Now you have returned from your expedition and must report to your peers on your daring exploits, proving to the hardened adventurers, geniuses, and polymaths gathered before you that you are worthy of their respect in your every endeavor. You\xe2\u20ac\u2122ve gone into danger and survived driven by your desire\xe2\u20ac\u201dnow is the time to bring it to fruition.

I'm happy to announce that The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries is now available for pre-order. The Committee is my storytelling game of exploration and adventure in the vein of Raiders of the Lost Ark and the tales of Jules Verne. Think a crunchier riff on The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron Munchausen mixed with the pulps and adventure tales.

The Committee is playable in a single evening or over multiple sessions, and it allows you to tell tales of harrowing danger, daring deeds, and furious action with freeform narration and pulse-pounding timed conflict resolution. No pre-game preparation is required, so grab your hat, your whip, and your .45 and get going!

The game is $20 plus shipping and handling. All pre-orders will receive a PDF of the game shortly after ordering and will receive the book in August. If you've got any questions about the game, feel free to ask.

    \n Sophie,

    Tony Dowler ran a late-stage playtest that he talks about here, and he facilitated the Go Play NW game as well.

    Play is designed to be immersive, focused on telling the tale of an expedition in-character and using set phrases for many mechanics to avoid interrupting the flow. Narration during conflict resolution is limited to 3 minutes to keep the pace brisk and add tension. Whatever you describe your character doing happens - no whiffs or ineffective actions. Rather than a set GM, each character has another player acting as their opposition who frames hazards for them and then adds additional complications during resolution to keep things lively.

    I'd be happy to answer any additional questions. Anyone who has played is welcome to jump in with their own impressions, too.

    Edit: You can also check out the playtest reports Mike Sands provided me to see the kind of content that the game can produce.

    \n Sophie,

    Yes, I did make changes to address Mike's feedback. For example, group hazards now increase in difficulty over the course of the expedition to produce a climactic final scene and ramp up tension. The difficulty spread for character hazards also ramps up at the various stages of the story so that the easy hazards only appear at the beginning as everyone gets familiar with the rules and their character, and the expedition site only sees more difficult hazards that test the mettle of the heroes. My playtesters all gave me great feedback that I took very seriously in finalizing the game.

    The game is a 120 page 6x9 book, color cover and black and white interior. The vibe of the layout is a contrast between pulp-related art and the trappings of the Committee's genteel well-mannered atmoshpere.     \n Guy,

    One of the things that bothered me a bit about The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron Munchausen (as brilliant as it is) is that it is almost entirely unstructured improvisation, which can be really tough for some players. The Committee also features improvisation, in that you can describe your character overcoming a hazard however you like, but there is a lot more structure to help you along.

    At the scene level, you're using particular attributes and descriptors to get the job done, so these guide your narration. You can spend currency to modify the dice you're using to meet the hazard's difficulty, but each type of modification (rerolling, getting more dice, etc.) requires a certain type of fictional content in your narration. After you put forward one of your dice toward the goal of meeting the difficulty, your Opposition adds a complication to the situation, giving you further things to riff on in your next bit of description. Plus, the three-minute time limit keeps you moving and helps avoid overthinking things.

    At the macro level, prior to play everyone brainstorms where the expedition is going, what they hope to find there, and what kind of things they may encounter along the way. So everyone is already on the same page before any improvisation starts. During play, there is an expedition log where everyone writes down story elements they want to see in the story again. Players get a currency reward for reusing story elements created by other players, so you're mechanically encouraged to use the expedition log to guide future narration. Once you've got several story elements on the log and the notes taken during brainstorming, you've got flags galore to inspire future scenes.

    The end result is hopefully a game with improvisation but enough structural support so no one is ever left flailing about.

    Does that help, Guy?
