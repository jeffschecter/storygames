\n The constant stream of very interesting settings/set pieces for this classic game keeps my interest in it high.

However...I've never actually run a game of it!

Can people with experience with Mortal Coil talk to me about a few things?

1 - Long term play. It seems very well suited to long term play, as the building up of a detailed magical "system" really only can occur over many sessions. Does this create a viable "setting bible" for magic or do people tend to forget about earlier facts when they're not right in their faces?

2 - Shifting characters. I like the character statements that are the building blocks of the character and play. How did your characters change over time, can you give some specific examples?

3 - Player screens. Craziness, you all reveal at once?! Did you use player screens or just your hands or just trusted each other not to look?

And tell me all about how great your Mortal Coil games have been, with special attention to games that went 30 sessions or more.

    \n I loved the game on paper. And I loved the collaborative world-creation session we had before starting play -- we created a "Twin Peaks"-like setting, bored psychic teenagers in an isolated town in Maine where Something Bad is happening in the woods.

    What I found in play, though, is that the diceless token bidding was complex enough to derail the sense of immersion in the game. I expect any kind of combat/conflict system to shift my mindset from narrative to game; but usually it's a matter of making a quick decision about what skills/aspects to use, working out a target number, and rolling dice. In MC I ran into analysis-paralysis trying to compute how many tokens my opponents were likely to bid, what kind of margin I needed to succeed, and how many I could afford to sacrifice. It was quite difficult and became not-fun \u2014 kind of like splitting the bill at a sushi bar when everyone's ordered different items.

    I think part of this is purely psychological \u2014 when I know that the outcome is ultimately in the hands of a randomly-rolling bit of plastic, it excuses me from trying to work out the perfect choice of action. But when it's just me against the GM, even though I can't predict his actions much better than I can a die, it feels less random and more like pure strategy; Go instead of craps, say. And I like Go, but it's totally the opposite of a roleplaying game for me, and the two didn't mix. Of course, that might just be me. Other people demonstrably like the game a lot.

    (The same criticism might apply to other diceless systems like Amber; but I haven't tried any of those.)

    \n Hey snej: I haven't played Mortal Coil enough to know if this will be a thing for me, but I definitely - DEFINITELY - understand where you're coming from 100%, I have the same thing.

    I don't think the criticism with Amber will apply: The system is so simple, so transparent ("high number wins! unless you spend enough time doing plots-within-plots for why they wouldn't!") that it can't break immersion.

    However, games with let's call it "a high degree of handling", especially in conflicts that require lots of "hooyas/pieces/bits" to be on the table and shuffled around and can take a while to resolve. FATE is aaaaaaaalllllmost on the edge of that blade for me. While I like Nine Worlds on paper, for example, all the card handling really stabs roleplaying in the face as we narrow our eyes and play The Card Game Inside of Nine Worlds. Whereas Dust Devils, which also uses cards, has a much, much faster resolution (one draw) and thus immersion is preserved since it doesn't jump off into fiddly bit land.

    Not sure a longer campaign of MC would do that for me (everyone's threshold is different), but I totally get where you're coming from.

    \n Seth & Jason, do you have the revised edition rules? I'm just curious, because I did add in some rules for conflicts not against a person in there. Also, I have been able to run multiple-person conflicts using the system, but they do get complicated as you say. You need to stay really clear on what each person's overall goal is, and what the goal is for each individual action during the conflict. I've done conflicts with up to six different characters all trying to get different things, but that required a lot of concentration from me as a GM and took a bit of time to work through.

    That said, these are all legit criticisms of the system and some problems I've run into myself in the past. I don't think the rules are great for every group. I've got a lot of system familiarity, which helps when I'm running it, and some groups seem to have no problems. One thing that I think can throw groups for a loop is the fact that the game requires a strong GM hand, which combined with the high degree of player input can be confusing.

    \n Hey, so I'm back at this again now that I got my print copy of Revised, and the campaign frame collection, WOOT WOOT

    I'm trying to understand the helping rules.

    Let's use three of my NPCs from various magic games to demonstrate:

    Theseus Munsey, half-fae musical theater impresario, has teamed up with Harold, angel-channeling vampire, to battle Sierra Carlisle, hippie sorceress. It's good natured roughhousing.

    Everyone secretly allocates:

    Theseus has 2 in Force, he allocates 3 tokens and chooses to battle Sierra with
    Harold has 3 in Force, he allocates 2 tokens and chooses to help Theseus battle Sierra
    Sierra has 2 in Grace, and chooses to defend herself with 5 tokens.

    (Let's pretend nobody has applicable aptitudes and any other actions are resolves separately)

    Harold is helping, so we resolve his attempt to help first. 3+2 = 5 < 2+5 = 7, he fails to help.

    Let's say the same situation happens and Harold allocates 5 tokens. 3+5 = 8 > 2+5 = 7, he helps. Theseus gets a +2, taking him from a clear failure (-2) to a near success (0).

    But if Harold had just targeted Sierra directly, he would have gotten a +1, a success.

    Sierra is better off because Harold decided to help his friend.

    It seems like helping only actually helps if both the helper and the helped are both going to beat the target (this increases the margin of victory by 2 and pushes the degree of Harm (for example) into a new category.)

    If a helper couldn't overcome the target on their own, they are better off either switching to a different realm of conflict (intimidation, magic) for their own action. Or, now that I think of it, to help in a different way than direct assistance.

    Is that right?
