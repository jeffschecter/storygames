\n A year or so ago, I joined (and somewhat instigated) a design group called Splitting the Party. The purpose of that group was for a bunch of us to design in public and help each other out and whatnot. It never really got off the ground (I'm definitely at fault for that, since my game tanked on the first playtest and was not salvageable), but I still like the idea.

So I turn to you, SGers. I want to create a group of designers that will give extensive feedback and support each other. The design in public bit I don't care too much about and neither should you, if it's a problem.
I'd like this to model writing workshops, so things like having to submit material and give feedback once a month should be included (to push each other on and stop being lazy). Furthermore, here is what I propose:

1. You have to be actively working on a game that you plan to publish (how you publish doesn't matter).
2. Feedback for submitted materials once a month!
3. Groups should be created out of similar games or similar systems or some kind of similarity, as to help feedback.
4. Playtesting of other member's games if possible.

I have some more ''rules'' in mind, but they can wait.
This isn't a recruitment thread per se, if there's enough interest I'd rather that people whisper to each other and set up their own groups.
Personally, I'm working on a horror game called Dark Days and I'm interested in helping others with horror/dark fantasy games.

    \nI'm currently actively working on Mage Blade. Mage Blade is a fantasy adventure game with a fairly traditional set-up, with a GM and players. The resolution system is designed to model the characters' abilities, and requires a small amount of tactical consideration. It is intended for closed-form campaign play, with a pre-outlined end-game, with pacing defined by how hard the players push toward it, and how often they wish to sideline. This is based on players setting goals for their characters, which gives them a lot of directional authority for the story, because the GM is obligated to offer them the opportunities to overcome adversity in pursuit of these goals.

    \n I gave it some more thought and decided to add the ''rules'' I came up with. These don't have to apply to every group, but it applies to the one I want to be in.
    I'm posting this here because it may turn someone off and turn someone on to this group. I don't want to trick anyone into it.

    1. Relaxed Enviroment: Although there are a few core rules, I really don't want to have too many and I definitely don't want to stress anybody out. Still, you should be relatively serious about game design, otherwise you won't get your money's worth out of the group.

    2. Feedback: Like in fiction workshops, everyone has to give feedback and critique. This means dedicating time to go through the material and writing a few paragraphs of helpful critique, to the best of your abilities.
    When someone posts a message, he should also tell us what he needs help with, as to help us provide good feedback.
    He should also provide a timeframe like ''Guys, I'm attaching these 2 pages of my game, you have 2 weeks to provide feedback''. I think this is key to make sure we don't get lazy and just let messages lie dead in the group.

    3. Don't post your whole game: You can post up to 3 different parts of your game (or ideas, or problems or whatever) at the same time. We're dedicated, not insane. If you post too much material, we won't have time to give you good feedback.

    Posted By: northerainblogs are public
    Not necessarily--you can set a blog post to be viewable by Members only and require Registration, just like a user group. Best thing about a Wordpress blog is version control--it maintains a History of all updates to a post (and page).
