    \n Mark, can I assume that three of a kind, four of a kind and so on counts as a "pair"?

    If so, it's easiest to calculate the probability you don't get a pair (or three of a kind, or four of a kind, etc). So it's the probability all the cards you draw have different ranks.

    For two cards

    You draw your first card. It could be anything. Now there's 51 cards left in the deck and only three will make a pair

    You draw your second card.

    Probability it's not a pair: 48/51.

    For three cards

    You draw your first card. It could be anything. Now there's 51 cards left in the deck and only three will make a pair.

    You draw your second card. Now there's 50 cards left in the deck and six of them will make a pair with either of the first two cards.

    You draw your third card.

    Probability there's not a pair anywhere within the three: (48/51) * (44/50)

    For four cards

    You draw your first card. It could be anything. Now there's 51 cards left in the deck and only three will make a pair.

    You draw your second card. Now there's 50 cards left in the deck and six of them will make a pair with either of the first two cards.

    You draw your third card. Now there's 49 cards left in the deck and nine of them make a pair with any of the first three cards.

    You draw your fourth card.

    Probability there's not a pair anywhere within the four: (48/51) * (44/50) * (40/49)

    For n cards

    It's a product, from 0 to n-1, of (52 - 4*n) / (52 - n).

    Probabilities of pairs

    It's 1 minus that product.

    So...

    2 cards: 6%
    3 cards: 17%
    4 cards: 32%
    5 cards: 49%
    6 cards: 65%
    7 cards: 79%
    8 cards: 89%
    9 cards: 95%
    10 cards: 98%
    11 cards: 99%
    12 cards: 99.9%
    13 cards: 100%

    Usual disclaimers about correcting me if I'm wrong apply.

    Graham
