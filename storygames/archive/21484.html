\n I could use some help developing a fantasy setting.

Basically take the ending of Cabin in the Woods, where giant evil gods have awoken and are (along with other monsters) are destroying the earth. In response, the military or secret government project\u2014like in The Mist, open a rift and the Elder Things come through battling the Evil Gods. The world is nearly destroyed. Supernatural disasters uproot/alter land masses and oceans.

A small segment of humanity survives. They flew in to a high valley west of an impassable mountain range. Think James Hilton\u2019s Lost Horizon. This is a pristine and wild place. The world as they know it is gone. Perhaps far to the west, there may be remnants of the destruction\u2014but is unreachable for now.

So you\u2019ve got a modern population of roughly 15K that have managed to survive\u2014nearly equally divided between women and men (25% under 10, 50% aged 10-50, 25% over 50). The survivors escaped with their lives and what personal/survival items they could quickly grab. Aircraft would have some cargo\u2014MREs, small arms, medicines. Every computer, tablet, and phone is unusable. Only older aircraft\u2014those without sophisticated electronics were able to fly in. Some people could use these as shelters\u2014at least at the beginning.

No major pandemics burn through the population. They live a very fertile area, mild climate, and plenty of water. Wildlife is plentiful and there are no supernatural predators roaming around. There is a range of unpassable Himalayan-sized mountains surrounding the survivors on three sides (North, East, and South), habitable lands stretch westward.

The warring gods and monsters seem to have killed themselves off. The rebuilding begins. The setting will take place sometime after this, but I need to get the origin set up.

So I\u2019d love to get some help filling in some details.

    vgunn said:

    I\u2019m not sure how long it would take to get to a million people. At that point an event occurs.

    \n15 000 people to start with, as we remember, in a reasonably normal distribution.

    If they settle on a pre-industrial lifestyle of some sort (big families, shorter life-spans, younger marriages) after a modest amount of start-up difficulties, then I'd say it's going to be about 100 years of struggling (ups and downs as social upheaval and adapting to environment take toll), followed by a stable 100 years of growth, after which they're roughly at 1 million people, assuming no large catastrophes. The growth would continue at a rather fast pace until natural resources cause some constraint; to find out roughly where the population caps out naturally, figure out the primary mode of subsistence and the total amount of resources (probably land) available for it.

    If they retain a modern social order (small families, longer life-spans, a lot of education to make progeny self-sustaining, somewhat later marriages), then it'll take about 500 years to achieve a million people. This latter number is much more arbitrary than the former; even small changes in social customs and such have a big impact on average family size, which in turn has great impact on the population growth rate when death is more rare and lives are longer. Even moderately natalistic policies (rather reasonable in that situation) could take that down to e.g. 200 years.

    \n One thought that occurred me is: with our contemporary world just a few centuries in the background, the standard weaponry technology (a fairly basic gameplay consideration in dungeon/hexcrawl) is probably more like rifles & revolvers than the usual sword & chainmail or rapier & platemail level of most D&D fantasy. My rationale for that is, once the engineering principles of basic firearms are known, such weapons aren't actually any more challenging to manufacture than the best alternatives - plus, people in your setting are more likely to have preserved (albeit crudely) the manufacturing process for firearms than to have re-discovered the (already near-forgotten) processes for manufacturing Renaissance weaponry.

    \n If I was seriously modeling that sort of survival situation, I would be heavily biased towards assuming that a technological civilization (not necessarily industrialized) survives; it only takes a handful of intelligent and well-read writers in your initial 15 000, and enough paper or the equivalent during the first 20 years, to produce a codex or several that distill everything that you really need to know to figure out the rest yourself. Science would be much, much easier a second time around if had a set of hints and principles written down by somebody trying to be helpful. Even if that someone doesn't remember the exact ingredients for gunpowder, they'll remember enough to put you on the right track, and to remind and reassure you that it is possible. Vague generalities that we take for granted like "it's three or four semi-common chemicals, I think one of them's a major ingredient in feces; people used to mine it from bird droppings". That tidbit alone, combined with the basic principles of science and materialist world-view, should drop the time rediscovery takes from 5000 years to a hundred on the outside.

    On the other hand, the forgotten past is a striking genre trope, so I'd be fully on board with just saying that in the tumultous times of early survival much was lost and misunderstood, and consequently a New Earth arose from the ashes, a world of sword and sorcery.

    By the way, I'm reminded of the Anomalous Subsurface Environment. It's set in this type of D&D setting, a far-future that has forgotten science and discovered magic. The setting is very cool, well worth a recommendation.

    \n It's an intriguing premise. What I think you need sooner than later, rather than fleshing out the background to much, is to determine a situation to go with the setting.
    Some intriguing elements that could fit into that situation here are:
    --a small starting colony, which could have a strong Founder Effect -- maybe it's got some strange or cool cultural or even biological traits based on who the survivors were and who the first generation of leaders were.
    --A starting base with a lot of resources, but not good preparation. It's not clear to me whether the refugees are going to scratch out a living from the ground or if they're going to rebuild their numbers and living standards relatively quickly. If you are going for the second option, Steven Stirling's novels Island in the Sea of Time and Dies the Fire are both going to be inspirational. Not great novels, but they talk a lot about how to rebuild from a semi-random starting position.
    --Based on historical examples of colonies, I could imagine that the humans in the valley could expand pretty rapidly and quickly get into both prosperity and some serious population pressure, which helps drive emigration and exploration.
    --A funny geographic setup where you have a rich starting base and then expansion only possible in one direction. That would allow the leaders of the initial settlement to have a lot of control over supply and travel to the frontier, and maybe extract a lot of resources from the colonists. Particularly if the settlement maintains a monopoly on good transportation (navigable rivers, the best road, a railroad?) That's a perfect setup for an American revolution-type setup.
