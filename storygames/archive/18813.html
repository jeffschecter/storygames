\n Hey there cats,

Here's something I've been thinking about.

Anima Prime's powers combo together so well that I've begun conceiving of "game effects working together in the most exciting way possible; in all of the ways their user wishes to apply them" as The Griffen Principle, named after AP's creator, Christian Griffen.

For example, if I have a spell that creates a slick surface and the spell is called "Magic Oilslick," I'm going to imagine that the slickness is also flammable, even though the spell's write-up doesn't expressly say so. A GM-driven game in line with The Griffen Principle would have some sort of support for reacting positively to the player who tries to light their magic oilslick on fire.

A different example would have a mechanical power where a bonus of "1" is gained if a particular user is on fire (let's say that they're also immune to fire's harmful effects). Can they jump in the way of someone else's fire spell being cast, adding themselves to the original target in order to enjoy the bonus of "1" later in the round? Could they instead have ended their previous turn rolling into the crackling fireplace to rest for a split second? Can they cast one of their fire spells on themselves? All yes; especially if these are player-generated ideas and not ideas fed by the game material or facilitator.

A third example shows that mechanics bounce off of one another even if completely divorced from the fiction. Let's say we're playing a fantasy deck-building rpg in which your character powers that are available to you each turn are jurisdicted by what cards you've drawn. Your character has a power that lets them draw a card whenever they take a point of damage. Sweet! Then you draw a card that says, when you draw this card, take two points of damage (and then there's some other effect too, when you play it) do you get to draw more cards now that you've taken the damage? Heck yeah! Griffen Principle.

Give me some more, weirder examples of this if you've got them. They don't have to be for games that exist yet.
Also "no your rong"s always welcome.
Thank you!

    \n I feel there is a big difference between inferred, logical interactions of actions that are in no way covered by rules, and explicit, rules based interactions of mechanical "cogs".

    Free-form stuff like AW relies heavily on common-sense and adjudication to cover implied or logical interactions that are not covered explicitly in the rules. Even if there are no rules about it, if it makes sense it happens. In Magic, interactions are explicit. Circle of Protection: Black protects you from Lord of the Pit because the rules state that Lord of the Pit is Black. The mechanics interact because somewhere (either on the card or in the rule book) there are rules that cause them to do so.

    In a hypothetical "freeform" Magic the Gathering, playing Claustrophobia on a creature would prevent it from attacking me if I had a Maze of Ith. That's not what the rules of Claustrophobia does, but story-wise, an NPC afflicted with a crippling curse of claustrophobia may rebel or freeze up if forced to journey into a cramped maze.

    Actually, a "freeform" M:tG would be kinda cool. No stats, mana cost or card text, just the names, art and flavor text.

    \nHowever, because the "cog" approach only works with rules-as-written, they are not open to interactions unless specifically noted. For example, what would happen if you shot a lightning bolt into the center of a lightning elemental? In Magic, because there are no rules that cover the element of spells, it has exactly the same effect as it would against a goblin, or if the spell had been an ice spell.

    To be fair, many of the interactions that happen are due to the creators of Magic making an effort to support a modicum of narrative and 'fluff', and are more than aware of the tropes of fantasy. However, there are many interactions there which work mechanically even if you have to jump through mental hoops to justify it within a common-sense narrative, and many things that "should" work if this was a story, rather than a game with strict rules. (Why are elementals susceptible to Fear and Terror, while a golem is not. Or for that matter, why are brigands, cut throats, rat-men and all manner of gutter scum completely fearless, simply because they're summoned by Black mana?)

    \n Board (/card) games like Magic provide specific, set interactions. The fun here can lie in:
    - discovering these interactions, especially when they were not intended by the creators (unlimited Splinter Twin copies etc.)
    - setting up your resources to pull off the interaction reliably/at the right time/by surprise/etc.
    The disadvantage is that the interactions are limited and predetermined.

    Fiction-based games can provide non-specific, emergent interactions based on interpretations of the fiction:
    - reincorporation of something another player contributed earlier
    - interaction based on a new interpretation
    - using the agreed-upon frame of reference ("realistic physics", "ritualistic magic with x principles") to make new connections
    The disadvantage is that the interactions are unreliable, subject to "approval" (moment of judgment), and can feel boring if all they give you is a static bonus.

    One great thing about RPGs is that they can combine both of these. One such approach is DnD 4e's Page 42. It translates fictional interactions into a mechanical framework. But the framework is itself pretty static.

    Another approach is Anima Prime's effect goals. These provide a variety of mechanical effects, but which ones apply are based on the fictional interactions.

    I think there's still a lot of room for innovation and game design in this combined space.
