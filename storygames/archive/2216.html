The Unloved Sub-Genre: Normal People, Fantasy World

Clinton
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
There's a particular genre of fantastic fiction that is usually given short shrift: the kind where people, usually kids, discover a secret world and go there and have adventures. The Lion, The Witch, and the Wardrobe is the classic example, although I particularly like Pamela Dean's The Secret Country, in which some kids find out that their imaginary world they made up in their play-stories (replete with character ownership, and an RPG in all but name) is real.

This sub-genre seems perfect for RPGs: you take yourself into a fantasy world and explore. Yet, outside of Multiverser, this hasn't been explored to my knowledge. In Googling for research on this post, I found this essay: "Pamela Dean's The Secret Country and Authorial Creation" and it's well-applicable to game design.

My point for discussion: why is this such an unexplored area of fictional content in role-playing games? A follow-up: when we talk about making some sort of cross-over game (or if we're Ryan Dancey, "the emergence of a new game platform"), why are we not exploring this popular, if not critically acclaimed, natural fit to the entire concept of role-playing?

    Hmmm. Yeah, IIRC there was an article in Dragon magazine way back when that discussed the idea of doing the "Modern Folk (or You Yourself) get whisked to a fantasy world" thing. But yeah, I don't recall there ever being a game about it (there was the backstory of Castle Falkenstein which was 100% that, but never suggested that OTHER people could be play kinds of characters). As Fred suggested, you can always do it with another system, but I guess it might be interesting to explore what More could be done in a system or whatever to enhance that feel.

    There are some other central themes to a lot of those kinds of stories: That the main character, or one of them anyway, is the Chosen One: They were brought from far away by God or Province, and they were destined to save the world somehow. Twelve Kingdoms, the above Castle Falkenstein, etc. For this kind of thing, it might behoove the GM to set up a lot of stuff in advance: How the world works, which PC(s) is the Chosen One; How they discover this; possible repurcussions; Why they were chosen, etc. It's a lot of work, but maybe it can be distilled into a quick template or formula. I'm really interested in this, because in the next 1-2 years I want to run a long Twelve Kingdoms game, complete with one of the PCs being a Chosen One (and even the players not knowing which one it is). I'm really interested in hearing more thoughts on this tangent, because the Chosen One thing is a big sub-category of this "Normal People, Fantasy World" thing.

    As for your idea where you work on a game that itself proposes to be a device to travel between fantasy worlds, that's an interesting idea and I'm interested in seeing where you take it. Might want to give Castle Falkenstein a quick read, as it does kind of a similar thing: It professes to be the diary of the person who went to another world, and maybe you'll find cool tips or tricks with which to build what you're writing.

    If anything, though, I would actually go ahead and create a fantasy world for it. Maybe not do the "You can go anywhere and do anything" thing... or leave that option open, but yet still build a well-defined starting setting as the Default World. This will help you with having people jump right into the game with little trouble, which may be one of your goals (and also make suggestions that they can jump into LOTR, Harry Potter, Eragon, Neverending Story, Game of Thrones, etc if the players are familiar with those settings). But if you leave it at "go to whichever world you want to", it might be hard for some folks to start it up; that's a lot of work for the GM to do and the players to buy into (unless again they use an already developed fantasy world).

    Some things to consider when building such a fantasy world:
    * Make places for the PCs to fit in and do cool things. Make a few "Orders/Cults/Etc" like a Knight Order, or a Mage's Guild, so that PCs have a place to plug themselves in and go on wonderous adventures or do cool things quickly.
    * Write well how to play up the First Journey to the fantasy world, and define clearly (even if it's using random charts) how NPCs in that world will react to the PCs, like when they see them for the first time, and when they realize they come from another world. Also how the PCs can interact quickly with the Bigwigs of that setting and get set up into doing Big Things (and not spend 4 adventures simply sulking, living out of gutters, afraid to interact with anyone for fear of being locked up, etc).
    * "Chosen One(s)" Option: Y / N . If yes, figure out how the whole Chosen One thing works (maybe like CoS's Conspiracy Creation templates), how the PCs get picked, how the PCs find out about their fate, what to do to make the PCs disbelieve their Chosen One status (hey, it always happens), etc.
    * Imminent threats to get involved with. The world always seems in danger from without and within.

    BTW, Twelve Kingdoms the Anime has the most awesome Chosen One scenario: As Ben puts it, there's a Chosen One, and the Chosen One's companion. The companion is pretty much "one of us" (a fantasy geek) and FLIPS THE FUCK OUT when that person realizes that they are NOT the Chosen One. It's an awesome, humanistic take on that genre.

    Anyway, just some stuff to think about. I'm particularly interested in pursuing more how to do this now (before such a specifically-focused game gets developed in the next 1-2 years). Like, practical tips, templates and ideas, or side-rules (like inserting new abilities and meta-rules that can be added to ANY game) so that we could do it now, with any game system, but without leaving all the work with the GM (cause it would be a lot of work to do without simple templates, especially if there's the Chosen One stuff).

    -Andy

    January 2007
    There are a few concepts that are potentially getting confused here.

    1. The "I" game. That's a game where you stat up and play yourself. The first I can think of is "Villains & Vigilantes." And, yeah, it's an uncomfortable enough subject that in most play we did of the game, players made their characters using the rules for generating NPCs and did not play themselves in an alternate earth backdrop. Some few did, however, and often we'd have some of each in the same game.

    2. Playing "mundanes" thrown into a non-mundane world.

    Multiverser combines both of these ideas. But it's very possible to do either on their own, too.


    Now, why don't we see more of these? Well, let's take the segments individually. For "I" games, the uncomfortableness comes, I think, from the intimacy of it. First you have to stat up your character and try to be accurate about it. The problem here is that you can be easily accused of false modesty or of self-inflation. How does a GM step in and say, "Hmmm. 15 Int? I dunno, I think you're more like a 13 or so" (and he's actually thinking 12).

    Worse, once you start playing, you're going to basically be asked questions that come down to, "If you encountered a burning building with a child in the window, and the villain is getting away, which is more important to you?" To be playing "accurately" you have to give the truthful answer about yourself. Depending on where the GM goes with this stuff, you may have to reveal some deep things about yourself.

    Further, there's the fact that there's this oddity about the character not really being you. For example, does your character play RPGs like you do? Wouldn't that lead to a lot of self-referentiality like PCs saying, "Wow, this is just like as if we were in a RPG" (see the relevant section of Over the Edge). When does the game start in your timeline? I mean for you to be you, you'd have to have gone to the same school, met the same people, had the same experiences. In V&V, the problem is that you then say, "Oh, and it's all the same, except that superheroes and villains exist." Well, how would your life be the same in a world that different?

    Now, in an "Alice in Wonderland" situation you could say it's like Jumanji, or something, and that you all enter the game as you start playing the real world game. In which case, however, the self-referentiality becomes the centerpiece of the game, "Oh, wow, we started playing a RPG where we're ourselves, and now here we are in this other world. But how do we know we're real...I mean we could just be the characters in question." See Robert Heinlein's series of books on this subject (can't recall all the titles, but I think that "The Cat Who Walks Through Walls" is one of them).

    Not saying this can't be done, or couldn't be entertaining. Simply that it's all nothing like the source literature of Narnia.

    Oh, and then there's the "fictional fantasy" factor. That is, if you make up something about somebody else that none of you have met, well, it could be real, right? I mean as much as Santa could be real. If you say, "I did this" as the basis of your story, you know with as much certitude as possible that you did not. As much as RPGs are mythmaking, telling them about yourself is not as effective as telling them about fictional people, because the lie is more apparent. If you will.


    On the subject of point 2, why not play a normie thrown into an other world...well, simply, it's the power trip. When the main character in Gor comes to the world of Gor he comes not as himself, but as this super-powerful dude. Thomas Covenant comes across and becomes central to the entire fantasy world.

    I think that most players looking to play fantasy - possibly because they were first introduced to D&D - think of it as a chance to play a character who can do things that they, themselves can't. That doesn't just mean playing an alternate mundane character, but playing somebody who can employ magic, or be a great warrior, etc.

    Not kids from London.

    From a "simmy" POV, too, it's simply interesting to explore the idea of what it would be like to be from that other place. Sure, it's easier to do the transposition with a character, because player knowledge will match character knowledge about the world. But then you don't get to ask "What's it like being from a village in the Empire of the Petal Throne?" I had one player tell me that his entire reason for playing RPGs was to do precisely this sort of exploration.

    Which, again, is not to say that you couldn't have fun doing this. Little Fears shows, I think, that "mundane" avatarism can be fun. But it's a departure from what RPGs have been thought of as being for a long time.


    Neither of these are good objections, particularly, but they explain, I think, why we haven't seen more of this.

    Mike

    Mundanes into a faery-tale world, though ... isn't the advancing empowerment of the mundane usually the same story as his or her acceptance and understanding of the world that they've been thrown into?

    I mean ... it's fundamentally a coming-of-age story, paralleling the idea of juveniles being thrown into the arcane and baffling world of adult interaction. The development of the characters may be outwardly flagged by getting the magic sword or drinking the Entish brew, but what's really happening is that the character is maturing into the type of person for whom this strange new world is natural.

    That's hard to do in RPGs, for one simple reason: it's hard for us, as players, to remember what it's like to fear and resist the fantastic.

    As a GM I've introduced mundane characters into fantastic situations in RPGs many times, and what I have seen the vast majority of the time is that the players bend their character to the utmost conceivable limit in the interest of them becoming comfortable with the fantasy as soon as possible. They take any sensible (and quite a few stupid) short-cuts in order to skip over that journey and get to "the good stuff."

    It is a very rare player who, even with prompting, is able to see that journey itself as the good stuff.

    How do we encourage that? I don't really know.

    There was a game almost like this that was published in the mid-eighties in Germany: "Der Geheimbund des Schwarzen Auges", better known as "DSA Junior".

It took place in the same setting, Aventuria (though whatever was established in DSA Junior was never considered canon of Aventurian history and metaplot), and used a dumbed-down version of the rules.
    The players did not play themselves in a fantasy world, though. The characters (choice of three archetypes/classes: warrior, wizard, or rogue) were part of a secret society of "Book Guards". In that function they had to enter the worlds of books whose villains were trying to taking over the plot. As book guards they had to make sure that Tom Sawyer found his way out of the cave, if that was the adventure of the week.
    The first adventure module (that came inside of the rules box) was called "John Silver's sinister plan - Adventures on the Treasure Island".

    I don't know who was more vocal in criticising the game - the more earnest DSA players who felt ridiculed by having to share their fictional-but-quite-believable fantasy setting with (anachronistic) kids books, or players of all the other RPGs for whom DSA Junior was the perfect proof that DSA as a whole was an infantile game (that could never match the sincerity of, say, D&D).

    It was a strange set-up, having characters from a fully realized fantasy world full of adventure (which would be "real" to those characters) enter fictional worlds that were not even part of the setting.

    Unexplored in what sense?

    OTE has the self-reference plot, but I suppose that doesn't really count. IIRC the original GURPS Fantasy had a suggestion of having everyone play themselves. I also seem to recall some White Wolf book suggesting basing a Vampire game in your own town and having fictionalized versions of players embraced -- but maybe I misremember. In any case, it seems to me that almost all games are open to treatment like this, but it is tricky to make a commercial game that is all about it, since the content and theme will then depend greatly on the group: you lose the ability to say "Characters in this game are X" without limiting the player population.

    As for non-commercial stuff, I have both played in and run games like this (including a LARP), and except for the LARP I never had the sense of doing something out of the ordinary. The games I'm talking about had various levels of "the player" injected into the the character, ranging from attempting to remain true to life to fictionalized/idealized versions ("If I were a character in a movie, what would I be like?").

    The setup of games like this is pretty strange actually: identification with the character becomes almost automatic, since... it is you. At the same time, THE thing about immersion (being someone else) is totally missing.

    At any rate, there is something to be said for these games as improvised one-shots: no need to spend any time at all in character generation or world building exposition.
