\n So my previous favorite monster was the Xorn. Something about unknowable radial creatures of neither good or evil brought that mad CoC flavor to any AD&D game.

After 4e rolled through, I really got hung up on the ATROPAL.



It's basically a floating dessicated corpse, like a super-evil sand lich. It floats around, and most of its attacks are basically "if you get close to it, its necrotic life-stealing field kills you. It shoots death lasers at you. If it's really bored, it may swipe at you with a claw". Basically, it's a Necro War of The Worlds-like Lich King. Totally epic creature, I can see basically a party of heroes in an ancient underground lava-city* in the desert, taking potshots at it while hiding, running through old ruins, avoiding its death field and death lasers.

Vampires are cool. But an Atropal will show up in nightmares.

What's your favorite monster? It can be one you invented, but you have to tell us what it's like.

-Andy

* Thief: The Dark Project fans: Karath-Din, The Lost City.

EDIT: Curse you Mark Causey for making me think of the buddy-buddy friendly version, the "Atro-Pal!"

    \n At the risk of seeming like I am continuing the silly in this (presumably serious) thread, I think the Gelatinous Cube is pretty damned scary. Consider:

    You're in a dark-ass dungeon; you can't see further than 20 feet with a torch before the dusty, moldering gloom soaks up the light. You and your party are bunched up, crotch-to-ass, and watching every crack and flagstone for a trap trigger or some kind of tentacle to come lashing out at you. All you can hear is your breath, the slight creaking and clanking of armor, and the dwarf's farts.

    Suddenly, the lead torch goes out--*ssssizle*--huh? What happened? The folks in back surge forward to help, but the corridor has tightened, and the lead (ah, the poor rogue) is pushed into... something. Some firm, suffocating liquid that's filling the corridor. AND THEN THE GOO SURGES TOWARD THE REST OF YOU!

    Gah. I mean, played right, the dungeon equivalent of a Roomba can be a fucking nightmare scenario. I always ran them as nearly invisible, though, not like Jello. Think the water tentacle from The Abyss, but acidic and prone to extreme stillness (i.e. hard to notice, in poor light).
    ----
    Runner Up Favorite Monster

    I was always partial to the simple things, like a swarm of rats. And along those lines are Blink Dogs (in the Vineyard--no, no, JK).

    In the last LARP I played, they sent me and another guy out to be a single, rabid Blink Dog (as no way one alone would attack humans). Basically, either of us could "phase out" with an instantaneous arm-crossing (unlike the Phasing Ability, which took a Three-Count: three seconds) at which time the other immediately uncrossed his arms and could continue to attack and defend. Talk about freaking the SHIT out of a small group we decided to waylay. We damned-near took down four experienced (both real fighting and EXP build points) characters by just constantly flanking around, using the "present" one of us to spread them out (drawing attack) and then "blinking" to let the other guy (now behind someone) get in a shot or three while the first guy repositioned.

    Even with them knowing where we were (though they were good enough players not to meta-game it TOO badly) they could barely defend themselves. That got me thinking... damn, a pack of, oh, ten of these (twenty paired NPC players) could wipe out this town (of maybe forty active PCs)! Easily, if they were smart and sneaky about it. And went for the Healer's Guild first.

    Running a pack of Blink Dogs against a party of PCs could make me want to run D&D again after all these years, especially with 4E's big tactical element....

    \n Mephits, but only for this:

    Way back in second edition, the Planescape Monsterous Compendium had this little bit about them: Basically, evil spellcasters of the lower planes had a complex language about conveying messages to each other based on the "gift" of elemental mephit. Like: giving someone a fire mephit means you're displeased with their actions, an ice mephit means they're unwelcome to enter your home, salt is a declaration of open warfare (salt mephits are really unpleasant), and so on.

    It's the Victorian language of flowers, except instead of flowers, you've got vicious, spiteful, and petty little elemental humanoids.

    Plus all the awkward things mephits were made to do. An Ice Mephit to keep a storage room cold! A steam mephit to power complex mechanical engines! Etc, etc. There's a game entirely about Mephits, somewhere between Maid and My Life With Master in tone, somewhere in the imaginary library representing books I'll probably never actually write.

    \n Never thought about it much, but here's what I'd imagine for the perfect monster. You make a deal for...uhm...something. Like power or eternal life. But the cost of it is that there's an anti-version of you created somewhere in the world. It wants to destroy everything you do. It doesn't have any powers, just looks and sounds identical to you. Pretty easy to kill, actually. Thing is, it's connected to you, and if it dies, you die. So now you've got a monster you can't kill, that's messing up your reputation, and you have to make sure it stays alive. You'll probably end up having to do all sorts of nasty stuff now. That's a cool monster in my book.

    Posted By: David BergWerewolf's Black Spiral Dancers are a glorious mix of chaos powers and toxic waste hyena color.
    Seconded, kind of: My favorite monsters were actually those BSD whipping-boys, the Fomori. This is because of the stunning, awesome, cool, perfect, over-the-top supplement Freak Legion, a miraculous, never-to-be topped splatbook made entirely of win wrapped in more win.

    In the game, fomori are humans that have become possessed by Wyrm-spirits, i.e. nasty, corrupt ones, in case that wasn't clear. They get powerful gifts but pay a terrible price, as you would expect from whiny emo White Wolf guys. But wait: They don't live long enough to do much emo whining, and the "terrible price" isn't some metaphorical curse or anything like that. Oh no. It's a horrible supernatural disease that makes you spurt infectious black pus from every square inch of skin! Or it's your limbs sloughing off every time you make a freaking roll and spilling out thousands of cockroach-like banelings! It's being on fire, but not in a cool Human Torch way \u2014 think burn wards and Throbbing Gristle's "Hamburger Lady". Oh, and that natural weapon that you can wield for six dice of aggravated damage? It's in your pants, it's prehensile, it has teeth, and it's always hungry.

    If you build fomori and (if you're a sadistic GM) BSDs using Freak Legion for your straightforward Werewolf game, you at least make some foes that have a shot against a pack of PCs. But it's even more fun to just play "my monster is grosser than yours" and then throw them into a pitfight. Great way to spend an hour or two. Gives you real insight into your friends.

      \n Forgetting the fact that most games haven't made good use of them...

      Werefoxes aka Kitsune (ie. those of a vulpinoid persuasion).

      Going back to the original Chinese and Japanese mythology, they are rarely good or evil. Such concerns are beneath them and the exist by the grace of the gods as tricksters and protectors of the world who work against those who would otherwise be overcome by their own hubris.

      Many portray them as evil, but this is usually the work of those who've been slighted by their practical jokes.

      Some portray them as good, but these are usually people who haven't fallen victim to one of their tricks or traps.

      Those with true knowledge know that they are merely a part of the cosmic order. Even if their part is ensuring that there's a little chaos to prevent the order from stagnating.

      \n My favorite monster is the vampire. It's a horror standard -- but there's about a thousand-odd different varieties from myth and legend, supplemented by another thousand-odd varieties that fiction writers have made up, so no two have to be alike. Add on that most of them can "pass" for human. Then tack on that most of them can make people into vampires, and thereby control them.

      A vampire can be anything from an easy-kill shambling corpse for your lowbies, up to a nigh-unstoppable engine of destruction. It can be anything from a near-mindless snarling fiend, up to a witty and urbane opponent. It can be anything from a lone opponent, up to a centuries-old mastermind with minions everywhere and resources you can barely imagine.

      It's the all-purpose monster.

      \n I'm in a terrible and delicious feedback loop.

      Githyanki are a monster I really dig. I love their home city built on the body of a dead god, their liche-queen and their silver swords. They just have an otherworldliness that I dig.

      The other is a dragon. I have had a cunning dragon in so many games I have run, entirely inspired by the dragon in Gardner's Grendel, can't really get enough of them.

      But here's the thing, githyanki have an ancient pact with red dragons. Rock!

      In an old D&D game I ran seven years or so ago, red dragons could, once in their lives, send a delegate and receive a legion of githyanki soldiers for their own use once in their lives for a year. One of the players was kidnapped by a red and had to go to Tun'Arath and ask the liche-queen for several legions, as an elder red was asking for one on behalf of himself and his two children and his late son. Good stuff.

      \n Orcs.

      They're used so often, but so often poorly used. Most people are familiar with their role as an archetype for bullies, moderately tough, but pushovers when facing someone hitting back.

      They're more interesting to me when they're examined in a colonial sense. We've all heard the term "orcish horde." The term horde comes from the Mongolian armies that conquered much of Eurasia. To apply that word to orcs suggests some things about them, that they are not reliant on city states, but tribal nomads, following strong, charismatic (at least for an orc) leaders, warlike and strategically mobile, attacking their targets more often than getting attacked.

      What makes this even more interesting is that this is part of how they are declared different from the cultures the PCs come from, which are agricultural, have formal rules of determining leadership, and are shown as being "good" by being defenders, rather than starting wars. From their perspective the orcs are "savage" with all the implications that holds in the way that the Victorians reduced other cultures to the status of lesser people by declaring them "savages."

      While this is enough to make them interesting for me, it also opens up the possibility of fleshing them out for greater depth by bringing in more elements from Mongolian and other nomadic cultures. Other avenues may be explored, too, as they are in the game Earthdawn.

      So, in a way I like orcs because they help define the gameworld as a whole. I like them even more in Earthdawn, where they serve a similar purpose, but it is accomplished through a more intricate manner.

      \n Favourite monster really depends on "use/purpose etc.", where my headspace is.

      Ghouls I've always liked - the prospect of a paralysis scares the Player not just the PC, once-human horror, bestial yet some among them more clever etc., visual image clear, some limited DM roleplay --- I admit I go a bit "Gollum" with "tasty it is, tasty".

      Evil cultist as DM design fodder - so many different ways to dress them up, can include any mix for tactical needs, reliable baddies that can target whatever squishiness you need (vampire cult, disease god cult etc.), inbuilt flaws/blindsights, fun to roleplay as DM with maniacal ravings etc.

      \n I wasn't going to contribute to this thread at first... but last night, I had a weird dream. In a nice suburban town, I was walking through a park, and there were some small children, with their parents, dogs, etc, playing with giant slugs. How big? Oh, I dunno, maybe 5' long. They could curl up and the kids loved carrying them around. They were gentle and slow and great pets... except you had to watch out because they might fight with one another. Also, they liked champagne.

      Now, that was weird enough, to have a dream like that. But when I woke up, I remembered that there was a Giant Slug in the Monstrous Manual (or whatever it was called).

      And that brough it all back to me: what's the deal with all those weird "monsters" in those books? You know, the ones that aren't really monsters? The Giant Slug, from what I remember, could spit acid--which allowed me to design a clever trap involving a Giant Slug imprisoned behind salt-covered bars--but other than that... what are you supposed to do with it? Can it hurt you if you get close?

      There were all those monsters that.... weren't really monsters. They existed to clean up after other monsters, or fill some other "ecological niche". But they didn't really make sense, you know? Not as monsters, anyway. Not as scary things that roar and attack people.

      (Like, seriously... I think it had a "bite" attack. Can you imagine a slug the size of a bus trying to bite a human being? Does it poke you with its antennae first, to see where you are, then try to bite you blind? Uh, jump to the left about 5 feet...)

      Who the hell came up with those weird things? And how were you supposed to use them? It seemed more like an exercise in creative thinking than any kind of "monster", as the word is normally used: "How can I come up with a situation where the PCs would need to fight a giant slug?"
