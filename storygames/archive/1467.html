    Jonathan Walton had a post about fans, teacups and hankerchiefs in traditional Chinese storytelling.

    That inspired this little tidbit that may windup in Dare:
    I'm specifically remembering the scene in Stephen King's IT where the asthmatic kid sprays the monster with his inhaler after declaring, "this is battery acid!"

    It works like this:
    Each player brings a prop to the game.

    As you face the hazards in The House, your only weapon is your imagination. To thwart a monster, you must grab a prop from the pool and declare its true nature while physically acting out its use. An umbrella can become a stake to impale a vampire or a flute to soothe Bigfoot's rage or a witch's broomstick craftily stolen from the broom closet.

    Every time you use a prop in a way that no player (including yourself) has used it, your character goes further through the house and your courage goes up. Every time you use a prop in the same way that a player (including yourself) has used it, your character still progresses through the house, but your courage does not go up.

    Every time a prop you brought gets used in a new way, your fear goes down. Every time your prop gets used in the same way it has been used before, the monsters can see through your feigned bravery and your fear goes up.

    If you ever use a prop as what it actually is, you immediately leave that room of the House and lose any courage or fear you had earned since entering that room. Do this three times and you find yourself walking out of the House entirely.

    When a new game session begins, no player can bring the same props that any other player has brought before.
    It's clunky, but my goal was:
    1) encourage players to bring versatile props,
    2) encourage players to make versatile use of the props that are available, 3) get players physically involved with their story, and
    4) make players come away with a renewed sense of childlike imagination, excitedly finding new use for random objects lying around the house.

    A while back I started developing a game where all the characters are members of a cannibals' anonymous style gentleman's club. The conceit was that you had to select a snacakble foodstuff (like: oreos, cheetos, M&M's etc) that you find irresistable, especially hard to stop if you each just one. These are your counters, and they represent your appetite. Over time, you accumulate these, as a stack. Eventually the idea is that the player gives in to the urge to eat one (or more) of them, and this corresponds to the character's inability to quiet his urges to eat human flesh.

    I liked the idea, but my wife didn't, and requested I stop working on that one. Pity, really.
