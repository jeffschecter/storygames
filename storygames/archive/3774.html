Guide to Talking Bad About My Games

\n
\n
\n
\n\nBen_Lehman \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2007 \n edited August 2007 in Directed Promotion
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n 1) Thank you! Talking bad about my games is probably the second most useful form of promotion, and you're going to provide me with it. Yay! This makes me happy.

Why is it so useful? Well, my game is pretty good, so there's a lot of people online talking about it being really good. This "wall of support," despite coming from a very sincere place, often comes off as mawkish or insincere. Negative comments help offset that, giving a more nuanced picture of the game. They're very useful for people who are deciding whether to pick up the game or not (and, contrary to common belief, most designers only want people who will enjoy their game to purchase it.)

2) The kind of smack-talk is pretty important. "POLARIS SUXX!" is not a particularly useful piece of bad-talk, to me or any reader. Neither is trying to talk around it: "well, on reflection, I think that there may have been a misinterpretation about (huge rules problem that totally sucks) so that's mostly our fault.) Here's a few styles that might be good:
a) Clinical. "This rule interacts poorly with this rule, giving this result in play."
b) Snobby. "Polaris is clearly lacking in any understanding of *real* role-playing games, which require yadda yadda bladda bladda."
c) Rantish. "What the fuck kind of dickweed jackshit is Ben Lehman that he thinks he can go around printing these words on fucking paper! I want to shove my dick down his throat and choke him on it!"
d) Buddy. "Hey, man, that game totally blows. It has all this crappy fairy shit in it. I wouldn't buy it if I were you."
e) It's a trap! Frame the bitching as a rules question. "Ben, I don't understand how to use key phrases to wipe my ass. Can you explain?" then later "oh my god this game doesn't have ass-wiping rules? This is crap! I can't believe you charged me $23 of my hard earned money for this!"

The key to all of these is that they provide information and, with the exception of clinical, entertainment value for the reader. Ultimately, your style doesn't matter so much as being a good writers -- expressing why you don't like something, whether or not you have direct (play or reading) experience with it, and writing in an entertaining enough style that it all goes down smooth.

3) Where to complain? Talking bad is promotion, so I'd prefer you'd do it on high-traffic sites. It's also going to get you a better reception on places like RPGnet and the RPGsite which aren't swarming knee-deep with fans of the game. (we'll get to the fans in a moment.) There are different stylistic approaches to complaining on different forums, so here's some ideas:
a) The Forge. An Actual Play post about how much the game sucked for you is always welcome, although be warned that I will actually show up and post probing questions and maybe apologized. More backhandedly, you could do it as a First Thoughts post (I want to get this thing right that Polaris does wrong...)
b) My personal forum, also at the Forge. Low traffic, but a response from me is almost required. This is great for It's a Trap! But really anything works out as long as I can respond.
c) RPGnet open. Ah... this is the best. Preferably start a pages-long flamewar that I don't have to even look at, although I will probably read it obsessively. At page 6 or so I will show up and try to talk calmly and politely, even though inside I am seething with endless rage.
d) RPGnet review. Please God! I love RPGnet reviews. I will politely thank you for your review and your criticism, and even if the review is "POLARIS IS TEH SUCK!" 10,000 times over I'll read every word.
e) RPGsite. Talking points: swine swine swine. Rants work well here.
f) Your blog. If you want me to be aware of this you'll have to point me at it.
g) Story Games. I don't recommend this. Too many fans.

4) Fans. There will be people who really really like the game, who will show up to defend it from you. You have two options here. First, you could realize that disagreement over a game is not silencing or censorship, and calm the fuck down and be a rational human being. This is the boring approach, and I don't recommend it. The other is to get het up: How dare other people enjoy a game that you think sucks! Get into fights about it that go on for pages and pages! It's awesome!

Any questions? Other publishers have stuff that they want to offer? Talking bad experiences? Questions, revisions, or (please) smack talk?

yrs--
--Ben
