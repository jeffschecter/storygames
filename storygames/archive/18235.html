\nOH Nerd just did an Indie Games on Demand event at MARCon (Columbus, Ohio). Something I want for our next effort is some kind of menu that attendees can peruse, to get an idea of what sorts of games we're offering. If this has been done before, links would be appreciated.

Here's my first idea: a single, image-laden sheet for each game, inserted into page protectors and organized in a 3-ring binder (for easy display and browsing).

Would this be doing these games a disservice? What would you do differently? Any feedback is welcome.

    \n I did this for four conventions. MACE, Dragon*Con, and StellarCon, and AncientCityCon.

    It can be done, and it's a pretty easy way for people who want to judge a game based on a few seconds of visual inspection to do so. It worked for MACE particularly well, because I only provided those bound "menus" to people who already signed up for a GoD slot, and were already at the table, and wanting to make a decision as to what to play.

    It did not work for most of the other cons, because the "menu" was available to people as a tool for deciding if they wanted to sign up for a GoD slot.

    Alternatively, Kelley set up a folding menu (11 x 8.5 folded twice) that had a picture of the cover (or a picture of an appropriate action-y sequence if the cover was unavailable) with a quick pitch.
    -The pitch was limited to 140 characters, so that it could be "tweeted" to friends if someone found a game and wanted to test the waters with their friends
    -The pitch was framed by the people who would run the game there at the convention, not by the game designer or some star reviewer, so that when someone went "tell me more" we could tell them more.

    It looked something like this:
    Click Here for PDF

    That was supplemented by a more in-depth menu at D*C GoD this past year, but when presented with both options, people consistently looked through the menu, talked with their friends, and then wanted favored more face-to-face information over the printed menu.

    The menu was the same presentation quality as the smaller pamphlet, with the same full-color, and often the same images (higher quality and larger.)

    So our lessons learned would discourage us from using a menu, unless it was just internal use to remind our GMs of the talking points.

    \n I would do something like this - double-printed paper that you fold once. It's in Swedish, but you will probably understand some of it. It contains the following structure.

    HEADLINES: the number of hours it takes to play the game (less than two hours, three to four hours, and four to six hours)

    [RPG title]
    keywords
    a very short description.

    The descriptions were written to plant a state of mind into the reader.

    Zombie Cinema
    conflicts, escape, aid, competition, eat, sleep, die.
    The greatest danger is your competitors. Or is it?

    I wonder how you all are using the menus. I'm sometimes part of a Swedish drop-in arrangement called the Indie Lounge, and we are a bunch (5-10) GMs sitting in a room and when visitors come by, one GM greets them by a table where all the games are laid out and presents the games, focusing mostly on the games that have available GMs. So we don't really need menus. The reason why I did the menu was to place them all over the convention.
