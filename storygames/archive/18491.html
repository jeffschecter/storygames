    \n Gazing into the abyss is about hunting for understanding and meaning in places of darkness and disquiet.

    It is often supernatural: witches and infernals setting up pentagrams in their bedrooms to summon forth demonic knowledge, fay convening with will o' wisps at the edge of the murky wood. It is often research-oriented: consulting occult tomes that are inexplicably lying around the school library, the infamous google montage. It is often navel-gazing: dropping ketamine in your basement, flipping through old photo albums while sobbing mascara all over your bedspread.

    The Google Montage is the most notorious and awkward example throughout the genre. It happens in Twilight, Buffy, The Vampire Diaries, Ginger Snaps, and several others. A character (almost always a girl for some reason) has overwhelming suspicions about the True Nature of a mysterious friend. They plug some innocuous phrase into Google, like "pale skin" or "hair growth." Through a series of close-up shots of their shocked face and their computer screen, displaying hackey purple-and-black rudimentary-html-skill websites, we see them learn the truth about Vampires/Werewolves/Witches/Themselves/Whatever.

    There are a lot of different gazing into the abyss variations in Buffy, but "No Place Like Home" (S5E5) is perhaps the most clear and textbook example. Buffy learns that her sister Dawn might be a figment. She takes some weird magic drugs and enters a slowed state of awareness where colors flicker and things are revealed to be what they truly are. She looks at a family photo. Dawn's face quickly fades out of it. Dawn isn't real!

    Finally, watch this music video: Hollywood Forever Cemetary Sings. (trigger warnings: drug abuse, murder, blood, possible kidnapping.) When she smokes that apple & gets wasted while searching for meaning? Gazing into the abyss. Out in the woods, when the fog rolls in and shows her both what she wants and what she must do? Gazing into the abyss.

    \n Another thing to remember is that this move corresponds to a move from Apocalypse World, the parent game that inspired a whole bunch of other games, including Monsterhearts. In Apocalypse World, characters can open their brains to the psychic maelstrom. What is that psychic maelstrom? How does it all work? Totally up to the players. What it does is serve as a tool for infodumping, with some interesting consequences. You can certainly consider it a deus ex machina, but it's one that's baked in, just as it is in the plays from which the term comes. You go to see one of those plays, the deus ex machine's just one of the conventions used in the play.

    I agree that you don't need a roll to use Google, but that's not what Gazing Into the Abyss means. It means finding the information on whatever topic you're gazing into the abyss about.

    In other words, there's the mechanic, and there's the chrome. The mechanic is: roll dice when you try to find out something, and it doesn't have to something your character has any logical way of knowing. What you roll lets you know whether you find out anything and what the consequences are. MCs can veto requests that seem off to them, but should err on the side of saying yes.

    The chrome is whatever you want it to be. It can be googling, because this isn't about using the computer the way we do in real life. It's about using it to find out stuff that has no business being online -- but suddenly, because of that roll, it's online. The player's decision made that part of the world -- and remember, the player is the one who decides what the chrome is. (Once it's part of the world, maybe other people know about it too. This makes absolutely no logical sense. We're in the realm of psychomachia, of dream logic, of books I've read where, once the protagonist learns something, everyone seems to know it, even though no one seemed to know it before. Or maybe no one else knows about it until there's a logical in game reason.)

    If it makes your head hurt less, maybe the PC did some serious research, not just googling, but a montage of searching out ever more obscure websites, finding ways of getting passwords to even more obscure ones, maybe even going to a library or crawling through used bookstores, and the key thing is correlating all the data no one's put together before.

    Like I said, the chrome can be whatever you want it to be. Player choice, and if you're the MC, you can encourage your players to come up with descriptions that are more interesting than "I google it".

    But the deus ex machina element is baked in. You can fiddle with that, and if you do, I'd be interested in hearing about the results. Just be aware that you're likely to tweak things to the point where you void the warranty, if you see what I mean. If that works for you, great!

    \n I come across as curt because I'm direct when I'm trying to figure something out. If I have a question I just ask it. Any frustration I have at the moment is due to being accused of things.

    Onto the metaphors... this is a tricky thing. Metaphors have to be used carefully. Act Under Fire - while being phrased somewhat oddly - is an easily and immediately understood metaphor. Barring English not being someone's first language the meaning is clear. Same for Turning Someone On, Shutting Them Down, Lashing Out, Running Away, and Holding Steady. All are easily understood simply from reading the name. They are clear and evoke the nature of the Move. They give you the spirit of the law as well as the literal.

    Then we come to Gazing into the Abyss. This is a term usually used to refer to coming face to face with horror and it's impact on you. If a philosophy student or a psychologist uses the phrase that is what he means. Thematically it's what this phrase has been assumed to mean for many years. From reading the name virtually no one would read it and say "Oh that must be how I get information". The logic doesn't flow. Then we get to the actual phrasing of the Move in which it says to have visions. It gives no alternate examples like what people have been given here but that flows into the comments of me "latching" onto definitions.

    If I cannot use the definitions of the words in the description how am I supposed to understand it? Even metaphors must make sense in context. Even if it requires someone to explain it to you. Language only has meaning in the definitions we give it.

    So - in my thoughts - if this Move does not represent dealing with horror and your reaction to it then it needs to be rewritten. Re-Skinned as it were. Something to make it clearer what it's purpose is without losing what it is meant to be. Allow me to attempt to do so.\n
    Seek the Light

    When you seek the light roll with dark and ask the MC a question. On a successful roll it answers your question. On a 10+ choose two options from the following list.

    - Your information is clear and detailed
    - Your information tells you what you must do and you carry 1 forward to doing it
    - Your information helps you, remove a Condition

    On a 7-9 choose one:

    - Your information is confusing and alarming
    - Your information is clear and detailed but you gain the Drained Condition

    You seek the light whenever you are searching for answers ('seeking the light in the darkness' as it were). The means of the searching doesn't matter. If you are playing phone tag with friends to bilk them for information you are seeking the light. If you take psychotropic drugs in an attempt to open your mind you are seeking the light. If you perform a rite to commune with demons you are seeking the light. What matters is the intent. You want to learn the answers no matter what.
    \nA simple thematic shift to produce a very different paradigm. Now here is how I would do Gaze Into the Abyss based off what the phrase means to me and what it means to most.\n
    Gaze Into The Abyss

    When you gaze into the abyss roll with dark. On a 10+ choose one:

    - Breakdown; give a witness to your breakdown a String on you and take one on them
    - Harden your heart; remove one String on you but gain the condition Flinty
    - Wall yourself away; give up one String you have on another to remove all conditions on yourself

    On a 7-9 choose one:

    - Give a witness a String on you
    - Go numb; replace a current condition with the dazed condition (or gain it if you have no conditions)
    - Flee the scene crying

    You gaze into the abyss whenever you face the darkness in your life. If you express horror at finding your murdered teacher you are gazing into the abyss. If you break down because your boyfriend slept with your best friend you are gazing into the abyss. If you see the Infernal give someone to the Dark Power and you express horror you are gazing into the abyss. If you are the Infernal and your Dark Power makes you give someone to them and you express guilt you gazing into the abyss. If you have done something and you ask yourself "What have I done?" you are gazing into the abyss.

    This Move is all about pain and fear. It's about dealing with all the shit life throws at you like a fastball and it's about sharing it with the world. Just like every Move it should move the action forward. Preferably by complicating an interaction or driving an interaction. It's a mechanical reason to react like a real person.

      \n Aside from research, I've often seen Gaze into the Abyss (and Apocalypse World's 'Open your Mind') used as a 'What the hell can I do now?' move.

      As an example, there's a 20 minute section of the Twilight: New Moon movie that I really like. Bella's been missing Edward and now she's going on her first date with Jacob. She ends up hanging out with some bikers and the danger in the situation ends up psychically connecting her with Edward.

      I can easily see a player in my game saying "I totally miss Edward but there's no way I can ever see him again. What the hell am I supposed to do?" A Gaze into the Abyss move would give her clear visions of Edward and tell her what she has to do to make the visions continue (keep putting herself into danger).

      But I was profoundly influenced by the text about Gaze into the Abyss that Joe cut from the playtest draft (that Paul linked to earlier):\n
      there\u2019s this thing called the abyss. you know where to find it. it knows where to find you.

      it\u2019s the shit that\u2019s too awful to wholly exist. it\u2019s where kids get their nightmares, and schizophrenics get their delusions. it\u2019s where monsters get their power. the abyss can make offers, and demands. it can cast visions and prophecies. but without you, the abyss is weak. it needs a partner with which to tango. your relationship to the abyss is your own. like a terrible mirror, everyone sees something different.
      \n[Paul's link from earlier ]

      Thinking about the Abyss as something that's out there, that wants to make contact with the characters, and wants them to fall into their Darkest Selves totally informs ever time I run the game. It makes me very willing to let players trigger the Gaze into the Abyss move on the flimsiest of pretexts because I love the momentum that it adds to the game.

      The key for me, though, is what Joe wrote (in the playtest draft) here:\n
      each Skin has a different sort of relationship with the abyss. some of this is hard-coded into the options fo reach Skin, but a lot of it is left open to interpretation. ask about it. ask, \u201cso, how do your kind deal with abyss? is it responsible for the way you are?\u201d ask leading questions, and let the answers imply a sprawling mythology about the world.
      \nSo for the Mortal in my current game the Gaze into the Abyss move is triggered a lot by her looking deeply into the eyes of the people she's meeting in this strange town she's just moved to. The Ghost has a fractured memory of her death and doesn't exist in an-entirely-linear timeline, so for her Gazing into the Abyss is like recovering memories.
