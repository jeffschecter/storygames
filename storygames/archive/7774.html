    Posted By: Rob DonoghueSo thiswriteup about The Wireis aimed at writing and writers, but I must say that to my mind it applies to a good campaign just as easily.
    \nThat piece on The Wire and why it satisfies is excellent.

    When I read it a week ago I realized that that the feel I enjoy in games I play these days is very much of the feel and pleasure serialized cable drama (Dexter, The Wire, BSG, The Shield and so on), which had never existed as a form until the Sopranos knocked it out of the park. They're kind of different than Lost or Six Feet Under, but more like the first season of Twin Peaks, in that they're long form stories centered around major story arcs for each season or across the whole series.

    I believe the arrival of DVDs and Teh Internets, which made it possible to catch up quickly, have allowed the studios and networks to play around a bit with formats that would have been impossible before. They're still not as profitable as episodic TV since it's harder to slot individual episodes into random syndication slots -- which is where the real money is -- so they will always be rare. But I love the depth and breadth the writers are able to pull off in this format. And the pleasure loops back to story focused RPGs.

    (This is an American thing. The BBC has been doing it for years. House of Cards is still one of my favorite TV experiences.)

    \n [1] Placing a higher priority on group loyalty and authority than on fairness and freedom from harm is one of the distinguishing features of the Manager mode of thought I discussed a while back. The Radical mode reverses those priorities, valuing fairness and freedom from harm over group loyalty and authority. That's why strict roles are OK from a Manager perspective, even if they're unequal -- they provide group structure and clearly delineate authority -- but from a Radical perspective, those unequal roles would be unacceptable, because they're seen as unfair and causing harm.

And just between 2003 and 2005 - the latest statistic year - have dropped nearly 30% more (with a 3% drop in overall deaths) by my figuring.

The thing that's run rampant the last few decades is misinformation about how diet and weight contribute to health and mortality.

      \n [3] Basic idea: when a program is running it's represented as a 'process.' A process can create more processes (child processes). When a process stops running, it leaves a return value. If the parent process hasn't checked for the return value yet on a child that's exited/been killed, the system has to hold on to the value, even though the process is 'dead,' in which case it's called a 'zombie process.' It's dead, but it's still in the list of processes... neither dead nor alive...

      On the shirt, the process ID and parent process IDs (PPID and PID) indicate that the last process in the list is a zombie, indicated by the 'Z.' More on zombie processes here: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zombie_process
