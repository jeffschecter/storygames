Happy Birthday, Robot!
Robot's family of kittens has gathered many balls of yarn.
Robot's kittens want milk, but they also want to hunt!
"Ack!" says Robot. "I'm made of tuna cans!"
Robot finds a bush of catnip nearby and quickly knits several tiny toy mice.
Robot thinks this was a good first day of kitten school.
Robot's a cool cat!
\nMegan and I playtested the new draft last night, using the two-player option. She's has a traditional board game perspective, having played games with kids and her family all through childhood.

One of the first things she asked was how to win, which up til now I've not even addressed. I think hippie indie gamers might take it for granted that the story is the goal itself, without any sense of competition. So, I started thinking about ways to surreptitiously phrase the rules so that there is indeed a "winner," but not change the game at all.

In one sense, you could say the player with the most coins at the end of the game wins, but then that discourages people from sharing their coins. I had this experience playing with Megan. I asked if I could have some coins and she denied my request, kind of like if we were negotiating an exchange of resources in Settlers of Catan.

You could say the player with the fewest coins at the end of the game wins, which might encourage players to share their coins. The other benefit of having the fewest coins is that you get the last line in the story. Then again, that might just encourage players to just say fewer words so that they don't get any coins in the first place.

There might be a roundabout way of doing this, but it would introduce a new rule. You could say that players get one point for each coin they give to another player and the winner is whoever was the most generous at the end of the game.

But, with all these solutions I worry that introducing the word "win" might touch on Dan Pink's assertion that extrinsic rewards (victory) undermine intrinsic rewards (creating a story with your friends).

    \n I mostly think that playing this game to "beat" the other players would ruin it.

    OTOH, I could see having different rewards or goals you could shoot for: longest word used, best plot twist, best use of a foreign phrase, silliest player, etc.

    I could also see variations on the game where you have a group goal, and could win or lose as a team. Create a story without using the letter "e." Recapitulate the entire plot of a movie/play/novel with Robot as the protagonist. Make a story in terza rima. The possibilities for group victory conditions are endless.

    \n Playing this game to beat the other players would ruin it. Luckily as we've seen from the board games thread 90% of story gamers do not play any game to beat the other players. But still group victory seems way better. Even including an optional "if you like you can beat the other players by achieving x and y!" means that some personality types (who me?? ) will feel they've lost if they do not use the optional win conditions, because "surely the author wouldn't have included them unless they were legitimately a good idea for those who enjoy playing to win". Yes, I've thought exactly that.

    So, group victory... include the results of a sample game in the instructions. To win you must read your Robot storypoem and the sample one to someone who does not know what the hell you're doing, and make her choose which she liked better. If it's yours, you all win! Otherwise you have failed, do better. Once you win, for further play use your previous nonlosing story for comparison. And a new unsuspecting judge each time!

    One of my friends who played our test round pointed out that my examples aren't exactly "group victory conditions" as much as they are another rule constraining the story. Which is true. But these rules are also kind of fun to think up, so here goes:

    \n
      \n
    1. Don't use the letter "e."
      2. \n
    2. Don't use the verb "to be."
      3. \n
    3. Don't use the same word twice (with the possible exception of "Robot")
      4. \n
    4. Write the story backwards, sentence-by-sentence. So the first sentence you compose is the last sentence of the story and so on.
      5. \n
    5. Use pig latin..
      6. \n
    6. Recapitulate the plot of an agreed-upon book or film. The longer and more complicated the source material, the better.
      \n6 Compose the story in the style of a well-known writer.
      7. \n
    7. Compose rhyming couplets.
      8. \n
    8. Compose terza rima.
      9. \n
    9. Compose a series of haiku or renku.
      10. \n
    10. Compose the story in the style of a Craigslist personal ad.
      11. \n
