\n So, in my DnD 3.5 game, we swapped out the whole advancement system, swapped in Keys, and made advancement all modular and cool. The players love it. But one of my players is having trouble finding Keys he wants for his character, and wants to write a new key. I want to support that, but we're running aground a bit. He wants his character to be a summoner, and wants the key to have to do with that - getting XP for summoning creatures to help with combat or something like that.

Clinton's advice on the subject (which I want to follow) is "A Key must involve a motivation, problem, connection, duty, or loyalty."

So, Story-Games, help me write a key that will move play forward the way Keys are all supposed to and at the same time help my player feel like he's getting the chance to involve his summoning extensively through the key. Thanks!

    \n Okay so:
    1. You have a player who wants to be encouraged to summon things for combat effectiveness.
    2. You don't want to fiddle with the D&D system so that he has to contact the summoned things, talk to them, or risk having them go out of control.

    So essentially these summoned things are swords. Why doesn't the Key of Bloodlust fit? It will encourage him to fight and be effective in combat with his summons (he won't get XP otherwise), and he'll get greater rewards for defeating big enemies. Tie it to a Key of Reknown (I want to be feared as the greatest summoner, ever!) or the Key of Power (give him lots of opportunities to get favors from demons and other extraplanar creatures) and, boom, I think you've got this guy's number. The summoned creatures don't have agency of their own, so don't just give him XP for flipping the summoning switch, it's what he does with the summons that matters.

    \n Right on all accounts. My suggestions weren't focused on being successful in battles (where Bloodlust works better) rather on the Summoner focusing in on that type of magic.

    Why? In D&D although different schools exists there is often a host of spells that are so effective a crafty player chooses them every time no matter what school he focuses on (and choses what schools he cannot use based on those spells not being in the banned school). Ploymorph is one such spell, as WAS Haste in 3.0. Since the mechanic of memorization developes and economy there are often spells in a caster's specialist school that gets left out to focus on more effective spells (like no savvy player would be without magic missile since it hit's automatically and scales in damage as she gains levels).

    This idea then is to reward the player for using spells from a focused group. It says to him "I can do the effective combat thing or I can use a less effective spell and gain XP for it." Of course my approach was to then push it further and say the summoner gains XP for using his magic despite it causing problems for him and those around him.

    Here's the mental recipie I used for creating Keys when we converted our game from D&D;

    \n

    1xp for things you want to do all the time and have a minor impact on the story.
    \n3xp for things you want to do on occation and have a pretty solid impact on the story.
    \n5xp for things you want to do rarely and have an awesome impact on the story.
    \nBuy off: Do that thing that proves that you will never be like this again.

    \n

    And where it said "you want" I'd put in the desires of the player in question. Which, naturally, meant hammering it all out with the player right there in front of me. Naturally.

    Rules Change: Summoning & Controlling:
    When you summon something, it gets a Will save (vs. DC of the summon spell [10+spell level+ability mod]) to resist being controlled.
    If you're summoning above the level of the spell, the beastie gets a bonus to its Will save to resist control as follows:
    1 level above spell level - +2
    2 levels above spell level - +4
    3 levels above spell level - +8
    4 levels above spell level - +16

    Key of the Summoner
    1XP for summoning & controlling something easy to control (e.g. using Summon Monster 4 to get something from the SM4 list or lower)
    2XP for summoning & controlling something harder to control (e.g. using SM 4 to get a 5 or 6 level beastie who might break out)
    5XP for summoning & controlling something very difficult to control (e.g. using SM 4 to summon something from the 7, 8, or 9 lists)

    \n Forget the summoning. The whole "Key of Xenophile" above is a great example about how summoning can be just one way that a person could pursue such a key. I'll make this recommendation:
    \n
    \n
    Key of the Master
    \n
    \n1 xp: Give an order to someone allied with you and either as powerful or less powerful, and be obeyed.
    \n2 xp: Give an order to someone either (a) substantially more powerful than you or (b) violently antagonistic to you, and be obeyed.
    \n5 xp: Give an order to a substantially more powerful being who hates your guts, and be obeyed.
    \n
    \nBuyoff: Let someone less powerful than you give you orders, and obey them willingly.
    \n
    \nThis has the advantage that, in the small scale, he can boss around familiars, imps and party members for 1xp a shot ... but he'll also be looking to summon Asmodeus and sign a contract with him, to get his 5xp bonus.

    \n I think the current draft is way off the mark with regards to existing TSOY keys and the role they play in the game: it seems to be operating in a Gamist mode where the player is confronted with a set of connected but not at all opposed risk/reward decisions: not only does the player now have the normal D&D incentive for summoning more powerful creatures at a risk (a reward in tactical effectiveness), he also gets XP based entirely on taking that risk. The game mechanics and the Key are so perfectly aligned that they might as well be part of the same rule.

    More importantly, Keys are not to my mind about risk v. reward. They're about reward, flat out. Keys are there to make players want to do awesome things, not risky things. This is especially true of the 1xp gains, which almost always are either things that will happen constantly and have almost no 'additional' risk beyond the everyday risk of being a character in an RPG. Consider the numerous Keys that pay out XP just for being in a scene with another character. In any case it seems like the idea with Keys is that they offer their own independant rewards (XP) that work orthogonally with the tactical bits of the game. There are always different ways to get XP from Keys and they never correspond to a specific mechanic (that I am aware, maybe some of the racial Keys are not like this?) This seems like something worth aiming for, and something that many of the suggested Keys achieve (the contact with outsiders one, for example. Summoning is only one way to contact outsiders.)

    \n In a situation like this, I think it's useful to NOT try to make a key that wraps up everything about the character that is tied to their interest in summoning. Instead, make a key that rewards them for driving their keys in a specific way. For example:

    Key of the Summoner
    1xp: whenever your status as a summoner of extraplanar beings comes up in a scene
    2xp: when you attempt to solve a problem using summoning
    3xp: when you succeed at solving a significant problem using summoning

    Now pair this with some other motivation, such as "key of renown". Now whenever the character does something to ehnahce their fame, they get XP, but if they do it by summoning, they get to double dip, essentially doubling their XP reward. In my experience, this is where the engine of TSOY becomes a rocket engine.
