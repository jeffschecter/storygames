    \n Some ideas of various utility:

    Design your game to require periodic supplements. Or make awesome non-essential supplements.

    Make your game really good and charge a lot for it. Make your game really beautiful and charge a lot for it.

    Make your game inexpensively or tiny or crappy and charge the going rate for it.

    Adjust your price point in tandem with demand to capture every possible sale.

    Ransom it for more than it is worth but less than the market will bear.

    \n I'm personally trying to make an evergreen game. The games that make the most money are the ones that can keep on selling year after year. Monopoly, Clue, D+D, etc. I know it's a tough goal but a good one.
    \n
    \nAs to getting money out of people at shows that is a different matter. Squeezing is not an effective tactic. The hard sell means a failed sale. It is best to be engaging, interested in what they are interested in, present your game in a quick manner and wish them a great show whither they buy anything from you or not. In other words basic retailer etiquette.

    \nHamster Press

    \n My plan involves hosting a game and character database (generic LARP system built for character portability) for free, but having highly targeted banner ads on every form and report (i.e. every page). Views and click-throughs slowly earn revenue, at no cost to the user. I *might* offer a subscription model that gets rid of the ads and allows for larger character rosters for a particular game, but that's still open for debate (and mainly will be a factor of my server bandwidth usage and costs).

    Other than that, I plan to do the supplement thing, with a sort of progression:
    * shotGLASS - free, barebones rules, few or no examples, mainly for reference, though someone could build a whole game with it with some effort (and/or experience with HERO System).
    * GLASS - soft or hard cover digest book with all rules, multiple examples, some art, and a number of popular, ready-to-run genre write-ups.
    * Supplements - soft or hard cover digest books with highly detailed settings, Templates, and Packages for the setting genre, along with archetypal characters, major NPCs, and common adversaries.

    I will also look into various accessories, but LARP has very few necessary accessories that are "brandable." The main thing I've thought of is a stand-alone application that runs the GLASS database and synchs with the online one, so that folks can use netbooks or laptops on-site, then update their "main page" on the GLASScutters site. As a bonus, because it's most useful through the web interface (e.g. assigning Exp to characters so that they can spend it), and because I can use product keys to authenticate the app (i.e. a copy can't be used online), I can hopefully get a small revenue stream going from those CD sales.

    Beyond that obvious accessory/playing aid, I can't really think of anything else to provide revenue, without actually going into a different "line of work" (e.g. I could sell boffers... but why, when there's a ton of folks already doing it and who might pay me to advertise on my database site?). MAYBE sell some kind of "Official Experience Tokens" as a sort of novelty item--in tenths, halves, and full Exp units!--though folks can (obviously) use anything they want that's not too tough to forge (e.g. cheap custom poker chips).

    \n Make a game that's crunchy, so that there's lots of room for expansion rules and new, overpowered kinds of characters that need their own books.

    Make a setting that's richly detailed and interesting, so that you can sell book after book about different corners of the setting and different aspects of it.

    Create alternate or historical versions of the setting, and sell books for those, too, with slightly different rules so that you can justify selling another version of the core rules.

    Give the setting a metaplot, so that you can get people addicted to it and then they'll keep buying books for the lonely fun of following the story.

    White Wolf has done all of these things very well, both for the old World of Darkness and, except for the metaplot, for the new World of Darkness and Exalted -- and I think metaplot is creeping back into the new Vampire game, too.

    Of course, this is a strategy that is not easy to replicate, and I don't think it worked like magic for White Wolf either, or they wouldn't have ended the old WoD lines. But a lot of those things run up against the traditional culture of story games. I used to joke that we would know that Vincent Baker had sold out when he released the "Back East" sourcebook for Dogs in the Vineyard, with rules for playing Pinkertons. There's a real potential for pushback if a designer is perceived as cynically trying to milk his customers.

    Posted By: David Artman
    How would you make a destructible game element that is both affordable enough to destroy and yet can't be trivially copied by the end users (to bypass destruction of the original)?
    \nI hadn't really thought of the details. And essentially nothing reasonable can't be replicated by someone dedicated to the idea. But, some first thoughts:

    If you're printing in color, you can print using inks that won't replicate properly on normal copy machines and laser printers. There must be some clever way to use that advantageously.

    You could require shining a light through a page and having things lined up precisely to form a whole image (assuming your printer can maintain QC). I'm not sure if this would actually work, but it's something to check out.

    Give each product a serial number and make part of the game sending in the sheet for some online segment of play -- advancement perhaps. That seems pretty solid. Destruction isn't really needed, but each product gets used (if used fully) only once.

    I don't really have any great ideas for it.
