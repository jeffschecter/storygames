\n I was thinking the other day about how I game mastered in my old days with the traditional games. One skill from the game Drakar och demoner (eng. Dragons and Demons) was called Sense Danger where the character could start feeling uneasy if nearby a trap, ambush, or any similar danger. As a game master, I wrote their values in the skill so I could roll them behind the game master screen. I later moved on to rolling these in advantage, because my adventures at the time were more sequential so I knew they would arrive at certain places that needed checks. By eliminating the rolls, the description of the place got a better flow. Finally, I just looked at the highest value and gave them the information they acquired without any rolls.

Anyway, what I realized this other day that this was something that the game master used more than the players - to give out information to them.

What other kind of character traits (or whatnot) are there that only the game master uses?

(As an example, the sources of flags are used by both the players [as skills] and the game master [as adventure material] and that's not what I'm looking for.)

    ---

    Some things that I didn't roll for:
    Search: I just said the clues to the one with the highest value whenever they entered the room.
    Leadership: the one who were the leader in the group (or had a correlating skill/attribute) were the one who got contacted for quests, or addressed when talking to the whole group.
    Charisma: people revealed things to the character with the highest Charisma.
    Intelligence: the one with the highest Intelligence got the most clues.
    Luck: the most luckiest characters stumbled upon things, when there were things to be stumbled upon.

    What I realized when thinking about this was that I used these things as tools to describe the environment for the players. "And you, Hawkeye, notices how they are wearing armor underneath their clothes." I had these things written down and just picked one or two of the characters, or switched between them, with the highest values and addressed them with more information. You could also switch this around. Someone with the lowest luck were the one who stepped into traps, as an example, or someone with a low Charisma (or related skill) met people that were harder to persuade.

    I also wrote down stuff that they did so they could be recognized for it later, possibly even be the reason why they got a certain quest.

    So you could probably design a subsystem around that. Imagine having five of these things on the character sheet, and then having the players assign values between 1-5 between them. It would say something about the character but mostly be a tool for the game master.

    \n As GM, I make rolls for efforts whose outcomes might be hidden. Anything where the player would certainly know whether they've succeeded or failed, I let them roll.

    That's my first interpretation for your Sense Danger roll -- if the players fail to sense danger, you don't want them to know that's what happened, because knowing that would in fact tell the player something the character doesn't know -- that there is danger! Groups and GMs who don't care about player/character knowledge separation would just let the player roll.

    My second interpretation, though, is that maybe some of your GM rolls function like classic random event tables, but somewhat character-based instead of completely random. That seems fun. "This room could have been full of any Level 2 monsters, but because the paladin here is a sworn enemy of bugbears, the room is 17% more likely to be full of those! Time to roll!"

    More generally, I think operating outside the agency of a given character player yields all sorts of neat design options. In Penny for My Thoughts, there are no dice, but there is still the situation where other people are deciding what your character did in the past. In Unknown Armies, if you fail a Stress check, the GM takes over your character for a bit. There are also games where you roll to see if your character can overcome their own psychological barriers to take the action they intend -- this strikes me as the sort of roll that could easily be handed off to the GM. ("I charge into the horrific dungeon room!" "Your legs refuse to obey. You stumble and your vision swims...") I could also see the GM making D&D saving throws for this reason (unconscious rather than conscious use of character abilities).

    Agency aside, I also like your use of character comparison as spotlight guide. When it's time to reveal environmental subtleties, tell the generally perceptive character; when it's time to reveal ancient signs, tell the history expert; when it's time to reveal NPC deception, tell the manipulator; etc. And then when someone's going to be the first one sneak-attacked, look to the worst Luck stat.

    \n I'd like to mention that there are some forms of gaming where pretty much ALL the mechanics are like this.

    The GM rolls dice and handles the "system" behind the screen, while the players can only interact with the fiction directly.

    (In such games, an oft-seen concept is that the players are actually playing "themselves" in this virtual reality.)

    Also, surely there are variations of playstyles of Fate-type games where the GM can "compel" character Traits but the players don't use them themselves. (A simple example is a character Aspect like "I always seem to draw the wrong person's attention", which the player never activates, but the GM makes extensive use of. Of course, in most incarnations of Fate this would be available to the player as well, but I can see variations where that is not the case, whether by design or by choice.)

    \n Oh hey - while we're mentioning D&D, let's not forget about alignment! I know it's a hairy subject for many but in light of the OP, this is definitely one of those things the GM pays attention to in a very different way than the Player does.

    In my main AD&D campaign (which ran for a little over a decade), I used alignment charts and actively tracked the characters' alignment positions over time. Highly mathematical, this was one sheet that was never shown to Players. A PC's align position would directly affect their chances of success when involved with anything that had to do with their deities. I would give Players warnings when they began drifting close to the edge of an alignment boundary - things like dreams and omens portending deific dissatisfaction or highlighting moral dilemmas - but they wouldn't know for sure that a line had been crossed until their deity stopped responding.

    PS - Love Stafford. BRP and its descendants were a huge creative kick in the pants for me.
