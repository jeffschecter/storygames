\n I'm new to the story games thing, but I wonder if beliefs, keys, aspects, etc are all that. We played Lady Blackbird just last night, and the role-playing seemed..."forced" Don't get me wrong, we had insane amounts of fun, but I think we might have had just as much fun even if there weren't roleplaying incentives. Seems to me most of the players I know like to role-play, to the point where they'll do it even if the game doesn't support it or even works against it. In Pathfinder our barbarian will charge at the monsters even though it's not a smart move, it's just his character - and now he'll take a couple full rounds of attacks and possibly die. The cleric won't heal the druid's tiger because his cousin now likes the tiger more than him and he's jealous. That feels to me like *real* roleplaying, really making the hard choice...and In A Wicked Age and Fiasco don't give you rewards for pursuing your goals, but people pursue their goals anyway in those games...

Another way of looking at it is from another semi-famous behaviorism experiment: kids like to draw. The experimental group of kids is given money for drawing. The next day, the control group is still drawing, but the experimental group stops because they're not getting paid.

Yet another way of looking at it - Jesse Schell defined "play" as stuff you do that you don't have to do. Once you incentivize something, you turn play into work. Obviously there's a zillion World of Warcraft players out there who disagree, but that's why I personally don't play WoW (don't like doing chores) and I'm not sure how much I like bringing similar incentives into my real roleplaying.

SO: What do you think? Would you and your friends still roleplay (in the strict sense, play the way your characters would) if there were no incentives, and if so, why use incentives?

    Yeah, we would, but we'd spend a lot of time trying to get to the point where we're playing on the same theme.

    \n

    Your group is playing by certain rules, where, for instance, it's OK to make tactically suboptimal choices because it's funny, or because that's how the character is, or whatever. But they're rules. What's going on with reward cycles is that you don't need to improvise the rules for that stuff all the time \u2014 you're getting the techniques that another player (the designer) has discovered work well.

    \n

    The point isn't that it necessarily works better for everything. The point is that it works best for that specific thing.

    \n

    So, like, In a Wicked Age gives you Best Interests, but no mechanical incentive, right? Nope! The mechanical incentive is that your Best Interests are known by the GM, and he's doing what he can to make their pursuit sticky and fun. That's the mechanic: tell the GM. The reward is getting (or failing to get) that which is in your best interest (and whether you still want it when you start to see what it costs). Without that back-and-forth, the game would be much flatter.

    \n

    (There are some problems with the way IaWA works, and one of them is making sure this link is concrete, so it's not the perfect example.)

    \n

    In Hot War, those things are almost your only resources; it reminds you to show everyone that this is the thing your character's about, and gives you opportunities to show stuff you might not have thought of otherwise: "Hm. I need more dice to get this guy to tell me where he lives. Oh! I'll bring in my 'Sacred Arts of Isis!' I go back to my flat and burn some incense along with a piece of paper with the guy's signature on it, then breathe the smoke."

    \n

    The point is the focus, not that you can't do it otherwise.

    \n Keys were one of the things that really changed my gaming when I first learned about them. They are super great.

    I was playing D&D 3rd edition, mostly, when I first learned about Keys (through the Sweet20 system, which is actually kind of unsatisfying after a while).

    In D&D, a good role-player decides what his guy is all about. My guy is the sneaky thief who's all about secrets, or my guy is the wizard-hunter who lives for cold, black revenge. And in a good D&D game, everyone plays those roles, and that's fun.

    But no matter how well you play that role, or how much it enriches the game, the system itself never rewards you. My guy is all about learning secrets, and I portray that wonderfully, but the only way the game itself rewards me (which I want, because I want my character to advance) is by killing monsters. The stuff about learning secrets, which is so important to my character concept, is completely divorced from the rules.

    But when I have the same guy with the Key of Learning Secrets, I now get rewarded (which is what I want) for doing the very thing that is making the game fun in the first place.

    To me, that is what makes these kinds of mechanics fun, and good, and worth using and emulating.

    Well put, Jason.

    \n

    Well, the precise nature of that reward cycle has a lot to do with how freely the feedback loop moves. If you're gaining CPs in GURPS, then whatever; that doesn't really help you generate fiction \u2014 they're just points to make you approach a practical maximum of efficacy. But if it gives you particular narrative powers based on particular circumstances, it's another matter.

    \n

    E.g. "you can only kill someone if you have 'murderous' written down, and you can only write down 'murderous' if you first have 'desperate'".

    \n

    We get used to thinking about these things as numbers, but even if they have numerical significance, they don't have to be written down that way.

    \n

    An example from Shock:Human Contact: When someone convinces you of something, you write down what the character's been convinced of \u2014\xa0let's say that someone convinces me my husband is dead. And then my husband shows up while we're in a conflict. Because that's been challenged, I get to reroll some of my dice \u2014\xa0effectively, it's enhanced my chances of success, but I never wrote down "+1" or anything. And it's a bonus because I want players to have opportunities to play their characters hard, even when it's not to the benefit of the character, and not lose the narrative power that that character represents.

    \n IME there's a continuum of playstyles that runs from hardcore market-driven players (i.e. earning incentives is the point of the exercise, and various behaviors usefully emerge when you do so) to hardcore ... virtuous players, for lack of a better, less loaded word (i.e. roleplaying is its own reward, and again various behaviors usefully emerge when you do so).

    Every RPG I've ever been exposed to sits somewhere on that continuum, and at least for me is one of the main determining criteria for liking a game's ruleset as well as any given group's playstyle. There are market-driven players I can't connect with: every action must be incentivized, even to the point of breaking genre assumptions, agreed-to setting/situation, even the social contract to not be a dick to each other. There are also virtue-driven players I can't connect with: studiously hewing to their play goals even when the system actively punishes them, and sometimes others, for doing so.

    There are also game systems I can't connect to for similar reasons, although I find myself liking the creative constraints and inputs generated by more market-driven games like BW, PDQ# and, to a lesser degree, Fate. I'd go so far as to say that any RPG that relies solely on virtuous play -- you play a certain way because playing a certain way is its own reward -- is more often not going to excite me.

    \n Well, in a number of games, the focus is primarily narrative, but there are still gamey things going on too. Roleplay in the story is the primary activity, but it's not freeform -- the rules inform the fiction, and the fiction informs the rules.

    So one of the ways that the rules inform the fiction is when they give power to the players or take it away. In the beginning of a session of the Dresden Files, for instance, your powerful wizard may start out with fewer Fate points, which means that you need to get into trouble in order to earn more of them, building them up in order to succeed later. Once you have lots of Fate points, you can use them to gain a significant advantage toward the climax of the story. That sort of building tension more and more all the way up to a climax where it is released is a pattern of fiction, and the economy of aspects and Fate points helps recreate that in a satisfying way.

    So from one perspective, the players are trying to do their best to succeed in a tactical sort of sense, building up points now so they can succeed later. But this same tactical choice leads to a satisfying narrative experience as well. The game design of currency is support to reinforce that sort of thing in different ways in different games.

    Keys, as another example, reward you with experience for doing a simple thing, more for doing a dangerous thing, and a lot for making a big narrative change in your character -- the number of experience points you gain is simply a way of marking how fast your character is growing, whether it's a slow and steady buildup of growth, or a quick and speedy dash to excellence with lots of drastic changes along the way. In systems with keys, both of these are viable options and advancement is designed not to imbalance the system greatly.

      \nI don't want to claim any sort of precedence or innovation for TSoY in this - not the least because Heroquest did this thing years earlier, but also because it's pretty obvious that the things done by TSoY were already done for years by people playing roleplaying games. The most that can be said for TSoY in this regard is that it's more coherent as a text regarding the purpose and technique of its XP system; it's easy to say that "we played Rolemaster in 1992 and it was just like that", but that just means that your local application of the game was like this. With TSoY (and several earlier games, such as Heroquest) these techniques are not merely local applications and preferences of individual GMs, but the way the game text has been written.

      The TSoY XP system is a pacing device because it tells you when your character changes. To become a master of Zu your character needs an Advance, which equals (usually) five experience points. When your barbaric character washes from a shipwreck to a Zaru village and lives with the locals, coming to appreciate their way of life, the XP system tells you when and how the character can integrate aspects of the culture to himself. And when he leaves the gentle yet naive Zaru behind, we can look at the character sheet and see what impact this episode in the hero's life had on him. The XP system very concretely paces these changes in character, as the system tells you when you have earned the privilege of making permanent changes to the character; you the player can't just decide that now my character will learn the secrets of Zaru culture, but rather the system tells you when you've earned the right to this interesting new character identity as a half-Zaru. The overall character development system also provides a framework for the end-point of this development, the Transcendence, which is where the character's story ends.

      Now, most any game's XP system can be interpreted as a pacing device and as a system of developing character identity. After all, in most games you specifically do get XP at some rate that limits your character development, and it can also be said that this character development defines and refines the character's identity. What is different in TSoY is that the pacing of character development is much more flexible and controlled - it's all about goals you set yourself, and the speed of XP gain can shift dramatically compared to most games - and of course the XP is specifically gained by dramatic development instead of facing challenges, which ties the XP gain into the XP spend as your character gets provoked to spend XP in the process of gaining XP. What is also different is that the "character crunch" or "kewl powerz" in TSoY are specifically and intentionally created to be tools of expressing and defining character identity instead of tactical tools of bad-assery like character development options are in almost all traditional games. The difference is subtle, but it's real: when we define and describe a character identity framework such as a culture or occupation in TSoY, the description strives to pinpoint the ways in which this character identity is different and interesting instead of trying to give a balanced yet exciting set of ass-kicking options like is more typical of traditional design. Specifically, TSoY avoids dramatically empty crunch; generally speaking all crunch strives to mean something about your character in the fiction instead of just being another +2 to brawling.

      What the above ultimately means is that in TSoY your character learns the magical language of Zu foremost because he is given the opportunity and he appreciates the culture that comes along with the skill, rather than because the player read about Zu from the rulebook and wants to add this facet to his character. A pretty good comparison in this regard are D&D spells, which are almost completely culturally neutral in the game's setting: a wizard chooses his spells like a craftsman chooses his tools, and the fact that he happens to have Fireball instead of Haste does not say anything about him dramatically. (Necromancy is an exception, incidentally - if your character is willing to animate zombies, that certainly says something about him culturally.) This same phenomenon tends to be true down the line; for example, your superpowers in Vampire are typically static indicators of how your character kicks ass in practical terms, while those same powers rarely say anything about who your character is, what he believes in or how he's interesting as a dramatic actor. In TSoY it's virtually impossible to pick a set of kewl powerz for your character without getting a hefty chunk of cultural stuff to go with it; in this regard the game is similar to Glorantha, which also works from the assumption that if you can fly, then you'll also necessarily have some dramatic religious convictions or such in your backpack.

      You'll note that the above difference in approach is more about the content of the game's crunch sheets than about the immediate experience system. As I said above, I think that the more important difference in TSoY's experience system comes from how you spend the points than how you gain them. The two facets are connected, though, as the attitude and motivation the player has for gaining the points is obviously affected by how they are spent: if you're gaining the points to make your character more powerful, that's a different thing than if you're gaining the points to make him more defined. I don't find the player choice nearly so important in the Key system as the fact that the Keys give XP based on dramatic pacing, allowing you more character development when the character goes through dramatic events. Whitewolf games in general tend to be full of these sorts of really cool "missed opportunities" in that they talked the talk, but largely failed to walk the walk when it comes to successfully implementing game procedures and advice that would have made playing the struggle with the beast within or whatever a given game's idea was a reality. It's a very interesting historiographical question as to whether WW missed the boat in this regard because the game texts and design were limited by current presumptions, or because the ideas were simply so new and radical that only a minority of the audience could and would shift from their party-based superhero paradigm into playing towards the ideas presented in these games. I never was a player of WW games or most of '90s design in general myself, so I don't have the immediate textual expertise to hazard a serious guess on the matter.

Scene framing, bang-based scenario design, dramatically oriented NPC play, relationship maps and many other techniques are all things that I'm absolutely sure were around in local play cultures in the '80s already. At least part of the reason for why this stuff never made it into the actual game texts seems to be because of respect for the GM's independence and the traditional form of a rpg text: a roleplaying game in the mainstream tradition consists of a character modeling section, a task resolution section, perhaps some monster and reward lists and GMing instructions.       \n I really appreciate the insightful conversation going on in this thread, but two very simple points sum up the original post for me. These are courtesy of Luke Crane and Jared Sorensen, though the interpretations in this context are my own and you can't blame them for those.

      Game design is mind control. Game mechanics are there to ensure that everyone's behaviour is in-line with what the game requires, not what they'd usually do or have been conditioned to through long-term play of that game or similar games. The corollary is that if everyone's behaviour is already in-line with what the game requires - usually due to long-term conditioning - then game mechanics are not required. Sometimes, when a group has played together for a long time and are happy with their experience doing so, they'll try a new game and their conditioning clashes with the mind control imposed by the new mechanics. This makes things feel clunky and stilted. Perhaps the group ought not to play this game, because it doesn't serve their collective agenda; perhaps it is simply a matter of requiring practice with the game to reverse the prior conditioning.

      but:

      Role-playing games are Games. This is contentious, of course, but if you agree with the idea that when we play this game, the elements we manipulate through play ought to be (directly or indirectly, a la the Fruitful Void idea) the thing the game is about, and if you're playing a game that's focused on the idea of telling a dynamic story then Keys/Aspects/Beliefs etc make far more sense as play elements than XP or hit points. As mentioned repeatedly above, Keys/Aspects/Beliefs etc govern the transformation of character personality and traits, which is an integral part of dramatic stories. Getting more XP in Pathfinder will transform your character's competence on an ever-increasing axis, but any transformations of the character's personhood is not affected at all - the only reason that would ever happen is due to a long-standing agreement within your group to add these elements to add flavour to the core game.

      I think it's frequently overlooked that mechanics such as Keys/Aspects/Beliefs and many other common "story gamish" elements do clash with the conditioning imposed by the vast majority of game designs, and that in order to get full enjoyment out of a particular game you ought to practice practice practice. As a case in point, playing Dogs in the Vineyard with people for the first time often feels very clumsy, as people realize they can call on all kinds of traits to get more dice and "win", resulting in a contrived narrative. After a while of playing that way, people realize that they're putting the cart before the horse, and instead start shaping the narrative such that they'll be put into positions where their traits make narrative sense. After that, they realize that when a trait isn't bang-on for a given conflict, they shouldn't use it - not only does it create a worse narrative if they do, but the fallout they collect when they "lose" also serves to improve the narrative. The reason that's not intuitively obvious is because the game's explicit admonition that characters ought to be retired is seen as a fail condition by 99% of players, and it takes actually experiencing the game in action for an extended amount of time to understand that the game is not "about" becoming more competent at saving the towns from evil.

      I'm still plowing through Apocalypse World, but one thing stuck out at me as specifically addressing some of the concerns above: while the central mechanic is the execution of particular "moves", you are never to mention the move by name. The game text will refer to "acting under fire" as a mechanical um, thing, but the resulting narrative is not intended to be "you're refusing to provide the night vision goggles despite being seduced? well, you're acting under fire now" but rather "Olaf is distressed, tossing and turning in his sleep, guilt-ridden by his failure to provide for the needs of his more than generous lover, fearing that she will leave him soon to continue his bleak existence, alone". I suspect you won't get to that later state without practice though, much like it takes time to go from "you hit for 5 hit points" to "your sword finds a chink in his armour and cuts a deep gash in his chest".

      The game fiction tells us how to engage the mechanics, and the mechanics can both inform us of outcomes and inform our decisions about where to drive the fiction. That's pretty awesome.

      \n I think the key thing here is the acting as a bridge between Gamist styles and Narrativist styles. On reflection, I've come around to thinking that XP based rewards are probably a little too coercive compelling.

      I really like Gamist play, and I really like Narrativist play (Sim play, not so much.) If I had to say it, I'm "more" Gamey. I think I *enjoy* Narrativist/Story-based play/RP more. But when push comes to shove, that Gamist part of my brain is always going to be a little louder, I'm always going to feel that little twinge of "I know this is dumb, but..." If the game is very Story-focused, it often won't have any sort of punitive mechanics for doing a demonstrably dumb (but in character/story) thing. But if there's much Gamist crunch going on, that little guy in the back of my brain is going to tell me exactly what the "right" thing the rules (or the situation and the GMs expected reaction) are telling me to do is at any point in time (especially at character creation.) That's usually enough cognitive dissonance that the Gamist side wins. So I'm unlikely to play a minotaur with a deathwish if the system is likely to kill me, or to skip the planning stage of a shadowrun and rush in, etc.

      These Aspect/Key/Belief mechanics take the edge off for me, and placate that Gamist voice in the back of my head. Now I can happily take the "Enough planning!" Aspect, say to hell with it and charge on in. Gamist voice gets a cookie, I get Story-fun. If it bites me in the ass, I still have a FP, the story is moving forward, everything is good. If someone else at the table doesn't have that little Gamist voice, we've leveled the playing field. Now a much broader range of the Gamist-Narrativist spectrum can play happily at the same table. *That* is what I like about these mechanics, they avoid some of that risk-aversion that's inherent in human psychology.

      The risk is that they might take it too far, to the point of compelling the player, rather than just removing a punishment for a non-optimal action. Now that you bring it up, I do think that XP/Permanent benefits might be too far on the compulsion side. XP are just too big a cookie for Gamist-voice to ignore. I know in the AW one-shot I was in at PAX, I kind of felt obligated to Open Myself to the Maelstrom a few times because my Weird was checked.

      \n I once heard a story about a manager trying to improve production in his factory; he'd tried everything and nothing was working. One day he came down to the floor and wrote a giant '3' in chalk on the floor. Word eventually got around that this was the number of engines produced by the previous shift. Apparently this was enough to engage the workers' spirit of competition, and the next day, the day shift produced 4 engines. The manager came down, erased the '3', and wrote '4' on the floor.

      It's a bit like a pinball game - the points go up. What's the point of points? Nothing, they're useless - they're not an inherent incentive, except that they give you a means of measuring yourself. If you, for the sheer joy of mastery, decide to engage the system, the points give you feedback on how you're doing.

      So keys are a first step to this - the incentives they lead to may not be inherently valuable (particularly if you were already a pinball master who learned on machines that don't show a score), but they can give you a means of measuring whether what you're doing is what the designer intended, and helps align a group of people around a particular way of playing.
