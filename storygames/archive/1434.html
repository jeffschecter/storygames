    framing a RPG in the guise of episodic television

    This is also a really striking tool for thinking about the structure of your game.

    Like, you can start in like Ep3 and say, "Okay, this episode is gonna be all high-tension conflict stuff; we don't need to do a lot of characterisation because we got to know the characters in 1 and 2. So let's cut straight to the action." And it works. Then later, when you're feeling like a slower episode, you say, "Hey, let's play our pilot now. I'm sure we all have really good ideas about how our characters work in our heads, so now we can let the viewers in on some of that..."

    edit: Um, in other words, PTA is awesome.

    Ronaldo,

    Obviously a lot of people love PTA, as you can see above. As to your specific point about replayability, I have a few comments.

    First, consider that if you get only, say, 2 series out of the game, it's a good buy. I mean, for the same price as a big scenario for another game, you get two whole series. Let's say that's 4 players (including the producer), playing short seasons of 5 sessions, one session per episode, 3 hours per session. That's fast, but let's suppose for argument's sake. That's 120 man hours of fun, total. At $18 for the book. That's fifteen cents per man hour of fun.

    And it's all fun.

    Compare that to a movie at about 5 dollars per man hour of fun on average. It can't be cost you're worried about. For most RPG players, the real "cost" is how much time it takes that could be used for other games instead. And with PTA, if you only play twice, you can move on to something else.

    Now, that said, I can't think of any reason why, once you've run two series, you wouldn't want to run more. I mean, given that each series is completely different from the next, why would replayability get old? Put another way, what could possibly give you the idea that the game will get old to play? What are you seeing in it? The low number of rules? D&D has lots of replayability because...of all the different monsters? Is that the argument?

    As long as you're creative, I think PTA will provide loads of fun. And at a tiny price. What do you have to lose?

    Mike
