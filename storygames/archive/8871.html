\n I've just tried with some friends Dogs in the Vineyard for the first time. My problem is that this happened:

- After a conflict the player rolls the fall-out dice (all d4s).
- The player sometimes gets long-term fall-out.
- I ask the player to choose from the list.
- The player always chooses 1d4 trait or +1d4 on any d4 trait.
- At the next conflict the player always "takes the blow" with these d4 traits and gets many-many d4 fall-out dice.
- The conflict doesn't esacalate, because the many d4 traits are enough to save the high rolls for raises.
- The player gets even more d4 fall-out dice, wich generates even more probably d4 traits.
- Perpetuum Mobile.

Okay, I understand that this is a "hippy story-game", but I think it's too much to expect the players to choose -1d6 stat instead of +1d4 trait if the above cycle works. Why should (s)he?
Am I doing something wrong? Should I choose the fall-out result instead of the players? Should I be more restrictive when accepting the usage of traits to make them hard to use the d4 traits? But that would defeat the purpost that d4s are causing problems.

For now I'm planning that next time I'm choosing the fall-out for the players, most probably by rolling a die or two to be fair.

I'd appreciate any helpful input. Thanks.

    \n On the flipside, about always choosing the +1d4 over the -1d6 fallout: it works differently for different groups. For some, choosing one of those options is a form of expression (trumping the need for optimal choices); I'm the sort of player who may pick a -1d6 because I like what that represents to me.

    But, the last long-term game I played, most people given the choice would pick the +1d4. I wasn't fine with that at first; eventually I was.

    There is the theory that continual d4 traits around is a self-balancing thing, i.e. that rolled d4's have low value and are likely only used for the character to "take the blow", i.e. cede fictional stakes and stay in conflicts longer than they should, which will create problems if they're responding to raises that are more than just talking. I'm not sure that all players are convinced by this logic; in some sense, having those extra dice around can't hurt.

    So on one hand, harder raises (either because they are escalated, or because they have heavier fictional content) will disuade players from taking the blow. Meanwhile, you can either be comfortable with players taking the +1d4 fallout, or you can hack the game to change the options. (If you want fallout to always have some negative connotation, you could have players take a negative fallout trait, and take on a +1d4 trait if they want though I think many Dogs players would disagree with such a hack. Like any hacks, use only as directed, YMMV, IMO, etc.)

    \n As other people have said, it's what the NPC is doing to you that determines the size of the fallout dice. If they're shooting you, and raise with an 16, and you see that with 1 2 3 3 3 4, that's going to be 6d10 fallout dice, not 6d4.

    Likewise, remember, even if you're just talking, Talking the Blow doesn't mean a clever refutation of somebody's argument or even shrugging it off. It means the attack leaves a mark, so to speak. So when your Aunt is spitting in your face and calling you a worthless disgrace to the Order, are you really going to take the blow? What about when a sorcerer who's packing 5d10 demonic influence dice whispers in your ear to reject the King of Life and all his empty promises, with dark tendrils of hate curling around your mind, and raises with a 20?

    Also, regarding fallout: "All of these choices you get to make, whatever you choose, you have to justify it out of the events of the conflict." You don't just get to add "Awesome 1d4" to your character sheet because your aunt spat in your face and you stood there shocked and hurt. It needs to be an appropriate new trait, and if you can't justify it to your fellow players, well, you (or any other player) is within their rights to ask them to justify it better, or pick something else.

You have to look at it through the lens of progressing sin to play it "right" though. The simplest, most basic sin in the book is Pride. Think about it. The "bad guys" don't just want to do their thing and maybe get sanction for it. They want to prove to the Dogs that they are right and the Dogs are wrong. They want to flout their superiority and their transcendence above the Dog's Authority. Every single character who is part of the "problem" in a Town started by being too Proud to submit themselves to the King of Life. Think about that mentality, then play it. You are right, almost every person in a Town just wants to live their life and get on with their daily activities. EXCEPT for those people that are part of the problem. They want to prove they are right and get acknowledgment for their innovation or revolution. Of course, they may be sly about it at first. But once the cat is out of the bag, they are going to push until the Dogs leave or accept their moral, mental and/or physical superiority.

That is an awesome feeling (as a player and a GM) when a character hits that Clint-level of badassery that he can win a gun fight without drawing his gun! But, it is never going to happen if you aren't willing to take responsibility for your part of the story and push the conflict.

    \n Welkerfan, d4s can keep you in conflicts longer and sometimes let you win, but that comes at the cost of Taking a lot of Blows, with attendant fallout. Sometimes they don't even help you stay in those conflicts, as your opponent will often be able to Reverse the Blow and get to double up his big dice while exhausting your small ones.

    Consider that on average, you need to use 3d4 (expected value 7.5) to see 2d6 (EV 7), and 5d4 (EV 12.5) to see 2d10 (EV 11) \u2014 the fallout builds up quickly. On top of that, your opponent will be able to see even your best possible d4-powered raises (that is, 8) with 1/8 of his d8s and fully 30% of his d10s! Against Demonic Influence and other d10 traits, d4s are serious baggage. As I know from experience, a trait like "I know myself unfit to lead the Faithful 4d4" is the kind of thing that's likely to get a Dog killed.
