\n No, this isn't about dropping acid...but close. What game was the one you read that expanded your mind about what games could be? I'm talking like mindblown.

I would say mine was Fiasco. I got into pen n paper/tabletop gaming late, just the last four years, and was burnt out on the tactics of D&D 4e. Then I read Fiasco and it changed my life, leading to so many other kindred games. I just finished reading Apocalypse World and I have taken the chapter on MC-ing to heart, both in games and in my professional life (I'm a teacher).

So what game was it that did that to you?

    \n I actually experienced my students (9-10 year olds) go through a sort of revelation moment in the classroom regarding games. During the day before our Thanksgiving break (rather loose in terms of cracking open textbooks on this day) I pulled aside a group of four while the others caught up on work, played your typical educational games, read at their desks, etc. and played 6X The One Page Role-Playing System. I used some of the ideas of Vincent Baker's MC-ing where they established how they knew each other and what their world was like. It was the MOST engaged I had seen them in pretty much anything, their eyes were wide and they got increasingly excited the more they realized how free they were in building this world and the story.

    It'd be cool if we all said a couple concise sentences about why these games blew our minds.
    \nI suppose that would make more sense.

    AD&D was the first. Mind blown. You could pretend to be a warrior or a wizard or an elf or...

    Champions wasn't D&D. By then I was getting tired of some of the ways D&D was doing things. But it wasn't just not-D&D. You got rewarded (Disadvantages) for giving the GM and the other players story hooks! You could decide what your character could do. There were tactics and story and you could knock an enemy out without killing them! And then Fantasy Hero and Danger International and other HERO System games came along and cemented it into my head.

    Talisman wasn't overly mind blowing on its own. But you know how you're in the endgame, and pretty much everyone can throw spells, and everyone is drawing and throwing spells back and forth? Poof, you're a toad. Counterspell. Now you're a toad. I thought that that spell-throwing minigame was at least as fun as the boring roll-and-move parts that led up to it, so I tried for a few years to design a game based around characters throwing spells at one another. The mechanics never really gelled for me though, and then a few years later Richard Garfield went and designed...

    Magic: the Gathering. It was about ten times better than whatever I might have ended up designing would have been. And it was another new genre of game. There was a universe -- hell, a multiverse! -- in these cards.

    Nomic is a game that's all about changing the rules. Kinda like MtG but different. Mind thoroughly blown. Nomic led me to Fluxx, which led me to...

    Icehouse. If you didn't see those brightly colored pyramids and not immediately start designing games with them, I don't want to be your friend anymore.

    Primetime Adventures was to me the first kind of game that could generate Moose in the City as an artifact of play. It was my intro into this world of dirty commie hippie games.

    Fiasco was awesome. Mind blown yet again. I sat in on a playtest at Andy's house in December of 2008, though I was the sixth out of five. I was happy enough to just watch anyway. I knew I was going to want it as soon as I could get it. (Oh, and @Andy, I'm back in Raleigh in a couple of weeks if you're interested in meeting up.)

    Apocalypse World was yet another new way of looking at RPGs. The players rolling I'd seen before, but it just worked. Every part of it just worked.

    \n In more or less chronological order...

    Hero Wars blew my mind by defining intangibles like relationships and personality traits as more mechanically important than physical gear, and for fully embracing the inherent abstraction of hit points and making it an explicit pacing mechanism for all forms of conflict.

    Anima Prime blew my mind again by giving me specific terms to define and talk about aspects of the group's social contract (this was especially striking because I'd recently had a game fall apart when a GM assumed something I thought of as Background was a Link, which took the game places I was not interested in).

    The Mountain Witch was also fairly revelatory for the way it used a relatively railroady quest structure as a familiar backdrop to allow the group to experiment with improv and fishing.

    Weapons of the Gods' Chi Conditions were a shockingly elegant way to handle advantages and disads (and from an in-character standpoint too!), which also mechanically empowered social characters by letting them create and manipulate them. (I imagine this space would be devoted to Aspects if I'd read Fate before WotG)

    Smallville managed to take a license I hated and a style of game I hated even more (PvP) and create one of my favorite games out of it. It is an amazing piece of genre emulation, and extra credit for being a genre I hadn't seen handled well mechanically.

    Tenra Bansho Zero would have utterly crushed my mind if I'd gotten to read it when Andy was originally talking it up on RPG.net, but even a decade+ later it compares excellently with current English game design.

    Microscope is also genuinely A New Thing from top to bottom; I find it hard to even talk about it in the context of RPGs.\n

    I want to say Champions, but not for any positive reason, I'm afraid. It took probably ten years of playing Champions for me to finally have the revelation that effects-based point buy was a horribly seductive trap that had no place in a roleplaying game.

    \n Paranoia -- That player vs player conflict can be fun

    Amber Diceless -- That player vs player can end friendships

    Vampire tM -- That a relationship map, motivations for every NPCs, and few locations is all I need in prep to run a great game. Then toss the players in as a de-stablizing force and it almost plays it's self. I GMed this game for multi-year long campaign and learning to stream line prep time and improvise was very important. I wish Play Unsafe would have been available back then.

    Sorcerer -- not so much the game, but Ron's ideas of system matters and drive with bangs gave me a new way of thinking about the games I was running. It was also my gateway game into the indie/forge game scene.

    Full Light Full Steam -- Great guide on how to set up situations and then play them out. I still follow a cross of this and Apocalypse World's fronts when setting up a short few session game. The only thing I like to add is a relationship map.

    Dogs in the vineyards -- Escalations are great, and how to set up a situation and let it play out.

    Fate -- That characters don't need stats, they can be a collection of phrases, and these phrases can have mechanical effect.

    Risus -- That a super simple game can do everything and enhance the fun.

    Apocalypse world -- it coded fide a method of GMing that I was slowly discovering for myself, and then shown a spot light on it. The ides of moves, and they happen when the players do something specific in the fiction, really helped me bring the fiction and story to the front in all my games.

    Don't rest your Head -- That a dice mechanic can really effect the feel of the game. The dice mechanic was able to bring apprehension to the players something most horror games never did. Also that a good meta game can be enjoyable and enhance the main game.
