    I wrote it last year and I think that talking about your own game is frowned upon here, but I'll try to keep it relevant to your request.

    You play people who happen to meet in a nightclub in Miami Beach called the Plumbago Lounge. You don't have any special powers; you just come from different wealth and social strata. You have Desires that you either suppress or indulge, you develop relationships with other people, and outside the club you have your life to carry on. There aren't any restrictions on your actions; it has a combat system but is designed to always carry a risk of PC death no matter how powerful you are.

    \n I wanted to do The Wire, but I also wanted to have my own setting. I worked in Ft. Lauderdale the year before and was inspired by the scene in South Beach.

    It has a GM, all very traditional and simulationist.

    You have a single numeric stat that is always good to have more of, named Discipline (average is about 3). You declare an action and the GM sets the difficulty. If you have enough Discipline, you succeed. If not, then you can either give up or take a risk. Put one or more Discipline stones in a cup and the GM adds stones (of a different color) equal to the deficit. If you draw your color, you succeed. If not, you fail and lose the Discipline you risked, which keeps you from suppressing Desires or even making enough money to pay the bills.

    The idea was to have a mechanic that wasn't very thrilling. You can use the same system with Fastlane's roulette wheel, but then there is this awesome roulette wheel in the center of the table that you really want to use.

    By the way... Fastlane, another game with no supernatural or heroic elements.

    There is an AP, but I never converted it from Audacity's lossless format. Even in MP3, I would expect a big overuse bill from my ISP.

    I think linking would violate the mores. It's for sale in PDF, you see, and giving it away undercuts the distributor who took a chance on me.

    \n The way I play Dogs usually is pretty mundane. All the outre demonic elements are just social manifestations and paradigm talk, they don't really exist.

    A Dirty World by Greg Stolze is a really nifty Noir game with absolutely no supernatural or heroic elements. (Interestingly the first thing most people ask about it is how to add supernatural elements.) It focuses on the human dimensions of the protagonists and puts their very nature on the line in order to replace the tension and thrill often gained by introducing supernatural in other games. And yea, there aren't even really any heroes here.

    Jason mentioned Jeep stuff. Doubt being a great example -- its about if a couple breaks up due to the pressure of doubt on their relationship, or manages to stick together. Nothing supernatural at all, but fascinating scenario construction.

    Dust Devils, of course. The "devil" is figurative, and this game is about westerns being their human issue best. It can verge towards heroic, I suppose, but the truth is it tends to play more like Deadwood than Fistfull of Dollars.

    PTA is value neutral on this one. You can totally do it, but there is no real reason to do so. Super or not, its all TV.

    Hmm... oh, yea, of course: Spione. Yes, there are spys, but these aren't James Bond. These are real people in over their heads in a really nasty world, trying to decide if they can be human beings or not.

    Others have mentioned Breaking the Ice, Shooting the Moon, Grey Ranks, Steal Away Jordan, and Carry.

    There are also some historical games that do this: Flashing Blades was mentioned, and I think some of FGU's other games probably fit the mold -- but its been ages since I looked at any of them. Privateers and Gentlemen, I think, and maybe Merc were right on the edge of heroic, but based in a sense of realism that may qualify them. I seem to remember that Sengoku has a "real history" mode of play. (It also has chanabara level play, but the option is there.)
