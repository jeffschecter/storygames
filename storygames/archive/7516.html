    \n My Life with Master has been translated into Finnish by Eero Tuovinen and his brothers (Arkenstone), and into Italian by Claudia Cangini and Michele Gelli (Narrattiva).

    The two translation arrangements are the same in all key particulars:\n
    1. They translate the game and do a quantity-limited print run. (Based on expectations for sales they each chose to do 200 copies.) Additional print runs require a new contract.
    2. My name goes on the cover as the author, and their translation has to be faithful to my text. If they feel additional rules clarification or play examples are important, they have to be clearly indicated as text not of my authorship. (I suggested achieving this via footnotes. Eero did it via grey boxes, and I didn't have a problem with that. And actually, I don't think Michele and Claudia did this at all, other than including an introduction written by Michele.)
    3. They do their own cover, their own layout, and their own illustrations. (I don't own rights to Colin's or Will's artwork for anything other than the English language version of My Life with Master, and for promoting the game and Half Meme Press.)
    4. They make a single up front payment to me. It is a percentage of the expected gross revenue from the selling of the whole print run.
    5. I get a few copies of the final product.
    \nSo, no rights to selling a pdf version, or for doing an adapted translation.

    I've had a number of translation requests and they're almost always laden with ongoing nuisances. Lots of guys want to do pdf-only translations and make monthly payments to me for pdf sales, for instance. I don't want a fucking marriage. I don't want monthly accounting. I don't want the anxiety of having to track whether the other guy has paid me this month or not. I want someone to do the translation as a project. I want them to be smart enough to know how many copies they want to print. I want them to pay me up front. And if they do a great job, then I'm going to have no problem doing another contract with them.

    Paul

    Both The Shadow of Yesterday and Donjon have been translated. TSOY's in Finnish and Spanish and German, and Donjon's in Spanish.

    \n

    Here's how my process worked:

    \n
      \n
    • An interested party emails me.
      • \n
    • I say yes, with the caveat that there should be some free, non-commercial version of the translated text, and I should get a copy or two.
      • \n
    \n

    In some cases we move to step 2:

    \n
      \n
    • The interested party asks me how much money I want.
      • \n
    • I say none.
      • \n
    • Repeat until I am understood.
      • \n
    \n

    I know that's not for everyone - I'm not judging on the money thing; people got to get paid - but I figure it this way. I can't write any of those languages. (I can read Spanish, but that's it.) Whether it's translated or not, I make the same amount of money. Another culture benefits by getting an RPG. Everyone wins.

    \n As a commercial proposition? Always make sure that your up-front royalty is enough to keep you happy, even if you don't make another penny, and don't expect any more. Anything more than that is then a bonus. Ask them to account once every six months; really that should be enough. Give the translation rights a guaranteed fixed term, and then allow a notice period to terminate the agreement. Show the translation to a friendly native speaker who understands RPGs to make sure they haven't screwed around with it. Retain approval. Don't grant secondary rights unless they are paid for.

    \n Paul and Clinton described our preferred process. Insofar as hobby publishing goes (which translating to Finnish very much is), a fixed up-front payment per-printing makes sense. The only things that come to mind in addition...\n
      \n
    • Get a contract in writing as soon as possible. Some designers have a distressing habit of backing out of a deal for the most spurious reasons, which can get just brutal if you've already sunk a hundred hours of work on the project at the time he decides that he doesn't want you messing around with his game after all. Not that I'd probably do much with a contract, even, but it might offer some incentive to the designer to take the work of others seriously. This is, historically, the single greatest problem I've faced when doing this stuff.
      • \n
    • Get the vital data, such as Paypal address, snail mail address and anything else you need for logistics out of the guy as soon as possible. Trying to get some of these guys to answer straightforward questions (or even acknowledge that they've got email) is like drawing teeth, and sometimes they disappear in ways that seem rather improbable - we've still to even pay for one project where the designer disappeared between signing the contract and providing his Paypal address for paying him. Just flat out disappeared from the face of the earth, insofar as I could discern.
      • \n
    • Have enough time between finishing the translation and printing it for approval rounds. We haven't really messed this up majorly yet, but I'd like for the designer to have ample time to suggest anything and have a real chance at getting his corrections; in our style of translation the designer really has just as much of a hand in the book design and such as he wants to.

    Posted By: Grant DavisI would get it verified, then buy the translation and publish it myself. -Grant
    \nEarly on after publishing My Life with Master I had an offer from a German who'd translated the game for her own personal use to sell me her translation. I wasn't ready to do it then, and in retrospect I'm glad I didn't. I wouldn't have been successful with a German edition. I have it now on authority I trust that Germans aren't particularly resistant to buying English language RPGs. And I've heard from Spaniards, Portuguese, and Italians that they learned English as part of their involvement in the roleplaying hobby. The authority I trust has run lots of convention games of My Life with Master in Germany, and informs me the actual resistance is to using PayPal to order games from overseas, something that would certainly have been part of my plan for a German language edition if I'd done one.

    The reason to license a translation is because the translator knows whether the English language text is actually a barrier to the game in their home country (or they wouldn't be soliciting you for translation rights), and because indie games succeed from the way they're actively represented to the market. I couldn't have participated in the German RPG community and represented a German language My Life with Master to German gamers the way Eero represents his Finnish translation to Finnish gamers and the way Claudia and Michele represent their Italian translation to gamers in Italy.

    Paul

    \n In response to the very strong statements that the original not be changed in any way...
    \n
    \nI understand a creator's expectation that their work remain true to them and true to itself. Just dealing with language, though, it's impossible to avoid some change. An overly literal translation will change the tone of the text, for example. And when there are no one-to-one correspondences between words, the translation of the names of key game concepts can, unavoidably, change their flavor.
    \n
    \nI am wondering about RPG-specific issues in translation. A game that was originally targeted for one audience could have cultural assumptions built into it that would make the game difficult for someone from another culture. Aren't there cases where some kind of cultural translation would be necessary?
    \n
    \nFor an example of this, I will mention the French translation of Over the Edge, which I only know about through the review by Philippe Tromeur (rpg.net review of Over the Edge). This edition has made major changes to the setting, apparently to retain the necessary suspension of disbelief for a European Francophone audience. It appears to be a very successful translation of the game, as opposed to a translation of the text.
    \n
    \nThis could be a very rare case of a game author willing to accept such changes, willing to work very closely with the translator and having confidence in the translator based on their reputation and past work.
