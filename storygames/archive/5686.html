\n Dear friends and also John Harper,

I have a metric assload of great headshots of Soviet lady air warriors. They look like this:

I am thinking that, thanks to erratic airbrushing, poor paper and printing quality, and other factors, these will look better as engraved images, like the Wall Street Journal's hedcuts with which you are no doubt familiar. I'm looking for tools and techniques to approximate this for a bunch of photos, using Photoshop CS2. Any help would be appreciated! I'm also open to other suggestions regarding a way to manipulate these images that will account for their low source quality and make them look sharp and awesome.

Also, if you think you've got the chops, let's see what you can do, personally! The original for the photo above is here, scanned as a 2MB 600 dpi .tif without any manipulation at all.

Again, thanks.

    \n Converting to bitmap is a good way to go.

    First, isolate the figure from the background, and mess with Image > Adjustments > Levels... until you have something like this.



    Duplicate the image. Then, Image > Mode > Bitmap. Choose "Halftone Screen" in the Method pulldown. You then set the Frequency and Angle of the halftone lines. I chose 125 frequency and -30 angle. Shape = Line.

    After the halftone is converted, copy it and paste it over your image above, and set the halftone layer to Multiply. Now, adjust the opacity of the grayscale image beneath to around 25% or so. Now you have a halftone line image with just a faint hint of the grayscale image beneath, providing a little more contrast. It should look something like this.



    Not perfect, but not a bad start. Play around with the halftone settings for some very different results (frequency makes a huge difference). Also note that once you do the conversion to bitmap, scaling the image will destroy the effect. So convert to the exact final size you want.
