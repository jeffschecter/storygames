For @vgunn!

This is something I haven\u2019t been really interested in a long time but in the 90:s we were super into it because it was all the difference there really was between the various 90:s games.

Static linear die

1d20 (D&D, Pendragon), 1d100 (CoC), 1d6 (Gumshoe)

Dynamic linear die

The Window, Savage Worlds

Static pool, summing

3d6 (GURPS), 2d6 (Travelller, AW), 4dF (Fudge)

Dynamic pool, summing

xd6 (Star Wars d6, Over the Edge)

Dynamic pool, successes

Vampire, Star Wars FFG, Burning Wheel, Hollow Earth Exploration

Dynamic pool, best of

MHR, Cthulhu Dark

Static pool, best of

Otherkind, advantage/disadvantage, ability score generation

Weird symbols

  • Static (Fudge)
  • Only few configurations (Hero Quest boardgame)
  • Dynamic (Star Wars FFG, Descent boardgame)

My strong preference is static linear. As a teen I parroted a lot of the pro-curve argument I had heard (in the GURPS camp and other places) but the more I kept thinking about it the more value I put in the clear and intuitive probability that static linear gives. Like 1d6 as per Gumshoe is actually pretty sweet & simple. 1d20 is good because it has finer granularity while still being a single die.

I\u2019ve seen so many disastrous & mathematically iffy rule designs coming from dice pool camps over the years. Like try running a fight using the GURPS Ultra Lite rules, it goes on for ages.

That said, I happen to have a very soft spot for a static or limited array of weird symbols, like Fudge or Hero Quest boardgame. I know that goes against what I said about prefering static linear.

I love Fudge dice because while they do hide the math, I know the [1,4,10,16,19,16,10,4,1]/81 chain by heart, bonuses are often small and doesn\u2019t throw the probability off by too much etc (or so I thought before Fate started throwing around +4 and +6 here and there). Like I see Fudge dice as essentially a way to have a low-but-knowable percentage chance of outlier results but hidden and unobtrusive math. The triangle tables that were popular in the Fate 3 era was a great innovation as well. If I were to make my own game I\u2019d base it on 4dF.

Similarly board games like Hero Quest, you can learn your probability chains if you want to design for it, but if you just want to use theready made stuff you don\u2019t have to spend time with numbers and adding, just count up the skulls&shields.

Again, what we learned during the system matters era is that this dice system design is such a miniscule part of what makes a good game. But a nice dice system is like a comfortable sword, you just want to use it.

    IMO these three games have the least bad dice systems:

    ACKS
    1d20 vs [your throw value + their AC]. Is good because simple, linear, fine-grained, roll-high, fortune-at-the-end, intuitive prob calculations (+1 here and there is 5% here and there). Is bad b/c somewhat high&hard-to-remember numbers
    Fudge
    4dF moves your finger on the scale for a word result. Is good because lagom chance of outlier results, low&hidden numbers, small&meaningful steps, flavorful, the best-of-two-worlds between coarse-grained non-outlier results and fine-grained outliner results. Is bad b/c misunderstandable probs (as evidenced by Fate), and hard to make fortune-at-the-end without sacrificing some of the elegance (\u201cYou have Good skill and need Fair so you need a -1 or better\u201d \u2014 wait what happened to my numberless game?)
    Hero Quest board game
    Roll skulls they are canceled by shields. Is good because easy for kids to use, simple, low&hidden numbers. Is bad because weapon&monster design needs to be made by mathematician in order to get some bespoke/designed probs, can\u2019t just toss any number of dice in there for risk of wonky

    \n I definitely agree with mabgriff; One of the things that pisses me off about D&D is when I have a character who is literally the best they can be at a given thing at a given level, and my buddy is playing someone who is pretty bumbling at said thing (Say, +6 with ability score/proficiency vs -1 total). Then the dice are all "Eh, whatevs to your character concepts, you rolled a 4 and he rolled a 14 and he shows you up." This is not precisely a function of linear dice mechanics so much as it is a function of the size of the random range on the d20 (too damn big) vs the amount of differentiation in characters, but it is mitigated by methods that make it less likely.

    I really have no problems with diminishing returns. If people can't be trusted with them, I regard that as their own difficulty.

    I agree that game designers should have a firm grasp on the math of their systems and how it works, but I argue that that is actually awfully easy to get in a "design" way, because you can just sit down, teach yourself Anydice, and ergo, you know how all the dice permutations in your game work and can make sure you file off the weird edge cases (and they honestly have to be pretty weird to damage the game, though it's absolutely possible.) You have months and months of time to figure this stuff out, so unless you've designed some kind of gonzo crazy dice system (and some folks sure have -- games with stuff like making sets out of your rolled dice is impressively obfuscating) you should damn well understand it. This is, in fact, my expectation of ALL games, and I don't think that dice pools vs linear sets meaningfully influences the amount of time required for a DESIGNER to do this. It's a question of whether they care enough to do it, not a question of whether they can. It's very possible to design a game with fundamentally broken mechanics just using a flat d20 plus bonuses methodology if you don't care enough to be rigorous on the design side, and if you do care enough on the design side, then the complexity of your dice mechanic isn't going to impede you.

    tldr: I don't think "it's easier for the designer to understand" is a merit in a dice system at all.

    \n (With respect to probabilities, Burning Wheel is very easy to set Obstacles for. It's simple -- if you're trying to calculate the probability your players will succeed at a roll as an input to which Obstacle you set, you're doing it wrong. The Obstacle is how difficult it is, not how likely a player is to succeed at it, and you should judge difficulty by the examples in the skill lists, not how likely e.g. an expert would Really Be to succeed at the thing and then translate that back to okay 5D vs Ob what)

    A couple of heuristics that can help a lot to have good intuitions about how dice pool probabilities work:
    - the most common result when taking the maximum of, say, 4 identical dice, will be 0.5+4*(range/5) (generalize appropriately)
    - flip an odd number of coins; 50% chance of getting half-round-up heads or more

    Speaking of dice prob calc heuristics, a while back I came up with this:

    OK, so we know that if you want to know the probability of either (or both) .4 and .4, you do 1-(1-.4)\xd7(1-.4) = .64.

    Today I came up with another way of doing it: X ior Y = X\xd7(1-Y)+Y. To me this looks more straightforward somehow. In the example of .4 and .4, it\u2019s .4\xd7.6+.4 = .64

    I like it better not just because it looks more straight forward, but also because you can use reduces & folds with it if you\u2019re doing fp.

    I also have some other ways to make dice pool probability calculation faster.

    Let\u2019s say we have a die with these sides: 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1, 1, 2, I\u2019d represent that as (6 3 1). A normal sixsider, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, is represented as (0 1 1 1 1 1 1).

    Then I made an operator that for every combination of two elements adds their product to the sum of those elements indexes.

    For example, two d6:es added together using this operator is (0 0 1/36 1/18 1/12 1/9 5/36 1/6 5/36 1/9 1/12 1/18 1/36). There are zero ways to get a 0 or a 1 result, and there\u2019s a 1/36 chance of getting a 2 or a 12 result.

    It\u2019s fast because you can do reduces and folds easily. I wanted to know the the probability of getting at least three on seven of the WoD-style (6 3 1) dice from before and it came up right away even on my old slow machine.

    \n I'm not entirely against static linear, but I've finally figured out why I don't agree with your assessment that it's the most intuitive system:

    In my experience as a math teacher, most people have a deeply held expectation that any "random" (read: probabilistic) process will display strong central tendency. They use the word "average" indiscriminately, so that mean, median, and mode are conflated. When they ask "what's the average result on 1d20?" the answer is 10.5, which is both the mean and median. But then they will, in many cases, unconsciously assume it is the mode as well (or, since 10.5 isn't actually in the set of outcomes, that the mode is 10/11). Students almost never make the distinction between "this is the average outcome among all outcomes" and "this is the outcome that is most likely" until they are painstakingly taught to do so.

    I can absolutely see the value in the uniform, linear increments provided by a static linear system. However, I have found that people tend to assume that, on any given roll of a die, they are more likely to roll near the "average" than far away from it.

    Optimistically, I'm saying that any system models some of the intuition people have about probability.

    Pessimistically, I'm saying that most people will make wrong assumptions about probability no matter what dice system you use
