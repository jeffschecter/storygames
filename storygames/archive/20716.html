\n In playing *World games lo these last couple of years, and noting which are an easier sell to players coming from a trad (or even non-) gamer background, which have the greater opportunity to cause potential hard feelings after hard moves, I think there are basically two typologies of World games:

The first is where if you play your character, follow her moves, and interact with the fiction using the common moves, you'll basically be all right. Sure, you might end up in a burned out van in the wasteland, with Dremmer somewhere out there with a sniper rifle ready to kill you the minute you move an inch, but probably a lot of things had to happen and snowball to get you there, and there were a bunch of chances to have avoided this fate.

The second kind is the one where even if you play your character by following her moves and interacting with the common moves, Death can still reach up and yank you into an early grave. You can be doing everything right in Night Witches, getting 2-3 in the mission pool every time, and two bad rolls over a German fuel depot can result in planes falling burning into the snow and massive death all around.

In those games, to really survive you have to go beyond following your moves and actually push against the fiction in order to guarantee survival. In Night Witches, being in command is crucial to your survival, but then you have to balance the attention the authorities pay you against the needs of your girls. Your moves won't save you; what will save you is clever use of them.

I'm not sure this is a system mastery thing per se, although it does somewhat fit into that pattern.

As a part of my recent encounters with tone issues, I am wondering if this needs to be presented more up front. When I introduce Night Witches to people, I have started saying a version of what I wrote above, so that everyone understands that when I kill two PCs because they have a fiery wreck, that's how the game is designed to work and they should be aware of it. My problems running Sagas of the Icelanders have generally revolved around the need to make really hard moves in combat, and not everyone being prepared for that.

Here is where I see some games falling in this typology:

First Kind:

Fellowship (maybe the first AW game explicitly designed around this idea)
Apocalypse World
Monster of the Week
Dungeon World
Maybe Monsterhearts (haven't played it enough)

Second Kind:

Night Witches
Sagas of the Icelanders
The Warren

Your comments are greatly appreciated.

    Maybe Monsterhearts (haven't played it enough)
    \nCould be. Monsterhearts, like AW and MOTW have a 'cheat death' and/or 'avoid really bad outcome mechanics' (Luck, marking debilities to stats, or losing all strings/going darkest self to avoid Death, saying "Whoa, that was his 4 harm shotgun in my face? Of course I'd have handed over the fuel canister!"). Dungeon World has a buffer pool of HP and a Last Breath roll, but that can OFC be failed.

    Though I own NW, I've not played it recently enough to remember whether there is anything of the kind in it? Same with the Warren and Sagas, to be fair, but I don't think there is to the same degree.

    Is this a kind of 'movie star' genre assumption difference? You are playing sexy badasses in most of your first list, and exceptional but frail, fallible mortals in extreme situations in the others. Does a cheat death mechanic explain the differences for you, or is there more at play? Is it in the differing 'Agendas and Principles' or actual move differences you see?

    Monsterhearts is very much a game of giving you sufficient rope to hang your hormonal, monstrous pseudo-teenage self (and a few of your most intimate frienemies and lovers) and in AW at least one of the key questions for me is to find out whether the PCs stay strong and allied against a hostile world. Which supports (requires?) a more drawn-out death spiral, rather than the random outcome of one or two misses.
