\n OK, so I know that filesharing is bad - its called piracy for a reason. But what about sharing files with players?If I purchase an RPG book, I can lend it to friends and then we can all sit down an play. I think that's pretty ethical. You purchased a game and you need others to play so you get one game for one purchase.

What do you do when you want to purchase a .pdf of an RPG book? Is it ethical to send copies of the .pdf to your players so that you can play? Should they each be responsible for purchasing a copy of the .pdf? Should you just try to teach them the game without having them read the rules? Again, I'm not advocating piracy, I'm just curious how others handle this situation.

    \n Filesharing isn't bad. It's sometimes one of the only ways to find a long OoP game. Fortunately companies like Mongoose are catching on to that and rereleasing games that are true to the original, but they can't cover every game out there. It was a stroke of luck that I was able to find Dragonlance 5th Age at all, let alone at a decent price. Filesharing's precursor, tape sharing, is also the reason that the complete Dr Who is actually available. So there's simply that factor that makes it very much a good thing. Naturally there's a concern of people only pirating and not otherwise buying, but in a practical sense you need to read off paper, so in all likelihood if you're seriously using the game you'll buy it anyway - it's not worth the inconvenience of reading off a computer screen if you have the money. I actually ran into a guy in a local store who was looking for 3e FR material, the storeowner said they were OoP and that PDFs may be an option to which he responded that he already had them but he wanted the physical copies for actual playing. If you've got people who just download games and don't ever pay for them, odds are they're not playing the games.

    As for sending PDF copies to players, go for it. Most of my players are happy to play but they wouldn't buy it, so if I want to make any use of my purchase I have to pretty much give them free material to work with. I imagine your players are likely the same if you're asking this question. Copying the PDFs is a necessity to actually get anything out of it in the first place - otherwise you're just buying it and never using it, and at that point you just don't feel like buying anything anymore.

    \n This is legal in Finland, no bones about it. Whether it's ethical is something that I think depends on the attitudes and perceptions of the person doing the deed, just like other piracy: done with firm purpose and noble motivation the importance of the act overrides any legacy concerns about creator rights.

    I have an interesting experiment running on this in our webstore - I offer the Solar System pdf under two separate licenses, one for a single person and one for the single person and anybody he plays with. I don't particularly emphasize this anywhere, it's just available for people who feel that they need the permission. The file is the same in both cases, so the other just has you pay $15 more for the explicit permission to copy the file around. I think the sales ratio between the two licenses is something like 50:1 for the cheaper one.

    \n Interesting thoughts.

    Personally, my operations are much like Anna's: The whole "For Personal Use" clause: My gaming group has a Dropbox account where we store the games on deck to play: One or more people have a physical copy of the book (or in some cases, all of us; Dresden Files, frex), and the rest have access to read or review the rules before the session through the PDF.

    I'm really not sure how I'd address a multiple-copy license as a consumer. If, for example, the PDF was $8 and the "group license" was $10 or 12, I'd consider it. Maybe.

    After seeing 4-packs of virtual copies of Left 4 Dead on sale for preorder ($10 off a total of 4, so where normally the price would be $160 for 4, it's $150; you give the three download keys to your friends, who presumably paid you in cash or Paypal or whatever), I have been thinking about this option for physical books. Some of these fat fucking books like Dresden or Hellas are really prime for a "group all in" purchase. It would be interesting if someone tried to, say, offer a "4 copy deal" for a gaming group (or 5; 6; making adjustments to price accordingly) where the total was the combined prices of all four books (with likely cheaper shipping than if they were all shipped separately) at a $20 off discount for the lot or whatever (on a $40+ game).

    but that's another conversation, sorry for the derail.

    But yeah, we do this a lot: For those "games that come with a free PDF", 1-2 people have the physical book (or more in rare cases), and everyone gets the PDF for review.

There are some fringe cases like people thinking that Windows 7 is shareware due to the 30 day activation grace period, but that's a small minority, and Microsoft leaves that ambiguous on purpose.     Probably the easiest way to deal with it though is if you have a large enough fan base, follow Stolze's ransom model.
