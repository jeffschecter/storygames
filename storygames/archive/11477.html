\n Graham was earlier saying that Snakes & Ladders doesn't have an intrinsic theme and story, that it's all just random die-rolls and moving up and down some arbitrary play space. And he started a thread where he poked fun at the game, and also at Story Games in general.

But... I think that Graham maybe missed the point of this classic, old-skool roleplaying game. A quick look at the game reveals a strong theme (risking death by snake in order to reach heaven), mechanics that really push to support it (dark deals?! options surrounding betrayal and trust and even forgiveness?!), and a nudge from the designer to really invest in the roleplaying aspect.

In short, Snakes & Ladders (or at least, the Deluxe Advanced Edition, which is the one I am familiar with) works hard to bring story and premise to the table. It's a bit dated, sure, bit it helped shape the field of roleplaying as it stands today.

For the record, if you've never played or read this classic, the designer recently re-published it as a free online PDF. It's available here: Snakes & Ladders, deluxe advanced edition

    \n Deluxe Advanced is balderdash, frankly. I wonder how you could even consider a game that ditches the original, balanced gameboard set-up to be an improvement. Heck, isn't that what makes Snakes & Ladders such an enduring classic, the way it condences everything you need in roleplaying from character sheets to adventure design into a single gameboard shared by all the players? Besides, the original is inherently more flexible, considering how it revolves around spiritual development and questing for dharma instead of simplistic combat encounters. The Deluxe Advanced edition does not compare favourably, if I didn't know better I'd almost consider it a plagiarization of Dungeons & Dragons, a well-known fantasy wargame. And snakes are not dragons!
