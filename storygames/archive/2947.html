    Posted By: MatthijsJudson, did you check outthis threadbefore playing?
    \nActually, Matthijs, thanks for asking that: I did check out Joshua's website for the game, read the errata, downloaded character sheets and read all 11 threads on the forum. I didn't search The Forge or any other gaming forum for advice on the game. And I wanted to start off with this proviso: I don't think that a published, finished game should require outside text to be interpretable. Joshua, I don't know how much haze there is to rectify, but all the same, a certain amount of what I've got to say here is regarding what I feel is a less than clear presentation. So, an important premise to this critique is this: one of my criteria is that the rules text should stand on it's own. While my comprehension might be aided, or my enjoyment improved by a direct dialog with the designer, if the text doesn't get me to an enjoyable game, most readers aren't going to take the extra step.

    And, for context, this critique is based on the game of Shock: I played with Josh BishopRoby and Mark Valliantanos. And other members of LA Gamers were sorely missed.

    That said:

    First of all, there's the ongoing whine about indie games needing a copy editor. For Shock: I feel like it's inescapably apt. It wasn't until I was reading passages aloud to try to resolve our confusions that I found gems like "When deciding who owns Shocks and Issues, remember: someone's going to have a say in how these work." Which seems better out of context than in, honestly. Then there's weird mid-paragraph non-sequiturs, like the top paragraph on page 24, where we go from the components of Antagonists ("After that, Features...") from Antagonists having intentions. Reading over the text critically, my overall impression is that there's a decided amount of unpacking that needs to happen. I get the impression of a complete game design that didn't entirely make it into text.

    For the record our grid looked like this:
    Issues: Corporatism, Judson; Family Identity, Mark; and Ecology, Joshua.
    Shocks: L-5 Orbital Colony, Josh; Human Cloning, Judson; Robot Slaves, Mark.
    Protagonists were: Gardener Peng, played by Judson, Ecology/L-5 Colony; Nanny Josephine, played by Josh, Family Identiy/Human Cloning; and Emanuel Sextus, played by Mark, Corporatism/Human Cloning.

    The Grid is an awesome idea. I think. And I'm tentative because there's a lot about the Grid that isn't clear from reading the text. I couldn't find any suggestions on how many Shocks and Issues we should come up with (except for implicit limits on which ones would be represented by Protagonists.)

    More troublesome is that, there's no explanation of what anything past an Issue or a Shock is supposed to mean. What should we take away from the fact that Emanuel Sextus' name appears in the Corporatism/Human Cloning block? He had a job with the corporation that managed real estate within the colony, but that was rapidly mooted, and never really addressed Corporatism as an issue. The text doesn't really address that at all. There's some suggestion to the Antagonist player to look at the Shocks and Issues of the Protagonist, but there's no indication about how they should relate to the Protagonist, which left each of us finding our own solutions.

    We sort of absorbed Robot Slaves into the Minutae, and I think that kind of worked, but it'd be nice to have a discussion of what happens to unrepresented Shocks and Issues.

    As far as the artifact of the Grid sheet is concerned, we all agreed that it would have been nice to have the Praxis Scales appear on the Grid, so that we could all note them down together. This is really a niggle though, but all the same, it'd be nice to see. Even more trivially: a Minutae sheet would have been kind of awesome. I mean, we had a blank for everything else, but on Minutae we're on our own. Like I said: niggles.

    Our Praxis looked like: "Rebel v. Obey - Adapt v. Innovate" I think we liked them more at the beginning of the game than by the end, and that's a larger criticism. We were pleased with how we'd related the world we'd created with the Grid to our Praxis Scales, but over the course of the game we were less enthralled. We had a lot of "oh wait, I need to pick a scale" after we rolled dice, and we came to think that it was because we didn't feel much of a connection between conflicts and the Scales we chose to resolve them. Maybe that had to do with the Scales we chose? But all in all, there wasn't much in the way of advice to warn us off the ones we did choose.

    For that matter, from what we could tell, Features didn't seem to actually matter much either. Correct me if I'm wrong, but our collective and careful reading of the text was that: for Protagonists, the number of Features you have was the number of dice you roll, and the names of those features is sort of irrelevant to their mechanical purpose. I know that I liked the Features I built up for Gardener Peng, but I really couldn't tell you what Features Emannuel Sextus or Nanny Josephine had. And for Antagonists, the text appears to say (and forums I read seem to back this up) that Features are pure color, and dice rolled are completely based on how many credits the Antagonist Player is willing to spend. Calling both things "Features" made that understanding harder to reach.

    Now, here's the thing Shock: did really well: there's a strong, tight reward cycle on presenting bangs to other players. I really enjoyed handing Mark interesting situations, and he certainly seemed to enjoy having his Protagonist's kidney harvested and legs amputated. I don't know how much those activities we enjoyed by Mark and Josh in their respective roles, but they seemed to being enjoying themselves, and the d10/d4 mechanic certainly attached a Skinner-like reward to presenting suck around the table. Once we started, it was hard to stop.

    In the post-mortem, though, we all agreed that we felt sort of like we were kicking the guy on our right in the nuts and yelling "Bang!" And while that was kind of fun, the evening as a whole wasn't very satisfying. We wound up with three series of scenes that linked up like box cars, rather than anything that satisfied as fiction. And that was sort of the inspiration for the experiment that Josh mentioned last week, because I feel like the three of us can actually tell stories, but that in trying to follow the rules of Shock: we were inhibiting our own storytelling skills.

    Now, there is one mea culpa required. I believe we were misapplying Minutae dice. Often, as third player, we were throwing in after the result was clear to throw things off. The visible consequence of that was the escalations were more frequent than they might otherwise have been, although I think not much more frequent, since only the Antagonist doesn't have a choice about which dice he's going to use. And we did agree, escalations occurred far too frequently. My mild mannered horticulturist was choking officious suits right and left, and shooting his way into office structures.

    My take away: I think I would like the game that Joshua had in mind when he wrote Shock: I'd love to see a second edition with more pages devoted to clearly laying out how the game works. I'd also like to see a clearer line drawn between the Grid, the Protagonists and Antagonists, Praxis Scales and scene framing. I'd like to see unique Features be more involved resolving conflicts (maybe you need to name a specific Feature?) and likewise for Antagonists (or else rename Antagonist Features.) I'd like all of the single sentence advice, (e.g. what an escalation is, how Story Goal relates to scene framing) expanded into its own subsection. And for heaven's sake, back off on Protagonist credits. They only apply once you've played through a whole story, and until then they're just confusing. Until then, I don't feel like Shock: as published is a finished game.
