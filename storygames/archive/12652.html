any of the above and any thing else you could suggest to give guidance... I'd sure enjoy and appreciate it!!

    \nI think "relationship map", Sorcerer-style, has a very specific meaning that I am not concerned with.

    For a relationship-heavy game I really like a sort of bibliographic relationship map, that lays out physical and social connections. This is a really useful tool at the table, because people can reference it both as a memory-refresher and a source of connections, complications, and hooks.

    Here's the R-Map for our PTA game, The Big Store, at the beginning of season one, and at the beginning of season two.

    This game revolved around some iconic locations, so those went on the map. THe lines denote general relationships of some kind, and power within each faction flows from top to bottom. But it is mostly a hash.

    Looking at those two shows a lot of fascinating changes, and comparing them is another way to generate stories and conflicts.

    I did these in Visio, because it is what I had. The tools and format make no difference.

    How We Came To Live Here builds this into setting design, and over continued play you continue to develop and tweak a relationship map for your village. It works well and is a very good implementation of the concept.

    EDIT: One more thing about my maps above - they are very dense, but not all those people showed up in-game. Many, many people were there to prompt player investment, as tempting hooks, as handy fall-backs. I threw some promising people out there and maybe half of them stuck. At the same time, even if they didn't appear as NPCs, they gave the impression of a big, complex world.

    \n For my Birthright game that I'm running now, the relationship map I'm using is just in my notebook. I started with the set of circles for the PCs and how they were connected, and expanded from there. Two PCs are brothers, draw that line. They father is a duke, circle him and draw that line. They meet a neighboring duke and some of his vassals and make rivals out of them, draw a bunch of circles and lines.
    \n
    \nI also have on there dotted lines, representing relationships the PCs don't know about, but which are influencing events. Likewise, people they haven't met yet get big parentheses rather than circles; when they meet them, I join it into a circle.
    \n
    \nNodes on the graph can represent either a person or a group; my latest version features groups of werewolves and trolls, for instance. This is shorthand, the details are in my notes.
    \n
    \nI version it by going forward in pages, redrawing, and adding. As I flip through my notebook, I get a very satisfying evolving picture of the situation.
    \n
    \nI'm thinking some locations need to be on the relationship map. The big fortress full of monsters they partially explored, or some of the places they know about but have not gone to. Maybe I'll represent them as rectangles.
