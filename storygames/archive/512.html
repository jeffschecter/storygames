    It has been great to be able to talk about all of this in a civilized and polite fashion. But as people come closer and closer to the idea of "Let's have a great big talk and discuss what Story Games should be," I'd like to point out that there's a fair amount of bias in even presenting it that way.

    I think the best of people, so I assume that you don't see how you're slanting the rhetorical field firmly in favor of your own propositions. I hope that you will accept this in the spirit of an observation and maybe some food for thought, rather than a criticism.

    When you put forth the proposition that things like "Who belongs and what are they supposed to be talking about?" will be resolved or even refined by a discussion on the topic, you are proposing a way that the board should be. You are proposing, roughly, that we should have such discussions and that they should be important to everyone. Which is certainly one way to deal with the issues, but far from the only one.

    Personally, I am totally happy for you to have such discussions. Have a ball. You can have a whole sub-forum for discussing how people should talk, as far as I'm concerned. I certainly don't see how talking about such things, in and of itself, can do me any harm. I'd even listen, and contemplate, and maybe ask for clarifications.

    But I don't particularly want to put forth my own opinions on how people should talk. Because, really, I'm pretty happy with people talking however the hell they want, so long as they afford me the right to do the same.

    See ... here's the thing. If you think those discussions are important, if you think that the discussions are resolving these issues rather than just examining them, then suddenly it is not possible for me to simply stay out of them. Either I post something (which I'd rather not do) and then stand by it or else by my silence I give (at least in the eyes of some) tacit agreement that I will live by the boundaries that people set up.

    Now that, again, doesn't matter directly to me. The fact that (say) Bobby Sue believes that by my silence I promised to never use the word Macho again is Bobby Sue's problem. I will remain Ã‚Â¡Muy Macho! Bobby Sue cannot stop me. But Bobby Sue can get all upset because she misunderstood my silence, and contrary to what people may believe I don't actually seek that out.

    Out of courtesy, I might decide to occasionally post in such discussions, so that people won't later feel that I betrayed them later. This post is one such courtesy. The worrisome pattern I see (in many other places and increasingly here) is that when I post once, people respond instead of listening. They don't just say "Okay, that's how Tony feels," inevitably someone says something stupid like "Well, that's a crummy way to feel! I guess you're entitled to it, but only because you're entitled to be wrong." And then they think, because they've provoked me, that if I don't respond it's because I am tacitly agreeing with their point. Because if I disagreed with them, of course I would want to talk about it, right?

    It can become a treacherous pit of quick-sand. The person who wants to talk just keeps on talking. The person who doesn't want to talk either has to grit their teeth and stay in a discussion that they hate or else just walk away from it and accept that people are going to feel that their silence indicates agreement (or at least lack of dissent). You get threads where five or six posters are talking and talking, and then they think they speak for the other 295 of us, simply because we're not interested in keeping their discussion going by telling them we disagree. It sucks.

    Now, in all seriousness: I do not believe things have reached that stage here. But people seem to be taking step after baby-step towards that with grim implacability. A vocal minority seems, to my admittedly biased eyes, determined to make that the default mode of conversation here. Which, really, is just going to make them unhappy because, dudes and dudettes, I at least am not going to feel bound by any of these discussions. I will, now and forever, listen politely to what people say, contemplate it, and then do whatever the hell I feel like doing without feeling obliged to justify it to anyone.

    So: Peaseblossom has raised good topics. I'm interested to hear what people have to say, and I'll certainly think about it seriously. I probably won't post again. This is your one warning that, no matter what the talky-talky people agree upon, I'm still going to do whatever the hell I feel like.

    Wow--this has been a long conversation. As a mere dabbler in these places, I have hesitated to jump on board...yada-yada, I'm posting. Rather than answer any of the three questions directly, I'll respond in a slightly fuzzier manner. In part, because the questions as framed by peaseblossom seem too big, nets so loose that all the fish slide through them. In part, because my response is a little impressionistic.

    Do I occasionally use what has come to be described as macho language, including genitalia references? Yes, but I do so exclusively in an all-male, relaxed social environs. The only obvious exception I can think of are occasions with my wife. Why? Well, because everyone in that environment who knows me, knows how much camp is involved for me in that. I use it with scare quotes, if you will. If I want to make a meaningful statement beyond goofing and posturing, I drop the language and use words better suited to the task.

    This is always sticky online, where tone is more difficult to determine. It becomes even more nettlesome when the words are being used as a vehicle of meaningful content. People talk a lot about signal to noise around here--words like 'macho' are noisey and unfocused. The buzz of conflicting meanings around them obscure the message. That's not terrible for a 'relaxed' place like story games, but not ideal either.

    I feel like the indie game community should dig this kind of discussion. If system matters, if the substrate of the roleplaying experience matters, can it be so wild to claim that words, the substrate of communication (which roleplaying is all about), also matter? The clearer and more focused the words, the clearer the signal, the better the conversation becomes. I'm not a fan of the political correctness police, but I far prefer them to the anti-pc response which essentially shuts down the process of clarification.

    I think there is a pragmatic point in all this, one that doesn't have to evoke all the questions of pc-ness, though. It's simply this: words matter. As we talk, we find better words, just as we talk about conflict vs. task resolution, we find better mechanics. No more, no less. If we can open up our game mechanics to critical reflection, why not our words? Especially (as peaseblossom's concerns point out) when those words relate to how people are able to access our ideas about gaming.

    Peaseblossom's comments read like this to me (yes, *to me*):

    "this word macho is off-putting, loaded with too many meanings for me to sift out a useful one that I can put in my game theory sack and carry off. Some of those multiple meanings are offensive to me, so I have an even harder time ignoring those to get to the meat of the discussion. I think I am not the only one (and people agree). Perhaps if you want to engage more people, you'll think of finding a better word. The forums tone suggests that this is, in fact, one of the hopes of some people who hang around here. Let's chat about that."

    Now, I think her framing questions were too broad, but I think the 'spirit' of them is good and (with better words, because those matter) there is some good stuff to talk about.

    --Ian, lover of words (as shown by how many I use ;)

    1. Community: What is the role of Story Games as an internet community of gamers? Who belongs and what are they supposed to be talking about?
    Well, it's pretty loose, as we can see. "Story Games" isn't defined, but it does have a clear focus on Forge-related games. So, say, we probably wouldn't be comfortable if a bunch of D&D players came in and started making twenty D&D mechanics threads a day.
