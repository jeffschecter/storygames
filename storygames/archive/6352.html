    \n I was reading through some feedback thread and the reviewer asked, before posting anything, something to the effect of, "How polite do I have to be?" I found the question fundamentally offensive. Can you not write even extremely harsh criticism in a polite way? Impoliteness means you're verging on personal attack or unsubstantiated assertions: Everything about this concept sucks! How is that critique?

    I've done my first deep pass on both my games (Chrysalis Revolt and True History of the Revolution, both excellent starting drafts). I think both designers would agree I delivered pretty harsh criticism of both -- but there's no reason in the world to be nasty about it. There just...isn't.

    OTOH, folks have no business submitting creative works for peer review if they don't want their peers to be honest. It's a waste of everyone's time. If you want to showboat, get it out there, stand by your vision, and be eager to take whatever they dish out.

    p.

    \n Like I mentioned in my thread - I do want honesty, but I also want fairness. Honesty I can live with: "I couldn't connect with this game, it had nothing for me, it made no sense, I don't know why you'd want to play, I don't want to play." I-statements.

    By fairness I try to take an objective stance and say, "Well, I personally am not jazzed about this, but I do enjoy the way you did *that*..."

    I find myself frequently want to break the wall of critique and say, "Wow - this would be SO much better if you did THIS and THAT and re-wrote THIS and completely threw this away but keep THAT...." To me, that is not critique it is development, which is a whole 'nother thing. Isn't it?

    Posted By: SambearPoetTo me, that is not critique it is development, which is a whole 'nother thing. Isn't it?
    \nI disagree, and in fact all of my critiques had some amount of suggestions for where to modify system or expand content. Even Ben's questionnaire ask a reviewer "What doesn't work to fulfill the game's goals? Why not? Could it be removed? Should something else be added? Should things be changed?"

    Also, for me, if I had to review under a gag order not to make any suggestions for changes... well, hell, what am I supposed to help with, then? You get to see how I interpret your words, sure; but that will only lead to some edits and maybe a few new sentences for clarification. Helpful, but not nearly as much as tuning a system to be tighter, or finding drains in a resource economy and plugging them, or what-not.

    For example, Jane and I are talking about how I found the GM's role in 618 to be too-open to favoritism or railroading; and I've made a few various suggestions about how I'd tweak two system elements so that they are automatic or put back into the hands of the players to decide. She can take them or leave them, as she sees fit--but putting them on the table at least offers alternatives and might inspire her towards some utterly different system.

    It's non-confrontational and productive to suggest alternate ways to do things that "better-align" with your tastes as a reviewer. If nothing else, a designer can see what "the other half" thinks and perhaps use the game text to soften the blow or to better-persuade folks who don't like (say) high GM fiat or lots of randomness.

    Also... ain't we punk rock, and shit? I mean, c'mon... sure, it's "your baby;" but don't be scared to let it put up its dukes and defend itself against the bullies, should they show up kicking sand. It'll be tougher for it than if you'd kept it safe in the crib....

      \n Attack all that is wrong with a design with vigor. You're doing them a favor. Sure, keep in mind the context, but do point out every gap. They know that you know that it was done in a rush, and they'll take the critique for what it's worth. And that's a lot even as a checklist for things that they already knew were missing.
      \n
      \nNone of this is mean, it's all good.
      \n
      \nIf the designer on the other end takes it as a personal attack, that's his problem. If they can't stand the heat, then they need to get out of the kitchen!
      \n
      \nI've yet to see anyone have a problem with any real criticism, however, everybody who participates is too cool for that.
      \n
      \nNow, that's not to say you get to be mean. But you know when you're being mean. Don't do that. Being harshly complete and right is helpful. Being mean is, well, mean.
      \n
      \nCriticism also means adding suggestions where you have them. No, that doesn't mean you ommit a criticism where you don't have a suggestion. But it means that if you come up with zero suggestions overall, you're probably really not trying to help much. Even so, a criticism that includes no suggestions is still not being mean.
      \n
      \nMike
