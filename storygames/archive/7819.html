\n Just wondering...
It's something I've always always tried to do - ask for feedback specifically between game sessions.

My groups have discussions and plan our schedules, etc.... via a Yahoo Group post forum.
After every game session I toss out the general questions for "what did you think about the game session?".
I usually do this through asking the group for ideas in the form of:


GOODS?
(noting your review of all the cool things you liked and want to see more of in the game or from the group, either in-character or out-of-game)

BADS??
(noting your review for the things you disliked and want to avoid in the future, again in or out of game)

UGLIES???
(noting things that were just weird or odd or not fitting in Good/Bad that you'd like to mention)



Then I might ask 1-2 specific questions...
My entire group doesn't always respond. Sometimes no one does. It's mostly a time to offer and solicite a chance to give opinions, ideas and constructive feedback to make each game session better.

Just wondering if anyone else does this? What exactly do you do? How?
thanks

    \n We do in our group. Usually we wait 1-2 weeks after the game wraps up to solicit opinions and questions (asking for background details or behind-the-scenes information that the characters never found out in-game are pretty common).

    If we try and get feedback right away, it sucks because nothing's really sunk in and no one's prepared to answer anything clearly. If we wait longer than 2 weeks (and honestly, 2 weeks is really pushing the bleeding edge), it sucks because we've moved on to something new and people are already forgetting details. After a month it's not even worth asking anymore; you might as well just wait and see what, if anything, about that game gets mentioned whenever the group starts reminiscing about What We've Played.


    Usually what our GMs ask about is what people liked and didn't like (as regards scenes, characters, plot points, and/or mechanics); usually what players ask about is clarifications or outright explanations for things that they didn't figure out during the game. Sometimes it's useful for planning another game, sometimes it's just interesting, sometimes it's self-contradictory and confusing. Like any other survey, I guess.

    \n That's a good point; in our group, complaints get made immediately and we try to address them just as swiftly, but we don't spend a lot of time (or any, really) analyzing what we're liking and how much we're liking it and whether we need to make any adjustments to the things we're liking. If we're happy, we'd just as soon spend our time enjoying that happiness and skip the introspection and analysis. (Honestly, I think anyone who'd do otherwise is probably not as happy as they claim to be.)


    The more I think about it, the more I suspect that the only truly honest post-game review in our group is what gets mentioned without prompting months or years later. If something is worth mentioning when we're sitting around reminiscing about all the games we've played together, then it clearly made an impression (good or bad, depending on what kind of war stories we're telling each other).

    I think we get more information -- not to mention more useful and more accurate information -- about how happy we are with our gaming and what we like or dislike from that sort of kibitzing than we get from anything else. It's not particularly timely information, in that it might be over a year before you know if the game you just finished hit the target or not, but we've been good friends for years now, so timeliness isn't all that important. If we won't really know until next year, so be it.

    \n Yeah, not being comfortable complaining is definitely not a problem me or my friends have. Similarly, positive social comments are common. We compliment each other, we make suggestions, we even say things like "we should do more of that" and so on. It's just part of normal social interaction to tell those things to your friends, isn't it?

    What I was talking about was the more formalized gaming postmortem, which as I mentioned, is something we also do: the e-mail sent around, or the roundtable discussion about how the last game went and what we liked or didn't like about it. And that's a very different animal from our normal "instant feedback," and has not thus far been of much use to us. Sometimes we get some useful insight out of it, but most of the time we really don't; we're having a good time, and trying to dissect that good time is a lot of effort for very little reward.     \n Jason, you're right on. Other creative or competitive situations can have a very similar dynamic. I've been a part of sports teams, bands/orchestras, and similar groups of people that will always discuss afterwards, just to deepen their understanding of what they're doing and how they can improve. Just as importantly, it lets them get to know each other.

    As a really different example, sometimes after a birthday party or something like that, I've had detailed chats with friends about the organization: "Hmmm, so and so didn't show up. We need to send invitations next time." Or: "Man, setting up the hors d'oeuvres in the living room really kind of made everyone more social, didn't it?"

    I think the common element is people who are interested in the details of what they do. Not just the results (although that's part of it), but the specific details. Like a film fan who wants to know who the director was, where the sets were, who made the costumes, who wrote the soundtrack... the fan finds those details interesting, whether good or bad. Another film watcher, maybe even one who loves films to the point where he or she will starve rather than stop watching them, might only be interested in the experience, and has no need to pick it apart.

    In gaming, I'm always frustrated by the lack of feedback. I'd say that, even when trying something radically different and new, maybe one person out of 20 will take the effort to make so much as a comment about it, even after aggressive prompting. As a game designer, I find that incredibly frustrating. I mean, if you gave your best friend a novel you wrote, you'd expect a little more than, "oh, thanks" as feedback, right?
