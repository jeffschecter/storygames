\n I ran Lady Blackbird for the second time the other night (amazing), and discovered something about 'newbie' gamers.
\nI usually run completely free-form improvised games, and I like to mix groups of gamers and non-gamers. I'm all about trying to figure out what makes Story Games fun for people who don't normally role-play, and what turns them off. As a case study, I'll be talking about my friend Lucy, who has played two games with us now.
\n
\nThe first was completely freeform, we made a story situation based around a map of Redwall, the mouse kingdom (think mouseguard). We made Characters, who had no stats/traits/rules of any kind, and played a game where ideas where accepted and situations escalated or resolved based on the story's dramatic needs alone. Lucy got stuck in more than everybody else, playing her Character with gusto, getting herself into trouble, the works. Awesome!
\n
\nThe second game Lucy played was Lady Blackbird. Interestingly, giving Lucy a bunch of traits and keys and secrets, and a way of gaining 'points', seemed to break the game for her. She felt hampered by the Character's statistics - telling her that her Character could do X made her feel like she couldn't do anything else. She was playing the traits instead of the Character - she stopped making "What would my Character do?" choices, instead moving to a "I could help because I can charm" position, and very-often even asking "can we find somebody to charm?"
\n
\nI've had the same leap with other Players.
\nIn contrast to Lucy is her b/f Neal, who is overwhelmed by the brevity of choice in a completly freeform game. He needs a list of his Characters capabilities in order to give him 'permission' to make up certain kinds of things. I mean, in a freeform game, he could invent something which his Character would do, but he's afraid that it'll be 'wrong' unless there are rules to back it up.
\n
\nHow can we marry these two forms of Player? How can we create a system that does not restrict players like Lucy, and yet offers permission to pretend to players like Neale? Does anybody have similar experiences?
\n

    \n I've noticed the kind of thing, too, where putting skills or traits or whatnot on a character sheet limits what the player has the character do. In some games, giving the traits values will lead people to always try to use their highest trait no matter what the situation, because that's what the game encourages, which leads to very one-dimensional play. I've had a lot of success stripping away all specific traits like that and playing (and designing) games where the characters don't have those kinds of attributes.

Because I can imagine this would be more of a problem for an experienced roleplayer who has always had these traits to fall back on and now suddely doesn't.

    \n With traits/tags, there's a real tradeoff when it comes to picking tags. A bunch of synonyms allow you to bring many dice to bear for specific situations, a diverse set of tags gives you a bump in many situations but you've got to supplement each of those rolls with pool dice. It's a fertile mechanic but you can unwittingly make it so that you never have many dice to roll or only occasionally have a ton of dice to roll.

    \n Jon, I think that's the point of Lady Blackbird to be honest with you. People sit down and talk, sometimes at length, at the start of the game, to establish facts about the world in play collaboratively, using the character sheets as a jumping-off point. "So, Snargle, is it unusual that a goblin is a pilot? Are there more like you? Are you a dying race?" Obviously not all those questions in the same breath, mind, but it's a place to jump off. Ask Naomi about the pits and slavery in the Empire. Ask Lady Blackbird how she uses her charm and why.

    The Forgeinistic term for it is "The Fruitful Void"; start with a little bit of setting and detail, and let the people in play fill in the rest, instead of playing to a game's bible.

    So, the traits/keys/abilities are really designed not to be restricting, but allow the players to cut loose.

    \n One way to view the problem is to ask "What IS the character to the player?"

    In Nates case I guess the answer would be like
    A character is a person represented in the gaming system with stats and traits to represent how they interact with the world. Hence Nate prefer to have a system level of the character to interact with the character in the game. The system might even "be" the character for Nate, and how the character behaves is a representation of the number. (Not the other way around)

    In Lucys case I guess the answer might be:
    The character is a person living n another world that she going to roleplay. She going to pretend to be the person, and while pretending to be the person, she want to be able to reason the same way a normal person would do. Act in whatever way seems fitting to the character in any giving situation. Because Lucy is a person, she want to use the same kind of thinking when her character makes choices, as when she makes choices. Because that how person normally makes choices.
    For Lucy the level of abstraction of traits and system to describe the character makes no sense at all. She can't really understand where the character sheet and traits com into her playing scheme of "Presenting to be another person". They not only a way to only a way to describe the character. They describe what you can do. She not sure how to fit that together with her earlier playing scene of being able to do whatever seems reasonable in the situation.

    ---

    But this is just guesses. Ask the players.

    I would look for a system where Nate get enough system to understans how he can interact with the gaming world, and where Lucy have the cracter describe to her in a way where she don't feel like she no longer can't do anything-
