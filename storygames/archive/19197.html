\n From Shaintar: Legends Unleashed, page 79:\n
\n

Rule Number One \u2013 Cheat

\nThis is hardly a new concept in the annals of
gaming, but it absolutely bears emphasis as the first
rule of running the campaigns being discussed here.
Feel free to make sure your players know that you
will sometimes \u201ccheat\u201d; honestly, they should expect
it, and they should appreciate that it\u2019s really the only
way you have a chance of giving them any kind of
challenge.

That word \u2013 challenge \u2013 comes up a lot in this
context. It is not the place of the GM to win out
against the players; even in \u201cEmpire Strikes Back\u201d
and \u201cThe Two Towers,\u201d the Heroes were far from
utterly defeated, lest the stories just be over with a
lousy ending. Yet the Game Master is very much
expected to challenge the Heroes, or else the game
becomes little more than an exercise in flexing might
and cleverness for a few rounds, wondering what the
next campaign might be like.

Realize that from the very beginning, you\u2019re being
ganged up on. Your players are each maximizing the
abilities of their Novice Rank Heroes against whatever
you throw at them, and doing so in conjunction
with their fellows. By the time they reach Heroic
and Legendary Rank, they know their characters
like snipers know their favorite rifles, and those
characters are pointed directly at everything you do
and are used with precision to devastating results.

However, you have the right and, frankly, the
responsibility to at least occasionally grab the
strands of Fate and twist reality to make things more
interesting and entertaining. In order to enjoy and
savor their victories, battles need to be hard won and
successes need to come at great effort and, sometimes,
cost.

Blatant, naked cheating, however, is going to
be held with a dim view. Your players are going to
expect you to go through at least some kind of ritual
or process to pull any stunts against them. At the very
least, you need to carefully conceal your cheats so that
everything happens behind-the-scenes, while out in
front of the camera, all seems to be going as it was.

    \n This is what I call "classic Illusionism advice," and I thought I'd share it here. This is a book released very recently (2014, actually). The hallmarks:

    * The GM must cheat or the game will suffer.
    * The GM has an adversarial role with the player (the GM is "ganged up on"), but also a responsibility to entertain the player.
    * The GM should carefully conceal the cheating.

    Please don't get me wrong. I think the Shaintar setting is fantastic, creative, and fun. I backed the Kickstarter at a high level. I am a fan.

    I am not a fan of this GM advice, however.

    \n It's clear what it considers cheating, on the next page:\n

    Here are some of the best kinds of cheats to employ,
    in no particular order:

    More GM Bennies

    Your players can clearly see your basic stack, and
    they count on being able to watch that stack dwindle
    as they force you to spend them. So you cannot just
    dump more on there. You can, however, subtly add
    more bennies to individual Wild Cards. Though the
    standard enemy Wild Card has two personal bennies,
    I will often have more important ones carry three,
    four, or even five of their own if they have a key role
    to fulfill. Players do not necessarily need to know
    how many personal bennies such a character has; this
    can be an excellent way to give one or two more Soak
    rolls, re-rolls, or whatever when you need to juice the
    opposition. Save those core bennies for the final run
    down to the end of the conflict.

    Surprise Abilities

    I cannot tell you how many times I\u2019ve realized
    \u201cDang it, I should have given him Improved Frenzy!\u201d
    Well, guess what? Do it. Right there, on the spot.
    From a narrative perspective, the villain can reveal
    her talents unexpectedly; she didn\u2019t open up the
    fight showing off a given skill level, Edge, ability, or
    Power, but that could just mean she was waiting for
    the perfect time to catch her opponent by surprise.
    Not only is this a perfectly acceptable cheat, but your
    players will get a kick out of the cinematic conceit.
    Although they might also get a stabbing, burning, or
    chomping out of it, too.

    Unlisted Bonuses

    In other words, \u201cfudging.\u201d When a Bad Guy misses
    a roll by one or two, you really should feel free to
    at least once in a while kick the number up. Maybe
    the sword has an extra enchantment to hit, or the
    assassin got a Boost Trait for his Stealth skill that only
    just kicked in. You don\u2019t even really have to justify
    these number-nudges, unless you\u2019re the kind of GM
    who rolls everything out in the open. If that\u2019s the case
    be ready to simply say there are bonuses at work and
    leave it at that.

    Healing Items

    Magic items do not flow from factories in Shaintar,
    but if anyone should have a potion of Healing or a
    ring with Fast Regeneration on it, a main villain for
    a Legendary campaign should. These are not meant
    to be found and stuffed into the Heroes\u2019 trove of
    goodies; they should be used, and used up before a
    battle is over.

    Surprise Allies and Reinforcements

    Are the Heroes cleaning up the board before the
    first round of combat is even over? Fine, that was the
    warm-up; now comes the force of warriors, monsters,
    or even a handful of bad-to-the-bone Wild Cards
    that are the \u201creal threat.\u201d It pays to have such entities
    written up and on hand for use as needed.

    Bad Guys Pray, Too

    Remember it\u2019s not only the Heroes who have allies
    among the divine; Demon Lords, Necrolords, and
    their more powerful agents often take a liking to a
    particular servant or chosen warrior. Sometimes,
    when all seems lost for that person, his patron may
    well decide to not leave him hanging in the wind,
    instead providing some kind of assistance. Or,
    perhaps, an escape.

    \n It is genuinely difficult to create a challenging game, though. In boardgame and video game design you basically either create symmetrical scenarios, or you playtest your brains out to ensure that there are interesting, fair challenges in your setup. For a roleplaying game the latter is very, very difficult, as the scenarios are usually not played thousands or even millions of times, which those other types of games are, thus justifying the amount of work it takes to craft balanced challenges manually.

    As everybody here probably already knows, D&D went the way of systemic encounter balancing with the 3rd edition in an attempt to solve the problem and get out of the illusionistic solution that Shaintar seems to use. I personally find both of these solutions severely deficient in comparison with the classical negotiated challenges used in wargaming and old school rpgs.

    Shaintar's solution is the traditional one, though: pre-make something that seems roughly right, and ensure a flexible approximation of balance by fine-tuning on the go. It works in the sense of not requiring playtesting before using it in play, which is sort of a minimal requirement for make-it-yourself rpg scenarios. I myself lose all interest in the supposed "challenge" when a game is played in this way, but if you're into it for the theatrics (as opposed to genuine player-level challenge), then it doesn't make much of a difference, which explains a lot about its popularity. And it's not like there are too many alternatives: I identify three solutions above (negotiate challenges, cheat, or use some sort of point-buy system), and can't off-hand name any more.

    \n I found this to be something of a problem with Savage Worlds generally, although I'm willing to believe I was not using the system very well. Simply put, the combat has a high degree of randomness. I found that frequently enemies that seemed quite tough would be defeated in the opening moves of a fight by exploding dice. But if you pump enemies up enough that they can survive the occasional super successful player action, then they are then way too hard the rest of the time.

    Also, combat is based on beating toughness/parry only a small proportion of the time, but once you do enemies only being able to take a couple of hits. Unlike a hitpoint system where progress is more consistent. So I found that if you made enemies even a bit tougher it was easy to fall into a situation where randomness would lead to a fight that took forever, because the players couldn't land any blows.

    I ended up fairly rapidly cheating in this way, just to try and even out some of the randomness and keep combat interesting. It was shortly after this that I stopped using Savage Worlds.

      The thoughts are messy right now, so I'll just throw them out there, in no particular order for discussion.

      Particpipationist game play has a GM who actually is an entertainer.

      The sandbox has edges
      At the most basic level, this means there needs to be some core player buy-in to the game concept, an awareness of the concept, and some commitment to stick ith the concept. If the players collectively start to go beyond the borders of the sandbox, there needs to be some kind of Tiime Out and assessment procedure and this will inevitably cause tension of some sort, usually a tension with immersionist desires.

      Particpationism might just plain work better with some kind of discrete block of gaming, like the scenario/module/adventure/arc, with re-assessment and player input between blocks

      This kinda goes with the last two. Players volunteer to participate and respect the sandbox's edges, just not forever. As an entertainer, the Gm throws what they hope is good, entertaining material together, and the players join in and agree to engage with it. But that material does come to an end, in one fashion or another. The GM runs out of prepped material and/or the players collectively say "we're done with this thing" and abandon that material. The GM needs to then wrap it up, and go on, probably after a real-world play break.

      It's during that play break when out-of-character player input is important. Or maybe something like blue-booking. Or the GM throwing out a choice of several other options that they'd consider developing for the next block.

      If all of this is important to the play style, it probably ought to be somewhere that the players encounter the concept very early on, so they're clear about it.
      Where exactly? Got me, but it should be front and center in something the players are for sure going to encounter. It may mean rethinking how game texts are written. Maybe it needs to be in a seperate handout. Whatever the case, it strikes me as the sort of thing players need to know and be clear on from step one, long before they dive into something like chargen.

      This style probably works best with only soft-challenge/soft gamism involved
      Yes, players still want challenges for the characters and the GM still wants to throw out stuff that challenges them. OTOH, everybody wants to follow the characters and keep them involved and alive for the most part. What does that mean for mechanics exactly? I don't know, but it probably needs to be in there. On the flip side, there still is a desirable tension that every now and again, a character does die or an adventure "block" ends in failure or incomplete success for the player characters.

      All of this stuff probably pairs best with strong genre or source material emulation
      Everybody involved needs to be down with that part from the get-go, and have some idea about when they are willing to up-end those expectations and the limits on that. Notably, the rules probably need to be built from the ground up with that sort of thing in mind.

      \n I realize I'm responding to a necro'd thread, but since I wasn't here then:

      What it seems to come down to for me is a false dichotomy between the story and the game, when in point of fact it's a failure to properly educate DMs on the game or narrative construction in the first place.

      There is no 'cheating' in telling a story. Ok, there are plotholes, tired tropes, etc...but that's not cheating. It's not like people accuse the writer of The Usual Suspects of cheating by having Kaiser Soze be who he turns out to be at the end...the writer told an incredible story utilizing misdirection and other elements. The story wouldn't have been successful without that. That's the point of the movie, not all the stuff that happens along the way.

      'Cheating' in a game atmosphere is only useful if someone is 'coming out ahead' because of the cheating. If the DM is trying to beat the players, except as a story device, then they should put the dice away and break out the Texas Hold'em. One of the fascinating things about roleplaying games to me is that players are really only competing with themselves, and sometimes the game mechanics...but NEVER the DM. The DM writes the screenplay and directs, and the players are his actors with creative input control. The only 'cheating' that can go on is between the agents, accountants, producers, and marketing departments (game designers and mechanics).

      As I said, I think the real issue is DMs that aren't experts in their craft yet 'writing or casting themselves into a dead-end', aberrant mechanical probabilities, or players failing to buy into the contract requirements of the game. That's when most DMs find themselves considering a fudge or cheat in order to progress the story. In my opinion the first two can be addressed with practice, education, and forethought.

      The third is a particularly nasty issue that's very difficult to deal with, but it HAS to be. Honestly no game should ever begin without unanimous agreement on what kind of game/story is being used/told. If everyone, on both sides of the table, is on board with that vision then the only impetus to 'cheat' should come from weak story elements or fickle mechanics/probability. If anyone breaks their contractual obligation then as hard as it may be all must accept that THEY are the issue, and you can't correct for a participant by 'cheating' story or mechanics.

      \n@Troy - I enjoyed the article but I think there's a clarification needed on your end statement. Classical RPG methods (ie DM writes the story, players operate within it) should be avoided IF your group doesn't enjoy those classical RPG methods.

      See, there's absolutely nothing objectively bad about that style of gaming. For millions of people, it's actually very much the POINT of gaming. It's just that for many others (perhaps as many or more than the classical stylists even, I don't know of any hard data on it) it doesn't work as well. For those that don't enjoy it, it's vitally important to be aware of it and avoid it.

      Even for those that do enjoy the old school methods I would agree that it's better if they're fully aware of what's actually going on. Since it's usually very clearly spelled out in the printed materials of those games, that shouldn't be a problem. However when a GM/DM reaches a certain skill level they really can become master craftsman of the narrative, effectively hiding the 'behind stage' work that necessarily constrains the story.

      Further, it isn't that the phrase you focus on is nonsensical. It's that you either fail to understand it, or disagree with it or the warrants upon which it is based.

      Let's return to my Hollywood metaphor for a moment.

      When the actors and director show up on set there's an overall plan for the story they're going to make. They have some rough idea of their characters, the different scenes, and even the dialogue. Now many producers and directors are open to creative input from the actors, allowing the movie to change a bit, sometimes into something very different than originally imagined. There's a constant flow of rewriting, sub-plot introduction, and character evolution. However, they still tell the same basic story they set out to. They have to, or the movie could never get made...it would just be filmed random improv. After all, you need sets, props, special effects, permits, and the capital to make that all happen. So basically this is how a movie gets made: A writer/producer (who may also be the director) makes the story, and the actors decide the nuance of how their characters maneuver through the story and interact with the intended audience.

      Gaming, in the classical style, is no different. Someone (either the GM, or a 3rd party module designer) makes a story, and the players decide how they will portray the various roles and make it entertaining for the entire group within the context of that story. It's not nonsensical, it's the definition of the thing, in order to accomplish a certain style of play.

      Now, that's not to say that it's the only style of play, or a superior one. It's not. There are games that work in a near total sandbox, and players who prefer that style. We cannot, however, make a normative judgement one way or the other. Instead we need to be aware of bias, and our warrants, and choose accordingly.
