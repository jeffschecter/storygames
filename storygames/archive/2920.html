\n
\n In playing a game that doesn't have a GM, there is still authority that needs to be distributed. Sometimes it is hard-coded in the rules ("The player opposite you provides adversity"), and sometimes it exists on the social level ("Somebody needs to keep things on track and that person is obviously Sarah").

I'm interested in the latter - the social distribution of authority in GM-less play. What structures emerge, in the absence of rules or in spite of them? How is GM-less play facilitated at your table?

From my perspective, one trend I see quite often is a true facilitator emerging - typically the person most enthusiastic or knowledgable about the game at hand (look to the guy who brought the book to fill this role). This person often serves as procedural resource and pace-keeper. Another social role I've seen pretty regularly is the guy who is alert to the meta-narrative and consistently challenges fellow players to step up ("That was weak. I want to hear more about that. Set the scene for us.") I'm sure there are many more, and in all sorts of interesting combinations. Share your observations!

    \n Some random thoughts thusfar:

    So there's a continuum of social engagement that leads from bewildered passivity and turtling through pretty standard "player" mode to increasingly assertive levels of authority-taking. Either end of the continuum is a bad place. I think the sweet spot is just right of center, where everyone is engaged enough to take on a bit more authority than a traditional player would, but not enough to be overbearing.

    Designer seems like a mild form of Skeptic.

    We're really teasing out individual social roles a GM plays here. That might be a productive lens through which to look for bits of authority-taking.

    This is all probably bringing tears to the eyes of hardcore theorists. It's really useful to me, though! Thanks again. I want to hear more about actual roles you've seen at the table before I peel off in a new direction.

    \n I'm surprised no one has mentioned The Yes-Man: this is the person who's the first to support anyone else's suggestion, essentially providing a general wave of approval for basically everything that comes his way. At his best, he's "Yes, but..." or "Yes, and...", expanding and/or modifying everyone else's ideas. He's also the group's cheerleader, providing the bulk of the praise/fan-mail/validation to everyone else.

    At his worst, he's the fog that obscures the consensus, by making it socially difficult for other people to say "No." Get two or three people like this in one gaming group and you'll get those games where everyone seems to be having fun and says they're having fun...and then after the session comment on all the things that they didn't like, or that they wish had been done instead.

    \n Textbooks on communication in small groups identify two main orientations, task and relational. Task orientation means you're interested working toward accomplishing the ostensible goals or aims of the group; relational orientation means you're interested in maintaining the group's "groupness" or cohesion. A third orientation is "self-centered," in which you're mainly interested in what you get out of the group or how you appear to other members.

    So you can see rules-oriented roles, those which focus on "doing it right," as being high on the task dimension; you can see group cohesion-oriented roles, those which focus on maintaining the group's solidarity and freedom from conflict, as being high on the relational dimension.

    I wonder if coding each role on those dimensions (maybe high low or high medium low) would produce any insights.

    I was about to comment about how many of these archetypes/roles might be bundled together as subtypes of ultrabroad roles. Bill beat me to it, but lte me offer my suggestions as well.

    \n

    I see the roles already named as falling into some broad categories based on what they affect:

    \n

    Answer-Focused Roles include Rules Sages, Rules Lawyers, Authorities, and any other roles focused on providing answers to questions that rise in the game within their area of expertise. Positive versions will be sought out by the other players (like the history buff being asked to suggest useful color.) Negative versions interject their answers, even when unasked (like the rules lawyer who argues every point.) Other examples would include the False Authority, the Living Chronicler (distinguished from an ordinary Chronicler,) and Odds Maker.

    \n

    Mechanics-Focused Roles, such as the Air-Traffic Controller or the Bookkeeper (player who writes down treasure,) is focused on doing things to the game itself: doing the math in a game that requires lots of calculation, recording treasure found and working out expenses, laying out map tiles (or mapping, in old-school dungeon crawls.) I'm coming up blank on specific negative examples for this, but they would be something likeplayers obsessing over economic strategies in a Traveller game when the other players don't care.

    \n

    Fiction-Focused Roles, such as the Adversary, the Water-Bearer, or one interpretation of the Funny Guy, are concerned about what's happening fictionally, either the dramatic tension, the internal consistency, gaps in the setting, or new avenues of exploration. Positive versions of these roles keep the the fiction entertaining for all; negative versions try to control or derail the fiction for their own purposes.

    \n

    Group-Focused Roles, such as the Opponent, the Yes-Man, or the Vibe Taker, pay attention to group interactions and either keep them working (positive) or derail them (negative.)

    \n

    Some of the roles are hard to sort into these categories, but I'm not sure if it's because there are some missing categories or they are combos of one of the above. One problem is that any of the negative roles will affect the group; the more self-centered the behavior, the more it's going to seem like pure, negative group-focus ("it's all about ME!")

    \n

    A side note about the Funny Guy: Chris's original phrasing makes it sound like a potentially-positive role (pushing the fiction creatively in a humorous direction,) but the follow-up references to the Funny Guy sound more like a negative role (using humor for self-centered focus.) Maybe Chris meant the latter, but then there's no real difference between the Funny Guy and King Gonzo.

    \n So here's the gist of the small group stuff. Group members take on roles, or more precisely they enact repertoires of communication behaviors that stabilize into roles. Task role behaviors include initiating processes and group actions, requesting information, giving information, facilitating procedures, requesting opinions, giving opinions, clarifying, summarizing and evaluating. Group maintenance role behaviors include giving social support, harmonizing, relieving tension, energizing, leading, following, compromising, and gatekeeping. Self-centered role behaviors include "negative blocking" (nay-saying), dominating, attacking, and clowning around.

    I suppose it depends on which part of "keeping things on track procedurally and keeping an eye out for inequities in spotlight time and so forth" you're looking at. I was focusing on the first part and interpreting it as handling mechanical procedures for the group, keeping track of who has initiative order and things like that. Of course, if you focus more on the second part, and if spotlight time is not apportioned mechanically, then Air Traffic Controller sounds more like a Group-Focused role. I think several of the roles already proposed are kind of murky, mixing both tasks and relations.

    \n

    Group-Focused roles, at least the way I interpret them, are pretty much identical to relational orientation roles. The other three are all task-oriented, but broken down into kinds of tasks necessary for role-playing:

    \n
      \n
    1. creating the fiction your group is building;
      2. \n
    2. manipulating numbers, dice, or other fiddly bits; and,
      3. \n
    3. recalling factual information independent of the fiction (rules, historical or technical information, inspirational material.)
      4. \n
    \n

    So, the difference between Answer-Focused roles (in relation to rules specifically) and Mechanics-Focused roles is: one role recalls the rules of the game, the other applies them. It's the difference between the player who remembers all the DR, PD and hit point values of spaceship armor in GURPS versus the player keeping track of how much damage the ship has taken in different locations. In some cases, that's the same player, but it doesn't have to be. And either role may overlap with the Fiction-Focused role, a player who remembers what fictional details have been established about this class of spaceship, or that spaceship continuity guy may be someone else entirely.
