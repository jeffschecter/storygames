    \n You have several options, assuming you want a scientifically rigorous test and not just forum warz ammunition.

    The best way
    1) Find a predictive, falsifiable claim that the theory makes. Note that this might be a prediction of a statistically meaningful trend.
    2) Design a controlled experiment to test the prediction in a rigorous fashion using objectively quantifiable observations.
    3) Accumulate a statistically relevant sample size.
    4) Compare the results to the prediction.
    Example: most of physics and chemistry, where we can design and oversee experiments directly.

    A good way
    As above, but with an uncontrolled experiment. This requires more sophisticated statistical analysis, and is more prone to an ambiguous outcome.
    Example: most of biology and astronomy, where we must often look at the data that nature has provided rather than design experiments ourselves.

    A reasonable way
    As above, but relying upon observations that are not objectively quantifiable or are linked to the prediction via weak or non-rigorous links.
    Example: most of the good social science work and a great deal of medical work, where we can neither experiment freely (not ethically, at least), nor can we directly access what we are most interested in (free will, why we make our decisions, how the brain encodes information).

    A terrible way
    Use a claim that is not predictive or not falsifiable.
    Example: too numerous to count.

    \n (adapting a post from elsewhere)

    Note that the Big Model is primarily an arbitrary classification scheme. As an arbitrary classification scheme, it can be based on grouping together things based on real-world observable data, or based on subjective properties. Many studies have been performed on human behavior in casual social settings that have been rigorous. That means that you can potentially link the arbitrary categories to real-world observable data, but that in itself does not make them less arbitrary.

    There are very few classification schemes that we use that can defensibly be considered "real" and "fundamental to the natural world" rather than arbitrary. Some possible examples would include material properties based on phase transitions, and classifying things as alive or not alive, though the latter still lacks a completely rigorous definition (and thus maybe shouldn't be on that list!).

    However, just because something is arbitrary doesn't mean that it isn't useful. The periodic table of the elements is an arbitrary classification scheme based on several real-world properties. It is super-useful, despite the fact that you could have picked other properties and found other periodic trends. It's important to understand the box that you're thinking in - the periodicity of atomic properties based on atomic number says that the number of outer shell electrons should determine chemistry, and this is mostly true despite the fact that heavy water is poison even though it has the same outer shell electron setup as regular water.

    So, the Big Model = arbitrary. Actual data = great. Actual data + language that makes it understandable = useful. But that doesn't make your classification scheme objectively real, even if it is based on data as objective and repeatable as controlled chemical reactions. As such, it may not be falsifiable any more than the periodic table is.

    \n

      \n Zac, I'm going to represent the aerobic stance more in this post, but I think for the purpose of illustrating the difference and how it's relevant in action, it'll be acceptable.

      Let us look at the theme of sacrifice/loss/failure, or even if not at the theme itself, at what is truly important: The emotional reaction and the thoughts such a theme's examples in the fiction would raise.

      The aerobic case is when you know a character for a long time and they fail. Such as having known a character who was the seemingly protagonist (or one of several), and then having them die. You react strongly. Think of many TV shows; it is not necessary for the character you've grown to like to die, but it is sufficient for them to suffer a minor setback, such as not getting into the school they wanted to get into for you to have an emotional response, especially if what they failed in had been shown as something they've cared for many times before in the fiction. You've grown to sympathize with their desires, and even desire their eventual success.

      Now look at the unaerobic case, where you meet a character in a TV show and they die a minute later, or a character you've grown to love over the length of a book suddenly has a long-lost love which is introduced a couple of minutes before the credits, and hadn't been referenced before, it is much more likely you won't care for them at all. If you do care for them it is often because the writer uses the "Heavy Guns" of emotional manipulation, truly heart-rending sob stories, and if you recognize that attempt, you may accept it, but you may also grow to resent it.

      Now, seeing as these are stories, it's all "Emotional Manipulation", but the value-judgment isn't inherent. After all, the reason they introduce the character and their struggles over a long time in an aerobic case is the same as when they bring the parental death, car crashes and abuse for the unaerobic: They want to make you care emotionally, they're trying to make you react.

      Now, if you somehow wonder at the relevance of this so-called "Emotional Manipulation", let me show you cases from the real world: When you have a family member or a long-time friend who's in a bind, even if it's non-serious, you feel for them. Their issues could be minor, even from their stand-point, and you'd still care, because you care for them. But if someone approaches you on the street and tells you how their parents killed themselves, and now they live on the streets with their malnourished children, you'll know what is going on, that they are employing emotionally manipulative tools.

      In the fiction, aerobic cases are also emotionally manipulative, because nothing there is shown for no reason. You are given calm and relaxed scenes so they could be used later as fodder for the loss and sacrifice.

      This distinction is important because we tend to distrust "Deus Ex Machina" moments, where the emotionally manipulative tools are seen as such, and we hadn't had time to actually grow to care of the characters, their struggles, and the themes (it can be otherwise, but usually requires much stronger storytelling). The distinction is also important because some of us like to cut out the "filler" episodes. They/we (unaerobic) want one powerful scene after another, and they want each scene to have a message now, rather than have them mean something in retrospect through the eyes of the theme.

      \n To start, you have to clearly define what you think the Big Model says, including any Big Model terms you use to describe it. Since the Big Model isn't really written down anywhere, and it's not really the brain child of any single person, that'll be tricky to validate. There are a bunch of things about which certain authoritative people seem to agree, so there's where you have to start. You can't use the essays alone because they're out of date and often trumped by bits and pieces of discussions strewn about the Forge.

      Pick a part of the Big Model that you think is testable. I reread a bit of the three main GNS essays and there are some predictions in there -- not just editorial comments about Vampire or some other game you care about. Picking those parts of the essays (Jason, you know I'm talking to you) just allows you to prove that Ron doesn't like Vampire.

      The main prediction of the Big Model is that Coherent design leads more reliably to fun play, or that Incoherent design leads more reliably to play that is not fun. You have to dig pretty deep into the essays and read between the lines to see Ron Edwards actually come out and say this though. In re-reading "GNS and Other Matters of Role-playing Theory," I find that he mostly wants to talk through issues and not make those kinds of predictions. There are *value judgments* about how certain games of the past produced dysfunctional play for people, but those aren't really predictions. Chapter 4 offers this (emphasis in original):\n
      System, system, system. Or more appropriately, design, design, design. The listed elements in Chapter One (character, situation, color, setting, system, initial premise) may be organized to facilitate greater coherence in Chapters Two (GNS, developed Premise) and Chapter Three (Stance), and thus to facilitate more enjoyable play. This principle is often summarized in the catch-phrase, "System does matter."

      By "coherence," I mean the degree to which a group of people can hit upon and sustain a shared Premise (or topic for Exploration, in Simulationist play) - and by definition, continue to enjoy the social role-playing activity consistently. The people do not need to agree in every detail or event of play, and they certainly do not have to conform to a single, immutable Stance or GNS profile. However, to role-play together most successfully, their shared agreements do need to go beyond simply sharing the initial Premise. To whatever extent they do this, they are cohering.
      \nThe thesis is, "The listed elements in Chapter One (character, situation, color, setting, system, initial premise) may be organized to facilitate greater coherence in Chapters Two (GNS, developed Premise) and Chapter Three (Stance), and thus to facilitate more enjoyable play."

      This is testable, I think. Really, testing this aspect of the Big Model tests games that were written with the tenants of the Big Model in mind, to some extent. Here's how I would do it.

      1. Select a handful of games incoherently designed and a handful that are coherently designed, according to the essays. Select a handful of modern (post-essay) games that people agree are incoherently and coherently designed, according to a survey of people. The "coherent" games should fall into various GNS categories, if possible. People tend to agree about the Gamist category, even on non-Forge sites. You can probably average a "coherency" score over a large number of people for each game.

      2. Put together random groups of players to play these games. Keep each group together for the experiment to isolate interpersonal issues.

      3. Have these groups play the games. They probably should play each game for several sessions. They probably should play a wide sample of games. They should be told to play the game by the rules. They should not be told what category a game is in. Good luck with that, since so many people have read the Forge articles. Better, don't let them know they're in a GNS experiment.

      4. Survey the players individually, then as a group, to determine how much fun they had and what the problems with the game were. Also determine how closely each group followed the rules. (It'd be interesting for a game expert to review a transcript of play to determine how well the group followed the rules, too.)

      5. Compile results. How well does a game's "coherency" score correlate with its "fun" score? If the statistical correlation is low, then the Big Model's Coherence theory is likely not to be true.

      \n Back when there was some doubt about whether the big model would become a testable theory, I started working on some ways to try to make the ideas concrete. To some extent it remains feasible, but you need to add some additional axioms to the theory, most importantly that intentional or engaged play will leave behind a trace in the individual so engaged. Think of it as the relationship between play and learning. If you play a game and are focused on certain aspects, tasks, and events, then we expect you to retain those best out of everything going on during the game.

      The result of adding this axiom is that we can start testing theories of player and group preferences by testing player retention after the game, using a recording of the game to construct our test. We can test the overall degree of retention as a measure of engagement - a rough test of the difference between 20 minutes of fun in 4 hours and the full 4 hours of fun. We can test the commonality of retention among a group's members, seeing if they do share the same interests - relating that with agenda (creative or otherwise) and using other results to test the CA coherence encourages functional play hypothesis. We can investigate ideas which are only barely explored in the current Big Model, such as single mode incoherence - where (for example) just because everyone is narrativist doesn't mean they share the same CA.

      But ultimately all of these results would depend on two factors: the quality of the retention tests (which is difficult to calibrate) and the axiom which I added to the model.

      After arguing about it on the Forge every time I brought it up, I ended up taking my axiom elsewhere. It turns out it can be more powerful than just something shoe-horned into an existing edifice. So I started using it as one of the first principles for my own theories.

      My last point about The Big Model is that it's sorta been science all along. I'm not saying it's rigorous. But from the start, people have put forth theories, then other people find/write actual games, actually play them, post actual play, and discuss how the play supports or invalidates the theory. Then people corrected their theories and started the cycle over again. It's not rigorous science, but it's basically loose scientific method. The experiments are terribly loose and uncontrolled, but it's all we have to go on, since no one wants to spend $10,000 to test any of this shit.

Since these games have gone in directions orthogonal to what was there at the time and have produced successful play, I argue that there's some value/truth in the theory. Maybe all those designers just got lucky, but it's unlikely.

      \n I've honestly been thinking about this, and for the life of me, I can only think of TWO assertions that the Big Models makes. Everything else is purely a matter of description. For the most part, the Big Model is just about applying labels to various phenomenon. And to that end, you can't say whether its right or wrong, you can only argue whether its more/less accurate or useful than other categorizations and labels.

      Both of the assertions that the Big Model makes are about CA---HOWEVER---the concept of Creative Agenda, in and of itself, is descriptive. It basically says "for what end or purpose do groups play RPGs for?" It then says, when a group shares a common purpose or acts towards the same creative ends, we call that "coherent", and when individuals exhibit divergent social purposes, we call that "incoherent". The model than labels three different CAs. Everything I've just said is about labeling. You can't argue with it, and you can't "test" it. If you want to define things differently that's fine, but doing so doesn't invalidate the Big Model.

      Now, the first assertion is easy to test---the Big Model asserts that coherent play is "more fun" than incoherent play.

      The second assertion is tricky---the Big Model asserts that there are three primary CAs that exist within role-playing, GNS. And while the Big Model leaves the door open for more, no others have been widely accepted.

      The trickiness with GNS is that a lot of the discussion comes down to labeling again. Most people that want to argue with GNS either want to argue with the individual definitions of GNS, or they want to change the scale or scope of CA. With the latter, they usually want to narrow the scope such that GNS gets broken up into more focused categories.

      If you wanted to "test" GNS, the only way to do it fairly would be to accept the scope and definition that the Big Model provides. And when you do that, the vast majority of table-top RPG play falls under GNS. (Earlier in the thread I talked about CAs in non-table-top play.)

      I will mention that the Big Model makes one other assertion, but I don't think it's worth talking about---the Big Model asserts that role-playing is a social activity and thus bound by social/cultural conventions. This is the whole social contract thing, but that's social theory, not RPG theory. As such, it's an idea about life and society in general, not RPGs specifically. You could cut social contract off the Big Model and it wouldn't effect the rest of the model.

      Those are honestly the only two assertions I can think of. Can anyone else think of any other aspects of the Big Model that AREN'T just about labels?
