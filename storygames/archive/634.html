So, I've been kicking around some ideas for a Gormenghast game, but I wanted to run some things by the fine folks here first.

First off, I want to tell you what I'm seeing as core elements of what I want to capture. I'll provide them in handy-dandy list form, and I'm hoping to find out if I'm missing anything that other people see as important. What would people suggest I add? Are there things here that don't belong? I may or may not take your advice, but it will most certainly be helpful to me in thinking this through.

Themes and Design Goals
- Class matters.
- Ritual matters.
- Place within the castle matters, but geography does not. (The castle resists maps.)
- Fate is more important than destiny.
- Karma is a bitch.

The Castle
- The game takes place entirely within a castle or mansion or other kind of building.
- The castle (etc.) is a character in play.
- The inner nature of the castle is reflected in the sorts of conflicts the characters play out.

Other Characters
- Characters have roles within the castle, and natures within themselves.
- Characters can escape neither their role nor their nature.
- Characters are somehow larger than life.

Second off, I don't want to create a game in which you just have to play out Gormenghast. I want to encompass the entire genre of fantasy-gothic play. What I want to know is this: what else would you suggest I make sure to look at in this genre? I've already read Feersum Endjinn, which is technically sci-fi-gothic but feels the same to me. What else should be on my list?

Finally, do you think I should keep it fairly tied to that genre, or should I try to expand it to cover other forms of the gothic? I think it would be fairly easy to do, say, "A Rose for Emily" or other Southern gothic stories with the game I have in mind, but the language is going to get tough if I have to flip back and forth between talking about mansions, castles, etc.

Hey, if this game is as kick-ass as I think it's going to be, maybe I can just do the Faulkner expansion.

-- Jess

    As for ritual matters, I mean this mostly within the world of the fiction (rather than in a Polaris-like sense, though that might also come up). Doing things 'right' is important and gives you an advantage in succeeding, but is also the main way that other players and/or the GM (who I am thinking plays the Castle, and may have statted play/advancement in ways that will make Tony very happy) can introduce obstacles for you. When you fail to sneak down the stairs, it's not because you lacked the skill, it's because on the third Tuesday of the month, all the stairs are draped with tiny bells painted blue and silver, making it impossible for any sneaking to happen. That's the kind of feel I'm heading for.
