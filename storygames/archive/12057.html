\n First off, I totally and completely acknowledge this is MY issue, and nothing I'm about to say is meant to insult anyone else's tastes or whatever. Caveat emptor, read charitably, one love.

So I've been running a Trail of Cthulhu game for a couple months now. By any measure I think I can call the experience a success: players are having fun, I'm having fun. But it's not high-grade fun. It's ... just fun. We laugh a bit, we dig into character motivations, we poke at the system. But at the end of every session there's this feeling that 90% of what just happened was on me, as GM, to bring to the table. And so I thought about that a while and came up with some conclusions about the experience.

For one, it seems like the very structure of a ToC-style investigation is that all kinds of interesting shit has already happened. Possibly all the interesting shit has already happened. Of course none of it happened: the players weren't present, it's all just in my head, guiding the logic and creativity of the investigative effort. It's lonely fun.

I get that there are two basic ways to make an investigative game interesting: Either you make an interesting puzzle for the players to chew on and/or you explore the effects of the investigation on the characters. And that's fine too: I hit the Pillars and Sources hard to make them relevant to the characters, I give them good "core clues" so they've always got a lead to the next scene.

Just thinking out loud here, I think some of it is the fact that it all feels so reactive. The players are reacting to the facts of the investigation, either by OOC noodling over the puzzle and/or IC playing toward their flags, such as they are in ToC.

I think that, in the end, this model of investigation-focused roleplay just isn't that enticing to me or my Tuesday crowd. I've been gnawing on a more...I don't know, Burning Wheel-style investigative model but that one's hard. You'd have to undermine the whole "Beliefs are instructions to the GM about what I care about" model, I think. Or else build the entirety of the mystery upon what the players have staked out -- which, allegedly, I've been doing via ToC's Pillars of Sanity. It could be that that's the missing piece for us: the game doesn't really care about how you protect or pursue your Pillars, it just cares about how you lose them.

Anyway, just some self-indulgent reflection on a model of play that I thought I'd explore again after a couple decades away from BRP's take on it.

    \nWell, two things. One, you're not wrong. Mysteries and investigations are reactive. They just are. That's as true in the real world as it is in fiction. You can't investigate without a mystery to dig into*. In the world of RPGs, if you've got a GM whose job is the world, and the players, whose job is the investigative character, you've got to bring something to the table. If you don't like that job, nothing in the world, no system, ever, will make you like mysteries and investigations in games with GMs (that includes Burning games). And in jointly authored games without GMs, where nobody has that job, you never have the feeling of discovery that you do in games where there Really Is a Real Answer and you are deploying your character to figure it out. So pretty much I'm nodding along going "yep, yep" - everything you hate about mysteries (like pre-planning them, etc.) I really love, including their reactive nature ("You think you've got such a cool character, well what about THIS bah ha ha") - and I recognize them in what you're talking about.

    Two, I think the Dirty Secrets model really isn't all that different from what you describe as problematic above, and it's not going to solve your issue. In Dirty Secrets, the mystery is never solved, in the sense that you work out what's going on through your character interacting with the fiction - not even the Investigator solves anything. Because everything is made up in the moment and there is no means of enforcing continuity on anyone, the creation of a coherent mystery relies on the ability and the inclination to remember and improvise, which is going to differ with everyone at the table. Most importantly, the culprit for each crime is generated randomly. (Yes, randomly.) So while you don't have to bring a mystery to the table, the most anyone at the table can do with the mystery is lean it in a particular direction, which means there's really no there there. That's not to say that it's bad, but Dirty Secrets isn't really a mystery game in the sense that the players are there to puzzle out something that's going on.

    * Welllll, you can. Maybe that's a new post. But you can have an investigative game without mysteries. Political games, for example, are highly investigative, but it's rarely because information is hidden, it's usually because it has to be traded and evaluated. Exploration games are all investigation but the mystery is just "something is over there and you don't know what it is". Still, I knew what you were getting at.

    \n I started a whole conversation about this with respect to BRP Call of Cthulhu here. You have my sympathy. I always wind up running Cthulhu in our group, but I hate GMing for a whole variety of reasons - not least of which is that I need to know the answer to the mystery, which leaves me resenting the other players for their chance to discover it in play. To make it fun for myself again, I have, over the course of many years of Cthulhu, applied democratization-of-play techniques as I've learned them. In particular, I've not allowed myself to do significant prep; I've treated skills that players buy up as flags; I've used fishing and reincorporation to get everyone into the details business instead of making it my job alone; and I've... there should be a word for "focusing your efforts on maintaining a reward cycle that reinforces the game's themes"... Baker'd up my games by picking out a reward cycle based on sanity-blasting revelation in the fiction and accompanying SAN loss in the metagame (rewards to the players, of course, not the characters!). That last one is what drove me to write up my OP in the linked thread.

    The trick is to get the non-GM players to feel free to introduce clues. Once you get everyone over that hurdle, the rest comes naturally. Start them off describing the wallpaper or the traffic, and then ease them into describing the body, the spoor, the assembled cult. Let the mystery emerge (or stay hidden - that's fine and thematic, too), so you don't have to write it. Then you get to be surprised along with everyone else at the table. You get to marvel at the ingenuity and deviousness and malice of your friends instead of setting up your own ingenuity etc. to be marveled at, because even if it's fun at first, that latter option gets boring after a while. It's so much cooler if you, the Keeper, are investigating the space of possible Cthuloid badness right alongside the characters.

    Of course, I'm the dirtiest hippie I know, and I haven't played a game with anything like a traditional GM in about three years. I've been getting my Cthulhu fix via A Penny For My Thoughts. YMMV.

    \n I think the issue is always on where the focus is. Is the focus on *solving* the mystery in the puzzle/mental challenge sense? Or is the focus on what do the character do as information is revealed to them.

    Dogs in the Vineyard is effectively a mystery game. One where the GM preps the mystery before hand. But the focus isn't on solving the mystery. The focus is, once you discover John is sleeping with Mary what do you do about it?

    Dirty Secrets has a similar emphasis the difference is that information is aggregated as you go along. But the focus of the game is still on what the Investigator chooses to do with each new pieces of information. The Mayer is having an affair with a 15 year old prostitute! Tell the police? Use it as leverage? Try to rescue the girl? What, what does he do?

    I think people who are focused on that "Do" part are generally more satisfied with twists and reveals that are knowingly made up on the spot because all that does is give them something new to DO. But people focused on the puzzle/mental challenge aspect aren't satisfied because there's no sense of accomplishment with working things out. There's no AH HA! moment of self-congratulatory revelation.

    \n Have we just identified 3 types of investigative fiction?

    1) the investigation is character-focused; it's a vehicle to explore the people involved

    2) the investigation is urgent, with looming consequences for failure

    3) the investigation is forensic, piecing together an inert puzzle

    Let's look at the TV show House M.D.

    There's always an urgency, as the patient's symptoms get worse while the doctors try to diagnose the ailment. There's always forensic research, as the doctors learn all the patient's secrets in their effort to make an informed diagnosis. But the show doesn't succeed off these qualities. Each patient is a one-off who the viewers will never see again, so there's minimal investment in their life or death for it's own sake. And the diagnosis involves laundry lists of weird diseases most viewers have never heard of, so the audience doesn't get to "think along" with the puzzle-solving.

    The meat of the show is the way in which the patients' symptoms and secrets reflect and pressure the main characters' issues. Well, that, and the interactions of the main characters that have nothing at all to do with any investigations. So this is clearly character-focused fiction, just like the movies that inspired Dirty Secrets.

    As for urgent investigative fiction, I think it's epitomized by Agatha Christie's And Then There Were None (aka Ten Little Indians), wherein a bunch of folks are locked up together and they keep dying one by one while they try to deduce which one of them is the killer. The reader is there along with the characters, watching everyone's behavior, learning everyone's motives, and tracking who had the opportunity for each murder. In the end (I think), a final piece of evidence is found, which, when combined with all the rest, conclusively shows the murderer's identity. We readers figure this is coming, but we still enjoy completing the puzzle amidst the suspense of seeing who'll die before completion.

    So. That leaves pure forensic fiction. Is there anything that's really driven by this, without urgency? The first contender I can think of is The Purloined Letter by Poe, wherein a villain has hidden a letter, the cops can't find it, and the protagonist reasons his way to the answer. By thinking like the villain, he finds a clever solution the cops have missed. The reader enjoys seeing the protagonist collect and synthesize data, and finally reveal his process and conclusion to the cops. I think it might have been urgent that the letter was recovered, but I don't think that played a big role in the story. Some Sherlock Holmes mysteries seem to work this way too.

    I don't see any reason why RPGs can't emulate all this and help players tell any of those types of stories. The complication arises when players are looking to solve it themselves, exactly the way you can't when you're an audience. I suspect there's a split, wherein few story-tellers go the forensics route and few puzzle-solvers go the character-driven route. Urgent investigative fiction seems to fit best in both agendas, but you still need to know which one you're working with.

    Crap, now I don't know if any of that was helpful. Oh well, it was fun to think through...

    Posted By: Paul T.Dave, I think you're suggesting some kind of solution along the lines of "GM techniques that help guide the players towards the mystery's answer". Want to share more?
    I get the sense that it'll take a whole group of techniques working in concert. Right now, all I have are some individual ones.

    First example that comes to mind is fill-in questions. Basically, when the players miss a solution to a puzzle, the GM poses them a question whose answer is that solution.

    Example: the GM presents a locked door with a weird knob, and mentions the knob is of the same material as the altar. He expects the players will go, "Same material? Same procedure! Burn incense on the knob!" No, it's not obvious, but that's intentional, as the GM is trying to allow the players a "we figured it out!" experience. So, the players stare at the knobs, note the material, but then start turning them and doing other stuff that won't work.

    So the GM needs to pose a question that fills in this gap. Like, "There's a weird smell on the knob that's vaguely familiar, but you can't immediately identify it." The GM's now given a hint (smelly!) that's probably useless on its own, but he's focused the players' attention on soemthing productive: asking, "Why would the knob smell?"

    This is a great technique to bust out when the players are embarking down a dead-end that threatens to be time-consuming. I have no idea, though, whether this is a component of an ultimately functional system, or just a patch for bad puzzle design. But it at least speaks to my point about signalling via the fiction (rather than through direct social leading like raised eyebrows, exasperated eye-rolls, etc.).

Pretty much my prep every week was lonely-funning 15 or so crimes, often no more complicated than "he shot her because she preferred his brother".

    \n Man, I had this whole post about mystery games planned for my blog, then I read this and it destroyed everything I was thinking about.

    I don't want to wade into the pre-planned versus emergent mystery debate (I think both work), so I'll just offer some ideas about pre-planned mysteries, as I've been thinking about GUMSHOE recently.\n
      \n
    • Hit Those Drives. Make sure any mystery you create ties into at least two characters.
      • \n
    • Add Urgency (as David B put it.) Why does this mystery need to be solved NOW, not next week?
      • \n
    • Switch up the GM role. Every one in the game must write their own mystery and run it, round-robin.
      • \n
    • If you're doing a mystery campaign, make sure you have a long-term mystery. Look at recent shows like Veronica Mars and Life. They had season-long mysteries that you got little bits and pieces of as you go. I don't feel GUMSHOE has systemic support for this yet (although I haven't read Armitage Files) This can keep the players interested even if the mystery-of-the-week is so-so.
