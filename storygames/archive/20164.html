Non-traditional RPGs with less interactivity?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nRicardo Tavares \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMay 2015 \n in Game Design Help
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n We have learned from the success of boardgames like Agricola or Dominion that a lot of people like the way certain games give them some kind of space where they can play by themselves and only eventually interact with others. Traditional RPGs have done this for decades by separating the world from the characters and assigning each of these "toys" to the DM and the players respectively. Contemporary RPGs have gone to the other extreme where everything is integrated through the group's interactions and there is not much room for the traditional solo activities like preparing plot beforehand or optimizing a build for a character.

Personally, I love games with full interactivity, but I also understand how people may prefer something where they have their own little corner where they can think things through without the "noise" induced by direct compromises and negotiations.

However, I don't recall any non-traditional RPG where this happens. Is there really only one way of having low interactivity in RPGs?

    Is this a discussion about introverted play vs extroverted play?
    No, this was posted under "game design help". It's about each person having some gamespace where they play by themselves. This is easier to identify in boardgames because this is often a real physical space at the table, like my deck in Dominion or my farm in Agricola.

    The classic example in RPGs is the DM building encounters/monsters or the player choosing the most effective classes/feats/equipment for a character. However, this works within a forced separation between an imaginary world and its protagonists which, as we know, creates all kinds of problems.

    By the way, discussing alternatives leads us to question the role of the party in RPGs, namely as a simple way to have everyone in play during the session. RPGs without a party raise the problem of having a majority of the group always out of play. While it may be fun to just watch while one person plays with the GM, there are several partial or complete solutions that have been implemented. For example: In A Wicked Age PvP, Polaris rotating roles with a fixed number of players, Mouse Guard spotlight management, Wraith: the Oblivion two characters in one, Capes! instant role creation, etc.

    However, only in few of these examples does the player have a "safe space". Maybe in Polaris? In Mouse Guard, a player still has to earn enough checks and work within the fiction after the GM's turn.

    \n Emergent interaction, like you find in Dominion, is definitely achievable in non-traditional RPGs. In fact, you can fairly easily produce both competition and cooperation by having the game rules impose a communal space and/or some sort of scarcity. But since most indie games heavily involve scenes centered on cross-player conflict, you don't exactly start from a point of low interaction from which to grow.

    I've found a lot of fruitful design ground by indiefying task resolution, because in low interactivity mode you need quick contributions from players that have clear impacts on the game and fiction. Those impacts should link into the overarching conflicts and how they evolve and are eventually resolved. Those overarching conflicts can then be your communal, scarce place where players eventually cooperate and compete. This is how my game Coming of Age works, to pick a simple example.

    \nFrom a similar topic in Barf Forth Apocalyptica:

    Recently I've been thinking about dice & clouds stuff and I believe that it's a good thing to strive for symmetry. That's what RPGs are all about, right? The symmetry can even go to a point where we try to mesh the dice and the clouds together so that one thing can't exist without the other. Still, it can be more of a push-and-pull where different people may lean towards one side or the other while the game may be trying to keep that back and forth going.

    Therefore, I feel that there might be another axis in play whereby the game can either be this very interactive thing or it can almost be a kind of solitary puzzle. And I think that a majority of new designs go for the interactive end of the spectrum where you dice the clouds to cloud the dice so that everything is shareable and playable. On the other hand, I feel that a lot of people prefer something on the puzzle side of the spectrum where they can do their symmetry on their own and eventually share some content and play it with the rest of the group.

    An example of this puzzle-like approach: let's say I've got this cloud-thing I've been thinking about and that I show just a little bit to you. If the game tells me to ask the question "This happens, so what does your character do?", you might be able to mess directly with my cloud-thing before I'm comfortable with what I've got. If instead I ask something like "What would you like to do?", I can filter your intent through what I've been thinking about for my cloud-thing without messing too much with it.
    And this example may be not just about my preference. Maybe you don't know what to say if I ask you what your character does, maybe you don't feel comfortable messing with cloudy stuff right now in front of everybody.

    Personally, I'm the kind of gamer who loves interactivity turned up to eleven, so this is just the vibes I get from different people I play with. Maybe I can be a little more clear:

    On one end of the spectrum, mostly everybody goes left or everybody goes right. Together. I can tell you about my cloud-thing, ask you a question about yours and you come back to me on the same side. It's a direct conversation.

    On the other end of the spectrum, players spread themselves left and right thinking about their own thing. I can tell you about my cloud-thing, ask what you want to do and you come back to me from the other side saying that you do this dice-thing. It's a parallel conversation.

    I also drew this, perhaps it's helpful:


    Vincent has answered saying:\n
    Now, this second axis, more interactive to more solitary, sure, okay. Granted that some games are more interactive, some more solitary, and maybe some people have a preference between them, what next?
    \nI don't know, I lean very heavily towards interactivity, so I'm not sure what to say about the other side of things. If we define roleplaying as a conversation, maybe it's also one that you have with yourself? Maybe there are several Shared Imaginary Spaces in play and not just one.

    What does this mean in terms of game design? Make mini-games that indirectly play off each other?
