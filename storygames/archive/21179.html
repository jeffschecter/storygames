Here\u2019s an example from our CC game last night.

\n

One player wanted to look for an alchemist to brew a custom potion. I said \u201cThat\u2019s probably not so easy\u2026 I don\u2019t think you can do it\u201d and between sessions I looked it up and texted with them today. We use ACKS for the economy including magical research & hiring alchemists, there are 1d3 alchemists for hire in Hawa, it\u2019d take them 24 weeks + 12000 dinars for every attempt (30% chance) to create the recipe and once they have the recipe, it\u2019ll take them 12 weeks + 6000 dinars + 6000 xp worth of monster blood for every attempt (45% chance) to brew one flask.

\n

So, probably a couple of years of them paying 500 dinars per week (which is more than what they own right now) and some monster blood. And they said that they might want to embark on that as a long term goal.

\n

This isn\u2019t an endorsement of ACKS specifically (I usually say \u201cthe important thing is that there is a rule, not what the rule is\u201d). For the purposes of what I\u2019m going to say, the rule could\u2019ve just as well said it\u2019d taken two months and cost 50 dinars, or anything else.

\n

Had we played Hillfolk [we do use Dramasystem, but, in conjunction with various D&D rulesets] or Microscope or Fiasco the question would\u2019ve been adjudicated in a much different way. For example, consulting a card, the palette, or a die color. The primary issue would\u2019ve been \u201cwill this make for a good story, will this address the premise\u201d etc. Which is cool too. We would\u2019ve found an answer right away instead of me spending a couple of hours trying to understand the ACKS text (it\u2019s a treasure trove that has answers to most questions, but it\u2019s, due to its denseness, opaquer than consulting the I Ching).

\n

Also look at it this way:

\n

If we were playing Brooklyn cops in the 1970:s and a player would\u2019ve asked for an alchemist to brew a custom potion, the answer would\u2019ve been no. (I mean I probably would\u2019ve said yes. Don\u2019t know how to make this point really.)

\nI guess what I'm trying to say that I would've probably said "yes, here you go".\n

Instead, here, \u201cYes, it\u2019s possible, it takes a couple of years and lots of money\u201d.

\n

Another thing that happened was that a player killed an NPC accidentally. The rule, and we did a refresher just before, is that when someone reaches zero HP, you can decide then and there that you were only subduing & capturing them, not killing them. But it doesn\u2019t work for ranged, only m\xeal\xe9e. (The attack of the last type is what matters.) But even though we had just gone through it moments before, the player forgot and then shot an arrow at the NPC (who were running away). So they died.

\n

That\u2019s also possible in some games with partials like AW or Blades. But in vanilla Hillfolk, it can\u2019t really happen easily. You set up obstacles and draw cards to see if you can overcome them.

\n

These sort of unexpected events, some story games do very well too. But they\u2019re part of the \u201cconsequence engine\u201d\u2013layer of the game.

\n

This isn\u2019t me trying to say that one or the other style is better. I\u2019m trying to think about my campaign\u2026 which is a drama game (Dramasystem) on top of a procedural game (D&D).

\n

We set scenes as per Hillfolk and that\u2019s where most of our play is. We go to Azira\u2019s opium den or the Inn of the Billowing Sails or to the mosque of Najm or are out on the open sea, and we look at the precedence card to see who\u2019s turn it is to set the scene. When and where are you, and who are you with? If you do a time skip or a location shift, that can trigger procedural mechanics such as encounter checks, cost-of-living rolls, navigation checks etc. I was concerned this was going to kill the drama (obv I went ahead and tried the campaign anyway). But\u2026 it grounds the drama. I like the mix a lot so far.

\n

In Ben\u2019s terminology, everyone\u2019s limited to the world using only their own character, just like in an adventure game. You control one character, and that\u2019s it. That's your interface to the game. It\u2019s physical, it\u2019s difficult to do things, even though we\u2019re in a world of magic and djinni. But big parts of what drives play are story-game concerns, including interface affordances like the scene setting, the \u201cwhat do I want and why can\u2019t I have it\u201d-setup, and the drama tokens.

\n

Everyone has two character sheet, a Hillfolk character sheet (I modded it to remove the skill ranks) and a D&D character sheet.

    \n The original idea was that by imposing these three overlapping, restrictive frameworks

    \u2022 Dramasystem relationships, desires, DPs, needs and scenes
    \u2022 D&D consequence engine
    \u2022 Setting adherence

    it'd be so difficult to even play that you kinda have to bring your A game. "Restrictions breed creativity". We've had three completely new people and put them on the spot and they've delivered.

    So, switching out components in the mixtape\u2026
    I'll do reverse order.

    1a. This particular setting. It's a setting very different from our everyday life here, but where a short pitch gets you a long way to understanding it ("A Thousand and One Nights with elves and orcs"). It's a setting that has a lot of detail over a large geographic area but no time span or time line. It's a closed set \u2014 ten boxes, three books, no meta plot. It's a setting with lots of language and customs and clothing detail (and, above all, religion, which is a huge part of the game). It's a setting that's politically appealing. It's a setting with lots of crossover potential \u2014 people can come from all over al-Toril or even other planets and planes. There are portals to/from Earth also. It's a very appealing set up with a chaotic city vs a lawful city. Also, one book ("The Adventurer's Guide to Zakhara") happens to be very well organized. All those things I think favor the game. But\u2026 I kinda think this rules mix would work with other settings well, too. I'm going to apply Dramasystem to my next campaign, too, provided I can get the player count.

    1b. Setting adherence in general. I'm thinking perhaps that it's less that setting adherence is what makes the game work, and more that something in how the game is set up encourages buy-in and setting adherence. Will have to observe more.

    2. D&D. 2a. Task-level resolution is important because the rules are "the guns on the table". You don't pick them up on a scene-by-scene level, you only pick them up for that one thing. But there are other simple systems that could work just as well (Fudge, perhaps?). 2b. All the other subsystems like rules for "How many healing potions are on sale in Hawa this month?". Good rules for things like this isn't easy to come by anymore since the Age of Sim is so long gone. ACKS is a pretty good start. 2c. The brand recognition of D&D might come in handy down the road to pitch the game to new players locally. 2, summary: It might work well with other settings and games, too. The magic is:

    3. Dramasystem. There's nothing like it. Unlike Fiasco, it's built for campaigns. And, 90% of the time, scenes are coming to their natural end. You play things out, and then judge them retroactively, instead of steering things with dice color. The 2dt push is an exception, not the norm (but the threat of it is always there, which affects play). But the most useful tool has been the precedence cards. This is present in Fiasco and Microscope and Remember Tomorrow and well, most story games, too. (Well, not cards always, but the concept of "Now it's your turn to frame".)     \n

    So, it\u2019s interesting to me. Any \u201caction\u201d rules are tightly tied to the SIS. Sneaking triggers Stealth checks, acting violently towards someone triggers Attack rolls.

    \n

    The meta resources (HP, XP, and slots) are the opposite, they\u2019re extremely loosely tied to the SIS. But\u2026 players tend to fear for their character\u2019s life when the character has low HP. It\u2019s almost magical how that works. HP is your vague ability to be alive, XP is your vague ability to wield procedural/practical might, slots are your vague ability to use magic.

    \n

    But in dice & cloud terms, it\u2019s very appropriate that the rules have this division. You never interact with \u201cthe variables\u201d (HP, XP, slots) directly, they\u2019re always (in functional programming terms) \u201cside effects\u201d of the procedures \u201chide\u201d, \u201cattack\u201d etc.

    \n

    You do something in the SIS, you go to a \u2018dice\u2019 function (Stealth check), you get a y/n result, you go back to the SIS. Some functions have side effects: gaining or losing HP, XP or slots. Some questions have no direct function (there\u2019s no \u201cCan I kill them? y/n\u201d \u2013 there\u2019s only \u201cCan I attack them? y/n\u201d). The variables have triggers associated with certain states. (If you have no slots, you can\u2019t magic. If you have a lot of XP, you get stronger. If you have no HP, you can\u2019t live.)

    \n

    That\u2019s why the rule for just capturing someone without killing them is so genius and also use HP. Because HP, as a variable, doesn\u2019t have to mean anything in particular; it\u2019s just a pacing mechanism for the functions. It\u2019s a counter that measuers how many times you\u2019ve successfully \u201cattacked\u201d, and the extent of the successes of those attacks, and that\u2019s it. I\u2019ve written extensively in Swedish about this concept of abstract \u201cextracted variables\u201d (i.e.\xa0HP/xp/slots/clock ticks/stress track/Harm/barter/Hx) vs concrete function output (the \u201cy/n\u201d part).

    \n One hybrid form of HP I've considered for my own "house D&D" (which is sadly still unplayed at the moment) is to have abstract hit points but with "tags" or "labels".

    For instance, a huge, feral beast monster might have:

    3 Hit Dice (Physical toughness)
    3 Hit Dice (Hyper-reflexes)

    While an enemy wizard might just have:

    5 Hit Dice (Prepared magical spell defenses, amulets and trinkets)
    1 Hit Die (Preternatural self-discipline, against mind control, torture, etc)

    Note that both have 6 Hit Dice.

    The idea being that, after the Hit Points are done, future damage causes severe injury or death (or whatever your rules are for 0 hit points).

    It accomplishes much of the fun of abstract hit points (you can ignore these labels MOST of the time and just add up the hit points), but allows some interesting interactions with tactics and decision-making.

    For instance, is it worthwhile to shoot poisoned arrows at the huge beast? Well, good luck with that. But if it's immobilized somehow? Well, then it only has 3 Hit Dice (effectively) against your poisoned arrows. It's still tough, but it can't dodge anymore.

    What about the wizard? He can stand still and you can shoot those poisoned arrows at him... but he'll laugh at you and prepare his magic to strike you down. He doesn't rely on mobility. However, strip him naked, break his concentration (or maybe break his spirit by capturing his daughter!) and submit him to the same poisoned arrows and he'll fall from the first one that strikes him.

    Same if you have some kind of anti-magic sword: it is obvious that it will penetrate his magical defenses and bring him down.

    We can adjudicate this pretty easily and fairly in play without having planned ahead to do so. That makes for interesting tactical interactions (at least in my mind; this is still unplaytested, as I said earlier).

    This seems to me to be more fun and flexible than D&D's default method, which is abstract HP and AC and then additional, complicated rules for *anything* which might bypass them. Poison? Special rules. Weapons which have improved function against armour? Special rules. Magical weapons? Special rules. (My favourite example is the Vorpal Sword, or anything that tries to "bypass" hit points for some special effect!)

    These special rules are almost impossible to balance or make work consistently - they either create weird unbelievable situations or they threaten to invalidate the whole point of HP (like save vs. death effects, as per the vorpal sword).

    Oddly enough, I'm totally fine with ignoring this for PCs - my sense is that D&D PCs are already defined by so many abstract quantities that we just accept as markers of their "special status" (XP, levels, fast advancement, character generation rules, etc, etc) that fully abstract hit points are no stretch of the imagination. (Though I do want to change the way armor class works so that we know when someone's been hit; that just seems like too important a piece of information to ignore in a tactical warfare game, where bleeding, poisons, damage to important items, fire, slime, magical effects, and so forth all often depend on whether you managed to avoid something or not.)
