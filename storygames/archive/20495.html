Some Background

\nThe year I got my hands on Feng Shui changed how I played roleplaying games. From creating experiences for my players through the adventure, I started to focus on creating an experience for us all by emulating a certain feeling during the sessions. This changed on how I looked at game mechanics and what they should do in roleplaying games.

One thing that still amaze me is how Feng Shui were using the same skills but made them feel differently by having the player interpret the skill through the character's archetype. For example, a maverick cop used Martial Arts totally different than a ninja would.

Another thing that changed my world was letting the players do their own descriptions. I was brought up having the game master tell the players what influence the game mechanics had on the game world. Doing their own description, however, come with several advantages that I will cover in future posts in this thread.\n

Matin\xe9 Was Born and So My New Design Philosophy

\nOne major disadvantage in Feng Shui was that the description didn't really matter. If you used Martial Arts, you could as easily describe how you kicked someone to the moon or knocking someone out with a bat. The game didn't care about how you described it, so the descriptions were merely flavor.

I realized that, as I brought many of the design ideas from Feng Shui into my own game, so I had to think around this problem. How could I make the descriptions matter?

Other problems that occurred during playtest was that the characters were supposed to succeed with everything. If I threw them into a prison cell, then they could easily escape by just doing a few actions. In other words, obstacles for the characters were never obstacles for the players. Having them roll a plastic cube never challenged them.

I had to accept the idea, if I wanted them to succeed with everything, that I - as a game master - should be enjoyed by their creativity and the descriptions thereof instead of feeling the joy of having them struggle with my obstacles. This kind of implies that the game should create situations where the game master, or the other participants, could be surprised and amused by someone else's description.

I brought this up on the biggest Swedish roleplaying community around 2007, about this "new" way of playing and, mostly, enjoying roleplaying games. People started to discuss and come up with theories, one called \xbbbanana descriptions\xab based on my catch phrase example "Describe how you get passed the locked door using a banana". This spawned new games, and together we shared our experiences and insights.

    Traditional Games Doesn't Support Describing

    \nThis post consist of short comments about how traditional games takes over the description, making the player accept rather than describe. These points probably wont make any sense now but they will in later posts.

    The game master describes it all
    I think most groups play like this. Let them instead all narrate their actions to decrease the work load for the game master and increase the involvement for everyone.

    Skills (or similar) are too descriptive
    If you are using Lockpick, then you already know what will happen. Imagine using a banana instead.

    Mechanics supersedes descriptions
    If doing ten damage is what matters to a game system, then doing a description will do nothing but add flavor. Instead, use Scrabble letters as damage token where the participant only can do damage by using the letters in words to describe their actions.

    Default state is failure
    When I was playing traditional game systems, I always told my players that they should describe what their characters were trying to do because the roll could cancel what they were about to do. Compare this to telling, after the roll, what the character is actually doing.

    Each element in a game system takes time
    When the game mechanics takes too much time, for whatever reason, then people just want to move on - often by leaving out descriptions - to make the game flow better. I would suggest to think of the game system as a conversation, where one person responds to another through descriptions.

    Players have to adopt to (descriptive) game mechanics
    Being prone gives someone -2, and you can only use Lockpick on doors. How about player wanting to give -2 to someone but it's up to the players to describe what actually happens?

    XP (or other rewards) for descriptions
    If they don't want to do it, don't try to bribe them. Gather players wanting to play a descriptive-heavy game, by pitching the game to them in the right way, and let the interaction between them demand descriptions.

    ---

    You can probably smooth out these kinks in a typical roleplaying game, and many do, but doesn't it sounds better to, instead of patching something broken, make something work from the beginning? Do note, roleplaying games work perfectly in their own way but not if you want the players to be challenged in a creative way by making descriptions. That is what this thread is about.

    \n Using a simple Technique, like asking back to the players "How you do that?" either before or after the roll works wonders, as long as the GM builds upon their input to reestablish the scene with the changes that have taken place.     Create a Gap Between Mechanics and Fiction

    \n
    First of all, please remember throughout that this:

    ...is shorthand for this:

    ...which, in the real world, means something more like this:

    \nhttp://lumpley.com/index.php/anyway/thread/431

    (Clouds are fiction, faces are participants, boxes are game mechanics. That's my definition in this thread.)

    It's the third picture, and the people to the right, that we want. It's an interaction between them - a conversation. The result from this conversation is fiction, and fiction feed back into the conversation, at some point, so the conversation can move on. This is what happens when we play, and we could leave it at that by controlling the conversation through procedures (of how to talk to each other).

    We could however expand this by introducing a pool of things to talk about. If procedures are a structural list to follow, for example an act structure, then the pool can consist of other types of structures - game mechanics.



    Roleplaying gamers can understand this above, but one important thing is overseen - the arrow itself. If you want to create a game that makes the players describe their actions, create a gap between the mechanics and the fiction. Let the participants wonder what the mechanics really means. Have them use descriptions to create a bridge between the mechanics and fiction so the mechanics can make sense.

    Use a banana to pass an unlocked door? How?
    Give -2 to someone? How?

    That's how you make descriptions matter. Done right, you can even surprise the other participants by how you achieve your task. By combining things that seemingly doesn't fit, you can make your descriptions amusing for others to listen to. As a bonus, it's also one way to create active listening, partly because of the previous sentence but also because what you do results in a fiction that, hopefully, affecting the other people. That will affect the conversation between the participants and in their turns creates replies on what happened.

    It shouldn't exist in these types of descriptive-heavy games. One reason have been given already - players don't describe if they have to adopt to (already descriptive) game mechanics - but I will give more reasons in a latter post.

    \n I agree that evoking colorful description from players is great fun, that overcoming fictional obstacles is often not the best way to do this, and that there are downsides to a system whose descriptions don't "matter" in certain ways.

    I also agree that slow game features (including slow mechanics) can cause groups to hurry past description; very precise game options (e.g. "pick lock") can render description superfluous; "this is just an attempt, it might not work" can truncate description; and bribery misses the point.

    Non-obvious input from the system (mechanical or otherwise) which leaves a gap which description must cover? I agree that's one way to focus the group's attention on a necessary description. It's pretty excellent in that regard -- everyone sees the gap and is curious how it will be bridged.

    I think the various techniques you've described go very nicely together -- let players succeed, putting the focus on how rather than if; put the description after the outcome, so it isn't provisional or abridged; create gaps that require non-obvious combos, interesting and surprising the whole table.

    That's about as far as my agreement goes.

    As much as these techniques and systems aid in description, they also create a very specific play style. They're good for free-wheeling creative improv and collaborative storytelling, but not so good for internal fictional causality, or in-character decision-making, or consequential strategy, or dramatic suspense, or most of the most popular traditional roleplay modes.

    And, in my opinion, narrating your way to a predetermined fictional outcome doesn't "matter" any more than narrating your way to a predetermined mechanical outcome, regardless of whether the narration is obvious or non-obvious. I am flexible on "mattering" -- often I am content to enjoy description purely for its own sake -- but when I do want description to "matter", that means wielding causal power (either to the mechanics or just straight back to the fiction).

    "Narrate how you pass an unlocked door with a banana" fails this test because before you start talking we know the end result is that the door is opened. That's exactly my problem with roping in traits in Dogs in the Vineyard and Burning Wheel -- we know you want the extra dice, and we know you'll get them. In both cases, the only question is how you'll justify the outcome. And if we're content listening to that justification just for its own sake, then why would we be any less content to just listen to the Feng Shui cop player describe his martial arts moves?

    I would enjoy playing in the style you've described (I have done stuff that's very close) as an occasional change of pace from more traditional styles. I like variety! However, I prefer that the quest for description-heavy play not limit itself to your style. Personally, I'm more interested in taking that moment where the D&D player is choosing the strategic option they think will win them the combat, and squeezing some quality description out of that. And I think fiction that impacts mechanics (cloud -> box) is absolutely an option to encourage this.

    By far the most description-heavy roleplay I have ever engaged in is challenge-based play where the only way to overcome challenges is to illustrate to the GM through the fiction exactly what you're doing to maximize your chances of success. Maybe the end result is a large bonus to a die roll, or maybe the end result is the GM says "yes, I agree that would work, so it does" -- either way, it's common to have a lot of description feed into that result. Perhaps that's not the type of description that you like best, but it absolutely achieves the same state of intense group interest that your non-obvious gap-bridging does. There's a gap in challenge-overcoming description too: the gap between the objective (victory) and how you're going to get there, using the constraints of your character's position and abilities.

    Intentions and Means, then Description

    \nMechanics can release each other.



    And sometimes, the result of the mechanics can be released into the fiction.



    But more importantly, the mechanics in themselves shouldn't ever describe anything, or at least make any sense in the fiction. Rolling damage is actually describing something in the fiction. If someone gets 0 hit points, that's a description of what happens.

    Using game mechanics can be a mean that influences what the communication is about.

    Imagine a conversation, like a discussion on a forum. A conversation must have a topic, and it can also have colliding opinions or different perspectives on the same thing. This comes from the participants, but we can also impose new things to talk about, or steer the conversation in a direction, by using game mechanics. If the use of game mechanics releases new uses of game mechanics, and each time this is done, it demands descriptions to make the usage feed into the fiction, you got a game system that creates fiction; you got a game system that forces the players to describe (to make sense of the game mechanics).

    To achieve this, the description must come after the use of game mechanics. So what do we need to establish before that?\n
    Intentions + Means -> Description
    \nThis was an old formula I came up with.

    1. You need to tell the others your intention of the character's actions. What do you want to achieve?
    2. You need to tell the others what game mechanics you use. What tools do you want to use?
    --- go through using the game mechanics.
    3. You finally describe your action. How do you achieve what you wanted with the tools you used?

    Just with descriptions, it's not WHAT you want to accomplish that is important, it's HOW you achieve it. Just like you already know the ending in the movie American Beauty, where the interesting part is what leads up to that end. Baker writes something similar about how to use Dogs in the Vineyard. (Read up to the sentence "Dogs in the Vineyard's IIEE has teeth, it's self-enforcing.")

    Can the fiction determine the intention? Yes. So fiction could be said to lead into the use of game mechanics. But it doesn't have to. The intention can come from somewhere else. Perhaps the participant just wants to take the "conversation" into a totally different direction just to see where it would lead?

    So it's more about this (arrow = intention) ...

    ... than this ...

    But I said that Intention + Means -> Description was an old formula.

    The new one removes Intention. You don't need it, unless for pedagogic reasons, and removing it will make the surprise of the description increase.

    Further on, the intention can be obvious. All of the Swedish games that I listed in my OP creates a situation where the characters must fight to overcome a problem. The intention is then obvious and doesn't need to be said out loud.

    EXAMPLE:
    We're playing This is Pulp. The enormous rebel ape king Kang have thrown you out of his zeppelin. This is a situation that the players need to describe their way out of. I'm going first, and declares my turn.

    1. I want to blow up Kang's ship. (Intention)
    2. I want to use my move Blam, Blam, Blam. (Mean)
    3. [description]

    But fighting Kang is obvious. The intention is already outspoken by the game master that set up the situation. So I could instead say: "I want to go. I use Blam, Blam, Blam", and then surprise everyone by how my character is blowing up the zeppelin.

    So Intention is still there, but it doesn't need to be declared by the player.

    Keep Up the Flow

    \nI started to notice some behavior from the players when I tried playing different kinds of games with descriptive challenges.

    Continue to Change the Situation
    After five to seven descriptions, the inspiration for the players starts to fade and the players will focus more on the mechanics, if you got any, rather than the narration. Dungeons and Bananas, to give an example, fix this problem by updating the situation after all participants has taken one turn each.

    Have a Maximum of Three Elements to Include in the Description
    "You must include a banana, a rope, and your fame." is an example of three elements that needs to be included in the description. This creates a descriptive challenge for the player. Three is the absolute maximum, because the player also needs to take into account what has previously happened and also their intent.

    If the game requires more than three elements, then the player usually starts listing things rather than describing. "I smash in the door with my SHIELD, raising my HAMMER to INTIMIDATE the goblins inside" flows much better than trying to use five elements.

    Make Them Know What to Expect
    A friend of mine made a game that had descriptive challenges. During fights, when we usually killed someone, a crit could instead kill 1d6 enemies. That was sometimes very unexpected and made some players freeze because they couldn't come up with how to describe it.

    I myself have been in situations where I thought my game master would describe something but s/he instead gave me the power to describe when I didn't expect that. That sometimes froze me. I've done the same mistake as a game master too.

    There is a reason why all moves in This is Pulp have the same three side effects.

    It's better if these elements are known before using any game mechanics.

    Keep (Rule) Acknowledges from the Game Master to a Minimum
    I said, in an earlier post, that if the mechanics takes too long to go through with, the descriptions usually bites the dust. Warhammer FRPG 3 and Burning Wheel are two examples where the game mechanics chops up the flow of the description.

    I did phrase it differently though, saying "each element in a game system takes time". Every time the player needs to ask the game master something, it takes time. Did the enemy go down? What's the difficulty level to roll against? Is there a chair there? Give out all necessary information to the player before the roll so the player can start describing the result after using game mechanics.

    As for the chair, let the players create things in the environment that should be there, or have the character have all the necessary stuff needed for achieving their intent.

    Funny Games, Easy Worlds
    I could start quoting stuff from Impro, about player spontaneity and things like that. Instead, I will say this:

    Another way to make it flow is to let the players be secure about what they can describe. Let they describe what they think is fun. Have everyone set the bar for what is decent descriptions, lowering it at some moments. Play in well-known worlds, like Indiana Jones or Star Wars. Play humorous silly games to help avoid any thoughts that the descriptions must be good enough in the other participants' eyes. That can otherwise be a creative block.

    Visualize in the Head
    Avoid maps and figures because they tend to steal inspiration. The players will focus on what's on the map instead of making up new stuff. Let nothing exist until it's described, like that chair in the room.

    Build For That Flowing Conversation

    \nA normal conversation rarely have breaks in the flow. These breaks can happen in a game because a roll failed, because the player needs to ask the game master, or other things that momentarily blocks the participants.

    In Impro, Keith Johnstone talks a lot about \xbbaccepting\xab and \xbboffering\xab. You can accept other ideas ("Yes"), you can accept by building on them ("Yes, and [this happens too]") but you shouldn't block ("Yes, but [this happens instead]"). Roleplaying games usually thrives on putting obstacles in the way, and I can understand that, but something has gone wrong when people tries to go "No, and" and "No, but". This happened because they haven't understood the principle of the "and/but" procedure. If you're going to block another person, at least offer something like "Yes, but" does. I usually go "No, because [of this new situation]" so, even if it's a failure, at least another situation is generated so the participant can respond to that.

    And so is a conversation between the game master and the player born. The game master starts with creating a situation and then it's a back and forth between players and game master as they respond to each others actions.

    \nAfter years of playing with this, we started to look at new horizons.\n

    The Next Step - Taking It To New Genres

    \nUp until now, I talked about a very specific (and silly) playstyle where the players are challenged through the restrictions given by either the game master or the game mechanics, but challenged in a very particular way - by having to do creative descriptions.

    What I really want to point out is that everyone are challenged in this way, no matter what role you got - game master or player.

    What I also want to raise an awareness around is the structure in how to make the descriptions important - by having the descriptions create a bridge between fiction and mechanics.

    But up until now, I talked about a very specific scene building technique where the game master comes up with a situation that the players need to overcome with their characters. It's possible to take this a step further, and people have touched this subject earlier in the thread.

    Psychodrame has Feelings and Social Communication Techniques as stats. The game is mostly about dialogues, and when using a stat to win a conflict, the player needs to play out what feeling or technique that the character using. The technique can be Avoidance or Manipulation while feelings are Anger, Love and stuff like that. This is another example of stats that influences but doesn't describe in themselves. Angry doesn't tell you WHAT to say, just HOW to say it.

    Another game, Nerver av st\xe5l by @Simon_Pettersson, has rules for how you talk. The game is inspired by film noir and has the same rules of conducts as old noir movies - a list of what the scenes cannot contain. For example, you're not allowed to swear or show non-married couples kiss.

    The scene framing is set through a monologue to the tunes of an appropriate song. The melody in in itself is a really powerful influence in how you choose your words and how you talk. Even when I played a different game a couple of tables away, I still got affected when Simon played those melodies while playtesting his game.

    Other examples from this thread: \n
    It's Complicated is a rather succinct example of this. You draw a line and the system tells you "Now you need to set a scene to explain your relationship to Jenny and how you have a drug problem". The entire scene is the description and it's collaborative and when the description is over, the scene is over. This is actually kind of a descriptive challenge and the game I played wasn't silly at all (though the game at the next tabel had an inflation of alien babies). It's a game about exploration (of the characters' backgrounds and relationships).
    \n
    Moments are framed in turn by each Player.
    To frame a moment one has to say WHERE is your own Touched One (TO) at the moment, WHEN the moment happens, and what other TCs or NTCs are present.
    You must then add one or more sensory detail relative to the LOCATION (a color, a smell, a sound).

    Then everyone start playing the Moment normally, with various fictional constraints:
    - only POV descriptions
    - absolutely NO unnatural, weird, creepy elements of any kind
    - ANY element of gore, violence, exotic stuff or shock material can only be described if filtered through 1 or 2 "degrees of separation"

    For what it's worth, my own favourite mechanical designs are those which take fictional description as input and then create fictional output, as well. There is a need to engage with the fiction, and to narrate descriptively, but also no "obvious" mechanical output, so we must go to the description to move the game along.
    \nThis is basically what I'm talking about. Usually fiction steers what mechanics to be used. "I jump down on the ogre" makes the player do a Jump roll before executing a Attack roll. This is just wrong. The player cannot describe anything that isn't supported by the game system. Some may protest, but listen: you're protesting because you're coming from a game design philosophy where the players need to adopt to the game system. I would prefer systems, in heavy-descriptive games, where the players adopt to the genre and the game system adopts to the players.

    Here are two examples of systems I like:

    Fiction -> Fiction

    In Matin\xe9, you can describe how you blind someone, but unless you use a game mechanic, it's a description that can be countered by the other person. "I throw sand in your eyes", "Oh, yeah? I slash with my sword before me as I back up and wipe away the sand." The game is designed so game system only looks at what the person wants to achieve mechanically but it can be described in different ways. They can use a fighting technique, in combination with the attack roll, that lowers the other person's defense, but they have to include a description how it looks like. If they do, then the opponent is blinded and cannot do anything about it because it's supported by the rule mechanics.

    I actually used this narrative technique in describe-before-roll, where I describe someone throwing someone else out a window. A failed roll then allowed that person to counter that description. Yes, they were thrown out the window but now I want to hear how they got back in again. Of course, if you want to use this technique in a describe-before-roll games, only certain descriptions are suited for it. "I chop off your head" is not.

    Mechanics -> Mechanics

    Imagine a PbtA game but where using a move can trigger 2-3 other moves. "OK, now she used [this move] so I have to respond by [this], [this], or [that]". This is where game mechanics release each other, but the result of using them are leaking into fiction to make sense of the use of them. Now imagine only having eight of these moves for all participants and where each move is more changing how the scene is played out.

    Finally, imagine a session where different situations makes each move be interpreted in a different way. Just like, in Feng Shui, where each archetype changes the meaning of the skills (Martial Arts is described differently for a ninja than a big brute). This could be a diceless mechanic where the only uncertainty is the choice and directions taken by the other participants. A game that is different every time, only because it had a different starting situation.

    Something I really want to point out here is (something I kind of hinted at earlier and) that is that you have both an intention with the fiction and what actually happens in the fiction. I described the game mechanics as starting as an 1) intention and then use 2) game mechanics to 3) produce a description of the game mechanics. I think there is the same thing with fiction. You have 1) an intention, and then 2) use fiction to 3) create a description.

      \n The issue of reward systems and incentives is pretty complicated. I've railed against stuff like Fanmail much like Rickard has, and I think there's lots of bad stuff that can happen with reward systems. With rewards that are given out mechanically (that is, triggered by somthing in the game), you can for example end up in a lot of situations where you have to choose between what would be awesome and what is rewarded. This happened a lot to me in Apocalypse World, for example, which I think has a very problematic reward system. You know, "It'd be super sweet and fun if I punched this guy in the face right now, but I've got Hot highlighted, so I'll manipulate him instead". I also think reward systems are responsible for those campaigns where people keep playing past the point where it's fun and the campaign finally just peters out into nothing.

      For those and other reasons, I'm very wary of reward systems, and usually just use rewards to highlight that an action is bad and you shouldn't do it, by rewarding you for doing it (it works wonders, and the fact that you can discourage an action by rewarding it highlights that rewards are pretty complicated). But at the same time I'm sure they can be used well, too, and there are obviously a lot of people who have fun with games that use them heavily. It might be personal preference to some extent, it might well be cultural differences (socialist Sweden vs. capitalist US?), it might be a lot of reasons. It's a pretty complicated issue and I think it's hard to draw sweeping conclusions about them in general, though I think there's a lot of discussions on pros and cons to have about specific reward systems, where the research Rickard has pointed to can play an important role.
