\n In Master Plan #18 Ryan babbles happily away about emergent play. He mentions three examples:

- How Diplomacy turns into a tangled web of intrigues and backstabbing, although the rules hardly say anything about that
- How he playtested a game where going into conflict meant your scene had to end, so nobody went into conflict
- How in CoC, the sanity drain you get from learning the Cthulhu Mythos meant that people just started burning old tomes when they found them instead of reading them

These three things point to one common, basic rule:

People will do whatever they can to stay in the game.

You can play Diplomacy without the intrigue - but in a few turns, you'll be maneuvered out of the game completely. In the playtest game, you could go into conflict - and then not get to play for a full turn. In CoC, you could read books - and have your character go insane and become completely useless.

For game design, this means that if you put in some way that players can end up with nothing to do, you'll create emergent play where they avoid that. A fairly obvious insight, but an insight nonetheless.

    \n Thanks for listening! This is a good point, and it deserves unpacking. And countering.

    Let's talk about my Diplomacy game. At the time of that recording, I was loving the shit out of that game. It was around the next week or so when the game turned bad. Not in that I was losing, but in that people were really bad about being late with orders (and the rules we played on allowed that), and eventually people stopped sending me press. So, for two years I played a "quiet" game of Diplomacy, getting frustrated with people not responding to my press and being late with orders. Then one turn I got some hate-press from a neighbor, and I just quit that game. I was hanging in there not to be the jerk that quits, and because I thought I might want to play with these guys in a face-to-face game (as we're all in the area and one of the players knows everyone). But, as my interest eroded every season and with that hate-press, I decided that I didn't care if these people wouldn't want me in their game again, because I felt the same way. "Life is too short to keep playing bad games." So I resigned. (And, to be honest, I was actually doing kinda well at that point, as a 7-build Italy with the Iberian in my possession in 1910.)

    So, what does that say about the premise? That it should be amended:

    People will do whatever they can to stay in the game, until unless they gain sufficient motivation to out-right leave the game. Perhaps that's what buy-in, socially and in-game, counters.

    Now, how does this relate back to game design (which was my point on my show...well, is always the point on my show)? Does it?

    Lenny Balsera and I will talk at length on this subject, and we've come up with two categories of Emergent Play:\n
    • Weak, or Modelable, Emergent Play -- Emergent Play that can be predicted and can be used as a direct influence in game design. The strongest and most-common that I have seen and used is to, instead of giving only one choice, give two choices but one is particularly undesirable. The players, feeling like they have made a choice, are less likely to rebel against the game because them could have made that bad choice if they truly wanted. (And, hey, sometimes they do -- that's okay, because that's a release valve, and the system/setting/whatever handles it rather than breaking.)
    • Strong, or Unmodelable, Emergent Play -- Emergent Play that cannot be predicted at the design time and cannot be used directly as an influence in game design (though, perhaps indirectly). One common example is when one game is similar to another in theme, system, scope, etc., there is sometimes rules-bleed. When we first played Dogs, one of us forgot, or didn't know to, narrate our Trait dice in. He just rolled all his relevant Trait dice at the beginning of the conflict, like we might do in a more traditional game where we would narrate the after-effects of rolling, but not really narrate what dices we're putting into out hands (and saying "I'm using X, Y and Z traits" isn't narrating). We started playing like that for our first few games, until someone who actually knew how to play right corrected us. That's Emergent Play that can't be modeled.
    \nThose are just examples, and rather easy ones at that.

    \n Yeah, I think I agree with the altered premise.

    Sometimes I encounter emergent play that I find hard to analyze - it's hard to isolate the reasons for it. Examples are:

    - Why do some games produce silly play more often than others, even though they have serious settings and systems?
    - Why do some games seem to encourage building on other players' input, while others encourage challenging other players' ideas?

    (I'm not posting these as questions to be answered, just examples of what sort of fuzzy questions you can get when trying to talk about emergent play).

    \n I should quickly note that, in my Dogs example above, the mistake itself isn't emergent play. It's the group act of repeating that mistake and integrating it into how they play that game that makes it emergent play -- it becomes a mutation of that game based on the play of that group. That's part of why it's not modelable, at least from my understanding.

    Incidentally, the "weak" and "strong" terms come from the Wikipedia page on the science of Emergence.

    I'm going to change my altered premise from "until" to "unless" -- "until" makes it sound inevitable.

    \n Very interesting - though I agree with Ryan that some emergent play may be the same across cultures and geography, it is not always the case, IME.

    Looking back at my gaming life, I can see that one of the reasons for leaving the "safety" of a well-known system with its setting etc., have been the emergent play, ie. the emergent play has become the same in every game, and it's getting a little boring or clicheed.

    My Call of Cthulhu emergent play experiences are not the same, fx. as Ryan's. I played A LOT of CoC in the late 80s and into the 90s as well, mostly oneshots and only few longer campaigns. We actually enjoyed and embraced the (thematic) downfall of the characters, whether by physical damage or by insanity. What seemed to be emergent play in my circles where to grab the problems by the horns from the outset, ie. simply skip the phase where the characters discover that there's something wrong. Seasoned CoC players KNOW there's something wrong and this spilt over into the characters' behaviour. I remember in some of out last games before we left CoC behind for good was characters, even if they were timid upper-class citizens with an academic background, in the very first scene trying to locate a shotgun or other weapon and the player saying: "I'm going down to the basement." Just like that.

    I've never experienced a player avoiding to have a character read a book in the game, or even destroy artifacts to avoid Sanity problems fx. I hope that's enough for me to assume that emergent play might not be totally universal across roleplaying cultures.

    Good Masterplan podcast, Ryan, and even better was #20 with Luke Crane: I recommend that to everyone trying to understand how game mechanics may influence player behaviour
