    \n Some things that feel like beats to me are a change in the situation, a change of location, a distinct increase or decrease in tension, a new character being introduced, an important bit of information being discovered, an exchange of dialogue that advances the story, and maybe a few other things I don't recall right now. The words that lead up to all of that sort of feel like the space between beats. Words plus beats feels more like a time signature. So I would say that the "something important" is the actual beat. I guess it depends on how granular you want to get in calling something a beat. But maybe I'm over thinking this.

    \n My writer friends say Beats in writing is like Immersion in RPGs. It's one of those terms that is used in different ways but always sounds cool! But more seriously, Beats are a tool for making sure your story doesn't meander and each structural element serves a specific agenda.

    Stories aren't real life, every moment is crafted to elicit a very specific response. If part of the story doesn't serve its overall intent, often it's cut. So once you know where your story is headed, how do you stay on course?

    In scriptwriting, story is broken down into Acts which are further broken down into Scenes which are further broken down into Beats.

    If you broke down your story into a series of index cards, where each card stated 1 thing that happens in the story, those are likely going to look like Story Beats.

    A Story Beat is how you build up a scene, step by step, each step communicating a discrete piece of information to the audience. If you look at a story as a series of questions and answers meant to keep your audience hooked, each discrete question raised and answer revealed that advances the story is a Beat. Some break down Beats even further between Emotional Beats and Plot Beats to help make it clear what purpose the Beat serves so you can decide to keep it in or edit it out.

    Be careful not to confuse this with Beats from screenplays which are used to indicate a pause in dialogue. "Tell me who killed Bob!" Beat "Please!"

    \n From my own studies into screenplays, a Beat isn't used as a measurement of the story. Instead, there are Acts, Sequences, and Scenes.

    A Scene occurs whenever there is a shift in the story in space or time. Cutting to another pair of characters would be a change in scene, and so would a jump ahead n time by 20 years.

    A Sequence is usually a number of Scenes strung together by a common theme. For example, a car chase could cover a number of changes in space, but is held together by the chase itself.

    Acts indicate the type of action that should be occurring at that part of the story.
