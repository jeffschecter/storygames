\nThis is a link to the norwegian ezine Imagonem.net, and an english translation (at last!) of the rather famous norwegian roleplaying game Until We Sink.

What does the StoryGames community think of this rather different game?
It highlights some of the differences between Scandinavian and "american" (I realise that not all indie designers of the Forgeish tradition are american, but its a convenient shorthand) design communities IMO.

    \n yeah, I think it could go in a lot of interesting directions. It's nicely set up for a paul bowles/malcolm lowry style story of westerners facing an ill-defined existential crisis abroad. Or an 'and then there were none' mystery.

    the system with everything resolved (or not) by role play is a useful contrast to many indie games

    ('through' is misspelled 'trough' a few times but the translation and layout are nice and user friendly)

    I'm running a session of somewhat lesser known indie rpgs at the upcoming gamex con in LA and maybe I can add this to the mix

    \n It looks beautiful. I'm going to be carrying it in my back pocket whenever I see gamer friends, and hopefully running it soon.


    However, a spot of bad taste:

    As I read this, I kept thinking to myself "But what would Cornell Richardson say about this? It totally doesn't have anything about childhood trauma in it."

    Then, on page two:
    "It's not necessary to speak only
    about the mysterious events on the island \xe2\u20ac\u201c as long
    as the other players are interested, you can talk about
    music, religion, marital problems, events in your youth"

    And later on I was like, "Man, I wonder what RPGPundit would do if he read THIS one."

    Then, on page four:
    "On the fresh cross on the sport
    fisher's grave, someone has
    written \xc2\xabswine\xc2\xbb with black paint."

    And I was basically in hysterics. Blame it on the fever I'm struggling through, I suppose.

    For me, it showed me how roleplaying games can reach beyond the little subculture they exist in now and become a part of other peoples lives. Until We Sink is not a typical roleplaying game, most of all because its target audience isnt roleplayers but everyday people. I can see Until We Sink in a slim cardboard box being sold next to games like Cluedo and How To Host A Murder Mystery in bookstores al over the country!
