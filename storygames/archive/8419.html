\n I have been bitten by the Aspects bug. Every idea I come up with has Aspects - or something like it - in it. The ability to mechanically characterize a character so directly, and in a way so closely tied to the way the character exists in my own head... it's brilliant. Brilliant!

The questions: in what ways are Aspects not awesome. When working with Aspects and similar tools, what - if anything - do we have to watch out for?

    • A disconnect between players as to just how encompassing a given Aspect is intended to be. This one sucks the worst if you have players who can't/won't talk it through. Mostly a non-issue if everyone just slows down a little bit.
      • \n
    • A lack of clarity regarding what'll actually happen when you Invoke for Effect. Might be a stagefright thing as well. Tied to point #1.
      • \n
    • Aspects that are just 100% awesome all the time. Good Aspects should IMO be double-edged so they can be both Invoked and Compelled.
      • \n
    • Players who get so wrapped up in the mechanisms surrounding Aspects (tagging and invoking for effect) that they forget to get on with the actual conflict.
      • \n
    • Players who won't live up to what (you think) a Compel should compel them to do. Or not being sure how long a Compel should remain in effect. This one came up a lot with a very tactically minded player in one of my groups, who wanted, like, very tight timing rules associated with Compels so he could garner maximum Fate at minimum personal risk.
      • \n
    • Players with so much Fate that they never feel any urgency to Compel. That's probably a pacing problem for the most part; I'd back the pool/refresh down to 5, personally.
      • \n
    \nThese are big points that came up the first few times we played SotC. None of them are the system's "fault" or anything; just old players grinding up against new ideas.

    \n My biggest fear of Aspects is that they can lock a character into a functional set of behavior expectations rather than trying to find the "breaking point" of those behaviors. In other words I don't like it when another player taps my character sheet (or even their own character sheet) and says, "But he's Vengeful and wouldn't do that." In other words they divest themselves of responsibility for their characters and act like they're slaves to their character sheet (or worse force you to act like a slave to your character sheet).

    \n Another player as in "the GM"? Or just any old player?

    We're okay with Compelling between players, although IIRC SotC's RAW (wow that's a lot of acronyms) specify that only the GM can Compel. When we decided it was okay for players to Compel amongst themselves, we decided that payoffs to not be Compelled would go into the central pool, and not to the player who decided to push your button. Otherwise you end up with some very evil juice happening between players.

    The Fate economy seems to rely, maybe only implicitly, on encouraging rather than discouraging action -- I don't recall the "but he's Vengeful, he wouldn't do that!" thing ever coming up in actual play.

    \n When you use a maneuver to put an Aspect on a character, the fact that you get one free tag can kinda screw with some players' heads. Like if you use your camera flash to put the Aspect "Blind" on the villain, you get a free +2 once, anything past that and you have to pay for it... but why would you, if you have your 10 normal Aspects to draw from.

    And the player is looking at the GM going, "but he's blind! shouldn't he have a penalty to everything?" Maybe too many Aspects was a factor here (10 is a lot!).

    Also, if you want action scenes to go like SotC, you have to explain to your players that they are supposed to use maneuvers to stack free-tag Aspects on a guy, then have one of them lay him out with one blow. Otherwise, they will just go in turn order trying to hit a guy like they do in most rpgs.

    9.5.4 Shuffling: Staying Put is Still Travel

    There\u2019s a final kind of advancement that a GM can make use of in her game, with one catch: it\u2019s not really advancement, per se, even though it can and does represent a kind of character development and growth. In fact, this is the safest option for a GM to offer her players, and even if the GM is running a \u201cgrowthless\u201d campaign, this approach should be strongly considered for use.

    At the end of every session, the GM may offer her players the option to shuffle their sheets around a little. This means the players may do one or more of the following (but not one of the things listed twice or more), all subject to approval:

    * Swap out one aspect for another
    * Swap out an Average (lowest rung) skill for another not currently in the pyramid, or, change the places of two skills in the pyramid that are not more than one rung apart from each other.
    * Drop a stunt that hasn\u2019t seen much use, and pick up a new one.

    The idea behind this is that characters, even if they don\u2019t grow in power or necessarily deepen in story, still change over time. Lessons are learned. Some abilities fall out of practice, while others come to the fore. This technique helps cover that, and can give players a real feeling of their characters not being the same-old-same-old they started out with on day one.
