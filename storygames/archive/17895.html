\n To grok is not just to understand, but to "feel" it as a part of you and to get a intuitive basic understanding to why it exist.

Game theory is great when it comes to communicate how to make games. Game theory is bad when it comes to explaining the game. Does anyone have an idea of how to write so that I can present game mechanics without having to tell the reader why they exist? Sure, I can use headlines and a terminology that explains what it does, but what if I had headlines that said P\xe4sadkjlf or Fjodifa? How should I give structure to the text so people can grok it?

Yes, the question is vague but that's only because I hardly know what I want myself. I want a game text that purely talks about structures and that the reader instinctively understands the use of while reading it. Procedures that feels natural to use.

    \n Vague, indeed! But I'm game (so to speak). Maybe I'll say something that helps some-one else (if only myself ).

    Hemingway Clarit-ay: use the simplest words to express what you mean - when you exhort your circumlocutious epistemology on structures in a cromulent idiom.

    Dr. Seuss Pompatus: Repeat important KEY CONCEPTS and technical/game specific JARGON until the unusual JARGON which is KEY to your CONCEPTS becomes familiar. Then you can leave out the adjectives and simply refer to the KEYS and CONCEPTS. Repetition is very effective in children's literature and technical manuals.
    C: DOS - C: DOS\\RUN - RUN\\DOS\\RUN ! (stoopid DOS)

    "Use the Tone, L\u014dc": Here are a few types of tone that games use:
    The Buddy: Speaking directly to the reader in a familiar manner. One YouTube (and Several RPG.net) reviewer(s) find this overly-familiar but the Octane Rpg uses it effectively.
    The Narrator: always-Always-ALWAYS in a Morgan Freeman voice. This also speaks to the reader directly but in an authoritative voice. There is no cajoling here, you WILL listen and you WILL follow the rules.
    Stereo Instructions: insert Tab A into Slot B. Best used in small blocks and followed by -

    examples, Examples, EXAMPLES (followed by more examples that exemplify exemputaneously!)

    And here I finally get to the heart of your question : "how to write so that I can present game mechanics without having to tell the reader why they exist" - remember, this isn't just for you; I'm finding it immensely useful...

    Show, don't tell.

    Fjodifa: the Kumbaya Maru (also known as the "Everybody Wins" scenario)
    When two or more more players wish to Fjodifa together by co-operating in an action, each rolls their die normally but only the highest result counts towards Success. Participating players have used up their individual Turn but all participants share in the Success or Failure of the highest roll.
    Ex: Cpt. Spock and Lt. Savik agree to jointly Fjodifa, by both aiming at the same Bird of Prey. Spock rolls a 5, Savik a 3. Fortunately, the Target Number is 4. The Bird of Prey is destroyed and both Vulcans share in the Glory. However, there are two more war ships and it is their Turn to act (since the players have Fjodifa and jointly used up their Turns). Savik questions the logic of spending two shots on one enemy; Spock points out that, all things being equal, two enemy war ships are one fewer than three enemy war ships.

    \n There is a thing that the Fiasco book does, which is to identify what kind of reader is currently reading, and guide them accordingly.

    Whatever your game is, chances are that it relies on certain unspoken assumptions that some people will already have (so it's a waste explaining them again), some people don't have (so you have to instill them from ground zero), and some people misinterpret (so you have to show them how to loosen their preconceptions).


    I also have a suspicion that there's really no such thing as a ground-zero grok. You 'grok' when you're already well familiar with a game, but then encounter something that will explain the core of your experiences succintly. That same something won't do it if the guy's not already familiar with the rules.

    Meaning, reading an understandable text is all well and good, but it needs to be something that can be refered to by people who definitelly don't understand what they're doing even as they play it.

    \n Hey Rickard:
    Not quite an answer to the exact question you were asking, but have you looked at the way Matthjis Holter set up Fuck Youth?

    I'm pretty fascinated with it myself, mainly because of the way he approached reading/teaching the rules to the game. The form it takes is a text that is handed around the table. A person reads off an introductory section, which then follows up with some activity for the group to do that helps set up the game fiction.

    When that is done, the text is handed to the next person, who reads a section out loud and the grop follows further instructions for another activity related to setting u the game.

    And it passes around like that until the fiction is set up, and play begins.

    I'm really not doing justice to how clever I think this approach is to conveying how to play a game to a group.

    \n Always read what you've written aloud.
    Always show drafts to multiple willing proofreaders.
    Always revise, again and again revise and revise.
    Try it multiple ways, and keep what proves to convey what you want.
    If you have the budget for it, hire a professional editor.

    There's really no answer but: to do it, you have to do it. Experiment and be ruthless with the result.

    I looked through some notes that I had for disposition models of informative articles, and found the following:\n
    The informative articles should preferably not just say things but also to show it. The motto is: "Show it, do not tell it!". It's all about using visual material and educational opportunities. This can be done via:
    - ANECDOTES - dramatization - dialogue - lines - quotes - rhetorical questions - create riddles.
    - ANALOGIES - comparisons: "a solar collector is like a thermostat, only reversed" - explanations - metaphors - personification - shift in perspective - "human interest" stories.
    - EXAMPLES - diagrams and other graphical representations - course of action
    - ACTIVITIES - "write down exactly what you will think of", "first of all, solve this assignment."
    - ILLUSTRATIONS (informative, enlightening) - typography.

    Always think in illustrations, either graphical or linguistic (eg. metaphors).
