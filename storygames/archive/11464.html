\n A few years back \u2014 and when I say a few I'm talking about 20 years or so \u2014 I was writing down a sort of wish list of games I wanted to run. Most of these were original worlds or ones attached to particular games (like Cyberpunk), but a few media-based games poked their heads in. One of these in particular stands out because it's resurfaced with me from time to time as something I'd like to run, but have no idea how.

The game in question: Logan's Run. And for the purposes of this discussion you should probably take a few moments to read the basic theme of the novel before continuing, because I'm going to pretty much assume you're familiar with it.

Anyway, if you only know the book through the movie adaptation then you only have half the story. The novel has a much more expansive palette to draw from. The movie has humanity confined to domed cities and, perhaps more significantly, sets the maximum age a person can live to 30 years, which makes a real difference in terms of the character maturity. The novel turns the dial down to 21 and posits an entire world dominated by the immature, the pleasure-seeking and those possessed of the callousness of youth.

My vaguely formed idea was that players would occupy the roles of Sandmen, those men \u2014 and, I presume, women though we see none in the book \u2014 who hunt down and kill those members of society who refuse to have themselves euthanized on their twenty-first birthday.

Looking back on this game concept I see some problems with it, largely because I couldn't think far beyond the base situation, much as with my idea to play in the world of Blade Runner. The setting of Logan's Run is fascinating and complex, but how to engage the players in that as much as with the hunting and killing of Runners? And how to stay away from simply restaging the events of the novel?

With all these questions hanging over my head, and no easy answers, the Logan's Run game has remained just an idea, not a serious contender for the next thing I'll run.

I figure I can't be alone in this. Media properties are an attractive ground for play because if you find the world interesting it's more or less defined for you, thus eliminating the need to do the heavy lifting necessary for play in a brand-new world of your own devising. Surely someone else has their own Logan's Run out there. What's yours?

And if you have any ideas for how to make Logan's Run a going concern, gaming-wise, I'd love to hear them.

    \n Logan's Run would need short attention spans, self-centeredness, and pleasure...but also ways for PCs to struggle against that, if they want to change the world. Go look at pleasure games, like Bacchanal, or at games where you're fighting your own dark nature, like, well, Bacchanal. Take a look at 'Grimm' or 'Innocents' -- the character development of Grimm assumes that the kids are going to grow up (I think they go to 6th level then they're gone, and that definite end-point is a finish line, not a punishment), and Innocents has rules for the shorter attention span of a child -- and both work. In a Wicked Age has some stuff, but players will need to read the book. Who else has a finish line? I'm thinking Montsegur:1244, only with lots of casual sex.

    \n I think the thing about Logan's Run is that although it has a very interesting setting, it's only really been used to tell one particular story, and it doesn't readily suggest other stories. (The novel has a sequel called Logan's World, though I haven't read it yet, and it seems like hardly anyone has.) That's something that comes up in character-driven anime too. For example, I really like Card Captor Sakura (a manga by CLAMP), but while it hints at a very interesting world with wizards doing all kinds of things behind the scenes, the manga is pretty much entirely about what Sakura and the people immediately around her are doing, and unless you use those characters directly, any RPG adaptation is going to involve filling in a ton of setting elements yourself.

    I would take a very close look at what it is about Logan's Run that you want to game. To me it's an intriguing setting, but the stories it suggests are all basically about how characters might come to understand the horror of the world they live in and try to fight it. That doesn't necessarily mean the PCs would have to be Sandmen, but it still strongly points to doing the same general kind of thing as the source material.

    \n For me, the way to do media properties is the Burning Sands: Jihad way. Everyone knows that's a Dune game, but instead of having to deal with canon and all that nonsense, they strip out all the minutiae, and just present the core of what's good about that novel in Luke's eyes.

    It's what I was going for when I asked What's cool about Star Wars? right here back in (wow!) January of 09. It's what I'm going for for the Dollhouse, Generation Kill, and Battlestar Galactica games I'm planning on writing.

    When you do the non-canon/what's-cool way, you don't have to deal with what it means to say "we're not playing in canon." See, maybe the most important part of Star Wars, the thing that makes it Star Wars, to me, is midi-chlorians. It's not that series if that's not there. But for you, midi-chlorians are like having your childhood raped. Instead of having to "deal" with midi-chlorians, they're only in there if the game designer includes or if some mechanism allows the players to build midi-chlorians into the game.

    In other words, all setting material is additive, and you only include canon if it occurs to you, and you design into the game what's important about the property rather than being concerned with replicating all of it accurately.

    \n There was briefly a "Logan's Run" TV show in the '70s, and they must have extended the bounds of the movie to tell some different stories. (Probably very, very stupid stories; I was too young to tell at the time.)

    It's been a very long time since I read the book, but IIRC there were other societies outside the city, and a big mostly-abandoned post-apocalyptic world. Wasn't the climax set in a ruined Washington DC? Is that where "Fallout 3" got the idea from? Seems you could do stuff with that, too.

    Similarly with "Blade Runner", you could go back to the source book, "Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?" and find all sorts of setting elements that are missing or only hinted at in the film: the bizarre VR religion of Mercerism, the Penfield Mood Organ, the near-extinction of animals and the social prestige of owning one, the inevitable gray market in fake robotic animals that gives the novel its title. I can't even remember if they kept the population collapse of Earth \u2014 in the movie the city seemed pretty crowded, but in the book the whole world was the way Detroit is now, due to massive emigration. (And if you want to know what life on the colonies is like, Dick wrote several novels and stories set on Mars, like "Martian Time-Slip", that tie in somewhat.)
