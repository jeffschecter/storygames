    Joshua, it's all about how the DM sets things up.

    You score XP for overcoming obstacles. An obstacle can be an encounter with a monster, but it can also be a trap, or a situation requiring political, technological, or social savvy. The CR (challenge rating) for combat encounters is strictly defined, as are those for traps, but for others the CR is loosely defined.

    John, yes, this is precisely the problem with saying that D&D can reward anything. Sure it can, in the same way that cars can fly if you put wings on them. That is, any system that says, "The GM can determine a reward for whatever he wants" and then doesn't give him any tools for this particularly, is trying to pull a fast one.

    I mean the GM can just say that circumventing the army is worth 1000 points arbitrarily. But how does he come up with that? When the only thing he's got is the CR's saying that they're worth 15,000? He wings it, that's how. And then everyone feels that there's no real system in use, that it's "cheating" or something.

    D&D is designed to reward combat, and that's what it does well. You can also use it for other things, but it's not as good for anything else. So, no surprise, that's how most people play it. With a small subset who simply drift the game so that EXP aren't really important. (In Rolemaster I used to just tell everyone to level up after a couple of adventures rather than calculate the spreadsheets for EXP).

    So when people say that D&D is about Killing Things and Taking Stuff, it's quite accurate. And that's just on the "what do we reward for?" side. On the reward side, what do you get? A level, right? What's the one thing that everyone gets with a level? Hit Points? Which are for? Combat (and surviving traps). So the output side is about combat too.

    This is an intentional feature of D&D, not a bug. If you don't like it, play something else.

    September 2006
    When 3.0 came out, the idea of challenges as abstract units that must be overcome got talked up a lot, with some interesting ideas that were essentially about scene framing as, in D&D reward terms, a scene ("Encounter") is very nearly defined by its potential reward. It was clear, from discussion, that the game _could_ support more flexible challenges, but the complexities of doing so in practice led to some painful discussions (*cough* ECL *Cough*) and a lot of the core principals falling by the wayside.

    Now, -that- said, the restructuring of XP in 3.0+ allowed for the creation of a "Standard Award" which was equal to the amount of Xp a party would get for overcoming a challenge of their level. A number of products (All third party to my knowledge, though I'm not sufficiently up on the WOTC releases to say that with authority) hit upon this as sort of a standardized currency by which they could reward _other_ behaviors. Rather than just arbitrarily say "Sneaking around the army is worth 1000xp" designers could say that "Finding a peaceful resolution to this problem is worth a standard reward."

    My absolute favorite use of this approach was in Chris Aylott's magnificent Dynasties & Demagogues, which effectively introduced roleplaying feats, which granted some manner of fortune point, which could be turned in for a standard award *for the group*. Wonderful example of where the system can go.

    Anyway, I'll weigh in agreeing that you will get much more support from a purely combat & trap driven XP system in D&D, but I think that there is some support of other approaches, and while none of it has the pure mass of C&T XP, it does have options that are better structured than pure arbitrariness.

    Personally, I tend not to use XP at all, but I recognize that if nothing else, that means I need to figure out some extra rules to let artificers be cool.

    -Rob D.

    There's a classic Dragon cartoon to much that effect, with a box contianing a minotaur labeled "For 183xp, inser sword here". Boiling Ants is another such classic.

    Really, CR was _supposed_ to be the solution to this sort of silliness. A monster had a given Challenge rating, for a fair fight, and based on numbers, environment an domplications, those monsters would all combine to form an effective encounter level, which determined how much XP you would get based upon how challenging it was. This could be a genre enforcement tool - getting past a gate was a CR2 encounter, since it required a bribe or a decent diplomacy check, and if the players decided to fight, something that would bring in a whole city guard worth of opposition, winning would still only award at CR2, because they had chosen to do it the hard way.

    When someone, and I think it was Monte Cook, framed it that way, I was intrigued and excited.

    Now, as noted, this sort of thinking has fallen by the wayside, much the same way that any kind of focused thinking on the purposes of Prestige classes did, However, i think it's still alive in sneaky ways.

    Mike Mearls runs a weekly game over lunch up at WOTC, and if you hear peopel give secondhand accounts, they describe it as him running a fight, and that that's pretty cool. Thing is, if you hear Mearls describe it, there's a subtle difference: he sets up a _situation_. There's a fight, or even a sequence of fights certainly, but it's a dynamic set of events within a closed stage. That is fantastic, laser-focused use of D&D's strengths in a way that illustrate more depth than even a lot of its fans give it credit for. Now, Mearls is a grand master black belt in D&D, so maybe this sort of thinking will never spread, but if it does just consider what happens when D&D is a tool for _situation_ and the tools for reward _reinforce_ that. The doors get blown off, and a lot of what was promising in 3.0 cones streaming back to the forefront.

    Maybe it's a crazy dream, but it's one I'm on the lookout for.

    I don't think I noticed someone mentioning the big picture of experience awards so please forgive if I missed it in the thread...

    Note also that XP awards are meant to level PCs in established intervals. It is assumed that the characters will level once every four sessions. An equal CR assumes that it will deplete a four man party of the same level 20-25% of it's resources in an encounter and in an average number of encounters for a four hour session this comes out to be roughly 1/4 of the experience needed to get to the next level. I think this is an important part of the equation. Sure, D&D is made to kill stuff and get treasure (ie XP) and by removing the kill stuff from the formula you certainly break the intent. However, we found it an important part of the design to understand that players get really antsy if the level every four sessions isn't adhered to (explicitly or subtely). As a general rule we just play and every four levels the PCs level unless there are extraordinary circumstances.

    A special note: I played a lot of D&D for several years between 2000 and 2003. A LOT of D&D (including being one of the Keoland Triad for Living Grayhawk). I railed against it after a while and it certainly is at the bottom tier of games I will play. However, it raised considerably once I removed leveling from the game. Granted I also tend to run True20.

    In regards to "rules create play" I found too many folk think more about leveling (myself included) than about the process at hand. By removing it I have had a lot of success bringing more focused play to bear where the players begin to care more about what the character can do now, and not so much what it WILL be able to do.

    D20 isn't just a VAST improvement over prior D&D/AD&D incarnations, it's a pretty solid game as general task resolution goes. IMHO it still is bottom tier in many respects but that is due to so many cool games out there, rather than just it's own suckage.

      Yeah, D&D has always been super fuzzy in that regard.

      In principle, you should have gotten xp for "beating" the guards, but D&D does very little to guide folks on how non-combat challenges are still worth xp, other than a very fuzzy GM Fiat. 2nd edition came a little closer in that you got rewarded for using your class abilities (magic, thief skills, etc.) to solve problems, and I think skills (aka, non-weapon proficiencies- what an awesome name), got you something like 25 xp or so per successful use.

      The unstated issue, is that D&D only specifically awards you xp if your character's life is immediately on the line. Therefore, getting into or not into the castle, managing to evade pursuers or not, successfully or failing to negotiate a peace treaty, all sit on the GM's whim. This being the case, you'll find many gamists simply focus on combat because it's the only place they're a) guaranteed of having rules they can negotiate with, and b) a guaranteed reward for a definable behavior.

      Agon nicely resolves much of this, because it follows a simple formula: Dice rolls always have a risk of damaging your resources, and a reward of Glory. Therefore, everytime the dice get used, a player knows what's at stake and what's to be gained.
