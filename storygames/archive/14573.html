\nWhat RPGs don't:*

- have combat mechanics?
- assume conflict resolution will involve violence?
- have conflict?

*games that meet some but not all of these criteria work

What RPGs:

- focus on non-violent ways to resolve conflicts?

What is violence?

- Physical force intended to hurt, damage, or kill someone or something.
- Verbal or physical force pressuring someone to act against their will under threat of being hurt or killed.

    \nG x B does not have combat mechanics. It doesn't assume conflict resolution will involve violence, though it does hint that violence might come up; i.e. the tough boy suitor might get in a fight. The basic resolution of G x B is, you roleplay a series of dates, and the Momoko gives each Suitor a card each time he does something she likes. So if getting into a fight for her honor will please Momoko, then it could conceivably help resolve the conflict. Speaking of which, G x B does have conflict--specifically THE conflict, which suitor will win Momoko's heart? But it isn't about conflict in a way that most conflict-y games are...in practice the game ends up being mostly about enjoying the hijinks and awkward sweetness of teenage dating.

    Shock: Social Science Fiction is an interesting case because while there's usually violence at the core of a Shock: game, it certainly doesn't have to be. You could define Praxis so they mostly rule out violence, or at least steer away from it. It is about conflict, front and center, though, and that plus the focus on ripped-from-the-headlines thorny issues probably means a Shock: game without physical violence is rare.

    Microscope is pretty violence-agnostic, and certainly doesn't have combat mechanics. You craft the timeline of an epic, sweeping history, and play out scenes not to resolve conflicts, but to answer Questions: How did Marcus Antonius' illicit affair spell doom for the Republic? Who truly caused war to erupt in the Outer Galactic Reaches? Did the aging Tyrant of the Middlemarch Kingdoms truly love his only daughter? Again, I think Microscope skews toward violence because of the thematic material: is there a national, global or galactic history that DOESN'T involve horrible violence and brutal war? But Microscope doesn't require that you ever roleplay a scene of violence; you can easily keep to exploring human scenes around the edges of the violence of history.

    The Dreaming Crucible doesn't have combat mechanics. It doesn't assume conflict resolution will have violence. it does skew toward its Heroines having trauma in their lives, which varies a lot in both severity and explicitness. So there can definitely be violence involved the Heroine's mortal life, and some of the threats of faerie can be physical, certainly. But the game doesn't push toward solving problems with vipolence, and it's fairly rare that a Heroine's response to an obstacle is "I beat it up" or "I kill it." Crucible's "conflicts" are called Perils, and they're deliberately sort of "soft conflicts," that is the Dark Faerie player declares something threatening, unpleasant or scary happening, then he and the Heroine player do a procedure to determine whether she passes through it pleasantly or unpleasantly. Not pass/fail, not win/lose, just "was the experience painful or not?" The focus is not on Conflict per se, but on being in the grip of an experience that changes you.

    \nThere are various forms of conflict in Uml\xe4ut, but none assume violence. The most direct form is a Clash Scene, which is generally intended to be one band talking smack about another (on their Facebook page, in song lyrics, in the press etc) but it covers any time one Band tries to "get one over" on another.     \n There's a Japanese game called Yuuyake Koyake that specifically sets out to foster heartwarming, non-violent stories. The rules have a nod to "quarreling" being possible, but make it a wholly unrewarding use of the basic action resolution rules. Instead the rules focus on forming connections with other characters, which in turn power special abilities that you can use to solve problems. The way I like to put it is, D&D tells you, "You're a young adventurer, and goblins are going to attack the village. How are you going to stop them?" Yuuyake Koyake tells you, "You're a fluffy bunny, and you see a boy who's crying. How are you going to help him feel better?"
