\n I've been kicking around ideas for a game that would do "What is Reality?" fiction, fiction in the vein of Philip Dick or movies like the first part of the Matrix, Truman Show, Existenz, Jacob's Ladder, Dark City - and thinking it would probably also have to support just "lots of weird shit is happening, why?" fiction like Lost. (Otherwise the players would go into it knowing for sure "it's all just a dream" and that would suck a lot of the fun out of it.)

And then I thought, somebody *must* have done it already.

What game out there best supports, or would best support, this kind of fiction?

Is this the closest we've got?: http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forge/index.php?topic=28190.0

If nothing you can think of does it already, what would you hack to do it?

    \n Lacuna is awesome. Some of the mechanics (which I can't post here, due to high security classification) model this stuff really well.

    Don't Rest Your Head can do this, if you start by throwing out the "mad city" setting wholesale, and start playing the creepy-unsettling-mystery really hard. This is often referred to as the "Daniel Wood method" by anyone who's ever player Don't Rest Your Head with Daniel Wood.

    Sweet Agatha does "what is reality" in the vein of David Lynch-ian weirdness. All you have to do is preface the game with "imagine this is a Lynch film," and you're good to go.

    Solipsist and Mage do this, via the "we are reality-altering wizards" route.

    Call of Cthulhu-inspired games could easily do this with the right adventure.

    Ghost/Echo.

    \n Settings aside, because those tend to be easier to manipulate than story structure there are two parts that most of those source materials seem to share, in descending order of importance:

    1) Allegorical Examination of Free Will - I don't believe you can meaningfully play these stories without the presence of choice and freewill being in the forefront - whether it is "red pill or blue pill", "becoming a serial killer via injection", or "falling in love with an extra".

    2) Dawning Gnostic(layered reality) Revelation - There is a mystery and it is reveled to the audience. Which leaves an important question. Should the players as a whole be more like the audience (and be in suspense as to the mystery) or more like the writer/director and be aware of the mystery in advance?

    - Mendel

    \n I think that there could be a style of play where you let things enter the narrative freely for the first half of the game and then begin answering them through the second half. The important thing is to be completely agnostic about them as they enter and then on the back half try to make the answers tie into premise. It is a tight rope walk but I think the rewards would be worth it. I have been trying to design a game that does this mechanically, but I think it would be easiier to just get a group together and explain the process, agree to it, then start.

    \nThe only time I played Over the Edge proper (as opposed to just using the base system for something else) it went like this.

    Robin Laws was GMing a con game, and for the first hour or so it consisted of each player asking a Yes or No question. After a few rounds, we realized that he always answered Yes. Sometimes Yes And or Yes But, but never ever No. Once we had enough material made for the game, we played regular Over the Edge for the rest of the session, facing the reality destroying weirdness we had created in the first half. (Mr. Laws wrote about this technique in on of his Page XX columns, which no longer seems to be available for free.)

    \n Being a big fan of LOST, I have been toying a long time on a way to play mysteries like it. Layers upon layers of mystery.

    The rough idea I had was for a player to introduce something in the fiction that doesn't seem to make sense. Like a polar bear on a jungle-island in the South Pacific. Players would get points for introducing engaging mysteries. When those mysteries are referenced (dreams of polar bear attacks, polar bear cages, polar bear skeleton in a desert in Tunisia, etc.) in the game, there might be more rewards. As when it is solved, the player solving it gets a reward based, maybe, on how long the mystery lasted, or on how engaging it was, how often it was referenced, any or all of that.

    It's really just the shadow of the beginning of an idea.

    \nI would put #2 first, myself. #2, for me, is what makes the stories fun to read / watch. "What's going on? What could possibly explain all these things?" Which means the players need to be somewhat in the dark, I think.

    Then, allegory about semiotics is usually the more interesting theme for me than free will. In Truman Show there is a transcendent signified; in Dark City there is not; in Total Recall and Inception they deliberately leave it unanswered; in Existenz there is, (they even call it Transcendenz), but there's a final joke at the end making you wonder.

    Then - how satisfying a story is it, just as a story? "It's all just a dream" is an incredibly lame ending, unless the characters' actions in the dream matter.



    Left Coast - love the seed content! It's like it's made for me. *A Scanner Darkly*, the game...
