\nIF YOU DON'T THINK GAMES SHOULD BE SEXY THEN THIS THREAD IS NOT FOR YOU

I don't think most games are sexy enough.

We should have more sexiness in our settings, stories and designs.

I mean, sex is this huge part of the social landscape and yet it's curiously absent from most game texts except for sometimes in the art. Which can be fun, silly or problematic - just like porn or other spectacular fantasies. Fantasies which most of us can distinguish from reality.

Thanks to all the designers that highlight sexiness in their games and have sexy art!

Especially Joe and Vincent!

Maybe we could talk about games that are awesome and sexy and what we like about them?

[And before someone starts shit about my positionality and subjectivity (concepts I can shred to ribbons if you want) I'm a polyamourous bisexual queer bilingual anarchist (with masculine morphology) with extensive training in sociological method and feminist & critical theory. I adopt non-normative anti-oppressive frameworks in my thinking and praxis.]

    \nWell, since I've been shouted out specifically:

    Gun Thief was my first game to really, explicitly involve sexiness. It involved a very messy sort of sexiness indeed - that of beautiful, jagged women who manage to play the Gun Thief's sympathies. Gun Thief was an exploration of a very specific thing, and the sexuality present in the game is pretty limited and specific as well. It's the kind of sexiness you see in The Wrestler, or in all three of Sin City's major story arcs (the movie), or even Black Snake Moan - that of a beautiful, jagged woman who can rock the hell out of a low-cut top and a cigarette, but can't seem to keep herself out of trouble, searching for something she won't find in a man who's got a heart of gold but a body made out of scars and stone. It's a sexiness that I'm incredibly drawn to in fiction, in a way that I totally understand is problematic. It's a sexiness I'd never want to duplicate, but it's still got its damn tendrils inside my brain. That's part of what Gun Thief is about.

    Monsterhearts is much broader, but its sexuality is problematic too. It's a game about teenagers and monsters, in the vein of Twilight, Buffy, The Craft, Ginger Snaps, Jennifer's Body and True Blood. The mechanics (based off of Apocalypse World) turn emotions into battlefields, and sexuality into leverage.

    So, uh, why is that? Why am I drawn to games that have problematic, messy sexuality at their core? Why am I designing them?

    I think that a big part of it is that I am drawn to that in fiction. To grimy, messy stories where people are desperate and horny. To stories about coming of age, and suffering some big "learning moments" along the way. My favourite fiction is deeply problematic, problematic enough that I need to struggle with big questions as I digest it: does this sort of stuff really happen? does this hold a mirror to something in myself? what would I do in that situation? would I want that kind of fire?

    It might just be a safe exploration of a dangerous thing. Or it might be that there's something, deep inside my head, that I'm trying to claw my way to.

    \n I've been wondering something about this for a little while now; our gaming group steers clear of sexual situations in our games, keeping fairly close to an Old Hollywood standard, where you can imply romance and sex and things like that but you never, ever show it. Characters who are in love bicker with each other or coo at each other in an all-ages-friendly way, characters with sex lives keep it strictly off-screen and without any specific details, that sort of thing.

    My suspicion is that this has a lot to do with real-life social boundaries: we are essentially people who do not discuss our sex lives or sexual fantasies or even sexual opinions with anyone other than the people we are actually having sex with. None of us are comfortable or interested in having those kinds of discussions with even close friends whom we've known for years and years and with whom we discuss any number of other highly personal topics. (Speaking for myself, it just seems...inappropriate to talk about it. I don't exactly know why, but it must be how I was brought up: what people get up to in private is no one's business but their own, and it feels like violating my partner's trust and discarding the intimacy of our relationship to discuss it with uninvolved third parties, etc..)

    But obviously, other groups and other people apparently do put at least some sex into their games...which makes me wonder if that goes hand-in-hand with (more) open discussion of their real sex lives. Or is it some kind of transgressive thing, where the appeal lies in framing things in a "safe" context that you ordinarily shy away from introducing into "real" conversations? Or perhaps some combination of the two, or something else entirely?

    \n I find it fairly commonplace for there to be sexual content in games I\u2019ve played, but sex doesn\u2019t equal sexy. Description alone doesn\u2019t do it for me either. I love bringing relational stuff into games \u2013 it\u2019s a quick way engage emotionally and it usually adds a lot to the game because of it. Even though it\u2019s fiction, doing so makes you a bit vulnerable as a player, which is great if you love a good story gut punch. Multiple times I\u2019ve added potentially sexy additions to a game \u2013 anything from aggressively pursuing a love interest to dropping subtle hints, but it ultimately requires willing reciprocation from the other player. Sometimes other players just aren\u2019t interested and other times they love a good unrequited love story, which still makes for an engaging game. But the sexy games? Those are the games where another player gets what you\u2019re doing and wants to go there too. Doesn\u2019t even have to be sex. Just the electric hint of it.

    I mentioned willing reciprocation because there are some games out there where you can manipulate another character in the way they relate to your character. How We Came to Live Here and Misery Bubblegum come to mind. These games can both be very sexy, but because it\u2019s possible to mechanically manipulate other characters, you may also get love or sex in the fiction without it feeling sexy to the players.

    Other games like Kagematsu, The Mountain Witch (if you pull the obsessive love Dark Fate), and Shooting the Moon can also be very sexy (or involve a lot of sensual and sexual content, Kagematsu especially), but they can all easily take on a certain desperation if the hoped for lover withdraws. And that can get downright ugly (but still awesome as the story goes).

    The games I\u2019ve played that have provided a more reliable means to sexiness (or any good emotional bonds for that matter) tend to involve a feeling of camaraderie among the players and their characters (starting out as a team or friends somehow) in desperate conditions, like in Misspent Youth or Grey Ranks.

    Jeepform and other more freeform games like Under My Skin can be pretty sexy too, as they often tackle emotional issues, but they\u2019re often mixed bags. I\u2019ve encountered both some of the most sexy and most horrifying sex/relational stories of any games (which, for an emo gut punch story lover like me keeps me coming back!).

    Also worth mentioning: long term campaigns like those you can get from Burning Wheel and Burning Empires are awesome for developing multifaceted, deep relationships that can be incredibly sexy. My favorite was an unlikely pair that developed and slow cooked for months and through many adventures and awkward situations in a BW game (a PC & an NPC). In the midst of a world gone crazy they were both struggling with religious restrictions and they managed to keep chaste until their wedding, but the sexual tension was fantastic.

    I don\u2019t know how much it has to do with talking about sex in real life. Some, I suspect. But I don\u2019t discuss sex all the often with friends even though I bring it into many games. I guess I feel like a story is incomplete if emotional connections haven\u2019t been made and sexual situations are an easy way (but not the only way) to do this. I am all in favor of more sexiness in games.

Fast Times at Ridgemont High has this one scene that's positively beautiful and endearing.
