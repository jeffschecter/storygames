    \n I like to design one-shot scenarios in my head. Assume 5 pre-gen characters, a game system you know well, with a slightly drifted setting of your choice. Design pre-gen characters and opposition so that you can sit down with any combination of 2-5 players and start off everyone with a bang. If that's too easy, you should probably be solving Go problems in your head or something.

    \n If you could fit a laptop in your lap (perhaps you have an EEE or something similar?) then you could be making playlists for mix CDs.
    Perhaps this would be for Ribbon Drive, or you're soundtracking upcoming sessions of games you'll run, or you're trying to make a list to impress the hot girl sitting across from you.

    I write poetry on transit often.
    The brilliance is that 5-30 minutes is the ideal writing window for most poetic forms, I find.

    Reading books.

    If you want to play the Arr Pee Gees, you could totally grab a Fighting Fantasy or Choose-Your-Own-Adventure book! (Some "adventure books" are like choose-your-own-adventure novels, but you get a character with stats, and sometimes roll dice... You could carry your necessary dice in a small tupperware container for such purposes).

    Talking to people is good, but I find talking to strangers sometimes quite draining. Because I have to force buy-in on a lot of the things they say, because I've got no relationship with them that AUTOMATICALLY generates that buy-in for me.

    You could call someone and talk with them while in transit.
    Maybe even telephone gaming with someone who has an overlapping transit/downtime schedule!

    Take 12 index cards. Write a treasure hunt. Hide them in really remote places on the train, and if people look at you weird, explain, "I'm designing a treasure hunt for whoever rides this train next. Want to help me?" Then, leave the first clue in plain sight when you get off the train. Have a prize attached to the last clue! LIKE, A DOLLAR!

    \n That looks great, Guy.

    At the risk of being all 19th Century, you could read, of course. That's a wonderfully useful way to spend three hours a day. Either take that game design course that's going round, which gives you something to read for; or the complete works of Jack Vance and Lovecraft should keep you going for a while.

    What helped me most, in learning game design, was reading old Game Chef games. What helped me second-most was giving feedback on other people's designs, often by scribbling on printouts on train journeys. Those might be good, if you do have the room.

    \n On car rides and train rides (e.g., to and from Montreal recently) I tend to "pimp out" (cf car reference) plotlines or encounters.

    E.G., review encounters to see if they flow okay, can be spiced tactically, have enough links to story potential; review things like NPC's that I may have under or over invested effort in and figure out "so where do they fit in, how do they matter."
