    \n You know, I never really thought about that, but I think because most of my gaming buddies and I learned how to GM by having rotating games all throughout junior high and high school. We all had a chance to run things, figure out our strengths and weaknesses in GMing, and got to learn from the strengths and weaknesses of everyone else's GMing style. It is, though, probably something that should be given some thought, as I notice more adults getting into RPGs later, and sometimes have to start from scratch.
    If anything, I'd say make sure the players, up front, know that you're trying to improve your GMing ability, and would appreciate feedback or maybe even helpful hints in-game to make things run more smoothly. And maybe start with simpler games or ones with very defined structures, and then work up to ones that are more complex or offer a lot more GM control and improvisation.

    \n How's our current Apocalypse World game going for you, Jamie? Because, for me, it's been a very fruitful learning experience trying out a bunch of new techniques for running the sandbox-y sorts of games I aspired to (but mostly failed to produce) playing Rifts and In Nomine in my youth. It's definitely not my best GMing work, by far, but thankfully AW gives you enough tools and support that it still works out pretty well even though I'm kinda faking my way through an unfamiliar set of GM techniques. Are the players mostly being tolerant or is it actually fun? Probably some of both, I reckon.

    In my experience, most roleplayers who are not John Harper will put up with a lot of mediocre-to-terrible play before they would choose to stop playing. One of the things I really admire about John, having played with him in a few games now, is that he's not afraid to call out when things don't seem to be working after a few sessions and suggest moving on to something else, often with a different mix of people. And there's no real judgment or blame thrown around. You just move on and look forward to playing the next game.

    And, honestly, maybe that's best when things aren't working. Sometimes you can learn to be a better GM thought poor experiences, but often you need some time between those experience to reflect on what you've learned, internalize it, and enjoy playing games that you're actually good at running. Because it's not like GMing is some kind of universal set of techniques -- despite what all those 1990s games and GM handbooks tried to tell us -- and if you only play games that you're not that good at running, that sounds like an easy way to get burnt out and decide you don't really like GMing very much.

    So, yeah, the best way is probably to keep trying new stuff regularly, learning and growing and pushing yourselves, but also enjoy playing stuff that you know will be awesome. Personally, I'm always ready to fall back on The Mountain Witch or Bliss Stage or Dogs in the Vineyard as indie games that I know I can make work under nearly any circumstances, as long as the players are engaged. You can't be pushing yourself all the time.

    For which games?

    I'm very skilled at running Dogs in the Vineyard but running D&D 4E requires a completely different skill set which I have trouble with.

    I don't agree with Jason that Scene Framing = Railroading but I do agree with him that running dungeon crawl modules isn't railroading. You could also consider running a Trail of Cthulhu module. Probably harder than a dungeon crawl but their modules are excellent.

    Start small. Aim for a single 4 hour session with 3-4 players and pre-generated characters. Low commitment, low pressure. Burning Wheel scenarios are excellent examples of perfectly timed convention games: http://www.burningwheel.org/wiki/index.php?title=Downloads#Scenarios_and_Demos

    I was a terrible GM for many years. Playing with the same group of people didn't improve my skills. Instead I strived to play with as many different people and different games as I could. I started as a player and tried to observe as many GMs as I could. Absorbing everything I could from Luke Crane was a game changer. He's a very skilled convention GM who learned how to do more with less. The above scenarios may give you a peak into his brilliant head!

    Posted By: stupidgremlinAnd maybe start with simpler games or ones with very defined structures
    \nWhat are the best games to learn to GM with?

    The 'professional' skill that has the most applicability to Gamemastering isn't public speaking, it's meeting facilitation. Openness is a very valuable trait for a facilitator, though if you're running games for strangers with no previous experience, that muddies things a bit, and makes public speaking skills more important.

    One game I'd recommend for learning to gamemaster is Mouse Guard. It gives you very simple procedures for gamemastering, and if you practice those and get good at them, you will find yourself using them over and over again elsewhere.

    If you know you are going to run a game for strangers or inexperienced players, then practice your pitch and your explanation of the rules. This is the place where public speaking skills can be really important. Gamemastering is always easiest if the players come into the game excited about playing. If your explanation of what the game is about and why it's fun is weak, slow, rambling, etc, that excitement and energy is going to dissipate. Similarly, if your explanation of the rules is boring and takes you a long time, the players will start losing enthusiasm. They will also be less likely to actually understand the rules, which means you're going be stopping play for momentum-killing rules explanations during the session.

    \n This is, of course, a huge topic. But I want to roll off of a couple of posts:

    I have found:
    - Listening
    - Going Slower

    ...to be helpful to a huge degree as a Game Master.

    Oh. I also took the the whole connotation of the term "Game Master" out to the back yard and shot it twice in the head. I'm pretty sure we're stuck with that ugly, unfortunate term for all time to come. But the following has become my default (and only apply to my approach, so there's no big argument to be had):

    - the things I play these days aren't "games." Sorcerer or Hero Wars or Dogs in the Vineyard or whatever, each is toy we play with socially. Or, another metaphor, tools or instruments, like playing a piano in a duet.
    - I am "master" of nothing. I'm a fellow player, a peer, playing on the same team as the Players. I have a different function than the Players, the way a first baseman has a different function than a pitcher... but we're playing together.

    The whole notion that I'm supposed to know everything, unlock the wonders of creativity for the Players and somehow be the Inception device that the Players can plug into is nowhere to be found.

    Which leads to a corollary proposition: that the Players better show up to play as well. They better want there to play this game with these people at this time. They have to bring the fun to the table to, to invest in jiggering solutions if the game gets snagged, to create characters they fucking care about investing in situations they care about. They have to be ready to help build the fiction as anyone else would in a creative endeavor. Again, they do it from a different role than the Game Master -- they're only playing their character. But they better be there to play the fuck out of that character with passion and investment. If things are slowing for them,they have to be willing to speak up and say, "I'm going to need more here," or push harder into the narrative or something.

    This isn't a one way street, in other words. If you're friends are coming over because you told them you just bought a cool magic kit from the novelty store and they're willing to come over for a couple of hours because you said you want to show off some tricks for them, and they're like "Hey, why not, Jamie's a nice guy," there's probably going to be complications at the game table, 'cause that's not how these games work.

    Posted By: jenskotI have a few friends who did all of the above and still felt stuck GM wise. The single biggest thing they did that drastically 100% improved their GMing skills was go to\u2026 therapy.

    2 of my friends in particular were as different as night and day at the gaming table after a few months of therapy. I don't know if this will work for everyone, but I've talked to a lot of people in private who claim that was their tipping point.
    \nI'm not one of those two guys, and I don't think it happened in a few months, but I have had this conversation with you and and am glad for you to share what you got out of my contributions thereof!

    Basically I'm totally in agreement that psychoanalysis helped me stop worrying about being the best GM and instead pay attention to what I myself enjoy and what I can control, not what others think of me or how I match up to some imaginary ideal. I know this leads to me having more fun, and I'm pretty sure my players do too.

    It might be extreme to say that all problems people have as players and GMs are problems with their psychology, but there are sure lots of things that come up in gaming that can bring out those kinds of patterns - e.g. GMing tends to be surrounded by narcissistic fantasies about having power and control, but also being dependent on others to give that to you, so that you're alternating between ego trips and disappointed rage that your genius is wasted on swine.

    Getting my head a little more straightened out about this stuff made it possible for me to understand & embrace the kind of right-on indie ideas that Mr. Kubasik is dropping above, or the really old-school ideas of being a Referee and a neutral arbiter of oracular dice and player input rather than the johnnie-come-lately trad notion of the Dungeon Master. So yeah there's technical things I can point to and say "these made me a better GM" but without therapy to work on the psychology behind 'em I don't think I would have been able to pick up those tools.

    \n Try figuring out some way you want to evolve your play and then work out a training ritual around it.

    For an example: once upon a time I wanted to move our GURPS-by-Skype game towards conflict resolution and away from task resolution. I also wanted to generate more interesting failures -- complicating failures rather than full-stop failures.

    My ritual was this: whenever someone asked to make a skill roll, I would ask "What are you getting at? What do you want from this? And what are the dangers?" I'd do this even if the answers were completely obvious to me, and I kept focusing on those questions until we had consensus about stakes and risks.

    The ritual gives you something to directly refer to when you get to the friendly feedback phase. This helps ground the technique experiment in something concrete, so you can know if you're on the right path or not.

    \n Jamie,

    I just realize something I do to GM... because I just caught myself doing it.

    When I'm getting set to GM a game (well before I've even set a game or players) I dig into the rule book and make a kind of cheatsheet/flowchart for myself to really establish clearly the process of the tame. (I'm doing this with the Hero Wars rules right now.)

    A lot of game texts are not that clear, with rules and exceptions and subsystems all over the book. Moreover, how the pieces of the game fit together to impact each other and/or produce certain results in play is not always explicit.

    By laying out the process of how one makes characters leading into how one makes setting (or vice versa), which leads into how one settles conflicts and so on, I get a good idea of how the pieces feed each other and the gears mesh. My putting the game elements in my own process of translation, I make it mine to understand.

    I tend to type this all out, building a set of steps for myself, so I know how I'm going to play the game and how I'm going to walk the Players through the steps of play when different aspects of play show up.

    It also means that when I find portions of the game that don't make sense to me or I don't understand how they fit with other pieces, I have to sit there and struggle with them. If I'm building a kind of Flowchart of Understanding the Game and I really don't see how or where one element goes, it usually means I misread something, or didn't read it clearly, or don't see yet how it does actually mesh with the other gears of the game. By having to figure out where to put it in the process of play, I tend to sort out how it actually does fit in the game.

    \n I'll add another benefit of this:

    So, over at RPG.net I was involved with some great folks about HeroQuest 2. It was a great thread, and really helpful, with everyone, contra the Internet, being really great together.

    Anyway... When I started reading HeroQuest 2 when it was first published, there were all sorts of alarm bells that went off for me. Specifically about Robin Laws' focus on Success and Failure primary structure of building stories. I eventually started skimming the rules, because I could just tell that it really wasn't a game for me.

    But the truth was, if you had put a gun to my head and said, "Justify your position!" my brains and blood and little shards of bone would have ended up on the wall. Because the truth is I hadn't done the work to know really why I was getting so turned off. Not that it's my job to do that work, mind you. I have enough to do already. But still, I didn't really know.

    So, as I was rummaging through Hero Wars, I thought I'd take a look at HeroQuest 2 again, because what the heck. It's got a decade of thought on top of it since Hero Wars. Maybe I was missing something. Maybe the game was the right one to use instead of Hero Wars.

    My rummaging led me to the thread on RPG.net, and the folks there answered a bunch of questions I had, and clarified some of the text for me. And this, in turn, sent me back into the text to read it more clearly. And I began to see how the rules and the text formed little gears that reinforced each other in play and I began to get a better handle on exactly what Laws had built. And then, armed with this understanding, I asked more questions on the thread, about how actual play works out for these folks when using HeroQuest 2, in regard to a few elements of play that would either confirm or deny what I think the rules did. And the answers confirmed what I was thinking.

    What all this did was let me see, specifically, how my instincts were right. But now I had taken the gears apart and laid them out on the table and saw how the teeth fit together into a game I really would not want to play. Because what Laws wants for fun isn't my idea of fun. And he built his fun pretty specifically into HeroQuest 2.

    My point is, that by doing this work of really sorting out the process of play, rule-by-rule, chart-by-chart, I was able to say, "Okay, this game is about this, and that is not something I really want to do."

    That's valuable! I think at least. All these games are doing all these different things. And some of those things might not be things that actually turn you on once you open them up and take a look inside.

    In my view, to Game Master these games means boring down to the gear-interlocking level of understanding where you can make a judgment about whether you even want to play it or not.
