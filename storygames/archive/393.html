The cheap way out of design

suffusion
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
To put it quite simply, I've been working on a Firefly game. I am dissapointed with the number of stories out there and so set out to write something. I know there are Firefly systems already, even an official one, but I wanted something that actually emulated the feel of the show, with Whedon's relationship driven story.

Enough background. Essentially, I'm having trouble fitting a character such as River into my mechanics. After several unsatisfying attempts, I just decided that she is an abberation and can not be described in the terms that other characters are.

I am left with a feeling of dissatisfaction. It feels like the cheap way out of design. I feel my system should support any story the players want to tell (within the conventions of setting and mood and so on) but I honestly think that part of what made River a big deal in the series is that she Did Not Fit In. But is it okay to just leave it at that? Make the players of the game figure it out themselves?

    A couple of things.

    First, I didn't really intend on this becoming a 'best system to play firefly with'. My answer to that is always, "One designed specifically for it." And no, the Serenity RPG published by MWP does not count. I have not read the book myself, but descriptions from people I know suggest that it is fairly generic with lots of color tacked on. This leads into my disdain of the wide variety of 'conversions' out there. A properly designed game is going to give its own feel to the game. Frex, Dogs is completely the wrong system to use of you are trying to tell a story like that in Firefly. The characters, for the most part, are not agents of authority wandering around making moral choices for the people living on the planets they visit.

    Likewise, PTA is all about emulating the feel of a television show. While Firefly was indeed a TV show, PTA just doesn't seem right for it. I have not read the book, but from my play experiences and discussion this topic with others, PTA seems to be more about TV than the actual story in a show. I don't think the Act structure or the way some characters are more prominent in some episodes as opposed to others is necessarily the feel I want. PTA also seems to lack the relationship-driven conflict that Whedon loves to produce. I'm trying to make sure my game keeps relationships as the primary agency for players to affect the story. I'm also making sure that the Ship plays a big role in the game, on the level of being another Character instead of just backdrop or a plot tool.

    I guess it really boils down to what you are looking for in a Firefly game. PTA does not encourage the kind of play I want to see. I'm sure you could drift it far enough that it does, but then one might as well design their own system. Which is exactly what I'm doing.

    Second, to the question I was really asking and only Danny K really addressed. My system is meant to produce stories similar to a certain body of work. However, I find that one of the prominent characters in that body of work is not supported by the system. As it happens, this character is an exception anyway, so is it alright to leave my system without a means to adequately describe this character? Essentially, any instance of this type of character in the game would require the players to fudge some of the rules.

    The central problem is that River is a Macguffin throughout most of the series, until you get to Objects in Space and the events of the movie (and, ostensibly, in between and after). You can make a case that she starts to get unMacguffinlike (whatta word!) after Ariel, but that's pushing it.

    So ok, she's a Macguffin. Accept that for a moment, and let it sink in. She's a plot device, not a character. The rules you've set up for characters don't really apply to her. But what you have instead is the fact that almost every episode of Firefly (and most Whedonverse shows) is a Macguffin of some sort. It's the Monster of the Week, the art piece that Joyce brings home, the girl who stumbles in off the street on Angel. Macguffins EVERYWHERE.

    So what you need for your system is a means for he characters to form relationships, if not to each and every Macguffin, at least to classes of Macguffins which strongly interact with their personal issues. For example, wossname, the old army buddy from The Message, is a Macguffin. He's not a character, he's a plot device to explore Mal and Zoe, and their baggage left over from the war. Will they do anything to help this guy, because of their history and what he represents (the remnants of Mal's unit and by proxy his failures in the war), or does he side with his new crew?

    So in your game, you need a means of generating Macguffins, linking those Macguffins with the heart-strings of your characters, and then using that mesh to move the plot along, by creating knots of situation.

    But what about River? Well, she's still a Macguffin, but she's a special kind of Macguffin - something that each and every Whedonverse season has as well - she's the link to the Big Bad for the season. Granted - we don't get to know much about the Big Bad, even in the movie, because Joss changed up Big Bads on us. But she's a Macguffin who's directly tied not only to the Big Bad, but the overall theme - River symbolizes what happens when the Alliance keeps fucking with you.

    She's the apotheosis of what Mal (and Zoe, one would suppose) hate about the Alliance. Jayne just wants to make sure she doesn't get him killed, but otherwise he doesn't care one way or the other about her. To Simon, she's a total betrayal - his former life was inside the Alliance, after all - and she represents the indifference that system has to its own inhabitants. I think to Book she represents a ghost of the past he's running from as well, but then again I have my own theories about Book, who was a mystery to the bitter end. Inara and River don't mingle much during the show - there's a weak connection there - but she's still an Alliance insider. River could represent the threat of ostracism. Without the Guild, Inara's just a well-trained hooker. And finally, Kaylee just wants to be friends - she sees River as a person, not as a Macguffin at all.

    So that's what your Firefly game needs - a means to generate Macguffins, both episodic and seasonal, and tie them into the relationship map game mechanics. Because River isn't an anomaly in terms of the Whedonverse. She's one of a class of individuals/objects - and even then she's not original.

    Dawn is the prototypical character-as-Macguffin of the Whedonverse, although you can make cases for Riley & Faith, previously to that.

    Now this doesn't mean that a Macguffin can't be a character - in fact, most of the Whedonverse Macguffins become characters in their own right. Hell, you can make the case that Angel is the Macguffin of Season 2 Buffy, and he got his own freakin' show. If you think about it, just about every clearly Macguffin character (Fred, Dawn & Kennedy - if you accept Kennedy as a proxy for the Slayerettes) gains characterhood after a while, and a clear role in the narrative (science-side geek, Junior Watcher and ... well ... unknown because there's no Season 8 Buffy).

    But that happens after their Macguffinhood is revoked by resolution of the situation that they represent. After that, they just become characters. And if what bothers you about that is that you'd have a superninja PC/NPC walking around that you can't model in your system, well, we don't know that for sure, do we? Maybe after the events of the movie, along with the terror and general gibbering nonsense, she loses a lot of her ninja powers as well, as they're subsumed. She's still hella smart - but then she'd fill that function in the team, which, frankly, could use some actual brains in the operation.

    What do the protagonists do?
    I saw my original question as transcending my game plans and so only posted what context I thought was needed to understand it. Had I realized this would be my game's coming out discussion, I would have presented more up front.

    The protagonists will be the crew of a small ship in the edge of the law. They'll pull jobs and maybe have grand adventures. I want to see the relationships between characters be the primary driving force behind the game. So the activity of their relationships is directly proportional to their ability to affect the course of events. I'm emphasizing this to the point that while the characters are probably pulling a job for the coin, the players are pulling the job for Relationship Economy Units.

    I also want the Ship to be an important element, so you'll be able to form relationships with it too. It will probably serve as another source of support in conflicts, but you'll have to upkeep it with Relationship Economy Units (which really I may end up calling coin).

    Anyway, I've got some other ideas but they are not ready to leave my head just yet. I'll let them swim in the primordial soup a bit longer before I drag them up, gasping upon the beach that is the internet.

    EDIT: I forgot to tie one to the other. The Macguffin deal will probably be the primary method for the GM to move the story along, as he can throw it into play and make everyone frantically rework their relationships. I guess then the game plays like this: People toodle along on a few jobs, building up Relationship Economy Units. The GM introduces a Macguffin, which slowly matures, all the while messing with established relationships more and more. Eventually the players can't take it anymore and resolve the Macguffin for good or ill. After that they'll probably have to pull a few more jobs to build up coin again. What they don't quite get is that there is still a Macguffin among them: its just maturing slower!
