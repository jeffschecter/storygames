    \n One of the things I find so bothersome about cheesecake is that it typically has nothing at all to do with the product that's being sold. It basically just says, "Hey dudes, we know that you are so stupid that you'll buy anything that gives you an excuse to look at boobies." So, really, it's sexist and demeaning to MEN as well as women, because it treats us as if we're all Neanderthal douchebags that can be easily manipulated by crass marketing attempts. Men don't tend to get offended by people waving boobies at them while trying to steal their $$$, but that's a different issue.

    If you are selling a game about Conan, then some kinds of cheesecake are expected (even if it may be distasteful or demeaning): that's what Conan is about, partially, and people either like it, dislike it, or try to deal with it somehow. But if you're selling a game that isn't about reveling in pulpy fantasy tropes (and the sexism and racism that come along with that stuff), then cheesecake is really just using sexism to sell stuff, like they do on commercials for shitty mass-produced beer. That's pretty terrible, but it's also ubiquitous, so I think a lot of people just become numb to it after a while.

      \n Hi Graham,

      Am I covertly sexist? I don't think so, but it probably depends on what you call sexism. In any case, the OP specifically mentioned that this thread was not about sexism, so I suggest we whisper on that topic, if you are inclined to do so.

      What I can tell you is that I take these issues very seriously, and that in my post I was trying to address the OP question as honestly and sincerely as I could. The problem is that although the OP's question is very simple (Do you like cheesecake art?), the answer is very complicated, hence my perhaps poorly written and convoluted answer. Allow me a second try:

      There are two problems to consider when trying to answer OP's question: 1) what is "cheesecake art" 2) in which manner and context is "cheesecake art" used. Depending on the answer to those questions, I could answer with a straight face "yes", "no" and "sometimes" to the OP's question.

      What is cheesecake art? It's a term I did not know before reading this thread, and there seems to be a drift of meaning between the mainstream "cheesecake art" signification (pin-up style illustrations), and the rpg community one. I'm more comfortable with the manga/anime term of "fan service", which I would define as "sexually appealing art which is not mandated or justified by the setting or context": the depiction of a naked woman living in the tropical forest is not necessarily fan service, neither would be the usual "scantily clad" girl, if she's a courtesan on duty.

      Note that if one would define fan service as "sexually appealing art which is not compatible with the setting or context", then I would for sure say "no" to the question, at least in the context of rpgs. Rpgs use a lot of suspension of disbelief, and by definition art (erotic or otherwise) which breaks the inner logic of the setting is a problem in this respect. But fan service can be at the same time "not required" by the context, but still "compatible" with it, and this is where things get complicated. For instance, let's say you have to illustrate a fantasy scenario in which there is a sorceress. The only thing you know about her is that she's a sorceress. If you draw her with big boobs, it's fan service, because it is not justified by the setting (she could have been flat chested, and still be a sorceress), even though it remains compatible with it.

      Of course, it also depends on what you mean by "sexually appealing". No one will question that a graphic shot of a vagina is sexual (even if not always appealing), but for me an attractive face is also "sexually appealing". Faces, hands, body shape are all modified by secondary sexual traits. Women often say that the eyes and hands of a man are the first spots they appraise when evaluating a male. This is perfectly obvious and is used in a massive scale in the movies industry. In most of the movies, the script does not require or justifies the use of a physically attractive actor or actress to play the protagonist role (not all movies are about fashion models), but this is exactly what you see, and to me is a pervasive form of fan service. Can you say with a straight face that you don't like to see attractive actors in the movies? I can't, and I wonder how the OP's husband would answer to that question...

      Which does not mean that I always wish that the protagonist should be a physically attractive person, on the contrary. One of the reasons I liked spaghetti westerns so much more than american westerns from the fifties is exactly because you started to see "ugly" people, or more exactly people with faces full of flavor (real brown people, big noses, wrinkles, etc). It made the whole thing so much more interesting than the bland repetitive wasp faces of old westerns!

      I also do not think that fan service is inevitable. Fan service gives you buy in from the (target) audience, which is a goal in the entertainment industry. Of course, it's the lazy path to audience buy in. If you have an excellent plot, or a charismatic (but physically average) actor, you may do well with the audience, even though you don't use fan service. Fan service is not a requirement, but it can help, and it can help increase sales of a good movie, and minimize losses of a bad one.

      Fan service can actually be a liability if 1) if offends or fails to entice the audience 2) is so clich\xe9ed as to become a source of frustration for the audience 3) the aesthetic of the work or setting themselves are really non conductive to themes of sexual attraction.

      So, to answer the OP, I like pictures of attractive people in an rpg book, as long as those illustrations actively support the setting and do not destroy its coherence. I do not demand illustrations of sexually attractive people in an rpg book, actually I don't even need illustrations at all in an rpg book to buy it (APs and reviews carry a lot more weight in my buying decisions). But if an rpg does have illustrations, for instance cover illustrations, a non sexual but evocative illustration beats hands down a sexually attractive illustration in giving me a good impression of a rpg book.

      For instance, on of the books I bought in recent years is "Mythic Russia". I find the cover illustration extremely efficient in evoking the setting, without having the least shred of fan service



      Now the reason I mentioned the RuneQuest image in my previous post is that I find the consideration on whether "I like cheesecake art" besides the point in terms of why publishers place it in rpg books. I think that publishers of rpg books use fan service not to directly increase sales (buy a book because of fan service illustrations), but to raise the interest of the reader (mindshare) in their product.

      And to this effect I can report that it works splendidly well, when the circumstances are right. I am certain that I did not buy more books as a direct result of watching that picture, but it is equally certain that I was marked by the illustration to the point that 20 years later I easily remember it. This is an unqualified success for the illustration, it solidly placed a chunk of the RuneQuest paraphernalia in my mind.

      You criticize me for somehow implying that there are two groups of people, the "teenagers that are affected by fan service", and "us", the "rationale people". But the reason I took an autobiographical example is precisely to show that the same people can react very differently to fan service, depending on their stage of development. If I saw that picture today for the first time, I would barely register it, I've seen thousands of others like it, and my sensibilities are different. That's why when I answer the OP's question, my answer is only valid as the person I am today, which may not be very indicative with what is happening in the heads of the people who are buying rpgs books. Even if we remain men all our life, our tastes and behaviors are very different at different ages. For some reason, even though the OP divided the audience into segments based on gender when asking her question, I wonder if the age criterion would not be as or even more important.

      You also criticize me for using the term "imprinting" in an incorrect fashion, but I flatly reject your contention: I'm using the term imprinting in its psychological sense as popularized by Lorenz, i.e. phase sensitive learning. I talked about imprinting because I saw that picture at a phase where I was maturing sexually, and so not only sensitive to an erotic stimulation, but also capable of storing the memory of that experience for decades. I can assure you that I regularly see plenty of fan service that I promptly forget, the only reason I keep that one in memory is not because it's particularly good, but because I was exposed to it at a "sensitive" period. This is exactly what imprinting is about.

      As for your comment on avoiding taking responsibility, I am not claiming that I am not responsible for my actions. What I mean is that I don't remember ever buying a rpg book because of a particular illustration, much less an erotic one (GM screens are a different story). But I can't deny for certain that some erotic imagery may have contributed to maintain my interest in a setting long enough to cause me to buy additional books. I can't deny it because I don't know for sure in either direction, not because I do not wish to accept responsibility for it.

      As for the suggestion of inevitability, let me put it like this. Publishers put fan service in their books mainly targeted to male teenagers. I was very impressed by a sexually attractive picture in my teens. Those are data points that you may accept or find nonsensical, but either way it's something that does not concern me.

      What concerns me more is whether fan service (or cheesecake art) has its place in RPG books. My opinion on that topic is that setting evocative illustrations are way more efficient than fan service in getting "buy-in" from the reader, with the caveat that creating setting specific illustrations is more difficult than producing generic fan service. If the publishers decide to be lazy and use fan service, they should at least take care that the it does not hurt the setting coherence or alienates the audience.

      Fan service by itself will not scare women away. Western movies and Asian manga are full of fan service targeted to women who are huge consumers of that sort of entertainment. I think this whole thing boils down to respecting your audience, and cater for its specific needs, if you want to sell. Traditional american comic books have often been full of cliched and offensive fan service targeted to young males, and their market was often restricted to that segment. Japanese manga are full of cliched fan service (often offensive), but segregated and targeted to different audiences. As a result manga publishers managed to expand their market not only to older man, but also women. RPG publishers should take the hint.

I don't know anyone who's bought a book for its cheesecake art, but comics also pander to this strategy; someone's supporting all that Rob Liefield Danger Girl stuff.

      At the same time, I'm more inclined to forgive a cover that puts, say, a Red Sonja type in a Conan-esque sword and sorcery context than I am the pulchritudinous Elf Maid with the bare midriff on another book. That's probably because I recall the old Frazetta style art with some nostalgia and harbor some latitude for a perception of genre fidelity. Any hypothetical tolerance doesn't mean a thing when it comes to me buying a book, though. I just don't care enough about art to make that the principal reason for buying a book. Poorly executed art can discourage me from buying, though. And like some other folks have said, an otherwise awesome book that sports a cheesecake cover is very likely to convince me not to buy, as much for the embarrassment factor as for the insulting content.

      I suspect the real function of cheesecake art isn't to close the sale, but simply to draw the attention of what's perceived to be -- and may still be -- a predominantly white male consumer demographic. And as a male, I do tend to notice that stuff. I don't presume to speak for all guys everywhere, but sexual imagery kind of leaps out of the background for me. Noticing it doesn't mean it's compelling, however. I can roll my eyes and move on past the E3 booth babes, for example, but they've definitely attracted my attention. I find that kind of manipulation insulting, particularly because I resent being susceptible to at least the surface level effect.

      Actually, there is one book I'd buy for the cover, but it's not cheesecake. The first edition of Hero Games Star Hero had a cover by an artist I particularly like (can't recall her name) and it captured a dramatic, even iconic, space opera moment. That kind of cover works for me. Matt's Galactic Cover also does the job.
