\n So here's the deal - the entire purpose of this thread is to illustrate the good things about the Apocalypse World text. I'd like you to highlight aspects of the document that are really good, really innovative, really effective for you. Organization, layout, tone, use of examples, conventions it establishes, pedagogy, rhetoric, style, whatever really, really works for you - and why. If, as a text, it is way ahead of its peers I want to understand what's going on so I can steal that stuff.

This isn't the place to talk about things you don't like. Please keep it positive and productive.

    \n One thing, off the bat: You can refer to the book, right in the middle of the game, to get re-centered and refocused as a GM. To answer the question, what should I do here, what should I do now? You can read the book and change the way you interact with your own GM style\u2014talking about it outright instead of feeling your way along, if you like. In that way, the text is encouraging and specific and expressing what you can do as a GM. It aims to demystify the job.

    I can say more, but I'll wait until (a) I have the book in front of me again, and (b) the ball is rolling.

    \n I really like the flow of the book. I sat down and, over the course of a couple nights, read straight through the book. It was an enjoyable read as a book. I didn't feel like there were parts that could be skipped over, e.g. equipment lists in more traditional RPGs that tell you explicitly what is available in the setting. Instead, a lot of the setting was implied - that drove my imagination crazy. I'm also going to throw out that I liked the art, each piece looked like a real person and not a cookie-cutter artistic archetype with some stylized trappings to differentiate individuals - that helped keep me immersed in the text. Lastly, I like the way the text talked to me, conversationally, rather than informed me.

    \n Do the playbook character sheets count as part of the text? They should. And they're really good. Keeps the essential information right in front of everybody. "Knowledge exists in the world" - Donald Norman.

    Then the Moves indices - for when we're doing something we don't usually do.

    Examples, examples, examples. I usually learn game rules by induction from the examples rather than the rules themselves.

    And, yeah, me, I like the conversational writing style, and the cussing.

    And, I wrote a bunch about it on my blog - http://www.gamedevblog.com/2011/01/notes-on-apocalypse-world.html - but one relevant bit:

    "Part of the fun is that half of the book is rules for the GM; not guidelines, not advice, but rules. The section on GMing begins: "There are a million ways to GM games; Apocalypse World calls for one way in particular. This chapter is it. Follow these as rules. The whole rest of the game is built upon this." The rules are a contract between GM and players, a contract where the GM is promising to deliver certain kinds of fun ... it has rules like Ask Provocative Questions And Build On The Answers. Which means when I play Apocalypse World with anybody as the GM, I know the GM is going to ask me to contribute to the story and respect what I add (unless they're breaking the rules.) "

    \n It's both the most educational and, at the same time, the least didactic instruction manual I've ever read. It shows you the rules. It hints at them before it exposes them and it doesn't patronise. I feel like I'm in a conversation when I'm reading the book. The guy (I love Jonathan's narrator above, by the way) mentions something and I feel like I'm asking him to elaborate. Not like "huh," but like "go on..." And it does go on, but not too much. And when it does go on too much, it's because I didn't get it the first time.

    I wish I could express this better. Sorry.

    Apocalypse World is more advanced than its peers because it explains its storytelling functions quickly and charmingly. Often, it explains the mechanics just with the names of the rules. The naming of the stats, the naming of the rules, the naming of the characters, NPCs, gear, powers, and all the other stuff: this requires such a tight economy of expression. In that way, it's poetic.

    \n (Really, this is moving to talk about the rules rather than the writing but...)

    Oh man, Fronts. Fronts are so good. Fronts are how a lot of good sandbox-y, player-driven games have been run (or, maybe, should have been run) ever since the dark ages of gaming. But AW lays them out clearly for what they are, puts clocks on them so you can think about how to pace those developments, tells you to be both a listener and a proactive manipulator in managing them in play, and that's way, WAY more explicit than any text I've read has ever done it.

    Fronts have totally changed the way I think about running White Wolf games. It makes them seem so much more managable and obvious. They're affecting the way I think about running Freemarket (which I'm currently doing), even though that game calls for a slightly different approach. I guess that's kind of a writing thing, because Vincent could have been far less explicit about Fronts, telling you to just make up some Fronts and "play your NPCs" without more explicit guidelines.

    \n Usually, after I read a game text, I scour the forums here and at rpg.net to read some AP reports. I don't have the capacity to properly imagine how the game rules would work out in play. This was true for other Vx games I read, Dogs and IAWA. With Apocalypse World, though, I didn't have to.     I know we're supposed to keep it positive, but one thing that I had some problem with is the list of threats.     \n The fonts are really good. Take this to heart if/when you are laying out your own game, or somebody else's game, "and I am NOT fucking around here":

    The body text is nice and readable, not fancy or anything. It looks distinctive enough that you know it's not Time New Roman, and it's not quite as familiar as Garamond or Minion (and let's be honest for a second: Garamond italics can look pretty fucking bad sometimes). It's a little bit different, and a lot familiar. Comfortable, readable, interesting, distinctive.

    And then the header and title fonts are fucked up and scream post-apocalyptic destruction, burnt-out buildings and rusty blown-up cars. But they're not illegible. It might take a fraction of a second longer to read them, but they convey lots and lots of mood.

    The moral:

    1. Fuck around with your titles and headings. Maybe make them harder to read than in AW, as long as they broadcast what your game/book is all about.

    2. Do NOT fuck around with the body text. Fancy body text = bad person who hates readers. YES IT DOES.

    \n I found the physical artifact 'bookish' rather than 'rulesey'. What I mean is that the digest format, the text layout and fonts, the conversational style and focused chapters / headings meant the book was unremarkable, yet tantalising in its iconography. The minimalist, block print art is consistent throughout and really entrenches the archetypes of the game.
    \n
    \nThor wrote a great piece on his blog about the power of a name, in conjuction with Jared's descriptive 'rule of three'. AW uses these concepts throughout. Everything must be named and described simply and evocatively. All you need is a name and a few descriptors and you have a great skeletal frame to hang the fiction on. This process is also encouraged to be a group activity via the principle of 'asking lots of questions'. Players can be adressed to encourage them to narratively contribute and roleplay. This is awesome.
    \n
    \nHaving a list of names and tags and conditions and small (consistent) mechanical effects re-inforces the 'prescriptive and descriptive' nature of the rules (and roleplay in general). I am amazed at how purposefully identifying the processes we as story gamers have been doing for ages and hard coding them in such an effective and simple way has galvanised play. Its right there in the rules and it makes no pretentions of being anything else.
    \n
    \nI love how the game pays homage to 'classes and levels' without using such misappropriated jargon. The specificity focuses the player's fiction, enjoying what makes their exclusive character so badass, rather than the 'optimal build'.
    \n
    \nAs with most story games, the mechanical iteration of conflict via moves is consistent across all conflict. Fighting is no different to fucking or finding luxe at the market. I've found that players invariably want to 'try-out' moves, so its a great facilitation tool to have the narrational outcomes right there on the sheet, and all in the relatively same format.
    \n
    \nThe fluidity of time scale without 'turn, rounds or seconds' is so refreshing and liberating. Normally I find the slavish tenacity of these segments so 'wargamey'. AW skirts around this with the excellent paradigm of to do it - do it. The moves snowball back and forth and naturally escalate the fiction to a reprise. Brilliant.
    \n
    \nThe reward cycle uses tried and true story game entaglements betwixt player, character, relationship and selective improvement. This is succinct, easy and satisfying to both player and MC. The temptation as always is to complicate and create endless improvements or advanced moves, but the rules, despite giving you the leeway to do so, encourage a trust in Vx to play as written, explore the playbooks and develop your unique fictional vision of AW using the same rules as everyone else.
    \n
    \nI really, really have been converted to the 'Miss, 7-9, 10+' Move format. So simple yet so deep. The possibilities come through the fictional 'openess' of the statistcally common 7-9 result. 'You succeed, but with complications' is the source of all great roleplay. It engenders a play to see what happens style, backed by the specific prep encourages through the fronts and threats.
    \n
    \nFronts and threats require the MC to focus on their craft. Purposefully avoiding plot development, rather focusing on NPC motivations and how they can directly threaten the characters. Nothing new here, but the format is surprisingly specific. Countdown clocks reinforce this tension with a fluid time pressure, and the idea of 'putting NPCs in crosshairs' was so disturbing to me as MC, yet so refreshingly obvious in play, and allowed for far greater focus on the PCs (as any story game should).
    \n
    \nThe Principles are a fine condensation of vast tomes on 'gamemastery'. This makes the role of MC approachable, achievable and evocative. It also highlights the difference between the players and their power of 'rolling the dice'. Sure as MC you can choose any hard move you'd like, but the players.... The players have the power of deciding chance (as they do their moves) to send the story in another direction indeed. Heady Stuff.
    \n
    \nOh, and the system is the epitome of hackable, and Vx gives you the tools to do so. Exemplary form dear boy. :)

    \n Everyone else has touched on most Of the stuff I like about the game, and in particular the way it systematically spells out as Principles a bunch of GM techniques Ive been using without understudying them as specific techniques.

    It may be silly, but I have to say one of the things that I find immensely appealing that Ive never seen before and wouldn't have ever thought to do in a million years is the tri-fold character sheets. I have no eye for design and the way they distill not just the typical stat info for each character type, but the creation rules and special Moves all wrapped in a format that's essentially a classy advertisement for that particular class, is the most revelatory design element to me.

    Maybe other games have done something similar; if they did, they didn't make an impression. The way you lay the brochures out before each campaign and essentially say 'Pick One' is a subtle and powerful means of getting everyone into the same boat before you proceed.

    There's also something especially cool and subversive about handing my sheet back to the MC at the end of every session and not having it home to comb over for mechanical fiddly bits; if I want to mess with my PC between session, Ive got little choice but to engage the fiction of him instead of the numbers.
