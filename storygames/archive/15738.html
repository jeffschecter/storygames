\nTED talks are short (approx. 15-minute), entertaining talks about all sorts of things. Mostly they try to save the world, or enlighten you, or whatever; sometimes they're incredibly interesting.

This would be a really cool format for gaming talks, of course. One person, one basic idea/subject, fifteen minutes.

Who would you like to see, talking about what?
Or, what would you like to talk about yourself?

    \n I'd like to see a politically aware hipster of 25-35 years (thinking of Mike Pohjola or Jonathan Walton here) discuss the potential of gaming as grassroots culture that remains viable and independent outside the machinery of the increasingly commercial culture industry. Specifically focusing on how in roleplaying you are the artist, and what this means for the practitioner as a citizen and cultural person. Also, a follow-up where a grognard foams at the mouth about how all these anti-geek gamers with their talk of "art" (has to be air-quotes in there) are ruining gaming, which used to be "just about fun" and slaying dragons.

    \n More seriously, something along the lines of what Eero is describing would be really interesting to see.

    I'd love to see the concept of "story games/RPGs" thrown at a wider audience as a "counter-cultural" effort to re-create home entertainment. Pitched as an escape from mass consumerist media and presented as a way for the consumer to develop an ownership of his/her own creative entertainment.

    Perhaps something like an aural history of group storytelling traditions and how they can be seen as evolving into a modern version of a communal activity which challenges us to grow together. There are a lot of people out there who are unhappy with what the entertainment media offers us, and it would be fun to see what some of those crowds would make out of our hobby.
