Here's my thesis: your chestnut bad-mechanic says something about you.

The motivation: in the "Pistols at Dawn":http://www.nutkinland.com/forums/showthread.php?t=894 thing over at Nutkinland, the RPGPundit refers repeatedly to what he calls the "wheelbarrow of weapons" effect in Runequest. Briefly, you get one advancement per weapon for the first successful use of that weapon in the session, so by implication you should carry around a whole armary to increase your skills. Now, I played years of CoC and never felt that pressure, so there you go.

On the other hand, I tend to pan ??Amber?? because[1] an informed playgroup can end-run the character creation rules, and spend 4 points each to all have awesome stats and then go on to spend the rest of their points on other character resources. I see this as a flaw, because the rules depend on the players being ignorant of the game text, and on the players being competitive with each other.

So, what's your chestnut bad-mechanic, and why, and what do you suppose that says about you and your preferences?

    Dev, I totally agree. And it's not just you: the game promises something, looks to be about stuff, and then a) prevents you from doing it and b) makes your moral decision for you: if you use vulgar magic, They will get you. That makes the only interesting story one of self-sacrifice.

    I've got a lot of grumbly stories about rules, but I'll just say a couple off the top of my head:

    In Warhammer, D&D, GURPS, and probably some others, immunity to poison is an economical and obvious choice for anyone in a court setting. Which invalidates poison completely, which means you can't really do cloak and dagger stuff. Particularly since, in two of those systems, it's practically impossible to kill someone with a dagger. It's all about open combat. (In GURPS, you can make a called shot at a reasonable cost to stick 'em somewhere good).

    Any rule that says "These are the rules, except when the GM says something else, and the GM is responsible for opposition, mediation, arbitration, and everyone's fun." That's totally butt and nonsensical prima fascia. It's the elephant in the room of most game systems. It's why there's a GM's screen, which is a complete admission that the rules don't work and are just there to make you feel like there's systsemic support for stuff.

    JoshAC, I totally second that about the GM rules. I really dislike games which do that.

    The 3rd ed DM screen is actually kinda useful, IMO, since the one side contains various frequently-referenced tables and means the DM doesn't have to keep grabbing books back from the players to look things up.

    Other than that, I have to say I really disliked the character advancement system in a homebrew a friend of mine made. It was a stat+skill+2d10 thing. Every 10 you rolled, you got one *point* towards improving that skill. The number of points required to improve a skill was 60 divided by your Learn score. (Learn was in the 1-10 range.) Characters basically did not advance--in 3 months of weekly sessions, one character increased their Sense: Sight skill by one point. There was no other systemic way for characters to change. This really bugged me! (The game was, in general, horribly dysfunctional, but this was one thing about the system that stood out for me.)
