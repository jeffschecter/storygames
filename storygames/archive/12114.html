Eero and Zac, you guys should know better.

So here's a promise: if you and I can agree to stop starting threads and posts about GNS, commenting on them, or taking their bait in any other way, I will pay you a dollar next time we meet. I will even hug you, if you so choose. I GUARANTEE that is more worth and more friendship than you will ever get out of another goddamn theoretical, play-free forum wank-off.

Go play, go play, go play.

    \nSee, this is the bit that gets me. 'That part of the revolution is done.' What? Are you really saying that GNS and the Big Model actually created - definitively and for all time - a model for the process of engaging in collaborative storytelling? Really? Because that's a really big statement, considering (and I actually haven't read this for myself) that the originator of the model declared it 'harmful', and there are real, serious questions about the validity of the model to begin with?

    As it happened, I published the first draft of my own AGE Model (hey - a LINK) when the Forge decided it had moved past theory and wouldn't be talking about it anymore - and I got this exact feeling back then: that there was this idea that the whole theory thing had been decided and it was pointless to talk about it anymore, and that there was some sort of 'graduation' from theory now that we've figured out the world is round and everyone can go play happily ever after.

    It's BS. GNS exposed, as far as I can tell, one solid mode of play (narrative play), and everything that's come from it has been in that vein - and the things in that vein (like Mr. Czege's games) are exceptional examples of that one mode. But who knows how many other modes of storytelling there are, or if (shock!) narrative game construction could actually be improved if the model for examining what goes on at the table were improved, revised, or even completely altered.

    My crack about Einstein was just that: Up until 1906, everyone pretty much accepted Newton as the end-all of physics. The fact that Newtonian physics is perfectly acceptable for putting a man on the moon doesn't change the fact that relativity is pretty much as (if not more) important than the Newtonian flavor.

    I also agree that there's a lot of wankery out there (or at least was when I last checked) - but painting all theory discussion with the same wank is completely unfair.

    The craft of game design demands play - no doubt about it. But it also sorely needs a theoretical underpinning as well.

    Posted By: Herman DuykerIf you want to catch up on university-level research and discussion of roleplaying, take a look at the International Journal of Roleplaying. It's not just restricted to pen-and-paper / tabletop roleplaying either.
    \nIt should be noted that there's no bright shining line between "university-level research and discussion of roleplaying" and gamer-level discussion. As Hitchens and Drachen note in their IJRP article, "Some researchers participate in both the academic and the players' discourse on RPG theory." Of course, Hitchens and Drachen also say that the RPG community's self-reflective introspection "lacks connections to wider game [studies] theory."

    But I'd argue that Hitchens and Drachen would benefit from a stronger familiarity with the "players' discourse" on RPGs. For example, they talk about the "presence of a game master" as an important distinguishing characteristic of RPGs. And that's true enough as far as it goes, but the conflation of social role (game master) with game function (the game-level tasks typically associated with a game master) means that their discussion of what RPGs are has the potential to lead us astray. "Oh, no GM--can't be a role-playing game. Sorry, Polaris; better luck next time, Shab-al-Hiri Roach. Where the fuck are you going, Universalis?" Their definition can of course be saved by recognizing the existence of GM-functions distinct from and distributable among the players at the table. But that would require a more sophisticated understanding of RPGs, one that's frankly only available at, say, the Forge. Or here.

    Now, none of that is GNS, of course, but even there I think Hitchens and Drachen might have an easier time distinguishing RPGs from other game-forms by acknowledging the kinds of experiential rewards that are collectively produced in RPG play, which ultimately is what I think GNS gives you.

    But my point is that to defer to academic game studies as somehow automatically more sophisticated than our own conversations is to give academia too much credit.

      \nGNS in General
      I haven't really seen other theories discussed here, though. For a time, Bryan (Kuma) had some interesting generalist RPG theory papers he made, and posted in threads here and there, but while they were interesting they didn't really attract any attention; reads, passionate responses, flames, etc. So all that we're left with for the most part is GNS theory, and a lot of folks confused about "Where do I post now?" that those theory forums are closed, and the requirement at the Forge added to discuss it only through interpretive dance play.

      Here, there's lots of game theory being discussed. Some of my favorite threads were speculations of:
      * "How do we get players more invested in their characters?"
      * "How does music and lighting affect play?"
      * "What happens when you play one game using the rules tied to another game?"
      * "How can we as a group create a setting?"
      * "Can NPCs be 'Situation'?"
      * "How come all my Vampire games were never really any fun?"

      All the above are theoretical... but they can also be directly addressed by testing, or folks can relay their experiential data into the thread ("We tried this and this is how it worked").

      As soon as we step further and further away from questions that came up from actually GMing and Playing, we move further and further away from the premise of this site. The same was true recently for Game Design discussion, so it was pulled off of this site and moved over to Praxis (that top-right link up there).

      ======================
      My Experience with GNS

      Personally: GNS was awesome when I first encountered it. I read the essays, I got the jist: People come to the table for different reasons; when those reasons are different there can be friction. There were some prescriptions and suggestions from that point, but I had already gotten a lot out of it: I could see in my own game groups why 1-2 people seemed to be dissatisfied, or why when I tried to run a game a certain way others would complain, and so on. I pretty much ditched the idea of having a "one category only" group, and instead focused on my own with ways that I can still hit everyone's goals and have fun, possibly meaningful (in the "fulfilling" sense) sessions. Yay, it worked, and my gaming improved dramatically thanks to Ron pointing that bit out. And I gladly talk about it when such a discussion comes up, but most discussions of GNS are not "did it work for you?", but rather "these categories seem wrong, let's talk about these categories..."

      Beyond that, though, I really had no interest in the discussions, or what the prescriptions for correction were (at times: ditch your group, get another), or analyzing the three little categories that the theory proposed as the only ones out there (and in fact drawing the lines in the sand for which behavior/game falls in which category has been the part of the theory which drew so much attention, rather than "Well... did your games actually Improve after reading the central idea?").

      So it worked for me, and awesomely so. But the further Big Internet Discussions that followed both baffled and bored me, so I largely ignored them, especially when it was clear that folks were talking about theoretical stuff and not stuff that applied directly to their sessions, etc (and of course everyone has a pocket example of someone who discussed theory on forums of years because for whatever reason they weren't actually playing RPGs with friends or acquaintances, so the best they could do was talk about playing; likewise examples of people discussion theory because they play 5 times a week and they're just bored/not busy at work; and so on)

      =======================
      GNS and broad theory at S-G

      As the S-G mod, I prefer of course to see the discussions and theory tied to play, especially questions and discussions that ask (and provide various answers) to the question of "How do I make my next game session more fun?" The "Best of Story Games" category (click on Categories, then The Best of Story Games) is filled with those kinds of threads, if you are looking for examples of what I mean. My own bookmarks links are basically a litany to that question.

      The occasional GNS-themed thread that comes up once about every 6-12 months: Just like other side-discussions not really tied to play (like the Movies of 2000s thread that's going on now), they can be interesting. They might go over well, they might go over like a turd in a swimming pool. When too many build up too fast and we start to see 3-4 of them, all actively collecting posts (as GNS theory does), it starts to get a little suffocating. It starts to feel like the Exalted threads bumping out all other conversations over at my beloved RPGNet.

      I don't think there's the need to call them out and say "let's not discuss this/let's ignore it", cause honestly it's not that bad currently, and even after talking to the active posters through whispers and stuff, the discussions were already on their way to that slow dive. It just started getting a little stuffy in this room, that's all.

      I don't mean to kill all theory discussion where it stands, but yeah that thing I wrote a few years back that Christian quoted above (probably need to change a little bit of the wording to make it up to date), that's pretty much where I still stand on that sort of thing.

      However, I do go as far as to encourage people to take GNS and other "broad general gaming theory" discussion to their own blogs (or create another website even), and tell them to go ahead and post in the "Stuff to Watch" threads when they have a new blog post, a cool new insight, a heated discussion, and so on: It's interesting to a lot of us, even if we don't want to see it play out Here, in This Place.

      All is Cool.

      Posted By: Paul T.So, what doyousee as signposts of bad design?
      \nGreat question. It's something I've thought about a long time. I'm still thinking about it, whenever I read a new (or old) game. It's very difficult for me to answer, seeing as I see all game texts (and other materials) as toolkits for play - no matter how focused they are, the players still bring the actual play to the table, not the designer. In RPGs and other story games, the players bring an overwhelming amount of creative material to the table due to the extremely wide bandwidth of choices that they have in every moment of play. Even very strict and very focused RPGs have this quality. It is one of the greatest things about the hobby. It is in fact the very reason I like to play. But that makes evaluating a design extremely challenging. To some degree there really is no difference, in a player's experience, between a bad design decision and a design decision that doesn't accomplish what the player wants. They will experience those two things exactly the same way and walk away just as disappointed with both. This is not a mistake on the players part - they are the same thing, in play experience.

      I absolutely agree with the System Does Matter essay, in that there are bad designs and good designs, and the intent of the designer is a big part of that question, but my dilemma is that I think that designer intent has the same relevance to a play experience as bandsaw designer intent to a carpentry experience. That is to say, if they're really going in an absolutely different direction than you want them to, it can be a big problem, but that is pretty rare. Conversely, just because the tool was made well, or even brilliantly, doesn't mean your result will be bad or good.

      Look at Lady Blackbird, this is a great example. Here is a game/module that is made for one thing and one thing only. It even comes with pregenerated characters and situation. Yet play experiences with this game vary dramatically. Even the groups' interpretations of the sort of world it's set in vary dramatically. In no conceivable way could it be said that two different groups playing Lady Blackbird are creating the same play. Indeed, there are people who play it and don't like it. It doesn't fulfill their desires, whatever those are. Yet they are "playing the same game" as people who make an entirely different fiction which might have been much more satisfying for them.

      I guess the most important thing - and here I am pointing out the positive rather than the negative, so I am still dodging your question a little - is clarity and accuracy in terms of what the game will produce. It is good to seek a fictional result and promote it and communicate it clearly to the players and bad when that doesn't happen.

      Let me give an example. I love GURPS. But nowhere in any version of GURPS does the game say "Hey, by the way, it's pretty hard to dodge things in GURPS, unless you put some effort into being good at it." or "As you can see, a regular old average guy on the street will have a hard time surviving more than a single gunshot." That is a problem because both those things are true and until you get some experience with the system, you might not know that they were true, which can put you in a bind where, to achieve the fictional goal you were after with the game, you have to make a change in situ and without clear preparation. That's a bad design decision, not to communicate those things to me. But it is not a bad design decision to make those two things true. Nor (for example) if I decided to change those two things and make it easier in GURPS to dodge or make it easier for an average guy to survive more than one gunshot, is that a sign that GURPS has made a mistake.

      Here's another example - in most editions of Vampire (actually all, until the most recent, and maybe even the most recent depending on how you read it), you can buy Backgrounds, which include things like Contacts, Allies, Influence and Resources. The acquisition of those four things are at the center of political games. Yet the price for their purchase at character creation is ridiculously low compared to powers and skills that are far less useful in political games. You can easily create a starting character who has already won the political game before the game even starts, if you use your Background points wisely. In fact, given how hard it was to obtain Background points in those editions, you were foolish not to spend your points on those things at the beginning. This is a bad design decision - to give the option and support, even the support of this very system - to the play of political games, but to price things in such a way that you don't even need to play to achieve everything you need to achieve in the fictional world you're creating. But it is not a bad design decision to include Backgrounds in general, or those backgrounds in particular, and in a non-political game such as punching guys while wearing trenchcoats, the price is not even a bad design decision. So even in this bad decision there is still something good that will produce good results for a lot of people playing the game. If I don't acknowledge that, I'm being dishonest.

      Both of these are really about accuracy. You want your game to produce the fiction people expect it will if they follow most of the instructions (it's vanishingly unlikely anyone will play the game exactly as the designer wants, or at least vanishingly unlikely that I will.) In the case of GURPS, the problem was that the fiction created was likely to have some relevant traits that are not clear from the text. In the case of Vampire, the problem is that one of the ways of using the tool is well-supported but others are problematic and that's never explained (perhaps nobody noticed? This is why playtesting blind is so amazingly important.)

      There's also something in here about rigidity and flexibility. The reason I used the Vampire example above is because the game is very flexible, and this is a very good thing about it. You're not locked into using everything word for word and comma for comma. If I want to change something, for the most part, the game will still run well. But as I noted in the example above, the flexibility has limits. Not only do you have to express those limits well, you have to choose them well. But I don't have a clear picture of this. It's difficult to describe because there are two relevant factors here - the flexibility of the material to hit the target that players want to hit, and, when the game is changed too far, how it fails and how easy it is to diagnose the failure. On the one hand obviously I don't want any play of any RPG to flop. But on the other hand there is nothing wrong with losing a few chess games humiliatingly badly while you learn. I think it's a legitimate design decision to let players be unhappy with a game until they understand it better. (Here I am thinking of your first Champions build, or Burning Empires. You really fucked that up, didn't you? But you saw that if you kept at it, it would get better. And it did, right?)

      I am very cautious about making theoretical statements about what the whole hobby is like. I bet there are some good counterexamples out there that could make me back off of even these ideas. I think we've got such a huge and ill-defined hobby that you have to be extremely skeptical of any theory purporting to explain it. (Not to mention we're operating in the virtual absence of data.)

      \n Walker,
      I agree that Drift and Incoherence are used negatively. The words feel negative in the English-speaking mouth, to be certain.
      How I've seen Drifting manifest in AP reports at the Forge is, essentially: the poster is asked to explain their Drift up front, to avoid confusion. That's it.

      Jason, in such an AP post, any such changes (ANY! ^_^) would undoubtedly be brought up by the poster, for either relevance or completeness.

      However, Walker, you're misusing the term Incoherence - it doesn't have anything to do with house-ruling. Its main definition is
      "Play which includes incompatible combinations of Creative Agendas among participants. Incoherent play is considered to contribute to Dysfunctional play, but does not define it."
      The rest of the listed entry here relates to other definitions and such, so I didn't include it.

      Incoherence is often seen in short-hand as an attempt to simultaneously give equal spotlight to multiple CA's. Plenty of games have a Narr angle with Sim acting in support, or Gamist with Sim support; having multiple CA's in evidence is not the issue per se. The "per se" usually refers, in practice I've found, to when a game design attempts to handle all three CA's with equal weight, or at least more than one.

      For example - World of Darkness has hundreds of pages dedicated to setting material, but (thankfully) all the metaplot and what-have-you is not actually mechanically relevant unless you make it so (i.e. yes, System is everything that happens at the table, period, but if a particular piece of it is only in use if you care to employ it, as opposed to it being connected to the rest of the mechanics, then it's irrelevant). At the same time, the rules are fundamentally based on the D&D/etc. model of individual warriors with special powers, who fight combats in a particular way. Finally, there are essays in the texts that give suggestions on how to tell a good story through your World of Darkness game.

      That's a crack at Incoherent design, speaking theoretically, because you have a game that likely draws people in for the vivid setting material, but its mechanics alternately give you magic powers that focus on the Why (why is it so, explain it to me!) and then, for guns and combat and stuff, clearly focus on the What (what are my options in this challenge?). Combine that with an overall self-assessment that the game promotes good story-telling (how? Essays are not the same as mechanics, mannnn), and you have a game that's telling you it does one thing and clearly does another. IN NO WAY does this mean you can't have fun or that there's something inherently wrong with the game - merely that from a very specific design perspective, this game has some conflicting priorities.
      That bit about dysfunctional play in the definition says it all - sure, it could exacerbate a situation in which folks are already having trouble getting their goals met by the play experience, but it's not going to be the sole cause.

      Very importantly, most Glossary terms that refer to Dysfunction actually deal with player behavior, not with game design - powergaming, Typhoid Mary, turnin', wimpiness, etc., all point to behavioral player issues, NOT to games or game texts. Even the contentious Illusionism concept is fundamentally a human issue, not a game issue.

      \n JD,

      So, OK, drift of the rules is not a sign of bad design, you say. But unclear explanations and directions--unclear marketing--are?

      If so, we agree. And I'm sure so would any hardcore "Forgie": a good design has rules that support what it claims to do, and, vice versa, it claims to do what the rules support. When this is properly carried out, this allows the gamer looking for a particular thing to pick up the rules that give them that particular thing, resulting in a fun time. Those two go together pretty tightly, I would think.

      As for the whole "dwarf beards" issue, I think that, for me, personally, the Big Model helps explain where that line lies. I do NOT believe that dwarf beards and, say, a GM's limitations to creating monsters of a certain Challenge Rating--or even the GM's right to describe what monsters are in the game, or what they look like--are in the same category it all. The former is a detail of the fiction that we presumably all can agree on, or agree to disagree, or whatever. If you say, "in my game, all PCs are thieves," I can say, "OK, sure!" Or maybe, "How about I play a wizard, but his *profession* is stealing stuff, he just uses magic to do it?"

      But the Big Model's concept of "System" is the set of interactions that allows a group to bring into life some kind of interactive fiction. It's about the interactions between the participants, and how they relate to each other. All of us agreeing to stick to three Vampire clans changes the game, yes, but the way we play the game and decide on what's happening in the fiction, and who rolls when, etc, is still exactly the same. Meaningful changes to System, in my opinion, require that we actually change how the players interact with each other at the table: who says what when, how conflicts and situations are resolved, what new situations are brought into play, etc.

      There's a huge difference between changing some of the details of a particular game, which may or may not be Drift (e.g. giving people more hit points) and changing the way the game shapes the form of our gaming as a social activity (e.g. "How about the players now decide what monsters go in the dungeon?").

      Also:

      What I find odd about your account, JD, is that you seem to totally ignore the difference between "I changed some stuff in the game to get a different effect" (e.g. let's all play thieves this time around!) and "I had to change the rules, because they were screwing up my game" (e.g. I need to raise people's odds of Dodging, because everyone keeps dying in the first five minutes of play, and the game claimed it was an action adventure). Am I misreading you?

      I don't think it's always easy to tell the difference between those two, but I find it hard to believe anyone would claim that they aren't two different things, at a fundamental level that "theory" (i.e. my understanding of game texts and design) should address.

      \nThat is one way people have tried to rehabilitate the theory, but I don't really think it succeeds. The theory doesn't just identify games with conflicting priorities, it makes a value judgment about them. They are bad and damaging. This value judgment cannot be wronger. If a game has a lot of different priorities, it can be good or bad, helpful to a wide variety of groups to achieve a wide (but naturally not infinite) variety of preferences, or it can be a mess, or it can be good in places and bad in places. Vampire's conflicting priorities, or, as I call it, flexibility, is a very good thing about it.       Posted By: JDCorley...I see all game texts (and other materials) as toolkits for play - no matter how focused they are, the players still bring the actual play to the table, not the designer.
      \nThis is something that I've been thinking about as well, particularly when reading discussions here and on the Forge. Quite often, you see people saying "the game does this" or "the game was designed like such and such"; however, that isn't really what is happening, at least in my head. When someone says, "I designed a game", what they are really saying is "I designed a product". The game is something that happens when people sit down and use both that product and their own minds to play.

      I hypothesize that if some elusive, perfect theory of roleplaying game design exists, it will have to explicitly deal with this difference between product and game. An important axiom of such a theory is that "the designer only has control over the product, not the game". Another would be that "players are assumed not to have had any control over the production of the product". (Naturally, neither of those is always true, but I'd think that's how you'd have to proceed to make a theory real.)

      \n Caillois is an interesting source on Creative Agenda, to be sure. I've yet to see a serious treatment, but I've myself thought that Ilinx might be a convincing explanation for a type of immersionist-advocated play; this would imply a separate CA mode of "immersionism" as sometimes discussed. Experts on Ilinx-type immersionism seem to be uninterested in the Big Model (and writing rpg texts, notably), which might be why we've yet to see any in-depth look on the matter. Still, some discussions with Finnish immersionists (the sort that does equate roleplaying with an altered state of consciousness at a drop of a hat) indicate to me that there are people out there who find this phenomenon of immersion central to their interest in roleplaying. Whether it's a CA-type interest or something else (or even real; I'm not quite convinced) is what would be interesting to find out.

      I'm less convinced of the distinction between Agon and Alea, they smack of intellectualization of a psychologically joint phenomenon: as orthodox gamist theory has it, the psychological drive behind both skilfull and luckfull competition is the need to overcome challenges, and while we can rationally distinguish whether you actually can influence that resolution yourself, this does not change the fundamental phenomenon of confronting challenges, but rather only our perception of which challenges might be worthwhile for us.

      And then there is narrativism - as you said, Bill, Caillois ignores a phenomenon that is pretty conclusively existant. Not surprising when you remember that Story Now didn't really come to be widely practiced in ludic play before the '70s; Caillois couldn't have seen it in any real-life examples he considered.

      Still, I for one wouldn't mind seeing CA analysis that does not utilize GNS, Caillois or otherwise. This seems to be hampered by the fact that only people who don't have a problem with GNS seem to also find Big Model useful and interesting, and therefore we're left to choose between GNS-accepting theorists and theorists who don't understand Big Model. This is not necessarily surprising, of course, as the Big Model might very much be characterized as modeling the influence of creative drive on the social activity that is roleplaying; take out GNS and you're left with a model in search of a message.

      \nPart One: Why Bother With Alternatives to GNS?\n
      Posted By: Eero TuovinenI especially agree that while challenging the existence of CA seems ludicrous to me, I could well imagine alternative or even more useful descriptions of of the entire field than GNS. . . . because Creative Agenda has many parts that can be tickled better or worse by different approaches -- different techniques. I'd like to say that the best thing I get out of GNS is that once I'm able to clearly verbalize that I want to do premise-ful play or whatever, that allows me to set aside all the other vistas and focus better on the smaller nuances that were never available to me when I spent all my time trying to negotiate the big basics.
      \nYes, exactly. The notion that descriptions of CA like GNS should be at root pragmatic game design tools strikes me as dead on. The further implication is that, because of the multiplicity of possible agendas for play, any particular description of "why people play" or "what people get out of playing" serves a normative function as well, helping to create the culture of play it purports to describe--at least insofar as people subscribe to it as a guide to design and play. This is as clear a case of the reflexivity of knowledge as one could ever hope to encounter.

      In the case of GNS, one thing that it does very well--as Eero rather astutely points out--is call attention to a mode of play that it calls "narrativism." When you think about it, you realize that this mode is actually rather obscure, a kind of play that works by having one stand in a kind of alienation or distance from the character one plays in order to more strongly appreciate the moral weight of the decisions the character makes--this in contrast to the strong identification with character that is the desideratum of immersive play and the elision of character that is the hallmark of competitive play.

      One side effect of this way of slicing up the phenomenon, as people use it to explore the design space that results, is that purely gamist designs become interesting as a way of examining the negative space of narrativism--in other words, of challenging the basic assumption of narrativist play and saying, hey, what if there's no character, not really, it's all just a contest between us here at the table, via the medium of a set of rather elaborately detailed pawns. It recapitulates the hoary old narratology versus ludology debate in a new form.

      But another side-effect is to make "simulationism" inexplicable, or even pathological. I remember seeing a post on Vincent Baker's blog to the effect that while he understood gamism and he understood narrativism, simulationism struck him as puerile wish fulfillment power fantasy at best. I seem to recall the subordination of simulationism in some other context having driven David Berg to distraction, given his efforts to make sense of and reclaim the term. And I think the reason for that is that once you make yourself aware of the distance between player and character, and use that as a tool in your designs, then you have a tough time with a mode of play in which minimizing or hiding that distance is a sine qua non.

      So in devising an alternate set of CAs, the answer to the "why bother" question (i.e., "why bother coming up with an alternative to GNS?") has to be in order to reveal a different space for play, or to open up new possible avenues for design, or minimally to render us more sensitive to the alternatives that may exist.

      Part Two: Reconstructing Creative Agendas

      What follows should be read with an eye on Eero's remark that the absence of narrativism from Callois's scheme has to be attributed to the paucity of "premise-ful" modes of play in the instances of play he examined, and that were he around today he'd surely incorporate that into his scheme. In other words, our theories should be constructed in light of the empirical evidence available. On the other hand, I always assumed that the reason that Caillois identified four categories--rather than three or five, say--had something to do with the linguistic structuralism associated with the Paris School of Semiotics, in which a huge semantic space is produced by the conceptual entanglement of opposites. So a "positive" term implies (the existence of) a corresponding negative, like yes implies no. But those terms can be "entangled" as both yes and no (i.e., "maybe") and as neither yes nor no (i.e., "I don't know"). Similarly, agon (skill) implies its opposite alea (not-skill: luck or chance). And those opposites can be entangled such that "both agon and alea" can be understood to mean mimicry, as successful mimicry is at once a skillful (albeit not wholly competitive) performance as well as a willingness to subject oneself to the dictates of fortune. By the same token, ilinx is clearly "neither agon nor alea" in that the dislocations or dissociations one experiences in ilingic play obviate both skill and luck.

      Now, in this post-structuralist era, we don't believe that our categories are in any way essential or fundamental; they are analytic conveniences only.

      So what happens if we imagine agon, alea, mimicry, and ilinx as the corners of a semiotic square? First of all, we notice that the underlying distinction in both the agon-alea and mimicry-ilinx pairs has something to do with the activity of the player; in the former, the player is active while in the latter the player is passive, in the sense that he or she is the object of the forces acting in the game, whether that\u2019s the turn of the wheel of fortune or the elasticity of the bungee rope.



      Secondly, we notice that the underlying distinction in both the agon-mimicry and the alea-ilinx pairs seems to be that the former in both instances presume the unalloyed agency of the player whereas the latter see the player as part of the field of play. In other words, whereas in both agon and alea you play the game, in both mimicry and ilinx the game plays you. You adopt a new persona, take on a new role, feel scared or excited, or otherwise lose control of yourself.

      But these dualities are capable of being entangled, and we can use those entanglements to rebuild the CAs. We\u2019ll follow Eero in suggesting that the combination of agon and alea is the Contest, in which players are interested in victory as the outcome of play. Conversely, the combination of mimicry and ilinx is Drama; players are interested in experiencing thematically resonant moments as the outcome of play. So far we\u2019ve reconstructed the Gamism/Narrativism dichotomy in slightly different terms.

      Looking at the orthogonal dimension, starting with the combination of agon and mimicry, I\u2019d suggest that the appropriate metaphor or model is the Pageant, where players are interested in the skillful performance or faithful recreation of alternate roles. Its converse, the combination of alea and ilinx, is the Thrill Ride, in which players are willing to subject themselves to external control in return for the promise of a powerful or pleasant emotional experience. The effect of this, I\u2019d say, is to reconstruct Simulationism as a kind of \u201cagonistic mimicry\u201d that stands in the same relationship to \u201cImmersionism\u201d as Narrativism does to Gamism.
