\n I'm writing up the introduction to Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple and I'd like to list some examples of "Coming-of-Age Road Trip" stories.

By this I mean the kind of story where the protagonists have a series of adventures as they travel towards a destination, but wind up learning more about themselves by the end.

I'm looking for movies, tv shows, anime, novels, anything you think would fall under this description. If you could also tell me why you think it fits in this genre, or just point me somewhere online, I'd be much obliged.

For example:\n
Wizard of Oz - A lost girl travels through a magical kingdom in search of the wizard who can send her home. Along the way she finds three unusual companions who also seek things that only the wizard can provide. Their journey is interrupted by a wicked witch, but by the end the companions discover that what they sought was with them all along.

Harold and Kumar Go to White Castle - A pair of stoner friends search for a fast food fix, but along the way they deal with family expectations, unrequited love, racism and that rat bastard Neil Patrick Harris. In the end, according to wikipedia, the duo gets their sticks and sliders, finds a small measure of revenge against Harold\u2019s co-workers and have their car returned by a repentant Harris.

Almost Famous - A teenage journalist lies about his age to covers the rise of fictional rock band Stillwater for Rolling Stone magazine. He falls in love with one of their groupies and chronicles their journey from small-time gigs to megastardom. Tensions nearly break the band apart, but they safely fall into rock n' roll obscurity. The journalist survives the tour, confesses his love and eventually gets his cover story.
\nI imagine Stand By Me, Journey to the West, Blues Brothers, Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas, and Road to Perdition may also qualify but I don't know enough about them to say so certainly.

    \n Stand By Me, definitely. Some kids go walking just doing whatever, and then find stuff - some hard to deal with - and grow up a bunch on the way.

    Fear and Loathing In Las Vegas is not really the same kind of thing. There's no coming of age element, and it's not a road movie (it just has a trip at the beginning). It's more about Thompson rejecting the hopes and dreams of the 60s as empty.

    Avatar: The Last Airbender is one that I'd expect to see on your list. Lots of martial arts, young protagonists growing up, not always obvious answers to the problems they find.

    \nBottle Rocket is superb.

    The underrated Coupe De Ville is about three brothers who meet after a gap and take a car cross-country.

    Spy Kids is about a couple of... kids... who are spies. But I do like their relationship and competence.

    A Bug's Life has travel, exotic strangers, and returning home to kick some tiny ass.

    Narnia, obviously. Especially 'Voyage of the Dawntreader'. And Lemony Snicket, including the movie.

    I Went Down is an excellent Irish movie (with Brendan Gleason!) about two crummy Irish lowlifes who try to kidnap a man. They're both adults, but idiots. They do some maturing.

    There's a few other movies that could count, like Priscilla, Easy Rider, Wild at Heart. They're not about kids, but they are about changing while on the move.

    \n Just fiction? Alexander Topolski's autobiography, Without Vodka, is an amazing story of his coming of age as a sixteen-year-old Pole who:

    1. Fights the Germans in Poland
    2. Is sent to a Soviet Gulag in Kazakhstan
    3. Walks to Iran
    4. Fights the Germans in Italy

    Most of the book is his experience as a Soviet prisoner, and he grows up fast.
