\n
\n
\n
\n\nJack Aidley \n\n\n
\n
\n\nNovember 2007 \n edited November 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n
The warlock is evidence of a philosophical shift within D&D R&D. When we did the 3.0 classes, we sort of asked ourselves "What would a barbarian be like?" and "What would a ranger be like?" The warlock arises from a different sort of question: "How can we design a class that provides this-or-that game experience for the player?" The warlock's not the only class like that, but it's a clear example.
\n- Jonathan Tweet, from here

I think, for me, that sums up why I think 4th edition is going to be great, and is a real shining example of how the Indie Ethic has altered the mainstream. In my head I call it 'Intentional Design'; designing not for some abstract, unreal goal but towards producing the play experience you want to achieve. I first saw it on the Forge and it changed the way I design and run games, and I think it's been the driving force behind everything good about the Indie movement - sure there's been a host of other theoretical advances but that central principle has been the driving factor that's pushed it all along and made it great.

And now it's infected the big beast of the industry.

    \n You may have first seen this approach to design being implemented at the Forge but, rest assured, it existed before then. It's literally sickening (i.e., the behavior makes my stomach churn).

Can't people be proud of what they've actually accomplished? Must they also take credit for the work of others?

    \n I'm sorry I'm bumping a long forgotten thread here, but I want to say a word or two about the game...

    My life with RPGs started with DnD (ADnD 2nd Ed back then). I was shocked when 3rd Ed came. After I actually gave it a look, I started digging it.

    I know most people complain about how it was all about Power Playing. And I agree that the rules can encourage that style of playing, but that depends too much on the playing style of the DM and the players. (A Storyteller kind of player is not suddenly going to become a Power Player just for playing DnD.) And I actually loved the way the character customization was enhanced. That actually got to be a role playing tool for me.

    When 3.5 Ed came, I was actually a supporter. Having played 3rd for some time then, I knew there were some blurry spots that could be improved. And when it came out, I wasn't disappointed. I did feel it was the same old DnD, polished and enhanced. Ok, maybe not "old school" DnD, but it didn't compromise anything important (storywise or role-playing relevant).

    I have to confess. I do have some mixed feelings about 4th Ed. As far as I have seen, I feel that the Power Playing issue was augmented, not fixed. No to say that they are trying to make the game more smooth and enjoyable. I totally understand, and even support some of the changes. The most hated part in any campaign I have ever played or mastered is that of those hateful low levels, when the players can't do much, thus getting kind of frustrated and bored. And they seem to be addressing that sort of problems.

    I haven't played any MMO so far. By choice, rather than by being unable to do so. But I see MMOs as The Power Playing games. And being a Storyteller myself... Lets just say they are not really my thing.

    That is precisely the main advantage between DnD and any MMO out there. No matter how many overly powerful and marketable thing they put into it, they will never be able to substitute those amazing hours of In-Character arguments, story development and plot enjoyment.

    And that is why I'm willing to give the 4th Ed a try.

    Who knows? I might be pleased once I actually play it.
