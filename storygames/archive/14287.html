    \nCheck out the statistical simulations of Werewolf games. For one wolf, you want seven or nine players. For two wolves, you want 19 or 21 people. You never want an even number of players, or humans are hurt badly by how voting works.

    That assumes that the skill of the human collective and the skill of the werewolves cancel out. In shorter games, wolves have an advantage, while in longer ones, humans get an advantage.



    My friend Ross was trying some crazy Werewolf variants at Origins last year. I'll have to see what he tried and how it worked.

    \n In my experience, the two crucial roles are the Sheriff and the Doctor. The Sheriff gets to ask if someone is Mafia, and the Doctor gets to pick one person to save from death. Both roles are IMO crucial for the game to really fire on all cylinders, because they create opportunities for deceit, risk analysis, and occasional heroism. Having a Doctor adds a lot of variables to the Sheriff role -- it makes 'coming clean' earlier in the game an option, because painting a target on your head is no longer a guaranteed death sentence -- and also adds wiggle room in terms of the optimal ratio of Mafia/Civilians, since a single deathless night can easily turn the tables.

    I have never heard of the Witness role, so maybe I'm biased, but it sounds really terrible. How do you determine if they've been noticed in a way that doesn't disrupt the gameplay, or require the Mafia/Werewolf players to risk further exposure? Also, combined with the Detective/Sheriff/Whatever that's two vectors for 100% reliable information on who is Mafia/Werewolf. The only advantage I can see is that the presence of the role would encourage people to claim that they 'hear something' during the killing phase -- which is of course the major source of hilariously overwrought speculation during the early rounds.

    In terms of facilitating, I have no tips -- the most important thing in my experience is getting people over the hump when they realize that they are basically lynching someone on almost-zero evidence the first day. One or two zealous Civilians (or pretend-Civilians) go a long way.

    \n Nick's got the science there, with regard to werewolf/villager ratios. And yeah, stick to the basics - keep the Seer/Detective, add the Witness if people like, but don't get much more complicated unless people are really super excited about the extra roles. (Also see the "neighbor" role in Jane McG's article here.)

    Hm... A good facilitator tip is to keep moving around while you're talking, and keep the daily routine the same every time, regardless of who's in or out. Human ears can be pretty awesome at discerning direction and location, so keep your facing and position varied when you're addressing werewolf-y type stuff at night so as not to accidentally give them away.

    \n For sillier games (even though I don't think it hurts serious games) I like to add the sausage man. He wakes up every night and chooses someone who receives a sausage (could be a letter or something instead if you don't like it, but "korv" is a funny word in swedish since it's got a k). The only rules about him are:
    - He can never give himself one
    - He can't give a second helping to anyone before everyone currently alive has got a first
    - He's on the villagers' side

    When I have a kill-free first night to learn who the wolves are, this means two things happen: the sheriff gets a first lookup and someone gets the first sausage, which sometimes works to start off the discussion.

    The trick is to be just clear enough on the three rules: anyone that actually listens have to know what his deal is, but the kind of people who distrusts the facilitator or sticks to unreasonable accusations will try to draw strange connections to the sausage man and the mafia. Very often the mafia catches on and kills whoever gets the first sausage.

    \n Oh yeah! For fun, sometimes we have "The Flautist". Their only role is that when they are killed, they give a last plaintive trill on their flute.

    Its purpose is to let everyone know that it was a villager, not a werewolf, that was just killed.

    People can get frustrated being a villager in game after game, so it's cool to add little roles that don't increase the length of the nights, but give the player something special to do.
