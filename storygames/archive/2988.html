    Journalism is defined by the moral stance it takes. You can't say something without meaning something.

    \n

    But you can try. The cool, removed, stoic, and frankly autistic perspective that is prevalent in many RPGs (as well as wargames \xe2\u20ac\u201d you're sending thousands of dudes to their deaths with a wave of your hand) is a power thing, I think. "I'm so powerful, I'm unmoved by these deaths."

    \n

    But remember: "Objective Journalism" was invented by William Randolph Hearst in the interest of not offending advertisers. The same man who said, "You furnish the pictures. I'll furnish the war."

    \n

    So that means that, if you intend to say something in your story making process, the rules of the game should give you the pieces you need to say that. It means playing a game where you must take a moral stance; where you can sacrifice fictional things for other fictional things that metaphorically stand for real life things that you care about. Win or lose, as a player, you've made a statement when the conflict is over.

    \n

    It sounds to me (correct me if I'm wrong) that the question here is whether a "story" is "the events as they objectively, and therefore truly, happened" or it's "the statement made by the arrangement of fictional elements." I'd say that the former is a fiction even greater than the events described.

    \n Eric wants to make the analogy that narr play is to sim play as literary fiction is to news reporting, as a way of helping his friends understand RPGs in general. The essential difference, he suspects, is that the former in each case is thematic while the latter in each case is not. He defines "theme" as "the moral of the story": the argument it makes or the lesson it teaches.

    (Let's stipulate that we don't need the terms narr or sim right now; we can just say, "One mode of play . . . " and "Another, contrasting mode of play . . . " without evoking a whole slew of GNS baggage. Eric is mostly concerned with the appropriateness of the analogy, I think).

    I think the analogy holds up, but in a trickier sense that deserves some thinking about. The thing about journalistic conventions of objectivity is that they mask the extent to which assumptions about how the world works are built into the routines and practices of reporting in a way that's not the case in literary writing, which is intended to call those assumptions into question. Literary writing is supposed to make us re-examine taken-for-granteds about the world and the things in it. I'm re-reading Harlan Ellison's anthology, Dangerous Visions, right now, and there's a story in it by David Bunch called "Incident in Moderan" where the narrator is a buffed-up cyborg warlord who like all the other citizens of Moderan is happy as a clam in his gleaming Stronghold punching the launch buttons to fire missile after missile in endless and pointless warfare, his hate a thing of joy. When a "flesh-bum" approaches his Stronghold during a brief truce to thank him for his decency in halting his fire so that he could bury his son, the narrator is puzzled: the cease-fire was so that some other Strongholds could repair their ammo loops and thus enable a more glorious and destructive war. When the flesh-bum is burned to a crisp by the resumed fire, the narrator shrugs and returns to his launch buttons. This story is "thematic" in the sense that calls attention to our ideas about what it means to be human, and points ironically back at us as we read it.

    A newspaper report about a man being killed does exactly the opposite: it assumes a particular set of ideas, and proceeds from them. The conventions of objectivity mean that news reports follow a script, and so we know what information we'd expect to see in such a story: who died, how, under what circumstances, and whose fault it was, with the additional fillip that the death must be somehow "newsworthy" in order to warrant coverage. We are not challenged in our assumptions about how the world works; instead, we are confirmed in them.

    Within the same medium, then (i.e., print), there are two different modes of dealing with "theme": dig it up or bury it deep. Is the same true of RPGs?

    I'm inclined to say yes, and that you can look to a game's system the way you'd look to a newspaper story's content-in-context to identify its ideological presuppositions.

    Of course, the analogy breaks down at the point where you say, "but literary fiction and news reporting are separate genres, and RPGs don't really have such a thing as separate genres for games." But that's when somebody would say, "And that's what GNS gives us."

    \nJoshua -- I agree with you; there is a particular "moral order" built into the activity of a wargame just as there's one built into the activity of journalism. In a wargame that moral ordering is strictly neutral, as you suggest: there are no "good guys" or "bad guys" per se, only opposing sides of equal merit. Nor are we supposed to draw any moral from the victory of either the Allies or the Axis in a game of Axis & Allies. But the game does incorporate a model of "how things (ought to) work": troops obey, commanders command, war machines produce materiel. The moral ordering of journalism as an activity is a little different; the ethos of (post-Watergate) journalism valorizes being a "watchdog" on government and political officials, championing the "little guy", speaking truth to power. The reality of journalistic practice is a little different, of course, with its cozy interdependence with and reliance upon official sources and its relative inattention to voices outside the already-powerful, and that too is part of the moral ordering of journalism.

    Bracket all that stuff off, and concentrate for a second on the idea of authoring, in the sense of making a (thematic) statement by virtue of the choices one makes in creating a text -- whether that text is the recorded moves of a board game, a newspaper report of a murder, a short story, or the transcript of a roleplaying game session. To what extent have I created, or helped create, a narrative of grit and determination if I play a chess game in which my forces are slowly ground down by your superior manuevering but I manage to even things up with a few desperate exchanges and eventually force a draw by perpetual check? Now, the moral ordering of chess is built into the game: two sides, equal in dignity, neither one in and of itself "good" or "bad." But the choices made by the player(s) to some extent "tell a story" with a particular moral. Is that right?

    And if that's authoring, well then what isn't? You'd have to say that an activity moves further away from authoring the more the choices available to the creators of the text are constrained by the "rules of the game." And to the extent that much news reporting is exactly so constrained, routinized by the organizations in which it is located and standardized by competitive pressures with other organizations, it is less "authored" than a short story.

    Of course, I bet many journalists would vociferously protest that characterization, and I'm willing to give them their due. Let's forget about that, and stick with games.

    Does the notion of different levels of "authoring" help us make sense of the distinction that Eric was trying to raise way up top in the OP?
