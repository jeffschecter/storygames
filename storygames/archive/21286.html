\n Is there still a hunger to innovate and theorize about RPGs as a community? Are there enough people interested in doing so and having such a community?

I wasn't part of the Forge community but I feel as though it was a place that was serious about rpgs about design and about theory. You can't say that the Forge didn't improve RPGs; the form was largely stagnant and the Forge caused a swell of innovation\u2014yes, not all of it good, but lots of it was and is, and there is still a ton of work to do. What am I missing? Why aren't we still doing this? What happened?

If I hosted a website strictly for this purpose (i.e. design, theory, innovation, etc.) would people come? A place to come with goodwill and offer each other solutions and to put our egos aside and try to make really, really good games, and to solve the problems of certain forms and create new ones. To think critically about games and theory. Is this something people still want? And, if not, why?

    \n There is no particular reason for not doing "another Forge", except that it's a rare sort of phenomenon that's not solely up to people deciding to do it. Some things you can do on a "build it and they will come" basis, while other things are like capturing lightning in a bottle.

    Specifically, what made the Forge work for us was that a large number of roleplayers in the X generation (those who'd started in the hobby in the late '80s or through the '90s) shared common ground in being dissatisfied with the traditional rpg culture that was still the standard in the early '00s. In hindsight there were specific issues in the rpg culture that we were interested in addressing, and we did. These concerns were a multitude, and they all interconnected in a way that made the Forge very important for an ambitious young designer; it wasn't just game design, and not just rpg theory, but also publishing practice and marketing.

    Notably the people who did the Forge thing haven't disappeared anywhere, it's just that our interests have diverged sufficiently for us to have less to talk about together: some have fixed their roleplaying and are thus happy playing (as opposed to doing self-therapy on web forums); some have become productive designers keen to work on their own stuff; some run contests or workshops or local scene support. Some even do rpg theory on occasion. What we do not have, however, is a pressing collective need to lay out the rpg conundrum in the open and solve it together, day by day. I can't speak for everybody from the Forge, but I think many people simply don't need that sort of thing anymore because we already have all the answers, and have thus transcended the need for the Forge [grin].

    For a "new Forge" to be a meaningful endeavour, there would need to exist a similar generational experience, a need for collective action. Roleplaying's a small subculture, so we're not talking huge numbers, but that scale's still not something you're going to achieve without an actual demographic need for it. If one were to hypothetically start up a new Forge-kind website, I would expect it to lack in membership interest, unless one happened to tap a new demographic need with it.

    I cannot say whether and when the time might be ripe for something like that, I'll note. The OSR trend was the most recent equivalent of the Forge, and I don't know what the next "big thing" will be. One could imagine it to be something that requires new rpg theory, new game design, new methods of publishing, in which case something pretty similar to the Forge could be the thing to do. A forum is, after all, a great place to host in-depth correspondence and invite new people to participate as you go along. But you absolutely got to have something to talk about first, and that's far from given.

    One might also consider whether it'd be possible to do something similar to the Forge, except less intense, because the people are in different places in their lives and so on. Various things have been tried in this regard, one might say that Story Games has developed into one of those attempts - more of a cultural saloon than a workshop. You can see how SG compensates for the lack of that intensity and zeal by having a much wider and more casual range of topics, for instance. Other attempts at doing Forge-like things at lower intensity abound: we do mailing lists, blogs, con gaming, lectures, IRC channels, design workshops and such instead of a 24/7 forum, because that's perhaps a more appropriate medium for where we're at.

    \n A practical note on setting up the forum, by the way: hold off on doing that until you actually have a mass of on-going conversation sufficient for justifying a new forum. This is very concrete: you need to know many people who want to talk with you about this stuff all the time, such that you cause some place like Story Games (or wherever you meet these people) to congest with the traffic. That's when a new forum is necessary. Starting the forum first is pretty backwards, I personally think.

    This is incidentally how Ron Edwards set up the Forge forums, by the way: he originally started the Forge as an article hosting site related to rpg theory discussions he had elsewhere, and then started up the forum to have a more friendly place to discuss the related issues. When he started the forums, he did that in the context of an already-existing community of discourse, even if its size at the time paled with the later popularity of the Forge.

    \n The Forge existed to serve two propositions.

    One was, you can create a roleplaying game that works exactly how you want it to work. The reason for all of the theory was to tear down conventional wisdom about how rpgs must work so that we could see how they might work.

    The other was, you can publish your game yourself. When the Forge began, PDF was new, PayPal was new, and print on demand was very rare. Lulu was new, OneBookShelf didn't exist, Kickstarter REALLY didn't exist yet.

    When the Forge closed, it was because you can create a game that works how you want, and you can publish it yourself was a done deal. It was how the world worked in 2012. We didn't need to keep reaching toward it.

    My advice is, if you want a "new Forge," have a project. Have something you're trying to accomplish, not just something you want to talk about, have a plan, and make it ambitious.

    -Vincent

    \n Jeff, I think there are plenty of groups out there working to innovate in RPG design, and none of them have taken off Forge-style. @DanMaruschak created Re-Forged and it didn't see a ton of work. @James_Mullen seems to have had more success with Games on Trial, and you should probably check that out, but that's not huge either.

    As for theorizing, I think individuals are still having new ideas about how RPGs can work, and talking about those ideas, but I don't see any desire to assemble all those words into one place. "Sharing new thoughts" also may or may not be what you mean by "theorizing" -- I think the intellectual battle to pin down definitions of RPG function and history has largely died because it's shown diminishing returns over the last dozen years. Sometimes agreeing on a thing's exact parameters and what to call it is way harder than getting the actual design point.

    If you're looking for inspiration and examples of diverse approaches, I think your mammoth game library is the best source for that! But if you want to share your thoughts and chat about them, I think here and RPGnet are decent options if you don't have a better one (i.e. a following on G+ or Facebook).

    \n I was there on the last days of the Forge and learned a lot there too. And I can confirm there are many things that are given fro granted today. Before reading anything at the forge my approach to making/hacking games was mostly about finding practical ways to do things that I felt the games I played were doing wrong. There I found so many people making the same mistakes that I did that they had coined a term for it: Fantasy Heartbreaker. It was nice to learn about other games, ideas and different points of view about roleplaying games.

    But at the end discussing about loose mechanics and game fragments became impossible, Ron himself closed ant thread that wasn't about a full completed game as he considered these loose discussions spam. That was the thing that killed the forge to me and I ended up finding and moving to SG before the forge finally closed.

    Things here were more hectic, casual and chaotic, but even with that it felt less confrontational than they were at the forge on the last months. It was fun and even when at times there were more players than designers, not too many people frowned at you when you started a thread on crazy mechanics that could or not end up turning into a game.

    As time went on SG had its troubles and personal drama too, some rules had to be enforced and we found out that it was for good. Here I ended up learning even more than on the Forge, that games were more about how the people playing them understands them, that how good they turn out depends more on the dynamic of the players than of the actual mechanics used, but that these are the ones that transmit the players what's the game about and if they aren't clear to start with, players will either need tons of additional advice, end making up a different game from your text or completely ruining the experience.

    So, if this new Forge were to be about theory... I'd probably read some of the stuff every once in a while. If it were about design I'll definitely like to participate. But the thing that got me more interested in the last few months was the idea developed in these two threads:
    -Subscene that assumes GMs will hack but that strongly values design
    -[Open Design] Guides for collaborative design using Markdown, git, etc.

    The basic idea is this: at Github programmers share bits of code for others to use as they want, so nobody has to figure out and write a whole program/application by themselves. People there also collaborate on other people's projects with pieces of programs and other stuff, so projects get completed faster.

    Now imagine that applied to RPGs: you could design a conflict resolution mechanic and ask people to help you develop the rest of the game, establishing some standards like genre, etc. But there's more: those people could take those parts and make a different game. Mechanics could also be edited by others to suit their needs. It's more or less the part that I liked most from the Forge and SG, except that everything is CC, free to use and modify.

    Sadly it died way too fast, I managed to post something at the Github reservoir but had no feedback and haven't seen anybody else post anything. The Github platform is definitely way too complicated for some people and there's no similar but simple enough solution able to help people share info in an organized way, keeping different versions of the same files nor different collections of the those files.

    Who knows, perhaps this will happen when the proper technology is available or gets easier to use.

    Back in the early days of the Forge, in one of their weekly conversations about running the place, Clinton Dreisbach (n\xe9 Nixon) said something like, "About 5% of people will never get this": that the Forge is about creator ownership, about self-publishing for tabletop role-playing games. And while the theory stuff "became bigger and bigger," in Clinton's words, by the end of the Forge, Ron was pretty adamant that it should be a place where anyone, regardless of their design approach, could come and talk about what they were trying to do and get advice and support from the Forge community.

    With respect to "Forge theory" (and Ron insists he never used that term), it seems to me that the Big Model in particular was less a theory of design than a heuristic for unpacking unsatisfactory play--necessary for designing the game you want, as Vincent puts it. That's why you can find a whole bunch of threads where people are interested in trying to use the Big Model to unpack why they're unhappy with their current game. The Big Model invites paying attention to the social context in which the game is taking place (i.e., the real people and the relationships among them) as well as the clashes or disconnects in terms of aesthetic preferences and expectations among the players.

    That's not to say that there weren't RPG design-related concepts, like the "lumpley-Boss principle" that defines system as the means for determining who gets to say what is true about what in the fiction when, the "Czege principle" that it is unsatisfying to have to both establish (fictional) adversity and determine the means through which it is overcome, and ideas like handling time, reward cycles, and IIEE (i.e., intent, initiation, execution, and effect as elements of resolving a task). But by and large there was an attempt to focus discussion on "actual play" in a way that now strikes me as a kind of "ethno-phenomenology," i.e., a community's attempt to understand or make sense of its own mental experience via systematic reflection--to address, in other words, the question of what it is like to role-play.

    Of course, there is actually a very strong connection between Story-Games and the Forge; Andy Kitkowski--who was one of the people who also participated in the discussions on the Gaming Outpost that ultimately gave rise to the Forge forums--set up Story-Games to be like the "Forge birthday forum" all year round.

    The Forge birthday forum, for those who haven't heard of it, was opened each year for a few days in April between 2002 and 2006 to celebrate the "Forge's birthday." The normal moderation rules didn't apply there. Instead, the rules were "post whatever you like, and have a good time doing so!" with the added proviso that you should "be nice, damnit!'

    The idea was that you could just shoot the breeze with folks with whom you'd been interacting in a really focused, structured way during the rest of the year. Maybe it served as a kind of "backstage" where people could let their hair down. There's a nice thread started by Emily Care Boss where she imagines what games she would play and who she would play them with if she were at Forge Midwest, and one where Judd talks about the impact the Forge has had on him.

    So, anyway, it occurs to me that you could do a kind of mini-Forge here on Story-Games by inverting that formula: establish a category called something like GMless Forge and moderate it in order to support the project of articulating and refining GMless games and best practices for play.

    You'd probably also want something analogous to the Forge Booth; that is, a real-world project that served as the focal point for a lot of people's efforts at GenCon. Maybe something like the "Indie Games Explosion" that Michael and Kat Miller started; it helped showcase indie games and eventually morphed into the Games on Demand rooms that you see at Gen Con, Origins, and elsewhere.
