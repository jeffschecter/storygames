\n The recent thread on what is the minimum of rules showed that tasks like, framing scenes, setting stakes, and other regular story game activities are hard to describe in rules but easy to learn when you do them. I want to follow up on that idea.

I encountered this problem over twenty years ago when I started working on Matrix games. I would say something like "Make an argument for what you want to have happen next." and people would give me blank looks. It was like I'd spoken Greek. But when I reframed the question they were able to do the task easily. I'd say "If this were any other game, this was the situation, you were your character and you could do anything, what would you want to do first?"

Interestingly after the first turn I could say "What happens next?" and they had no trouble making up the next scene.

It became clear to me that people under went a mental systems shift.

Once they had made the shift they could play story games until then they couldn't. I believe this is what Ron Edwards was describing when he said traditional RPGs caused brain damage. Trad games teach a process where the GM describes the situation, the players can only say what their character does and the GM narrates what happens. The GM is the main player. The players are largely an audience. I never agreed with what I though Ron was saying because I think people can be taught the system shift.

What I'd like to explore in this thread are ways people walk people though the different mental shifts story gamers go through to be able to play.

My day job becons me now so I'll get back to this later with an example of how I do this with Matrix games.

Chris Engle

    \n So my theory is that people need to make a mental shift to play story games. Trad games teach players to be more audience than active participant in scene staging, stakes setting and most narration. With board games players are taught that they can only do things that are within the rules. You can't make things up as you go along. Of course we know that you can do that and that it is very fun.

    Since it is a mental systems shift you need to keep things simple. Put forward too many rules and players get confused and shut down. I've seen my wife do this many times when confronted with new games. There need to be very few barriers to frighten them off.

    The shift I'm shooting for is to get people making up scenes and to contradict one another. I have one player assigned the role "game host" who is also the referee. The host read a script to guide the players through the game.

    The host picks a player to go first. "Bob - Tell us what happens next?" On Bob's first turn the host offers more encouragement, like "Tell us what your character does when he gets to the house?" Whatever Bob says is his first scene (or rather the fragment of a scene). By making this up Bob is beginning to think differently about the game. He made up the narrative rather than the host.

    The host decides what Bob has to roll or less on 2d6 for the action to happen. The default roll is 7 so new hosts don't have to know what they are doing at first either. When they feel more confident in their rulings they can move the number up or down.

    The turn could end here but I want players to know they don't have to agree with one another so the host asks "Does anyone want to make a counter-argument to this?" If a player does, they make an alternative version of the scene. They also get to roll. As many players as want may join in this pissing match. The ones that pass the first roll get to make a second roll. The high roller is the one whose version happens.

    That is a very simple procedure (I think). I don't tell them that players can build on one another's scene instead of contradict them. I don't mention that the host can decide to let all the counter-arguments happen without a second roll. I don't tell them that the host can also run a character (he just picks a different player to be his referee). I don't tell them that the host can pass on the referee job to other players. And I especially don't tell them about how to run criminal trials, handle secret actions, or counteract trouble. All that flexibility would just confuse them.

    After they have played a few games with the basic format they will be ready to add in the variations on the theme - but not until then.

    So how would you go about doing stakes setting in say Dogs?

    \nAn interesting distinction I got out of your post, which I will not attempt to theoretically locate at all, goes like this.
    \n
    \n- In much standard-as-I-have-observed-it RPG play, there is tight control over what gets introduced into the shared imagined space. Typically there is a GM who handles most of this control and player inputs are either through shared mechanics (the system lets me input this if I make a roll) and/or GM permission (it's understood that at any time player inputs can be vetoed or restricted if they don't fit the integrity). The GM's job is preserving the integrity of the shared imagined space.
    \n
    \n- It is possible to play RPGs or RPG-like games in which this control is weakened, in a variety of ways. One way, which you describe, is for multiple players and/or the GM (if there even is one) to all propose alternate contents/stories/frames for scenes and to roll that off directly, or put it up to a vote, or have those who do not propose dispose, etc. The construction of the SIS is not filtered through a GM figure so much as put out there as open content for the group to negotiate, and/or for system elements to deal with integration problems directly rather than through the lens of 'what can my character do now?' for players and everything else for GMs.
    \n
    \nIf this is your point I would agree.

    \n I observed such a paradigm shift at Ice Station Nerdly - Adam Dray had to remind one of the players that games like My Life With Master and Dogs run on player input and agency. There would be the player, expectantly waiting for Adam (the gm) to give input in a particular way, and then Adam would consciously ignore the trad-gamer social cue and instead further complicate the situation.
    Eventually, Adam or one of the other players (me or Brian) would say something along the lines of "hey, man, this part is up to us, the players!" At which point there'd be a sort of "Oh, really? Hm, okay, then... [almost immediately] how about this?"

    This sort of thing highlights the intense importance of procedural text, of very clearly establishing who says and does what, when. If it's hand-waved, or not really covered effectively, then it's up to the play group; this is not an inherently bad thing, but games like Dogs really, really rely on giving the final say about, f'rex, the nature of demons, to the players, and NOT to the gm.

    Chris, I think that board games can teach us a lot about good, solid procedural text, but I fully agree that a little improv, a little color here and there, has to happen. I think Vincent is really onto something when he talks about making sure that player input meaningfully affects both the fiction and the play procedures - but of late, I admit I'm willing to sacrifice some of the "screen time" that Color gets for the sake of nailing the procedures perfectly.

    It's a wiggly balance to strike - running Chronica last night, working with a brand-new RPer, I intervened several times to point out when we were "just narrating" and when we had to use the rules - it came down to spelling out when you pick up the dice. Chronica handles this pretty well, although it does call for some artfulness - it tells you to pick up the dice whenever a character tries to do something "difficult, dangerous, or daring". Pretty standard, yes, but it's specific enough that the game doesn't ask you to "just know" when a conflict begins.

    On the OSR - Where Do We Go From Here thread, I think that's where someone said the middle editions of D&D contain lots of rules and tables that are devoid of procedural text. This is the sort of thing that was my bane in middle school, demanding that I infer without any outside guidance (social or textual) how the bloody hell to play the thing. It was totally unclear how to frame an adventure, using only the text of AD&D 2e, how to start a campaign, what the campaign should be about... it was confusing enough that I briefly considered that you were supposed to only play modules each session, until my mom (ah, my mom) insisted that buying modules would cost too much money, and my friends and I were imaginative enough without that.

    This led, again, without any real guidance, to games that consisted entirely of combats, games that quickly disintegrated due to lack of investment in the activity. In Everway, for chrissake, there was enough of an idea of what to do that we picked powers, the GM threw problems our way, and we thrashed around in Exploration, seeing where things went. AD&D2 failed us as far as presenting, clearly, what were were supposed to be doing. I had similar problems with 2nd ed. Werewolf and Mage, and 3rd ed. Vampire - we had plenty of essays to read on what sorts of themes and moods to cover once play got going, and that all made sense, but as far as what we should DO, we just scratched our heads and went with whatever stream of consciousness plot we could devise.

    I, as resident GM, eventually devised elaborate plots for the players to explore, but these ended up being metaplot-style play - the players did as they wished, I didn't railroad them much at all, and I tried to introduce cool ideas I'd had, when I could. The net effect was a bunch of random Tzimisce beasties running around, dream sequences, and off-label applications of Disciplines, while the players had fun killing stuff and staying well-fed. It was enjoyable, but it was a lot more spastic and random than, perhaps, was intended.

    \n Hi

    I wanted to chip in with my two cents about this topic as it is a pet peeve of mine. It got me thinking about how many RPG texts are examples of tacit knowledge. There is an implication that you will "get it" by playing the game repeatedly. I would relate this to the hackneyed metaphor that often gets used is that of the band. The GM is the bass player and the players are the other musicians who improvise around him/her. Well, I play music (not wonderfully, but I did get paid \xa350 for a gig once) and to reach a standard to improvise effectively takes enormous practice. Additionally, it seems important for a musician of that level to "know" what the other musicians are about. If that musical analogy is the analogy that is being offered then I would say I am a punk. I want to plug in turn up the amp to 11 while someone else beats the living crap out of the drums and if we start and finish the song at the same time then that is a victory.

    Another way I would frame it is perhaps I would like to learn to play better. I can practice on my own - something I'm planning to do having recently bought Mythic for its solo potential - and at last I might be able to have a Tarkovsky RPG experience. I can learn from others, either through play or by asking questions. The other option is to request greater clarity from game designers.

    A great designer does not a good explainer make. Would it not be useful in the process of writing the rulebook to get someone in who either played only a mainstream game, such as D&D or someone who had never played anything before just for proofreading?
