\n (I hope this isn't stating the obvious and well known - or possibly I hope it is).

As many people know,desire lines are the paths created by people actually walking. And a good way of deciding where to put in paths is to look for the desire lines, them make them official. On the other hand trying to block them can get ridiculous.

One of the reasons behind D&D's success, I'm convinced, is that it was designed pretty much this way. Gygax didn't come up with the basic structure (Arneson did), and Gygax was not, to be honest, much of a designer (for evidence I submit Dangerous Journeys: Mythus and Cyborg Commando). But Gygax playtested the hell out of oD&D for years, adapting and growing it based on the desire paths that people created (Mike Mornard summarises the whole process as "We made up some shit we thought would be fun"). In fact I don't think there's been one RPG that's been playtested nearly as well as oD&D in history. And that put a whole lot of depth into the game (including, for example, the narrative arc of characters that later inspired Adventurer Conqueror King, and such a narrative arc built into the rules was barely seen again in a published RPG until My Life With Master triggered the invention of the term Story Game).

On the flipside this, I think, was the mistake 4E made with AEDU (and just about all over tbh). 4e is, at a theoretical level, a fine game and At Will/Signature Move/Once per Episode is fine narrative pacing, but no one was walking that way. So it had ... issues.

And I was also thinking how to apply this to game design, and realised that a lot of my favourite RPGs did. To me the obvious one is Apocalypse World; the desire lines there are the rhythm of freeform RP, which is a big part of what makes it feel natural. It also has genuine character (as opposed to numerical power) development built in and tools to match what people do. Thinking a little deeper I realised that two of my other favourite RPGs also follow different sets of Desire Lines; Monsterhearts (as well as being Powered by the Apocalypse) uses a graduate level deconstruction of its genre that then goes on to use the deconstruction to show what the story pieces are and how to use them. (This was, of course the same thing that made My Life With Master so revolutionary - and I need to get round to playing that sometime). The other game that springs to mind as following Desire Lines is Fiasco which started, I think, with a standard theoretical five act structure borrowed from literary theory, but due to playtesting made the final act not quite vestigial (and it's now arguably three acts).

Am I onto something? And if so where are the other desire lines for building shared fiction; the ones we aren't using?

    \nI actually checked this with Vincent Baker a while back. The look of AW may be trad, but the decision as to when to roll is quite purposely matched to when the handover in narration in freeform RP (and especially conflict resolution like Rock/Paper/Scissors) would happen. So it's a trad RPG (laid paths) with the paths deliberately built where freeform RPers would walk (the moves being used only where there are handovers or things like rock/paper/scissors in freeform). And therefore causes as little disruption to freeform as is possible because the parts where you tangle with the rules are where freeform would be disrupted anyway.

    This, incidentally, is why I consider DW (and a lot of the other hacks) to have missed the target. They miss this aspect and e.g. start including questions that you wouldn't directly ask in freeform ("What happened here recently?")

    \n I'm not sure how one gets from observing desire lines to designing a good game.

    Certainly the D&D example is iterative -- its rules don't just manage what people would do freeform; they manage what people do in response to other D&D rules. So starting with a solid mechanical core as a starting point, and then following desire lines might be good for fleshing out a design.

    If you start from freeform, though, and observe "this seems to be what people want to do", I'm not sure what you do with that information. I believe some designers' response is "what you do is stay out of the way until there's a problem", with such problems either being player-to-player (you and I can't just agree on what happens) or group-to-fiction (we can agree, but what we agree on winds up being weak). The desire lines are implicit in that recurring problems also signal recurring interests, but the desire lines aren't the focus in and of themselves.

    Maybe the chance of not being caught, chance

    In brief:
    \u2022\tChance is an everyday word used in a situation where we are talking about an event taking place whereas probability is a precise measurement of that chance
    \u2022\tProbability is a special branch of mathematics that helps people decide the percentage of likelihood of an event taking place whereas chances of an event taking place in daily life are merely opinions.

    One desire line I feel is between opinions and chance or narration moving away from probability.

    Not saying this good and better just possibly unexceptable like cutting across the grass.
