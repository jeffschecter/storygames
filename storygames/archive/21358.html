\n Hi, I wrote a little game about relationships on the brink. What I want it to do is explore the area where we think we understand how our partners feel but we often misinterpret what they are telling or showing us. Any feedback is greatly appreciated. Here's the game:

Relationship Brink
By Johnathon Grach

After a fight you and your partner are having an \u201cI remember when\u201d conversation talking about the good and bad things you\u2019ve gone through together.

1d6
Negative story
1 = -3
2 = -2
3 = -1
Positive story
4 = +1
5 = +2
6 = +3
ALL ROLLS ARE PRIVATE.

First player roll 1d6. Write it down.

Tell an \u201cI remember when\u201d story and make it more or less severe based on the result.

Second player respond however you like. Write down what you think the other player rolled.

Second player then roll and tell your own \u201cI remember when\u201d story.

First player respond however you like and write down what you think the other player rolled.

Tell 3 stories each. Add your results and the guessed results of the other player (6 numbers total). If the result is positive you want to stay in, negative you want out and zero choose either way.

First player say what you want, second player respond. If you agree then that is what it is.

If you disagree you can make one last plea and reiterate any story told. The other player can change their mind or not and you must accept it.

    \n I left it wide open in the sense of the type of relationship. It could be friendship or a more intimate relationship.

    I would suggest using stories that didn't happen with the person you are playing with. I feel it is in the telling of the experience that we try to judge how that person feels about it and we can still do that with a fictional story.

    Using real memories between the two players might be too stressful based on how the ending is supposed to play. You don't want some dice rolls telling you how to manage your relationships.

    Take away the ending I've written and using real memories between the two players might be cathartic and there might be some insight into your relationship, I don't know though. I don't want to suggest there is more there than there actually is.

    I think there could be something there for kids and friendship but again without the current ending. I would also suggest maybe only negative stories on a 1 and maybe 2. I would want it to be more positive for kids. I wouldn't take the negative out though. Kids often have negative experiences that they would like to tell.

    \nI mostly agree.

    The deck of 2 x 2 x (card values 1-3) is great for replacing the rolling.
    But the guessing part of the story, and the math at the end game, still handles roughly.

    In fact, I wonder about this bit, and not just because the math seems wrong (3 stories each = 6 x (1 roll each story + 1 guess each) = 12 rolls+guesses in 6 stories):\n
    killertick said:

    Tell 3 stories each. Add your results and the guessed results of the other player (612 numbers total). If the result is positive [then] you want to stay in; negative, you want out; and zero, choose either way.

    \nWhy would I "want out" just because the cards--in the worst-case scenario--came up -3, -3, -2, -2, -1, -1 and we guessed each others draws perfectly?
    (That is, the sum of those is a whopping -24... and so in spite of being completely "on the same page" WRT finding a rough time/story and guessing the roughness... we "want out" because of the final-score resolution rule?)

    I think the absolute value of the numeric spread between drawn-card value and guessed value is more to the point:

    * Draw a card, then tell a story that reflects the card's relative positivity or negativity.

    * Your partner then tries to guess what you drew, and writes it down.

    * Each of you tell 3 stories so that, when done, you have 6 pairs of draws + guesses between the 2 of you.

    * For each draw + guess pair, determine the difference between the highest integer and the lowest.
    ** Example: +2 draw, +1 guess = +2-(+1) = 2-1 = 1.
    ** Example: -3 draw, -1 guess = -1-(-3) = -1+3 = 2.
    ** Example: +2 draw, -2 guess = +2-(-2) = 2+2 = 4.

    * Add up the end totals: The larger the result, the less in tune you are with each other.

    [I leave as an exercise for the designer, determining the maximum possible result given the potential range of bad guesses... but it's huge. Obviously, a result of 0 means you are totally psychic, share a hivemind, or are able to read each other like books. ]
