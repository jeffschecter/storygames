    \n Yeah, having been in a number of those threads, I feel ya, Ry.

    The flip side is that reading those threads, I do get a pretty good impression of where the System Doesn't Matter folks are coming from.

    System Doesn't Matter
    In this stand system only means game mechanics. These game mechanics are tools which cover way more than any group will likely ever use. The GM is the master craftsman, who will select which of those tools too use, filtered through a lens of Best Practices. Some tools will be used more frequently than others. The GM, as Master Craftsman will occasionally alter or add tools as they see fit depending on their own set of best practices. Best practices come from both GM advice (perhaps appearing in said toolkit rulebook or other sources) and a feedback loop of response due to the reaction of the other participants.

    System Matters
    The selection of tools + selection of best practices is what the Actual System is in play. This is different from system=mechanics alone. If you were to examine a group's use of best practices + mechanics and the frequecy with which they were used, you could isolate them, formailze them, and write them down.

    Why is this good?
    You could make a vastly more focussed text, which would then allow you to share just that. You could cut right to the heart of a given sort of play experience and come closer to replicating it between groups.

    Why is this bad?
    Added responsibility to learn multiple rule-kits for different effects, with burden for this being shared out more amongst the play group.

    As for folks knowing what they like, per Jason's post, I believe that folks absolutely know what they like.

    For the vast majority of character players, what they like is participating in a kind of verbal-costume party where the person hosting and organizing the party has put some limits on the theme of the verbal-costumes the invitees can create and has a bag of party theme related games to play and party theme related activities to particpate in.

    If the themed verbal costume party goes really well, they want to do it it again and again. Their verbal costumes will become more evolved and intricate, and they expect the party host to likewise make the party themed activities and games more involved or challenging and more geared to their evolving verbal costume.

    What's in it for the Party Host: Dude, you have people who love the parties you throw! They love it so much that they keep begging you to throw more! And they're really into it, even suggesting ideas to you by the way they constantly evolve their costumes.

    The downside? Most of them will never, ever bother to throw a party of their own. For some, it's because they are just plain having too much fun at yours. For others, especially those who join later parties, they're just plain intimidated by what they see and don't realize just how basic things really start out. They're also likely intimidated by the thought of trying to please the other party-goers who regularly attend your intricate shindigs.

    Posted By: RyWhen someone asserts something like 'System doesn't matter, it's all about the GM.'
    \nI would assert that what people are saying when they say this is...

    Gestalt Matters.

    The right group, the right social contract, the right game. That's what matters. Not arguing over terminology and voice and narrativism vs. storytelling, etc. For most people, role playing game means adventure game. People can't differentiate between those two terms because they are consumers first. Not connoisseurs first. They're not trained to (nor should they be).

    System, to most gamers is "what kind of die do I roll." It has nothing to do with playing a game that feels like the world/story you are trying to invoke, i.e. Game X playing the way Game X is supposed to play and Game Y playing the way Game Y is supposed to play. People play Game A, the same way the play Game B. It just has different monsters and different treasure types. (Yes, that's overly contrived.)

    But to illustrate my point\u2026

    Scott Haring said something amazing once about a book he was reviewing. "Oh look, it's another world setting where they've changed the word "sword" to something I can't pronounce." I thought, then. And I still think it now, that this is the most important statement about the first 30 years of RPGs. Companies that are still make products like the "Lost World" books aren't seeing anything but the trees. And people arguing about syntax and system are only seeing the forest.

    To use the above Cthulhu example, so long as the game play is about stemming the tide of madness, it doesn't matter what "system" you use. But as soon as I check for traps before rolling initiative against a byake and a mi-go both inside my zone of attacks of opportunity, I'm not playing Cthulhu anymore. Given the GM, even the old Chaosium version can be tooled to play this way.

    Sorry. That got a little preachy.

    \n When most people talk about system they mean specifically the game engine, the written rules (excluding fluff, setting, stories, whatever).

    Redefining it is fine and all, but if you talk to people using that (frankly fairly intuitive) definition using some other definition you can't sensibly expect a meaningful conversation.

    If I say system isn't that important, replying by talking about how people interact at the table is simply a non-sequitur. All too often the system matters crowd are really arguing for a redefinition of terms, and I just don't see any value in that.

    Usually therefore when I see someone argue that system matters I just move on. It's not worth my time. If the statement is that the actual rules used may impact play, that's a truism. If the statement is more than that it's usually a redefinition of the word system to something other than the normal usage, and I don't find semantics particularly helpful.

    As a general rule, I assume that when people say something is true for them it is in fact true for them. So, when someone says that system doesn't matter I assume that's true for them. If I found that surprising I might try to learn why it was true, but I wouldn't assume that I was better at saying what matters to someone than they are themselves. If someone tells me they can immerse in character while playing Dogs (as someone once did) I assume that's true for them. I can't see how you could do that, but they're not me and I don't assume my truth is theirs.

    The problem with the original post is it assumes the people speaking are wrong in what they say. I think that assumption ends conversations. If you assume they're right in what they say, that opens them.
