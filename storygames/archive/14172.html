\n I made up this little conflict resolution system for "freeform" play, which is something I'm kind of interested in right now. It's for resolving situations where one character is trying to make another character do something, and they're arguing about it.

Making people do what you want

If you want to change someone's mind about something, the only thing to do is just talk to them. They either come to see it your way or they don't. It's up to their player.

But if you just want to make someone do something, and you don't care how they feel about doing it, then you can use these rules.

When you ask or tell someone to do something, and they refuse, choose something from the following list and do it. They can choose to either suck it up and do it, or choose something higher on the list. You can go back and forth for as long as someone is willing or able to do something higher on the list.

Wheedle or plead
Make promises
Raise your voice
Insult or demean them
Threaten consequences
Invoke a family relationship
Invoke your own authority
Invoke someone else's authority
Call in a favour or a promise
Bring up history
Bring up secrets

Shove or grab
Throw a punch
All-out fighting
Go for a weapon
Go for a gun

    \n We had the same problem when we used similar lists in a game we were playtesting called Blackout.

    The list were too long and broke up the flow of play. But we loved those lists, how detailed and flavorful they were. And they were especially great for some new players who needed more inspiration than general categories.

    This is how we solved it...

    - Take the list and make it a singular play sheet everyone shares.
    - When people use an item from the list, they check it off.
    - No one can use a checked item until all the items have been checked. Then the list refreshes.

    Later on we included check boxes next to each list item. So if we wanted specific items to be used multiple times, we simply added more check boxes next to it.

    It worked incredibly well. Playtested with over 40 people. I've since used this technique in 2 other game designs to great effect.

    Posted By: jenskotWhat are your goals?
    \nGood question! I dunno!

    I guess that where I'm coming from is that, while I find freeform (whatever that means) really interesting, and potentially a fruitful design space, I also find it kind of difficult. I like playing out conversations and arguments, but I often find them kind of interminable and pointless when they're not tied to a resolution system. I wanted to strike a balance between "just say what your character would say" and a resolution system that gives direction and structure to a roleplayed conversation.

    There should be a kind of dialogue between what you do and the list. You say what your character does, and you look at the list to see how people react to that. But of course, you also think "How can I get him to do this?" and look at the list for the answer.

    Ideally, the list should cover pretty much anything that you could do to get someone to do what you want. To achieve that and also keep the list specific enough to provide inspiration, and not to allow too much wiggle room is a challenge though. I tend to think that's a surmountable challenge. It's possible I could reduce the number of things on the list by incorporating some related things, for example. "History" and "Secrets" could be one item, for example, as could the two kinds of authority.

    In the end, I think the specifics of the list would depend heavily on the specific game you were playing, and what's important there.

    David,

    You have me pretty much correct. Especially about the escalation thing. Have you been following this thread?

    But it's not just about reminding people to escalate. It's about tying actions in the fiction to assured results. If you want to "threaten consequences", it has to be a credible threat. That means that every time you threaten, your reputation is on the line. If everyone knows you won't follow through, it's not a credible threat. It means that every promise you make in the fiction is loaded with the powerful hold it gives people over you. Unless your promises become worthless because you never keep them. It means that when you walk into a room carrying a gun, we all know what that makes you capable of.

    "Undistracted attention to the fiction" is a good thing, and I think for some people it's the main thing. But for me, the fiction feels weaksauce without that concrete assurance that actions have meaning and consequences, that things are building towards something.

Otherwise, you'll have players who just jump to the top of the list, shoot everybody, and are like "What? Your game is broke!"

    You'd just need to add fallout rules like:

    After someone calls, the GM makes some moves:
    -- if you're just talking, one or more of these (somebody's mad, somebody's humilitated, somebody's vindicated).
    -- if you're getting physical, one or more of these or anything above (somebody's hurt, somebody's betrayed).
    -- if you're fighting, one or more of these or anything above (somebody's injured).
    -- if you're shooting, one or more of these or anything above (somebody's dying).

    If you're the somebody, change your descriptive tags to match your recent experiences. If you won the conflict, whatever the GM tells you, it can't be worse than what happens to the other side, though occasionally it can be the same.

    How's that? See, freeform design isn't so hard.

    \nSimon: I don't know about "how it should be." That's just the first thing I came up with and it really depends on what's happening in the rest of the game. Maybe there's not a GM! Here's a more Bliss Stage / BW inspired version:

    After someone calls, the players not directly involved answer some questions:
    -- Did the character show signs of growing as a person? (If so, mark XP).

    -- Was the conflict potentially injurious to one or both parties? (If so, blah blah fallout).
    -- Did the conflict have the potential to spill over and affect others? (If so, blah blah fallout).

    \nChris: Basically, they're relatively sophisticated resolution or structuring mechanics that don't involve dice or other randomizers (i.e. Fortune), just the emergent results that happen when a bunch of players make different choices or provide various kinds of input. Emily Care and I (plus some others) have been calling these kinds of mechanics "structured freeform" since about 2006 or so. There was even a dedicated forum for a while. The Danish scenario tradition (Montsegur 1244) and Nordic larp/jeep stuff (The Upgrade) is also "structured freeform" but from a different design/play tradition. Lately, since some people have been confused by the term or want to use it more narrowly to describe something else (like Vincent does in his post), I've been learning more towards "deterministic resolution" as a label. It doesn't roll off the tongue quite the same way, but it's probably more descriptive of what these mechanics are about.
