\n One of the things that lead me into Indie games was becoming disenchanted with the large use of numbers and stats in Trad RPGs. I made some stat heavy RPGs and noticed how the numbers got in the way of simulating the world. They became static rather than real information. A dialogue between players seemed like a better approach. After going down that path a short way I noticed that it changed the way I thought. I was looking more at what could possibly happen and which option I wanted to pick rather than worrying about the most probably (i.e. most likely to pass a task resolution roll).

Opening up people's way of thinking to possibilities rather than probabilities is one of the biggest gifts that Indie games can give. I would like to see if other people see this and/or think that it is also a big deal.

I'll throw out an opener: Dungeon World

By saying up front that it is a conversation between the GM and players, and making dice rolls very simple when they happen, and that they might not even be needed, the game pulls our thoughts away from numbers and onto actions and consequences. The neat thing is that DW just codifies what I always did in running D+D anyway. We ignored most of the numbers and focused on having fun.

So please tell your game observations but more explore how/if this leads to better story games.

Chris Engle

    \n I'm quite interested in probabilities versus possibilities and it's a subject I often think about when constructing a game, in the form of questions like why make that random? or what happens if the probabilities produce outcomes of this specific type?

    Part of this stems from the growth of RPGs from their strategic gaming roots, with their min-maxing and careful advance planning before any action. I much prefer jumping in feet first and will often work against my character's agenda, because it creates a more interesting and dramatic story if they fail: this isn't well supported by systems that assume you always want your character to succeed.

    One very eye-opening game is Ron Edward's Elfs: it's a traditional fantasy game, with some tongue-in-cheek comedy elements that are supported and encouraged by the game's mechanics. You can play it straight and say what your character tries to achieve, but you can also add a meta-layer to your narration by saying "That's what my character wants to happen, but what's going to happen is..." Doing so gives you an extra die to roll, thus raising your chances of success, but it makes that success a result of circumstances beyond the character's control, instead of their own expressed intents and actions.

    \n My observation is that the "RPG scene" has been moving towards rules-light story games for two reasons:
    1. A lot of us who used to play D&D in school/college now have a job, family and other responsibilities.
    2. Computer-based and online RPGs have drawn a lot of people who are into stats and simulationist/tactical play.
    It has left the Pen&Paper community with people who enjoy telling a story together.

    For game rules shifting from simulationist mechanics to story mechanics, here is an example:
    - in traditional RPG, the character would spend his gold coins on a bazar based on a price list from the rule book.
    - in i.e. Fate, the player makes a "Resource" roll. Based on the result, the players roleplay their characters accordingly.

    In story-based mechanics, you don't need rules to simulate the whole game world. You're allowed to make up what you want within a certain game framework. This gives games the freedom to have a lighter, more abstract rule-set that can be unified, combined, stacked etc.
    Example:
    Cortex Plus Dramatic Roleplaying (Smallville) gives you attributes for i.e. "Values", "Relationship" and "Assets". You could save your colleague John using:
    - Value "Justice": D8
    - Relationship "Colleague John": D6
    - Asset "Super-Speed": D10
    So you bring these three attributes together in one action, throwing three dice D8+D6+D10 together.
    No way you could do something like that with a traditional more simulationist RPG.

    \n JDCorley, the difference between probability and possibility, isn't that they speak to different things. They both deal with what happens in the game. The difference is that one focuses play on an algorithm (usually expressed with numbers) which can have little to do with the interesting bits of story while the other focuses on bits, trying to find out which are interesting.

    What probability does well is limit and hyper focus play. You know what you have to do. This makes it much easier to teach. Possibility focus is so wide open that it can easily lose focus. This makes it hard to teach.

    Mechanics that both limit play but keep the focus on the wide open excit me. Picking the elements in Fiasco, and the limited bits I've heard about Storium, do this without a focus on an algorithm.

    Games that lead to mini maxing are fun for me in Euro Games but bore me in role playing. I don't think my preferences really matter, but do inform my work.

    Love what people are coming up with!

    \n I don't think the two are mutually exclusive ideas at all. Even heavily mechanical/dicey games don't necessarily restrict story possibilities, though they may take greater expertise and/or finesse to prepare for. I came here to these forums because for almost thirty years my gaming groups have been focused on the stories rather than the numbers. However, we tell those stories primarily in very numbers-heavy games (D&D, Shadowrun, etc).

    For us the numbers part is about simulation of reality in that it (supposedly) reduces personal bias in the resolution of conflict. Some games do this well, others not so well. Generally speaking our groups prefer not to know how things are going to come out. Most of us like to 'play in the DMs world', allowing stories to be revealed to us in the same way a reader experiences the author's stories, or moviegoer's experience the screenwriter/director's stories. While we enjoy the limited control of choosing our responses within that world (or maybe even developing unique subplots on our own), rather it works or not isn't up to us sometimes; it's actually that facet that attracts us to those games. In that way they're more like a choose your own adventure than a fully open narrative, though rather than being fully pre-determined the dice (or whatever) use probability to pick.

    What I'm getting at here is that there's no inherent tie between the level or type of mechanics/diceyness, and possibilities of or for storytelling. They're merely different forms of games...not better or worse, but different.

    \n Splitting off onto another point, that of math and complexity.

    Just because there are dice (or cards, or coins, or whatever) doesn't necessarily mean there needs to be stats, derived numbers, math, or so on. We developed our MandE system precisely because some of our group love the randomness of traditional RPGs, but dislike too many numbers or complex formulas. We just tune probability with the number and type of dice to garner a single result against a table, rather than bonuses and penalties to derived numbers. Likewise we found that we could do without attributes altogether, at least in our superhero game.

    The system also focuses on the idea of unintended consequences and the relation between potential power and the results for good or ill. In this way it achieves an enormous quality of simulationism by focusing on narrative elements with an economy of rolling and almost no math. Just goes to show that there's no inherent possibility/probability dichotomy.

    So this could go both ways. Can you share more of how it works on the flip side?
    \nSure. It's as simple as the difference between deciding "is this possible, or interesting?" and "how probable, exactly, is this thing over that thing?"

    You make a great point about the failure of wide-open possibility: it can paralyze people. But "possibility" doesn't have to be wide-open. For example, I might say, "Character X fails miserably in this situation. Player C, tell us how that comes about!" Or, "but his actions have an unintended consequence. What might that be?"

    The coupling of constraint to possibility gives an easy instrument to play with.

    In comparison, trying to figure out how *probable* it is that Character X fails, and how that might come about, and whether it might have unintended consequences, can very difficult. Is it a 50% chance of failure, and a 20% chance of failure because of his lack of skill, but a 30% chance of failure because of his lack of courage, and a 40% chance of failure because his opponent is stronger than him? And a 37% chance of unintended consequences? Not obvious questions. Should that be a -4 because he's clearly not having a good day today, or?

    In the first case, all you need is one (or more) ideas that *could* fit the situation or produce interesting play. In the second, you need to consider ALL the possible outcomes and then rank them in order of probability (which is sometimes a nearly impossible task - well, how often DOES it rain in the supernatural desert caused by a demonic curse?). In comparison, if we have a game mechanic which tells us that "on a failed roll, the weather turns sour", that leaves the player with only the need to provide some colour or flavour to that outcome. She doesn't need to know how *likely* it is to rain, she just needs to decide whether it's *possible*, and then she can go ahead and describe it.

    \nSure.

    Hero #1 is armed with a roman candle firework. Should he hit or miss with it, the results will be fairly small.
    Hero #2 is armed with a military flamethrower. Should he hit or miss with it, the results will be fairly large.

    In game terms Hero #1 will roll a d4, while Hero #2 will roll a d10.

    Now picture a simple table, numbered 1-10 (actually the FIAT is 1-20, but I'm simplifying for this example). On that table all even numbers are success, and all odd numbers are failure. The impact of success or failure increases with the number. Hence a 3 or 4 does very little, while a 9 or 10 does quite a lot. Either hero may hit or miss, and both may end up doing very little (by rolling low), but only the flamethrower stands a chance at having great impact directly (by rolling high).

    I used a combat example, but it holds for any test.

    We wanted a system that accounted for the dangers, as well as the boons, of wielding great power. We did that through Magnitude (ie higher sides on the die = higher table results = bigger impact), and a reduced base chance of success (50% at the absolute stripped down minimum).

    We also wanted it to happen without multiple rolls, derived numbers, heavy math, excessive modifiers, etc...but to retain adjustable probability. We did that by using the set table with a variable number of dice of variable sides, instead of one die with variable modifiers against a variable target.

    Finally we wanted the focus on narrative. We accomplished this through consideration of unintended consequences (tied in to the powers and skills themselves during creation, and again at use) and something I haven't even mentioned yet (Encounter Balance) that gets a bit too complicated for this, but the overall point is that the narrative objectively impacts the outcome in the same way the dice randomly impact the outcome.

    Here's the example we use to intro the game:

    Imagine the following scene from a comic book:

    A superhero who transforms into metal is walking with another that can emit flames. The two are near the beach when a villain strikes at the resort ahead, destroying a pillar which supports the overhang. The first hero shifts form and leaps to stop the collapse of the arched entrance. The second hero envelopes herself in flames and runs to the dunes to distract the villain and protect the fleeing citizens.

    Seems straightforward enough. Now let\u2019s look at the same scene as it would be intended to play out in a Stupid Heroes game session.

    Steelboy declares he is shifting form. Unfortunately for him they were walking on an old wooden boardwalk at a beach where the salt and humidity rot wood faster than reality television rots your brain. Now weighing several hundred pounds more than he did the hero crashes through the planks and sinks calf-deep in the soft sand underneath.

    Cinder-ella flames on with a lot of adrenaline and good intentions. This ignites the wood she was standing on, creating a potential funeral pyre around her companion. If she\u2019s burning hot enough she may be turning the sand into glass fields, fragile and very sharp. Speaking of temperatures, how is it that she can surround herself in flames without being burned, or at least using up the oxygen she needs to breathe?

    However the battle between \u2018good and evil\u2019 pans out there could be Hell to pay once it was over. Will the city want payment for the ruined boardwalk? Will the bad guy be acquitted since the heroes weren\u2019t sworn law enforcement officers? Does a father become an anti-superhero vigilante when his daughter is injured on the flame-created glass dunes?

    \n Something that I thought of when I read the original post was that it was a topic about emergence. When you can't predict the outcome because of the number of possibilities. Emergence is created through making it possible that several parts in a system can interact with each other. A conversation can create emergence, where different persons in a room are parts of a system. Abilities that can be combined can create emergence. Instead of having four abilities, if they all can be combined with each other you get (4\xd73\xd72\xd71=) 24 different ways of using them. If you only can combine two of them, you still have (4*(4-1)/2=) six different ways of using them. Numbers, by themselves, can't create this kind of synergy where Y+X only results in a different number. Sure, probabilities can make the end result uncertain by saying it's a 60 % chance to succeed, but possibilities is about obscuring what choices the others will do and therefor make the end result unpredictable.

    When I'm talking about flattening out a rule system, I'm talking about the latter.

    \n From a RPGs as Language perspective:

    "probability" lets you easily communicate how likely you think an event is - then the effort or depth you spend toward changing that likelihood communicates what you or your character cares about.

    "possibility" lets you directly communicate your interest and what you or your character cares about.

    So if you want to easily communicate what matters to you or your character "possibility" works better. If you want to more elaborately communicate how and why it matters, "probability" gives you tools for that. Certainly there are other ways to do this, but it seems like these approaches trade-off between speed and depth of characterization.
