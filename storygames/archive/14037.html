\n Last night after nerdnyc's season premiere of the gotham gaming guild, many of us attended:

D20 Burlesque presents A Night of Role Playing (Games)

Anja (a member of nerdnyc and also vegan!) put everything together, including working with our local gaming store to gather prizes for a raffle.

The show was fantastic and everyone was having a great time but when she revealed the prizes... people booed!

Several of the prizes included over a hundred dollars worth of D&D 4E books. Which didn't make the crowd happy. There was a strong Pathfinder vibe representing! People were much more interested in the $5 blow up D20 balloon than anything to do with D&D 4E, even though they could make a profit selling the books on Ebay. It was simultaneously hilarious and completely caught me off guard.

One thing about the show that I found endearing, everyone involved from the performers to the host to the announcer were much more tabletop RPG nerds than RPG video gamers. They included a few video game references to have a wider appeal but it was hilarious to be in a situation where video games were more alien than D&D. Their next show is going to be boardgame themed!

    \n Unrelated but another strange event from last night...

    Red Bull has a marketing team of women who drive around in a Red Bull car and costumes scouring the internet for events to market Red Bull to. Somehow nerdnyc's gotham gaming guild event came up on their search and they walked into the theater rehearsal space we host our games and started marketing and distributing samples of Red Bull. It was bizarre. But the weirdest moment is when one of them said...

    "Wow, you actually know all those rules to these games... Red Bull will help you concentrate and game better."

    \n Also, not trying to boost up JDcorley's ego but at the GGG we had a few players who quit playing RPGs regularly after D&D 4E came out and started playing again last night.

    4 different people who don't know each other and spoke to me independently effectively said...

    D&D was a toolkit, it didn't tell you how to play, so your group could decide or support multiple play styles. There was always tension between the story / talking in character / drama gamers and the tactical / minis / minmaxing / combat all the time gamers. But even though it was bumpy, we all tried to get along. But when D&D 4E came out, it said, the way the tactical / minis / minmaxing / combat all the time gamers play D&D is now the official way to play. And many gaming groups broke apart because people stopped being flexible and started saying, "there is only one way to play."

    That said, they like many small hyper focused indie games even though they said they aren't presented as toolkits because it is a smaller investment. You can learn the rules in a day and play just a hand full of sessions. They are great to play in-between playing other games. And in the short term, focus was better because it meant faster play. But for longer term games, they wanted more flexibility. Especially because in longer term games they couldn't guarantee who was going to be around to play and what their play style is.

    I'm not saying I agree with the above but just wanted to note how many different people were saying the same things.
