\n In the 30s thread, Jason said\n
Posted By: Jason MorningstarEvery time is fascinating and game-worthy, especially the times no one pays any attention to.
\nWhich made me think about how neat it would be to figure out the fascinating and game-worthy aspects of various decades. Decades being chosen as a convenient way to talk about discrete periods of time, though of course events tend not to fall neatly within them as well as we'd like...

So whats a decade you know something about? Whats the fun and game-worthy stuff in it?

Oh, oh, the 1820s! In the US, you have Andrew Jackson denied the presidency by the House of Representatives 1824, despite winning the popular vote, guiding him to take helm of reshaping the Democratic party and creating a populist legacy that sticks with us to this day. Then he did get elected in 1828. Also, the Eerie Canal was opened. In England, the first public railroad (Stockton & Darlington railway) opened. Also, apparently the first still photograph was taken sometime in this decade. Also, Mexico declared it's independence from Spain, and Greece fought it's war of independence from the Ottomans.

Game ideas:

You play Representatives in the 1824 election, possibly campaigning for the candidates, and then having to make a case for your guy to try and sway the House. Considering that this was an era when politicians still fought duels with pistols, I think this sounds fun.

I'm willing to bet that the opening of the Eerie Canal contained a lot of drama.

I know nothing about it, but I bet something set in the Greek war of independence would be fascinating.

    \n Ignoring the Next Up thing and going back to decades we like...

    1910 to 1920 is the most extraordinary decade. At the start, there was conspicuous wealth: the upper classes lived in houses with servants, eating astonishingly sumptuous meals. We tend to forget this time, lumping it in with the 1920s, but the start of the decade was equally interesting.

    Then the Titanic sunk.

    Soon afterwards, Europe decided to send all its young kids to kill each other. Millions died in a strange, pointless war. It was the Great War, the war to end all wars: and, of course, the way it ended ensured another war within 20 years.

    Finally, from 1918 to 1920, there was the Spanish Flu epidemic. 50 million died, making it arguably the biggest killer in history. Young, healthy people were more likely to die than anyone else, as the flu turned their immune systems against them.
