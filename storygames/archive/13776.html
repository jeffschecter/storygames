\n I've got the Smallville RPG. I adore the Smallville RPG. I think it's a brilliant and thoroughly intriguing game.

But I have yet to play the Smallville RPG. Likely as not, if I were to play the Smallville RPG, there would be some constraints on what I could and could not do, simply from a realistic point of view. So I'm interested in hearing how Smallville functions with regard to a few specific questions.

(1) Can Smallville accommodate a shifting group of players, where not everyone is always there for every single session? My impression is that the answer is no; every player is playing a character of such importance that it would feel awkward to not have everyone there every session. But then, maybe it's the equivalent of having episodes which are spotlighting certain characters, and not others?

(2) Does Smallville function in a PbP form? I know there's the chapter in the book that specifically talks about online play, but I'm hoping to hear from people who have some experience with it, actually attesting to good times.

    \n"Vanilla" Smallville is highly reliant on strong relationships between PCs driving much of the action. In practice, in a group of 6 (say) players will have 30 relationships between them. Not all of them will be lighting up the table. The danger of a subgroup showing up is that you might have a plurality of not-so-hot Relationships, and things will drag .

    You might also have some trouble with the normal episode creation guidelines, which ask you to create situations based on strains and stresses in relationships between the leads - if you create a situation that strains the people who don't show, it won't have traction.\n

    Posted By: Brendan Conway(1) Can Smallville accommodate a shifting group of players, where not everyone is always there for every single session?
    \nI'm tempted to say no.

    Why:

    1. Your relationships to the other players is central to the game. When you make a roll, you roll 1 of your Relationships and 1 of your Values. If the roll isn't about a Value or Relationship, the game suggests it shouldn't be a roll.

    2. The relationship map you create at the start of the game is an important resource for all the players and the GM. It helps the GM prep for the games and it ensures the players are interconnected.

    3. Smallville is a somewhat GM prep heavy game. Less than D&D but still considerable. The GM also stats up important NPCs in almost as much details as the PCs themselves. There is plenty of work involved and because it all centers around challenging Relationships, new players would 1 have to have Relationships featured in the game and 2 would need to make their character before the next game to give the GM enough time to prep.

    Possible solutions:

    1. Have players who drop in and out play the featured NPCs. They usually have a full character sheet and they don't have to be too interconnected into the existing relationships as their purpose is to challenge and drive wedges between relationships.

    2. There is an option to have episodes (sessions) centered more around Values than Relationships. I would imagine Value centered episodes would be easier to incorporate rotating characters.

    \n Do you have a core group who are likely to always be at the table, rather than occasionally be absent? If so, these can be your "all 22 episodes" Leads while the others are your "13 episodes a season" Leads. It happens on the show, and the game can support it.
    \n
    \nRelationships aren't just with the other Leads. They're also there for important recurring Features. If you find you're down a couple of players, so long as your supporting cast is strong enough to give you some flexibility with who to frame scenes around you're not going to be too disadvantaged.
    \n
    \nRemember, as Watchtower you have a lot of power over who shows up to a scene and who doesn't. As long as you have at least three or four players at the table, the game won't be thrown off by much, if at all.

    \n I think it's probably easier to get around not having everyone at every session if:

    - you have a "core group" like CamBanks describes above, or

    - you reach a good episodic stopping point at the end of each session (so that the next session isn't affected if one of the important Leads from that storyline is absent), or

    - you have multiple storylines going and a cheerfully lax view of continuity (so that you can pause stories involving the missing Leads and move forward with other ones).

    But I do think that everyone who's playing needs to participate in the character creation part, and they need to have a reasonable idea of how available they will be for the game. Everyone's going to need to put a little extra thought into their Relationships and which other parts of the relationship map are shared among multiple characters, asking questions like "If this character is absent from the game, do I have enough other Relationships left to feel okay about playing?" and "Can we still do a scene in the Fortress of Solitude if Superman's not around, or does that kind of miss the whole point of having a scene there?" You'll have to think about how the map -- and consequently what kinds of stories your game will support -- will change when you drop someone off of it, and whether that fits with what you all want.

    And maybe some of the answers you find will tell you that, no, you really can't run the game you want without a stable group, in which case you shouldn't. Or maybe you can, with some effort: something like the Mission bolt-on JDCorley did might help in a situation like this, for example.

    \n My thinking falls in line largely with what Cam says. Get all of the players there at the beginning to do character creation. Make sure that those players all have strong relationships with Features. A couple of good Locations will probably help as well, that way there can be a thematic tie to an absent character by having scenes take place in their Location. Encourage players that won't be able to make every session to choose Pathways stuff like "Sidekick", that way they'll get strong supportive elements to their character stats.

    I would say that, given the Growth system, players should try to miss no more than two sessions in a row. Otherwise those characters may start to fall behind a bit in scale to the other PCs and may start to get beat up pretty badly by the larger Trouble Pools that more advanced characters generate.

    Also, when you don't have your whole group there, do your episode prep using Values instead of Relationships. Using this you'll still have really strong wedges between the characters, but you'll be stressing different parts of those characters.

    \n Lots of great points so far. I really like getting all the players together for the character creation session and then letting characters drop in and out with a small consistent core cast. And rotating between Value and Relationship focused episodes depending on who is available to play.

    Michael makes good point that due to the Growth system, you may have players start to fall behind power wise.

    You could use the advice regarding upgrading Features so that they don't fall behind on page 69:\n
    Be sure to mark on each Feature\u2019s character sheet the number of each episode they appear in. All the Features you introduce in the first episode should be marked with a 1; any Feature that appears in episode five gets a 5.

    To make your Features a match for the Leads, you may need to step up a few of their Traits. A rule of thumb is to step up a Trait approximately every three episodes\u2014one Trait for episodes 1-3, another for episodes 4-6, etc. You get the idea. So if your Feature last appeared in episode 3 and it\u2019s now episode 8, step up the Feature\u2019s Traits by 2\u2014either one Trait by two steps or two Traits by one. If a Feature seems underpowered, add another Trait. Whatever feels right to you is probably fine.
    \nMissing players could describe quickly in flashback where they've been and what happened that allowed them to step up a trait.
