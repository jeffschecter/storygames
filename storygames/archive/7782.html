\n So I'm at my university's board games/roleplaying games society meeting. we've been playing a mixture of board and card games up until now, and it's been fun. I suggest around 8.45pm that we should play InSpectres, and I get four people to agree to do so, although one of them seems a bit doubtful. I'd overheard him ahaving a conversation earlier about some horrifically dysfunctional roleplaying experiences he'd had previously.

Meanwhile, there's another group of guys who are doing set up and pre-chat for a planned D&D campaign. They seem to mostly be talking about what kind of characters they will eventually make. It's reasonably quiet over there, and remains so throught our InSpectres game.

InSpectres goes well, as it always does, even if it is dominated ratehr by the more voluble members of the play group, something I try to compensate for by prompting the two newer, shyer players into action at various points. It's high energy, silly, and we can NOT. STOP. LAUGHING. At one point, a buxom Irishwoman character gets a 3 on her Academics roll to reattach someone's testicles, just removed due to an accident with a shovel. Since it's a 3, I ask the player to suggest how they failed, but with a beneficial side effect. He suggests "well, maybe she manages to reattach them somewhere".

I'm aware throughout this that the D&D guys seem to be watching our table a lot. And, we're having a lot of fun and saying very silly things.

The whole thing is wrapped up, from franchise and character generation to the completion of the mission, in around an hour or so.

At the end, one of the newer guys, the guy who seemed a bit doubtful earlier, is asked if he enjoyed himself. He answers in the affirmative but then says:

"But it wasn't really real roleplaying."

I laugh in a sort-of "agreeing with what I think you mean" sort of way.

But I can't help but also be amused by the contrast between our hour or so of near continual laughter and the D&D guys, talking about planning the chargen needed for playing their real roleplaying.

    \n I assumed he meant that, while the game was fun, he hadn't actually invested in his character in any substantial way for this one-shot game. For some folks, that investment is critical to "roleplaying"- caring about the character, advocating for them, etc.

    It's partly a short-burn vs. long-burn issue- a longer running game gives a player more time to develop and understand their character (or characters), getting into the fine nuances of who they are and how they push and pull the overall story. There's a different sort of pay-off for that investment than the over-the-top hilarity of a short-term game.

    \n I don't really think this is unique to those D&D guys that you seem to have some kind of grudge against. I know quite a number of people who are hardcore role-players and story-gamers, and who prefer long-running D&D/d20 campaigns, and who in fact continue to prefer those over anything else even after trying out and enjoying TMW, DRYH and other indie stuff.

    Yes, they spend long hours discussing their characters before the game, after the game and all the time between the game sessions. Even if they are not laughing constantly, it doesn't mean they are not having fun.
