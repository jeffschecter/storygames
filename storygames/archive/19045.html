NPC Secrets

\n
\n
\n
\n\n2097 \n\n\n
\n
\n\nDecember 2013 \n edited December 2013 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Have the players state clearly what they believe about the NPC (in the relevant, specific area of interest\u2014 for example: \u201cWe believe that Sonya is innocent of the Jewel Theft.\u201d)

Then, make them roll the applicable finding-out skill, such as Empathy. It\u2019s not meant to be a hidden roll. Make sure they know what they roll against (what level of passive or active opposition) before the dice hit the table.

If they succeed, what they believed becomes the truth retroactively. (\u201cSonya was innocent all along.\u201d)
If they fail, the opposite of what they believed becomes the truth retroactively. (\u201cSonya was guilty all along.\u201d)

Take care to not create some contradictory states with conflicting beliefs\u2014every belief should have a single point of truth\u2014a single roll attached to determining whether it is true or opposite-of-true.

The cool thing about this is that you can turn the dial on how much IC/OOC divide you want. To create the hitchcockian \u201cticking bomb\u201d of OOC knowledge that IC doesn\u2019t know, make the roll immediately. To keep the players (and the GM) as much in the dark as the PCs, wait with the roll until it the last relevant moment.

This is a bit of a crosspost from something I just wrote on rpg.net, but it was buried in a thread and I\u2019ve been thinking about it for over a week now, finally finding something that seems cool.

    \n We used to call the technical principle behind this sort of technique "No Myth" at the Forge. As in, instead of a scenario actually existing independent of player observation, anything and everything is up in the air until it has been established in play. GM-less games usually rely on this kind of structure, there not being a GM to retain any authoritative-yet-hidden facts about the setting.

    \nYes, you can do this, but as long as the rolls are made before each new question (about this particular \u201ctruth\u201d).
    What you can\u2019t do is to have one \u201cunresolved truth\u201d about whether or not she stole the jewels, and then add another \u201cunresolved truth\u201d about why she did it.
    Because what if the first truth resolves to: She was innocent.

    So as soon as it becomes necessary to know whether or not an \u201cunresolved truth\u201d is true or false, you have to resolve it. Like if Sonya comes into a position where she can betray the players.

    What you also can do is to create the more specific version from the beginning. \u201cSonya is guilty of the jewel theft because someone made her.\u201d\n
    Or does something prevent this?
    \nThe rule is \u201cyou cannot have an \u2018unresolved truth\u2019 where one of the outcomes contradict the fiction or another \u2018unresolved truth\u2019.\u201d

    Also, some games have stuff like \u201cLet It Ride\u201d or \u201cfour outcomes\u201d in place to discourage this question nesting\u2026 \u201cI push the door \u2014 fail. I take three steps back and run and try to push the door \u2014 fail. I take twenty minutes of rest and then really really try to push the door \u2014 fail.\u201d
    That\u2019s part of the fun of explicit stakes, I think\u2014that you have an interesting failure and an interesting success, and let the dice (and stats) determine which happens.

    \u201cNo Myth\u201d yeah, that\u2019s a cool name. I hadn\u2019t heard the name, but I\u2019ve seen the technique and I was thinking about the various \u201ctruth-finder-outery\u201d skills in Fate Core and to be honest, I had to think a few days to come up with this protocol. I wanted a strict and consistent and easy rule.
    I\u2019m glad that the technique\u2019s been around, of course.

    \n What I wrote first was sort of irrelevant to what you were talking about, sorry.

    For secrets, one thing that I've experimented with is using inductive reasoning; specifically the Bayesian formula. I try to find evidence that supports an event being true and if I can use elements that have already been established, all the better, as it feels less like ret-conning.

    I would try to roll for the likelihood of evidence that Sonya is innocent (maybe find a witness that can place her somewhere else, etc). Or,I might just switch the statement, and try to find evidence that Sonya is guilty. If I fail to find it, then I must conclude that she is innocent.


    I\u2019ve been using something similar to keep some tension in my solo gaming. I sort of thought of it as a \u201cSelf Fulfilling Prophecy\u201d on the part of the character I\u2019m playing. I like to represent it to myself using relationship maps, where each connection has an arbitrary percentage of the chance of some belief about that NPC being true. Those percentages either go up or down depending on the result of the roll.

    Say, Caesar believes that Brutus is trustworthy, and I choose to represent that aspect of the relationship with a note \u201cI can trust Brutus\u201d, and a value of 90%. I roll 2d10 whenever I want to test that aspect of the relationship. Whatever the result of the roll, I adjust according to my own judgment. A minor failure, like say, Brutus stealing my towel at the public bath, would drop that value to 85% (entirely arbitrary number that I think is objective and proportional to the importance of the event). Now, if Brutus happens to stab me violently, that value would go down to absolute minimum, maybe 1%. Never zero to allow for luck to have an effect if I want to see if he repents in a future roll (if I survive the stabbing).

    I also may decide at some point to freeze the percentage if the relationship feels "defined" after a few of these rolls.

    The only downside to this method is that it might cause zombie Shakespeare to roll in his grave.

    \n@David_Berg:

    Yeah, the fictional result is the same, but I feel there is a difference in how one experiences making a roll for "Sonya is innocent of the theft!" vs making a roll for "I find the stolen object on Sonya" (edit to say that I would much rather frame it as "Do I find the stolen object on Sonya?" The former perhaps requires you to retrofit the fiction with the details, whereas in the latter you're sort of "living" through the process if you make your character look for the evidence.

    The other difference is that the first way of framing the roll means that upon success, it is pretty much cased closed as far as that particular secret goes. The second way of framing the roll, that is by looking for evidence, is that unless the evidence is pretty damning (and convincing to you as player/character), you could still leave it as a somewhat open question: what if someone planted the stolen object on Sonya? Or, if I didn't find it, does that mean she didn't stash it somewhere else?

    I can see cases where one might not want this kind of open ended result, and it would be much better to know for sure with a roll so as to move on, but sometimes this kind of inconclusive answer adds to the fiction.
