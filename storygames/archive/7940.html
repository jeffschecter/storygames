\nAndy expressed disbelief in this idea, but mostly because he was thinking about releasing a single book every year. If you release a book plus a couple of supplements you can profit in the $30k~$40k which isn't impossible to live with in the US. For people like me, living in a country with lower costs of living and with a weak currency, even $20k is a pretty good amount of money, it can pay the basics (rent, food, health insurance, transportation) and still leave some for cheap entertainment. It's not the consumer's paradise but if you want to live for art it's a decent deal.

I can see two major issues with this approach:\n
    \n
  1. Actually finding and keeping, at least, one thousand true fans.
    2. \n
  2. Creating consistently good content, worth between $50~$100 for each fan, every year.
    3. \n
  3. Managing the sales of that content, as it's unlikely that the 1000 true fans will buy your stuff in the moment you release it.
    4. \n
\nAlso did anybody tried this path, actually living on art targeted to a specific group?

    \nThis might be a tangent, but...

    Who said it had to be "sold" in a standardised kind of way?

    Let us imagine, if you will, that Greg Stolze (a person who lives not far from the 1000 fans model), was a bit more monetised, he could:

    1. Resort his downloads pages into cleaner catgories, with automated or "add-it-yourself" outlinking to resources (including forum threads about his games). This would add some utility to his site, as well as increasing the real estate (number of pages). Then he might organise his site design to make room for a couple of tower ads here and there (nothing animated, and screened in advance for "being topical"). The combination of these thing increases traffic and adds another (puny) revenue stream.

    2. Organise his free PDF publications/supplements into little books and compilations, and hook these up to Lulu. Anyone can still download them for free post-ransom - but you can also buy 'em in print. Yet another little revenue stream comes online.

    3. And so on. Wander around the world of Webcomics for a while; this is where they live.

    If you don't mind the (not very large) time and energy required to maintain a bunch of (not very large) streams of revenue, it becomes quite possible to play on the 1000 fans model.

    \n There's also a ton of other revenue trickles to tap, if you think in terms of an indie band model:
    * Licensing (source books, novels, shorts compilations)
    * Custom play aids (dice, GM screens, lammies, notebooks, card protectors)
    * Custom content commissions (one-shot modules, GM services, art)
    * Merchandising (T-shirts, coffee mugs, plush dolls, patches, badges)
    * Speaking fees (cons, local unis, game stores)

    I think we banged hard on this idea back when the notion of "how to make money with open-source IP" stuff was being discussed, lo, back in spring (?). Some author had written an article with, IIRC, nine models for revenue generation when your creative output was released for free. I am a bit busy or I'd search it out (I should have made a bookmark of it, as I intend to do as much of this as possible with GLASS, excepting the Main Book, which will be "traditional" POD).

    \n My understanding of 1000 true fans is that it's not about finding 1000 cows and milking them. It's about reaching the point where there are 1000 people who will buy whatever you put out, talk on your message boards, talk on other message boards, blog about you, and so on. They aren't your revenue stream, they're just the core of it. I think it's about not climbing the mountain again for the first time every time you put a product out there. I think of Burning Wheel in this context.
