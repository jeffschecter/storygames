    \n Well, I like it for those Edwardsian qualities it has - thoughtful, understated, aesthetically nuanced, complex cultural tradition and artistic thinking in the background. Solid and innovative theoretical underpinnings, too. So it's an overall package for me. Vanilla narrativism in a light, accessible package. Maybe not under-rated (compared to something like Fastlane, say), though, as the game simply isn't directed at the majority of the vocal indie gamers. Trollbabe certainly gets play from the people who like it.

    \n Trollbabe is kinda like the mother of a number of other games I love. Dogs in the Vineyard springs immediately to mind. But the reason I still like Trollbabe, that it hasn't been replaced by all its successors, is that it retains a light, fast frame of play that few other games manage to match for me. Its got that combination of personal interest and community focus, its got moral questioning, its got a reroll system that pushes towards the kinds of developments you see in the source literature without needing to slavishly imitate the source literature, and because of the way narration rights resolve, it also works better than most games for online play. (Though you tend to lose something in the smaller amount of table chatter that online play allows for.)

    I also have to admit I like Trollbabe because its given me some really good games. Including one of the best games I've had with my younger brother.

    \n I think the deal with the "Trollbabe" iconography in the Trollbabe ruleset is that they're trying to hit the stuff of 1970's cartoonist Vaughn Bode. He's a pretty clear influence on Ralph Bakshi's movie Wizards, and I presume on James West as well.
    \n
    \nIf you can get into Vaughn Bode, then the "trollbabe" part of Trollbabe makes perfect sense--it's almost exactly the sort of goofy laid-back cheerfully incoherent fantasy muddle that a nifty little Nar game with a heart of gold and a few grim spots, built out of equal parts alienation, magic, and relationships, should deal with. (Not that there's no point in hacking it for other things.)
    \n
    \nBut I think that mode of approaching fantasy has been out of fashion for a long time, in part due to the pressure exerted by other fantasy role-playing games and big-budget movies and other IP franchises.

    \n Thanks for the great responses, everyone. And Matt, yes, we definitely should play this soon!

    I actually played my second mini-session of Trollbabe last night, and had a great time. However, there was some serious confusion about a) how injury and rerolls, especially with multiple "conflict types", work, and b) quite a bit of a "death spiral" effect.

    As a designer, I'm curious about figuring out which bits helped make the game great, and which didn't (if any). Would any of you like to get a little more specific about the nuts of bolts of the game? Like, for instance, "this mechanic/play advice/whatever really helps me achieve this thing..."

    For me, I think the basic setup is very clever, and perfect for the kind of fiction it tries to create. Putting two cultures contrasting cultures into a vaguely-defined but relatable setting, then defining the protagonists as being neither of one nor the other, is a wonderful formula for a story of this kind. This is golden!

    And I loved a lot of the little details, like choosing on a map where you want the story to take place. The map is so vague and open to interpretation, that it sparks the imagination in a certain way which, again, is great for this kind of play. It makes things feel friendly and whimsical.

    (By the way, sorry to hear that about fantasy publishing. A few years ago, I remember lots of books coming out from Steven Erikson and R. Scott Bakker, both authors I enjoyed. Are they out of the picture now?)

    \n I've played two Trollbabe variants, using Per Fischer's excellent Tears in Rain (PDF). It's Bladerunner. If I might be so bold as to describe that game...

    We didn't use a map, but did develop a couple of key 'sets' that helped the mood. Like Trollbabe, we wanted the game grounded in a specific place. And we didn't use Relationships a whole lot, but I'd very much like to continue a TiR game beyond a session or two and genuinely develop a cast.

    \n The fantasy context with trolls, humans and trollbabes is definitely a big draw for me. I wouldn't be that interested in a cyberpunk game with the same rules, say. Folks who've read my blog know that I'm an evil elitist who thinks that modern fantasy and especially gaming fantasy is rubbish, but Trollbabe is not modern fantasy at all - it's nice and evocative fantasy from my childhood, like a crazy mix of Chronicles of Prydain, Conan and Heavy Metal.

    The rules of the game feed very well into that cultural context, especially by their simplicity and the spots they choose to emphasize (relationship-related choices, choosing sacrifice, victory and loss in conflict). It's actually sort of painful at times to work on my own game Eleanor's Dream in relation to Trollbabe - Ron said a couple of years ago that I'm basically working from very similar social and cultural inspirations that he was, and as the work approaches a state of readiness, I see myself gravitating towards very similar solutions. Perhaps this is why I've sort of avoided Trollbabe for the last three years; not quite with intent, but enough to not to have to think or read or play it too much.

    \n I like the way char-gen and set-up compels you to add & define some color. In the game I just played with Paul and James, we wound up equating Trollish magic to druidic and animal-based ritual and insight, while human "magic" became about metal and clockwork mechanisms. James gave the Trolls themselves a very Midsummer Night's Dream feel, both through their names and the variety of types of creatures they comprised.

    All this color was my favorite thing about our game.

    We could have thrown all that on top of any game that leaves as much stuff undefined as Trollbabe does. And Trollbabe certainly didn't force us to go as far as we did. But I found the combo of prompts and gaps to be a good facilitator.

    \n I love it- and in fact, am running Stranger Things tonight in a cobbled-together form with some further input from John.

    I dearly loved the TB core mechanics in equal measure to how little I'm interested in the default setting/character assumptions. Harper's Stranger Things takes the system in some very interesting new ways (including ensconcing in the mechanics the ballance between the two worlds the PC's are caught between), so shifts in the ballance have a character-level mechanical effect.

    it's also got the friggin' brilliant dream resolution system for handling the 'what do the PC's do?' type questions.

    and it has geomorph map tiles for laying out the weird city in play.

    and a really cool advancement mechanic.

    and a really cool form of lingering consequences.

    And... well, it's a game a really want to pay the author money in exchange for.

    But back to Trollbabe.

    It really hits my sweet spot in several areas- interesting failure, simple but nuanced conflict rez, mechanical efficacy linked to doing interesting play+ stuff in character, and it's nicely scaleable.

    I don't know how underrated it is... there are some issues with accessibility (Edwards writing style is a known factor), and the name is extremely offputting to a lot of people who'd otherwise love the hell out of the way it plays.

    \n That's fantastic news.

    I ran it last night- for only two of my group- but we managed to resolve four of the six dream people's problems, and as expected, some of the situational and scene-level AP was brilliant and memorable. I'll do a proper writeup tonight, but we had a couple of questions (some I may have known the answer to... and forgotten), and a couple of tweaks. I printed out the Geomorph tiles I could find, and shuffled them like cards, and the players played them onto the table when asking for scenes, or saying where they were going when seeking out their dream people. I took a pic of the arrangement which I'll post with the AP. I let them take a free re-roll if they were on a tile they played, and added something cool to the setting in the process. From this, we got the monkey-demon prostitutes of the Murder Mews, who helped out with a Passionate (human) conflict.

    As always, running the game involved me as GM getting a lot of 'play' in- I had about ten minutes of prep as I filled out my part of the two dream sheets, and then I was juggling all the stuff on there to try and work it in. I found a really useful technique was to look for parallels between the two sets of stuff- particularly in the obstacles and desires and dream moments. Where a common theme popped up on different sheets, I linked them. The 'Donkey Cart full of Roses' linked to the 'Flowers slowly turning to stone' in one moment when brought two strangers together.

    I called conflicts several times to wrong-foot the players (picking keywords which favored the low-end of their number ranges), and I aggressively used dream-people traits to push conflicts by forcing rerolls.

    It's really a fun one to run.

    -B
