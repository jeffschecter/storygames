\n I just got my copy in the mail. I haven't had time to properly read the entire thing as of yet, but I got up to page 40 or so (the pages are small). So, some initial impressions:

I like the size - it's small enough to fit in a cargo pocket.

I was very impressed by the introduction to roleplaying/"how do you play this game?" section. Bliss Stage devotes 3 or 4 pages to it, as opposed to the stereotypical paragraph, and it seems to me like it would actually make sense (and sound appealing) to a stranger to the hobby.

As with Polaris, I'm enchanted by Ben's writing. There is a pretty high number of typos though, which I'm hoping won't be reappearing in the more final version that's to come.

And finally, a random observation: Ben, you've got a messy signature!

So, any other folks who got in on the preorder and would like to share their impressions? I'll say more after I've read more, probably the day after tomorrow.

    \n First impressions:

    - It looks like it'll produce some heady stuff: deep immersion and gripping, ugly stories.
    - All the game mechanics--the stats, the different kinds of scene, managing Bliss and Stress etc.--are one great big machine to regulate pacing, delaying or accelerating the inevitable Pyrrhic victory or crushing defeat. Since those are your only two choices, the players have to choose their own goals for the interim: fight off the bliss as long as possible? keep my related characters from suffering Harm? achieve lots of mission goals? Part of me cringes that none of those things have an impact on the ending except by consensus, but it's a fascinating design nonetheless.
    - It is, of course, very well written, with more examples of play than I've seen in most games. A few places I did struggle with understanding what something meant, reading a thing over and over five times but not getting it; usually the problem was that the concept being employed wasn't introduced until a few pages later. (The idea of "picking" dice most sticks out in my mind. I also still don't quite get how relationships work at chargen, but I might just need to study the pilot templates closer.)

    I hope I have a chance to play soon!

    \n I just finished reading Bliss Stage a couple of hours ago. I read it front-to-back, page by page.

    Whew.

    The weight of the game doesn't really hit you until near the end, in the section with essays and advice. Every single one of the essays on what it's like to be these characters in this game had me choked up. Ben does an admirable job of detailing the pure devastating fucked up-edness of the Bliss Stage world.

    I really can't complement the writing enough. It's concise and technical when it should be, and emotional and poetic when it should be, in just the right proportions. Bliss Stage, as a book, manages to tug at the heart strings and be an effective technical manual both at once, goals that are in almost direct opposition to each other.

    Like I noted earlier, there really are a lot of typos and similar editing mistakes in Ignition Stage. I'd be pretty upset if the final product were the same way, but I can forgive it in a pre-release version. We'll see how that turns out, I suppose.

    More than anything, what strikes me about this book is that it's a vehicle for play. Everything in it is arranged in the order that you would need it in play, sort of the way that board game instruction manuals are often arranged. There's advice on pretty much every aspect of the game, and a lot of care taken to make everything accessible to a new player.

    The dice mechanic is simple, yet deceptively nuanced. It gives you a lot of information in a short amount of time. I don't have any experience with the One Roll Engine to compare it to, but reading Bliss Stage's mechanics reminded me of the praises people sing of the ORE and how much stuff it can handle in a single roll.

    I know that one of Ben's stated goals was to foster immersion, but I'm not sure that it will satisfy folks like the immersion pack over at RPGnet. I think that the game will foster very deep (potentially freaky deep - it's modeled after guided therapy) immersion in the middle of actions (which would be called scenes in most games), but also seems to require you to "come up for air" between actions in order to interact with the scene economy. I like playing it that way, but I think some people would find it irritating to keep breaking out of their in-character headspace between scenes.

    I love the simple insight of the minimum requirements for the varying levels of Intimacy. I love how pretty much the only stats in the game are relationships.

      \n I thought Chris' explanation was quite neat, but I guess it's not immediately clear how it relates to the rules on the previous page without some thought about the numbers. All he's saying is: work out the number of dice you need to place, and then you (almost always) want to take as many plusses as you can up to that number followed by blanks and minuses. The confusing part is why the number of dice you need to place is equal to (active relationships) + (trauma) + 2. The categories you always have to place dice in are Mission and Pilot Safety, plus then another category for each active relationship. If you have no Trauma, you place one die in each category, so that's the number of dice you need total. Mostly you assume that for each point of Trauma, the GM will threaten one category, which increases the number of dice you need to place by one, so you end up just needing to place as many extra dice to deal with threatened categories as your Trauma score, hence (relationships) + (trauma) + 2.

      Forcing a relationship costs 2 Trauma but only adds 1 die, and endangering a category costs 3 Trauma but only adds 2 dice, so each time the GM does one of those things, the number of dice needed goes down by one. If you're panicking, no extra dice are needed to deal with threatened categories at all, hence you can ignore your Trauma score. (If Chris were really anal, he could also point out that if the pilot and anchor have a broken relationship, an extra die is needed to deal with the Anchor's safety.)

      Hope that makes sense!

As a Pilot, you always roll the same number of dice (= the sum of the Intimacy of all their ANIMa's relationships). If the GM Threatens a relationship, an additional die must be allocated to it; you generally have Threats equal to your current Trauma. If you roll more dice than you need to allocate, you're happy because you can ignore the worst dice results. If you need to allocate more dice than you roll, you're in trouble because some things will automatically get a '-' result. Chris's explanation is a quick way of working out if you're happy or in trouble.
