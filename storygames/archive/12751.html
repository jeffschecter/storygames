\n This idea comes out of a posting I made in this thread on playtesting and from thinking about recent discussion of how to get people to purchase real copies vs pirate .pdfs.

Ok, so the idea is to make a "bonus disk" to be included in the back cover of a game book (perhaps a convention bonus edition) similar to those you get in the deluxe version of DVDs. In said disk would be a comprehensive collection of the whole design process, including: a design diary, forum transcripts, audio recordings of AP and playtest sessions, each and every version from playtest to finished product with designer's notes on what did/didn't work and why, pictures and video from conventions, etc. This would obviously be something that you would have to plan to do from the beginning (not because it would be that much extra work, more so that you remember to keep all the materials in one place and remember to get permissions along the way).

Has anyone done this in the past?

Is this something that is viable to do?

Is this something people would be interested in?
(I know other design diaries and "secret histories" have been warmly received online)

Is this something that people would pay extra for in terms of a deluxe edition?

[note] I'm not designing anything, if you like the idea, please steal it.

    \n I like the idea of a bonus disk, but I don't think I'd be particularly attracted by any of the things you've mentioned as potential items on that disk. When it comes to CDs glued into the back of manuals, I like to see stuff like background music, PDF files of useful stuff like character sheets, sample NPCs or example adventures and player handouts in case you don't want to damage your book photocopying it, maybe art to use on my computer's desktop or utilities to make playing the game easier. Design notes are nice and all, but they don't really grab me as being a must-have.

    Still, I'll happily note that I may not be typical of your target market. I rarely watch DVD extras for instance. I'm more interested in the game as it stands than how it came to be, despite being a game designer myself. Or possibly because of it, who can say?

    Posted By: mease19Has anyone done this in the past?

    Is this something that is viable to do?
    \nRibbon Drive is published with a compilation CD.

    It's upped the manufacture cost, upped the shipping cost, and introduced technical headaches like CD-burn-quality.
    I've received mixed responses about how much value it's added to the product.

    In the future, I would not include a CD or DVD with one of my products, unless the game required it on a fundamental level, or the game was taught through the CD (like Serial Homicide Unit).

    I would strongly urge you to consider the following options instead:
    *sending a .zip file to your buyers, containing the same contents without upping manufacture cost
    *hosting video files via youtube

    Posted By: TylerTRecording a game is great way to take notes but i think maybe having an AP segment or highlight real of actual play recording, inside of a wider range podcast may be a better way to go?
    \nI think a lot of it depends on taste, and not a lot of experiments have been done to see what works best, at least as far as I know. The now-offline Virtual Play podcast went the "highlight reel" route with actual play, and I think the "How to Run Ganakagok" episode featured AP snippets. Actual People, Actual Play does a synopsis like you might read in a forum post before they discuss their play. They seem to be pretty hot in terms of audience reaction, but I personally find the lack of context a bit frustrating and wish I could listen to the full sessions rather than just the summary they give.

    I'd personally love to see a conversation somewhere about what people do or don't like about AP podcasts, since there seems to be a lot of variation in things like how tightly they are edited, etc., and most of the criticisms of the format that I've seen seem to be blanket dismissals rather than critiques.
