    Posted By: JuddHopefully, the game has bits for us to connect the character directly to the setting.
    I believe the character as a whole is an element of the setting, and so is all the small tiddly-bits too. ;-)

    I also believe we tend to think of the character in system-terms, due to the traditional way of creating them in classical rpg's: by allocating numbers, min-maxing the tactical abilities, making "strategic" decisions and so on. Such a singular focus on characters as a system-related element is detrimental to our understanding of the abilities and the potential of the character.

    By focusing on the character as a setting element I believe a lot of players will fare much better. And by "much better" I mean to say: they will have more fun, they will be able to participate more and to feed the interaction in better ways, and they will learn more from the experience.

    It may even have a similar effect on GMs too ...

    \n All the Character, Setting, Situation, Color, and System are all related. Character is a natural outgrowth of Setting, and Setting is what the Character interacts with. Situation is where Character meets Setting. System is the crux of how you get from one Situation to the next. Color is the little details of each of these. And those stats and numbers and stuff on the character sheet? That's the System of Character. There's a System of Setting -- usually subtle, and usually the charge of the GM, but very pronounced in, say, Universalis. There's a System of Situation -- also usually subtle, but very pronounced in, say, My Life With Master. There's also a System of Color, again usually subtle -- it's the way in which we express in-game things in concrete, actionable terms (usually without numbers).

    You can't design any one piece of a game without thinking about all the others.

    \n It depends on how you're defining Setting. If by Setting you mean a whole bunch of preplanned world elements like the way Forgotten Realms is laid or 7th Sea's Theah when you include ALL the source books, then I'm not sure what to say about that. I've never been drawn to all that pre-play detail and now that I know different ways to approach setting I don't really see a reason to go that route.

    I much prefer thinking of Setting the way Sorcerer & Sword presents it. Basically Setting starts as a collection of thematic aesthetics. From there a cycle happens. Characters are built from those aesthetics and then as needed the concrete details of the setting are constructed story-by-story to serve the story needs of those characters (i.e Character + Setting = Situation). As the Situation at hand resolves then that updates the Character which in turn informs the next bit of Setting detail. And the cycle continues. In the end you might very well end up with something that resembles The Forgotten Realms or Theah but highly specific to the exploits and thematic needs of the characters.

    \n Of course the characters are part of the setting. This is something I was trying to reference in a big thread that blew up recently.

    You can look at it a lot of ways; I look at it this way:

    If you generate a character through a 100% random generation system, and that system is designed to produce an "average" person in the setting, then you are, in effect, picking a random pre-existing person from the entire setting. To possess, as it were.

    If you have a say in the creation of the character, then by creating the character you're saying, "Whatever the nature of the setting, it has this character."

    Furthermore if you get to create (or at least relate your character to) family members, allies, enemies, etc., then you are creating some of the setting--again, at very least, by specifying relationships that wouldn't exist otherwise.\n
    If the character belongs to a guild, the player has (or should have) at least some say on what the guild is like.
    \nThere's no should. Minimally, if we're not doing random chargen, the player who makes a guild member says, "The guild has this member." More than that, is a property of specific games and groups.

    \n Characters can also be setting in another cyclical way.

    Just finished a relationship map exercise with Players for a new campaign. From a rough outline based on an earlier shared brainstorm, Players then created relationships with NPC's and each other. In so doing they adjusted the setting. By deciding what to focus on and because this must be "plausible" the setting has more old-regime partisans, more dwarves, more interesting goblins and probably more sexy fey then it did before.

    On character + setting = situation, I see that as a fomula that can be solved for any missing element. If you have characters and situation, you need a certain sort of setting. If you have setting and situation you need a certain sort of character etc. etc. The key to me is not "what + what" but "they must all balance and solve in the equation".

    \n What are you saying Simon? If we are not talking "big model" then it's all semantics? A bit weird to say such a thing, isn't it?

    My own thinking on the bigger picture is like this, simply put:

    vision -> interaction -> fiction

    Vision = the goals and efforts of the gamesmith
    Interaction = the way players use the game (the book and dice, system and setting)
    Fiction = the product: drama, emotions, insights

    To create an engaging fiction is the ultimate goal.

    The situation is part of the setting, yes, but let us concentrate on the character and the player. Do my claim have any significance for the player? Is it possible to give players other ways of relating to their character, that makes them more able to contribute constructively to the game-play, and to immerse themselves more fully in the interaction?
