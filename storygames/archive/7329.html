\n Since I listened to the Evil Hat podcast on "Escape or Die" I got interested in the idea of using a stopwatch as a rhythm tool, but my Google-fu is weak and I'm unable to find other games that use such time-boxes. Anybody here knows about other games using this?
\n
\nAnyway I'm thinking about using it to timebox flashback scenes and tie it to the reward system: each character can have X minutes of flashback scenes during the game, whenever he flashbacks the timer is released (i.e. multiple flashbacks are possible but each has only the time unused by the earlier ones) and he narrates a piece of his background to justify some character development (in game terms he can add a skill or resource on the fly). The quality/power of the "remembered" stuff depends of the length of the flashback (i.e. shorter flashbacks means less effective stuff). I'm trying to balance fun and reward: the player flashbacking monopolizes the narrative (although other players can participate they can't affect the outcome of the flashback) so I want to keep the flashbacks scarce but reward good scenes (other p\u013aayers can veto a flashback if the scene wasn't fun or interesting enough, as I don't want to let players just wait X minutes to get a powerful toy from the past). I still need more playtesting but I wanted to know what you folks think about this.

    \n It's not an RPG, but when I have limited time to GM an adventure (for example on a contest), I always use realtime to structurize the adventure. Lately I GM one-shots and I always write a time-limit into the adventure. If the realtime end of the session is closing, than the event arrives in-game wich prevents the PCs from winning.

    In the 2nd part of Traitor's Gambit (first adventure of Dawn of Defiance) when the PCs set the clock of the bomb, we agreed that it will blow up in realtime one hour before the end of the session. It made things really exciting. They started to force scene-framing instead of me.

    You should always use realtime as a tool, even in games wich don't use it.

    The "Escape or Die" playtest podcast conveys the idea much better than I can do with my words, but I'll try it anyway. In "Escape or Die" the timer runs for X minutes (where X equals to the number of alive characters in play) and after each time it ends the run every character gets a doom point. When characters get 12 doom points they die spectacular deaths. But characters also get doom points when they roll critical failures, so the character can die (of doom) much faster. Also as characters escape (i.e. leave play by winning) or die the timer starts to go shorter and shorter.

    \n

    As the time goes by the players start to do more intense scenes, they push their rolls trying to accumulate escape points (when you get 12 you win and get out of the game) faster than doom points. So the rhythm of the game is influenced by the timer (actually by the doom, but this is influenced by the timer). I hope this clarifies things.

    \n In the game of Gorilla, each player is a GM with a specific agenda, and their own stopwatch set to a reasonable amount of time depending on the number of players, say, fifteen minutes. When it is their turn, they frame a scene for one or more of the other players, and start the timer, calling the scene when they feel that it's done. Each scene is voted up or down by all assembled, and when you're out of time, even if it's in the middle of a scene, you're done as a GM. The player with the most yea votes "wins". It is incredibly fun.
