\n So, I am often quiet/lurky here, but there's something I'm struggling with lately, which I could use some input on: I'm having a hard time making truly new characters.

Many times when I sit down to make a character, I get all the usual rush of creation as I do whenever I engage in a creative project. But when I'm done, and I look at the results, I realize that my new character is remarkably like a character I've played before. And I then I feel disappointed with myself.

It feels like a slump. Like I don't have many new ideas? This doesn't happen when I'm in the GM seat of my Monsterhearts game, or while I'm playtesting my side-project. And, of course, similar characters in different stories will end up really different, so perhaps I'm just being hard on myself. But still: I'd like something new and challenging, but it's hard to find my way there. And I really don't want to start second-guessing every move I make while creating new characters; no-butting yourself is rarely productive.

Has anyone else struggled with this? How did you work through it?

    \n I've handled this phenomenon by recognizing that it's natural, and not nearly the problem it's often made to be. Essentially, I think that the hidden premise here ("Players should express creativity in their character designs the same way a GM expresses his in campaign development") is faulty, despite having been pounded into our heads by traditional dominion theory of roleplaying.

    (The "dominion theory" is, of course, this: "The GM has creative control over the setting. The player has creative control over character." When you stare at that framework long enough, as most of us have done, you start thinking that it implies that players should be creative artists. After all, when you only have creative control over the character, that obviously means that you should be using that control to be amazing, right?)

    Thus, my recommendation: think long and hard, question your assumptions here. Is it really necessary for each player character to be a work of art right out of the gate? Are the games you play really supporting that? Do they actually make use of the originality of character? What are you, or the game as a whole, getting out of it? How would the game be different if you just played Harry Callahan (or whoever your go-to character is) every time?

    Maybe you'll come to conclude that yes, the games I play really don't work well if my character isn't a clever and unique masterpiece out of the game. If you get that far, then I'll hazard that you'll also be a lot closer to figuring out how to make the character into such a masterpiece, because you've obviously understood the precise mechanisms in the game that utilize that character uniqueness - and when you understand that, you'll be in a much better place to actually pull those levers.

    Or, you'll come to conclude like I have: the vast majority of the games I play don't care one whit of how unique my character is. Often it's not even possible to make unique characters without breaking the game. In fact, many games work better when my character is easy to understand and archetypical, as that way the other players understand the character better and faster. Often the character takes on an unique nature during play, but that's exploratory play and not character design.

    If anybody here thinks that they can name a game that actively requires novel high-quality player character portraiture as part of game prep, I'd be interested in hearing about it. I have a few candidates myself (hint: they're all princessing games), but frankly I am not at all convinced that even the best ones really benefit from uniqueness of character, rather than simply not being harmed by it.

    \n This is both a good question and a bad question! That makes it hard to answer.

    That is, I don't know what "new" means to you. To know that, I need to know what your old practices have been. Maybe you've made a gazillion rich and unique characters already. There are a limited number of human problems, so a certain amount of repetition is inevitable. In that case, maybe you don't really have a problem.

    On the other hand, maybe every character you've made keeps turning into the ex-military loner who carries a black long-sword and has a demon girlfriend.

    So... yeah those are two extremes, but the point is I need more info about where you are in relation to them. What makes a character distinct to you? How he looks? The things he can do? His past experiences? The people and social circles he has relationships with? His emotional attachments? His ethical / moral ideals?

    A related issue is, what rules do you typically use? A perennial problem in RPG design is that many system offer you a large number of mechanical choices that don't matter much. That is, you have many mechanically distinct but blandly similar characters.

    \n It's just a stupid theory term I like to use. Here's a definition:

    Princess play: An agenda of play where the player focuses on depicting an interesting character being interesting - cool, pretty, strong or whatever floats your boat. Princessing is often immersive, but it doesn't have to be. Princess play is distinctive in that the strong and specific vision of what the character is like comes primarily from the character's player, and it is not questioned as part of play; the character should not face set-backs that cast doubt upon the character's identity.

    As a simple example, in my experience Exalted and modern D&D are often played as princessing games: the point of playing the game is that you get to create and develop this cool character who gets into fights and does a lot of victory poses and so on. The events of play may feature stuff like combat or drama, but their deeper purpose is to spotlight the characters who engage in those things. The fight exists so your character can show off how cool they are in a fight.

    Of the various sorts of games that come to mind as candidates for actually benefitting from unique characters, a princessing game seems to me the likeliest sort. Even there, though, I know many people who specifically want to princess play rather stereotypical characters - one of our players created a dwarf fighter who uses an axe, carries grudges and is fond of honor when we played a bit of 4th edition D&D recently, for example. It could be that some people simply have pretty staid imaginations and that's what counts as an original character for them, but I rather suspect that perhaps uniqueness simply isn't a requirement for princessing in 4th edition D&D...

    \n I feel your pain because I've gone through it. To me, it felt like I played every single character that was possible. It ended up with me giving up on immersive play.

    One thing that you can try out is to play for change. Have one character switch into another. It doesn't have to be a totally new character but something that has a major impact on your current traits. For example, I played a half-orc that were the leader of the group and I had planned that he was going to go through a religious crises. Another example were a magical forest spirit that wanted to explore the human world, but the more she learned and became human, the more she lost her magical abilities. Be sure to tell the game master about your plans so s/he can include these changes into their adventures.

    \n Something in the line of John Fiore's Aladdin Technique might help:

    1.- Write a brief background for the character.
    2.- Identify several key elements in it.
    3.- Randomly alter some of these key elements.
    4.- Develop further the character by embracing the new elements.

    \n I think that Eero makes a really important point about the value of "originality" - rarely do great things come from people trying hard to be "original" or "unique", and it's certainly not necessary in a character concept. (I think a character's *actions* in play are a far more interesting element to explore here, instead. Ultimately, it's the character as defined *in play* which is important here, and that's an organic process you can't control entirely by yourself.)

    Without understanding your exact issue (more specifics, please!), it's hard to give targeted advice, but here are two ideas which come to mind:

    1. "Mad Libs" character creation.

    Come up with a character idea (it can be the same idea you used in your last game, no problem), but leave a few blanks.

    This character is obsessed with _________. He desires revenge against _________. Her approach to her chosen field/specialty is fundamentally shaped by her relationship with her parents: in her childhood, her father ___________________________________________.

    If you have the material to play with, you can randomize these. (Simply assigning your character's gender at random, for example, could do a lot, particularly if being of a certain gender means something specific in your setting - e.g. a fantasy setting where only women can perform magic, or a game set in the Catholic Church.) Are there four possible factions in the City? Let's roll one at random to be the character's most hated enemy. And so on.

    However, open-ended blanks are even more interesting:

    It is prophesied that the character's destiny is ______________________.

    Among the clergy, my character's reputation is ________________________.

    Come with a couple of such blanks in your next character, and then hand the character over to your fellow players. "Ted, tell me what my character's childhood was like. Lucy, what does my character believe her Destiny is, as seen in a dream vision?"

    Let the other players fill in those blanks, throwing a twist into your character concept. I guarantee you'll end up with something you didn't expect, and a fun roleplaying challenge.

    You can go even more open-ended, like so (this is a good general technique):

    "My character is a veteran of many political campaigns, and closely connected to big finance. How is she unique - how is she completely different from anyone else who would fit that description?"

    Sometimes you can answer that yourself, and it's enough. Better yet, though, ask the other players to each offer you an answer. (And, as a bonus, they'll be more interested in your character as a result!)

    2. Pick an important or fundamental aspect of the character and conciously invert, change, or throw it upside-down.

    Here's a way to apply that technique to a PbtA game with playbooks (AW, Monsterhearts, etc), for example:

    The Easiest PbtA Hack: a Custom Character Creation Move

    I think that might give you some ideas.

    \nExcellent! So, for my first thought, I will use a sort of parable or metaphor about game design. Many years ago I was active on a Yahoo group called "RPG-Create." (Still a member, in fact). Every year or so someone would start a discussion about "What are the best Attributes for an RPG?" We all of us (myself included) were awash in Platonic thinking. We would very sincerely investigate the topic with the attitude that there existed - somewhere out there - an ideal form of RPG design with the perfect set of ability scores, or stats, or whatever, and that if we just thought about it hard enough we could discover what they were. It never occurred to any of us (or at least, not for a long time) that an RPG just *might not have* universally shared scalar descriptives attached to each character.

    We took it for granted that it was part of an RPG's identity as an RPG that it have some set of things like STR, INT, WIS, DEX, CON, CHA, even if that particular set wasn't a very good one.

    I wonder if you are taking it for granted that story-play characters, structurally, are made of these grabby engaging components ( emotional attachments, etc.) but forgetting that the point of them is to grab and engage *you.*

    You said "I want to have feelings when I play," and I absolutely agree with you! But structural components are just tools; they don't guarantee feelings. You can make structurally perfect characters and situations that conform with glittering precision to narrative theory etc., but if you personally don't have feelings about them they're pointless.

    If you're bored by yet another family drama, or yet another troubled past, ditch it! What will grab your attention right now and makes you go *I WANT TO PLAY THIS CHARACTER?*

    So, this might sound weird, but, read more philosophy, especially ethics. Read the news. When something really grinds your gears, make a note of it. That's an issue to hang a character on.

    My second thought is, you might be well-served to just stop worrying about this stuff ahead of time. You're going to know, when you need to know, what it is about the character that zaps you. I have the perfect game for you to do this with, and it just happens to be one of my favorites: Trollbabe! @Eero_Tuovinen and @Paul_T and I have been trying to scramble up some actual play in the #indierpgs IRC channel on irc.magicstar.net, but Real Life (TM) keeps intervening. You're welcome to join the fray!

    One of the design features of Trollbabe is that it pushes back against games like Bushido where you have a plethora of trivially mechanically distinct characters. That is, there are lots of permutations of mechanical choices to make, but they all are more or less the same aesthetically, or where it only makes sense to play a small subset of the available characters.

    In Trollbabe there are only 8 mechanically distinct characters. The rest of character creation is straightforward visual in-the-moment description. Character creation takes like 10 minutes or less. And then all of the things you're accustomed to frontloading your character with and that are currently causing you mental suffering are developed through play.

    \n Colin,

    That's very interesting! Having more insight into your situation gives us a lot more to work with, and I'm glad you're getting some good advice in this thread.

    My advice for you would be:

    a) Start by asking the other players what/who they'd like you play. What does this game need? Who does *your* character need to play against, or as a foil? Etc. Maybe that will already solve your "problem"! But, if it doesn't...

    b) Don't try to be original. Instead, allow yourself to create the outline of a character who fits the very "template" you're trying to avoid. Do all those things you're tired of; make a "stereotypical" Colin-character, intentionally!

    c) Pick a couple of things about the character which seem really important or central to you. Maybe it's the dead lover, or their social identity, for instance.

    For each one, either:

    * Turn it into a "blank", then ask the other players to fill it for you. ("My character has a relationship with a dead person. Who are they, and what's the relationship like?")

    * Deliberately "invert" it; make it the very opposite thing. Maybe instead of a ghost lover they wish was still here, it's someone who almost killed them, and instead of protecting them, it's them who craves forgiveness from your character. Or, maybe, if your character was a team leader, now they are a mole in the organization. And so on.

    I think that would get you your results pretty quickly and effectively!

    You can also play around with randomness as a tool. I have a few suggestions along those lines I've fooled around with:

    * Dogs in the Vineyard Random Character Creation uses "themes" and "statements" to create your character.

    You could make your own such list of themes and statements by writing down all the things that your characters have in common, and then randomizing them to make a new one. That will create interesting variations on a theme for you, occasionally reinforcing it and occasionally breaking it!

    * It All Ends in Tears is a customized Fiasco-style playset I've made for Monsterhearts games. The cool thing it takes from Fiasco is that it defines your relationships to the other characters *first*, and only then do you define the character. Between the table elements and the starting Backstory questions, you get a character in a situation you wouldn't normally have chosen.
