\n What stuff should we watch, read, play?

1.Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b.Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.

Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.

    \n Risus creator S. John Ross opened a Kickstarter project yesterday to fund writing and publishing a new free Risus adventure. It's already gone over double the modest $500 goal.\n
    The adventure, if funded, will be made available to the public in PDF form, free of charge, for keepsies and always, just like Risus itself. This first outing will be a traditional fantasy module ... or, the Risus idea of what a traditional fantasy module is like! You can expect monsters and ruins, muddy town-streets and eccentric NPCs ... it won't be a comedy adventure, exactly, just the kind of kick-your-shoes-off approach to high adventure I'm most fond of (if you're familiar with Points in Space or Uresia, that's the tone I mean). The adventure will be designed for a single session (3-6 hours) of gamerly madness and panic, and be designed for expansion and contraction to fit the GM's needs.
    \nFor $5 you'll get access to the finished PDF six months before it's released to the public. Higher pledges get you beta access, and for ridiculous contributions John will fly to your town, GM a Risus game for you and your friends, and even cook dinner.

    \n [Black Mirror 2]
    Hey, Kira. I recommend against reading the synopses. It consists of three unconnected short stories in TV form. The only thing they have in common is a very dark satirical (and tragic) look at how contemporary and fictional near future (inspired by contemporary) technologies impact society and individuals. When I say it like that it sounds really dull though...

    Seriously, Black Mirror is my favourite piece of television since the Wire (and I'm excluding a lot of truly excellent stuff to allow space for it there). It's not perfect but it's very powerful stuff. The second episode is semi-autobiographical fiction, it might help to know the life and work of Charlie Brooker to appreciate it fully. The first one is more of a dark humor tale of old-media vs. new-media (and many other things). The last one is a personal drama that left me completely devastated.

      Posted By: JDCorleyThe Out of Character webseries crew discuss whether game systems exist to provide "unexpected, disagreeable outcomes". Pretty fun, we try to get our heads around it.Unexpected, Disagreeable Outcomes.
      \nCool discussion, but there are a few times when Derek equates disagreeable with "bad" or "unwelcome." You do this as bit as well, particularly in your example of a fighter trying to hit an Orc with a sword. To me the system is clearly producing Vincent's disagreeable result here. The GM and the player could just agree as to whether the fighter hits the Orc, but it's more fun to continue to disagree and engage the system. The fact that this particular result isn't highly emotionally charged for either of you is really not at issue.

      Now your example does complicate the "unexpected" part of Vincent's position. Hit or miss, the result is not going to be very surprising. But even here, in this simple example, things like random damage and variable hp mean that the outcome isn't fully known ahead of time.

      \n Sooo...one or two people have asked me over the years what they can read in order to do cool lawyer stuff in their RPGs. Well the best, funniest, coolest book about what a lawyer actually does, how lawyers approach problems, etc. is my old professor Kenney Hegland's Introduction to the Study and Practice of Law. You can get it for a penny because, well, every friggin 1L in the country should read it, and lots are required to. But it's awfully realistic and normal, so if that's not what you're looking for...

      For more dramatic lawyering, including my FAVORITE PART of lawyering, my bible is The Art of Cross-Examination by Francis Wellman. Originally published in 1906. It's dramatic and funny and historically interesting and very good. And you can straight up read it for free on Google Books. Check it out.

      Edit: Links fixed, thanks for the whisper.

      \n So I've been reading Willem Larsen's Language Hunter's Kit, available as an ebook from Leanpub, and it's a delight. It performs marvelously at the impossible task of conveying the principles of techniques of Language Hunting, which is an in-person, oral skill, through a printed text. The prose breezes by and proceeds in bite sized pieces, so it practices what it preaches. And it relates directly to my blog post series I mentioned above: all the "Rules of Accelerated Learning" are in here!

      Anyway, I wanted to share with you all this entry from the acknowledgements page, which is a chapter unto itself as Willem honors all those he's drawn inspiration and life from:\n
      To the Story Games Community, a strange un-looked for haven of something nobody can quite agree upon, for the inspiration of boiling creativity you have shared, for the support of Fluency Play, I shake your hand and bless the pirated .pdf of this book that finds you.
