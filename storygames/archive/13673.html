\n System does matter.

It is obvious: games differ in how we interact within them, and in the fiction they produce. Our interaction is framed by "the system". If the system leaves a lot to player-fiat, it is still the system dictating that this is allowed. System does matter. End of debate.

LOL! The debate will continue, of course. Let it. But please do so in another thread.

This thread is for a talk on "system" and "mechanics" and "rules"; are these three terms equal? Some of you seem to have no second thoughts interchanging them. As a designer and player outside this sphere of terminology, I find that strange, and believe it must be a little befuddling for those of you trying to reach a deeper understanding of role-playing games. It seems to me that the theoretical apparatus we apply to role-playing games is limping. There seems to be little coherence between reality and apparatus, and little understanding of how the applied terms confine our thoughts on the matters at hand.

My simple alternative
I prefer the term game method, understood as "the way we play the game", including both mechanics and techniques.
- Mechanics are focused on abstracting a character and giving inspiring results in task/conflict-resolution.
- Techniques are focused on how we influence each other and word the fiction, the important storytelling part of it.

I now understand and discuss how we build and play role-playing games by the term method, with the sub-terms mechanics and techniques ...
- and by pairing up method with the term setting ...
- and by applying something that may be called a design staircase; vision -> interaction -> fiction, to my work as a designer.

Vision = the idea, and the game-text expressing the idea of the game (including setting and method). This is the step for design.
Interaction = the applied method, the way players act to produce a fiction in accordance with the vision. This is the step for game-play.
Fiction = the actual and individual result of the game for each and every player. This is the final step; what the game creates, the aim of it all.

I find that this way of thinking on how we play role-playing games, has left me with a slightly skewed, and fruitful, viewpoint. Some minor problems of communication aside (I have to explain my terms a lot, of course, when discussing rpgs), this simple theoretical apparatus has left me less confused, and more able to pursuit my design goals.

So; what about you;

- Do you believe the theoretical apparatus of role-playing games is working?

- Are there insights to be had by a simple change in applied terms?

    The definition of "system" that you refer to makes sense, of course. The problem is how players/designers really understand and use the term.     \n Hell yes! Down with the term "system"!

    Game-method is okay. I might prefer Play-method because it includes non-gamelike features more naturally. Or Play-process as a nod to the unintentional ("method" sounds planned, to me).

    In my head, the next level is play techniques, which are regular features of the Play-process.

    Where play techniques derive from the game text, I divide them into Rules (stuff you must do, and when you must do it, clearly specified), Guidelines (stuff you should do, but with plenty of room for interpretation about when and how), and Advice (stuff you might wanna try).

    Looking at Rules in terms of who does what when frames the rules as Procedures.

    Looking at Rules in terms of input-process-output frames the rules as Mechanics.

    I'm generally fairly able to chat about this stuff on the internet using these terms. (Though I rarely discuss "system" in total. Hey, there's another term idea: Total System.)

    \n In the last 10+ years, in tabletop RPGs, system = mechanics. D20 was (possibly still is) the world's most popular RPG. The first year D&D 2E was out, all year, it sold 289,000 copies of the Players Handbook (PHB). D&D 3E went on to sell 300,000 copies of the PHB in only the first month and then sales continued to grow.

    With D&D 3E's amazing success, the "D20 System" and "System = Groupings of Mechanics" was solidly codified in the minds of millions of gamers.

    I know that definition is technically incorrect depending on context. But the Forge redefining System to mean "everything your group does to agree to imagined events during play", while possibly more correct in some ways, also further isolates itself from the larger RPG hobby and creates confusion.

    When people said, "System doesn't matter", they were saying "mechanics don't matter, all you need is a good GM and if the rules get in the way of the story, change the rules". The Forge argued, "System does matter, mechanics change how you play". But then redefined "System" to include almost everything you do to reach consensus. Which included changing the mechanics and empowering the GM to ignore mechanics. With the new definition, no one who said "System doesn't matter" would keep saying, "System doesn't matter". You almost couldn't. It would be like saying, "everything we do together doesn't matter."

    But it completely sidesteps and misses the original points. If they wanted to change the definition, great, but stop saying "System matters" after you have completely redefined the context of the original argument and meaning. They moved the "goal posts". It's like a manipulative "cheating" tactic from a toxic anonymousness internet debate.

    I try to use the following definitions when I discuss stuff:

    "System (including but not limited to 'the rules') is defined as the means by which the group agrees to imagined events during play." Basically, whatever it is that you actually do at the table.     \n The divide is theoretical. I am proposing this as a theoretical apparatus.

    In praxis we have to recognize each and every game as a unique whole, and apply these terms only to the degree they fit the game, or as reference-points by which we understand and talk about the game. Any term for describing a phenomenon as complex as a single role-playing game, or as diversified as the form role-playing games, must be recognized as a tool, nothing more. What I give you here is a set of tools that may help you contemplate role-playing games in constructive ways. It has certainly helped me.

    Having techniques/mechanics affecting setting-elements is not a problem. They are meant to do so, in the interaction. As players we apply the method to the setting (in most games; some of them is about creating the setting too), in order to create the fiction! The method informs us how to do this, and the setting inspires the content of our fiction.

    Some techniques are meant to feed right into the fiction too, as mood-tools. The way we talk at the table, the use of rituals and ceremony, touch and silences, stares and gestures ... all these, and more, are meant to strengthen the fiction by adding some sort of mood/emotion/tension. So the fiction becomes more than a narrative; it is emotions, internal group-dynamics, and a weave of real and imagined relations criss-crossing the table. These layers of the fiction may be hard to perceive, but they are almost always present, and may lend enormous strength to the fiction.

    If we progress further along this line of thought, it becomes increasingly clear that the fiction of a role-playing game may be perceived to be as complex as life itself, even though it is an artificially created phenomenon. By being created in fleeting layers of social relations and mutual sensory-stimulation, the fiction mimics nature. The fiction may lend credibility from nature, by being close to it in comparison, but it is still a game we play.

    To know it is a game, and still being able to immerse ourselves in the experience, creating a fiction of great power, is at the heart of role-playing games to me.

    \nTomas, I think these questions are revealing. The theoretical apparatus of RPGs is not something that should be taken as singular. Very, very coarsely, as you want to apply theory to improving your play and design of RPGs, you should be passing through three phases:

    1) learning a theory (or theoretical apparatus), including its limitations and valid domains of application.

    2) learning multiple theories and the framework around them so you can handle both the complementary and contradictory explanations of those theories.

    3) learning the craft of building new theory frameworks when you require the additional detail or are in a domain that requires it.

    Ultimately a theory is just a codification of insights, concepts, and ideas into condensed form - it compresses knowledge into something we can more easily apply and understand, in exchange for sometimes being wrong or ill-suited to the current situation.

    Personally I believe there is a wealth of useful RPG theory to be found, and I use it regularly, switching from theory to theory on different problems. But I rarely use the Forge theory you seem to consider as "the theoretical apparatus of roleplaying games". And, yes, one of the reasons is that it doesn't handle non-mechanical elements of play particularly well. But I don't see the point of trying to make the Forge theory work on everything, it does seem to serve particular design and therapeutic purposes very well. Because, we shouldn't expect any theory to work well for all applications.

    My suggestion with what you are trying to do is focus on capturing and communicating your particular insights and codify them into a self-contained theoretical structure. Reference other theory as you need, but don't attempt to retain the unnecessary bulk.

    - Mendel

    Reading games

    What makes a game?

    I believe that a game as read from a book consists of three parts.


    The setting \u2013 The fictional world in which the game is set.
    The rules \u2013 The mechanical stuff, dice and tables.
    The form \u2013 The way the game is supposed to be played.


    For most gamers the first two ones should be pretty obvious. \u2018A game consists of two parts, the rules and the setting.\u2019 But I think that is not enough to describe a game, perhaps it once was, when games actually just had a setting (a dungeon beneath the city) and rules (how to bash the monsters down there and take their gold), it was implied that you\u2019d play a party of adventurers that went down dungeons to bash monsters and loot. These days games are played in so many different ways, just take the GM for example. Once the natural hub of any gaming group, the provider of adventure and fun. In some \u2018new\u2019 games there is no GM, or the role of GM rotates amongst the players during play.

    Gamer A: \u2018I don\u2019t like Game X.\u2019

    Gamer B: \u2018I love Game X. You\u2019re playing it wrong!\u2019

    Provided that the preferences of A and B are similar, why did they have so different opinions about Game X? They played the same game, with the same rules and the same setting. The answer lies in the form. The mysterious set of implied conventions of how a game should be played. Some games make the form very clear, some ignore it entirely and you have to guess from reading the rules and the setting. How did the author play this game? How should it be played? How often should the ninjas attack? Should the characters feel angst after bashing the ninjas? Who decided that ninjas should attack; you, I, the GM, all of us together?

    \n I'd like to add something on players; what their agenda is and how it may impact the game.

    It is first and foremost very nice to talk about players; no one ever mistake that for something other than it is; the physical representation of human emotions and willpower, participating in the interactive social procedure we call "a game". LOL

    Players come to the table with varying agendas. They may be completely blank on the game, but looking forward to a night with their friends. They may be totally in the know on the game, but still looking forward to a night with their friends. They may be seriously engaged in the game itself, and with less sympathy for chitter-chatter and other social derailments. Most of them have mixed agendas; friends, playing something, challenging themselves, experiencing a gripping tale ...

    Whatever agendas the players bring to the table, the challenge for the gamesmith, through his game, is to get them on the same page, and have them discover the true magic of his game.

    If your game is any good, it will convince the players it is worth engaging in, and it will deliver them the tools they need to work the magic themselves. And then the players are left to decide; will they set aside their various agendas, and accept the game as their purpose for the evening? That is the great question for any designer, because his game rise or falls with the answer to that question. And the fact is, that one player alone can ruin the chance for a game to shine. If one player insist on ignoring the interaction, to chat with his friend over the table, the game may be lost. Games are not equally vulnerable to this kind of conflicting agendas, but most games that want to work the magic is. There is no magic without the synthesis of game and players.

    We have similar ideas, yes, but we're not "just using different words". The reason I have made my nomenclature is that I have found a lot of rpg-terms in popular use to be inherently unfit for the use their intended, largely misinterpreted and often so clotted with connotations that their functionality as communicative and intellectual tools are severely reduced. In some ways terms like "system" is indeed a stumbling block to further developments. So my set of terms is designed to un-clutter the field, first and foremost for myself.     Techniques have mainly been left to convention, yes. There's been little research done by game-smiths, and little engineering done, on tools of techniques. In classical games of old the advice has been rather vague, so there's been a void here (in some cases quite fruitful, but more often not so) that various GMs have had to fill in, thus creating the convention. Games of the new millennium, both classical and modern ones, are generally a bit better. Some of them are quite good. But still; this is a field largely un-explored, and I believe that is our next frontier!

    Mind you: I do not propose that game designers of old were bad designer. They were pioneers in a new form of expression! In my view the classical role-playing game Dungeons & Dragons range alongside the early drama Antigone, the early novel Don Quixote and the early full lenght feature-movie Birth of A Nation, as a groundbreaking work in its field.

    And I do not say that designers of today could have done better. We are doing better, exploring and inventing our form of expression with each and every new game.

    My idea is to contribute to the development of role-playing games of the future, by helping out in understanding the potential, and by pointing out particular fields of exploration I consider to be worth focusing on.

    You are, of course, on to much the same picture I'm trying to paint here. I see that you put emphasis on the way the game is supposed to be played, and that is good!

    I see you explains the setting with "the fictional world". The meaning of that is quite clear, and in liege with popular use of the word. Still: to me there is inherent danger in using it like that. Both players and designers have a tendency to think of the setting as something their characters "live in". I hold this perception to be untrue. As I see it the setting is created by the designer, as a set of tools used to inspire the fiction. And the fiction is created by the players, as they play the game, using said setting-tools (amongst others). The characters comes alive in the fiction, as does the "world" they live in. The setting has no life, no movable parts; it's a set of tools, to be used alongside the methodic tools to create a movable and dynamic fiction. That's why I use the fiction as a central element of itself in my nomenclature. I am vary of the popular use of it.

    I'm not particularly fond of the term "rules". It functions very well in boardgames, were the procedures are set. In role-playing games the term is slightly off, in my opinion. I cannot (or care not) serve any thorough argumentation for this, so lets just leave it at that.

    As for the term "form"; I do not like it. For one: the term could be misinterpreted to point towards the art-form. But mostly my dislike is due to the term indicating something stagnant, non-dynamic. I very much prefer the term method, as it carries the definition of "how we do it" in itself, and thus is indicative of something movable and dynamic.
