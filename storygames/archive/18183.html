\n I thought Maroons was about the Jamaican Maroons at first. Anyway, this game is totally fun. (Jason, I'm going to say a thing, let me know if you want me to edit it out for super secrecy). The game has semi-randomized character creation using stacks of cards. You choose a stack at random, and two of the cards you have to hand to others after reading them. What this means is that you immediately know important details about your fellows. You chose who to give them to, so it adds buy-in.

Jason, I haven't seen that in a chamber larp before, did you come up with that? I will steal it!

    \n Totally OK to discuss!

    Mathalus it's me bringing tabletop techniques and sensibilities to live action. See The Roach, Montsegur, Danger Patrol, the family tree should be pretty obvious.

    For a live action game this solves a few problems - it makes replayability pretty high since relationships are somewhat randomized and by putting all the character information on a few playing cards it is portable and easy to keep track of. And as a designer I can still control the possibility space - the game will always have, say, a trio of characters who have issues around power and authority for example, even though we don't know who will be in which position in that triangle.

    \n Played this game twice now, and the characters felt really different. Like, still on the same spectrum but not at all the same. It's a cool way to do something in the Lady Blackbird and Montsegur 1244 space of established characters, but allow for a variety of identities and relationships, in addition to character interpretations.

    The thing Jason says about certain things being emphasized and certain things fading into the background is also really true. In the first game, some of the family members being sick was a big issue, while in the second game it didn't really come up at all. Feels to me like watching multiple different stage interpretations of Shakespeare in that way. Sometimes Hamlet is really crazy, but sometimes he's angry or confused or full of trickery.

    In this larp, there are also two groups who start separated for a long period before they can interact with each other, which is weird for a chamber larp. Where does that show up besides boffer larps? I will steal it.
    \nBecause many one shot larps take place during conventions in hotels, the idea of starting in multiple locations and then converging is occasionally used.

    I have a fuzzy memory of having played a game where we were groups negotiating for control over the city - like crime bosses or something. We started in separate rooms planning our strategy, then converged for negotiations, then a twist happened and we used the separate rooms for different scenes with subgroups.

    jim pinto probably ran it. He ran most of the experimental larps I played in the late 90s and early 00s.

    But yes, the split and converge structure is uncommon for a parlor/chamber larp.

    The character creation through cards, some of which you assign to others (thereby knowing things about them) sounds like an awesome way to create a game you would want to play more than once, which is commonly an issue for larps. They tend to focus on either one-shot awesomeness or endless campaigns. Trying to capture something similar to the structured replayability you get from a Fiasco playset is a challenge.
