    \n Robert, I don't know how to construct a definition per se.

    But IMO, any identification of Innovation in RPGs should be holistic...at the level of the game as a whole, not at the level of individual rules or mechanics. A revolutionary new initiative system on a bog standard Fantasy Heart Breaker...isn't innovative...its one of the things that make it a heart breaker.

    Likewise a truly innovative game doesn't require unusual never seen before mechanics to be innovative. I'm actually opposed to envelope pushing / bleeding edge mechanics just for the sake of doing something different.

    My aforementioned BRS design goal is to demonstrate this by creating a truly innovative RPG game using absolutely nothing but mechanics that have been liberally lifted from other existing games.

    I'm not sure what you're really after in this thread, however.

    If what you're really after is "show me all your hawt never before seen rules", then I guess my only "issue" (such as it is) is that I'd prefer you didn't conflate that with being "innovative".

    If what you're really after is "show me how your game designs are innovative and pushing the art of design to new levels", then I guess my isse would be that you're missing the boat if you're looking for that at the level of individual rules.

    YMMV, of course, but its a distinction I think is important.

    \n I think Diaspora's explicit recognition of "table authority" as distinct from GM and/or player authority is novel. I don't know that it's unique. I do think it's implicit in a lot of other games. To some degree I think it's implicit in all games.

    Edit: specifically:


    We also talk frequently about \u201cthe table\u201d and many things happen in the context of the table\u2019s authority. The table is, simply, the sum of the players, the referee included, with all opinions weighed equally. The table is the consensus, and it is more important than any single player\u2019s authority, including the referee\u2019s. Many aspects of the game are explicitly under the power of the table, but it is true whether a game says so or not, that all of the game ultimately lies within the context of table authority. Referees and players alike should pay heed and seek consensus.

    So when we say \u201cat our table\u201d we are not talking about rules or even house rules per s\xe9, but rather interpretations arrived at consensually. We leave a lot here to your table and encourage you to seek genuine consensus when elaborating your Clusters, your Systems, and even your Characters. To this end we grant equal weight (though your table may choose otherwise) to all players throughout the First Session. Cluster, World, and Character creation are all egalitarian pursuits.

    We\u2019re also, then, tacitly acknowledging that every table is distinct. We wrote this game and play tested it, so it reflects the interests of our table. Your table, however, will necessarily create a different game. That\u2019s not just okay with us, or even expected, but rather that\u2019s awesome. And so, in realising this, we have decided to stay as far away from territory that belongs to your table as possible.

    This game recognizes that almost everything past the mechanical is your territory.

    \n Ribbon Drive is a game about road trips and self-reflection. It is GMless.

    The innovative rule is this: The last person to speak, in a given scene, has the option to introduce a conflict. You can't declare to the group that you have an idea for a conflict, though you can give a hand signal (holding up a "C" for conflict, silently).

    Here's what it looks like in play: People who have ideas on how to "push" the game in new directions need to wait for their turn to contribute, if that's what they want to contribute. People who want to explore character are given the floor. The people who are typically the loudest (myself included?) are silent and tuned in for most of the scene. Two people having good conflict ideas, but not being able to communicate them to each other, leads to one of three possibilities: (1.) one person decides to trust the importance of the other's contribution, (2.) one person finds a non-conflict way that they'd actually prefer to enter the scene, (3.) a stalemate results in a scene without conflict, where characters idly drive along the countryside - worried about many eventualities that may or may not ever come. All three results are beautiful and spot on for the game.

    \n ReCoil is the only roll for a number of successes system that I know of where every successful die rolled is used as a success. Specifically, if I roll 3 successes and you roll 5, you may spend 3 of your successes to succeed in a specific action. If that action was what I wanted to do in the first place, I cannot do that... but I can spend those three successes on another successful action. Alternately, if you rolled 5, you may spend two dice to hinder my action. It's not "You rolled 5 successes, I rolled 3. I guess that means I failed, because your 5 cancels my 3, leaving you with a net 2 successes."
    \n
    \nRats in the Walls is the only system that I know of that allows you to change things that were never established AND change things that were established. Player characters may be narrated to death by other players, and narrated right back to life again. The same is true of NPCs. Your dude can say a thing happened in his back story, then I can say it never happened, and it's all mechanically supported.
    \n
    \nMage Blade is the only game I know of to have a meta-mechanic where you dictate to the GM and other players what will happen in the story, and by accomplishing those things, gain more power to dictate, in addition to more traditional character effectiveness advancement. Specifically, the players set goals for their characters, and the GM's responsibility is to give them the opportunity to pursue those goals. The points granted as a reward for accomplishing your goal are usable for setting new goals and for advancing your character. Additionally, this same mechanic works as a pacing device for the over-arching story, placed collectively in the players' hands.
    \n
    \nNone of these games are complete or at least polished enough for me to consider publishing. Rats in the Walls and ReCoil are both available for playtesting, and Mage Blade is hung up on some thorny design areas. My other projects are less complete.

    \n Secrets and Lies is, as far as I know, the only RPG to use a draft and trade for character creation.

    Robert asked for a link, but be warned that it's a pay link. So I'll try to describe it.

    In the basic game, each player starts by drafting one negative trait. This can be an appearance flaw, lower social class, lower education, and so on.

    Then you draft something positive: natural beauty, good metabolism, better education or higher birth.

    Continue with three more rounds for age, job skill and "adventure" skills. All the while, players are barganing across the table, planning out which drafts are best for themselves and/or the group. No two players can take the same skill.

    Then there is a period of time for card trading, with more bargaining and planning.

    Then the GM goes over each character, discarding contradictory drafts and any Job skills that lack the necessary education (for example). Of course, they can advise the players during the whole process.

    In the Advanced Draft, all the positive attributes are laid out and drafted at the same time. The negatives are still drafted on the first round so as not to ruin people's planning.

    The result is that, by making character creation into a game, game play starts immediately. Creation is a social rather than a solitary experience. The greatest drawback is that you can't come into it with an immutable character concept. This isn't the only game that does so (Spirit of the Century comes to mind), but it's how Secrets and Lies did it.

    -Grant

    \n In Beast Hunters, conflict/maneuver resolution is unique in that:

    a. The player who is facing the adversity sets both the pool for the whole adventure and the limit that the challenger can spend on each conflict;
    b. The challenger uses the pool to create diverse opposition on the spot (i.e., it's not just a Primetime Adventure budget expense, it's creation of tactically-varied opposition); and
    c. The challenger offers a success level for each maneuver that the player undertakes, and the player can take the success or go to dice for a risk of gaining more or less.

    This trifecta empowers players and alleviates the burden on challengers.

    If you need to focus on one specific unique aspect, take c. I don't think that had been done before.

    \nI had never seen a game that had customizable magic before. It was either "always the same effect, no matter who casts a spell" or "wildly open rules and magic systems with no real system behind them". So, for Apocalypse Prevention, Inc, I introduced the idea of learning spells, but then the magic-user can personalize certain elements through practice and research.
    \n
    \nFor instance, if we both learned how to throw a fireball, then we learn the basic incantation to conjure up the flame and launch it at out opponents. However, we both have different personal styles and our fireballs shouldn't be exactly the same. So, I decide to learn how to make mine travel farther. You then learn how to intensify the amount of damage yours can inflict. I then learn tricks to make it take less time for me to cast my spell than you and you learn to make yours explode when it hits its target, hitting others in the area. I could then make mine heat seeking and you could decide to make casting said spell take less effort. So, we learn the same spell but implement it in different ways.
    \n
    \nI've gotten a lot of great feedback about this feature of my game. If you guys know of others games where this type of magic system exists, I'd love to know. I may be able to get more inspiration to make my system even better perhaps.

    \nI used a riff off of Universalis and Story Engine for a PBEM SF game a while back, and did something much like that.     Posted By: David ArtmanPosted By: Great WolfInShowdown, certain attacks deal damage to your opponent's character concept.
    Ooo! Unpack, please. One example or something.

    It works in two parts.

    Part The First:

    When you make your character, you define four Qualities about him. These are specificially of the form "My character thinks that he is/has _______". These Qualities are narration constraints; both players must narrate consistently with these Qualities and may not narrate against these Qualities.

    Example Quality (from my last playtest) "My character thinks that he is a family man." So, I could legitimately object if you narrated my guy sleeping around. He wouldn't do that! He's a family man!

    Part The Second:

    When you win the Flashback part of the round, among other things, you earn the right to rewrite one of your opponent's Qualities. The rewrite is specifically of the form "...but really he is/has _____". Doing so removes the old Quality and creates the new Quality. All the rules on Qualities apply

    Example Quality (from my last playtest) "My character things that he is a family man, but really he has a violent temper." Now, not only have I lost the Quality of "family man", I've gained the Quality of "violent temper". This Quality (in addition to the "manipulative" Quality I had picked up earlier) led to my character getting his girlfriend to lose her temper in front of the children, while she was in the middle of moving out on him. Yeah, vengeance.

    Never mind that my original concept was a calm, collected fighter for good and righter of wrongs. Oh no. All that was taken from me, one Quality at a time.

    Does that answer your question, David?

    Seth Ben-Ezra

      Albedo Platinum Catalyst has this really excellent rule that the designer doesn't know is as awesome as it is. It's hardish military SF with anthropomorphic animals and no humans. (The reason they're anthropomorphic animals is wondered at by characters as much as players).

      \n

      You have a regular character sheet with stats, skills, and equipment. Nothing interesting here. Move along to the bottom of the character sheet. At the bottom are the guys in your unit. They've each got a job, a rank, and a name. They're played by the GM unless you're in combat, in which case you pretty much control them. So they develop personalities, jokes, and relationships. And this is where it's awesome: you can't die until they're all dead. They're your hit points.

      \n Christian,

      There are two versions of Hong Kong Action Theater (HKAT). The later I believe was published by Guardians of Order. The main idea was instead of playing the movie heroes, you played the movies stars. So you had things like catch phrases, the ability to die and come back next movie, and a fluctuating stat called star power. You bid star power for your role in the movie and your effectiveness during that movie depended on both your base stats and the importance of your movie role.


      On the note of innovations from some games you may not have heard of:

      The original printing of the Everlasting: Book of the Unliving contains a detailed ritual which may be performed before play - making explicit the shamanistic idea of story-telling. Interestingly enough this section seems to be omitted in the currently in-print version of the book.

      Underworld used pocket change as the randomizer.

      In Spookshow you must roll every round to determine how corporeal you are - with benefits and penalties depending on the outcome - this roll involved two unlike dice (d6 and d10) to produce a variety of interesting outcomes (matches, highest values, both 1's, etc.).

      Here I though my own GLASS rules were the only ones that had a toggle that basically flipped them from high-simulation (and gamism) to high-narration. I use the terms World and Player Priority: Toggle on the former, and anything can kill you if it chooses to and you (or your allies) can't stop it; in the latter mode, you can only be killed if you Commit in a situation and are then reduced to 0 Health. The former is "World" Priority because verisimilitude takes precedence of protagonism; the latter is "Player" Priority because each player (alone) chooses when to risk it all, free from "whiff-like" vagaries of chance or "griefing" villainy of intent.

      Posted By: Robert BohlPost to this thread and tell me one thing (only one per game) that you think the game you designed does that, as far as you know, no tabletop roleplaying game has done before. Please provide a link to your game, tell what the rule is, and what its result is.
      \nIn Ars Magica, you use a point buy system to design your home, in a medieval world with magic. My new rule ("published in "Covenants") was that castles have a negative point value. The effects are that:

      * In the previous system, most groups spent about a third of their points on their castle, and if you accept a point as a vote, it centred the game on the castle4's defences, making the world the sort of place where sieges were likely.
      * It makes home design easier and faster.
      * It makes it clear that if you own a castle then you are volunteering for stories in which the local nobility are peeved with you, and its actually those stories that are the basis for the negative points. If there are no stories, you can have whatever you like and its not important, so not worth points. That is, it sets the tone of the vanilla setting better.

      \nI'd suggest you look at spontaneous casting in Ars Magica. In Ars Magica, your character has two ways of doing magic: formulaic and spontaneous. Formulaic spells are things you have learned from books, so they are just like the writer's version but with a personal twist on top called a sigil (for example, my character's sigil is oranges, so their smell, taste or colour shows up in all of his spell effects.) The other type, Spontaneous, is where you choose a spell effect base level (say a ball of fire is level 20) and you then up that based on what you choose as your Range, Target and Duration. So, you have basically talked about Range, damage (which is base level), Tatget (a bigger area), Requisites (in the heat seeking case) and some Mastery abilities (which is where you can cast formulaics more like spontaneous spells because you have spent experience ot be damn good at them).

      So, basically if you want to join the fun, we'd like to see you over on the atlas-games forums. Also, the previous version of Ars is downloadable for -free-, so where the harm in checking it out?
