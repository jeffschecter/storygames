\n Roleplaying games are not really games.

They are game engines.

The real game doesn't exist until the game master writes an adventure. The adventure is what happens during the session, and I would like to see some roleplaying games that enforced how to play a session. I will therefor take a lot of different roleplaying games and see how they explain how a scenario should be written, because there is where I think the game actually exist.\n

Method

Each game in this thread will have
1. Its name and edition,
2. a short quotation to show what the game is about,
3. followed by a summarized version of the step by step procedure used to create the scenario.
It might follow a personal comment about that procedure. Feel free to contribute with more games using this structure.

How To Sort Each Method Into Different Models
I will also use a couple of terms along the way to loosely categorize the roleplaying games in this thread to help me with future analysis. I will try to read with on open mind and apply all terms accordingly until one, or more, categories fits the game. The first sentence in each explanation is the requirement I have to see if it's fits that particular category.\n
Sequential play: have a linear structure of one scene leading to another. There are several substructures of sequential play. \xbbLinear play\xab has fixed scenes from start to end. \xbbAssembly play\xab has scenes that must happen but can happen in any order. \xbbBallistic play\xab has a fixed beginning and end but the path between those two is unclear. Sequential play is used to create a sensation for the players.

Fish tank: focuses on the revealing relations between different factions to the players, to make either the players act or the factions react to the players' actions. Often suitable for mysteries or intrigues.

Sandbox: creates places for the players to explore. You can see it as an amusement park, where the players can pick any ride in any order. Each place can have factions and occurring events. It's mostly a model used for exploring and discovering.

Bang driven: the model gives out flags to the players and each flag is a signal for what the player wants the adventure to be about. From these flags, the game master creates \xbbbangs\xab to invoke emotional or moral choices. It's not a model I've used at all, only experienced as a player, but I guess bangs are best used for creating drama through dilemmas.

Collaborative storytelling: improvising a plot, story, setting, or the background of any of these. It's a model that embraces self-expression, creativity, and the feeling of togetherness.
\nWhen I feel that I got enough material, I will start to compare the different games and their structures.\n

    \nDEADLANDS [sandbox]
    1st edition\n
    The year is 1876, but the history is not our own. The American Civil War rages on, heither side able to establish a clear advantage. Most of California has fallen into the sea. The Sioux Nations have reclaimed the Dakotas. And the dead walk among us.
    \n
    1. The Story So Far. Create a backstory to help the game master improvise reactions.
      2. \n
    2. The Setup. Hooks the characters onto the story. Two advice is given; to keep the character poor to use money as a drive; to have the players explain why they are involved in the mess.
      3. \n
    3. Chapters. Each chapter describes a location, an event that takes place there and clues to what's going on.

    \nUNKNOWN ARMIES [fish tank]
    2nd edition\n
    Beneath the living mirror of heaven, beneath the world of our desires, there are streets with secret names. They connect the back alleys of civilization with the urine-stained vacant lots of the cosmos. /.../ That world is the occult underground. Find it, before it finds you.
    \n
    1. Choose a theme. The book gives out a couple of suggestions.
      2. \n
    2. Pick an instigator. It's antagonist that makes things going, and suggestions can be found all over in the book.
      3. \n
    3. Instigation. Create a key event that kick things off, and let the instigations form it. Some suggestions are given.
      4. \n
    4. McGuffin. What the instigator wants.
      5. \n
    5. Interested parties. More factions wanting the McGuffin.
      6. \n
    6. Scatter all these things around on a paper.
      7. \n
    7. Create relations between them by drawing lines. Describe each line, but for every line that points one way, an opposite line should be drawn and described. A list of descriptors are given.
      8. \n
    8. Write a summary of what the campaign is about.
      9. \n
    9. Create one or several hook for the characters.
    \nThe book then explains how to use this fish tank during play. It's basically a relationship map for intrigues that will create consequences of the players' actions.

    \nFENG SHUI [sequential play]
    1st edition\n
    I could get the position of the five remaining mooks from the reflection in the mirrored windows of a nearby Saab. I took a chance, turned, and broke for a spot behind a panel van. On the way: BANG BANG BANG. Three went down, but one of them popped up again. Okay, so I was having an off day.
    \n
    1. Create three set pieces. Look at pictures of three places and imagine a fight going on in each one of them.
      2. \n
    2. For each picture, write a list of things that could happen during the fight.
      3. \n
    3. Create a backstory, that connects the three set pieces.
      4. \n
    4. Create hooks to get the characters involved.
      5. \n
    5. Create clue paths so the characters can get from one set piece to another.

    \nARCHIPELAGO [collaborative storytelling]
    3rd edition\n
    This game works best if you play it slow. Sometimes, the best thing to do is wait a little and see how things unfold. Ged stayed with Ogion for years, learning about the old language, the names of flower petals and bugs. There\u2019s time to let the characters evolve.

    Breathe in. Breathe out. Take your time.
    \n
    1. Decide on a setting.\n
      • Create a map or use an existing one.
      • Decide a few elements that are important for the setting, and which participant that got the ownership of which element.
        • \n
      • Name and describe at least five places.
      2. \n
    2. Create characters. Do it out loud so others have a feeling of who the character is. Create an indirect relationship to one of the other characters.
      3. \n
    3. Each participant writes a destiny point for each of the other character. The player playing the character chooses one of the suggestions. This is the end of that character's story arc and the character cannot die until the goal is fulfilled.
      4. \n
    4. Take turns and...\n
      • ... frame the scene. Provide a starting structure by telling where you are and what's going on.
        • \n
      • ... portray the character by showing the reactions to what's going on. Other participants can influence the scene by using ritual phrases or narrating certain elements that the participant got ownership over.
        • \n
      • ... finish the scene.
      5. \n
    5. When all characters have reached their destiny points, the session should come to an end.

    \nINSPECTRES [collaborative storytelling]
    1st edition\n
    So if it's ghosts or goblins, spooks, bogies or bumps in the night, you can rest easy knowing that our trained staff is on the case. InSpectres: Because it's not just a vampiric infestation, it's your vampiric infestation!
    \n
    1. Getting the call. The game master rolls four times on a client chart to get who wants help and vague descriptions of how the paranormal phenomenon takes shape.
      2. \n
    2. Research/Investigation. The players come up with a possible explanation.
      3. \n
    3. Suiting up. Acquire the right equipment for the job.
      4. \n
    4. Fieldwork. The characters travels to the location and fixing the problem any way they want.
      5. \n
    5. Successful rolls in any act acquire Franchise Dice, and when the pot is enough, the mission is done.
    \nThis game is interesting because it has a solid act structure, so one might think it should be sequential play. The acts only gives out a theme in form of a goal, and each goal strives towards the goal in the last act. What happens in each scene within the act is still open, hence this game being collaborative storytelling. This is a difference between InSpectres and the next game below.

    \nTHIS IS PULP [sequential play, collaborative storytelling]
    not published\n
    Explore ancient pyramids, brave the dangers of the jungle, or rescue a lost expedition from cannibals. The most exciting adventures from movies like Indiana Jones, The Rocketeer and King Kong can come alive before your eyes.
    \n
    1. Create a map with tropes typical for this genre. Each person will create two tropes each. The game gives suggestions of what the tropes consist of.
      2. \n
    2. Draw characters. Each player draws three and picks one.
      3. \n
    3. The first scene.
      a. The game master rolls on the Where Are We? chart.
      b. Icebreaker. An exercise is done on the location to teach the players how to communicate in the game.
      c. Introduce the characters, and create a common goal that includes one of the tropes.
      d. Danger. The game master creates a danger on the scene, using tokens as a pacing mechanism.
      e. The players describe their ways through the danger, removing tokens as they go
    4. When the tokens are gone, the scene is over. Create a new scene and repeat step d-e.
    5. The game master got tokens for each act to spend on dangers in each scene. When the tokens are gone, the act is done.
    6. The game is over when the third act is done.
      7. \n
    7. Epilogue. Everybody discusses how the adventure ends.
    \nEven if everybody contribute in creating tropes on the map, and even if scenes are created in an ad hoc style, it's still one person that enforces the scenes upon the others. The structure of play in this game creates a starting point and an end, but tropes can appear in any order. This is typical for ballistic play. The game is however also about collaborating in building a story, where the character cannot fail. This game creates an exchange between the game master and the players as if they were in a conversation. The game does that by imposing a structure for the communication through the ice breaker.

    \nTRAIL OF CTHULHU [sequential play (or fish tank?)]
    1st edition\n
    You are among the few who suspect the truth - about the mad gods at the center of the universe, about the Great Old Ones who dream of clearing off the Earth, about th extra terrestrials who use mankind in their experiments, about the ancient legends of undying evil that are all coming true.
    \n
    1. The hook. Create something that gets the investigators' attention. The book gives some examples.
      2. \n
    2. The horrible truth. "the Keeper determines what the Big Bad is, how it got there, what it wants, and where it's going. /.../ The Keeper then determines what will happen if the horror goes unchecked..."
    3. Victory Condition. Decide what has to happen to solve this issue.
      4. \n
    4. Trail of clues. Create a clue path from the hook to an understanding of what's going on, revealing or triggering the victory condition along the way. The book also talks about floating clues (free to throw in at any time) and leveraged clues (revealed from interrogation). The book gives examples of scenes, but I couldn't find any good guidelines in how to think to create those scenes.
      5. \n
    5. Optional: antagonist reactions. Plot out their possible reactions for when the players accomplish something.
      6. \n
    6. Spine. Either you got a straight line with scenes where improvisation is to be able to throw them around, or you got loose notes on things that can occur.
      7. \n
    7. If you got a loose structure, create scenes around the spine.
    \nWhen I read what Robin D Laws wrote as a response to the critique that Esoterrorist, that Trail of Cthulhu is based upon, and how that game's structure of play makes a freer experience, I instantly thought of the fish tank. When playing an intrigue, using the fish tank, I sometimes felt that the scenario played itself outside my control. When reading the model for writing an adventure for Trail of Cthulhu, I feel that the text suffers from double personality disorder. At one side there is clue paths and a straight line, and on the other side it's a more free form by creating reactions of the players' actions. The book also spends two pages on explaining how to not railroad using clue paths, mostly by making the notes vague and not the reveals of clues dependable on scenes or events so they can be introduced at any time.

    Thirty pages after the text about adventure writing, I also find this: "When creating an adventure, use this table to record which abilities are essential to the adventure, which are optional, and which are not used at all. Let your players know which not to take. If you are using pre-existing characters, /.../ then build your adventure around them. [the abilities]" Why isn't this part of the text for how to write an adventure?

    \nSPIRIT OF THE CENTURY [sequential play]
    The Structured Pickup Game
    'net edition\n
    The Great War is over, and the world is a darker place, rippling under the surface with unsee dangers - dangers few are even aware of. /.../ One organization stands in their way. The Century Club.
    \n
    1. Endanger the Characters. In media res style. The book gives some examples.
      2. \n
    2. Reveal the True Danger. Present new information to explain the previous point. This new information should lead to a new place. The book gives some examples.
      3. \n
    3. The Pursuit Encounters Complications. The journey to the next place has obstacles. Examples can be found in the book.
      4. \n
    4. Certain Doom. Ramp up the previous complication. Some examples are given in the book.
      5. \n
    5. The Twist. Reveal information that turns things around, and sometimes to save the characters from the previous certain doom. Suggestions are given in the book.
      6. \n
    6. Final Showdown. Arriving at the new place and fighting the "boss". Guidelines and variations are given.
      7. \n
    7. Optional: Breakneck Escape. The place may be threatened by destruction. A few examples can be found in the book.
      8. \n
    8. Wrapping Up. The characters' heroism is appreciated and rewarded.
    \nSpirit of the Century got three suggested models for the game master to follow. This is the first one. All three models should be able to execute while improvising.

    I got the book, but I don't think there are any major changes compared to the free online SRD.

    \nSPIRIT OF THE CENTURY [bang driven]
    The Aspected Pickup Game
    'net edition\n
    1. Pick one or two aspects from each character sheet that you want to incorporate in the adventure. Pick more for games to last longer.
      2. \n
    2. Think up situations to put each aspect at test. This will create tension. You can even pair aspects against each other.
      3. \n
    3. Think about how these situations can be related and you got an adventure seed.
      4. \n
    4. Start off with one situation.
      5. \n
    5. Continue to throw situations on the players when the pacing is slowing down. Riff off the players' choices to create consequences.
    \nHaven't played much bang driven games, game mastered one of them one time and read a few of them. It was hard to write a summary of this one because the author mostly talked through examples, so I had to re-engineer some of its parts. A lot of tips are given, especially about pacing, and that the players' decisions in these dilemma situations are the things that should be given the most weight.

    \nSPIRIT OF THE CENTURY [fish tank]
    The Dynamic Pickup Game
    'net edition\n
    1. What is the hook? It's something people would want to have, like a person, item, information, or place.
      2. \n
    2. Who wants it? It can be a person, an organization, or a henchman.
      3. \n
    3. What is that person going to do with it? Ask yourself what the next step is for each person when they get their hands on it.
      4. \n
    4. Is that enough? Repeat step 2-3 until there are sufficient material to move on, enough for creating several conflicting agendas. There is a notation to involve the characters' aspects into the NPC's agenda
      5. \n
    5. Think of a storyline that had happened if the character weren't involved.
      6. \n
    6. Put the characters somewhere in that storyline. If they do something, answer with the proper consequence brought by any conflicting agenda.
    \nThere are advice for pacing and how to expand or change the scenario on the fly.

    This was the last model for Spirit of the Century.

    \nDrakar och Demoner [sequential play]
    Eng. Dragons and Demons
    3rd edition\n
    Forget everything you learned about games. A roleplaying game is not a "game" in common meaning. It's rather about you, together with your friends, experiencing a series of adventures in a world far beyond the boring reality of everyday life.
    \n
    1. Start with the last point below and work backwards. A summary later in the text repeats these as questions.
      • Create a motivation; to push the adventurers in the right directions. Few examples are given.
        - What could the players' goals be?
        - How would the characters get the information that is needed for the adventure?
        - How much information should be given? Are any non-playable character interested in the adventure?
        • \n
      • Decide where the adventurers should travel. One or several things should happen along the way. Some examples are given.
        - Where do you (the game master) want the characters to travel?
        • \n
      • Create the highlight at the end of the journey. It usually comes in form of a bossfight.
        - Who (or what) is/are the characters' enemy/enemies?
    2. For a longer adventure, that stretches over several sessions, create more highlights in form of side quests or required places to pass before the final showdown.
      3. \n
    3. Draw a map of the area. The text gives guidelines of what kind of maps that are needed and what they should contain.
      4. \n
    4. Prepare a random encounter table, and monsters or people that will stand in the characters way.
    \nThe first edition were a pure translation from RuneQuest but grew to be something else in the second edition. It was the biggest roleplaying game in Sweden. This edition was published '85, and it didn't come with a world of its own so the book had a suggestion to create maps and lots of non-playable characters that inhabited that world.

    \nNoir [bang driven]
    1st edition\n
    The streets darkens by the gigantic zeppelins crossing between the air ports. A constant thumping comes from the machines. It's true that the smoke from the chimneys spreads a sick fog over the city and that the acid rain falls often, but in the womb of the despotism that the city stands for, beauty and freedom can be found.
    \n
    1. The storyteller and the players discuss what the story should be about. Should it depend on the main characters choices or is it an intrigue?
      2. \n
    2. The storyteller may want to prepare a relationship map before the main characters are created, but should be prepared that it might be revised.
      3. \n
    3. During character generation, that should be executed as a group, the storyteller identifies the players' flags.
      4. \n
    4. The storyteller draws a relationship map, that mainly consist of the main characters and the people they got relations to. The NPCs are created with the flags in mind.
      5. \n
    5. The storyteller creates bangs, situations meant to give the players open choices when it comes to how the story and the main characters are developed. The bangs often relates to the flags (point 3).
      6. \n
    6. Depending on what the discussion in point 1, the storyteller creates an intrigue and thinks about a possible chain of events.
    \nThis is a roleplaying game in Swedish.

    \nDying Earth [sequential play]
    1st edition\n
    In ages gone [...] a thousand spells were known to sorcery, and the wizards effected their wills. Today, as Earth dies, a hundred spells remain to man's knowledge, and these have come to us through the ancient books.
    \n
    1. Make up a weird custom in an isolated village.
      2. \n
    2. The characters should break this custom. The game suggest several ways of how this should happen, mostly by the use of game mechanics because the characters can be convinced by successful rolls in social skills, or be tempted by a predefined weakness of the player's choice.
      3. \n
    3. The characters must in some way solve this precarious situation that they walk into, often by doing a task.
      4. \n
    4. Setup. The characters will be drawn into 1-3 con artists' plans during the course of the adventure.
    \nThis was a hard adventure model to write because it consisted of a list of nine points, but half of them only add flavor. The game also have a mechanic where the players must try to say two prewritten lines each during the session, and one suggestion was to improvise a whole session based on the situations that the players have to come up with to use those lines.

    NOTE: a third game from Robin D Laws. All are sequential play.
