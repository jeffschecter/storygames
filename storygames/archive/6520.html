    So rules are for maintaining fairness and order in the game. Be them formal, explicit rules, or casual, implicit ones.

    \n Well, a legal theorist's view.

    Some rules are prohibitive. They suppress conduct that is undersirable (don't kill people).

    Some rules are constitutive. They enable something that could not exist without the rule (corporations law permits the creation of "artificial persons" to act as vehicles of limited liability investment etc., condominium law makes "parcels of air" in to real estate, etc.).

    Many rules have elements of both of these - the first still-intact legal code in history stated that it was not acceptable for a member of the upper class to beat a member of the lower class for refusing to sell them something at the price offered by upper classer. This is both part of suppressing coercive conduct amounting to theft and also part of creating a free market.

    A game's rules suppress conduct that is not determined to be valid in the game. This is usually because "it would be less fair, less fun". The rules of some sports are adjusted, for example, to "increase the fun" (e.g., the fastplay version of cricket that is gaining popularity world wide). It is more fun if the rules say there is some risk of my intent not coming to pass (as this creates tension and uncertainty, anticipation etc.).

    A game's rules constitute things to be then pursued. Just as some law creates forms of property a rule defines an advantage or benefit, some "cookie" that play pursues. Magic items, experience, gold, "points towards the next advance". Various designers have noted that "people love any kind of currency/counter, create something that allows they to rack up some kind of points and that alone can increase their enjoyment". Baseball constitutes "bases" to progress around for example, where "gains are made but not yet solidified". That "increments towards success that can still be lost" element is common in many games and sports for it permits a feeling of progress, the tension of win/loss, and continues it as "it ain't over till it's over".

    Rob

    \n I tend to agree with Vincent (he has an earlier version of the same idea on Anyway that's a little more reader-friendly, probably on the Hardcore Theory page).

    Other things rules do, in my opinion:

    --Help apportion authority and spotlight. For instance, a good set of rules can guarantee that no one person dominates the game.
    --Help resolve conflicts between players
    --Help resolve conflicts or even just creative differences/discussions, etc, _quickly_ (i.e. help everyone save some time--for example, a vote can help a group decide _which_ game to play on a given night than just sitting around and arguing about it)
    --Help create dynamics, either within the story, or between the players, consistently (a given group may not be able to create a thrilling moral dilemma every time; a certain set of rules may exist that can help them achieve it more consistently)
    --Establish fair ground for competition (without it, some forms of enjoyable competition are impossible--just imagine playing chess without having rules for where and how certain pieces can move)
    --Push people to do things they're not normally comfortable with (but may still enjoy and want). An easy example might be, "I am an immersionist/actor stance player, and I want to act so as to fight for what's best for my character all the time, but I most enjoy games where my character gets into real trouble and significant danger and I have to sweat a little."

    \n We need rules because there are distinct pleasures which gamers pursue that require rules to exist.

    If I want the fun of tinkering with a simulation, I need a simulation to tinker with.
    If I want to be guided to emulating the fiction in play without needing to think about it, good rules can do that.
    If I want to gamble and win, I need something to gamble.
    If I want to feel like I've really won something, there had to be a chance of losing.

    ...And that's just for starters.

    Of course, if I want to improvise in a freewheeling way? Well. Then I may not want such things, or may want rules I can improvise with, adapting them moment-to-moment.

    \n You have to have rules otherwise nobody can play with you (most of all Joshua Newman). How are they supposed to know what to do?

    That said, I've played freeform tabletop games were people just riffed off the background and each other. With the right people, i.e. those who know what to expect and how to play unsafe, this works really well. But even if you don't have explicit rules , there is an older deeper magic that controls the game. Things like narrative consistency, closure, reincorporation, doing the obvious, not blocking. These aren't rules as such, there's no in game penalty for breaking them (because there are no rules), except that perhaps the other players won't talk to you much if you do. If you've been sent to Coventry you can't join in, play, so it's not in your interest to do so.

    There's a balance to be struck between bringing in new ideas and developing old ones, between change and status quo. The groups that can address these issues without recourse to explicit rules are likely to have a stimulating time.

    Which is not to say that rules are bad. Rules encourage a certain kind of play. If you want to give that kind of guiding hand, then that's interesting to explore too. There are always some parameters to play and it's fun to play with them.

    To the address the issue of how freeform can be a game, there are several things up for grabs in the game:
    - status
    - narrative control
    - narrative function
    - creation of conflicts
    - closure of conflicts (or not)
    - performance
    - persuasion
    - creative input
    - etc...
    Players compete for or with these aspects of the game. I'd agree that it's not a game if your doing this on your own but as soon as there's somebody else just the fact that you don't have direct access to their inner mental states creates a space to explore, playfully.

    \n It's got nothing to do with taste, or "some people like playing with rules". There are always rules - sometimes they are unspoken or unwritten, sometimes they are explicit and agreed upon, etc. ad nauseum.

    The only thing here where "taste" comes in as a factor, is the term Rule. Oooh, Rule, that's something you must adhere to, like the Law or something, that can't be good for anything, right? If you suffer from that, often adolescent, problem, call it something else instead. Procedures, agreements, boundaries or what ever rocks your "stick it to the man" boat. Then you'll be fine.

    That was just me knee-jerking because JD decided to answer "What do we need rules for again?" with "You don't have to have rules." Ah, the internet.

    \n The thing with freeform is that eventually your going to get in each others way somehow. And it's going to come out of the blue.

    Rules let you know how your going to get in the way of each other, well in advance. So you know if that sort of getting in the way of each other is what you want and not turn up if you don't like it (while with freeform, you've already turned up). It also tends to let you know the other person has consented to you getting in his way, rather than it being a 'OMG wut r u doen 2 mi character!!!!!1111!!!one!?' moment.

    \n Sam,

    I don't think Vincent is saying that the unwelcome cannot, will never happen without rules. He's saying the rules help make it more accessible, or more reliably accessible. Force you to throw in stuff you might otherwise avoid or not come up with.

    It's like the Oracles in IaWA: you can just as easily sit down and create your own situation from scratch, but after a while most people might start repeating themselves too much and want something different. A procedure like the Oracle draw can help you create new stuff all the time instead of falling into a rut.
