    \n Something that for some reason helped me:

    1) Naps. If nothing is going on for an hour or two, and your hotel is nearby, go back and take a nap for a few hours. You'll be refreshed for the evening.

    2) Exercise/Relaxation. I do yoga because I'm a filthy hippie. But, to loosen up I also would wake up sooper-early and hit the jacuzzis in the hotel, and more often than not there was no one else there (as opposed to after dinnertime, when Everyone is there), like before breakfast (or earlier, then return to my room to sleep for an hour. That worked well.

    Other times, I would hit the gym. Most hotels have a shitty gym with second-rate equipment. I would do some weights once a day for even 15-20 minutes, which also helped (especially after nap).

    3) If you have a pillow or blanket that helps you sleep better (therapudic pillow, etc), bring it.

    4) If you're working the Forge booth, make sure to wander around to the other halls as well. See what's happening over in board games and minis, there's usually crazy-cool shit going down that you'd never be exposed to if you didn't check it out.

    5) If you're an RPGNet regular, it looks like on Sat night there's going to be an RPGNet gathering at some hotel lounge, for handshaking, greeting, and some gaming. I'm totally going.

    6) I make this rec every year, but what I do is on the first day I wander the dealer's hall, buy stuff that's on my Must Buy list, then immediately take it outside the hall to the UPS booth, and mail it back to myself so I don't have to lug it around. I picked up that lesson from the huge Japanese Komiket (the largest comic convention on the planet), and it served me well for several years. My stuff is usually waiting for me by the time I come home, in great condition. You're not going to have time to read ALL the crap you buy at GenCon, after all...

    \nFood Notes

    Man cannot survive on Steak & Shake alone. Well, perhaps you can for 4 days, but you will most likely feel like shit around Day 3, if not earlier. In the evening, try to get a ways away from the convention center and find something reasonably healthy that will balance out all those double cheeseburgers you live on during the daylight hours.

    EDIT: Crossposted with The Keith.

    Games On Demand

    If you find yourself with nothing to do, come to the GOD room. There will be games happening ALL THE TIME, with mad stacks of hot indie rpgs to pick up and play with whoever's there. This is your chance to try out Game X that you've always wanted to play and never have. Hell, bring along something that you just bought at some booth, which no one's even heard of before, and we'll test drive it right there. Boom. Throwdown at the hoedown.

    \n Furthermore on the food idea: sit-down places that aren't fast-food are your friends. The lighting is usually less harsh that convention lighting, the chance to sit down for an hour in a booth would probably be welcomed by your feet & back, and being served refreshing beverages by someone when you run low is just enough of a morale booster to pick you up. For the extra cash you pay, you're getting a bit of a relaxing environment. I enjoyed going to The Ram last year -- I can't remember where else I went, though I know I didn't get to St. Elmo's then (and that must be corrected).

    Of course, as with everything at Indy, expect crowds and long lines, but it's worth it just to get out of the convention center for a bit.

    I'm also a fan of two showers a day at busy cons -- one for the morning "not smell like the stereotype" freshness, and one to rinse off con funk (physical & metaphorical) and give yourself a bit of chill time. But I find hot running water therapeutic in general.

    1) Friends approach each other cautiously.
    2) Friends look at each other's badges to confirm that they've met online.

I'm much more a fan of granola bars and the less-sweet breakfast bars, than energy bars--they tend to actually taste like food, they're like a quarter the price, and i've found many of them are nutritionally as good or better (especially since you're not at Spring Training, or otherwise putting huge demands on your body, so the extra protein, frex, of the energy bars is just wasted).       Posted By: Robert BohlYeah it's funny, when I took a plane the first time to Gen Con I'm like, "Ok, so those 8 people are going for the same reason as me." We have geekdar.
      \nI like striking up conversations with other folks who are obviously going. You get to hear what other people are into, and can make some friends, too!

      Two years ago when I flew in from Charlotte, I got on the plane with this dude from Atlanta. He was the store manager at a Circuit City in Atlanta. he was big into RPGA: Apparently there's some sort of Rome-themed "Living" D&D game in the RPGA (later some company released a d20 core book and some supplements), and this guy and his group were some of the longest-playing members of that campaign. It was interesting to hear about the RPGA gigs, how things go down these days (I was a member way back when: It was the only way to get into events at GenCon),etc.

      The coolest part? That Circuit City was doing some sort of network upgrade/switch thing for all the stores in the area. The manager was not specifically needed, but the executives at Circuit City commanded that all managers be there "just in case". The switchover was pretty foolproof, and really didn't require anyone; plus, if shit did go down the crapper, the manager could literally do nothing: The executives/national chain would fix it, the servicemen were contractors, and this all could have been run by assistant managers with no problem.

      He rarely takes vacations, but GenCon was a sacred time. Everyone knew that there is One Week a year when he will not be at work, Period. He schedules it one year in advance, and has gone to GenCon every year that he'd been manager there, some number of years (I forgot if he said 7, 9 or 13 years,but one of those). And yet, with all the above, the national chain execs still said, "Sorry, you have to be there anyway. It's your responsibility."

      So he quit his job to go to GenCon.

      That, my friends, is some brass balls with steely commitment.

      I kept asking if he's cool with it, he looked like that decision was the easiest decision he'd ever made. Like, "Do you want $100? Or do you want to get punched in the face?" It sounds like he had some other gig lined up for when he got back, but still. His commitment floored me. I wanted to wave a flag or something, it was that awesome.

      So, the moral of the story is: You've got an hour before the plane boards. Find some fellow geeks and say "Hi"!
