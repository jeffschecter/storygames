    \n After getting burned by GMing a chain of campaigns of a trad game, trying to prepare for everything started to feel as the wrong approach to me. So I went pursuing GMless/GMfull designs, only to find their focus was too far away from what I was looking for. I like to be able to express myself creatively and eventually learned to impro and enjoy playing unsafe, but I couldn't ask the same from my players.

    It's not that I don't trust their creativity, we are used to write comic books together and brainstorm stuff, it's mostly that when checking GMless games I found most of the ones I read asked for creative input in a way that didn't helped players prone to blank page syndrome or/due to analysis paralysis. It's certainly a skill that can be learnt and it doesn't mean you need to make quality material just to play, you can keep your input simple and later build over other players input; the quality material will then appear by itself as a byproduct of engaging the system. However it was mostly how those systems ask stuff from the players the thing that ticked me off.

    As for GMless games, those that come with integrated fixed themes and viewpoints of those themes are definitely the best, but then I never found one with a theme or viewpoint I'd like to play, and had to accept that my own tastes on this tend to change over the course of a game -often depending on the mood of the group- and that I was looking for versatility more than anything.

    So I ended up going for the easiest solution: go back to trad GMing but integrating all the things I learned from GMfull games I could, like:

    - Brainstorming the setting with the players (manage to reduce those to 8 questions)
    - Ask the players about each choice they took in their character sheet, to develop more content from there and add it to the setting.
    - Handling the players some keywords for them to turn it into rumours, which they can follow until they become a quest.
    - Mountain-witch the players when I had no answers as a GM.
    - Keep building upon player input (like consequences of the PC actions, etc) through the game.
    - Play to find out.

    It has worked for me so far but I'm really interested on you vision about GMless games, I also felt that they could be better but didn't get too far exploring that path.

    \n I think the main problems/issues GMless games face are:

    * Challenges in establishing and maintaining consistent tone (particularly across a disparate group of players with varied tastes).

    * So much weight placed on group consensus that communication at the table isn't always simple or smooth. WHO gets to talk WHEN, and HOW MUCH are they allowed to say?

    * Vincent Baker had said in the past that the purpose of RPG rules is to fruitfully maintain a conflict of interests (usually in the fiction). Freeform collaborative gaming does not do this well, or, often, at all.

    * Drawing stories to logical and satisfying conclusions. Most GMless games I've seen largely depend on player skill to do this well (although some more focused games have good tools, overall this is lacking).

    Those are the main points, I think. A minor point that is nevertheless important to some people is:

    * The game makes it difficult, impossible, or awkward to play it from a first-person ("in-character") stance.

    I've been exploring various solutions to these issues for some time, but it's rare to see any technology for these issues which can simply be "borrowed" the way a variety of, say, solid conflict resolution techniques exist and can be drawn upon.

    \n Why does being GMless entice? I think I don't really get the special distinction, to me being GMless is just an arbitrary structuration choice, same as any other. I am not convinced that prioritizing it as a design paradigm makes any more sense than doing "dice pool mechanics", which some people used to think was this really special distinction that set a game apart from others, once upon a time.

    My own answer to this question comes in the form of utility statements: there are purposes for which various kinds of GMless structures are better than having a GM would be. For example:
    * If the goal of a game is to boot-strap a group of players into a shape where they're all equally capable and committed to playing the game, then you will probably want something similar to a boardgame's structure: a facilitator exists, yes, because it's useful for somebody to master the rules in advance, but the end-goal is for them to discard the role once everybody's on board. This is better than having a GM because of the infantilizing influence that the GM's presence generally has on the creative dynamics of the gaming process.
    * If your subject matter and goals benefit from pure, unadulterated creative input from many different players, delivered right there at the table and possibly judged right way by the rest of the group, then taking the GM out of the equation probably heightens the responsibility and removes a player who could act as an unwanted focal point in lieu of the actually contributing player. In other words, when you don't have a GM you actually have to take that other player's contribution seriously, because there is nobody else there legitimizing or delegitimizing it.
    * The GM-based traditional rpg structure has many advantages and only a moderate procedural footprint, which actually makes it a great framework for doing various sorts of things - it is no news outside gaming that having a chairman in team exercises is usually the way to go. However, when your game definitely needs to go even lighter, a GMless structure can get you there even faster and with more simplicity than adding a GM could. A similar phenomenon occurs at the high end, theoretically, but in my exper\xedence a gaming group so committed that they would have a performative edge without the GM is so rare as to be impractical.

    Isn't that just like dice pools? I could make up a grab-bag list of for instances for dice pools, too, to show how they're the appropriate tool for a given set of design parameters. Yet despite that, I wouldn't elevate "having a dice pool" into the guiding principle of my design.

    \n Thanks. So we're looking less at how the game is structured, and more at the creative agenda. That makes a lot more sense to me.

    I have observed in my own game design a principle of sorts, one that has been very relevant to me when it comes to creating story-oriented games of various sorts. One might say that it's a monolithic obstacle that I have found fundamental in the sense that one has to design around it to create a good story game. Here's an attempt at putting it to words:

    Authoring good stories is a specialized craft, performed by people we call "authors" or "storytellers" or some such. If your game's payoff is supposed to be "a great story", you're faced with appropriating the dual task of the author: your game now has to make the players talented storytellers, and it has to entertain them while they perform the author's task.

    That's probably only enlightening if you happen to be in a similar creative place to mine. The reason I find the above thought fundamental is that it appears to me from experience that it is very easy to fail in making story-creation both easy and fun for the players, unless you're presuming a player base consisting of actual storytellers (who will, obviously, bring the skills and passion themselves). Much of the cultural history of roleplaying games is about the fact that not that many people are storytellers, after all.

    There are certain established ways to combine story-creation and gaming, so it is not impossible to work with the challenge, but one has to admit that the vast majority of the ways are fundamentally work-arounds if one has the ambition of creating a pure game of story-creation for the sake of story-creation. For example, the vast oeuvre of Forgite story games rely nearly without exception on a combination of mad-libs and a type of "automatic writing" that occurs when protagonist characters are given independent advocates at the gaming table. Understanding how, precisely, these games achieve the ideal of being a fun game where story occurs is quite enlightening, I think; it's not quite that "creating story is fun", so much as certain fun activities that are closely related to storytelling happen to produce stories effortlessly as a by-product.

    \n I like Eero's somewhat Socratic approach here: why not question your assumptions even further?

    The incredibly powerful and useful structure of a GM-type player who can put to use dramatic coordination and portray opposition paired with people portraying protagonists has, upon closer inspection, a whole bunch of interesting design advantages, and they shouldn't be overlooked.

    However, the structure of a GMless game has a lot of attractive features, as well:

    * A certain democratic format and style.

    This affects how people relate to the game and to each other. It's appealing, I think, just to have even a vague sense that we are all equal participants.

    That spins out into other features:

    * By necessity, this usually means a lighter social footprint.

    It's easy to put together, start up, and finish. No single person has to shoulder the burden of organizing, planning, or preparing.

    * In play, no one person has to be "on point" at all times, the way a GM does, which makes it an easier option from the perspective of whoever would otherwise GMing.

    * Similarly, that means that everyone has the ability to participate at any given time. You are unlikely to sit there idly - if you want to participate, these games typically allow you to do so.

    In comparison, in a traditional, GMed game, we rely on one person constantly being under a lot of responsibility; this also means that if you're a player and two scenes have gone by where your character wasn't involved, you have to either suck it up or go to the social level to ask the group to remember to give you something to do (which can be difficult or awkward for some people/groups).

    These are nice features for a lot of groups. Of course, they also have implications for creative agenda-type stuff. For instance, not being limited to participating in the game as one character DOES free you up to play that character in different ways, because you know that, for example, having the character sidelined doesn't also mean you don't get to play anymore.

    Personally, my interest these days as a designer is in finding structured but still asymmetrical organizations of player responsibility. One person GMing all the time is one extreme of the spectrum, perhaps, and totally equal distributions of authority and responsibility is the other. What lies in the middle of that spectrum? Some interesting business, I think.

Stands for cognitive theoretic model of the universe and is a truly mind bending logical treatise on the fundamental structure of reality. All that is irrelevant however to the utility of the following info on the mathematics of decisions. I haven't yet played w the equation enough to say exactly how I'd use it... Basically it provides a mathematical model describing analysis paralysis. here is the post (reposted without permission actually... sorry if that offends)

    W. E. Hick (1952) devised a CRT experiment which presented a series of nine tests in which there are n equally possible choices. The experiment measured the subject's reaction time based on number of possible choices during any given trial. Hick showed that the individual's reaction time increased by a constant amount as a function of available choices, or the "uncertainty" involved in which reaction stimulus would appear next. Uncertainty is measured in "bits", which are defined as the quantity of information that reduces uncertainty by half in information theory. In Hick's experiment, the reaction time is found to be a function of the binary logarithm of the number of available choices (n). This phenomenon is called "Hick's Law" and is said to be a measure of the "rate of gain of information." The law is usually expressed by the formula RT= a+b log _2(n+1), where a and b are constants representing the intercept and slope of the function, and n is the number of alternatives.[8] The Jensen Box is a more recent application of Hick's Law.[1] Hick's Law has interesting modern applications in marketing, where restaurant menus and web interfaces (among other things) take advantage of its principles in striving to achieve speed and ease of use for the consumer.[9]"

      David_Berg said:

      Historically, reciprocal playtesting has been hard to arrange. Most folks with the time, group, and inclination to play something new usually have a stack of games they're already itching to try. Many others really want their own game tested and would genuinely be happy to test yours but don't actually have the time and the group to pull that off.

      \nEven further, my experience with various sorts of playtest-trading schemes for rpgs is that they produce unreliable, low value return at the best of times. The reason is, I think, that most people who are interested in game development do not, most of the time, need stochastic playtest data, and you can't really get qualititative data out of blind-date playtest relationships except by accident.

      For this reason the actually productive playtesting and studio critique relationships that I've seen have always, practically without exception, been predicated on mutual creative respect: you first discover somebody who respects and understands your creative vision, and then arrange for that person to critique or playtest your game. This then produces committed, thoughtful, high-quality critique that actually may help you further your game development. Forced blind-date playtesting is weak in comparison because the odds are that the other person doesn't really understand or appreciate your work, and they're merely going through the motions in consulting you.

      This is why I generally recommend construing the social capital of game development in terms of on-going cultural saloons, workshops and consultative correspondence: these structures facilitate first the initial discovery process of finding like-minded consultants for your project, and then developing creative rapport and commitment to working on the project. I generally advice against all sorts of quotas and speculative mutual commitments except in the most general sense that one good deed deserves another. You do not need to promise to playtest my project for me to playtest yours; I won't playtest your game unless it is actually a worthwhile thing to do, and if it is, that certainly has nothing to do with whether it'd be useful for my game to be playtested by you.

      People go through cycles of social and creative positioning in life, and this means that, just as David says, people are very rarely in symmetrical positions where their needs are equivalent to each other. It is very common for somebody to be deep in the headset of creating their own game, which directly impacts their ability to contribute useful support functions for another person's game development. Likewise, a person who is well-situated to offer development support in the form of playtesting and such is often not at a stage in their own game design where they would need similar development help themselves. A system that presumes reciprocy actively militates against the discovery of useful allies.

      In a nutshell: if you have a project that could benefit from outside perspectives, I recommend putting it up, explaining where it's going, and perhaps specifying some specific concerns and questions. If luck is with you, you'll attract somebody who gets what you're trying to do, and they'll be able to help you going forward. This is potentially much more useful than participating in some sort of playtest-secret-santa that ends with you playtesting something stupid, and somebody stupid playtesting your game, after which neither of you has benefited at all.

      \n So I'll just do my studio critique thing here for now. Let's see...

      Firstly, I am on board with the premise here. I quite like time travel stories, the philosophical themes and high stakes are quite appealing. So in that regard this seems subjectively a worthwhile game to make, and I agree with the chosen scope: a time machine, an iterative time loop, and its end.

      Second, have you read M.J. Young's time travel stuff? Your time travel framework is somewhat reminiscent of his, and amusingly your conceit of "players play themselves" is similar to his magnum opus, the wonderfully quirky roleplaying game Multiverser. If you haven't, there's some quite exciting reading for you right there [grin].

      Moving on, one thing I dislike in story game design is "design by scripting". It's this thing where the game is constructed in the form of fill-in-the-blanks scenes, instead of formulating it in more game-like ways like stages of play and possible moves. You've got a bit of this going on here, and I'm saying this strictly as a fellow designer with my own weird convictions, but I'm pretty sure that you could make the game more intense and less write-by-numbers for the players by getting away from this habit.

      An example of what I mean is the way you conceptualize the initial scene with the time machine: you lay out some quite strict parameters for what the scene is like, and then ask the players to "play it out", despite the fact that you have already determined every important event that will occur in the scene, and those you have not (e.g. who will be transported by the machine) are actually actively randomized. This approach reduces the players into mad-libs (or exquisite corpse, rather, in this case) contributors: they get to say what the time machine looks like, but they don't get a say in what happens with it. To me the role of merely enacting the game designer's script is too petty to offer to players.

      Were I making a game on the same premise, I would probably establish one of the players as a lightweight GM-like figure whose character is the creator of the time machine; this player would have the task of chairmanning play and framing action forward in the "present time", until the moment the time machine is activated. I would allow derring-do of whatever sort this player envisions related to the machine, too - the frame story in the present could well have some minor complications of its own. And I would allow the players to determine naturally who travels in time and where, through play, as constructively coordinated by the inventor-player. I feel that this kind of looser framing allows players to engage the play more passionately as compared to being essentially handed a script. More of a sandbox you get to play in than a play you get to act in, that's my ideal for game design.

      As the game text moves on, it continues providing game design predominantly in the form of plot arc design - the game tells you which scenes to play in what order, and you get to fill in the blanks. I know that this is a relatively popular approach in the freeform circles (presumably because it lowers the skill threshold), but it leaves me grasping for something to care about in a game, apparently because I don't care about filling in blanks.

      But that's that - we can talk about the philosophy inherent in jeepform design (which this kind of freeform storygame greatly resembles) in depth later if you want, I'll stop harping about that one point for now. We have other things to address here:

      One concern that I have with this game is that it approaches its subject in a pretty lightweight manner. This may be frustrating to players who are familiar with the conceit of time travel and like the intellectual element of plotting out time-lines and determining the consequences of various actions. The game actively mitigates against story elements that many players (myself probably included) would like and expect in a time travel game: there are no real ways to wrestle with the paradoxical nature of time travel, there is no rigour to the cosmological rules that apply to the exercise, and there's no thriller plot regarding whether or not time or the people involved survive their mad game with the powers of the gods. It is of course fine to be what you want to be, but I personally like Time and Temp much more, specifically because it is so much more hands-on about the subject matter. It is somewhat counter-intuitive for a game to pave over such central parts of what makes a literary genre - it's a bit like if one were making a western game and ensured carefully that there would be no gunplay whatsoever in the game.

      Beyond the above points, the game seems to me to be a well-formed specimen of its type - this is what freeform style games are like, for good or ill. I would consider this to be quite ready for playtest and eventual productization, assuming that it is generally in the creative place that the designer wants it to be. If there are any technical foibles left, they are of the sort that are better addressed by playtest than studio review. While I have my misgivings, as documented above, they are of such higher order that it doesn't really make any sense to attempt to address them in design - this simply isn't the time travel game I'd like to have [grin].

      \n Yeah, I can totally get behind the idea of doing a running playtest and development workshop in real life. I've done that myself (in 2013-15 in Helsinki was the last time), and it can work very well when you work week to week to build an intelligent development team that can take various kinds of games and execute successful playtest sessions.

      In my experience the clever thing to do is to build up a playtest team and then collect projects for them to chew through, rather than try to find a lot of designers who need playtesting; game development is labor intensive, you'll benefit by not trying to take too many games on board at once. When we did this in Helsinki only about 50% of the games we dealt with were created by locals who actually participated in the proceedings; much of the time was spent doing in-depth playtest and development feedback for other people who just happened to have worthwhile games under development. I think that we did some really great work then, and it was largely thanks to success in building up a core team that was interested in game development and willing to playtest other people's stuff.

      Thinking of reciprocality, in our case it was much more complex than "I'll playtest your game if you'll playtest mine". Many of the best members of the team aren't very much into game design themselves, for instance. Often the big payoffs for participants in our playtest ring came in the form of social capital (friends, contacts, respect), play experience with often highly experimental state-of-the-art projects, and actual experience in game development, which all they could then leverage to improve their hobby life in various ways. I've moved out of town since then, and the playtest ring has been somewhat quiet lately, but we're still constantly reaping the fruits of the labour done then: people found new friends, games developed then are being published now, and several people found new directions for their own hobby life out of the project.

      \n The reason why preview doesn't "get" your quote block is that it lacks a few attributes. HTML tags can each have their own "settings" established within them. If you'll create a quotation block with the Quote link in a thread you'll see what the default blockquote tag that this forum software likes looks like:

      <blockquote class="Quote" rel="davey">

      As you can see, there are two attributes in addition to the name of the tag in there. In comparison, your quote block just has the tag name in there:

      <blockquote>

      What a given website does with HTML is totally contextual, the web designer can make HTML code jump through hoops. In the case of the Vanilla forum software the setup is as we've seen here: if your blockquote tag does not have any settings on it, the preview mode apparently doesn't display it correctly, but the actual thread view does. It would be fair to say that it's a minor bug.

      To fix the preview (if you cared to - not like it matters for actual thread view) you'd need to add the "class" attribute to the tag, it seems like. Like so:

      <blockquote class="Quote">

      The attribute name is "class" as you can see, and it has a set value, which is "Quote". Apparently the style code for the Vanilla forum software looks at the class value of the blockquote tag to determine whether to treat a given blockquote as a quotation, at least in its preview window. Sort of unnecessary in that all blockquotes are generally quotations in this context, but I assume it's been done this way for future compatibility - there could be some Vanilla add-ons or something that used that same tag "blockquote" for something totally different.

      As you can see from my first bolded example, there's a second attribute in there as well, named "rel". That's short for "relation", a traditional name in HTML writing for various associations. In this case Vanilla uses "rel" to express attribution for the quote. As you can see, your quote block doesn't have that little caption on top telling us who wrote the text you quoted - that's because there wasn't a "rel" attribute in your blockquote, so the software had nothing to put there.

      You can put anything you want in the "rel" attribute to change who the box attributes itself to. For example, when I'm quoting from Bram Stoker, I just write him into the "rel" attribute to make his name appear at the top of the quotation box:\n
