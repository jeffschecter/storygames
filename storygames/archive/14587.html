    \n I think Vasco has the right of it. Since your hostage and your hostage-taker are both NPCs you, as the GM, just telegraph that the hostage-taker is taking off with the hostage. Telegraph so that the players have a chance to respond. If the PC's move to stop the hostage-taker than you've got a contention on your hands, or a continuation of the current contention if the hostage-taker is currently involved in the gunfight. The results of that contention determine if/how the hostage-taker gets away. Maybe the PC's have their hands full with other opponents and let the hostage-taker go. It could play out all kinds of ways.

    As the GM, having your NPC decide to try and escape is no different than having an NPC take any action that doesn't directly effect a PC. You just do it. Like I said above though, it's usually pretty important to give the PC's a chance to act or react. Especially if the NPC is already part of an ongoing contention.

    *Basically, telegraphing the hostage-taker attempting to leave IS the turn for that NPC. The scope of an "attack" (and by extension other actions that don't require rolling) during a contention is pretty granular because it's tied directly to the specific action you're taking. The hostage-taker isn't likely to get very far into the escape attempt before the PC's have an opportunity to respond.

    \n The term "contention" is totally confusing, so I'm dropping it as a game term in the text. It's only going to be the title of the chapter from now on.

    An action\u2014and the rolling dice procedure that goes along with it\u2014is a complete unit onto itself. If it involves asserting a negative adjective on another character I call it an attack. If it involves asserting a positive adjective onto a character, I call it aiding. Afterwards, if it leads to another action in which you need to roll dice, great. If not, then you just play on in conversation.

    You might have a scene in which a lot of people want to perform actions. So then there are rules for taking turns to do so.

    Something I've learned during the playtesting is that the rules in the Contention chapter are poor rules for playing out gun fights. They are rules designed for manipulating other characters and you might just shoot guns at them in order to do so.

    I've rewritten the chapter's text to make sure that's communicated better. I'm working on finalizing those revisions now.
