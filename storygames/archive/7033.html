\n We're starting a 4E game this Thursday. I read the DMG and ran a few skill challenges this weekend and there was a bit of confusion.

At first skill challenges seem easy to run. But the instructions are vague and seem to be missing quite a bit of needed information. Reading the examples of all the skill challenges only confuses the matter as option are used in the examples that are not included in the rules.

In the examples, sometimes:

- using a certain skill, intimidate for example, is an automatic failure, no roll required
- using a certain skill will reveal which skills are automatically failures
- you can use any primary skill included in the skill challenge
- you must use the primary skills in a specific order
- you may use supplementary skills whenever you want
- only certain skills may be aided with supplementary skills
- supplementary skills give you a +2 if you succeed to your primary skill
- supplementary skills give you a +2 if you succeed to your primary skill and count as a success towards winning the skill challenge
- supplementary skills have no penalty for failure
- supplementary skills give you a -2 if you fail to your primary skill
- supplementary skills give you a -2 if you fail to your primary skill and count as a failure towards losing the skill challenge
- the number of primary skills available seems to be random

In general, there are no rules or even guidelines that govern any of the above.So we decided to stick to the 7 examples in the book rather than create our own.

Additionally to the above confusion, we also ran into the math problems detailed in other 4E threads (even with extensive use of +2 for good ideas and aid skills, when permitted). Roughly we failed 5 out of the 7 skill challenges. Additionally, consequences for failing a skill challenge were very vague. They potentially make future encounters harder but it's not clear how and how far to go (make the next encounter level 1 level higher?).

Has anyone else run into these issues?
How did you resolve them?
How do you run skill challenges?

Thanks,
John

    \n As I recall, Skill Challenges are not really set up to be run on the fly, you're supposed to set them up like you would a combat encounter. So just like its your choice to set the flaming pit in the middle of the room, its your choice what the effects the individual skill checks have.
    \n
    \nSo a fully written up Skill Challenge is meant to look like those examples. Here are the skills you can use. Here are the special effects of certain skills if players think of using them. Here's a skill players can use to learn what those effects are. You pretty much map those out in advance however it makes sense to you.
    \n
    \nI don't have my book with me, but as I recall there are some broad guidelines for the sorts of things you can do. Its not called out but rather buried in the text that your eye slips past when you're looking for a header or other flag.

    Posted By: jenskot- you must use the primary skills in a specific order
    \nIf this is true, I misread the book. You can use them in just about any order, unless you program in certain triggers (like you need to use Insight to uncover that the baron is fond of religious proverbs, allowing Religion to be used to help charm him).

    You are over-thinking skill challenges. Take a look at the section on creating your own skill challenges. You are making a lot of assumptions that just aren't there, but you are extrapolating them from the examples instead. All the variety in the examples are just that. The variety and potential. It's not really your fault, the lack of good examples of process is a systematic problem in the core set.\n
    Additionally, consequences for failing a skill challenge were very vague.
    \nAnd they should be (although again, some better examples and duscussion would have been welcome)! Because the rulebook can't pretend to know the variety of skill challenges that you will use. If you fail to convince the baron's wife that he's a dragon in disguise, what does that mean? It's up to your story and the player's actions. You can either plan for it as something that happens automatically - if it fails, she tells the baron and he attacks them in the night, or she defends her husband but then sneaks into his study to find out the truth and is discovered and then imprisoned (needing to be rescued), or something else? Or you can let the players direct things and simply give them conditions of failure. Like tell them the only way to get her away from the dragon is to kidnap her, or since they didn't convince the baroness, her loyal household guard won't be able to help you if you attack the baron anyway, etc.

    The main rule of thumb is do not completely shut down any paths that stops the story from continuing. If they fail trying to get a clue trough the skill challenge, have a condition of that failure be a tough combat that gives them the clue when they win (this combat could flow right out of the challenge, or days after, whatever you need). You can even make them like stakes setting. Perhaps they get the clue no matter what, but failing means they did it in a way that they've made enemies.

    Or look at it this way, since skill challenges (at default DCs) are heavily stacked against PCs without careful and skilled skill use, so plan for them as assumed complications resulting from the failure. Skill Challenges are the events in the story that makes things irrevocably worse for the protagonists. Like opening their hand to the baroness and having it slapped away. The challenge to the PCs is to make their fate better. If they fail, then the horrible stuff is already going to happen as ordained. If they succeed, then it's better for them. Maybe they avoid a potentialy deadly encounter (and know they avoided it), gain some help, or catch their enemies unawares.

    Also, John's method highlights some great tricks that the text really should have flashing arrows pointed to it and be in the book. The complexity of success and failures is a great tool to put in shades of gray other than win/lose. If you want some quantitative results, use these. An example: for each failure, there's an extra goblin in the next fight (or the boss is increased by a level). Each success should also do something, perhaps turning one goblin into three minions, or reducing the boss's level by one for every two successes. Or they could be story triggers. Like one success means that, whether or not the king is swayed, one of the servants is swayed; two successes and it's a guard; three and it's a noble; all four successes and it's the king (duh).

    But specifically how bad or good things get as a result from failure or success is up to you and the fiction of the current story.

    \n We had a really pivotal skill challenge as the second encounter of the last adventure.

    The group needed to investigate the Cult of Vecna in the city. We hadn't done anything in the city yet, and it was just a big blank slate. I knew this would turn into everyone hemming and hawing over what to do, without any hand holds (and no Circles rolls!). So, I called a skill challenge. Each round would be a day of investigation. Roll initiative, go!

    I set out the Moderate difficulty skills (Streetwise, Insight, Religion). On each player's turn, I asked them to say what skill they wanted to use (one of the Moderates or one of their choice, at Hard). Then I framed really hard into a scene:

    Ryan: "I want to use my Streetwise."
    John: "Okay. How are you gonna use it?"
    Ryan: "I'm a fighter. I know people in, like, a city watch or something."
    John: "The Winterguard. Yeah. You know a captain of the watch, Viraka. You're in her office. She's all, 'Kalil! You bastard, where have you been these days? Ready to come work for me yet?'"
    Ryan: "Uh... well, I have a job. But I might be able to help you out if you help me out."
    Viraka: "Hrm. Fine. I may have something for you. The Council is telling us to look the other way with these nobles who are up to no good. They're into some kind of cult or something. I need to shut 'em down, but it can't look like the Guard did it. You up for that?"

    And so on. Each player had their little scene (about 2 mins), their skill roll, and the attack roll of the challenge (which was the cult trying to mess with them -- in one case it was an actual ambush). By the time we went around the group a couple times, we had fleshed out NPCs and factions of the city and created all kinds of fodder for future stories.

    \n John Harper: I play skill challenges very similarly. Smaller goals are achieved or not within a larger framework. It really generates a lot of world material, and makes each roll important, as well as giving lots of space to roleplay and improvise.

    I admit, though, I'm pretty hippy when it comes to what skills people can use for a Skill Challenge. I pretty much let the players set out how they're going to approach a problem, and rule from their initial choices of skills what constitutes a reasonable contribution. I also encourage lots of helping rolls. Helping rolls, especially when everyone at the table piles bonuses on to one player, really build up a scene's details and, as a result, the tension of the final roll.

    However, I don't understand what you mean by 'the attack roll fo the challenge'. In the course of a normal skill challenge, especially using Stalker0's modified rules, there seems to be enough failure to create a sense of forboding. What does the attack roll do? Is it damage?
