\n I'm writing a fantasy roleplaying game, but the purpose is what's important: The game needs to create a sense of camaraderie among the players. I'm going to call it Camaraderie for the purpose of this discussion. Thanks to everyone who dove into Camaraderie thread 1 (especially Graham).

This thread is all about what I see as the big problems for the game.

The first thread agreed that there needs to be a strong authority of adversity (i.e. a GM). This adversity needs to be structured carefully, so that individual results are much worse than group results. Also, characters need to grow meaningfully in the course multiple sessions.

Problem #1: The GM facilititates and observes the feeling but isn't part of the group - which is OK for a session, but not for EVERY session. If the responsibility rotates, what about the player/GM who kills a character, or runs a bad session?

Problem #2: A game of Camaraderie can actually result in a lot of NEGATIVE intra group feelings if a player doesn't show and the game gets canceled or the opposition cannot be defeated without a missing player.

How can I solve these 2 problems?

    \n I think I'm maybe on the same page as Hituro, though before I continue, I should state that I have something of a bias against rotating GM / GMless games.

    Basically, even when the GM's primary role is as the author of adversity, they can still feel the same sense of accomplishment when their players win. It's like "Man, I thought I had you guys dead to rights! That thing where Collossus threw Logan into the air, and Storm guided him to the target with gusts of wind to take out the Sentinel was intense. I was sure it wasn't going to work, but you pulled it off."

    Now, the resentment of the players for the killer GM is a little different issue. One thing I think that could alleviate the problem is to utterly, totally, completely forbid Rule Zero, hidden rolls and fudging of rolls. At that point, the GM has no power, without breaking the game, to fudge a roll to save a PC, nor to kill them. It's all the system, right there in the open for everyone to see. Combine that with something along the lines of Gerald says, and you'll eliminate a lot of the problem.

    Frex: GM: "Hey guys, I got this mission, it's Comparative Difficulty Level (CDL) 0, meaning it's right at the same power level as your group of PCs. I could dial it up to CDL 1 or 2, and you guys could get better rewards, but it'd be a lot tougher."

    \n Keeping with the idea of group effort how about the chips you pay come from the pool you create as a group

    So you have this group pool, which starts empty

    As characters help each other, (e.g. by deferring to another character on use of a skill, or by letting another character go ahead, or taking a hit for another character, or whatever) then chips go into the group pool

    Players can then pay out of the pool to either decrease or increase encounter difficulty. Either they can use each chip to boost some roll they make (making things easier for themselves) or hand them to the GM to use to increase rolls by the opposition. If they do the later the spent chips go into a 'rewards pool' that increases the rewards they get

    \n Ryan, I wrote a game called Perfect (about criminals who rock the Victorian dystopic system in the name of passion). The game is a very isolationist game (in terms of PC interaction), but the way I've handled GM role is something which Camaraderie could learn from.

    The way the game works is that each player has a seperate scene for their PC (go around in turns). The player narrates a crime for their character, and the GM acts as a Facilitator, asking questions ("Why does your character want that book?") and prompting ideas ("What about tying in your Contact?") and offering to play NPCs ("Did you want someone to act out the baker's daughter's part?").

    Eventually, the scene is set and the crime is committed. And then the GM asks, "On a scale of 1 to 10, how much does this matter to your character?" The player answers 7. The GM asks self, aloud, "On a scale of 1 to 10, how much does this matter to the society?" The GM answers own question with 4. Adding 7 and 4 together, the GM has 11 points for this scene (plus any previous leftovers).

    Then the GM stops being Facilitator, and starts being Interrogator. The GM's job is now to fuel and play the Inspectors, who seek to hunt down the PC and brainwash them into being better citizens. And the GM has a limited point economy and is expected to play it to the hilt.

    And then, at the end of the scene, the player gets a Payout, equal to 11 in this case. And that's a cool advancement worth 11 points, and there's a chart of that stuff. So it self-balances by rewarding the player to the same degree that it empowers the GM's opposition.


    So, that's a formula that works. GM facilitates and is involved, acting as a creative partner and provider. Camaraderie is established between the GM and player, and then together they decide how deadly the GM's opposition should be. Then they shake hands like old friends about to fight a duel to the death, and the GM radically transforms into Interrogator and Adversary, and plays that to the absolute hilt, knowing that the social contract that was established demands it.

    \n Well, you can reward greater cooperation with easier victory in a few different ways. Since the games that people reported in Camaraderie: Origins were all fairly long-running niche-protection specialist kinds of games, if we're supporting that then we have a lot of room to play with mechanics other than the coins.

    Say we do a game, 1d6+Your Stat for each action. You have some stats: might, agility, insight, will. These are basically the 4 specialities. Whatever else the coin mechanic does (action points, etc.) if there was a list of modifiers like this:

    Basic Advantage (i.e. higher ground): +1
    Big Advantage (charging on horseback vs. footman with knife): +3

    Teamwork with an NPC: +1
    Teamwork with another PC: +3

    Then you're rewarding big teamwork with success even though it's not part of the coin mechanic specifically.
