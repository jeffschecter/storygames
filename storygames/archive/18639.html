\n So, my game Walkabout is spiritual and quantum mechanistic.

That means time is wibbly-wobbly-timey-wimey, rather than directly linear.

There are flashbacks, and there is foreshadowing. These are inherent in the narrative, but they aren't solely in the control of the GM.

I don't want to create a monster post here about how it works, or another monster post providing the in-game examples, if you want that sort of thing you can look...

Here (idea behind the mechanism)

and

Here (clarification and play examples)

Given that the whole game narrative develops through consensus of the group, and nothing is really set in stone when the story begins, I'm just wondering what people around here might consider to be the problems inherent with player driven (rather than GM driven) foreshadowing in a story.

    \n I wouldn't expect all players to pick a foreshadowed element, in fact it wouldn't make sense for some characters to pick an element, especially if they were the type who 'live in the moment'. I think the key here is that the foreshadowing doesn't guarantee a particular direction when the plot twist comes, instead it gives clues about the future...just like in a well written novel.

    As for GM decision about when a plot twist occurs, I hadn't really thought about this. It's pretty arbitrary, but when the GM feels it's right, or when things get slow and the GM decides a twist will liven things up, they can call on one of the foreshadowing sheets (as long as the characters noted on the sheet are all present in the scene).

    In a pre-set plot, I'd probably use the plot twist to pull the characters back on track if they deviated too far from the intended storyline, or I'd deliberately leave something vague towards the end...something that can be filled in by the foreshadowed event.

    \n I've successfully used foreshadowing a number of times. The important thing to remember is that foreshadowing is a tool to introduce a story element, in detail, yet hidden. A foreshadowing must never reveal how, where, when, who, why and what.
    Only shadows.
    For instance, the foreshadowing may reveal that someone will die, maybe even who, and maybe even the how, but never everything. it must always be open to interpretation, and yet what is actually shown must be literal and happen or be introduced to the story. It must happen as shown, hence the importance of leaving an openening to introduce the element/event/object/person.

    \n Some ideas:

    Require the players to provide theories. Be Neil or go home.

    Provide some blanks to fill in. Like, in a murder mystery, you get three pieces of foreshadowing: a Means, Motive, and Opportunity. When a player picks something for the GM to reincorporate, the players knows those are the options, and might offer some related thoughts. The GM then has (1) expectations set from the beginning, (2) an eye out for what might make a fun M/M/O, (3) three options to look at (M/M/O) when figuring out how to reincorporate a given element, and (4) possibly help from players, whose jobs are also made easier by knowing what they hope to find.

    Provide some charts or roll-on tables for the GM to use in converting player input into What's Going On -- e.g. using entries like "yes, but with an innocent explanation", "no, and it's the opposite of what they suspect", "this is the key to unlocking the problem's solution", "this is an elaborate ruse by a scheming antagonist", etc.

    Make the players generate lots of theories from which the GM may choose. Example from my Game Chef game:\n
    Question or intent: Is your Mole attempting to learn about the Interlopers? If so, ask a question, as per below. If not, state your intent.

    Question: If your Mole is attempting to learn about the Interlopers, state what question you are hoping to answer, and then state your current best theory about what\u2019s going on. This theory can be a direct answer to the question you asked, but it doesn\u2019t have to be. Vagueness is fine.

    GM, write these both down.

    Roll the dice! It\u2019s actually one six-sided die. Roll it, add your modifier if applicable, and refer to the relevant chart:

    Question Roll - What kind of answer do I find?
    0\tA truth and two lies, one of which is mute on your theory, with another contradictory**
    1\tA truth and a lie, one of which is mute on your theory**
    2\tA truth and a lie, one of which contradicts your theory**
    3\tA truth and a lie, one of which supports your theory**
    4\tA clear truth that is mute on your theory
    5\tA clear truth that contradicts your theory
    6\tA clear truth that supports your theory
    7\tTwo clear truths that both support or* both contradict your theory

    *GM chooses
    **GM chooses whether the other answer is mute or contradictory

    Truths and Lies: When the roll result includes clear truths, the GM narrates the answers and then, on the GM sheet, marks down a check mark for each truth, along with a few words describing the truth.

    When the roll result includes truths and lies, the GM should provide the answers indicated, choosing as necessary. Either before, during, or immediately after speaking, the GM must decide which answers were true and which were lies. The GM must then jot down a few words describing each. No check mark is made now. If the Moles follow up on a truth, treating it as true, they then earn a check next to it. Believing lies earns no checks; the GM only writes down a lie in case the players forget it and want to follow it up.

    Truths: GM, keep a count of the check marks for truths! Once you hit 18, you should be getting a handle on the gist of what the Interlopers are up to, because the next two truths they find will reveal the whole deal! 19 should leave one question remaining, and 20 will answer it!

    \n I actually do a lot of these things in my game FUBAR, and Walkabout is a descendent of that game.

    In that game, I require players to provide an NPC (who acts as direct antagonist to one of the other characters), a scene, a macguffin and a secret conspiracy group; but since these are shuffled and not revealed before play, none of the players know what will come next, they only that they're ideas will come up at some stage. I was actually trying to move toward a more traditional narrative structure with this game, but something that still retains a free-flowing collaborative spirit. The foreshadowing system is designed to be a mechanism that reflects a common narrative technique but makes it more collaborative.

    I think that the way Walkabout uses different token colours to flavour action results could be used to advantage here (white is bad, black is good, green implies growth/improvement, blue implies change, red implies erosion/destruction). In the clarification and playtest examples I've already described a bit about how these colours might apply to the foreshadowed elements, but they could be integrated into the colour system further.

    I guess I'll only know how well it works once it hits play with a few different groups (probably at the next convention where I run the game again).
