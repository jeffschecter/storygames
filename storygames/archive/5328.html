    \n Played In a Wicked Age with the SGBoston crew last night. First time I've played it since John Harper ran it for us at GenCon. It was loads of fun: wizards exploded, babies were killed, demons and rune magic were all over the place. However, there were a few things I noticed which have me a bit worried:

    - No one ever chose to roll d4 or d6 forms. Generally there would be multiple forms that would be applicable to a given situation and people inevitably chose the ones with the best dice. We rolled d12s, d10s, and d8s all the time, unless folks didn't have those available because of Injury of Exhaustion.

    - People rolled the dice for their specials in almost every conflict. No one ever rolled just two dice, hardly.

    - It was hard to find the place in the book where it describes how you compare the size dice you're rolling to see if you get on the We Owe list or not. It's not part of the We Owe list instructions, but is a kind of throw-away line in the example somewhere. Also, now that Eric's figured out that he can roll, say, d10 d10 d10 against d12 d6 d4 (where he has a 75% chance of winning) and still get on the We Owe list (Is that right?), I think he's probably going to do that at every chance he gets.

    - Bigger dice almost universally won conflicts, because they tend to gain the advantage die in the first round and then just keep rolling through all three rounds. We only had 1 time in the whole game where the advantage die swapped players once it had been assigned. That said, being Exhausted or Injured REALLY sucked, because if you went up against someone who hadn't lost a conflict, you were basically toast. We had one demon character lose two conflicts in a row this way, which meant she was pretty much done after that.

    - April mentioned that sometimes you want to roll for a conflict, but you don't want three rounds to work it out and you don't really want to negotiate. For example, there was one conflict where Eric was trying to throw the baby off a cliff and I was rolling for the baby (who was actually an NPC with the special trait: Destiny), and the conflict ended up stretching to three rounds, so I was forced to invent this magic albatross that was swooping down from the sky to catch the baby and shit. I mean, it was kinda cool, but we also felt like we should have declared that conflict to be only 1 or 2 rounds long before we started rolling dice. I suspect, in future games, we may think about altering the number of rounds for certain conflicts, in a sorta TSOY-ish style of having straight rolls and then Bring Down The Pain.

    Reading through the alternate rules suggestions at the back of the book, I think those would work really well and may solve some of our problems, especially about always rolling your big dice. If you risk your big dice by rolling them, that might mitigate some of the problems. Similarly, if lasting through all three rounds means you weaken your opponent as well, that makes conflict more interesting, I think.

    Anybody else have these issues or got any advice that may help me run In a Wicked Age in the future?

    Trickery and ruses are not just conversation - there is something at stake if you get caught. "I think this is what you should do because blah blah blah blah" is conversation. Persuasion and discourse respects the freedom of the participants, and that's not what a system like this is about. If you are still respecting the freedom of the other guy to do what they like, who cares. If a player decides to use trickery, threats, guilt, whatever to get something from a character, that's action and now we're talking.       \n Wicked is made for multi-chapter play, with emergent protagonists (not pre-ordained protags like most RPGs).       \n Want persnickety 3-dice vs 2-dice rules for who's rolling bigger dice?

      If the two dice surround the three dice, the three dice are bigger. Examples: d12 d4 -vs- d10 d10 d6; d10 d6 -vs- d8 d8 d8. The three dice are bigger than the two dice.

      If the two dice and the three dice overlap, the two dice are bigger. Examples: d12 d10 -vs- d10 d10 d10; d10 d8 -vs- d10 d6 d4. Believe it or not, the two dice are bigger than the three dice.

      (Oh, and this rule is only for when the biggest of the three is smaller than the biggest of the two. d12 d6 d4 is obviously bigger than d10 d8.)

      (Also, I'm not 100% that this rule is correct for every single possible case. However, when it's wrong, the two sets of dice will be for all practical purposes equal. Example: is d10 d8 really bigger dice than d10 d6 d6? Maybe it's not, I didn't check. However, either way, it's within a point or two of 50-50, so who cares.)

      A big part of what strikes me about IAWA and some of Vincnet's other work is that GM's characters are not "equal" to PCs nor intended to be developed in the system system detail because they aren't the stars, the GM is not "living through them" in the way Players do through PCs. That's a trend I see in a number of games that I applaud both in terms of "how a GM should approach NPCs" and also as a GM workload issue. PCs need enough fiddly bits to satisfy Player design hunger and can have more because each has a specialized caretaker Player, NPCs only need enough fiddly bits to fill their role on stage and should have fewer recognizing they have a generalist GM caretaker. The way IAWA collapses NPC stats to still give them 2 dice but not worry as much about the distribution among styles/objectives is a great example of that.
