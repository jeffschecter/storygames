\n IAWA is 95% of the only game I want to play for the rest of my life. But I want things like sets, who's holding the ball, and so on, to matter, and I want the players to create and engage with artifacts at the table.

I'm thinking that the second Form die that comes off of your sheet that really doesn't need to work the way it does.

I seriously think that should come out of the fiction in another way; not a front-loaded way. It has to be defined and written down in free play, but then called up at the moment conflict starts based on what makes sense in the fiction.

I'm just wondering how to go from "thing we talk about" to "write this down and give it a d8" in free play. I'm kind of talking about creating Particular Strengths on the fly, which Vincent has covered before, but I'm putting more emphasis on them because I think it's warranted.

So instead of

Roll 2 Forms and maybe a particular strength

I'm looking for

Roll 1 form, 1 situational particular strength, and maybe 1 inherent particular strength

This will change how damage and negotiation work but that's a solveable hurdle compared to "how does our play produce situational particular strengths?" and "how they be allocated to players in a fair way?"

So...

How can free play produce situational particular strengths, and how they be allocated to players in a fair way?

    \n I like this idea, actually. Especially since I often have trouble deciding which second form is appropriate to a situation. Usually the first form is very obvious, while the second form can be one of two or three.

    With the collective pool, would those dice reset after each conflict? I'm also wondering if said pool would only have one of each die type. Or, more to the point, what's the point of defining a "pool" if there's no restriction as to the available die sizes? If the pool has more than enough dice, then you might as well just use "player decides, but GM calls bullshit." And if there is a limit to the pool, you might be forced to take a die that you feel doesn't quite represent your situation (i.e. "I've got tiny knife that I barely know how to use, but all that's left in the pool is a d12").

    You could have a player or GM-controlled vote to see which participant gets which die, based on situation, but methinks that the advantage die system already takes this into account. Note that IAWA focuses on determining situational advantage after the first roll, instead before the rolls (as is the case in, say, D&D).

    Another question that arises is how to handle taking dice damage. We play with "use it, risk it" rule, so the two forms you use are the two forms that are damaged if you lose the conflict. However, what gets damaged here if you lose? Obviously the first form, but do you arbitrarily pick a second form? Does your opponent choose it? Do you negotiate? Etc.

    Anywho, these are just some ramblings that might help you think about how to go about this.

    \n Fast, fun, fair: pick two.

    Old joke, I know, but could be salient to the thread. I don't think you can get fast and fair at the same time, without a metric. Developing a metric requires that all players have equal access to scene (normally somewhat uncontrollable) and a strong understanding of the stats behind dice as used in IAWA. It's not enough just to think Bigger Is Better, because of the We Owe list and how it can provide a major bonus (+d6 to another die).

    And what of this "fairness" of which you speak? Could there be a bit of competition for narrative control amongst your players? I'm reminded of all the DITV threads with folks saying, "Damn, I can't beat the Dogs--they invoke every Trait every conflict!" and all of the replies of "So?" What breaks if you just let players pick up to a d10, by their own decision, based on the "oomph" of the situation invoked?

    Hmmm... Idea: Maybe you could just make it player fiat, BUT when they do it, their chosen form is the one that MUST be exhausted/injured/shamed; they lose choice if they lose The Stick? So if I just choose two of my forms, I am free to choose what forms get exhausted/injured/shamed; but if I go yanking a situational die, my chosen form MUST be the one reduced by a die rank, if I lose.

    OO! Or to REALLY make it a touch call, and reduce die munchkinism, the chosen form will go down by the absolute value of the difference in die ranks between it and the situational die (minimum of one die rank). For example:
    * I choose a d6 form and pull a d10 die - that's a difference of two die ranks, so I could lose the form entirely if I lose the conflict (-2 ranks = d6 -> d4 -> d0)
    * I choose a d8 form and pull a d4 - again, a difference of two ranks, so I could go down to a d4 in it (-2 ranks = d8 -> d6 -> d4)
    * I choose a d6 form and pull a d6, d4, or d8 - a difference of zero or one rank, but the minimum is one rank lost, so I could go down to a d4

    Workable? It's at least a bit self-correcting, and makes for a strategic choice by the player rather than a game balance ("fair") choice for the GM.

    So if you mean something like, player describing the hot humid jungle air that hangs like a cloying blanket over everyone and everything, then can put a dice on that element, then can bring that element in - "I sneak up on the guards as they drowse under the influence of this smothering blanket of humidity and run them through." for a dice to add to their Form?

    To keep balanced in current IAWA rules, you can't bring in a component this way with a bigger die value than a 2nd form you would use in standard rules.

    Such elements are now a permanent feature, with that dice, and thereafter others can call on it too. Elements like these are written down on a list (like the Owe List is kept), dice values put beside them - perhaps physical dice (or they're on index cards, whatever). When dice are taken off them they don't refresh until (xxx, next conflict or next chapter), to stop boring use of same thing over and over. So A creates element, B reincorporates it in scene, but then it can't be used again until the refresh.

    When an element used, if the user wins contest the element is deemed more significant and gains a dice size when it refreshes (though if increasing over d12 it has "graduated" and isn't used again). If the user loses contest, the element is deemed not significant and loses a dice size (being erased completely if sinking under d4). That keeps new elements coming in rather than just same stable in longer term.

    Posted By: lumpley
    How much better is it?
    \nWell, that's what the various ideas mentioned above are for, I guess. Personally, I would consider not asking the question "how much better is it?", but instead asking the question "who has it better?". To illustrate with an example from D&D 4E, there are a LOT of situations that grant a PC "combat advantage", a static +2 bonus to an attack roll, but even if you have multiple sources of combat advantage (let's say you're flanking a prone target who's also blind), you only get the +2 bonus once. Either you have combat advantage or you don't.

    To translate this into IAWA, you could just say that one person gets a d6, the other gets a d8*, based on which one has the advantage. No need to know exactly how much of an advantage is involved, but only that there IS an advantage. In multiple-person conflicts, you could have one of each die size, and null for the rest (if there are that many participants). As a bonus, is much easier for the group to decide who has the advantage, rather than nitpicking of the exact degree of advantage.

    *or d8 and d10, or d6 and d10, or whatever. Playtesting would be required.

    Posted By: lumpleyThen why do you need a pool of available dice?
    I don't think you do, necessarily (other than the pounds of dice you need for ANY lumpley game! ;^) ). As I've read this thread, Ryan seems to want to tweak the use of forms rules to accommodate advantages gained during the situations leading up to the confli-- ...Oh, wait, I think I got it. I think I see what's gone horribly wrong....

    Ryan, you might be trying to do Fortune at the End (FatE), but IAWA is Fortune at the Beginning, from round to round. You don't "leap down from the roof" and, thus, get a d10 rather than one of your forms. Rather, you roll forms to determine IF you've gained advantage in the overall conflict, which you could well narrate as leaping down from roofs, if you win narration rights.

    Not saying you can't do it as part of a preamble--pre-round-one. But after the FIRST roll (initiative), the dice tell you who's winning or losing advantage; not YOU (i.e. any player, GM included) presuming advantage from the fluid situation and getting a bigger or smaller die. The +d6 handles all that (and it's a damned sight more advantageous than a d12 all alone).

    I think you're getting Right to Dream in our Story Now, and finding it hard to make it mesh. (See, Vincent? I read your blog and knew better than to say "sim in our nar"! :^) )

    I think the pertinent question is actually "Who decides how much better it is?"

    You could do it by a GM call (which would give the GM something to do; I seem to recall IAWA GMing is scene-framing and conflict-instigating, but then sort of trails off). Or you could make it the call of the player who's picking up the die (ie, he decides what die to represent his action with). You could do it by a vote of everybody not involved in the conflict. You could constrain their available choices in any number of ways, through pools of available dice, a story-escalation checklist, some sort of guideline (see Exalted stunting), and so on. But the real question is who is translating the fiction into game effectiveness.

    \n How exactly do you want fiction to drive the dice in play?

    In so much as decisions made at the spur of the moment in regards to having an advantage should net bonus dice?

    Or, interesting choices/creative input in the fiction should be rewarded?

    I see a separation between rewarding creativity and tactical aptitude if it meets the GMs (or groups) criteria.

    If you're looking to reward creativity why not have everyone create a pile of particular strengths before play (maybe before the campaign even starts) and write them on index cards and then throw them into a pile in the middle of the table. During play, a player can pick any particular strength from the pile and use it, either putting it back when they are done or keeping it for themselves; if they keep it they cannot claim another from the pile until they get rid of their current choice. That way, you're sort pre-prepping interesting colorful choices and reincorporating those ideas for use at any time.

    That would almost function like an Oracle of particular strengths.
