[Agora] Help me refine starting Ideals

Josh Roby
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
Okay, so one of the many game designs overburdening my brain right now is Agora, in which you play a group of people and their leader, defined primarily by your Ideals and your Resources. Ideals are rated in dice and change through the course of play -- specifically, you use Ideals to fuel actions, and fallout from those actions can rewrite your Ideals. You get like "three words fallout" and have to change three words of your Ideals. "Change" being defined as "add one, remove one, or change one." Ideals are all answers (or half answers, or pointers to answers) to the question, "How shall we live?"

Character generation has you pick a Faction and a Culture, both of which come with starter Ideals, and make up a Background, where you write an Ideal for yourself whole cloth. I've got four factions and eight cultures below. I'm taking a page from Burning Wheel and not providing any more context than a name and its Ideal for each one -- players are more than capable of contextualizing everything in actual play.

My question to you: are these "grabby" enough, do they get you thinking about playing a character/group that follows these principles? Do the factions mix-and-match with the cultures in interesting ways? Additionally, is the wording (which is important in play) open to manipulation, or should it be changed? I'm very interested in suggested rewrites of these Ideals.

Factions
The War's combatants included too many nations, alliances, cartels, and religions to count. Here are four -- they may have been the most prominent or simply the most immediate players in the PCs' experience of the War.

The Diamond Alliance -- "Those who sacrifice liberty for security deserve neither."

The Great Circle -- "From each according to his ability, to each according to his need."

The Hierarchy -- "Lessers serve their betters to become greater themselves."

The Uprising -- "The oppressed must rise up and claim what we were denied."


Cultures
By the time of the War, the peoples of the galaxy had shifted and intermingled so thoroughly that no culture was unknown in any faction. Here are eight of the galactic cultures. Again, these may be the eight most populous cultures in the galaxy, or the ones that the PCs know and interact with the most.

Ardent Orthodoxy of Seraphim (Seraphs) -- "The Divine shapes and guides the Faithful."

Augustan -- "My family was born to rule those without the Blood."

Bucoli -- "Tradition gives us pragmatic solutions to modern problems."

Greens -- "The natural world teaches us life lessons."

The Legion (Legionnaires) -- "Life is a battlefield of victors and losers."

Oppidan -- "Diversity combines many strengths together."

Plutonic -- "Greed creates and commerce animates all worthwhile things."

The Savan -- "Observation and investigation yield the curious the universe."

Looking forward to feedback!

    Hey, Danny!

    Firstly, the War isn't so much the focus as the backdrop of the game -- while you can certainly go in with guns blazing, you can also forge alliances with your erstwhile enemies. The game is far more focused on the question "how shall we live?" On the other hand, a principle like "The will of the people creates peace and prosperity" is an ideal that Bush is using to forward two invasions and counting -- it's all about how the ideal interacts with real situations.

    There isn't really a definitional distinction between faction and culture -- it's not like one is a group of one thing and the other is a group of another. If you squint, the factions are more grandiose 'how the world works' sorts of ideals and the cultures are more on the ground 'how we interact with the world' ideals. That distinction isn't very prominent, though, I agree. It's also more than possible for the players to create their own factions and cultures if they like.

    The real brunt of the faction/culture divide is to allow players to have one in common and not the other -- so two players could be from the same faction, and therefore start off working together, but still have that cultural difference to make them distinct. Or the other way around, players with different factions but the same culture can find similarities that let them overcome their differences.

    As for how they come up in play -- I have a (long-ass) example of play posted to my blog. Maybe that will clear things up a bit?
