\n One of the tensions I've come across when dealing with games and gamers is the expectation to win. And not just win, but basically win all the time. Like all the time. Any failure is a disaster of epic proportions and they will do anything and everything in their power to avoid failure, even on the most basic or meaningless tasks. (By basic and meaningless I just mean things that are important enough to roll the dice for, but not life or death, character loss, loss of control, etc). They will spend whatever plot/fate/story points they have to avoid failing any roll they have to make, and if possible, spend the same to avoid any setback/damage/stress/mild complication that gets in their way.

Yet, by and large, these are the same people who eat up the fiction associated with their genre(s) of choice. One of the things about fiction (whether prose or film) is that the characters basically never win till the absolute end of the story. In gamer terms, they "fail forward" throughout the story, and only decisively, clearly, actually succeed at something in the last bit of the story, the climax. There might be a few minor successes here and there along the way, but they're mostly going to be "success at cost" rather than a simple, decisive success. Why? Because it makes for a boring story if the character is just winning all the time.

So it seems that what makes for good stories and good gaming are polar opposites. Or at least, what makes for good stories and what most gamers seem to want out of game play polar opposites. I know there are quite a few games that have versions of "fail forward" and "succeed at cost" mechanics, they're really popular in the last few years, but are there any games that rely almost exclusively on those? And as a gamer, would you get frustrated with that setup?

To go all fiction, you basically have a character with a goal enter a location where there's someone or something who opposes that character's goal. There's a bit of action/reaction that happens, then the scene ends with some kind of disaster for the character. Good story boils down to the drama and tension that result from the constant conflict between the character's goals and their failure to achieve those goals. Two steps forward, one step back. One step forward, two steps back.

To go all traditional gaming, you basically have a character with a clear goal who wades through minor adversity at best, slowly whittling away at some resources (if any) as they march irrevocable onward toward their goal. Yes, in lots of story games this is flipped around and the games are designed to more closely match fiction, but a lot of them fall short, to me at least. I wonder if something as structured as most fiction is would really work as a game structure. Could you really take a game system, input the players' choices, and get a result that was actually something akin to the fiction?

For me it seems to come back to that again and again, more closely mirroring the fiction in structure and form. The closer a game gets to that, the better I like it; the further away a game gets from that, the less I like it. I know there are a few games out there that explicitly do this, Fiasco and Primetime Adventures are two, are there any others where the end product of the gaming experience is meant to be at least a fiction-adjacent type thing? It seems the gamier they get, the further they are away from producing fiction. Even games designed to mimic licensed TV properties done in more story gaming formats don't produce the kind of fiction I'm talking about, and suffer from the players bending over backwards to avoid anything bad happening to their characters at all, for example Smallville and Firefly. Great games in and of themselves, but to have an end result that mimics the TV shows they're based on, you need to seriously railroad at times and simply deny players the ability to avoid the consequences of the story (re: plot points).

It's not a question of imposed/railroaded linear story from the beginning, rather a question of what results from play being a coherent, story-like thing. Rather than looking at it from beginning to end and imposing a linear story on the game (ie railroading), looking at it from after the session(s) are over and seeing a coherent story there. I don't mean imposing a specific story, I mean imposing the structure of a story.

    By "coherent story", I mean scenes that have characters with a goal, in a location, opposed by someone or something. That scene having a question (will the character accomplish their goal?), there being some back and forth between the character and their opposition (twists and turns), the scene resolving with a setback for the character (a gap between their expected outcome and the actual outcome). The character deciding what to do next, declaring their goal and moving to the next scene. On a larger scale there being an arc to the story, rising action, a low point, a climax. You know... Story.

    Fiasco and PTA do a lot of this by structuring play around act breaks and making scenes have explicit goals. What the specific story is from the start is irrelevant, that game play is structured around story structure is important. That what comes out through play, and the artifact of the play experience is story-like is important.

    \n Are success and failure really so central to fiction?

    Obviously some stock plots do revolve around characters failing or succeeding - sports movies, for example. But even then, the outcome is never really in doubt (the underdogs will win at the last minute), so the success-or-failure tension must not be central to our enjoyment. And many genres have little to do with success and failure - the buddy cop movie, the road trip, family drama, etc.

    I think perhaps "does the protagonist succeed or fail in this scene" isn't such a useful way to examine a story. Maybe "what does the protagonist give up or insist on" (as in Drama System) comes closer to what actually grabs us as consumers of fiction.

    Which isn't to say game mechanics shouldn't model success and failure, only that these are secondary concepts. (Much like D&D mechanics revolve around hit points which don't exist in the fiction at all.)

    \n This is an interesting discussion. My first reaction is that there are two things at play here:

    1. A system/game/procedure which produces satisfactory story-like narratives. We have a LOT of these here in the Story Games community - I'd argue that MOST of the games coming out of this culture are very much "fail forward" games, where success is not only the default assumption, but often the players delight in seeing the unpleasant and horrible things that happen to their characters (e.g. Fiasco).

    In other words, what you're describing is very, very common in these games, with various levels of success/failure built-in. There are dozens and dozens of games which achieve this very well. I'd look at games like Grey Ranks, Dogs in the Vineyard, Fiasco, With Great Power..., My Life with Master, Polaris, Monsterhearts, Otherkind Dice (or Ghost/Echo), Archipelago, In a Wicked Age..., Shab-al-hiri Roach, Kagematsu, nicotine girls, Dread, etc, etc. Gun Thief is a particularly interesting and very short game which might be worth looking at for this purpose.

    The OSR might be an interesting study, too - here everyone TRIES to win, but everyone plays with the awareness that they will most likely lose, at least a lot of the time, and, at least at some level, really looks forward to those losses.

    Also: Call of Cthulhu. Here, like in Fiasco, at least in some modes of play, no one even tries to "win", but plays to see what it looks like when they "lose".

    2. A group of players who approach the game with the right mindset. If you can't get the players on the same page about why to play the game, you're not going to have much luck. A hypothetical group of players who play every moment to WIN will simply not be able to play a game like Fiasco without adjusting their assumptions (or changing the game somehow). The game falls apart if you try to "play to win" at every step of the way.

    In my experience, it is very much not true that all gamers approach games this way. Lots of people play while looking forward to losing just as much as they do to winning. (We'd had a number of discussions here in praise of a technique called "playing to lose", which is considered pretty key for a number of games. It's notable, I think, that this is what the GM does all the time in a typical RPG. And in the games we're discussing, a common feature is making all players share the GM role to a lesser or greater extent - so there may be some connection here, as well. Look at games like Trollbabe, where the rule is that each player narrates their own failure, for example.)

    However, I have seen some gamers exhibit this attitude. A very, very few really do play "only to win". The majority of this subgroup, instead, are used to a certain form of gaming which takes the form of jockeying for advantage continuously, as much as possible. In my experience, most of these players turn around (and really enjoy themselves) when they play a game which operates on a different basis enough to learn to trust that process: "this will be fun and I will enjoy myself even when I stop trying to win". Once that thought settles in (and is supported by a game which is still fun even while they are "losing"), the player drops their playstyle and adapts to a different one (and usually finds it quite liberating).

    Many, many indie RPGs (e.g. Dogs in the Vineyard, Fiasco) straight-up fail when played "to win", and only function when all the players stop trying to win and pursue the creative goals the game presents instead.

    So:

    Yeah, you need a game which supports what you're trying to do, and a group of players who understand and trust that.

    Just like any other game or pursuit!

    \n I find it interesting that, even though in a lot of drama (the "comedy" genre, in the ancient sense of the word) the protagonists repeatedly fail until they achieve what they want, in most "story games" the process we've fixated on as a community is, rather, the opposite one:

    The protagonists decide what they're going after, and typically are given the ability/tools to achieve that thing. However, the game is structured so that the victory they earn is more costly than they expected.

    For example, in Dogs in the Vineyard, it's pretty darned hard to actually challenge the players: they can win just about any conflict if they are set on doing so. However, the system forces the characters to engage in things they don't want to in order to achieve the victory (typically violence, or the risk of injury/death).

    In Apocalypse World, the most typical outcome (a 7-9) gives the protagonist what they were after, but with accompanying complications.

    So, rather than being "fail forward", I'd argue that most indie/story games do a sort of "succeed forward": OK, you get what you want, but at what cost? Was it still worth it? You got what you wanted, but you screwed things up in the process. What will you do now?

    This is, arguably, the method of tragedy (success with unwanted consequences) rather than comedy (an endless string of complete failures, but ending in success).


    (However, in most of these games, including Apocalypse World, this is balanced by character advancement of some sort, so that the character eventually develops the ability to overcome their opposition and have some kind of happy ending. Games in which this does not happen, but actually end in tragedy, like Polaris, are rarer.)

    (Escalation, as pointed out above, is also key here. Many games put apparently very powerful "tools" in the hands of the players, like Dogs in the Vineyard, Sorcerer, Monsterhearts, Poison'd, and Apocalypse World. Then why could these games be considered to be games where "failure" is a common state? Because what they do is give the players/characters the wrong tools for the job. When your goal is to repair a broken relationship, and the tools you have are a gun, a bomb, and the ability to tear out people's throats, the game rewards inappropriate escalation ("You won't help me reconcile with my mother? Then I'll blow up your house!"). This produces its own form of "failure" - once again, the character got what they wanted... but the outcomes are probably not at all what they were hoping for. This is common in Vincent Baker's games, in particular.)

    \n Wow. This thread moved. I'll try to hit as many of the questions/comments as I can.

    @IceCreamEmperor. Not quite how Fiasco works. You establish or resolve a scene, and the dice determine if it's a positive or negative resolution. That doesn't necessarily mean you achieve your goal for that scene (to say nothing of your goal for the story). Depending on how the scene is established, a "positive" outcome can be anything from you simply survive up through getting your scene goal. So yes, half the scenes end "positively", but no, half the scenes don't end with you getting your goal.

    @JDCorley. Again, not quite. Yes, Smallville has a lot of good advice for scene framing, and getting in late and leaving early, which is spot on writing advice just as it's spot on scene framing advice. There's a lot of great stuff about setting up conflicts, wedges, pushing at relationships to create drama... it really does a lot well. But there's practically nothing on the episode level of structure. The GM advice is to work up a lot of great conflicts and wedges, reveal what's going on, stay on those conflicts and push them more... and after a few hours of play, round everyone up and hit the climax. And once that's done, hit your tag scenes. Smallville games I've played in or seen often have the "sagging middle" problem. It's not a bad game at all, it's great, I love it, but it goes about halfway to providing proper story structure onto play.

    Also, there's a huge difference between "the mechanics don't engage until there's conflict" compared to "the mechanics enforce conflict". You can still have floppy scenes without conflict in Smallville. But you can with any game if things don't fall together right, so that's not inherent to Smallville. Firefly goes a step closer with its scene framing, act structure, and the advice on framing scenes. The complications mechanic is great for that twist and turn within a scene, but by-and-large the characters will still succeed.

    @Christopher Kubasik. I've played RPGs for something like 30 years, so that would be a really long list.

    @Asif. Well, I'm drawing on my 30+ years of gaming experience for my sample of gamer tendencies. Admittedly, the "must always win" vibe is from the traditional quarter rather than story games the products or story-games the site. Don't take my word for the definition of what good story is, consult the link provided by @AccountingForTaste to that David Mamet memo. You'll find nearly identical advice and definitions of what drama is in practically every writing book you pick up (prose, genre, television, film, or comic books). So yes, that is exactly what makes for good stories.

    To quote:\n
    "EVERY SCENE MUST BE DRAMATIC. THAT MEANS: THE MAIN CHARACTER MUST HAVE A SIMPLE, STRAIGHTFORWARD, PRESSING NEED WHICH IMPELS HIM OR HER TO SHOW UP IN THE SCENE.

    THIS NEED IS WHY THEY CAME. IT IS WHAT THE SCENE IS ABOUT. THEIR ATTEMPT TO GET THIS NEED MET WILL LEAD, AT THE END OF THE SCENE, TO FAILURE - THIS IS HOW THE SCENE IS OVER. IT, THIS FAILURE, WILL, THEN, OF NECESSITY, PROPEL US INTO THE NEXT SCENE.

    ALL THESE ATTEMPTS, TAKEN TOGETHER, WILL, OVER THE COURSE OF THE EPISODE, CONSTITUTE THE PLOT."
    \nIn short, yes, a character with a goal failing to achieve that goal in various interesting ways till the absolute end of the story, when lo-and-behold, they achieve their goal. If they achieve their goal in the first scene, the "story" is over. It would also be boring and really short. The interesting bits are the scenes in-between with the character failing in interesting ways.

    So @Oren, absolutely. Failing through the story till the end where you finally win is utterly vital to good drama. Once the character wins, the drama is let out of the story, they've achieved their goal. That's what's known as the end of the story.

    @summerdown, absolutely. Rising action. Escalation. Same, same. The characters are inherently lazy and will try the simplest, easiest means to achieve their goals. Once that proves fruitless, they'll try slightly less easy means, when that fails, slightly less easy means... etc on down to the rousing climax when the wild and absurd action finally gets the character their goal.

    @Paul_T. I would say that there are a lot of the components present in various games, yes, but only a few with something close to the "total package", like Fiasco or Primetime Adventures.     \nSo of the two dominant resolutions to dramatic scenes we have one that's fairly well represented and one that isn't as much. Which leads back to a question in my OP, "I know there are quite a few games that have versions of "fail forward" and "succeed at cost" mechanics, they're really popular in the last few years, but are there any games that rely almost exclusively on those? And as a gamer, would you get frustrated with that setup?" With the added experience of the thread, that comes down to the question: Would "yes, but" along with "no, and" be enough to keep you* as a gamer engaged throughout a session of play?

    * General you, rather than specific you.

    Comparing "yes, but" to "no, and" it's interesting that either way, something bad is going to happen. Your option is binary, you either succeed or fail at the scene goal at hand, however, no matter what, something bad is going to happen as a result of your actions. And generally speaking, that "something bad" is going to dominate the next scene.

    There's also something that seems to have been somewhat lost in the conversation, the distinction between task resolution and scene resolution. While a character may succeed at a simple task that's one step among several to achieve their scene goal, ultimately something bad is going to happen at the end of the scene that propels the story into the next. So if your scene goal is to crack the safe and you have several tasks related to that goal (say breaking into the building, getting into the office, then actually cracking the safe) then you could certainly succeed on those steps leading to the scene goal, but by and large the result of the scene will be negative. Either you crack the safe ("yes, but") the alarm goes off; or you accidentally trip the deadlock ("no, and furthermore") you create so much noise the guards come running.

    There's also the distinction made by several posters between the micro and macro goals. Your overarching macro goal won't be accomplished till the end of the story, but some micro goals might be, though there will always be a cost ("yes, but"). If you want to really go wild, there are task questions, scene questions, act questions, and an overarching story question.\n
    But to be able to willfully call up that sensibility and access it in a pro-active way that's both meaningful and collaborative? That's a talent that can be trained. Like all talents it can be viewed as producing a broad spectrum of performance quality. Like all talents, some are born with a lot of it, and yet we can all train ourselves to do better at it. The people on this site (by and large) have already put themselves through an awful lot of training.
    \nI get the gist of what you're saying, and writing engaging fiction (no matter the format) is certainly a talent, but I'm not sure someone needs to be talented in that regard to come up with a few pieces of dramatic story. And that's all we're talking about here, a few pieces of drama each over the course of a session. Like with Fiasco and PTA, you're dividing the workload between the players present. All you've got to come up with really is a few engaging scenes for your own character in the milieu of the setting. And honestly that's what the structure is there for, to break down a big question into smaller, more manageable pieces.

    Take the one-hour TV drama structure:

    Teaser: action, inciting incident.
    Act One: confusion, determination to figure out what's going on.
    Act Two: conflict between the characters and whatever stands in their way.
    Act Three: despair, the low point of the story, all seems lost.
    Act Four: redemption, the characters rally and start to overcome their obstacles.
    Tag: reflection, coming to grips with the story that has just unfolded.

    It takes the dreaded "what's the story" question and turns it into six pieces. The teaser kicks off the story, the tag wraps up the story, so that leaves the acts. Each act has fairly obvious distinctions between its neighbors, and reacting to a situation presented by the GM will typically have some fairly obvious responses and necessary first steps.

    Take PTA as an example. The Producer has an idea for a general story, something that's pregnant with possibility and presents it to the players as the teaser. Say a murder for the police procedural game they're playing.

    Every act each player gets one spotlight scene. It's got to be dramatic, the spotlight character must have a goal, they will struggle with their opposition in whatever form it takes, and ultimately they will face a setback ("no, and" or "yes, but").

    In act one the PCs are doing the initial investigation of the crime. They're confused but determined to do what they can. You're playing a detective and one of the first questions you'd likely ask is "Did anyone see the crime" or "Who found the body". That's a scene. You've got a character (your PC), in a location (the crime scene), with a goal ("Did anyone..." or "Who found..."). Your opposition would be the witnesses or the person who found the corpse. You try to get information out of them and you have a rough time of it, but whether you get any information (yes or no) you're going to suffer a setback of some kind, such as the witness is a drunk, or the guy who found the body was committing a crime. Cool. That's one scene down. Who's next. Now you as a player get to spend the rest of the act thinking of a good question or goal for your character's next scene.

    Scene goals are ultimately simple questions, it's the answers that complicate things. Coming up with a few questions that suit the act you're in over the course of an evening's play isn't that hard. Each player asks one or two questions per act, play through those till you get through the acts, tag that puppy, and suddenly you've got a game session that's played like a one-hour TV drama. Sure, as mentioned above, it's likely a first draft version of a story, but it's still an actual story with drama in roughly the right shape for an engaging story in-and-of-itself.

    And ultimately that's what I'm talking about. Games designed to explicitly produce something adjacent to proper story structure through play. It doesn't need to be polished, perfect, or a best seller, but it should contain all those necessary elements. Dramatic scenes, cliffhangers, rising action, climax, denouement, etc.

      Teaser

      For that matter, just what the heck is an Act, anyway? That's really not been discussed. Film Crit Hulk has a pretty good essay on this (which criticizes the the three act structure of movies, btw) that gives a good definition, namely:

      An Act is an irrevocable decision point.

      An Act ends when something has happened that changes the story or the characters in a way that can't be undone. It's propulsive, it creates the new situation that the characters will respond to.

      Let's look at an example probably most folks here are familiar with: the Star Trek (TOS) episode, City on the Edge of Forever. Here's how the Acts break up, based on the commercial breaks:

      Teaser: Sulu gets hurt, McCoy injects himself with a drug, goes nuts.
      Act I: McCoy escapes to the planet, Kirk et al follow. Gateway discovered, McCoy goes through, present is altered wiping out the Enterprise
      Act II: Spock and Kirk go back to the 1930s in pursuit of McCoy; take refuge at the mission. Spock begins building device to read the tricorder. Kirk becomes attracted to Edith Keeler. Spock discovers there are two possible futures; act ends on the words "Edith Keeler must die."
      Act III: McCoy arrives, shelters at Mission without Kirk and Spock seeing him. Kirk realizes he is in love with Edith. Spock finds out which future they need: "Edith Keeler must die."
      Act IV: Spock warns Kirk about protecting Edith when she nearly falls down stairs. Kirk, Spock, McCoy reunite; Kirk keeps McCoy from saving Edith.
      Tag (note that there's no break here, ST: TOS didn't have one between Act IV & the Tag): Kirk, Spock, McCoy return to the future, everything as it was; reject offer of the Gateway. "Let's get the hell out of here."

You could make the Teaser integrated with Act I (it really is--this was more a function of the medium), and have Act I end when McCoy goes through.       I stopped reading Jim Butcher's novels after the third one because they were literally the same thing over and over and over. I honestly do not understand why anyone would want this to be a model for story games. The character always failing is exactly as boring as the character always succeeding; prescribing this as a model for fiction -- let alone interactive, improvisational roleplaying -- just makes no sense to me. Die Hard was not a good movie because of the structure of its conflict, or because it adhered to some formula of endless protagonist failure. And lest it be suggested that this formula is somehow a minimum or baseline required for success, there are tons of great movies that have nothing remotely like this sort of structure.

      It's obviously a good idea to think about narrative structure when playing and especially designing a game, but the notion that only games that adhere to this One Specific Model are producing 'real stories' or 'good stories' is poisonous to interesting gameplay and design. Every game has its own needs, and every game can have its own conventions and structures and ways of having players interact with conflict and storytelling.

      The whole idea that it is the failure or success, the 'need to win' or not, just seems way beside the point of what makes for interesting dramatic scenes. It's the aboutness that makes them interesting, and gives them focus; it's that the character wants something at all, and that the thing they want and their desire for it is in of itself interesting and compelling to us. That they get it or do not get it in any particular scene is not what makes it a story or not. The need to 'win' is not the problem; the need to resolve things conclusively is, if anything, a more damaging impulse, because it defuses or negates or fulfills the desire too quickly.

      Either way, 'protagonist failure' and 'protagonist success' both presuppose that there is something relevant that can be succeeded at or failed at -- that there is a goal or desire that matters to us, as players or as readers. If you've reached the point of deciding whether the character succeeds or fails, then you already have a story. It may turn out to be shorter than you want, or longer, but the 'real story' part is already done, even if the very first thing the hero does is kiss the girl and kill the dragon and grab the treasure and ride off on his perfect white horse. Even if the hero suffers an interminably tedious number of socially and physically battering setbacks until finally they manage to have a shower and solve the case with the help of their talking skull. And sure, if that happens every time, it could get boring, but it won't stop being a story.
