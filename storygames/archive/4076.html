\n As I've mentioned on the Game Design forum, I'm writing a Shoujo Manga Storytelling Game.

It features Guan Yin as a dim-sum delivery girl from Anywhere, Japan, training those who are detached enough to see Hungry Ghosts - yet love enough to care that the Ghosts are making their victims obsessed in order to eat the good in their victim's passions - training the player characters as Magical Psychopomp Guardians, who give the Dead the rest they crave and the Living the love they need.

Cool stuff, I think. And I hope you agree.

I think that this, in fact, the sort of thing that would get new blood, especially Girly Narrativist Yingers (as opposed to Macho Narrativist Yangers, discussed in jest at length on www.lumpley.com) into the hobby.

Now, I am not afraid of my capacity to approach girls about this Cool Thing: Now that I have accepted that I will always be afraid of staring into places other than their eyes and babbling about stupid minutiae like (frexample) hit points and armor class, I am paradoxically much more likely to look them in the eye and have normal, interesting conversations with them.

What I am afraid of is presenting them with the core concepts of one of those are pee gee thingummies without confusing them and hence, boring them to tears.

How does one (by which I mean, "how do you") present a storytelling game to people who have no concept of "storytelling game?"

    \n That is a pretty tough question and one I grappled with for a while. The best solution I came up with?

    Don't.

    Don't try to explain "This is what a storytelling game is" at all. Just tell them how your game works. Instruct them in how to play it. Then they know how to play your game, which is ultimately, what you're aiming for.

    That's what I've decided to do. I'll let you now in 6 months or so if it works

    \n I used to sell card and board games in a mall, and I found the best way to demo a game to anyone was to just jump in and play it with them, showing them the basics as we went and letting them make the jumps they need to make to understand the game. It worked well then and it works well now. We demo Panty Explosion for a lot of first time players, many of the anime and manga fans. Many of them women. Instead of trying to explain the game, e just play it. It's like test driving a car. You don't need to explain how the engine works or how the steering column connects to the whatever. the joy of the test drive comes from the drive itself.

    \n i use the following phrases when describing THE IDEA OF STORY GAMES to people who've not played anything they describe as story games before:

    1. "kind of like a radio-play"
    2. "kind of like playing 'Pretend', but with more rules to point it in the right direction, so someone dosen't swerve away from what you've all agreed on"
    3. "kind of like improvised theatre, but less stressful"
    4. "kind of like making up a movie with your friends, with no audience except each other."

    and then i also make sure that they understand the IDEA i'm talking about generally DOESN'T include:

    1. computers
    2. costumes
    3. acting things out, "except if your character is doing a sweet move of some kind and you need to illustrate it".

    and my responses to other posts:

    jake: makes sense that that would work. when dealing with a specific game, deal only in the specifics. that way there is not the same opportunity for confusion.

    idea of American folks knowing about rpgs: i'd say that "knowing there is such a thing as rpgs" and "knowing anything about those things" is very different. one person's "oh yeah, a bunch of friends making up a story after dinner once a month" is another person's "don't you meet every week and dress up funny and if you die in the game you have to kill yourself?" is someone else's "my cousin does that, he sold his character on ebay for $200" is also "yeah, i pay some guy $X to look after my kids and he runs games for them, it's kid stuff."

    ben, sometimes "starting from scratch" is way better than "starting from misinformation" IMHO

    // i'm also into the idea of explaining from scratch generally. not "like this and this, but different", just "this is what this thing is."

    hope this all helps.
