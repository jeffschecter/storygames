\n Ever been tired, underprepared, and basically out of the groove, and have a player step up and carry a session?

I've seen this kind of thing in lots of other social situations, where the normal social tempest has an off night, someone else shows leadership, and everyone else just has a great time anyway. Happens with board games too.

Have you ever experienced it? Is there anything that a game can do to make this more possible?

    \n I've definitely seen this happen. It gives me a fuzzy, warm feeling.

    As for game design--maybe a design that provides people with some in-game advantage for taking a strong leadership role and pushing for conflict or action could encourage this?

    What to avoid, probably--designs that have "choke points" where everything hangs on the decision or creative input of one player, and no one else can contribute or help them out. The GM in a traditional game setup can be in this position, which can end badly if the GM is having an off night.

    \n erm, I think I carried an entire 12-session campaign as an "alpha player" in the last traditional campaign I played in.

    The problem with this approach was that I made the campaign center around me and my character. This worked ok with 3 people (GM + me + other player) but quickly caused problems with 4 or more people, as some people felt "left out"

    As far as more possible, I think of Polaris as institutionalizing alpha-playerness, where the Heart is kinda a conventional player in the sense of controlling a protagonist, but also "carries" the game.

    Actually, on reflection, I carried the first half of the campaign, but didn't do so the second half. Things that changed that made me abandon carrying the game:

    1. Average number of players increased, so I intentionally mellowed out so others could have scene time.

    2. Went from a high-information environment (city we all grew up in) to a low-information, mystery driven environment (mysterious magic wilderness)

    3. Character had special resources in the first half (contacts, social status) that did not apply to second half.

    4. GM constantly declared almost all my schemes catastrophic failures. Eventually whenever I got into character I felt as if she had "lost confidence" in her abilities and turtled.

    5. Stakes increased from "can you help these other people" to "can you survive?"

    \n I've had this happen, though in a slightly different sense.. I won't say I've never had fall-down days where I wasn't up to the task of GMing, but that's not what I'm talking about.
    \n
    \nI'm talking about those players who are enthusiastic and proactive. They don't wait for the GM to lay out the plots before them, they go forth and forge their own trails. For a GM that is cool with player empowerment, this type of player is a godsend. For more controlling GMs, this player is a nightmare.
    \n
    \nIn my longest-running V:tM game, this player became the leader of the Pack. At first he was just the social leader, inside and outside the game.. But eventually he decided that his character would make a bid for Pack Leader. Due to his proactivity, I essentially just reacted to the players' actions, instead of having to plan it all out and lead them along. Outside the game, he was the driving force for getting the players to meet every week. He's a large part of the reason I ended up having 12 players on the roster of that game. Thankfully, the number of active players at any time was never that high, but there was this one, crazy night...
    \n
    \nIn my other long-running groups, it was usually my Co-GMs who ended up being this "Alpha Player", as I ended up being the Alpha Player in their games. The guy above hadn't ever really gamed before we met up, and had never run a game in his life, though.
    \n
    \nOne possibly bad thing I've noticed, on both sides, is that the Alpha Player can sometimes, unintentionally, hog the spotlight. As a GM, I found I tended to focus on the proactive, strong player more than the others. As a player, I found that my proactivity tended to turn the other players into followers, rather than equal contributors. This sort of thing is only really combatted by both the GM and the Alpha Player being aware of it, and actively working to not exclude the other players from the spotlight.
    \n
    \n(Cross-posted with William: That's exactly what I was talking about above!)
    \n
    \nAs for how to encourage this sort of play... I think player empowering mechanics are your best shot. My ReCoil (which I've been playtesting) definitely flows smoother with one or more strong, proactive players. For players less sure about taking the reins, it slows down a lot. I'm also thinking, in the case of ReCoil, that the possibilities are a little too wide open, which can contribute to the slow down, but the general point remains.. Player empowering mechanics only work when you've got at least one player who wants to be empowered.

Dust Devils does this for sure, Cold City similarly though to a slightly lesser degree. Any more examples?

    \n There's always a player who is most in the groove and is the social leader. Sometimes it's the GM, sometimes it's not. There's no special requirement that it should be the GM, other than some gaming traditions. Decoupling the alpha player role as a requirement for the GM role has done nothing but make my game experiences better.

    The people I play with now have no expectation that the GM take on this alpha player, social leader role by default.

    EDIT to answer Ryan's last question:
    Several games have mechanics that support the decoupling of this role from the GM duties. Our group made it happen more often simply by playing these games a whole lot.

    - Universalis
    - Primetime Adventures
    - Trollbabe
    - The Shadow of Yesterday
    - InSpectres
