\nDrazhu's Slave Pit, which saw a lot of use at Gen Con. A map with some keyed encounters, treasure cards, and a new playbook tied to the setting. I ran this in 1:50 without any problems - it is very simple and straightforward. Make sure to ask if anyone has played before and give the new playbook to experienced Dungeon World players.

    \n So this is where this adventure came from. I remember seeing it when I was looting one of the playset packs from GoD when I was looking for an extra copy of the cleric spell sheet.

    Just for the heck of it, I ran and adventure based on the map and monsters without actually reading anything else. We established an entirely different scenario for it based on the way players were answering their setup questions. Basically, in the session I ran the party was trying to bring down an ice demon that the wizard accidentally summoned. The wizard had recruited a small army to seize the fortress, but only six (the players) survived the earthquake. The orc's had taken up worshiping the demon, because he brought cold to the tropical area the adventure was set it. It actually went pretty well, and I had a good laugh just now reading the way it was "supposed" to be.

    When a slave follower aids in the escape\u2026
    Give the slave a name and a brief description, and Roll
    On a 10+, they help the Hero, allowing them to Reroll or take +1 Forward.
    On a 7-9, they help the Hero, but are exhausted (physically or mentally). Mark off one Escaping Slave.

    I'm thinking of providing maybe 5 Escaping Slaves per player count below 4. So, for two players, they'd have 10 Escaping Slaves with them. It also allows for a resource that I can make moves against.

    \n I like it Lor!
    But to me it seems like a grand opportunity to introduce the Hireling rules to new players
    Plus, rather than a simple +1 forward, the custom move could give some narrative tags to hang your story on... as well as the mechanical advantage and access to those laughable advanced moves!

    When you help a fellow slave pit to escape...
    Give the slave a name and a brief description, including a skill.
    They have a 0 Loyalty 0 [skill] and a Cost of freedom

    Then just use the order hirelings and skill rules as normal:\n
    Order Hirelings
    Hirelings do what you tell them to, so long as it isn't obviously dangerous, degrading, or stupid, and their cost is met. When a hireling find themselves in a dangerous, degrading, or just flat-out crazy situation due to your orders roll+loyalty. On a 10+ they stand firm and carry out the order. On a 7-9 they do it for now, but come back with serious demands later. Meet them or the hireling quits on the worst terms.

    \n Bumped my game up to tonight! It was loads of fun. All Pathfinder players and DW n00bs, and they all had fun. The item cards were a big hit, especially having to choose one side only. As a demo of the system, I think they picked it up well, although they got the sense it was better suited to one-shots (I explained that in a longer campaign, fronts would help construct a bigger ongoing story). Timing-wise, we played for about 2.5 hours, but I could have easily tightened it up if we had a fixed deadline. The party was a chaotic druid of the blasted wasteland, a goody-two-shoes young fighter, and a stoic ranger (no pit slaves, sadly).

    Highlights include:

    - The druid convinces the giant spider to let them pass, in exchange for feeding them other pit slaves; the other characters just think he made friends with it
    - The young fighter goes after every shiny-thing-just-out-of-reach I can throw at him
    - The Druid's opening question determines that he was helping Drahzu summon a colossus to destroy the world, but they clashed and he got thrown in the pits
    - The sticky bridge and the assassin vines provide much excitement - the druid, in spider form, carries the ranger across on his back - they tumble off, and the druid transforms into a giant eagle, and they fly around tilting at the vines with a goblin spear
    - The stuff in the mysterious spider jar turns out to spawn thousands and thousands of tiny poisonous spiders (the druid, in spider form, covers himself with them)
    - Drahzu is keeping the ranger's animal companion captive in his sepulchre
    - In a very weird climax, the party slays Drahzu, but in the process the young fighter dies. His personification of death, the evil blacksmith god, offers him a bargain: he must complete Drahzu's work and forge the colossus who will make the world his anvil. Now the fighter needs to kill the druid; the druid dies, and the death of the wasteland berates him for his foolishness - Drahzu was in league with the Forger, and the druid played right into his hand. His bargain: the druid must fight against the agents of the Forger and prevent the colossus from being summoned. After some awkward PVP (the system doesn't really support it but we did it anyway for fun), the two avatars of opposing gods agree to escape the pit and part ways... but their destinies are now in direct opposition, and the world will be shaken in the ensuing conflict.

    Now I kind of want to run a whole campaign set in the world of this epic battle!

    \n We played it the last two days at PAX GOD.

    Yesterday's wizard charmed the goblin whipmaster then cast light on him, the idea being they wouldn't have to spare a hand for a torch. The goblin fell into the pit and splattered. His glowing head still served the party, until they were confronted by an apparition of Drazhu summoned by an "unwanted attention" roll on an Unseen Servant casting. Drazhu's ruby eyes lich-lasered the goblin head, spraying glowing ichor everywhere, which was spread upon shields as a kind of gooey luminescent butter, providing sufficient light to escape the pit and the dungeon.

    In today's iteration, Drazhu was destroyed, but the bard was decapitated in the final battle. Death told him that he could return to life ... if he swore to remain in Drazhu's hall forever after. And so the sad voiceless bard was resurrected long after his friends had left, to inherit Drazhu's obsidian throne and its curse.

    Next time I run this module, I'm going to add a bit about anti-magic wards being inscribed on the walls of the excavation area. These will be destroyed by the initial earthquake and will explain why the Wizard hasn't charmed the goblin whipmaster yet...
