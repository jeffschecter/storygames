    Here's something neat that you might think is a death spiral, but isn't:

    In Pirates of the Spanish Main (and its sequels), your ships shoot the masts off other ships. When the ship has no masts, it's "derelict" and can be stolen. When it takes one more hit, it sinks. Ships have between one and five masts.

    Each of those masts is a cannon, so they represent offensive capabilities.

    Now, what's interesting is that you'd think that whoever struck first would win the game, right? Well, what's neat is that, very often, you have to break off an attack to go do something else pressing. Since the number of masts (ironically) doesn't relate to the speed of the ship, that ship's almost always as good as new except in a fight. So it can go get repaired or do non-combat stuff.

    What's interesting is that it's a strategic decision much of the time: "Oh, you've got some good guns there. I'm gonna make you waste time fixing your good ship while I'm doing something else, even though I'll lose this little dinghy I'm attacking you with."

    Because you reduce your opponent's offensive capability when you attack, it often makes sense to attack dudes bigger than you for a strategic advantage.

    The game's almost always tense until the last turn, as a result.

    Cold Iron (my version available here) does have both individual and party death spirals. It has a particularly strong individual death spiral if a character is hit for more than half his hit points in one blow since defense for the next round drops dramatically (3 for being below 1/2 hit points, 4 for having taken more than 1/2 hit points the previous round). The party death spiral is strong because defense against a 2nd opponent is less than against the first, and the 3rd and 4th defenses are even worse.

    One thing that I think is important in Cold Iron though is the effect death spirals have on the opposition. It usually means that once the PCs start winning the battle, it finishes quickly.

    Hmm, for a board game, an interesting example is Backgammon, where the player who is significantly behind may not be able to get captured pieces back on the board, and thus get's stalled (and when they do, the winning player often can immediately capture the piece again).

    What's interesting that a friend pointed out, is that if one actually uses the doubling cube, Backgammon then has a significant incentive for the losing player to capitulate.     And this reminds me of a very nasty death spiral D&D has. If your character dies, and is raised, he loses a level. Which sets him up to be behind the power curve and increases the probablility of future deaths. This is a rule I almost always ignored. It's interesting to note that Cold Iron does not have such a rule.
