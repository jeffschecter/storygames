Hi Dan,\n
It's not about who has "final say" ("The GM! Because the GM is God!" "No, the players, because they elect the God and can walk out!" -- You're better off not going down the "final say" rabbit hole to begin with).
\nThis is why I refer to 'sympathetic decision'. In the quoted example, that sympathy between participants about contributions is absent, it's an out and out power struggle. If it were a jam session then it'd be one musician raising his instrument and bashing the other musician in the head with it, so only the first musician is heard.

The irony is that that bashing in the head is usually triggered by the very idea there is no final say and we all 'know' the supposed 'one' fictional space. Primed with this myth, someones idea of the fiction goes out of sync with others, but they don't acknowledge it as having gone out of sync; under the idea of the myth, to them there is only ONE fictional space and someone else is failing to refer to it! So to them it's as if someone has shifted the pieces on the chess board - just out and out cheating! "Of freakin' 'course I have height advantage!?' 'Of freakin' 'course I can grab the can of peaches! It's right in front of my character!'. At that point, they think they can shout over the person who, textually, has the final say. Cue the power struggle from the quote. Which ironically, by the text, isn't legitimate play.

Once the ignoring of who textually has the final say is legitimised, then over some amount of time participants realise that they have hit a Mutually Assured Destruction situation. Cold war like, they retreat and avoid the rabbit hole. However, it tends to lead to play like Matrix Gamer describes, where certain individuals, mostly based on social politics, get the most say. Which Matrix Gamer then goes to fix by...having a mechanic which determines who gets the final say. I think he does really well at simply approaching and dealing with the taboo rabbit hole.

In the former, though mechanically dense, fiction is really king, and in the later, the mechanics have more teeth.

    \n I'm not yet sure if I follow Callan's issue, but the issue described by Komradebob and Zachary_Wolf has interesting practical effects. There was a recent thread about player vs. player battles in Dungeon World where this point seemed to matter. The crux of the argument was that (to me anyway) it didn't seem fair that "fictional positioning" determined which player used which move (and, thus, who had a mechanical advantage) because the fiction was ultimately determined by the GM. So (to me) it followed that the GM was deciding who is more likely to win or lose a fight between two players. I felt like the disconnect in the discussion was a result of friction between two points of view: on the one hand, that fiction "exists" in some platonic state to be uncovered by the questions of the GM -- which would mean that the "fiction" and not the GM is determining which player has an advantage; or, on the other hand, that the GM at least influences (if not outright creates) the fiction and, therefore, personally determines who has an advantage.

    I don't particularly want to get into that Dungeon World discussion again -- that dead horse was soundly beaten -- but, I think unpacking the "who is really making the fiction?" question is useful when talking about the relationship between fiction and mechanics. It seems like there is a tendancy to say something like, "if you are so worried that the GM isn't fair, then maybe you should get another GM," or "just trust the other players and have a good time," and that's all well and good, but I feel like there are cases where the problem isn't a lack of trust, but rather that the GM (or somebody) MUST make fictional decisions which have mechanical consequences that are more fair to one player (or group of players) and less fair to another -- even if that GM is the most fair being in the world -- and that seems to be a design problem that is worth considering.

    For another concrete example, my group was playing Stars Without Number, and the creative Expert character kept doing really smart fun tricky things to get out of trouble and harm bad guys using his skill rolls. The Fighter Space Marine character noticed that the Fighter niche thing "fighting" was a) much more likely to fail and b) much more likely to get him killed because the mechanics of fighting -- in that game -- are different from the mechanics of skill rolls. So, simply because of the way the rules worked, the Fighter character felt like he was at a disadvantage to contribute to the game. (We ended up switching to a hack of Lady Blackbird to finish the campaign). In retrospect, I could have been less willing to roll with the stuff that the Expert was trying to do or made the consequences of failure more dire in order to enforce greater balance and allow everyone to have similar risk and similar spotlights -- but that just means that the GM is turning the difficulty dials with fiction and that the rules are, as discussed upthread, secondary. Thinking about this kind of thing is worthwhile for the reasons also mentioned upthread.

      \nI think this is a great point.

      I agree that it can seem problematic when mechanical procedures are based on unregulated subjective fiction -- i.e. when the "crunchy" parts of the game (rules, procedures, tables, dice) are controlled by the "mushy" parts of the game (imagined fiction). The reason this appears problematic is because the crunchy parts become more or less illusory. Why even have rules, if ultimately they only function at the whim of a GM (or a consensus agreement)? Why not just skip the middle man and make everything fiat or agreement? I think the answer is that there is no reason not to make everything fiat or consensus. Moreover, in practice, that is pretty much what happens in many games -- the "rules" are simply guidelines to help the omnipotent decision makers apply their judgments in a more or less consistent way. And, though it seems problematic if you don't recognize what you are really doing (MotherMayI) it's a perfectly functional way to engage in collaborative storytelling. I have players who WANT to be rewarded for creative descriptions with a mechanical bonus to success. They would be annoyed if they COULDN'T speak fiction to seek GM sympathy. That's the game they choose to play . . .

      There are other ways to do it of course. In games like Archipelago and Polaris, it's the other way around, the crunchy parts control the mushy parts. In games like 3:16 Carnage Amongst the Stars, the crunchy parts and the mushy parts don't really interact much at all. In games like Apocalypse World, the GM's fictional powers are limited (the GM can only make soft moves until a player either blows a roll or intentionally ignores imminent harm). In games like Lady Blackbird, the consequences to crunchy failure are mushy story stuff, so all the mechanics ever do is redirect the mushy story part in a different direction -- the sympathy issue isn't such a big deal.

      So, Callan's donut hole doesn't strike me as a dangerous pitfall. It's just a thing to be aware of and to fiddle with.

      \nSure, okay. But if it's persuasion and influence, why call it sympathy? That's just special pleading to make the claim that role-playing is essentially arbitrary without actually arguing it. Because when we play, we agree to be bound by its rules--by some accounts, that's the very definition of "game"--and those rules will tend to bound off some forms of influence as being outside the rules. "Convince me to activate this mechanic," says the GM to me. If I say, "I'll give you $20 if you do," I'm cheating, right? How about if I say, "I'll be a fucking bitch for the rest of the game session if you don't"? What counts as legitimate persuasion in any given instance is bound up in the rules and the larger "system," and designers can definitely play with that. But we imagine that the GM (and in GM-less games, everyone at the table) is making judgments about the diegetic plausibility of the claim "I do X," and it's that judgment that affects decisions about which mechanics to implement if any, not merely the exercise of interpersonal influence.

      Maybe I missed it in the foregoing discussion, but where is there room for the fiction in the ideas of RPGs as pure interpersonal influence? Players' understandings of what's happening in-game matter as much as who's clamoring to be heard, et cetera, and so talking about "the person who controls the fiction" is kind of begging the question. Callan's example demonstrates that: the GM didn't know that "following the left-hand wall" is a tried-and-true maze-solving strategy. So his reaction to Callan's I-do-X assertion seemed to have been skepticism, not because he disliked Callan or whatever, but because he couldn't see how it would work in the fiction. If Callan had demonstrated the empirical verity of his claim--by showing how with Legos or referring to some authority on labyrinths, for example--you can say, "Oh, yeah, see, he's exercising interpersonal influence," but I'm not sure how far that gets you. The (player's understanding of the) fiction still exerts its effect.

      Notice that I'm not saying that players don't influence each other; they absolutely do. But part of the medium through which that influence is exercised is the fiction. And there are people who "play the table" rather than playing the game, so Callan is not wrong to observe that it's a thing. But it's not the only thing, and taken to a ludicrous extreme it's either cheating or being a spoilsport.

      Also, I'm not arguing for the independent existence of the fiction outside of the players' understandings--it is indeed imaginary. But it does "act" upon the players' judgments, a joint creation that, if I am bound by the rules of the game, I will treat as having a real impact on what can happen next.

      \n Hey Callan, I must admit I haven't read the second page of the thread, but after reading the first and third, I have a few thoughts:

      1) Yep, RPG rules lack "final say" powers re: which procedure we engage when, instead leaving those to one or more players.

      2) This is also true of every game ever, though to a lesser degree. When the rules in Chess say "a player's turn ends when they take their hand off the piece", the two players still need to agree when this has in fact happened. "You let go!" "No I didn't!" will mess up Chess just as much as "I don't have to roll to climb the stairs!" "Yes you do!" messes up an RPG. Every shared activity, even ordering a pizza, has its MotherMayI component. So the idea that rules will directly lead to game actions without player assent is completely out the window.

      3) Given #2, I'd like to point out that games use a variety of techniques to elicit that assent. Off the top of my head, I can think of "define it precisely", "show why it's functional", "provide procedures for dispute resolution". Most non-RPG games seem to go with precise definition, letting functionality be demonstrated in play (i.e. "this game works and is fun when we use the rules"). If there's one way in which RPGs differ on this front, it's in the difficulties of applying precise definition to spoken fiction, and the consequent reliance on (1) eliciting buy-in with various schemas and spins and examples, and (2) providing troubleshooting assistance in the form of endless advice and optional procedures.

      4) I am not a huge fan of spin and advice. Fortunately, we've seen some good techniques for producing rules-based assent with a minimum of player/GM subjectivity. Take for example the Apocalypse World move "Seduce/Manipulate", which has the following features:
      - a rule states "When you try to seduce or manipulate someone, roll 2d6 + Hot."
      - another rule states "to make the roll, you must take the in-fiction action"
      - another rule states "to take the in-fiction action, you must make the roll"
      - success on the roll guarantees you good, useful outcomes
      - failure on the roll is likely to produce a fun and entertaining development for the table
      - there are certain things the GM can't do to the character, ever, unless such a roll is made

      Accordingly, when a player wants to seduce an NPC, who's going to say "no, I don't assent to that"? Usually, everyone at the table has some reason to want the seduction attempt to happen.

      If the GM doesn't like the idea of this NPC being seduced, they can certainly go outside the game to lobby for that, just as a player in Chess can lobby against a move on out-of-game grounds like "we played that way last time" or "it'll be over too fast". But that's not a hole in the game.

      If the GM simply says, "No, you can't roll," then they are explicitly breaking the first rule above, the one that tells the player, "when you try to seduce, roll". Similarly, if the player tries to seduce someone who's in a coma, just because that technically falls within the bounds of the most immediate formal rules, that's just bad behavior that I can't lay at the design's doorstep.

      5) No, Apocalypse World isn't actually like Chess, and yes, AW offers more potential room for reasonable disagreement regarding things like which NPCs it's fictionally plausible to seduce when. Please forgive any exaggeration in my prior point. My real point here is that AW's approach turns the link between the Seduce/Manipulate rules and player behavior into a relative no-brainer at a relatively good rate, eliciting a relatively high rate of automatic assent from all participants. Relative, that is, to (e.g.) a D&D climb check, whose viability and difficulty and relevance and success/failure outcomes can be debated endlessly and really demand an explicit player "final say" rather than an implicit one.

      So, if we can move a little bit away from D&D skills, and a little closer to Chess, who's to say we can't go even a little farther, with rules that are more precise, and convincing, and thorough?

      In other words, in terms of your original A/B formulation, I don't see it as either "A: no hole" or "B: hole", but rather as a continuum between "A: small hole" and "B: huge hole". I agree with your advice against treating B like A in RPG design, but with the caveat that one has options to move toward A if one so desires.

      3) I move my queen's pawn 2 spaces. You instantly acknowledge that as an allowed move, as per the rules.

      5) You remove your pawn from the board and play proceeds.

      1) I learn what my heat vision power does.
      2) At a moment in the story, I imagine a circumstance that lets me use my heat vision. Maybe someone else imagines a circumstance that doesn't let me use my heat vision. Now we can't proceed until someone gives the final say on which circumstance exists.

      1) I learn what my heat vision power does, and that I can use it on any visible stationary NPC within 14 meters, and that the GM controls NPC movement and position, and that the fiction must remain plausible, and plausibility is voted on.

      1) I learn that plausibility is voted on whenever there's a disagreement.
      2) The above disagreement happens.

      2) It is established that the NPC is visible, stationary, and within 14 meters.
      3) I announce that I shoot them with my heat vision. Everyone acknowledges that as an allowed move, as per the rules.

      Philosopher of games Bernard Suits, in his book "The Grasshopper," defines a game as a goal-directed activity we undertake having accepted certain means-related limitations that prevent us from achieving that goal in the most efficient way. A foot race? The goal is get from point A to point B, but we can't take shortcuts across the track and we can't use a bicycle, even though that would mean getting to point B faster. Even make-believe games could be more efficient by providing each player with a script, he says.

      So the rules specify the conditions under which they pertain, and players in general make a good-faith effort to apply them under those conditions, having agreed to abide by the restrictions the game imposes on them. The players who don't do that, according to Suits, are either cheaters (who care about winning, but not about following the rules) or spoilsports (who don't care about the game at all). There's not a lot you can do to police those motivations, as it turns out--and in some cases, one person's cheating is another person's how you play the game. My friend J.       \n Callan, I think the "holes" metaphor is getting away from you--or maybe I'm just getting lost. I read you originally, in wanting to distinguish between "chess-like" and "hole-like" rules, where "chess-like" means something like algorithmic (implying some degree of procedural certitude or specificity) and "hole-like" means something like underdetermined, as saying that this under-determination was somehow problematic, perhaps on principle, because it meant that whoever had to decide in the moment how to deal with whatever the rules didn't cover--specifically, whether they applied in a given instance--was either (a) susceptible to social-level pressures in his or her decision-making ("Shouldn't I get a Luck bonus on this roll? Pretty please?") or (b) able to substitute personal preferences or affect for more measured or objective judgment ("Ah, I don't want this to happen because that player is a pain in the neck--I'll tell him no"). Now I'm not sure anymore if you're saying that it's problematic, and "hole-like" seems to have morphed into sheer randomness, based on the poker example above. But maybe it's just a bad example.

      The thing is, we knew about rules being underdetermined already; it's the basis for the distinction about task vs. conflict resolution, for example. I may have a 75% chance to Move Silently, but how many times do I have to do it before I'm past all the guards? In a task resolution system, the GM sets up the situation, and there's nothing stopping her from just making me keep rolling until I fail, because she wants me to get caught (or, conversely, allowing me to roll something else to avoid capture even when I blow my Move Silently and alert the guards--"You hear the guard approaching! Quick, roll Hide in Shadows to see if he doesn't notice you!"). In conflict resolution, we know that if I blow this roll, the guard wins and I lose--the consequences of which are set either when we explicitly set stakes beforehand, or when the GM "acts in the interests of" the guard following an NPC victory. Now that's not to say that we can't resolve the conflict between me and the guard using a task resolution system, but the rules as such don't pin down exactly what needs to happen before we can say who's won and who's lost.

      Now, there are lots of different solutions to the problem of rules underdetermination. One is to add procedural specificity to the rules. Burning Wheel's "Let It Ride" rule is one such example--in fact, Burning Wheel strikes me as entirely geared toward addressing issues of GM whimsy by adding formal structures of play--in that it says the GM can't require multiple rolls of a single type to get past a guard, climb a wall, or what have you. But that doesn't solve the problem entirely, since the question of when it is permissible to make that roll falls into the realm of the underdetermined. However, we might imagine that the circumstances in which the GM would legitimately wish to say a proposed PC action is impermissible occur when there is a disconnect between the fiction in the GM's head and the one in the player's.

      Other solutions therefore emerge not from the rules per se but from the GM's own artfulness. For example, the GM's own scrupulous adherence to the implicit procedures of play can help a lot. When Eero talks about his way of running D&D, where he mercilessly consults the fiction in order to determine the consequences of PC actions, that's scrupulousness to the nth degree. Another thing that helps is the GM's painstaking attention to the interaction at the table, so that she is able to identify when she needs to clarify the fictional situation because it has hitherto been unclear to one or more players. The example I cite in my Knutepunkt article, where the player playing Rex Rich in "Rex Rich and the Warlord of Mars," is unclear about the source of a claim the GM is making, requiring time be spent at the table to clarify what's happening in the fiction (and what's happening at the table) is an example of why that's necessary.

      So that's why I'm not terribly concerned about "hole-like rules" as a theoretical issue. Because we grapple with it all the time, in ways that are easier for me to understand and that lead me to what I think are better design solutions. Now, it's entirely possible, Callan, that you and I are in total agreement, but I honestly can't tell.

      \n To me, it seems fairly obvious that these "holes" Callan is talking about exist, are a real thing, etc. What's much more interesting to me, however, is discussing where to put them in design, and why.

      Here's an example we might be able to use for the sake of argument:

      You're playing D&D, or some similar game, and you're the GM. You've got to "stat up" a monster. What are some different ways to do this?

      In the "chess-like" design, your options are tightly constrained, and maybe you spend a budget of points to pick options off an existing list. It's easy to follow this procedure reliably (but whether it's fun or not, and whether its results work well or not, depends on the game's design, of course).

      However, in a "hole-like" design, the GM can design the monster however he or she wants. Still, there's a big range within this "hole" depending on how the rest of the game works. Some hypothetical options:

      1. You design the monster however you like, or not. Go!

      This is tough, for obvious reasons, but some people will love it for the creative freedom it affords and praise your game endlessly. (This is along the lines of "I love D&D because you can do ANYTHING you want!" A true selling point for some people.)

      2. You design the monster however you like, but the game tells you that the thing you should do is make a monster which won't kill any PCs, but is tough enough to remove about 50% of their hit points.

      Now it's still arbitrary, and the GM could very easily ignore these directives. But for the GM who buys into these directions, there is now a specific challenge, a specific goal to accomplish. The GM can aim for a certain level of success and measure her product relative to that benchmark. ("That monster took away 75% of their hit points. I'll have to make the next one weaker.")

      The success of the monster design is now a matter of player skill/game mastery, which some people will fail and some people will really enjoy.

      3. You design any monster you like, but the goal is to push on the characters' Issues/give them opportunities to hit their Keys/Beliefs. (Or maybe: design any monster you like, but your goal is to scare the *players* - look at their faces as you play and you'll see if you've succeeded!)

      Once again, here we have a clear goal, something which the GM can attempt and succeed or fail. There is a "hole", but the game gives some guidance on how best to take advantage of the hole. Depending on the design of the game and the quality of the game text, this might be really easy to do (e.g. Dogs Town Creation) or really hard to do (e.g. Call of Cthulhu for some people).

      4. Design any monster you like! Pick the options which you think are coolest/most scary/most true to your vision of the monster's lair.

      This could work or it could fail, clearly. Not a ton of guidance here, especially if the whole group isn't on the same page.

      5. Design any monster you like! Pick the options which you think are coolest/most scary/most true to your vision of the monster's lair. However, make your monsters more dangerous the deeper the adventurers go into the dungeon. Here's a list of what kinds of monsters might be appropriate for the first level of the dungeon. Here's a list for the second level... etc.

      This would work very well in conjunction with a game which clearly communicates and supports a playstyle where the players must choose which dangers to confront and which to avoid (and therefore variations in monster ability and threat levels are a feature in play), right? And there's quite a bit of guidance, even though there's no restriction or policing (and the GM could easily screw it up in a number of ways, either by underestimating a monster's capacities, or by failing to communicate the danger of a situation to the players, or whatever else).


      I tend to think that my favourite games either a) give clear guidance on how the various tools (and "holes") are supposed to work and then set you free (e.g. Solar System, morality in Dogs in the Vineyard), b) find the perfect tradeoff or balance between freedom and constraint (e.g. should Chess mandate that the players *must* use standard openings?), or c) constrain our options to a tremendous degree so as to make the game easy and reliable to play (e.g. Fiasco playsets).

      But I do think that game mastery/art is a valid part of play, and that many games will turn away enthusiasts if they make the game *too* reliable or too easy to play without skill.

      (I've been thinking about this a lot with regards to Diplomacy lately, since I've played several games with beginners, and ultimately find "beginner" play to be, very often, unsatisfying or downright unpleasant. I would love to plug some of those "holes" with either solid rules or solid advice/principles of play, but that's not an easy thing to do with such an elegant game.)

      As for the specific merits of conflict vs. task resolution, far be it from me to argue that one is inherently or obviously "better" than the other. Conflict resolution procedures have their own issues. Some players find them immersion-breaking, preferring to simply experience the action and its outcome from a purely in-character perspective. The coupling of conflict resolution with a stakes-setting phase can also be a problem for some, leading as it does potentially to "playing before we play." The fact that "you can resolve a whole battle with one die roll!" is disappointing to some, who find it anti-climactic to do so ("You mean that's it?"). And certainly the one thing that a task resolution system permits is secrecy and suspense, so that players face both the strategic challenge of trying to figure out how best to deploy or husband their resources and the dramatic thrill of fictional surprises, reversals, and narrow victories.

      I'm suspicious of how you've been talking about "holes" because I think it leads to bad design conclusions. "My game is more hole-like than chess-like," someone might say after reading this thread, "and that's how I like it. Players will just have to muddle through." As Paul has suggested above, this is more likely to lead to confusion, frustration, and bad play than to serendipitous and exultant discovery. I don't think you as a designer get to say, "Oh, yeah, people will just figure it out"; rather, you give them the procedures or techniques you think they need to get from beginning to end. Sometimes you'll leave something out, maybe because it seemed obvious to you and therefore didn't need to be said, maybe because you just didn't explain it right. Remember the foofaraw over "half-baked games" a few years ago? (But maybe that's not what you mean when you say "holes.") Other times you'll give players too much, and they'll reject your vision of how to play the game. I received some playtest feedback for a Gumshoe game a little while ago where the GM said, "Yeah, I tried handing out clues the way you wanted me to, but I found it slowed things down and made my players clam up, so I dropped it quick smart." But is that a "hole," as in a space for arbitrariness, as you seem possibly to have been suggesting? It doesn't seem so to me; it seems like an instruction that you can try to follow, albeit one that's sufficiently imprecise as to account for a wide range of in-play practices.

      That's not to say I don't think there are things that you can and should leave to the artfulness of the GM, or the players; there definitely are. But figuring out what "best practices" are, and working to develop one's skills and mindfulness as a player is a concomitant of that.

      Let me make sure I respond specifically to your question. I am not saying that conflict resolution is "better" than task resolution. I am saying that as an idea it seems to me to be a response to issues emerging from the underdetermination of rules for handling some kinds of conflicts in traditional games. There are other possible responses, among which are obeying the "implicit system" and letting the dice fall where they may (itself a kind of conflict resolution, to be sure). In that case, the GM is probably wise to strive for a situation in which either success or failure is interesting, which is exactly the sort of advice that many rulebooks offer in their discussions of how to run the game. This principle thus serves in effect as a rule--not a very precise one, of course, but hopefully good enough to go on.
