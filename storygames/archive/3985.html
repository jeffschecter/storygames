\n Reading the Passages thread I gave some serious thought as to what questions I need answered to decide whether I want to play/purchase a game. I posted these questions in that thread but then I thought I'd post them in their own since others might be interested.

1) When a conflict arises in the created fiction what is likely to be at stake?
2) What mechanical resources do the players have at hand to affect the outcome? How do those mechanical resources manifest in the fiction.
3) Once a conflict is resolved how is the outcome measured both mechanically and within the fiction?
4) How does the mechanical answer to 3 feed back into the mechanical answer for 2? How does the fiction impact of 3 feed back into the answer for 1?

Now I admit these questions I think slant slightly towards a bias I have in favor of conflict heavy, rapidly evolving, cyclical mechanical economy games. I do X which results in Y which directly impacts what the next X is.

So for designers and players do these questions interest/resonate with you? Any games for which these questions don't apply or better any games for which these questions don't apply and that does not reflect a flaw in the design?

    \n I agree with Ryan that conflict is becoming a loaded word. In fact, I mean it here as a loaded word. I'm fully willing to admit that I think my questions greatly reflect my personal preferences or biases. For example, I really dislike simple "Conch Shell Mechanics" where the mechanics decide NOTHING about the actual fiction, they only decide who among the players gets to speak or decide. So, if the mechanics do not resolve conflicts that arise within the fiction between the fictional characters something else about the game really has to grab me.

    To answer Alex with regard to question 3, I find question 3 is often absent and yet I consider it rather vital to my enjoyment. Hit points would be a weak example, yes. However, Fallout in Dogs is better. Fan Mail in PtA. Roll over bonuses in Sorcerer. The stat consequences in My Life with Master. I call these things Momentum Mechanics. Mechanics that measure the impact of the outcome of Conflict A on Conflict B. A lot of time I see games where conflicts are resolved almost identically mechanically every time. At the end of the resolution system the fiction has moved forward but the mechanics have not.

    As for setting, sure I care about setting. I consider Setting, Character and Situation to be elements of the created fiction I mention. But what I care about most (assuming the potential created fiction grabs my attention in the first place) is where the mechanics touch the fiction and how. How do I as a player move from fiction to mechanics back to fiction.

    \n Interesting set of questions. I can, with a little effort, answer them for two of my in-progress games. For my own benefit, and as a data-point, I'll answer them for the closest to playtesting one, ReCoil, now.
    \n
    \n1) When a conflict arises in the created fiction what is likely to be at stake?
    \n
    \nReality! Or more directly, a party which is intentionally or unintentionally impeding the PCs efforts to save Reality, and lives, on a local scale. Intentionally would most likely be the games bogeymen, the Naughtwraiths. Unintentionally, it could be anyone, from well-meaning cops, to mischief-minded gangsters. In the latter case, the question of whether their lives are worth the mission. Also, conflicts have the ability to damage reality, which would be against the PCs assumed goals.
    \n
    \n2) What mechanical resources do the players have at hand to affect the outcome? How do those mechanical resources manifest in the fiction.
    \n
    \nThe Players have their character's skills and supernatural abilities, most of which allow them to determine or alter facts within the fiction. These are largely literal in-game abilities. A player character can create something out of thin air, teleport, many different things. Non-literal abilities can effect undefined sections of the fiction. If it hasn't been determined that a given NPC works at a bar, an information check (or sometimes simply saying so) can allow the player to decide it is so.
    \n
    \n3) Once a conflict is resolved how is the outcome measured both mechanically and within the fiction?
    \n
    \nWell, for one any facts successfully declared (which is to say most; The declaring player's fact cannot be negated unless they knowingly declare a fact with less strength than an opposing character can match; Facts can be anything from "I knock the gun out of her hand" to "there's a door in the wall; there wasn't one a moment ago", depending on the circumstances) are in effect. I'm not sure if that counts as mechanically, or just within the fiction. Additionally, any 'wraiths destroyed lowers the total active "resistance" to the PCs efforts, and any people killed as a result of PC's conflicts will raise the total active resistance. If Reality is damaged, a Rift (a fictive and mechanical state) may occur, which intensifies the conflicts between the PCs and their enemies, and can create circumstances where more humans may die.
    \n
    \n4) How does the mechanical answer to 3 feed back into the mechanical answer for 2? How does the fiction impact of 3 feed back into the answer for 1?
    \n
    \nMechanically, winning or losing does not really affect the player's ability to affect a conflict or the fiction, unless of course the conflict is actually a PC's "death" conflict. In that case, it can allow it to continue, end it until a new character is made, or reduce it by reducing the character's abilities (the only concept of PC damage). Also, due to the dual nature of the PCs (spirits possessing hosts) any conflict that involves the death of the host will limit the player's contributions until the character can enter another host.
    \n
    \nFictionally, it is largely situational. A conflict that ends with a PC convincing a cop to join their side will affect future conflicts differently than a conflict ending in that same cop's death. Any conflict that ends by destruction of a Naughtwraith will not necessarily reduce or increase the chances that future conflicts will be with Naughtwraiths, until such time as all active resistance has been destroyed. Any conflicts that damage reality will increase the chances of a Rift, which will increase the likelihood of direct Naughtwraith conflicts, as well as life-saving conflicts.
    \n
    \n--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
    \n
    \nWow. Those were surprisingly complex to answer. I'm not even sure I've answered them as fully as I could. As for whether there are games where these questions could not be applied, I cannot think of any off the top of my head. Whether any such games would be necessarily flawed is anyone's guess. But I think the questions are definitely useful and interesting.

    \n These are actually great questions for me to look at right now because this is exactly the part of the Pentasystem that's giving me grief.

    1) When a conflict arises in the created fiction what is likely to be at stake?

    Two-pronged answer here. Going into the conflict, players declare what is "in question" - what attributes of the setting, other characters etc. are going to be changed if they succeed in the conflict - and what is "at risk", that is, which of their own attributes may be modified (positively or negatively) as a result of the conflict, depending on the die results. At the moment I'm not sure whether there are also simple narrative outcomes which can be in question, with no mechanical bits attached; I'm inclined to say that there are.

    So, for example:
    "What's in question is, does the king let us cross his land?" (narrative)
    "What's in question is the king's attribute Hostile to Strangers." (mechanical)
    "What's at risk is my Dignified attribute."

    2) What mechanical resources do the players have at hand to affect the outcome? How do those mechanical resources manifest in the fiction?

    The players control both characters and other aspects of the fiction. (There's no GM. I killed him, took his stuff, and gave it to the players.) But only characters can cause changes to come about, and only characters can enter into conflict, because a conflict is about a clash of character motivations. Character A wants something, character B wants something else; without this precondition there is no conflict.

    So the players have, as mechanical resources, several features of their characters. The character's rating in the attribute they're using in the conflict determines how many dice are rolled. The character's rating in the arena of conflict (mental, physical, etc.) determines what numbers on the dice are successes. There are additional mechanics for rerolls and Special Effects, which involve spending currency to have both narrative and mechanical effects beyond the core mechanic.

    Rerolls, for example, involve spending either a character currency point and narrating a special use of an advantageous attribute, or a player currency point and narrating a bit of good fortune. This bit is still in flux.

    3) Once a conflict is resolved how is the outcome measured both mechanically and within the fiction?

    The attributes put at risk get consequences (good and bad) applied to them, which depending on how much the conflict escalated may lead to future rerolls (of failures and successes, respectively) or new attributes.

    The attributes put in question can be increased or decreased in power, created or destroyed. This can affect the setting as well as the characters.

    In parallel with these mechanical effects go fictional reflections of them, described by the losing player.

    4) How does the mechanical answer to 3 feed back into the mechanical answer for 2? How does the fiction impact of 3 feed back into the answer for 1?

    Great question. The consequences of the conflict resolution feed right back into the resources available next time, as I've described. (Some of the lower-level consequences, from when you haven't escalated too much, disappear after a while, but the higher-level ones are permanent, at least until you use basically the same mechanics to change them.)

    The second part of this question I haven't given as much thought as I should. What's at stake next time because of the outcome last time? What conflicts will now arise because of the way this conflict came out?

    Let me get back to you on that one.

    Totally cool Ryan,

    unWritten is based on the Otherkind mechanic by Vincent Baker (I'm sure you've heard of him;) The coolest thing about that mechanic is that conflicts have attributes not characters. These attributes, in unWritten, are Success, Consequence, Action, and Narration.

    So, to answer your question the result of conflict resolution is whether you succeed or fail, to determine whether your actions have consequences or not, how you act in resolving the conflict, and who narrates, which ultimately determines how the conflict goes down.

    So, to restate what I just said in plainer english is...
    The stakes of a conflict are whether you Succeed or not, if there are any Consequence, the level of control you have over you Actions , and who has Narration.

    These are stakes because you might be in a position to have to choose between succeeding or narrating, or having consequences or maintaining control over your actions (whether you succeeded in the conflict or not.)

    Does this make sense?

    Wow. This is all really interesting. Let me finish clarifying question #3 for Alex. Josh has it right a few posts back. I'm not saying that after any given conflict the mechanics somehow radically change. All I'm saying is that there is some mechanical impact after a conflict is over such that over time we can mechanically see the impact of multiple executions of the resolution system. Fallout in Dogs impacts the characters traits that reflect the conflict that has just been resolved therefore entering the NEXT conflict they have something different about them mechanically than they did in the last conflict. Overtime that accumulates and the impact is significant.

    Even simpler then Fallout in Dogs is Budget/Fan Mail in PtA. When you spend Budget/Fan Mail in PtA your ability to impact the next conflict is lessened. So it's rather significant when you spend A LOT of Budget/Fan Mail on a conflict because you're saying "this, right here is so important that I'm willing to risk being totally hosed in whatever the next conflict is."

    I'm disappointed by games like, "One Can Have Her." Where the endgame (appears) to be totally independent of the mechanical momentum of the game. We play this game over here and then oh at the end everyone just votes.... what?

    Okay, now on to the meatier question of #1

    Now I wasn't thinking of question #1 as mechanical. Rather I was thinking about it contextualizing the mechanical nature of the follow up questions. Although, Ryan's phrasing of: "I think something closer to 'tell me what situations provoke conflicts.'" is very appealing to me.

    As an example let me try answering the question for my own game Silent Sound.

    1) When a conflict arises in the created fiction what is likely to be at stake?

    In Silent Sound each character has committed a transgression sometime in his past which has gone "unpunished." The game is structured as a supernatural ordeal in which the characters are confronted with situations that reflect their past transgression. Will the outcome be the same? Does this absolve or condemn their past actions?

    Now Silent Sound definitely falls in the My Life with Master/Dogs in the Vineyard camp as it has a tight, tight explicit focus. But I think even a more generic game like Primetime Adventures can answer the question. It would be something like, "Each character has an issue. Conflicts arise from the intersection of the characters issues and the situational setup of the rest of the TV show's basic premise."

    Does this help?

    Let me get a little bit example-y here.

    'Til Death Us Do Part
    A Game of Fear and Love in the Land of the Dead

    1) When a conflict arises in the created fiction what is likely to be at stake?
    Usually success at an action - i.e. the resolution system is largely task-driven. However, as in BW/BE, failure is not a block but a branch - and as in HeroQuest, Sorcerer, Donjon etc, success or failure at an action means follow-up actions will receive modifiers.
    In the case of a Struggle (i.e. combat, be it physical, mental, social) the stakes are "who gets to decide what happens to your character - you or your opponent".
    Additionally, players can put resources at stake in order to win conflicts, so the character may be betting with hir equipment, loved ones, convictions or mental stability.

    2) What mechanical resources do the players have at hand to affect the outcome? How do those mechanical resources manifest in the fiction.
    Everything on the character sheet is a resource that can either be used with no chance of ill effects (skills), activated by paying points (traits - actually, receiving negative points, but that's a technicality), or gambled with to increase chance of success (prized equipment, loved ones, etc). Characters can also gain Convictions which will give them bonuses while they hold true; it is the GM's job to try to prove them false. Characters also gain potentially massive bonuses by performing actions in stressful or frightening situations, but this can backfire in the form of mental shutdowns and permanent instabilities.

    3) Once a conflict is resolved how is the outcome measured both mechanically and within the fiction?
    A character's sheet will have been altered, often drastically - their Panic points may have increased or decreased, they may have gained Neuroses, they may have gained or lost loved things, their skills may have temporarily or permanently increased or decreased, they may have gained or lost useful equipment, they may have gained or lost Convictions.

    4) How does the mechanical answer to 3 feed back into the mechanical answer for 2? How does the fiction impact of 3 feed back into the answer for 1?
    With all the changes to a character's sheet, their ability to participate in future conflicts will have changed, often drastically, sometimes temporarily and sometimes permanently. All changes on the character's sheet reflect directly on the fiction - for example, a Negatrait -2 "Shot in the leg" will mean just that, a Neurosis "Scared of the dark" will mean just that, a lost Prize "Lost: Margie (Sister)" will mean just that.

    Posted By: noclueWhat will I be doing with my time? Why will it matter to me?
    \nFirst off, in my first response I absolutely diverted from answering the actual question after the first sentence. The mechanics, to me, has nothing to do with the question, that was just me rambling off some stuff I'm excited about / defending the fact that the resolution system is task-based rather than a more in vogue conflict resolution system.

    Secondly, to answer your direct questions: You will be spending your time trying to survive. Your character may have driving goals akin to BW's "Beliefs" but these are implicit and require reading between the character sheet lines, usually in the Prizes or Convictions sections. In general, the game simply tries to do good zombie fiction - i.e. the characters are usually reactive, not proactive, and their reactions are usually based around survival of some form. Survival could mean trying to find food, killing zombies, gaining power over others, organizing resistance groups, trying to find a "cure", etc.

    Why will it matter to you - do you mean you, the player, or you, the character? If you, the character, survival will be paramount but so will the protection of your Prizes, which you nonetheless have to gamble with to survive. Losing Prizes will make it harder to survive in the long run. I'm trying to build mechanics that consciously rewards those who maintain their sensibilities and do not revert into "combat monster mode" - the game supports uncaring, hardass combat monsters but they tend to meet unfortunate dooms rather quickly. So to your character, your Prizes and sanity will matter, as will your own survival.

    As a player, which is what I suspect you're asking, I think it will matter because the players of this game enjoy zombie fiction on a different level than "gore porn". They want to see everyday people (Shaun of the Dead comes to mind) step up to a challenge in the face of true horror, or see a successful alpha male who betrays his loved ones die a horrible death. They want to see people they sympathize with having to make choices between saving their spouse or saving themselves. They want to see heroic sacrifices and horrific betrayals.

    How question #1 conveys that, I don't really know.
