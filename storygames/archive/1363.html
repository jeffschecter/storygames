Good Lessons from Mainstream Games

So, what are the best lessons you learned from Mainstream Games? This is spinning off from Bad Lessons from Mainstream Games, obviously. It's not to disagree with that thread. I agree that there is tons of bad advice out there, both in mainstream games (i.e. Torg, Deadlands), magazines and advice articles, as well as in small-press and indie games (i.e. Senzar, Theatrix, etc.). However, I usually prefer to talk about good advice rather than bad advice.

Top lessons from Champions: The players get to decide the enemies and loved ones who will most often show up. Listening to your players. Making adventures which were about opponents with color and personality. Having PCs that are personally powerful and skilled, rather than just being pawns to circumstance.

From James Bond 007: Jumping into the action straight out. Making the PCs cool, where the question isn't whether they will win, but how and how well they do so. Having solid action and mechanics which isn't combat.

Top lesson from CORPS: Everyone has 110% chance of success, at their level of competence. Rather than always turning to the dice, most tasks should just succeed.

    From White Wolf: The Overwhelming Importance of Color. If you look at early gen Vampire and Werewolf the thing that makes them go isn't really the system, which is bland, nor the setting, which is bland with sparkles tossed in. It is the color, and the ability of the game to get players and GMs to generate color in spades. Changeling is the game that both lived and died in this, as the four games in one that is Changeling led to massive amounts of color being generated by players -- but everyone at the table was generating color for a different game.

    From Pendragon: Personality traits and mechanics. This one was a brutal pill to swallow at first ("what, I don't control my PC completly?") -- but once taken it lead to some great Sim RP. Ever since then when given a new game my first thing is to look for the part where who the character is matters (as opposed to just what they can do).

    From Exalted: Stunts. Need I say more?

    From Tribe 8: The importance of The Hook. If you have a vast and metaphysically diverse setting containing lots of ambiguity and player-choice the thing you have to do to get players into it is have something simple, powerful, and right at the front of the book to cock-punch them and drag them kicking and screaming in.

    From Cyberpunk: Lifepaths as a method of building plot, world, and character at the same time. I mean, yea, Burning Wheel is completly awesome in this respect -- but the core of the idea was planted in my brain by Cyberpunk 1st.

    From Call of Cthulhu: Sometimes you have to let the PCs die.

    7th Sea by John Wick has Backgrounds, which are really a form of Flags:

    Players can spend up to 6 of their (typically 100) starting character points on Backgrounds, which are basically "personalized subplots" -- like a vendetta, a map to unknown treasure, a lost parent, a terrible fate or what-have-you. The GM is encouraged by the rules-text to interweave these plot elements into the overall story and to reward players with a number of XP equal to a Background's Rating (from 1 to 3) whenever it plays an important role in the session.

    This isn't quite as focussed as, say, TSOY's Keys or TROS's Spiritual Attributes, but it's definitely an earlier stab in that direction. I know I had a lot of fun weaving Backgrounds together when I ran 7th Sea. I kind of do this anyway when I GM, but it's nice to have some mechanical weight to it.

  • Warthur
    September 2006
    Pendragon: It is possible to design a game where Gamist min-maxing and Simulationist immersionism can sit together happily. Min-max for maximal Glory point accumulation in Pendragon and you will end up... behaving much like a Knight of the Round Table would be expected to. Immerse yourself in the setting and your character, and act chivalrously like a good knight should, and chances are you'll get a stack of Glory points. (Get a bunch of people doing this, and chances are the narratives you come up with will be strikingly similar to Arthurian myth, so it even accomodates Narrativist story-weaving.)

    Paranoia: As long as there is exciting interactions going on between the player characters, the rest of the gameworld can go hang.

    Palladium (edit: Heroes Unlimited specifically): HP/health/etc loss doesn't have to represent exact physical wounding (SDC, which was explained as John Wayne getting beaten, shot, stabbed, and beaten again, then shrugging, "It's just a scratch, Ma'am.")

    Champions: It's cool to be able to make a character be exactly how YOU want him to be, right down to the frills and trim.

    Over the Edge (yeah, debatable; blow me.): The statistics/traits in games don't have to represent what you think they have to represent. (Also, the "gain half HP back after the fight" rule fed into the Palladium SDC princicle above.)

    D&D 3E: Instead of trying to please all people and all types of play, maybe you should just streamline your game engine toward ONE type in a concentrated fashion.
