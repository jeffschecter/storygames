What character types do you play?

eruditus
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
One of the primary reasons I roleplay is to learn about myself and the people I play with. I like to explore what decisions we make, push buttons (obviously with limits, usually as defined by the social contract), and expose ourselves to issues and challenges.

In a recent blog entry I talk about character types and what they say about me. It was quite inconclusive however it was a very armchair discussion without a whole lot of thought to what the character types mean or how they are defined. Certainly all of our characters are just copies of ourselves to one degree or another. Some gamers take more of themselves than others. Some of us are method actors when we game

So I open up to you bright folk

What types of characters do we play? What sorts of categories might we use to conveniently file them. These categories should travel across systems and convey a bit of information while remaining broad enough to be useful. Now, of course every measurement of this type is most useful if it's geared toward "what are you looking for?" I realize this but I am trying to find a more general measurement and see what happens.

Disclaimer: I realize that various fields and internet quizzes might accomplish all that I need. "Yeah, Don, all you need to do is run each of your PCs through the Meyers-Briggs exam or do the 'what Order-of-the-Stick character are you' quiz." Obviously I want to discuss this and come to some sort of concensus of "sure, this works for me." Not that some of those tools won't assist us in doing it however I do prefer to explore our own way

So I think that the first step in this process is figuring out what the basis to our character types will be. I think I have been going toward character directives - how a PC approaches situations and how the character is designed to best approach those situations. Maybe we could use two or three ideas so that each character gets three words that make up their category - one for character directives, one for familiarity (as an example) and one for deviance (how far from reality is their world?). So any suggestions where to start?

    Warriors and philosophers.

    Often, I play the philosophical warriors. Some times, I'll play a savage or ruthless warrior and study his personality from another angle. Other times, I'll play a diplomatic type, who tries to talk things out and avoid combat as much as possible. Unfortunately, most games being what they are, the latter concept is less supported.

    For me, the most interesting themes to explore are trust, love, and violence (or a lack of any of the three). My characters reflect this regardless of concept. I play passionate (inside, if not without), often driven characters who tend to focus either on themselves (they protect those they love because it's in their best interest) or their ideals (they become distanced paragons of their lofty goals in order to protect what they value).

    I've had an acute interest in the martial arts, mythology and philosophy all my life, so playing this way comes naturally to me. In gaming, I love having the chance to play a character who makes difficult (and potentially wrong or terrible) descisions in order to make his way down the path he has chosen for himself. Part of that, of course, is inevitably suffering the ultimate consequences of those descisions (or, on occasion, reaping the rewards; though the warrior tends to live and die by the sword).

    It ends up looking something like a chanbara or western flick, nine times out of ten. ;)

    I tend towards two different character types. First, since I GM so much for the various groups that I'm involved in, when I get on the other side of the screen I like to play the hero. Driven and dedicated to a cause, confined in action by a strong sense of personal morality, all of that good stuff. In the last two games that I was a player in, the three characters I played were a road warden, a sheriff, and a town's guardsman.

    On the other hand, I also like to play characters that are deeply and terribly broken. People who question their role in life, people who hate themselves or what they've done. The town guardsman character from above was bitten on the right arm by a zombie pre-game and that part of her body had turned terrible and zombified, the rest being spared only because of a makeshift tourniquet that she had quickly tied her arm off with. She hated herself and her lot in life and, as things got worse for her and she felt herself get further divided from her humanity she became cold and murderous. She crushed a man's skull with her zombie arm, she lit creatures on fire. It all came to a head when she purposefully hunted down a man through the woods and remorselessly murdered him.

    In truth, a lot of the stuff with that character came out because of some truly terrible stuff that I was going through, and still am, in my personal life. Even still, the part that I have the most fun with as a player is getting messed with. I love making characters with built in trouble. I'm playing in a fantasy campaign right now where my road warden character is a drunkard and gambler and owes tons of money to the local mob boss. I got absolutely no system-based reward for setting these things up for my character. What I do get is some personal trouble, tailor-made for me, that I think is fun that also adds something to the GMs toolbox to help spice things up whenever he wants.
