Mark and I got to play Mechaton tonight and we had a great time. I managed to defend my moon base's water purification unit from Mark's invaders, but I may not have been so lucky if the invaders hadn't been hazing their newest recruit.

\n

The one thing in the rules that got under my skin was that green d8. Getting a bonus die for loosing your weapons just bugs me. So, I have every intention of hacking the thing.

\n

However, before I get with the hacking, I'm hoping that maybe the Mechaton veterans around here can give me a little insight into the effects of the rule. Why is it there? What greater effect does it have? Does it patch some huge un-fun thing? I was hoping to find a thread on Anyway where Vincent might have discussed the addition of the rule, but no such luck.

\n

Also, here's the replacement rule I think I'm going to suggest to Mark for our next game:
\nCharge! - When a mech make an attack with a HTH weapon, an unarmed attack (HTH with no weapon), or no attack at all, that mech may bump it's movement die up by 1.

\n

I'm not sure if it would fill the same void as the green d8 rule, but, at first glance, it looks like it'd be a fun rule. And we'd avoid giving beat-up mechs the ability to cross terrain that they couldn't cross when they were shiny and new.

\n

Any thoughts?

    \n In my experience I found it to be a very effective rule.
    \n
    \nIt ensures that even damaged mechs remain a threat. It encourages mechs to have HTH weapons so after they've been damaged they can take full advantage of the bonus green d8.
    \n
    \nIt ensures that you have to defend in depth even when you're winning because the mech you beat the crap out of is still fast enough to run around behind you and take the targets.
    \n
    \nIt ensures that the attrition based damage mechanics actually work. In D&D; the attrition based Hitpoints works because there is no death spiral because the damage doesn't actually hinder you. In Mechaton the damage does hinder you, but the bonus you get helps offset that...not by canceling out the hindrance or allowing you to ignore it...but by transforming you into something different.
    \n
    \nIts a very effective rule.

    Sure.

    \n

    Say I'm playing normal, by-the-book Mechaton. I send my guys to different places on the board, knowing that when I send them there, I'm deciding not to send them somewhere else. Deciding when and where to send guys is the better part of Mechaton strategy, and it's a lot of fun, if you consider stomach aches fun. If I make it so my guys can reach twice as far in a turn, that effect is reduced, making it much less important where I put my guys.

    \n

    However! If I'm rolling and adding, that changes the statistics. Now I have a whole bell curve going up my curve of choice dice. So I'm more reliably going to go an average distance. Making it so that you can more reliably move a certain distance is potentially interesting, as it reduces the effects of chance, and you might want that.

    \n

    So! Here's what I propose: use your rule if you want more reliable movement. But, at the same time, use small movement units. You'll never move less than 2, you'll usually move around 8 or 9, I'd guess (since the average is skewed by getting to choose dice), and make Direct fire range 12. That way, someone can still run up inside Direct range and do HtH damage and, if they're charging, their chances are even better. You might want to make HtH range 2 because your models are small and the units will be, too. Just for handling purposes.

    \n

    Dig?
