\n I know there's a similar topic already up on this topic, but I thought this was dissimilar enough to warrant a new thread.

Looking at the role-playing mechanics in 5th Edition D&D I'm sure anyone familiar with Fate had a 'huh' moment where they saw the vague similarities. Personality traits, Ideals, Bonds, Flaws, and Backgrounds. Almost like categories for potential Aspects in Fate. The analogy falls flat of course with Inspiration when compared to Fate points.

My D&D group likes bits and pieces of Fate style games, but they're never going to change from a D&D-style game to a proper story game. So, house rules. My initial thoughts are to just drop Inspiration in favor of Fate points straight across, but instead of a meager +2 on a d20 roll, replace that with the regular 5E 'grants advantage' effect of Inspiration, though I doubt we will let players give each other Fate points.

The hope is that the inclusion of the more wide-open Fate points coupled with the new Aspect-like personality traits subsystem will mechanically reward role-playing whilst not punishing a player for not engaging in Aspect / personality trait prompted role-playing (i.e. no compels to buy off and the like).

So really, I'm curious if anyone has house ruled a more Fate point style subsystem to replace Inspiration?

    \n Well, as I said above, replace Inspiration with straight Fate points but instead of just a +2 keep the 'grants advantage' effect.

    You'd earn Fate points for playing up your personality traits, ideals, bonds, and flaws (self compels basically) along with the typical 'good role-playing', 'brilliant plan', and 'making the table laugh' occasional points.

    You'd spend Fate points to gain advantage on a roll, re-roll something, and declaring a story element.

    So expanding Inspiration into a more Fate point-like space, really.

    If you think of a character's race/class combo as their high concept, then the rest (personality traits, ideals, bonds, backgrounds) are easily mapped as your other aspects with your flaw as trouble in Fate parlance. There's really no need for compels as such, much less buying off compels. If the player wants to engage, they will and therefore also earn a Fate point, but that's likely a part of our table culture rather than widely applicable. We want to reward engaging in role-playing, but not punish players for choosing not to.
