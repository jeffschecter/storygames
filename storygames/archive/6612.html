\n In the game design challenge thread, I've been given the challenge by Kynn of creating a rpg that relates to racing cars. This concept takes me back to an old problem I never solved: how do you simulate the learning curve found in stories like Initial D?
\n
\nFor those who aren't familiar with it, Initial D features a young driver who is a very talented streetracer with a lot to learn. Each race presents him with a new puzzle, and he cannot win (or progress) until he solves the puzzle. What makes this different from regular rpgs is that there is nothing (or almost nothing) random about his challenges. Neither are they resource-based or a skill number. They are mysteries to be solved.
\n
\nThe combination of cars (plus tracks sometimes) being raced present the components of the puzzle. Things can get very detailed in terms of their characteristics, but other features are simply overlooked or compensated for by driver skill. For example, the top speed of one car may be much greater, but it doesn't matter if the driver isn't fearless enough to go that fast before the next turn. While these drastic differences may be overlooked, seemingly minor differences can determine the winner. This puts a very different spin on how rpgs normally handle vehicle performance.
\n
\nThe car designs of the 30s had enough space in the front for some pretty serious engines if I'm willing to downplay the dominance of internal combustion and allow steam and electric. That should give lots of variation between cars. I seem to be looking at big lists of characteristics, for the drivers' skills, for the cars, and for the tracks themselves, so I need to start gathering that info.
\n
\nOnto the question: can anyone point to, or suggest a book containing, information on steam engine performance and weight (piston or turbine)? Early electric car engines? Early internal combustion?

    \nI was trying to explain why some details may be under-emphasized, while others are over-emphasized.
    \n
    \nThink of it this way: if one car has a maximum speed of 155 mph and the other has a maximum speed of 185 mph, the game wouldn't measure the difference in distances covered by the two cars as they race down a stretch, but it would need to know that one has a higher top speed than the other. That's a clue to solving the puzzle to win the race, but it's probably not enough on its own.
    \n
    \nI'm not sure how well this aspect of the story came through in the live-action version of Initial D. I know the anime versions spent a tremendous amount of time on it, analyzing very particular details of each car (e.g. the differences in acceleration patterns between twin-turbo, single turbo, high-rev and supercharged cars) to find a weakness that could be exploited. It's a pretty common formula in sports stories (e.g. Hajime No Ippo, Eyeshield 21, Aoki Densetsu Shoot, etc.).
    \n
    \nSo, to build these puzzles, I need the details that will become the clues, and that means gathering performance characteristics (I could just make things up, but I like to mix a little truth in with my lies). Anything that could become a clue for a race puzzle could be useful.
    \n
    \nNow that I think of it, this story pattern isn't too different from a game I was raving about in Game Chef, called "Be Real," where players were given clues one at a time. Players form a hypothesis of how to win, try it, and if it fails, they get another clue with which to alter their hypothesis. In this case, I think there will be just one player, plus the gamemaster.
