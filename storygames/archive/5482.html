\n One thing that's bothered me, recently*, is GMs who start a new game with an opening monologue.\n
This is a game based on the works of H. P. Lovecraft. It plays in two styles: Purist and Pulp. Purist is pure Lovecraft: as the game goes on, you realise humanity's impotence in the face of unfathomable ancient monsters. Pulp is more about shooting eldritch things. We'll be playing Pulp today. Now, this is based on the Gumshoe system. The big thing about the Gumshoe system...
\nAnd so on, for about ten minutes. Now, of course you need some introduction, but beyond a minute or so? It's just too much. Much of the information won't affect the game; and much could be explained during the game.

Often, they'll explain the conflict system in advance of actually using it.\n
So, this is how Combat works. First, you roll to hit. Usually it's 3 or over on a d6; but if the Athletics rating of the person you're trying to hit is 8 or more, it's 4 or over. You can spend points from your Scuffling pool to add to this roll.
\nI've listened to many long spiels like this recently. I never take them in. Especially, I never take in the explanation of the combat system. I've learnt to stare malevolently at the table until the GM stops speaking.

And although I said "ten minutes", above, what actually happens is this goes on for about an hour. There's an explanation of what the game's about. Then there's an explanation of all the different character types. Then something else. And so on. It's spiel after spiel.

Perhaps I should be more proactive, though. Perhaps I should say "I'm just going to get a cup of tea. Give me a call when you've finished talking". Or just bang my head on the table until it all stops.

Or is this me? Am I being unreasonable? Is there some deep underlying function to all this I'm missing?

Graham

* If I have played with you recently, I'm not specifically talking about the game we played. I'm actually thinking about a few different games I've played over the course of the last year.

    \n This is a problematic phenomenon.

    Partial solutions:

    * We need to stop viewing "gatekeeper to the rules-text" as part and parcel of being "the GM". Ideally everyone should own the rules and know them before play begins, but in lieu of that ideal one could certainly make some effort to pass the book around in advance of the first session, explain the rules in text form somewhere in advance of the session, etcetera.

    * One of the reasons an up-front explanation may be needed as opposed to "learn as you go" is to do with character creation. If you don't understand what the various widgets on your character sheet mean until you've already been playing for several hours, this leads to serious problems if your character is then not want you wanted him or her to be. This is mostly a problem in long-term games with strong character continuity, but can cause problems in short form games as well -if the entire game lasts four hours, it's not great to only figure out three hours in that your character was crippled form the start for what you wanted him or her to achieve.

    * Related to the above point: the first time you play something, or in a context where you can't guarantee other players knowing the rules in advance, have pre-generated characters made. Then you can explain game procedures as they come up, with players having reassurance that the character they have is guaranteed to be effective in the scenario.

    * Set your game up deliberately to have a "learn the rules" phase. The only game I can think of that has anything like this is Dogs in the Vineyard, where the initiation conflict (which also serves other purposes) is useful as a relatively low-stakes introduction to the conflict system. If you screw up that conflict due to not understanding the rules, it's not generally a big deal - your Dog's life can't be on the line, and you get a trait out of it either way. this works especially well if you do the initiation conflict starting with the most experienced player first - less experienced players can watch and get the hang real quick.

    \n What works best for me is getting to read the rules before we play, because I learn best from reading. But if that's not possible, then there's a two-stage process that can work fairly well:

    Step one: On one night, we get together and talk about the game -- the premise, the system, character ideas, "What do we do in this game?", all that stuff. Rules and gameplay are sometimes demonstrated like examples in a rulebook -- as isolated vignettes whose only purpose is to answer the question "how do I do combat?" or "how do I win a conflict?" If we're feeling really ambitious, maybe it'll be like the tutorial level of a videogame -- a brief, highly simplified collection of scenes that serves to familiarize us with how the game works.

    Step two: On another night (never, ever the same night), we get together and actually get the real game started. Usually by this point someone's put together a cheat sheet of important rules so that everyone can have some reference material on hand during play, and those of us who like to read (like me) have had a chance to at least flip through the manual between step one and step two. We may be shaky on a few details, but we've got the gist of it well enough that we can get some momentum going during play.


    The downside to this system is, of course, that it's nearly impossible to do a one-shot of a system that we haven't played before. Which is fine: in the past, when we've tried doing a "cold" one-shot of a new game, that game has generally been much less fun than it could have been...and that's not only a waste of time we could've used to do something more fun, it's also not fair to the game.

    \n Background presentation and introduction is the subject of a thread I've often promised to do and have never done, because I am a fundamentally untrustworthy soul, but one thing that has helped me over the "big background dump, in order to get a couple of things to stick" problem was to get in my own mind a clear and finite list of what I, as GM and organizer of campaign background expected the players to know and absorb, and reducing that to writing, and explaining my expectations.

    If I'm running a fantasy game and the name of the coin of the realm is a thaller, and I want the characters to refer to thallers, I need to put that on a piece of paper and tell them "learn that and use it". How else are the players going to know what they are supposed to listen to?

    Even relatively complicated historical backgrounds (Poland in the late 18th century, plus vampires) have been able to be reduced to a one-page typewritten set of background facts that I expect them to know, and then they can ask questions or I can explain elements as they're introduced or invented.

    It's worked a LOT better so far.

    \n Graham, I am with you 100%. I hate the long preamble. It's been happening to me a lot more with boardgames, lately. Explaining the entire game before we start (or worse, reading the entire rulebook outloud) is so pointless. The first time playing is a learning game, for me. I expect to get a general overview and then learn each procedure as we come to it, and absorb the whole thing after a complete game. I don't feel the need to be awesome and beat everyone the first time out.

    But I have noticed that some people really, really need that. They get all uncomfortable and squirrelly if they feel like they don't have mastery over every last permutation of the game before it starts. As if they're afraid of "getting screwed" by some unknown factor and looking stupid. So this need for the long preamble can be instigated by players, too. The GM gives a brief overview, then the players start in with a thousand ever more detailed questions, trying to learn everything before the game starts so they can do everything perfectly the first time.

    My magic words to alleviate some of this fear:
    "So, now that you know about attacks of opportunity, you can totally move back and do that over."
    "You should have gotten the bonus for the last roll, too. So we'll say it was a success."
    "Actually, you only get one of those re-rolls. Do you want to go back to your original roll and keep it for now?"

    Free and easy, no pressure, everything is re-doable while we're learning. That's my preferred mode, since it fosters some trust among players, and also lets people learn the mechanics (or setting or whatever) in context.

    \n John,

    Actually, I think that fear and the four step cycle Graham laid out above work hand in hand.

    I mean, look at the 90s school of GMing and think of how scary it is. You've got all your friends sitting there, and you are responsible for their fun, you are responsible for their understanding, you are responsible for the system, you are responsible for them being able to work together. You probably have spent a long time thinking about the game, learning the rules, and have a lot of that bottled up inside and no clear idea about how to organize it for effect presentation because that's not a skill you've ever been schooled in.

    So the nerves set in, and you start talking. The fear sets in, and you keep talking.

    ....

    As for the folks who need a rule vs. folks who need all the rules thing, I have found that there are people I cannot play with unless they've read the rulebook. Which can suck, sometimes, as they might not have time or inclination to read it -- but if I have to teach them, I will go nuts and the other players (who need just one rule to get going) will go even more nuts. They have to know the rules, all of them, and in a fairly set order. So I have to make them read the damn book.

    For the folks who are somewhere inbetween, I still stumble sometimes. I often give Mo, when teaching her a new game, too much or too little info. She'll sometimes get this glazed look in her eyes and then will say to me something right out of G's first post. Other times, thinking on that lesson, I'll give her the "get going" info and not think "oh yea, you do really need to know this one thing two or three steps ahead of time" and then when we get to the moment she suddenly realizes I've not told her something critical.

    I'm still working on it. Right now my best friend for it is games like Days of Wonder (well, most of their games) that have quick play cards, introduction rules booklets, and then more detailed rules books. The different levels of information not only helps me learn the game, it helps me teach the game to people who have different levels of need for understanding.

    ----

    On a player level, I think the reason for the fear you see has to do with whammy GMs, a history of bad play, and other things. I know a couple of guys who get really, really defensive if they do something "stupid" in a game because of past family dynamics in which being "stupid" in a game resulted in hours and hours of mockery, ridicule, and sometimes the sort of light physical abuse brothers love to heap upon each other. ("I can't believe you fucked us by attacking China! Never get in a land war in Asia, bitch! I don't care if this is the first time you've played Risk you whining baby, I'm gonna wedgie you until you can taste your own anus!")

    Combine that with the fact that if you've got a step on up to social fulfillment, then stepping stupid can very easily be seen as a route to being socially shunned or mocked, and you've got a recipe for disaster. For fixing that, I've found no better solution than ideas like yours about do overs and such. If the first game isn't gonna be so hardcore (no matter what the later games might be like) and really is something of a do-over for learning, it helps vastly.

    \n(Hello! This is my first post here\u2026 I hope I won\u2019t do anything inappropriate but if I do please tell me. Thanks!)

    Some sources for this problem, which overlap what others already wrote:

    Issues of trust: the GM fears potential future troubles if everything is not perfectly clear at the start. In some gaming groups it might be justified, or maybe the GM has had experience with such a group.

    Expectations: some players actually like or demand such data. The GM might be one of them, and carelessly assumes everybody shares his taste, or he knows (or believes) such players are in the group. Some games demand long and detailed explanations either setting-wise or system-wise.

    Writer\u2019s block, stage fright: maybe the GM somehow fears to begin the game and unconsciously postpones.

    Unaware: sometimes you just get stuck in \u201cboring mode\u201d and aren\u2019t enough aware of it to shake yourself out of it. I know it sometimes happen to me when I am tired or distracted.

    Likes to explain: some people just love to explain things.

    Shows off: isn\u2019t my setting/rule system cool?!


    If you\u2019re the culprit GM, sometimes just becoming conscious of the problem is the solution.

    The first thing I do is to determine what is the minimal information needed before play. Everything that can be explained during the gameplay should be.

    I find it helps to create a first scenario specially to introduce world and rules elements progressively. (In medieval settings, a tournament is a great setting for this kind of scenario.) You can use pre-generated characters to speed this up and start to play before character generation; therefore the players will be more knowledgeable for character generation. Or you can use it to test-drive the characters and adjust them afterwards.

    If the game demands to explain much material, I prefer to have one or more explanations sessions, usually coupled with character generation. And/or I give the players advance documentation. Some players will prefer the explanation session, usually those with much free time, others will prefer written documents. Some don\u2019t like either and that probably means that the game is inadequate.

    If I believe some players demand much information because of trust issues, I try to reassure them: we\u2019re all here to have fun, everybody will be treated fairly. If they suffer bad consequences from misinformation, we\u2019ll retcon or they will be compensated. I quickly switch from tell (reassure, explain) to show (play!).

    If some players demand much information because it is their taste, they can read the fine manual, cant\u2019 they? Or, when possible, we can schedule a special session with them.


    If you\u2019re not the GM, first check if the others are bored too. If enough are bored, slip into your guerrilla facilitator outfit and try to re-focus the session.



    Thanks for bearing with me.
