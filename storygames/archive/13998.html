\n So I just want an excuse to use Scoutbooks.

Here's an idea. A bunch of people (five? ten?) get together and make an anthology of good, tested pick-up-and-play games that fit on just a few small pages. Games that can be played with no prep, and take anywhere from 15 minutes to a few hours to play.

We print maybe 250 and sell the books at a reasonable price. Everyone invests a share. We edit each others' games. Someone does the layout, someone does the taking-care-of-money part. Maybe that counts as part of their investment, I don't know.

So what do you think?

    \n Cool, here's my suggestion on organization / editing. What if we get groups of folks (in 3s) to be responsible for the content of each booklet? That way we don't have to have one mega-editor for the whole project, and each group of 3 people can collaborate to make their booklet happen, helping each other with editing, proofreading, and maybe even playtesting.

    On top of that, I think it would be good for one person to take the lead on each booklet, the head of the triumvirate of creators. So, for example, I could decide I want to put together a booklet with one of my games and 2 Murderland finalists, and I'd contact those creators, get them on board, and then be responsible for wrangling the text together to prep it for layout, though I would collaborate with the other folks involved to do so. And maybe Em could organize a booklet of 3 solo games, Matthijs could organize a booklet of Archipelago hacks or whatever, etc. I'm not saying those are the booklet themes (we might want to mix things up more), but having core volunteers serve as "captains" of their respective teams probably makes coordination easier. Plus, that way, if some of the teams end up not getting their act together, the other teams can still move forward.

    Yeah?

    \n Pinball definitely prints pretty badass looking cards too, on the same chipboard stock they use for covers:


    Joe, if you're not convinced on the format, you should definitely do whatever you think makes the most sense for you games. I wasn't trying to pressure you into contributing Deserting Paradise unless you want to.

    I can't speak for anyone else, but, in my mind, I think Scoutbooks makes sense over making your own booklets for a number of reasons. First, I don't think I want to make 200+ booklets by hand; that just sounds like a nightmare. If we sell out and want to do another run, calling up Pinball again is way easier than assembling another 200. Second, these are actually REALLY nice printed products, done on a real printer with 100% recycled and oil-free materials, with a level of quality that I think would be really hard to replicate on a home printer, especially the ones with multiple colors. For example:





    Even the pure black and white ones look really sharp:







    In my mind, this is a great, professional looking format for commercially publishing games, period -- with a bunch of advantages over the 6x9" perfect-bound books that have gradually come to dominate this wing of indie rpg publishing. So I see these, really, as competing more directly with that format rather than competing with hand-made or Kinkos printed booklets. Yeah, there are definitely some limitations as well and the limited range (32-page 3.5x5" mini-booklets) offered directly by Scoutbooks.com is not as interesting perhaps as some of the custom products I've seen Pinball produce (including larger 5x7" booklets with pages in multiples of 8 or 16), but it's worth trying out I think, at least with a limited experimental project like this one.

    But yeah, if people would rather do other things with their games, that's totally cool too (and really, that goes without saying). But I'm with Matthijs that I can't wait to try out something in this format and see how it turns out, both as a physical product and in terms of relating to potential customers, especially those who are not necessarily attracted to more traditional rpg product formats. High quality printing on chipboard, I think, looks hip and modern, a step up from hand-made booklets, but clearly with a different vibe than full-color covers on paperback or hardback books.
