\n In all the playtests I run, the number one piece of advice I give my playtesters is to forget it's a playtest.

It's worked well for me, so whether you're a designer or a player, this might make your playtests better.

    \n Whenever I playtest my own stuff at gaming conventions, I always tell the rest of the players that it's a playtest. I actually ask them if they would like to playtest a game, rather than pitching the game in the first place. I do that to ease them in to my questions at the end of the game. Because if people don't give feedback, then part of the playtest has failed.

    I don't see why it should be any different with external playtests. They should take into consideration that it's a playtest, so they can oversee any bugs, and keep those in mind when it's time to give feedback.

    So I don't agree with your statement. Not at all. I don't even share your own experiences, and I playtest a lot of tabletop games - more than my own games.

    \n Reporting on my experience \u2014 which specifically concerns playtesting my own as well as other designers' (role-playing) games at small Italian conventions or meet-ups, centered around the GenteCheGioca web forum or other preexisting communities \u2014 I of course tell people it's a playtest. That's what honesty demands, and I also want them to harbor realistic expectations, so that if the game somehow breaks down mid-play, or has to be jury-rigged, players don't experience it as a betrayal.

    That is, I use "it's a playtest" as a code for "it's an alpha stage product: I provide no warranties". I expect people to jump onto the playtest wagon despite that disclaimer, out of curiosity for trying something new, or the sheer goodwill of helping me/the designer with our project, and in fact lots of people do.

    But I've noticed a hiccup as well: some people, probably just a minority of them, understand "playtest" as the code for quite a different activity from "playing a game". They either play with reduced commitment (specifically, reduced creative or emotional involvement) or actively attempt to sabotage the game. Both approaches are warranted to make the game a failure, and a waste of everybody's time including mine, and the latter is also very demanding of the sabotaging participant, leaving them exhausted. When I read Ben's post, I thought it was about those situations, and specifically preventing them.

    For example, short after first drafting Enter the Avenger, before submitting it to Worlds Without Master, I took the game to a local convention and wished to try it out. I was going around and asking people to playtest it with me, and a friend of mine replied: \xabNo, thanks. I'm quite tired at the moment. I could stand some role-play, you know, but not play-testing! That's exhausting.\xbb

    Me: \xabActually, I've just drafted out this game and want to try it out for the first time. I need to know whether the idea of it holds together in practice, but there's no need to, like, "stress test" it yet. It's too early a stage. I just want to play.\xbb

    My friend: \xabWhat's the point of testing, then?\xbb

    I was baffled \u2014 I always am \u2014 but, truly, this isn't an uncommon perspective. My current understanding is that this specific outlook on playtesting, that it's a chore because you have to try hard and break the game, comes from boardgames. For one thing, I never encountered it in the chamber-larping community, as opposed to the tabletop role-playing crowd. I believe those friends of mine who think like that are all hardcore boardgamers who either identify as boardgamers first and roleplayers second, or consider tabletop RPGs to be a subset of boardgames (myself, I'm just a casual boardgamer - I don't feel like I belong in the subculture, if there's one).

    But my experience so far is that no role-playing game can actually be "stress tested" that way! As a shared creative endeavor, if there's one participant actively trying to "break" an RPG, it'll break \u2014 i.e. fail as an activity \u2014 no matter how "solid" the design is.

You might have fun playing, you might fall in love or hate with the premise of the game, you might be sold or unsold on the game, but none of those things are as important as the test.     \n I've play-tested a great deal of stand-alone games, as well as just run them at conventions. Mainly Fastaval, and as all the games there are meant to be run by total strangers and usually only tested once or twice beforehand, I'd say I have some experience.
    Here's what I've learned: Don't try to change what doesn't make sense when reading it. It can turn out to be an essential mechanic in play, even if you can't understand why it would work when just reading it.
    Do a read test. Simply read the game, and note what doesn't make sense to you, or what you need clarified. All writers have things that make sense in their heads. Sometimes they manage to put it in writing, sometimes they don't. Tell them.
    When running the game for others, try to get them through as many nooks and crannies of the game as makes sense. Don't try too hard. For the players, it's still a game they play. If they consider it a test instead of a vessel for having fun, they can't test the game. After all, a game is meant to be fun. If you're too focused on testing, you're not having fun, therefore not testing.
    Do a debrief. Ask what worked and what didn't. The players will exchange war-stories. Let them, but sort the wheat from the chaff. Ask leading questions, such as "What made this bad".
