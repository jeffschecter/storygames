Is it possible to make struggles against "Nature" interesting?

Andy
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
Hey all:

I like the idea of struggling against nature. Like finding water and shelter and food in the wastes in Dark Sun, or an arctic setting. However, in all the games I've tried it with (from old school AD&D 2E with the assistance of the 1E Wilderness Survival Guide, to even Sorcerer or the like) it always seemed to come out kind of cold.

It usually devolves to one of the following things:

1) Very simple resource management ("You fail your survival check. Dehydration sets in." Player: "Crap" {checks off one 'water' box}).

2) The GM regaling the group with a narrative of how the environment threatens them, while the players silently listen. Eventually, they overcome. Dice, if rolled at all, influence how easily/hard they overcome the threat.

I'm thinking that there might be something to be done about this. What I'm looking for is a solution where:

0) It can be applied to any regular/trad system
1) The environment presents a challenge to be overcome
2) The players have a say/stake in what happens (to their character, at least): Not just "sit back and listen to the GM tell us the story of the sandstorm"
3) Environmental challenges become interesting, when they happen. Not just "lame filler".

-Andy

    Couple of things spring to mind, I have worked on this on and off for some time:

    1) A survival mini-game, like Spycraft's chase mini-game or D20's combat mini-game. This survival mini-game would break down survival resources like shelter, food, water, temperature (heat in the cold, cold in the heat) and players would prioritize them over a period of time using various strategies. Your survival skill would affect both how well you used the strategies you picked and how you could "multitask" (much like how the D20 combat mini-game tells you how well you perform each attack and how you can perform multiple attacks.) The environmental danger depletes those resources in a way that's not visible to the players, although again, skills can give them some idea of what to expect. When a resource runs low or runs out, that's when the character really starts to get in trouble.

    You could incorporate either the living-in-harmony or conquering-nature in the strategies and equipment you selected.

    2) Man vs. Nature is also the battle in an exploration game, although there's also the element of the unknown. Basically the exploration mini-game is "how far can we go on the resources we have", there's the safe strategy of turning back when you're less than half-consumed your resources, there's riskier strategies of counting on hunting or trading with natives in order to continue onwards, and there's choices about whether to press on to the next outpost or go back. The Oregon Trail computer game has tilled this ground so well there's really not much I can add to it. Go play it here if you forgot what it was like: Oregon Trail at virtualapple.

    Aw crap. JD beat me to it.

    Tony got it first. It's about decisions, as always. John is right that personalizing the thing only helps if that allows you somehow to create these decisions.

    This is true across all styles of play, too. For the nar side, it's "Do you eat that rat?" For the gamism side, it's "Which decision leads to the best result?"

    In addition to the mini-game idea Jason has as a way to create decisions, there's also using these sorts of conflicts in a larger context. Do I use up resource A now to deal with this disaster, or save it for something I see coming up later that might be worse? Or brave a dangerous natural situation to gain otherwise hidden knowledge that can help me?

    I'm sure there's lots of other ways to engineer this, as many as there are facets to gamism. Nature is just a backdrop to these decision points.

    A problem with "disaster" per se is that it often puts survivial up on the block. This is bad because it means you can't fail without the game ending (meaning it might be very short and undramatic), or because the decisions presented often have only one choice. Eventually the librarian will probably eat the rat if she realizes how dire her situation. Rather it's only a question for a select few. You can't present that situation to a character who's like me, because that's no choice at all, I'd eat the rat in a moment if I were hungry.

    Basically there are few things as valuable to a character as their life, so finding something to put up on the other side is hard. And if they do choose to risk their life then you either have to bail them out, or allow the character to die.

    Consider using the environment to pressurize less direct problems. Uh, the disaster means choosing slight danger if we go left, or insulting a friend. Use resource X, or resource Y, or die (really only a choice between the first two).

    Save the actual survival portion for the climax.

The most solid use I know for really horrible weather is a horror story, because it eliminates (or better still, it balances out the benefits of) the option of running. Any scene someone wants to try something in the environment, share the checklist. Go down it, looking for precautions they overlooked, resources (internal or external) they're consuming or random mishaps. If nothing else this simulates the kind of cumbersome extra work that everything becomes in a hostile environment. Ideally, the checklist work gets done by an audience player, so that the effects occur in the middle of other things going on.

    • Josh Roby
      January 2007
      Larry, here's an example. In Agora (think Sid Meier's Alpha Centauri in tabletop), the PCs are pitted against elements of the environment (and occasionally rival factions, but we'll ignore that bit for the moment). So you might be up against The Black Swamp as you try to establish a landing site. You play through the scene with Dogs-like raises and sees, and at the end of the scene the PCs either triumph or they do not overcome the obstacle. If you beat the Black Swamp, you establish your landing site and you take a trait off of the Black Swamp, rewrite it a bit, and put it on your sheet. So the Giant Swamp Lizards 2d8 are removed from the Swamp and put on your sheet as Swamp Lizard Cavalry 2d8. If you lose, the Black Swamp crushes your landing site and sends you running, but one of your ideals is strengthened for later.

      Additionally, win or lose, you may have to rewrite your ideals based on the traits of the Black Swamp. So if the Black Swamp's position was something like, "I will poison any who enter" your ideal "The tools of state are diplomacy and violence" may be rewritten to "The tools of state are poison and violence." So just as the PCs are trying to leave their imprint on their environment, the environment leaves an imprint on them.

      The real core of my point, I think, is that real Man vs Nature is all about a real, visceral, bone-deep engagement between man and nature, not whether one can get past the other without getting too beat up. One or both of them will be forever changed by the conflict. Without that sort of potential for imprint, the conflict is going to come across all loose and unengaging.

      One of the mechanical keys, at least for me, is that Nature gets a character sheet, traits, and especially resources to spend. As long as you treat Nature as this bottomless well of ambient danger, you can never 'beat' it, you can only survive it, and you can't take anything away from it -- there's literally nothing there, mechanically, to take. As soon as Nature (or the Storm or whatever) gets statted up, all the sudden there's something to engage with mechanically, there's a metric to measure victory, and there are resources to be gained from it. Then (and in my thinking only then) can you have a Man vs Nature conflict that is expressed in the game as well as in the fiction.
