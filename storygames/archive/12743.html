\n So here's the Debate That Never Ends on Story Games: What to criticize, and when?

Once again, I find myself with a product in my hands that I think is, well, pretty okay, but definitely not very good. And I'm considering writing about it, but...

1. I maybe sort-of know the person behind the product, the way that a lot of us maybe sort-of know each other here.
2. In the past, open criticism has had some surprising results; if I don't reeeeally sweeten the pill, people can send some surprisingly angry whispers.

Of course, we never reach a consensus on this issue. (I'm not even sure this community (or whatever it is) CAN reach a consensus; maybe we lack the tools or structure). So if I go out and post criticism of a product, it's a personal risk. I'm essentially taking a chance on weakening the maybe sort-of friendship I have with whoever's behind the product.

Right now, on Story Games, criticism of each others' products is Simply Not Done. Competitions and conventions, sure, but not products.

Good thing or bad thing? It's a thing.

    \n I guess the real question is, what purpose do you have in providing criticism?

    * Is it because you want everyone to know what you feel about the work? That's really the core of a public critique, right there: it is you telling an audience what you think. If what you think is "This is awful," and you want people to know that, you post your criticism and take whatever reaction you get from it. It's your opinion and you get to choose how you state it, but you're arguably a better and more decent person if you try to be polite. That may not keep your criticism from hurting anyone's feelings, but at least you didn't set out to offend them.

    * Is it because you think your criticism will be useful to the author? Then you should probably just send it to the author directly, and not bother with making it public.

    * Is it because you're not sure if you're right and need to talk it over with other people? Then you'll probably get more mileage and a less-obstreperous discussion out of posting questions rather than a criticism: "I don't understand how this combat system works, it seems awkward and prone to producing bad results -- am I getting it wrong, what do you guys think?" instead of "This combat system is awkward and poorly designed." Admittedly, you will get more responses by making a flat declarative statement, and even more if it's an inflammatory flat declarative statement (yay internet), but you're also going to get sidelined into all kinds of stupid arguments that may not actually address the topic you wanted to discuss.


    Fact is, if you have a criticism of the work, where and how you post it has nothing to do with honesty versus cruelty or who's in the in-crowd and who's not: just figure out why you want to share that criticism, and that'll tell you who you should share it with and how you should phrase it.

    Many years ago, years before I ever found my creative medium in RPG design, I dated a quite intelligent woman. I would show her my fictions and nonfictions and tell her about my ideas. And she would provide constructive criticism, upon which I could base improvements. Because, of course, the world ignores works which aren't excellent. That, or it rides roughshod upon them with cruel hooves.

    There is a great deal of generally accepted wisdom about the value of constructive criticism that I now believe is bullshit. The institution of "constructive criticism" in creative communities is born of anxious, self-serving neuroticism and white-knuckled paternalism.

    Danielle taught me that I need none of it. It does nothing but keep me from wielding the full force of my creative powers. What I need is feedback that puts energy into my efforts. What I need is feedback that helps me see the full elephant, to understand the meaning of the whole beast that has yet only a crude shape under my mortal hands. I am already scrutinous and critical enough of my creative efforts. What I need is feedback that strips away the bullshit that's holding me back, empowers and armors me against the certain doubts and contrary notions of others, and gives me energy and momentum.

    Look at this game you have, find the love you have for it, and instead of criticism give it the feedback it really needs.

    Paul

    COLD CITY
    some brief criticism by Jonathan

    Malcolm is one of the coolest people I have ever met. Just to get that out there. He's also writing his MA thesis on WWII stuff in New Zealand, so he's a badass.

    Malcolm came to GenCon with A|State one year, the same year that The Mountain Witch broke big, and apparently went back to the UK with his head buzzing about what The Forge-based indie scene was up to. Cold City came out the next year and its indebtedness to the Mountain Witch was obvious from the beginning. Basically, it's a Mountain Witch hack more than a real stand-along game, in many respects. And the places where it drifts away from the Mountain Witch are perhaps where it is weakest. Statting up monsters differently from each other -- rather than having three different kinds of challenges ala TMW -- pushes the game in the direction of being a traditional dungeon-y monster-fighter, especially when combined with the locales suggested in the game, which grinds awkwardly against the premise and color. Also, the addition of positive/negative traits, potentially a clever idea (drawing, I suspect on the idea of TSOY Keys and fallout from Dogs), plays havoc with the reward systems and other things. Agendas come off as an interesting attempt to build on Dark Fates and are perhaps the most successful of his extensions. However, Cold City views itself as a longer running game than the Mountain Witch, perhaps contributing to the problem by making things less focused (both situationally and mechanically) in general.

    That said, the book is beautifully written and evocative and the overall concept is compelling. Playing it can often be successful, but it often feels like the rules are inconsistent in helping you achieve awesomeness. Some things have been fixed in the more recent revision, which is even more evocative and beautiful as a written and illustrated product, but perhaps don't go far enough in resolving underlying mechanical issues with the game. Likewise, the "sequel" to Cold City, Hot War, is somewhat more focused, but I haven't played it and don't feel confident in asserting whether or not it has transcended some of the issues with Cold City. It is even more evocative and beautifully written than Cold City Revised, though. So Malcolm continues to push that bar forward, at least.

    Cold City is significant because it marks -- along with Best Friends, Mob Justice, and Contenders -- one of the first attempts by the Forge indie scene to connect with indie tabletop communities outside the US and Canada, presaging the connections that would eventually develop with Japanese and especially Nordic designers. Also, design-wise it marked the first real use of CG illustrations in a Forge-style game (A|State was written pre-Forge-contact) and melded some of the White Wolf school of game book design (background textures, fake documents, messy typography, evocative fiction) with the dimensions and small page count common with Forge-influenced indie games. It was also, though this is more controversial and subjective, part of a post-2005 wave of new games that were brought to publication with the expectation of following the success of The Mountain Witch, Polaris, and Dogs in the Vineyard, but quickly found themselves aswim in a flood of new games, many of which were inconsistently awesome at the table. This began a round of introspection and self-examination on the forums and blogs, talks about "quality" and "criticism." As such, many of the games of this era are unfairly lumped together and receive less overall attention than those published before and after.

    Malcolm would go on to be a significant figure in the UK indie scene, partially through being one of main faces of the Collective Endeavor. He has been significantly less active in recent years since the publication of Hot War, since he's moved to New Zealand. Paul Bourne continues to be an active illustrator and designer for indie games, especially ones from the UK.


    \nSo... not a ringing endorsement, but places the game in context, explains why it's important, and finds the value and significance if the overall community.

    EDIT: In a perfect world, this would contain more about the game itself and how it works, plus be fact-checked and experience-checked with a bunch of other knowledgeable people, including Malcolm himself, before being posted or published somewhere.

    \n So, in art school there was a pretty formalized structure for giving critiques and a pretty strong etiquette that you were supposed to follow. Putting something on the wall for people to judge is terrifying, so if it was clear that someone had put effort into a particular work then it absolutely was not okay to say "this is shite".

    FIRST you find at least one thing, preferably a couple of things that you admire and provide more than trivial detail. What is it that you like and why? ("I like the choices of color and the sense of energy. The brushstrokes really give a sense of movement.")

    THEN you bring up things you find problematic that you can see concrete solutions to. ("The composition is problematic. Because only a tiny part of their arm is cut off it looked like it happened on accident. I would re-crop the image so that it's clear that the framing is deliberate.")

    LAST you bring up things that you find problematic that you can't see an immediate solution to, admitting that you may be speaking to a personal bias and leaving it an open question. If it is a valid criticism, someone else can probably put their finger on what is bothering you. If no one else agrees, its probably a bias. ("I get why you chose to finish your piece this way, but I think it might be detracting from the message. I'm not sure that I can see an easy solution, though. Does anyone else have an idea, or is this just me not liking grungy pieces again?")

    This is important because:
    A) Providing positive feedback first indicates that you're trying to help the creator improve their art
    Providing positive feedback first means that it doesn't get ignored. Positive feedback is just as important as negative feedback when you've been working on something for two weeks straight with four hours of sleep a night and you are convinced it is THE WORST THING EVER.
    C) Pointing out problems paired with solutions is way, way, way more helpful than just pointing out problems. If you point out a problem and don't offer a solution, I have no idea if you're right or if you're talking out your ass. Offering solutions will let me evaluate where you're coming from and help me to see my art in a new way.
    D) When you point out a problem that you don't see a solution to, admitting bias can be painful but it is neccessary. Sometimes the answer will be 'yes, your bias is talking right now'. More often you will help someone else articulate something that will lead to a solution.


    I would really love to see a lot more game criticism framed this way.

      \n "Criticism is hard," is what ConceptArt.Org told my brother in a recent expo in Berlin.
      \n
      \nMy brother has had discussions with some pretty influential people around the world on the topic of art critique for education. The problem, he tells me, is that people don't critique for the right reason. People critique for themselves, not for the artist. They want to demonstrate their power, perception and intelligence. This is totally unhelpful for an artist in training. If you look at a life drawing, you should try to indicate to the artist what he has done well. Don't tell the artist that the proportions are all wrong, tell them that their lines are good, or their rendering is good, and so on. As long as it is positive and honest, you can say it. A good artist knows his proportions are all wrong. Your job is to give him the confidence to continue to practice.
      \n
      \nThat being said, it is important, in addition to the cultivation of confidence, to train the eye. The easiest solution is to tell the artist he's shit. Or his lines are shit. Or his composition is shit. But it's also totally destructive. Instead, you might encourage the artist to ask questions. "What could you have done to improve your line?" The trick is in directing the artist toward his weaknesses without direct criticism, not because you think he's a pussy, but because you want to build a pattern of detection and repair so that, in the future, he doesn't need you to tell him he's shit any more.
      \n
      \nThe difficulty here is in directing a student to self criticism. It's much easier to do if you loads the student with tools before you get to that point of critique. For example, if you know that judging proportions in life drawing is easier if you are more aware of 'negative space,' then you should teach the student about negative space before you ask him to look for it when his proportions are wrong.
      \n
      \nThe important thing is to encourage, reward and expose the assets and at the same time endorse a self critical eye. I think it sounds like a successful model for art education and maybe it will work as well for indie game critique.
