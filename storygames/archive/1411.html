Conflict of Interest Hack for Shab Al-Hiri Roach

Josh Roby
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
I've been meaning to post this up somewhere because it's an interesting little wrinkle we found by accident.

At the last Gateway convention, we played Roach. I ended up leading a table because I'd played it before (once, months prior). So I explained the basics from memory and a few little flips through the book and we played. We had an awesome game, and it was only afterwards when we were discussing the other game of the Roach that tanked that I realized that we had accidentally introduced a new rule to the Roach.

The rule is this: it's not a conflict unless somebody rolls against you.

This means that you can only double your wagered Reputation chips if you can convince somebody to oppose you, usually because you've set something up that their character would not want to happen. This means no automatic win when you do something that's so awesome everyone agrees it happens. Oh, it happens; you just don't get more chips for it. You have to keep going and find something that another player is going to take issue with. Which means that we ended up finding and hitting player and character flags rather than doing the tempest-in-a-teapot thing that Roach can occasionally become.

In our game, we had more than a few scenes where the player would say something to the effect of, "And I seduce your character," and the other player says, "Okay, my character sleeps with you." No conflict! So the first player has to redouble his attempt, "I seduce your character and convince her to see me exclusively." To which the second player says, "Oh hell no," and then the dice come out. We went from "Oh, it's Paul's turn" to real engagement with what was happening. It made for some fantastic play.

    To be fair to the original point I think it's actually more reliable to put personal effort into scanning what others want and attacking that than it is to throw out things that interest you and hope others step up to oppose you.

    This was my initial problem in Capes. Originally I had created a Mind-Reading/Controling Psychiatrist called The Raven. He had an exemplar named Violet who was a 12 year old girl with mental problems and immense Sorcerous power. *I* was invested in the struggle between The Raven's obsession with Violet's illness and Violet's uncontrolable powers. I kept throwing down Goals and Events related to these things.... which no one opposed, ever. In fact we all started like this, basically throwing down Goals and Events relevant to what we wanted to see our characters do and deal with and then promptly ignored each other.

    Then in the third session, I finally had a revelation. I looked over at my wife's pile of characters and noticed they were all relatives of one another. I ALSO know that family is a big huge deal with my wife. So in that scene I picked up one of her characters to play and immediately introduced a Goal that would threaten the stability of the family structure she had created. BOOM! The game and the system took off like lightening.

    I suspect The Roach is ultimately much the same.

    I remember a thread a long time ago on The Forge that came to the conclusion that it's fun to present conflict and it's fun the resolve conflict but it's not fun to do both simultaneously. I think that issue is what we're talking about here.

    Jesse
