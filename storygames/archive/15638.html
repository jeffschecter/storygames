    \n
    \n I'm personally convinced that price discretion is the key to commercially successful roleplaying projects. It has been proven time and again over the last decade that the paying audience for rpg products is not only heterogenous, but also wealthy and capable of financing the things that interest them. Any product with static cost of entry fails in that its price is inevitably too high for those who are not interested, yet unnecessarily low for those for whom the product is the best thing out there. This latter audience will gladly pay more for more. It would not be amiss to say that the supplement treadmill is an artistically failed attempt at a commercial solution that enables your core audience to give you more money than those customers who in hindsight are not that interested.

    (One could also argue that high-end luxury product has done consistently well in the rpg market, managing to spark the jaded interest of utterly cynical gamers over and over again. However, having only an expensive entry seems like something that would work best with an already existing audience; with a new product you should have some low-cost interactive material for your audience to convince them that this is something they want to spend money on.)

    In short: yes, if you can and care to produce extra value, and you offer it with similar distribution as the core product, I would expect that you would find such extra value products worthwhile. The people who are truly excited by your game will thank you for the extra effort.

    \n I've been thinking a lot about limited edition games because some of my most interesting game concepts probably have a rather limited audience, due to both interest and the necessity of certain physical components.

    Examples!

    I bought a copy of Cunningham & Venezky's Diaspora playing cards a few years back, which describe humans abandoning civilization and animals slowly taking over the cities. There are only 300 decks in existence and maybe only a few owned by members of the indie games community (because a link to it was posted on SG a while back). So if I design a game that uses the deck, it would be a very limited-edition thing, only playable with people who owned a copy of the deck. Could be a big hit at conventions, since it might be your only chance to play it! But not very effective as a commercial product for the masses.

    Another thought: my game Metrofinal is really crazy and weird, but the components are really difficult to produce in a way that makes them reusable. Players have to be able to draw on the game board and write on the components, but -- unlike Risk Legacy or something -- it's a single session game, not a campaign-length experience. So either I produce components as pads of sheets in a boxed set -- sorta like Luke and Jared did with Freemarket -- or I produce the game in packets of printed products that you dispose of afterwards: you'd effectively purchase the material for playing the game once and would have to buy a new set to play again. Maybe you destroy the components in a ritual fashion afterwards? Still pondering that. Maybe it just shouldn't be turned into a commercial product at all.

    Lastly, I own two copies of Hodge & Wright's landmark photographs of the Small Magellanic Cloud, which are 11x11" cardboard prints in a box of 200+ sheets. These will eventually be crucial components for playing Fingers on the Firmament, where players will draw on the star photos to make maps of space. There's probably a limit to how many copies of the game I can hand-assemble from used copies of Hodge & Wright's prints. Plus, the 11x11" dimensions are going to make them really hard to ship or do much else with. So maybe I'll make 20-50 copies and that's all the copies that will ever exist.

    I realize that many designers feel a natural desire for their games to be played by as many people as possible, but sometimes an experience can be more special, intimate, and valuable if it is extremely limited and special. And, as indie designers, we're not dependent on selling a bunch of games for our livelihood, like the folks at WOTC or even Green Ronin. Nobody's going to lose their job if you just sell 10 copies to the folks who really believe in and desire your game.

    Which is to say, yes, definitely go for it. Having a PDF version also available even allows you to have your limited edition cake and let other people eat it too.

    To clarify my question, I was asking, "Why, as a businessperson, do you want to offer a discount for an upgrade?" I can understand the logic from the consumer's side: "If I bought A already, I should have to pay less for A+B!" But if you do this, you're missing an opportunity to make several customers happy. Frankly, the dude who's willing to put down $45 on a boxed set isn't going to suffer from putting down $50 instead, if your product is worth $50.

    \n

    I think the strategy Marshall espoused is a much cleverer way to drive sales: Give PDF coupons with your boxed set (by the way, I love boxed sets and I wish you the best of fortune with yours), and each boxed set has the potential to create several new buyers, who come in with the positive feeling of having been given a gift. You end up taking in less cash per PDF sold, but you're creating many more opportunities for PDF discounts, which push your file into the impulse-buy range, and ultimately this might get your game into the hands of more people.

    \n I would think really carefully about the logistics of your chromed out version, because there are many opportunities there to lose money. Production costs increase, obviously, but so do shipping costs and loss from damaged goods. Nail everything down and make sure it makes sense. If it does, price it accordingly and give the PDF to the buyers for free. It costs you effectively nothing, is a gesture of trust and goodwill toward your very best customers and advocates, and gives them ultimate flexibility in how they learn and use your game. Make it available at an appropriate price for anyone who else who wants it, like the legions of cheap DIY nerds.

    To address your question as a consumer, I am not willing to pay for "play out of the box" convenience if it means I'm getting stuff I already have. I don't want dice, blank padded paper, glass beads, poker chips, pencils, etc. If your game uses custom cards, I'd love to have really nice ones I don't have to make, though.
