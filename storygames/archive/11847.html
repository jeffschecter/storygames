    \n Here's how I see it:

    - There are a bunch of traits any game/activity can have. For instance, "has a board", "has a game master", "makes you talk like your character", "involves dressing up".
    - Clusters of such traits that occur together in common usage when people talk about a category, are what "defines" that category. For instance, "makes you talk like your character" and "involves dressing up" are part of the definition of larp, because the games people call larps commonly involve these traits.
    - There are lots of grey zones and overlap. Is jeepform larp? Is freeform tabletop? Is interactive performance theatre larp? Is the HeroQuest boardgame a role-playing game?
    - Some of these categories have very little in common, although they're all part of what we call "games". Arthaus larp has very little in common with board games. They're fundamentally different.

    \n I'm with John and Matthijs on the definitional thing, but I wanted to add:

    What is interesting about breaking through the barrier of one "type" of game to another is that we can free ourselves to use all the tools that have been developed in other media. Magic: The Gathering encodes all but the basic rules of play on the cards, which are collectible and can be combined to create different play experiences. That's useful stuff for an RPG, but rarely used. Same for any game that has a board, and contains all the rules on the board. Another slick implementation that has potential for an RPG.

    I think it's fun to look at clever applications in different "genres" of games and see how we can apply those to our own designs.

    Although I don't expect the next edition of Monopoly to include some kind of fictional cause-and-effect, like Community Chest cards which read, "Has your character attended a charity fundraiser up until now? If so, collect $200 and draw another card." Though that would be kinda cool.

    \n Personally I don't really agree with that statement, but this does rely on that I understand the foundation of role-playing to be the creation of a story, whereas board and card games are founded on the concept of winning the game. That, to me, is the defining root of RPGs, and puts them clearly in their own category. If anything, I'd think that the marketing strategies between the three would be more similar than the design goals.

    However I do agree that I think certain aspects - particularly the use of gadgets and utilities such as markers, cards and special boards - are not presently being used to their full potential in the hobby. The recent revision of Warhammer attempted to do this, but to my mind went in the wrong direction. Instead of using utilities to simplify the process of role-playing, they merely felt like gimmicks to me. The many different symbols on the dice for instance could have been so much more intuitive and concise in their usage, and didn't bring nearly as much to the game as I had hoped they would.

    So personally, the crossover between the triad of tabletop games has at least made me think of new ways to structure the rules beyond funny shaped dice and endless look-up tables - not that I used much of either in the past, but it's certainly given me new ideas. I do however think that these would be better implemented with direct reference to the central concepts of storymaking, rather than being simply used to turn an RPG into something more akin to a board game.

    -Ash

    The question "Is this object a blegg?" may stand in for different queries on different occasions.
    \nDisguised Queries

    Perhaps when Ron asks "Is this object an RPG?" the question could usually be more precisely formulated "Does this object have shared fiction and character portrayal informed by and affecting game mechanics?" But when Ash asks the same question, it's closer to "Does this object facilitate the experience of creation of a story more than the experience of winning a game?" And when John asks, it's "Is this object asymmetric under 'what you describe'?"

    Shortcuts are fuzzy but a useful compression of reality. By looking at other parts of reality that compress to whatever you just compressed your game to, you might find surprising parallels. My answer to Tyler's question is that it allows bringing solutions to problems in from a much broader pool. For example, I sometimes play a D&D variant with a GM that rotates by vote every scene (and even every combat round, during combat). The biggest large scale problem that's come up is plot continuity. Threads get introduced and then accidentally abandoned all over the place, or threads we want to wrap up we instead stay with 'cause someone else just came up with a great scene and got voted for. My roommate looked at the problem not as "How do I fix the scene economy in this RPG?" (one answer: the Sons of Liberty model worked all right), but as "How do I fix this supply/demand/throughput problem?" and has started looking at a Kanban-like solution.

    Regarding the Disguised Queries link: maybe it turns out you already have a neat machine that cheaply sorts kittens by fuzziness, and you can use it to sort bleggs and rubes pretty well.

    \n I think what is useful in terms of design is not the name of the game type but the physical properties of that type.

    An RPG usually comes as a book - that allows people to create their own world.
    A board game comes with a board and counters. The location of things is usually meaningful.
    Card games come with cards. The rules of play can emerge based on the cards drawn.
    In a LARP people dress up and talk in their characters voice but can only do what we can actually do.

    I see these as different poetic forms. One may be a limerick, another a sonnet, a third a haiku. All are poetry but different.

    I now make board games to do story games because I have the machines to do so. Many people make RPGs because they can go to a POD printer to make them. Card are the hardest because they are art intensive and difficult to manufature in small numbers.

    As to how they play I think that you are right, the actual rules can produce very similar ends. They can all make story games but they don't have to.

    Chris Engle

    \n My thinking is that in an RPG:

    * The rules provide support for each player or "playing piece" representing an individual person (a character)
    * The rules provide support for the characters in the game to interact in ways that can't be represented solely by arrangements of physical objects (including notional ones such as points)

    If it has these elements it's an RPG. The physical presentation of a game could be nothing but boards, cards, and pawns, or anything or nothing at all, and that doesn't matter.

    \n I think this largely depends on why people play certain games. Within board games, card games and RPGs there are different reasons for playing different sub-groups, but within those sub-groups there is appeal.

    Someone who likes Magic: The Gathering probably has a tendency to like games like Warhammer and D&D - they both involve collecting and a large amount of combinations. Another person might like chess, poker and one of the "This book is all you need" RPGs. They want endless replay value without having to expand on what they've bought. This could be dictated by financial constraints, but both cases you have something different. Someone who likes Risk might equally like Snakes & Ladders, Paranoia and War - relatively mindless fun where you can leave it mostly to the dice and worry less about your own decisions. These games wouldn't appeal at all to people who want tight control over their game-play and like to emphasise strategy. What sets RPGs out from the rest is you can have all these aspects show up in a single game. People who like collecting books will play at the same table as people who want only the core book. People who want to sit back and relax will play at the same table as those who want detailed tactics. Magic is going to be unsatisfying for anyone who buys just a single set to play. Snakes & Ladders and Monopoly would be unsatisfying to someone who wants control over winning. An RPG has the potential to bridge all of that. It's also very distinct in that card games and board games don't allow for disregarding rules. RPGs generally explicitly advise disregarding them.

It was hard enough to achieve as a nerdy junior high school kid in the 80s and all but impossible as an adult. It's something I still struggle with, really: if I run a one-shot game or a mini-campaign with a pre-set number of sessions, even if we all have fun I feel like I'm somehow Doing It Wrong.

    \n Wittgenstein wrote something that might interest some of you on this topic. The following bit of information: Family resemblance

    The main point being: ...was that things which may be thought to be connected by one essential common feature may in fact be connected by a series of overlapping similarities, where no one feature is common to all. Games, which Wittgenstein used to explain the notion, have become the paradigmatic example of a group that is related by family resemblances.
