\n You see, here in Toronto, you can only get a story game off the ground if you're willing to meet people through Toronto Area Gamers or maybe the Toronto Role Playing Association. Also, you have to have somewhere to play, like... a house that you own. Oh, but good luck if you live a hour away and don't own a car. The transit system is awkward.

But if I lived in Seattle, I could just take the public transit system to any game I wanted. In fact, there are gangs of friendly, co-ed groups looking for an extra player right now before they start their next big IAWA campaign.

Tell me about where you are, and where you WISH you were, for the gaming.

    \n So I have some really cool friends here in Ithaca. I do envy the more regular that seems to happen elsewhere that I haven't been able to recapture post-college, though. Really what I want is a utopic city where me and everyone I've had a blast gaming with could all live together in harmony and love.

    \n Here in Baton Rouge, LA, there are exactly two gamers. Both of them only play D&D 4e, and by "play D&D" I mean "play World of Warcraft in an apartment that also has D&D books lying around in it somewhere." When you try to talk to non-gamers about your hobby, they look at you like you're speaking another language. Which you are, since the local English dialect only contains words pertaining to college sports, hunting, fishing, and crawfish recipes.

    But in Portland, OR, you can sit down in any one of the charming local coffeehouses that dot the landscape, crack open an RPG book, and just wait for the hordes of native gamers to roll in on their bicycles made of hemp. They won't approach you right away, either because of their hipster geek standoffishness or because their glasses are all fogged up from coming in out of the rain, but once you bribe them with a microbrew or some ridiculously delicious microroast espresso and assure them that the game you're looking at is printed with soy ink on recycled paper, you're in for an evening of awesome gaming, interrupted only by frequent trips outside to smoke American Spirit cigarettes and surreptitiously ogle the packs of naked bike hippies riding by.

    Apparently, all these bastards moved there right after I moved away.

    \n I am constantly and incredibly grateful for the gaming scene that's sort of accreted around me the past couple decades here in the metro Phoenix area. I mean, I personally know only a tiny fraction of all the area's gamers, but I have two very large groups to choose from, and those groups roughly know each other so there's even nice cross-pollination going. And then those players in turn have roots into the larger gaming scene, which we treat as a farm league. Every year or two, one new player will make the cut. It's been at least 10 years since I've gone to a gaming con or FLGS looking for fresh meat.

    Honestly, two nights of gaming is more than enough for me anyway. Any given night I could have 3 to 8 people on-deck to play pretty much whatever. I've got mini gamers, chit gamers, haaaard-core story gamers, trad RPGers, German-board-game gamers, CCG folks, whateva.

    So, yay Phoenix.

    \n I live in Pawtucket, RI. It's... not too bad to live in, I guess. Near Providence, which ought to help, but doesn't. All the gamers I meet are either TCGers or Minis players. The handful of roleplaying groups I've met have been hardcore D&D players, usually with the kind if DM driven/controlled play I don't go for. I've even been known to attempt to entice passing Magic players at game shops to join me and my friends for a game.

    Which leads me to them - my usual play group. They are, I'll admit, a delightful bunch. They'll play almost anything I throw at them, though with mixed levels of enthusiasm. And the enthusiasm is a slight problem, as I'd prefer if they were really into what's happening - picking out new games to play, giving me feedback on what they like and don't like, helping me playtest, and the like.

    Also a problem is scheduling. Most of them lead hectic late-teen lifestyles - finishing high school, getting ready to go to college, job hunting, partying, and so on. Amongst the 4 or 5 of them, I'm lucky to have two or three show up once every week or two - if that.

    Unfortunatly, I'm not exactly sure where the grass would be greener. Seatle, or Portland, or if I were closer to Boston maybe.
    Or maybe if I had the same exact situation, except in like Hawaii or something. I mean, I'd still be lacking for gamers, but... palm trees and beaches and ladies in bikinis, no?

    \n The Triangle, NC is the world capital of awesome. Chicago and New York have more City Life, but far less nature. Other places have far more nature, but less metropolitan style. The gaming here flows like boxed wine in college. Meetups go down here like Thai Lady-boys.

    But man, if I lived in Japan, me and my gamer friends could go up into the mountains to a Japanese-style inn (Ryokan); where we could soak in the hot springs, eat delicious natural food prepared from regional plants and fish, and then get our game on like motherfuckers. In fact, the Japanese equivalent of GenCon, "Japan Gaming Convention (JGC)", is like this, just closer to a city and in more of a modern hotel. Still, lots of folks throw down at the convention tables in their Yukata robes.
