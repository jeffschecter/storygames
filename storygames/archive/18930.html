\n This is a truly half baked idea.

Recently been day dreaming about the story games I want to run in the future using my 15mm figure collection. Rock Paper Scissors Card combat is part of it along with action cards used to limit choice (and thus ramp up creativity). Reading in a biography of the Grand Conde (a 17th century French Prince) the influence of Ladies of rank came up and social combat demanded attention. Which naturally lead to the thought of extending the card combat system to social combat.

The idea is that strong attacks defeat steady defense, steady defeats yielding, and yielding defeats strong. This should fit easily into social conflict since there are strong power moves, steady straight forward conversation and passive aggressive back stabbing moves. I'll try and come up with a set of cards today. If I can I'll share them here.

The next step of the thought was that one card combat system can interact with another like system which would allow a player to use social combat in a combat situation (which was what happened just outside Paris on July 2nd 1652. A Princess stopped a battle by strong will, a few lies and tears, lots of tears.

Of course this leads to the silly idea of holding a conversation with monsters. Will tears stop the zombies? It could with these kinds of card rules. It would be so silly but might be huge fun.

Which bring me to the last point. Card combat - where each card has a descriptive word - games social interaction but is absolutely not role playing. Even the action cards can go off the role play gaming rails so easily with players saying what happens without really assuming a role at all. At that point it becomes a board game about a story. There can be tension over what happens next but no real immersion. I know a lot of players would just hate this and yet other players (non- role players and likely a lot of non-gamers) might like this more because it doesn't put them as much on the spot as highly creative RPGs can.

Anyway, it is all half baked and pretty scatter, but this is pretty typical of my creative process as it is happening. A crazy thought pops up, I plug it into the things I've been working on and then guess what the implications might be.

Chris Engle
