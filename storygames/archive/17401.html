    \n Solar System could do this, would perhaps be my main choice (aside from the original system - it has its virtues). This is because I find Paranoia interesting specifically for how loose the situation is and how much room there is for exploration of different themes. Playing e.g. My Life with Master or Primetime Adventures would be a very defined slice with a predetermined viewpoint on what it's all about.

    The original system or a sufficiently loose story game system like SS, on the other hand, enable you to retain a sense of dramatic tension: is this comedy, tragedy or drama, is this about secret society wars or bureaucratic kafka, does class society critique make an appearance, who makes the next punnish joke by introducing some farcical elements. And these aren't development choices in advance, they're constantly reappearing questions: will this scene be grim as hell or will it devolve into comedy? Solar System shines in these types of complex, organic weaves.

    Yes, thinking about it, I think I'll add Paranoia into my roster of "SS campaigns I offer when players ask for SS". We just started a cyberpunk SS game that's been hanging around for a couple of years, so I could definitely add a new thing to the roster. I'll have to reread the rule book to decide whether I'd go with the original system, though; there's all sorts of excellent stuff that one could do with SS here, but I'll need to look at the original rules to decide.

    \n Just to add my 2 cents, every game of Paranoia I have played where the majority played in first person survival mode has SUCKED! Because all the things the game encourages, such as backstabbing, hiding information, and taking no more risk than necessary, leads to inexpressive and BORING play. However, when people start playing in third-ish person irony mode, those same exact things can lead to engaging and even humorous or tragic play. It's the emotional distance between player and character which is important, but not too distant or else you end up with meaningless Zap style play, and that DOES get tedious real fast.

    The current system does nothing to support any of this, and Perversity Points are a bolt-on which though useful should be less necessary than they are, and I think act as a quick fix. Fiasco would be a great base, but even that is too character distant for me. Out of every RPG I have played, Dying Earth / Skulduggery has led to the most humorous character focused sessions I have encountered, so I'd probably start there, because Paranoia has always been satire first and foremost for me. But honestly, ANY system other than its own is preferable AFAIC.

    \nI'm not sure what's keeping you from it exactly, other than the players' expectations as to how Paranoia is supposed to play. I doubt that would change if you switch to a different set of rules.

    Paranoia at its core is "System Doesn't Matter" par excellence. It's hard-wired into the whole set-up, setting and tone of the game. It's the games central conceit, actually. The fact that it's wrapped up in a satirical package just makes most people accept it more easily.

    Paranoia is all about what the GM wants. It's about how you want to run the show/game and rewarding and punishing your players for appropriate and inappropriate behaviour. That can include non-traditional means (like verbal cues, use of props or just general LARP-style antics) as well as traditional ones (hard rulings, blatant favoritism, vocal enthusiasm).

    Really, nothing in the rules is making you run Paranoia as a game where life and death is cheap, where characters don't live long and don't get to explore Alpha Complex or the politics within the secret societies. It's the complete opposite in fact. The rulebook is almost overbearing in the way it continually proclaims how the entire thing is completely in your hands. There is no system to get in the way or guide the game. It's all on you.

    For what it's worth, I agree with Georgios on what the game text says.     \nI think that's the tragic misunderstanding that lies at the heart of a great number of "funny" but un-fulfilling games of Paranoia. Yes, it is a comedy game. But the humour doesn't have to come from the silly, blatant ways that the characters live and die at the whim of the GM.

    Much like, say, the Coen film "Burn After Reading", the comedy stems from the f'ed-up nature of the situation; the grimness of the setting set in stark contrast to the incompetence and pettyness of the characters.

    My most enjoyable and memorable games of Paranoia did NOT come from games where people internalized that their characters lived and died for a laugh. Those were some awfully stupid and silly games. Whenever I had players like that in my games, I tried to minimise their influence, because they would constantly steer the game towards some very hollow and dumb gaming.

    Instead when played like a traditional RPG, with all the assumptions, expectations and short-term goals that it entails, Paranoia opened up beautifully and became this unfathomably hilarious game of petty bureaucrats, loud-mouthed buffoons and self-involved idiots.

    I've found that the key to Paranoia was making sure you fight the assumptions and "internalised fatalism" that often accompanies veteran players new to Paranoia.

    I can sort of see how Fiasco fits well with this tone and style. But by switching to something as free-form as Fiasco players don't get to experience this hilarious tension between doing what their gamer instincts tell them and realising that this will just exacerbate an already insanely convoluted situation. There's something about Paranoia's humour that really only works when you are a gamer; when you have certain assumptions and trained behaviour as to what it takes to "play an RPG". Al of which are beautifully upended in a well-run Paranoia game. When Paranoia actually clicks with a group like that, there's really nothing like it.

    As far as I can tell, Paranoia is the only "roleplayer comedy" game out there.

    \n My point about the GM fiat is mainly what Georgios laid out there: playing Paranoia necessitates fighting against fatalism and petty short-term comedy exactly because players are inducted into the game with the powerful message that this game is about arbitrary GM powers. He'll kill your character if you read the rule book, hah hah.

    You could probably approach the game differently, and nowadays I would, but I think that it's pretty clear that the reason the game plays as chaotic comedy is because the players don't feel like putting serious work into characterization and setting exploration, and the reason they don't do that is because the game has been sold to them as an inequal power situation where the entire point is that the GM's going to unfairly hose you.

    I do think that the setup has its virtues, and I wouldn't be averse to trying it anew (haven't played the game since the '90s). Just like Georgios says, I would put special emphasis on encouraging the players to take the setting seriously and play their characters with forethought and cunning. This would be a balancing act, as I would also want to retain the arbitrariness of the Computer's reign of terror.

    And to clarify, there's nothing wrong with this being natural to the game. I just think that it would probably be easier to get over it and look at Paranoia as an interesting fantasy setting for other kinds of play if you used a different system to play it, one without the strong emphasis on GM fiat. I'm not convinced by the argument that the system is perfect for all kinds of play because the game is mechanically traditional and doesn't prevent you from singing opera librettos during sessions if you want to.

    \n John is right, of course - it would be a different game if you swapped out the system. That was the point of the discussion, right? What systems might be used instead of the original and why.

    The way I envision using e.g. Solar System (Burning Wheel, even Dust Devils) for Paranoia would be to reduce the level of implied character immersion. I don't think that the themes of the game require players to feel feelings of futility and despair (which are the reason for how the game affects players, make no mistake - some feel these things, while others specifically block the frustration by focusing on superficial comedy). It would be quite OK for the players to play these sordid losers, made so by the social system of Alphacomplex, with ironic detachment typical of many dramatic roleplaying games. I suspect that a happy balance between crazy comedic moments and horrible dark satire would be easier to achieve if the GM didn't need to find that balance alone.

    For example, what's wrong with the idea that your Communist traitor gets accused as such in one of those ad-hoc tribunals the Computer is so fond if, and we resolve this by entirely ordinary conflict resolution? Throw in some legalistic ability - speaking of Solar System here - perhaps support with computer logic (or whatever the English name of the skill is), see what the dice say. Seems to me that this could be potentially quite fun, in a different than if the players simply play-acted as hard as they cared and then the GM gave an arbitrary decision on whether your character gets to live another day.

    Of course it would be a different game ultimately, as something like SS empowers characters as well as players. I don't find this a bug, it'd be interesting to have the slim possibility of redemption hanging as a carrot out there in exactly the way SS enables if you make sure to turn the screws as much against the characters as the system allows. Some of the original Paranoia material flirts with actual success, too, what with the various rebellion storylines. Now it'd just be the entire group determining whether the Computer can or should be taken down instead of the GM pondering over his bookshelf as to which story to run next for the players.

    That being said, the player empowerment is more important than character empowerment here. I would totally go for something more desperate, too. The key is that the system is something that invites the players to explore the setting instead of punishing you for it. The reason for why I characterize the attitude of Paranoia as a thin joke is that it is genuinely a better read than play: the GM can appreciate how very Orwellian the game is, but does the attitude fostered by the game text really invite the players into the joke? My experience is that sometimes it certainly does, but far more often it seems that the only joke the players get is that the game is chaotic and irrelevant because it disenfranchices them.

    Perhaps a comparison will clarify the point: My Life with Master would be exactly like Paranoia in attitude if the game did not presume player buy-in into the scenario. MLwM gets the buy-in by explaining the premise up front and by having the players design their own Master figure. Then play proceeds with a definite ironic flair as player characters get abused in heart-breaking ways: oh how awful things are being done to my character! If the game were handled in the Paranoia way, with the game text emphasizing GM/Master authority for comedic effect, it would be a different experience.

    --

    After all that I'd like to again emphasize that I would totally be game for trying out some classical Paranoia out of the box, too. That system has virtues that you can't capture in a consensual cooperative game. For example, the fact that all characters have secret societies and mutations that are actually secret at the table, that's good fodder for asymmetrical tactical play. I believe that the Paranoia rules are intended for a sort of old school gamist play, with players using utmost cunning to survive and prosper in Alphacomplex; that would definitely be what I'd be driving at were I to play it now. Online play preferably, considering the challenges of running proper plotting face to face.

    In Paranoia all such assumptions are toyed with explicitly. And I'd argue a less-skilled GM would go overboard and outright break them. Leading to the many Paranoia games where death is random, the GM is lying and makes arbitrary decisions and the mission is a hose-job. While those games are still Paranoia, they are kind of bland and tacky.

    The key to running Paranoia is in the name. You want to make the players paranoid about their characters. If you put too much emphasis on the fatalism and helplessness of the players, then players will mentally and emotionally disengage from the game. It becomes a different game of Paranoia then. But if players are given too much influence over the game, they will at best affect an air of paranoia for playing their characters. Instead of experiencing an enjoyable tension within the game it skews closer to play-acting with a wink and a nudge. If that's what you're going for, then by all means Fiasco is probably the safest bet. It is far more accessible than Paranoia, gives players more room to contribute in any way, shape or form they feel comfortable with and just plays much faster.

    And from what I can tell, Graham's suggestion of hacking Cthulhu Dark might also work as it takes some of the creative weight off player's shoulders. Something that can be intimidating to inexperienced players.
