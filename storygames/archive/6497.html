    \n If you're trying to inform but not recruit, I think Dogs is a great choice. It's written in a very approachable style, it's really interesting, it's got great examples that illustrate the act of roleplaying in it. You can gloss over the intricacies of how dice work and what the terms are and still see what people actually do at the table, and in the fiction. So if your friend wants to understand what roleplaying is about, it is instructive - you typically portray a character in a setting and face adversity - and Dogs shows all that clearly. It also sends the message that the activity of roleplaying isn't confined to only one kind of adolescent power fantasy, but can embrace many kinds of adolescent power fantasy.

    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Show them Polaris, introducing it like, "This is a unique little RPG that personally blew my mind."
    \n
    \nBut... frankly I think that RPG texts are unlikely to make play more attractive to somebody who doesn't understand how to play. Very likely the opposite. Like studying to have fun. A good tone in the text might counteract this. But I think that typically this is not true.
    \n
    \nWhat I've done on occasion is this little exercize. I actually do some "hypothetical" character generation. Like:
    \n
    \nMike's Friend: I still don't get what these RPG things really are about.
    \nMike: OK, let's just create a hypothetical character, quick, and then I can give you a better idea.
    \nFriend: OK, how do we do that?
    \nMike: We'll use a really easy system called "Over the Edge." First you select a central concept for the character, like "Reclusive Businessman" or "Desperate Housewife." Somebody who might have a reason to flee to a little-known Mediterranean island.
    \n
    \nFinish with chargen and then say stuff like:
    \n
    \nMike: OK, let's say we started play with this character. I, as the guy running the game, would come up with a situation on arrival at the little-known Mediterranean island to which you would then dictate the character's response. Like I would tell you that he's arrived, but his luggage is missing, and he gets the feeling somehow that the loss isn't accidental. Then you'd tell me what your character would do?
    \nFriend: Like I could have him go to the desk to inquire about it.. right?
    \nMike: Exactly. Then I tell you that your character sees that one of the desk attendants has a strange pin on, that one that's associated with the conspiracy that you mentioned in your character's "secret."
    \nFriend: Ah, I see, and then I have to decide whether I should run or press him about my luggage?
    \nMike: Yeah, or whatever you think is plausible and entertaining for the character to do in that situation. Then we just continue this back and forth.
    \nFriend: Cool, how often do you play?
    \nMike: well I have a game every Thursday night...
    \n
    \nYou get the idea. Basically actually play, but just co-opt them into doing so by telling them that it's just an "example" to illustrate. That way they can go into it with plausible deniability (they can feel that they would not have to say afterwards that they had played a RPG). Once they get it, they'll be too interested in all likelihood to need that excuse... you could even form your game around the character and situation you had created. But it allows them to be more comfortable going in that they're not joining the "cult," but just observing it's ways. Once they see it's not a cult, they'll probably be interested.
    \n
    \nAt least that's been my experience. I think that doing an exercize like the above, and answering questions asked by the potential player, is a far better way to introduce somebody to RPGs than to toss them a book.
    \n
    \nMike

    \n I've done a lot of introducing non-gamers to play in the last few months, and I'm surprised no one has suggested my #1 absolute go-to game: The Mountain Witch. Depending on the potential players' interests, you can spin it heavily toward ghost story, samurai story, historical drama, or soap-operatic relationship stuff. That means you only have to be an expert in pitching/explaining one game to hit a lot of potential players' interests. The system is totally understandable and the back of the book provides really nice supports for play. As a text, I've also found it very readable/accessible for new players. In practice, it's been hugely successful - to the point where I've given away four copies of the book in the past year to brand-new players asking to take it home and show it to their friends. Strongly recommended.

    I love Nobilis, but it is severely intimidating to new players unless you are playing with an experienced group. The rules are relatively easy to learn but getting your head around what you do is shockingly tough. Again, this is my experience as a very active introducer-of-RPGs-to-non-geeks. They love it, but it's not a book you can hand people without adding some social capital of your own.

    --Jess
