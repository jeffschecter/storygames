\n Oh man, I had one of the best gaming sessions in recent memory last night and it was deeply satisfying. We started a PTA season and it was just screamingly good. My Monday group have mostly played a PTA one-shot or two but this was the first time we've committed to a full season. PTA has many dialects, and we're playing "Jason style", which means I did a little research up front and declared the general setting. So:

The Big Store (That's what Mafiya goons call America) is set in Brighton Beach in 1990, just before the Gulf War, after the introduction of Windows 3.0, as the Soviet Union disintegrates. The protagonists are Russian emigres involved in organized crime. They are, in fact, a group of losers known as "the Potato Bag Gang" working for mobster who has broken away from the Organizatsiya, the Jewish-Russian mob in America.

Misha Sokolov is a quick-witted and largely assimilated cab driver. Issue: "Ambitious - it's our turn."
Oleg Koslov is a defector who was spit out by the US government and now sells trinkets. Issue: "Broken hearted"
Grisha Berezin is an Afghan War veteran with an eye patch and a deep love for America. Issue: "Self control"
Ilya Pirogov is a wet-behind-the-ears newcomer sent to America to keep out of trouble. Issue: "Redemption"
Yakov is a by-the-book mob soldier and tattoo artist whose entire family is Americanized. Issue: "Hates America"
Vassili Lavrov is a retired enforcer from the old country who wants no trouble. Issue: "The Past"

We tied everybody together - Ilya is Lavrov's nephew and lives with him. Misha and Grisha are both from Gomel and knew each other growing up. Ilya and Yakov are similarly bonded (I forget how).

I ran the pilot pretty traditionally, aggressively framing scenes and intercutting two (and later three) storylines. Toward the end, once everybody had the hang of how the conflicts are organized, we opened it up a bit, and going forward I think it'll be straight PTA scene framing.

So two threads: The first had Misha and Grisha tasked with picking up Vyacheslav Lyubarksy, a jewel thief who owed their boss money from gambling debts. Lyubarsky was under the protection of the mainline Organizatsiya, so they had to recover him, which involved a failed conflict and a beating for Grisha out in front of Gastronom Odessa. Once they had him, he persuaded them to trade some uncut stones for his life - he knew their boss would cap him. Where does he keep his stones? With his good friend Lavrov, of course. Joel, playing Lavrov, orchestrated a wonderful scene where they negotiated for Lyubarksy's life. They ended up giving their boss the stones, letting Lyubarsky go, and having Lavrov (who has a great reputation) smooth things over.

The second had Yakov and Ilya tapped to assist the gang's scariest enforcer, Emile Puzyretsky, by guarding a door behind which he performed some mysterious beat-down. They were jumped by rival hooligans, failed a conflict, and were run off, leaving Puzyretsky to his fate. This was especially awesome because he'd given his own gun, a gaudy gold-plated .45 - to Ilya, who had none. So they abandoned him to die and ran off.

This gave me a great opportunity to introduce Oleg, who was selling Soviet trinkets on a blanket somewhere when Emile Puzyretsky hobbled up, a bullet wound in his thigh. The enforcer tossed him a wad of hundred dollar bills and told him to get him to a hospital. Various conflicts later Puzyretsky had decided Oleg was his fast friend, and was going to set him up as a made man, much to Oleg's consternation. But how does one refuse a guy who has killed 100 men and disarticulates corpses with a linoleum knife in his tub?

The final scene was Emile Puzyretsky tracking down the guys who cut him loose, which ended up with all the protagonists (except the old man, Lavrov) in the back room of Chicken Masters restaurant in Coney Island. We had a wonderful conflict about how his revenge would be exacted, and Yakov, the good soldier, ended up assuming all the blame. Forced to name his own punishment, he decided to tattoo COWARD across his own hand. Fan mail flowed.

We ended with a very satisfying round of "Next week on..." which includes such gems as "Oleg's initiation kill" and "Grisha chases his glass eye". Basing everything on reality (Puzyretsky is real, as are all the other characters they interacted with, as are all the locations they found themselves in) really seemed to click. I had a ton of fun and everyone seemed really engaged and having a good time. Our crappy Russian accents are the best.

    \n Last night was episode one of The Big Store. It was Misha's spotlight episode, and his issue is "Ambition". We did some interesting stuff.

    First up, Steve as Misha framed a high-stakes card game in which he was the most junior player, staked by his friend and using Vassili's name as his bona-fide. There were heavy hitters from the Organizatsiya, the Genovese family, and ... him. We started that game and then played most of the session as a series of flashbacks, discovering how he got into the game and who was really there - at some point we discovered that Micky Rourke was also playing, which was excellent. I established that Yakov and Ilya had been tapped to do some dirty work earlier in the evening, and they were at the card game as muscle, so I telegraphed a bit about their job by describing them as disheveled and sort of haunted-looking. What the hell had they done? It amped up the tension, and playing with time was effective and fun.

    Turns out their sociopathic boss, Emile Puzyretsky, gave them some marching orders that morning:

    1. Go find Oleg and bring him along.
    2. Buy ten liters of hydrofluoronic acid and some handcuffs.
    3. Meet him at a dry cleaning business on Avenue X.

    So, you know, that went well. Oleg, who doesn't even want to be in the game, was somewhat reluctant to torture the owner of the cleaners into signing away his assets, but business is business, right? It was a very disturbing scene. Yakov had been humiliated earlier in the session by the gang's supreme boss and took out his rage on the guy who didn't want to pay protection money. The best best best bit - they didn't know where to buy handcuffs, so they went to a sex shop and ended up with pink-fur-trimmed "prisoner of love" cuffs.

    Other cool stuff: Ilya, who is gay, made his first connections in the Brighton Beach gay subculture. Misha won the poker game, earned the unholy wrath of Biba Nayfeld, usurper of the Organizatsiya, and managed to survive the evening. Mickey Rourke punched Yakov Grisha in the face. Emile found poor, innocent Oleg after the protection/torture caper, and tossed him 20 grand in cash, promising more to come, and a chance for the lonely sap to bring his family to America. Vassili, "retired", was drawn deeper back into the game by defending Misha from Biba. The noose tightened - it was awesome. A two-parter is up next, with the Issues of Redemption and Self Control.

    \n Last night was a double spotlight episode for Ilya, whose issue is whether he's going to be a gangster or not, and Grisha, whose issue is straight-up self control.

    Things are a little tense - last week they beefed with Biba and the Organizatsiya is on their asses looking for payback. The two mobs are poised for a war that the Zilber gang (and the protagonists) cannot win. Biba himself paid Ilya's aunt Marfa a friendly visit, inquiring after her health. She begged the boy to do something, because people like Biba don't just drop by with the best of intentions. So this eighteen year old gay kid promises her he'll take care of it. Later he hooks up again with Leo Slutsky, who is a genuinely nice guy and not in the life at all, and they become sort of boyfriends. It's a sweet moment, with a wonderful conflict over how much Leo knows. Nothing, it turns out. Innocent as a lamb. They kiss on the boardwalk. Ilya resolves to leave the life, but first he has to take care of Biba. He's maddened by the thought of his aunt in danger.

    Meanwhile Grisha, who has thusfar been a hapless loser in all things, watches his world further crumble. His American girlfriend Melanie, who already humiliated him by macking on Mickey Rourke, seduces his best buddy Misha and has some manner of sordid sex-type activity with him in his taxi. Grisha picks up a Russian girl (the nurse of his brain-damaged uber-boss, Zilber) named Xenia and she begs him to take her to Club Odessa - enemy territory. He does, it does not go well, and he is further humiliated by Biba himself. He vows masculine revenge.

    So we're primed for two parallel stories of misbegotten action, and we cut back and forth between them for the rest of the evening. Independently, Ilya and Grisha set the course for catastrophic war in Brighton Beach.

    Grisha and Misha have it out and end up pals again, after Misha agrees to be the wheel man for Grisha's next foolish scheme. Grisha ejects all but two rounds from his Glock as they pull up around the corner from the Odessa.

    Ilya enlists Yakov - the group's only true soldier - to break into Biba's house and assassinate him when he returns that morning. They break into his palatial house and set up in his living room.

    Grisha, under a full moon, rounds the corner and walks purposefully up to the two Organizatsiya thugs at the Odessa door who have caused him so much pain.

    Ilya hears a noise - a heavy footfall, then the racking of a pump shotgun. "Run!" he shouts to Yakov. Yakov bolts like a rabbit. There's a conflict, the producer utterly owns, and Yakov is blown through a window, nearly killed. It's Boris, the psychopathic brother of Biba, standing there in his underwear with flat affect going on, like the wood chipper guy in Fargo.

    Grisha says not a word. Two head shots, one in each of the two soldiers. They drop like slaughtered beasts. One is dead and the other nearly so.

    Ilya and Boris face off. Boris sizes him up and realizes Ilya is soft, no threat, he doesn't have the sand to shoot him. He smiles. Ilya raises his gun, hand shaking, and sort of accidentally shoots Boris in the face. He blinds him, shatters a cheekbone, and gets the fuck out of there, taking Yakov to the hospital.

    So the war is on. Ilya's made his choice (he never really had one). So has Grisha, who is now totally out of control.

    When Ilya gets home, he finds out his uncle (Lavrov, played by Joel in a brilliant and note-perfect turn) was visited by Leo Slutsky, who told him they were lovers, and who was beaten within an inch of his life by the rabidly homophobic ex-mobster. Things are utterly fucked up and it's going great.

    \n Some PTA notes - we had a conversation about supporting the screen presence 3 people before play and it really helped - almost every scene was about these two guys and the world of shit they were creating for themselves.

    It turns out we've been playing slightly wrong! I've been adding audience pool chips for all of my cards, including the free one, not just budget I spend. So they've been awash in fan mail.

    We've also been very loose about scene framing - I've been treating the producer (myself) like another participant rather than the only guy to set up scenes based on player input. It works fine with our group to distribute that authority a bit.

    We've also resolved ties wrong (most hearts wins, if you want to do it by the book), but I don't see what difference it makes.

    \n Last night we had another really kick-ass session, episode three. It was Lavrov's spotlight episode and the old man got worked over emotionally. He's 63; he's married to a nice American Jewish woman, he wants out of the game. So far he's stayed out.

    We started with a flashback - Moscow, 1966. Lavrov's Vor partner calls on him - an enemy has been released from the Zone and has already mutilated one of their confederates. He's too powerful to take down right away and they need to go into hiding. Lavrov's not buying it - he wants to go crush the guy head-on. We establish that he's a stone cold bad-ass who takes the code of the Vor very seriously. Joel, playing Lavrov, is scary intense.

    Flash ahead to 1990, St. Vincent's hospital, where Yakov lies with shotgun injuries to his posterior. There's a cop guarding his door but they know that won't stop Biba and the Oranizatsiya from coming to kill him. The war is on, and Zilber's mob - the formerly laughable Potato Bag Gang - is either going to step up and finish the job or they are going to all die, along with their families. To make it happen they need Lavrov, and there's a battle to persuade him to fight. Finally he agrees, fooling himself that it'll be "one last time". There's some awesome back and forth with Emile Puzyretsky, who mocks the old man and gets slapped for his trouble, and takes it. Things are tense.

    Meanwhile Misha is thinking ahead - organizing his little band of cabbies and mafiya couriers to keep an eye on the Organizatsiya and telegraph their moves, and getting access to an abandoned fun house on Coney Island. Grisha, who murdered a guy and maybe two the previous night, checks in with his police contact in the 60th precinct to see what's up - the cops are clueless but know there's some kind of Russian mob beef going on. They think the Italians killed the Odessa dudes. Ilya, humiliated and outed by his uncle, mans up and plays the hard guy, confronting Lavrov and getting a beat-down for his trouble. Things are tense. They need the whole crew if they are going to survive. First up - pulling Yakov out of the hospital.

    I set up a scene where Yakov, in pain, calls for a nurse. Too late he realizes she's talking to him in Russian - it is Xenia, Zilber's nurse, who has switched sides and is pumping him full of fentanyl for Biba and the Organizatsiya. When the protagonists get to St. Vincent's, he is already gone. They have a satisfying confrontation in public with Biba and his goons, and then vow to get Yakov back before a furious Biba tortures and murders him. Things are seriously crazy.

    Yakov wakes up to find Mickey Rourke at his side. Biba's brought the actor by as a final humiliation for Yakov, who hates the actor. Rourke thinks the Organizatsiya people are Yakov's friends, and there's a cool scene where Yakov begs him to alert Lavrov to his location before Biba carves him up with a linoleum knife. Mickey Rourke finally gets the hint and rushes to Club Rasputin (Zilber's home base) to alert the good guys. But the only guy there is Grisha, who hates him, and there's some more excellent interplay. Eventually they assemble and prepare to raid the hotel where Yakov is being kept.

    There's a big jump-cut series of scenes as they break into teams and bust into the hotel, commanded by Lavrov. Ilya proves his toughness by breaking an Organizatsiya thug's fingers one by one. Grisha goes apeshit on the room's guards, cycling his AK-47 full auto and killing a bystander. Two guys bust through a window and there's a blood-bath - Biba's shot in the head, the two thugs are killed, and Yakov, from his bed, executes Xenia as she begs for mercy. It was insane. Five dead in fifteen seconds. The game just tilted into very dark territory.

    Meanwhile Misha's got the getaway vehicles idling, and on the radio his couriers inform him that a bunch of Organizatsiya goons are headed for Lavrov's apartment on Mermaid Avenue. There's a fun chase as Misha leads the arriving cops away from Lavrov. Misha and Emile Puzyretsky end up arrested.

    Lavrov gets home in time to find two Organizatsiya thugs and a 60th precinct cop dousing his apartment with kerosene, his wife and two boys tied to a couch. The Issue conflict is clear - his family is in the balance. One outcome has his family safe and him deeply, permanently back in the game , and the other has him free and clear and burying everyone he ever loved. We fight it out and Joel wins. He wants to save the innocents. He beats the cop to death with his bare hands.

    So everything has gone utterly to hell, but they've basically dismantled the Organizatsiya. Nine dead, two crippled. Next week we'll find out whether Lavrov is taking Biba's place or not. On our "next on", we also saw Yakov back in Russia, meeting some important people, and an assistant DA handing someone a wire.

    \n Some game play notes - I abducted Yakov to provide some focus and tension. The players were sort of planning an ambush where they'd draw the bad guys to them, and I pre-empted that because it seemed too passive. I'm not sure if this was a good move in retrospect - it turned out fine, but it feels like I stole their thunder a little bit. Everybody was OK with it but it may have been some inelegant producing.

    It was really fun to see existing relationships called back and heightened. I kept looking at our massive R-map and just calling people in. Yakov's nemesis had to enter the scene where he was helpless. The fact that Mickey Rourke has bad blood with Grisha meant that of course Grisha was the only guy there when he showed up at the Rasputin. The Organizatsiya needed a nurse to dope Yakov? Of course they'd bought Xenia. In many ways PTA is an easy game to run.

    Looking ahead, one of the two guys that Grisha shot - the one who died - is best buddies with Monya Elson (his former bodyguard, actually), who is the baddest motherfucker in the Vor world and whose arrival in Brighton Beach will be the focus of season two. I didn't do anything - it's all on the R-map.

It doesn't help that mysterious forces within the District Attorney's office have been feeding false information to foment a war between the Organizatsiya and the Genovese crime family.

    One nice thing this season is that the protagonists are a bit more spread out - we've got a cop on the take as well as Vor at three different levels within the hierarchy, instead of a bunch of equals.

    We're trying to play season two by the rules as written, which has resulted in some confusing conflicts as we carefully parse the rules. It hasn't been bad, and they really are good rules that we drifted in counterproductive ways.

      After producing the first season, I approached the game as a player with an eye toward amplifying weak spots in the power structure and providing contrast. Since everyone was playing a guy who clung to the old ways to good effect, I created a protagonist who dismissed the old ways as outmoded nonsense. Since the recurring protagonists have no connection to their new boss, I played a guy who was his "left hand man". This contrast has provided a lot of dynamic tension and fun play. Last night was where it all got put on the table after weeks of low screen presence and vague hints.

      Another beautiful scene - last episode, the Organizatsiya had given up Yakov (Greg's protagonist) to the Italians as "payment" for a bad mistake we made that got some of their soldiers killed. Yakov's good friend Joey Pagano drove him out into the Pine Barrens to kill him. Nothing personal. We know Yakov can't really die but nobody knows how he's going to get out of it. A gun battle? A wrestling match? Turning Joey? They all sounded a little lame. So Scott buys into the scene and has his corrupt cop, Jimmy Millbrook, roll up in his Honda Civic. He's in deep with the Italians and knew it was going down, and also knows Yakov's been cast off by the Mafiya and can tell him stuff he needs to know. Millbrook wants him alive. So he says hey, gets out a ticket book, and writes them both pissant misdemeanor tickets, way out of his jurisdiction, for littering and a broken tail light. Fixing them together in time and space and saving Yakov for another day without any heroics. It was utterly sweet.

It defined both our character arcs - suddenly the old dog is ascendent and Ivan is a simpering nobody, working the edges, on the way out.       \n Shazam! Last night was the second-to-last episode, and crusty old conflicted Lavrov was in the spotlight. Joel plays this guy brilliantly - he's an old man, he's the original gangster who did twenty years in the Zone, he's tough as nails but exhausted, and he's deeply, deeply conflicted. He's broken the Vor code - he is married and he loves his estranged wife. And he wants out - that's his issue, with two season's worth of pressure building. Last week we tried to set up a crazy situation for him with our "Next Week On...":

      - Millbrook and his partner find surprises in Spoonflower's trunk as sirens approach
      - Nayfeld opening a case of cash for Misha
      - Tina talking to Misha about taking over for Lavrov
      - Judith driving a car with Nayfeld as her passenger
      - Igor the accountant going over the books with Lavrov - "I don't understand, we should be making ten times what we are!"
      - Yakov in a trial before the Vor leadership, including Lavrov

      So there was some juicy stuff to work with, as well as some scenes to help set up Misha's episode, the season's last, next week. Not all of this happened - the guy playing Millbrook had to bow out, so he was barely a factor tonight, which was a shame. We pimped him into a later scene, where he was tricked into helping Lavrov, the guy who murdered his partner in season one. The continuity and backstory that develops over multiple seasons is so satisfying!

      In the end we discovered that Lavrov had been playing the Organizatsiya, waiting for his moment - he'd siphoned millions to an account in the Caymans, and last night he pulled the trigger on his plan. He killed the accountant and made a run for it, and through an extraordinary series of conflicts, managed to leave town with his shaken wife Judith. The arch enemy of the entire series, Boris Nayfeld, was gunned down like a dog. We were all a little surprised that Lavrov lived. Next week he's screen presence 2, so he'll be back, and this time i think he's going down.

      Another interesting thing - Yakov was banished by the Vor tribunal. This is basically a death sentence, since pretty much every gangster on the east coast wants him dead. He's been frozen out, and is hiding. It occurred to me that his story would be a fantastic spin-off, where he goes to ground and runs a gas station somewhere in the midwest, builds a life, fights off assassins and repays old debts while maintaining a good-guy image. Sort of a cross between Burn Notice and A History of Violence.

      We had a surprising number of scenes where the Prodcuer beat everybody, which was a little unsatisfying. I think this was just random, though. It did lead to some weird and unexpected scenes, but also a few that felt sorta flat.
