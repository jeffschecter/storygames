Some obvious choices:

"One Hundred Years of Solitude," by Gabriel Garcia-Marquez
"Collected Stories," by Jorge Luis Borges
"Ulysses," by James Joyce (warning: secondary texts recommended; hardcore index: 8)
"White Noise," by Don DeLillo
"Wind-Up Bird Chronicle," by Haruyuki Murakami
"Man and Superman," by George Bernard Shaw (okay, fine, it's a play, not fiction)
"The Brothers Karamazov," by Fyodor Dostoevsky (warning: crazy Russians; hardcore index: 6)
"Collected Stories," by Mark Twain

    Here's some fiction I've enjoyed recently:

    A Hazard of New Fortunes by William Dean Howells - Howells's reputation has been on the wane for a while now, but I got as much (or more) pleasure out of this book as I've gotten from its more illustrious contemporaries. I guess I'd describe it as a pretty standard social novel/comedy of manners. It isn't quite satire - it's too generous and Howells is too sympathetic to his characters' flaws - but it is pretty sharp in its depiction of the late-19th Century New Yorkers connected by their involvement in a brand new, Atlantic-like literary magazine. I especially liked the "journalistic" parts of the novel: there's a long sequence at the beginning all about the struggle to find a suitable apartment in NYC that should be interesting to anyone who's had to brave that gauntlet more recently.

    The Coup by John Updike - I'm not sure if this is even in print anymore, but I found it pretty easily as a used bookstore. It is a weird one for Updike, about the ups-and-downs of a fictional African dictator. It suffers a little from the tendency of writers of that era (the 1970s) to drift into a kind of half-assed surrealism/fantasy, but in general it is engaging.

    Norwood and The Dog of the South by Charles Portis - Portis is best known for writing True Grit, but if you only know that novel from the John Wayne movie, these books might be a real surprise (as they were to me). They're both comic picaresques featuring a somewhat out-of-it, somewhat immature, low class Southern everyman hero. They're also both laugh-out-loud funny. They remind me a little bit of Charles Bukowski's novels.

    The Wishbones by Tom Perotta - This is my favorite of the "twenty-something dude belatedly coming of age" novels that I've read. It's pretty funny and touching and all that, but it also has some great scenes about the differences of making art for the public and making it for yourself that has some relevance to story-gaming.

    Men at Arms by Evelyn Waugh - Funny and sad and quietly vicious in the way it treats the patriotic British response to WWII and military life in general. It's weird to watch Waugh skewer all the pretentions surrounding WWII and its acts an an antidote to the nostalgia that nowadays comes with a lot of the writing about this "good war".

    I also really enjoyed and would recommend Gore Vidal's Burr, but I don't really have anything to say about it.

The protagonist kills a man as part of a feud at the start, and then knows in doing this that after a set period of days has elapsed he will be killed in turn himself to continue the feud. Brilliant stuff, very powerful.
