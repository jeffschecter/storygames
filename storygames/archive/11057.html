\n When our Thursday night game session was threatened by an unavailable GM, I brought out Shock: for the three of us that were able to make it.

We crafted a really great world, where humans shared the Earth with a sentient insect race (our shock). The bugs were generally kept on-par with humans, but they had a cyclical breeding cycle that had 1/3 of the population breeding every 3, 7, or 11 years. 5 years ago, however, was one of those synchronous years wherein the whole bug race was breeding, and so now, they outnumber humans by a fair factor (all of that minutiae about the bugs).

This led to (or, well, actually emerged from) our issues: pollution, food shortages, and racism. The bugs were very efficient users of resources, but when their numbers were so high, they tended to consume everything in sight and generate a lot of waste... and they are better-equipped to survive the potential coming starvation. So tensions are high between bug and human populations.

Anyway, the game went pretty smoothly (a few glitches came up as we tried to figure out which ends of our fulcra we wanted to emphasize; we ended up writing down the numbers that would "hit" each fulcrum option, and then when we tried to parse out what happened when someone landed exactly on their fulcrum). After everyone had had one go-round and a single conflict, though, everyone at the table lost focus.

Now, that may have been based on circumstance, and it may be just my group, but it's something I see every time we play a GM-less game: after we get through the setup and initial play-stages, we all start to drift away from the game. Is it because we don't have a GM reminding us that there's a game on, and constantly pointing back at the table? Does anyone else have this problem? Does anyone have a suggestion for correction?

    \n You know, I've played Shock three times now and each time it has had a very different flavor for me. When I played at Origins, we DID have a GM, but her role in it was very limited: she was mostly there to right down the minutiae and bring us back if we were "wandering too far afield" with a conflict. In the other two games, this was not case, but we still had a tendency to wander a bit.

    So, if anything, I think your players did what any of us do with a new toy: they played in the proscribed manner and then played with it in their manner. It just so happens that their manner managed to lose focus. It happens.

    There's an important and subtle thing: when your second turn comes around, your Antag asks, "What are you doing now?"

    \n

    What that does is, it brings you back to what happened last time. And for pete's sake, don't pull your punches as Antag, and don't punch too soon. Talk back and forth, play your characters, and when you see something as the Antag that the player really wants to happen, offer a difficult choice.

    \n Joshua: I think that might have worked if we'd ever gotten to the second turn, but by the time we had gone around the table once, everyone had already zoned out. I'm not sure it was a failing in the game, though. Everyone seemed happy with the game itself (other than the issue with the fulcra that I mentioned earlier). I noticed particular enjoyment in the collaborative part where the protag and antag square off, each adding details to a scene, trying to bring everything to a conflict.

    Peter: I think you've hit the nail on the head there. We probably treated the game a bit too much like a toy, and flicked all the switches and twisted all the knobs, but ultimately didn't interact with it as intended. That may well be common with a first-time-through. In trad games, we tend to make characters and then have them fight each other and use social systems against each other until we get a feel for the mechanics, and then let it sit for a while until someone gets an idea for a game.

    I suppose the problem is, with my group and small-press games, unless I bring them something that gets them excited from moment one, a game might never get a second chance.

    Reading these last few comments, though, and then parsing and editing my reply, I've come up with a plan. The next time I break out the Game With the Sexy Orange Cover, I'm going to explain the rules with a little twist: I'm going to tell everyone that when neither of their *tags is in a scene, it's their job to keep the scene moving and moving smoothly; basically what the GM did at Peter's Origins game.
