\nSigh, you think to yourself, as a weary gasp escapes your cracked lips. I am sick unto death of flames searing across the internet under words like "4e = MMO n00b!" and the inevitable reply of "I will gut your mother!"

Be content, tired traveler, for here you will find your rest, and a place to gird up your strength once again.

What I'm saying is, this obviously isn't a discussion of all that.

I got an epiphany while walking around my cold damp streets this even, and the epiphany is this:

I want to kit-bash a light setting and a layer of rules over 4e to play it like an MMO. You understand? They call it an MMO or they don't, but what if you were to try and replicate the experience (you know, as much as possible) of playing an MMO at the table? This would be a "beer and pretzels" RPG, with probably little real RP to speak of*. You would take 4e and do what 4e does best (sweet combat), while simulating the feel of an MMO by having mechanics for things like Instances, Raids, Mining/Gathering, etc.

the one mechanic I have in mind, that came along with the epiphany: Limit the number of letter-characters a player can use when naming his/her character, just like an MMO, so you get crazy-ass unpronounceable MMO names. Like, "This is a rule: Come up with a phrase that describes your character concept. You must somehow incorporate that phrase into a name for your character that is no more than 8 letters long."

Example: "Final Fantasy V is awesome! I want to play a bard. I name him SPNY_BRD."

Between this and Spelljammer come words I wasn't sure I'd ever say: I really want to play D&D 4e!

Thoughts? Reactions? Suggestions?


*So why am I posting this to Story Games? Well, even though the mechanics aren't there to support story, this idea is completely informed by all I've read on indie game design and a lot of ideas that get thrown around here. It just feels to me like this fits here. If it really doesn't, please take this down, Andy.

    \n Replace IC talk (and OC chatter) with an actual chat. Place a small whiteboard in the middle of the table, give each player a differently-colored pen, and communicate by writing.

    You'll still talk about mechanical and table things ("hand me the d12", "I rolled a 16"), the DM will describe things vocally, and the players will do the same for their PC's actions... so the table won't be silent at all. But people can neither speak to each other in their PC's voice, nor chat about off-topic things like: the rules require them to do those things in writing, instead.

    \n The ".HACK//SIGN" property that came out of Japan around 2003 was a phenomenal idea (it also was a crossover between manga, anime, video games and more; almost all of it was total crap, though, which proves that sometimes you *can* mess up a great idea... anime soundtrack is incredible, though, so that's a good thing):

    Everyone is playing an MMO in a kind of home virtual reality console, and has the ability to log in and out at will. The main characters (PCs: all or some of them) somehow lose the ability to log out. They're stuck in the virtual world, and have to uncover some secrets stored within the servers to eventually unlock the game and return to the real world.

    Basically, TRON meets WoW.

    Anyway, it adds an extra "wall" which I think is cool, plus an overarching goal to work towards as the campaign progresses.
