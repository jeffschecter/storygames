\n Hey!

I'm trying to write the second edition of Perfect right now, and finding certain procedural points hard to express without using gendered text.
(By that, I mean hard to write without saying "...then HE does this, and HIS stuff is affected, and HE says something.")

I'm fine with using "they" and "their" as a singular pronoun in some cases, but I find that it's confusing if it shows up a bunch of times in a single passage.
Similarly, I'm fine saying "the character" or "the player" in some cases, but it sounds super unelegant if I keep repeating that language OVER and OVER again.

Can you help me re-write the following passage to be ungendered, elegant and clear?\n
The leading player has a lot of leeway in framing the Crime Scene. He might choose to describe the backdrop first, and then introduce his character. He might start out with a monologue of the character's thoughts, and from there explain where the character is and what he is doing.
\nI might need help with future passages too!

    \n As a non-native I don't really have a stake in this English gender thing (I write standard English myself usually, not caring about the language gender issue), but were I in your stead, I'd consider language constructs that disallow using the third person singular pronoun a pretty crippling tack; I'd probably choose a new word for the concept, being that you obviously have need for such; game have lots of special terminology all the time anyway, so no big deal. In your case, your game being set in a fantasy Britain, you could just decide that there's this one word they use that's different, and it just happens to be a a genderless personal pronoun.

    Alternatively, I might decide that this time I'm writing English in which the third person pronoun can be implicit (like pronouns can in many languages), after which I'd just leave it intentionally out of everything. That'd be linguistically pretty elegant, I could imagine a version of English that worked like this. Like so: "The leading player has a lot of leeway in framing the Crime Scene. Might choose to describe the backdrop first, and then introduce their character. Might start out with a monologue of the character's thoughts, and from there explain where the character is and what they are doing."

    If my train of thought seems like it involves completely ignoring normative grammar, that's probably because I don't ascribe to that view of language and communication. Use whatever works, and that's that.

    1.
    Graham gives good advice, in both posts. Second person hides gender quite well, and is also appropriate for instructional texts. I might be inclined to trim even more words, though:\n
    As the leading player, you have a lot of leeway in framing the Crime Scene. You can describe the backdrop first, then introduce your character. Or, start with a monologue of the character's thoughts, and from there explain the situation.
    \nHis pluralization is also nice - you really are adressing mltiple people aren't you? As Rob points out, the main problem with using the singular "they," or "one," or even "he or she" is that it's OBVIOUS. People who are used to a certain style won't notice it, but everybody else will. Using only "he" makes you look lazy at best and mysogynistic at worst. Using "one" makes you look, yes, like a ponce. Using "he or she" kinda makes you look like you get payed by the word. Using "they" makes you look like you're trying, just not hard enough. Using whichever one you damn well feel like can make you look ballsy and independent -- or just inconsistent and flaky. Systematizing different usage (eg "he" for players, "she" for Storyteller) makes you look like you have a few dozen contributing writers. And they did, too!


    2.
    Eero's solution looks like vernacular. If it's the way you talk, it can work, if the style is consistent throughout the text. One of the goals of Standard English is to avoid vernacular, including dropping the subject (He/She/It/They/You) from the beginning of the sentence. After all, you wouldn't want to end up with this, would you:\n
    So, fuckin', the lead player, eh? He can, like, frame the Crime Scene however he wants, eh?

    Eero's solution looks like vernacular. If it's the way you talk, it can work, if the style is consistent throughout the text.     \n Hey Joe, what's the tone of your rules? Given your setting, it might be entirely appropriate to use the male pronoun throughout as a mark of a paternalistic society. On the other hand, maybe some anarchist got to your text as it was being prepared for print and changed every pronoun to female, even where obviously inappropriate. You could even include a note to that effect.

    I struggled a lot with this issue writing Principia. The rules have exactly one grammatically-incorrect-yet-gender-neutral "they" (and the editor's draft is in my inbox, so we'll see if he let it stand). I use second person when explaining procedures and slavishly rely on examples of play where the player and character gender are known. With all that, I still have instances of singular person ambiguous gender. In that case I flip the gender each time, but remain consistent within a single block of text: so the first ambiguous person is a "she", the next is a "he", and so on.

    My preferred solution would be to use Spivak throughout, but I don't have the guts: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spivak_pronoun.

    \n Sounds like you've come to a good list of compromises, Joe. You've surely got your answer already, but here's my 2 cents.

    I use they in my games, and only sometimes feel like I've had to work to reduce the use of pronouns in a sentence to avoid awkwardness. People use "they" quite often in conversation "What did they say?" etc. It feels good on the written page even when referring to someone in the singular. ("When it is a player's turn, they say what their character says and does.") Using he or she in that case seems odd to me now. We don't actually know what ones hypothetical gender would be, why should we use one over another?

    The times I don't use it are usually in examples. If a player or character is named and given gender, then I use he or she, just as James Brown said. "He or she" is of course a good fall back, and "one" is a useful tool.

    One can balance the relative awkwardness of the pronouns, and use what he or she may think is the best choice for the game, and the paragraph, and the sentence that they are writing at that time.
