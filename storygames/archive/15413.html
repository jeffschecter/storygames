    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Dragonmeet was good; it's more a shopping trip than a "play games" con, but well worth it if you're within spitting distance of London.

    This year has, for me, been quite lean in terms of small-press role-playing games and I'd already bought everything I'd wanted to come Dragonmeet. That freed up the day for pure play, and me and the rest of the gang from the London Indie RPG Meet ran a whack of small-press games and games on demand upstairs all day.

    I played Witch again in the morning, which is a Montsegur 1244/Love in the Time of Seid-style structured freeform game. Good times, and we played with two folk that were just wandering around looking for a game: they were great, and one of 'em hadn't even role-played before and he rocked it.

    I played Love in the Time of Seid in the late afternoon slot: I've played this plucky wee game 10 times this year and I love it. We had a corking game full of Stirring! Viking! Imagery! with white stags, heart-eating, a cultured and somewhat effete Earl played with great aplomb by Alex, a male heir being smothered at childbirth by a scheming Seidkona, and a withered King trying to do his best for his crumbling kingdom while his very body withered around him thanks to the poisons being poured into him by the Earl's ambitious son the Knight Tjor.

    (The LitToS session did raise some interesting conversation afterwards regarding expectations and Theme Guides which you can read here. I'll post here on Story Games about it once I've had a chance to think about it some more.)

    Cheers!

    Posted By: GB SteveI played nothing at Dragonmeet but I had an excellent 3 days catching up with Ken, Robin and other gaming friends, old and new. I announced the winner of my Menacing Missive postcard scenario competition (40 entries!) at Paul of Cthulhu's Yog-Meet and generally had a thoroughly enjoyable but exhausting time.
    \nYou also purchased a very excellent t-shirt...

    There were two slots - I was running Mark Majcher's 'First Impressions' as 'Dungeon Crawl Speed Dating.' It went down like a pork pie at a Bar-Mitzvah - no takers whatsoever. Another gentleman who runs a gaming group in a southern coastal town gave me his interpretation of this in no uncertain terms in that many gamers there were not very social (he used slightly more exotic language). However, I had a good chat with a few folk.

    I ran 'Dogs' in the afternoon. I wrote my own town and shall post on an alternative process for town generation later. We are having an interesting discussion on Lines and Veils on UK Roleplayers and my group ended up 'exorcising' a young woman with learning disabilites as she was 'a temptress.' I had based her on Remedios the Beauty in 100 Years of Solitude, a book so dull that I kind of wished they'd stayed in solitude.

    Didn't play anything but the best thing that happened to me? Well, there were two: the first was having a number of us from London Indie RPG running loads of different indie games and getting some good feedback from the players. Our six GMs were bedecked in glorious t-shirts and it was inspiring to be around such an enthusiastic bunch of people.

    The secondbest thing was going into the gents where some dad had taken his little girl into. She began to sing a song about peeing which was so funny I had to leave the bathroom and come back later.

    \n
    \n Seemed quieter than previous years, but that meant less of a scrum for game signups, so some positives there.

    We sold some games! Andy Kenrick had his new game of sinister courtly elves: Lost Days of Memories and Madness and I sold out of the limited edition (black & white cover - "original series") The Agency. So commercially pretty good too.

    I also ran a game of The Agency, where the players were caught in a breakdown of the containment facility for zombies, shook down an east end gangster and stopped a foreign spy escaping down the Thames with a mysterious briefcase.

    Apparently the interview of Ian Livingstone was also pretty cool.

    \n Dragonmeet was great fun, as it usually is! I didn't get to play or GM myself, although I gather games of Dead of Night were run regardless! I did release Lost Days of Memories & Madness, however, which was a Game Chef entry many years ago and I've finally finished.

    The absolute highlight was meeting Ian Livingstone, the founder of, well, the UK games industry (having founded Games Workshop, Fighting Fantasy, White Dwarf, Eidos and so on), who was delightful and said some nice things to me about my own time at White Dwarf. Definitely a meeting your hero moment and not being disappointed.

    \n I got to play DramaSystem run by Robin, which really does do a GUMSHOE on character interaction and drama in roleplaying games in a simple and, only in retrospect, obvious way. I enjoyed erudite mockery from Ken and Robin, met customers in the flesh and did a seminar and interviews. We also decided on some of next years' releases. I also learned that two freelancers were worried that I was annoyed with them (not in any way), in one case imagining me stroking a white cat when I emailed "I know I can rely on you." This is because of a terse style of email communication, which I will endeavour to rectify. Some suggested haikus in advance of the text body.
