    Paul : HQRP is a somewhat traditional game where you play more-or-less normal people in a low fantasy world dealing with the stuff heroes usually handle. Its structure and system are very, very light, so much that there is no need for numbers on a character sheet (something I really enjoy for some reason). The structure and procedures of play are (as far as I understood) Old-School, meaning you can run it however the hell you want with easy prep (I improvised a little dungeon from the random tables provided in the GM section during chargen, as advised, and it went perfectly well).

    I'm not super good at explaining games, and two or three articles here would give you a significantly less awkward run-down on it, so I'll just focus on what I love and stands out for me :

    You play regular people. It's as close as it gets to playing 0-level characters in classic D&D without all the (war)gamey trappings of the original game. I like this, because I'm a firm believer that heroes are made, not born (that is, for trad gaming). I like that there are no stats at all on a sheet, only a bunch of words. I like the bestiary which also has no numbers, only a short description for each (classic) monster and a few considerations that the GM uses in play (like "take prisoners" for kobolds or "always hungry" for gobblies(sic)). To me, it really feels like the 1974 version of D&D, including the openness to interpretation of certain sections, with far less cross referencing and brain-memory required to use in a variety of contexts.

    Also it has a particularly clear and nice writing style, with a few humorous touches there and there that make it super easy to get into. I can only suggest you read it and see for yourself as it is quite short, and a far better advocate of itself than I am.

    Other iterations of HQRP on the Internet
    -Someone made a Choose-your-own-adventure type of thing on 4chan's new Quests boards, but it fell flat quite early. It used HQRP.
    -I tried to raise interest there in the traditional games section, but again, no success.

    \n Thanks for the love for the game!

    It's very explicitly a simpler version of Moldvay-edition D&D. At the time I wrote it (I want to say ... 2008?) I wanted to bring my love of Moldvay D&D to the Story Games crowd, so I wrote HQRPG as a bridge game. Then Dungeon World (which is an utterly different game, but does a similar thing with presenting D&D to the Story Games crowd) beat me to press, and did a better job at that, so I never really took the game anywhere.

    The name is because, when I wrote the game, I was living in Taiwan, and one of the main mild brands is "High Quality Milk." I liked the straightforward braggadocio of that name, so I stole it.

    \n Wow, it's been a while since I've had to answer these questions.

    In combat, if you're not in shock, you always roll two dice. You can roll them in the following arrangements:
    * One attack, one defense.
    * One action, one defense.
    * Two defense
    * Two action (requires Focused)
    * Two attack (requires Fearless)

    When you roll two dice for the same thing, you take whichever one is better, which is probably unclear.

    The other bonuses in combat aren't dice, they're adding one to the die roll. So if your armor is better than my weapon, you get a +1 on whatever your defensive die rolled.

    \n I honestly never thought about the initiative problem. Raisin's solution seems to work, to me.

    I actually wrote a mass combat system for the game! Since I'm probably never going to publish the expanded text, here it is. It's untested.

    Mass Combat for HQRPG

    Mass combat is any combat situation in which there are organized units of troops fighting each other, in sizes too large to be dealt with via the standard combat system. Each turn is divided into leadership and action phases.

    First, leadership. The leaders on each side announce their orders for the unit that they lead, then must make a roll to determine whether the unit can carry through with the orders. Difficulties as below.

    Difficult: Simple orders which the unit has practiced. A trained commander can do this without rolling.
    Very Difficult: Simple orders which the unit has not practiced or drilled, complicated orders which they have practiced and drilled.
    Astoundingly Difficult: Complicated orders off the cuff, including any orders with a conditional involved (IF they attack you, then trigger the trap).

    If the commander fails the roll, the unit mills around and doesn't do anything. It cannot attack this turn, and automatically defends with a roll of 1.

    Then, we roll attack and defense, as per normal, with normal modifiers, as appropriate to the situation. Note that creatures which are "too large to effectively deal with" (such as a dragon or giant) may count as a unit for the purposes of mass combat. While we make these modifiers, we take into account the orders given.

    Any hits, roll damage to the unit as follows, with standard damage modifiers:
    1-3: Rattled
    4-5: Routed
    6: Destroyed

    A rattled unit gets -1 to its command roll next turn. If the command does not require a roll, then this does not matter.

    A routed unit is broken and devastated. It no longer functions as a unit unless the commander can rally them, an astoundingly difficult roll.

    A destroyed unit is beyond hope. Individuals may survive, but the unit is toast.

    Any individual in the unit that we care about (like a PC) should make a test to see if they take a hit. This is difficult in a rattled unit, very difficult in a routed unit, and astonishingly difficult in a destroyed unit. Appropriate traits (coward, veteran) can give bonuses to this. If they take a hit, resolve it as a normal hit.

    \n Y'all can credit me as "Mike," somewhere waaaay below Mr Lehman.

    Generally, I think it's easier to adjust the scope of our imaginings with the mechanics we have, rather than trying to adjust the mechanics to reflect the scope. If there's ever a "Potemkin Principal" it'll look something like that. Ha!

    HDRPG plays to this Occam's Razor business well; there's really not much I want to do with the system that warrants significant mechanical additions.

    I'm currently mulling over a draft for a Kids do Call of Cthulhu-style adventure using the HQRP. I don't need a "insanity system" over the top of HQRP: I can just make it a type of Injury that player's can receive for a failed roll, letting the fiction define the cause and severity of the madness.

    I maybe - maybe - would consider writing some new tables for the fighting rules to work out how a chase-sequence might work (because kids running from things or after them is a pretty central part of the genre), but that's really just altering the colour of the results, not the core 'danger that\u2019s time sensitive' mechanics.

    Oh, the project's working title - and you'll like this - is High Quality Spooky Town.
