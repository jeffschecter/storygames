\n ...And I don't mean from a game design standpoint, overconcerned with probabilities and bell-curves. I mean directly tactilely, what kind of dice do you prefer to roll? Do you prefer pips or numerals? Clear or opaque? Stylish colors or simple high-contrast black & white? Do you roll raw on the table top, or do use a square of cloth to silence the clatter? If you think of your dice as tools for a task, what simply works the best?

All the above conjecture came about because I'm shopping for a new set of d6's online, and a host of practical concerns began to occur to me (will it be hard to read that color scheme? Would frosted textured dice dice catch on my dice mat?) and I realized that almost no discussion of them exists.

    \n I love unusually tactile dice. Your standard Chessex or Koplow plastic is all well and good, but I prefer breaking the norm.

    I have a set of slightly-larger (~22mm, where standard Chessex size is ~16, I think) wooden pip d6s. They're light, and have a slightly rough, grainy feel against my hands.

    I have a set of Crystal Caste quartz dice. They're smaller than I would like, but when you pick them up from rest, they're slightly cool to the touch, which gives them an immediacy not present in plastic.

    I have 3 pip d6s made ouf of hematite. They have the same cool feeling as the quartz dice, but they're dense. You cannot ignore the heft of them in your palm, even though they're slightly smaller than Chessex standard.

    \n Responding to my own query;

    Back when I first was playing T&T, I picked up great handfuls of cheap wooden D6's at a toy store. The were fine at first, but the pip paint wore off after a few years, and they got lighter with age (by drying out, I assume) until they rolled too wildly for my tastes. I got those dice nearly two decades ago, and I still find them here and there in boxes and bookstacks.

    I used te prefer all my dice in a unified red-on-black color scheme (for shear gothy coolness), but my aging eyes don't appreciatte that much anymore, and I'm looking for clearer contrasts. I'm also developing a preference for old-fashioned cubes-with-pips. Again partly because they're easier to read than numerals, but also because they're so much cheaper, easier to find, and come in a wide variety. They also, frankly, just look classier than polyhedrals.

    I roll on a dice mat, actually just the velvet-lined lid of a fancy shoebox. I don't mind plastic, but I keep a look out for affordable glass, ivory or stone dice for the different feel. Problem is such are made more often as novelties than with function in mind, so I doubt their fairness.

    \n I love dice of all different sorts and kinds. When playing games I like numerals over pips, but can deal with pips if necessary. I waffle on my favorite sixes, but my favorite tens are in a box - they're almost neon in 3 colors, mostly-transparent. And if I need more than ten, there's 3 opaque glow in the darks to help out. And then 11 vampire: dark ages black-opaque dice on top of that (when I played riddle, the black dice were the "normal" dice, and the neon dice were the SAs, made it really easy to differentiate). I honestly don't use stuff other than 6s and 10s enough to have a favorite in those categories, although I prefer 4s with the numbers on the bottom to 4s with the numbers on the tips.

    On the other hand, I've vowed that every GenCon will end with me taking a big scoop from the 'random dice' pile at Chessex, so over time I'm going to wind up with an ever-growing bizarre and eclectic collection of dice.

    There are some things I don't like that are easy: barrel dice are the ugliest things in the world, especially 12s and 20s, and I try never to use them, though the barrel 4s are a bit tempting. Similarly, more I think about them, the more I find myself turned off by FUDGE dice - if a game uses FUDGE dice I'm significantly less likely to buy it (which is why I only have TSOY 1st edition). It's funny, 'cause if a game wants me to buy, like, 30-sided dice with alphabet letters on it, or some 5 and 7-sided weirdos, or a bingo set or some viking runes or sheepbones, or something, I'll run at it with gusto.

    \n I like d6s, d8s, d10s, and d20s. The d4 and the d12 are problematic for me -- 4-siders don't roll enough (they aren't round enough) and are a pain in the ass to read, whereas d12s roll too much (being too round) and tend to go flying off the table.

    I like numerals on everything but d6s. d6s should be white with black pips or red with white pips, and should come from those packs you can buy at the grocery store for 99 cents or just be scavenged from old board games for economy's sake. Quite frankly, if you bought a d6 at a gaming store or a specialty online retailer, you got ripped off. (E.T.Smith, take note! :-D)

    I like firm-not-sharp edges on d8s, d10s, and d20s, because I don't like having to chase them all over the place. When they roll, they should roll a few times and STOP, god damn it. Color and texture don't really matter much to me: there are some that I dislike and avoid buying (really sharp-edged gem dice, really pukey colors, etc.), but none that I prefer and seek out above all others.

    \n I wouldn't call myself a collector, nor a dice fetishist, but then again I have no excuse for the tins overflowing with dice I have around the house.

    Favourites are still my most basic Gamescience set: solid colour, opaque yellow with black numerals, making them easy to read and simple to re-ink with a permanent marker should the inking fade. The edges and corners are crisp and sharp, there is no obvious bias and they're very light in the hand. The single best type of die I've ever found is their Plus Twenty, which is a d20 numbered 0-9 twice except that half the numbers have a "+" in front of them. It's a very good compromise between a d10 and d20 and does the job of both, as I've always preferred the way that a twenty-sider rolls. After a couple of decades of use none of my Gamescience dice show any real sign of wear, which is more than can be said for some of the other polyhedrals I have.

    Then again, other people have told me that the sharp edges and light weight are not to their taste at all, which at least means that they don't keep borrowing my dice
