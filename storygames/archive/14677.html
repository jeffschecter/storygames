    \n This is basically the d66 that we discussed here not too long ago.

    If the order of the die matters (32 is different than 23), then you get 36 possible results, each of which is equally likely as any other. If the order doesn't matter, then you get 21 possible results, and double results (11, 22, 33, etc.) are twice as likely as any other. (The post is inconsistent about whether ordering matters or not.)

    The only other weird part is the "no dueling int he kitchen" described in the blog post. That's simple enough: each die only has a number once, so 5 can't be the top number (determining room type) and also the side number (determining wall or entrance). So events with a 5 are less common in that area (if order doesn't matter) or events starting with 5 are impossible (if order does matter).

    Note that on standard dice, opposite sides add up to 7, so category 2 is less common in 5 rooms, as well. And the layout of numbers will be relevant for determining results. Weird dice, low quality dice and Chinese style d6s with the big 1 pip will give some odd results.

    A careful design might be able to design around the opposite sides adding up to 7, to make corridors that go in straight lines for several rooms. If both category 3 and 4 are open corridors, then you'd see hallways that stretch for several squares at a time.


    If you're using two colors, you'd have to decide how the two colors interact. You could have white/white and red/red be the same, while white/red and red/white are a separate category, giving two possible lists of connections. Or you could have three: red/red, red/white and white/white (in this case, red/white is twice as common as all one color assuming equal quantities of both). Or possibly even four, if red/white and white/red are different.

    Strategic use of color distribution could also be used: there's no reason you need equal quantities of red and white dice. Maybe only a few rooms are red and most are white. Maybe a single black die is the villain's central lair.
