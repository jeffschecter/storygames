\n What part is the most fun? What part makes you feel excited, rather than obligated?

By "part" I mean activity; or, failing that, an immediate payoff (i.e. something you like now, not just the anticipation of liking something later in play).

    \n
    \n I like thinking about the opposition. When the last session ended, the PCs had done a bunch of things and had plans to go on and do even more things in pursuit of their goals. So I sit there and think about what this means to the NPCs in the setting: how much do they know about what the PCs did, how much do they suspect about what the PCs will do next, and what will their response to all of this be?

    I just really enjoy responding to what the players have put out there, and so the best part of pre-game prep for me is where I think about and refine that response.

    I plan out alternatives and build up little "kits" to use in the next game, so that when the PCs say that they go and confront an NPC, I know that she'll be at her nightclub and will have such-and-so resources at hand, and if it turns to violence, these lovestruck goons will be ready to fight on her behalf. I workshop little speeches and turns of phrase that I'd want to use with particular NPCs. I decide what NPCs are doing "off-screen" and think about at least three different ways that the PCs might learn about it. And then when I've done that, I go back over it a few more times, knocking out the stupid parts and making the good parts better, and when it's really gone well, I go into that next session with my brain fully wrapped around our game, ready to grab on to whatever the players throw at me and make fun things happen.

    That's where fun prep lies for me. I wouldn't give it up for anything.


    Boring prep, to me, is mapmaking and writing up detailed histories, because both of those really don't scratch my "the game is where the characters do things" itch. And I can occasionally get into a crafts project or putting together an in-character handout, but I don't think I really think of it as prep for the game...it's just a fun side project that I'm doing because I want to, not because I need to.

    \n I like GM prep a lot, when I have time for it.

    When I ran a regular D&D 3E game, I really liked doing adventure prep. If I had a 2-3 hours free one night, I'd get all my nerd books out on my desk in front of the window, flip through the monster books and Dungeon issues to get inspired, stat out bad-ass villains, sketch out maps, come up with short handouts printed in "old-ish medieval-ish" font, and so on.     Sadly, when I didn't have time to do that stuff, my games didn't go as well.

    So it's not a fun parts vs not-fun parts for me, it's just having the extra time to dedicate to it, something I rarely have anymore.

    \n Ooh GM prep! I love and hate it.

    Stuff I love, when I have time and am feeling inspired:

    * Tying together continuity, no matter how outrageously out of control various threads have gotten.
    * Dreaming up NPCs to challenge the characters.
    * Creating maps of NPCs, organizations and factions to help me visualize the game's social landscape. First person to call this "relationship mapping" and demand I bow down before Sorcerer gets punched in the throat.
    * Dreaming up rough sketches of challenging situations, by sussing out flags and weighing them against the style and preference of the player.
    * The various details and facts of a mystery. I've really been enjoying this element of running ToC lately. I think this is strongly tied to my first bullet.

    Stuff I hate hate hate:

    * Location mapping
    * Threat balancing
    * Figuring out how to beat a PC's stats/abilities
    * Statting out npcs and critters
    * Practicing speeches, set piece descriptions and any other canned content, which I mostly don't do any more but occasionally it pays dividends.

    \n For my bi-weekly Farscape using Spirit of the Century game I'm currently running, I've enjoyed:

    *Creating NPCs that tie into the characters interests, both friends and foes
    *Thinking out the political stances of all the factions in the game, and how they're shifting (I have about 15)
    *Following my formulaic weird shit of the week chart: 1 weird bit of technology, 1 new alien, and 1 disgusting bodily function per game
    *Finding images of sci-fi landscapes to use as my new setting/planet that game (thanks deviantart!)
    *Really getting a visual sense of what everything looks like in my head, then writing down keywords as notes to myself when describing said things to my players (Farscape is a really beautiful setting, costumes are important, colors and emotions vibrant and obvious)
    *Coming up with really silly ideas or clever story hooks, reacting to what the players have given me so far

    What I typically hate:

    *Statting up NPCs and dealing confusing system details because I need to know how much money the PCs earned or some such thing. Yuck.
    *Coming up with new ideas every 2 weeks! I don't hate this, but I find it difficult not to fall into cliche traps sometimes with my plots, due to lack of time to really consider it. I try to avoid this if I catch myself.

    \n Once, during a Big Important Session for an old D&D campaign, I went nuts with the prep. I had maps, NPC stats, notes for every eventuality, rumors, false leads, handouts, pictures of the Ghost Bear (of course there was a Ghost Bear), and so on.

    \n Looking for patterns, thus far I'm seeing:

    - prop creation (maps, handouts)
    - inspiration gathering (reading RPG books, internet searches, fiction)
    - getting into NPCs (motives, plans, portrayal)
    - creating bad-ass adversity (dungeons, monsters, encounters)
    - hooking the players (designing NPCs/situations to hit flags & expressed interests)
    - pattern-synthesizing (tying aspects of the gameworld together, making sense of it all)

    With fairly little enthusiasm for attaching numbers and mechanics to creations (esp. NPCs/monsters).

    I think requiring prop creation is too demanding. But the rest of this is looking like stuff I could see doing in a structured way to produce a cool adventure in under 30 minutes. I think a key would be to have the rulebook provide (a) setting-appropriate flavor for inspiration, (b) fictional elements to plug in, as well as perhaps (c) some stats/mechanics blocks to slap onto the GM's inspirations. Nothing revolutionary there. The key will be the structure; enabling those fun parts and minimizing any un-fun documentation etc.

    Flexibility may also be vital, as different GMs seem to like different parts of the above.

    This concludes your State of the Thread announcement. Please continue!

    \nIf anyone is interested in this option (props and short prep), there are some alternatives. For instance, handouts delivered with the product that must be interpreted by the players and/or GM to construct the scenario and/or play. Like a map with no names on it. What is it? What is that X in the middle all about? Are those mountains or symbols for a forming cavalry front?

    It's kind of like IaWA Oracles, but in prop form:

    "A mysterious letter, delivered signed and sealed in blood."

    Except instead of that sentence, you actually have the letter. And no one can look inside without taking the relevant in-game action that would open it (including the GM).

    Or, on the other end of the spectrum, maybe something like:

    "Here's a weird-looking cube, this long snake-like rubbery thing, and three cylinders. Assemble them in whatever manner you want. That's the layout of the dungeon."
