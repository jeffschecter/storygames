    \n Those of us involved in the playtests can't really divulge any details, as per our agreement with Reality Blurs.
    Structurally, it's pretty much a straight reskin of ApocWorld, with separate tracks of Health and Sanity. The playbooks involve moves that really help to tie them into the fiction, whether through contacts, contracts, employment, businesses, etc., but without being as involved as, say, gangs or followers in ApocWorld.
    I think the main differences come in how they slightly tweak the Hard Moves, giving descriptions that better fit a Lovecraftian setting. They also have a quick and easy method for making horrors on the fly.

    AND their Ebon Eaves setting is amazing for one-shots. I playtested it last Thursday, with two players relatively new to RPGs, and we had characters made in five minutes, they took another five minutes to make their choices on the Ebon Eaves checklist, and five more minutes for me to read over what they had selected so I could get it ready to play.

    ...in retrospect, I should've had the players fill out the checklist BEFORE making characters, but hey, live and learn.

    \n For me, these are really what hammer home the Lovecraftian feel of tremulus. I'm going to be as general as I can with the details, and if there's some questions about them, I'll try to be a little more specific without revealing any critical info.

    -You know how normally you can take debilities in ApocWorld to avoid killing blows? It's not always a given in tremulus. Depending on how the Keeper sets it, you might have that option, or you might have to deal with death as a constant threat. Also, if the Keeper allows it, you can do the same thing when your Sanity is at the breaking point, with mental disorders. Unlike debilities, which are permanent penalties, these either permanently affect your moves, or give you new moves, that involve the disorder itself and how it affects your character.

    -Rituals are BAD for you. Even if you have everything you need prepared, crossed the "t"s and dotted the "i"s, be expected to suffer. A lot. From my Call of Cthulhu experience, this is the way it should be, but in far too many other Lovecraftian games, it tends to be glossed over, ignored, or made too easy to overcome.

    -If your friends die or go insane, your Sanity will suffer. The closer a friend they are, the worse off you will be. This is something I haven't seen in other Lovecraftian games, at least not as specific as this is.

    -A simple framework is provided that helps people who may have trouble putting together a horror game. You pick a couple of options, a couple of threats, and put them into this framework, and you're ready to go. Nothing really pre-planned, but with helpful cues for turning it into a good, sanity-sapping story.

    -Horrors are easy to put together, based on the number of players. While they have plenty of stock Lovecraftian examples, they also encourage you to come up with your own terrible creatures, to keep everyone on their toes.

    -And again, the Ebon Eaves playset is amazing. Even if you don't use it as it is presented, it gives LOTS of great ideas for threats and twists that you can use in any game.

    \n Noofy,

    The basic moves are, granted, basic. They are tweaked, refined, and altered a tad for tremulus. A couple were added to take the system into a more investigatory direction. The special moves (and Lore move) each playbook offers is what puts them firmly into new territory.

    I had read Harper's article some time back, and I see the merit contained within the article, but I counter that gamers need an option like that even if they use it rarely, because anyone "in the real world" can try Take Control from time to time. It has been clearly refined and defined within the text, and multiple examples of all the moves are contained within tremulus.

    There is no general open your mind to the Mythos. It kinda comes when it comes. Whenever any character rolls a natural 12, they get a bit of Dark Insight where they earn a point of Lore and the Keeper may, at his whim, provide them with some insight or information (presented via revelation, vision, or what-have-you, as best suits the story). One of the playbooks, the Psychic, can try to tap into the weirdness, but that's a bit iffy (as you'd expect, right?).

    Trust determines your relationship with other characters, and the Help move has been bumped slightly to encourage cooperation (7-9 = +1 and expose yourself to danger, 10+ = +2 to help) but has a built in consequence to mitigate abuse (a miss can result in a loss of Trust or the Keeper holds one). The benefits of the Help move also serve to make a character feel very alone when they invariably go off to do something by themselves (as they miss the possibility of aid). People have found establishing the web of relationships forged by Trust to be simple and enjoyable and goes a long way in determining the group's dynamic. Trust can be lost relatively easily and is hard to gain (much as in real life). There are some options to establishing Trust. Each character has a number of Trust points equal to the number of characters at the beginning of a story. Four players, four points of Trust. Trust is then privately assigned to each character from 0 = Casual acquaintance, 1 = Friend, 2= Close Friend (trust with secrets), 3 = Closest friend (Trust with Life). Trust doesn't have to go both ways. No negative Trust by default (though some folks have tried it in playtest and it doesn't break things). When Trust is going to go beyond -3 or +3, the Trust resets, the character gains a point of Lore, and gains a secret from the trusted character.

    Weird fiction is not about a lot of character progression. It's more about solving mysteries and dealing with emergent threats. As such, XP has been discarded as a driver. The Keeper may opt to allow characters to advance at a story's end or whenever he feels the characters have made sufficient progress in a plot line (as in a particularly long story). Ultimately, finding information, discovering clues, and earning Lore are the big drivers of tremulus.

    There is not a chapter on converting adventures over, because the Framework system in place allows you to create an adventure outline in about 15-20 minutes which generates around 6-8 hours of play or you can use town generation system and start playing immediately. With the Framework system, you can easily port over traditional scenarios with little issue, but we are more interested in the stories you can create.

    Now, there are a lot of Cthulhu RPG games out there (I wrote Realms of Cthulhu a few years back for Savage Worlds), but I felt that a narratively focused game could explore some of those other, quieter spaces of Lovecraftian horror. When asked to describe tremulus in a nutshell, I say it's Twin Peaks meets Stephen King by way of HPL. Ebon Eaves (the playset town) is weird as are its people, and a slow burn approach provides a lot of tension and possibility. You can certainly ramp it up to more direct conflicts with cultists and Elder Gods and all that, and that's cool, but this is a more intimate experience for me.

    As far as rituals go, there are guidelines to handle those, certainly. And they are powered by Lore and the Trust dynamic comes into the equation wherein the person who knows the ritual can use Lore from other characters to aid him, but those other characters can only provide as much Lore as their Trust with the caster, so if a person is sitting on 3 Lore and has a Trust 1, he can only share one Lore. As imagined, this can make some situations a bit dicey, and the person who is most trusted may have to step up and learn the ritual (whether they want to or not).

    Regards,

    Sean

    Johnstone: It definitely works best for the slow burn. Extensive playtesting really allowed me to tweak Trust to its final state where people use it to drive the narrative and their relationships with one another.

    Noofy: Glad to hear it. Trust is used to establish a relationship web as in FATE or Fiasco. The players describe how the characters know each other, and how/why they trust each other. We had considered lists, but making it wide open allows for a lot more ownership on the part(s) of the players, as they get to choose the how and why. In play, this works out similarly to what you see in Fiasco as people determine how much Trust they put in the other characters privately, and then reveal it narratively in a round robin before game play actually begins. Once play is underway, people largely use the Trust value as a guide of whom they hang around and to color their interactions with other characters.

      \n Alright, I was already a backer for this game, but when I read the last update I got excited. Playsets sound awesome.\n
      What is a Playset?
      At its essence, a playset serves two functions. It provides a context within which to tell a story (the milieu) and serves to define the type of story being told (the framework).


      The milieu is defined by the playset itself, be it Ebon Eaves or The Frozen Wasteland. This gives us the general locale in broad, sweeping strokes. It anchors us. Certain particulars are brought center stage depending upon results found in the framework.

      The framework is created by answering a series of questions contained with each playset. These answers, in turn, serve to establish threads (1 per set of questions) that are combined to create a unique gaming experience based upon the interplay of the threads. There are other bits contained within the rules which can add even more variation to play.

      Within Ebon Eaves, there are two sets of seven questions which serve to define the local color of the town and its town lore. The players answer yes to three of seven questions from each group, creating over a thousand combinations. There is information that is player facing, and provided to them at the start of play, and there is secret information only the Keeper is privy to (which contains what\u2019s going on behind the scenes, as well as establishing the various hazards\u2014the individuals, entities, and obstacles which will be brought into play and operate at cross-purposes with the characters). Some elements are brought into play based upon character choices made before play (for example, if The Heir is in play, there is an ancestral home in the area).

      The playset can be used to create short stories or can be used once to create the foundational game space for an ongoing campaign. Because of its ease of use, this can make preparation before play non-existent. However, if a Keeper wants to take some additional time to flesh out the framework, they can certainly do so, and easily have something ready to go in under an hour or take their time to add more detail and potential complexities to the experience as suits the needs (and sensibilities) of their group.
