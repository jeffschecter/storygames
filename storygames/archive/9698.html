\n You know what I mean:

You're working on a new game idea, and you develop this interesting idea for a mechanic or other such game component. The game evolves beyond that point, and later you shed that component in favor of something that more closely fits the intended vision of your game. The game continues to evolve, you either complete it or shelf it for later, and that once-interesting component is now discarded.

But man, that was a cool idea. That component was nifty. Where did it go?


The Question:

What do you do with your discarded ideas? Do you throw them away like the true artiste you are? Do you catalog them obsessively because maybe, just maybe, you'll find a use for them later? Do you chuck them in a box and occasionally reach in to draw one out later, just for a bit of random inspiration? Do you paste them into greeting cards to mail to your other gamer friends, accompanied by fingers and letters of ransom?

    \n I tend to toss ISOLATED ideas I have onto my web site, where I can go back and ponder them from time to time.

    As for things that ebb and flow in the course of a development, I haven't yet cut something that didn't totally deserve it (and pissed on its grave), whether because it was some derivative sacred cow crap or just plain mathematically broken. I could, in theory, go back through older drafts and run diffs to find things cut, but it wouldn't be worth it in the long run.

    ASCII @HACK is an exception to this, as I've dropped so much that it's losing its original angle of being "Nethack for table top" and becoming too freeform-trait driven. I have yet to determine, however, what's busted with the original premises (I think it's in the math).

    I also tend to design simple and small and then add as necessary, so I do not have as much to cut--more often, it's "what have I missed" that I worry about, in blind playtests.

    \n My 2007 ashcan, Know Thyself, is pretty much in the Idea Graveyard. I took much of the interest in that game and poured it into working with Paul on Penny, and the lessons from it to other games -- if you're willing to buy "interest" and "lessons" as pieces reused.

    An early concept in Mythender of "the PCs never fail -- but the issue is if they succeed with or without cost" died a horrible death to make my game better. Turns out I squashed any sense of "interesting conflict" with that idea. I would later keep poking at that idea until I understood the missing piece: success or failure, but being about to turn a failure into a success with a great cost.

    I had this idea for a game about playing the end of the Nephallim called Flood, where when there was a conflict, everyone rolled a different color d20 -- and the highest got narration rights. I never tested it, but without more meat I'm pretty sure today that that would have sucked hard. Every now and then, I think about that game idea. I may one day come back to it.

    Three journals ago, I wrote down notes for a hermetic magic game dice mechanic thingy. I haven't found that journal, but I want to go back over those notes.\n

    \n I have tons of half games on my computer, with lots of mechanics I thought were interesting at the time. They're not undone because they were broken, but because I got bored. Or I started to hate them.

    However, I'm still becoming a better game designer with each game I tinker on, so all of them are crap. Each time I realize something new about game design, I find that I now hate the project I'm working on and I hate the old projects even more. Anything more than six months old is too sucky to be of any use to me.
