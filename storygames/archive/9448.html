\n
\nDogs in the Vineyard taught me how to let go as a GM and give the players total freedom.
\n
\nPlay Unsafe taught me how to get over myself as a player.
\n
\nDread (Jenga) taught me that sometimes simplicity and elegance can create some of the most engaging RPG experiences I've ever had. How if you pull the numbers back, you can get to the core of what drives characters. And how memorable a game can be when the system and fiction are closely united.
\n
\nGeiger Counter taught me how to pace and reveal monsters cinematically.
\n
\nBurning Wheel taught me what characters are really made of.
\n
\nBurning Empires taught me that non conflict scenes are just as important as conflict scenes.
\n
\nPrime Time Adventures taught me that player and character effectiveness can be completely separate.
\n
\nWith Great Power taught me that what you care about is as dangerous as what's trying to kill you!
\n
\nLand of Nodd taught me that consequences can be a part of success as much as they can be a part of failure.
\n
\nWhat have you learned?
\n
\nFrom which games?
\n
\nRock,

    \nContenders showed me games could be exciting because they were about ordinary people.

    Sorcerer reminded me that indie games need prep. Oh, the prep.

    Cold City showed me how infectious an author's enthusiasm could be.

    The World, The Flesh and The Devil showed me how a character's narrative success and a character's capabilities weren't the same thing. Pow, brainfry.

    Mage showed me flights of fancy and genuine adventure.

    \nCall of Cthulhu taught me never to fudge the dice - the players are getting what they knew they had coming.

    Puppetland taught me how to get out of my skin.

    Fiasco taught me that character monogamy and role monogamy are as arbitrary as what size dice you use.

    Everway taught me... something. I won't do it again. I wish I could say explicitly what "it" is. It did teach me fishing, to its credit.

    \nMarvel Superheroes showed me that making a character can be a blast.

    Call of Cthulhu taught me the limits of authenticity as a source of gaming fun, something Trail of Cthulhu incorporates nicely.

    Stormbringer taught me the fun of attaching prices and tragedy to escapist fantasies.

    Rifts taught me about buying toys and making your own game out of them.

    Cyberpunk 2020 character creation taught me about the synthesis of setting and character in creating an aesthetic for game fiction.

    Telvar taught me the wonder and tension of an un-rigged sandbox.

    Primetime Adventures taught me about priming ripe situations with group brainstorming, and about signalling the other players on how to make my character's life interesting.

    Sign in Stranger taught me that anyone can make a GM-like contribution given the right constraints and springboards.

    Don't Rest Your Head taught me to get over some mechanics-first thinking and still maintain strong contact with the fiction.

    I've probably learned the most from my own playtests.

    \nMuu taught me that role-playing games may be beautiful, meditative and totally free from conflict.
    Pervo taught me joyful and unfettered manipulation!
    Korus taught me the power of low-key and consistent NPC-work.

    Fabula gave me everything I know about classical rpgs, and revealed the power of ordinary games on ordinary people.

    Until we sink ... gave me the key to effective GM-less games.

    Primetime Adventures showed me how important theme-knowledge is in game-design.

    3:16 showed me elegant simplicity.

    Warhammer gave me grit.

    The nameless fantasy homebrew, of my friend Tore Stuedahl, opened my eyes, and made me love role-playing games.

    Thanks!
