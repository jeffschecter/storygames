    February 2007
    (Via the Book of Judd) - Looks like there's an upcoming ALA TechSource Gaming, Learning, and Libraries Symposium for all you librarians out there.


    The potential topics of interest include:

    * Game creation/development/learning opportunities that libraries are offering or planning to offer
    * Community partnerships and cooperation
    * Recommended games and implementations for specific age groups (especially middle schoolers)
    * Creating staff buy-in
    * Hosting events where kids bring their own handheld gaming devices (such as Playstation Portables or Nintendo DS Lites); using handhelds for networked/internet gaming
    * Tips, tricks, best practices for using online game rental sites (either for online games or games through the mail)
    * Best practices for doing a grand systemwide or statewide tournament where events are held at individual libraries then compiled to find the winner?
    * Creating board games or video games for libraries to use
    * Teaching information literacy through gaming

    But wait, there's more:

    Malcom says:

    "This is the only class metaphor I've read where the plucky self-starting "working class" folks on the continuum engage in more worker exploitation and have more money before business than the middle class. Generally, the dudes trying to "fight the power" pay art, layout and (when it comes up) writing awful wages, throw money at projects and then measure all profit by the gross (because their net would be negative an is paid for out of pockey with other money), and are fighting against the apparent tyranny of people struggling to make and pay the bare minimum honest wage."

    and

    "I think part of the problem is that there are people out there who are willing to work for any price. A lot of small companies wouldn't even exist if not for this. Is it exploitation when the exploited party volunteers? I think so, since it really relies on the exploitee's sense of powerlessness.

    The other aspect of it isn't really bad ethics so much as vanity business combined with hubris.

    Right now my own business is very . . . humble, because I have to work within the restrictions imposed by my business plan and my ethics. I'm sitting on 144 pages that I can't do anything with until I can fund it. If I just pretended my gross was found money and poured it all in, I'd have had a print release last year. If I felt right about hiring the guys who wanted to do (good) art for under 20 bucks a 1/4 page, I'd have a very *pretty* book. I supoose I'm a touch bitter about that."

    JD, I suspect that the also-rans and garage presses are exactly the same, except that one batch is attempting to perpetuate (or, apparently from the perspective of some, abuse or bottom feed off of) the traditional model of game production, sales, and play while the other is searching for, building, and taking advantage of new models.       Tycho from Penny Arcade shares the following (after his ping-pong smack talk):
      • That he plays boardgames, of the geeky well-designed "Euro" variety.
      • Microsoft is bringing a number of those games to their Live Arcade service.
      • His insights about how the Nintendo Wii controller has a unique potential for bringing "the tabletop experience" to electronic form.
      I mention it because (as I have said in the past) gamers - by which I mean gamers of the electronic variety - would find a lot to like in these games, because they are simply well-built systems which accept time as an input and produce fun. They have a delicious logic and a competitive thrill that strategy gamers especially might find irresistible. Microsoft needs games of this type badly, because as a strategy platform their hardware simply doesn't exist. These games need a presence on the other hardware though, and fast - the Wii especially, whose novel sensing and pointing apparatus might be used to retain the appealing physical elements a digital interpretation might lack. Dice rolling, tile placement, that kind of thing.

      I'm aware that admitting a deep desire to roll virtual dice makes me one of the biggest dorks ever and perhaps even a kind of dork sovereign.

      I'm baffled quite often myself.

      Two possibilities:
      1) "Playing D&D" is like saying "Using Kleenex". It's a brand name that's become the term for the broader category. This isn't entirely what's going on, but it's part of it.

      2) Learning to play D&D is work. Having mastered it and identifying it as the norm from which all other things in the category are measured, there's a sort of assumption that all things are equally hard to learn in that category. Going along with that, there appears ( to me) to be a general idea that there's no real point to learning anything else, since you can bend, fold, spindle, mutilate, mutate, and ignore D&D to be whatever you want it to be anyway.
