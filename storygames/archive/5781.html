Over in the thread ...in which I ream many RPGs John Harper said to me that he did not see any criticism there. I disagree. Everything there was criticism. Everything seems genuine and honest, save possibly for Mike comment about Polaris. That looked like an off-color joke to me. The dictionary says that criticism can be defined as the act of passing judgment on the merits of anything. You don't have to like it and it doesn't have to be valuable to you to be criticism.

\n

I mention the dictionary definition of criticism because I want to be on the same page as you when we talk about it's place here. If you want to specificially talk about the value of different forms of criticism, then that's cool with me. But please do not tell someone that a word doesn't mean something when it does. I would like to avoid dead-end semantic arguments.

\n

So, let's talk about criticism. Specifically, criticism of RPGs, both homebrew and studio built. Is it appropriate here at Story Games? Should there be a line drawn somewhere in the sand? Is one form of criticism ok here and another not? If so, which is which?

\n

Personally, I'd like to see any and all criticism up to name calling to be groovy here. I'm cool with you telling me that you think my game sucks. I don't care what part of my game you pick on. You could tell me that my art sucks, my layout sucks, my rules suck, my grammar sucks, or my subject material sucks. I just don't want to hear you telling me that I suck because of the game I wrote. That's too far in my book.

\n

I want this all laid out and in public. Mike just got bashed real bad for talking about games. He didn't even say that they were bad games. He was just pointing out some things that he saw as less than perfect. Which is the same thing I did. Which is the same thing that everyone there was doing. We were pointing out the imperfections we saw. Can we do that at Story Games? Or will every thread where someone wants to talk about the things they don't like get shut down by someone accusing them of ruining their day?

    \n So, six months ago, I wrote the following:

    So I'm going to start doing some game reviews. I'm going to start with Dogs.

    I'm doing Dogs first for several reasons. First, I've now been playing the game for years and have played dozens of sessions with dozens of different people, and have talked with a lot more people about the game. I feel I have a good handle on what the game does, how it does it, and what the strengths and weaknesses of the game are. I feel that I can really review the game critically and honestly, and have a good chance of being accurate and possibly even insightful in what I say.

    Second, its well known that I love Dogs. I've repeatedly gushed and raved about it, and so everyone knows about where I stand on it. I think it is a very strong game, and I want to talk about why that is in detailed terms -- sort of creating a bar for discussion. However, I also don't think it is a perfect game, and there are some issues with it that I will be talking about. This too is a bar for discussion.

    Which leads to the third reason: there are many ways in which the future reviews are going to build off of the Dog's review. I won't just be reviewing games in isolation, I will be comparing them to each other and talking about how they reflect, progress, or assume upon conventions and paradigms of game design. This is because, in my not humble opinion, reviews should be more that "do/do not buy" guides or lists of one liners and snark designed to show how clever the reviewer is or how in with the crowd and down with the hotness they are. Good reviews should help form a community of discourse that can help consumers find the right material, but also to help push and spur the community and designers. They should also give enough material for consumers to decide on their own about a game, rather than looking to the numerical score so as to rely on the judgment of others as to the report card grade of a game.

    I have to admit I've been spurred in this by several recent articles about book reviews and their import. From Salman Rushdie talking about the importance of book reviews with Stephen Colbert to my personal reflections about Walter Pater, to opinion pieces about what's gone wrong with reviews, there has been much entering my mind of late about the way that reviews and criticism, when done right, are actually constructive and promoting of a community. I do not think that the majority of RPG reviews in the past have come up to that level. Some have, and there are a small handful of people whose work I will be using as inspiration, but looking back on my own work in reviews in the past I'm disappointed. I want to do something better. Maybe I fall on my face, maybe not.

    So... first: Dogs. After that I'll try to do one or two a month. Beast Hunters and Polaris will probably be the next couple, with TSOY and Trollbabe coming close after.

    Now we all know I never actually finished that. My computer crashed. I cried for a month.

    But despite my personal, brutal, public failure -- I still stand behind that as a guide to what criticism is, can be, and can do. It also stand behind it as a guide to the fact that good criticism is not easy, and it is different than just saying good or bad things about games.

    Though hell, saying good and bad things about games is just honesty sometimes. And I've got no problem with honesty. You can do "honest first blush" in 15 seconds, but real, informed, reasoned criticism takes more than that.

    Seriously, check out the links above. They give some real food for thought about the place of criticism.

    \n I haven't read the original thread, and I'm 105% sure that I don't want to. If this is some sort of meta-discussion about that thread, then what I'm going to add here is pretty pointless.

    Of course, what I have to say is blindingly obvious: Of course criticism belongs at Story Games. In fact, FAR MORE criticism should be taking place here. And while that criticism can be constructive or destructive (I know that I've benefited from some very harsh criticism) - it also needs to be grounded in positive intent.

    We bandy about the whole "dirty hippie" thing a bit tongue-in-cheek, but as with anything there's a core of truth in it as well. I have a growing sense of the Mr.-Rogering of the community - if you can't say something nice, don't say anything at all; as long as whatever you're doing is making you happy, it's a good game.

    Neither of these things is a positive for the movement as a whole. Good design is about melting your ideas down and assaying the truly precious stuff. The whole idea that so long as you're enthusiastic about your project you'll get a pat on the back and a little plastic trophy is the fastest road to mediocrity I can think of.

    The indie scene has always been big on support, but there's also a growing need for honesty as well. And being honest sometimes means bruising someone's enthusiasm a bit.
