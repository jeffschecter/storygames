    \nSexism in General

    (As Brand said, above) I don't see that sexism is pervasive in Japanese RPGs any more than in US RPGs. That is to say, there's a lot. There really is a lot. Seriously. And no one with eyes and common sense will deny this.
    There's about as much sexism as anywhere else, though.
    I've actually seen far more flagrant, titilating guys-only cheesecake in German and French RPGs than I have in English and Japanese games combined, for instance (well, ok, maybe if you remove from memory the "Avalanche Press d20 line of Squatting Hot Chicks" from the Western camp).

    The other interesting thing is that there are far more women involved in RPG writing and production in Japan than the US. Like, it's almost 50-50 depending on what organization you look at: The folks that did Meikyuu Kingdom have 3 female writers/artists/staff and 2-3 males IIRC. The largest RPG company is "Group SNE" (#2 in rank of RPGs only, but they also produce lots of board and card games which pushes their published numbers up higher). Here's a picture I took of their office last year:



    This shows most of the staff of group SNE: 3 men, 3 women. Not pictured: 2 other guys (including the president of the company), one other woman. The women in the white is Sanae-san, she's actually pretty famous for her series of replays she writes for various RPGs, "girl gamer columns" and the like. And Group SNE's works are not in the "offensive" list, but there are the usual pictures of strong, kick-ass women in piecemeal bikini armor and the like. In fact, one of Sanae's replays for an otherwise serious RPG called "Demon Parasite" is called "The Demon that Makes Your Clothes Fall Off!", basically featuring her and some of her gamer girlfriends tearing a game up into an anime hijinx game with plenty of anime-fanservice, complete with surprised almost naked chick on cover link to pic of that replay book from Sanae here.

    In general, though, most games feature about as much splatty fanservice as you would expect to see in a western RPG, which is incidentally far less than in your average TV anime these days. The art is surprisingly tame in general in comparison. I know this sounds like a gross generalization, but I'm staring at 4 shelves' worth of JRPGs now and only finding a few that I would put my finger on and say, "Yeah, as an adult I'd rather not be seen reading a book with this art from that page on it". Like 3, maybe (the cover of Beast Bind above is one of them, a supplement for another FEAR RPG by the maker of Tenra Bansho (more on that in a bit) and some of the hijinx drug-sex-violence art in Satasupe Remix (basically the RPG of a Guy Ritchie Movie).     On Maid: RPG

    I'll be blunt: Macho Hardcore Disfunctional Roleplayers from the Forge Fucking Ruin Maid. Period, full stop.

    Let me explain.

    (editing/localiztion issue with the first print run aside) The game has titilating material (random item table, among other things, features a vibrator in all but name as one of the hundreds of items listed). It has replays that feature some titilating acts (seduction between PCs, etc). However, normal game reports basically read out like a transcript of a game of Toon or Teenagers From Outer Space. Me and Ewen have Google Search Alerts that trip up when someone mentions the game, pointing us at various online blog posts, replays, chat logs from IRC, and so on. We scoured the internet for "touched in a bad place" play reports, even roaming through 4chan (English) and 2ch (Japanese, at least I did a few times) and found: Butkis. Nada. I sometimes see anonymous posts where people rail on about how a bunch of pervs somewhere did horrible stuff with it, but when I go through those chat logs waiting to see the paint peel, I see anonymous anime-lovers just playing normal games of Maid: Wacky hijinx, maybe some fanservice. I see some awesome sessions, I see some boring shit sessions where people quit playing, but I've yet to find this secret lair where people take Maid and play domination fantasies with it.

    I've had people tell me their experiences with the game, far and wide: Face to face at conventions, in private or through blog comments ("We played Maid last night!" "Hey, what happened in your game?" etc). The folks that are into anime play it "right". We are maids. The master is our master, and we serve him in some way. The undisclosed (UNNEEDED to be disclosed through even lines-and-veils discussions that are so popular in our circles with small press games before we get to the child-shootin' and animal-abusin') discussion of "the master's player (if a player, or the GM) isn't a dick to the players above and beyond the plot. the maid's players aren't dicks and will just simply try to follow the guidelines of the master's role unless the drama calls to shake it up."

    In all the few dozen games I've ran or played at this point (most of them in the last year), all goes well. Master issues orders like "make me lunch! take me to school! protect me from the ninja please!" The Maids run around trying to dodge bullets while cooking casseroles, deal with the scenarios threats (yakuza, wizards, monsters, whatever) and have some laughs. Normal anime-viewers *get this*. Normal anime-roleplayers *do not need this explained to them*.

    However, when I see someone who is into hardcore indie games give the book a whirl, I tend to have to brace my head from having my eyes roll out of their sockets. In our community, The Rules are important, and THEY, not the feel, setting, or advice of the game, are what Set the Feel of the Game. So put it into the hands of a hardcore Forge-style macho roleplayer, and what happens? Again, I've never seen the flood of base indecency that some people expect from a "pervy anime game", but I see some shocking shit that I never, ever see with /normal people/:

    * The game gets a lot darker. Quickly.
    * The master, if owned by a player, starts quickly "jumping to throat-ripping conflict" by issuing gut-wrenching orders that fuck up the lighthearted feel of the game.
    * The same starts to happen with the players.
    Since the rules have at their core a reward cycle of "players getting points for doing as told and pleasing the GM/master character", you can see the gears turn in the Macho Roleplayer's Eyes:
    "Aha! It's a game about Domination! I see it in the core of the game! Well, therefore we have to make a beeline to get at the core of this game. Dominate, Dominate, DOMINATE!": I have seen the first order issued in such a game from a player-master (female) "Give me a sponge bath!". We had The Lines And Veils Discussion right after.
    * "We can't have just silly antics, I need to take this shit to the Next Level to show how hard I can be! More adult themes, More in-your-face conflict!"
    * "I'll get in your face, but you can totally get in mine, too! That's how I like it! Hit me harder with your conflict!"
    * Then the game breaks, as the macho hardcore player forgets the social line between Maid and Master, and tries to make their Maid the master, the master the maid and so on (only fair: There is no number or rule attached to the role, snicker)

    I mean, seriously, it sounds like hyperbole, but I've seen this shit happen in three games/demos I ran (from quick demos to full-length sessions), for what I would call "Hardcore Macho Roleplayers who are into Intense Forge-style RPGs". If it was just once or twice, I'd think it was a fluke. But then I turn around and run a session at an Anime con or local RPG gathering, and it plays out again like an episode of a silly anime (ohhhh, /maybe/ with a single fanservice pantyshot or something), all without me having to explain lines and veils, without having to carefully state what is and isn't appropriate for an anime-themed game.

    I don't hate that it happened or anything, to each their own fun and all that. But if you look at the game Maid and say,
    "Huh, this looks like a lighthearted game"
    "This looks like a pervy game. I mean, seriously, MAIDS?"
    "This looks like a game I totally would not like"
    ...then I say to you, "thumbs up!"

    But if you look at the game and say,
    "This game has some mechanics about relationships and a core where the players get points for following the master's orders or pleasing the master, and optional rules for romance between PCs. Therefore, the game is all about dominance, in-your-face hardcore competitive relationships, and every moment that you play this game you are not invoking those rulesets you are wasting your time", then my response is
    "Oh, what's your handle at The Forge? I'm 'Andy Kitkowski' there. PM me".

    OK, now, the above was just an air-cleaner about what I see that kinda irritates me about Maid discussions that happen around S-G, RPGNet, and The Forge. I totally respect peoples opinions of "It's totally not my thing", "I just don't like the pervy elements that appear in the book", "I can't see much interest in it with me or my group", "I'm not into anime", "I'd rather spend my time on an adventure-themed anime-style RPG", etc. But I wanted to get beyond the whole "the set of rules within the game are all the game can possibly be about" stuff I see here and there. And I also totally welcome discussion on that matter.

    \n In Japanese "service" (saabisu) is a loanword referring to giving a customer something extra for free. A waitress might give you a free cup of coffee and say, "It's service." (\u30b5\u30fc\u30d3\u30b9\u3067\u3059\u3002) "Fanservice" is a thing from anime, basically stuff like panty shots and so on to titillate the viewers, usually included with little to no real justification in the storyline.

    A "replay" is a thing from Japanese tabletop RPGs where they provide a full transcript of a game session. Japanese RPGs often include these in rulebooks as examples, but they're also sold separately, both as introduction and entertainment. Beginners in Japan more often have to learn RPGs without help from other people, and it's ingrained in the hobby to the point where people read them as a matter of course. Maid RPG includes a whopping three replays (though in hindsight I don't think we really needed to include all of them), and Jason Morningstar's Fiasco is, as far as I know, the first Western RPG to explicitly include a replay.

    If you're very interested in this stuff, Andy and I did a two-part podcast (Part 1, Part 2) on Japanese TRPGs that's kind of an overall introduction.
