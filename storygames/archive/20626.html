\n What would that entail?

For the purposes of this thread, let us assume that Immersion is Fun, and something lots of players of RPGs really groove on. Let's also put aside other types of gaming fun that folks may shoot for, and just focus the conversation on supporting immersionist ( in character) fun.

Further, I'd like to focus on rules-lite design. I'd like to keep it there because I think there is ample evidence that people can achieve immersion even with heavy mechanical designs. I want to talk about the other end of things.

One of the things that I like which has come out of dirty-hippie-indie design is the concept of hard-coding things that otherwise tend to end up as advice or Best Practices in more mainstream designs.

Here's my example of something that might appear in a GM advice section of a more mainstream design:

To help your players really get into the setting and envision the scene, be sure to use more senses in your descriptions than just what the characters see.

Hardcoding it dirty-hippie style might look more like:
When a GM sets a scene, they mustuse at least two of the five senses when describing it.

Okay, that's my cheap and dirty example.

What are some of yours, and how could that fit into a rules lite/mechanically lite design, meant specifically to support Immersionist fun and do it easily?

    \n Basically it is very easy to support immersion, by not doing the things that tend to disrupt it.

    My crunchy mostly-trad game Soul's Calling (a work in progress) is built around the idea of supporting immersion. Many of the techniques it uses could easily be ported into a rules-light RPG. One of them is that before the game the GM gets everyone to close their eyes and envision being their characters, answering (privately, in their heads) various questions as their characters would, such as "what really matters to you right now?" and "why?". In this process each player is encouraged to privately identify and focus on a "golden motive", their PC's single core drive and burning desire.

    Another tip is that you can require each PC to have an identifiable voice and identifiable mannerisms, to help people get in the zone.

    My "trindie" game Implodarc (also a work in progress; willing playtesters please identify yourselves!) is intended to be rules-light (by my yardstick, which is still pretty crunchy to some people) and supportive of immersion for those who want it as well as other styles. The main thing is not having intrusive social mechanics. The game does have social mechanics, but they have been designed to work around immersion rather than disrupting it. Simply put, you roll before the interaction takes place, and the roll merely biases the GMing of the scenario. Because many people find it easier to maintain immersion when their knowledge mirrors that of their character and when they can be involved in the game at all times, I use the concept of the Deputy GM (in the Intro to the Implodarc book) so that you can split the players into two groups but keep both groups fully occupied, when the PCs are split as a group.

    \n Basically make a game that fades into the background and just lets you run theater of the mind style in character sessions. The less the players and GM notice the mechanics, the more immersive it will probably be. Having any kind of requirement, even the one in the OP, could potentially trip that up (because you always have to do it, even when it doesn't feel right or might be completely unnecessary). I tend to go very minimal when it comes to describing things, precisely because I don't want my players focusing on my words, I want them imagining things as immediately as they can. General advice I would give is the system should always give way to the realities of the table. If you have to shoehorn a mechanic in, it is more likely to interfere with immersion.

    \n Well, outside of "pure freeform" let's come up with some mechanics that can, potentially, help with immersion. As discussed in the other indie games thread, there are plenty of people (myself included) who don't care about the details of a very granular / specific skillset of my character - such as hacking a computer terminal or piloting a complex starship. What would pull me out of my immersion as my character is reminding myself (out of character) that I don't actually know what I'm doing.

    I want to say, confidently, that my character knows what she's doing and is capable of pulling it off, because they're a star-pilot-hacker. If I had to adequately describe or justify through out of character knowledge those abilities, then that would pull me out of the situation.

    The flip of that is the GM/group/self-adjudication part. Would my character be able to pull this off? Yes, no, maybe, etc. I think for a very rules light approach, you'd have essentially three bands of difficulty that the GM/group/self would decide upon:

    - Is it impossible for this character to fail at this specific action in this specific context? If yes, don't roll. If no, roll.

    - Is it possible for this character to fail, but also possible to succeed? If yes, roll.

    - Is it impossible for this character to succeed? If yes, don't roll. If no, roll.

    Basically you'd have a possibility spectrum - Impossible to Fail, Challenging, and Impossible to Succeed. The game would reside strictly in the "Challenging" band, if your specific character would always succeed at this particular task then there's no need to engage with a randomizer or specific mechanics, and the reverse is true also. It's just those finnicky middle parts, and that's where some interesting tension comes into play.

    Continuing this train of thought, a very simple system that employed some sort of Rank or Modifier for particular Skills or Action groups:

    - 1d6 + Mod
    If even rolling a 1 the character would always succeed, then don't roll.
    If even rolling a 6 the character could never succeed, then don't roll.
    Otherwise, roll.

    So you'd have mods say of -2 to +4, and Difficulty TNs of 1 to 11. A totally maxed out character might roll 1d6+4 for a particular action, so anything with a TN of 5 or less wouldn't necessitate a roll.

    Likewise a character with 1d6+1 could never accomplish a task with TN of 8 or more.

    I'm not sure this system above would be particularly fun, but I could see it working and essentially "getting out of the way" in a concrete, unobjectionable way. The only question would essentially be "What TN is this thing?"

    \n Here's a take on this off the top of my head:

    Choose an interesting, static fictional situation that has a low entry threshold. By "interesting" I mean something that is likely to compel people's imaginations and make them want to experience it. By "static" I mean that the overall situation depicted in the fiction is not prone to change of disruption; there may be activities, but they are part of the routine, not its end. By "low entry threshold" I mean that you need to be able to organize a gaming group with sufficient entry-level knowledge of the fiction you'll be playing without too much trouble.

    Once you've got something along these lines (think Houses of the Blooded, or whatever it is that floats your boat), the majority of the design work will concern the activities of the GM, who is the only player upon whom we may make actual demands of learning and conforming - everybody else will be allowed Immersion, which pretty much implies that they will not be useful participants in "running" the game in terms of learning and applying systems. Therefore the game will necessarily be built upon an auteuristic model of creative responsibility: the GM presents the players with the chance to immerse.

    The things we can design here fall into two important categories:
    * Analytical structuration of the setting material we chose earlier; there needs to be a game plan for how, what, when the GM is going to present to the players. What kinds of activities occur in the game? How often, in what order? What kinds of Color has to be delivered? All this material can be proffered for the GM in the form of structured lists and tables and even adventure design sheets and tables they can use to compose their own content and game plan for when they'll need to become the holodeck during the actual session of play. This is not mere setting design in the traditional sense of writing fiction; the fiction can also be structured for the GM's optimal use, and that's where the majority of the game design for this sort of thing lies.
    * Advice for how the GM should conduct himself during gameplay, perhaps with exercises they can use to improve their performance. What is conventionally considered "rules" falls into this category, but because we want to go rules-light, what we want to do is to not make the players worry about the rules. Rather, they will be rules of conduct and strategic initiatives for the GM; sort of like if Apocalypse World had the GM moves but not the player moves. These are things like "ask the player explicitly for a decision, so as to help them get in the mindset of their character".

    I think that with this recipe there is no particular reason why one couldn't accomplish a highly immersive, rules-light game. I've friends who play in this exact manner, though without any game-text support. The weak point here is trust in the GM, but that is a) something we can design for (in the second category of design above: e.g. start the game with some trust exercises or other didactics), and b) often enough caused by creative agenda conflict when a player is not satisfied by the creative contract of the game as presented here.

    I think that it is a shame that games in this mold have not been designed very often and very well. In fact, considering game texts, I think that something like Ars Magica beats the pants off most of the rules-light immersion games in their own game: it's got the appropriate type of static immersive setting, it's got the highly-supported princess play, many, many GMing tools and all - you could strip out all the dice and character sheet tomfoolery and still end up with a better rules-light immersion game than most of the actually dedicated texts I've seen.

    \n@Eero_Tuovinen, the game doesn't have to be immersive for all the players bar the GM; why can't it be immersive for all the players bar the GMing team? As I've said upthread, the Deputy GM is a key tool in making the game maximally immersive for the pure players. Sometimes this can be because for the sake of a player's own immersion each player is, as you suggest, allowed free reign - to the extent that that player is not required to help the other players' immersion. So you can have a table with five people not consciously contributing to anyone else's immersion, and only one person (the GM) consciously doing so; that's fine of course because the players are still subconsciously doing lots of things that tend to help each other's immersion, but it may be that bit better for those five players that you add a seventh person, the Deputy GM, who helps the GM in making the game immersive for the other players. I would even go so far as having two Deputy GMs and one GM for four pure players. This also helps make the game more inclusive for friends of yours who are not looking for immersion; the Deputy GM's toolkit is much the same as the archetypal storygamer's and quite similar to a GM's.

    @ other posters upthread, personally I find it counterimmersive if a significant action has an absolute certainty of failure or success with no possibility of the GM in his discretion deciding to require a roll - however I prefer to design mechanics that allow that chance of failure or success to be almost negligible (say, around 1 in 144 for some tests in Soul's Calling; harder to calculate for Implodarc but possibly a much smaller probability given how the dice "implode" in a complicated way), provided that there is at least some chance. And the GM always has discretion not to allow/require a roll. Rule Zero addresses this just fine.

    "Princess play" is a specific Simulationist technical strategy ("technical agenda" as it's sometimes called) wherein a player is entertained by developing and practicing an idealized fictional alter ego characterized by uniqueness, firm pre-play theming, protagonism and generally being interesting and exhilarating for the player to depict. "Character immersion" and "wish fulfillment" are relevant creative agenda concepts related to this focus of play.

      \nI'll try.

      Back when I started gaming as a kid in the early 1980s with D&D, we had a general attitude that while there was a small core set of rules that would almost always be used, most of the stuff in the book rules-system-wise were more like suggestions or recipes for the GM to use or not. If a GM thought a subsystem was fun at that moment, or if they weren't sure how to rule or what on-the-fly decision to make, they could always fall back on them. But they weren't rules in the sense that a board game has rules.

      To some degree, this attitude/approach was backed up by the myriad of Dragon ( and other gaming) magazine articles that would come out, offering different mechanics that could be plugged in and used over existing mechanics in the rule books. It was also backed up by the way modules would often have a small, specific set of rules for some encounter or aspect of the scenario the designer thought was important in that specific situation.

      So that attitude stayed with me ( and other gamer friends) even with other games, and even as we quickly drifted away from dungeon-bashing and more into play for the sake of play, play for the fun of just playing your characters approaches to gaming.

      As players, we didn't really need to know the rules, and often didn't really want to know them. We just relied on the GM's knowledge and skill and good judgement.

      You can't get much further along the lines of mechanics fading into the background than that!

      So that's where I was coming from with that comment about GM Fiat.

      And (mostly) it worked, which is what got me thinking about this thread topic.

      For me this boils down to:

      What's the most minimal we can get mechanically, while still hitting the following stuff?

      Enough stuff for players to dig into, to help visualize their characters and get started exploring them

      Behaviors of the GM to help the players envision the environment/setting that characters are in, to help feed back into the players enjoying playing their characters and immerse in both character and setting

      Enough info/mechanics/methods for GMs to fall back on when they aren't sure how to make a reasonable ruling consistent with the setting/genre expectations, but lite enough to mostly keep in their heads to prevent breakdown of immersion as they go searching for that info

      One thing I've noticed in many earlier discussions of immersionist supporting approaches was there seemed to be two rather distinct groups of players gong after immersion: The folks who really, really liked rules medium-to-heavy systems and the folks who would have gone with almost no rules at all if that had been an option.

      From what I could suss out, the Pro-Rules folks would have felt deeply uncomfortable without a whole lot of Phys-Sim style rules to create their characters, and put a whole, whole bunch of trust in GMs to follow the other rules of the game during play.

      Mind you, those same players didn't necessarily learn a whole lot of those other rules. Whether the GMs did or not s also open to question.

      I know I've been on both sides of the screen in those sorts of groups. I did have fun in them too, and also some horrible crap times that were immensely frustrating.

      Which seems a bit paradoxical to me. If the rules are mostly not that important in play, why are they there? They clearly have some importance in getting into the character and setting, but they don't seem all that important in play.

      In play, GM techniques seem to be vastly more important. More important than anything else, really.

      \n I'm finally catching up to this thread - and it's a good one!

      I also have my own take on the "paradox" you're describing: how is it that some people looking for immersion will play basically freeform, while others like to do so in a rules-heavy, simulation-based structure?

      I think immersion has to do largely with familiarity and trust.

      Sure, some things could be said to be inherently opposed to your sense of "immersion", like having to interrupt play on a regular basis. But beyond that, each player and each group seems to have different criteria for what benefits immersion and what doesn't - and sometimes these differences are subtle, but at other times they are glaring (like the rules-light/rules-heavy "paradox" mentioned above).

      I think that "immersion" in this context is a voluntary behaviour: what in other media is referred to as "willing suspension of disbelief". You are willing yourself to buy into an illusion and (however momentarily) accept something fictional as "real" enough that it makes you forget about reality - where you are, what you are doing, and so on.

      However, to be willing to do such a crazy thing, you must have a high level of trust in what you are doing, and who you are doing it with. You must also have some level of familiarity with how the things works, so you aren't constantly engaging in mental gymnastics just to figure what you're supposed to be doing just now.

      This explains the rules-heavy/rules-light dichotomy pretty well, in my opinion. Rules which are internalized and familiar seem to 'get out of the way', because they no longer require a great deal of mental energy to engage. We see this with, say, D&D players feeling distracted by Beliefs in Burning Wheel but completely fine with alignments. Or, similarly, being unwilling to engage with rules or mechanics for managing a group of employees, but having no hesitation about complex tracking of encumbrance or bizarre rules for combat situations (like abstract hit points, as a perfect example).

      The familiar can be brushed aside and relied on as a tool. Combine this with trust in the tool (and the people using it), and you're free to "immerse" as much as you want.

      Objectivity in the form of rules can help create trust - a sense of impartiality or realism - which can contribute to immersion, as well. Consider how much easier it is for you to "lose yourself" in a novel written by a well-liked author you've been admiring for a long time than it would be if your aunt handed you a text and said it was a book she wrote. You're working from a foundation of being willing to take the fictional as real, and this has a lot to do with your trust in the people involved behind crafting the fiction in the first place.

      Generally speaking, young children have a much lower bar for this level of trust, and can "immerse" fully in experiences like, "Hey, let's pretend we're rabbits on the grass." "Ok!"

      So, from this perspective, I would look for tools which help to create familiary and trust. If you've had bad experiences with freeform roleplay, you probably won't be able to immerse in that medium as easily - but adding a bunch of rules, however arcane, once familiar enough not to require constant thought, could increase your trust in the process enough to allow you to immerse yourself.

      Here's a technique I've been thinking about lately:

      Flowcharts.

      Creating flowcharts or sequence tables for GM use could be very handy for navigating certain types of roleplaying interactions. The player is not aware that the GM is doing this, necessarily, but in a situation where the GM feels like they would benefit from some procedural assistance, she places a token on a flowchart and then moves it in response to player actions, until the situation is resolved.

      This could help bring in things like improved social skills (for characters) or NPC dispositions without having to think about it constantly, and bring forward some of the helpful aspects of mechanized interactions without making it too overt.

      A PC is negotiating with a noble about something they want, and behind the screen, the GM places a token on a flowchart. Since the PC is supposed to be very charismatic, the GM starts the token on the left-hand side. As the conversation progresses, when the GM feels that a crossroads has been reached and the conversation needs to move forward, she looks down at the flowchart and moves further through the decision tree. Eventually, the token ends up at an ending point and we can move on.

      Essentially, I think the best purpose of GM-side mechanics is, much like for the player, to allow the GM to focus on the current state of the game - like playing an NPC - without having to worry too much about the next step. When some form of procedure helps handle the next step, the GM is freer to focus entirely on the current action and engage with it 100%. I think this kind of thing could be very useful, if it was easy enough to use, and it could help both the GM and the players increase their level of trust in the processes of play (if it worked well, of course).

      \n I'd say that if immersion is indeed the primary value, then you have to be willing to sacrifice the idea that NPCs might have mechanically-determined social influence over PCs; you just need to let that go and be willing to run with the idea that every character's social influence is whatever it happens to become out of the interactions you have with the PCs. In terms of the well-trod ground of "narrative control" this might even be seen as a progressive choice, as letting go of social rules leaves the issue genuinely up to free mutual roleplaying.

      At the most you could have some sort of a game mechanism where the NPC makes their rolls and that influences the way you describe the NPC to the players, so that you intentionally handicap them when they fail. This is so haphazard, however, that I personally would just run each NPC according to their nature and let the players determine with their gut whether they like and trust each one, or not. Whether the GM personally is a smooth operator or not isn't really important here, as people tend to calibrate their expectations; even a badly communicating GM will still have some NPCs that the players perceive as comparatively likeable, and some that they don't. It's not even that simple to try to predict which will be which, as anybody with gaming experience well knows; you might plan for this NPC to be a love interest and this one to be a hated villain, but for a logically structured immersive game you're simply not the boss of that.

      Personally I tend to value true to life social simulation so highly that I wouldn't let go of social rules lightly, but IMO it's pretty clear that for immersive purposes it is better to accept that the players are the egos of their own characters, and this means that gut instincts like trust or amusement or fear are theirs to determine and nobody else's. (As opposed to the arrangement in most of the games I play, in which the PCs are character super-egos, determining norms and goals, but not necessarily gut instincts - the primal nature of a PC is out of player control, just like people do not really control themselves.)

      \nDefinitely one thing I became aware of slowly over the course of my experience gaming was that social mechanics irked me for some reason I couldn't quite identify. It really took a while to figure out. I find a lot of people I talk with who value immersion, but certainly not all, have similar issues with these mechanics. That said social mechanics in games are so ubiquitous at this point, my approach has become more about learning to work around them (though in an ideal world, I wouldn't use them at all.

      I find social mechanics that function like knowledge skills don't bother me so much. It is when social mechanics supplant or interfere with the RP at the table that I get tripped up. So a social roll that undermines a specific line of questioning the player was doing where it is just obvious that he or she should have gotten somewhere, but the social skill mechanic leads to a completely different result.

A law created by the ordinary legislative process, according to the constitution, is not fiat; a king deciding upon a law with nobody being certain whether he has this authority, but nobody daring to naysay him because he is the one with the power, is fiat. But I do think these are important core principles if you are shooting for immersion, and they would be a good starting point. However I am wary of building up a list of procedures because immersion isn't about characterization or character sim. It is about just feeling like you are there as the character. At its heart it is letting go. Procedures are fine. Advice is fine. But I feel like folks here are in a rush to build up some kind of working vocabulary and system around immersion, but it is kind of missing much of the point. Again, perhaps I am misunderstanding the intention of the advice without knowing the full context.

I am not particularly worried if every has the 'right' experience at the table. I just want to make sure people have a good time and that those who like to immerse can do so.

I do agree it is nice when you can do more theater of the mind combat where the flow is natural and you are not listing off initiative numbers, but I've also found freeform initiative can be very hit or miss depending on the group. Generally I use initiative now just to keep things organized. I just think it is worth the trade off and in the end it isn't particularly harmful to immersion if you keep things moving.

      You can certainly make a game without an initiative system and that will work for the right crowd. I don't think it is a shortcoming to employ initiative though.

      In terms of my experience, yes, I've been playing since 86, so I have had time to find what works for me and figure out how to run games. But I don't think it has been about internalizing procedures. And I'd also only describe myself as an average GM. Really I think it is about just going with the natural ebb and flow of things, and keeping my eye focused on my goals as a GM. There is also just the natural skill you develop at running a game regularly over the course of time. You can try to analyze that and hand it to a new GM, but like anything that is gained through experience, they ultimately need to start with relatively simple instructions and develop the muscle memory over time through practice and use. And there is always the risk that you try to analyze the process, get it completely wrong or miss the point, and then give people advice that looks good on paper but doesn't work in practice (with gaming this is particularly common I think).

      I am generally wary when folks try to break down what they think is going on at the table. A lot of times, it seems like people are seeing what they want to see, and just overthinking it in general. Not saying don't do advice or procedures. I am just question the foundations being built here.

      For some players, having to ask questions about obvious things leads to their immersion suffering.       \nBeing a good GM for immersive freeform for noobs:

      This is just a first draft. It'll probably be unnecessarily skewed toward things I personally have done. Maybe we can weed those out and move toward more universal generalities later.

      The first things that come to mind are more about functional facilitation, rather than engaging narration.

      1) Maintain clear division of labor. You are the GM; they are exclusively playing their characters. Make the roles clear before play begins.
      - reframe attempted GMing as character searching -- if someone says, "I find a fire hose to spray them with!", instead of saying, "No you don't! There's no fire hose here!", you should instead say, "So you look for a fire hose..." and continue GMing

      2) Maintain character sovereignty. Don't ever tell anyone what their character thinks or does. Don't let anyone else tell anyone what their character thinks or does. Make this policy explicit. Refer back to it aloud as needed to rule out fictional elements like mind control.

      3) Facilitate player character unity. I don't mean that all the PCs must agree, but they do need to all be in the same place dealing with the same situation. (Cutting back and forth between different characters in different stories is possible, I guess, but I sure wouldn't screw with noobs' immersion by making them jump back and forth between in-character and audience.) No matter how well you interact with each player, the game will still get clunky if the players have no interest in interacting with each other. The easiest solution is to create characters that are all:
      a) up to the same endeavor
      b) close friends, relatives, or otherwise greatly invested in each other's welfare

      4) Make sure no one gets skipped. One player may perform an action that prompts you to respond, and they may respond with another action. Wait! Fictional time is passing here! And if other players want to act during that time, but can't because they couldn't get a word in edgewise, that's terrible for immersion. To avoid that, you have to:
      - pause the roll of any player who's monopolizing the action
      - check in on everyone you haven't heard from since the last fictional development

      5) Help turn others' thoughts into words. Some players find it easier to imagine than to share. This is fine, but a bare minimum level of sharing is still necessary for group fun. If you check in on a silent player and ask what their character is doing, and they say "just watching" or "waiting to see what happens", you do not need to drag an action out of them. However, if they say "nothing" or "oh, don't worry about it" or they just shrug, you should try to help guide them to at least the point where they say "waiting to see what happens". There are two ways I do this:
      a) suggestive prompts -- "Are you cautiously assessing the situation? Are you bewildered?" -- followed by a validating reaction ("Gotcha! Cool. We'll see what you determine shortly!") when they eventually say "yes"
      b) asking "What do the other characters see?" and then prompting as needed ("Do you look scared? Tensed for action? Total inscrutable poker face? Are you stock still, or moving in some way?") and responding as in (a)

      6) Treat the fiction like it's real. Unless you're taking a break, talk about fictional people and places and things as if they're real, and fictional events as things that actually happened or are happening. Convince yourself of this reality, and others will pick up on it.
      - if anyone else tries to use the fiction as a toy to overtly play with, such as narrating nonsensical character actions for the sake of humor, advise them against it, e.g., "that was funny, but if we do that again, no one will be able to envision this imaginary stuff as being real, so let's not; let's keep the jokes and the roleplaying separate"
      - note: players joking to each other about the game without warping the fiction is usually fine; don't police that unless you can see it ruining your or another player's ability to immerse

      \xa0
      I think maybe that's enough to play together? Probably worth noting that these responsibilities could be shared by the entire group, not just the GM. Might be good to go over them together before play begins, actually. So, finally, on to engaging GMing:

      7) Be a good tour guide. The players are visiting your world! Show them the highlights, and check to see if they have questions, and answer their questions.

      8) Only steer until someone else wants to. New players might be content to let you do all the steering, in which case you can feel free to unfold your world and plot in whatever way you think will be most striking. However, you cannot ever insist that things go that way. If the players want to explore a different direction, then you must embrace that. For me, this combo means:
      - don't prep or get overly attached to a vision
      - be ready to improv and talk a lot
      - feel free to follow your inspirations

      9) Fun trumps Likely. Don't be a probability simulator unless you really enjoy it. Do what you're enthusiastic about. If you get inspired to introduce some extremely unlikely outcome or event, don't censor yourself. Roleplaying is a conversation, and an enthusiastic conversationalist elevates the whole conversation.

      10) Say yes. When players are trying to immerse, the last thing you want them to worry about is how to succeed. Your "yes" doesn't have to always give the characters their hearts' desires; it just has to sustain the players' sense of agency.
      - if you can't see how a PC action could work, ask! "Do you think that could work? How?" Sometimes players will happily admit that a thing should fail, and in that case, you don't have to say Yes!
      - "yes, but now you have this other thing to deal with!" is totally fine
      - "yes, but the basic problem remains" is often fine as long as you don't overdo it
      - if you think a PC action was particularly clever or cool, run with that appreciation -- don't censor yourself from rewarding it "too much"
      - if your sense of the fiction truly dictates that you must say No, make sure that's fun, so the players don't learn to fear it (my go-to response: "That doesn't work, but it does produce [entertaining color] and in the process you notice [another way forward]"). If you get really good at this and your group embraces it, you can eventually move away from "say yes" if it serves some other purpose to do so.

      11) Always allow reaction time. If you want to give the players a new problem, or shut the door on an extant opportunity, go ahead, but announce the change before it's too late for them to do anything about it. "The door begins to close" is better than "the door closes".
      - sometimes the players will embrace the change instead of resisting. When they do resist, "yes, but the basic problem remains" is often your best response: "You've stopped the door from closing just yet, but the mechanism is grinding and the wall is trembling and before too long the whole thing may collapse into a pile of rubble! You've bought yourself some time, but not a ton. What do you do with it?"

      12) Ask, "What do you do?" If you give the players something to respond to, usually they'll just respond, but if they're ever unclear that this is their time to do that, prompt them.

      13) Be tonally consistent. If you run out of ideas, whatever jumps to mind next is probably fine, but just make sure you aren't ruining the established tone. Better to take a break than do that.

      14) Break before you burn out. A pause to recharge is always wiser than addled GMing.

      \n But you can actually do that in an immersive way, you know? That's what the Mountain Witch mechanics are for. Whenever a player asks about some detail you haven't thought of as a GM, you can turn the question back at them but ask them to answer in character.

      It goes like this:
      PC:I look as far up to the mountain as I can, what do I see?
      GM: You've been in this region before, you tell me. In fact, you can tell anyone in the group what you remember about this place.
      PC: Hmmm, okey, I stop for a moment and pointing at the top of the mountain I tell everyone the story of the Tower of Tim the mage, which now is in ruins because drinking and spellcasting never go well together...
      GM: Excellent! (rolls some tables and look up his notes, but is actually inventing something on the run)... and as the fog above the mountain clears a bit you do see the tower... though now it looks not just rebuilt, but brand-new, tall and magnificent.

      \n Alright, I'm gonna say something once suggested by the late and great Johan Rising: Good rules for supporting immersion are rules that bring your experiences and decisions as a player in line with those of your character. I have an example, too.

      Some time ago, playing Svart av kval, vit av lust, my diceless Vampire game, there was a scene between Dr. Braunschweig, a Frankenstein-like character who was a human but knew how to create vampires, and his creation Karel, a vampire. In the rules, it states bluntly that a human cannot win a conflict against a vampire (unless they're a vampire hunter). It cannot happen. If there's a conflict, the vampire simply wins.

      Now, this scene was pretty tense. Karel had gone behind his "master"'s back, and figured out the secret to making new vampires. Dr. Braunschweig was furious, and the scene escalated. Karel got cocky, then threatening. Dr. Braunschweig drew a gun and pointed at his creation. "Not one step closer, or I'll shoot!".

      At this moment, Gustav, playing Dr. Braunschweig, knew full well that he couldn't defeat Karel. It was impossible. His gun was completely irrelevant. Nothing he could do in this moment, as a player, could make him defeat Karel. All he could do was try to get out of there alive (and if Karel wanted to catch him, he would).

      Afterwards, Gustav told me how the rules had really helped him get into his character, aided immersion. Dr. Braunschweig was terrified, and knew that he didn't stand a chance against Karel. The rules made Gustav feel the same thing. Immersion.

      ---

      Rising's point about immersion was that rules could do more than just stay out of the way. They can aid immersion, as long as they're made the right way. If your character is thinking about how to outmaneuver your opponent, and you as a player are thinking about the same thing, that will aid immersion, according to his hypothesis. But if the rules are making you think about initiatives, special abilities and hitpoint management, that will take you out of immersion, since those aren't issues your character is facing.

      So, according to Rising (I'm unsure, but I think it's an interesting path to explore), make rules that make your IC and OOC perspectives merge. You can do it by making rules that follow the fiction, as in my example above, or by making fiction that follow the rules. I haven't played Freeport, but I get the impression that this game does that, by having the gauges and tokens present in the rules present in the fiction as well.

      \n Design for a rules-heavy, anti-immersion game:

      * boil everyone down to numerical attributes arranged on an unrealistic scale
      * have a fantasy setting
      * center the game around combat and magic
      * use a pulp-fiction style damage system like a number of points of damage you have to take before you can't fight anymore, instead of trying to imagine injuries
      * Make sure religion in the setting does not resemble any religion or spirituality likely to be experienced by the participants
      * Center the core, basic play around the exploration of unrealistic locations whose internal logic is that of a really cool fever dream
      * Attach a morality system that does not resemble any morality system that any person at the table is capable of embodying personally, or considering philosophically for more than thirty seconds,
