    \n Watching the movie a second time made me think how well it sets up a GM-less RPG. One person plays the Architect, who describes the scenery, but play happens inside of dreams, and it's the Dreamer who provides the opposition. You can have nested dreams, so in the middle of your dream, you enter another dream in which someone else plays the Dreamer. When you break out of that dream, you're back in your own dream.

    So, one person's the Architect, one or more people are Dreamers, and other players can just be involved. The more missions you go on, the better you get, but missions also leave you with Issues, and when things go wrong, your Issues might show up to try to kill you--whether it's your dream or not.

    \n I finally saw Inception. It was pretty good, I was entertained.

    Playing it with Lacuna could probably work. A few points:

    Luke's a bit off on hostile personalities and the Lacuna/Limbo connection. There are no HPs in Inception. Mal isn't extracted from another personality, and the only thing that is is information. And Limbo, as a fully shared space, seems closer to Blue City conceptually, than the Lacuna, but since Jared doesn't explain the nature of the Lacuna, that's really up to the GM/Control.

    I think John Powell's spot on. The pacing system in Inception seems to be the hostility of the dream's inhabitants to outsiders. The two pacing systems in Lacuna, heart rate and static, both do parts of that, so combining them would probably do the trick nicely. It seems like they go to deeper levels partly in order to buy more time, so perhaps going a level deeper allows you to "start again" with a lower heartrate/static so that you can continue to push your rolls, when you have hit max heart rate at a higher level.

    I don't think it's such a great match morally and ethically, though. Inception contends that if a woman is so unreasonable as to still believe she is right after a man has explained to her she is wrong, then it is perfectly acceptable to break into her mind and reprogram her brain without her knowledge or permission. The movie considers this action necessary and does not condemn or even challenge it, even though it does contend that the fallout from performing this action imperfectly can cause a lot of problems.

    Lacuna, on the other hand, is pretty clearly telling you that going into peoples' minds and "fixing" them is fucked-up and wrong and that it will lead to a lot more problems than some dude's wife killing herself or the possibility of him ending up trapped in a dream. Amusingly, the game gives characters much better motivation to believe they are doing the right thing than Inception does.

    So in conclusion, I think it would be more useful to use Inception as inspirational material for a Lacuna game than using Lacuna to play Inception: the RPG.

    Oh, and sorry for the necromancy if y'all are over this by now.

    • Seeing how players use Architect to come up with cool ideas to bend the physics in probable ways.
    • Seeing how players come up with a solution to kick themselves out of each tier of the dream world during the end game.

    \n So I've seen Inception again, this time on the watch for all the "tells" and I've come to the following conclusions.
    \n
    \n1) All the theories about the movie events not being real and pointing to the whole thing just being part of Cobb's dream indicating he's never left Limbo at all...nice theories but I think all of the "clues" that point to that are red herring meant to make things interesting. All of those supposed tells are actually completely explainable taking the movie on the level its presented without needing anything more elaborate.
    \n
    \n2) As such it would be a great fit for a Blowback Hack. You'd just need a way to nest several Analysis Operation cycles together. So you do the initial analysis and collect a bunch of dice. Then you design a seperate operation for each level of the dream. At some point in the operation of the first dream the step would kick off the next operation...and the entirety of that next operation would occur within the time period of the remaining steps of the previous operation. Dice from analysis could be allocated to any of the levels deeper than you where you are now or any of the steps of a shallower level that occur after you return to that level. That would allow for the dive into Limbo to save the mission after it had failed using dice generated in Level 4 to help recover from the failure in Level 3...and allow for dice generated in level 1 (oh shit he has unexpected defenses) to help with deeper adjustments (as with the "running with Mr Charlie" gambit in level 2).
