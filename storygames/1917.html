Do you really believe in game systems so strongly?

Frank T
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games
I have read and seen some things around Story Games, the Forge and generally the indie rpg scene that have got me wondering. There has been all this talking about "gaming the system hard", "narrativist game design" and the combination of both making awesome things happen. There was this discussion where Ron Edwards was telling people that yes, guess what, you can just give in a Dogs conflict because you like the other outcome better now, even though you were winning. And people go like: "Really? Wow, thanks for that insight!"

Some time ago I started a discussion on Vincent's blog about reward system, but then could not follow up for real life reasons. Still I am having a feeling that people who have been exposed to Forge theory for a while tend to overrate reward system. I have the feeling that some people check out a new game, identify the reward system and then drive for the reward as hard as they can, no matter what, expecting the game system to deliver great fictional output. Or if it's not the reward, then it's something else the system tells you to do. And if a system doesn't tell you what to do, it's broken. Maybe that impression is just dead wrong.

This goes along with the "play by the book" mantra and the "Rule Zero sucks" mantra. I think that Mike Holmes is taking a lead on this with the discussions he has started lately. I have seen more than one Forge-style game break because people were trying too hard to guess how the game was supposed to run, instead of just leaving it be and playing the way they liked. "The designer knows better than we do", seems to be the reasoning. And if it's not fun, well, at least we got someone to blame.

As you may have guessed by now, I do not find much sense and merit in thinking and acting that way. But hey, maybe I'm just seeing things that aren't there.

So, do you really believe in game systems so strongly? Do you really believe that a game should tell the players exactly what to do, should be played exactly as written, and should work better for you that way than any other? If the game is not fun after an hour, will you rather keep playing by the rules or quit, than just change the rules? Because, frankly, I am wondering why anybody would.

    Ok, before we enter into the argument, let me stress that phrase Shreyas quoted, and especially that bit about the system telling the players exactly what to do. The part with "telling people what to do" can come in very different guises, be abstract or conrete, rooted in the fiction or rooted in the interactions between the real people. It may be achieved through hard procedural rules ("frame a scene"), or through rewards ("you get a token if..."), or through some other nifty designer's toy.

    Argument starts now. I am not claiming that any of the above features of a game system are bad. Depending on how they are used, they can be great tools. But what I am claiming is that the idea of a game system making players behave in a certain way is problematic, and the idea that the main criterion for judging the quality of a game system should be how well it does this is just plain wrong.

    As with all discussion of such issues, it suffers from the fact that everyone is argueing from the very limited database of their own experience. Bear with me. In my experience, with any shared creative endeavor, and with role-playing especially, inspiration and opportunity are what sets creative energy free. Telling people what to do isn't. There is no such thing as a "creativity manual".

    So, a player turning to the game system to tell her what to do seems upside down to me. The game system should ask the player what she wants it to do. Give her some idea of what it can and cannot do, for sure. But the idea of the "perfectly oiled machine Forge game" that produces great fun play by just running the formula is, in my humble opinion, a myth and not an ideal to which to aspire.

      Being Theory-Talk

      I tend to use the "lumpley" version of system. That is why I didn't use the term in my post. The things I'm trying to relate are "how we play" and the game text. The former is more general than system, and the later is often confounded for system, but is something quite different.

      Technically, I call "how we play" play constraints, specifically the emerging regularities in play among the players. System is a part of this, specifically the play constraints that relate to credibility of fictional or imagined content.

      In relating "how we play" to the game text, it is essential to recognize that these are entirely different levels. In fact, they aren't even adjacent, the game text isn't read into the constraints, it's read into the content which is constrained by the players. The game text, the history of play, and many other things feed play on one side. On the other, the emergent constraints of play describe what is talked about and what is done.

      Game design is the art (and perhaps science) of constructing game texts so that they will cause specific constraints in play. I generally call this process, induction.

      Hence, a good game design describes the desired constraints, and if successfully induced through play to that level, will in fact play that way. A great game design takes the induction into account, and induces desired play constraints, even if those constraints are not those in the game text.

      End Theory-Talk

      Dropping back out of technical language, a great game design accounts for, or even exploits, the fact that players cannot play a RPG as written, and that "how they play" will naturally diverge from any method in written in the book.

      My position is that we should not be caught up in games that play as written, so much as using every tool at our disposal to make games that play well.

      - Mendel S.

      Technical Section Begins

      I see two distinct hypotheses running around, I will semi-arbitrarilly name them:

      Strong Interpretability - The game text can be understood and played arbitrarilly accurately. The only deviations occur from conscious decisions, including the decision to stop at some point in interpreting and move on to playing.

      Weak Interpretability - The game text does not impart an arbitrary understanding. After some point interpretation is more contigent on the reader than the text. In this case, the text is a participant, not an authority.

      With Strong Interpretability, the two answers to the question are consciously play the game correctly and consciously deviate from the text.

      With Weak Interpretability, the two answers to the question are unconsciously deviate from the text and consciously deviate from the text.

      Technical Section Ends

      Now, I'm on both sides of this wall. On one hand, I generally hold that you cannot play a specific text as accurately as you wish.

      On the other hand, I work with formal semantics, so I can see situations where a text can written so that it actually can be played as written. But I've never seen a game written that way, and frankly I doubt it would sell.

      So for practical purposes, I assume that no one (even the designer) can play a game text with arbitrary correctness.

      If you want some specific reasons why play differs, I direct you to an article I wrote a while ago: Planning for Failure. Pay special attention to the two ways of dealing with failure: Fixing it or Counting on it.

      As I've suggested above, part of designing RPGs is to accept that deviation will happen. And the challenge is to see them not as a burden, but as an opportunity.
