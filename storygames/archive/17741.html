    \n IPR is pretty much the go-to for selling books. They get to conventions, they have their online storefront, etc. I've sold a few copies of School Daze through them (5 or so) since they started stocking it in late August. I'd contact IPR again, as it's the holiday season, and your email may have just slipped notice. They've been good to work with in my (limited) experience. As well, I sell the PDF through DriveThruRPG, and if your PDF is set up properly (something Daniel can do if he knows to do it) you can do print-on-demand through DriveThru also.

    Other than that, I just talk about the game and ask others about it. Twitter, and G+ are my main sources. I have a Facebook account for my business, but it doesn't see much activity. If folks play your game, ask them to talk about it. See if a fan will run it for you at Origins, GenCon, Dexcon, Dreamtion, wherever.

    Start engaging with people (this post is a good example of doing so) and you'll be surprised at how your network grows.

    \n You can actually run the numbers on the print runs and make a rational choice on the risk vs. reward. It's not like the numbers on something like this are complete unknowns.

    Just research and approximate the following variables the best you can - the better your numbers, the better your prognosis.

    How many copies of the game are you going to sell over the next five years?
    How much would it cost to print this number of copies now, while you're fulfilling the kickstart?
    How much would it cost over time for you to print those later sales one by one as POD?
    How well can you afford to commit your capital in books for a couple of years?

    What a publisher normally does is that he tries to figure a conservative, reliable estimate of that first number (how many copies you're going to sell), and then prints e.g. 150% of that number on the premise that the last copies are cheap enough that you can cover their cost out of profits even if they never sell. The trick here is that your estimate really has to be the realistic conservative number, not a pie in the sky fantasy; tragedies are wrought when enterpreneurs prove unable to distinguish between the two.

    Furthermore, by comparing the cost of POD with the cost of the offset run you can find out how many copies you can actually throw away and still end up profitable by going offset. For example, if your offset costs per copy are 20% of the POD cost, this means that as long as you don't print five times as many books as you actually end up selling (that would be a massive mis-judgement, you'd end up sitting on 80% of your print run), the offset is going to be more profitable even if you overshoot your future sales. When you compare this efficiency factor with the reliability of your own estimate of sales, you can find out how large your print run needs to be so that you're almost guaranteed to do better than POD printing would.

    (This being said, don't forget the logistical costs involved in offset; for the above sort of calculation you need to distribute the warehousing and fulfillment expenses for your offset print run on a per copy basis, the same as the POD cost calculation does. Also, if you're really professional about your business, you'll factor in insurance: as long as you haven't sold those offset books, it's out of your own pocket if something happens to them.)

    My own opinion is that the only real reasons to not do a traditional print run are these three three:
    1. You're selectively nonfunctional as a human being, and somehow find yourself able to do business with a POD printer, but cannot do the same amount of work to establish a fulfillment arrangement for an offset print run. If it's simply too stressful and hard for you, while a POD service is easy, then what's a guy to do.
    2. You don't expect to sell more than 100 copies (or 200, whatever - the exact breakeven point depends on where you print and what you're printing, exactly) of your book within a reasonable time-frame. In this case it's simply cheaper to do POD, as the start-up costs of offset would eat away all of your profits.
    3. You're so poor that you simply can't invest the several hundred to several thousand rupees that printing several hundred books at once takes. Even if you know that your profits would be better with offset, but the cost of money is too much for you in your situation, then you're forced to use a publishing method that is less profitable.
    \nIt's probably not a surprise that I suspect POD to be more prevalent than it needs to be, nowadays. Of course every project is individual, but in my eyes the territory between "this project is too small to bother printing it, I'll just publish digitally" and "this project is big enough for a print run to make sense" is really narrow. I mean, if you're selling 200 copies, it's probably already wise to do a print run. And if you're just selling in the dozens, how much work were you planning to put into producing a paper book, anyway?

    \n I stock my stuff with Indie Press Revolution. If you make enough per-copy that you can afford to sell through distributors, there's no reason not to have a small stock sitting with them.

    I sell my PDFs through DriveThruRPG. I think it's a different market than my own site connects to, they only take 30% of your cover, and I don't see any reason not to sell through them. Once your PDF is listed, there's no work involved, and is basically like sporadic drops of free money.

    You could find someone living in the US or Europe to fulfill your international orders. Different arrangements exist, but you might end up paying them a monthly warehousing fee and then [cost + fee] to ship your orders for you. I think IPR also offers these services, separate from what they'd stock/sell normally. It's a good idea if you're worried about losing US/Euro customers to the expensive shipping. I'm Canadian, and I have a fulfillment person who takes care of all my non-Canadian orders. It's great.

    Once you've got a fulfillment person set up in the US, it's easy to spend an evening emailing US game stores and pitching your game to them.

    \n I suggest starting pretty conservatively. You don't want to end up with hundreds of copies just sitting around your house (especially since it will tie up a lot of your money). I would suggest printing just a few hundred extra (like 150-200) at first, since you say printing is very cheap (often times, we tell new designers to print just 100 or so at first). Sell those, see how long it takes you to sell them, and then reprint based on how many you think you can sell over the next year, again being pretty conservative. Then, each year, watch your monthly sales go down (as they typically do for older games, unless you plug into a major market) and recalibrate how many you order for the next year based on current sales and how much you expect them to continue to decrease. It's always better to be surprised by success (and have to reprint) than ending up with books you can't get rid of! Also, it gives you a chance to get used to being a publisher and dealing with any issues along the way.

    \n That's exactly the sort of particular situation I understood you to have, insofar as the numbers go. Jonathan's advice is good in general, but when you're doing a long print run anyway, it's no great burden to print a few hundred extra copies, even if you're a bit uncertain about the sales and inexperienced. $300 is nothing, you'll cover that with the first two dozen sales. Even if you end up printing 500 copies and only sell 50 of those, you're still making a profit. Maybe it's not quite as much profit as it would've been if you hadn't printed those excess copies, but then that's why it's called risk management. Use the extra copies for marketing if it seems that you have too many, give them out as prizes or whatever.

    (My overall tone in this thread is such that it's good to emphasize that Jonathan's rule of thumb "print a hundred and see how it goes" really is good as a a general principle for a beginning indie publisher - I wouldn't want to give the impression that generous print runs were a good idea all of a sudden, after all those years we've talked about having some sense in your publishing dreams. Kyle's situation is just a bit special, as he's already paying the offset press to print books that have been already preordered. At those prices it's just going to be a lot less hassle to print 500 rather than 100, even if it's going to take a few years to get rid of them.)

    \n My experience with IPR differs, but it's entirely possible that they've tightened their system lately (I haven't sent in new stock for a couple of years, so haven't had reason to notice it). It's still up to Jason Walters and his warehouse, ultimately, so it's worthwhile to ask, at least; it's entirely possible that Jason might allow a larger warehouse load for a foreigner who can't ship more books every quarter. For me, at least, the main reason for why I use IPR for states-side fulfillment is exactly because they've never had any trouble warehousing basically the entire print run of whatever I've done. And I'm not even an A-list seller, for those it'd be a no-brainer to take the books, what with the way IPR profit-sharing works. IPR doesn't quite work like a distributor in this regard, as they're more committed to the long tail, as befits an indie-focused warehousing/fulfillment/webstore service.

    Also, now that I think of it, if you need/want separate fulfillment for Europe, we can talk about that.
