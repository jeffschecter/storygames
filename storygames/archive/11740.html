\n Suppose you are playing a one-shot roleplaying game. For the sake of argument, let's say it is a swashbuckers and pirates game about swashbuckling and piracy. You have three or four hours to have as much fun as possible.

In this game each player gets handed a coupon book. Each coupon allows your character to do something meaningful and exciting. If you don't have a coupon, you don't get to do it. Some are very specific ("Kill a man") and some are very general ("Impress the crew") and some are meta ("Double coupon! Multiply another coupon's effect by two"). The game allows you to do boring, tedious things without a coupon, but otherwise - no coupon, no action.

What would you definitely want a coupon for? What would you want multiple coupons for?

Why did you choose those particular things?

How many total coupons do you think you need?

    \n An interesting game design puzzle, this. You see, the way this mechanic is phrased, it's not value-neutral; some agendas of play benefit more from this set-up than others. Specifically, it seems to me that a game built like this has to have a major hang-up about characters doing cool things; your task and enjoyment as player is to develop a static character identity which you're then expressing for enjoyment, fighting the other players for spotlight time - princess play, if I'm allowed some jargon. Just like the average Whitewolf game, really.

    From this viewpoint, I suppose I'd go for a mystery coupon. Something that allows me to imply a mysterious character heritage and back this up with ill-defined SFX. Those are always geek-cool characters, the ones that stand outside the story, smirk at human pain and swoop in at their pleasure to dictate the Real Truth about the situation. "Reveal a mysterious birthmark." could work in this regard in a swashbuckling genre, perhaps backed by "Prophecy comes true." Also, "Exit a problematic situation handily, accomplishing nothing but avoiding commitment or loss." I'm willing to bet that I could recontextualize a pretty long story with something like those to actually revolve around my character after the fact.

    Ultimately this seems a bit problematic as a game mechanism, if I'm allowed to critique. The coupons would have to be written in pretty clever ways to make them both flavourful and flexible, so that the game avoids going Once Upon a Time about avoiding situations where the other guy could "Impress the crew", for example. For a longer game cycle and a larger pool of coupons you'd also have to struggle with the issues of random access data processing; having a large booklet of coupons would potentially suck ass because you'd have to master a large dataset to make good choices. Could be ameliorated by only giving out half a dozen coupons per session, of course.

    \n Ha, I was just talking with John the other day about how I'm thinking about restructuring the conflict mechanics in Geiger Counter, which works similar to this.

    Basically, what I would suggest for something like this is to have a list of things that you can do, with a number of checkboxes next to them where you mark them off after you've done them. Maybe some things you can do twice, others once, and a few things (like basic attacks in 4E), you can do however many times you want.

    The main benefit I see from the coupons approach is the opportunity to trade coupons with other players, so I'd want that to be a big thing. If you need to kill some dude but don't have a coupon for it, I can trade you that coupon for something else. At that point, though, this might as well be a printed card game, where you have a hand of cards that let you do certain things.

    I agree with Gregor that having more than 5-7 coupons at a time is too many to keep track of. Also, it would make trades more complicated, unless there were specific rules like: you can give someone else a card (which you can't tell them what it is) and get a random draw from their hand.

    \n My wife and I ran a Firan-based LARP for our FiranMUX players at the annual minicon we used to host. All pre-gen characters, and each got 5-10 little cards that sound a lot like these coupons. There were almost no mechanics other than the cards, except an RPS combat system (3 "hit points" per character and each RPS loss costs 1 hit point).

    The cards were things that broke the rules.

    Things like:

    * Make someone answer one question truthfully.
    * Avoid answering any one question for ten minutes.
    * You may lie when you answer any one question, but you have to tell them you're lying.
    * Completely seduce one person.
    * Blow off any other card someone just played on you.
    * The next hit you land does 2 points of damage instead of 1.
    * Shrug off all damage from one hit.
    * Initiative! You cause one point of damage before combat starts.
    * You can poison any one item of food or drink that you touch.
    * You can ignore the effects of poison once.

    They all had clever names like "Iron Liver" and "Lightning Reflexes." Each was created for specific purpose in the LARP.

    \n Trying to go a little more directly at Jason's question:

    I've been listening to recordings of the game I'm running right now, mostly for my own education, and it seems like our units of fun break down into a few things:

    Being really awesome at something, and succeeding at it impressively
    Being really awesome at something, and failing at it dramatically
    Being really bad at something, and succeeding anyway
    Being really bad at something, and failing miserably
    Moments of mechanical character improvement (We're playing BW, so these happen during play)
    "Right skill, right time" moments, where someone has the exact right skill/ability for a situation
    The moment when an important outcome is decided
    Moments when a character changes in outlook, beliefs, or relationships

    Each of these things usually causes smiles, laughter, appreciation, whatever. However, I think they would be significantly diminished if they were all that happened. In most sessions, I'd say maybe 3-4 of these things happen. I think the 'unit' of fun is a scene, or a series of scenes that leads to one of these moments and flows in the fiction.

    Going to the card idea, I think for these to be much fun (at least for me), I'd need to have a small, random selection of them available to me, along with some less desirable outcomes, per session. Without moments of average success and average failure, I don't think these would be fun.

    \n Jason, while I agree with Sage overall, I think you can pack the fun in tighter in some games, but that it's not really sustainable over long periods of play. For example, the times I've run Danger Patrol it has been at an incredible breakneck pace of pure insanity, where characters are realigning moons and spawning evil shadow twins within the same 10 minute period. But, unless that slows down at some point and the players are given the opportunity to reflect and recover, you can only do that for about an hour or so (with 6-10 "units of fun" in that period) before you have to quit and wait until the next session. But if you run it a bit slower, as I imagine most people do, you can sustain the less-densely-packed fun for a longer period of time and might actually have more fun in the long run (even though fun isn't really comparable like that).

    Overall, yeah, I think if you pack your fun too tightly it has a chance of seeming gonzo or too artificial. It doesn't fit within the established expectations about narrative that we've come to expect from novels, movies, television, comics, etc. Well, maybe comics, but less so in modern comics, unless it's Sharknife or something else that embraces gonzo insanity. You really want one dramatic situation every 15-30 minutes or so, with cool but not fundamental content happening in between.

    \n I think having very specific coupons would shape the game with a too-heavy hand. If "make another player look awesome" is too general, I'd like to see things at the level of: "support another player's lie," "man the ship with excellence," "back someone up in a fight" and "highlight a reason to respect someone."

    I think it's reasonable to want to play one coupon as the star of an action in every ten or fifteen minutes and some number of supporting coupons in that same time. Also, the more meta-effective coupons there are, the better. I'd like coupons that let me end the scene at a moment that I feel is appropriate, bring in stuff that happens off-screen and author adversity for my peers -- even if there's a GM who normally decides that stuff. I'd expect a four-hour, four-player game to have something like fifty or sixty coupons.
