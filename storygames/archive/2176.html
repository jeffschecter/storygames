Should the true FINAL conflict be treated differently?

TonyLB
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Story Games
I've been thinking about tracking conflict arcs as they sporadically combust their way through a given piece of fiction. Star Wars has lots of really clear examples, so ... "Luke wants to become a hero." I've posited elsewhere that the process by which these conflicts progress is by stripping away the complicating little details that surround early conflicts, and eventually getting to the final, true conflict.

Like, the first conflict of Luke's heroism is complicated by Vader, and his friends, and spaceships, and the death star, and what he thinks he knows and ... lots of stuff. His first great success is to remove "what he thinks he knows" from his way of dealing with conflicts ... 'Let go your conscious self, and act on instinct.'

By the end of Return, however, all of those complications have been stripped away. Vader is no longer the enemy, his friends cannot help him, there are no spaceships or death stars or all that between him and the fundamental choice.

Now the stipped down conflict would be wholly uninteresting before the process of stripping. If the Emperor dropped in on whiny-farm-boy Luke at the start of the first movie and Luke dropped his ... what ... garden hoe? ... and said "No. You failed. I am a Jedi, like my father before me," we'd say two things. "Who the hell is this wrinkled guy, and why do we care?" and also "That Luke kid has not earned the right to have that much impact from that small a decision."

Well, lots of talking to get down to the final question: I'm thinking of making a system where you need to roll dice for all of the steps leading up to that final, true, most-fundamental confrontation, but then letting the players do (and achieve) anything they want if they can manage to reach that summit. Like, Luke rolls dice to blow up the death star, and rolls dice to fight Vader, and rolls dice in the forest of Endor ... but then if he can manage to get past all the complications, get down to the very heart of his characters conflict, there are no more dice. I just say "Okay ... the Emperor is goading you to kill Vader. This is your moment. What is Luke's destiny, now, at the end?"

If you were playing Luke would you feel cheated by that? If you were playing Han, watching, would you feel cheated by that?

    I want to bring out an aspect of your theory of narrative that's important in answering the question you're asking. You suggest that what's going on is a "stripping away" of complications to get to the "one true conflict"--but you rightly say that it doesn't make any sense to charge right into the "one true conflict," because it wouldn't make any sense for the protagonist to confront that final conflict without having first passed through those intermediate steps.

    So isn't it more correct to say that the "one true conflict" isn't some pre-existing end-point, but something that emerges from that process of stripping away complications? Because the protagonist at the beginning of the story is a cipher. Through narrative action (play), he or she gains attributes/properties/characteristics -- we see him or her become defined as impulsive or disciplined, brave or clever, compassionate or vengeful. And in most cases the antagonist takes on some kind of contrary or opposing attribute (Luke is kind to droids, Vader is cruel to everybody). And the logic of narrative is such that it is the character who displays the attribute more valued by author or audience that ultimately gains the title "hero" regardless of his or her ultimate success.

    But notice what most narrative action is doing: it's defining a character in a particular way, assigning to him or her an attribute that literally characterizes him or her. In the classic form of the heroic tale, the hero undergoes a test that reveals his or her heroic qualities (a "qualifying test") before meeting the main challenge he or she faces. Some heroes even face a "glorifying test" after their main test--e.g., having slain the dragon, can the hero forestall efforts by false heroes the claim the credit? Having come back to Ithaca, can Odysseus answer Penelope's riddle about his bedpost? In each case, though, the issue is: Is the hero brave enough, clever enough, wise enough, virtuous enough, lucky enough, something enough to be the hero? Not only do we not know this at the beginning of the tale, we don't even know what that something is until it's revealed in action.

    You're with me on all this, right? You agree that the complications aren't important in themselves, don't you, and that the obstacles only exist to let the character show us what he's made of? Once we know who the character is, we can decide (as author, as audience) if the hero is heroic enough, when facing some defining "final" challenge, to meet the test and pass it (even if "passing" means only to die without betraying his or her principles). And it may be that the "hero" isn't heroic enough: he or she fails the test, succumbs to the opposition, or betrays what he or she stands for. Think Winston Smith in 1984.

    So much is pretty basic. The answer to your question, I think, lies in the notion that multiple protagonistic viewpoints can reflect or comment upon each other more or less ironically, so that one character's arc can often be taken as a "counter-argument" (or complement) to another's. I first noticed this in Kim Stanley Robinson's Red Mars, where John Boone and Frank Chalmers stand in this kind of relation: idealistic dreamer vs. savvy politico--which one is more effectual in bringing about their vision of Mars? Han and Luke have this kind of relationship as well, but it's less strongly drawn after the first movie, the whole point of which is that Han comes around to Luke's mode of heroism ("I always knew there was more to you than money!")

    So the way to make one character's "final conflict" relevant to other characters (and thus hopefully interesting to other players) is to have that conflict center on exactly those points of differentiation that form the contrast between the characters, and to make those points of differentiation consequential in the outcome of the final conflict, in some way. "All right, in Luke's final conflict we established that a son's love can turn a father from evil to good. What do you want to go with, Han? How about 'Can a son's hate turn his father from good to evil?' Nah, too formulaic, and you don't have any kids yet. I know! You were once a criminal, so how about 'Once you've been on the dark side, can you ever really come back?'"

    I'm not sure mechanically how that would be implemented, but I know that if my "score" depended on how well my arc inverted the meaning, thrust, or significance of yours, I'd be paying attention.

    I take your question to be, "Is it anticlimactic or unfair to just let the player say what happens when his or her character reaches the 'final conflict'?" And I think that it's not anticlimactic only once you recognize that the purpose of all those intermediate tests (conflicts) in the narrative is to give the character (or more precisely, let us see in the character) the attributes he or she needs for the final one. In that case, the point of "leading up" play is show us that the character either has or doesn't have the particular attributes that are of interest.

    At the end, the character will either bring a track record of preliminary success or failure to the final conflict, and the player will choose for the character to ultimately succeed or fail in that conflict. One way that a player is motivated not to choose to always succeed is if ultimate failure is thematically interesting:

    Preliminary Success + Ultimate Success = Glorification Tale (Hagiography)
    Preliminary Success + Ultimate Failure = Tale of Hubris
    Preliminary Failure + Ultimate Success = Redemption Tale
    Preliminary Failure + Ultimate Failure = Cautionary Tale

    The point of giving a player the power to unilaterally determine success or failure in the final conflict has to be to allow him or her to shape the structure of the character's story in a way that "addresses premise" or lets the player say something meaningful.

    But that powerful message that emerges from the player's choice for the character shouldn't be allowed to hang out in the vacuum, a closed-off, incontrovertible statement. To the extent that the choices of other players can be seen as commenting on, responding to, or addressing the first player's choice, you have a conversation.

    Now, to some degree this will happen automatically. But it's only fair, if you're going to design in moments where one player gets to make powerful statements about the world, that other players be explicitly allowed to respond to those statements.

    Cool discussion, Tony! I'm working with similar issues in my game Mathematica, and I think we have some issues in common. I'm basing my game on the TSOY system, so transcendence plays a role in all this. I apologize if I misuse theory terms.

    Transcendence hands narrative control to one player for a brief period. The "price" he pays for this is not being able to play that character anymore. This makes it a natural choice to transcend when your character's story arc is over. Any time before that and you're missing out on the bulk of the cool things your character might do.

    The questions you have to ask are:
    - What's the barrier to narrative control?
    - When and how does a player get narrative control?
    - What are we fighting over?
    - What is the scope of their narrative control?

    Here's how TSOY answers them:

    Whatâ€™s the barrier to narrative control?
    In TSOY, players transcend by driving up an ability high enough to roll a transcendent success. Ability level is a sort of clock between the player and transcendence, but it's one that the player has a lot of control over. The clock is pushed by spending advances, and advances are gained by pushing story (through keys). A player can advance the clock all the way to transcendence in a session or two with a little work. By contrast, Burning Empires has a clock that's hard to push, and the GM pushes back!

    When and how does a player work towards narrative control?
    By rolling the dice. An interesting effect of this is that transcendence only ever happens inside of a conflict. A good question to ask here is how winning or losing a conflict impacts the answer to the ultimate conflict. In Burning Empires, it has only an indirect effect at best. The players can do everything right and make amazing rolls and still suffer a major setback in the greater action.

    What are we fighting over?
    What is at stake in the larger action? In TSOY it's whatever the player wants to be at stake. The transcendence narrative isn't pegged to a particular goal. If PTA had transcendence, it might be built around resolving character's issue. In Burning Empires, it's who wins the war.

    What is the scope of the narrative control?
    If I understand you correctly, your idea is that the character is resolving their story arc. The sub-questions here are what MUST change, what CAN change, and what CANT change as a result of the narration. In TSOY, IIRC, the player has very wide control over their narration. In Mathematica, players compete to narrate the actual fate of a person, place, or thing in the story. They are limited in narrating how this fate impacts other things that are also up for narration. In Burning Empires, players get fairly limited narrative control over how they narrate the final result of the infection. In TSOY, if you want to change something in the game world, you transcend. In Burning Empires, your character probably has to go over there and change it themselves in the course of play.

    Capes is another game that can provide some ideas here. In capes, questions to be resolved are written on cue cards where the can accrue resources on either side of the question (actually, I barely understand how Capes works, even after I've played it). This is a great approach, because it puts the "big issues" out there for all to see. The answer to question 2 then becomes "How do we put resources on this question?"

    In Mathematica, big questions go on cue cards and dice from in-game conflicts go to these cards after the conflict. So in this case, each character conflict is also a conflict over who gets to advance the clock on a big question and in what direction.

    So to summarize, weâ€™ve got:
    - a clock that ticks down towards the final narration
    - a possible relationship between what the characters do in play and the final resolution
    - whatâ€™s decided in the conclusion
    - how much latitude the player has to narrate the details of that resolution

    I realize my verbiage isn't entirely clear, but I hope it helps.
