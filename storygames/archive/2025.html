    Fantastic, so its Shreyas, Brand and Alan.

    The one thing we can all agree on about pulp is that it was incredibly broad. After that, shit starts to break down for this genre that engulfs magazines that were printed from the 20's to the 50's and still effects comic books today.

    So, rather than trip over what one person thinks is pulpy and another doesn't, I present some choices.

    Feel free to alter and brainstorm until we get to where we want to be.

    Don't think of a character just yet, let's all get there together.

    1) We could just go with pulp madness, embracing the idea that this group could very well be Indiana Jones, Rocketeer and the Shadow all suiting up at the local Century Club to fight giant robots and such. I'm running that with my buddies at home now and its loads of fun.

    We could have a unified group such as:

    2) Doc Savage's posse: maybe the man of brass is dead and it is left to all of you to carry on your mentor's legacy or an adventuring family.

    3) Plucky Reporters: You are staff on the official newspaper of the Science Hero. It is up to you to go into the maddest of situations and get that story before the printers roll tonight.

    4) Vigilantes: You are all denizens of a city, doing justice and keeping the streets safe.

    5) -Ism's: The 20's was a decade not without its sexism, racism and colonialism and the pulp literature was not without its problems. We could make characters aimed at not only have two-fisted adventures but in confronting some of that shit in some way.

    These can be mixed together a bit to taste.

    So, uh, I'm posting some of my thought process here, since if we were physically present with each other when making characters, this is what I'd be talking about.

    Name
    Vernon? What's reasonably '20s and doesn't overlap too much with the sound of the other names? Vernon is too much like Jerome. Claude? Yes, Claude is perfect.

    Last name? God, I'm baffled. I'm really tempted to buck the pulp custom the book describes and take a real name like Pirbright. Let's set that aside for later.

    Phase I
    Maybe he grew up on a farm and went to the city to be a photographer? No, that is way too much like every superhero story you are totally uninterested in. Forget it.

    Maybe, um, there was a death in the family? Yeah, the father died, and so Claude got a newspaper job as a way to pitch in with supporting the family, and he worked his way up to be a star photographer by force of (raw animal charm? No, that's not quite right...) dazzling personal magnetism and staggering, unschooled talent alone. Yeah, yeah. I think they could have lived off the family's money, but that wasn't acceptable to him; someone had to be refilling the coffers too.

    Aspects: Unschooled Talent, My Father's Dead

    Phase II
    This phase is so frustrating. I don't care about the war. What if I just went on with Claude's personal story...

    So, working just for the paper was just not enough, so Claude maybe indulged some of the interests he had to push away earlier in life, learned a little about anthropology and archaeology, did some art photography...maybe he found a mysterious object in the attic among his father's old things, an amulet? Something made of old enamel and dusty gold...Yeah, and somehow he traces that back to the Century Club, I guess. But no, not among his father's things...maybe it was his mother's secret, that she was never going to tell him about.

    Stunt fodder, being an art photographer means knowing people. Shouldn't Century Club membership come with some of this as part of the deal?

    Telling the story about a character's past like this makes me feel like I'm losing the opportunity to use this stuff in play, and ending up with a poorly synthesized and bloated character idea in the process. Anyway, somehow Aspects come out of that.

    Okay, there're my thoughts for now. Please feel free to brainstorm at me, gentlemen, while I give my brain a rest. Chargen this heavy really takes it out of me.
