    \n I saw this movie and it's quite good. Really, it's a psychological thriller that takes place at a grandeur nature (this is what LARPing is called in Quebec; it means lifesize scale literally) camp. It is much more of a critique of humans and our baser tendencies than of LARPing culture. You should check it out. It's really a decent little small-budget flick, quite entertaining and engrossing.

    Edit: and the director is a tabletop gamer, playing in a long campaign of 3.5, I think. Here in Quebec, the grandeur nature scene is really widespread and while it's usually not the popular kids who participate, it is still seen as not a whole lot weirder here than being super into circus stuff (which is also pretty popular sub-culture here as evinced by the success of the Cirque du Soleil). Check out theBicolline website to see the scale that some of these LARPs operate at.

    \n I saw the movie as well, over a year ago while it was doing the film festival circuit.

    For positive elements:

    The scene is presented as pretty cool. The sets and props all look impressive and make you say "hey, that looks neat and fun" rather than "that looks totally dumb" like some larp pictures and films look. Things look good but realistic - some of the costumes are better than others. But none of them look as silly as some of the costumes in Role Models looked.

    Yes, some folks take their fantasy too far and get out of control. But interestingly, the guy who starts off the movie the most lost in the fantasy (the older brother who is spending his morning bathing in the river and invoking Thor) is the one who comes around in the end and integrates back into his life (after a little vengeance murder, but the story is based on Norse epics, so it fits).

    And the going crazy stuff is obviously exaggerated for dramatic effect. It's presented in exactly the same way it would if you made a movie about a paintball game where people brought real guns and started shooting (which, of course, was made).

    Yes, I'm annoyed that, when roleplaying is presented in a movie, it's almost always about the point where fantasy and reality collide in a bad way. It gets old.

    But most hobbies are presented in a similar light. The only time I've seen Civil War reinactors on TV shows other than to illustrate a history moment is when they're worked into a murder detective show, and it's that one time when someone brought in a real gun because he either wanted to kill someone or did it on accident and is trying to cover. I've seen that plot happen on multiple detective shows, and I don't even watch those.

    If all you knew about the world was what you learned from CSI, you'd think that everyone was about to kill you for the slightest provocation all the time, and the only solution for any problem was murder. RPGs don't make people paranoid schitzophrenics, TV does.

    \n Overall, if you combine Role Models and Wild Hunt, you get a realistic and positive look at larp, which is pretty awesome.

    Wild Hunt is more realistic in terms of showing what a really good larp looks like. The costumes look cool but not too cool. The style of play is about 95% right, with teams of player factions interacting with each other in character and building up toward a big fight at the end of the weekend. A lot of campaign fantasy live combat larps look extremely similar to how they look in that movie (though they have unusually good buildings, but those are from a real larp group, so that's accurate too). The biggest downside is that the guys go crazy and kill people. The other downside is that they added stunt work to the combat, which makes it much more dangerous than the actual style of combat used in most story-centered fantasy live combat larps - some of the heavy contact games get a little rougher).

    Role Models consistently presents larping in a positive light, which is pretty amazing, considering that it's a Hollywood crude comedy. Yes, some characters rip on larpers, but the movie presents them as being in the wrong when they do so, and they all come around in the end. Heck, Oggie even gets the girl! Also, the fighting in the movie is a pretty darn good movie representation of what live combat larp fighting looks like - they didn't throw in lots of rough fighting stunts. The downsides: The style of game, where it's pretty much just pure fighting and you have a persona but you don't have a naturalistic sense of story, you just fight and die and then rest and get up and fight again, is more representative of the heavy combat foam sport games - specifically Darkon, with its factions and magic (and maybe Amtgard, though I'm less familiar with that one). And some of the costumes are really silly, like the aluminum can armor worn at the end.

    So if you have someone who is unfamiliar with larp watch those 2 movies and you tell them what parts to take from which, you can give them an excellent sense of what a live combat fantasy campaign larp is like.

    And that makes me happy.
