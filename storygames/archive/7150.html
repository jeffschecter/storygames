\n Let me frame this point by saying that I have never played a tabletop RPG. Sad, but true. I've read an awful lot about the hobby, but I've yet to become involved in any sort of actual play.
\n
\nThe biggest question I have for players is what person you use when you respond in roleplay? First or third? My natural instinct seems to be third person - I am more interested in telling a story and reacting to the other players, than embodying my character. Yet it seems that the default pronoun is "I."
\n
\nI've tried researching this myself, but all I've come up with is an article on RPGnet:
\nhttp://www.rpg.net/columns/nextlevel/nextlevel16.phtml
\n
\nI don't think the article particularly hits on my point though - I think my natural preference for third person might be that I come heavily from a literature perspective, and most of my writing has been done in third person. I'd want to be involved with the creation of the story just as much as anyone else. Since I don't have an acting or theatrical background either, I don't know how satisfied I'd be with myself "taking on" the role of a character that is completely unlike me (and I'd love to create fictional people that are nothing like me!)
\n
\nSo, what are the drawbacks and perks of using the particular pronouns? Which do you prefer, and for what reasons?

    \n Hey Christine,

    I think one of the really cool quirks about roleplaying games is that it feels absolutely natural to switch between these as tone and situation fits. If I'm in a scene where my arch-nemesis just poisoned my daughter and she's in her dying seconds, I'll most definitely be using "I". If I'm storming down said nemesis' corridor, fighting his henchmen as they show up, I'll be more likely to use third person.

    I think you use first person more often when you are confident of your credibility, as well.

    \n Hi Christine, and welcome to Story Games! I hope you get a chance to play some games soon, because that will definitely inform this conversation.

    From my perspective, person varies according to circumstances. I think first person is way more common as an "in character" voice. A game like Prime Time Adventures offers many opportunities for describing scenes very broadly, from an audience's point of view, and I find a lot of third-person going on. So it depends.

    One disadvantage with third person, for me, is that you can't really interact in it, at least not in a natural way. If I want my character to talk to the (imaginary) Dean of Students, first person is direct and conversational. It feels comfortable, because we do it all the time anyway. Acting skills don't really enter into it.

    \n I think like a lot of people I tend to start in third person, using that voice with new characters, or near the start of a session, or when I am slightly less caught up in the moment, and then switch to first when I get more passionate, or when I am in the middle of speaking in character (so I might break my in-character speaking for a moment to say "I move closer while I'm talking"). When the situation becomes calmer, or I am not as much involved, or it switches to planning between players, then I drop back into third.

    \n Fine,

    Yeah, unlike the written word (or even the recorded word) a shift in POV isn't very noticable, oddly. I find that I use third person early in a scene, or early in an evening, sort of warming up. And then later on first person becomes more common. Of course there are some games that push a certain method.

    A Penny For My Thoughts is a game about recovering lost memories. Wh's memory is being recovered changes hands. So most players are doing second person past tense, with the person who's memory it is doing first person past tense. It helps this with some ritual phrases, like you begin with "I remember..." so sticking to one POV is more natural.

    But when I choose, it's based on how I feel. I'd really like to explore POV in games more and instead shift it based on what I want the other players to feel.

    \n Also, the more I *want* to share the experience, the more I speak in first person.

    Me and a few really cool cats played a game called Mythender at a recent convention. It's a game about mythically powerful warriors, blessed by the Christian god, who travel to Mythic Norden to slay the last of the magical beasts. It's basically a game about taking down gods and dragons and the boogeymen that we've threatened children with all these years.

    And I made this character was a violinist that'd defeated the metatron in a violin duel, and who had spurned god, and then eventually developed wanderlust and changed his mind on the whole nihilistic-prick thing. And while I was setting up this backstory and making my character and narrating his entry into the action... it was all very much third person. After all, I'm not a violinist, I'm not a mythic warrior, I'm not Christian, I'm not ever going to beat an angel at anything.

    So, we're in this fight against a gigantic dragon and the mud and earth around it, which became animated and attempted to swallow us whole. And still I'm in third person. The action is awesome, I'm loving it, but it's not something that feels in any way connected to me. And then the earth splashes over me, knocking me down. It hardens into a casket, with me trapped inside. Claustrophobic and dying. And I've obviously never been trapped inside of an earthen casket that's trying to kill me before, but imagining that feeling of being trapped under that weight, with no stability or way to escape (after all, it's THE EARTH).... suddenly everything was in first person. I died. God shone his light down on my casket. My inanimate body burst through the clay and hovered in the sky. I coughed up clay and mud, as I sputtered back to life.

    Before, this guy had been there to kill a dragon. But suddenly, I was there, ready to fight back against the very earth that I walked upon. Ready to take what had recently been a fantastical and slightly ridiculous story, and make it personal.

    And I'm sure I didn't make a conscious decision about tense or pronoun usage in that case. But it did BECOME a first person moment for me.

    \n I mostly play D&D, and I find that, while there is the organic switch that Joe mentions, we seem to fall into first and second (!) person. E.g., as DM, I'm usually saying, "What do you do?" or at least, "Okay, [PC's name], what are you going to do?" It's then pretty natural for the answer to start off with "I". "I walk up to the gate." "I use my Twin Strike."

    Sometimes we use third person ("Balthazar is going to use his Second Wind"), but it's less often.

    \n It's remarkable just how fluid that is--also, within a game, it is common for some players to use first-person and others to use third-person. The activity of playing an RPG is really completely unlike story-telling, so the language doesn't enter into consideration so much. A transcript of a game session generally looks and sounds very... odd and unnatural. Certainly not something you would want to read. So the style of narration and so on is rarely a big concern.

    However, some games do lead more to "storytelling" or "narration" style. For instance, Polaris (even though definitely a roleplaying game) has the players use ritual phrases (which are past tense and third person) on a regular basis, and in my experience that makes everyone begin narrating in past tense and third person. In contrast, I've played a number of storytelling games (unlike RPGs, no one portrays a particular character), and while no one ever narrated in first person, it was impossible to remain in past tense. I don't know if it was just because the story was being made up as we went along, or some other reason, but on many occasions, with different players, no matter how hard we tried, the narration would switch into present tense. People just couldn't handle staying in past tense. I find this interesting!

    \n I often say "Joe the fighter does X" or "He puts his hand on his sword and says: Fight me!"
