Kuma
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
Quite a few of the threads I've read here lately have taken aim at traditional RPGs, and there's a lot that goes on in the Narr scene which seems to be a direct rebellion against RPGs-as-was. Getting rid of the GM, getting rid of characters, funky authorial controls, minimalist game design ... etc. Gamist is almost a perjorative term - I almost expect to see the words 'You fucking ...' in front of it whenever it's used.

But I think that in the process, I think that the strengths of the game part of Story Games is getting chucked out the window as well. As far as I'm concerned, RPGs work best when they skate the line between game and story - between collective enterprise and competitive. I wonder if maybe something is getting lost when the gut reaction to anything that may have appeared in an RPG prior to 1995 is to burn it to the ground.

Granted, I think that RPGs as an object-class are well represented, and that there are a lot of gameforms out there left to be explored. But I also think that it's wise from a couple of different viewpoints to keep the touchstones of the past in mind - certainly not every single aspect of traditional RPGs is worthy of scorn.

    February 2006

    Okay, Kuma, I am, like, a poster child for the unwise discarding of tradition that (I think) you are perceiving. I will go on record saying that I very much do perceive that attitude in my own design, and that I've been thinking about it recently.

    When I look at (oh, say, not-at-all-randomly) the role of the GM from first principles, I conclude that there are lots of different ways to structure that power. It's about pacing, and adversity, and all that what-not. You can give the responsibility for doing those things to one person, or you can do something else (like Capes's competition to be the one to do it best, Polaris's breaking into ritualized structure, Breaking the Ice's conflation of adversity with the enticing other, etc.)

    So I ran ahead, like every innovator who ever discarded tradition as the meaningless constraint of the small-minded. People were clinging to the GM role even in places where it wasn't the best solution, because they are hide-bound and ignorant, and don't want to consider new possibilities.

    I really needed to do that. It felt good. Got that out of my system.

    But when I think about it without that prejudice, I realize that tradition is its own justification. Human beings want to create some traditions. In a world where there are many, many possible solutions to any given problem, they want to use the solution that they have used before, not because it's the globally best solution, but because their familiarity with it, their confidence in it, will make it the best solution for them.

    Sometimes: "Good enough solution" + "Power of tradition" > "Perfect solution"

    Now that I know I can toss tradition out the window if I want to, suddenly I don't want to any more. I want to take advantage of the ways that I know people are comfortable playing, to make a game that's easier for them to pick up and run with. Yeah, a GM may not be the globally ideal structure for (say) Misery Bubblegum, but I'm pretty sure I'm going to have one, and that it's going to look very familiar to people who come straight from D&D. It's a touchstone that I consciously give them ... a tool of communication, me to them, them to me.

    Tradition has a unique and powerful strength, and I know that as a designer I got so fascinated by the fact that I didn't need it that I totally overlooked the idea that I might want it. Not gonna make that mistake twice. If I write another game that discards RPG tradition, I'm going to do it consciously, because I get something from ignoring tradition which is more important that what I get from embracing it.

    February 2006
    Okay, so I read Tony's post and I think "that's awesome!" but I also think there are good reasons that other designers aren't necessarily following the same path, experimenting with crazy weird stuff and then pulling back to more conservative design schemes.

    Yes, sometimes designers ditch roleplaying's tradition and don't really replace it with anything. This can result in games that are rather hollow, stripped of all familiarity and thus really difficult to grok and play.

    However, roleplaying's traditions are not the only traditions available. This, to me, is really, REALLY important and one of the critical components of my own design philosophy.

    If you look solely at roleplaying for design inspiration, you're going to be able to remix old mechanics indefinitely and play with whatever hot stuff just flowed out of the heads of guys like Steve Kenson, Robin Laws, Rebecca Borgstrom, Vincent Baker, folks like that. Sure you can come up with a dozen neat new dice mechanics that nobody's ever thought of before. And some of them might be pretty damn cool.

    However, you have so many more choices at your fingertips if you consider traditions outside of roleplaying. Just because these are not roleplaying's traditions does not make them alien to your audience. Roleplayers participate in other aspects of creative life and so are also the heirs to many, many other traditions in literature, art, film, theater, sports, other types of games, politics, philosophy, oral storytelling, myth, etc.

    So, yeah, I agree with Tony that tradition certainly has it's place. People need the familiar, the comfortable, but this doesn't necessarily have to be dice and GMs and maps and character sheets and numerical traits and the like.

    In fact, if part of your intended audience is people who have never been hardcore roleplayers, these are not their traditions at all, they're alien invaders from another planet. Traditionally, people have said "roleplaying is like storytelling" or "roleplaying is like a board game" in order to appeal to the traditions of these people.

    However, I find it more interesting, in my own design work, to include enough material from traditions outside roleplaying that both hardcore roleplayers and people who approach my games from a different standpoint will find enough common ground to reach a necessary level of comfort and familiarity.

    So yeah. That was long-winded. I hope there's something worthwhile in all that.
