\n I'm reading the Skills chapter of the 4e PHB right now. Most of the Skill entries have defined consequences for success and failure. The two most common failure conditions are "You can't try again during this encounter" (i.e., until you're out of the scene) and "You can't try again until circumstances change" (essentially).

I feel those people who always point to a feature in D&D and say, "They stole that from Rolemaster/HERO/Parcheesi/whatever." Still, I'm seeing a similarity here to Let It Ride from Burning Wheel, and I am happy.

    \n I'm not sure that's what is unique about 'Let it Ride'.

    You see, in my mind, and I'm sure people might now jump on me and prove me wrong, most role-playing games had text on "You can't try again until circumstances change" rules. Many a DM may have ignored them, but that's a different matter. Repeatedly trying a skill as you stand at the locked door wasn't something people hadn't figured out as a problem.

    The slightly unique thing about Let it Ride is the granularity of what the skill roll encompasses, in that Let it Ride tends to suggests if you make an infiltration roll to get into the temple then that's it, you don't have eight infiltration rolls in every corridor. I'm not sure how 4E sits on that.

    \nIan's also right in that let it ride encompasses letting successes ride as well as failure. The skill system for 4E, however, does seem to function that way however. Its not a single roll that's allowed to ride, rather non combat tasks require a series of rolls (much like combat)...but I suspect that the result of that series is intended to actually resolve the conflict...if you succeed at the series of infiltrations...you did, in fact, infiltrate...at least that's my hope.
    \n
    \nThe series of rolls to resolve outside of combat stuff is actually a great solution mathematically. Combat in D&D; has always been a "dice pool" variant (you just roll 1d20 10 times instead of 10 d20 once) in terms of ensuring players get a reasonably average result over time. Non combat stuff was purely linear and thus enormously subject to whiffs and golden b-bs.
