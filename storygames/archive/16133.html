\n Pelgrane Press is publishing a new game by D&D 3e and 4e designers Rob Heinsoo and Jonathan Tweet. Rob ran an early playtest version last year at GenCon, and it's now ready for general playtesting. If you are interested please email beth@pelgranepress.com. A tiny bit more info here.
13th Age is a love letter to D&D: a rules-light, story-oriented RPG that honors old school values while advancing the OGL art. Players create unique heroes using flexible interpretations of familiar D20 character classes. New indie-style rules connect each character\u2019s story to the Gamemaster\u2019s customized version of the campaign setting..

    Posted By: Hans c-oAre we allowed to read the game before we commit to playtesting?
    \nHi, Hans,

    You know the writers, the background and the pitch - that should be enought to decide, so the simple answer is no. There is a more nuanced answer.

    You commit to playtesting in good faith, that is, if you are taken by the description of the game, you'll run it. That said, if you do sign up in good faith and can't abide the idea of running it because it's not what we promised, or otherwise can't play, it then fair enough. But, if you think that's likely, we'd prefer you didn't sign up.

    Typically, we get playtest reports from about a third of the people who apply. Another third email with apologies. The rest go quiet on us. A decent proportion of each group still end up buying the game.

    \n
    \n
    \n General positive comments that don't give anything away are fine. In fact I'm rather enjoying them. The main reason we don't want discussion is because first, discussion affects playtesters' views and we want their views to emerge from text -> actual play, and secondly because we don't want people making judgements based on the feedback from a first playtest draft. But, the first reason is by far the most important. In other words, we have to balance the potential advantages of initial buzz against the best possible playtest feedback. Of course the latter wins, even at the expense of potential sales. It's only a short delay before playtesters can talk, anyway.

    \n I totally understand Simon and co. wanting to get feedback from playtesters without us influencing one another. If I write an AP report here and say, "I love this game, but the Muckraker class is totally broken!" then they'll probably get twenty other people reporting, "Oh yeah, the Muckraker class is totally broken." If they'd come to that conclusion independently, it's far more valuable to the designers than if people are just parroting the most vocal playtester on the Internet.

    On the other hand, I've worked a fair bit with folks like Fred Hicks (via Evil Hat or One Bad Egg) and their model is basically, "Tell people anything you want about this!" and it sells games and they do just fine. My excitement and enthusiasm for 13th Age right now is a precious commodity that's being squandered.

    That is, the purity of independent playtesting might not be as important as building a community around this thing. But that's just my opinion, and it's not my decision to make, and I don't have a pony in this race. I'm just a fan.
