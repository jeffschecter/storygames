The Cult of the Mystery GM and rotating GMs

\n
\n
\n
\n\nwalkerp \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2011 \n edited August 2011 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I do not deny that the Cult of the Mystery GM exists, but I call utter bullshit on it being somehow profoundly connected to traditional styles of play. There are clearly elements of early RPG design that encouraged this kind of behaviour, but far and away the larger factors of influence are social and demographic (socially maladjusted white male adolescents being the primary consumers). As traditional rulesets have evolved and gamers themselves have grown up, this "cult" is on the wane and (though clearly no real way of statistically knowing) I would venture represents a minority of traditional gamers today.

My main "political" agenda in posting this is to try and break down the assumption that some people seem to make in the storygamers community that if you are playing with a traditional ruleset than you are also playing with all the negative elements that have been associated with bad gaming over the years: fascistic GMs, avoidance of critical discussion around the game, player disempowerment, etc.

So here is one phenomenon of evolution that happened in all my long-term gaming groups: rotating GMs. Generally, in the early days there was one or two motivated GMs or the group that I joined was started by a single person who was the GM. But over time, other people wanted to give it a shot or someone else joined the group who had GM'ed. Eventually, in all these groups, it turned into a revolving door of GMs where each person got their shot to take over and run a campaign.

Why is this significant?

So many things happen when a group has multiple GMs. First of all, it opens the doors to other players having a GM's experience and thus that perspect on the current GMs game. This encourages dialogue and growth. Second, the transition from one GM to another always encourages a lot of dialogue. The new GM has to pitch his or her game to the rest of the group. Sometimes the order isn't set, so there is something of a competition of campaigns. A new GM that may have wanted to do something different with social elements of the game can now introduce those changes, which may or may not get integrated into the culture of the group (but the door becomes open to ask those questions). The change of GMs often also encourages the changing of systems as well (though not always, which would be an interesting phenomenon to look into).

    Posted By: walkerpSo here is one phenomenon of evolution that happened in all my long-term gaming groups: rotating GMs. Generally, in the early days there was one or two motivated GMs or the group that I joined was started by a single person who was the GM. But over time, other people wanted to give it a shot or someone else joined the group who had GM'ed. Eventually, in all these groups, it turned into a revolving door of GMs where each person got their shot to take over and run a campaign.
    \nThat's how it was for me, too. Actually, in most of the groups I played with when I was younger, there was a lot of peer pressure to run a game. "You should totally do it," they'd say. "You'd be good at it. It's not that hard, either. Tell you what, just run this module to get your feet wet, you'll get the hang of it in no time." The more potential GMs you could create, the better!

    Sometimes that pressure was a bad thing; we played some shitty campaigns because the person who had been harassed into running a game really wasn't into it, wasn't having fun, and their lack of enthusiasm was contagious. Or someone's eyes were too big for their mouth, starting a campaign and abandoning it after a few sessions. Or someone was just bad at it, or their style/what they wanted didn't mesh with anyone else's. At those times, everyone would cast significant glances in the direction of the people who were generally acknowledged as the best GMs in the group, and wonder (sometimes out loud) if they had something they wanted to run at some point, you know, if they wanted to...

    But other times, it was pretty much awesome. New blood, new ideas, new game systems...and a lot of out-of-game conversations between people about the best way to run a game and how particular rules worked and all that kind of thing. Switching GMs from game to game helped keep things moving and postponed gaming burnout.


    One thing no group I've been in has ever really been able to manage has been rotating GMs on the same campaign, though. That seems to be a game-killer for some reason, I'm not sure why. (I know that I'm not a big fan of doing it because I find trying to coordinate with another GM is more stressful than fun, but that wouldn't explain why no one else I've played with has been able to sustain it, either.)


    edit: Oops, I didn't mention how my current group handles it: GMing a game is a strictly voluntary deal for us, no peer pressure allowed. If you've got a game you want to run, pitch it to the group, we'll probably play it. If you don't want to run something, don't. If that means that some month everyone is burned out or too busy or whatever to run a game, we'll do something else on Saturday instead; eventually someone will get the urge to run a game, and we'll go with that.

    In practice, this puts the same person in the GM chair for most of the time, as we have one person in our group who really, really loves running games (and whose games we really, really love playing in). The other potential GMs like doing it maybe once a year, so they bank their idea and wait until the usual GM wants a break before pitching it. But we still change campaigns/systems pretty much every three months, even if the GM doesn't change: we like endings, and we like getting to switch genres/styles.

    Really, I only have one objection or counter-point to this whole issue:

    A lot of very significant/influential "trad" RPGs textually and mechanically supported the "GM Mystery Cult". I remember reading everything from, "roll dice behind a screen, and never let the players know you changed a rule, it's your job to keep them happy", to, "In an RPG, you can do Anything You Want! The GM will make whatever you choose into a cool story, don't worry!"

    Texts which implored over and over again for GMs to do many many things to mislead the players and lie to them... for their own good. That told you in no uncertain terms that the group's fun was the GM's responsibility, and that if the game was not going well, you just needed a better GM.

    How big an objection is that? How much of an impact did those texts have? I have no idea. Maybe lots, maybe very little. But if they did have an impact, I'm sure it had something to do, even if just a little, with the GM Mystery Cult and similar phenomena.

    \nWe're not actually talking about mystery games, but the tradition of GMing in which GMs try to preserve certain mysterious elements. It starts with the dungeon: it's secret. Then you get the Player's Book/GM Book format (perhaps with admonitions to keep the GM book away from players). This is in fact precisely why rotating GMs in the same game is rare \u2013 you need the visions to be in sync, or the co-GM could spoil something. Hell, the co-GM doesn't want to spoil things for himself. If he has any regard for the integrity of your game he will probably prefer to run something else.

In the second guy's mind, the only reason the first guy is having a good time playing D&D is because that guy is applying stuff from indie games: "Let It Ride" from Burning Wheel, "Say Yes or Roll the Dice" from Dogs, or whatever else he's seeing in that group's playstyle.

      Posted By: Paul T.The person who is really enjoying "trad" gaming and identifies as a "trad gamer" (and proudly so) is saying: "OK, there used to be some issues, maybe they were even widespread. But they have nothing to do fundamentally with the type of gaming I enjoy. It was some combination of players being dicks and maybe a period of unfortunate gaming texts. But that's all in the past, right? We know better now, whether because we've grown up and gotten smarter about it, or because the texts have improved, or both. So this has nothing to do with *trad gaming* in my mind."
      \nHm. This doesn't ring true to me. I don't think most traditional gamers see the old days as being dysfunctional and current period as fixed. From my experience, there still are and always have been some bad gaming and bad gaming texts - just as there has always been fun gaming and good game texts.

      \nNo, I can't think of anything I have chagrin over - and never had a problem like yours that I recall. There was plenty of my adolescent gaming that was, well, adolescent. Sexy slutty supervillains featured in some of my early Champions games, and there was plenty of dumb jokes and low humor in D&D and other games. Still, I don't regret or express chagrin over that - it was part of my youth, and I don't think there was anything dysfunctional about it.

      I suspect this is a common difference of experience. Some people had very bad experiences with traditional RPGs that prompted them to entirely switch to indie games as an alternative. Others have not, though, and may be fine with traditional RPGs.
