\n In some GM-less games (improvised and otherwise) there's been some drift of vision along the way. The setting we started out with isn't the same as the setting we end up with after some hours + bottles of wine.

One of the functions of a GM is to keep the vision - theme, setting etc - coherent during the game. If players start getting silly, or suddenly forget the existence of entire countries or laws of nature, the GM can get things on track again, usually by blocking unwanted input & reminding everyone of specific elements of the vision.

Perhaps GM-less games need to take care of this function, for example by giving one player (possibly on rotation) the responsibility of keeping the vision intact? With the mandate to block/silence/correct/reframe "wrong" statements?

    \n I play in a long running GM-less game with my house mates. We actually have a very strongly established world (that predates my involvement with the game), with it's own rules, laws and whatnot. While our game has no rules in the traditional sense, the things you talk about (vision, theme and setting) are all safe guarded and protected by the players without the help or need of a GM. The purpose and point of the game varies from player to player, but one of the things I think we all agree on is that the game isn't just about playing roles and telling stories, but about building, growing and exploring this fictional world.
    \n
    \nTo this end, we tend to police ourselves when it comes to unwanted input. When one player suggests something that just doesn't fit, the other players are quick to say so. Sometimes (often) we can make it fit. Sometime we can't. We always discuss why. This works for us, becasue this is how we've decided to play the game.
    \n
    \nI've been telling people who want to play GM-less Panty Explosion that the key to making it work is to set down befor ethe game and talk about what you want. What kind of stuff do you wnat in the game.What do you not want? What kind of tone would you like to set? What kind of themes and ides do you want to explore? Coming to an agreement ahgead of time puts everyone on the same page and avoids many of the problems that can sometimes come up in GM-less play.\\

    \n The only way a GM could "enforce" a specific vision, is by way of the use of authority he/she already had in the group BEFORE playing the game. If the people at the table don't respect his guidance, the rules can't help him. Because the rules can't force a player to act in a specific way or to take anything seriously.
    \n
    \nIf a player has that social authority, he doesn't need to be the GM. He can enforce it even as a player.
    \n
    \nAnd this happen in many groups, where the "guardians of the game integrity" are the oldest players, or the "alpha GM", and not the one that is GMing in that moment. Because this role, and the role of the "npc player" are completely different and can be done by different persons. And this role exist in one or more persons enen in GM-less games.

    \n Okay, an example from actual play.

    I recently played Polaris for the very first time (RPG.net review coming soon, I hope). We started out just fine - describing frozen wastes, spiky ice fortresses etc. Then, towards the end, we suddenly found ourselves playing out a scene in a Standard Medieval Fantasy Castle. We'd slowly drifted, probably out of tiredness and lack of concentration, to the lowest common denominator.

    I don't mind - in fact, I love - setting that is created and expressed during play. But here, nothing was actually created; we were just recycling old stuff.

    Perhaps, if one of us (say, the New Moon) had the additional task of making sure we stayed true to vision, for instance by encouraging certain types of description ("how does the coldness around you affect this scene?", "what does her skin feel like?"), we wouldn't have strayed, and would have had a more pleasantly intense/refined play experience.

    \n There are some games where everyone is on the same and perfect page in terms of imagery and style, like magic. (It's great when that's the case.) For those other times... sometimes the group will work with one person (or a few people) just stepping up and saying sometime, and the rules can help encourage that, but some groups really will not have someone emerge like that naturally - for those, having someone take the extra role of guiding vision is what's needed.

    So, "depends on the game and group", but take that as just a starting point.

    Personally, I occaisionally see the problem of vision drift; I therefore try to step up and offer opinions to keep the vision unified (sometimes), and try to listen really hard for others' opinions. I'd rather get *more* feedback then be saddled with something lame I just narrated in for the rest of the games.
