    \n Easiest example is Romeo and Juliet:

    Two households, both alike in dignity,
    In fair Verona, where we lay our scene, <-- The Setting
    From ancient grudge break to new mutiny,
    Where civil blood makes civil hands unclean.
    From forth the fatal loins of these two foes
    A pair of star-cross'd lovers...

    ...take their life; <-- The Climax

    (The Denoument:)
    Whose misadventured piteous overthrows
    Do with their death bury their parents' strife.
    The fearful passage of their death-mark'd love,
    And the continuance of their parents' rage,
    Which, but their children's end, nought could remove,

    Everything in the first paragraph, sans the Setting line, is the Situation. It's the unbalanced set of fictional things that the players will address (ie talk about, attempt to manipulate, and inevitably change), usually through the agency of player characters.

    \n This one, oddly enough, is one of those words where the "jargon" term is actually exactly the dictionary definition of the term (or one of them). Sayeth the OED: \n
    a. A position of affairs; combination of circumstances.

    b. A particular conjunction of circumstances (esp. one of a striking or exciting nature) under which the characters are presented in the course of a novel or play.
    \nThe situation is the specific things, people, circumstances, and setting elements that will mix with the PCs to go boom.

    \n Specifically, Clyde, situation is how all the stuff in the game is interrelated in such a way that the ensuing gameplay will be interesting. I mean, you can make a situation that's a town full of happy people without a care in the world, but if there's nothing for the characters to do, that's just a crappy situation. A 'better' situation is a town full of unhappy, desperate people who want or need the characters to do things, preferably to other people in the town. Or in non-Dogs examples, a pile of gold in a perfectly safe cave just outside of the city walls is a boring situation; put a dragon on top of the pile of gold and orcs guarding the tunnels down to it, and maybe some traps and gelatinous cubes, and now you have an engaging situation.\n
    Posted By: Clyde L. RhoerSo it's a fancy way of saying it sets the parameters or scope of the game?
    \nThe key is that situation is composed of the elements of the fiction and how they're related. "Scope of the game" could also include what system you're playing, when you're getting together, who's GMing, and so on. Situation is just fiction.

    \n Oddly, Mike, I want to say that the situation is the prepositions (including prepositional phrases which include verbs, yadda yadda).

    In R&J, it's not "Two households fighting," it's "From ancient grudge break new mutiny" and "From forth the fatal loins" and so on. The Montagues and Capulets aren't an interesting feud unless they've got some history behind them; the star-cross'd lovers are just another two kids in infatuation love until they're from the two sides -- and then it's on.

    Because, once you start playing, the 'verbs' that have been operative in the setting may or may not continue; however, the relationships between things almost always continue on into actual play, and are the things that are most likely to be changed by the action of the characters.
