    \n I'm working on what may turn into Omnifray's successor system now. I don't know if I can fix the page count. Including 10 pages blank for artwork, the character generation chapter is like 62 pages long. And to me that feels like bare bones, really bare bones.
    \n
    \nIt's been a while since I played the full-fat game but I'm playing it again this Saturday. Having left it so long, it took me a bit of concentration to get back on top of all the rules and the plot of the game I'm running (that's 800-odd pages in 9-point font in the full-fat game - maybe half a million words), but I think I'm there now. We're looking at some pretty big battles, some very high-powered player characters with maybe some more basic allies taking on large numbers of enemies. There might be 50, even 100 involved in a single battle so it could momentarily look like a war-game. I started this campaign to see what full-fat Omnifray was like at the highest levels of complexity and it seems to run fine, but then again, that's with me in the driving seat.
    \n
    \nI'm wanting the successor game to be far more accessible. It'll never be Risus, it'll never be in remotely the same ballpark as Risus, but that's not the point. It's hard to articulate what the point is, but like an elephant, I know it when I see it. I think what I'm about is proving (to myself first, then to those who care to be convinced) that you can have a roleplaying experience which painlessly combines almost perfect quasi-realism, game-balance and meta-mechanics across a vast plethora of play-styles without buggering up your speed of play. How do I get that across without sounding as if I'm trying to say "a better roleplaying game", a claim that no-one will take at face value? Ho-hum.

    \n How about looking at GURPS Lite and EABA Lite as examples? EABA is something like 132 pages but the lite version is 15 pages, GURPS hits around 300 or so and the lite version is 32 pages. In both cases they show the basic mechanics and some example powers. BRP QuickStart is 48 pages.

    See what the core mechanic is, and use just some example powers, it doesn't have to be even remotely complete, just enough to give a feel for the system.

    \n The question for me as a purchaser of games: what does this game do that I can't already do? If the answer is "not much," it doesn't matter if it's 20 pages or 200. If the answer is "quite a bit," then 200 pages is no object. But the bar for this one is very high: I have about six shelf-feet of GURPS, and several FUDGE and FATE books, and Basic Roleplaying, and even Savage Worlds.

    The question for me as a player of games: what kind of investment will it take to get started, and is that more than my fellow-players will put up with? This is the main reason I'm not playing Burning Wheel, despite its elegance: one of my usual players will not put in the effort it would take her to learn the rules, which means she will be a major drag on the game; and as she suffers from GSF#5, if I don't invite her to a game, even one she won't enjoy, I'm an ostracizer.
