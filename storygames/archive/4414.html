\n Commondialog, Noclue, James Brown, any other interested parties,

Talk in another thread about Rifts perked all of our interest. It looks like we're going forward on playing a game of Rifts via Skype.
I'd like to use this thread to discuss both scheduling and game expectations.

Commondialog, are you going to be GMing? I got that impression, and I think that's awesome.

Hopefully you don't consider this too control-y from a player, but I think we should all throw out some suggestions and expectations, and see if we can establish some consensus on some things.

1.) I'd like the game to have a party structure, and when we're all online and playing together, the group is together as well.

2.) I'd like our party to have a fairly unified feel to it. A backstory, a goal, a couple uniting factors. That doesn't mean we can't all be wildly different, but I'd like there to be some sense to what brings us together, rather than "you were all hired by a mysterious man".

3.) I was thinking about playing a renegade/ex Coalition soldier, who suffered through a lot of chemical experimentation, and as a result can't control all of his nervous impulses. So sometimes his body goes ahead and shoots the woman, even though he's screaming at himself not to, in blind panic. I want to play some form of tragic specialist soldier, I know that.

4.) I want to be from Free Quebec, personally. Free Quebec is detailed in World Book 20 - Canada, and World Book 22 - Free Quebec. Maybe elsewhere as well, I don't know.

5.) I just want to mention, I torrented a bunch of Rifts stuff. If anyone wants a certain book and doesn't want to spend the monies to acquire it, I'd be happy to email it to them. Just, y'know, if your ethics are as bad as mine are.

6.) I want to push for playing a fairly "straight" RIFTs, with two exceptions: players have some pretty heavy flagging capabilities (and the GM pay attention to them), and use explicit scene framing.

    \n I'm cool with a wiki being started.

    As for scene framing, what I mean by that is: If what's important is a knife fight on a rooftop with two Headhunters and a Juicer, we frame to that. If what's important is getting onto the rooftop using tactical prowess and all of our cunning, then we frame the scene back a bit further to include that. You move to the important parts, skipping past the mundane and uninteresting.

    Now, that's tricky, because what's important is a very subjective thing. But I think that if you've got no stealth types, and no one has made any flags regarding sneaking or using technology to avoid surveillance, you can frame like "You manage to make it through the security, dispatching a couple patrollers and dog boys along the way. You finally arrive in the Library Chamber." No one is particularly invested in that part of the mission, so why drag us all through it?


    Does that make sense? Does it sync up with your idea of scene framing?

    Others: Am I way off? Is this something that's unappealing?
