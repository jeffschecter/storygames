    \n 1. Purchase a white tablecloth.
    2. Pick a game you want to run at a con (e.g. AW or Lady Blackbird)
    3. Use iron on transfer paper to print character sheets on 3 of the sides and GM notes on the 4th.
    4. Print or draw a map in the center.
    5. Take to con and spread out on a table.
    6. Wait until all seats are filled.
    7. Enjoy.
    8. Reuse or trade.

    \n For example, if you wanted to demo DW using The Bloodstone Idol:


    Worth noting: Getting poster-sized things printed is way cheaper in black and white than color. However, consider giving each player a four-color box of crayons like at a restaurant and letting them color during play. Also consider getting some super magnets so you can hang it on your fridge when you get home. Alternately, consider that you can get poster-sized prints laminated to allow writing on them with dry erase. If you were going to use this technique to, say, demo the same game all day long then the re-usability could make lamination quite the bargain.

    \n If you're re-purposing an existing game, probably no advantages other than cool factor (which I don't think is enough to justify the expense in money and ease of use).

    But if you build a game around the idea, it could be interesting. For example, a game could be designed:

    - to be played standing up. A horizontal poster could be very useful hung on a wall.

    - where the entire game was 1 large map with the mechanics embedded inside it (this isn't uncommon in board games)

    1 central play sheet may also affect the psychology at the table. Everyone's focus is on the same thing. You physically have to move closer together to use it. The emergent emotions could be tied to the theme of the game.

    \n -I think that the posters would make powerful game-as-artifact works of art, especially with the annotated maps and notes and coffee rings and little stick figure scenes and etc...
    -The novelty factor might also be a good draw for a game that might otherwise have a hard time attracting playtesters at a convention.
    -Because it would be one large sheet, there would be less concern about people walking off with your demo or playtest materials (perhaps important if you were concerned about reusing materials, piracy, or nondisclosure).
    -Poster games would be awesome table dressing at weddings.
