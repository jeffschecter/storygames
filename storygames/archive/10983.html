    \n In brief

    Mechaton: relatively small numbers of mecha, individually different in capabilities, in battles where terrain and control of specific locations matter.

    Macross: large numbers of mecha standardized on a common platform, operating in combined-arms with capital ships and mobile artillery. Transformation between modes is an important part of tactics for Veritechs, too, and that's hard to capture in Mechaton.

    Pace-wise, conflict in Macross tends to happen at high speed and cover a LOT of space.

    I guess I just see Mechaton as being better at the "big iron", lumbering tank style of mecha. More Battletech.

    I guess I just see Mechaton as being better at the "big iron", lumbering tank style of mecha. More Battletech.

    \n
    \n

    This has more to do with particular mecha designs than the character of the game. Design some fast guys and watch them rocket around the table.

    \n

    Here's how I'd do most of what you're saying:

    \n
      \n
    1. Capital ships are just regular things. Spec them appropriately. We played at spaceship scale \u2014\xa0miles wide initially, potentially with entire planets as cover, if we kept increasing in scale. We also played at a scale where my Sisters of Taw were human scale and mecha were maybe twice their height.
      2. \n
    2. Spec all your guys the same, or around a couple of designs. That's what I do most of the time, anyway.
      3. \n
    3. You set the unit size your ownself in Mechaton. If you want little guys spread over the entire floor, make a ruler the appropriate size.
      4. \n
    4. Have "hero" mecha and "unit" mecha \u2014\xa0a single guy and a bunch of guys on a plate, respectively. Have one mecha be a "tough" guy (blue), one guy be the "crack shot" (red at range), one guy be the rookie, eager to help (yellow) and so forth.
      5. \n
    \n

    Transformation is something that's never quite worked right. That's a big deal. We've tried a couple of things, but transformation has always worked out to be a disadvantage.

    \n

    On the other hand!

    \n

    It's not at all an RPG. As much as I love the idea of using as a resolution system, the moral standpoint in Macross is that there's a fundamental element of human nature, the Protoculture, that, if we acknowledged it, would obviate war altogether. Mechaton sure ain't that. It could be, though, a way to figure out who's around to make the moral decisions later, perhaps using the aesthetically compatible Capes rules.

    \n A buddy of mine here is a total mech-head. At the same time, he's in the process of becoming a very nar/indie-style RPer. He desperately wants to run/play a nar-style mecha game and it's proven to be quite a challenge. What's the purpose of the mecha? Are they color or something more thematically substantial? Are we considering one of the various anime-inspired aesthetics, or the more American-style vibe of Battletech? And so on.

    Our current best solution is to run a Mechwarrior-style game using Mouse Guard. Team of mercenaries who receive jobs from a central source (I'm not clear on the Mechwarrior canon, but this fulfills the mission-driven requirement of MG), with the mecha representing sets of systems and bonuses that plug into the existing "combat" or "war" scripts.
