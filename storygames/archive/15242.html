Instead of putting your RPG into a book, could you do it as a poster?

\n I was surfing the net and I discovered this really cool poster of where our money goes.

It got me thinking. Why does a RPG or game have to be made into a book? Would a poster work? Something that all the players could see at the same time, perhaps reading different parts, would be pretty cool. Flowcharts, thumbtacks, dry erase, and even ease-of-reference strike me as some powerful tools for having a dynamic layout.

Could you make a game and post it on the wall? Can you think of games in which this WOULD be the ideal medium to present the information?

    \n I think there are a few games where this would work, and some which are practically poster-games already. Hell4Leather (the Tarot-based version) and Sea Dracula would be easy ones to do, as well as the current version of Panty Explosion or GxB.
    Many others may need small books to explain the nitty-gritty details, but only need some basic references when the game begins. I've made play references that incorporate all the important stuff for Fiasco, Happy Birthday Robot, and Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple, which are all essentially 11x17 posters (okay, so my first incarnation of the Fiasco playmats were much, much larger, and the HBR reference is 8.5x11, but you get the point).
    Hey, if some games can fit on one page of paper, why not fitting a game on a poster? I'm all for it!

    \n I don't have any particular ideas about putting a whole game together as a poster, but I have condiered on a number of occasions creating something like a big idea board/collage thingie with mechaical bits sorta like a game reference/cheatsheet.GM+Players screen on the borders.

    Mostly, i'd been thinking in terms of game that had a lot of general background info that i as senario presenter/starter wanted the other players to work with, but didn't reallly expect them to delve into pre-game.

    What i was envisoning was something with a scattered amount of art in the center, some key setting elements, and maybe even some tone stuff or genre appropriate events and then just plain leave white space around those things for players to write ideas around as the game progressed.

    Really, the idea was just to have all that stuff up and in view for all players to be able to reference. I think I may have compared it to the way Kevin Spacey's character in The Usual Suspects uses the stuff on the walls around him to weave his version of events in that film.
