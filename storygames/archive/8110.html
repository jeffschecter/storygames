\nLeft 4 Dead looks like it is going to scratch some itches I have, and having played through the demo, I'd like to deconstruct it and see what there is to learn from the way gameplay is structured.

I'm particularly interested in how they reinforce team play and allocate resources. The "Director" feature, which apparently enforces adaptive gameplay, is also really interesting to me, because I suck. This is one area where tabletop play has an obvious advantage, so it's interesting to see that gap maybe narrowing.

Anyway, lessons for tabletop games from the way this game is structured? Other video games with interesting bits that could be stolen/adapted/applied?

    \n Some things grasped after hours of playing the demo that might be of use :

    _group prevails, loners always die in the end
    _the zombie boss give hints about their presence (especially the Tank and the Witch) which seems to create tension among players ("Fuck there's a witch ! Don't bother her !").
    _limited resources but your pistols does have unlimited ammo. So you can always carry your weight in a fight. You're never useless.
    _even when you're down you can still fight. When you're dead another survivor takes your place.
    _railroading is good : players want to escape through the fastest and shortest route, no time for exploration. If you wait more zombies arrive. Run, shoot and keep runnin'
    _as always weapons correspond to style of play : shotgun, hunting rifle, M-16... This is the only thing that sets the characters apart, the rest is pure color (which doesn't mean it isn't important in the game experience).
    _the zombie boss does not only encourage teamplay, it forces it : if your grabbed by a smoker and no one can help you, you're dead, end of story.
    _the use of cover to limit the zombies attacks on you (in a corridor, subway wagon... so they can only attack you from 1 or 2 angles).
    _rankings ; thought it is a cooperative experience you can always see if you are "better" than the other players.

    There's no better example of this when you leave the safehouse on the second level, right when you enter the subway: You drop down into a pit and can choose to take the stairs down or the ducts. However, underneath the safehouse is a dark area with lots of concrete pillars and zombies.
    There's no reason at all to kill them in-game. In fact, it's perhaps better to just avoid them and move on. But it's kind of a staple of tabletop games (and, admittedly, most other FPSes out there) to linger behind and make sure that Every Enemy is Dead, even though they won't follow you or cause problems later.

    Also, it kind of makes me want to create a dungeon with some sort of time mechanic, or something to keep players on their toes by having enemies repopulate areas that the players cleared, if they dawdle too much.
    Attack them from behind with a powerful sneaky monster.
    "But we cleared all those rooms! And we locked the doors behind us!"
    "Looks like you missed a spot, because it was hiding, waiting for you..."
    We use the logic too much when doing dungeons: "They cleared the rooms behind them, nothing is left." Throw a monster behind them every once in a while to up the stress and get them moving. (is maybe a lesson one could take away)\n

    \n Interesting idea.
    \n
    \nThis is one of the things about certain PC games that makes them highly replayable.. The Fallout series is a good example. No matter what set of abilities, gender and name you choose, you're still essentially the same character: You're the Chosen One (in Fallout 2) every time. But the choices you make during play define that character differently. There are still 2 things you must do to win the game (get the GECK, save your peeps) but how you approach those two things makes the game play different.
    \n
    \nNow take that out of the computer game where there are a finite, measurable number of ways to play the character, and move it into the RPG where the only limit is time and imagination.. A very interesting concept.
    \n
    \nAs for the principle of room-clearing, I'll say that Half Life 2 has already begun to cure me of that one, considering that there are areas in that game where the enemies just keep coming. There's a particular place in Ravenholm that I dread every time I play through it. My strategy is to make a single run through, kill everything once, then get the FUCK out of there. Why run through and kill everything once? Because you have to run through the area so many times to hit this switch and pull that lever, then you've got to make this jump, then this one, and don't get knocked down by that charging enemy.. It's good to have a relatively clean map. Sure, it'll get dirty again if you linger too long, but that's the point.
    \n
    \nAnother thing about HL2 (since I can't play L4D.. Yet) that I think has mineable potential for table-top gaming is the interaction between ammunition scarcity and the gravity gun. Putting bullets into flesh is the easiest and most reliable method of making hostile things stop biting you. But bullets don't grow on trees. The Ravenholm portion of the game is especially nasty in this regard, so that I've gained the habit of using my gravity gun and crow-bar to make kills even later in the game when bullets are incredibly plentiful. Basically, the scarcity bit combined with the option of using your environment makes me think outside the box a lot more than any other shooter I've played so far.

    \n I'll be playing this on the 360 (I make console games for a living, what do you want?), and the thing I'm most looking forward to is seeing how their asymmetrical Versus mode comes off. One team plays as the survivors, and they aren't allowed to respawn until all 4 of them are killed. The other team plays the zombies (specifically the special ones like the Boomer and the Smoker and so on), and they respawn infinitely. The teams switch which side they play every time all the survivors are killed, and for the most part, Victory Points can only be scored by the team currently playing the survivors, giving the whole thing a football-like quality where "being human" is the ball.

    This kind of design takes forever to balance properly, so you rarely see it. If it stands up to Valve's history of quality, it will be a wonderful thing to experience.
