\n
\n
\n
\n\nMike Montesa \n\n\n

\n\nAugust 2007 \n edited August 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n On our last podcast, Luke Crane made the point that three of the major licensed games, Star Wars, BSG, Warhammer, are all RPGs in the traditional mode. I added that they are pitched to sell to veteran gamers, rather than general fans, and Luke finally pointed out that the dirty little secret is that the publishers count on non-gamer fans buying the books for what little extra info they may provide and never playing the games at all. If this is actually what drives sales of these products, and makes them worth the big companies' time to publish, "Why design a game at all?" Luke asked.

I agree. I just can't help but wonder how well these major properties would do if they were done new-style, as story games, with the assumption that the person picking the book up really has never played an RPG before (as opposed to the cliched newbie gamer that allegedly picks up most traditional RPGs and sees the - nearly useless, appendix-like - "What is a roleplaying game?" paragraph), is a fan of the property, and might just like to spend an evening (rather than the weeks, months, or even years of a trad game campaign) telling their own story set in their favorite universe with a group of friends.

Traditional RPGs are fun for me, but I've been at them for almost 30 years, but new RPGs, especially those based on licensed properties that are designed this way aren't likely to appeal to a wider audience outside of gamers who are already fans.

Generally, the story games I've played have been very easy to pitch, teach, and play with complete gaming newbies. They're also usually short-form, and can be completed in an evening (or three, tops). Trad games can of course be pitched to newbies as well but generally require more of an investment to get them rolling. Man, imagine if Star Wars were a full-on story game, something people could learn and play in an evening, with the level of design, graphics, and marketing resources an outfit like WotC could provide. Instead of selling 15,000 copies of a roleplaying game designed for gamers who are fans, they might sell a million copies (so I'm pipe dreamin' - sue me) to fans who might become gamers.

    \nDon't give them "rules". Give them "methods", "techniques", and "procedures"

    Yeah, I know this sounds like a semantic quibble. It is. But, a person's perceptions are often driven by the language you use to describe something. No matter how many times you point out that "game" as a term can describe very casual, non-competitive or not-overly-competitive activities, it will almost never be the first thing people think of when they hear the word.

    "Role-playing game" is like that too. People ( those who are even aware of the term) have a similar reaction; they have a very clear personal idea of what an rpg is like. Including, apparently JK Rowling.

    Now "Story Entertainments" ( to steal Chris Kubasik's term that been discussed recently)? I don't know anyone who has any particular strongly held mental definition of that term. That, or something similar, is open territory to define and claim.

    Licensing: why bother? Other than to hold to the indie ideal of being your own publisher, what does that really hold for you*? I would think that something along the lines being suggested would work better as as a pitched concept to a publisher or ( heaven forfend) to Rowling's people ( or other appropriate persons for different source material).

    As for increasing the body of players for pre-existing styles of game/activity? Who knows? I guess it's mostly a mass exposure idea. What is the ratio of people exposed to [Leisure Activity X] to people who become avid participants in [Leisure Activity X]? What's the ratio of people who would participate in [LA-X] after exposure, provided they didn't have to do much work? I suspect that almost any hobby activity will follow similar patterns in that regard. RPGs/Story Entertainments/Whatchamacallems are a smallish hobby. Any increase in numbers above a make-up-for-natural-attrition point would be noticable. Or, if really successful, you might generate a related-but-seperate hobby, which is cool in its own right.

    (* "You" in the generic sense, not Ian specifically)

    Added Afterthought:
    Big Game Companies and missed opportunities
    BGCs are set up to do what they do, much like koalas are set up to eat eucalyptus. I can't really fault BGCs for not doing this anymore than I can fault koalas for not enjoying peach cobbler.

    Posted By: buzzThe question is whether combining the license with an RPG system that was actually cool would have made into more than a moderately well-selling RPG that sits on the shelves of Firefly fans everywhere (collecting dust), and instead got those people really excited about story-gaming and made them want more.
    \nThis is the crux of the matter, and there are two ways to look at it:
    Financially: a knock-off game system that doesn't need to be rigorously playtested is cheap and easy (and easy translates to 'cheap'). An innovative game system that requires rigorous playtested is costly. And here's the clincher: an innovative game system would not measurably increase sales -- even folks who got all excited by the system and got others to buy and play would be a drop in the bucket compared to the legions of buyers who got the book because they get everything that's Serenity-related. Maybe, maybe, a really cool system would have people continuing to play the game afterwards, but from the financial perspective, unless you're selling them supplements, you don't care.
    Game Proselyte: creating an innovating game system and rigorously playtesting it is a really rewarding way to spend a few hundred man-hours. Acquiring a license to make a game about an identifiable property is expensive, but perhaps it's worth it to eat that expense if it gets more people playing the game. Maybe it would be commercially successful, and maybe you take additional steps to make it so, but the commercial success is not a factor in deciding whether or not to shell out the cash for a license.

    Or, in short:

    Cheap System + Expensive License = Optimal Profitability, Moderate Cost, Low Proselytism
    Innovative System + Expensive License = Optimal Proselytism, High Cost, Low Profitability
    Innovative System + Knock-Off Looks-Just-Like-It = Moderate Proselytism, Moderate Cost, Moderate Profitability... ie, your typical Indie Game.

    Oh, and:
    Cheap System + Knock-Off Looks-Just-Like-It = Low Proselytism, Low Cost, Moderate Profitability... ie, crap d20 products

    RPGs are a lot more freeform, dramatic, filled with "let's pretend", driven by imagination. I think that's the hard part for people to get into. Play can go off in so many different directions, often the ruleset doesn't match what people are pretending, or the needed rules are buried in the midst of a bunch of rules for things that just never come up, or in endless examples and flavor text. And even if you find the rules, in SGs they're often tied to what's imagined and how that imagining is received by others. What kind of game this side of figure skating lets you have more efficacy in play if you impress the judges with something fancy?

    Going back up to the original post, I think SGs may be easier to teach than other RPGs. Though it would be interesting to drop an SG on a group of interested but completely untutored adults and see what they make of it...

    Posted By: Joe BeasonRPGs are a lot more freeform, dramatic, filled with "let's pretend", driven by imagination. I think that's the hard part for people to get into. Play can go off in so many different directions, often the ruleset doesn't match what people are pretending...
    \nThis is, uh, well... this is bullshit. It's said over and over and over again, usually in a rather self-congratulatory "my, aren't we the creative smarty types" sort of way, and that really doesn't make it true. (Joe, not saying you're coming at it from that angle, it just seems the common delivery.)

    Specific RPGs don't go off in so many direction. Sorry, they don't. If you sit down to play D&D, you do not spend the four-hour session making cheese and talking with martians. You go out, you kill things, and you take their stuff. Maybe you're in one of those "we just call it D&D but we hardly ever roll dice" game, in which case you've got a homebrew system that emphasizes the things that you like -- courtly politics and secret romances, say -- and you're not playing D&D as presented in the book. You're playing some other game that you call D&D.

    There seems to be this perfectly ludicrous assumption that RPGs should be written to support players ignoring the text in this way, and if a given book doesn't do this, it's not really presenting an RPG. The limitless vistas of your imagination is not what RPGs are about -- that's fucking ad copy. RPGs are about providing you a support structure for imagining and collaborating on a limited subset of those vistas. An RPG doesn't need to be written so that it can be ignored because it's a book, and as such, it can always be ignored by the players closing the book and setting it aside.

    To reign things back in, a good RPG can describe what's supposed to happen during a game in such a way that you and your group don't go careening off into interplanetary cheesemaking land and get confused. I mean, seriously. If you're playing a new game and you get to a place where you don't know how to play the game and you can't find the rule in the book, the book was written poorly. It's not that RPGs are such a magnificent fucking hobby they can't be adequately described in text.

    I'm talking about Piggy-backing a game in something else.

    Here's what I mean, in case I wasn't clear:

    Forget the concept of "game" as a stand-alone, with all the license buying nonsense.

    Let's pretend that a new film comes out: Star Soldiers. Woo-Hoo! Someone, somewhere is inevitably going to have a tie in book or ten for that film with titles like "The World of Star Soldiers" or " Technical Guide to Star Soldiers" or " "The early adventures of Luke Solo: Star Soldier". Even if the film bombs, those books will still be made and will still be on bookstore shelves. Those books are geared to fans of the source material, the people who are very likely to be potential gamers.

    A short S-G would add up to about the length of a chapter in one of those books. Essentially, the cost of putting an S-G in that book is almost nil.

    Of course, at that point, you're running into a conflict between the Indie ideal of maintaining full rights to your work versus selling it off for publication by someone else.

    You're also accepting hat the game has just become the prize in the cereal box, so to speak. It isn't actually the primary focus of the product, although it may be a reason that some people choose to buy "The World of Star Warrior ( Includes the Story Warrior Adventure Game!!)" as opposed to "The early Adventures of Luke Solo:Star Warrior".

    So back on topic, no I don't think Big Game compnaies are missing out, because they aren't set up to reach casual gamers or would-be gamers anyway, even if they did have money for a license. Only WotC has any significant distribution in non-hobbyshops (other than bookstores).

    Small, independant game producers don't have the distribution outlets or the money for licensing to make standalone game products. What they do have is experience with short form games that very well could be piggy-backed into books, novels, comics, kids' books and similar items that are then distributed through those channels by the parent publisher.

    Given what I understand that writers get paid, I think we can safely assume you will not get rich this way. You may, however, get a bunch of people playing your game that normally wouldn't. How that stacks up against other things you value? I dunno.
