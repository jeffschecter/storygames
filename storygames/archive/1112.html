Serious Games, Pedagogy, and Ricardo Montalblan

Jason_Morningstar
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Play Advice
Yesterday I met with a faculty member at a local University who is really interested in the pedagogy of gaming. His proposal to a teaching and learning grant program piqued my interest because he used the term "roleplaying" and specifically mentioned Dungeons and Dragons, so I called him up and we met for a chat. It turns out that he's a WoW player and his concept of roleplaying refers to MMORPG stuff. He wants to explore constrained quest-type content with built-in status indications for his students. Anyway, I showed him my Gen Con haul of indie games and explained what I do, and what's up with independent publishing and small games, and he was very excited to hear about RPGs with explicit end states, PRGs you can play in four hours, and RPGs that are not about slaying gnolls.

However!

He expressed his particular conundrum very well by describing an axis. On one side is content. His discipline is astronomy, so you've got a line from Star Wars (almost no useful content) to the movie Contact (A tiny bit) to Sagan's Cosmos (lots and lots). Then you've got engagement, which runs from Fantasy Island (totally passive) to Jeopardy (you can sort of participate) to gaming (where you are pretty engaged). Academics create stuff that is content-rich but engagement-poor, and most gaming experiences are the opposite.

"I want a game like this," he said, holding up Nathan's Carry: A Game About War, "but about astronomy." And not a sci-fi game where you interrupt the flow every so often to calculate the density of a neutron star, because that's not too engaging.

So some questions. First, what games, out of the box, can or could support rich academic content? If you play PTA about botanists, can that lead to the productive and engaging transmission of botany content?

Second, what does an explicit, intentionally designed, engaging, content-rich RPG look like?

    Okay, here's me jumping up and down and cursing, having lost a big chunky comment. =P

    Jason, I think it's important to distinguish between teaching academic knowledge and teaching academic practice.

    Knowledge -- identifying stars, calculating neutron star density, reciting the main sequence of stars -- is hard data and information. It's the base of Bloom's Taxonomy (Know, Comprehend, shading into Apply), and it's important stuff. However, it's also kind of boring; this is the stuff that gives school a bad name, because at root it's pretty much memorization. This is what you'd get if you create a game set in a content-appropriate setting and give players bonuses for recalling and applying bits of information. This would, I suspect, always be transparently a classroom activity dressed up like a fun game. The other important thing to remember is that content only gets into a game one way -- through the players, and so no matter what you put into the book, you'd need to piggyback into the game via a player. At this point, the game isn't teaching the players anything, it's the prep for the game that's doing all the teaching. (See GURPS sourcebooks for a prime example.) At best, you could get some small-group learning involved, with each individual bringing different expert knowledge to the group and sharing it around.

    Practice -- the things that actual professionals do in their field -- is all about process and procedure. It's the upper portions of the Taxonomy (Apply, Analyze, Synthesize, Evaluate), and this is jump up and down fun. It requires some grounding in the hard-data Knowledge to base your conclusions off of, but less than you might think. What's important is that you take some information and you play with it, rather than simply recall and recite it. Process is actually easier to embed into a game than knowledge; you just put it into the rules (I think somebody suggested doing resolution by picking out the moving point of light between two star fields, for instance). This is also -- to be a little arrogant for a moment -- the territory of indie games. Games like carry, Dogs, and Primetime Adventures are all about exploring and experiencing process. It's not what you play about, it's how you play it.

    So I know next to nothing about what real astronomers actually do, so I'll use history as an example. To approach things from the Knowledge angle, you could set up a game in a historical setting and give players die bonuses for correctly recalling pertinent historical details. To me, this screams "After School Special" or "Marginally Entertaining Cartoon Designed to Teach Kids About Egypt." However, if you approach things from the Practice angle, you could make the game about time travellers (because all historians are time travellers) who astrally project themselves into the past to follow clues and collect evidence to support or disprove a given thesis (like "Abraham Lincoln intended to abolish slavery"). Note that you could pretty easily embed the die-bonus for historical recall into the second design, as well. But the time-traveller game, where players are actually able to take the raw historical data (even if some of it is fictional -- important point) and play with it, to "be historians" for a little while, that's going to be far more engaging for the players and let them actually do history rather than study history.

    As I understand it, the ideal learning situation is one in which the learner is required to know slightly more than they currently do, and are aided by an expert who has the requisite knowledge. So you'd really want a game that starts out very basic, then builds in complexities as the game goes on: once the players have the basics down, you throw some quirks at them, or flesh out ideas you glossed over before.

    Which is a perfect description of how D&D's level system works, really. The new player with a first level character only needs to know a small fraction of the rules (insert snide comment about the large number of D&D rules here), and learns more each time they level or face an unusual encounter: you can't hit this thing with your sword, so you need to learnt the grappling rules, or whatever. Also, the traditional model of corebook plus supplements follows a similar learning pattern: start relatively simple, then add in more complex subsystems as you go.
