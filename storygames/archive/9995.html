\n There's an article on Gnome Stew that pertains to the six words in the Topic.

I generally agree with the assessment that a GM should NEVER utter those words, and in fact one of the major design considerations when I was dreaming up Vox was that character behavior should be like human behavior -- occasionally random and unpredictable -- and that "you wouldn't do that" was a bad idea.

But I wonder if that's really true for every game, and every gamer, and every circumstance. It seems easy to say that it would be, but I wonder...

What about an RPG played with a young child, where the child deals with every encounter by bashing the NPC in the face with a hammer? A gentle "Your character is a friendly gnome and he probably wouldn't hit everyone he meets" would seem easier than having to run around the issue in-game, especially if one also wishes to teach manners to the child in question.

What about an RPG played with a novice who has never played a Wizard before? If the player hefts a sword and charges into battle like Gandalf, does the GM let them get cut down because of their mistaken assumption, or does he gently remind them that Wizards in this game have no more than 4 hit points, and can't wield swords well?

What about an RPG wherein elves are incapable of crying because they have no tear ducts, and a player says his elf cries? Does the GM remind the player of the biological impossibility, or does the GM rewrite his game world and retcon something because a player made something up?

What about the example given in the article, where "the consequences of the player\u2019s action result... in the death of the entire party"? Is it acceptable to simply let one player pull the lever that destroys the world, ruining the evening for the other six players? Or is it better to tell one person "no" and let the others continue?

I am all for "Yes, and...", but I really do wonder if "No" is preferable more often than might be thought.

    \n I think in my local play culture, when people trespass on previously established canon, the general attitude is "Huh, I had no idea. I guess elves can cry, awesome." So implicit agreement, and moving on. It's not even a thing. It happens pretty regularly with stuff like NPC names, etc.

    This permissive attitude is, I think, predicated on an expectation that players are all engaged and aware, and aren't going to introduce a magic wand into my gritty police procedural. So the dissonance that crops up is minor and not worth worrying about.

    One counter example - in our TSOY game, one guy has a character with a hidden past. It's transparent to us as players, but our character's don't know his "real" name. When I used it once in character, we stopped and retconned the scene a bit, because the big reveal is going to be a fun and important part of the campaign. Accepting that I just knew it would have been the wrong choice in that case.

    Posted By: droogWhat about the example given in the article, where "the consequences of the player\u2019s action result... in the death of the entire party"?

    Bad set-up, bad communication. Let them die.
    \nI generally agree that there are better ways, but I don't think this is one of them. If TPK is a certain consequence, make it less certain. Certainty is boring. Besides, if blocking one player is bad, then surely blocking all the other players via TPK is worse? If it's a mistake that kills the game (ignoring why that shouldn't be the case), then the GM has at least as much at stake as the player. If it were so much more fun to kill off the party, then the GM wouldn't even have considered blocking the player in the first place.

    ***

    Of course, one thing the article didn't clarify was whether the statement was a challenge or GM fiat. The author seemed to assume it was the latter just because it was bluntly worded. But maybe he only needed to justify why his character would act in such a manner. "I'm making a once in a lifetime mistake because my mind's on X. Happens to everyone." And maybe the GM was only defending his own right to make such challenges as a fellow player.

    Suppose the player's solution to the security problem was, "I'm exploding like an atom bomb and taking out the entire world with me." As a challenge, "Your character wouldn't do that" seems perfectly acceptable to me in most games. "You fail" is also acceptable, for obvious reasons; but in the beginning, couldn't and wouldn't are identical. If the player tried to use his hacking skills, as the article suggests, the GM might rule that the character failed so quickly that, in matter of fact, they failed to even conceive of way of applying their hacking skills to the situation. Ultimately, "Your character wouldn't do that" is just another way of saying, "You fail too quickly for meaningful consequence to stem from your actions."

    So if the player were being rational, then he'd admit his real problem was that he wanted a meaningful hacking scenario right then and the GM wouldn't supply one. "You would give me a meaningful hacking scenario now." He's making the same kind of challenge against the GM as the GM made against the player. It boils down to negotiation; and while the GM did a poor job of it, at least he brought justifications to the table, while the player just made demands (or so the article makes it seem). That said, I can't fault the player for leaving the table if there are others more agreeable. I'm just not sure he should be categorically blaming the GM for saying, "Your character would not do that."

    Posted By: BurrSo if the player were being rational, then he'd admit his real problem was that he wanted a meaningful hacking scenario right then and the GM wouldn't supply one. "Youwouldgive me a meaningful hacking scenario now."
    \nNot to sidetrack my own point for a moment, but I have to say that in particular is one of my gripes about some games (or at least the way they can sometimes be run). I take issue with the belief that every player must have something equally meaningful to contribute to each and every encounter, which to hear the creators talk about 4e was a central design decision there: to give the fighters as much to do as the wizards, and to give everyone something cool and awesome to do at every moment, both in and out of combat. IMHO that's a bad lesson to teach. Just because players want their characters to be important to every situation does not mean that they are, or should be. It sounds like it might be a way to help push role-playing and narrative, but in practice it's never worked out that way for me.

    "What do you do?" turns into "You should do something" when in fact sometimes it's OK for the rogue to hide in shadows and lay low for a few minutes, or for the hacker to simply duck and cover while the bullets start flying. Otherwise, it simply becomes "Oh look at that crazy dwarf, always Intimidating everyone we meet" or "Oh, I guess because I'm a hacker then I'll try to hack every computer/door/robot/traffic camera/calculator I come across" or "Look at that crazy Mesmero, always mesmerizing the first person we meet in every encounter" because that's the one thing that character happens to be good at. Just because you have a hammer does not mean the world is full of nothing but nails, and sometimes the guy with the screwdriver should get a chance before you bash the screw in. A GM who magically inserts computer locks outside every single door in the entire world just to give the hacker something to always do is just doling out antibiotics to someone with a cold. It's not helping, and it's probably hurting in the long run.

    And to loop this back to the original issue, I think this sort of gameplay sort of contributes to issues where "Your character would not do that" is a tempting thing to say, if ill-advised. Because the issue is often not that your character would not do something, but that sometimes your character would not do anything.

    Posted By: TonyLBI refuse to believe that the player's whim of having their character cry is more important than my whim of removing the tear-ducts of a race.
    \nI agree with you, but I also refuse to believe that my whim of removing elven tear-ducts is more important that the players whim of having their character cry. That's why I'm going to ask why instead of just saying no. Sure, I might still say no afterwards, but I want to hear their reasoning first.

    There's a big difference between:

    GM: Elves don't have tear ducts, remember? Why do you want your elf to cry?
    PLAYER: Because that tear duct thing is lame, man. It's my character and I'll cry if I wanna!

    and:

    GM: Elves don't have tear ducts, remember? Why do you want your elf to cry?
    PLAYER: Because the elven goddess of Sorrow has returned from exile, signaling the end of the elves' Age of Innocence.

    If the player negates my creative input, that's not cool. But if the player wants to build upon my creative input, I'd rather remain open to that instead of missing out on a cool storyline because I said "no" too soon.

    \nOh, absolutely. Negating other people's stuff is bad, whether it's the GM or player or whomever.

    My main point is more one of technique than of fiction authority. If the GM has a beautiful cathedral in his head, or coming in from some 300-page sourcebook-- then in my experience it isn't practical to expect the players to know every cranny of that cathedral preplay. And I hate during-play canon correction; to me, that's like hearing a stylus drag across a record.

    I know I've been terribly frustrated at times, trying to add contributions to a largely mysterious and invisible canon. Whenever I see con ads for games that are episodes in some GM's private world that has a real-time history of decades of play, I run away.

    So, at our table, I try to proceed under the idea that if some setting detail hasn't come up in the game ever before, it's a blank slate and anyone can kick into it so long as the consensus is positive about it. And if anyone has an element like 'elf' or 'mercenary' or 'mountain fighter' in their backstory, they have fictional authority there -- I try to always ask players' permission before riffing on their backstories, so that their view of it and mine do not evolve in contrary ways.

    Posted By: Leonard BalseraPosted By: aeoniteSkill challenges
    \nHave you sat on the sidelines and watched your friends do a Complexity 4 or 5 skill challenge yet, the ones that are meant to replace a normal encounter?

    If so, how'd that go? Was it fun?

    I've been in three 4th Edition groups now. I have two friends who are in 4th Edition groups and report the same behavior. In every case, the skill challenges all go like this, pretty much verbatim.

    "OK you guys have to negotiate with the orc king or he will attack you. You need 5 successes before 3 failures. What do you do? Ed you go first."

    "Well Intimidate is my best skill so I'll try that." (rolls)

    "Ok that's one success. The orc king is impressed by your bravery. Who's next? Fred?"

    "OK I'll try Diplomacy since that's my best skill." (rolls)

    "Ooh, sorry, that's a failure. Diplomacy is not on my list of approved skills for this challenge. The orc king gets mad at you. Ted?"

    "Hmmm. Well I guess I'll try Knowledge: Local History." (rolls)

    "Ok that's two successes. The orc king is impressed that you know about his people's history in this land. Ed, what about you?"

    "Is it my turn again? OK, well I'll try to Intimidate again because that's my best skill." (rolls)

    "OK three successes. The orc king is even more intimidated now. Fred?"

    "Well Diplomacy is my best skill and we can't use that, so I pass."

    "Ok, Ted, your turn."

    etc.

    The argument I always hear when I bring this up is "Well you have a bad DM and/or a bad group of players and/or a bunch of novices" but that is patently untrue. It's occurred in 5 separate instances with 5 different groups and both experienced and inexperienced GMs. Something about the way skill challenges are described eats away at the very soul of role-playing and seems to turn what used to be role-played scenarios into a half hour of die rolling.

    I understand that many many many people have had different experiences but I have not been impressed. At all.

    I am enjoying my 1st and 3rd edition groups immensely, however.

Just because I have final say over what my character does, does not mean that I am allowed to ruin the game for all with bad play. I could as easily claim that refusing character monogamy is a reaction to My Guy Syndrome.
    \n
    \nI can understand if somebody makes an argument that getting rid of character monogamy for a while in play can help people better understand how to work characters for the benefit of all. However that's just a good skill to take back to the relatively traditional power split. Character monogamy is no less evolved a way to play, and some people do it perfectly well without ever having to be shown any new skills.
    \n

    \n These are different approaches for different games. In my long-running D&D game, I was in charge of the setting. It is what I call black box GM'ing. Incorporation was present, but not direct, for example "is there an X here?" Any "your character could not do that's" are dealt with quickly, and we allow players to backtrack if their actions were relying on something working which can't. There is no "should not", but I'll certainly warn people of the consequences of their choices. Introducing new players is not tricky; you simply make their characters unfamiliar with the world, so the player's ignorance is the character' ignorance. It also allows them to do things that are socially unacceptable in a plausible fashion.
    \n
    \nIn other games, we all add and incorporate material quite openly. It depends on the players and the system. I think there are a number of people who frequent story games who have either had bad experiences or no experience at all in black box GM'ing, and they all want to have an open say in inventing material. Dare I say it, they appear to be a little sensitive about this issue. I think they are missing out. Playing Mortal Coil and black-box D&D are very different, but I enjoy both. The latter does put more onus on the GM, and the players have to rely on the GM a lot more, but sometimes it's good to play in a world without the veil being lifted.

    Posted By: lantern314This may be oversimplifying things, but it is a viewpoint I haven't seen in the thread.

    The first D&D campaign I ever ran died a very quick death because I refused to say "your characters wouldn't do that." I was running the Dark Sun campaign that came with the boxed set. During one of the first encounters the characters said they were running under the Mekkilots to seek cover from an attack. The Monstrous Compendium entry said that if anyone did that the beast would flop down on them squashing them. So they did. Not everyone died, but everyone was hurt significantly by it. End of campaign.
    It was quite a while before I understood GM'ing enough to recognize why not stopping them, or changing the behavior of the animal was what destroyed the campaign.
    \n

    This seems to me to be a good example of strict Sim play trumping Story and thereby killing the game, but maybe that's reading into it too much.
