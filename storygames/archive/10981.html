    \n Well...there are already some good predefined guidelines for what's possible as a phase intent: change the government, change the military, take out a FFON, stuff like that. Then you can add windowdressing to it, narrative context.

    Like, in our current BE game the Vaylen's intent is "change government to dictatorship." The color for that is: "Dismantle the human government and put (Vaylen Supreme Badass FON) at the head of the new regime."

    I think it's easy-ish for players to get kind of scared of what's possible, but since everyone's discussing intent out loud, I think both sides can/will settle on just how "big" is "too big" as far as the intent's fictional color goes.

    BTW the human's intent in our game is "Capture (Vaylen Supreme Badass FON) and execute him." It's big, it's bold, it's a game changer.

    \n Paul's game's phase intents are cool for several reasons (and what he said).

    Every bit of narration is a weapon. You only get to say so much, so you need to make it all count. If the GM takes over the government, it may just be fictional, but holy cow that gives him ammunition to hammer the PCs super-hard with. Plus he gets to look over at the players and say "Yeah, your government is mine. One of us goes, both of us goes. Whatcha gonna do about that?"

    On the player's side, killing a GMFoN is a serious blow to the other side. If the GMFoN is tied to a future phase, the GM can't replace him if he gets killed. Otherwise, it's just a huge morale boost for the players. Killing a FoN with a face-stab can be awesome, but might also feel anti-climactic, while killing a defeated FoN at the end of the game can come as a given. But working for a whole phase to whack one dude and then succeeding has the potential to be the most emotionally and narratively rewarding of all.

    \n So I was thinking about the opposite case as well: What makes for a lame Phase intent?

    IMO lame Phase intents are when nothing consequential changes. IE nobody at the table cares that the planet stops producing raw materials and starts producing hovercars. Big fucking deal. There's nothing ideological at stake (assuming of course the game's premise isn't built on some sort of "industrialization will ruin our planetary heritage" type thing...in which case, starting up a hovercar factory would be a huge blow to the space hippies).

    In our own game, the "dismantle the imperial stewardship and put the Vaylen at the head of the new regime" Phase intent is ultrahot because one of the FONs is the Imperial Steward, a powerhungry, protective bastard who sincerely believes the planet's well-being is only possible through his rule. It was already hot because it hands the Vaylen a bunch of implied narrative authority, but it crosses over to sublime because it punches a player's character concept square in the nuts. It's the ultimate challenge: if you really are the planet's only salvation, how hard will you fight to maintain control?

    I suppose all I'm really saying is that Phase-level Intents have to matter exactly the same way as roll-level Intents: Say Yes or Roll the Dice, right? We gotta care if we're gonna roll.

    \n The main reason orthogonal intents are useful and interesting are because of compromises at the end of Phase. Check out page 435 in the Burning Empires Book. Compromising on Orthogonal intents is far more interesting because you can have a lot more varied consequences occur. If one person wants to take over a faction and another wants to decrease quarantine levels, then there is more wriggle room to get more of what you want without depriving another.
