\nThe OP (Jim) considers writing a game where the players play multiple characters, possibly belonging to a certain common grouping or faction. He believes that the traditional approach to roleplaying, mostly focused on increasing the personal power of your character, prevents this from becoming a popular mode of roleplaying. This lack of popularity might potentially keep him from writing his game.
\n
\nI am inclined to agree, with reservations. My thinking is that traditional approaches to roleplaying, especially as emphasized by "RPG" video games, teaches that roleplaying is at its core the simulation of a heroic character in a fictional world, defined by statistics, and that the purpose of the game is to increase those statistics. WotC propaganda and authoritative claims from stodgy old D&D grognards reinforce this idea. Since most gamers arrive at the hobby through either RPG video games, WotC advertising or association with D&D grognards, this is the preconception that many players have. Simply sitting one of these gamers down with a game that doesn't operate using this formula, without explaining to them how they work differently and what the object of the game is, can result in nothing but misunderstandings and aversion. I believe there are methods of dealing with this, by destroying the preconceptions of what roleplaying is and what makes it fun.
\n
\nSome (perhaps rhetorical) questions: Are gamers as single-minded as some claim? Are there ways of consistently generating motivation and fun other than emphasis on increasing character ability? Most importantly, are there methods of generating motivation and fun that mesh with multi-character roleplay?

    Posted By: lachekAre gamers as single-minded as some claim? Are there ways of consistently generating motivation and fun other than emphasis on increasing character ability? Most importantly, are there methods of generating motivation and fun that mesh with multi-character roleplay?
    \nThe first question is reductive and rhetorical, so I'm going to skip it.

    Character ability need not increase. However, I'd support the hypothesis that player ability needs to increase. In addition to creating a story, RPGs are still games. Games need some sort of progression to keep them interesting, and generally speaking what happens next needs to be more significant than what just happened. Note, however, that doesn't mean that adversity keeps getting ramped up. It just means that each bit of the game is more important and more rewarding to the players than the bit before it.

    We've all played in games where you finish "the important bit" and the rest of the game is just a formality. Consider the last ten turns of every Risk game ever played -- everybody knows who's going to win. The value in those last turns are the winning player get to gloat and exult over his victory -- and those ten turns happen pretty quickly. Contrast with chess, where it can become quite obvious that black is going to win, but not after a prolonged whittling down of white pieces. The game has a 'surrender' mechanic available for white to end the game and recognize black's victory. Why? Because if white 'sticks it out' and plays until checkmate, you get a really boring last ten (or twenty) turns that aren't fun for anybody.

    Now, in Risk, the winning player's ability to affect the game keeps increasing. The losing players' abilities are extinguished, yes, but the balance of power is still shifting. In chess, you get to a point where the winning player's ability is no longer increasing -- they are not getting any more pieces, obviously, and they are not improving their tactical situation (since they already have a lock on winning). All that's happening is that the losing player's ability to change the game is dwindling, and that's not that much fun.

    Back to the question: consistently generating motivation to play and fun means that there's something in it for the player if they keep playing. There's a payoff in the next part of the game, either in terms of increased ability to affect the course of the game or some emotional catharsis or resolution. None of that needs to be explicitly tied to the character-bit.

    Now, the simplest transition from "egotistical" play to troupe-centric play would be to replace the character with the faction. Instead of increasing the character's stats and resources, you increase the faction's stats and resources. Hit points become members, for instance. However, I don't think this would really generate a very different play experience, since mechanically nothing has changed. (I also foresee some real trouble in getting the players to meaningfully interact with each other -- if I play the Theives' Guild and you play the Palace Guard, we can both think of reasons to get them to interact once. A roleplaying game needs to get them to interact in every other scene or so.)

    Another approach is to disengage the players' ability to affect the game from their characters. A character sheet is just a list of resources that the player can use to affect the game. You can just have player sheets which list off how the player can interact with the game without character-bits necessarily getting involved. Universalis may work like this with its coins and such, although I am a bad indie gamer and have not actually read it.

    The real trap that underlies a violent reaction against self-serving egotistical play is to try and get the players to act for the "good of the game" or for the "good of the story" without providing them any mechanical support to accomplish that. If I have to sacrifice my ability to affect and enjoy the game in order to make the story better, I'm not going to do it. Neither are most players: we'd rather hold out and wait for a better opportunity to make the story better with the resources we have, and do so in a way that doesn't hamper our long-term ability to enjoy ourselves.

    The trick in game design is to mechanically reward and mechanically support the "right" decisions that you want to foster. Dungeoncrawl games do this by investing cool fictional stuff with the ability to interact with the game. The more cool fictional stuff you have -- magic swords, awesome abilities, arcane spells -- the more you can affect what happens in the game. The more likely you are to be able to craft an emotional catharsis and payoff that pleases you. None of which is incompatible with "players control more than one dude." Any design that wishes to implement that gimmick, though, needs to figure out what the payoff is and how they can make the payoff happen when the players control more than one dude.

    Posted By: Joshua BishopRobyneeds to figure out what the payoff is and how they can make the payoff happen when the players control more than one dude.
    \nYep. So what payoffs can we list off? I can list:\n
      \n
    • Character identification and immersion
      • \n
    • Witnessing the personal power progression of your character
      • \n
    • A competitive win over other players
      • \n
    • First hand experience of an interesting story
      • \n
    \nGames may strive for, and achieve, more than one of these. I'm sure there are more, and I'm sure mine could be boiled down and refined. Please feel free to do so! I'm very interested.

    In a multi-character game, though, I don't think the first one works. It's very hard to identify with more than one character at a time, at least to the level where you connect well enough to take full enjoyment from the experience. The second one, obviously, would be modified to "witnessing the personal power progression of your faction", if that's what you're playing. The third and fourth would work beautifully, but may be at odds with each other - competitive wins tend to lead to strategic and abstract gaming, while deep storytelling tends to be much more concrete and detailed to be effective.

    Can we add to the list? Can we think of other motivators that would work with multi-character games?\n
    Posted By: lachekAre gamers as single-minded as some claim? Are there ways of consistently generating motivation and fun other than emphasis on increasing character ability? Most importantly, are there methods of generating motivation and fun that mesh with multi-character roleplay?

    \n I think it's the striving more than the completion, though the goal of completion or success is what gives the striving meaning. In dungeon-crawl D&D, some of the striving is exploration, and some of it is pitting one's character(s) against the traps, monsters, etc. The gear is the goal of the striving, and the passkey to new levels of striving, but isn't important in and of itself.

    That striving works is aprt of lots of other games: engaging in a battle fo wits with a Faerie Lord, stopping Dr. Methuselah, finding why a town of the Faithful has gone sour. And the striving may not be an in-character thing. There's the striving to immerse oneself in a character, or to tell a good story, or come up with a plot twist that makes the other players go "Whoaaaa!". The striving to play hard, to reference another discussion.

    \n I have some questions to pose.

    Is it possible to turn a MASSIVE war game into a story?

    Is it possible to emulate the woes of a squad of men in a Gettysburg reenactment?

    Is it possible to like numerous character in a wrestling league for instance, at the same level and not know who to rout for?

    Is it possible to like all of the Yankees? Or care when they win or lose?

    Is it possible to be involved (emotionally) with every single character in the Game of Thrones?

    Is it possible to find the same kind of enjoyment you get in killing a dragon with a single swing of an axe in the non-stop chills of project management or armchair quarterbacking?

    Is it possible to rout for the bad guy knowing that by extending his life in a story, you extend the life of the drama of a story?

    Is it possible to sit through the last 30 minutes of the Lord of the Rings trilogy?

    Moving on.

    I know that I have a different view of what makes for good fiction.

    I'm reading 100 Bullets right now and what I love about this story is that I don't like ANY of the characters. I'm not invested in any of them. They are all assholes. They are all selfish people.

    But the WEB of story is soooooo good, so meaty, so thick with context and subtext and plot ... I read 20 issues two nights ago, without stopping because I can't wait for more.

    Stray Bullets is another gem of a comic.

    I think Deadwood is the best Television show ever made.

    I think The Plague is among one of the best books ever.

    Despite it's obvious "issues," I love the complicated nature of the WW Vampire universe.

    [I think Star Wars is bad. I know turn in my nerd badge.]

    And I hated Preacher.

    I think storytelling, for me, is more that just some amoral mercenaries flying through space and not paying their bills.

    It's more than just wise-cracking demons playing poker with cats.

    It's more than just a woman working in a chocolate factory and eating too much.

    It's more than just a fighter gaining a level in ranger so he can get a bonus feat with two weapons.

    I think it's more than what we've been doing with it.

    I realize that's judgmental, but it's my judgment.

    It's my wish to play games that are more than just sumless parts, but rather to play something the engages and challenges and forces me to stay awake for the next "round of combat."

    Anyway. Thanks for picking up the thread and going with it here. I'm sure I'll drive this discussion into the ground soon enough.
