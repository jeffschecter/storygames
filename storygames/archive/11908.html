\n Personally I think it'd be fun to compare/contrast what we feel are ingrained and assumed enough to be considered "traditions" from our various histories. It's a tricky line of thinking but interesting. I'll start.

* One guy buys the vast majority, or all of, the games. That one guy -- almost always a guy -- is also singularly responsible for pitching new ideas, learning the rules, teaching the rules, reading the background, and generally digesting the material. Consequently he's also the one singularly responsible for synthesizing all that, making it his own, and presenting it back to the players.

* When there are disagreements between players, the GM arbitrates the disagreement and hopefully gives each aggrieved party something.

* When a player disagrees with the GM (who is by trad definition not a player), the disagreement is tabled until after the session. Period. But also, there's a guarantee that the disagreement WILL be discussed. Given the GM's role as continuity enforcer -- see below -- this may also include documenting house rules so everyone would know how the physics rules of the game had been changed.

(Side note: I claim this only as my own tradition of trad play -- I've been in groups where there is no player-GM disagreement, ever, by definition of the GM's absolute authority).

* The players are solely responsible for everything their characters do, and largely responsible for what they think. The GM can also kibbutz and suggest thoughts and feelings, but really it's on the players to act on that or not. They also agree to share that stuff, to think out loud, to verbalize internal monologues. Players also agree to plan with the GM present, with the (unspoken, tacit) assumption the GM will grab the best ideas and make them true within the game. This means the players often "plan toward" the GM. "Pitch" is probably a better verb here. But it's all indirect and passive and deferential to the GM's vision (see next).

* The GM keeps track of continuity and is responsible for tying up loose ends. This also includes ensuring screen time goes to the players who want it, and identifying those players who do not want it. Just good entertaining/emceeing skills, really.

* The entire roleplay experience is presented, by the GM, in as "you are there" mode as possible. We'd take explicit breaks for OOC talk, even including gestures to indicate "this is the player saying this". Other than that, once you're strapped into the driver's seat you're expected to stay there. For the GM, this aesthetic extended to providing secret information/notes to select players, sidebar 1 on 1 sessions, blindfolding "blinded" characters' players, etc.

So what are your traditions of traditional play?

    \n Those are all familiar to me, except the last one - I think that IC/OOC wasn't really that big a deal in my pre-Forge trad gaming; that sort of thing would've felt like unnecessary attention to detail that has nothing to do with the game itself. We did do information control, interestingly enough: many a session was spent sitting outside the playing room, waiting for the GM to get a solo bit sorted out with some other player. This wasn't even in the interests of player vs. player play, it was just something we did to enhance the atmosphere by asymmetric information flow.

    Speaking more generally, "the tradition" in Finnish roleplaying is quite handily defined by the set of games that were published in Finnish around the beginning of the '90s: red box D&D, Runequest, CoC, Stormbringer, Cyberpunk 2020, Paranoia, Traveller 2300, Twilight 2000, MERP and probably a couple of others that I don't remember off-hand. Most Finnish roleplayers have these games in their past, so they were very influential in defining the shared common understanding - tradition - of what a roleplaying game is like. Without large cities and a surplus of players a GM-dominated play culture was a pretty obvious thing, which means that auteurism is a big deal in Finland: the tradition allows you to do all sorts of things as long as the GM is in charge and the players are relatively passive audience to the contents of play. Obviously enough the tradition that develops in those circumstances is highly literate and ambitious, but unable to really make things happen in play - the GM has an elaborate setting and plot, often challenging genre conventions, but there's not much electricity in the proceedings of play due to how emasculated and careful the players are in participating.

    For me personally the killer in traditional gaming was the fact that the GM is responsible for the success of play and the high artistic quality of the endeavour. My teenage years were marked by a very definite striving for more powerful performance of roleplaying, which was understood in terms of more robust fiction, more complex fiction, more immersive fiction, more continuous and stable campaign - and the GM was largely the responsible for providing these matters by his technique and choice of rules and setting. This was exhausting when your co-players weren't as committed as you were, and impossible when playing with new people who didn't share the same exact history of assumptions in terms of mechanical procedures and correct ways to play. Obviously enough in hindsight deconstruction was exactly what I needed: reading the Forge and Ron's System Does Matter provided me with the tools to really look critically at the creative agenda of my activity, what I was actually doing. Stripping away false and harmful assumptions embedded in the traditional wisdom then enabled me to transform my skills and my play into something that is actually fun and sustainable.

    \n *Game play is to take place over multiple sessions, with each session being 3+ hours in length. The majority of that three hours should be actual game play time.

    *The particular roster of players should be largely constant for the majority of those sessions. The roster _may_morph a good deal if the play takes place over an extremely long amount of real time.

    * As play continues over a long period of time, playstyle and rules modifications inevitably occur until the game-as-played may have deviated significantly from the original state. The trend will be towards complexity in some form, although not necessarily mechanical complexity ( increasing setting and character portrayal complexity being equally good bets).

    [All this based on my own, divided and contradictory Love/Hate feelings about campaign play.]

    \n Interesting points, JD.

    I was going to contend that some of what you've said in your last post wasn't traditional, and discuss some things as counter examples I've heard about very early D&D campaigns run by Gygax, Arneson and their immediate inner circle.

    And then, I realized I was wrong. Because by the time that there's a "tradition" to point to, gaming has already moved significantly away from the initial style of play of the creators of the hobby at the time of its creation (including by them apparently). And it is that tradition that is making its way into game textys and reinforcing itself among later adopters.

    To be less obscure, the change in the real world social environment in which the games are being played causes a feedback loop that encourages several forms of play that are different from the initial play.

    IIUC, the initial social context (at the time of creation of D&D) is one of a largish pool of players getting together to play in the game of a referee-like DM on an erratic basis. The composition and size of any night's play group may be significantly different from the one before or after. The only really consistent group member is the DM. Players and their associated characters may be making all sorts of alliances and enemies of other players+characters.

    By the time "tradition" has become establsihed, it's getting to be more the norm to have a small, constant group of players meeting regularly and playing the same characters with the same DM.

    It's a difference between gaming club play with whoever shows up (interestingly, this style seems to hae been ressurected with modern online games like WoW) and gaming with a small group of pals. It's the second formulation which beomes in a feedback loop with tradition.

    \n My 'trad'-iest years were from 1986-1994, roughly from Grade 10 until my graduation from university. GURPS was enormously influential for us in the early days; we had started with Moldvay D&D and AD&D, but moved on because we regarded those as childish and limited. With its detailed character creation system, focus on 'realism,' and huge number of expansions, GURPS felt very grown-up to us.

    Our traditions:

    1. Spheres of authority were inviolable. Players had absolute authority over their characters. GMs had absolute authority over everything else. "Mind control" plots, or anything else that impinged on PC sovereignty, were verboten.

    2. It was forbidden to kibbutz on scenes that didn't include your character. When it's not your turn, you shut up.

    3. The GM was the owner and author of the experience. The GM got the possessive pronoun -- for instance, "Joe is my gamemaster. I play in his Marvel Super Heroes game."

    4. True player mastery was walking the fine line between my-guyism and groupwrecking assholism.

    5. "Firewalling" knowledge between player and character, or between NPC and other-NPC, was an essential skill as well. Its sneaky flipside was something I intentionally tried to practice: allow the authorial impulse to inform play of my characters such that I could direct the story in author mode from behind a veil of justifiable character knowledge. Having a character-driven alibi for defense against IC/OOC violations allowed me to hustle towards the fun. In theory.

    6. All these rules applied to all roleplaying games. The only thing that changed were the details of character creation and rolling to hit. You only read the rules to understand what the differences were, and the differences didn't really matter.

    7. Extended campaigns were seen as the highest achievement. We had a few campaigns go for years and these were spoken of with great reverence.

    8. Detail level = goodness level. The guys who were rumored to play Chivalry and Sorcery at the university -- those guys were the supreme badass hardcore gamers of the galaxy. For us mortals, a detailed, fine-grained skill list was essential, because swinging a shortsword was obviously not the same as swinging a halberd. There was no higher art than accounting for every round of ammo in your gun and every ounce of encumbrance on your person and every neuron of knowledge and skill in your dude. If you were super awesome, you used different character sheets for different modes of your character -- one sheet for evening entertainment mode, with your kevlar tuxedo and ceramic pistol, one sheet for when you were in full battle armor. Games that did not feature hit location were childish and stupid.

    The first edition of GURPS Vehicles completely beat the shit out of this idea for me. Immediately post-Vehicles, d6 Star Wars and Castle Falkenstein became my go-to games.

    9. Outright munchkinism, power gaming, and grandstanding through rules expertise were regarded as distasteful. We were there to have a fun collaborative experience with no winner, as per the play advice in Moldvay. The power leveling effect of the points-buy character creation of the 80s and 90s was a great way of ensuring equitable contribution to the game.

    10. Good characters were built, not rolled randomly, for Christ's sake, because our characters were works of individual expression, beauty, and sovereignty.

    10a. Everyone had Impulsive and Overconfident.

    11. Some players played the same character over and over again. If you did this, you were a weak roleplayer and a target for scorn.

    12. Gender-switching was weird. Habitual gender-switchers were regarded with suspicion, although occasionally one of us Serious Learned Gentlemen would attempt it as an Experiment. There was no such suspicion of GMs whose campaigns featured prominent female NPCs.

    13. Railroading was the worst GM sin. The truest roleplaying happened in what we would call 'sandbox mode' today.

    (edit: GM mastery was exhibited by anti-railroading: presenting a seamless experience, rolling with the punches, appearing like you'd thought of everything -- a blend of preparation, illusionism, and improvisation. I remember clearly this one game of that era -- maybe it was d6 Star Wars -- had a note in its GM section that said, if the players stump you, feel free to call a break so you can think about it away from a table. And I was like, whoah, you can DO that?)

    \n The "pitch" of the game is often chosen by a subset of the players. If the GM selected it to try to recruit players there will be significant modification before the game starts.

    The players will drive the game in any artistic or story sense, while the GM will handle pacing, spotlight, and keeping the players happy. The game is essentially player centric, where if a player has a difficulty it is the GMs responsibility to adjust the game to remedy while not causing other problems for the rest of the players. The GM is also organizer or host, depending on the kind of event.

    Most GM advice is bunk, it is obvious to anyone that a GM can make a campaign as much as he or she wants, but it is players that make it actually happen, so it is players who have the power in the GM-player relationship, which can sometimes turn borderline abusive.

    Table talk is fine whether chatting or giving advice. Players can relax about the game relying on the GM to make sure stuff happens, although players will often be cued by out of game indications when they are being too relaxed.

    One shots or campaigns occur, but campaigns are universally preferred because then the dreaded character creation is a single event while you still get to player your own character. Campaigns do not go on indefinitely, indeed the mark of a good GM is to keep a campaign going, the mark of a great GM is to end a campaign well (rather than suddenly because players have rebelled). If the campaign isn't fun any more, try to fix it. Barring that player rebellion is imminent and the campaign will end on its own.

    Player filtering is difficult to do directly, so its safer to let people try things and self-select out. That means letting players show up or not when there is a game. This can sometimes cause a bit of grief. Of course this flexibility is never to be extended to the GM. Likewise if the players are enjoying the game, then the GM is assumed to be enjoying the game, regardless of the truth of the matter. Sometimes this causes spontaneous problems as the GM fixes what players don't see as broken or otherwise simply a decline in GM morale.

    Prep varies considerably based on the system, the group, session plans, and the GM. Generally you don't advertise exactly how much prep went into something until after the fact, but afterward it becomes a (dubiously) valid topic for bragging in either case "I wrote up that entire dungeon" or "I ran that from three words on the back of a napkin".

    In general systems get used differently all the time, hacking, drifting, and house rules are accepted and will happen even during play. Players generally just want a level of consistency from the GM + the game, given that they prefer things to "make sense" for the game and the group so this often makes them happy. Likewise bending and sometimes whole breaking of accepted rules will occur, this may generate some bad blood in the most severe situations, but generally will be overlooked unless someone else is being directly hurt in the process.

But I've seen numerous other traditions in different groups I've played with, some of which hit some of the things other people have mentioned here, some of which are at odds.

    \n I think Matt's pretty spot-on there, concerning the usual meaning of "traditional". That word does not generally mean "original" or "oldest thing possible" - for a comparison, consider the different brands of Christianity, such as traditional Catholicism in comparison to protestant lineages that claim to follow original Church beliefs and practice from before the Catholic tradition took hold. So I don't think there's anything weird in saying that what is considered the dominant mainstream tradition of gaming today has its roots in the early '80s (Dragonlance, for instance) and had its most dominant phase in the early '90s, after which we started seeing all sorts of deconstructive protest as people found the limits inherent to trying to combine story with party-mission-challenge play.

    It's the development of roleplaying we're talking about here; a historical phenomenon, not some sort of Platonic set of idealized roleplaying schools. What we have today in terms of rpg theory and design wouldn't make any sort of sense if our current around-30s generation, us who started playing during the '90s, didn't live through the experiences we had. Just like the older guys who made the '90s game texts and led their generation got their own start with the texts and attitudes of the first generation of hobbyists.

    I think that even just looking at this humble discussion thread will make it pretty obvious that aside from fundamental features that have been present since the '70s, our generation really shares a great range of experiences in our early gaming. That's the tradition against which many of us have moved in our own gaming, simply because the tradition wasn't working for us. Perhaps it would have worked if I had different people to play with, different game texts, somebody to teach us what to do or one of many other things that make people react differently to the same phenomena - no way to know. What I do know is that the very features prominently listed in this thread are the ones that seem genuinely traditional in a historiographical sense: we did not have 50 independent designers from mid-'80s to mid-'90s just accidentally hitting on the same ideas of story-prep, GM-auteurism, character backstories, literary conceit, role-playing vs. roll-playing and supplement treadmills; rather, those 50 designers (number out of my ass, I'm sure somebody can count how many people were prominently involved) were working in intermittent communication, influenced by the same earlier game texts, going to the same conventions, selling to the same audience, and ultimately using the same language and sharing a viewpoint - if not a viewpoint on how to actually execute roleplaying, at least a viewpoint on how to write about it. That's what a tradition is, I think it would be strange to argue that these people were all designing in their closets and not taking influence from prior art and each other.

    (That thing about separating textual practice from actual play is perhaps more understandable if I note that I'm not very convinced that traditional rpg texts reflect very well the real praxis of play among the people who wrote them. At this point the hobby already had a rich textual tradition backed by furious theoretical discourse on the pages of Dragon and other magazines. A sense of how rpg texts are written and how things are discussed existed, and it seems at least to me that people were faithfully, almost blindly following the conventions of what a roleplaying game text needs to look like. This is why we get the same GMing instructions in all games, the same focus on combat mechanics, the same xp rules and many other things always the same, to such an extent that people playing these games have developed the sense that "system doesn't matter" because it's always the same system in the games they play. But that's just texts, the people themselves have played in many varieties of ways all along. I've had some striking discussions about this very thing with some Finnish traditional designers who've confessed that they don't actually even use the rules-texts they write; they just write the sort of rules they imagine their target audience to expect from roleplaying games.)

    I think that it's completely understandable if a middle-aged guy uses the term "trad gaming" to refer to early D&D/AD&D, say; that's the tradition he's familiar with. After reading some interesting OSR text critique and historical analysis (Grognardia, say) I'm pretty convinced myself that interpreting the entire pre-millenial history of rpgs as a singular evolution is a simplification; many things were cast aside in mainstream design in the early '80s, things that made way for storytelling ideas and by their abandonment paved way for where we are today. So I'm not at all surprised if a person who had more D&D in their background and in general has stayed away from story-oriented gaming through the '90s will have a different idea of what "traditional gaming" is - he's talking of a different tradition, the earlier one! Still, the fact that people can disagree about what makes traditional gaming because they have different experiences and don't talk of the historical developments in a clear manner doesn't prove that there does not exist a tradition - or several traditions, as the case may be.

    Somebody really needs to write a history of roleplaying, it's starting to be about time for that. Some work that'd take a serious shot at tracing the evolution of ideas, where lineages of influence exist and where people have started building anew. Might be an educational alternative to the relentless progressivism (thinking that old art is necessarily outdated and worse than new) and ideologism (thinking that there are static, discrete types of roleplaying, among which some are better than others) that seem to plague rpg discussions.

    \n Hmm, it seems to me that there might not be any reasonable way to debate the existence of the tradition in a short term discussion like this. The way this sort of argument is normally formulated in the practice of history seems to require a long and detailed narrative, which is then supported by relying on various proof available from the era. Nobody here is probably up to that sort of thing, so it seems that we'll have to agree to disagree here, ultimately.

    One more question to you, Jason, if you don't mind: what is your understanding, where does the Golden Rule come from, when and where was it a dominant theory of roleplaying, and where has it been most vehemently criticized? It seems to me that the Golden Rule, whether by that name or just as an attitude, is one of the more visible individual signs of the traditional mind-set in roleplaying. I'd be interested in hearing whether you think that the games and gamers who applied the rule consistently to everything in roleplaying were just a scattering of independent cases rather than a consistently developing tradition, as I've been arguing. To me the incompatible assumption of complex, nuanced rules systems and a central GM right to ignore those systems at will is a very definitive feature of traditional design, whether for good or ill, and ignoring it to claim that every game is an unique snowflake seems like outright refusal to try to understand how people play and think about roleplaying.

    As for games being different, I have not and would not argue that traditional games are all the same. What I would say is that they all share a range of important assumptions, some of which have been sketched by polling in this thread. Just like early game designs all assumed dungeon-crawling and challenge-based sandbox play, the next stage of development shared the assumption of GM-crafted plot, detailed player characters, Golden Rule and increasingly complex and varied mechanical systems. Even with these features in common there are wide vistas of potential difference between games, just like your list acknowledges - Amber and Feng Shui and GURPS and Vampire are indeed different from each other in many, many ways. This does not prevent them from also being associated in basic assumptions of what roleplaying is, what is good roleplaying and how you go about executing it. Some games might take some assumptions more to heart than others, but all of those designers knew to pay obeisance to these features that were recognized as modern, interesting rpg design at the time.

    I agree with you in that it's ultimately useful to speak of individual games and experiences with them, Jason. But ignoring the similarities between games will break down any chance of comparing experiences or even understanding the experiences of others just the same as ignoring the differences will. To be able to speak of our experiences in roleplaying as anything but consumers of product, we must be able to talk about what works and what doesn't work for us in individual games. And if we are able to do that, then we're also able to draw comparisons between games, at which point we'll notice some things, such as the fact that for some people the basic play experience didn't seem to change significantly between games during the '90s because the underlying assumptions of game design and play at the time undermined any superficial mechanical or setting design the game might attempt. Forbidding us to give a name to this phenomenon and reference it in discussion threads seem unwarranted.

    Posted By: Eero Tuovinenwhat is your understanding, where does the Golden Rule come from, when and where was it a dominant theory of roleplaying, and where has it been most vehemently criticized?
    \nThe Golden Rule, as I understand it, is:

    "If something doesn't work for your group, change it so that it works for your group."

    And it comes from an understanding of what the hobby fundamentally is - small groups of people who may have different preferences from the designer and often each other.

    I don't think it was ever a "theory of roleplaying", I think it was based entirely on the realities of the small-group tabletop hobby. For example, the Golden Rule definitely was NOT a feature of RPGA organized play or (in the 90s) massively multiplayer LARPs run by White Wolf and organizations it promoted. It is NOT a feature of MUSH play. Those games recognize that to try to accommodate the preferences of that many people would be a very bad idea and probably impossible.

    The reality of the tabletop hobby is that mass produced texts will never, ever, cover the preferences of the target audience precisely, and that customization will definitely occur, will always occur, will inevitably occur, and that can either be vainly struggled against, or celebrated. It was eventually written down because a lot of people (usually young men in my experience, but not always) were vainly struggling against it and not really grasping that a game designer 3000 miles away might not have a perfect psychic insight into the preferences of their group and might not have written the game just for them and their friends, and they might benefit from overtly changing things together.

    I can't think of a single cogent criticism against the Golden Rule that has ever been advanced against it by any person in the history of ever. I mean, I guess some people said it caused me to have brain damage, but Vampire said I was just a filthy roll-player and Gary Gygax said I was ruining AD&D by not keeping proper time sheets, so pretty much I ignored them. There were some strawmen that got knocked down, some cherrypicked quotes that were terrible, and a few rare games were truly awful at presenting it (or never really intended to present it in the first place, replacing it with the idea that the GM's view of what's best is the only view that counts and relying on people to change groups to find the GM that suited them best - sure, there were a few like that), but here's the deal.

    Before anyone ever wrote the Golden Rule down, it was already true, and it is true in all games forever, today, right now. If something in a game doesn't work for your group, your group will have more fun if you change it so that it works for your group. And something that promotes customization and diversity can never be responsible for homogeneity and dullness. Only the diverse customizers can have that responsibility.

      \n That is an interesting viewpoint, Jason, and it also perfectly illustrates how different understandings of the rpg history ultimately spring from different bases of experience and knowledge. We each have our own narratives of what's what, all based on what we know or think we know.

      For instance, you know what I would tell if somebody asked me to phrase the Golden Rule as it is intended in the tradition? Here:

      "If you the GM don't like something in the game, ignore or change it until you do."

      I do recognize that there are games that are more ambivalent in expressing this idea, but to me it is obvious that the "you" the Golden Rule is directed towards has always been the Gamemaster, not the group of players; and it has never been the group's direct benefit that is served, but rather the GM's personal vision of how the game should go. And all this is only made possible by the fundamental idea that a roleplaying game is something possessed by the GM and given for the benefit of the players. His is the responsibility, and thus his is the power as well.

      The above understanding comes to me most personally from late '80s games that do not yet write the Golden Rule explicitly - I've listed the rpgs that were most common in Finland in the early '90s; what they all had in common was the idea that the GM is responsible for content while the players are essentially passive in experiencing the GM's scheme. Some games had some little verbiage of how this is to be accomplished; the most effective advice was that it is better for the GM to have an attitude of ownership, to be ready to cheat and lie and do whatever it takes to make the experience work. His job was to make the experience right, no matter the method. I experienced the schizophrenic Impossible Thing in full force through the '90s, trying to constitute my understanding of roleplaying on this basis; the practical effect for us was that while the GM's technique and skill grew more elaborate, the window of participation, of "good roleplaying" for the players grew smaller.

      Later on, as I came to be more and more disillusioned by roleplaying as a method (even while I always appreciated it as an idea and accomplishment that seemed to work for others), I also read many game texts from the early and mid-'90s, and later from the late '90s. (I didn't play these a lot until around the millennium, just before the Forge came around.) As time wore on, we saw the game texts become more explicit about the idea that System Does Not Matter. I've been reading AD&D material from that era lately (never got into the game before the third edition, really), and even those show the sign of the times: Ravenloft books and Planescape, which I've been enjoying as literature, are all very much predicated on the idea of the game table as a theater stage with the GM the main player of the piece. All procedural instructions and theory in most mid-'90s games I've read over the years is pretty explicit about the very ideas that we've been marking as the fruits of the tradition in this thread.

      As can be seen, we see the same times and game texts differently, no doubt because we've sampled a different slice of experiences from those times. A single person can't hope to experience an entire cultural movement directly, so it's no wonder if individuals will report different details and different conclusions from their experiences. This does not have to mean that others are stupid for misinterpreting what they've experienced; the fact that you've experienced different things yourself doesn't need to be a reason to deny the experiences of others.       \n Having a GM is a good technique, I agree; most of my favourite games have a GM for the benefits it engenders.       There's one auteur-GMed game that I like, Dread. I adore how the game doesn't make any bones about being auteuristic, one-directional storytelling exercise that is not going to work unless you have a story and you want to entertain your friends by telling it.       \n I find the dichotomy of Rule Zero and Anti-Rule Zero (i.e. the rules are inviolate) is about as useful for understanding how people take a game text and turn it into what happens during play as the dichotomy of Roll Under and Roll Over is useful for understanding the design of game mechanics. I mean, sure, they are abstractly opposites and you can see some extreme things happen when either is taken as all there is, but ultimately they are each one little piece of much, much bigger puzzles.

      In the very least, "we follow the rules", or "we can change the rules if necessary", or "the GM can change the rules if necessary" are social conventions handed to the group - and extremely blunt ones at that. The truth is almost certainly real people were not playing some silly extreme version, but a nuanced consensus (or if no consensus was reached, the group likely fell apart) on the subject of what was good faith attempts to follow potentially confusing rules, what was solutions for situations outside of the rules or to address specific group needs, and what was driven by group comfort level. And almost certainly this consensus would evolve, as would the methods for achieving it.

      And while the game texts and some of the external elements (such as the expectations before playing the game) are different, I expect the methods of achieving consensus on how the game is played still evolve in using much the same methods whether you are playing a traditional or an indie game.

      I think the best thing to if we are interested in how play differs from text is to both take a hard look at how the actuality of what we do differs from how we talk about it and to try to accurately describe the tendencies of the "traditions" we have known in that vein.

      That being said, traditions with long form games will tend to have different consistency problems than short form traditions. Long form games are more susceptible to "mutation" style changes in play, as the game is played based on previous sessions rather than on the initial starting point (which given the more extensive investment may have been more carefully tied to the text). On the other hand, short form games are most susceptible to "translation" changes in play, as there is much less chance for a later correction of a comprehension error or simply a change in interpretation.

      Now if the text (or the designer) has high status then there is a strong tendency to modify toward re-interpretations of the text and if a player has high status (GM or otherwise), then there is a tendency to modify toward that player's preferences. (This symmetry is why I keep telling people to treat the game text as a player who has just left the room.) Of course you can have multiple such status issues, not to mention changes in status occurring during play. But you can imagine a tradition that holds a GM as top dog, or gives the most status to prima donna players, or to the rules lawyer can all have a distinctive effect on the dynamic.

      And none of this is even considering the case where people are actively changing the "rules" or the social dynamics. House rules, overt fudging (GM or player), rule extensions, rule bypasses, and what not are above and beyond this.

      But that caveat aside, I hope you can see how an "all players are equal" (although some more equal than others) tradition of short form games can, using the same consensus methods, arrive at very different sorts of variations on the game than a GM is highest status tradition of long form games. This will be true even if they both have a very high respect for the designer/rule book (analogous to Anti-Rule Zero) or a very low respect (analogous to Rule Zero).

      \n Accounting, I think it has to do with the idea of building up a skillset at playing a particular game.

      Everyone on my side of the debate, AFAIK, has conceded that the Golden Rule can be used functionally\u2014the group as a whole can notice a rule that's not working for them, and agree to mod it. It's in concert with a lot of other memes, memes propagated by rulebooks, mind you, that it often becomes dysfunctional in my (our) experience.

      Vernon hits the nail on the head when he mentions the original idea of GM as referee. This relates to what I was saying earlier about the initial slippage from mission-based tactical skirmish play towards more "story-oriented" play and using old tools to do new things. If the group's goal is to tell a story together, it doesn't really make a lot of sense to make one player primarily responsible for that story. But that's what was done, because it seemed like the correct intuitive leap to make in the evolution of game design.

      Another thing that the second wave of design did was to de-emphasize player skill, attack min-maxing, and encourage groups not to look up rules during session as a means of maximizing immersion or suspension of disbelief or what have you. (I was pleasant surprised when talking to the OSR guys about the importance of player skill, the idea that it should be rewarded and emphasized. It's not perhaps the same kind of skill as one can have at playing, say, BW, or even 4E for that matter, but there is a real love for it in the old-school that is not present in the "trad" school.)

      In other words, the Golden Rule alone may be fine (though I do maintain that is not an inherent part of the hobby and is a definitive signpost of one particular tradition, that which is known around here as "trad"). However, anti-page-flipping + GM-as-auteur + GM-as-god + player-skill-at-manipulating-the-system-is-bad + Golden Rule = badness, IME. Even two or three out of those five ingredients is enough for badness, in fact, and those ingredients aren't exactly rare among people whose seminal game texts include, for example, Vampire.

      ETA: Specifically, what I mean by "badness," I guess, in relation to Jesse's comments, is that a group operating under such assumptions as I outline in the paragraph above is unlikely to ever climb the learning curve of the particular game in question\u2014even when that learning curve isn't actually very steep or long! I saw that when I tried to play DRYH with a very traditional-minded GM\u2014it sucked! But DRYH isn't an especially complex game, it just requires that you engage with it on its own terms. When I tried to GM it I found it really easy to make it work just by doing what the book says to do!

      --

      There's a thin but very important line between, "The RAW doesn't cover this" and "I'm going to fudge dice results now." I've had some productive discussions with Rob Donoghue about this, who is about a generation older than me and perhaps not coincidentally on the other side of the Golden Rule divide. Before you have things like universal conflict resolution, yeah, GMs would have to make up mechanics on the fly to handle situations not explicitly anticipated by the rules. "OK, uh, Jimmy make a Craftsman check, DC 12, Sally make a Riding check, DC 16..."

      But that's very different than, "Shit, I don't want my players to defeat the bad guy this easily, I'm going to ignore that successful roll the fighter just made to hit him."

      On paper, the divide looks obvious. But in play, with GMs schooled in the idea of "their" story, the two techniques are often conflated. What's worse, they may not even use the first one honestly. For instance, they may not have anything in mind if Jimmy fails the Craftsman check.

      \n All I have to add to this thread is anecdote. I'm a bit of a statistical outlier, I suspect: I started GMing D&D in 1980, at 16, with a group of friends who were all new to the activity. A couple of years later (after the end of school) I hooked up with a different crew, and GMed for them (and hung out and lived with them) for the next 20 years.

      So I was never a tween gamer. I had a foot in the tail-end of hex-&-chit, which led me to D&D. I played one or two semi-obscure games almost exclusively throughout the 90s (not one prominent 90s game took my fancy). And most of my roleplaying experience is not of breadth but depth. You really get to understand what's going on in a game when you have a group like that.

      And yet, I read many of these comments and nod along. I would only very reluctantly play in anybody else's game from 1980 to 2000. Because I was pretty convinced that only I could run a game the way it ought to be run. I could sit in on any game and see exactly what the GM was doing \u2013 what strings he was pulling, what fudges he was fudging \u2013 and 90% of the time it shit me to tears.

      Social huddling was concomitant. With MY group I knew that MY decisions would be backed up and that MY way of playing was supported. This was also very exhausting.

      Forge deconstruction said: there is no one way to run a game. This is danger and opportunity. First, relinquish your preconceived ideas about what roleplaying is. Then try to design games that exploit particular angles of play; that codify them and drive towards them using mechanics and setting.

      I'll point to Pendragon as a game that actually did that, in a huge and rambling fashion, in the 80s. It's a game that seems to create difficulties for people in much the same ways as e.g. Dogs in the Vineyard.

      \n Accounting,

      That might be the fundamental difference between me and you and JD. I'm not talking about individual successful local instances of play. I'm talking about ideas across multiple groups.

      JD's personal activity of Vampire sounds pretty cool but it is not the product of the literal textual application of the processes laid out in the Vampire text.

      If we put three groups in three different rooms and tell them to play Vampire we might see something like this:

      Group A might have all the characters act as a team working under a prince carrying out dangerous missions. They roll dice A LOT utilizing the combat rules to the fullest.

      Group B might have all the characters hanging out at a theater they own. They're all friends but intersect with their personal lives a lot and seem to be focusing on the alienation of being a Vampire. They almost never roll dice at all.

      Group C might have all the characters being in the court of one prince but not necessarily friends. They are vying for the attention of the prince. They roll dice occasionally, mostly to resolve sharp moments of clashes but the social activity is clearly organized around pvp political maneuvering.

      This is the question I'm interested in: Which group best exemplifies the activity described in the text of Vampire. When someone walks into the room and asks me, "What does playing Vampire look like?" which group do I point to? Who is most achieving the game in its ideal form?

      The answer is none of them, or maybe all of them because Vampire does not describe a coherent unified activity. All three of these groups have either added or subtracted HUGE, HUGE non-trivial systemic properties to form *their own local activity*.

      I have no interest in putting that much work into a game. I do not want to form my own local activity (obviously to *some* extent I do, but not THAT much) around a game. I want to read the promise of a game, buy into that promise, and, when I follow the instructions, have the game deliver on that promise. And when I meet strangers who are fans of the game and we both say, "We like game X" that we are both imagining roughly the exact same thing because the game is an organized, unified activity that produces more or less the same organized results BETWEEN groups.

      And to be super clear: I'm not talking about the aesthetic content. The aesthetic content may very WIDELY between groups. I'm talking about the organization and flow of the activity.

      \nI just realized that there was a pretty big tradition that was in effect when I started gaming (*cough*mumble*) years back that I broke with early, but seems to still exist out in the world. (Even here!)

      It was assumed that if, for example, you liked to play D&D, then you would want to play D&D if you had an opportunity to do so. So when a gaming con came around, you'd register and show up and play in a bunch of D&D games. If the kid who sat two rows over in English class talked about starting a campaign, you'd ask to join. If you were going off on some kind of overnight field trip and you just happened to have your rulebooks and dice along, you'd drum up a game with whoever was assigned to your cabin. It was traditional: you are a gamer, so you want to play this game.

      ...but at some point, I started making a distinction. It wasn't so much the game that I liked, it was gaming with my friends. Where the traditional hierarchy would've gone "gaming with friends is better than gaming with strangers, which is better than doing some other activity," I started thinking of it as "gaming with friends is better than doing some other activity with friends, which is better than doing some other activity alone, which is better than gaming with strangers." And so I hopped right off the convention circuit, stopped asking to join the games of people I wasn't already friends with, and (most tellingly) stopped really caring about gaming as a big community. Don't get me wrong, I like that there is a gaming community, because I like hearing what other people are doing and learning from their reported successes and failures. I just don't expect or want to sit down and play with any of them, and ultimately I know that whatever they do has nothing at all to do with what I'm specifically interested in. I care about gaming on a personal level, not a general or universal one.

      Today, if my friends all moved away, I would pretty much stop gaming entirely until I'd gathered together a new circle of friends who were interested in gaming.

      That tradition still seems to be around, though, seeing as there are still massively successful gaming conventions going on and still people who are excited to go to them, and people who still react passionately to the idea of a big gaming community and still people who will absolutely love jumping into a session of their favorite game no matter who else is playing.

      Put it this way: I seek consent when I want to use my DM powers to put something in the game that may be controversial to the play tradition that our table has evolved.

      Mostly, this is about tweaking the numerical part of the RAW -- doubling experience, docking monsters 1 point of AC, etc. But sometimes it is about content. If a player originates some backstory that I want to run with, I always ask permission about that, and I check in while I'm running with it to make sure I'm not harshing the player's fun with my interpretation. Like, one of our PCs was estranged from a big family that I made to cause all kinds of trouble in the setting -- Dad was a temple-sacker, Mom was planning to murder Dad and destroy an entire city for the greater glory of Asmodeus. But I wanted to be sure I wasn't shitting all over my friend's creation, so I asked him if it was okay if we involved his family, and then I sequenced the game so we got little sniffs of these things during sessions, gradually revealing just what the elders were up to. All along I did ongoing checks via email to make sure I was still respecting his vision.

      Let's look at your what-monster-goes-in-the-room example. D&D grants the DM the right to populate the dungeon. This is uncontroversial at our table, and I would suspect also across 99% of D&D AP. But D&D also has rules about leveling challenges to the PCs. I started our campaign by following these rules very tightly. Our play suggested to me that we could modify these rules to offer more challenge to the PCs, so I did, after ensuring that everyone else felt like I did.

      If I step outside the spirit of those rules -- like if I put together an encounter where I suspect that the game is underestimating the challenge level -- then I mention my misgivings before we begin proper play. And if I eyeballed the session wrong and smoked the whole party, that's on me, and I'm open to any consensus that emerges as to how (or whether!) to continue the game.

      I do this because I believe that our convention is that if there are minis on the table, the challenge is leveled according to the rules the players are accustomed to.

      (Alas, my players are much more capable that I am, and when I issue such warnings, my forces generally eat it raw. *sigh*)

      To come around to the original point of the thread: some of these traditions of play are nearly universal, and some are merely very prevalent. Take, for instance, "the DM has the right to populate the dungeon." That feels pretty universal to me. If I came to AZ and ran some D&D for your crew, I wouldn't bother to ask them if it was okay if I housed the dragons with the ogre magi. They don't get input into that, not by the RAW, and not by universal tradition, and if they claimed the right to rearrange my monster condo, I'd say WTF? And hopefully roll with it from there because maybe their style of play is awesome...

      Or, say I decided that for your crew, I was going to run a game where no one ever received magic treasure. That could be controversial idea. It certainly has big play impacts, especially in 4E. 4E RAW clearly states that the DM has to award magic treasure on a tight schedule so that the party's capabilities evolve at the expected rate. Maybe that matters to your guys, maybe that doesn't -- but I'd be an asshole to just assume it didn't.

      Hell, another example: say I got down to AZ and discovered that your crew was entirely composed of Grognardia old-school-revolution types. I'd probably feel free to ignore any kind of challenge rules and sic a squadron of red dragons piloted by pit fiends on them at every turn. They're old schoolers; they understand that their characters are food for my trough.

      In summation: if I know what the universal traditions are, and what the local traditions are, and what is negotiable, and what is not, then I have a much better chance of contributing to fun for all. Thus, this thread is very illuminating to me and is a good thing, and you shouldn't worry that it's going to destroy the hobby like the Forge nearly did.

      \n The discussion about Rule Zero it's a difficult one because it mess with the identity and self-image of a lot of people.

      \n Just as a data point, I mod the indie games I play sometimes. Sometimes I don't tell the players what I'm doing, usually because I forgot to, but usually I do.

      Examples:

      I hack My Life with Master a bit for one-shot play by messing with the Reason and Fear traits during play. This accelerates end game. There's nothing in the rules that lets me do this.

      Sometimes I skip initiations in Dogs in the Vineyard, but only for the 45-minute demos I run at cons. I'd rather get to a meaty, multiple-PC conflict than spend time running four or five intro conflicts.

      When I run carry, I explicitly fail to warn players how the combat scenes work with dice balance. They give all their dice to Sarge, and I cackle and spring the surprise on them. I usually warn them at the beginning of the game that I am going to spring a surprise on them but I don't tell them what it is. This is totally dicking with them, with their partial consent. I feel it strengthens the experience of the game, especially in a demo, but I do this for full games, too.

      I change or ignore the rules, or fail to explain the rules, in order to make the game more fun for the players. I often use my social position as GM or host to force these changes. Players often give their consent, but I've tilted the balance in my favor and I feel I'm likely to get that consent. I feel the games are more fun as a result.
