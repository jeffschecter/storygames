    First Edition Vampire. How well I remember it. And how well I remember those first games. At the time we had really only played modules and module-style adventures in both Call of Cthulhu and D&D. Fun stuff, but we did it by following the rules. You remember. The party will go left at the fork in the road. If the party goes right, punish them until they go left. Now we weren't worried about "what would make a good story" then. We were just following the instructions and having a great time.

    We got Vampire and a couple of Anne Rice-loving girls wanted to join up and play. Hell yes! We were going to play. We went over to their house. I had never really cared much for Rice. I liked rollicking adventures at the time. But I immediately loved this fucking setup. Nature + Demeanor + Clan was such a cool way of describing characters, a lot like Class + Race + Kit (or whatever) from D&D. So we made characters pretty much in line with the clan stereotypes, got ourselves some modules and went and played them. I don't know if you remember those early Vampire modules. They were, often, dungeon crawls. It wasn't until someone actually looked at Chicago By Night and went "wait a second, what the hell did we do here and why is it so great?" that things seriously changed.

    Anyway, we played a dungeon crawl, and we really loved it, we got the same kind of things we got from D&D - plenty of colorful character moments, adventure, supernatural hoohah, badassery, and fighting badguys.

    We did another one. Loved that one too. We were diablerists and badasses. I was playing a Tremere (I usually played 'the wizard' in D&D, so.) and during all these adventures I was reading through the book and I came to a realization.

    So at the end of the next dungeon crawl, I snagged the Macguffin, tricked the rest of the party into staying in the dungeon (I think it was the lair of some ancient Mayan vampire or something) and dynamited the entrance, crushing them to paste and getting a total party kill. Then I went back to civilization and informed the quest-giver, I mean patron, that tragically, only I had survived to bring him the Macguffin and would - with deepest regret - be the one taking the entire payment.

    Now, remember, the GM in these games was folloinwg the instructions just like everyone else, and the instructions made clear in Vampire 1ed that the biggest liar and most deceptive backstabber would win in Vampire society, so the GM said "kudos" and gave me the reward and everyone thought that was pretty cool, and they made new characters.

    And I did it again.

    This was our group's wake up moment. We all went back and looked at the book, and for me at least, I hit my forehead and went "Doh!" Because Vampire was the first game I ever read that actually, right in its very design, took the railroad out behind the shed and shot it.

    How? Because it emphasized extreme diversity in character concepts. You could easily have a Toreador hoity toity artiste, a Ventrue mobster moll, a Tremere nerd, and a Gangrel wandering badass Wolverine clone (as I recall this was our original group). And there was no way, outside of the dungeon walls, to A) keep this group together, and get this group motivated to do something together unless you planned for it. Those words: "unless you planned for it" were addressed over and over in the text of the book, emphasizing pre-campaign communication and discussion repeatedly. It was the opposite of every game I'd played in, in which the GM would go home and work up an adventure and world, and the players would pass around the books and - usually silently, to my loud "LET ME TELL YOU ABOUT MY COOL CHARACTER" chagrin - work up their characters with no coordination or communication beyond tactical concerns.

    Hell, tactical concerns? There were a total of four combat skills! Now, we weren't blown away by having non-combat areas to cover. Call of Cthulhu did that as well. But once we grasped that "oh shit, we have no reason to hang around or like each other because we didn't design for it", that was when campaign design became important. Really important.

    It blows my mind when I hear about "railroaded" Vampire games as somehow being some kind of norm. Because that goes directly, exactly, against the very design of the game itself, and all the suggestions for how to make characters and how to create situations for them, the game would sabotage your every effort to railroad - characters were powerful, the system ensured they would succeed at virtually everything they tried... If you don't plan for cohesive characters to be doing something together, you'll be running 5 separate games, and I actually tried that for a while, and it was brilliant, but a bit unsatisfying for a group that loved to banter.

    \n Chicago by Night was a distillation of a lot of things that had been floating around RPGs for a long time. As has been mentioned in other threads, the "faction" element had been introduced in D&D modules like Keep on the Borderlands, and I had even worked up a crude relationship map to help me run those modules. Chicago by Night had relationship maps, and relationships, at the core of everything it did. I didn't at first notice this. I was looking at the character stats and figuring out how long it would be until my character was that badass. But eventually I did work out what it meant, when I designed a plot for the setting. A piece of information would flow relatively quickly and/or freely along lines on the map that were positive, and would be blocked or stymied by lines that were negative. If a piece of information ended up on one side of a negative relationship faster than it ended up on the other side, it would be used to the advantage of the person who got it first and the disadvantage of the person who didn't have it yet.

    The same was clearly true of favors, and mysterious occult artifacts, and money and influence and whatever it was that I wanted my plot to be about. Once I had that worked out, it became clear how to make my own cities and clans and plots and so on. Unfortunately, I didn't get back to Vampire until Second Edition was out, so I wasn't able to put a lot of the design work I did into practice for a while. Second Edition improved the system, improved the mechanics, but kept it the same core game. And my own interests and awareness of different approaches also improved, including the hobby that would make Vampire one of my favorite games ever: I learned how to wallow in Watergate.

    \n But before we talk about Watergate, let's back up a minute and talk about some other Vampire "issues" that came up for me between the beginning and the next campaign I actually sat down and ran.

    First, Werewolf came out. Although I couldn't put my finger on it right away, what ended up making me not like the game that much was that it lacked flexibility. It seemed, no pun, dogmatic. But I did like that they made them incredibly combat-oriented and enemies of vampires. That way, I thought, they can be seen as fearsome but not trample on the Vampires' niche, which was, as I understood it, deception. I spent the next fifteen fucking years explaining this to idiots on the Internet, that the World of Darkness had made perfectly clear from page one of day one of edition one that for Vampires, physical prowess was secondary, sometimes pathetically secondary, to the power of lies and corruption. I didn't have any problem with deciding that Werewolves existed in a game, did not exist in a game, or existed but were unimportant in a game. Again, idiots on the Internet did, and many still do. I found this bizarre and contrary to the stated intent of both games.

    Second, TEH INTERNAT. As you will know if you search any Usenet archives back around 1992-1997, I was a real college misanthropist know-it-all jerkoff on the Internet, but I did have a lot of fun because this was during the era when whenever someone would post a change to a game, a proposed alteration, a bug fix, anything, the authors of the game would leap forward to hysterically attack the contributor. It was a wild and woolly time. Giants walked the Earth then, or Michael Scott Brown did, anyway. rec.games.frp.advocacy actually was used for what it had been intended for: "NO U" style flamewars. I survived the talk.bizarre wall of flames and became an oldbie, posting a Christmas special every year. You kids today don't know how it was back then. Scientists debated with fat goth morons about whether the depiction of science in Mage was fair.

    All this fed into my approach towards Vampire (and gaming in general) as I prepared for my next campaign, where I would discover some more new strengths and some weaknesses in the game, bigtime.

    \n I've often stated on this and other forums that anyone looking at Vampire and thinking that it would give them what the virtuous young folks call "narrativism" these days must have been completely drunk. What it promised and delivered was Anne Rice-ean nonsense, with a highly flexible system at its core. And in the source material there was never any real examination of philosophical or moral issues except as a fairly blatant means of dolloping easily-digested intellectual content onto what was essentially a purple-prose Romance romance. That didn't mean it wasn't important color, but it really wasn't that much at the center of the Rice-ean mythos. Thus, the Humanity mechanic, while innovative and flexible, wasn't really the center of Vampire either.

    \n Been a busy week, here is some more Vampire-y goodness.

    The main thing I liked in the umpteen Vampire supplements I got before I ran my college Second Edition game that I really liked was that there was always some kind of new weirdness I could put in my game. There was always something bizarre and creepy which could be added for more atmosphere. Indeed, morons on the Internet tried to put everything possible into their games and were amazed that they suffocated. I never had this problem. Instead, even the really crazy supplements were fun for me because I could always pull something insane from them to throw into the game.

    And I was really enthused about the political aspects of the game, particularly since I had discovered The Presidental Transcripts, the bound collection of highlights of the Watergate tapes, and was fascinated by the venality and complete backstabbing sonofabitchedness of the Nixon White House. This, said I, was the place to go for political oomph and tomfoolery. (Go and learn.) I also discovered the very very very best "practical" political book of all time, Plunkitt of Tammany Hall, and devoured it eagerly.

    The supplements dealing with Vampire politics were essentially worthless in comparison. Anyway, i was psyched. I built a volatile situation for the city, with plenty of conflicts of interest and nastiness, and pitched a game to my pals which was going to primarily be about being "fixers", supernatural troubleshooters for the various forces in the city. I figured this would give them the most amount of cohesion, as well as freedom to poke into everyone's various secrets and corners, so that all my great prep work would eventually be on the screen. The Vampire NPCs were as awful a collection of monsters as ever populated a soap opera.

    The players seemed to grasp what I was after, making mostly lighthearted blooddrinkers capable of commenting on, and being astonished and horrified by the terrible things in the situation they were in. We didn't do much personal stuff and we didn't hammer on Humanity that much, we just wanted to mess about with the setting and have some adventures. This the game did with only one single hitch. I had pitched the game as having political implications, and one of the players looked at the character generation rules and went straight for Backgrounds. Backgrounds permitted him to buy a regular feeding ground, sufficient Status in the vampire community not to have to constantly scrape and bow, enough Resources to keep him safe from mortal scrutiny, and so on. He basically made a character who had already won Vampire: the Masquerade, and getting him out of his haven and into the thick of it was a hair-pulling nightmare. Backgrounds are cheap, and if you take what the books say about vampire politics and resources as being even remotely accurate, you're a fool not to buy as many of them as you can at the beginning of the game. Ideas for threatening them were, as I recall, even discouraged, since after all, you paid points for them and couldn't actually buy them with XP. This was a hideous design mistake, but once I recognized it, I could plan for it in the future.

    However, the game was, other than this, largely a success, demonstrating another giant advantage of the game: it's highly, highly flexible.

    \n I haven't forgotten about this thread - quite the contrary, writing all this crap about Vampire has made me want to run the game again, so I've been prepping materials for it recently, and that's where my Vampire attention has gone. I will return with tales of the 5 years of LARP that I ran. Ah, what great days those were.

    One thing I want to mention is that Vampire, throughout all editions, including the most recent, is the same game. It is exactly the same game. You have different groups of vampires, some of which you don't have any choice about being a part of and, and others, intersecting like crazy maniacal murderous Venn diagrams where you have a bit more choice, and thus every game has conflict, conflict, conflict built right into it.

    Now lots of Internet morons concern themselves with minutia like whether the first or second or revised edition of this faction or another has changed, how it has changed, and why, but frankly that is a giant waste of time. From the very first day it was absolutely clear that you could run a game with (par example) 7 bloodlines of Vampires, or 13 (there were originally 13, the legends say), or 2, or 0, or 5 with 2 of them known in the world but Not Appearing In This Game, or 4 in one faction in the city and 2 outlawed to hunting in the fringes of the suburbs, or whatever you wanted. It was blatantly obvious and clear from the start that a lot of the narrative oomf was the fact that Vampire society was arbitrary, that it was not fair, that it was not just, that it had no rules except 2: 1, the Masquerade, okay, everyone pretty much believed that, even those that lied and said they didn't, and 2: what you can get away with. This was marvelouslly freeing and let you push the aforementioned Humanity system as strong, or weak, as you wanted.

    The game has always been like this. Someone who tried to use every supplement in the game line would inevitably create a shitty game but not because any particular part was shitty, but because you can't possibly fit everything in and have a feasible game. There was too much in the first damn volume to fit into one game! Having realized this when I was a sophomore in high school it has baffled me the rest of my gaming career how someone could be so fucking idiotic as to think Vampire was anything other than a toolkit for making Vampire games. But plenty of people have, and they struggle and fight to fit every single piece into some kind of cohesive whole and give that to the players. Nobody ever told them to do that and plenty of times they were told not to.

    Anyway, there will be more later as time goes on, but right now I'm working a bit on an actual game!

    \n What I learned from running a Vampire LARP for 5 years.

    Tucson had the longest continually-running, no-reboot Vampire game in the world a lot of the time. We started right when the first LARP rules came out, kicking off the group with a Con game.

    What I learned at that very first Con game was that even with very clear objectives and a crushingly simple plotline, the multiplication of factions can make any plot have all the twists and turns necessary to satisfy. The plot at this, my first LARP, was about the murder of a Vampire and the recovery of a computer disk reputed to contain mysterious occult writings on it. There were three main power groups seeking it, one smaller group attempting to bury it, and my group, which was opposed to all of them. Two of the main power groups allied early on, and crushed most of the opposition, but the smaller, bury-the-disk group got this idea and sent someone offsite to buy a computer disk, which they then marked appropriately, and sold to the alliance. After the alliance would check the disk (on a real computer set up in someone's hotel room), and discover it was blank, they would set it aside, whereupon I would sneakily palm the blank disk, go back out into the hotel and sell it (or give it) to someone else, who would think they had the game-winning mcguffin and would run back to the alliance to check it out, they'd check out the same disk again, exclude it, I would grab it again, and go seed it again, over and over. It was hilarious - especially once they figured out it was me. I can still remember the hotel door closing behind me while a female voice, the light dawning, yelled, "Wait..WAIT, STOP HIM!!"

    The Vampire LARP book had a lot of rules we considered very strange. For example, service to the game (hosting, setting up, cleaning up, etc. ) was rewarded with your character gaining Generation, which in the tabletop game was a powerful indicator of how far your Vampire was from the original vampire progenitor and could not be changed without embarking on a truly despicable deed to destroy a more powerful vampire and absorb their soul. We could grasp giving in-character rewards for doing out-of-character things - what else did a game have to give? - but Generation seemed totally bizarre. A Vampire embraced by another could soon have a lower Generation than the sire did if they helped the game a few months.

    So we made some changes - service to the game would be, for the moment, its own reward. We didn't have any shortage of people willing to help. Most important, we made the conscious decision that it would be a fairly low-powered game, in terms of starting characters, even for those claiming important roles in the city. Everyone was vulnerable, everyone had come to Tucson from far away, usually because they got screwed in those towns. And because we weren't handing out Generation, the most powerful characters and the least powerful would not be very different. We created an initial situation with two warring factions tussling it out for leadership and launched the game before we really knew what we were doing. It exploded like a beautiful, backstabbing firework. It was gorgeous.

    A few problems immediately presented themselves and, with more luck than skill, we hit on solutions that were astonishingly above our level of competence and the level of most people who ran LARPs in those days:

    1 - Shared Universes. There were at least four shared universes that came knocking to ask if we wanted our game to join up with theirs. We always said the same thing: "Fine, but we keep complete control over our game, you can't bring your super-powered character here and expect us to just let them walk in." and we mentioned we didn't hand out Generation for game service and that was the end of that. Even when White Wolf launched its "official" shared universe, we didn't even really look at it that closely, we only noted that their guidelines would have made virtually everything good about our game completely impossible, and moved on.

    2 - Visitors. A similar situation. Visitors from other towns/games who wanted to play their character were politely told we would be rewriting them or giving them an NPC to play. Only one visitor out of perhaps 20 over 5 years elected to have his character temporarily powered-down to play in our game and he told us it was approximately a trillion times better than playing his character in his home game.

    \n The NPC thing was equally problematic, because nowhere in the books did it tell people to do what every other LARP on Earth apparently thought was required. If you have too many GMs, the books said, give them a NPC to play, but it did NOT say "and make them super duper powerful". We read it and thought, "Oh, this is our chance to show off how great the PCs are, and would inevitably make NPCs that were the minions, contacts, allies, and/or snivelling enemies of particular PCs, so they could get a bit brighter spotlight - the only conceivable purpose of having GMs, we thought. When we did hand out superpowered NPCs, we gave them to people who were at the game for the first time, and made sure that all the PCs had some relationship with them, something they wanted, or didn't want, or something. That way they had a constant wave of PCs giving them attention and they would want to stay.

    (We also had our "twink inoculation" characters, for new players who insisted on playing a super-duper-mega-powered character ("twinks", from online parlance). We would say, for example, "Caine and all the Third Generation vampires are already assigned, would you like to play Merlin?" and give them a character who was deluded into thinking they were the reincarnation of a powerful magician. This was always one hundred percent fun and the player would never ever feel bad that we didn't let them play their mega-powerful characters ever again, and their recurring character would be of the appropriate level and they would jump right in.)

    Visitors tended to fall into the first category - the ones we wanted to assign support NPCs to. The ones that agreed walked out with their minds blown, since they were treated in a way they never got to experience in their home games. Imagine being a part of an improv troupe but you never ever get to play anything but the straight-man authority figure. Then you show up one day and suddenly you're the wacky taxi driver. It's relieving and eye-opening.

    \n I am baffled as to the division between setting design and game design. Setting design is crucial, absolutely crucial to game design, especially if you're using a task resolution system and a central authority for the setting. Strong setting design is strong game design. It just is. Settings have to be playtested, they have to be worked on, good settings just don't "happen", good settings are hugely important and without them there's no way you can get good characters and no way you can get good conflicts. Even setting-building games like Primetime Adventures recognize this - you have to establish the world the characters come from and live in so that you can see where they're going and why. As story generation material, what Vampire gave you, whether it was dice or decision or central authority and players playing a character, was pure gold.

    If the mechanics are bad (and they sure as heck weren't good in 1st Edition, they were better in 2nd, still better in Revised and now are quite slick in Requiem), okay. That is a good topic. As I noted there were places where the mechanics fucked up the game a bit (underpriced Backgrounds for the importance they were supposed to have). There were other mechanics that were problematic (the designers themselves seemed to forget about tenets of the system like "2 successes is a complete success" later in the life of the game). Still other mechanics (Humanity, Generation) were brilliantly tailored to the game and very effective in a wide variety of ways. You say these were modded for what individual groups did as if this was some kind of bad thing about them. What's the alternative? A system you can't change without screwing things up? If that's the pinnacle of good design, holy shit, sign me up for another hobby, like being hit with sticks over and over.

    The "how you roleplay" stuff was actually very strong in the essays of both 1st and 2nd Edition's essay section. It had to be, plenty of people were buying the game that were never interested in D&D. It didn't talk much about the system, but why would it? The system was task resolution, it was going to tell you whether you did what you wanted, it didn't tell you how to decide what you wanted, that was the roleplay part. It included ideas for troupe play, rotating GMs, GMless play (especially the LARP rules did this), all sorts of weird optional things you could try. To say it was "my" Vampire that was cool and not "theirs" is to deny that they accomplished what they set out to accomplish - to give people the tools to run something like I ran. What other conceivable purpose was there for task resolution, GMs, and players with singular characters that were "theirs"? If they wanted me to run a game a particular way, why didn't they eliminate the GM position entirely?

    There's of course plenty of things that could have gone better, as I noted. But it was still just a really terrific game.

      \n Here's another interesting thing - "crossover" World of Darkness play. Now this was primarily an issue in the old World of Darkness. After the Requiem reboot, there's been explicit support all over the place for different sorts of supernaturals to team up, and the newly unified mechanics mean that it's as balanced as you can reasonably expect it to be. But back in the day, there was this giant urge to put different supernatural sorts into the same game, either as antagonists or protagonists. I found this to be problematic because, as I noted above, they all had niches, and White Wolf was very good at making the niches relate but not really overlap. In other words, my Vampire character and your Werewolf character wouldn't really hang out and do things together even if there wasn't inherent hatred, because the Vampire character is blind to the spirit world the Werewolf character thinks is the center of things and the Werewolf character is at about the fourth grade level of deception and betrayal whereas the Vampire character, even if they're not any good at it, at least have a college degree in the fields just from being in the world Vampires live in. There just wasn't much in common there to build a campaign from even if you wanted to.

      Oddly, one of the books that addressed this most directly, Dirty Secrets of the Black Hand, was reviled by most players and even later developers. I thought it was quite good for what it did - it created a global creepy/occult problem, and positted a cross-species conspiracy working to address the problem. It showed how emphasis on tone (gothic + Hellraiser) and careful planning of what everyone brings to the table can overcome the "we have nothing in common" issue. Nobody else seemed to see it that way.

      I'm going to show you how I DID use it. Then afterwards I will show you how I NOW use it.

      I generated a quick R-Map of a fictional game to help with this example:



      This is a really simple game setup, for a game with only modest political content. Why? Because there is only one central power struggle and - most important - it only has two players, McNulty and Glossop.

      Another thing you can see is that this city is of significant importance to Vampire society. These are pretty powerful vampires. However, there is a lot of mobility and involvement of Kindred of lesser power, so this may be the very pinnacle of Kindred power in the city. (There's even a character right in the middle there, Bauer. who could be a stand-in for the PCs, a 10th Generation Brujah is very easy to make with normal character creation.)

      The two power players each have one group or person completely in their pocket. (Bauer and the Brood). They each also have someone that they have a bit of a shaky relationship with - Glossop knows her relationship with Josef is shaky, but McNulty doesn't know Burton is not completely in his pocket, giving Glossop a bit of an edge in "seeing the board". However, McNulty has the edge insofar as in the current setup, he's still got Burton playing on his own side (not to mention his Brood would almost certainly be underestimated by any of Glossop's plans).

      What relationships are stable and which ones are tenuous? McNulty/Brood is stable. McNulty/Glossop is stable. Glossop/Bauer is stable. Josef/Burton is stable. Burton/Bauer is stable. That's it. McNulty/Burton is tenuous because Burton is looking for a way to get out from under McNulty's thumb. McNulty/Josef is tenuous because McNulty hasn't exerted the full measure of control over Josef.       The above sort of R-Map is what I've described in previous threads as a "glass house and a pile of rocks". It's actually fairly stable. You can play an entire game without that R-Map changing. And that's how I approached it based on what I saw in Vampire supplements like Chicago by Night. The R-Map is what it is. The things that created these R-Maps are spread over decades of Kindred existence and there is not much that is going to happen in the scope of a game to affect it. (That was never in the text, but neither did it say that these relationships would change, so that's how I thought of it at the time.)

      So even with that restriction, what did I do with these R-Maps? Here are some examples:

      * The McGuffin

      Plenty of Vampire plots involved some magical McGuffin which someone wanted or did not want others to have. This is cool because it gives PCs something concrete to seek, destroy, distribute, use, etc. However, because of the way the line got started, a LOT of these plots were fucking lame. I believe the phrase I used on Usenet was: "If the Vampire plot you are in involves the acquisition of a 'magic item', RUN, do not walk." There were a few exceptions, naturally. The Tremere had to be involved in magical stuff or they were sort of pointless, for example.

      Anyway, let's say there's a McGuffin that is going to be the focus of our game. For whatever reason, the PCs acquire it and give it to Glossop. Who knows or can reasonably guess Glossop has it? Follow relationships that involve exchange of information. Bauer and Josef. Josef, as a Tremere, would be highly interested and may even have more information on the McGuffin than Glossop herself. I decide what Bauer and Josef think of Glossop having the McGuffin: Bauer is okay with it, Josef opposes it. Josef and Burton discuss the matter (their Iron Alliance allows them to trust each other, and Burton might be able to confirm Josef's information via Bauer) and together they hatch a plan to get it from Glossop. The R-Map has given me the questions I need to answer to get to the next step in what happens.

      Now, that's how I did it at the time. I looked at each character, worked my way back to the person who had the McGuffin, and (separately from the map) figured out what they would know and what they would think about it, and (back on the map) who they trusted to achieve what they wanted.

      These days I handle it a little differently because I'm okay with the R-Map changing, and in fact I want it to change over the course of the campaign. So I built this little rules extension (OH NOES TEH GAEM IS BROKAN) Basically I look at each character of interest and I say "All right, how bad do they want this thing?" There are 6 characters (the Brood is indistinguishable at this point), so I assign them a number from 1-6. Normally I would do this by looking at each character and thinking about their history, goals they think the McGuffin might help them achieve, desire to keep it out of the hands of others, etc. For this example, let's just randomly assign them in reverse alphabetical order:

      I should note that the R-maps in Vampire are different from a lot of others that I've seen or prepared in other games, they tend to define a very static situation. That is, if nothing happens, the situation is going to continue as it is, it's not going to tip over from anyone's actions. Virtually all the R-maps given (and implied, when they were not explicitly prepared) are like this. That can hold you back if all you're looking at is the R-map and trying to figure out what sort of plot to introduce. It should be done the other way around. A plot should be introduced and then applied to the "static" R-map, which is then a tool for figuring out consequences, like rolling dice in the skill system is a way of figuring out consequences at that granularity level.

      If you are more open to the R-map changing, as I eventually became (and as I think certain aspects of Vampire suggest they should), then you start to focus on different things on the map...but I'll get to that in a bit.

      First let's talk about PCs on this map.

      Now, the "default" for Vampire is a group of characters who have a common interest, usually from different clans and usually with different specialties. This is a natural "party" for Vampire due to the ridiculous amount of backstabbing in Kindred society and the crappy position of most PC-level Vampires. That said, they don't stay together and not all of their interests are necessarily contiguous.

      The first and easiest way to use the R-map, both as a player and as a GM, is to leave the PCs off the map entirely. To some degree this is supported not just by Vampire but by a huge amount of "team of specialists" fiction and other games. They are considered plot elements like McGuffins and events outside the control of the characters on the map, and identifying, evaluating, and responding to their actions are the same as they might to, say, the FBI showing up in town to shine flashlights on exsanguinated corpses. This is how I started out using PCs. This has the advantage of being quick and easy and you can just make a list of PC actions that need to be responded to along with other game circumstances and just go down the list. The disadvantage is that again, this is a static thing and if the PCs want to affect the situation they are beating their heads against this document behind the GM's screen. (Or a document they themselves can see.)

      The second and somewhat harder way to use the R-map, though certainly different, is to spread the PCs out around the outside of the R-map relatively evenly, say, if we had 4 PCs, one would be up above McNulty, another below Glossop, another to the left of Burton and another to the right of the Brood, far enough out that we could get lines from the NPCs to the PCs. All lines go outwards - how the PCs respond to the NPCs is entirely the work of the players. The advantage of this method is that the emergent properties of who-knows-what and who-does-what can now include the PCs to their (and other NPCs') surprise. The disadvantage is, first of all, it's fucking unwieldy if, like with my group, you had one PC who was really interested in going around and cultivating a relationship with everyone, others who were interested in one NPC and not many others, and some who were pretty disinterested in the whole politics thing altogether, and did their best to stay separate and not get involved in anything other than the tasks at hand. Basically this mean that only one player was getting any benefit from the R-map and everything else was filtered through them. Not ideal. But if you have a group where everyone is interested at similar levels, this is a good way to handle it.

      \n Okay, eventually, though, I did come to think (particularly when PCs were actually attached), "these relationship maps should be able to change". Nothing told me differently and there were some bits of text that told me they should, though I had never really given them much thought before.

      I did it sort of haphazardly until I started becoming a DVD commentary track junkie. One of the things I heard from several different actors and writers stuck with me: never say, "that character wouldn't do X". Eventually you have defined yourself into a corner, the character will never do anything. Instead, decide what you want the character to do and say "under what circumstances would the character do X". By contrast to 'wouldn't do', this is freeing, it opens up possibilities instead of closing them off. Taking this insight back to the Vampire relationship map, here's what I came up with:

      1 - Pick a relationship on the map (both ways, if it has two directions).
      2 - Under what circumstances would that relationship change to what I want it to be?
      3 - What would those circumstances do to other relationships on the R-map?

      Example:

      I want the Brood to break away from McNulty, to hate him ineffectually while he disregards them. I look at the relationship and figure, "He would have to gain some permanent, lasting advantage in some area to offset that this collection of vampires would go from being his willing servants to outcasts". The permanent, lasting advantage might be, say, hooks in one of Glossop's trusted underlings (not necessarily on the map) who could tell him Glossop's plans and next move. Perhaps by framing the Brood for something this underling did he could obtain blackmail material, and everyone would trust that he wouldn't accuse his own Brood without the evidence being overwhelming? Yes, that works nicely.

      What's interesting about changing the R-map in this way is that, in contrast to the above analysis method, it's plot-generative, rather than plot-reactive. You work backwards from your target to how to aim at it.

      \nDon't worry, this is a GREAT question for anyone interested in "simulation" in their games in the normal everyday walking around sense of the word. And it's got a lot of different answers that go a long way past Vampire, to every extent of the GM-runs-the-world milieu.

      Sometimes something happens and nobody ever knows but the GM. Hell, a lot of times this happens with facts in mystery games that just aren't uncovered by the PCs. Have you ever finished a Call of Cthulhu scenario and still had some player handouts behind the screen? Sure you have.

      There's not always an intention associated with it - sometimes the players just don't go where you have a detail generated, aren't as interested in uncovering the details as you were in simulating/generating them, or decide that the thrust of what they're after is in a whole different direction. If, say, on this R-map, I throw:

      * The fallout of a Masquerade violation committed by Bauer.
      * A magical dingus.
      and * The Sabbat is in town targetting assholes who wear black tennis shoes and talk on their cellphones in movie theaters.

      I'll quite easily get a lot of plot developments that just won't ever hit the "screen" because of time, let alone anything else.

      The benefit that these offscreen details have is, in a way, prophylactic. The players (GM included here) feel the world is real, because if they keep pushing in a direction, they'll find more details, every development has an in-world reason for it, and you never kick over a cardboard cutout standing in the background of your game. They might not see every single detail you generate, but it's more than just an inner smile of satisfaction nobody else gets to share (and it's at least that, in a lot of cases), you're preparing for many player options.

      There are ways the players find out about these background things, though:

      "Cutscenes" in which players take on the roles of NPCs experiencing the events of the change. (Vampire suggested these in, I believe, the Revised Edition Storyteller's Guide, so fairly late in the process - but in First Edition, they suggested troupe play, which included multiple point-of-view characters for each player.)

      A post-game recap (mentioned above, particularly a good practice for our LARP where far too many details were being generated in each session for any one person ever to figure out what was happening without a recap)

      GMs can push their favorite details into the fiction in various ways. Vampire never suggests this, though I've experienced it before, and Dogs in the Vineyard's GM advice put it into text for the first time (for me), with the stuff about just having NPCs come up and lay it out for the players and figure out why later.

      There's probably a lot more ways to look at this....

      Vampire did have the problem of being "5 games of Vampire" if you have 5 characters that are created without communication or consultation or a clear campaign idea.       \nYeah, it was the Humanity thing, here's what I discovered in the Humanity rules (I don't know if this is what I am supposed to find or not): losing Humanity is a strong motivation to seek to make amends. In other words, it's much easier to regain Humanity and stave off your dissolution by specifically making up for the shit you did than it is by just generally being a good person. That means when you're a GM, you have to constantly think about the consequences of someone's action, really trace it out, because if they come back and say, "I want to make up for it", you want to have a detailed situation that they can see their own cold dead hand in.

      The main method of personal horror in Vampire is this:

      Acting inhumanely, i.e. murdering people for political gain, is the fastest and most effective way to achieve your goals. Doing things nicely and diplomatically is less likely to work and virtually impossible to make stick against people who are willing to act more inhumanely than you. I don't know if this is ever explicitly stated, but I think it's implicit in the rules and setting.

      Vampire really suffers if you let an outbreak of Reasonable NPC Disease develop, or even too many "cold and calculating" NPCs who will rationally tote up their best interest and never send a bunch of assholes with gasoline cans to set fire to your house. Actually this was a great strength of the citybooks. Even in the worst of them, there was always some fuckhead ready to go over the edge because you didn't sufficiently praise their begonias.

      I've been thinking about this over the past little while, trying to work out why whenever we picked up our Vampire books, we never said "Okay, let's do a horror game!" And I can't speak for the other people in my gaming group, but for my part, I think that on some level I looked at the tools in the box and thought, "Ah ha, there is a hierarchy here, with lots of different organizations and lots of different goals, and some very pragmatic rules that everyone more or less must adhere to. That looks like fun!" And oh yeah, there's this personal horror angle, too, but that doesn't seem to be nearly as cool or as fraught with interesting play opportunities.

      But then there's Wraith, and that produced almost exactly the opposite reaction in me. I read Wraith and thought, "Look at all the fantastic, horrifying ghost stories you can tell with this! Everything that a ghost cares about is personal!" and shrugged my shoulders at Stygian politics, immediately classifying it as being less interesting and less relevant and clearly Not What The Game Was About.

      And Changeling, where again, my first reaction upon reading the books was "Holy shit, these guys are SO SCREWED. What a terrible place they're in, look at all the horrors that surround them, imagine all the stories you can do with that," but didn't connect much at all with Court politics.

      But why? Why did my brain look at three toolboxes which are more-or-less equivalent and say "Vampire games are about politics and intrigue, Wraith and Changeling games are about horror"?

      My preliminary conclusion so far is that I think I see wraiths and changelings as victims: something was done to them against their will, and now they have to deal with the aftermath of that. Their lives were derailed and they've been thrust into a bad situation and it's not their fault. But vampires are victimizers: yes, something was done to them, but now in order to survive they have to do pretty much the same thing to other people, forever. So while a wraith or a changeling could feel bad about their lot and the stories of how they deal with that could go lots of different directions, a vampire doesn't get that luxury: you have to grow some pragmatism FAST when your continued existence requires you to drink the blood of the living. Bemoaning your curse of undeath starts to ring a bit hollow when you're actively hunting down new people to assault.

      So maybe that's why whenever I picked up Vampire, my mind grabbed at the Realpolitik and the philosophy and the grasping, needy hunger in all things that (to me) epitomized vampiric existence, and scoffed at the "personal horror." I didn't want to get personal with those guys. The kind of personal stories that I could think of for a vampire seemed to be either tediously depressing, or hypocritical mopery.

      Today I can think of some other options for how to do a campaign focused on personal horror in Vampire, but...when I think about it honestly, I would still prefer to put my main focus on how vampires deal with each other, and keep how vampires deal with themselves as a minor part of the game. It's just how I'm wired, I guess.
