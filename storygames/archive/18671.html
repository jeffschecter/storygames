\n So I've been playing more of both Monster of the Week and Dungeon World, and I've been pondering about teamwork - and wondering about how other people approach it. I'll posit a semi-hypothetical example, simplified from my actual campaign...

The party encounters a single big, armored monster - an enormous alien centipede. The PCs are big armored fighter Gwen, sneaky thief Rat, and Thistle the druid. Gwen steps forward for sure to attack it. Rat and Thistle are not as good in fighting - they have less damage and less armor. Fighting the centipede, they would take more damage than they dish out. The question is, what do Rat and Thistle do?

There's the "Mob" approach, where everyone rushes the monster. There's the "Tag-Team" approach, where Gwen fights the monster one-on-one, then tags out when she runs low for someone else to step in. And there's the "Backup" approach, where Gwen fights and the others rally around and help her attacks. \n
    \n
  • The "Mob" approach is presumably everyone doing Hack-and-Slash moves. Rat and Thistle will take a lot of damage by doing this. It might be the quickest in some sense, but it probably multiplies out the total damage they take.
    • \n
  • The "Tag-Team" approach means that Rat and Thistle aren't rolling at first while Gwen does Hack-and-Slash, then they switch in to do their own Hack-and-Slash. Gwen takes the brunt of damage, but the total damage everyone takes is probably less.
    • \n
  • The "Backup" approach means that Rat and Thistle are rolling Aid moves. This is a little less risky than Hack-and-Slash, but they are still risking damage - and their success is a +1 to Gwen's roll rather than their own damage.
    • \n
\nIn practice, we have been doing the Mob approach for big monsters - but I think there's something to be said for the Tag-Team approach. How do other people approach this - either as MC or as player?

    \n Big armored centipede? I think we would run mostly. Or maybe stay waaaaaay back and try to set it on fire. Certainly I don't think anybody would be keen to go toe-to-toe with it. Trying to Hack N Slash something like that really only makes sense if you have some way to keep it from hitting you right back. If it only has pincers at one end, I can imagine somebody trying to lasso and pin its head or jaws while the others try to attack it from behind or maybe even underneath, where it's less likely to have armor. In general, though, I tend to think of DW fights as much looser and less "each player makes a move in turn" than what you're describing above.

    It might go:

    P1: I'll try to lasso its pincers and horns with my rope.

    GM: Cool. That's probably Defy Danger with DEX, with the danger being you get its undivided attention.

    P2: I want to help!

    GM: Okay, how do you help?

    P2: I'm going to try to get its attention by banging on my shield, so it won't see the rope coming.

    GM: Sweet, roll them bones.

    P1: An 8.

    P2: +1 on Aid... so still a 9.

    P3: Crap.

    GM: Your rope is totally caught between it's horns and mandibles. It yanks back and pulls you slightly off the ground, but its head is caught for now. Your tough choice, though is... do you yank back on the rope and get it's attention, or do you let it focus on chewing through the rope or focusing on one of your companions?

    P1: Well, I'm a self-interested thief, so... sorry you guys.

    GM: It's going after the one making all that racket. Giant rope-tangled head coming right at you; what do you do?

    P2: I raise my shield to bash it in the face!

    P3: While it's head's down, I stab it in its many eyes with my blade!

    P1: It's lowering its head? I'll reel in all the rope and tie it to one of the stone pillars, to try to hold it in place!

    GM: Awesome. Let"s handle those in order. P2, roll Defend.

    P2: 10+! Sounds like I might need this 3 hold.

    GM: Probably! The centipede's metal-reinforced head slams into your shield. Roll 2d6+2 damage and let's hope it doesn't break your arm.

    P2: 12 damage, ouch. I spend one hold to halve that to 6, and then [uses armor and shield to reduce a bit more]. I also spend one to open the centipede up to an ally.

    P3: Nice, so then I stab its eyes!

    ETC.

    There's basically not a lot of OOC planning that happens once a fight starts. It's moment-to-moment decision-making based on whatever crazy stuff is happening in the fiction at the moment. Sure, the players can try to plan things ahead of time, but once things actually get moving, they're reacting to and building on things that have just been said. Sometimes they're all doing different things at once, sometimes they're helping one another, sometimes they're doing stuff that involves formal moves, sometimes they're just doing stuff in the fiction that doesn't require a move. Honestly, it doesn't really sound much like any of the models you're describing, but maybe I'm just thinking about it differently.

    The reason I wrote out the example, is that (1) everything comes from the fiction, so it's difficult to talk about without that context, and (2) to show that there are attacks that aren't Hack N Slash (the rope using Defy Danger, the shield bash using Defend, an infinite number of other things) and helping that isn't Aid (both the rope thing and the cooperation between the shieldbearer and the stabber).

    \n Yeah, the "melee" part is meant to mean "they're fighting back, toe-to-toe with you." The trigger specifically doesn't mean "when you attack someone close to you" (but unfortunately the wording of the move kind of looks that way).

    If there's ganging up, for most monsters, they can only be "in melee" with one opponent at a time, and that PC rolls hack-and-slash. Everyone else just does damage. Intelligent monsters will try to avoid this situation, naturally.

    And the reverse is true for PCs who get ganged up on.

    And the details of the situation determine all this, of course. Sometimes a monster can melee with multiple opponents because of the terrain or their size or speed or weapon(s) or whatever. Sometimes they can't melee at all. Sometimes they get to choose who among the group they engage with, sometimes the PCs are in control of that, etc. etc.

    \n The "maybe" depends on the fiction.

    - Is the hallway wide enough that one of the orcs can circle around and come at Gwen from the other side? Then she probably just takes damage from that one unless she has a special move or something that lets her engage with two opponents at once, or describes some way of keeping both of them off her. Maybe she has a sword and a spear, for instance, and can use the spear to keep one of them at length while chopping at the other with her sword. Might be Defy Danger to try to do that effectively!

    - Is Gwen able to manuver such that one orc steps in front of the other and gets in the way of the other one joining in (might be a DEX move of some kind to do that)? Great! Then, for the next move or so, she only has to worry about one of them at a time!

    - If she can't manage to do either of those or something else, then the 2nd orc just hits her, right? Not much she can do about it. But she could try to Defend herself instead of Hack N Slash and, depending on the situation, might have a better chance of surviving on a 10+, since she could halve the harm from both opponents. Or she could run. No reason to insist on fighting when you're outnumbered or overpowered.

    AW and DW have all those question-asking moves, right? Part of the reason they exist is to help you interrogate the fiction more to figure out what's actually happening and which moves are possible. But even if you don't use the question moves, you still have to interrogate the fiction until you figure out what's actually going on. Can a person Hack N Slash a dragon or multiple enemies at once? Maybe, but we have to examine the fiction closely to see if that makes sense to us. Often times, the answer will probably be "nope."

    \n I agree, it's hard to describe on the internet. JWalt's description is super helpful, though! That's about as good as you can get.

    Don't you find it hard to be consistent with this kind of thing, though? OK, Gwen can roll Defy Danger and if she succeeds, she can just fight one of them at a time, at least for now. (For how long? Until the MC makes a move and decides to change that situation. That could vary a lot, especially if other characters were involved and the turn-taking wasn't so obvious.)

    What if she was facing three of them? Four? What if she only had a *short* spear? What if the corridor was slippery? Etc.

    I can't put my finger on it, but it seems to me that there's something about the whole model of Player move - MC move - Player move, roll - MC move, the AW conversation, which works against standardisation and against a clear representation of risks. It's much more art than craft.

    For instance, if the corridor was slippery, the MC might be inspired to say she falls down ("put someone in a spot") as a Hard Move. However, in a regular, non-slippery corridor, the MC wouldn't say that. She might have them deal damage, instead.

    So, what? Now fighting in a slippery corridor is somewhat safer, because you might fall over and delay the death blow by another "turn" in the conversation?

    This works really well in AW or Monsterhearts, where the art of handling the conversation and the moves is used to create drama and tension between characters: lots of unpredictable, rapidly snowballing situations. But it seems to me that it's very hard to try to infuse that conversation with elements of consistency and measurable risk: it's resistant to attempts to do so, because that's not what it's designed for.

    \n Hey Paul,

    You know what? It is hard to be consistent about those threats. And that's actually ok. It's an acquired skill.

    You know what else is an acquired skill? Having monsters use appropriate tactics in a game of D&D 4.0 or 3.0. I keep wanting to do things like... Make monsters attack into squares that they don't know about. Or make my zombie horde attack in intelligent convert, and mob the clerics. Or take the player's stats into account even though the monsters might not be able to tell the plate wearing cleric apart from the plate wearing fighter. Or sometimes I want to move into countering the player's usual tactics, even though the enemies don't know what the player's would be doing.

    Also, I have this tendency to have my monsters move in perfect concert.

    And, over time, I got used to having the monsters use appropriate tactics. I got ready to think of things from the monster's points of view. I got better at doing things faster in ways that the players have more room for different tactics.

    And, extrapolating from my time running Apocalypse World, I suspect it's the same thing.
