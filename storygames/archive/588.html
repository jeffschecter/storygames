So, Ben Lehman spent much of the morning trying to convince me to work the Forge Booth at GenCon, but that's not what this post is about.

His next goal was to convince me to start a new online arena (forum, etc.) to discuss and promote inclusivity and greater diversity in both the roleplaying community and in the styles of play being practiced (since one would imagine that these are somewhat connected, though not in a 1-to-1 way).

A transcript of this part of our conversation is posted here:
http://thou-and-one.blogspot.com/2006/04/ben-can-convince-you-to-do-anything.html

To summerize:
- How do you create a space to talk about inclusivity and diversity in roleplaying?
- If you let everyone in, the people who are normally excluded from these conversations will continue to be excluded, because they will not feel welcome.
- If you limit the people you let in, to create a more diverse space, you run the risk of really being exclusive, and only allowing the voices that you're comfortable listening to.

Do we really need an open forum or a think-tank? Or something in between?

This is sort of a question of unrestricted capitalism (something like RPGnet) vs. a dictatorship of the proletariate (something like 20x20). Surely there are new fangled ideas and tools we can use to find some sort of middle ground, here, because neither of these really sound like they'd accomplish our goals. Story Games itself is more like the modern US economy, mostly free market, but with regulations in place which hopefully ensure that people are more-or-less fair to each other. This is AWESOME (I love you, Andy!), but not exactly what would be needed in this case.

How do we get the roleplaying forum equivilent of say, Sweden? Well, Sweden with more diversity. Or, say, the Carribean, where people mostly chill the fuck out and talk about the One Love. Obviously, large amouts of hash are not the only thing they have going.

Also, in a somewhat related issue: if you're interested in participating in such a forum, what would you want it too look like?

    Wow. Uh, this is a tough one.

    I'm going to agree with Brand and point out that I think there are two different things going on here - and having them happen in the same place may be complex.

    Having a community where some of the people who don't feel comfortable here can get together and talk is great. But setting the topic of conversation as "Let's talk about diversity" as opposed to "Let's talk about role-playing, which just happens to be happening with a more diverse group and different ways of talking to each other" is just going to create a "diversity ghetto," so to speak. It's going to send the message that these people, who want to have a discourse about role-playing with different topics and community norms and standards, are outsiders as opposed to peers working together. I don't know if that makes sense, but it would be a concern to me. I would want to be very careful about sending the message that what outsiders get to talk about is their status as outsiders and why that is, not actually talk about role-playing itself (or only get to talk about it through the lens of diversity).

    On the other hand, it's really important to have a diverse group of people to talk about these issues, because otherwise it's us sitting on our mountain preaching about how Things Should Be without being in touch with the people who are actually playing differently from us (or not playing at all!).

    So I think that if you're going to try to do both, it'll be really important to concieve of it as, "We need a diverse group to address these issues," not "The appropriate space for 'outsiders' is one where these issues are discussed." Does that make sense? It's a question of which goal comes first . . . .

    Jonathan, it sounds like the project you're really in love with is the think-tanky, let's-figure-out-what-we-can-do, diversity-discussion oriented one. Maybe one of the projects that's going to come out of those discussions is a forum where some of the people who feel marginalized can be welcome.

    I'd actually suggest that if you don't consider yourself one of these marginalized people, don't set up a forum for them - help them find a critical mass and tools and a moderator to do it themselves. That way they're not the lost children who need help from Daddy to make a place for them, but a community of their own which can reach out to other, similar communities like this one. Talking about - and, implicitly, treating - these people as "lost children" is making me very uncomfortable.

    On the exclusion/inclusion issue, I'm going to repeat what I've said several times already and say, "Let us be more." Let everyone in - but don't rely on who randomly finds the site. Go out and recruit people from very different communities, and make them feel welcome. Tell them, "Hi, I want you here, because you do Harry Potter role-playing on Livejournal," and you'll be shocked how much of a difference it can make to their willingness to participate. Have tiered membership if you need to, with core members who are chosen through concrete service (leading projects, moderating, recruiting new members from unusual communities) and who represent the diversity that you're hoping to get on the site. Or require every new member to bring in a member from a different role-playing community before you give them full membership. Those are just some ideas, but having an invite-only, limited membership is a really good way to say, "Hey, we're the insiders, and we're going to decide how to address the diversity of you peons out there." This is not going to do good things for your project, I think.

    I'll add that I have no idea if I'm invited, or welcome, or if I'm what you have in mind - but I'm really interested in getting down and dirty, and figuring out some concrete design challenges or web efforts or recruiting material or what have you in order to actually make a change. I don't just want to discuss this stuff, I want to act on it.

    Finally, I just want to say that I am someone who really does not feel welcome on the Forge, but does feel very welcome here - and a lot of that has to do with a couple of individuals who posted encouraging, welcoming things to me during my first couple of days and made me feel that I had worthwhile things to contribute. Don't underestimate what social ties can do. At the very least, it worked for me.

    Um...not that I feel welcome here or on the Forge, but may I make a few suggestions?

    1) Don't let the tradition of forum design breed the same problems they have. I believe Shirky has some of the greatest advice: http://www.shirky.com/writings/group_user.html There are several practical, tested ways to moderate content drift and flames which don't require much admin attention.

    2) Have both a open-wide forum and invitation-only circle. Best of both worlds.

    3) If you want to promote diversity, take a few notes from non-profits. In this case, invite a small number of highly visible, well-connect, diverse bloggers to have blog homes on this site (above the forum). This will clearly show that diversity is not just welcome, but 'enforced.' It will emphasize topics stemming from a more diverse viewpoint than 'what everyone else is talking about.'

    4) Do not allow pure democracy rule. Some mechanism is necessary to protect 'minority voice.' (Pure democracy becomes the tyranny of the majority, not what is being asked for here.) Create a strong mission statement and select and replace board members and leaders based on how well they 'produce' on the mission statement.

    5) Start with a _temporary_ leadership group to openly discuss the initial set up and take suggestions (to be explored and never outright dismissed; this can allow non-diverse people to architect a diverse platform) and USE DEADLINES! Once the comment period is over, it's over. (Don't succumb to the tyranny of the unbounded middle; 'as soon as we are ready.')

    6) Get heavy-duty back-end support; don't let interface difficulty keep out the less-computer-inclined. (Another kind of diversity.)

    7) Check out the stuff which Clinton Nixon and others discussed on the 'RPG Planet' stuff. Try http://www.google.com/search?q=anvilwerks+%22RPG+planet%22 (Click on Cached, I don't know where the archives went.)

    Just a few observations, memories, and suggestions. Hope this helps. Thanks for your time.

    Fang Langford
