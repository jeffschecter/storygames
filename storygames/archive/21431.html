\n I want to build a Leprechaun/Halfling hybrid class and need some help.

Halflings in Moldvay\u2019s Basic D&D are interesting, but I\u2019d like to alter them a bit by adding in some Leprechaun flavor.

Here\u2019s what I\u2019m drawing from for Leprechauns:

\u201cLay your ear close to the hill.
Do you not catch the tiny clamour,
Busy click of an elfin hammer,
Voice of the Lepracaun singing shrill
As he merrily plies.\u201d


Leprechauns are well-dressed wee folk, often angry or drunk (or both) with a certain fondness for gold. There are female leprechauns, although they seem to be quite few in number. Leprechauns are elusive, quick witted, and have magical powers to vanish into thin-air. They have fantastically good memories, but they tend to harbor grievances. Older leprechauns are known for being solitary, preferring their own company\u2014however, in their youth, they\u2019ve been known on occasion go trooping in search of treasure. Most \u2018Big Folk\u2019 see Leprechauns as diminutive rogues who cannot be trusted and will deceive whenever possible. They are lustful, nasty, capricious fey whose shenanigans might delight you one day and kill you the next if you displease them.


Around three-feet tall, Leprechauns most often wear green or red pants buckled at the knee with a matching coat and shoes with silver buckles on the tops. Sometimes they wear a pointed cap or jaunty hat (adorned with a four-leaf clover), and may be smoking a pipe. They have a fondness of drinking beer, wine, and whiskey. But love moonshine with a passion. Leprechauns are excellent musicians who are said to play the fiddle, harp, and tin whistles, while having wild revelries in the moonlight where many faeries gather to dance, sing and drink.


Older leprechauns are cobblers\u2014having long given up their wandering ways. They make shoes for fairies. Fairies love to dance and will frequently dance the night away wearing and ruining their tiny fairy shoes. When this happens, the leprechauns are kept happy and busy; paid by the fairies for their trade. But sometimes there aren\u2019t fairy dances, so there are no fairy shoes to be mended. It is during times like these that the leprechauns will venture into the mortal world in search of work.


When this happens, a leprechaun will knock on a human\u2019s door begging for work in the hopes that you have shoes in need of mending. If you do, the leprechaun will happily take them away to be fixed and return them, as good as new, when he is done. In exchange for his services he will be paid a gold or silver coin, which he would take to hide away at the end of the rainbow with the rest of his treasure (usually a pot of gold hidden in the hollow of a Fairy Tree). It is said that if you can capture a Leprechaun (or, in some stories, steal their coin purse) you can barter their freedom for their treasure (pot of gold) or granting the person three wishes.

Molday\u2019s Halflings are described as:

\u201cHalflings are small, good-natured demi-humans averaging only 3 feet in height and weighing about 60 pounds. They are outgoing but not unusually brave, seeking treasure as a way of gaining the comforts of home which they so dearly love.

SPECIAL ABILITIES: Halflings have better saving throws than most other character classes. They are very accurate with all missile weapons and gain a bonus of + 1 , in addition to Dexterity adjustments, on their " to hit" rolls when using them. Due to their small size and skills at dodging, halflings have a bonus of -2 on their Armor Class when being attacked by creatures larger than man-sized. When rolling for individual initiative (an Optional rule) halflings add +1, in addition to any Dexterity adjustments. Outdoors, halflings are difficult to spot, having the ability to seemingly vanish into woods or underbrush. Halflings have only a 10% chance of being detected in this type of cover, and even in dungeons there is a one-third chance (a roll of 1 or 2 on 1d6) that a halfling will not be seen in normal light if the character finds some cover (such as shadows), and remains absolutely quiet and still.\u201d

So I\u2019d like to combine the two. Adding some magical abilities, but also keeping close to the source material. Is it a thief/magic-user (illusionist) sub-class? I\u2019m not sure that the variations of the Leprechauns do that (AD&D Monster Manual, Dragon Magazine articles, and so on).

Very interested to hear your responses

    \n Hey, that's my idea!

    To be specific, my historical-fantasy D&D has been running for years on the notion that halflings are half-fae who mature into leprechauns (fae proletariat, if you will) over time. Works well. The way I do it is with feats and prestige classes and such, not really directly applicable to Basic.

    One way to keep close to the Basic D&D chassis would be to look at how Mentzer treats prestige classes for e.g. fighters and such and do the same with halflings: keep the class the same at low levels, but when the halfling gets to name level, give them new options. Their magic comes up only then, in other words. Unfortunately this conflicts some with the notion that demihumans are level-capped, if you do that. (Plus, who gets to name level, really.)

    Alternatively, if you want them to be magical right from the start, I suggest treating them as a thief/magic-user multiclass in a way parallel to the elf: slow xp progression (sum up the 2nd level limits for thief and MU and go from there), and give them the abilities of both thieves and magic-users. Flavour to taste, so perhaps they get the traditional halfling skills instead of the thief stuff, and perhaps their spell lists are more fae than usual, and maybe they don't use spellbooks, and so on (although if you do that sort of thing, then I think elves should get that treatment as well - I do that anyway with elves, I prefer their magic having subtle differences compared to human arcanists).

    Yes, that's probably what I'd do in a relatively conservative Moldway context: just like the elf is a fighter/MU multiclass, have the halfling be a thief/MU multiclass. The parallel pleases me in many ways. If I did this, I would probably just give the dwarf clerical powers to make it a fighter/cleric multiclass, because why not [grin].

    You could also keep the traditional halfling and offer the magic-using variant side by side with the magic-less one. If I did this, I would probably make a single-class elf to accompany the spread, too, and so on and so forth...

    Let's peek at the demihuman multiclass equivalencies table in case you want to run with that:

    Halfling Thief/MU
    Dwarf Fighter/Cleric

    Maybe they're evil races, split away from the divine systematics, yet redeemable by epic questing and playable from the start as exceptional members of their race? Greenskins for Fighter/Thieves, werepeoples (gnolls) for Thief/Clerics? Is there a MU/Cleric race proper, or have all such beings devolved into Aberrations, becoming the Aboleths and Eye Tyrants of the deep, unaware of their divine heritage in the Four Castes...

    \n Arguably, the Mentzer/Moldvay halflings are already the (slightly tweaked) fighter/thief multiclass, as they get full fighting proficiency (almost: they can't wield large weapons, but they have no AC restrictions, which is much more significant), a great chance of hiding (not exactly the same as thief skills, but comparable) and d6s for hit dice (the mid-point between fighter d8s and thief d4s).

    Which makes me think... To make a "magical halfling", I'd start by restricting armor use as a trade-off.

    You could give them a full spellcasting progression, d4 HDs and restrict them to daggers and no armor, but keep all of the halflings' racial special abilities - making them into sort-of MU/thief multiclass the way regular halflings are sort-of fighter/thief. Either create an XP table with bigger baseline numbers than a human MU, or keep the halfling XP table and use the cleric spell table rather than the MUs' (which means they only get spells if they survive to 2nd level).

    Or, you could just keep the halfling as your baseline, take away their armor (either restrict them to leather, which seems appropriate, or no armor at all, depending on how far you want to go on the next step), then give them a smattering of ad-hoc spell-like abilities at certain levels. Spells like "mending", "ventriloquism" or "faerie fire" sure sound like they fit the leprechaun shtick, while not really making any actual magic user jealous.

    \n The Tall Tales of the Wee Folk Leprechaun class is a limited caster (spells up to 5th level and uses the special Fairy Spell List from said book), essentially 1 level slowed advancement compared to MUs, at-will Invisible to Mortals, saves as an Elf, no weapon or armor restrictions.

    Depending on how fond you are of thief skills and how thiefly you were thinking of making them, the Invisibility to Mortals may give them enough stealthyness that you don't need anything else. But it's definitely more of a caster than anything else, and doesn't have much in common mechanically with the halfling. Even if it's not what you're looking for, the Fairy Spell list from the book might be worth borrowing; it's a nice thematic mix with some entirely new spells thrown in, less "blasty" than the regular MU list.

    \n How about this:

    Leprechaun

    Base XP: 2,000
    Max level: 10
    HD: 1d4
    Attack: As thief
    Saves: As dwarf
    Prime: Int, Dex
    Min scores: Int 9, Dex 9
    Max score: Str 12
    XP bonus: +5% XP if Dex 13+, +10% XP if Dex 16+
    Armor: Clothing Only -2 (7)
    Weapons: Shillelagh (1d6), pikestaff (1d6), dagger (1d4), or darts (1d4)

    Restrictions: Leprechauns use four-sided dice (d4) to determine their hit points. They may advance to a maximum of 10th level of experience. Leprechauns can use a shillelagh, pikestaff, dagger, or darts for weapons. Leprechauns must have a minimum score of 9 in both Intelligence and Dexterity. Their maximum score in Strength is 12. Cold Iron is poison to leprechauns. The effect isn\u2019t quick-acting, except in combat when\u2014if hit\u2014iron weapons cause +1 additional damage. But long-term contact, or if ingested, with cold iron will slowly and permanently drain a leprechaun\u2019s Hit Points and Ability Scores. In any case, leprechauns will never have any weapons, tools, or items made from iron; they use instead various stones, woods, or non-ferrous metals and alloys (brass, bronze, silver, gold, mithril, etc.). Leprechauns have spellcasting abilities and follow the same table as Magic-Users. However, they are limited to the Fairy-Charms List. Detect Invisible will reveal any Leprechauns within the spell range. Dispel Magic will cause a leprechaun to become visible, however after a round the leprechaun can again turn Invisible to Mortals. Even when a leprechaun is invisible, sometimes possible for mortals to perceive the faintest glimmer in the air\u2014especially at dawn, dusk, or in moonlight.

    Special: Leprechauns have better saving throws than most other character classes. They have a natural Armor Class of 7 and due to their small size and skills at dodging, leprechauns have a bonus of -2 on their Armor Class when being attacked by creatures larger than man-sized. When rolling for individual initiative (an Optional rule) leprechauns add +1, in addition to any Dexterity adjustments. Outdoors, leprechauns are difficult to spot, having the ability to seemingly vanish into woods or underbrush. Leprechauns have only a 10% chance of being detected in this type of cover, and even in dungeons there is a one-third chance (a roll of 1 or 2 on 1d6) that a leprechaun will not be seen in normal light if the character finds some cover (such as shadows), and remains absolutely quiet and still. In natural terrain (woods, underbrush, and even caves), they gain surprise on a roll of 1-3 on 1d6. At will, leprechauns can turn Invisible to Mortals, but the special limitation to this ability is that if seen by a mortal, the leprechaun cannot become invisible to that mortal\u2019s eyes until they have looked\u2014however momentarily\u2014elsewhere. All leprechauns have Second Sight, which enables them to see another fairy\u2019s true form, even if the fairy is Invisible to Mortals, polymorphed, or shapechanged. They speak common, elvish, gnomish, and the languages of the fey.

    Pint

    Perception: Pint is the pejorative for a Gaeling. They are well-dressed wee folk who have an unrestrained greed for gold. Most \u2018Big Folk\u2019 see Pints as diminutive rogues who cannot be trusted and will deceive whenever possible. They are carousing, capricious fey whose shenanigans might delight you one day and kill you the next if you displease them.

    Pints have a fondness of drinking beer, wine, and whiskey (this is how they came about their nickname). But love moonshine with a passion. Legends say that if you capture a Pint you can barter their freedom for their treasure or by them granting you three wishes. Pints hide away their treasure the end of the rainbow (usually a pot of gold hidden in the hollow of a Fairy Tree).

    Reality: Around three-feet tall, Pints most often wear green or red pants buckled at the knee with a matching coat and shoes with silver buckles on the tops.     Base XP: 2,000
    Max Level: 10
    HD: 1d4
    Attack: As thief
    Saves: As dwarf
    Prime: Int, Dex

Pints have only a 10% chance of being detected in this type of cover, and even in dungeons there is a one-third chance (a roll of 1 or 2 on 1d6) that a Pint will not be seen in normal light if the character finds some cover (such as shadows), and remains absolutely quiet and still.     \n For me the big issue with the invisibility power is that it's redundant with the hiding abilities, badly - pick one of the other, I think. Why would an invisibility-capable critter maintain the culture and practice of stealth, anyway? (Obviously they're tactically cumulative, but still: D&D doesn't usually stylize this sort of stuff with redundant layers of defenses that require multiple saving roll equivalents to bypass.)

    Aside from that, the way to fix the invisibility being overpowered is to make it something that is only available from third level (or other mid-level breakpoint you like) on. That way the class has something big to look forward to, and access doesn't feel cheap. The main issue with strong class abilities at 1st level is that the character becomes a delivery system for the ability. "Hey, we need to hire another halfling to be invisible for us, our tactics all revolve around that single trick. Fortunately players get to generate as many as they want, so we'll always have one."

    Obviously it would be natural to have the stealth abilities too if invisibility was something that you only got at 3rd level. Lower-level halflings rely on stealth, while higher level ones have real invisibility. You could even formalize the idea of them being overlapping abilities by saying that you lose the stealth when you gain the invisibility - real 3rd level leprechauns are loud and prideful asshats, they're more likely to fuck your shit up in broad daylight while being invisible than to hide in bushes.

    \n In a Basic context, though - is your Basic even the sort of campaign where rare classes exist, or do you fall back on the Basic Seven (four human classes + three demihumans)? Does the chargen process allow you to trade stat points or such to achieve whichever class you want to play?

    I think that it's a big step for Basic D&D when e.g. Mentzer introduces a Monk class or Gygax introduces a Barbarian class; now the world's wide open, there are all sorts of weird classes one might imagine. Some may be rare, some may be more common, but likely none are as common as the four basic human classes. If there's a fifth class (or fourth demihuman class), why not a sixth? Seventh? The doors are open for the concept.

    On the other hand, if you're just replacing the Halfling with a slightly different halfling, it's not a given that you'd want to make it a super-prestigious rare class. After all, you're operating on a Basic Seven paradigm here; do you really want to narrow it down further? Are some classes in the constrained framework more difficult get than others?

    I don't know, this sort of symbolic systematics cloud castle building isn't useful for all campaign constructions. Maybe you'll just want to simulate the setting, however that may fall out in party composition terms.

    \n But hey, so does adding Leprechauns, and this very specific flavor of Leprechaun. I think treating demihumans/humanoid races as "non-mortals" actually adds a really interesting thematic dimension.

    Another option (and again, it's entirely speculative that at-will limited invisibility will even actually become a problem) that I think could be thematically interesting is the old "Drow Compromise": the ability to hide from mortals could fade over time if you spend too much time associating with mortals, engaging in mortal (as opposed to fae) behavior, or something of the sort. "Mechanical advantage in exchange for fictional advantage" deals aren't always a great idea, but I do think they're much easier to handle in some OSR contexts where negotiation about whether and how fictional concerns apply in any given situation is often a large part of play.

    \n Well, obviously you determine who's fey and who's not on a per-campaign basis. That's straightforward enough, and I don't think that the feasibility of the invisibility really hinges off that any more than a cleric's turning ability does on the rarity of undead. If one is bothersome and intrusive, then so is the other.

    In my historical campaign goblinoids are human (as in, literally just some ethnically different humans), orcs are demonic, while trolls, kobolds and gnolls are fey (plus both goblinoids and orcs have fey equivalents as various troll sub-races)... this stuff can go any which way when you start playing with the lego pieces. No way to tell in a setting-agnostic way what something like "fey" or "undead" is even going to mean. I mean, ghouls are often not even undead in this campaign. You can't trust in anything.
