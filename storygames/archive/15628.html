    \n Here's a thought in favor of factions as playbooks rather than fronts.

    In both Apocalypse World and Dungeon World, there's a conceit that each class/playbook is unique: you're not a hardholder, you're The Hardholder\u2014there are other hardholds with their own governors, but in some fashion you are the only person in the world who is truly a Hardholder, as defined by the playbook. You're not a wizard, you're The Wizard, even though you will encounter other magic users. And so forth.

    Take that attitude to Planescape, and a player who chooses the Sensates playbook is not (just) playing a member of the Sensate faction, the character is The Sensate faction, embodied. Or Dustman. Or Free Leaguer. Or Xaositect. If you want to focus on the struggle between the factions, I can't think of a better way to do it. Of course that does almost guarantee a lot of conflict among the PCs.

    Factions will work as fronts either if all of the PCs are members of the same faction or none of them belong to any faction (which essentially is the same as belonging to the Free League), at least initially. Actually, I may be mistaken about that, but my understanding was that fronts begin as opposition to the PCs, so PCs would not start out belonging to / aligned with a front.

    Incidentally I just did a search for Planescape on the AW forums, and turned up just two passing references, so unfortunately there's no help for you there (presuming you haven't looked already).

    Another reason to handle factions as playbooks is if you're more interested in a campaign that uses Sigil as a jumping-off point for exploring the outer planes. In that case you have the older potential of fronts of the variously-aligned planes and conflicts like the Blood War (between demons and devils) or the Modron March. You can then play the factions as the players choosing their philosophical stance in reaction to the extremes of the outer planes, which is more or less what the concept of the factions is in the first place. So, the modrons are a threat, because they want to swarm outward and impose Absolute Order on everyone else. How do the players feel about that? Is it a problem or an opportunity or just something to ignore? Different factions will have different takes on that. The players' choices of factions may color how they react, or reacting to the front may cause the players to pick up a particular faction playbook.

      Longer term, I'm interested in looking at creating faction mini-playbooks, and maybe adding a rule about changing alignment during play.
