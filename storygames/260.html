Counterstakes in Dogs

warren
February 2006 edited February 2006 in Story Games
In the thread Thought on Stakes Setting there was the beginnings of a thread about setting counterstakes in Dogs. The rules don't indicate that there should be any counterstakes (and that's how I've run all my games so far), but in those game I have thought a few times that having counterstakes gives the NPCs something to "raise toward", which I think would help. I was going to test out counterstakes when I run Dogs for a new group next week, but then Joshua BishopRoby has said that they are poison.

So I'm creating this thread to ask opinions on counterstakes in Dogs. Should I set counterstakes? If not, why not, if I should, then are there any pointers I should take on board with regards to setting them and so forth.
  • TonyLB
    February 2006

    Counterstakes generally? I'd have to think about that.

    But I can say with some certainty that stakes that imply fallout are poison to Dogs, and counterstakes have a strong tendency to lead people to that mistake.

    "If I win we peacably get past the man to talk to the Steward, if I lose we have to fight in order to get there" is poison not because of the counterstakes, but because the stakes are phrased in terms of fallout and escalation. The right way to make those stakes is to say "If I win we get to see the Steward, if I lose we don't" and then let "peacably" and "fighting" be resolved by escalation.

    Primetime Adventures works best when you say "Okay, we know we're going to achieve the goal, the question is what it costs us to do it." Dogs gets broken if you import that same mentality into setting stakes, because it should be handled by escalation and fallout (especially since fallout can be way more than wounds ... new relationships, changes to traits ... all sorts of great stuff).

    Now if, by comparison, someone said "My stakes are that Sister Temperance marries young John" and the GM said "And if you lose, she runs off with the handsome stranger Rudolpho" then I'd say "Alright! Now we're settin' some solid stakes."

    Does that distinction make sense?

  • Brand_Robins
    February 2006
    In my experience counterstakes aren't poison in Dogs. However, they are difficult enough that they're not usually worth futzing with. The reasons for this are three:

    1) You have to make sure your counterstakes don't force fallout. As others have said above.

    2) You have to make sure your counterstakes don't force PC judgement or curtail their moral authority. If you have the stakes "I want to convince her that lesbianism is wrong" and then the GM counterstakes "And if you lose she convinces you that it's right" you're going to someplace interesting -- but not somplace that works for Dogs. (In Nine Worlds, for example, I do sometimes have stakes like that. In Dogs its death.)

    3) Countersakes can have a way of killing good followup conflicts. Look at the "If I win we get to see the Steward, if I lose the Steward hightails it out of town" example. Is the Steward already out of town? Shouldn't they get a followup? If they do get a followup why bother with the counterstake? Now if you phrased it "We want to see the Steward before he leaves town" and you lose, then it's easy to see that the logical followup is something in the range of "Damnit, now we have to track him down in the wildnerness."

    I don't think that counterstakes inherently force any of these problems, but IME they do make them common enough that using them is not worth the price of admission. Dogs works best with flat stakes, giving lots of freedom for fallout, moral judgement, and followup stakes.

    I'd also speculate that this is because Dog's punctuated conflict resolution is not the same as PTAs single conflict scene and dispute resolution, but that's an argument for another time.

    Ok, modification to my thougt. Remember, everyone has to agree the stakes are fun whether they are won or lost. So if Fred doesn't think it will be fun for his character to be killed, or resign from the dogs, then stakes forcing such are not acceptable (which is different from forcing him to choose between taking a gunshot or giving a conflict, and losing the stakes).

    And remembering this, I don't see any problem with NPC initiated conflicts. They're just a case of the GM saying "I think we have a conflict here." How the players go from not in a conflict to in a conflict is always up to negotiation. And it's worthwhile to note that players should be able to ask for a conflict also - sure the GM can "say yes" (or roll the dice), but the player should be able to say "no, I think there's a conflict here."

    Dogs works incredibly smoothly if you remember all of this. I was amazed at how smoothly the game ran by just observing when the players wanted conflicts, or they were needed for pacing.

    Huh.

    Brand and Joshua BR -- You do realize that "I kill you" conflicts aren't actually "I kill you" but rather "I force a relatively easy follow-up healing conflict?" (Likewise, if I was actually going to use equivalent stakes like "you give up being a dog" I would have a relatively easy follow-up inspiration conflict.) So, frankly, the best response to an "I kill you" challenge is to give.

    As to the innocence conflict -- I think it raises a whole passle of great moral issues. Will you punish a person you believe to be innocent if other people believe that she is guilty? Will you punish someone you believe to be innocent if all evidence points to their guilt? By not having NPC initiate conflicts you're essentially taking some of the bite out of the game. Even emotionally manipulative stakes (like "do you think I'm innocent" or "do you think I'm right") don't undercut judgement, they simply open up new realms of judgement.

    The GM says, "sister Annabelle runs up to you in tears. Stakes: does she convince you that she's innocent?"

    Totally legit. Know why? Because we all know she's not innocent - an innocent NPC doesn't launch a conflict with those stakes - and playing my character as a dupe to her wiles is fun. Will he catch on, eventually? How much evidence will he ignore? How betrayed will he feel? How stupid will he feel? That's fun stuff.

    What's not legit is "sister Annabelle runs up to you in tears. Stakes: is she innocent?" The GM already knows whether she's innocent. No amount of conflict resolution can change the town writeup.

    Re: death and abandoning your calling: also totally legit. Nobody says you get to play your Dog as long as you want to. Maybe you do, maybe you don't.

    Re: Frank's "Remember, everyone has to agree the stakes are fun whether they are won or lost": nah. The stakes should be mostly givable, that's all - and that's givable in the view of the most discerning player, remember, not givable in the view of the player whose character's at stake.

    For lots of people, playing Dogs where every individual gets veto over the stakes will be a fun, easy and safer way to play. I'm fine with that. But that's not the limit to Dogs' workings.

    -Vincent
