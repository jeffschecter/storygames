    \n Jason, that's hardcore.

    We played a ton recently, but completely different games because our schedules are all over the place and we are trying to run something regardless of who's attending...so we have a game ready of almost any combination of players. It's confusing a bit but we've gamed more in the last month than in the previous six months. It has also been an opportunity to try many older systems we never got to try before. (Some of this is July, but whatever...)

    Ghost/Echo: We kick it off by establishing it's a classic Matrix-y cyberpunk setting. Pretty soon we start making up our own dangers, with shared narration, running with vanilla Otherkind dice and ignoring most of the structure of G/E (as little there is). Being chased by the police, the characters go into a nightclub, looking for a contact who might help them. A player picks up the dice and says: "...and the danger is, I don't find him fast enough." He rolls and gets the danger. I narrate the contact suddenly putting a gun to his spine and whispering in his ear: "I bet you wish you had found me first." Lots of laughs, good moment.

    Pathfinder: Our silly dungeonworld game. They fight off an invasion of lizardmen riding giant turtles, then follow their trail back to the lizardmen city, infiltrate a temple and stop the ressuection-in-progress of a lizardman demigod with laser eyes. Good pulpy fun. I bemoan the moments where the system doesn't support the action and mood.

    Rogue Trader: Trying this out for the first time, we made characters and fought against an Ork Freeboota ship, after some good rolls their morale fails and the Ork captain apparently catapults himself into space and self-destructs his ship. Next time we'll investigate a strong energy signature on one of the planets. I expect lots of sanity loss. I love the mood of the game. The system is also pretty ok.

    Guts & Glory: Quick and dirty playtest for my quick and dirty WW2 skirmish game. Everything runs smoothly for the first mission. Then we try to stop an SS transport as two desperate commandos. We blow a bridge and try to machinegun the survivors from a belltower, but we have to retreat almost instantly as someone fires a panzerfaust at our position. We skirmish around the village for a bit until we both get a bullet between the eyes in rapid succession. The beauty of random dice rolls.

    BattleTech: As survivors of a failed mercenary mission we scrounge around on an enemy territory, infiltrate a base, sabotage equipment and topple a guard mech with a makeshift mine. Then we just fuck everything up and get out without a scratch.

    Call or Trail of Cthulhu tonight I believe.

    \n Sunday (uh.. yeah... that's July, but anyway) we played "Narrative Old-School" with Wordplay- my cynical, world-weary dwarf wizard (senior sub-lecturer of the Imperial University of Magic) accompanied an impetuous noblewoman to recover a legendary sword from a border fort - encountering orcs, skeletons, and some (luckily easily banished) madness spirits.     \n - the Kingmaker Device gets the Disposition check and we wind up with a map where Ord, Garrick, and the Kingmaker are at one end and Stefos and two Cogsquads are at the other. I want to keep Disposition because it creates unexpected arrangements to interpret that are tactically relevant (the winners decided this shape so it's for a tactical purpose (outside the narrative) but needs explaining (inside the narrative) and this friction is awesome). We offer a sudden burst of activity at the Kingmaker and the explosion tears Stefos out of the guts of it hurling him a thousand meters away along with clusters of machinery that turn into mindless bad guys. That's cool. JB offers that the junk transforms to bad guys IN THE AIR and Stefos is fighting them as he is hurled distant.     \n Psi-Run with a Story Games Newbie (me) and three trad-players.
    There was not much "running", as only two locations were visited, but come and meet

    A strange firebranded girl, walking around with an obsidian knife, knowing she's constantly follwed by something!!!

    An emaciated 50-year old man, with a scar over his left eye, communist and nazi symbols tattoed on his hands and a strange relationship with Wrath and Violence... he can control it in others, but the rage inside him keeps getting bigger and bigger!!! Also, he's got a keyring with infinite keys attached... why?!

    A darkish guy with a huge tattoo, a power over shadows and a glowing Machete!!!

    they are rapidly reached and attacked by their pursuer, a man in white with a burning green circle (which they instinctively know as "The Nethersun") floating above his head, and, after some collateral damage and two (TWO!!!) brainstorms, they manage to subdue him. Instead of running, the girl uses her knife to detach the burning circle from the man's soul, and puts it in the entity that was following her, which turns out to be her familiar... she's the Chosen One of a dark covenant wishing to control the world, and she's about to gain absolute power... but the man with control over wrath pushes the third guy to interrupt the ritual!!!

    In the end, it turns out that the third guy is a member of another cult, competitor to the girl's one, and that they both want to control the emaciated man, whose power was, supposedly, to absorb and metabolize the wrath in humans, with a cathartic effect: the rage he constantly feels is due to the fact that he was tortured and broken, and so he was not able to purify the wrath he absorbed from outside anymore. The keys he held openend and close the doors to the inner worlds living in every person, and he had to protect them to ensure the proper balance of the spirit world. when the little girl, her familiar/source of power destroyed, managed to take the keys from him and sever his connection to them, he resorted to the only resource he had left: the pain he suffered in life and that shattered his ability to control wrath. he released it over the world, and every living being suffered in one moment all the pain of its entire life: grown-ups and old people died, and the remaining children had felt the pain in such a way, that they would never consider again to hurt one another.

    I think the exxplanation is nebulous and not totally understandable, but the game was really great, and the ending was totally powerful. We sort of went astray, away from the "run for your life" theme of the game,to a full-fledged fight between the pcs, but in the end everything wrapped up fine.

The priest smites some demons and a few TVs and lead his flock in exodus but just as they escape from the city Jerry Springer and a cozy sofa and spotlight is there trying to tempt his flock away from him.

The town of Protection is rebuilt as Hollowood. This is the first Rustbelt session in which all players have "won".

    \n More Hollowppoint play testing with Toph's young son, Jonah:

    Two agents, whose job is to take out assassins, are tipped that the Prime Minister of Canada is going to be hit. Joel contemplates using terror to intimidate his employer into giving them more information, but is advised against it. Since both Agents have only a 1 in Dig, They rent a jet plane, land on Parliament Hill during a press conference, and let the PM know he's in danger.

    Bang. A shot rings out, and the PM is hit. A quick scan reveals a sniper in the bell tower, so Jonah's Agent Spike draws his guns and fires (Kill 5), while Joel (Bob Smith) calmly takes command and tries to save the PM (Cool 5).The sniper has eight dice.
    Round 1: 4x1, 2x3 against Bob Smith's 2x1 and Spike's 2x3, 2x1. Sniper is SHOT. The PM is still bleeding, on national TV.
    Round 2: Bob Smith switches to Take 2 (his combat action is to grab a walkie talkie); Spike gives him defensive fire (helping), and after burning some aspects still gets hit, so he is SHOT. There is some discussion about the implications of burning the aspect "thin black jeans", and it is explained that Bob Smith will still be wearing pants, he just can't tell a story with them to get two extra dice.
    Round 3. Spike is back to Kill, and he ends up getting three hits. Jonah describes the overkill with glee, as the body falls out of the bell tower, and is kept dancing by the impact of bullets (Jonah demonstrates by doing the robot). This is on national tv, but the agents walk away. Player Success, despite dead PM.

    \n IAWA, "Baby Got Backstabbed."

    Saltor Stormbringer, the permanently exasperated weather wizard, must resort to sedating everyone in his rental household so he can harvest the humors of his adopted baby-sorcerer daughter in peace and quiet. But the Hearts that Hunger, terrible torture implements that possess the maddened sentience of their victims, desires to spill the blood of the baby-sorcerer as well.

    And spill the blood they do -- spill it back into the veins of the babe. Their grim surgery is left blessedly undescribed.

    Meanwhile, the baby's wet nurse, who fancies herself Mother of Demons, seeks to ride Kreelios the Megapede, the Thousand Mouthed-Chorus, into the timecyclic abyss. She succeeds, despite despite the best efforts of Cassander, blind vagabond seer and Saltor annoyer. The cycle of demonic war that precipitated this wicked age continues; had Cassander succeeded, the age would have become a gentle, temperate one.

    Our IAWA games are taking a turn where mutually compatible Best Interests are popping up by surprise, leading to interesting, ephemeral alliances at the table. It is a wicked age, but also a congenial one.

    \n The emphasis here is usually on stuff that's awesome, and that's fun to read. Sometimes a session fails, though, and the post-mortem (especially when playtesting) is the interesting AP.

    "We had some trouble in this session and in post-game analysis we think that the duty system created a lot of internal planning that was in conflict with other players' planning. As none of it was announced aloud, this became friction at the table and play was slow and stilted. Also, Scopes were constructed without the intent to use them in narration, and so there was some awkward play towards fate points and so that needs to be addressed. Ideas include granting a starting fate point (for MYSELF) to reduce the tension of fatelessness, removing the implicit pacing of an IMPENDING CONFLICT, and changing who may give who fate points. Probably we will use all of these. For the last, letting anyone, including the acting player, hand fate points around for hitting a scope is probably a good idea. Keeps it casual but takes the self-reward guilt off."

    \n A long ship-to-ship battle was in the offing during this week's session of our GURPS campaign, which is set in a fairly trad fantasy universe during the Age of Sail. Our Dwarven pirate assailants were few in number, but magically formidable. About halfway through the conflict, their Mage summoned an enormous fire elemental, and it looked as though we were all about to be roasted like a chicken.

    But Chris' character, who'd done basically nothing all campaign but sit in his stateroom building explosives, walked up to the elemental and said "Yo."

    \n In an aerial battle between Ord the dragon, carrying his sorcerous and heroic allies, and chimpanzee-piloted airships with harpoon cannons and flying infantry with bamboo and leather and Levity-magic wings, Stefos the demi-god of the River is blown clear of Ord by a volley of harpoons that shatter against his shield. As he falls he uses his shield to guide himself into a flight of flying infantry, smashes their heads together, and uses their magic-leaking bodies and equipment to fly back up into the turmoil of battle.

    This narrative arose from the outcome of the first roll in the battle, which determined the map state (who goes where) and the nature of the fight (Warfare). Pretty good story density for one roll.

      \n Played in a one-shot of Legends of Anglerre today.

      My steampunk pirate pilot 'A. R(rrrr). McLeod used his aspect 'Slightly-Unstable Airship Pilot' to jump on an attacking stone gargoyle and start to ride it using a jungle vine as reins. He was chased by a second huge gargoyle and used his version of the Immelmann maneuver, called 'the McLeod'. Only this maneuver turns the rider around and aims them straight at whatever is following it.

      Then McLeod jumps off and the two gargoyles explode in a shower of stone as Honey (the mechanic's) clockwork spider turns into a gyrocopter and eases McLeod to the ground.

      Kinda makes up for skipping a flaming airship off a lagoon and onto a beach full of primitive cannibals. Kinda.

      \n Dark Sun 4E with folks at GenCon. I may have oversold the Reckless Breakage rule a bit to the players. (This is a rule where the player can gamble at breaking their weapon by rerolling a failed attack.) The Ardent\u2019s game went something like this:

      Encounter 1: Rolls reckless breakage on her metal spear. Breaks it. Fights with fists for a while, then picks up another metal spear from a dead Templar. Breaks it. She finds a obsidian spear after the fight.

      RP scene: \u201cMy daughter, as your Noble father, I cannot help you liberate the Brickyards. But take this: the magical metal Spear that is a metaphor for both our relationship and the fate of our Noble house.\u201d

      Encounter 2: (eh, blazing starfall + minions + strategery = let\u2019s skip this encounter)

      Encounter 3: Rolls reckless breakage on her ancestral metal spear. Breaks it. (Meanwhile, the Battlemind rolls Reckless Breakage for the first time and crits a templar. I imagine him basically pumping his weapon in the air like \u201cWHO\u2019S THE PSIONIC DEFENDER AND HAS FOUR ARMS? THIS GUY!\u201d)

      The ardent would probably not be using Reckless Breakage for a long while, but her game was also a decent string of badassery. Did I mention the psionic punching because she broke her spear on someone\u2019s face? Yes.

      \n Apocalypse World

      My brainer Brace had sex with the group's savvyhead Gitch. It was the first time she'd been with someone who actually dug her, instead of being repulsed by her leg braces and spooky psychic powers.

      So of course I roll a miss on my deep brain scan sex move even though she has a +3 Weird and end up doing one-harm to him during. She loses control and all the pain she'd healed from him earlier (Angel's healing touch) flows back into him. She was crying and saying, "I'm sorry, I'm sorry, it's not always like that!" Of course he gets to use his Things Speak sex move and asks, "What's wrong with this (her), and how might I fix it (her)?" Interesting answers follow.

      My MC Christian rewards my miss by having Brace wake up not next to Gitch for the pillow talk she was craving, but instead bound in a pitch dark room in an oil refinery, kidnapped by creepy mutants.

      \n Geiger Counter (Beta) -

      1923. It was clear that a nameless evil in the New England town of Kugan's Rest meant harm to Blaine and Celia Peabody (of the Boston Peabodys). But they couldn't just leave - they were throwing a party in a fortnight and they'd already sent the invitations!

      A cool game mechanic moment - Blaine is chopping at Celia Peabody (who is now possessed and basically the avatar of the Menace) with the 1-advantage-die sword that broke off the statue of Kugan in the town square; and Smitty, who is played by a guy who I've played D&D with for years and never before seen do anything but power-game - he says, "Don't hurt her! It's Celia!" So Blaine puts down the sword, and we just barely lose to the Menace - the last roll our one remaining character has 4 dice against the menace's 2, and we tie.

      Epilogue: a fortnight later, some Bostonians show up at the deserted mansion on the edge of Kugan's Rest, "I dare say, where *are* Celia and Blaine?"

      I was blown away that this game seemed to have brought out the real roleplayer in S. Not just in purposely playing nonoptimally, he was also playing NPC's with different voices (his Nestor, the house caretaker, was awesome), and, well, kudos to Jonathan Walton.

      \n [AW] We had our second session, which was kind of like a first session, part 2, in that we added two more players to our existing two. Our Gunlugger's often on the periphery of the action, which has been mostly social. I prompt a meeting between him and an NPC. It starts out well (mysterious summons, strange delicacy in the midst of squalor as Dog Head serves tea in porcelain cups at his junk shack in the dump), but then I find myself going out on an awkward, boring limb as Dog Head spouts some mumbo jumbo meant to point at a potential plot line. Not AW-esque at all, as I start leading the player instead of following the character. Fortunately, I had overrun the player a bit, and we decided to roll back the narative a little so his could do a read. He failed miserably. So the mumbo jumbo got replaced with, "You see a red dot appear on Dog Head's forehead. What do you do?" Gunfire ensues. Much more satisfying. Pity about the cups. But the samovar's intact.

      \n Our diamond heist PTA game. We've established that my guy Haddock has a fractious relationship with his Senegalese ex-wife, to whom he owes a lot of money. They have a teenaged daughter (my guy's Connection) at the Antwerp English Normal School. Snowy's got my guy in a car and is screaming at him about operational security (his Edge), a weakness of mine, while we drive to pick her up after school. And they get there and see the daughter on the front steps talking to a British cop who is obsessed with putting Snowy away (Snowy's Connection). Dude followed us to Belgium and he's messing with my guy's family!

      So I drive away, abandoning my daughter, knowing that I'll probably lose custody over it.

      One of my Edges is "inveterate coward". It was a huge collision of everything we cared about and who we were.
