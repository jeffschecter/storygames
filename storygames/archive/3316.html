\n Hi !!! Quite new to this site and while I was "looking around", I came upon the definition of "story games", which revives in me some thougths.

As a reminder, the definition :\n
:::A Story Game is a type of role-playing game or gaming experience with a lesser focus on My Character and a greater focus on Our Story.:::
\nNow, I want to adress an issue, taking the angle of plots and not rules, as a way to oppose story games to adventure games. And, please, bear with me : I'll need "charitable" reading on this !!! Here it goes, but first, a little note :

I've studied philosophy (with a university degree to prove it) and personally, I'm an existantialist (to be exact, hermeneutic, but let's put this detail aside). This means that I am the master of my destiny, that my path is not already written, that my life has a meaning because it can be told, narrated, just like any story. But, my own existence is linked to a context, a history, a language. I can't understand my existence outside this context. I can't understand "my self", my actions, my decisions while ignoring the events of the world that surrounds me. It is also "them" who define who I am (well, quite obviously, I wasn't born in the middle age, I'm not a native english speaker, I can read, for instance). For me, it is the central notion of "sense" that is at stake here. Now, back to rpg.

I quite understand the critics toward the "adventure games" since, I must admit, I'm no longer really interested in that type of game anymore. Because the game is centered on the PCs, as if the whole world was turning around them ; because, somehow, their path is already decided, fixed in advance, as if the PCs had just to follow the flow of a river ; because the PCs do not dispose of the freedom that would allow them to really define themselves ; because, finally, they don't really own the key to their destiny ; because it is not really about "their story", but instead about their characters in the context of "a story". Well, I know, I kinda simplify things here, but I think you get the picture ...

As an alternative, there are the "story games" which, normally, needs very little prep and the absence of any prewritten plots. This time, the PCs get all the freedom they need to generate "their story", without constraints and restrictions. But, on the other hand, any kind of context is driven off. The events of the world cease to happen ; and the stakes are lower. How can we give sense to "our story" in the absence of "the story" (the one of the world) ? How can an existence be "read" without a context ? How can one justifies its actions and decisions outside the story of the world ? Once again, I know, I simplify things, but I believe you get the picture ...

So, in one hand, I have "a story", the one of the world, into which the players take part without freedom ; on the other, I have players building "their story" without any kind of context, outside the story of the world. As a DM, I feel some kind of insatisfaction in both cases ; as an individual, I have a philosophical problem with both. Is there some kind of alternative, some kind of middle ground ? That's what Avalanche is about.

To allow the players to build, to write "their own story", but inside the story of the world ; a world that lives, but asks nothing from the PCs, imposes nothing to them, especially a prewritten path. To give the players a freedom, but which takes sense, is "inside", "the Story". Of course, this objective needs a completly new approach toward the design of prewritten plots, which is at the heart of our project, and that I won't present here.

But, maybe the most important thing : does this conception changes our approach toward rpg, does it change the way we play, does it change the role of the DM, does it change the way the players apprehend the game and the role they take into "their story" ?
To all of those, we believe the answer is "yes".

Well, I guess I could present "my solution" and its impact, but as I truly believe this is not the one answer, I'll let you express yourself on this !!!

    The middle ground isn't really an exception, it's the day to day.

    My example of running things "Story Games style" is the following, taken from some experiences I had back in college with AD&D2e.

    1) I joined some friends playing AD&D. I was told to be a cleric. "The Party needs a cleric". I played a cleric. I journeyed together with the other players in a Forgotten Realms setting that was set up by the GM. We were offered some side-stories by the GM, which we snapped up on because there really wasn't anything more that we could offer (personal suggestions often didn't fit with the GM's plans, you see). It was one of those games that lasted a few years, drifting from one superadventure to the next.

    2) Later, I joined a GM who was running AD&D with a specific setup in mind. If you read "The Complete Thieves' Handbook", there was this campaign idea for an "all thieves' campaign", set in this thieves' guild set in a Persian city, where the evil city watch guard basically burned out, rounded up and killed the members of the thieves' guild in one giant raid; The PCs are the survivors".
    "I want to run THIS", he said.
    It had a starting point, the middle was ill defined, the end goal was clear, though: Liberation and revenge. The GM had a map of the major NPCs in his head, but we helped create some memorable NPCs as well.
    The players were encouraged to make characters that fit within this "story backdrop". Naturally, it was 3 thieves (2 rogues with different kits, one elven thief-magic user), and one fighter who "used to protect the guildmaster". We played weekly for a few months. Then we concluded, and moved on to the next campaign (IIRC, it was a Cyberpunk game).

    That second game is the "middle ground" that a lot of us dwell in all the time. There was a GM focus and "story", and yet there was plenty of player input into the setup as well. Then the game proceeded into play. We didn't constantly pull out and create new shit from scene to scene, adventure to adventure, but rather just relied on the stuff we created earlier, and leaning on the GM to provide much of the coolness and catalysts.

    #2 is "story games style". Not an extreme, but a pretty balanced middle. Thing is, I had played RPGs for about *7 years* before I realized that "#2" was even a possibility. Nowadays it may be Common Thought. In the late 80s, though, the model of gaming was "the really long campaign that never really ends until all your characters get to high level/skill and retire".

    \n I think that this is a terminological problem, one that was hit on above, but not then recognized as the problem.
    \n
    \nPlot has two meanings, even in general use.
    \n
    \n1. The course of events as they play out through a story.
    \n2. The course of events that are planned by the forces involved.
    \n
    \nExamples:
    \n1. The plot took a sudden turn when the main character decided to off his partner.
    \n2. The madman had an evil plot to take over the world that started with offing the main character's partner.
    \n
    \nThe first is what folks who do theory often use to refer to plot, because it's in the context of player empowerment to create in play. The same folks (Josh, myself, etc.) would more often use the term situation to indicate not only the plots of the evil madman, but also just what characters are present, and anything else about the setting that's indicated as being immediately present.
    \n
    \nThe question of whether or not they have anything to do with the PCs right off the bat is irrellevant. They will have something to do with the PCs at some point, or there will be no play, however. For instance, in Paranoia, the "plot" or "mission" that the players are assigned has nothing at all whatsoever to do with the wants and needs of the characters directly - the computer doesn't care about them. But it will become important to the PCs, or Green Vulture Squad goons will descend upon them and make it important to them.
    \n
    \nSo, from this perspective, everybody agrees that a "Plot" as a set-up for interesting things to happen, is a great idea. What we all also all agree upon is that a "plot" where the GM decides what the PCs will do before hand is a bad idea.
    \n
    \nLet's leave the term plot behind. What we all want is the same two things. Some interesting set of creative elements that are presented to the players so they have something to react to. And then an allowance of the players to deal with those elements as they see fit. Then the elements respond as would seem likely. And the players respond again, sending the course of events in yet new directions.
    \n
    \nThis back-and-forth is, I think, what we're all in agreement should exist. When people hear me or somebody else say, "There should be no plot!" it's shorthand (I usually describe what I mean here in more detail) for, "Do not pre-plan the course of events in any way that assumes certain decisions being made by the players."
