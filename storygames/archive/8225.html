\n This is old but I just noticed it on En World... D&D; 3E included under powered feats and weapons to reward people for mastering the game: http://www.montecook.com/cgi-bin/page.cgi?mc_los_142
\n
\nAs people are playing more and more D&D; 4E, combat lengths are becoming more of a problem. Some interesting solutions are coming up including having the players spend a Healing Surge when the combat has solidly turned to the player's favor to end it early or have all the remaining characters turn into minions: http://www.enworld.org/forum/general-rpg-discussion/246131-i-think-were-done-d-d-4th-edition.html
\n
\nI know we've been playing 4E for around 16 sessions (almost level 6) and a combat round seems to be taking 12-15 minutes. We're probably playing 12 rounds of combat a night. And with our bonuses optimized, most rolls seem to have a 50/50 chance of succeeding. So out of 12 chances, we do something cool 6 times a night. When you throw in constant condition effects that limit our options, spending move actions to keep getting up from prone or being slowed or weakened and so on, I would say that I would reduce doing cool stuff from 6 to an average for 3-4 times a night. Plenty of great roleplaying thrown in helps and Kevin has started doing a great job of streamlining our encounters to resolve many of these issues. But I'm hearing more and more of these complaints coming up in person and online. The topic goes well with Jonathan's thread, Sell Me On: Nothing Happening (i.e. Rolling to Hit) and Chearn's earlier thread, Straight to damage.
\n
\nAnd over on Rob's thread, [Independent Insurgency] Episode 18: Luke Crane on Mouse Guard RP it's been announced that the Mouse Guard physical book is up for preorder on IPR and comes with an immediate free download of the PDF, character and GM sheets, and sample characters. It also comes with a free Mouse Guard comic. I don't know if this is a physical or digital comic.

    \nMearls talks about handling skill challeneges in 4e\n
    MearlsFailure should end the challenge and put a new obstacle before the PCs. Plus, failure can mean more than stopping short of your goal. Regardless of what happens, the PCs might get access to the tapestry, but they could end up accused of breaking into the palace, thrown into jail, in debt to one or more folks, and so on.

    The really fun thing for a DM when it comes to skill challenges is that you have a ton of options for making failure an interesting part of the game. Failing at a fight is pretty much always the same - the PCs are dead, captured, or running for their lives. Failing in a skill challenge can set up even more interesting complications, barriers, and challenges.
    \nIn how many indie RPGs have I read this same advice? Mearls FTW.

    \n [Independent Insurgency] Episode 19: Seth Ben Ezra on Dirty Secrets

    In this episode, I talk to Seth Ben Ezra of Dark Omen Games about his game, Dirty Secrets. Dirty Secrets is a modern crime/noir/mystery game without the mystery being predetermined. Our conversation touches on many issues surrounding the game, including how it intersects with Seth's religious faith and the roles of race and class in the game and in society. We also talk briefly about Seth's new game, A Flower for Mara.

    NOTE: This episode was shot in a hotel room where the air conditioning kept popping on and off. Hopefully it's not too distracting. Sorry!

    \nThis Gamasutra article on Luck is primarily interested in videogames, but could be useful in designing any game that may or may not involve randomized factors. It tries to address what influences luck has on the experience of play, by seeing how adding a random element changes the game experience. From that information, you can decide whether a random element matches a given game's goals.

    \n Thomas Malaby, over on Terra Nova, talks about how play and games aren't necessarily the same, pointing to the relationship between religious experience and ritual in anthropology. Play, he argues, like religious experience, is something like an orientation or a viewpoint, a framework or context for experiencing something. Games, like ritual, do not necessarily require one to approach them from a particular viewpoint (in this case, "play," since many things structured as games can be very serious or are open to being approached in a number of different ways). For me, this clarifies what people often mean when they insist "It's just a game" or "Roleplaying is about having fun"; what they mean is "You should approach this _as play_ (an entertaining diversion), not as an opportunity for creative expression, a challenge to be overcome, an exercise in cooperation, etc."

    It also points out, in my mind, how imprecise-to-the-point-of-near-meaninglessness the term "play" is in roleplaying discussions. It means, basically, "we collectively did some stuff," where "stuff" includes almost anything related to roleplaying.

      \n Anyone seriously interested in roleplaying theory should check out Ritual and Its Consequences: An Essay on the Limits of Sincerity, a new book from Oxford University Press by Adam Seligman (Religion, BU), Rob Weller (Anthropology, BU), Michael Puett (Chinese History, Harvard), and Bennett Simon (Psychiatry, Harvard Med) that proposes an alternative approach to the study of ritual and its ubiquitousness in our daily lives. I've heard Weller and Puett speak multiple times and they are smart cookies. Plus I was nodding my head on every page so far, due to the strong connection to roleplaying, and I haven't even gotten to the chapter on "play" yet.

      Here's their discussion of "shared imaginary space (SIS)" and/or Neel Krishnaswami's "shared symbolic language" and/or Ron's "exploration":

      When we say of a culture that its members share a symbol system, or a set of values, or a common idea of the sacred, we are in essence asserting that they share the potential space of a shared "could be." ...Much ritual action in fact provides this shared sense of empathy -- sometimes even in terms of an explicitly shared "what if." When Jews sit around the Passover Seder table and are explicitly enjoined to fulfill the commandment to feel "as if you yourselves had been liberated from Egypt," they create that shared symbolic space where the commonality of the "could be" becomes the very basis of the ongoing collective experience.

      However, they also suggest, as many folks have more recently suggested, that the shared symbolic space is ultimately illusionary, that different participants in a ritual don't actually feel or experience things the same way. However, the ritual space encourages us to act as if we do, which is the social medium (part of the social contract of participating in the ritual) that allows the group to participate collectively, while still retaining a crucial degree of flexibility and subjectivity.

      Can't wait to finish the rest of it.
