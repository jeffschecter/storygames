Playtesting at Cons

Ben_Lehman
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Directed Promotion
I was talking with Nathan about this at Dreamation, and I think that in the indie games exploded world it's a pretty big deal, so I want to have a talk about it. I have a few proposed solutions, but mostly I just want to hear opinions.

So there's two things that go on at IGE and both are important and valuable and I want to keep them, but they also interfere with each other.

1) IGE is a place where people come to try out these new kinds of games, looking for a new kind of fun, and as such in many ways it's a place where we, as progressive designers, should put our "best foot forward" with regard to games.

2) IGE is a place where a lot of new designers come with their untried games to figure out what to do with them. Often, playing these games isn't fun at all but it does provide very useful feedback for the designer, which can be useful in making the game good, later.

Can people see how these two things are contradictory to each other? I don't want someone to sign up for an untried or barely-tried game and have the experience suck, and come away with the thought that "all those indie games are just half-baked and unfun." At the same time, a vigorous playtesting culture is absolutely vital to our community, and ought to be present at cons as well.

I've got some of my own thoughts about this but I'd like to hear other people's opinions first. I'm likely to take a low view of people posting saying "this isn't a problem at all!" unless they have some very solid reasoning to back that up.

    Here's a distinction I'd like to draw for the purposes of this thread: Playtest versus preview.

    Playtesting is an untried / barely tried game that just isn't really fun yet. It could be fun, but the designer just doesn't know.

    Previewing is playing a game which is already basically done, having gone through several playtest cycles, and is now consistently fun every time it is played, but just isn't published yet. Previewing doesn't have the same tension with outreach that playtesting does.

    The line that Nathan and I drew was "%90 chance of awesome."

    The ugly truth of the matter is that not everyone who shows up and wants to play IGE games reads the forums, and might not know the difference between old reliable and the new, untested hotness. Imagine you're a gamer who has only heard tell of the independent gaming Shangri-la, you show up to a Con, don't know the difference between any of the games, and sign up for one at random. You might get a great game, you might get a playtest (probalistically less likely, I'd wager, to be a great game) and, like you said, have a sour taste in your mouth for the movement.

    However, I think there's a very natural solution to this which is probably already in effect. For starters, people who are new to IGE have probably heard about Dogs or Burning Wheel or Polaris or any number of other established, older games and will want to dig into those first. Second, I find it likely that people who are involved in the community and hatching new game ideas are going to want to work with the ("older") members of the community who already have a good grounding in other indie games, if only because they'll be much more able to suss out problems and offer very focused, constructive criticism.

    From a practical perspective, it might be wise to coyly note in an event description that its a playtest, or the game isn't yet published. I think this'd give uninformed players enough warning to steer clear if they're looking for a really polished game to sink their teeth into.

    I talked with a couple people about this at the con, and have been thinking about it.

    I don't think there should be any pressure or anything on people NOT to schedule their games in development in regular game slots. I think it's good form to clearly indicate the status of the game, and anything else that may be unusual (like "the last 1/2 hour of this slot will be devoted to feedback and discussion about the game"), but other than that, if you wanna run your game in playtest for 4 hours, cool.

    I do think that there should be some kind of structured thing for people who are playtesting a game that they may not want to run for 4 hours, or who know that the game needs play but may not be fun yet. I think that there are plenty of people out there (me included) who enjoy the act of playtesting and giving feedback on a game as it's own activity - the fun isn't necessarily in the game working, it's in helping the designer figure out what they're doing or how to fix problems. And, an organized thing would be very good in terms of fostering a community of playtest.

    Some thoughts: should this thing be inside or outside of the normal convention organizational rubric? It is certainly possible to put together an organized but informal "Playtest Track", with timeslots and everything, as long as it can be publicized beforehand and on-site. Petitioning con organizers to create an RPG Playtest track is also an option.

    An option that I like a lot is along the lines of what Jason mentioned above; scheduling a Playtest Event in a standard 4-hour game slot. The Playtest Event has, say, 12 slots open, and 2-3 designers bring their game in development. They could run side-by-side playtests, or play one game for 2 hours and another for 2 hours, or have two groups that each play for 2 hours and then switch game, so you effectively get 2 games of it in. This has the advantage of, if only 1 person signs up and 2 designers are already there, you have a group of 3, which may be big enough to playtest anyway.

    So, yes. Talking about this is good.

    Were I the draconian overlord of the IGE, here's what I'd do -- make a policy that all games scheduled on the convention grid be published games (even if "published" just means a free online download). Flat-out. No "preview" or "playtest" division. If it ain't already working and being played, don't schedule it during a convention slot. The scheduled slots would be the public face of the IGE.

    Now, I've personally benefitted from running playtest sessions at the local Double Exposure cons. I'm not saying not to have it going on, just not putting it out there with the finished games. Running games is a service that we provide in return for (a) our free convention passes, (b) exposure for our games, and (c) the joy of seeing our creations bring people delicious fun in four-hour bites. Playtesting our games is a service participants provide us, not the other way around. If you're a writer and you give someone a book you wrote just for them, that's a cool present. If you give someone a manuscript you're working on and ask for feedback, that's a burden you've dumped on them, not a present at all.

    So, how to gain the benefit of playtesting at the conventions? Simple...the playtests (and previews, as well) run off the grid. Making an internal schedule is great, but it has nothing to do with the convention program. We can even invite folks who show up at the booth or play indie games -- "Hey, if you liked this, you might want to get in on the secret playtest sessions of Game X. It's what all the cool kids are doing, anyway. It's a lot of work, but you get to have a hand in shaping the hot new games." This lets us put our best foot forward as a community, while still allowing for the vital process of peer review, analysis, and criticsm. It aslo makes it clear that this is something quite different from a normal play session. It also lets people feel like insiders, which is good because then they are part of something, as opposed to just doing something. I know that if Vincent or Tony or any of the other designers I look up to came to me and invited me to their room to playtest a new design, I'd be flattered and excited. If they asked me to fill out the slots on a playtest session they were running, I'd think it was a drag, even if I did it out of a sense of duty, friendship, community, or whatever.

    If you absolutely felt the need to publish the playtest sessions to the gaming community at large, I'd say you could put one single convention-long event on the grid that has a pitch to sell the idea of getting in on the playtesting as a thing itself, rather than pitching a particular in-development design. Then you could just route people to whatever is testing at that time, or provide playtests on request.

    Since I'm not the IGE draconian overlord, however, these are just my suggestions.
