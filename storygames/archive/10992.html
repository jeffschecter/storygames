    \n For me, it is extraordinarily difficult to win and requires a little luck and a lot of meticulous teamwork. The synergy of various roles leads to different strategies and different ominous weaknesses in each game. There's an element of suspense both in the initial epidemic draws and the shuffle-to-the-top mechanic (the game's killer app). It is cooperative so the raging competitiveness of some of my friends is attenuated. I like the color, too. And it plays in less than an hour.

    \n What Jason said.

    Particularly with the expansion roles added in, you get a huge variety in what you can do cheaply which really shapes how you approach the game. The degree and manner in which you travel and pass cards depends on the roles present which presents pretty different experiences from game to game.

    And especially because it's really easy to lose, but also clearly possible to win.

    The rules say that you can't show your cards, but you are free to completely discuss them. If you're being anal about constant communication, I think the game would slow down and be dull. We play a little more cagey with our hands than the rules strictly spell out because it's more fun. I think we sort of assumed that's how it was meant to be played based on our expectations from e.g. Shadows Over Camelot, but whether or not that's so, I think it's a good thing.

    We played twice on Wednesday. We lost the first game in literally eight minutes...outbreak-o-rama! The second game we finally lost on the very turn that we were going to cure the fourth disease. That was a really exciting loss!

    \n I like how the special abilities of the different roles combine with each other, like the Researcher and the Scientist, or the Operations Director and the Medic. The order of players' turns has as much impact as which roles are involved, and really increases the variety of strategies.

    When I lose (which is often), it's usually when we can see a victory condition, but bad luck swoops in and takes us down. Those almost-got-'em losses make the occasional victories sweeter.

    I like how the game forces you to choose between acting quickly and acting thoroughly.

    I introduced some extended family to the game during Thanksgiving, and while we never actually won, we always lost with victory nearly in our grasp, and we still had a good time. I'm impressed with how well the game is tuned to regularly create that condition.

    \n I enjoy it too but you definitely have to play it socially and not care overly win or lose.

    If you play it solitaire you can beat the standard difficulty pretty consistently (I use 3 pawns/roles when I do this), but when you don't have perfect information and one or more of the players are just sort of making silly/suboptimal moves you just want to suck it up and smile or it becomes an awful one-outspoken-person-deciding-everything-and-everyone-else-along-for-the-ride game. Bleh.

    I and my non-gamer family members like it enough to have purchased the expansion and I'm looking forward to giving the virulent strain, mutation, and bio-terrorist versions a try. The new roles have freshened the game too. The Archivist is awesome.

    \n Pandemic is a very well designed game, which is why I like it; the system is simple yet intriguing. It's also totally a solitaire computer game disguised as a boardgame, which is why I don't really care to play it. The multiple difficulty levels and such especially get my goat, a self-selected challenge doesn't really give me any sense of accomplishment. Playing the game feels like I really should be doing something to alleviate the boredom of everybody at the table by suggesting some real game, even when others might be enjoying it.
