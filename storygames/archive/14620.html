Chapter 1: Introduction

\n

What is this?

\n

This game an attempt to strip down and re-organize the 1974 OD&D White Box rules to be more in line with the game I'm working on for my own group.

\n

This version of Wrack & Wruin is precisely Breig/Finch Sword & Wizardry: White Box with a few clear changes in a changelog.

\n

Who is this for?

\n

This game is for

\n
    \n
  • anyone who wants to talk about OD&D in the context of Apocalypse World
    • \n
  • anyone who wants to talk about Apocalypse World in the context of OD&D
    • \n
  • anyone who thinks about Forge School versus Old School and who has some time to kill
    • \n
  • anyone who wants to follow the design work I'm doing for my home game
    • \n
\n

Setting up

\n

Someone will have to run the game; anyone who has read the rules should feel free. The person who runs the game is called the referee. Everyone else is called a player.

\n

The players will need to create characters. To create a character:

\n
    \n
  1. Choose a Character Class
    2. \n
  2. Fill out your Character Sheet based on that class's details at 4th Level
    3. \n
  3. Mark (3d6)*10 as your Starting Gold
    4. \n
  4. Spend some or all of your gold on equipment in the Equipment chapter
    5. \n
\n

The referee will need to prepare an adventure - a fictional situation for the player characters to unravel. The adventure usually comprises a map and your notes about certain locations on the map. At the beginning of the game, sketch out a single village and its surround. Then, as the player characters explore beyond this region, expand the map to the cities, forests, and kingdoms of the world beyond.

\n

Starting Play

\n

If you're a player, your job is to speak for your character, telling the referee what your character says and does.

\n

If you're a referee, your job is to provide interesting challenges and then present the fictional world fairly. This means cultivating an internal image of the campaign world, and speaking for the monsters and other characters the player characters will encounter during the game. When the players try something, refer to the map and your notes to describe what happens as a result. Sometimes the players will do something unexpected, and you\u2019ll have to think on your feet, making things up as you go. Stay true to prepared material, pay attention to details, make rulings dispassionately, and try to keep things consistent.

\n

Whether you're a referee or a player, sometimes what you say will trigger rules - that's called making a "move". Moves mostly revolve around combat, experience, physical movement, healing, dying, and magic.

\n

The game begins with the referee describing where the characters are, and what they see. As a player, describe what your character does. If that follows under some more specific move, like if you attack an opponent with a missile weapon use that move's rules. A few moves follow this section. Other moves, especially those in the Action chapter, can be used by either the referee or the players, depending on the situation.

\n

The referee decides where the game starts; it could be a rural peasant village, in a vast and teeming city spiked with towers and minarets, in a castle, a tavern, or at the gates of an ancient tomb. From that point on, each player describes what their character does. Going down stairs, attacking a dragon, talking to the people you meet: all of these sorts of things are player decisions. The referee then tells the players what happens as a result: the stairs lead down to a huge tomb, the dragon attacks your character, etc.

\n

Player Moves

\n

When you describe what your character does see if there is a more specific move that covers the action. If not, the referee will tell you what happens as a result.

\n

When you talk to the people you meet the referee will speak for them.

\n

Referee Moves

\n

Whenever you have a chance to talk and you aren't already making another move, describe where the characters are, and what they see. You can keep on describing, with little pauses for players to jump in, until a player makes a move or you see an opportunity to make another move.

\n

When the player characters go outside the bounds of what you've prepared, make something up.

\n

When the player characters try to do something that isn't covered by the rules, make a fair evaluation of the characters' abilities and their situation based on your understanding of the fictional circumstances. Describe what happens as a result.

\n

When you rule that time passes, inform the players that \u201can hour passes,\u201d or even, \u201ca month passes,\u201d in their characters' lives. You may use this move at your discretion.

    \n D&D written in Vincent's Apocalypse World argot? How... funky.

    I'm interested by the differences between this onthology of D&D and the way I understand the game. Would you say that this represents your true understanding of how D&D rules interface with the process of play, or are you intentionally applying the Apocalypse World construction? As a reference for where I'm coming from, I'll note that in my understanding the D&D rules system is almost entirely representative and realist in nature: it's not that certain actions in the fiction trigger certain pre-made rules systems, but rather that any and all activity in the fiction may or may not be interpreted in rules terms to quantify and bring the material into a gameable state as the group pleases; this process is wholly case-by-case, and the so-called "rules" in the game book are merely "common law" conventions transmitted to provide a rough basis to start with.

    In case the distiction I'm considering here is not clear, here's the part that attracted my attention:\n

    \nMy interpretation of D&D is that attacking your opponent with a missile weapon does not activate the specific rule regarding that circumstance, but rather merely puts the onus on the referee (and the rest of the group as his overseers) to evaluate the action for possible need of rules application, at which point the referee chooses an implementation appropriate to the situation - maybe most of the time it's going to be by the book, but that's up to the referee, and might change as circumstances dictate. Sometimes he's going to decide that the situation doesn't require a mechanical implementation at all, sometimes he decides that the standard implementation doesn't cover the special situation, sometimes he has a better (for a given value of "better") house rule, whatever. The point is that the ontological nature of the rules-text in D&D seems to me to be different from that of a game like Apocalypse World, say, which posits a duty where D&D merely posits an established precedence.

    Here's where I'm coming from:

    When I run earlier incarnations of OD&D, and I override the dice or make my own rulings where rules already apply, things go wrong. Players get annoyed - or if not annoyed, less engaged in play. The relationship between the rules and the referee is pretty ambiguous in the text.

    One way to resolve that ambiguity is to put the referee over the rules; another way is to make the rules a compact that everyone buys into. So I see what I'm doing more as collapsing that waveform in a way that wasn't apparent in 1974.

    Chapter 2: Character Classes

    \n

    There are three character classes in this game: Cleric, Fighter, and Magic-user.

    \n

    Reading Character Class Tables

    \n

    Level: Refers to the level of experience of the character.

    \n

    XP: This is the number of Experience Points (XP) needed to advance to this level.

    \n

    BHB: This is the \u201cBase Hit Bonus\u201d added to the attack roll. (See Combat)

    \n

    HD: This is the number of d6 Hit Dice at that level. Characters re-roll their HD each time they advance a level to obtain a number of hit points. (If you roll poorly, those hit points don\u2019t go down, however.)

    \n

    ST: This is the Saving Throw number for the character trying to avoid something terrible.

    \n

    The Cleric

    \n

    Most Clerics have a patron deity or serve a particular religion. Ask your referee if they use a particular mythology for the campaign you\u2019re playing in. If they don't, feel free to make up the details.

    \n

    You are an armored priest. You might be a sinister witch-hunter, an exorcist of demons, a shining knight of the faith, or a secret agent of the Temple hierarchy.

    \n

    Most of a Cleric\u2019s abilities are oriented toward healing and protecting, so Clerics tend to play a support role during combat.

    \n

    Clerics must use blunt weapons (club, flail, mace) and the only missile weapon they may use is oil.

    Chapter 4: Action

    \n

    Combat Moves

    \n

    Initiative

    \n

    When the party of adventurers comes into contact with enemies, each side rolls Initiative on a d6. The high roll wins initiative. Re-roll ties until there is a winner.

    \n
      \n
    1. Party with Initiative makes its moves (one per character/monster, in any order)
      2. \n
    2. Party that lost Initiative makes its moves (one per character/monster, in any order)
      3. \n
    3. The round is complete.
      4. \n
    \n

    Repeat until the battle is resolved.

    \n

    Movement

    \n

    When you move in combat, you move 12 feet per round. This can be done while making another move, such as casting a spell or attacking in melee. The referee may adjust this speed downward depending on the amount of equipment your character is carrying.

    \n

    When you run in combat, you move 24 feet per round. This action precludes taking another move. The Referee may adjust this speed downward depending on the amount of equipment your character is carrying.

    \n

    Melee Attack

    \n

    When you attack with a hand-held weapon in melee roll +Hit Bonus versus the target's Armor Class.

    \n

    On a hit, do your weapon's damage.

    \n

    On a miss, nothing.

    \n

    You must be within 10 feet of your target to take this action.

    \n

    Missile Attack

    \n

    When you attack with a missile weapon from range roll +Hit Bonus versus the target's Armor Class.

    \n

    On a hit, do your weapon's damage. A natural roll of a 20 deals double damage.

    \n

    On a miss, nothing. A natural roll of 1 is an automatic miss and the Referee may choose that this results in dropping a weapon or tripping.

    \n

    Firing into Melee

    \n

    When you fire into a melee, the Referee randomly determines which combatant will be the actual target (usually two opponents of equal size will be around a 50% chance). Then roll to attack with a missile weapon at range on this target, as detailed above. You may not decline this roll.

    \n

    Attacking Unseen Opponents

    \n

    When an opponent's general location is not known (i.e. to a 5-foot square), they cannot be attacked.

    \n

    If an opponent is not visible, and their general location is known (i.e. to a 5-foot square), they can be attacked, but they get a +4 bonus to AC. The AC bonus applies whether the attack is Melee or Ranged.

    \n

    Monsters with sensitive smell or hearing or more than six hit dice will frequently be able to detect unseen opponents within 5 feet; the Referee should determine the chance of this according to the creature concerned and the situation.

    \n

    Casting a Spell

    \n

    When you cast a spell you must declare you are doing so at the beginning of the combat round. Unless otherwise noted, the spell takes effect on the caster's initiative phase.

    \n

    When you suffer damage while casting a spell, the spell is lost. You lose your turn for this round.

    \n

    Saving Throws

    \n

    When a spell or other effect makes you roll a Saving Throw roll 1d20 vs your Saving Throw stat. If you meet or exceed that stat, you suffer the lesser effect.

    \n

    Turning the Tide

    \n

    When intelligent monsters face a hopeless battle, they will seek to retreat, surrender or flee, depending on their intelligence and type. Certain monsters, such as mindless or undead creatures, are fearless and always fight to the death.

    \n

    Damage and Death

    \n

    When a character (or creature) is hit, the amount of damage is deducted from his hit points. When hit points reach 0 the character is unconscious. The character can survive below zero by as many points as his level, so a 4th level character might die if he falls below -4 hit points., the character dies.

    \n

    Healing

    \n

    When a character binds their wounds after a battle they recover 1d4 HP. Note that the character can only recover hit points lost during this particular battle. Recovered hit points cannot exceed the uninjured maximum amount.

    \n

    When a character takes a full day of uninterrupted rest, they regain 1 hit point.

    \n

    When a character takes four weeks of uninterrupted rest, they regain full hit points regardless of how many hit points the character lost.

    \n

    Negotiation and Diplomacy

    \n

    When the party uses words to try to brazen past monsters whether or not this works is entirely up to the Referee. Players might use this to avert combat if the party is outmatched, or the monsters don't seem likely to be carrying much treasure.

    \n

    Retirement

    \n

    When you want to retire your character, ask the referee. The Referee has the final say on how and when retirement works for the campaign.

    \n

    Experience

    \n

    When you slay monsters you gain experience points (XP) based on the Monster's description.

    \n

    When you accumulate treasure you gain experience points (XP) equal to the value of the treasure in gold pieces.

    \n I find roleplaying stats to be rather straightforward: it's a challenge game, the purpose of stats is to be a tool that together with the environment defines your choice matrix, not a thespian guideline. Feel free to color your depiction of your character on the basis of the stats, but actually making bad decisions because your character is stupider than you are? That's screwing with the game, makes no sense and lets down the rest of the team. If you want to depict your character as stupid, go ahead and give your best ideas in a confounded voice and act like he accidentally stumbled on a good idea. Or tell your ideas to another player who can present them as those of his character's, while yours gives stupid advice that is roundly ignored. Your character's low Intelligence shows up in that the GM might require you to check it now and then for adverse consequences, and you can't rely on initiating checks yourself and expect it to succeed. The actual intelligence you play your character with is the same whether the character's Intelligence stat is high or low, but a high stat will help you play intelligently by allowing you to pass various knowledge, memory and abstract thinking checks that give you the tools for making good decisions.

    I find the above straightforward with the D&D mental stats, but I do admit that their names and superficial definitions encourage a thespian interpretation. For this reason I've clarified them for my own game by calling Intelligence "Education", Wisdom "Willpower" and Dexterity "Wits". They do the same things they do in standard D&D (except some things traditionally done with Int are done with Wits instead), but it's much more clear to the players that the statistics do not set thespian challenges to them - the stats explicitly leave the character's personal nature open for the player, opting to describe his mental qualities as "tools" of intellect that can be depicted as challenges and limitations without requiring the player to handicap himself.

    The above attitude does put me into diametrical opposition with some of the earliest D&D philosophy, but I don't find the thespian interpretation of voluntary handicapping in the name of "good roleplaying" to be in accord with the game's overall goals. There needs to be an explicit case-by-case agreement about such an handicap to avoid descenting into plain terrorism of the adventuring party's efforts: I do think that a player may well suggest that his character might suffer some handicap in some situation ("Shouldn't I make a Con check about staying in my armor all day in this desert?"), as this helps the common cause of exploring the fiction, but the GM needs to pick that up and run with it - it's just plain weird for a player to insist unilaterally that he should act suboptimally in some situation, even when the GM is not establishing such a requirement.
