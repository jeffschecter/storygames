\n So I am playing a 4th edition SR game with a friend, and we are having a good time, but the game text is not great for me. It reminded me that John Harper had whipped up that character sheet a while back, and so I am trudging through a more comprehensive conversion/hack. If I can keep with it I am sure I will run into more issues but the first and largest is finding an analog to 'seasons.'

It is a superb pacing mechanism for the MG system and I think that if used correctly it could be for SR also. The brainstorm ideas I've had that stood up to rudimentary review are:
1. A simple Mission/Downtime rotation, where the more missions you undertake between downtime counter a 'cost of living/maintaining SOTA' tax for downtime, at the risk of insufficient recovery time and probably increasing Mission Rating/check boxes (I am thinking higher rating missions have a better payoff, generally).
2. Subdivide the mission. First phase is recruit, research, recon, plan; second phase is approach, infiltrate, execute, retreat; phase three is regroup, deliver, get paid, debrief; phase four is downtime.
3. A 'Shadow Heat' cycle: Business as Usual, Easy Money/Milk Runs, Big Scores, Too Much Heat.
4. A 'conspiracy' cycle: The Pledge, The Turn, The Prestige.

With each of these comes the question of an analogous Weather Watcher skill and what it would affect.

Any help would be great. I've only run MG a few times, so my familiarity with any kind of emergent property of the season cycle is nil.

    \n A quick question, and hopefully it's not totally weird: Is your hack going to focus primarily on the hows and whys of the missions themselves, or will it perhaps just use the missions as backdrops for what happens between the characters? My thinking is, classic SR is pretty procedural, right? Like, the bulk of a session of SR is the actual doing-of-the-mission. Not saying that that's where you should put your hack's focus, just asking if that's how you want it to play out.

    The mix of mission-to-personal will impact your season and Turn rules. I like #2 very much if you want to play out your procedural, and #3 if it's kind of more about the lives of runners.

    I also like JWalt's thing about accumulating friction, and I'll bet you could touch on your #4 in that way: the conspiracy deepens as a Twist on failure.

    Perhaps you could have, kind of like weather twist economy in MG (more weather twists in the spring and winter, less in the summer and fall), a "fuck-over" economy tied to #3. Easy money means fewer fuckovers, while Big Scores and Too Much Heat mean more fuck-over twists. But they still constrain the GM so that not every single twist ends up fucking the team over with an ever-more-preposterous conspiracy.

    \n I dunno...seems like straight Cyberpunk would be incredibly easy to do via a MG hack. The mission/team focus, the authority figure who hands out a job, the breakdown between professional and personal effort. Nature is tricky, but it's always tricky despite what certain unnamed designers whose names start with L might say. The Nature-Mission-BIG triangle is probably the hardest to work through, mostly because traditional cyberpunk characters are so deliberately flat. But that's a limitation/feature of the genre, not the engine.

    Examples of character-driven cpunk literature would be useful to look at. I used to be totally bonkers for the genre but not for character reasons. I think I'd be more drawn to examples in the modern vein, Takeshi Kovaks type stuff, but even then characterization is perilously thin.

    But once you do have the triangle worked out, it then seems trivially easy to strap fantasy races (which is really just racism in drag, like always) and magic (which in SR is fundamentally just another tech) onto the whole thing. The equipment/upgrade list would be a crapton of work and I, personally, really dislike putting my energies into that sort of thing. I'm helping BWHQ with a project right now that happens to be equipment-heavy, and the spreadsheet makes my head spin. But that's where a lot of the juice is in cpunk gaming!

    \n It had crossed my mind. I have not played with it! But my theory is that it is a dangerous trap, when your edge pretty specifically doesn't cover fighting or building real relationships. The characters will be good shadowrunners, which is great, but the desire to use the hammer all the time will lead to tax and the need for recovery, and drifting toward the sociopathic Traits and being taken out of play until you can play nicely with others at 7; it is part of the GMs job to provide obstacles worthy of being challenged, both in terms of substantial and justified Ob ratings, and in terms of hard choices (like, using my Nature or addressing my BIGs).

    Shadowrunners aren't mice, though. They are supposed to be badeasses that are admittedly up against long odds, and more or less good at what they do. In this version, at least! Despite the shift, I think MG will hold up fine - as I said above, the twists and conditions let 'runs go bad without bringing them to a screeching halt. Having Nature be up-front what being a runner is about will push things that direction; if I were interested in a more dramatically sophisticated premise then I'd use Edge (Metahuman) and tag it with (off the top of my head) something like Being Passionate, Being Opinionated, Being Bought. Ok, those aren't great, but you know, something orthogonal to being a runner that pushes toward a bigger picture and opens the field for character exploration and thematic expression.

    \n Legwork in MG falls into one of several categories. The first is information the characters should know, which is up-front or for the asking. The second is information the characters will need to know, which will be an obstacle and test as normal (resulting in Conditions, Twists, or of course even possibly success), or might be the natural return on following through on an Instinct. The third is through spending checks in the player's turn. These are what I can think of off the top of my head.

    But admittedly the focus is the mission, where the legwork either pays off, is useless, or turns out to be a liability. Treating the results of useful legwork properly implemented as a gear bonus is the simplest method.
