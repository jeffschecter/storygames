Josh Roby
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Play Advice
So I recently got to play Primetime Adventures and Nine Worlds. I'm also playtesting Agora online a lot. In PTA, players throw around Fan Mail and accumulate litle piles in front of them. In Nine Worlds, you play half of every scene with a first full of cards. In both cases, the tactile experience of having this stuff in your hand, stuff that often represents your options and resources in the game, is pretty cool. By contrast, when I take the very dice-fiddly system of Agora into an online setting, it's been difficult to keep the dice in front of the players, which is essential information as they proceed through each scene. I think I solved this adequately, but the amount of effort involved in getting this accomplished underscored how much the dice on the table convey to the players.

So the thesis is: games with stuff that you hold in your hand or move around the table or whatever are pretty cool.

Can somebody who's played Exalted on tabletop talk about essence motes tracked with glass beads? I understand Iron Heroes uses tokens; how's that work out around the table? What about Deadlands' use of cards and poker chips? Do these fiddly bits make the game a little more tactile, hands on, and fun? Or am I barking up a ficus?

    All I can say is that you're dead right. Here are things that I've seen noted before about why "stuff" works well:
    - Psychologists tell us that when we touch an object it creates a feeling of ownership of that object. This is why music stores will insist you play the instruments.
    - Given that people get that little ownership high, handing out such tokens makes reward systems a bit more potent as players will (irrationally), want the objects more than if they were handled abstractly. On the other end...
    - Objects can serve as cues or triggers. This is something that people often miss, but if you have a rule that has nothing to indicate that it can be used ever, it will tend to get forgotten. Tokens remind people that they have them to use much better than pushing around numbers on a sheet.
    - Tokens movement can shortcut the need to communicate something. The movement can act as the communication itself, and say very complex things, too.

    In Universalis, for instance, people tend to pitch Coins into the pot as they narrate, obviating the need to state which things it is that they're paying for (though often they have to recap afterwards). In the game Crux - still not done, IIRC - the question of where a token is, in the bag, or elsewhere has significance about what it means (it's part of the character if it's in the bag).

    So, yeah. The only problem I personally have is that I like fine granularity, and that means lots of token handling potentially. I'd love it if somebody could come up with a solution to that problem.

    Mike

    I like that one, and it triggered another idea. The mage player must extract from the jar/bag a number of beans sufficient, but not excessive, for the spell he/she intends. The real-world time it takes to describe the spell, extract the beans, and place them in a bowl is the amount of in-game time the mage takes to cast the spell. So you can go for carefully, but more slowly, taking the right number of beans (and possibly getting beaten to the punch), or you can just grab approximately the number of beans and risk the consequences.

    Which are: If you take too few beans, the spell falls short in some way (distance, area of effect, power, whatever) and doesn't achieve all you hoped. If you fall short by more than a defined number, say 5, it doesn't go off at all and you have wasted your turn and your beans/mana resource.

    If you take too many beans, the spell is overpowered, and Bad Things happen. Worst case, you may face the Death of Insanely Overpowered Fireballs. Or you may just get some splashback, affecting your companions, yourself, and/or innocent bystanders, or causing the intended effect but not in the intended manner. (The target troll shrinks, but retains all his strength; he's now harder to hit but he can do serious ankle damage.)

    You grab the beans first, count them and work out the match/mismatch to the declared spell's required number of points afterwards. So you need to know your spells, and how many beans make five.

    1. Choose your awesome magical affect. We identify a casting cost in a number of beans.
    2. Scoop your mana!
    3a. If you select exactly enough, you're golden.
    3b. If you scoop too much, the GM gets the excess.
    3c. If you scoop too little, we figure out a Price. Then you choose to Pay it, or Fizzle.
    4. Effects are narrated.

    Posted By: Joshua BishopRobyI understand Iron Heroes uses tokens; how's that work out around the table?
    Technically, IH doesn't use literal tokens, it just uses the term. I think Mearls used "tokens" as an artifact from the video-game power-ups that inspired the mechanic, as well as to differentiate them from other "points" in the game, e.g., hit points. At least, the book never outright says, "Use a physical representation for these at the table."

    What's interesting is that the mere use of the term makes an impression; I knew, intellectually, that Mearls didn't mean "glass beads," but I used them for tokens in my IH games anyway.

    What's even more interesting is that the players in the IH games I've played eventually stopped using the beads. Tokens are generated by player choices, not handed out by the GM, and they come and go a lot. Ergo, it became a lot simpler to just track your own tokens as tick marks on paper; constantly reaching into a communal bowl for beads was a PITA.

    Of course, it's important to remember that IH is a D&D variant, and thus already has a big tactile aspect: minis on a battlemat.

    And this, gentle readers, is a reason why I think that it's cool when indie, non-mini-using RPGs find ways to "add back in" tactile bits (and why many "lite" RPGs that remand the battlemap to the GM's crainium kinda don't interest me).

    E.g., for the few BW games I've run, I printed out and laminated the Fight! and DoW scripting sheets for use with erasable markers. They not only serve a mnemonic purpose (having all the maneuver data), but also are like a scorecard the player gets to fiddle with during the game. There's a positioning comparison diagram on the BW wiki that is also very cool, and which I'd like to find more ways to use at the table. And the diagraming aspect of Firefight in BE looks really freakin' cool.

    I wonder if there's also a social contract issue at the core of a lot of fiddly stuff. Mearls has written about the importance of minis in tactical RPGs; they negate "Mother may I" in combat. I would think that fiddly bits in other types of RPGs serve a similar purpose. The proof of your fan mail, bennies, token abilities, whatever is sitting there right in front of you.

    Josh, it's like this:

    There's a mission you've arrived at collectively (via a fun method, not relevant). Everybody throws two dice in a common pot (these mean something and offer interesting choices but, not relevant). Anyway, you toss in your two dice, so does every other player.       Hmm. Okay, I like minis-A.Lot. But I've also regularly heard similar comments from multiple posters across multiple boards.

      I'd like to point out that often ( not necessarily refering to Drew specifically), these opinions seem based on very limited types of miniatures usage. Generally, that usage is only in the context of tactical combat, usually in math heavy systems, and not infrequently with systems that have lots of stop and start reaction effects.

      So yeah, fiddly.

      However, I don't think that as much emphasis has been placed on exploring other ways that minis could be used. Or for that matter, I don't think all that deep a look has been taken at both the potential and limitations of a minis heavy medium.

      Just off hand, I've gotten a lot of use out of the idea of using minis in a fashion similar to the use of the vision cads in Everway. The mini becomes a source of inspiration, questions, and background building. Beyond that, other models ( terrain, etc) further that concept. That's just off the top of my head.

      Things I don't see as much are fundamental questions. What does scene-framing mean in the context of minis usage? What part could the hobby aspects (painting and modelling) play in mechanics? How could real world concerns like dollar cost and building time be integrated into a game system heavy on minis. At what times are detailed movement and range systems valuable in relation to using minis, and when can the dispensed with in favor of something simpler?

      I think there are other questions that relate as well: With newer game designs experimenting with shared narration and shared adventure/world-building, can some of those concepts be translated for minis use? Is there room for mechanics that interact with both a metagame/club level of player interaction and the tabletop play itself ( not unlike the optional Universalis metagame duties awards)?

      I don't know the answers to those, but I am awfully curious.

      I actually like the emotional distance using miniature (etc) can put between players and the characters. It makes for a different type of game.

      "I swing my sword at the Orc!" vs. "The Elf swings her sword at the Orc!"
      You ARE the Elf. vs. You control the Elf character.

      For my game I want people to feel they are controlling characters rather than BEING those characters. The characters are working together... but the gameplay is competitive.

      If you're working on a game that's more on the RP side than the G side, I agree that things like miniatures can detract from the immersive experience.
