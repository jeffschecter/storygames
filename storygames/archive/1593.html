[M-B] Sliding scale of hippiness

TonyLB
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
So ... something I often say is that Misery Bubblegum isn't one of those dirty hippy games. It has a GM, and the GM narrates the outcomes of conflicts. Players have characters, and they're the ones in charge of those characters. Get a haircut and a job, you freaks!

Well.

I've also got all these wonderfully juicy internal conflicts, like "Where do I belong?" and "Why do I hurt people?" and stuff like that. And I know that they can sing, because I've seen them sing ... but here's the thing: They only seem to sing when I break the rules I list above.

How do I know? Here's my evidence: In first games with people (where I'm serious about the "Let's not be dirty hippies!") internal conflicts often go unresolved from lack of interest, or resolve early in the game to make way for (usually) relationship conflicts. In second games with the same people (when I forget that we're not hippies) internal conflicts resolve late in the game with high amounts of interest.

So here's my hypothesis, backed by that evidence and decades of anecdotal instinct: Any conflict is only fun when everyone around the table has the tools to get involved in it. Internal conflicts require hippy techniques in order to be fun for the whole table.

Specifically: Imagine I'm playing Lenny, and Lenny has an internal conflict "Why don't I stand up for myself?" We're not playing a dirty hippy game, and so I always get to decide whether Lenny stands up for himself or not. I play Lenny, I get him into a conflict about whether he gets beaten up by bullies, we roll and I lose. I decide not to have him stand up for himself, and he gets beaten up.

Nobody. Else. Cares. Why? Because they had no input.

Now, compare: I'm playing Lenny, but we're dirty hippies. If I lose this conflict then someone else gets to tell me what the character feels. Not necessarily what he does, but what he feels. I lose the conflict with the bullies. I decide that Lenny will stick up for himself (even knowing that the bullies are destined to beat him down). The winning player tells me that they beat Lenny into the dirt and furthermore that Lenny is filled with shame that he's not man enough to protect himself. If he can't do that, how could he ever protect the girl he loves? He couldn't!

Suddenly, everyone cares. Why? Because they had input. They got to add specifics (in improv terms, heighten in both details and emotion) to the nature of Lenny's internal conflict. They got to narrow down the arena in which it will play out.

So ... implications!

External conflicts don't need anyone to have input on anything other than what's happening. They should be resolved at the furthest point from hippiedom ... GM narrates what happens, players play their characters, yadda yadda.

Relationship conflicts are more fun when people are able to modify the relationship in a meaningful way. I think that means that when you win a conflict where someone else has relationship stakes you should be able to modify (read carefully now!) what they think the other person thinks about them. Not what they think about themselves, or what they think about the other person, but what they think the other person thinks. If I lose a conflict of "Lenny loves Mary" then Mary's player gets to tell me that Lenny thinks Mary thinks he's a weakling.

Internal conflicts are more fun when people are able to modify the internal stakes in a meaningful way. I think that means that when you win a cnoflict where someone else has internal stakes you should be able to modify what they think about themselves. Not what they do, not what they think about other people, but what they think about themselves.

It's a sliding scale of hippiedom, and it is established when you first offer those conflicts! If you don't want somebody to tell you what your character thinks about himself then you simply do not offer forth an internal conflict. You aren't comfortable with the techniques that are required in order to make it fun for other people. That's cool. You probably do a rockin' job with external conflicts, so go there!

Likewise, if you don't feel comfortable playing a character without that sort of group input then you don't offer external conflicts. You aren't comfortable with the techniques needed to make that fun for other people, so you just don't do it.

So what do people think of this? Do you think I'm right about what techniques are needed to make what types of conflicts sing? Do you think it's sane to offer a sliding scale within the same game? Do you think that the very idea is judgmental and arrogant, saying "Oh, well, if all you can handle is external conflicts I suppose we can find a minor role for you on the periphery of our great artistry"?

    Nice. You are definitely onto something here. This makes so much sense and seems so natural that in retrospect, it seems like a "Well, duh!" sort of moment even though at the time it was anything but.

    I think the observation that people can play the conflict types they are most comfortable with is very powerful. Far from arrogant, it is the opposite. People can do what works for them and offer appropriate input into other players' characters in terms of what each player is interested in. This also discourages the kind of projection you see in games where people give the sort of input to other players in terms of what he or she would want instead of what the other player wants.

    The only caveat is that you need the proper mix of issues on the table as a whole. If everyone wants to do only external issues, for example, you have a problem. Nothing can match up for conflicts. The question is do you want to accept this as a limitation or change the rules to make it possible to match conflicts of similar type?

    Rules thought: Right now, everybody in the game starts with the same number of their dice on the table, all (starting) in their own control. Dice that go to other people indicate that the other people can later have emotional impact on that player's character.

    What if you do this?

    Each player starts with ... say ... nine dice in their color.

    For each card that you add to the table (in prep-time) you dump some dice into a pool at the center of the table:
    - External: Add three dice of the GM's color.
    - Relationship: Add two dice of the color of the player who added the card, and one of the player of the other character in the relationship.
    - Internal: Add three dice of your color.

    After all the cards are added, people take turns pulling a die from the pool to add to their own. Maybe they'll steal back theirs. Maybe they'll hunt the dice of other people. Whatever they do, the dice will end up a little bit shaken up. Maybe it would jumpstart the game.
