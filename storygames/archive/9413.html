\n The first paragraphs are just background, you can skip down to the bold stuff if you just want the rules.

Our Mouse Guard game is soon ten sessions into the story. The plot started Spring the same year as the False Black Axe rebellion, and now we are in the following year, trying to find other sympatisers and rebuilding Lockhaven that took a heavy beating in the attack.

The group is huge, about eight players + GM, and we play as long as at least three players show (I am not the GM). Player background in the hobby varies greatly from the GM with a simulationist background (who wants to see what this Story game fuzz is all about) and me (with a story focus), with about 20 years of experience each to some beginners still struggling with understanding the whole matter of who decides what in the narration, and the rules.

The rules are a problem, everything takes FOREVER, we simply have problems getting any story done at all because the rules are so complex. After yesterday's game we had a talk, and the next time we play the game will be changed as follows:

Skill checks
When you roll for a skill check, you roll the dice from that skill as usual. You can use as many traits as you want. You can draw help from experience in other areas from ONE other skill, rolling it and using any successes as bonus dice on the check.

Helping
You can get help from ONE other player too, that rolls ONE skill and gives the successes as bonus dice on the check.

TSOY style bonus dice
Everyone gets five poker chips. They can be played to give another player a bonus die on any roll, no matter if your PC is in the scene or not.

Chips that remain after the game are discarded, so no point in saving. This will let everyone help if they really want (beyond the ONE player help described above), but without the burden of finding a reason why you can help, just spend the chip. I hope this will have the positive side effect of keeping players interested in the game, since they now can take part even in scenes where their PCs are not directly involved.

    \nThis was a great success! I had expected it to be a good thing in this group but it was simply great. The more quiet players got activated and started commenting on the game and stated their reasons for supporting a certain action and got really enthustiastic when someone helped them in the same way. This is definitely a keeper.

    The GM decided to change the rule so that we will only have two chips per player, since we are rolling so few rolls these days that the balance of the game is threatened. I think I'll try to convince him to increase it to three. After this game almost all players had three unspent chips left. But I think it would be better to have one too many than one too few, considering the effects the chips had on the metaplay.

    Failure in the player turn
    Also I see a tendency of the checks in the player turn to be set upp in such a way that the players expect to fail, to get the fail checks under "safe conditions" so they can raise skills. The GM grumbled a bit about it, and I am not sure I like it either even though I do it myself. I'll have to give more thought on the matter. I can see the reason for doing so, since failure in the GM turn almost certainly cause conditions that threaten the survival of the entire party, but a failure in the player turn where the characters have split up to do their own things the horrible backlash only affects one character. Taken to the extreme this would lead to supermice running through the GM turn with all successes followed by a slapstick player turn where everyone fails at everything. Might not be a bad thing, I'll give it more thought.
