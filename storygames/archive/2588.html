\n What's your view on terms and labels that are used in a game text to help explain concepts or refer to processes?

Things like "Attack of Opportunity" (D&D), "Aspects" (SOTC), or "Kicker" (Sorceror).

Do you usually Capitalize them every place they're used?

Do you just explain what they mean once and then use them with no other mark of their significance (e.g. no font difference, italizing, capitalization) throughout the rest of the text?

What do you find most helpful when you read a gaming text? Is Capitalization (or other differentiators) a distracting interruption to normal expectations of sentence construction? Or are they welcome signposts that remind the reader that key concepts are being discussed?

I'm kind of on the fence with this. I find that it's helpful when key concepts are called out in the text in some fashion (makes skimming easier). But it can be exhausting and disconcerting to read a paragraph dense with non-traditional capitalization or font use.

Your comments and experience (as creators and users of game design texts) appreciated.

    \n Overcapitalization tires out the eye. Overstyling the text (italicizing, bolding, using a different font) does the same thing.

    Ideally, you'd want to minimize the amount of special terms in your game, and make what few terms you have something approaching normal language. Rather than say, "Then, make an Acro[2]+Dex vs 2d6 roll" you really can say, "Then, make a roll using pumped acrobatics and dexterity against a 2d6 difficulty." Sure, it's longer, but it doesn't kill your readers' eyes.

    \n I'm an ex-technical writer. I bold any jargon words when I introduce them so it's easy to scan back and find the definition.

    (I really wish journalists would do that with the names of people they're quoting, so you wouldn't have to scan half the article to remind yourself who "Smith" is.)

    Easy scanning is probably the key. Capitals, in moderation, assist this; in immoderation, they detract from it.

    If you have so much jargon that you're capitalizing everything in sight, you probably have too much jargon.

    Also, keep your paragraphs short and have a single main point in each of them.

    \nIt depends on the level of the manual and the obscurity of the term. The style-and-define is ripped right from mid-level technical tutorial books. Higher level texts don't style defined terms at all, or use jargon with the assumption that the audience understands it already, which usually means finding a definition (idempotent, isosynchronous) or figuring out the meaning from context. Pulling the definitions is also common in more introductory texts.

    I'm surprised Josh BishopRoby hasn't already gone buck wild here, seeing as how he edits school textbooks all day.

    In an early draft of Greylight, I was using the Capitalized Mechanical Terms, and in a sort of crux paragraph is read like a German Book about Role Playing Games where every Noun Phrase had Capital Letters and was a Staggering Feat to read without suffering a Complete Mental Failure. So I've sort of cooled on that terminology style.

    The trick is to find a word or noun phrase that you can use identifiably and unambiguously throughout the text, without breaking the Strunk und Vargnles style guides.

    \n What Mike said above.
    \n
    \nUniversalis was interesting in this regard, because we had a ton of jargon that we needed for the game. And we started capitalizing all of these. So we'd get sentences like "When Framing a Scene the Framing Player must establish Time, Location, and Introduce at least one Component. He may not be Interrupted before he has made this first Introduction."
    \n
    \nHere's the big problem with this (other than the capitalization becoming useless in the mess). Notice how Framing and Framing Player are both capitalized? They have unique meanings in the game, of course, but what you should avoid are making new jargon terms from the existing ones just by modifying the term for situation. Instead the sentence should say "the player doing the Framing." Yes, it's more wordy, but you avoid creating a new term by doing so. Framing has been defined, and we know what a player is, so we don't need an additional capitalized term that indicates the player who is doing the framing. Similarly, it's Introduce or Introduction, depending on if you want a verb or noun for your term. And you probably want a noun. Because it's far less awkward to say, "...until he has made an Introduction" than to have to say, "He may not be Interrupted until he does Introduce a component."
    \n
    \nJust as a practical example. I believe that it was Rich Forest who taught me this lesson.

    \n Forests of capitals remind me of the blurbs on those genre-fantasy novels that I pick up in the library and always put down again after reading about two sentences of the blurb. "The T'Tin are invading G'foth, and Plador, the Man in White, is all that stands between the High Falutin and the fanatical Mazors..."

    So I try not to capitalize too many words unless not doing so will lead to confusion between the technical sense in which I am using them and the sense they normally carry. For example, in City of Masks I capitalize "Face" when I'm referring to the social currency, because I also refer to faces meaning the things on the front of people's heads. But "mask" I leave uncapitalized because it's clear what I'm referring to.
