\n Hi all,

I'm running a traditional rpg for a group of friends that have primarily played traditional rpgs. We've had attendance issues lately, so I've been thinking about starting a 'secondary' game we can play when don't have a quorum of players for the main story. I see this as a great opportunity to introduce them to more Narr or indie-style gameplay (which I suspect they will all really dig, once they've tried it out).

I have limited experience with indie games myself (I've played The Mountain Witch and Shab Al-Hiri Roach), and I was wondering if anyone could suggest a game that would meet two criteria:

1) A SG-style game that is great for people new to that play-style.

2) A game that will work well for one of two possible game concepts.

In our 'primary' game, the PCs are vigilantes in a pre-Batman, 1920s-flavored Gotham City. It's a plot-heavy game that focuses on the PCs efforts to stop the mobster-related violence that plagues the city.

For the 'secondary' game, I have two game concepts in mind.

First idea: the PCs are convicts at Blackgate Prison that are plotting a jailbreak. The game would focus on character status within the prison and perhaps trust/relationships between characters.

The second idea: the PCs are beat cops trying to keep the streets of their neighborhood safe, while juggling with politics within their precinct and the GCPD as a whole (think LA Confidential or The Shield).

Or, if anyone has some other awesome ideas for a secondary story, I would love to hear them.

Anyways, thanks in advance for your thoughts. Feel free to plug your own games, if you think it would meet my needs!

    Spirit of the Century is designed for pick-up play and seems tailor-made for your first (batman) idea.
    \n
    \nI think SotC makes a good game to introduce traditional gamers, because it combines elements of both Forge-style games and traditional ones. The default setting may be a bit too similar to the game you've got going now (the batman-ish one), since it's all about 1920's pulp. It is, however, pretty easy to modify for other settings.
    \n
    \nBurning Wheel might be a good bet for similar reasons - Luke has described it as a "gateway" into indie gaming, and I agree. When I introduced it, it was so well received that every single one of my play group went out and bought their own copy. Oh, and so did the game store owner who overheard us talking about it.
    \n
    \nIf you want to go with more purely Forge-style games, Primetime Adventures is tough to go wrong with as long as your players watch some TV drama. If anyone's a fan of Lost or Battlestar Galactica or Buffy/Angel, I'd definitely give it a shot.
    \n
    \nSince it sounds like you're having attendance troubles, InSpectres might also be a good one to try - it's rules are made with a revolving cast of players in mind, so it's great as a back-up game.
