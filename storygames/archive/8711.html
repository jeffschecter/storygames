\n The comment below is obviously from another discussion, but it so concisely describes the issue I want to address that I decided to start the discussion with it.\n
Posted By: JDCorleySure, in action games everyone needs a niche that is theirs and theirs alone, but few in my group are engaged enough audience members to care about anything that doesn't have something at stake directly for them.
\nI consider this to be HIGHLY toxic to ANY kind of group play.

I do not know if it's because some players are too narcissistic to have an interest in others, or not interesting enough to be engaging to others, but I do know that play sucks a fat one when this cross player engagement does not happen. Players who don't find other players engaging enough to care about what they're up to are simply waiting around till its their turn, making other players nothing more than an inconvenience. And the only things left to engage with are the GM and the rules.

If that's the kind of play happening at the table, then you're better off with a video game.

For a while, I was creating games which were designed to make sure stakes were shared, or at the very least co-dependent, in order to encourage players to become involved with each other. But even when it worked perfectly, the resulting game wasn't fun unless the players wanted to engage with each other to begin with.

Luckily, I don't do that any more...much.

So anyway, I'm interested in what everyone else's take is on this, and if anyone thinks I'm overreacting.

    \n I think it's one of a set of factors that combine for toxic play as opposed to "suboptimal":

    - the "prima dona" Player who expects they should be able to take up 20 minutes of table time with some play involving only their character, but become disruptive if the same is ever case for anyone else (e.g., tolerates no messing with their play but then becomes Thunder_God's hyperengaged, intruding into others' creative control of their PC's when it's someone else's turn in spotlight). I see this complaint on message boards fairly often though luckily haven't played with anyone like that in ages - the last time it was two competing "stars" who were really dealing with other issues between them as people.

    - "the samurai, cowboy and monkey" party phenomenon - the group of divergent characters with no crossing or aligned interests - if combined with "I don't want to be audience to the others' on their stuff" that is a form of self-adminstered fun-poison. I game with experienced Players who will talk upfront in creating characters how they will avoid this.

    \n I'm going to point at two of my own games because I'm the most familiar with them:

    So I built the central conceit of Full Light, Full Steam to address this issue. There was a player who often complained that nobody cared about her character, who her character really was, and only cared about how much damage she brought to the table when combat came around. So I built the scrip system that required character interaction along lines of strong characterization. Went over like a lead balloon. Turns out that while the player wanted everybody else to care about her character, she really wasn't too interested in caring about other players' characters, and was almost offended that the game required her to do so. However, in playing the game with other folks, I find that the scrip system facilitates a sort of play close to television serials where characterization is constantly being reinforced by inter-character banter.

    More recently, I designed Agora, which takes a slightly different approach. In this game, you and your character have an agenda, and you work to forward that agenda. You do not especially care about the agenda of other characters in the game except where they intersect with yours. Finding those intersections is heavily incentivized, because when you can work together you are nigh-unstoppable. And to add another layer onto this, everybody at the table is aware of this incentive and since everybody is playing rival characters and factions, it is in each player's best interest to prevent those alliances along agenda-intersections. So you have players pointing out, often through gameplay and not table chatter, the differences between player agendas at the same time that players try to discover and hold on to their overlaps.

    So here's the thing: in FLFS there is heavy, heavy awareness of where players plug into their characters and the rest of the game. And this is fine for some modes of play and some preferences. Sometimes, however, it's too much. You can only have the happy shiney love-fest for so long before it gets old. Or perhaps I can only have the love-fest for so long before I get tired of it. In any case, it's not always the right tool for the job. Sometimes, not caring about other players, except in the most cynical and self-serving fashion, is the order of the day.
