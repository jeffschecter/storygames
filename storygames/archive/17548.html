\nFrom my experience from other fora, gender-related topics always ends in flame wars. So please leave your personal opinions about gender out of this thread and please answer only my original questions.

In my upcoming game, I will leave out third personal singular personal pronouns like "she", "her", "him" and "he" but I need some advice on how to write so it comes naturally. I'm trying really hard to be non-gender specific when writing posts here, but I never feel it comes natural for me. I also don't want to use "hir" or anything similar to that.

Are there ways of how I should think? Are there any good home pages out there that I can read about this?

Right now, I'm leaning towards using "you" for the GM and "they" for the players. Does that sound good?

    \n Personal opinions about gender are easy to leave out. Personal opinions about natural ways to write English gender-neutrally are an entirely different sausage.

    For my part, I would go with "they", "them" and so on. I'm not native to English, so maybe it's easier for me to just ignore my own expectations, but be that as it may "they" just doesn't sound very strange to me. It's no stranger than the English insistence on using the second-person plural for the singular as well. If you have a good reason to introduce a gender-neutral third person (and who wouldn't, right?), then "they" is an entirely reasonable leap for how English works historically. Much larger changes have been made for much less reason.

    \n I find it's easiest to write gender-neutral instructional text in the second person. Not just for the GM, but for everybody. Also, if I start off a paragraph in the third person (where you can use "they"), that can set up who the text is for, and then I can switch to second person and provide instructions. Like this:

    "Framing scenes is the GM's responsibility. When you frame a scene, you do this, that and the other thing. Ask the player(s) what they blah blah blah."

    "Players portray one character each. In portraying your character, you yadda yadda yadda."

    Obviously this is unless I have some other way to select the specific audience (ie "Players' Section" or whatever). If it's all third person, they they they they them all over the place gets a little tiring (I mean, so does he him his). If you have two people in an example, though, using "he" for one and "she" for the other is a good way to differentiate them.

    \n There are lots of ways. (I am summarising some of the stuff that's been said above here.)

    Look how Vincent does it in his games: he keeps to the second person most of the time. ("If you're the GM, you...", "When it's your turn, you...").

    You can pluralise: "When players want to hit something, they roll X".

    You can use clever rephrasing. Rather than "Sam wants to do X, so she rolls Y" you can say "Sam wants to do X, so rolls Y". And similar stuff: "The player whose turn it is rolls X".

    You can alternate chapter by chapter. This is a little old-fashioned, but I really like it. Or you can assume the GM is female and the players are male.

    With a combination of these, it works fine.

    \n The following is not to criticize any specific poster, I just noticed that reassigning "he/she" or writing so you don't need a third person singular pronoun have come up several times.

    I don't personally care much for the solutions that rely on writing around the issue or using artificial conventions like switching pronouns arbitrarily by chapter or reassigning "he" and "she" to player roles. This seems unnecessarily timid. If a writer is comfortable outright inventing words for a piece of fantasy writing, then it should be possible to innovate with a more mundane language issue. Besides, anybody who's decided to forgo the standard English convention has already decided that they want change, so backpedaling on how you achieve it seems illogical. You're bold enough to say that the non-gendered "he" is not good enough for you, but not bold enough to actually choose an alternative?

    An argument from public interest won't sway a selfish person (meaning, somebody who just wants to solve their own problem and not the universal underlying issue), but for me a compelling reason to boldly tackle these types of relic culture issues is that an evasive solution reaffirms the status quo. If I wanted to make sure that the standards of language never change, then it would make perfect sense for me to attempt to be as sly as possible in the way I write around a standard that offends me. However, if I actually wouldn't mind change, then the thing that makes the most sense is to boldly choose for yourself to make that change. This publicizes the issue and enables others to follow you, and in the long run there will thus be a change if your solution has truth to it.

    As for whether that long-term solution should be "they" or "hir" or another newly invented pronoun, I couldn't say - as a foreigner I'm not politically part of the franchise, so to say; my choices might influence some in minor ways simply because I use English, but I don't have the emotional connection to the language that would make my opinion particularly authoritative insofar as beautiful and intelligent development of the language goes. I know that English probably needs some real solution to this in the future instead of endlessly writing around it, but the choice of a new dominant paradigm will have to be up to the natives.

    I could say, though, that when I've come upon similar linguistic issues with Finnish, I have been bold in taking a stand for the sort of language I prefer. It just makes most sense that if you actually have an opinion, you should act on it. Deciding to stop using universal "he" by removing the pronoun from your language but not replacing it with anything seems like a fool's errand, it can't work in the long term. You can't spend the rest of your life switching to second person every time you need to refer to a generic person.

    Or I guess you can, if one looks at how the English passive voice has developed over time

    \n It might be a weakness of mine but I struggle to read things that use "she" as a gender-neutral pronoun. It seems so jarring.

    BUT lately I have noticed that where texts use "he" as a gender-neutral pronoun it can seem excessively formal.

    What would you do in speech? You'd say "they", most of the time. "If anyone saw how quickly that Rottweiler turned on you, they'd never go near a dog like that in their life."

    I'm rewriting my current RPG project at the moment and I'm changing most of the gender-neutral pronouns from "he" to "they". I don't really care about writing in a politically correct style - it's not as big a deal in the UK as it seems to be in the US, for starters. But I don't want my writing to seem stilted and formal. It's part of the same stylistic reappraisal that's seen me change "is not" into "isn't".

    But for the GM (who I call the "ref"), it's always "he", mainly just for clarity. It feels natural to me that way atm, and to me the main thing is - write in a way that feels natural.

    Because you want people to concentrate on what you're saying about the rules and setting rather than how you're saying it.

    Your primary agenda in writing game materials is to convey rules and setting, not to change the way the world speaks. So whatever you do, don't write in a way that's going to distract your target audience from the main message.

    I guess the main gist of what I'm saying is that if you write in an informal style you can use "they" as a singular. Remember to singularise it as much as possible, e.g. "themself", not "themselves", as people would do in informal speech.

    If you have a good reason to introduce a gender-neutral third person (and who wouldn't, right?), then "they" is an entirely reasonable leap for how English works historically. Much larger changes have been made for much less reason.
    \nIMHO, it's not a leap - it's how people actually speak, people who don't care about being politically correct, but are just using an informal style. It reflects current spoken usage. It's less used in writing - English written style has been more influenced in the past by things like Latin grammar (hence the rather unnatural avoidance of the split infinitive in formal written English), and I guess the insistence on "he" as a gender-neutral pronoun could be a reflection of that sort of attitude, but in any event in spoken English it can sound very stilted, and the use of "they" is more natural. And the way I see it, the spoken language is the true repository of the core of the spirit of the language - written language is just an extra bit tacked on, and it might have some influence on the spoken language, but the influence of the spoken on the written is far greater.

    \n Regarding one:

    Cause it always does.

    When writing an RPG book, you are writing instructions. Using the second person in command form (with the implied "you" as subject) works well for everything outside of examples.

    When writing in command form, you avoid explicitly writing the subject very often, because you are telling people what to do.

    [ex. When writing in command form, avoid explicitly writing the subject because you are telling people what to do.]

    Command form is particularly good for the parts where you are giving explicit instruction. Like for moves in *World games.

    For examples, I really like having the GM be she and the default player be he. For longer examples with more players, you can include females as well, but at that point, you're naming personas anyway.

    If considering using "they" to make things gender neutral, consider the following:
    1) A large number of people were taught that "they" is never singular (I'm purposely avoiding whether they were taught correctly or not)

    \n In journalism, the most commonly used construction is "he or she." As in, "If a person wants a cookie, then he or she should get up and fetch one from the jar."       But I'm not finding that so easy.
      \nWhen you want to address the reader directly, use You. That's what it's for.

      When you want readaloud text that is unambiguous, use command form (implied You). That's what it's for.

      When you want to address roles, address the roles directly (leave out "player" because it's all confusing and meta). "When someone else is the Villain, do X. The Villain does Y." (Don't bother saying that the other non-Villain players should also do it, you've already covered that by giving a command to the non-Villain player that's reading the text - it applies from there to all of them.)

      Name the facilitator, and address them in the way you do the Villain.

      So it sounds like your primary "characters" in the text are You, the Villain, and the Host. And maybe "everyone else" for circumstances where You is temporarily the Villain and everyone else needs to do something different.

      If you can name the non-Villains (Heroes? Citizens? Innocents?) it would be easier than "everyone else". Sounds like you might be trying to avoid that, but the benefit may outweigh the cost.
