\n Joshua BishopRoby has a neat thread about a rules-free "control" game, and I don't want to hijack it with my tangent thoughts.

My idea was that when he sits down to play such a game, that perhaps you would see a game resulting as players feel a need to "create" ad hoc rules on the spot, and this could be recorded by an observer who notes when and how rules evolve.

One neat idea I'm thinking about is that such an experiment could also have a set of "tools" (randomizers such as dice, for example) available, and it would be interesting to see how the tools presented will bias the decisions made.

Consider all of these, either by their presence or absence, and what effect they would have on an improvised game when players decide they "need" them:\n
  • Paper (white)
  • Paper (lined)
  • Paper (graph)
  • Pencils (#2)
  • Pencils (colored)
  • Pens (black, ballpoint)
  • Pens (colored, assortment)
  • Crayons
  • Straight edges
  • Dice (d6)
  • Dice (d10)
  • Dice (d20)
  • Dice (random assortment)
  • Coins for flipping
  • Playing cards
  • Tarot cards
  • Stopwatch
  • "Markers" (those "melted marbles" things)
  • Poker chips
  • Miniatures (fantasy)
  • Miniatures (scifi)
  • Miniatures (female people only)
  • Miniatures (male people only)
  • Miniatures (monsters only)
  • Miniatures (vehicles only)
  • Battle map (square grid)
  • Battle map (hex grid)
  • Map of the world
  • Map of the United States (or any other country)
  • Map of the city you're in
  • Map of the London Underground
  • An assortment of hats
  • Pictures of people cut out of magazines
\nMy point isn't that you should have all of those; my point is that the inclusion (or exclusion) of various physical tools/props will shape the way in which people approach the game.

Will they be used or not used? Will they create assumptions about what the game "should" be like? Will players create new rules to incorporate the tools/props?

Anything you put on a table -- or don't put on a table -- will have an effect on how you play. That's assuming you even have a table. It's a different game if you have no table, and are instead outside sitting on the grass, or standing up looking at each other in a circle, or sitting audience-style in a classroom, or are out jogging around a track.

A lot of my games recently take place sort of "distributed" around a living room without a central focus, with people sitting on chairs and couches. This style of gaming actually bothers me, since the game we're playing (Dungeons and Dragons) isn't really designed for that kind of play, and what's more, it's not one I'm used to. My view of a gaming set-up for D&D, involves players sitting a table, a DM at the end with a screen, and a map with miniatures in the middle.

However, my idea for Bone White, Blood Red is players sitting on the floor (or the ground or the grass or in a kiva ) in a circle, facing each other. But nowhere have I made this clear in the written documentation (so far), just as most games never say anything about how you actually sit down to play them.

Thoughts? Is this obvious or not so obvious?
