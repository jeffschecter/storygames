  • Bruce Baugh
    February 2006
    For me, GMing has two distinct rewards, one for the GM and one for the players. The latter is the one I want to talk most about, though. In my group, folks pretty much all lives with some major elements of suck in them, whether it's chronic illness, death making the family rounds, or other stresses. Furthermore, we nearly all have experience writing professionally on gaming and related subjects, so we're familiar with coordinating the exercise of authority and all that. For us, the chance to set such concerns aside, and slide into a role and be someone else for a while, is a genuine escape - it's fun, we like it, we often do it very well.

    When this is a thing the players seek, the GM is the one who provides the service of a world for the alter egos to deal with. One participant refrains from diving deep so as to help the others go deeper, basically.

    I know that there are all too many GMs whose concern is with dragging the PCs through this neat plot or that cool setting, and I see the effort to challenge it as the automatic assumption as very healthy. But this other aspect, of GM as supporter of player wishes, is easy to overlook precisely because it's not very flashy.

    Jason, I'm not sure I agree about creative control in conventional GM arrangements. It depends on how you weight the part of the characters. This is something authors of fiction dicker over endlessly, actually - how do you tally up the factors that add up to significance? It's not just presence on the page/screen, or scheming masterminds would seldom be important. Narrative vantage isn't reliably it, either - Watson is important, but most sidekick narrators aren't, for instance. It's not...well, it's not - or not necessarily, or reliably - anything you can point to. It's just that some characters end up having a weight, a "reality", greater than a whole lot of the stuff around them, and not always the weight the author intended to put on them.

    This is just by way of noting that sometimes really different distributions of power as an outside observer would see them can end up meaning much the same to the different participants.

    One of the benefits of the GM is that it lets you do a lot of things by a very, very simple system: the GM says "Yes" or the GM says "Uh ... hold on a second." This means that the GM has a lot of power to insert quick, fun, mostly trivial things to just keep the world seeming real and twisty and exciting.

    Player: "I hit the orc in the head with the half-filled stein of beer!"

    GM: "The orc's skull is very thick. It turns around angrily, then sees your weapon. 'BEER! T'ANK-YEW!' It grabs the beer, takes a big swig and leaps to rejoin the brawl."

    Player: "Phew! Lucked out on that one!"

    Can that happen without a GM? Sure. But having a GM is a really good tool toward making it happen. It makes it easy. And it makes it particularly easy for groups that have practiced that particular comfortable dynamic for years and years.
