    Hey, I've got a technique: Imagine in your head or write out on paper an "Actual Play" screenplay. Like, word for word (or higher level than that) as to what people will actually say when playing your game. That will give you an idea of what direction to go in, what rules to emphasize, what is important, etc.
    I thought that was amazing, but it's a lot of work. Designing a character sheet first is another way to do the above, but in a more visual and less tactile-kinesthetic way. In fact, for the game I'm working on right now, I designed about half the character sheet with boxes for rules that I knew I wanted but hadn't designed yet. I designed the "Pack Sheet" (like a team sheet that everyone has control over) completely from scratch, putting in boxes and arrows, having ABSOLUTELY no idea what I wanted to do with it, just putting stuff I wanted to see it used for. Turns out that my first draft was really close to what I had in mind. I just want to hit it with some serious playtesting to make sure it stands up, and doesn't need further adjustment.

    But yeah, overall, I say it's totally a valid, and solid, technique for designing what you want your game to be about, and finding places where you want to stick rules. I'll go a step further and add, "This technique is excellent for designers who are Visually-Oriented learners", as opposed to learners who are more Audial (who will probably do most of their designing through their writing) or Tactile-Kinesthetic (who may take away something from the exercise, but are SOL on techniques, save for that Forge-born suggestion for writing up actual play reports for your nonexistent game).

  • C_Edwards

    Once I have a solid plan for the core functioning of a game, I usually make some prototype reference sheets to use as a visual record. This helps me keep track of who needs to know what particular information and the interactions between different game elements. This often includes some form of character sheet and, depending on the design, may include a general game reference sheet to track information that all the players need access to and any other communal props that display vital game information. They can also make a handy visual record of changes made from one version of the game to the next.

    -Chris

    Thoroughly. Project Senate popped into my head in a microsecond during a conversation at a bar. The glimpses in my mind were the character sheet and about two minutes of Actual Play.

    It took about five minutes of continuing conversation for the AP blurb in my head to expand into a full play session. The character sheet we're using in playtest (which wasn't really designed by me, by the way) looks almost exactly like the image that popped into my mind that night at the bar.
