\n Almost everybody "gets" generic fantasy. If you say "OK, you're the elf, you're the dwarf, you're the taciturn ranger, there's a hole in the ground full of goblins, go" people will approach the task without any cognitive dissonance, with a shared vision of what's going on, and with gusto. Sit me down with guys from Norway, Japan and Delaware and we can rock that pretty well. We can all work as a team to kill some goblins.

Personally I think the vanilla fantasy setup is simultaneously tedious and fraught with ideological peril, so I'm not down with it as a default. I want something more human but still big and colorful and a little simple, whose tropes are instantly recognizable from other media. I also don't want to fall back on a licensed property, which would be nice but is a non-starter.

For Americans, the wild west is pretty much perfect, but I don't think it scales globally too well. Correct me if I'm wrong, Japanese and Norwegians. If I said "OK, you're the gunslinger, you're the drunk sheriff and you're the schoolmarm, there's a hole in the ground full of outlaws, go" would there be conflicting visions? Dissonance? The same jump-right-in you'd get with elves?

Original science fiction or unique fantasy, for reasons of shared vision, seems right out. I don't want to spend time explaining anything.

Maybe the action/disaster movie would work. You have to find the Queen and disable the nuclear launch codes, despite the fact that the cruise ship has capsized on top of you and the world is upside-down!

What about post-apocalyptic? Is that something everybody implicitly gets? Lunatics with garbage can shields riding around in tricked-out tricycles scavenging cans of tomato soup?

I'm open to ideas. What do you think?

    \n Westerns are pretty good, yeah. With almost all genres this comes down to whether people have seen and become fans of the given genre of American television, though: it's from American television that the genres of police procedural, medical drama, western and most other television genres are learned. So there's no special pride of place among these various genres, it's just a question of whether the given person watches television a lot or not - if they do, they probably watch the same shows you do, albeit possibly at a couple of years remove.

    Looking for things that gamers specifically would get, my own pick at this point has to be zombies. I've yet to demonstrate Zombie Cinema to anybody who didn't know what the genre is about, and that sample must be several hundred gamers and dozens of non-gamers by now. Heck, even my mother gets what the genre is about - I don't know if it's osmosis or what, I don't think she's seen a zombie movie since they originally became a fad in her youth. There's something conceptually simple about people trying to escape from rotting cannibals, apparently.

    (If you want mainstream, then zombies can be replaced with a generalized catastrophe. Whatever you think would be familiar as a milieu - Titanic sinking would probably be familiar to many in the fairer sex insofar as a catastrophe milieu goes, I imagine.)

    Action movie is a good context if you want to be slightly more mainstream. That, or more specifically spies. The thriller as a genre has the big advantage of having a large movie canon as well as a literary canon to fall back on, both within and outside mainstream, so it's practically unfeasible to find anybody who doesn't have some inkling of it. In fact, I wouldn't even call action a genre so much as the breathing component of American drama theory.

    If you want an action genre that is intricate yet familiar to gamers, superheroes are a no-brainer. Perhaps shojo adventure drama gets to the same level in ten years, but at this moment the only competitor for superheroes is fantasy, really.

    A good question, however, is whether a given genre is approachable because it's familiar or because it is simple. One could argue that bog-standard fantasy has plenty of intricacies that can't be grasped without prior knowledge, so perhaps it's not the simplicity that makes it work here, but rather its popularity. The zombies, though, they are simple: is it that everybody has seen a zombie movie, or is that you don't actually need to have seen anything like it to grasp what the genre is about?

    \n Obviously there has to be a sense of "nonspecificity" in a genre if you want it to be internationally interesting. Like many commenters have pointed out, the details of post-apocalyptic, or police procedural are going to vary slightly or significantly, place to place. Every time I read an RPG book that involves "real world"-inspired crime stories or politics, I have to filter that through my "well, ok, but that's not how we do it here" matrix, because even in Canada our politics and law enforcement are significantly different from our southern neighbours.


    That said, it might be better to style something around -themes- rather than -genres-. If you say "this is a game about Pirates", people in Asia or Africa might have a different reaction than Americans. If you say "this is a game about Epics", then anyone with a passing familiarity with their culture/country's historical literature/mythology will know what you mean. China has great epics, India has great epics, many European nations have their own unique epics, etc. And then you can work out the common ground of the setting/genre with the group.

    So, "this is my game about personal tragedy" is something most people can understand (if not get behind), and then through discussion you can flesh out the setting (it takes place in a hospital, in a generic European country, the PCs are staff members, etc).

    \n The downside to that is that if everyone in the game doesn't know that they'll be transported to a fantasy world or will be dealing with an alien invasion or will be investigating a murder, it's going to come off as a total bait-and-switch. You're offering them one game, letting them get psyched up to play that, and then walloping them with a different game right out of left field that they didn't take into account when thinking up their characters.

    Which plays really badly in my group -- like, the game fails immediately and people feel jerked around and unhappy about it -- although I suppose that I can imagine people who are less invested in tuning their characters to fit a particular game wouldn't have so much trouble with it.

    We're pretty fond of having characters who fit into the game setting and who are able to interact with whatever the main features of that setting are, and that's something that can't really be done with a "you'll find out the genre after we start playing" plan.

    If I was even going to attempt doing something like this, I think I'd have to shove pre-generated characters at them (or partly-pregenerated characters), use those to introduce the genre and setting elements, and then hit the pause button and say "Now we make our own characters for real." Something like that so no one invests their enthusiasm in the game we're only playing as the entry point/tutorial for the ACTUAL game setting; we run around, learn what we have to learn, then start fresh.
