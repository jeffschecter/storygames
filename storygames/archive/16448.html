    \n Dev,

    I wrote out a little screed when you first posted this thread. It was half joke, half violent accusation full of name dropping, condescension and shitheadery. When I reread it, it wasn't funny, I was just being a shithead. So, I deleted it and moved on.

    My point, essentially, was that my experience with tech people is that, because of their aptitude with math and computer science, they tend to view any problem through this lens. A lens that ends up being overly instrumental and a bit arrogant at times, especially in fields where these people have no experience or grounding beyond a handful of pop non-fiction texts or a goddamn TEDtalk. If all you have is a command line, everything just looks like a problem of implementation or a hack. Unfortunately, the world doesn't revolve around hyperrational computation; especially not the world of human interaction.

    The main problem with this view is that someone has to be transcendentally correct and that this is even possible. I don't often encounter this attitude with biochemists, physics, or anthropologists, but I do encounter this flavour of arrogance often with CS and Engineering people. I'm not sure entirely why that is. It might have something to do with the physical implementation of maths, but I'm just speculating. While this position is excusable when it comes to their areas of expertise, I find it extends to any subject that they decide to think about for a few second. As if you could work out the entire history of philosophy, sociology or biochemistry, from first principles to this case in just a few moments. As if that's how human knowledge actually worked. So, this ends up with me patiently listening folks with BSci's in CS or Eng, or little formal training at all, telling me about how society works.

    I find that Story-Games is not unlike those parties sometimes.

    Which is kinda interesting, actually, and really provides fertile ground: I am eh, relatively good at maths but I generally don't like it. But because of my huge interest in sociology, I actually spend a lot of my tech career doing things like:

    * Improving documentation to make it easier to understand and relate to, using metaphor, varied instructive devices (pictures, drawings etc)
    * Fixing communication patterns between customers, engineers, developers, management etc
    * Maintaining an awesome business environment (morale) on low resources
    * Fixing social breakdowns with customers and partners, which are often the real source of business problems over actual technical breakdowns and problems/bugs/etc.

    So all in all, just wanted to chime in with data that even "computer/tech field" is far more rich and varied than a bunch of math/cis majors fixing programming problems in the Puzzle Palace all day!
