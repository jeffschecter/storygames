    \n It rather depends why they are coming to you and asking to be published.

    If it is because they are unsure of how they would go about doing it themselves then they could do with some basic technical help on laying out (i.e. they probably need to be able to produce a PDF), printing (outline POD vs. short run options) and selling (mention IPR, PDF sales sites and the like).

    Alternatively it may because it never occurred to them that self-publishing was an option, either because they equate publishing with "being published" (most people do even now), or because they think self-publication is very hard work (it is) and very expensive (it isn't). In this case they need more encouragement than hand holding.

    Or it may be because they like your company name, and game lines, and want to work for you. After all many more people are writers than they are writer / designer / creator / owners, and they just want to do something and be paid, rather than engage in a massive, creative effort. In this case they will never want to self publish, they want to *be published* ... *by you*

    Posted By: Pelgranewhat advice would you give?
    \nIf you are self-publishing a printed book:

    * It's not rocket science and you can totally do it. That said, don't spend money you don't have or can't afford to lose.
    * Get current advice on short run and on-demand printers from other small press publishers, since service and quality rapidly wax and wane.
    * Get a proof. Get two proofs if you need them. It is money well spent. Don't skip a proof because you are in a hurry (see next point)
    * Everything takes twice as long as you think it will, so either plan for that or don't set deadlines.
    * If your design requires something fiddly (special cards, a spinner, perfed mylar-embossed gate folds), make sure they are absolutely necessary and prepare to weep tears of blood. If you can design them out, you probably should.
    * Consider the utility of your form factor, both in terms of offering it in other mediums (Lulu doesn't do half-letter/digest, for example) and practical issues (an exotic format means retailers and gamers have trouble shelving your game).
    * Consider the implications of book pricing, particularly if you want to offer it through retailers.

    \n Build or find a market for your product before spending any money on it.

    Test your product with a small percentage of that market before releasing the product on a larger scale.

    Be patient. Building interest takes a long time and requires that you give of yourself more than you receive.

    \n#1 Book publishing is a small subset of the much wider world of publishing. If you make the game presentable and get it into other people's hands, that's publishing. A pdf, a webpage, a podcast, a magazine, a book \u2014 these are all publishing.

    #2 The best format for your game may not be a printed book. Think hard about this.

    #3 You will not get rich selling your game, no matter what format you publish it in. Additionally, you will not get rich if a publishing house buys your game and publishes it for you. Edit: You also will not become famous, even internet famous.

    #4 Especially for your first game design, far and away the best option is to choose a simple, low-barrier-to-entry means of publishing it. PDFs are fantastic. If the game takes off, it's trivial to turn a PDF into a printed book. If you find you enjoy game design and self-publishing, you can use your first game as a foundation to launch the second one. And if it turns out your game is great for you and your gaming group and of only passing interest to the rest of the world, hey, you still have a spiffy PDF.

    \n At a minimum, you only need three things to self-publish a game commercially (none of which necessarily cost any money):

    1. A game text.
    2. Somebody to do layout, turning it into a PDF (this can also be you).
    3. A place to sell the PDF online, like a Paypal button or RPGnow.
    4. You might also consider a simple print+distribution service like Lulu (which also distributes PDFs), if you're invested in having a print version available.

    There are other optional things you might need in order to achieve your own standard of "success":

    - a marketing plan
    - a website with more info about your game, a message board, a mailing list, etc.
    - a cheaper printer (Lulu is not cost-effective for making real profit)
    - an independent distribution service like IPR or Key20 or one the new thingees that are starting up
    - a booth to sell your game at conventions
    - etc.

    A good place to ask questions and find out more information about publishing is The Forge's publishing forum. If you're more interested in talking informally about your game, try Story Games. Also try to get in touch with other independent publishers in your region. We are everywhere. Just ask.

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonA place to sell the PDF online, like a Paypal button or RPGnow.
    \nI've been using PayLoadz to sell MP3s - you could definitely sell PDFs there too, they take any kind of file.

    The advantages are:

    1. They integrate into PayPal without you having to do any more than sign up for a PayPal account and provide them with the email address you used, and
    2. If you are selling in low volume there is no charge whatsoever. No upfront charge, no monthly charge, no per-item charge. By the time you are selling enough for them to charge you, you can afford it.

    So it's easy and there's no financial risk whatsoever. You can do the whole thing on literally zero budget - use free tools to lay out and PDF your file, promote it using a free WordPress or Blogger blog - and if nobody buys your stuff you're only out some time. You haven't spent even one cent on the project.

    \n It can be done. The online tools available these days mean you can distribute stuff widely for very little expense.

    The trouble with making it easy to publish is that lots of people do. Some of those people have no business writing, editing, doing layout, etc. So please be very honest with yourself about where your own talents and limitations lie. And don't treat this as a throwaway thing where quality doesn't matter. To create something good takesa bit of time and effort. And craft - like any skill or set of skills, the way you get good at it is to practice. When you look back on your early attempts you'll see them full of holes, but that's just part of the process.

    RPGnow/DriveThru still, as far as I know, accept anybody as a new publisher on payment of the $40. But there are, what, 400-500 publishers on there and I'd guess only about 20% of those (or fewer?) get any significant profit or name recognition.


    Tim Gray
    Silver Branch Games
