\n
\n Several people are designing in public to varying degrees. Here are a couple I know of:

John Wick is designing Houses of the Blooded on his LiveJournal. He takes suggestions and publicly makes changes and alterations.

Chad Underkoffler is frequently posting his design steps on Swashbucklers of the Seven Skies, also on his livejournal. I think he's more sharing than getting input, when compared to John's process.

I recently opened my Google Docs draft of Pentripolis to public view. The idea is to give insight into how much things change and get modified before a first draft is even finished (so this is more a collection of notes and ideas at this point). This is more direct for viewing, but much less direct for receiving input and feedback.

Adam Dray, on his LJ, frequently shares his process on Towerlands.

And, of course, a lot of Game Chef designing happened in public sight, with feedback going back and forth.

The purpose of this thread is twofold: to gather links to games that are currently being designed in public, and to discuss the pros and cons of this approach and its specific incarnations.

One obvious pro is that it creates interest in the game. It also allows the designer to share their excitement about what they're doing.

One obvious con is that you're already putting the game out there. But that's usually not keeping many people from buying it anyway when it comes out. However, sharing something unfinished is kind of like sharing your writing before you've edited it: it takes away some of the mystique of the finished process and it might lower people's view of the quality of your work, if they fail to understand that drafting is a process all written products go through.

Thoughts? Additions to the list?

    \n I'm designing Six Bullets for Vengeance in public, also on my livejournal.

    Greg Stolze is developing Reign in semi-public - he's been posting updates about it to his mailing list for over a year now.

    The reason I'm doing this is because I took the opposite, traditional industry approach when designing Dead of Night - I did so entirely behind closed doors, including all the playtesting, to the point where when it finally came out, nobody knew a thing about it. I've been trying to catch up and create buzz about it ever since.

    I'm not sure I agree with your con though - putting the game out there is more likely to do you good, as people who have been following the game's development have a vested interest in the game already, and can help you spread the word.

    Obviously as far as the "mystique" goes, I think you have to be careful not to post a load of crap that's not ready to be seen by human eyes, but equally do people really want mystique in their games? I know I'm far more likely to go for a game I've followed in development for a long time (Reign, I'm looking at you) and been involved with in some way than a game that is more mysterious that I know little about.

    \n I think that the real 'fear' is that by putting your game "out there" that someone is going to steal your idea/innovative mechanic/setting and either steal it for their game or not buy the game at all. Interestingly enough, I don't think that the fear is all that grounded in reality.

    For the 'not buy the game at all' concern the 'open source' method of development doesn't seem to have hurt the sales of the guys that have done it (D20, Fate/SoTC, just to name two off the top of my head)... although I would be interested to hear the stories and opinions of anyone who has first hand experience selling an 'open sourced' project on the matter.

    The 'steal your idea' bit is a little bit trickier. At the end of the day though, my experience is that we are all 'borrowing' ideas from other games/settings/mediums anyway... and then doing what we can to improve to or add to that idea. So unless they are directly ripping you off (in way that would be litigatable) I think that a little 'sharing' doesn't hurt sales either.

    In terms of mystique... I expect that the people who are looking for a wholly finished, complete game are probably not the ones who are going to be following a design journal. On the other hand, those that are fans of the author or fans of the product will get psyched with every bit of new information (in some ways, I would argue, actually creating some mystique when they 'sell' the game to others: "Man, did you hear that REIGN is going to have rules for..." etc).

    Just my .02

    \n
    \nCons:
    \n- If you get attention, it may remove your incentive to finish the game. It's SO much easier to throw out a cool idea and have people go "hey! cool idea!" than to write the idea down, playtest it, fix it, make it understandable to other people etc.
    \n- If you don't get attention, you might - erroneously - think people won't be interested in your game, and stop making it.
    \n- If you get feedback on specific ideas, you might have a hard time following your intended - or best - course of design if it differs from what other people think.
    \n- If you take too long, people will think you're unable to finish your game. Statistically, chances are they're right - most games never get finished - and you'll look like... what was it again? A cheeseball?
    \n
    \nPros:
    \n- You create some buzz.
    \n- You can track your progress. ("Shit! Haven't posted anything in a month! Time to do some work!") This can be a con if you're not used to long projects - there'll always be stretches of time where nothing happens, and you might think your project has crashed when you're just in a passive phase.
    \n- If you post your deadlines, and people are reading them, you'll have a stronger incentive to stick to them.
    \n
    \nIrrelevant:
    \n- People might steal your game and publish it as their own. I don't think this ever happens, for simple financial and legal reasons.
    \n- People might steal your ideas. So what? You stole someone else's ideas. Someone else is thinking of the same idea you're thinking of right now. Ideas are raw material, not finished product.

    \n I've thought a bit about why my design blog doesn't get a lot of feedback. Sometimes, no one cares and there's not a lot you can do about that, but I think some of the fault lies with the poster. Here is some advice gleaned from my own mistakes.

    I am breaking most of these "rules" right now in my own blog! Gah! That will change after today.


    In general, most of my advice regards writing clearly, using the blog medium to its maximum potential, and changing your style so your blog is a two-way communication and not a one-way infodump. Here are some specific suggestions that might help. If you have others, I'd love to hear them!

    Repeat Context

    Every time you post about your game, assume the reader is new. If they've been following along since the beginning, they'll skip that quick intro paragraph. More likely, lots of your blog readers skim your stuff. When you say something that finally grabs their attention, don't expect them to go reading back through your entire blog to get caught up. Make it easy for people to comment by providing the necessary context they need to engage in discussion with you. If you refer to prior posts or build upon them, summarize the content so they don't really have to read it unless they want the details. Link to those posts you reference.

    Link to the Game

    Every time you post about your game, link to it. If you don't have a work product to link, make something simple. Create a single blog post that summarizes your game with your "elevator speech" or your Three Questions or whatever. Link to that. Basically, you want a single place to which you can consistently link to provide a reader with the basics about your game and answers to frequently asked questions.

    Ask Questions

    If you talk about your game but never ask questions, people aren't likely to "answer." Even if your question is, "Does this sound cool?" or "Who would play this?" you should ask it. Make your questions stand out by putting them in their own paragraph or bolding them or something.

    Be Controversial

    Don't be controversial just to be annoying or contrary, but if you know you're saying something people might not agree with, go for the gusto. Don't be afraid to annoy people. If you're talking about something that people have strong opinions about, take a stance and watch people come out of the woodwork to disagree with you. Disagreement = discussion. Piss people off once in a while!

    Avoid Cuts

    LiveJournal and WordPress have the ability to "cut" -- that is, you can have a "leader" to your post and require the reader to click the "more" link to read the rest of it. I cut as little as possible because I want to make it easy for people to read my stuff. If I post something reaaaaaaally long, I might cut a chunk of it, but I am careful to leave enough in the leader to cue the reader about what exactly I am saying and pique their interest. Hopefully, anyway.

    Format Neatly

    Assuming you have something of substance to say, you want to encourage people to read it. Providing bolded headers, small coherent paragraphs, and bullet lists will make your text easier to read. Skimmers will be more likely to get the gist of what you're saying and get sucked in and really read it.

    Think about it:
    • If I had written this post in one giant paragraph with no headers, you might have just skipped it. Maybe you saw a header that interested you, or maybe you inadvertently skimmed a short paragraph and it caught your eye, leading you to read it all from the beginning...
    \nSummarize

    Don't forget all the rules you were taught in expository writing class. I can't remember where I learned this but, "Tell them what you're going to tell them. Tell them. Then tell them what you told them." Basically, you want to make sure people understand you, and redundancy helps get the point across. Redundancy is a part of clear writing.

    So, essentially, there's a lot you can do to encourage readers to participate in your design blog.
      \n
    • Remember all your rules from English class and add in what you know about the power of layout and graphic design.
    • Know your audience and don't assume they know anything.
    • Write clearly.
    • Summarize your points.
    • Don't be afraid to repeat yourself for clarity.
    • Use white space, headers, bullets, and formatting to make your points stand out.
    • Make it easy for your readers to read your work by avoiding needless "cuts."
    • Make each post independent of what came before.
    • Be a little controversial and have something to say, preferably something different to say.
    • Link to a game page and a FAQ (make one if you have to).
    • Ask questions to spur and guide discussion.
    \nSo, does this help? What would you add?

      \n Those are some really good tips up there earlier in the thread, especially about having a single post that encapsulates the premise of the game. I'd like to add a few of my own:

      Keep a scrapbook
      I've been posting whenever I come across some cool visuals that help communicate the atmosphere of Do. Movie trailers, anime stills, quilts, and illustrations. I noticed John Wick did something similar, posting a youtube video of a guitarist playing in an unusual style as an example of the music one might hear in the world of Houses of the Blooded. I find that it's useful just for my own reference when I'm trying to grasp the more ephemeral attributes of a game's setting and makes for a reasonable substitute for promo art in the very early stages of a game's development.

      Cite your Sources
      If you've suddenly been inspired by a bit of a podcast, a magazine article, a movie or song, provide a link to that material so readers can share the experience a bit. Again, I do this for my own benefit when I need to research my source material again.

      Tags and Icons
      When you're working on multiple projects, or your blog serves a dual purpose as a design journal and personal journal, tags can be a real lifesaver. When you post a link to your blog, you can just link to the specific tag's entries so new readers don't have to skim through your unrelated material. Similarly, if your blog allows you to use a custom icon for each entry, create an icon for your game and use that in every related post. It helps people spot relevant writings more easily as they're browsing through their friends list.
