\n This thread is inspired by the discussion of the Japanese slice-of-life game "Families."
\n
\nCan anyone recommend other RPGs that focus on the mundane elements of everyday life?
\n
\nIt seems most RPGs, even within the indie sphere, tend to focus on the dramatic and the profound: war, death, murder, zombie apocalypses, etc.
\n
\nEven jeepform games, which often utilize contemporary domestic settings tend to skew towards the sensational, often focusing on humiliation ("Fatmandown") or violence and abuse ("Gang Rape," "The Mother").
\n
\nIs there a reason for this? Does focusing on the mundane leave most players disinterested? How mundane is too mundane?
\n
\n(I know these are subjective questions, and I'm not looking for a single universal answer, just personal opinions.)

    \n When the topic comes up a lot of people tend to dismiss it out of hand, saying that they much prefer RPGs as a form of fantastic escapism. There are some games that involve ordinary people, but these tend to involve them doing rather extraordinary things (Fiasco and Ribbon Drive come to mind here).

    Yuuyake Koyake and (from what Andy's posted) Families both put some emphasis on ordinary people (to different degrees and in different ways) but also have a sort of "mission" structure to them. There's a problem, and you're going to go and solve it, and the game delineates what resources your character has. There's also another Japanese RPG called Aitsu wa Classmate! ("That's My Classmate!"), which is about students in a highly manga-fied but otherwise realistic Japanese high school (your otaku character might have a maid as an imaginary friend, but there's no supernatural elements to the game), which also has the PCs are troubleshooters.

    I think the real challenge for designing an RPG about something like everyday life is how to go about making a game that relates to that kind of role-playing and helps create an engaging experience, despite taking up subject matter that basically runs against the grain of the majority of RPGs ever made. That's been a major struggle in my attempts at an Azumanga Daioh type game, since (as I realized only after writing a complete first draft of the game) the source material really doesn't involve problem-solving, and success and failure are not so common or interesting as to need game mechanics to determine which you get. I'm kind of hoping this kind of thing gets a bit more developed, both because I find these kinds of things intriguing, and because I think it could help inform the human side of some more fantastical games. The Jeepform and Norwegian Style games point to a lot of interesting possibilities along these lines.

    Thematically, a whole lot of that stuff still involve some amount of escapism. Yuuyake Koyake has its idyllic countryside setting, and my goal for an Azumanga Daioh type game is to come up with something that produces silly, heartwarming stories about high school friendships. Aitsu wa Classmate! specifically bills itself as being a game about "the high school life you wish you'd had."

    Posted By: whduryeaIs there a reason for this?
    If you look to films, a relatively young form of art, the films of its "infancy" where quite primitive; short news-reels, curiosa, slapstick and such. Very few (if any) back then had any belief in films developing into anything worth taking serious. It's the same with role-playing games.\n
    Posted By: whduryeaDoes focusing on the mundane leave most players disinterested?
    Yes. Most people are seekers of high action and easy thrills.

    The art of games is still in its infancy. Most players are strangers to the idea that role-playing games could be used to search for insights on their everyday life. That's your reason for having few games on mundane themes. We will probably never get very many games on such themes either, at least not in our lifetime.\n
    Posted By: whduryeaHow mundane is too mundane?
    As we design the mundane games of the future, I believe we will find that there is absolutely no theme too mundane. It's all in the point of view, and how we tap into the dramatic potential of our chosen theme.

    I believe we are on the verge of developing the subtle tools needed to handle mundane drama, focusing on everyday people, as we are developing tools to do other types of drama with more precision and efficiency too. The scope of role-playing games are widening. We live in interesting times!

    \n It's too mundane for me if it's something that I could do more satisfyingly in real life. Like, I wouldn't be interested in playing a wholly realistic (no fantasy elements, no "heightened reality" components, etc.) game about throwing a birthday party for my sister, because it would be a million times more satisfying to just throw an actual birthday party for my actual sister.

    If it's a "mundane" premise that I can't do in real life, that's different; I can't go and have a tension-filled dinner with my ex in-laws who hate me, because I don't actually have ex in-laws (and if I did, they would adore me, I'm sure). Whether I'm going to be interested in playing a game based on that premise may depend on a lot of things, but "is it too mundane?" won't necessarily be one of them.


    That said, fantasy/heightened reality is a huge draw, and a much easier hook for players (especially new players) than offering them a rousing game of Desktops & Dayplanners. There's plenty of fun to be found in a roleplaying game about working at an ordinary office, but it doesn't really grab people the way going into outer space or saving the princess does.

    Posted By: Per FischerI don't have a huge amount of cases to base it on, but when I've tried to introduce non-gaming grown-ups to storygames/rpgs, they were all rather turned off if the subject matter was too "fantastical"
    \nYes, I've had this problem before. It usually arises when I'm trying to get a friend or acquaintance who I think, based on their other interests, would like RPGs to try a more traditional game, like D&D or one of the White Wolf titles. This was a problem that came up several times before I discovered indie games or started playing around with my own designs. For awhile I was contaminated by the idea that D&D was somehow the ideal starter RPG, just because it is what I (and most of the other role-players I know) started with.

    Lately, I've been able to hook a few nontraditional gamers--including my SO, who enjoys acting, but wouldn't touch D&D--by starting them with games that didn't immediately demand that they hold their nose and try to ignore the fantasy elements.\n
    Posted By: Accounting for Taste
    It's an interesting topic for discussion: I don't know for sure whether the observed predominance of games with sensational/fantastic elements indicates that most players are disinterested in playing a game that is 100% mundane, but I think it certainly suggests that they arelessinterested.
    \nI think that in order to answer this you have to look at where the hobby originated. D&D was a game designed to appeal to fans of war games and high fantasy novels. That was the original audience, and all (or at least the vast majority) of contemporary RPGs have grown out of that original audience. RPGs are hobby that largely grows by word of mouth, and people tend to socialize with people who share their interests. With that in mind, it's easy to see how a hobby that started life with fans of genre fiction would continue to remained mostly chained to that type of fiction, even as it grew.

    Or, to put it differently: It's not that the audience have dictated fantastical games. It's that fantastical games have dictated the audience.
