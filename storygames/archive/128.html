    I don't have a beef with multiple session games. What annoys me, though, are multiple session games that try and ignore the fact that they're played out over multiple sessions. Any other serial story -- whether in television, magazine, trilogy, whatever -- is patently aware of its medium and uses the characteristics of that medium to its advantage. Each episode/segment/novel has its own begining, rising action, and end, and most have their own little mini-themes that contribute to the whole season/series/trilogy.

    However, this sort of consciousness of medium is almost completely absent from multi-session roleplaying games, which tend to expect the players to just run from beginning to end of one very large story and not notice that they do it in hours-long chunks separated by days and weeks of downtime. This is why I am eager to get into a game of Primetime Adventures, as it reconfigures the traditional "campaign" into a "season" with explicit episodes taking the place of adventures. I'd find it far more rewarding of a hobby if, every time I stood up from the gaming table, I had enough resolution to feel satisfied and enough foreshadowing to still feel engaged. This is fertile development ground.

    That's what we've been doing with our Buffy the Vampire Slayer campaign -- i.e. each session is one episode, and one real-world season (Fall to Spring) is one season of the show with 15 episodes. (We're now coming up on our 40th episode, or the 10th of the third season.) It works pretty well, I think. During Season One we had some trouble getting the pacing quite right -- a few episodes were dragged out into two parts.

    On the other hand, I didn't have a problem with continuous time. Analytically, I think the seasonal structure works similarly to the somewhat shorter "adventure" structure of other games. It resets things so that there isn't an ever-increasing complexity to the game. For example, in my James Bond 007 campaign each movie plot took 3-4 sessions. But the Vinland campaign, which I loved, just went continuously for 52 sessions. The ever-deepening story was part of the appeal.

    One thing I note about session structure is that all games have some sort of start-up phase where you greet, review what happened before, and get started. However, most larps have a debriefing after a session, while doing so is rare in tabletop.

    Hnh. One interesting thing about the cadence of episodic play is that D&D ended up being really good for it, bizarrely.

    Normally, it's something I have to plan for, but with D&D, the fights are both critically important and are slow (if they're at all complicated, which the interesting ones should be). The net result is that a given D&D session (for me) tends to be a three act sandwich of either Plot-Fight-Plot or Fight-Plot-Fight. Having that structure more or less appear organically has, i admit, made longer D&D games easier to juggle than many lighter systems where it's easier to "Get to the point" as it were.

    This ended up being very rewarding when I took that knowledge of the cadence and intentionally broke it for an Exalted Campaign, System complexity wanted to produce the same sandwich, but actively steering away form it ende dup paying out in spades.

    Biggest advantage it had was that it took a lot of burden off my shoulders. _knowing_ that I could do a plot sandwich let me have a default position that was a lot more structured than needing to start from scratch, it let me pick a specific third and really drill down into it.

    At first, this produced lopsided sequences of plot-fight-AWESOME PLOT or Fight - Plot - REALLY COOL NON-FIGHT-CONFLICT or plot-CHARACTER SPOTLIGHT-fight. Now, I was absolutely helped by the fact that the exalted ruleset is a little more flexible outside of combat than D&D was, but even without that, the ability to really drill down into something did a great job of keeping the episodes structured while creating player buy-in very quickly and very deeply, because the basic structure provided form, and the something extra gave something to take away from the episode as particularly cool.

    What's more, it also helped me settle into a system that was pretty new to me, and it helped me develop ownership of the setting, and like learning to juggle, it let me move up to 2 or 3 parts awesome as everyone really hit groove.

    Net result was probably my most successful long-run game to date (we went about 60 sessions), and while I've had players want to go back to a setting again in the past, this is the first time I've also had other GMs in the group want to pick up threads from the game for their own games. The Exalted game I'm currently playing in is now a spinoff of the game I ran, and that is a pretty damn cool experience for me.
