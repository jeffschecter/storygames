    \n This does seem like fertile territory. Magic realism would be difficult to account for with mechanics that deal solely in the moment-by-moment physics of the game world (no matter how magical). It also would account for situations in which the narrative arc is basicly mundane events, with wild, speculative, forrays stringing it together. Borges is my favorite magic realist.

    \n My thoughts on this are pretty ... dense, by which I mean teasing them out individually without writing pages and pages is pretty tricky. I will now ramble somewhat.

    Here's all the stuff I've read that I think is relevant:
    100 Years of Solitude
    Of Love and Other Demons
    Franz Kafka's Complete Short Stories
    The End of Mr. Y
    The Raw Shark Texts
    Kafka on the Shore (reading now)

    I've got to read Borges as soon as possible.

    I think that magic should always have a point, if it's present in the fiction. The point might be empowerment of the individual, a political commentary, or whatever. But usually magic is systematic in some way.

    In magic realism is that it uses the absurd to situate, reveal, or pressure the characters. This isn't done in the context of a system, and doesn't always require actual mystical events; a character in Kafka on the Shore introduces himself as Johnnie Walker and starts mutilating cats in front of a cat-catcher that has been invited into Johnnie Walker's home. Johnnie Walker instructs the cat-catcher that the only way to save the cats is to kill him. There's really nothing magical in that scene but it still serves a very magical realist purpose: the local cat-catcher is faced with a choice of killing Johnnie Walker or watching them be mutilated. It's intense, surreal, and we're not told why the universe contains Johnnie Walker or how he manages to orchestrate such an elaborate situation.

    Now, sometimes magic realist stories go off the rails and try to provide some underlying metaphysic. That's when they're disappointing, in my opinion.

    So back to games: I think Mortal Coil touches on this when it mentions how for magic to be magical it has to be able to surprise you.

    \n I think an important constituent of magical realism is that magic is something that's not distinguished from reality. In almost every game I can think of, magic is specifically called out as something exceptional, different, and outside the normal rules.

    Another important fact is that magic is never explained and does not need an explanation. It's something that merely happens.

    Dungeons and Dragons: Magic using character classes are specifically set aside as having the ability to do magic. Magic may have any number of explanations in different campaigns, but the underlying assumption is that it has one.

    Nine Worlds: Magic is explicitly opposed to reality and the order of Zeus. In practice, magic is freeform and can have a very "magical realism" flavor.

    TSOY: There are multiple kinds of magic, each with its own rules and explanation.

    Nine Worlds: Magic has freeoform elements, but is also bound by what the players say it is. Magic is explicity distinguished as something the players make decisions about.

    Polaris: There's plenty of room for magical realism here. I can't think of any way that magic is distinguished from reality in this setting.

    Bliss Stage: The dreamworld has a magical-realistic bent to it. Though the dreamworld is distinguished from reality, in the dreamworld the dream elements have a magical realism bent.

    What would a game designed for magical realism look like?

    \n Speaking of Borges, there are a lot of ways in which his brand of magical realism can distill some essential property of the entire genre.

    There is very rarely anything overtly magical in the stories. Much of the magical realism comes from the character's surreal emotional reactions to the real, rather than something surreal itself happening. For instance, the Zahir is about a man who loses his mind seeing an unremarkable coin and being unable to stop thinking about it. This matters I think because it illustrates another trait of magical realism that is utterly essential. The mundane itself is magical.

    Another element of Borges that I find appealling, especially in the context of rpgs, is the nonchalant way in which big sweeping ideas are introduced. Characters will have unique/private views of a situation, but these things don't necessarily impact the action of the story, just the perceptions of one (or more) of the characters. Like in "The Gospel According to Mark" when Espinosa realized that "throughout human history, humankind has told two stories: the story of a lost ship sailing the Mediterranean seas in quest of a beloved isle, and the story of a god who allows himself to be crucified on Golgotha." The protagonist encapsulates Western history, and moves on; the other characters don't share this perception, but, for the reader, it flavors everything that happens afterward. The malleability of truth/reality (whether physical or psychological) seems key to me - perceptions are more important than truth (or perhaps are truth).
    I can't quite put together how this could work in an rpg, but I like the possibilities.
    Can different characters have totally different perceptions of a situation? Not just that some characters are delusional or see things incorrectly, but rather that there is no objective truth, just different accounts. The players have the benefit of getting to see all of them, and viewing the character dynamic from outside.

    \n Magical Realism doesn't have systematic magic, but it does have magic linked to themes and motifs throughout a work. Illogical things happen to illustrate a theme or reinforce a motif.

    In the Wind-Up Bird Chronicle, the titular wind-up bird is used as a motif. Hearing the Wind Up Bird is linked in the reader's mind to the image of his wife. These overtly thematic links between disparate elements reinforce the mundane world being fantastic and vice-versa. In order for the genre to really be story gamed, the players have to be able to shape their perception, and be able to connect disparate elements within the world. They need to be able to suggest details about the setting that may not have any impact on anything besides their own characters.

    I'd like to make a further distinction, however, since fantasy has more than one thing going on in it.

    In stuff like, say, Robert Jordan's monstrous Wheel of Time series, magic is definitely systematic. It's supposed to work on a defined set of principles. Whether or not the reader understands them or not, they should think they exist.

    \n Someone else who's read The Raw Shark Texts. Best book I've read recently.

    When I think about gaming magical realism I'm reminded of the quote that talking about art is like dancing about architecture. I've thought about this topic but not put anything to paper. I've usually arrived at the conclusion that it would be very difficult to create a game that facilitated the kinds of things that make magical realism such an interesting device. With coherent roleplaying you've ideally got a group of empathetic people sitting down to model a genre. If you've all all bought into the paradigm, where is the room for the jarring juxtaposition? A lot of magical realism has introspective, first-person narrative that can make for fascinating reading, but maybe not great gaming were it somehow transplanted to role-playing.

    While I've seen mechanics that help or hinder certain play goals or genre emulation, I've seen very few that, on their own evoke the kind of moods that magical realism realizes. I'd love to see someone attempt it. A hacked Mortal Coil or Penny For My Thoughts might approach it. But you wouldn't be driving towards those organic "Wow, I didn't know that about The World/Myself" moments, rather, "Of course the world is that way, but what does this mean?"

    John Crowley's Little, Big is closer to Fantasy than Magical Realism and is one of my favorite books. I'd love to play a fairy tale that felt like that book. If someone did make Magical Realism the RPG, I'd probably despair of finding other souls who were interested in the experience.

    Posted By: tony dowlerI think an important constituent of magical realism is that magic is something that's not distinguished from reality. In almost every game I can think of, magic is specifically called out as something exceptional, different, and outside the normal rules.
    \nYeah, this is how Fantasy works. Magic is "additive" or "extra" to the reality of the situation. Even in fairy tales. Vasilisa's mother gives her a magic doll that does her chores for her, but it's explicitly clear that this is exceptional, given Baba Yaga's reaction to it later on.\n
    Posted By: tony dowlerAnother important fact is that magic is never explained and does not need an explanation. It's something that merely happens.
    \nTrue to one degree or another. The Old Man's Enormous Wings aren't magical in and of themselves - the doctor examines them - but there is no explanation of why he has them. On the other hand, the spider-girl has a reason for having the body of a spider. She has an explanation, the Old Man doesn't.

    Kafka is the one who just throws the absurd down in front of you. The Metamorphosis isn't about Gregor becoming an insect - it's about the family's reaction and his relationship to them. They don't react the way a family would act to a person suddenly becoming an insect. They don't wonder about it at all, or try to investigate what is wrong. They behave as if he had some incurable, socially embarrassing illness. Note they don't call the doctor or take him to the hospital across the street. They shut him away in his bedroom, and don't talk about it. The transformation is so absurd in order to bypass the reader's prejudices - if Gregor had some specific illness, the reader would immediately (re)create the situation in his/her head, and decide on their own reaction. Which means they don't examine the characters with an open mind. But because he has turned into an insect, which never happens in real life, the reader has to accept the family's reactions without their own ideas of how the situation should be handled.


    How to game these moments? Do you need to use tokens in Mortal Coil to introduce magic? That might work - whenever you want to introduce something magical, or address it directly, you need to expend a token. Otherwise, you have to narrate only "normal" or "realistic" reactions and happenings.

    \nI think ignorance of the methodology isn't the same as there not being a methodology. It's pretty clear (from the LotR books) that Saruman's and Gandalf's magic are tied to and developed from the wizard society (or outside of the books, the fact that they are supernatural beings), they are reverential and powerful figures. Every bit of magic is weighed down with importance, imporance that isn't just reflected by the reactions of characters, but also the world itself. The wizards, Sauron, witch kings, the ring, Aragorn's sword, the elven connection to the world - everything has a resonance and great import, and a reason and logic behind their inclusion.

If Lord of the Rings were retold in magical realism, the Ring's threat to the world and ability to make Bilbo invisible would be secondary to the fact that it's extended his life so now he has to organize a 111th birthday party for a whole host of neighbors and relatives that he barely tolerates. Oh, and look, now here comes that doddering old magician, taking an odd interest in the jewelry, ruining the retirement plans. The idea that anyone made the ring intentionally, much less to "rule them all", wouldn't even enter into it.

    \n Somehow, I missed this thread when it started. Ironically, I was writing about this sort of thing at about the same time this thread started. Here's a few excerpts I thought I'd share.

    Magical realism -- a mode of writing, rather than as a depressingly culture-limited literary category -- is what I write. For a more commonly-available and thus more useful example, it's what Neil Gaiman writes much of the time, IMO. \n

    Magical realism aims to seize the paradox of the union of opposites and is characterized by two conflicting perspectives, one based on a rational view of reality and the other on the acceptance of the supernatural as prosaic reality.

    Magical realism differs from pure fantasy primarily because it is set in a normal, modern world with authentic descriptions of humans and society and involves the amalgamation of the real and the fantastic. It offers a world view that is not based on natural or physical laws nor objective reality, but is not separated from reality, either.
    \n((I've been using this term for a while now for my own writing because I dislike "urban fantasy" as categorical heading -- a feeling that shifts to outright loathing when it tries to associate itself with stories I've written -- my publisher is still at odds with me over it.))

    Now, I will freely grant that British-born, Minnesota-living Gaiman isn't writing magical realism by a literary grognard's definition of the term -- calling it Magical Realism doesn't obey the 'rules' of the official definition, but more the mindset. I don't think of it as a genre as much as mode of storytelling -- creating a story with two conflicting perspectives, one based on a rational view of reality and the other on the unblinking acceptance of the supernatural. Anansi Boys - with Fat Charlie and Spider representing the two views - is a really easy example, but there are many others.

    Writing that kind of story -- has a 'gotcha' that causes me a fair bit of stress during rewrites. I'm not going to be able to sum this up in a very tidy package, but here goes:

    [All the following is my opinion, naturally.]

    There are two ways you can approach the Fantastic in a story, regardless of the setting: there's the 'magical' way and the 'fantasy' way.

    The fantasy way is the most common, I think; especially in any book series where you learn more and more about the world in which the story is set. Basically, there is magic or something supernatural in the world, and as we spend more time in that place, more and more of the system behind the magic is explained, until we know all the rules. There are lots and lots of examples of this, but Tolkien either started or popularized it, and it's carried into any number of series. Laurel K. Hamilton is one, ad nauseum. Tamora Pierce. Harry Potter, certainly. Charles de Lint, sadly. George R. R. Martin, happily. In short, fantasy stories are set in a world with special rules, but once those rules are understood, said world works in predictable ways; what I think of as the Arthur C. Clarke version of magic. (To my mind, this often takes the 'fantastic' out of the fantasy, but that's my own problem with some writers, and only really a problem when they mean not to do it and do it anyway.) Clearly, games like DnD (or damn near any game on the market, mainstream or otherwise), largely follow this pattern.

    The magical way doesn't ever quite explain how things work. Fairy tales are like this. Things aren't predictable, and the magic isn't ever quite explained. Some things are the way they are because that's how they are. You don't question it; it just is. There is a kind of childlike acceptance of the unreal here; 'superstitious peasant' reasoning. Neil Gaiman does this very well, as does Holly Black some of the time. I think Mortal Coil can do a pretty outstanding job of this, provided the tone and setting that the players come up with leans in that general direction. (The direction in which one will encounter Stardust and Tithe and Shadowbridge and Coraline.)

    Please understand: I love a good fantasy. Nothing wrong with them at all... unless that isn't what you wanted. Fantasy for the sake of fantasy is great fun. Fantasy masquerading as a magical tale is going to feel flat and technical and lifeless.

    And therein lies the danger of the magical mode of story telling. You must be very true and accurate to the participant reading/playing the story. Stuff has to be clear (more importantly, it has to be true, but right after that, it has to be clear), but you can't explain everything, or you ruin it.

    What happens and why it happens has to be crystalline and solid, but at the same time you have to abide by the rule of magical thinking which is that sometimes, things are the way they are just because, and if you show someone all the gears and connections, the magic goes away.

    Said from the point of view of a writer: If you reveal too much of the wrong stuff, it's not magical anymore - it's just a fantasy.

Similarly with magical realism, you're aiming at contrast. The old man with wings is great because the village is so ordinary. Hell, you can have the same effect just with non-magical strangeness put in ordinary situations. I refer you, with great relish, to The Troublesome Offspring of Cardinal Guzman by Louis de Bernieres. Even if Father Garcia never levitated, or a Spanish count never got thawed out after 300 years of being frozen, and Thomas Aquinas never came back, there's a bit of the oddball in everybody, as well as a bit of a the normal all around them.
