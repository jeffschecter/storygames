    \n As in any human-to-human interaction, a lot more context than what was provided is needed to determine what the appropriate method of feedback should be. The rules and culture of the First Thoughts forum is insufficient to make this determination. The amount of detail the particular person provided when prompted was insufficient.

    When I read the initial post, I was almost convinced the poster was a 14-15 years old who'd watched Babylon 5 or DS9, and wanted to play some character from the series or "tell similar stories", while mixing in tropes from other genres and games, without having considered how it'd work in play at all. The mention that he'd played RPGs for 15+ years but never found a setting he'd like to play in was the only indication that I was wrong. If he'd been playing with the kind of parameters he implied in his own posts for 15+ years, I think I know why it's been unsatisfactory to him ("epic" world but without PC agency, d20-style system but modified with no classes and emphasizing skills, etc).

    So, the "kid" in question is probably my age (30+) or older, but it seems - from our ivory towers, at least - as though he's had a long-running series of incoherent games with a group that has placed emphasis on Colour and Character and remained ignorant of other game elements such as a coherent System and particularly Situation. Why, when Actual Play requires you to provide some background of the personalities involved and the particulars of the medium by which the game was played to provide context to the report, does nobody in this First Thoughts thread challenge the poster to provide context to his game? Who are you? What experiences in particular have you been dissatisfied with - provide actual play examples? Who are the people you have been playing with? What games have you played, how have you played them, and for how long? How do you foresee the actual play of this game will proceed (round-robin scene framing, GM-led frame-by-frame play, one big combat encounter per play session and then play-by-post character interaction between sessions, etc)?

    I agree in principle that tough love is the way to go. Nobody in this hobby has ever actually benefited from sugar-coated feedback, and there is certainly no lack of enthusiasm for game design, setting authoring or hacking/modding out there. But I have a 13-year old son with a stated interest in game design, and his idea of a good game is one where WH40K Tyranids co-exist with Bleach demon-hunters and Naruto kid ninjas in a Rifts-like "anything goes" universe, all overseen by an all-powerful GM who willy-nilly hands out "Prestige Points" to players he likes to use for advancement purposes. If he went on The Forge and posted his ideas, I'd want someone to say "hey, tell me about your gaming experiences and your design goals first - by the way, have you ever played Rifts, because it's possible you could just use that instead, also, you may want to check out these earlier Forge threads on the subject". I wouldn't want some wheel-burning motherfucker tell him his ideas stink and why doesn't he just go write fanfic and stop bothering the role-players (not to say that's what happened in this case, of course).

    Context matters. Adam's notion of interaction with people in different game design stratas is sound, but you have to know who is where to do that, and modify your interactions accordingly. It is not educationally valuable to treat someone you don't know as a peer by default.

They were made of who and what they were made of, and that was in large part a function of time, context, and some amount of accident. That's how intellectual communities have always happened - think the Inklings, for starters. The internet has brought a lot more power to such communities, but they've also brought a lot of pitfalls, including the invitation to ossify their infrastructures when the community's infrastructural needs have actually changed.

A lot of the same ideas made at the Forge are still going to be at the heart of those conversations. But the context needs to change, the same way the contexts of all intellectual conversations-in-the-large that have ever happened have changed: flexibly, with time and the human lives of those involved, and without attachment. There are going to be a lot more children of the Forge, many of them will be different, and a few will be demonstrably better (for more narrow mission statements maybe... but maybe not).
