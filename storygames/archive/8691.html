\n One of the fun things about Basic D&D is tracking hit points, which are an arbitrary resource that have little or no bearing on the fiction (Clinton has been juicing this up with some after-market critical hit tables). But as written they are like a video game life meter, which got got me thinking. What if everything about your guy was his arbitrary life meter?
Here's my guy:

He's been in a bad scrape and taken 11 points of damage. That could have reduced his physical stats to zero, killing him, but he chose to spread the hurt around to maintain some level of fighting effectiveness. So while his Vitality and Awareness did suffer, so did his skill, and when he gets home he's going to find out that his wife Clara is gone (dead? Run off? Kidnapped? Who knows), and although his love for her and their son is shaken it isn't shattered, and the farm is in ruins. But he still walks, and he's still got his dad's sword, so there's still hope, right? But another injury of that magnitude and everything in his life will be destroyed. In this system gaining a hit point might be having a baby or getting a promotion at work or expanding your pig farm. "Healing" from these wounds might be burying your wife and getting remarried.

I've explored ablative characters in my games but I know there must be some that I've missed, or are more like my example above. Any thoughts?

    \n Looks a lot like Risus. Each PC is defined by a number of "cliches" (anything from Astronaut, to Brooding Emo Vampire, to Gelatinous Blob, etc), which have numeric ratings. Whenever you're in a conflict, you roll a number of dice equal to the rating of the appropriate cliche. If you lose the round, you lose one point from the rating, until any one of your cliches hits zero (when you "die", or are otherwise removed from the conflict). Healing is accomplished arbitrarily, if I recall.

    The trouble with this system is that it promotes a downward spiral. All things being equal (that is, two opposing cliches with the same rating), whomever loses the first round of a conflict will most likely lose the conflict. This may or may not be a bad thing, depending on the type of game you're going for.

    \n Yes, this is essentially the entirety of Universalis's traits.
    \n
    \nA character can have "I love my wife" as a Trait. You can call upon it for dice in a Conflict. Resolving the conflict provides both you and your opponent with bonus coins to spend. Your opponent could use one of their Coins to cross off "I love my wife" from that character. OR could add "but I love my girlfriend more" as a Trait to that character.
    \n
    \nThe total number of non crossed out Traits determines the character's Importance, which is the number of Coins it would take to just write that character out of the story altogether...sort of a short cut to having to eliminate all the Traits one at a time.

    \n In PDQ this works out as follows: You take "hits" in conflict to the non-operating aspects of the conflict. Usually, you heal those back at the end of the conflict adjudication (unless there is continued danger). The extra items you are losing are just placeholders for an overall potency of the character.

    What Chad adds to make it interesting is the idea of a Story Hook - the first item you choose to "ding" in combat is recorded by the GM and becomes fodder for a story development or complication that comes up soon after. If you chose to drop your "Married to the Princess" quality first, expect some martial woes or for the Princess to get kidnapped (or run off with a Dragon). The risk is still there, but not in the conflict that opens the door to the issues to come.

    \n Nobody's mentioned Trollbabe, where you can burn your relationships to... I forget what, keep from losing a fight or getting killed or something.

    In my Pentasystem you can "sacrifice" something that's one of your motivating attributes (the things that you care about that get you into conflicts and power the refreshment of your pools). Attributes are also your "weapons", as it were - their rating determines how many dice you roll.

    Sacrificing a motivating attribute means that for the purposes of winning the conflict, all of your dice automatically succeed, so if you sacrifice an attribute rated at 4 you get 4 guaranteed successes. But you still roll the dice, and any that actually show failure - are gone.

    You always have to narrate how an attribute helped you in the conflict (like Dogs), so when you do a sacrifice, you also narrate how you're putting that attribute at risk of damage and how the damage occurs.

    So the bandits are getting away, right, and you have this horse that's faster than anything on four legs. And you really love that horse. But the bandits have rolled really well and they're going to escape unless... you narrate that you ride down a dangerous shortcut path at breakneck speed, and risk laming your beloved horse....

    \n It may just be me, but I can't help feeling that old school thinking is holding us back here. I don't know of anybody who enjoys the 'death spiral', and the concept of 'damage' making a character less 'relevant' strikes me as counterproductive. And I don't want to punish players for taking risks, I want to reward them by assuring such risks have a meaningful impact on their character.

    So how about when a trait becomes completely ablated, it is replaced by another one with an incremented value? So if you run out of Pig Farm (1), it could be replaced by Get Money to Buy the Farm Back (2), or even something less directly related.

    And once a trait reaches a certain value (say (5)), it will be replaced by TWO different traits each at (1) instead of increased. So Heartless Assassin (5) could be replaced by Man With a Past (1) and Protector of the Weak (1).

    Finally, traits should not become less useful as they are ablated. They should be an all or nothing kinda thing.

    ...

    Now that I think about it, this reminds me of the way Fallout works in DitV.
