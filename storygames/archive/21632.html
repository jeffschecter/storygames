    \n Each serves a different purpose, though I believe, overall, the +5 modifier is stronger. Advantage/disadvantage serves to flatten the math curve, allowing for heroic success or tragic failure even when the odds are stacked in one way. In contrast, a +5 (or -5) modifier creates a floor or ceiling, meaning characters can never succeed/fail on rolls with a set DC. This has the side effect of making rolling superfluous in some circumstances, while dis/advantage makes it only mostly superfluous.

    Both could be implemented alongside one another. My preference is toward smaller numbers in general, so I broadly support dis/advantage of the modifier.

    I view the use of dis/advantage in 5e skeptically. It is haphazardly littered throughout the rules, and the application of the mechanic is not well explained. What is the difference between raising the DC of a skill check and imposing disadvantage on the roll? (There is a difference, but when to do so is left to the GM's imagination.)

    \n Are you asking about the aesthetics or the mathematics here?

    My home D&D uses both of those (been running much longer than 5th edition, for the record). I generally have a bonus die on qualitative, big, character-defining special moves, while you tend to get a static modifier for having a substantial external advantage.

    For example, a "berserker" in our 14th century Sweden campaign would get an advantage die on combat checks while raging, which makes them feel rather powerful and special (particularly in a system with critical hits and fumbles). Meanwhile, it is rather rare for characters to get static bonuses from their character build, so a +5 would probably originate in something like "there are already two other guys attacking him, so you get a circumstance bonus for being the third". (That's the local flanking rule equivalent, basically: during a combat round, attackers engaging later in the sequence get an increasingly large bonus to their attacks.)

    I think that there is an aesthetic advantage in trying to distinguish the modeling on the various types of modifiers in some way like this. By preferring bonus dice in character abilities I reduce the need for the players to complicate their character math, and I retain static modifiers to the dice rolls more exclusively in the realm of circumstantial bonuses. This helps players keep math straight in the long run, I think, and helps give mathematical character to stuff; it just feels different to be a mighty berserker than to be the third guy ganging up on somebody, even if the average advantage is rather similar.

    \n Yes, the benefits of doing one or the other are all about *separation of purpose*.

    Do we want all the modifiers to stack uniformly, do we want natural caps, do we want clarity about which-applies-when? If the rest of the system does that well (as 5E does, for instance), I like it.

    If I had to choose based on aesthetics, I'd go with Advantage/Disadvantage, because there's less math involved and I like that (it's faster and less error-prone at the table). However, normally I wouldn't answer the question without knowing more about the game's design.

    For instance, if you have a game where combat is all about setting up advantages against your opponents to get the upper hand (and in ways that apply directly to the dice), Advantage would be terrible, because it would undercut that aspect of play.

    \n I agree with pretty much everything that's been said here. I'll also add, since I've been thinking a lot about probability distributions of dice systems in 2097's discussion, that +5 and advantage do different things to the distribution.

    1d20+5 is still a flat distribution, it's just now flat from 6 to 25 instead of 1 to 20. You're still just as likely to roll badly as to roll well.

    2d20K1 is weighted toward higher rolls. You are more likely to get a 2 than a 1, more likely to get a 3 than a 2, and the 3 is deep breath more more likely than the 2 than the 2 is more likely than the 1.

    The result is that the feeling we all seem to have of a certain flavor difference in advantage over a numerical bonus is actually backed up mathematically. Succeeding with a low roll because of a big bonus feels different than succeeding with a high roll, and advantage makes you more likely to get those high rolls.
