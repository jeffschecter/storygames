    \n It seems (and maybe no more than seems) that a limit of the collectible card game that implicit social contracts are required for friends to have fun playing. That is, you need to release more cards every three months. They need to be good, so people who have already bought cards in the past will buy more. Eventually, you reach some critical mass of cards where people who are dedicated enough can find and 'collect' certain cards such that they win the game in unfun ways (like the guy says in the article, it's easy to win). There's no provided rules for what 'casual' play means, or even for deciding what it means for a given group.

    It used to be unthinkable to turn the implicit competition of a game like D&D into explicit competition without ruining everyone's good time. But Agon, to take a single example, does just that. Are card games missing the analysis and re-analysis that for RPGs resulted in things like open, fair, fun competition? Or am I just not aware that there's another forum in some far-flung corner of the intertubes that is us or the forge, but about card games?

    \n From the linked article:
    "You can win, sure. But for most casual players, a game full of people who go all-out all the time means that it stagnates quickly. Mix it up a little."

    This statement is utterly baffling to me. To me, Magic stagnates the moment people stop playing to win, so the opposite notion makes no sense to me.

    Also, this sounds like sheer madness to me:
    "But the best way that I've found is to tell people flat-out when things are a little cheesy. As an example, someone asked me the other day, "So you run Vintage-legal lists? That's a little worrying. What's stopping me from going nuts with my crazy combo?"

    "You can do it," I said calmly. "And if you do it enough, we won't want to play with you. So that's up to you.""

    Right, I mean, the best way to make a competitive game fun is to make it less competitive. Also, imposing such a vague restriction is a wonderful policy in a game where it's so easy to be specific with a ban list.

    Honestly, the whole notion of "okay, you can win all the time, so long as you do it with shitty decks" or "you can dominate every 3rd match so long as you throw the other two" sounds a lot like "we should find a different game to play on Tuesdays, because this Magic thing isn't actually working out for us."

    Edit: I just realized that the article is almost pure marketing. Specifically, the whole "money imbalances" section seems really sketchy coming from a guy who runs a singles site. Here's an idea: if one guy has, like, no cards, don't tell him which cards to buy; just let him make proxies. Easy. Now the playing field is level again, it took very little time, and nobody spent any money.

    \n Ummm.... Eric, not to contradict your own preferences, but I know exactly the thing that's going on with not playing to win. Many casual groups have a variant winning condition. The winner is the person who comes up with the most "cool" (unexpected, funny, i-can't-believe-that-actually-works...) deck. In order for most "cool" decks to work, they need a less competitive environment - because a lot of the most interesting things you can do in the decision space of a CCG aren't the most effective.

    For an RPG analog, consider the common knock on optimized characters in D&D - that there is a degenerate strategy available, and therefore everybody should play a cleric or druid built according to a particular set of specs, because that's the most mechanically effective build. It's arguably true, yet this does not actually happen. Why? Because in most functional Gamist D&D groups, the actual winning condition is not "My character pwnz yoo" but "we. together, win against the monsters, and each of us gets to contribute to winning in a unique and cool way."

    \n To elaborate on Judd: there's another interesting thing that happens at our games with one guy, S. If S. is attacked early before it is absolutely undeniable that he is the biggest threat and must be dealt with, he will get very nasty with everyone and carry a grudge from game to game. It's a fairly effective strategy, because people so don't want to deal with his bullshit they usually leave him alone.

    So I play Magic a lot, but as a lot of you may know I am very not-into competing with friends. I have therefore been pushing to play team games as often as possible and have largely won the argument. When I have the opportunity to work with my friends to take out other people, I feel much happier.

    One of the games is very interesting socially. It's called Star. 5 players, each have 2 opponents, the ones not sitting to the right or left of you. If you or one of your allies loses, you lose, and the game is over (that person's two enemies win). It's interesting because there's a lot of politiciking and table-talk, because it is a team game, but in order to win you always have to betray at least one of your allies (unless you have a deck with a card that says "when X happens you win the game").

    \n As somebody who played Magic pretty much only as a beer n' pretzels game (I got out of playing before there really were tournaments and such, like less than a year after it came out), I can sympathize with the notion of not playing it as balls out competitive. I think there's a place for that, don't get me wrong. Might even be the best use for MTG overall. But it's just fine as a friendly game where we try to temper each other's decks so that it's fun to play.
    \n
    \nIt's like... "Yeah, you beat me with that deck, and I don't have a counter for it. Now why don't you try some other deck so that we can actually compete again with some of the decks I have. I wonder if the decks I have made up are good against anything? Heck, I didn't really plan them out all that well, and just want to play with what I've got and not worry so much about construction, and just have fun playing."
    \n
    \nI mean, that's actually what made me stop playing MTG, because game-play is pretty damn straightforward and it's all about deck construction. Often we'd just make random decks and see how well you could do with what you came up with. That was often far more fun than trying to see who could come up with the best deck.
    \n
    \nFor me.
    \n
    \nThe point is that I think that with any game, there are those people who are into it to really see how well they can do, and others for whom it's no more competitive than, say, playing Parchesi. Each is valid. No, you don't have to "mix it up" as the guy suggests - he's basically just pushing his own agenda here. But for the right group, there's nothing wrong with "Play nice" as part of the contract.

    \n I grew disenchanted with Magic when I came up against a series of hardcore players whose decks were built on a strategy of preventing their opponents from playing: freeze decks, mana burning decks, card-stripping decks. Unless I wanted to engage in the same kind of strategy or optimize to defend against it, I found myself just sitting there with my frozen deck, drained of mana and discarding my hand while the other guy played his cards and whomped all over me. Once in awhile, cool. Constantly, meh. For me, that style of play killed the creativity in building theme decks, outlandish strategy decks, intentionally awkward decks, or whatever.

    I can be pretty competitive, but I don't care to have to follow a straitjacketed strategy that means either I'm having fun (if you can call it that) or the other person is, but not both.

    \n I never played that much Magic, only casually for the first couple of years it was out. But this conversation reminds me a lot of how people play Scrabble.
