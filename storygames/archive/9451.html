    "It is important to know that no matter if the PCs succeed or fail in the SC the goal will happen."

    \n

    I would jump across the table to attack my DM if he played skill challenges like that.** If some event must happen (or must NOT happen) then it makes a lame goal for a skill challenge. A fun goal is one that is equally entertaining if it succeeds or if it fails.

    \n

    Using your story of tracking down thieves as an example and guideline, I would much more enjoy skill challenges like:

    \n
      \n
    • Is the local Imperial Judge impressed or disgusted with your style and skill as investigators?
      • \n
    • Can the PCs track the culprit to his hideout and still know where they are when they get there?
      • \n
    • Having found the control room for the hideout's trap system, can the PCs disable the whole thing before a roving patrol finds them?
      • \n
    • After the big fight, can the PCs convince the Imperial Judge that the captured (or killed) the right man?
      • \n
    \n

    I've only been playing 4E for about a month now, but skill challenges are one of my favorite bits. With the exception of using the success/failure chart from the errata, I wouldn't change a thing about them. They rock.

    \n

    -Eric

    \n

    ** Okay, not really. But I sure would make my displeasure known.

    \n Eric, I think the trick is more in the degree of failure. If you are tracking someone through the city, roughing up drunks at a bar to find out where she went, giving bribes to town guards for info, using your knowledge of local hidey-holes, etc., and you succeed, then you find your target and she has no idea you are there: You catch her by surprise.

    By contrast, if you fail the skill challenge, you still find her, but by then she knows you are coming, has a group of thugs waiting to defend her or can set up a trap for the heroes. Either way you find her, but the skill challenge gives you an idea of whether you have the advantage or she does.

    I think the big confusion with the "you always reach your goal" part of the skill challenge is, you arent seeing if you reach your goal, you are seeing under what circumstances you reach your goal.

    Degrees of success by how many failures were rolled is cool with me too. Like so:

    \n

    Goal: Do you find the hideout?
    \nOn the first failed check: You're so lost it'll take you all day to find your way back.
    \nOn the second failed check: You're spotted. Success or failure means you'll have to fight.
    \nOn the third and final failed check: They suckered you and led you down a bad path. You have no idea where the hideout is.

    In fact, that kind of stuff is so cool with me that I just assume that something like that is going to happen to one degree or another every time we do a skill challenge. My position is that the last failure must be a complete falure. Meaning that the goal was not met.

    I don't think we're hijacking. We're discussing the topic of the blog post which is the OP of the thread.

    \n

    "The idea is not to haver those situations where a failure stops the game dead."

    \n

    I'm agreeing with this. 100%. What I'm not agreeing with is the idea of saying that the goal is one thing then resolving it as something else. It's not semantics. It's the difference between being up front with your players about what's going to happen as a result of failure or pretending that the goal of the challenge is something that it's not.

    \n

    "...that is how the game designers have explained it was intended."

    \n

    It may very well be that the designers intended success with consequences as a possible result from a skill challenge failure. After reading your last post, Michael, I went back to the DMG to see if I read something wrong and found their example "Urban Chase" on pg 78. I'm dissapointed, but not surprised, at the lame example. I'll accept that I was wrong in my implication that the success with consequences type failure was a modification to the rules.

    \n

    On the other hand, the other examples in the DMG clearly show that the designers also intended skill challenges to occationally result in total failure. Therefore, saying that skill challenge failures should always result in success is certainly a deviation from the game as-written.

    \n

    However, I still stand by my position that a skill challenge goal that must succeed or cannot succeed is a lame goal, and not one that I want as part of my gaming experience.

    \n And I can understand your position. I heard interviews with the designers before I went to read the books, so I went in with a different perspective when it came to skill challenges. That doesn't mean I don't see where you are coming from. It is an issue for you, but not for me, and that happens.

    I do enjoy the idea of a failure becoming part of a story. I think there was an example posted on here a while back about a Mouse Guard game where the players were in danger of not completing a mission to redirect a swelling river before it flooded out a nearby village. A lot of people argued the game would be just as cool if they failed to stop the river, because then it would become "How do you deal with your failure?" and the characters trying to rescue what people they can and minimize the damage/loss of life. And that is pretty cool.

    I think the DM needs to be very upfront about what the skill challenge is about, and I don't think the DMG does a good job of spelling that out. I still think skill challenges are one of the potentially coolest yet most confusing aspects of 4E.
