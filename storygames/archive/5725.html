    \n Matt's using "illusionism" because it's a specific phenomenon. "Railroading" is a local phenomenon. The problem with this discussion, and many like it, is that Railroading isn't defined well, and everybody is putting their own meaning on the term.
    \n
    \nThe only way that I've found to use "Railroading" successfully as a term is to have it mean, "The players aren't getting to be creative in play in the manner in which they expect."
    \n
    \nThis is because EVERY RPG has limits on it's content. And these are mostly agreed to by the players. You could agree to have somebody read you a novel, and call that RPG play if you wanted, and it's still not "railroading" in any useful sense, because everybody expects to have the novel read to them, and not participate creatively. D&D is very tightly constrained in some ways, as is GURPS ("universal" my ass). All RPGs start out with a the set of infinite possibilities, and then constrain them some amount. Barring the absurd novel-reading example, even the most constrained of RPGs still has a subset of those infinite possibilities that is, itself, infinite.
    \n
    \nThe only question is whether or not that set is agreed upon by the participants, and they enjoy that particular set of constraints. If some player is creating unagreed to constraints on participation, they are "railroading." If not, then they're just applying agreed to constraints, and that's how RPGs work.
    \n
    \n(In fact, I've often pondered if, in fact, this is the defining quality of RPGs, and what sets them apart from, say, wargames and reading novels).
    \n
    \nNow others may have some other definition of railroading that makes this discussion worthwhile, but if so they're going to have to make them clear. It seems to me that all RPGs have constraints and a tighter set does not constitute anything problematic to play.
    \n
    \n
    \nBTW, this is a corrolary to the point I often make (Ron's point originally) that the GM doing his agreed to job is not railroading. That is, yes, the GM has some authority in the game, but there's nothing wrong with him using it in the agreed to manner. To, say, introduce an encounter in D&D. The only time charges of railroading are leveled is when that encounter is introduced in order to force the players to go south to avoid it, so as to take away their potential decision to go north. See the difference?
    \n
    \nIf "railroading" isn't refering to something dysfunctional, I'm not sure what it's purpose is as a term.

    \n Rafael, I totally agree with your original premise, once you remove the fighting words. Something like:

    Back in the day (and to this day with some backwards-thinking traditional-gaming throwbacks*), a GM would put together a game with his friends in order to share stuff he thought was cool. He put a lot of work in compiling cool content, cool conflicts, cool house rules, and in general, a "way the game works" that he thought would be fun. He then shared this with his friends, often like a sort of participatory art form: "here is my game; you play it, but it is still my game, and it is my artistic creation." Much honor, respect, and hullabaloo was accorded the "good GM" who could put together a good game.

    Now we have our brilliant and liberating indie games, which, generally speaking, take that GM role and toss it out the window -- pre-game prep is often replaced by in-game development, and the GM-as-director/players-as-actors thing is often replace with more equitable collaboration. (There are exceptions, of course, Burning Wheel especially.) And lots of folks were happy and did a little dance because now everybody at the table helped create the art together, at the table.

    BUT -- this still destroyed the niche of the creative GM sharing his cool game with his friends. It's a rare thing in indieland to hear "Man, Jason is such an awesome GM!" Lots of us have come from a gaming pedigree that honored and respected putting something together and sharing it with your friends -- for those of us who wanted to still do that, our go-to option has been dissolved. Dissolved by games printed using new technology that makes it easier to create whole new games. The easiest solution is to use the same techniques and technology to put together our own game, complete with all the stuff we thought is cool, complete with a "way the game works" that we think will be fun, and then package and print it up to share with our friends.

    Tellingly, when somebody says "We played Jason's game and it rocked!" it's assumed they're referring to Grey Ranks, not some TSOY game that he put together and ran. Moreover, the kudos and social respect that we give designers looks very similar to the social respect given on RPGnet to the guys who post running APs of the games they are running, or share some of their prep for the game they're about to run.

    So no, I don't think it's off to suggest that GMs turn into designers in indieland. The community is constituted in such a way that becoming a designer is the easiest way to get recognition and the easiest way to share creative, artistic work. Are there other motives? Sure. It just strikes me that traddy GMs who put together a creative thing in order to share it with their friends and indie designers who... put together a creative thing in order to share it with their friends -- those are incredibly close motivations, and the "creative GM" is not a role that is commonly available in indieland. No surprise that people who might otherwise seek that role turn to the closest alternative.
