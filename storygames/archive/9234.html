\n In Steering the Craft, Ursula Le Guin talks about conflict, and she said one thing which made me consider aspects of human life which have an outcome, but are not a conflict.

"Modernist manuals of writing often conflate story with conflict. This reductionism reflects a culture that inflates aggression and competition while cultivating ignorance of other behavioral options. No narrative of any complexity can be built on or reduced to a single element. Conflict is one kind of behavior. There are others, equally important in any human life, such as relating, finding, losing, bearing, discovering, parting, changing."

I know some games address this within their systems, but not many. How could we better reflect such non-conflict changes in our games, not just as hooks, but as central theme?* Which games do this well already?


*If you don't agree with the premise, take it to another thread.

    \n Some things which we call "Conflict" just mean there's some thing where the outcome is not given. The conflict sometimes is purely mechanical, to see whether one succeeds or not, rather than a conflict of interests between characters, or characters and the world. You could say it's a conflict between the character's wishes and luck, but that's life.

    I think some uses of "Fate Points" are just this, specifically in games where you spend a fate point to describe and create some fact in the world. Also, I think that "Sharing" or "Relating", in real life, are only meaningful because the default is not sharing, or at least, we know of its possibility. So in a game, even if there are no conflicts, the conflict should exist in the background - but I can agree with Le Guin; as a spice to give the main course its meaning, rather than as the main course itself.

    I think we also need to identify why we are so bent on conflict in our games if we want to see how to move past it. I think that different from stories written by an author, you can have several players who want something, something mutually exclusive, and that'd lead to a conflict. But would it be ok if it were only a meta-level conflict, one between the players, where the characters don't compete (whether Charles falls in love with Sarah or takes NASA on their trip to Pluto offer, but without there being a conflict between characters either way)? I don't see why not.
    Or because some people still look at the rpG part, where they want mechanics, and it's probably easier to have mechanics about conflicts, though quite possibly because we're used to that.

    In other words - I think we're already there for some value, but Simon, please clarify, when you say "No conflict", is it only conflict inside the world, or also between players? And is a character desiring a better job and not getting it, would that be a 'conflict'? What do you mean when you say 'conflict'? That's something to know if we try to explore non-conflict approaches.

    \n I have, amongst others, been an advocate for the idea that conflict is essential in building theme in role-playing games. It is not.

    Initiative is, together with consequence, experience and development. There do not have to be any conflict. We do not need to define initiatives taken in rpgs as conflicts either. Initiatives may lead to conflict, but not necessary. Initiatives may lead to a lot of different consequences, most of them as interesting to explore as the conflict. There is no need to simplify our playing field regarding this. Let's look at all the full spectrum of initiatives players may take in our games, and what they can lead to ...

    A broader scope in thinking will give us a fuller picture of the possibilities inherent in role-playing games. If we expand and deepen our view on rpgs, we will be able to make even better games.

    \n I thin Le Guin's example still relate to some underlying difference and deciding what to do about that difference. So whilst conflict is bringing that difference out into the open and deciding who wins, the others are:
    - relating, finding out what, despite differences, we have in common
    - finding is going from a state without to a state with, it's an internal difference
    - losing is the opposite of finding (and also the opposite of winning)
    - bearing is putting up with the difference
    - discovering is uncovering a difference, or lack of it
    - parting is walking away from a difference, or lack of it
    - changing can be about the underlying person or the relationships between them

    Not that I'm pretending that this is a definitive list. YMM and will V.

    But any of these can be resolved by conflict, in it's meaning as a resolution of differences. I want to go, you want me to stay (A), we part (B). That's a conflict. How do we move from A to B is the resolution. Conflict in games mostly looks at it from that angle, and then decides how we feel about it. It might be more interesting to say "we're going to part" and then play out what this means to us. In fact, that's the attitude that Koenig Hospital takes. You randomly decide what is going to be involved in the conflict and then the guy whose scene it is decides what is going to be decided, and then you play it out to see how everyone is affected.

    \n It's gotten to the point where games that are all about conflict resolution kinda bother me, for many of the reasons you and LeGuin set out, Simon.

    I think the indie designs that I know of that have structure to support non-conflict types of resolution often have them as "refreshes," small character scenes where something cool happens, relationships are reaffirmed or changed, themes are demonstrated, and mechanical resources recover. Specifically I'm thinking about refreshes in Bliss Stage, Agon (though those are often filled with conflict), The Shadow of Yesterday, and the players' turn in Mouse Guard, but there are others. Also, hitting and buying off Keys in TSOY (along with a bunch of other similar character development mechanics) is another way to mechanically support things happening that aren't necessarily about conflict. Also, I think one could argue that fallout in Dogs in the Vineyard is somewhat related. Fallout is like "So, the conflict happened, but this is what it actually means," so it gives you character developments that are independent of or perpendicular to the stakes and actions of the conflict.

    One of the reasons I've been excited about structured freeform design (even in its Nordic larp-inspired forms, like Jeep, which isn't necessarily where my personal design interests lie) is that it frequently structures things such that any given scene basically works like "refresh" scene, in that conflict isn't necessarily the goal that you drive towards, so things are often more exploratory, with different players coming in with different intents for the scene and something unexpected happening as a result. My early experiments, such as the Avatar: The Last Airbender fanfic game, worked almost completely based on this kind of play, with a few other mechanics pointing players at specific themes and such.

    P.S. I think part of the reason structured freeform tends to lean in this direction is that most freeform designs lack formal conflict resolution guidelines, so other kinds of resolution can come to predominate. But you can still have a lot of non-conflict resolution in games that are not freeform, but more mechanically structured, you just might have to explicitly make spaces for them to occur in, because a lot of roleplayers (especially Forge-influenced ones honestly, less so "we're playing Shadowrun but only kinda using the rules" folks who have been used to ignoring the resolution system) tend to have play focus on places where the most mechanics are. That said, Vincent's recent "follow the fiction" designs (Poison'd, etc.) are aimed at trying to get folks (even conflict-centric indie gamers) to ignore the formal mechanics more, which can lead to more non-conflict resolution, I think, because you can stab somebody and throw them overboard without there being a conflict, so play becomes more expressive. Just my 2 cents.

    \n What is often missed, in these discussions, it's that "conflict" as a RPG mechanic and "conflict" as a literary term aren't the same thing. The rpg mechanic with that name can be used for all the things in Le Guin's list, AS DEFINED, not as a sort of "lets' see all these things as a conflict". In PTA, for example, you can set as the stakes that you get to relax, that you find something, that you relate to someone, etc... they are conflicts IN THE GAME (because there are two people who draws cards to see what happen) but not in the fiction.

    Said that, there is the risk that the players don't see this, and seeing this game technique called "conflict", use it only to fight and hurt other characters and win fights.

    I don't think there is anything in most indie games that push to conflict (the ones who do, want to create a specific kind of conflict-based story). What should be changed is not the games, it's how they are explained to the players.

    \n I agree with Tim that literary conflicts are almost always non-mechanical.

    However, I disagree with the idea that indie rpg conflict resolution mechanics can be effectively used to model the kinds of things that LeGuin is talking about. The overwhelming majority of conflict resolution mechanics -- even when you're resolving whether trekking through the snowstorm together strengthens your friendship with your cousin -- are modeled by putting two players in opposition to each other (often GM vs. player, but sometimes players vs. each other) with both sides "fighting" over what happens. So even when you're not fighting in the fiction, you're fighting in the resolution, which totally fucks up, in some cases, the emotional feel of resolution and the players' connection to fictional events. The mechanics ask you to take sides in a situation where there may be no clear sides or where you may feel more strongly about your opponent's position than your own. You might call this "over-protagonization" (the opposite of de-protagonization), where players are conflated with characters or specific stakes/outcomes instead of being allowed to remain complex individuals with multiple interests and high flexibility in most cases. Turning everything into a binary or multi-sided struggle is often less than fantastic.

    (cross-posted with Josh, who is making similar points about the flavor of mechanics)

    Posted By: Jonathan WaltonI agree with Tim that literary conflicts are almost always non-mechanical.

    However, I disagree with the idea that indie rpg conflict resolution mechanics can be effectively used to model the kinds of things that LeGuin is talking about. The overwhelming majority of conflict resolution mechanics -- even when you're resolving whether trekking through the snowstorm together strengthens your friendship with your cousin -- are modeled by putting two players in opposition to each other (often GM vs. player, but sometimes players vs. each other) with both sides "fighting" over what happens. So even when you're not fighting in the fiction, you're fighting in the resolution, which totally fucks up, in some cases, the emotional feel of resolution and the players' connection to fictional events. The mechanics ask you to take sides in a situation where there may be no clear sides or where you may feel more strongly about your opponent's position than your own. You might call this "over-protagonization" (the opposite of de-protagonization), where players are conflated with characters or specific stakes/outcomes instead of being allowed to remain complex individuals with multiple interests and high flexibility in most cases. Turning everything into a binary or multi-sided struggle is often less than fantastic.

    \n It's still in playtesting so I don't want to go on about it too much, but one of the (smaller) things microscope does is center scenes around a question rather than a conflict.

    So you start off with a question like "why did the aliens leave the colonists alone" and then you frame a scene based on that, not the other way around. When that question is answered, the scene has served it's purpose.

    That question is the whole reason we are exploring the scene in the first place. It's what we want to know about the fiction (the history, in microscope terms).

    A question could encompass a conflict, like "does the king get assassinated" but it can also be something completely conflict-free, like "who finds the king's body."

    But of course that's conflict within the fiction -- player conflict about what they answer to be is a totally different level beast, as others have observed.

    \nGuy/Steve: Actually, I'm talking about something a bit different, for example, here's that snowstorm conflict broken down four different ways:

    We're playing Mouse Guard, so extreme weather is actually a thing. The PC in question is a mouse named Joseph and he wants his relationship with his cousin Ferdinand, an NPC, to be strengthened through surviving a trek through a snowstorm together. That's his goal (stakes) for a Journey conflict. The goal the GM assigns to the snowstorm is for the misery of the trek to make their relationship even worse and give Joseph the "Angry" Condition. This places the GM in opposition to the player, even if the GM would rather their relationship get better or the player really wants their relationship to get worse. Players take sides for particular goals in the fiction and advocate for them. This doesn't require them to want to win or hose the other player, though, just provide some antagonism.

    We're playing Dogs in the Vineyard, so the snowstorm isn't actually a thing, mechanically. The conflict is now between Joseph (a Dog) and his cousin Ferdinand (a no-good ex-Dog-turned-bandit). The GM plays Ferdinand now, as a NPC, and the conflict starts as Just Talkin'. This situation is even more directly confrontational, because the GM doesn't have the mediating element of playing an impersonal snowstorm raining misery on the characters, but instead things become very personal as the GM and player negotiate their way through the conflict as the two characters in question, again advocating strongly for each side, as required by the See and Raise mechanics.

    We're playing a game like Bliss Stage or Otherkind, where there are multiple categories for players to put dice in and different traits they can draw on to get more dice (however, each trait drawn on adds a new category to put dice in, so it's not always better to draw on as many traits as possible). In this kind of system, Joseph's player is essentially rolling against himself, a bit like playing solitaire. The player picks which traits to use, rolls the dice, and then chooses how to place them in various categories. The other players might have the power to alter the categories a bit (like when the GM treatens categories in Bliss Stage, making players put more dice there), but they don't really actively participate in providing mechanical antagonism (though they may provide some narrative antagonism). If Joseph's player succeeds or fails, it's because of luck and that player's choices.

    The fourth way is to not engage conflict mechanics at all. One player plays Joseph, another plays Ferdinand, and maybe a couple others describe the events of the snowstorm. And interesting things emerge from the interplay of those different players contributions, just as in the "freeplay" portions of heavily mechanical rpgs or all play in a freeform play style. The only problem with approaching the situation this way is that, in most RPGs, the things that happen during freeplay have no mechanical effect on the stuff on the character sheet or any mechanical things that happen later in the game. So there's this incentive -- if you want what happens to actually matter mechanically -- you have to engage the conflict mechanics and, in most cases, that means engaging in antagonism or a confrontation with other players at the table.

    \n So if we're going to take LeGuin's types of resolution seriously, I think we need to consider a couple of things:

    - have interesting stuff happen in freeplay too, instead of just where the mechanics are
    - have the non-conflict stuff have mechanical significance (if there are mechanics)
    - have resolution mechanics that don't frame things as inter-player (or player-GM) conflicts

    In some ways, I think this is kinda what Vincent has recently tried to suggest, having fictional elements ("having the higher ground") give you mechanical differences in play ("I get a +2 to attack and defend!"). The problem is that, in those cases, players are always incentivized to declare that they've seized the higher ground or have other fictional advantages, even when that doesn't make any sense for the fiction. So there's this problem of letting the fiction impact the mechanics without having purely mechanical concerns negatively impact the fiction. And I don't think we've solved that problem yet (and it should probably be a separate thread, anyway).
