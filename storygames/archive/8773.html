    \n I tried running Cold City last year for 3 players, but the game quickly dropped to 2 players and at that point the trust mechanics becomes worthless, so we just muddled our way through to the end. So, the main suggestion I have is make sure you're running for a fair number of players, say 4 or 5 at least. Games with trust mechanics (including Mountain Witch, which strongly inspired Cold City) really benefit from having more players than you might normally want in a personal, high intensity game. Mountain Witch is gold at 6 or 7 players, actually, which would overwhelm many game tables.

    Also, if you're running Cold City, you might consider mining Hot War for ideas, since I think HW reflects more of what Malcolm's learned in the years since Cold City was released.

    \n Well, we've played quite a lot of Cold City but as author of part of the book, I thought I should hold back.

    In our home game which I ran, we had between 5 and 10 players. 10 is a bit too many really, for any game, not just Cold City, but it was still fun.

    I did quite a lot of background reading to understand Berlin in the 50s, and asked my Father in Law about his experiences, he even got a credit, much to his bemusement.

    The game we played a home had rather a different feel to the one we played two years running at Gen Con (run by Malc). Given the wide scope for types of players I think there are a couple of things you need to think about when running CC that you don't for HW. Actually you do for HW too, but they're more explicitly covered in the game preparation phase.

    These are things about tone and scope.

    In one game we had a French petty thief and a Nazi Grand Wizard. The traits were on different scales so you have to work harder as GM and players to make sure that the Wizard doesn't dominate the scenes. That is one character has knife-fighting, the other has embody Thor.

    Talk to your players at the start about the kind of things they want in the game and about how powerful they want characters to be. After all, success in a roll doesn't depend much on the difficulty. To the same extent, talk about tone. Is it gritty and low-key, how much humour do you want in the trait names, how much overt magic. Can characters be scared by things (i.e. do they have to make, in effect, SAN rolls)?

    Having a set-up scene for each character is a good thing, even if it doesn't require any dice rolling.

    \n I take exception to your characterization of Malcolm "crawling the web". But I'll read charitably.

    I, for the record, could be argued to do that. I actively use Google to look for AP of my games and link to them. For two reasons: (1) the main one, is that for a little bit of work by me you can easily find and read AP of my game by people who aren't me and make up your own mind about the game, and (2) a very minor one, that is more for chip-on-the-shoulder reasoning, is to prove some stupid fuck (who's now evaporated into his own cloud of pixels) wrong when he said I wouldn't get any AP.

    The bottom line is a lot of people play games and don't write about it -- whether they are good or bad. So, more AP? Better AP? More diverse styel of AP write up? I agree entirely.

    But Cold City has been played a hell of a lot (it's been out a few years now, of course it has). A lot of the games of it I've seen at UK cons haven't been written up -- even though they've been successful and hot as hell. Why? Well, a lot of the people running and playing it aren't here on these forums or the sort of gamer that would come here. Cold City is, I think, one of those games that appeals to trad players looking at indie stuff from the edges. Same with Spirit of the Century.

    Hot War gets less play as it's new and also relies on the group coming up with the background together -- bigger buy-in and more tree-hugging required. Cold City is more "trad" in that the GM comes up with the world (based on the view of Berlin 1950 from the book) and the players come up with their characters, if that makes sense.

    Cold City has an AP page which might help:http://www.contestedground.co.uk/coldact.html
    Cold City has an AP forum: http://www.contestedground.co.uk/forum/viewforum.php?f=39

    You even get the whole System Chapter as a free download: http://www.contestedground.co.uk/colddown.html

    Scott Dorward runs Cold City like a f'in demon. He's been doing the same with Hot War, but since it's newer he's not run as many. I wonder if he's still around here. If so, he might chime in.

    Personally, I ran Cold City 1.0 once and we got a good short game out of it. Being a tinkerer it didn't work the way I would have written it to work, so that made me come up with something else. Malcolm then came out with Hot War and Cold City 1.1, where he tightened up on things. Read the system chapter. It's free. Make up your own mind. You can even run the game using that and then buy the book/don't buy the book depending on how that goes for you.

    \n I do still lurk here, and even post sometimes when prodded!

    Hot War has become one of my favourite games, and I run it a lot, especially at conventions. I am, however, terrible about writing AP reports for, well, anything. I start out with good intentions, but by the time I get around to it, I've forgotten most of the cool stuff that happened.

    The world creation routine in HW is wonderful, and has created some very great, dramatic and meaty stuff in my games. When it comes to running one-offs at conventions, though, I tend to either nick backgrounds and characters from less time-constrained games I've run, or to come up with them myself ahead of time. This isn't quite as satisfactory, but it makes it feasible to run a game in a three- or four-hour slot.

    The one caveat I would offer is a pretty obvious one: make sure all your players are on board with the idea of a game that depends on inter-PC conflict. I ran one problematical game at a recent convention where a player was deeply unhappy about other PCs being in conflict with his character, and the game lost a lot of its energy as a result.

    One tip I'd offer is that if you, as the GM, are hitting the PCs with heavy conflicts which end up giving you loads of points of consequences, spend most of them on giving the PCs negative traits with scary-sounding names (e.g. Blinded, Disfigured, Crippled). This makes it sound like the character has taken a real battering, but actually makes it more effective and fun and to play, and can have interesting consequences if the trait gives the high die in a future conflict.
