\n I have learned to like Firefly/Serenity
\n
\nTo me, it is character driven action drama, NOT cool guns and smart-arse comments.
\n
\nTrouble is, every time the discussion turns to setting up a possible space opera game people want to do it "like Firefly" which then expands to "you know, guys wandering around from planet to planet messing with the man, cool fights, smart arse quips."
\n
\nI suspect picaresque (characters wandering from place to place with no great story arc motivating them) can work so long as the crew of adventurers have personal motivations or conflicts that matter.
\n
\nSo, any thoughts on how to do it?
\n
\nI have come up with
\n
\n1) Heroquest: the ship is a "Community" resource which must be maintained and that provides the consistent motivations (kinda light on character motivation)
\n2) Solar System: characters have keys and secrets -- nice player-chosen breakthroughs and character shifts but where does the persistent inter-character interaction spring from
\n3) Apocalypse World: the character archetypes are a good match -- but rewriting a bunch of playbooks and the MC moves seems a big task.

    Posted By: epweissengruber3) Apocalypse World: the character archetypes are a good match -- but rewriting a bunch of playbooks and the MC moves seems a big task.
    \nActually, no need to rewrite--just roll with an Operator or a Chopper and disallow stationary types like the Hardholder or Maestro D'.

    The pitfall to watch for, if you really want Story Now, is that in Apocalypse World, when the PCs lack community grounding there can be a tendency to skimp on "motivations or conflicts that matter,"
    Wherever the wandering adventures roam, they have by default no stake in the local problems, so unless they make it their BUSINESS to have a stake, the group will fail to develop any empathy for the locals and their travails, and may even burn the place down without a second thought.

    I've had seen an Operator-centric game go that route, anyway.

    So, if your crew are a bunch of badasses with no ties, they need to care about SOMEthing, right? So they either care about each other, or they care about NPCs that travel with them, or they're just the sort of passionate fuckers who DO grow attached real quick to every poor soul with a sob story in every town in the land/system/galaxy.

    In the case of the first, the relationships need to be problematic enough that it creates genuine friction and hard choices. You need a Mal and Amara, a Simon and Jayne. That's mostly up to the players, but AW supports it quite well. Same with Solar System, with appropriately primed Keys.

    In the case of the second, AW is fine for that as long as the PC builds come primed with NPCs--crew for the Operator or Savvyhead, Gang members, Cultists, and so on. Again, the players have to bake them into the setup. These are great because not only will they have their own issues and problems for the PCs to deal with, they'll get entangled with the locals whether the PCs do or not.

    In the case of the third...well, awesome! But not every group will, by default. And if you're working with the kind of players who characterize Firefly as "cool fights, smart arse quips," I wouldn't hold my breath for this unless your setup bakes it in.

    peace,
    -Joel

    \n So, "picaresque" is how I'd describe an arc of Spacerpunk, a game I'm working on. You define your character in broad outlines form crew roles ("Pilot", "Doc"), as well as some characteristic and in-game fluff. Some random tables and oracles generate new worlds and situations to cause trouble and delight. It's heavily in-progress, but it's been fun so far.

    For more info, check out my blogposts/notes on the game or my very alpha rules documents. (Or ask me further.)

    Otherwise: HQ and Solar System both have been used successfully for this (and there was even a basically-firefly set of Skills/Secrets/Keys floating online somewhere).
