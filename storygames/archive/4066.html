\n What do you tell a first-time Dogs player? I bring this up because I've been in two one-shots in which I had rather mediocre experiences (no offense to the GMs) because I didn't really get the game. Having not read the book, all I knew is that I was a Mormon-ish teenager given the duty of enforcing God's Law.

Now, perhaps Jesus Camp warped my mind, but that description screamed "religious zealot" to me, giving me a black-and-white mentality that did not allow for the moral ambiguity and conflict that (I'm told) is the awesomeness of Dogs. So I should be introducing a competing value or goal to complicate my allegience to the Book of Life? Call me dense, but it would really help if the GM mentioned that, because it's not intuitive from the rules/mechanics (for me).

So, what do you tell new players? How do you push moral conflicts when all the players know is that they're in charage of upholding the Book of Life? What do you do to explain the "fun?" Is it just me, or does Dogs suffer from this problem (the conflict does not obviously derive from the rules/mechanics) more than other games?

I bring this up not to dog on Dogs (sorry, I had to), but to encourage GMs to be a bit more guiding for first-time players.

    \n First let me say I've had a lot of fun with Dogs one shots, both playing and running. But, I don't think that is the optimal way to play Dogs. I think everybody needs to get together for an evening to make characters. Talk about your backgrounds. Suggest traits and relationships for each other and develop your characters history. Most importantly here would be to not foll what seems to be standard advice of "leave most of your relationship dice unassigned." Assign those dice, and by doing so, tell everybody who is important to your character and why. After characters are made, leave the sheets with the GM so he can build towns around those relationships and traits. It may be easy to gun down a sinner for making pacts with demons, but what if they didn't realize what they were doing before it was too late? Still an easy decision; what if it was your sister?

    If somebody wants to run and five town campaign (I'm not talented enough to do this) I'd be happy to drive down and help give you a better Dogs experience.

    \n I find that the "the players get decide" answer is an unsatisfactory to those looking for a more "within the fiction" answer. These players use the classic if Demons result from Sin then doesn't that prove the "truth" of the sin and therefore I'd be playing "out of character" to do anything other than shoot sinners on sight. There are three sections of the book I point to to clear this matter up. Unfortunately, I don't have the book with me at the moment so I can't quote exactly and I can't give page numbers but trust me all three of these concepts are in there.

    1) I point to the place where it says that Dogmatists are listed among the Unfaithful. That to simply treat the Book of LIfe as a list of unbreakable laws is to be unfaithful.

    2) I point to the place where it says that the King of Life is a pragmatist. That the King of LIfe would rather two people have pre-martial sex in true love if their were extenuating circumstances as to why they couldn't get married first.

    3) I point to the place where it says that demons lose their power over sinners the moment the Dogs condone the sinful activity. I believe this is in the scenario with the greedy shop keepers. It mentions that the demons *DON'T* want the Dogs to side with the shop keeper because then the demons would lose their power.

    These three principles serve to eliminate both the "in character" dogmatist argument and the "reality of the supernatural proves truth" argument. The only thing left is for the players to judge the situation based on common humanist principles even if those principles are filtered through their "in character" point of view.

    Jesse

    \n I think you might be off on a tangent here. The question of how much faith your Dog has is an interesting one, but let's leave that to the side for now.

    Just for fun, here in this thread, let's say that all Dogs are true black-and-white believers who don't question a single sentence of the Book of Life.

    The fun of play is this: What will you do to enforce that doctrine on others? Each player has to answer that question, over and over again, for themselves (the players) and their characters. All while the GM escalates the stakes of that question more and more.

    Like this: Your Dog believes with all her heart that adultery is a serious sin. No question about it, no loopholes in the book. You find out about two adulterers. What do you do about them? You have an array of choices, from talking to killing.

    Then: What if they won't repent? What if they try to start a new faith where adultery is okay? What if they draw guns and tell you to fuck off back to Bridal Falls City? What if they're overcome with shame and want to kill themselves? And on and on.

    That's all fantastic stuff for play, and it's not contingent on your Dog's own journey in the faith. It comes down to what you're willing to do to enforce the will of the King of Life.

    \nI think that question would be better answered at the table. Why is your character a Dog if she doesn't believe it all?

    Does she believe it some? Does she believe it none? Did her father always want her to be a Dog and talk non-stop about it ever since she was yay high? There's that pride thing again. Is she a Dog simply out of her own prideful need to live up to her community's expectations? Maybe she's not a Dog, not really. Not where it counts. Maybe she knows deep down in her heart she's a sinner too. She's a fake. Now what's her answer when she finds those two adulterers, knowing she has sinned just like them? Does she shoot em?

    Morally ambiguous enough for ya?

    \n Although others have all said pretty much the same (I really like John Harper's advice), this is what I have said to my new players, more or less:

    You have the Book of Life, and Doctrine is based on what it says, right? But it is not the Dog's job to blindly follow Doctrine (in my opinion, that's what Stewards are for). Remember, The King of Life is pragmatic, and allows those He has deemed worthy to judge exceptions to Doctrine in order to preserve the Faith as a whole. That is the job of the Watchdogs. If a watchdog is simply following Doctrine with no thought to the situation, then he is not doing his job, and is a bad dog.

    \n I've run Dogs for a few new tables and I've never gotten all that philosophical with how I introduced it.

    Here's a few things I make sure to say:

    "We are going to play Dogs in the Vineyard, a game about teenage paladins with a Mandate from God with guns in a wild west that never was. You are going to roll into town and the town is going to have troubles and it will be up to you all to decide how to make those troubles better. I won't tell you that your decisions are right or wrong, all I'll do is up the ante and bring on the consequences.

    You aren't going to have to look all that hard.

    As your teachers told ya:

    'If life was perfect, you'd all name babies and deliver the mail but life ain't perfect.'

    The other important thing your teachers at Bridal Falls said was:

    'Sometimes you have to cut off the arm to save the life.'

    and

    'We don't love you; we love the congregations you are going to go out and help.'

    Training was rough.

    Let's start character generation."

    That does it.

    As the game begins, most people are excited to play a character with a mandate from a higher power. By the end of the game, everyone feels the terrible weight of that mandate and realizes that no one should have to shoulder such a thing in life.

    \n I haven't seen anyone add this comment so far: have you thought of playing "Princes Kingdom" as an introduction to Dogs? From reading it, I thought one of the things it did really well was boil the ethical conflict down into a much smaller set of choices, and put them in terms that were more easily understood from a modern secular point of view.

    The code of ethics in Dogs is excellently framed, but it isn't short 'n sweet, nor is it immediately accessible to the modern secular person. Of course, it does resonate, as is demonstrated by that "aha! the light bulb just went on moment" that seems so widely reported amongst DitV actual play reports; I find that the "light bulb" phenomenon is symptomatic of games that aren't immediately accessible, but have a strong undercurrent that can be connected to.
