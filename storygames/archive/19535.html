    \n Now now, I haven't used the Veritascitor username on here in a long while.

    As for what D&D Next is, it's pretty much D&D, from what I've seen. Last I checked, it fit really well in the 2nd/3.x version of things, with a few innovations stolen from 4E (at-will spells, advantage/disadvantage being an extra die, etc.) The playtest didn't seem to have the crunchiness of 3.x or 4E, but the rules are a lot more modern than 2nd. It definitely eschews that superhero feel of 4E, but characters also aren't as helpless at earlier levels as they were in 2nd.

    I'd recommend taking a look at Mike Mearls' Legends and Lore column during the playtest period. He had some interesting thoughts. One of the ones that definitely stood out for me was the flattening of the math curve, so that lower level characters stand a chance against higher level enemies, and lower level enemies are still a threat to higher level characters.

    So what's it trying to be? I'd say it's trying to emulate the core what we all fondly remember D&D was like years ago (whichever edition that was for you) but with a clean ruleset that takes advantage of some modern ideas.

    \n Last night something strange happened to my pal and me. He was trying to make a character using the Anima Beyond Fantasy corebook, and suffering with it. Finally he tossed away the sheets and started rambling about how stressful the system wants, that despite reading the whole manual he couldn't made a simple character the way he wanted, that the system was limiting as hell, making the character felt like a stressful chore and didn't had any fun while doing it.

    I had GMed a hack of this game for him and the rest of our group like ten years ago so, while I also found a good portion of the game un-efficient and unbalanced (which was why I ended up hacking it), I actually ended up trying to explain and defend the game. After a few moments ahead on a bit heated argument I realized we had been in that situation before, but in reverse positions.

    He GMed a Pathfinder game for us, which I found quite stressful because of the system. I still have a sort of post traumatic stress disorder from playing 3.5 D&D with a crappy railroading GM whose sessions were full of heated rule arguments and no character was playable unless it was minmaxed. Well, when my friend GMed Pathfinder he was actually doing his best to help me out of that playing style into a more relaxed one. And though he actually enjoyed the game and we had a lot of good scenes, I just couldn't fully enjoy the game, as I always felt the character wasn't fully optimized, that I was playing it wrong and he was just humoring me by letting it defeat any opponents he throwed at our group.

    And while I was creating the character, I just felt like he was feeling now.

    After we laugh at the situation, we finally realized what happened there. It wasn't exactly the system. I was fond of Anima because of the anime setting, the powers that went beyond limits and made grids useless, the lack of feats to be remembered to make combos and that once the character was made there was actually little need to check the rulebook for anything. All this made me able to want to understand the game (and later fix the math and simplify it's mechanics). My personal preferences are either low to no powered settings with simple systems or wild versatile overpowered anime games... with simple systems. Pathfinder and 3.5, standing in a sorta middleground with too many mechanics and rules to remember for my taste, just doesn't struck a chord for me anymore.

    For my pal it\xb4s almost the opposite. Anime isn't his thing at all and he can actually get through the numbers to get the feel of the game. He can be watching a block os stats and actually see an organic entity with a personality, where I just can see a bunch of useless numbers.

    So yes, in the end anyone can actually play and enjoy any game, as long as they love the setting and embrace the game philosophy. Oh yes, you can hate parts of it and never use it, but you will forgive the designers as long as you love the core of the game for what it is.

    My advice is, take a deep breath, put a smile on your face and go find something new and shiny you can love about Next. Or just go play something you really really like. If you want help finding reasons to love it, the best one I can give you is that the core rules are what Pathfinder should be, it's 3.5 reborn with no crap in it. Advantage rules well, rules. I loved the book for it... until I readed about the classes. That crap sucks big time, or, well, it's just not my thing at all. If it were for me, I'd just play characters with no class features at all. Maybe I'd hack True 20 classes into it and that's it.

    Good luck finding your own reasons to love it.

    \nTo offer the dedicated grognards view: I'd say it was pretty fundamental to the entire project.

    5th Edition was supposed to (and I'm paraphrasing here) "return to the games fundamental, common elements, and/or the parts of the game that made people first 'fall in love with it'". In a nutshell there was edition warring, which crippled sales beyond Hasbro's patience, and this one was SUPPOSED to be a game that could be played by those that only played White Box, BECMI, AD&D, 2nd, 3rd/Pathfinder, or 4th, as well as bringing new people in.

    Now, ignoring the impossibility of that for a moment, you have to ask 'what do people enjoy, or what is iconic, about D&D'? Part of that answer is going to be Rule 0, because it's fundamental to a certain playstyle which launched RPGs in the first place. Not everyone likes that style, but a large body of followers do. That means if you want a game that will attract those players, you must have that element.

    I would separate that from the fudging die rolls thing, which is different (and more readily arguable imo).

    As to the rest, 4th failed. Now we can argue about rather it was a design failure, a popularity failure, a marketing failure, an adherent failure, a business metric failure, etc...but if it hadn't failed there wouldn't be a 5th. You don't stop making things that are making you the money you want. That means any game which builds upon 4th (to any large degree) will meet the same failures (doesn't mean it wouldn't be wildly successful among its proponents, just not among those not interested). That's why the entire point of 5th was to look to the beginning. It was supposed to get back the players who wouldn't touch 4th (and possibly 3rd/Pathfinder) with their 10' poles.

    Now, the problem (imo) is that you can't serve multiple masters. The people (largely) who enjoyed 4th largely weren't people who enjoyed Pathfinder, and weren't those still playing pre-3rd. If those people enjoyed the same type of game, they'd all still be playing the same games. They're not. They're playing the games that provide the experience they want. One game is not going to be able to provide experiences which differ enough to please those three groups, so the project was doomed from the beginning. I hoped. I watched. They failed.

    I told them from the beginning: you need three game lines. Create each to serve ONLY that demographic, and abandon everything that doesn't work for those people. You'll get 3 games of ultimate popularity within their niches, and therefore 100% sales (just split between three groups/product lines). While I understand the practical reasons why they won't do this, it doesn't change the ignorant lunacy of their propaganda.

    Oh, and 13th Age was Jonathon Tweet, who left the 5th design team. There's rampant speculation about why, and the timing. Most focused either on his intent for 13th Age, or a fallout with Mearls and/or Hasbro over the shifting of the game design. His loss of input/control is CLEARLY noticeable in the playtest versions. Up until he left the game was looking one way. The moment he left it almost instantly became 3.75 TPE (not surprising, giving Mearls & Hasbro).

    Remember that none of what I've said is making a normative judgment. I'm not saying 4th was bad, or Rule 0 is awesome, or anything else even remotely similar. I'm offering my interpretation of why things went down the way they did with the design of 5th edition. Trying to avoid it going to edition warring or badwrongfun accusations, neither of which need to be involved to have this particular discussion.

      \n When you consider these historical phenomena, Jason, it's instructive to also consider the sampling bias involved. I mean, your comparisons to past cycles of D&D seems to be concluding that there's nothing new under the sun, and I do agree to a certain extent; however, it is also the case that these repeating features of the public discourse might be meaningful despite the repeating features.

      For example, maybe we aren't seeing the same people repeating slogans like idiot parrots, but rather what we see is a serial disappointment, as ever new members of the hobby scene decide that enough is enough and abandon D&D (or abandon the consumerist line, anyway)? I don't know if this is the case, of course, but if it is, that would mean that D&D is managing to shed fans in waves every time they make a move. That's either a really poor track record, or a sign that you really can't make people happy by making changes

      Furthermore, often in these Internet discourses people who have no horse in the race air their viewpoints. This makes it completely understandable that we hear the same complaints about commercialism or whatever for every new edition that comes out: to us as observers it might seem like idiot fans are complaining about the Earth being round, when what we're actually seeing is just some random outsiders muse about their own D&D relationship. It might seem like a stultified relationship stuck on repeating the same complaints we saw 20 years ago, but that just makes sense when it is the same person complaining about the same thing that hasn't changed in the meantime. It's wrong to even call it complaining, it's more like "observing once again".

      Of course I do not want to deny your point, either: it is true that hobbyists, like all people, are prone to lacking historical perspective. No doubt many of the people who are now voicing first impressions about the new edition will forget their complaints soon enough, and perhaps they'll even find their own viewpoints absurd in a couple of years, once they get used to the new game. In this regard it's far from a bad idea to draw lessons from history, if only to realize that maybe D&D is not a game for you at all. It does sometimes seem like people would benefit from not taking D&D so seriously.

      Myself, for example - I don't have an opinion on the 5th edition, and have yet to actually read any game texts for it. I'll probably take a look when inevitably many friends of mine buy rulebooks for it, but it is likely to remain as irrelevant to me as the prior editions have all been; a highly consumption-oriented game product like that simply isn't relevant to my own real hobby life. For me the real edition change is when we switch campaigns and therefore rework everything about the game from the ground up, not when WotC decides to push a new variation of the game. In this way, while I do play some D&D on occasion, I'm basically just an outside observer to the D&D Olympiad hullabaloo every four years.

      I do admit that it makes for a curious sight when people care ever so much about something that can hardly amount to anything except yet another house gloss of D&D. The Internet's full of those, and the deepest practitioners invariably create their own. Not that new variations on the theme aren't interesting and useful to research if you play the game, but it's not like, you know, a sane person would expect a WotC-published variant to be that much better than some blogger's collected notes. The minor edge WotC has for affording a whole team of designers and a comprehensive playtest program (not as much of an advantage as D&D fans generally take it to be - the game's performance ceiling hasn't for a long time been related to issues that can be fixed by playtest) is handily off-set by rogue gamers being able to hone their personal vision in a consistent and non-commercial way.

      So at some point I tried to run some D&D next \u2026 and I've completely lost track of what it's trying to be.       \nThe short answer is that D&D Next (or 5e now that it is released) is about nothing and everything.

      What the game gives are the tools to make it easy for you to referee your players while they adventure in a fantasy setting using the tropes of Dungeons & Dragons. Orcs, gold, wizards, dragons, dungeons, wilderness, etc, etc). The tropes are fairly flexible enough to run a wide variety of fantasy settings. Although not infinite.

      What it is about depends up to what you, as the referee of a campaign, want it to be about.

      Some folks think D&D is about dungeon crawling. Dungeon crawling a well supported in Dungeon & Dragon because it been found to be one of the easiest type of adventure to explain to a novice in tabletop roleplaying game. Make a maze, add rooms, populate them with monsters and treasures, and place the characters at the entrance.

      But like most tabletop roleplaying games, D&D supports many other types of adventures such as those founding underwater, wilderness, city, other planes of existence, and so on.

      With that many think that D&D is made to support heroic adventuring. There are indeed a lot of suggestions, tables, and advice for making heroic adventures in D&D. But the fundmental mechanic of D&D, the one that sets is part from its progenitors, is this; The referee adjudicate the actions of the players acting as their characters.

      This single rule is the only rule that is necessary for tabletop roleplaying. The rest are guidelines and aides meant to make the game more fair, manageable, and/or fun. There is no preconceived notion as to what actions the players may attempt. This doesn't not everything will be possible. The players are acting as characters with finite abilities and limits.

      This means if a session revolving around intrigue at a Duke's Ball it can be run using D&D, GURPS, Fate, or any number of tabletop roleplaying games as all of them share this mechanics.

      What differs between different RPGs are the tools in which you use to adjudicate what the players are trying to do. Fate has one set of mechanics, D&D 5e has another. Some games don't have much in the way of social interaction rules. For example OD&D (the original 1974 rules) only had the charisma stat as a rough guide.

      Does this mean that you can't run the Duke's Ball in OD&D? Of course not. But does put a bigger burden on the referee's judgment to come up with rulings for things like when Marvalo the Magnificent tries to talk his way out when he was discovered in a compromising position with the Duke's daughter.

      With all this being said, the key elements in your decision in running a session of D&D 5e are

      1) Do you have any fun and interesting adventures/situations that incorporate some of the fantasy tropes of D&D 5e?

      2) Will the rules of D&D 5e help you in adjudicating what your friends do when dealing with the situation you designed?

      If either one is no, then probably running a game of D&D 5e won't be fun for you. If they are both yes, then likely you can run a fun session of D&D 5e even though you prefer other games.

      \nThe idea is that the Starter set is just that a boxed set to get you started with D&D 5e. The game is the Basic D&D PDF that they released for free. Right now they are not releasing the whole game. It obvious they have a specific marketing and product release plan. On one hand they don't want to rush the release as they don't want to get socked with errata. On the other hand they want to keep people talking (and playing something) until they get the whole thing out there.

      Whether it is a good plan is something we can only see in hindsight. If they avoid the missteps and problems of prior releases and it sells like gangbusters then it was a good plan despite the frustration of many of not having the complete game.

      Ultimately what it will look like this in the first year. You have the free Basic D&D PDF which offers four classes, a handful of options (mostly backgrounds), a culled list of monsters, treasures, and other items to sustain a complete campaign. The Starter Set focuses on a initial adventure and a hold you by the hands set of rules and semi-pregen characters. I call them semi-pregen because if you look at them they are pretty much the description of the class in the Basic PDF. The main difference is that the rules on the sheets only go up to 5th level.

      Then you have a set of core books (PHB, MM, and DMG) that presents a core set of rules plus a bunch of options to tweak the core rules into something similar to how you like to run D&D. Lots of character options like 3e, lots of tactical options like 4e, lots of roleplaying options like 2e, or just ignore the option to gain the simplicity of the classic editions.

      Along with this will be a initial set of adventures.

      There also be a organized play rules booklet plus supporting adventures.

      That sums up what i know about their initial year of releases.

      They do have a plan whether it is a good plan remains to be seen. Certainly it been going better than the the 4e launch where they mocked the prior editions of D&D and alienated half of the fans.
