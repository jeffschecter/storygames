The Verb

DanielSolis
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
So for my first thread, I thought I'd toss out a little idea I just had. Basically, it's my humble addition to the long list of "questions you should ask about your game as you design it."


What is the most important verb in your game?

Examples:
D&D: Level.
Paranoia: Betray.
Vampire: Brood.

I think this provides a convenient thematic focus for your game. If you're not encouraging your chosen verb, you might be veering off-track a bit.

  • Jason_Morningstar
    September 2006 edited September 2006
    Hey, not to derail with wankery, but this is starting to tie in with an essay I'm writing about the connection between roleplaying and improv. To whit:

    "The audience of an improv performance is often asked for suggestions ... Any word or phrase can be parleyed into an infinite variety of interesting scenes. Group games are employed to extract as many connections and nuances as possible from the given word or phrase. The pattern game is typical of these collaborative exercises ... the players ... solicit a word, and then begin free associating. Each word is explicitly influenced only by the previous word, so no general theme necessarily emerges, although an emotional tone often does. The goal is to return, after an elliptical excursion, to the original word. So, as a simple example for the word "clay":

    Clay ... pottery ... wine ... drunk ... homeless ... cigar ... relaxation ... tennis ... clay!

    As in scene work, one of the goals is to trust your partners - even if you don't understand the jump from on word to another, you must believe it is the best possible jump and work from there. If you didn't know that tennis is sometimes played on clay courts, that leap might seem incongruous, for example. In practice, it is common for players to make a word arc that returns to the original phrase and departs in a different direction three times. This free association leads to an wide array of thoughts, feelings, and images that will be used as inspiration for scenes."
