\n From Vincent Baker http://lumpley.com/comment.php?entry=660

We had a sign that said "Scary Games" (thanks to our dear friends of the Final Girl Support Group), and it worked, it caught their interest. We had people stopping and saying "games, huh?" and "really, scary games? How scary?"

When we said the words "roleplaying games" to them, they visibly lost interest. On Friday we led off with "you're familiar with roleplaying games?" and it didn't matter whether they answered yes or no, as soon as we said the words we lost them. Once we just stopped saying the words, they started sticking around long enough for us to talk to them about the games.

If you want to make non-gamer play to these games, it may be not enough to avoid calling them RPG (= Dungeons and Dragons), but it seems quite necessary.

i've been already posting with this idea in mind in this thread :http://story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=12916&page=2#Item_5 but Vincent's words and experience are more accurate.

More experiences like this ?

    \nAgreed. I sense that rpgs have a worse (more frivolous, more socially dysfunctional) reputation in the USA than here, though, so that might have something to do with the difference in opinion. The only slightly problematic issue with "roleplaying game" hereabouts is that the less-read youngsters sometimes get this mixed up with certain types of computer games. That's a minor issue, though, and one that pales in comparison to the confusion if one were to invent an entirely new term that would in turn confuse everybody instead of just a few inexperienced youths.

    I can just about see the sense in not calling your game "roleplaying game" in a specifically rpg context: for example, it might make sense to go to a large rpg convention and call your game something else at your booth, thus establishing your claim that your game is different from what a "roleplaying game" ordinarily means. Outside the scene this doesn't make that much sense, though, as your audience won't have a clue anyway.

    I played boardgames as a kid. Stopped as an adult because they took too long to play, and it was boring when it wasn't your turn. So when Settlers of Catan became popular, I avoided it because it was a boardgame. Then Erica changed my mind:

    Erica: "This is a Eurogame"

    Me: ::that sounds weird and different, my attention is engaged::

    Me: "That's cool although I don't like games like boardgames anymore because they take 4+ hours to play and it's really boring when it isn't your turn."

    Erica: "This has what's fun about boardgames but you can play in 45 minutes and you have lots to do even when it isn't you turn. But it's easier to show you than explain. Luckily I can show you in 15 minutes and then we can stop or keep playing, it's completely up to you. Would you like me to show you why Settlers is fun?"



    Although I now love boardgames (Eurogame or otherwise) and now find the term Eurogame to become increasingly less useful... when all I knew was Monopoly, Life, Hungry Hippos, Zombies, and Talisman... the above is what I needed to give boardgames a chance again.



    My writer friends love the term Story Games. My nerd, atheist, and self labeled hipster friends love the term D&D. My video game friends love the term RPG.

    \n I use the term "story game" most frequently, because in my experience, it's been the most generally useful term. It helps me connect with the segment of gamers that I am looking for, it helps me entice the segment of non-gamers I think are primed for this activity (poets, artists, actors, makers, writers).

    When I approach someone who I think won't vibe on the "story games" label, I just say something else. Like "this game where you pretend to be dwarves" or "a vampire romance thing," or whatever, depending on what I think that person will be interested in.

    At a horror con where the potential audience is people looking for low investment activities? "Scary games" is an awesome tactic.

    At an anime con where people have lots of energy but nothing to do? "It's like fan-fic that you play" is an awesome tactic.

    In most cases, or as a baseline to work from? "Story games" has been a reliable choice for me.
