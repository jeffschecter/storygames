    \n Or, it could be the Czege Principle is bogus, or incomplete. I say this with all the respect in the world -- it stands up to real-world experience and scrutiny to me, at least. But I don't have any real-world experience with computer mediated adversity generation.

    I think the very closest real-world variation I could think of might be, say, you have three tables (Monsters, Traps, Enemies) and each table has 10 entries on it.

    Now...if you could choose the table (Monsters!) and choose the entry (Bugbear!), then the table isn't providing a "challenge." It's just there to provide some brainstorming help. That's not bad, right? But what if you could choose only the table (Monsters!) but had to roll? Or could only choose the value (6!) but had to randomize the table? Then you're basically dialing in your adversity but leaving some room for randomization.

    I'm still of a mind that this doesn't matter if the player doesn't care, or is told to care, about beating the adversity. If it's just there to prompt other discussion (story bits, themes, whatever), then there's nothing to "beat" and the player has no vested interest in the choice.

    \n I recall a ASCII text game called Omega, and in it players could choose to go to an arena, and fight the next monster in a series of ever-increasing difficulty monsters. If you'd ever been through the cycle, you were even aware of what the monster was that was coming next.
    \n
    \nAnd that's not a problem. Because you selected to fight these monsters as the character.
    \n
    \nI think that the Czege Principle is, again, about stepping out of the role of being the advocate for the character, and having to also be the advocate for the opposition. Figuring out how the opposition is going to beat you. As long as it's approached from the POV of character decisions, or even if it's player decisions that related to the general nature of the elements involved in the conflict, but not the specifics, things will be fine.
    \n
    \nFrom a limited POV, the Czege Principle says that a player cannot be his own GM.

    \n People playing WoW can choose to do a dungeon on "heroic" mode, which increases the adversity as well as the reward.

    People playing, like, almost any action or adventure console game ever can choose to increase the difficulty, just for the fun of playing against increased difficulty.

    There's an entire subculture based around playing Nethack with weird and arbitrary restrictions designed to make the game even harder than it already is.

    I don't think telling a computer how hard you want it to fight, then letting the computer oppose you as the programmer saw fit, is controversial at all. As for this opposition helping to generate story... let me put it this way, God of War felt like a much more satisfying story when I played through on the hardest difficulty even though the cutscenes and plot were identical. So, I don't think there's an inherent roadblock there. You could easy use a set-act-progression setup, already used in a number of games to good effect, so that you already had a pretty clear idea what sort of conflict happened when. Those games already work with partially-preprogrammed opposition.

    \n I think that the Czege principle only applies to challenges that the character does not want, to illustrate...

    If he chooses to go to an arena and fight, that's one thing. You aren't stepping out of the role of the character to do so.
    If he gets ambushed, the character didn't want that but if you made it happen you loose the tension.

    If you go into an arena and say 'difficulty 7 please' you are either playing your character in Pawn stance (so no tension is required) or it's your character that wants the extra danger.
    If you get ambushed and say 'I want double the number of bandits', again you have a discrepancy between character and player needs.

    So I guess we need the answer to Callan's question, since the nature of the game affects how the principle is broken or not. Either way I don't think there is any different between getting a computer to choose or rolling dice on a chart, apart from convenience.

    Does that make sense?
