\n A couple of days ago, I watched my little sister and some of her friends playing outside in the yard, dancing through the grass and spraying each other with water.

And I started thinking about how roleplaying games are often characterized as being "let's pretend" but with better rules, rules to prevent people from just plain disagreeing on something like who shot who.

I've always figured that kids "grew out" of those games in part because they eventually realized that such a game doesn't give you a good way to resolve those issues, or to compete meaningfully. Whereas other sorts of games (sports, board games, etc) have more established rules they can agree to follow, which is easier to manage on the social side of things. I believe (but could be wrong) that child psychologists often say that there is a certain phase in most children's growth where they become interested in strict rules and authority, and following those. This phase probably corresponds closely to the age where most kids stop doing the "let's pretend" thing--in the 8-12 age bracket or something like that.

(Now, I'm not an expert in this field, or even well-informed, so if anyone can either support that statement and give more information, or let me know why and how it's wrong, I would be very interested to hear that!)

Anyway, the funny thing I noticed was that these kids who were playing out in the yard, even though they weren't "playing pretend", were totally comfortable with making rules on the fly, with no apparent logic to it, and almost always accepted with very little argument. For instance, at one point one of the girls ran behind the tree to hide from the other girl holding the hose. Immediately, on of the other kids shouted, "You're not allowed to go behind the tree!" So, she stepped out and got sprayed. From then on, no one went behind the tree. Unless they changed that "rule" later.

It was tremendously fluid, with the rules of their games being negotiated, established, and sometimes (but rarely) broken, much like the way a roleplaying group negotiates and establishes what's happening in the fiction of a roleplaying game.

Interesting, I thought. For these kids, playing (say) a sport or a board game is not any more structured or any more rigid than play "let's pretend".

The same is no necessarily true of more open-ended play, such as is generally found in RPGs. I think that has some real implications for RPG game design that often gets oerlooked.

    \nThis is very interesting! Someone just pointed out in one of the other threads that most RPGs have unspoken rules along these lines. For instance, in a one-shot at a Con, there's a rule that's never verbalized but usually followed: if it all possible, character death is not on the line until the end of the session. If a situation comes up in the first five minutes of play that could kill a character, everyone will typically jump on board to making sure that doesn't happen. However, at some point later towards the end of the session, character death quite distinctly becomes "on the line": some level of dramatic tension is reached that makes death a worthwhile threat or meaningful outcome without removing the player from the meat of play.

    It's interesting that I've never heard anyone discuss this explicitly, or deal with it with explicit rules. Very interesting!

    \nI think you're onto something here, although the fluid rule breaking and making definitely serves multilpe purposes. In some cases it might a child who oversteps their 'authority' by making rules when they aren't recognized as having the right to do so. Sometimes it's about what's fair and fun.     I was quite shocked the other day at an event at my daughter's school when two first graders ran past me playing tag. When the chaser tagged the runner, the both spun around and did 1-2-3 rock-paper-scissors to resolve the tag. It totally blew me away. Apparently the boys in the class had a problem where everyone wanted to be "it" at the same time and there wasn't enough running. The teacher helped them devise a form of tag with multilpe "its" where conflicts are resolved using rock-paper-scissors. It strikes me as a pretty advanced social contract for first graders.
