    \n For what it's worth, Dev, that is pretty much my beef with the 4th edition: reading and playing it, higher-order strategic layers failed to make an appearance, which made the game feel dull and pointless. I have not felt like delving into it further after the half a dozen sessions I've done over the years since it was published, and I've yet to read a single 4th edition book that would have discussed, addressed or supported play that was not about stringing players into the next carefully balanced combat encounter devoid of strategic context. All the adventures I've read have been trad-style plot railroads with compartmentalized combat encounters, not unlike some computer games.

    Obviously enough nothing in the above prevents me from doing whatever I want with the game, including a free old-school sandbox, but the more fundamental issue of style prevents me from caring enough to set up a campaign and see about solving the design issues. From the beginning the 4th edition has failed to engage me in its world of fantasy, which has consequently left me uncaring and unfeeling about it. This is mostly because the game consists of endless power listings with uninteresting fluff and an unrelenting focus on the minutiae of combat. What flavour of fantasy is presented mostly seems to be there as a paper-thin context for the miniatures skirmish game; as I fail to see the game's content as true-to-life in some literary manner, I end up disinterested. I don't deny that the new planar cosmology is kind of neat, but that's just in the abstract - I haven't seen anything substantial enough to motivate me to play the game.

    Proponents claim that my two issues with the game are caused by a lack of imagination and ambition, but I don't have more sympathy for that view here than I have with any traditional designs: if your game was supposed to be true to life and exciting as a fantasy adventure game (as opposed to merely abstract skirmish), then the text failed in setting up such substance; if it was intended to present a free and multi-layered strategic game like its precedessors, then it failed to communicate the purpose and means to me. I don't doubt that people can carry these burdens themselves, but for that you'd need to find something appealing in the game to motivate you. The skirmish combat system doesn't do enough for me to base a serious campaign around it, really, it'd need something more.

    So there, at least some of us here can still edition war non-ironically. Have at thee, ye savage cur!

    Posted By: JDCorleyWhat did you think of the game when it did present a world, say, Eberron or Forgotten Realms or Dark Sun?
    \nI have to admit that I'm sort of a bad test subject in this regard because much of "D&D fantasy" as a genre sits ill with me. I like D&D best when it does understated, weird pulp stuff, and less when it gets all Gygaxian with the hobgoblin empires, utility magics and so on. Not surprising that I like I don't really care much for e.g. Eberron or Forgotten Realms - or most bottled D&D settings, really. Dark Sun feels better for how it frames the D&D stuff like e.g. weird monsters into a world that is recognizably exotic instead of just humdrum - it's sword & planet genre, pretty much.

    However, the 4th edition sourcebooks for these haven't really caught my attention. I did leaf through the FR and DS books, but nothing particularly interesting jumped at me. (Compare with the original Dark Sun, which is very in-your-face about the brutal lethality of the setting, going so far as to institute character stable play to support it.) I think that this isn't so much because the books aren't good, but because I'm not in accord with the creative agenda of the game itself, so I don't hook correctly into the campaign ideas and setting details. I would first have to get on board with the idea of unique heroic player characters who don't have strategic agency, and I'd need to find some way out of the "what happens if the adventuring party loses a combat" conundrum; I'm sure that once I got into that mind-set, the campaign arc ideas, setting details and such would seem much more relevant. As it is, I read, but don't really comprehend the point.

    I'll probably explain myself more clearly about why the 4th edition setting seems lame if I compare it with older D&D: in older D&D (Mentzer and backwards, say) I see a brutal challenge to the values of civilization, set upon the heads of the player characters, who are by presumption worthless scoundrels all. The game is very much about discovering through strategic and tactical sense, what is the value of a strong sword-arm in an arbitrary and amoral world. Everything starting from the choice of character class (is it really going to be of any use to be a "thief" in this campaign?) drives at these themes, which I find substantial regardless of the lesser setting detail. 4th edition, on the other hand, seems to be offering a story of heroic team-work, and there really isn't a survivalist edge to it: the game implies that the heroes will, by and large, win through difficulties, and it has no real procedures for what to even do should the party fail and die. (This might seem laughable to 4th edition veterans, but when we've played the game, it's been constantly and consistently lethal. I still don't know how you're supposed to balance a 2-hour character creation with dying in the first combat, unless the GM is supposed to cheat.)

    \n For what it's worth, I tried to reasonably reflect upon my 4th edition experience up there, perhaps as a counterpoint to remind the reader that not liking a game doesn't have to be insane and inane like Dev seemingly suggests. Perhaps I failed - this probably depends on whether you find my particular expectations and preferences as contrasted with 4th edition D&D insane and/or inane. I do genuinely think that 4th edition is intended to be played with pre-built combat encounters leavened with freeform playacting "cutscenes", though, so if I've misread the game entirely then no wonder if this kind of critique of the game seems toxic. I wouldn't like it either if people read a favourite game of mine and insisted on a radically different reading of the game text's intent.

    Also, I agree with Dev on the substantial point: games should be read constructively and with an effort towards using them in the spirit they're intended. I won't comment on the critique corollary - it's a complex topic as to what is toxic and what isn't, and when people should shut up and when speak their minds.

    For my part, the games I play I always try to play with the best of intents. I wouldn't intentionally foil the game in the way Dev suggests. It's difficult to learn the right way to play, though, if you aren't part of a large urban gaming scene where older and wiser gamers can show you the broader culture of play. In those situations you're left with one or more game texts as your primary guides into how to understand the game. Sometimes we end up with different games through this process of interpretation. For example, I have no idea if the "4th edition D&D" I've played would rank as the real game or as correctly played in the eyes of the expert. Maybe it's not, or alternatively maybe people like different things and the 4th edition D&D I don't much fancy is a game that other people find highly enjoyable.

    If I had to name one thing that's weird (and maybe toxic?) about critical gaming talk, it's that people apparently find it nigh-impossible to accept that there's somebody out there in the world who doesn't like their favourite game. It's not enough to explain in detail how it's played wrong and how it's perfectly fine if you use it right, but in these Internet arguments you actually need to collect some scalps and get everybody to admit that yes, this game is great. It's a strange standard of discussion in my eyes.

Page 42 nor the excellent advice found in both DMGs doesn't change the fact that a good 60% or more of the 3 main rulebooks is directly tied into combat, and characters primary means of affecting game results is via combat, and of the 3 or 4 primary ways to earn game rewards, all of them except combat can be completed in a half hour or less - most of which is talking and exploring the fiction of the world together at a rapid back and forth pace.
