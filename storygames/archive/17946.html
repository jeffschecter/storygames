\n I know that the announcement was made that Dungeon World would be released under a Creative Commons Attribution license, and in fact, this license information is included with the text at the GitHub repository, so I was surprised not to see any Creative Commons notice in the final release of this game.

Is the text of the final release to be considered to be under this license? Why was the decision made not to include such a notice?

    \n My understanding: The text of the game is available under a CC license, as per the GitHub repo. The book and the PDF (inclusive of art and layout) are not. The license notice was probably excluded from the print version of the book to avoid any suggestion that these features of the work were covered by the license.

    \n One liability issue for publishers who might want to use Creative Commons content is being able to prove down the road that they did indeed have the right to use it. This has come up in situations where photographers have changed the licensing status of their photos on Flickr, even though a Creative Commons declaration is not actually revocable.

    There's no Creative Commons registry, so if the only evidence is a website that may disappear or be modified, this introduces a risk.

    (Not that this would be a problem with Dungeon World, as the upstandingness of its copyright holders is without tarnish.)

    \n It's still 100% creative commons and always will be. Even if we were replaced by our evil mirror images from another dimension they'd have a hard time erasing the proof that DW is creative commons, since there's a versioned history mirrored to other people (through git). There is one issue with the text in git that I keep meaning to fix, but can never seem to find the time. Other than that they're as identical as was possible in our editing process (which is to say there may be minor differences because keeping everything in sync was HARD at the end).

    I kind of wish that the book did say "this text is creative commons," and the fact that it doesn't is mostly an oversight. I can see people being confused by that and assuming the art is too, but I don't know if it would be enough of a problem for us not to do it.

    I'm curious what others do in this realm. I don't have a copy of a Doctorow book handy\u2014does he say in the print edition that it's creative commons?
