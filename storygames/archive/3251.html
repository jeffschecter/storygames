\n Oh, what a vague and semi-misleading title.

What I'm talking about is using the table calendar format (that is, a book that can stand by itself, with a spiral binding on top) as a way of presenting information in an RPG. It's a good idea for when you want players to only read a little at a time - and you can use the reverse side for pictures that other players can look at.

(It could also be used as a monster or NPC book for GMs. The GM sees the NPC/monster info; the players see a pic of the monster).

I've made some example pics here.

Any pros and cons to this approach? Any thoughts on possible RPG-related applications?

    \n You know, the "player" side could have info on it that the "GM" side doesn't. This makes more sense for a game where the GM and player roles are all mixed up, but it could be really cool.

    I'm thinking of the "Traitor" and "Hero" books in the board game "Betrayal at House on the Hill". Partway through the game, one of the players becomes the "traitor" and goes off to read a randomized scenario, and get secret information about what is going on in the haunted house. The other players read the same scenario in the Heroes book, which gives them related but not the same information. Sometimes the Heroes know the traitor's goals and abilities, sometimes they don't. Sometimes the traitor knows the heroes' goals and sometimes he doesn't. This could possibly be incorporated into an rpg somehow that could be poetantially really cool.

    For example, a spy game where the two sides of the thing present different goals for the spies, like getting some bit of information from the other spy or getting an admission of guilt.


    Or incorporate the calendar into the conflict rules. At the start of a conflict, the calendar starts near the middle, possibly adjusted by the character's abilities. Like in July or so on a real calendar. One character is trying to advance the calendar to one end (eg, December), and the other is trying to flip pages to the other end (January). Each page gives different options or abilities to the person who can read that page, and which option you choose and your opponent chooses (and maybe some randomizers) determines whether it advances toward December or January.
