    \n Of particular interest for various reasons from 2011 or so, in rough order of interest:

    Kagematsu - a clean game in the best tradition of Forgite structuralism, matches mechanics to theme beautifully and focuses on moment-to-moment meaningfully focused play.
    Microscope - fresh gameplay, keeps together well despite the impersonal approach to fiction.
    Remember Tomorrow - a very ambitious game packed into a small package, it grows out like a flower.
    Mars Colony - a very compelling topic, quick, successful in the rare 2-player genre.
    Perfect - like Kagematsu, a fine showing in classical Forge ways; compelling setting.
    Rustbelt - grim and understated postapocalyptical fantasy remindful of Dust Devils with somewhat elaborated mechanics.

    (I lifted that from my store's list of newest products, which does not always map to exact publication years. In other words, I can't be bothered to check whether each of those games really came out in 2011 or if that just happened to be when I finally got my hands on them.)

    My library/store hasn't been updated since late '11 due to my being otherwise occupied, so anything that came out last year is only on my radar by accident. I'll probably be asking this same question about recent games whenever I get around to updating the store, so feel free to direct me back to this thread then.

    \n I, on the other hand, suspect that most of the OSR lightning is and will be harnessed just fine by the actual OSR crowd, which means that there won't be a redundant wave of OSR-emulation in the story games scene. Of course influence will be taken, but it'll be low-profile for the most part. Vincent's LotFP project is mostly of interest to the OSR and won't have much impact here (it will have impact in the OSR, though - the influence will flow that way); when I write my own book (if I get around to it), it'll probably be much the same. Luke's project is maybe the best bet for a true high profile SG synthesis of OSR wisdom in the horizon at the moment.

    (I'll hedge my estimate by saying that three years from now we might get a wave of true hybrids. They'll be a lot like Luke's project. That won't be the next phenomenon in the fashion queue if it happens, though.)

    I could see some sort of trad game re-evaluation coming soon, along the lines Jason suggests. We've had signs of that sort of thing for years, as people attempt to revise their favourite '90s games and utilize their elaborate settings with modernized mechanics. I suspect, though, that the specific people and environment here are not the ideal background for this becoming more than individual flickering; I would rather look for something similar to the OSR, where the grognards who never were dissatisfied with the '90s grow to have sufficient distance from it to really start putting down the precepts of their gaming in fresh, uncompromising ways. This could happen within the story games scene, I don't deny, but I won't be surprised if it grows into a scene of its own. Perhaps at RPG.net, the place has been a staunch stronghold of traditional gaming basically forever.

    One reason for why I doubt a wave of WoD clones occurring in the SG scene is that we've lived with a low-level background WoD radiation for the entire last decade. Every year new attempts at creating indie games with trad systemic approach come along. The individual projects might be successful in their own purview, I don't know, but they sure seem to have about as much impact on the local scene today as they had a decade ago - that is, not much. If we were slowly coming around to a fashion for WoD, I would expect the reception of the likes of Stalker and Maschine Zeit to show it first.

    \n There's definitely some crossover there, but I don't see AW (or DW) as really OSR for a number of reasons:
    * characters are special / don't determine the protags by who survives
    * it's pretty hard to die / doesn't encourage extreme carefulness
    * no mapping/calling by players, less extreme uncertainty and peril
    * no meticulous counting of torches/spikes or other gear, no emphasis on exact measurements of distance
    * lack of clear emphasis on exploration and personal enrichment (i.e. characters have complex motivations)
    * in general, I think the principles they operate by are pretty different (obviously, you don't need all of these things to be "OSR" or "OSR-influenced," but the lack of most of them makes me think AW and DW are focused on something else)

    That said, they're both sandbox styles of play that encourage very specific types of planning by the GM and for many things not to be planned at all (like specific events). So yeah, there's some crossover too.

    \n Well, at the risk of calling a spade a spade, OSR=D&D, so by the strictest possible reading, no, AW isn't OSR.

    I think it does, however, include and foreground pretty much all the stated philosophical ideals and principles of the OSR that don't directly revolve around or result from a specific mechanic of some edition of Dungeons and Dragons, and does it in a way that is both easily referenced and implementable.

    Basically, I'm not sure to what extent it's productive to talk about "the OSR" without distinguishing how that's different from just talking about "D&D". Their approach to playing D&D seems to be what's fruitful here, and I think AW captures it, or at least captures what I find interesting about it.

    \n Tooting my own horn, but Vesna Thaw came out over the summer, it's been getting a pretty positive response, and I'm really happy with it.

    Innovation-wise, it's on-trend with the whole collaborative drawing thing, as the group all gets a say in which each other's robots look like. It uses drawing as part of set-up, and not really during the game, but it's definitely a fun thing to do and gets everyone engaged with the mood of the game, which is a little off-kilter, possibly gonzo, but definitely kit bashed and weird.

    I also liked The Quiet Year a lot.

    Oh! And I love The Final Girl (2011ish, I think), really hits a good sweet spot for me in terms of balancing genre emulation with structured freeform techniques and pick-up-and-playability. Also, it totally messes with character monogamy in a functional and productive way.
