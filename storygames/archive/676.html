Abductive reasoning: WTF?

TonyLB
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Play Advice

So, I mentioned this and somebody said, basically, "WTF?" I'll unpack, and if anyone wants to play in the unpacked conceptual clothing, that's cool.

Deductive Reasoning: A implies B. B implies C. Therefore A implies C.

Inductive Reasoning: Man, every freakin' time we see A we also see B. Every time! Y'know what? I'd bet you good money that A implies B.

Abductive Reasoning: A+B implies C. We want to prove C and we've only got A. Now we need to prove B! Because if we prove B we've got it! B is the key!

Okay, all of you philosophy degree types out there can totally snicker at me. I know this is such a total layman's distortion of the concepts that it's almost unrecognizable. I think it's neat anyway.

People don't think about Abductive Reasoning as being a thing on its own, because they can't track the process. It's mysterious to the point of being miraculous, sometimes.

Like, if I show you a half-completed jigsaw puzzle, and hand you a piece and say "Where does this fit?" I can imagine a step-by-step deductive process by which you'd answer that. You look at all the possible places it could fit, check your piece against each possibility, and you do it really, really quickly.

But then if I show you a half-completed jigsaw puzzle and a whole box full of pieces and say "Find something that fits," my experience is that you will find a piece which fits somewhere much faster than you would have found the right place for the individual piece. And when I ask you "How did you find that fitting piece" you'll say something like "Oh, I just looked and saw that it would fit."

The brain has this wierd, wacky ability to parse a vast amount of possibilities in parallel and give an answer to a question more quickly than it can parse a more limited number of possibilities one-by-one and give the answer to a question.

So where does this come up in RPGs? Since I started thinking to look for it I see it all. the. time.

Josh: There's no freakin' way we're getting past the guards on that castle wall. They're too tough.
Betty: We totally need some entrance they aren't guarding.
Josh: Like a sewer. If there were a sewer we'd be home free.
Betty: Right! There has to be a sewer. It's not like they're storing all their waste products inside the castle walls, right?
Josh: Exactly! We have to find that sewer!

I love that moment. "If there were an X then Y and Z would follow, so let's discover/create X." That's what I (at least) mean when I talk about abductive reasoning.

Funky, ain't it?

    I do like this line of discussion, but I can't restrain myself from making a small correction. Your description of abductive reasoning isn't bad, but that isn't the same as the phenomenon you are describing. Abductive reasoning is, essentially, deductive reasoning where one of the components is lacking in certitude. In other words, "All men are mortal, Socrates is a man, therefore Socrates is mortal" is deductive in most cases but would be abductive if we weren't sure Socrates is a man ("omg--Socarates could be an immortal, soul sucking vampire!").

    Now, what is getting discussed is a touch different.

    Hamsterprophet / Graham--Aristotle has some discussion of this (methinks in the Rhetoric stuff, but I may be wrong)--if you are fairly sure that given A and C, most of your audience will think B, you should only present them with A and C, because the effort they make to fill in the middle term gives them a sense of 'ownership' that makes it more likely they will take it at face value.

    Which is why I think it makes sense for a GM to 'steal' a player's rationale for why things are happening--the fact that the player came to it on their own, makes it feel more like the 'real thing' to them.

    Back to the question about finding solutions by creating new interim steps--there is some really neat psych stuff that puzzles over how we make these sorts of 'leaps.' One of the most interesting elements of it to me is the notion of 'experts'--what defines such an individual is not (just) there mastery of the material at hand (in this case, rules of the game), but the ability to make lateral moves (definitely in line with 'parallel' process talk, Tony). A few ideas as to what happens in a lateral move:

    1. The expert draws on a different set of rules and applies them experimentally to the domain they are actually working in--this seems to be related to metaphors and such (this is like this, maybe, also like this, we can do this...)

    2. The expert begins to vary the rules internally and sees what happens (ok, so what if A is really B, can I get to C then? no, well what if it is Z...)

    It seems like you can encourage this sort of behavior by encouraging players to think metaphorically--which, counter some strains of playing, probably encourages more metagame chat (at least metagame thinking)--sort of like "oh, this castle is totally like that prison in a movie I just saw--they broke in by pretending to be doctors...maybe we could act as wealthy merchants and..."

    Brainstorming, too, goes a long way--as a group activity, you distribute some of the 'processing' power to others. In other words, you are varying X to Y, but as soon as you say Y, Joan thinks D, which makes you think of E, and D and E get you F (which is what you wanted all along).

    Although, if you do too much of that, you may lose the rhetorical element--the players see so much of the 'guts' of the process that there final answer doesn't seem so 'wow.' (although I don't think that has to be the case)

    Isbo:

    Actually, the current research on expertise suggests that experts aren't any better than ordinary people at applying outside rules to the domain that they're familiar with. They're no better at transferring in other skills; it's just that they literally see the domain that they're expert at differently.

    The classic experiment was done with expert chess players. They could look at a chessboard mid-game and memorize the layout of the pieces far, far better than a novice player could. However, when the chess pieces were laid out randomly, the chess experts weren't any better at understanding what was going on than the novices. In other words, through this and other experiments, they figured out that chess experts see the board in chunks which have a different meaning to them than the individual piece locations that the novices see.

    Similarly, physics experts classify and understand problems differently than novices do - not because they are conducting mental experiments, but because they understand better what kinds of similarities, patterns and activities really matter in being able to solve the problem.

    Expertise research is fascinating stuff! I don't know if this is what you were trying to say, but I thought it might be a useful addition to the discussion. It's also making me want to look at what "story" and "game" experts are better at than novices in both areas!!

    Oh, sorry, Larry, thread foul ; )

    Let me see if I can state my original point clearly:

    Abduction occurs when you posit a possible term in a deductive frame that allows you to draw conclusions of a tentative nature--because (at least) one of your antecedents is not full-proof, the resulting deduction isn't either. I am that philosophic nitpicker Tony mentions in the abstract ; )

    Now, what I think the abduction talk blurs into is the question of roleplaying a story where it isn't simply the case that you have a (probable) set of antecedents and derive a likely conclusion, but where you have an end result for which you then create the antecedents.

    I introduced the expertise talk largely as an analogy--as someplace you could rip some ideas off for creating an environment that encourages this creation of antecedents where there simply weren't any before.

    I think there are some real structural similarities between abduction and this creation of the antecedents, but I fret about collapsing them. The link to the Forge article highlights one reason why I dislike fusing them--abduction in a mystery occurs when the antecedent posited as possible ends up being the 'actual' antecedent (here we get to an Ockham's razor situation--abduce multiple antecedents until one is capable of supporting the conclusion and the evidence--i.e. until you reach an antecedent that can be supported by both the deductive and inductive dimensions).

    Confusing the two would actually spoil its application in mystery, where the fun is discovering whatis the case, not making things match what you want the case to be. *

    The creative version, on the other hand, takes a posited case and *makes* it the case--i.e. makes the deductive and inductive dimensions fall in line with it. In other words, it bypasses the question of probability and makes it the actuality.

    *Although, clearly, a game could exist where determining the mystery would be part of the game, in which case the two get a good deal closer (but I don't think they fuse--but ignore that, it gets us away from the 'core')
