\n When I run AW games now, I use a variation of Marvel Heroic's Action Order.


We:
1. Write down every character's name down on an index card.
2. Place a poker chip on each card.
3. GM picks a PC to go first.
4. The PC acts, removes their poker chip, then chooses who goes next.
5. Everyone has to act before anyone can act twice.
6. Once everyone act, we place the poker chips back on the index cards.
7. Then the last person who acted, chooses who goes first.

Variations:
- The GM may interrupt a PC's turn. That PC marks one XP.
- A PC may pass their turn. That means when poker chips refresh, they have two and can act twice later.

Note:
- When a PC acts, they don't just roll one Move or describe one action.
- Turns are fluid and may involve multiple Moves.
- Generally we try to limit a PC's turn to 3-5 minutes.
- NPCs don't take turns the way PCs do and don't roll dice. The GM says what the NPC tries to do and asks the PC what they do in response (if anything).
- Help / Hinder rolls can be used on anyone's turn.
- We use these rules for everything, not just combat.
- Everyone participates in the Action Order. You don't all have to be in the same physical time or place.

It's fun and makes GMing even easier.

    \n What's lovely about this is how easy you can make it. It could be as simple as telling one player to go first, then getting them to nominate someone else, and so on until everyone has had a turn.

    That move is great, in itself, because it democratises the process. It saves the GM selecting who goes next.

    And then you can add the other stuff on top if you want to.

    \n The one thing I've noticed... it sometimes makes it all too obvious, especially at a convention (but also at home), who the players are the least interested in.

    So if there is one person at the table who the rest of the group isn't as enthused by, it can come out in this process.

    In most cases so far, this has been useful information for me. But it can also feel a little weird. The process isn't creating the problem, it's just underlining problems that already exist.

    \n Good questions.

    What I've done in those cases is play it loose based on context.

    If two people are together, sharing equal spotlight, I'll ask have them to both remove a poker chip from their cards. But if one person is the lead, and the other PCs are playing small support roles, I won't remove the support players poker chips.

    This is why I have people remove chips after their turn, not before.

    That all said, if the group is good at managing their own spotlight naturally, I won't use this system. Sometimes I'll only pull it out if things seem to be getting out of hand.
