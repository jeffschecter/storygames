\n A few people asked me about hacking old school video game art, when I mentioned I was just getting into this. Let me first say, while I've been on the outskirts of several aspects of the retro gaming scene -- mostly through programming music with Little Sound DJ and Korg DS-10 -- that I'm not an expert, have very little experience, and have not really participated in any community aspects like blogs or forums. That said, here's what I've learned.

Making 8 or 16-bit video game art is easy. Making stuff that's actually good takes talent and a LOT of time pushing pixels around. Check out this image from the back cover of Scott Pilgrim 5. That's a fair bit of work right there.

One of the easiest ways to begin is by hacking existing art ripped from your favorite video games. You'll probably want to start with 2-D side-scrolling or isometric (like Zelda: Link to the Past) games, because the false 3-D stuff like Starfox, Super Mario Kart, Donkey Kong Country, and Super Mario RPG is another level of difficult.

Programs you'll want to use include Photoshop (if you have it) of any edition, Pixen, or even MS Paint if you have to. On my old Windows machine I have Photoshop 5.0 LE, which is old as all fuck, but perfect for this kind of low-res stuff. On my Mac I've been messing with Pixen and it's really great for this except runs a bit slow when messing with many layers or larger images.

Games that are good for basic hacking include stuff made for the original Nintendo, Gameboy, Super Nintendo, Gameboy Color, or Gameboy Advance. You could also do Sega stuff, but I know zilch about that since I was always a Nintendo junkie.

Good places to look for art to hack include The Video Game Atlas (for maps) and the Spriter's Resource for general sprites.

Important Things to Think About

8 and 16 bit video game art is typically created by combining tiles of 8x8, 16x16, or 32x32 pixels in various combinations. This is pretty easy to see in most games. These are the basic components and each tile has to look great by itself as well as in combination with other tiles. Mastering good tile design is at least 60% of the challenge here.

The other 40%, as far as I can tell is something that's slightly less intuitive to think about when you're not actually designing for a video game: screen layout. Let me use a Zelda example to show what I mean. When you play Zelda, the screen doesn't shift across the landscape as you move but only changes to a new location when Link exits the current "screen" by walking off to the side or exiting through a doorway. The original Gameboy screen was 160 x 144 pixels, but then you have to have a status bar across the bottom showing Link's life and current weapons. This means that the entire world map of Link's Awakening is divided into 160 x 128 pixel screens, each of which is always viewed independently by the player, who never sees them connected together. I've marked them on this sample to show what I mean:



Even games where the camera follows the movement of the protagonist are limited in what they can show a player. You still are looking at one screen worth of image at a time, so each and every screen's worth better have something interesting and aesthetically pleasing going on in it. Consequently, old school video game maps and images are hyper-dense with content, implications, and meaning. But this is also helpful to would-be hackers and designers because it means you can design in pieces, one "screen" at a time even if you ultimately plan on producing something huge. After all, each screen of Link's Awakening is essentially a 10 x 8 grid tiles that are each 16 x 16 pixels. Looking at it that way, things look much more manageable.

Anyway, there's a start. I welcome questions of all kinds and may post some great tricks I've found working with Pixen if anybody else is interested. Also, if anybody else has been doing this longer than me (i.e. more than 3 days) and has some great suggestions, I'd love to hear them.

    Some Thoughts on Tile Design

    At Eero's request, here's an example from a different Zelda game, The Minish Cap, which may be the last of the isometric games since the new ones for the DX (Phantom Hourglass and the upcoming Spirit Tracks) are in 3-D. I've never actually played Minish Cap but I've been blown away by the design and especially the color scheme of the images I've seen.



    If you look carefully at the tiles here, you'll see that there are essentially three kinds of tiles:

    1. Tiles that create a certain kind of background terrain (water, grass, etc.)
    2. Tiles that transition between different kinds of background terrain (water to grass, etc.)
    3. Tiles that show objects or characters that sit on top of the terrain (rocks, trees, houses, ladders, etc.)

    On any given screen, these three types of tiles are present in roughly equal proportion, which is important to giving each screen that dense feeling I mentioned earlier. You'll notice that type 2 transition tiles are not present in the Link's Awakening example I showed earlier. Each tile cuts immediately to a different type of terrain. My sense is that transition tiles are a product of the upgrade from 8 to 16-bit graphics and are very important to the feeling of Super Nintendo or Gameboy Advance graphics.

    You'll also notice that the color scheme of type 1 tiles is very simple, usually two or three closely related colors that denote a specific type of terrain. For water, you might have a medium blue base and then a darker blue and lighter blue to show ripples or edges.

    Type 2 tiles, since they transition from one type of terrain to another, combine two different color schemes and gradually shift between them. Still, you're not going to see more than six colors on a tile and they'll usually be divided between two distinct, related color schemes.

    Type 3 object tiles are very much like type 2 transitional tiles because an object sits on the terrain and usually has to be incorporated into it around the edges of the object tiles. Consequently, you'll see different object types that are always mixed with certain types of terrain. The rocks on land and the rocks in the water are different tiles showing different kinds of rocks.

    Finally, I want to point out one thing that blows my mind. Check out the cliff face in the lower right part of the example. Notice how the cliff part that sits over the water has a slightly different coloration than the cliff that sits over the grass? There are tiles in The Minish Cap that show slight lighting effects such as light reflecting off the water making the cliff face slightly blue. That's nuts but also adds to the feel of the graphics immensely. There are also at least two different cliff face tiles for different colors of water. This is the one for light blue, shallow water, but there's another one for the darker water at the top of the example.

    \nA lot of advice depends on the resolution you're working from.
    \nLower resolution (8 or 16 bit era), obviously you have to do a lot more with less, and keep everything precise. I worked things out with 'bounding boxes', like, boxy outlines around hands, torso and head, etc, kinda like an artist's mannequin. Then you fill in from there. On sprites, use black lines and contrasting flat colours to make things recognisable, and only then use texture, shade and highlights within that. There's plenty of tricks to make things interesting here, but mostly I'd just copy from the masters; the good thing about pixel art is that you can always see EXACTLY how something is done. Try and keep all lines at 45 or 90 degrees; if you deviate from that then you are drawing a new line.
    \n
    \nAt higher resolutions, you can pretty much forget all that. Just draw/paint/whatever and scale it to fit and correct anything that obviously doesn't look right.
