[Burning Sands: Jihad] Is it too much to ask to play a terrorist?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nSam! \n\n\n
\n
\n\nNovember 2009 \n edited November 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I want to play Burning Sands: Jihad (PDF), but I can see why others don't. It's got nothing to do with the Burning Wheel-based gameplay \u2014 I've never even touched one of the books, let alone played with it \u2014 and it's not because it's based around Dune. No, it's because the primary thrust of the game has you, the player, taking on the role of a religious fanatic and terrorist.

I won't go into the whole setting and concept behind Jihad except to say that it's Dune with a very light sprinkle of gaming dust on top. Those of you who've read the first two books know exactly what Jihad is all about: conquering the galaxy in the name of a living messiah. Those who won't convert go into exile. Those who won't consent to exile are killed. It's just that simple.

The game doesn't pussyfoot around this, either. On p. 10 the game defines three stages of the Jihad. Any planet on which the Jihad takes place will first be the target of an underground movement to create a logistical platform from which terrorist attacks can ultimately take place, followed by a full-scale uprising and invasion. The word terrorism is explicitly invoked.

Some effort is made to allow for playing the establishment side of things, but the meat of the game really is the Jihad and those who carry it out. Which means that there's a high likelihood that anyone playing this game is going to be playing a terrorist, committing acts of terrorism and feeling good about it from a character perspective.

From my point of view this is what challenging roleplaying should be all about. In such a gaming environment we're free to occupy all manner of roles and there's nothing to say that these roles have to be traditionally heroic or even heroic at all. But is there a line some or even most might not be willing to cross? To use Jihad as an example: is playing an unrepentant terrorist too much to ask?

    Posted By: Sam HSome effort is made to allow for playing the establishment side of things, but the meat of the game really is the Jihad and those who carry it out. Which means that there's a high likelihood that anyone playing this game is going to be playing a terrorist, committing acts of terrorism and feeling good about it from a character perspective.
    \nAs Luke noted, quite a few people also play it from the Noble side of things. They often engage in media takeovers, establish concentration camps, torture, hold mass executions, and all sorts of other wonderful stuff.

    BSJ is not a teaching game. We're not trying to promote the idea that one side is better than the other. I think we succeeded in making both sides of the struggle reprehensible. As an observer, my sympathy is with the people of these fictional worlds who get used and abused by both sides in their ideological struggle.

    I do like to think that people who play it often think about their values though. How far will you go to promote your cause or defend you way of life? What price are you willing to pay in human lives and misery? I expect your characters' answers will be very different than your answers. It's not unusual for players to shudder a bit when their characters take an action.

    In the end, though, the primary focus is a fun game. If you can read Dune and enjoy it even as Paul's fanatics spread their message across the galaxy at the edge of a sword, then you can play Jihad and enjoy it.

    \nOkay, so maybe the thread was posed in a controversial way. That's my fault, but it does come from the reaction I was getting from people I approached with the idea of playing the setting.     I've been very gratified by the discussion here because at the very least it points up all the reasons to play the game, whether it be on the side of fascism or fundamentalism. It's far too easy to fall into a rut when roleplaying unless the group or the game they choose directly challenges base assumptions.

Like it or not, the word terrorism is packed with all sorts of meaning, most of it negative, and it'd take some doing to unpack that enough to really dig into the whole "one man's freedom fighter" thing.

      \nI disagree. The World Burner creates setting and situation, but it also creates hard mechanical limitations, like the populace's Body of Argument. That's entirely about the conflict between the jihadis and the nobility.

      Moreover, the Propaganda War macro mechanic ensures that the Jihadis and the Nobility will maneuver against each other.

      Hard resource limitations, like the Jihadis lack of access to Propaganda Workshops early in the campaign, mean the Jihadis have to find other ways to reach the hearts and minds of the people.

      Finally, the structure of the nobility's Ancestry emotional attribute and the Jihadis' Fanaticism emotional attribute means that nobility players will write beliefs about their birthright, heritage or ancestry, and Jihadi players will write beliefs about the jihad or their mission. Both sides will angle play toward those beliefs, both for the artha awards and for the opportunity to use the special properties of their respective emotional attributes.
