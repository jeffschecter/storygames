\nMy game is finished. I just need to write it down. It's a fast paced pulp action game that takes place in an alternative 1930, where Americans seek adventures in foreign continents. One important thing in the game is that the "GM" and the players collaborate when they play. The players and the GM creates a P.R.E.P. which consist of things that will occur in the adventure and then the GM creates scenes where the players encounter the things made up in the P.R.E.P. The thing is that it's important that the players don't think it's the players against the GM. They should all collaborate and spin off from each others descriptions. Therefore I don't want to use "game master", because that can add to the feeling "we against the GM".

1. So what should I call the GM in my pulp game where the GM is the one who a) explains the rules and b) creates troubles for the characters? I considered "troublemaker" at first, but that word can bring the same feeling as "game master". I want to give the impression that the GM and the players are at the same level. My thoughts have been around making up a word that means something like "the one who is feeding the fire with wood", but can't find a word for it. I would prefer something short and catchy. Anyone with a suggestion? It doesn't have to be about the fire but it should in some sense tell the reader about the GM's duty in this game.

2. I don't want to launch my game as a roleplaying game, because if I do, then I have to fight against roleplaying gamer's assumptions of what a roleplaying game should be like. I did that with another game and I learned my lesson. I also want to reach out to non-gamers with my game, and "roleplaying games" is usually meaning "World of Warcraft" or something similar to them. Like I said, I don't want to fight assumptions. It's a game that you can bring out one evening, start playing directly, and it will take at most 1,5 hours to play. I want the game experience to be something social, like playing cards together, except that you share an adventure together in a fictive world. So any ideas of what I should call this?

    "What should I call the GM?" is a question best answered by "GM". A gimmicky name is just cognitive friction unless the role is truly unique.
    \nI would normally agree, just as I would agree that people should call attributes attributes and skills skills.

    But like I said in my first post, I don't like the "we vs the GM" feeling that the term may bring. The word "master" may also imply that that player is more important*. My game is all about planting thoughts into the reader's mind and the game is about collaborate storytelling where one player has a different role. I had other players GM my game for me, and it usually ended in "me against the players". So I really want to minimize any kind of element in an early state that may give that notion, no matter how small they are.

    So it's not really the role of that player that it's unique, it's more about all the parts of the game striving for the same thought - collaborate storytelling where all the players are equally important.

    * That's why "director" may be a bad term. I want something more like the banker in Monopoly. No one thinks of that player as more important, just that it's a person with more obligations.

    \n JM: I had to think about other games for a second that actually had a banker. Now you've given me flashbacks to Life, a game I loathe. Thanks Jason. Thanks a lot.

    Rickard: I like your idea about not giving a specific name, or at least not making a big deal out of it if you do. Lots of the terms you're considering are ones that I've considered myself for stuff I've puttered around with, and I've had the same concerns about things like Director that you express upthread.

    One possbility that I'd considered that might help to get around it: write your text to the person who is going to first encounter it and who will likely be teaching the game to their friends. Go with an assumption that said friends will never actually pick up and read the text.

    In my experience, that's how games are taught/learned/played anyway.

    Writing from that perspective, the text becomes a manual for teaching the game and leading by example. Instead of settling on one term, use all of them where and when they are appropriate. Hell, use inappropriate capitalization or bold print as necessary to get the idea across. Let the people reading the text decide for themselves whether you intend the terms to be official terms or are simply using the emphasized words to catch their attention.

It really didn't tell me how to play one of these wonderful, giant, multiplayer games that the author was known for putting on at conventions.

    I went to the related yahoo group and started posting, asking what probably seemed like some very stupid newbie questions. It took a while, but eventually I got some answers that clarified exactly what sorts of things were going on in these games.

    As it turned out, it only had a minimal interaction with the rulebook. That stuff I'd paid money for was deeply secondary to the important answers I was getting from the forum responses.

    I can't really blame the author though. He was selling the part his audience expected to buy, even though it wasn't actually the important part for someone like me.

    Rickard: Don't let anybody give you shit about having a game, but not having it written up yet.
    \nHaha. I don't mind. It's actually how I design games nowadays. For my first roleplaying game, I wrote everything down, playtested it and rewrote the text. It took me more than 300 (re)written pages to get a game consisting of 112 pages. Such a waste of time! So when I got into my next project, I playtested everything, built out the game as I continued. When I finally wrote it down, it took me two weeks to produce fifty pages that were both proofread and put in a descent layout.

    So for This Is Pulp, I started out with four tables and twelve descriptions of archetypes. After ten playtests, I did a character sheet. Now, after more than twenty playtests with all kinds of people, the game is finished. I know what I should put in my game, because of my research (the playtests). I just have to play around with how I should write it.
