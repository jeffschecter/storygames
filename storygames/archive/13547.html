\n [narrator] this is a continuation of the categorical discussion near the end of "catering to multiple tastes" thread, we join our hero's as they discuss the problems roleplaying games face because they lack taste, or rather they lack a solid method to discern and discuss tastes and usefulness like, player age, number, and play time) [/narrator]


i can see two problems holding back role playing games

1. Label controversy

2. Universalism, system neutral, \u201cthey don\u2019t need books\u201d, anti-structure.

The first is simple. If not many people agree or understand what is meant by \u201ctraditional rpg\u201d then the label will not be easily used, the same goes for labels like \u201cstory games\u201d.

In board games we have some helpful categories, there are plenty of games that blur the lines but still having these words helps people, these are things like \u201ceuro game\u201d \u201cameritrash\u201d \u201cabstract strategy\u201d \u201cdexterity\u201d \u201cparty\u201d. Each category of game serves very different masters and appeals to a unique psychology.

I'm not sure what over arching labels could be applied to role playing games, clearly there are some great unique tastes out there but I don\u2019t have knowledge to divine them nor a command of language to label them. To me RPG and story game are not quite working, I see a big difference in a game like Ocean compared to Apocalypse world.

As for the second problem, \u201cuniversalism, system neutral, \u201cthey don\u2019t need books\u201d, anti-structure\u201d. I think it's important to focus on how many RPG's largely do not qualify as games, most are not \u201ccomplete systems\u201d meaning they do not have rules that take you from start to finish.

Incomplete objects are hard to categorize and understand, you can not put AGE, PLAYERS, and TIME on the back of a dnd book because all dnd will give you is advice on how long a sessions should last, how many players are maybe a good idea.

    \n Well, you can take it too far, at least with the "incompleteness" of RPGs. One of the defining features of RPGs is the potentially infinite number of inputs at any given moment. You can follow absolutely all of the directions in Primetime Adventures and still have a problem if one person is doing a comedy and everyone else is doing a drama, or if one person is doing a pun-and-fourth-wall-breaking comedy and everyone else is doing a character-driven sitcom. The amount of customization of play experience that each player is capable of in every moment of play is stupendous. What "generic" games try to do is shift much of that customization to the preparation phase of play. (GURPS character creation is gigantic for a reason.) But the same amount of customization takes place in every RPG.

    Posted By: TeataineCan we make such labels universal and useful? Create a taxonomy. Is it even a good thing to do?
    \nPeople have often said that it is a good thing to do, arguing that it would make it easier to discuss games, to recognize games that have elements that appeal to particular people, and so on. I don't see any reason to doubt them. And given that, I think you could indeed make those labels useful.

    But can you make them universal? I'm honestly less sure about that one; I definitely don't think you can get there from the usual internet forum "top-down" approach, where some dude splits every game under the sun into two very very very broad categories (which curiously always seems to be some not-very-well-hidden variation on "the cool things I like" versus "the stupid things other people like"). And once that's happened, the usual heated arguments break out as people dispute which side of that imaginary line most games belong on, because (surprise surprise) hardly anyone can agree on such enormous and ill-defined categories.

    But I think maybe you can build up to being universal, by making very small, limited, highly-focused categories wherever it's easy to do so, and getting those established and accepted near-universally. You know, making little islands of games that most people generally accept "belong together," floating in the big gray sea of every other game on the planet. Then once you've got that dry land to stand on, it might be easier to divvy up the less-easily-classified games, since you'll be able to make better analogies and explain your opinions better (as in "this game is a lot like a Foobar game, but the way it handles GM authority is more like a Bargle game").
