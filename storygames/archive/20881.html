\n I recently stumbled across these two articles, by Will Hindmarch. He discusses the techniques of railroading, variations, and how they might be unrightfully maligned. (Very reminiscent of conversations we had not too ago here on Story Games - his position seems similar to @AsIf's in those earlier threads. He will have to chime to say if that's true or not, of course!)

The Illusionist's Lament

Railroading for the Best in RPG Play

It probably won't come as a huge surprise to anyone who read my posts in those earlier discussions that I disagree quite strongly with the general rhetoric here. In any case, though, the articles are thought-provoking and an interesting read. I like Hindmarch's take on "immersion" very much, for instance.

    \nIf you comment here, please keep it civil - these discussions have a history of getting heated. We're all friends here, right?

    Here's my take:

    Why do I disagree with Hindmarch?

    Point 1: All such discussions are so rife with metaphor and miscommunication that I think they must be paired with examples of play. It's easy to pontificate about the nature of deception, but, you know, what techniques does that actually translate to at the table? What's the practical angle here; how do you actually play?

    Point 2: I think most discussions of "railroading" and similar concepts (perhaps even ALL of them!) are easily resolved by realizing two things, and I find articles like these ignore both:

    a) Having some kind of conception of the social reality of roleplaying (as opposed to looking at whether a wall makes it impossible for Ben's character to reach the fountain - that, sure, could be used in a million different ways, good and bad).

    Instead: what's happening between the people at the table? Also, have the right expectations been communicated, and are they continuing to be communicated, when unclear?

    b) Having some kind of conception of Creative Agenda, or a game's object, or its unspoken promises (whatever you want to call this, if you're allergic to the term "Creative Agenda"). This cuts through "ambiguous" cases almost instantly.

    Instead: are these decisions being made right relevant to the goals of our game? Are the right people getting to make them?

    \n >>The unfair practice associated with illusionism isn\u2019t deception\u200a\u2014\u200athat can be a legit part of narrative\u200a\u2014\u200ait\u2019s dishonesty... Dishonesty is about misrepresenting the choices that matter, and it includes the GM lying to herself about her own willingness to incorporate the players\u2019 inputs into the unfolding fiction.<<

    This I agree with strongly. It's why in combat-heavy games I find GM fudging to keep PCs alive so frustrating. If combat is the only thing (or nearly so) of consequence, then why would you render null failure in that arena? Of course, there is a lot more to say here, about setting stakes beyond death, setting genre expectations properly, etc., but fundamentally it comes down to that: if death is on the line, then it needs to *actually* be on the line.

    >>If we trust the GM\u200a\u2014\u200aand if we ask the GM to be the player who invests time and money in books, maps, and prep\u2014 and then vilify the responsibility we give her, we players become complicit adversaries.<<

    This I definitely agree with. I can't tell you how many con or one-shot games I've been involved with where the GM had a clearly prepped premise, maybe railroady or maybe more of a sandbox-y dungeon-y, thing, but clearly one thing. But some asshole player in the game insisted on dicking around in town or asking the module hook NPC endless questions. The game could have been perfectly fine if we'd just skipped the preliminaries (and of course a more skilled or confident GM could have stopped the disruptive behavior in various ways), but the player was acting like even agreeing to go along with the basic premise of the adventure would be acceding to railroading, so they had to perform this elaborate kabuki of deeply angsting about what their character would do.

    Speaking of which, the immersion stuff is also great.

    >>If not, falling back on narrative tricks\u200a\u2014\u200aincluding illusionism\u200a\u2014\u200ato make the most of the moment is preferable sometimes to a joyless exercise in RPG theory.<<

    I have no idea what he's talking about here. That is, I can parse the meaning, but I can't think of an actual example of what this looks like.

    >> I keep the tools of illusionism in my kit because I play with a wide variety of players in a wide variety of game styles. It hasn\u2019t been my experience that everyone wants equal authorship all the time or that everyone will come around to a single style of play if only they saw things my way. <<

    This is pure conflation / false dichotomy. You can have a game with a strong central GM, who has authority over most aspects of the game world outside of the PCs, who nevertheless doesn't use illusionism.

    In the end, this a very poetic, beautifully-written article, but I'm not sure it contains any actionable take-aways. I also have no idea what Hindmarch means by Illusionism, and I don't just mean this in the usual "illusionism versus participationism" jargon wars way, either.

    The most egregious example is this:
    >>...falling back on narrative tricks\u200a\u2014\u200aincluding illusionism\u200a\u2014\u200ato make the most of the moment is preferable sometimes to a joyless exercise in RPG theory.<<

    What... what does this mean? What would this look like in actual practice?

    \n Speaking of a wide variety of players and a wide variety of playstyles, my own experience is that rpgs became about a thousand times easier for me once I started to work with agenda statements and explicit creative contracts (also known as "rules") instead of trying to lead my co-players by the nose. There may be reason for illusionism, but my experience does not support the notion that you'd want it to save time, get along with strangers and to increase reliability of the game. It hasn't done any of those three things for me.

    I mean, it's just about the simplest thing in the world to just tell the other players what you want them to do, and how you want them to participate. It seems to be straight up cultural bias (or brain damage, as one might wish to term it) that makes this difficult for roleplayers. I would be the first to admit that it's not in the traditional tool-set, but anybody struggling with these things should at least try it: the next time you have a player acting in a counter-productive fashion, instead of trying to mislead them into sticking to your plot (or whatever it is that you'd use illusionism for), try asking them why they're acting that way, and tell them what kind of action you're expecting of them. Sure, it breaks the precious illusion of freedom (forcing you to admit that you were promising things you couldn't deliver - painful, but honest), but in exchange you get actually cooperative players. I would not be surprised if this was a positive trade-off for at least some illusionists.

    \n Oh, yeah, that reminds me of the other thing I wanted to talk about here. Something that never really comes up in these discussions, but probably should, especially in the case of explicit comparisons to illusionist stagecraft: the personal charisma of the GM.

    I've known GMs who simply had a raw force of magnetism that's actually quite incredible. Not all these GMs engage in dishonesty, either, but the point is that they can easily get away with it if they want to. Their players are simply much more willing to, as Will puts it, suspend disbelief, than players of other GMs. So those GMs simply don't *need* as much in the way of careful communication practices, accurate applications of mechanics, flag-based plotting, or any of a host of other techniques developed in the Forge and post-Forge eras.

    Think of it like Sorcerers vs. Wizards in D&D, or musicians who play by ear versus those who learn from sheet music.

    \n Ah. Full titles:

    The Illusionist\u2019s Lament: Dramaturgy and Illusionism for the Best in RPG Play

    Sword-Fighting on a Roller-Coaster: Railroading for the Best in RPG Play

    That does sound a little more specific.

    It all sounds like the kinda stuff that, as Matt said, needs concrete "how to"s in order to be more than just philosophical ranting, though.

    I enjoyed Always/Never/Now's take on module design! It gave the players choices about what to pursue next while still keeping the overall arc aimed a prepared main plot. Neither linear nor "do *anything* you want". Our GM showed us exactly how it worked, which set expectations nicely, which was probably important, lest we'd developed a "can do anything" idea only to see it crash. At the same time, though: no illusions.

    Also, no guarantee that the next step in the plot would mean much to the players at the time, because getting there was somewhat independent of their primary motives and interests at that moment.

    I had actually planned for Delve to work similarly at one point -- the options out there are what they are, and the players encounter them in whatever order they ignorantly choose to -- but I wound up abandoning that. Fully-informed choice, or tailoring what's there to what they're currently interested in (which I guess counts as illusionism in sandbox play?), was way more fun.

    Matt and I actually collaborated on something last year which was similar to A/N/N (choose which module to hit next, they all add up) but better, IMO, because each adventure contributed to the characters' toolkit and a known objective (pieces of a magic weapon) in obvious and tangible ways.
