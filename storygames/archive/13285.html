\n ok, i'm from italy and i was at the lates Lucca Comics and Games convention. i had the chance to play a demo of the awesome Shock: with its equally awesome author, mr. Joshua Newman, and i bought the newly released italian edition. i showed my copy to a friend i met after the demo, and, totally excited, i explained him how the game worked. He's an historian, specialised in modern history. he looked very interested in the structure of the game, and, at one moment, asked me:

"what about making it in the past? we could choose some past "shock", and play it out... something like, i don't know... Movable type"

I'm actually perplexed. this looks Nothing like Science Fiction, obviously, but it totally looks like Shock: , or, at least, like parts of it. the amazing beauty of the pitch session in the beginning gets quite limited, i guess, because you do have a solid basis already. but the system could actually work. (note: we were thinking about starting form a historically defined situation, but we're not ruling out uchronical evolution: actually, my friend seemed interested in that)

What do you think about it? has it been tried already? is there something i didn't already get of the game (hey, i had it for less than 48 hours!!!) that rules this kind of thing out? is mr. Newman anywhere roudn here, and willing to give his opinion?

    \n I've run steampunk with it. That's super easy. You pick Victorian-era social issues, and some steampunk-y Shocks.

    Having played Shock: many times (over 20, for sure), I can safely say that it can do history in just the manner that your friend suggests. I don't think that it's that constraining, either. Let's say we set it during the industrial revolution. And given the number of people playing, we decided to go for 2 Issues and 2 Shocks.

    We could have some VERY different grids emerging. Labour as Power / Colonialism / Factories / Trains is a very, very different game from Charlatanism / Prudery / Photography / The Telegraph.

    One thing you could do, if your goal was to play to experience a real historical era, is to change how minutiae works: instead of inventing a fact to grant the bonus/detraction, you cite a real fact of the era.

    \nWell, you could always go the Half-Historical Hybrid Mutant route. As such:

    1. Issues are already era-transcending. They are issues that, if not universal, are at least repeated throughout history and into the future.
    2. Nothing's stopping you from listing "the printing press" as a Shock, alongside "Alien Overlords" as another Shock.

    So, go ahead and pick Shocks as you like, regardless of whether or not they're Shocks that our world has already dealt with. And then you'll have this half-historical monster setting, and that will be fun in its own regard. There'll probably be a lot of exclamations like, "Yeah, just like napoleonic era ships!" and "Yes, except, captained by Ferangi, of course!"

    I am so fucking tired, but I had such a wonderful time.

    \n

    Albi, the definition of a Shock is that it's a radical difference between our world as players and the world of the fiction. Playing in the past at a singularity moment is totally, 100% valid.

    \n

    We played a game we called Rock: where the Shock was that the women of our tribe had figured out that sex was connected to reproduction. It was really neat. (They weren't telling the 'men' about it \u2014 they were mostly 16 years old and their primary function was that a dozen of them would stalk and kill a sea lion and ten of them would bring it back. They weren't the kinds of people you'd want thinking they were important.)
