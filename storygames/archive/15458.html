\n I'm doing graphic design for Tim Rodriguez (ogreteeth) and his excellent game Ghost Pirates (4 days left on Kickstarter).

I've been given a number of drawn elements (background, title plate, journal, main object) and I'm assembling them into layouts and trying to get them to play nice together. The priorities here:
1) main object (here, a dagger) needs to pop, boom, right away
2) card type needs to be obvious (in this case, a Treasure Card, as indicated by the title plate, background, and gold coins at edges)
3) rules need to be easy to read for a player with the card on the table right in front of them
4) overall game aesthetic is a combo of (a) old-timey and (b) fun & exciting

How do you all think these are working along those lines?





Other thoughts also welcome.

The game itself is a quick-to-learn 2-player tactical game. If this looks like it might be your kinda thing, please swing by Kickstarter to see more. It's over 80% funded, but time is short, so it could go either way at this point.

    \n Dave,

    As John mentions above, scale (and resolution) are going to dictate a lot of what you need and can get away with.

    There are a couple of things in the current layout that need attention. Primarily, everything has the same weight, so the eye doesn't know where to go. That brown background has an overwhelming range of contrast in comparison to the other visuals. Is that wood table? Brown sky? Drop the contrast on it; maybe try blurring it. Your key element, the Dagger, doesn't seem to have a white. In fact it looks like nothing has a white. The gold coins are also sort of the same value as the "white" fields for the text blocks. If you drop the value on the brown and on the coins you'll have better read for the text and image. love them or hate them, drop shadows might be your friend here, but sparingly.

    Secondly, type. the decorative typeface works well for the field at top but is mostly not readable below. The sheaf of papers strewn under the type could be extended across the width of the bottom of the card which would give you more room. I would try a second typeface for the rules. Specifically a sans-serif that was designed to be read at a small point size.

    Most importantly, it looks like a fun project. Have fun with it. Oh, and move back to New York soon.

    Are the above backgrounds standard to each card or card type?

    There are several online color scheme generators to help choose complimentary and contrasting colors to differentiate background and foreground elements:

    - http://colorschemedesigner.com/
    - http://www.colorschemer.com/online.html
    - http://www.colorsontheweb.com/colorwizard.asp
    - http://kuler.adobe.com/#create/fromacolor

    I like colorschemedesigner because it gives you the option to view your color schemes from the perspective of different forms of color blindness (click Colorblind on the top right).

    But kuler.adobe lets you choose color schemes from sampling images.

    Some of these tools only give you hex or RGB color values but you can use Photoshop to convert to CMYK. Just make sure to look out for any changes you may have to readjust after you convert.

    I've been unsure how much tweaking/dissolving/destroying the artist's background was kosher, but it sounds like Tim's down, so, sweet. I hated the way it was fighting the other card elements too.     Posted By: David Berg
    Darkening is less good; I had some earlier demos that were deemed too gloomy.

    Eric, great call on thewhites. I actually quite like yours; what technique did you use for that?

    As forbottom text, more real estate is fine, larger type is fine, more tracking/leading is fine... but I'd like to avoid cold sans-serifs if at all possible. ...I think that overly large text gives a kid-friendly, cartoony look to cards, and Tim has told me he'd rather not have that. Readability is paramount, but beyond that, I'd rather keep it on the small side.

    As for readability, my experience with business cards has been that anything that's borderline too small to read on a screen has been easily readable when printed. Accordingly, my original demos above would read just fine in print. Alas, my printer is crap and useless for testing this, so I can't actually say for sure in this case.
    \n-fwiw, i like Harpers' background and darkened borders. I get the not liking dark. A mixture of desaturation and adding some sort of noise effect should create enough separation between the main elements and the background.

    - re whites: what technique? just contrast and dotting the metal elements with a soft-edged brush in PS.

    - i think i used Helvetica because it was there. Harper's is more friendly (Gotham?). It's an eternal debate between flavor and readability and legibility. There are a bunch of humanist sans faces that aren't cold like the 60s/70s era faces (again Harper.) But the more specialized you get into that the more expensive your budget. Fancy type faces are generally designed as Display Fonts which means they aren't meant to be reproduced as body text. I don't know that i'd try to match anything specifically to your display type; but if you want to go with a serif typeface for the info; maybe marry it to the body copy used in the rules book? Or pick something mundane that won't draw attention to itself: Times, New Century etc. (cheap, readable and somewhat invisible.)

    - designers like small type. humans hate small type. for the cards, i wouldn't recommend going below 11pt on anything. Of course, this will vary some based on the nature of the type\u2013but you're going to want to be able to read it at arm's length or exposed in public space. Your printer may not be able to get great blacks and/or the line screen might not be super fine which means that small type will become mushy. Best to err on large and easy, your gamers will thank you.

    -ps. no frayed scroll icons? look at you bucking convention!
