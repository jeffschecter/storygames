you can see above (a draft of) a boardiagram made for Project Dreamland [in playest]



The boardigram is a board for storygames.

Like a board, it have to be pleasant for the eyes, and make the players diving into the game.

As a Diagram, it help both players and DM to discover, understand and remember the rules. But it's also a real board : it's usefull to track the events, and to follow step by step the process of the game.

It may not be pertinent for all games. I think especially on those like CAPES or Universalis.

    \nWell, as Dreamland's playtest is not public, i don't know how far i can enter in details, but here it is:
    i felt like it was a bit difficult to follow all the details of the process of making a Scene. It seem also obvious that you had to track a lot of things.
    Concretely, the green circles represent the players (3 to 6).
    You begin a scene with an Overture (Ouverture), then you go to the Suspense and finally to the Climax.
    The succes (or failure) to the Scene is determined by succes/failure during the Climax.
    But succes/failure to Overture and Suspense grant you a bonus/malus for the Climax resolution, depending on how many players have succeed. So you can use the green circles to track succes/failure, and determining the final conditions for resolving the Climax.

    it's just part of the information on the "boardiagram" anyway...

    I don't know Pathdinder (yes, i don't) and just have an overwiew of Sorcerer so i can't tell you how it could work in this case. But GM-centric or not, i think it depend on complexity. Traditional RPG were more complicated than complex (the distinction is not mine unfortunately) and it was more about memorizing ( tables, tables...) than understanding. A game like CAPES is complex, you have to understand/figure out how it works, and it's hard to get the big picture and remember it.

    So i think it would be more usefull for this kind of games.

    \n I think you could make this board smaller and more consice as well, had you wanted to. There's not really all that much info going on.

    Anyways, the basic narrative structure in the game is a scene. A scene has 3 rounds - Overture, Suspense, Climax. During each of those rounds, every player gets a single action, mechanically (although it can represent a series of actions within the game world). An action is a basic 2d6+skill vs target number. There are ways to get bonuses but that's beside the point. Each round has a Round Difficulty, which is the number of successes the team needs to gather at that round in order to succeed.
    The outcome in the scene as a whole is determined by whether the team succeeds or fails in the Climax.
    Gaining more successes than necessary in other rounds gives bonuses to all rolls on the following round. Failing increases the difficulty of the Climax.

    The precentages represnt general guidelines for scene diificulty. Hard scenes either require everyone to succeed at Climax or everyone to succeed at both Overture and Suspense; Medium scenes require more than half successes at Climax etc. Those are mostly just rules of thumb though; after several scenes the GM should develop a decent feeling on how to determine difficulties.
