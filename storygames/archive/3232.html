    \n If it's not for a player, there's no need for a Key.

    Here's what I do:

    1. Urge the players to tie themselves to one another by Keys when creating their characters. This is super crucial! If they don't, encourage them to do it in play by suggesting likely relationships that are developing.

    2. I get everybody's Keys together (and, to a lesser extent, Secrets) and figure out ways to strongly invoke at least one per player per session. Preferably more than one. Somebody took Conscience? Dude, you are on a slave ship. Bloodlust? Dude, evil pirates. That sort of thing.

    3. Ask them what they want to see. Ask them for set pieces they want. "I want a kraken!" - give them a kraken, for Pete's sake, maybe in some surprising context. Ask for general motifs and singular images, too.

    4. Don't forget that you get Key scenes of your own, so think up some cool-ass stuff that you want to play. Also spend a little time thinking about likely pool refresh circumstances, and think up some colorful people and places for those.

    5. Remember that TSOY is all about the relationships, not about killing the bad guys. Bad guys are easy. Don't be shy about forming relationships and pushing hard.

    \n This isn't quite a pirate key as written -- I made it up for a fantasy campaign -- but it translates pretty easily to a certain type of anti-hero:
    \n
    \nKey of the Worldshaker
    \n"I die triumphant if my fear dies first!"
    \n\tYou have embraced turmoil as a way of life and made the road your home. Respectable folk step out of your way, maidens and young men watch in wary fascination, small boys follow you hoping to see a fight. You have probably taken a life and are most likely male.
    \n\tTypical backgrounds include soldier or sailor (but not officer), brigand, mercenary, gambler, and wanderer.
    \n\tGain 1 XP whenever you ignore good advice, credible threats, or legitimate orders.
    \n\tGain 2 XP whenever you help someone out in such a way that you receive only resentment or suspicion in return.
    \n\tGain 5 XP when you switch sides in a conflict out of pride or principle, without any material gain to yourself.
    \n\tYou may buy off this Key by pleading for help.

    \n I think TSOY works OK with interwoven, disparate storylines, but my play gravitates toward the player characters solving problems (internal and external) together. It's harder to hit Keys pointed at each other from a remove. So making one character an antagonist is probably not the best move for your first crack at the game, in my opinion. But it's a fun idea, and it could work - it's just a lot more complex than a bunch of guys fighting crime together.

    \n First off, I feel honor-bound to point out that most of the TSOY resources we've put together are based on the work of others (such as the fine folks who've contributed to the TSOY section at RandomWiki). You'll note that many of our Keys and Secrets are just retoolings of others to make them setting specific.

    As for "Keys for NPCs" I think that while NPCs don't really need Keys per se, they absolutely need motivations. Every good character wants something, whether it's the love of a PC, world domination, or whatever. You can easily use Keys to help shape motivations, but it's not necessary to keep track of their experience and advances. At least not in my experience.

    We actually have had the "speak with dead" bit come up in our current game, but the PC who has the "Secret of the Spirits" is pretty subtle about it. Essentially, treat the dead as an NPC and see what kinds of information the player can get through standard social conflict resolution (using Sway, Etiquette, etc.). I'd go for a narrative result (pointing them to a Key scene, perhaps), but you could also have a mechanical effect (bonus dice for a certain ability, etc.).

    \n My favorite precog trick is from (I think) Unknown Armies.

    At any point during a scene, you activate your precog power and state that everything that just happened in the scene was your vision of the future. You then rewind to the beginning of the scene and play it again. It's super fun in play. You could charge a steep price for this if you think you need to... 4 Reason maybe. I would charge less simply because it's awesome and I'd like to see it happen a lot.

    \n I dislike Key of the Sadist. Why reward introducing that to your table? Look at how Keys and Secrets are structured and you can divine the unwritten rules for creating your own.

    NPCs don't refresh, at least not in our games. I have them return with full pools.

    Our table is very traditional - GM at one end, players all 'round.

    We use glass beads for gift dice - you toss them into a common pot as you allocate them, and the recipient grabs an extra die to roll.

    We track harm with pencil and paper, right on our character sheets.

    Privateers. Tons of others.
