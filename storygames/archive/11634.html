      \n I had an interesting IM with Patrick Dugan over the weekend. He played Sleep Is Death with Jason Rohrer and wrote the Play This Thing review of the game. I made three observations to him about the game, not based on having played it myself but from having seen flip books:\n
        1. The stories have a sort of vomitous creativity, which is pretty raw and honest, and as a result, decently compelling. And probably moreso if you know the people who're playing the game, in which case it would be pretty voyeuristic.

        2. It quickly becomes excessive, like a poo flinging contest. (Elizabeth makes this same observation above.) We're all familiar with this from Kill Puppies for Satan play, creative one-upmanship along the shock vector. This is because true, stressfully creative circumstances provoke honesty, and the anxiety of self-revelation that provokes the defensiveness of going hardcore. You see it in live improv as well. (This is why Bacchanal advises you to play with people you trust, so you get the honesty without the in-yer-face shock-erotic one-upmanship.)

        3. The most most powerful feature of the game, I think, is the way that scenes are put together under the 30 second time limit drives reincorporation of elements. And this is utterly congruent with making narratives feel meaningful. You see the same setting again, the same forest of trees, the same surrey with the fringe on top, and your brain finds it to be meaningful. So I'm actually thinking massive shareable libraries of sprites might undermine the most powerful feature of the game.
