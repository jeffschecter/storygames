\n (The same topic as in rpg.net, but thought this would be nice place to bonder the same thing)

The basic way to make the combat more deadly seems to be the shortening the Stress track and the \xe2\u20ac\u201c2, -4 and -6 coincidence house rule, maybe with the addition of minion style stress. The question is how have these (or other modifications, and please share them) worked out in your games? They seem to go well with more lethal styles and campaigns on paper, but I would like to hear some experiences.

Is there any information how the system will be in Dresden files?

As a side note, I myself use the shorter stress track (three box base) and the \xe2\u20ac\u201c2, -4 and -6 coincidences, but we have not hit any coincidences yet as the players basically have gone through the minions till now. I also have been using more powerful minions (most being fair and some going to great quality).


The reason for the thought in first place:
I have been enthralled by the Fate 3 (SotC) system, most directly by the Chase rules, easy skill use and the all-prevalent Aspects. These sound nice things to use in more mundane campaigns also, with more mundane characters being easier to advance later, therefore giving more for those players looking to Augment their characters rules wise.

On this premise I have thought to bonder things like less stunts (two have worked well in our Crimson Skies game) and maybe either rename some skill levels (superb being too strong term for the peak skill for mundane character) or to start the pyramid from great level. In this premise the wounding strikes should be more common, therefore need for more \xe2\u20ac\u0153deadly\xe2\u20ac\x9d system. \xe2\u20ac\u0153Deadly\xe2\u20ac\x9d being somewhat misleading term as I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t usually want the danger be lethal, but injuring and feel like deadly. For example I would like to use Hollow Earth Expedition game setting with SotC rules. The battle with dinosaurs should be potentially lethal yes, but the combat against natives and the Nazis should be injuring, not immediately lethal, which is nicely done by the usual Conceding option. With beasts the conceding is usually not option, as the Dinosaur will most certainly devour the losing character, if the character is not conceding by some relocation, like carried away by river or similar..

    I have bordered a bit on the length of the stress track. With the Halfjack\xe2\u20ac\u2122s \xe2\u20ac\u0153taken out\xe2\u20ac\x9d by off track hit system it could be good to have some length for the track, maybe the three plus additional from suitable skill. With most characters this would mean four or five boxes. In this the consequences could be taken with \xe2\u20ac\u201c2, -4 and \xe2\u20ac\u201c6 modifications.

    Alternatively the base could be one base box, with additional from suitable skill, meaning basically that the first hit or two are grazes and the off-track hits could be softened by consequences, which would come in question almost immediately.

    In example we could consider professional soldier, noble native with spear or similar. The minion could be thought to be fair quality, at least if combat is the conflict in question. With some air or preparation there should be one Aspect tag in work, therefore the attack could be something in range from great [+4]. The average defensive skill for Superb skill pyramid could be thought to be Good [+3], with the Endurance being Average [+1].

    Therefore the Stress track would be marked, with average defensive roll of [+0], as the first box. In first system the marked box would leave three unmarked to right. In second system the marked box would leave one unmarked to right. As player you would see small rise of danger in first situation, but nothing immediately threatening his/her control of damage countering. In second case the danger seems to be more immediate, but the real life threat would not have risen.

    With weapon that does two Boxes of damage the situation would somewhat change, with both cases considering the consequence I think. In the second case the possibility to lessen damage in future attacks by minor consequence is non-existing, even if the multiple box damage weapon is removed. Therefore it should be valid to take the minor consequence now.

    \n I'm interested in this topic, as I'm currently working on adapting FATE to a Lovecraftian setting.

    I have a bit of a mixed feeling about Consequences in a gritty setting. I like the way they get players to come up with the way that they got hurt, and the way they function as temporary aspects. I'm not so keen on the way they make it hard to take PCs out, and the relatively slight consequences of getting shot up (which fits pulp, but not grittier genres).

    I'm currently looking at a system that has multiple-box weapon damage by type (1-4, more or less), and that treats Consequences as something you get as a result of being Taken Out (wounded too badly to continue fighting). The idea is that for each box of overspill (effectively there's a second Health track for Wounded), the Consequence (wound) has one level of power. The resulting Aspect still does only -2, and costs 1 FP to compel, but buying out of it costs FP equal to it's level. Healing (or medical attention) gradually brings down the level of the Consequences. The time between level changes can be tweaked according to just how gritty you want to be. If you somehow manage to exhaust the second track, you're dead.

    Thoughts?

    \n The system sound really promising, how long have you thought the base Stress Track to be?

    For the Lovecraft, or Call of Cthulhu games in general, I don\xe2\u20ac\u2122t think the multiple-box weapons are the primary way. The reason Characters die are the middle range horrors, not usually the mobsters or cultists. Therefore I think the difference of Skill level could be the answer for more dangerous damage, more than the tool used. Still it would sound cool if the trusty Shotgun would deal Two Stress Boxes. Still I would leave pesky humans and minor minions with small damage values, with lucky shots being the most dangerous and the ganging up naturally. The Major Minions of the Great Old Ones would then use skills far beyond mortal ken, maybe with difference of four or five, which would mean that they would but the character down with one or two normal swings. The Big Bad itself is basically a Scenery thing.

    In other hand with the more violent consequences would go long way, if the Stress Track is itself shortened, Depleted like Minion Track or removed all together. The name of the wound type, be that macro-typed as Consequence or something else, should be more dramatic, not \xe2\u20ac\u0153minor\xe2\u20ac\x9d. There could be additional effect of shock and trauma from the Consequence. Though before I can come up to anything more dramatic I will only use first level etc.

    How would this sound:
    First Level Consequence: Aspect concerning the wound itself, inability to perform initiative actions* for one round and finally; additional (Mediocre) Sanity check after combat because the violation of personal security.
    Second Level Consequence: Aspect concerning the wound itself, inability to perform initiative actions* and/or actions that require personal unaided locomotion** for scene and finally; additional (Fair) Sanity check after combat because the violation of personal body.
    Third Level Consequence: Aspect concerning the wound itself, inability to perform initiative actions* and/or actions that require personal unaided locomotion** and/or actions that would be hampered by great amount of pain*** for scene and finally; additional (Great) Sanity check after combat because the violation of personal health.

    * Initiative Actions being Aggressive actions versus opposition, Declaration and maneuvers that would require full mental and/or physical capacity. Would be considered a Stun.
    ** Meaning that the Character needs to aid to move him-/herself by either scenery (car, wall or similar support) or by other Characters.
    *** Meaning that the Characters vision and thought process is hampered by the amount of suffering, therefore needing additional argument from player how the character is managing the pain, this usually meaning that almost all actions are either denied or restricted by other skill.

    \n Tonpa, I'm glad you like the Sanity mechanic. I'm still tweaking it, to make it work with a track as per Fred's suggestion, which is good. I'm also now considering making each level of Insight reduce the PCs Composure track by one square, rather than give a -1 per level on Social rolls, as this doesn't need to be remembered, and is slightly less crippling/more interesting/more FATE-like.

    My goal is to make that mechanic, physical combat and social combat (including letting monsters have a Fear-based attack) work fairly harmoniously. It's not simple!

    I should add that I don't regard CoC as being ideal in all respects as a Lovecraftian game, though it has a lot going for it. So I'm not seeking simply to reproduce its effects using FATE, whether in combat or any other respect.

    Thanks for the pointers about SotC: there's always more in that text to discover. Is +1 enough when comparing the utility of a big axe vs. bare hands, though? I know which I'd rather have. Even if the Deep One closed with me, I could still hit it with the butt end better than I could punch it. Why would you expect the shotgun to have an associated aspect? I have run little SotC, so I'm sorry if that's a dumb question.

    More generally, do you not find it useful to be able to distinguish between accuracy and damage? Or is that just too fussy for you?

    What's your attitude to death spirals? I'm largely trying to avoid them, going with the "either you're OK or you're down and out" view of combat. Hence the post-Taken Out Consequences. I was also (btw) thinking of having a rule that once Taken Out (wounded) you can keep fighting for a while, but it costs you 1 FP per round to do anything at all. So you know you're going to be dropping shortly, and the Consequence-based Aspect is there to be tagged too.

    I want combat in a Lovecraftian game to be fast, confusing and scary. But I don't want to take away the players' options - that's dull. I just want to offer them choices other than the conventional tactical ones, if you see what I mean. Your comments about the Block action are interesting. The action that did least to threaten the PCs directly, did most to raise the apparent lethality of the situation? Fun. I guess that's because they were wondering about the consequences if they didn't finish the combat fast enough (being pursued by a horde of Germans, right?). It certainly sounds suspenseful.

    I'm not sure that your Consequences are necessarily too harsh (though they might be). My doubt about them is more whether they are idiomatic. Imposing blanket restrictions of that kind isn't very FATE, IMO. Still, it's a starting point.

    \n OK. Sorry for the delay in coming back.

    Picking up from your last post, we have some possible tools in our armoury.

    I'd like to focus on the options in #1, which relate to physical combat.

    I see the use of #2 as an adjunct to physical combat, but a very useful one. In essence, if you want to run a game where the PCs seem to be constantly under threat, but seldom die, making them run before they can get too badly hurt is a good option.

    And #3 is kind of a side-issue, though very interesting in its own right. I'd love to see you develop an example of that more.

    Getting back to physical combat, what do we have so far? I'll try to consolidate what we've talked about so far with some ideas of my own.

    a. We can shorten the track.
    b. We can make hits check off more than one box (sometimes). At first this looks the same as (a) but actually it isn't. Not only does it offer more flexibility, it also has consequences for how important skill is in the game. And as you've said, making guns deadly has consequences.
    c. We can subdivide the track into rows, and make moving to each row have a particular meaning. My proposed system effectively does just that, the second row being the Wounded row. This can be combined with variable weapon damage so that rather than weapon checking off more boxes, they start counting on different rows.
    d. We can use Consequences in many ways. They can substitute for a hit. They can be used to reduce the degree of success, or the damage (or both, or user's choice which). They can be the result of being Taken Out, or of moving between rows in the track (see c). There may be other things we could do with them, such as getting a Consequence for reusing an Aspect in a combat, for example.
    e. We can alter the meaning of degree of success. For example, Fred Hicks has suggested that successes of degree zero could automatically translate to Manouevers rather than hits. Extending this concept, every success could be a Manoeuver, and some could simply do damage as well. That could be good for your death spiral effect.
    f. We could use Aspects differently. For example, you could rule that each Aspect can be used only once in a fight. If you then ruled that any hit (or any hit over a certain level, etc) results in being Taken Out unless the player spends 1 FP and invokes an Aspect, that could be interesting.

    That's about it for ideas from me. I expect you can add to the list.

    Some other things I would like to think about include: single blows that only knock someone out, how a system might help a group to find its own balance between gritty realism and PCs spending all their time convalescing, how to deal with something like a shoggoth in game terms, damage from falling, etc. and where the limits of this kind of approach lie. How about ramming Cthulhu with a ship?

    Posted By: Dave Hallett Is it really their intent that so long as you're inside a weapon's usable range, your chances of success don't change with distance? Or have I missed something?
    \nSo far as I'm aware, there are statements on page 56 (last paragraph of "Framing the Scene") and 99 ("Guns" skill description) that Guns can shoot between 3-5 Zones, depending on how small the Zones are (with the elaboration that it generally takes a Rifle to shoot at 4 or more Zones away).

    To try to capture a "gritty" feel (in this case, meaning a greater likelihood of failure in difficult circumstances), it would be easy enough the say that for every zone beyond the second, Guns suffer a -1 to hit per Zone (so -3 at 5 zones distance). Or you could be more specific - Pistols suffer -2 for Zone beyond the second, but Rifles suffer only -1. The main problem you face with specific modifiers is that Zones can vary widely in size and the type of terrain (e.g. elevation difference, rooms, jungle, etc) they represent. So it's likely that once you start adding modifiers to account for circumstances affecting the difficulty of the shot, it's hard to know when to stop.

    Alternately, if you feel that a target is difficult to see or aim at because of distance, you could limit Guns based on Alertness (e.g. -1 if Alertness is lower) of the shooter. But my main preference is to reply on applying aspects (e.g. Halfjak's "Really Far Away") that can be tagged by the Defender for defense or to compel the shooter to miss. The flexibility of an aspect means that it can quickly semantically encompass any modifiers that might effect the shot without recourse to looking up charts or tables. So whether it's "Dark", "Raining buckets", "Foggy", or the target is "Obscured", "Hugging Cover", or "Running for their Life" - it's clear to the shooter where the penalty arises from and suggests possible Maneuvers that could be used to improve the chances of the making the shot.

    But I hope you find a satisfactory solution for what you're looking for. I definitely recommend the Fate RPG Yahoo group as a great place to go with questions about FATE or SOTC or to have others vete an idea.
