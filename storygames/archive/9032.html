    I think that it's better to have the players make characters they like, know and own rather than have pre-gens for expediency. 3 hours could work for a 10 franchise dice mystery if the group decides on characters relatively quickly and dives in. The franchise dice system is not super critical -- it's just dice. Like jim pinto says, remember the GM does next to nothing and the players do everything else. The key to the game is not the mechanics, but getting the players motivated to investigate and excited to come up with solutions to the mystery. So, if the players aren't sufficiently invested in their characters, it could be really hard to get them to step up and participate. The GM sits back and steers the game occasionally when it drifts, puts in some stress dice and does a little roleplaying here and there - that's it.

    \n You don't need to make pre-generated characters for a 3 hour, 2 investigation game of InSpectres. I'd advise just letting them make up characters and franchise, roll up a client from the tables and go from there.

    The stress/cool economy is much more fun than the franchise dice economy - I wouldn't push hard to get through two missions, just pace it naturally. On the other hand, if you start with a newish franchise of 5-8 dice, you probably will have time for two anyhow.

    \n I'll agree that pre-gens aren't really necessary, although you might want to take pains to prevent the franchise creation section taking ages. Don't feel you need to ask all the listed questions.

    It's very easy to blow straight through low-franchise dice mysteries very quickly. Pay a lot of attention to how many franchise dice have been acquired and accelerate the pace of events accordingly. A seven or eight dice franchise (therefore requiring 14-16 dice to complete the mystery) sounds about right.

    The book says this, but I'll stress it: don't prepare any aspect of events whatever. Describe some phenomena and then sit back and play reactively. Let the players define what the mystery is.

    You need to call for stress rolls early and often or you will find that the characters will overwhelm the mystery very quickly and might not even need to use their franchise dice. Start with one die stresses, which provide the opportunity to accumulate cool. Use the opportunity of any roll of a four or lower as an excuse to spring a stress on the player. Pacing the number and severity of stress rolls is pretty much the #1 thing to get right as an InSpectres GM.

    \n That's a cool idea for the first mission, although I feel like you might have already determined a bit much about the mystery. You have to be prepared to throw *all* of that away in play if the players take things in a different direction. I've found that it's useful to have a basic plot in my mind to set the initial scenes, but it's important that you don't try and prevent the players narrating what they want to when they win the rolls. The analogy I've used before is that my prepared plot is the 'working theory' of what the mystery is, but that anything in there is liable to be overruled by what happens in play.

    I think that asking the players what is stressing the character out is probably not the best way to go. Instead, pick on things that they mentioned during character generation or, as Jon said, just go with the mundane office stuff for stress rolls. The results of crappy gear rolls when building the franchise are often good for stress rolls later, in particular. "You go to get some coffee from the old possessed coffee machine in the basement: stress roll!"

    \n Roy,

    I'm going to go one further than Mike and say that you've said too much about the situation. Have you seen the structure of play stuff in the book. The Research, Suiting Up, Field Work stuff? That's not just "advice" that's how the game is played. You haven't presented them with a mystery to solve, you've presented them with a situation to deal with. You're at a party and your goal is to keep ghosts away: GO!

    InSpectres is supposed to be a Clue-Chain-Mystery style game alla Call of Cthulhu but with two twists. The first twist is that the players make up all their own clues (mostly, unless they roll badly). The second is that beyond just following the clues to the big baddie, those clues often produce real-world office management type level complications (that's YOUR job as the GM).

    Seriously this is how I started my last InSpectres game: "Hi, I"m from the zoo. We have a problem in our lion cage. Something keeps really freaking out the animals. Man, no offense but are you guys it? I mean, I know the city cut our funding and everything but you sure don't look like much."

    And that's it. I had NO idea about anything else.

    \n InSpectres can be a really hard jump for GM/players who are used the D&D paradigm. You have let go of everything you've ever associated with the term "GM" or "DM". You can roleplay all the interesting characters such as the gallery owners as they come up, but you cannot GM in the traditional way by leading the players - remember the players decide what's going on too. You have to really explain to the players that when they win rolls, they can dictate the scenes and describe what happens and they don't have to check with the GM or anything. They have the power. If you think the answer to "what's the hole under the table" is that it's a goblin hole, you might never get the chance to make it true if the players win a roll and say it's a hole to another dimension. You might think a shaman is behind the ghosts but then the player investigates, wins a roll and decides a secret government agent is really behind it. That's how it works.

    Re your scenario: From what you've written, ask yourself, what's the mystery? What's the question the players are supposed to answer? If there are no questions, then you don't have a good starting premise. If they're just supposed to keep some ghosts at bay, that's not a mystery, that's a task like "clear out the dungeon" which is not InSpectres.

    Possible mysteries from your scenario:
    "Why are ghosts and goblins haunting the art gallery opening?"
    or even broader
    "What are the strange noises that people keep hearing at night in the art gallery?"

    You pose your mystery question and you sit back and do nothing. Really. Nothing. The players decide what to do next while you sit on your hands and try not to say anything.

    \nLet me just throw out something that has helped my Inspectres games immensely - dice caps on scenes. Especially in early franchise mysteries, I would often find the characters at the mystery solution (or close) after a single scene - which wasn't very mystery-like. I since put a cap on the amount of franchise dice you can accrue in a scene; the players could keep roleplaying and making dice rolls after that, but the dice didn't actually add to the mystery pool. Make sense?
    \n
    \nFor example - if we have a 15 dice mystery, I might say that each scene has a max of 5 dice - thus ensuring we have at minimum 3 scenes.

    \n Jared, the problem is that a GM raised on a steady diet of traditional mystery games, running Call of Cthulhu scenarios straight out of Chaosium supplements and whatnot, will be expecting rocket science. All of these cautions and corrections are useful buffers against the gap between a new GM's expectations and what InSpectres really is. There are habits to be broken, as evinced above. Obviously, you would have broken those habits no later than when the game gelled into its current form; the rest of us have some technical catching-up to do, is all.

    We, mystery game GMs, are used to having "the answer," and now we're being told to go collect "the answer" as it's distributed among the many minds at the table. Having done it both ways myself, I can testify that it's like suddenly zenning distributed parallel programming, or having the epiphany that a market economy is actually a simpler thing than a planned one, or that evolution is actually simpler than design. It's also like suddenly finding the courage to jump off that high cliff into the water. Letting order emerge from the interactions of many actors, rather than imposing it from the top down, means learning a whole new set of skills, and transferring your weight all at once from your old skillset to the new. It's scary and exciting, and getting a little advice from other people who have gone before definitely helps.

    Jared, I imagine that if you have any response to this, you'll say that the way I (and I would guess "we") have been GMing is bad, stupid, unfun, and pointless, that it provides at best an illusion of authorship to non-GM players. I (and again, I would guess "we", everyone in this thread) concede in advance that you're right.

    \n Roy,

    Mastering the Stress Dice is probably the single greatest skill an InSpectres GM needs to cultivate. I used to hate it. Then I learned the secret: Be uncaring. I'm serious. It's not your job to make it "balanced" for the players. Just go with your aesthetic. If you see stress in the fiction make them roll Stress. Period. If they get hosed, too bad. Turns out that works better than you think.

    Here are the guidelines I use for *identifying* Stress. (I use that word specifically. I'm not talking about Pacing, I'm taking about noticing Stress when it's there).

    1) Every time the game stalls and you have to spike the fiction with your input -> Stress
    2) Every time the players do something and it goes badly (i.e. you narrate) -> Stress

    Works like a charm.

    Another thought: why not award an extra die for a confessional that foreshadows events in the adventure that then are brought about by the players as with characteristic confessionals?
    \nThere isn't a need to make confessionals more powerful then they already are, they can already effect the fiction, fundamentally change characters, make time and space your plaything, and provide any sort of deus ex machina needed.

    What really needs to be done with confessionals is allowing more of them, they more than any other part reinforce the Reality Television tropes of Inspectres. Getting players to use the confessional should be encouraged, but for asides and explanations, the game altering parts of confessionals should follow the rules laid out in the text.\n

    A last quick observation: when I was trying to think of good media to set the tone, besides Ghostbusters or Men in Black, I thought Shaun of the Dead might fit the bill\u2026 the juxtaposition of the brutally mundane with fantastical horror, presented hilariously.
    \nThat juxtaposition is important, but do not forget that Reality Television should be at least as big a part of your game as Ghostbusters. Though perhaps now it is the parody of Reality Television that should be encouraged in Inspectres. When the game was written Reality TV was still quite young and it's conventions and affectations where still being worked out, now we have shows like the Office and Reno 911 and other parodies of Reality TV that hit much closer to the mark for how to interpret Reality TV for Inspectres.

    Once you've got Ghostbusters and Reality TV then the third pillar of Inspectres is crappy jobs and start up corporations. Everyone has had at least one crappy job that they can draw on to understand the world of Inspectres and how their character fits into it. If they've had the experience of starting their own business, or working for some start up corporation that only provides more grist for the mill.

    Which is why it would probably be a mistake to skip franchise creation, part of the fun comes from saying "We need a coffee machine for the office" rolling the dice and then seeing just what kind of coffee machine your start up franchise has. A gleaming copper plated espresso machine of titanic proportions, a garage sale Mr. Coffee, or a demonically possessed percolator that produces coffee from the bitter, burnt, and blacken souls of a thousand sinners which no amount of milk or nondairy creamer can bring to the light. By going through the office and outfitting the franchise and leaving the quality of those objects up to the dice you mimic the feel of a start up business perfectly, it creates the fictional space and highlights what's important to that particular group of Inspectres. As for making Technology rolls during the suit up phase that is perfectly acceptable and should indeed be encouraged. But don't skip out on the franchise creation phase as it would make for a poorer game.
