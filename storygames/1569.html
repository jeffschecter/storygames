[M-B] Focus on conflict makes incomplete characters

TonyLB
October 2006 edited October 2006 in Story Games
Okay, show of hands! How many people came in because the topic line convinced them that I was going to say something you really, really disagree with?

Well, let's see: Misery Bubblegum creates characters for each session, so I've been present for the creation of a lot of characters recently. We usually latch on to the conflict cards, so the characters are defined by stuff like "Why can't I stick up for myself?" and "You don't love me" and the like. It is easy to make such a character ... we've all practiced the skills of it. And certainly they're easy to play. Drive straight the hell at the conflicts, beat the shit out of the character, resolve what you can (often everything) and you're done.

But there's the point ... when you do that, you're genuinely done. Those conflicts that defined the character? All gone. Burned up.

This isn't new insight. Sorceror said it way back when. When the character's Kicker is resolved they're no longer a playable character. They can become a playable character if you give them another Kicker ... if you inject more conflict. Now I agree with that. A character without conflict is not a playable thing.

Everyone with me so far?

Having played session of Misery Bubblegum, every single session ends with us saying "Oh wow, we could totally play these characters again, just by grabbing some new conflicts." But we don't, and at the end of the last session I said to people "Y'know, we should consciously try to do that. We should make characters with the intent of playing them through multiple sessions."

So I've been wondering what would be different about doing that, and I think that it would mean a focus on the stuff outside of conflict ... the things about the character that don't get burned to a crisp through the course of play. A smile that lights up the room. Shallow vanity that makes a charming counterpoint to the humility of another character. Metal claws that go "snikt!" as they spring out of the back of his hands.

When you start adding elements that just plain make you happy to be playing the character, even though they don't drive conflict, then I think you construct a character that you're going to like no matter what conflict gets layered on them. Now, as I said above, that character absent conflict is unplayable. You can't just sit around making "snikt" noises all session long and expect to have fun. It's boring. But bringing those happy-making elements into your conflicts improves the conflicts.

I'm starting to ramble. Dictionary of Mu has this character "Kid with a Rock." He's a Kid. He has a Rock. That is so cool. Amidst all these prophets and witch-kings he's a kid with a big rock. That's the happy-making thing about that character, for me. You could wrap that in any type of conflict, and I'd be interested. He's lost his water-skin? Cool. What's a kid with a rock do about that? He sees a young girl and falls in love with her? Cool. What's a kid with a rock do about that?

So that's what I think Misery Bubblegum needs now ... some guidance (which, as the guidance gets more reliable and more objective, becomes rules and mechanics) for how to do this one thing right. How do you make the bits of the character that aren't there to drive the story, but rather are there to reward you for driving the story by making you happy with the character?



NOTE: I suspect that this is what people have been trying to get at in past conversations with me when they said things like "If you're just driving to the conflict you never get a chance to really sit back and learn who the character is," and the like. Many gamers "discover" (by which I mean that they invent, but they invent in an incremental process that seems like discovery) the things about their character that make them happy through the process of play, and that process can be interfered with by a focus on conflict. It would be appropriate for anyone who has had those conversations with me to take this time to say "I told you so," and I will totally cop to that, so long as you aren't strident about it. If you're strident I'll blow you off, even though you're right :-)

    Since the only thing I can think about these days is FLFS, here's how I do it there: loosely speaking, your character's conflicts are set up in your three thematic batteries. That's what the GM will engineer the situation off of, that's what each game will revolve around. You can swap out one thematic battery at the end of each situation. On the other hand, the rest of your sheet is filled with "attributes" and "skills" which are sneaky. They look like regular old atts-n-skills, but they're actually flags for how you want problems to be resolved. You don't swap those, but you do build them up as you go. So I'll encode those "on his best day" traits into his atts and skills (the friendly guy gets high Savoir-Faire and Acquaintances, the supercool pilot guy gets Shiphandling and Gunnery, etc). Those will always be around. The things that he does with those evergreen traits, though, all orbit around his thematic batteries. So I can be Proving Myself with my Savoir-Faire and Acquaintances, I can be dealing with being Blackmailed, or I can be overcoming my reputation as a Prissy Socialite.

    I'm going to propose something I'm not entirely sure is true, but whatever...

    I think that the things we find evergreen cool (as Josh put it) are the things that we deeply sympathise with, or at least the things we sympathise with at one remove. And I think this is true of character traits in any form of media, even static ones like movies and books and stuff. Examples!

    I'm involved in a complex game. It's Exalted, set in modern day San Fransisco, using the mechanics from HeroQuest. I'm playing Katarina, a talented ballerina who teaches womens' self defense classes recently exalted. And a couple of sessions in, I discover something interesting/important/compelling about her: She's very protective. She's like a big sister, always looking out for everyone in emotional and physical arenas.

    And you know what? That's an ideal I'm highly sympathetic to. One that I tend to aspire to myself (which isn't always a good thing). This is a compelling trait because it is a trait about me as a person not just a trait about some bit of fiction.

    Batman is a character I tend to find very compelling. What do I find compelling? His intense drive, his incredible competence, but most of all his tendency to have plans for any contingency. And why is that compelling? Because I'm a control freak. The ideal of never being caught by surprise, never being in a position where you don't have a solution worked out is personally appealing (even if it's also creepy).

    So, I think that the things we find compelling in characters like this are the same things that we find compelling in ourselves. They're ideals we aspire to personally. They're evergreen because they are how we explore ourselves.

    The problem is that I'm not sure the connection is as close as I think it is, and further I don't know how to provide guidelines for this sort of thing. How do you help people to figure out what's compelling in themselves so that they can project that onto a character?

    The more I think about it, the more I think that involving these traits as stakes in any way whatsoever is a recipe for disaster.

    If Peter love MJ, that's cool. We all like that.

    And I am 100% fine with stakes like "Can you show your love for MJ?" or "Does MJ love you back?" and all that jazz. Those are conflicts which are inspired by the love, and which influence the way it is shown in the story, but which in no way affect the existence of the love.

    Because, on a pretty serious level, I'm not actually interested in those stakes resolving in that way. It's a place I don't want the game to go. Which is not a problem of fear. I'm pretty sure that the game won't go that way, because I'll just say "no." You know how it's lame to say "Our game will be about risking life and limb in search of treasure!" and then follow it up with "But, of course, PCs have script immunity and can't really die"? In exactly the same way, it's lame to say "Our game will be about seeing whether Peter and MJs love can survive the difficulties of life," and then follow up with "But, of course, no matter how bad things look the love has script immunity, and can't really die."

    There's basically two ways that a character can be something. They can be something (the way Peter loves MJ) because we just flat-out want them to be, and they don't need to prove it. Or they can be something (the way Luke is a Jedi, beyond hate and anger) provisionally ... not because we actually want them to be, but because we want to explore the question of whether they are or not.

    Continually testing the character traits that are provisional is good. "Oh, you're calm and controlled? How about now? How about when the rebellion is crushed? How about when he threatens Leia?" That's cool. That's drama.

    Continually testing the character traits that we just want the character to have is bad. "Oh, you love MJ? How about now? How about when she goes out with Flash? How about if she turns into a super-villain? How about if she kills Aunt May?" That's ... that's just a mistake. I don't know how else to put it. I feel in my gut that if their love really falls into the category of "stuff that makes the audience identify with the character" then doing that is a mistake.

    I don't know how well I'm expressing this, or how solid it is. At this point it's all new ground for me. What do you folks think?

      Wow, I'm really sad that I got to this thread so late, since I have this huge long rant about how constantly escalating conflict is a really over-sold way of expressing and developping character. Suffice to say I agree with the original post, and also totally disagree with the idea that a character without a central conflict is unplayable or played-out. Conflict is a powerful narrative tool. Not all roleplaying is about narrative. And no, I do not mean not all roleplaying is Narrativist or not all roleplaying involves narrating, I mean that sometimes it doesn't matter what happens next in the story. Sometimes that really isn't what's important to the players, or to the characters. I feel like the focus on narrative techniques and narrative momentum is starting to become a crutch; a single solution someone found to 'what do we do next in this game' that has been developped to a reliable point and whose usefulness has made it overwhelming.

      The idea that we must push characters to the limit to reveal important things about them is fundamentally flawed, and while few people seem to say it outright I feel like it is often a half-made assumption in conversations and game design in the community. The only thing putting a character in a highly stressful situation reveals about that character is how they act in a highly stressful situation. This is not any more who they are than how they act in a situation without conflict, without stress, where very little is at stake. If characterisation is an important goal, escalating conflict is really only one of very many tools. Every thing a character does is a piece of who they are, whether or not it makes a strong moral statement or involves a life-or-death situation, or any conflict at all. In fact, I would go so far as to say that most people react much more similarly to high-stress situations than they do to low-stress ones -- it is harder, not easier, to differentiate human beings in the middle of a battlefield.

      The only game I've ever even started-designing is an expression of this idea -- that when you really sit down and think about people you know and what makes them who they are, you do not generally think of dramatic actions they took under stressful moral situations. You think about their donkey-like laugh, or the way they constantly abuse the phrase 'the fact of the matter'; how you remember them covering their desk in epic doodles in grade six, getting sent to detention, and doing the same thing on the desks there; the fact that they can sing the entirety of the HMS Pinafore from memory. These things are not best expressed or arrived at through conflict. Most people are not Robin Hood, they do not regularly end up deciding whether others will live or die -- this does not make them any less interesting, any less human, or any less worthy of roleplaying about. You do not need to push someone to the limit of experience to find out what makes them who they are. Sometimes you don't need to push them at all. You just need to watch.

      Thomas, about ten years ago I had a conversation with Brett Evill where he pointed out that since dramatic conflict will transform a character, it's possible to "use up" a character and make them unusable for further play. This is a problem, if you're thinking in terms of serial fiction, because you can't reuse the character again.

      So you have to think about what conflicts are never going to get resolved. For example, Batman cannot ever realize that violence will not fill the hole in his soul, and Peter Parker can't ever feel that he's lived up to the responsibilities demanded of him. Those form constants of the characters, and provide the narrative motor you need to keep telling stories about them.

      Everything else about the character can change, and as long as there are those one or two conflicts that never get resolved, it still feels like you've got the same protagonist. (Steven Brust's Vlad Taltos novels are a great illustration of this principle -- Vlad has undergone radical transformations, and really critical aspects of his personality and morality have changed over time, but the thing that's been a constant is that he is a permanent outsider, who has never found a culture that he can fully belong to.)

      There are also interesting literary possibilities in deliberately refusing closure. Closure is a structural device we deliberately impose on stories, and you can push into interesting existential or naturalistic territory by deliberately violating it.

      That's Ron's idea.

      Oh, sure, some people were probably doing it before him, but he identified it and first encoded it in a game. The point is that, prior to this, the only thing that made a character worth playing through the next "adventure" (conflict) was the special nature of his effectiveness. Kewl Powerz, or just ability to kill, etc.

      Those things still work, BTW. Sorcerer is just Conflict with Kewl Powerz from demons.

      That said, I agree completely with Shreyas, in that what makes a character from whom it's easy to come up with renewable conflict is simply to make the character a complex human. That's why I think HQ works so well for this, without having any special rules for making the character somehow grabby. Turns out that any well fleshed out character is automatically grabby. HQ, unlike many newer systems, goes a long way to enabling players to make fleshed out characters. An average fully filled out character sheet will have upwards of 60 abilities on it.

      And do you get writer's block, just because you have the ability to make "any" character? Well, no, because, just like in D&D, again, we have a simple set of choices to make to create the character. It's like the above list of questions for HQ:

      1. What species/race is your character?
      2. What culture was he brought up in?
      3. What religion does he adhere to?
      4. What is his social position AKA occupation (why doesn't he starve to death)?
      5. What does he believe in most deeply (magic keyword)?

      Each of the answers to these questions brings along around ten abilities or so, and then you get to add on ten other random things about your character.

      Now, you don't have to add these all on at once - you can develop your character "As You Go." In fact, I've found that in actual play, starting with no character concept at all, you can have players develop a character in some detail in just one scene! Just by asking them the above questions, essentially.

      What's cool is that the situation also acts, then, as a catalyst for creating the character. So with the question structure, and the situation catalyst, I've never seen anyone have any trouble creating a character that, I believe, they could play through many conflicts.

      Why many conflicts? Well, the interesting thing is that the nature of the conflicts is, itself, a fruitful void. That is, there's a subtle difference between what people above are calling a character who has a conflict, and a character with potential conflict. Any character that has an ability of any sort has potential conflict, I'd argue. Any character with relationships and/or personality traits has obvious flags for potential conflicts.

      I say this specifically because a trait like "Love's Griselda" is not a conflict, but can easily be made into one. So can "Swordsmanship" actually, it's just not as obvious how. But you create the conflict simply by creating situation that makes the character make some decision about the ability in question. Like, "Do I take up the challenge to fence the master, or demure?"

      So, very simply, making characters with lots of "stuff" to them that says a lot about what's important to them, is quite a good enough way, it seems to me, to make a character playable across many, many conflicts. Which may be why my HQ game has characters with 80 sessions behind them.

      I'm not saying that short form with light characters isn't cool, too. Just that if you want characters who you want to play a lot, just make them realistically complex (or even dramatically overcomplex). It seems that simple to me.
