Chris and I have been knocking Push and Pull out very fruitfully over on Deep in the Game. Thanks Chris!

Here are your no-nonsense definitions:

Push is an assertion of individual authority.

Pull is a directed solicitation for collaborative buy-in and input.

Both Push and Pull are a part of fundamental human communication patterns. They are tools used in social interactions that provide movement to the interaction and provoke response and action within it.

In a RPG context, Push and Pull happen both as they do in a non game context (socially and incidentally because we are still people engaging in interaction), and as techniques used to affect the game, the social environment and the drama. Both Push and Pull can be mechanically or non-mechanically supported, functional or dysfunctional, effective or non-effective. Neither is inherently better or worse than the other, though people can have preferences for one or the other.

Push

A player, using a Push technique, uses his own authority to put something out there. This something could be an assertion of an element or action into the fiction, it could be something in the social contract that causes or prevents something from happening (E.g. identifying that an NGH or TTP line has reached a hard stop) or in other ways (IÃ¢â‚¬â„¢m not going to categorically list them here, that could be a discussion for a future time, suffice to say that although a push can be used as a technique to address the fiction, itÃ¢â‚¬â„¢s not tied to it).

    6.) What types of behaviors/styles of play does your game reward (and punish if necessary)?

    Read the game. The entire first half is all built on Pull techniques: the players contribute ideas, solicit investment, and earn the approval and buy in of the other players to create a communal endeavor. The second half of the game is all Push.     Fred,

    I'd think that a flag is either a push or a pull, at least inherently. A push or a pull is something someone does. A flag is a tool that helps people focus the game. So flags will often act as focuses for push or pull, but are not themselves either.

    For example, Keys in TSOY give you a good reason to push your characters story into the fiction -- you get XP when you do so. But until you start using the flag, it does nothing. If you make a TSOY character that is not part of a game and then show him to someone who isn't going to be playing with you nothing on the sheet can possibly be push or pull -- it's just things on a sheet waiting to be brought to life when you actually play. But until people start discussing and using, collaberating, and challenging the Belief it isn't either a push or a pull, it is a flag waiting for the wind to blow it one direction or the other.

    Now, this can seem confusing because it often seems like flags are doing those things on their own. You put a flag down, and magically the GM starts running an interesting plot for you. But the truth is that is a matter of social engagement, social systems, and people actually reacting to each other at the table. If it is in your social contract that when you change a belief the GM must immediatly set up a scene challanging it, for example, and you change a belief it is not the new flag that magically makes the scene, it is the GM as a person reacting to the thing you just did as a person, and not the flag as magical motivator. The flag just lets you communicate, it is not the actual communication.

    Flags are excellent, they say "Push/Pull me here" or "I will Push/Pull here" but they aren't themselves actions. In fact, they aren't anything but words until they hit the social situation and play at the table where they act as a spur and a frame for the actual actions of play.

    Pushes are things you can do when you have authority over something (or are making the claim to authority), so:

    In that kind of traditional Player/GM split:
    -The Player can Push using their character's actions and what is detailed in the rules
    - The GM can Push using any NPC, and often, making up any aspect about the setting or world on the fly. Depending on the game, the GM might also have the right to ignore the rules at a whim.

    -Both players and GMs can Pull using what is within their authority. ("I leave my magic ring on the table, I wonder if your thief will steal it?")
    - Both players and GMs can Pull using things -outside- their authority ("Hey guys, I'm at a loss here, what do YOU think the King would do?" "Hey Jim, what if our characters fell in love?")

    Push is bounded by authority, Pull is not.
