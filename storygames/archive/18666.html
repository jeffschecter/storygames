\n I've been mulling over relationships, more specifically - few, direct, simple, tension filled relationships.
For awhile now, I've had an idea about three relationships; a hunter, a dog, and a wolf. It seemed like a solid triangle.
That's all I knew for a few months. Then recently, the idea came to introduce cold as a another character.
Would people enjoy playing the Cold as much as they enjoy playing the Hunter?
I'm thinking Despair should be like reverse hit points, added to not subtracted from.
Maybe Cold has Sun, Wolf has Hunger, Dog has ?, and Hunter has Despair?
This could maybe make a neat micro-game?

    \n Just some food for thought. Nothing concrete. The words are inuit (for the Google-curious).

    I am Anguunasukpuk, the Hunter.
    Hard am I, and strong are my traps and sharp is the tip of my spear.
    For only in bringing food to my family may I gain honour in their eyes.
    And when my Pride is full, I may return home victorious, a Man.
    But the hunt is hard, the cold is sharp, and the Call To Home is strong.

    I am Kringmerk, the Dog.
    But no pet, nor companion, I.
    I am the tool of the Hunter, the master's slave, held fast by chains of Hunger.
    But stronger still is the Call Of The Wild, the white Wolf's howl, to follow the beckoning
    moon.

    I am Amaruk, the Wolf.
    This is the Wild, my home, my grounds and all within it my prey.
    But a Hunter approaches and at his heel my Brother stays.
    So I shall poach the poacher and drive off the Hunter and call my Brother home.

    I am Okralruserk. I AM the winter. I AM the Wild. I AM the Call TO Home.
    I humble the proud, I starve the weak, I tame the wilding howl.

    So the Hunter must fill his Pride with Food to return home victorious but he must also use it to feed the Dog to keep him loyal. Too much Wild and he is driven home.
    The Dog needs food to stave off hunger but longs to be Wild and free. If he fills his Wild, he wins.
    The Wolf feeds Wild to the Dog to weaken the Hunter but too much and the Dog becomes competition (Dog has full Wild). He must also steal food from the Hunter but only the Hunter can feed the Dog to keep it from starving. Driving the Hunter Home or freeing the Dog are his goals.

    The Winter doesn't want anyone to win. Winter can give or take resources to play the others against one another but by the end of a certain turn limit, he wins if no-one else has.

    Special moves such as "To Build a Fire" would cost one resource to gain/lose another or some other tactical advantage. Ie: Frost bite, Howl at the Moon, Whimper, Heavy Snow, Looking Back, see the Jack London story for others.

    So, the Hunter must feed the Dog to calm the Wild that Winter gives or takes that powers the Wolf to lay in the house that Jack built - or something...

    One last thing, from the story: "There was nobody to talk to; and, had there been, speech would have been impossible because of the ice-muzzle on his mouth." So, no dialogue, only narration and the howl of the wind (or something worse).

    In a darker version, perhaps one of the temptations is for the Hunter to eat the Dog...

    \n If you're reading this, I guess the thread isn't closed yet. Frankly, I blame you for planting the seed in my head and now I have to dig it out.

    I did something unfortunate in my previous post; I encoded clear win/lose conditions in the characters. That's fine for a "gamey" game of resources but it seems to narrow the possibilities of the fiction.

    I only have the vaguest notion of the actual play of the game and I can't really help with the mechanics. But I know that if I were tasked with it's design, it would be a game of words over resources. As such, it wouldn't be "heads" over "tails"; both sides of the coin would have to be appealing. This is most obvious in my unfortunate wording of the Dog. I was going by the story (Jack London was required reading in my school system).

    By simply changing the word Hungry to Loyalty (albeit probably bought by food), the "Tails" option is suddenly far more agreeable and identifiable.

    Yes, I've been hungry. I did the 30 Hour Fast ONCE! when I was younger and I think I cheated near the end with some broth. But I always had the option to eat. I've been hungry but I've never known hunger. I know Loyalty. I can buy into that easily.

    I'm Canadian. I've been cold (on job sites where it's a requirement) but I always had the option to get warm. I don't know cold. But I certainly know the desire to go home. That's one instance where I think I worded it right - as opposed to "Giving Up" which carries a definite negative connotation.

    Cold and Hungry should be infused in the game through words: omni-present; encoded in the language. But "desire versus need" should present both as desirable. Sitting comfortably around a table with snacks, Cold and Hungry seem distant and poor motivators. Again I will site the story, where-in our sympathy is clearly with the Dog and we don't feel much for the prideful and foolish Hunter. That should be one possible outcome but not the only one.

    In negotiations arguements should be equally viable for both sides of the coin. Physically, there should be words/phrases present but in what form, I don't know: character sheets/pamphlets/ cards. I'm afraid I can't help there.

    In short, all of this is simply to say that I think you have the germ of an intriguing game and I hope something grows from it.
