\n A long time ago I read about techniques at the Forge and got the idea that there was something important missing whenever people described game procedures. To the point that you had an easier time getting how a game worked by either playing with people who knew the game, watching a video or reading an AP.

Then I read Archipelago, PbtA games, Witch Mountain, etc. and finally got a more clear vision, which took me to start this thread on 2013 and later to post a compilation of the results here

A couple other things lately got me into the idea that while I had listed a good amount of techniques, I hadn't put them in order nor classified them. Turns out that putting them on the order we use them in the conversation at the table makes for a really good description of how an RPG actually works. So I spent the past week compiling a proper list, until this small derrail at this thread brought something else to my attention. But let's go over the list first:


Here's the actual list I personally use along with some caveats and subtleties for each one:\n

You are all in ... The Narrator begins the conversation by describing the place, who is present and what is happening. Pictures, music and sounds are also welcomed for this task, as well as using gestures, body language and your own sight's direction and focus to help players picture size, speed and mood of whatever you're describing. You can do it in many ways, but you should always focus on what the PCs can perceive from their points of view while considering all the senses that can give them relevant information. Doubles as a pacing mechanism as the Narrator can even advance time and jump to any place, or interrupt a conversation that is distracting the players by using it.

What do you do? The Narrator will use this question to signal players that her description is over and ask for their input. Players take turns to do so or talk with a Caller that then will explain the feedback of the whole group to the Narrator. Players must make sure that everyone gets an opportunity to answer before moving on to another round of the game. The Narrator can address this question to either the group or an specific player as required by the fiction.

Can I do X? Before deciding to take an action players can ask if it is possible and get some clues about the difficulty or consequences. The narrator answers by confirming or adding details. IMPORTANT: The character can only react based on what he knows as a character, not what the player knows. If the player knows that there is a monster but his character does not, he can not act as if he suspected or knew.

Yes, but Y could happen The usual answer to the previous question, also know as setting the stakes. Sometimes risks are not clear or get misheard/misinterpreted from the narrator's description. Setting the stakes is an important stage to make player's decisions matter. If possible the narrator should explain the risks and consequences even if no one asks.

Is there Z there? Players can use this question to suggest and / or verify the existence of something or someone not explicitly described by the narrator, but whose existence makes sense. If the narrator describes that PCs are in a smithy, anyone can assume that there is a hammer around and ask to confirm it. The narrator can then either confirm or deny it, though only with a plausible excuse.

What do you think? Sometimes the Narrator does not have all the answers, just when players seem to be asking something related to their own expectations. The narrator can always return the question to the player so that a clearer or fresh idea about what he hopes to find in fiction. In any case with this question the Storyteller can always decide if he takes and uses the player's response as it is or if he can improve it or simply discard and move on.

What you see is ... This sentence serves two functions. On the one hand, when the previous question is used, it is sometimes necessary to edit the contributions of the players to adapt those to what has already been established in the game. If the setting is medieval historic and someone introduces Mickey Mouse as a character, the Narrator can edit the character to be the mouse pet of another character, that has decided to call it Mickey and the mouse does not speak nor is it different from a real mouse.

I go there. What do I see? What other things / people are there? Players can ask for further information about their environment whenever they feel necessary, keeping in mind the limitation of the viewpoint and senses of the characters. Seeing a battlefield from above isn't the same than trying to understand what's going on while you're in the middle of it.

Come on! Both the narrator and the players may not like something, either because the description is very graphic, touches an unpleasant subject or does it in an uncomfortable or even unsafe way. Whatever the case everyone at the table can use this phrase to ask for changes, quickly end a scene or even take a break to discuss it, if it's necessary. The game is supposed to be fun for everyone and although a little tolerance helps, no one has to put up with something that will ruin their whole experience.

I will do X Once the player believes he has enough information he must decide what his character will do. Even if you just want to wait and observe the events, you should also let the group know.

Ok, how do you do it? / why? The Narrator uses this questions to get enough player input on how actions will be performed and/or their intent, to determine whether a dice roll is necessary or not, whether there are circumstances that alter the difficulty of the roll and finally to narrate how the world changes correctly according to what the players said.

Does your character has / knows how to use / knows about N? - Sometimes the Storyteller will need to confirm if a character has an item, skill or knowledge that allows him to overcome a challenge or reduce his difficulty. Usually the response will be on the player's tab, but this could also be argued using any other circumstances that help. The narrator will be the one who finally decides their validity and relevance.

Roll the dice! Once the Narrator has determined if there is a challenge, what is it and its difficulty, she will ask the player to roll the dice to see what happens. This is where the conflict resolution mechanic will be executed beyond the point of no return.

What happens is that... when the result of the roll is known, the Storyteller describes what happens in the fiction as a result. If it affects another player character, the narration goes there immediately. When each player has participated at least once performing an action, it is time to pass to the next round of the game. The conversational cycle starts over or jumps from one point to another when necessary.
\nNow, my whole idea for my game was to make a hardcoded procedure that uses the ritual phrases listed above, to explain better how the game goes. I'll probably add some moves over it, as I finally got from the other thread another insight about how to use them better; but for now my main concern was this list of phrases, so I'd like to know if any of these look or feel totally unfamiliar (there might be a language barrier here as english is my second language) and if you use them differently, what kind of changes you apply on them, which of them you hate and why they don't work for you. Anything helps! thanks for your time!

    \nThis one is so powerful.

    It's a careful negotiation of narrative authority between players and GM. By asking, the player is asserting that Z is there. The GM must then prove that it isn't, or accept that it is. Presumably, if the GM says no, then the player might further negotiate the position. "No hammer? How does the blacksmith do their work?" If the GM doubles down and insists there is no hammer there, then a hammer not being there has now become Important.

    I think establishing Importance is a signaling game players and GMs do all the time. Without coming right out and saying it, players ask probing questions to determine what is important and what is not. GMs toy with players a bit to misdirect or create paranoia, by giving information that seems like clues because it plays with that signaling behavior. Like rolling dice for no reason, or describing the details on the floor.

    Of course, Does your character has / knows how to use / knows about N? and Ok, how do you do it? / why? are similar tools for the GM to challenge the player to prove their side of things. I'd argue that how do you do it? is fundamentally different than why do you do it?; the former establishes fictional positioning whereas the latter establishes player motive (or perhaps character motive, but that matters less).

    \n Amazing! I mean, it's not entirely as if everything here is totally new, but noticing these subtleties really makes a difference! Just edited some of the phrases and added a couple more, the new versions would look like:\n

    Can I do X? (also used as I try to judge if...)Before deciding to take an action players can ask if it is possible and get some clues about the difficulty or consequences. The narrator answers by confirming or adding details. IMPORTANT: The character can only react based on what he knows as a character, not what the player knows. If the player knows that there is a monster but his character does not, he can not act as if he suspected or knew.

    Is there Z there?or Do I see Z? Players can use this question to suggest and / or verify the existence of something or someone not explicitly described by the narrator, but whose existence makes sense. If the narrator describes that PCs are in a smithy, anyone can assume that there is a hammer around and ask to confirm it. The narrator can then either confirm or deny it, though only with a plausible excuse. If there's no such excuse, the absence of the object becomes important in the scene as a clue for further situation development in the fiction.

    in a similar fashion, questions like How about...? - Wouldn't be lucky...? or wouldn't make sense...? can be used by the players to give the Narrator suggestions of not only objects and characters in the scene but whole turns of events that could happen given the current circumstances. The Narrator would still be free to take those suggestions, edit, improve and make them happen whenever she wants. Making them happen in the spot may look as if the player just read the Narrator's mind, while using other event as misdirection before introducing the player's suggestion accomplishes a similar surprise effect.

    Come on! or Objection! (ALWAYS said with a smile!) Both the narrator and the players may not like something, either because the description breaks their immersion, is too graphic, touches an unpleasant subject or does it in an uncomfortable or even unsafe way. Whatever the case everyone at the table can use this phrase to ask for changes, quickly end a scene or even take a break to discuss it, if it's necessary. The game is supposed to be fun for everyone and although a little tolerance helps, no one has to put up with something that will ruin their whole experience.

    However it's of the same importance to express this discomfort as calmly and in good humour as possible. Normally no player will purposely include such content knowing it could hurt the sensibility of other player at the table, they would usually just assume everyone at the table shares her own tastes and/or tolerance for the subject and won't know they are at fault until somebody else reacts. So basically, both players need to measure themselves.

    Ok, how do you do it? The Narrator uses this question to get enough player input on how actions will be performed, to determine whether a dice roll is necessary or not, whether there are circumstances that alter the difficulty of the roll and finally to narrate how the world changes correctly according to what the players said. "I attack" doesn't give enough information to the Narrator about what kind of things could happen, even if the roll for the attack is a critical. And while everything can be narrated based on the roll, what happens then is that the roll is actually robbing the player a lot of the meaning of her decision.

    why would you do that? Sometimes the intent and motivations behind the player's actions aren't clear for the narrator, and may seem to be totally out of character -thus breaking the Narrator and other player's immersion. It could be just a misunderstanding though. This question gives the player a chance to either rethink their actions, justify them or at least try to come up with a plausible excuse. Failing to provide a proper answer may lead the Narrator and other players to offer proper suggestions for a better motivation, a similar action more in line with the character concept and drives, or a different, more plausible reaction for the character. Or the use of the phrase Come on! as seen above.

    Does your character has / knows how to use / knows about N? - Sometimes the Storyteller will need to confirm if a character has an item, skill or knowledge that allows him to overcome a challenge or reduce his difficulty. Usually the response will be on the character's sheet, but this could also be argued using any other circumstances already established in the fiction that could help. The narrator will be the one who finally decides their validity and relevance.
    \nAlso, while lurking at the fictional causality vs resolving ambiguity thread I thought of this phrase I use on those cases. It's more of a crude fix than a proper technique, though. Eventually I'll have to make a list of these fixing phrases too:\n

    What? a description, character reaction or mood can totally fail to make sense for the players, or just confuse them -though not to the point to trigger a Come on!. When hearing this question the Narrator should try to rephrase or briefly explain what\u2019s actually going on, even if this means -in an extreme case- revealing to the players information that their characters don\u2019t know. In such case, it\u2019s also good to remind them that their characters can\u2019t act on such information.

    \n Ok, just reading through Levity and trying to put my design this in it's terms. So far in this particular design:\n

    -Game fiction statements are immediately distinguished form other types by the opening phrase used to expose them to the other players. These phrases will resemble the ones listed above.

    -The Narrator is held responsible of limiting the boundaries of the fiction, applying her own parameters of what is plausible and/or nice. However, players can negotiate and make suggestions by using the phrases listed above.

    -It's important that most of those suggestions are phrased as questions by the players, since it's thanks to recognition of the Narrator's authority that a good deal of the negotiation can be skipped. Whenever a player makes an statement clearly outside the bounds of her character expressing it as a fact instead of a question, the Narrator's authority is damaged. A good degree of trust and knowing the Narrator enough to suggest only things that appeal to her parameters of what's plausible and/or nice may suffice to dispense of this though. And statements entirely in the scope of their own character are exempt of this practice, of course.

    -If a Narrator is asked for validation for their choices and can't present a proper argument the. It's then quickly consulted to the group (Hmmm, this would need it's own ritual phrase, I believe) to either confirm or deny it. It's the same case when a player's argument for their choices is the one that doesn't seem to be neither plausible, congruent or nice.

    -For the Essences, the players have to decide ans clearly express what they want to happen in the fiction, however limitations apply here: For things outside the scope of their characters, they have to express them as questions. For things that are inside the scope of their characters, they can just make statements.

    Also, this design accepts the fact that not every player is always aware of their game expectations, nor is always even able to express them on time, nor accurately. So it's part of the Narrator's responsibilities to check each player's character sheet, ask them questions about their particular choices, and use those answers to build the setting and push foward the fiction.

    -Power of narration is usually divided between the narrator -whose scope is the NPCs and the world around the characters, and the players -whose scope is limited to their own characters. In cases where a PC isn't present at the scene, the Narrator could assign it's player a limited scope of the fiction in the form or an NPC, a monster, etc. It may also fall in territory of randomization under the form of reading the dice or using random tables.

    -Power of opposition is dynamically divided between the players and the narrator. If it's the players against the environment, the Narrator will have opposing rights; if it's PvP then involved players will have opposing rights. Sometimes the player will be able to roleplay her way around a conflict; this isn't only expected but recommended.

    -Power of resolution is handled by the dice whenever negotiation reaches a wall- which usually means two arguments and at least one quick poll, tops. Either the Narrator or the players are free to call for the dice roll. Though, for in-fiction opposition at least, the players roll to not fail and the Narrator defines a difficulty number, that can be negotiated up or down depending on the player's actions and world variables.

    \nI think that's basically it? I'll double check tomorrow if I can but if you spot anything important missing feel free to point me to it.
