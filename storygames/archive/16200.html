\n Hey there, flamers and trolls - just keep on surfin', nothing to see here.
This is a critique of a game that i played at a con the other day.

The game is an on-rails scenario, and there are some spoilers, if that's a worry for you.

My background with Larps: Playing a bit of Vampire and Werewolf in the late nineties. Reading a few things about Scandinavian larps. Watching the film "Monster Camp" about NERO, which is a tap-them-with-your-stick-and-say-numbers action larp. Hearing accounts from a few people around my town of their experiences with a game that Ross Cowman used to run here, called Fantasy Capture The Flag.

My background with Shelter In Place: heard about while it was being kickstarted, really like the cover, believe that it came out of the "story games community" & so therefore would prioritize stuff that i care about even though a lot of disparate games come out of this community. I saw some shaky dark video in which jason morningstar was ducking out of a tent but i couldn't really see what was going on so i didn't watch much. Harry (who moderated for us) had it on the convention registry thing & so i signed up excitedly with a lot of other people. I respect Harry a lot and love playing tabletop games with him. I knew shelter in place was about zombies & hadn't read anything about it.

My expectations: That i would run around trying to avoid zombies, that there would be some elegant abstracted way for the zombies and humans to physically interact, that the humans would have fun goals that would be doomed, that i would get to fluidly become a zombie in a way that was satisfying and then obey a different set of rules. I didn't think there'd be much story creation - there isn't really room to create new material in a zombie story. I expected to portray a generic human who was very similar to myself.


How it went down.

Harry had assembled a lot of props, taped rules to them and scattered them about a room which was one-third of a ballroom. It was pretty sparse, terrain-wise, but it was still interesting. Reminded me of DOOM style games, where there's just hallways and bonus stuff. In the middle of the room was a craft-fair-style tent, dilapidated and set up with cubical dividers as walls. There was a Door and a Window clearly marked with signs on the ground. It looked super promising, and myself and around 20 other players divided ourselves into two groups and, as Mathalus would say, vibrated with excitement.

We were handed rules sheets to read, which were two-page spread blocks of text. I was a zombie, and told that i'd be a human in game two. The mechanics for combat, which seemed to be the thing that the zombies were trying to make happen in the game, seemed simple.

We started play, with the humans outside the room. I wandered around as a zombie, dragging a foot, flopping back and forth, letting my spine be fluid. That part was fun. The humans suddenly ran into the room! I shambled towards them, and they all ran into the shelter. My fellow zombies and i prowled around outside the tent.

In fact, the game was fun up until the time when I interacted with the mechanics.

Most combats called for a "time out, moderator come untangle this for us." The zombies were universal, but the human team each had an exception-based rule of some kind, which led to immediate communication breakdown. In short, our first problem was a pedagogy one: we zombies didn't know to expect that the humans could all do different stuff and the humans didn't know one another's powers. Since things had to be paused but the game instructed us zombies to moan and the humans were excitedly yelling at one another, AND the setup had the "shelter" blocking sightlines, there were no universal timeouts. While the moderator was untangling something, other zombies would shamble up and try to interact with it. For example, one of the humans "counts" as being involved in a combat if he's not touching the other people in the combat [where most combats count only the participants who are touching one another] and zombies wouldn't realize that he's part of that combat b/c he's jumping around and yelling encouragement.

There was a lot of counting in the game. When a zombie attacked a human, they counted to three to start combat, then counted to ten to eat the human. The 3-count was so that other people could join in by touching, and the 10-count was so that more humans could show up and start a new three-count combat. After each three count, the number of players involved on each side count up their individual numbers (in act one, humans get 2 adds and zombies get 1... but individual humans can gain additional adds for completing mini goals. More on those in a sec.) and numbers from any weapons that they're carrying. This is not negligible time. More people gather, another combat starts on the tail of that last one, more counting happens. When a zombie is destroyed, they watch a clock for a minute to come back as another zombie.

The counting felt like work, or like punishment. Why are we being made to count so much? When do i get to run around again? The problem of combat worsened when the zombies laid siege to the shelter - now the humans also had a basic Door or Window add, plus barricade adds from pieces of furniture strewn about for that purpose... and the combatants weren't within one another's sightlines, because some were in the shelter and some outside. Won or lose, the zombies destroyed the barricades - but if they lost, they couldn't attack again for 10 seconds, and one of the humans could fix furniture if they counted for a minute. Lots of counting.

The mini goals, which the humans could do to give themselves more adds for combat, were each unique to one human. At least one of them (get everyone to stay in the shelter for the whole first act) rewarded a terrible strategy. So whoever got that character has a secretly sucky character, who will fuck the humans over if people listen to them. Quick note - the wall of text was super hard when i became a human, trying to tease information out of the "flavor text" that i skimmed to find out the bonuses my character gave me. (I was supposed to be a businessman. I was so filled with adrenaline, though, that i just wanted to portray a scared human.) The mini goals in general i guess were in the game to have the humans have something to do... i feel like they were a bad way to do that. One good thing about them: at least three of the goals had a human trying to address the group and convince people to do something. The desperate yelling that took place resulted in confusion, which was very fruitful.

So, yeah, this act structure. Both times we played, the humans built a machine partway through the first act and that meant that they got to skip the second act and all but five minutes of the third act. Weird. I guess the five machine parts (brilliantly portrayed in our game by an N64, controller, rumble pak, etc.) are the plot device to end the game, but it was really disconcerting to have the moderator pause the game, advance us to almost the end, then all the numbers for our personal adds change we spend the last few minutes having to double-check our math and making bad calls because we think we're stronger than we are (as humans).

Also, spoiler alert: It seems that there's one near bulletproof strategy for the humans in act one: get the kid (who can't be bitten by zombies, though he can be engaged in combat) to roam around and pick up all the items and bring them to the shelter. Zombies can't attack the shelter in the first act. All the other humans, do nothing. Zombies, to counter-attack, spam-combat the kid. You can't kill her, but you can maybe slow her down long enough for the first act to end (half an hour) and then you can attack the shelter.

I think, as come to in conversation about this, that the designers were more interested in being faithful to movie tropes than focusing on the experience of the players.


What was fun: Being excited about the game before playing. Standing around as attendance was being called (to let in overflow sign-up if people were missing) and being stoked that i was gonna run around and do a physical thing with some of my friends who i see at conventions and don't really get to do physical things with (Johnzo, Jonathan Walton, etc, etc.) Zombie-ing around at the beginning. Hitting the cubical dividers and raoaring. Running (as a human) into the room and into the shelter. Stepping out of the shelter momentarily was exciting and nerve-wracking, which was fun in this context. Most important: Doing a new, weird thing with people i love a lot was really fun.

What was not fun: Counting down for any reason. Counting how many people were frozen in a combat. Watching other players try to play effectively and having that be undercut by special rules that they didn't know, whether they were on items that other people were holding or other people's character sheets. Having numbers and rules that i was learning how to compare suddenly change on me with the changing acts. Having stereotyped human characters. Having the most effective strategy be for the humans to stay in the shelter. Most important: Stopping running around for any reason (in this case, constant rules clarification & counting.)


This game is hard to teach without playing it, or seeing it played. It seems more like a sport than a game - if you already knew what the different positions (aka characters) could do, and knew the numbers really well, i bet it could be fun. As it stands, we had fun despite it.

I'm very happy i played. Harry was a responsive moderator and a great host. I loved the work that he put into the props and shelter. The game could've obviously used a little more space and terrain, but i think we all made do quite well. Most important: now several of my cohorts and i are talking about LARPs again, excited about the possibilities of using our bodies in play.


I wonder what situation this game is meant to be played in?

Any and all thoughts about Shelter in Place are welcomed, be they by people who've played, heard about, read it, whatever.

    \n I'm the one who ran the game Jackson's talking about here, and I'm really happy that he's started this conversation. I had a good time when we played the other night, but it was definitely a learning experience and there's A LOT that I would do differently if I could do it over or ever decide to run it again. I don't necessarily want to speak to the mechanical details of the game quite yet, because I think a lot of the trouble folks experienced with rules confusion was my responsibility. I'd never run a LARP before, and there were a couple of key factors that I hadn't considered when we started playing, both of which had a bit to do with having much more experience with tabletop games.

    The first was time management. I scheduled a two-hour slot, because my understanding was that the game took a couple of hours, and I really wanted to play later on Saturday, so when it was slotted between another LARP and the midnight game of Werewolf, I figured that would work out perfectly. I'd heard that it was really important to go through the full range of resolution rules before play, so as to make sure everything's clear for everyone, but I'd imagined that would take maybe ten minutes, and then we could get started without a whole lot of delay. What I'd failed into take into account was waiting for stragglers, taking attendance (!), bathroom breaks, etc. By the time we got started with rules stuff, it was close to twenty minutes into our slot already. If this had been a tabletop game, I wouldn't have sweated it; we could always work together to speed the game up or end on a cliffhanger or whatever, and there would be a break after our game, so it would be no big deal if we went a little over. But SIP games run on a timer, and there were folks waiting to use the room right after we finished, so I felt like we should start as quickly as possible, and I really breezed through the rules.

    Handily enough, that leads into my second issue. When I play tabletop games, and especially when I teach them, I almost always like to get started as quickly as possible, bringing in the rules a little at a time as needed, rather than frontloading everything at the very beginning. That wasn't even something I was consciously thinking of when we were starting SIP, but it became clear pretty quickly that an approach like that doesn't work for a twenty-person LARP - there was too much going on to keep everyone on the same page as rules questions came up, and everything ground to a halt whenever we had to sort something out. In addition to that, one of the things I wasn't sure about was how much the players should know about the different items and everyone's powers beforehand, and I decided to err on the side of secrecy (or surprise), since that's what characters like The Evolved Zombie from our second round seemed to encourage. As we played, though, that seemed to cause much more confusion than it was worth.

    In the end, Ross, I agree with you about elegance and transparency. Just out of curiosity, do you think there's a way to present SIP (as written) that might have preserved that? For example, if everyone wore a 9"x12" card with their resolution number and any special powers on it (preferably boiled down to a short phrase), would that have mitigated things at all?

    Anyway, thanks again to Jackson for starting this thread. It was really interesting to see how everything shook out the other night, and I'd love to hear from other folks that have played or run the game about what worked well for them and what they ended up stumbling over.

    \n Jackson and Harry's experiences sound pretty much exactly like mine. As I said in discussions later on, I think there's some technology that's typically used by larps in these kinds of situations (holding up fingers to represent your current value, or writing numbers on nametags or something), but none of us were experienced with any of that and the more experienced larpers who showed up to play seemed to be following our chaotic lead. Stopping to do math seemed to bring the drama of the game to a complete halt, as the frantic screaming gave way to a bunch of folks all trying to verify who had won, but -- as Jackson says -- typically this was happing irregularly all over the playing space, and the loads of varying rules meant that it happened uniquely every time, making it hard to feel like you had any real sense of what you were supposed to do in human-zombie combat (the core of the game).

    Like Harry, I feel like it's hard to draw really solid conclusions about the mechanics from our first attempt at this, since a bunch of things happened that seemed to not be what the rules intended (like the machine being really easy to assemble in Act I, combat being totally baffling). I can imagine that -- if the larp took place in a much bigger space and the locations of the item weren't very clear -- the game could feel a lot more like running around playing capture the flag in a forest, and the 30-minute time limit on the first act would make a lot more sense, with the humans and zombies roaming around a large area, not often coming into close contact. Maybe that's what's intended? I haven't read the rules, so I don't know.

    \n Seems to me this larp is an example that mechanics in a larp should be kept at an absolute minimum. Too much of them weakens the "live action" part of live action role-playing. Having to stop interaction due to "counting" (or other strange mechanics), in conflicts, is really the antithesis to the form of live action role-playing.

    Giving rules that makes interaction flow on through conflicts, is a key to making larps that takes the players into the realm of intuitive physical interaction. Try to shed yourself of the competitive stance, and the mechanical debris of rpgs, and you may have a chance at designing and playing larps that blows your mind away.

    \n So, I played in the SiP game that Harry ran. I was on the opposite team from Jackson, Ross, Hans and JWalt. I had a blast! But I wasn't immune to the grinding halt of rules confusion or the limitations of the play space.

    I was the businessman, and I had tons of fun with my goals and powers: I got a permanent bonus if I managed to lead everyone out of the shelter at once in Act 1, and I gave a bonus to a fight by yelling encouragement. The great thing about those two rules was that it put everyone into frantic conflict: fruitless argument with "stay in the shelter" guy, and blind terror and refusal when people begged me to help them in a fight. it didn't seem to me that the Goals were there to be completed (though bully for you if you do! I almost had mine, but one person decided to "stay behind ad guard the supplies."), but to create incompatible demands on each other and make tension and (fictional) tempers soar.

    I had a great dynamic with Zach ("stay in shelter guy") and Johnzo ("Let us all pray for deliverance,") and when I got bit Zach had a great "He's infected, don't let him in!" moment with the Doctor who was dragging me to safety. The Doctor miming slapping me to my senses as he treated me was a great bit that possibly nobody else noticed.

    That, in fact, it was extremely striking to me how much in LARP, moreso than any other roleplaying form, everyone's experiences differ. You may have a moment with one or two other people that most of the group simply doesn't witness, and that's just the way it works. I was really proud of my initial snooty corporate stooge interactions "Don't you know who I AM?!" "My company MAKES the weapons you take over to Afghanistan, who are you to bark orders at ME?") but it's possible none of y'all even heard them. similarly, other folks on my team told me about hilarious shit you zombies were doing--like Ross commenting on the quality of the furniture as he chewed on it, that i never noticed. Even in this small, very constrained game (spacewise, timewise, premisewise) most of us weren't aware of most of what was happening outside our bubble. I don't know if that's a feature, a bug, or just a thing.

    (I just realized I was telling everyone this was my first LARP, but of course I played in The Upgrade! two years ago, hosted by Morningstar and Macklin. Which was hilarious and rad. Of course, that was a Jeepform, thus a divergent tradition, and didn't have the above quality near as much even with a similarly large cast, partly because you're all focused on what's "on screen" for the reality show.)

    Since I was high on my really rad experience at the start of the game, the rules things didn't bug me so much--the confusion and issues were there, but they slid off my back a bit. By the time I got to be a Zombie for the second game, they were starting to stand out more.

    A lot could be solved by, as people said above, having clear markers of who's got what. Even just fictionally, knowing who's the kid, especially when he's a dude taller than me, would really help with roleplaying interactions. I still don't know what character Zach had. And for the sake of points, yeah, delineate that shit. Any handling time you can shave off of ANY game is a boon, and LARP moreso times a billion.

    The other thing that could help is a clear audible signal for timeouts. We tried handclaps for the second game, but it didn't seem to help much. Some kind of whistle or something, piercing but not painfully/annoyingly slow, could work wonders. 1 blast, everyone IN THE GAME freeze, 2 blastes everyone resume. (If you play in a larger space, this wouldn't work, but playing in a larger space would alleviate a lot of the difficulties anyway.)

    Neither of these solutions will take away the "Counting ain't fun, it jarringly halts the tension and excitement") felt experience that a lot of us had. But they could make the game as designed run a lot smoother.

    Peace,
    -Joel

    PS for those confused by the "skip to the end of the game" rule, it made a lot of sense in context: the game timer represents the time the humans have to hold out to get airlifted to safety. the device you assemble is a transmitter; once constructed you can call early for an airlift. when we got ours assembled in the first game, there was palpable sense of achievement and relief, followed by panic as the zombies renewed their assault. I found this mechanic a feature rather than a bug, but I understand what people are saying about its unpleasant side effects, particularly catapulting you into a new game phase when you were barely getting the procedure right in the first phase.

    Such things invariably end up with half the players (or more), hanging around doing nothing while they wait for moderator(s) to handle other parts of the game.

    In my experience, there are only two ways to minimise this.

    One has been brought up previous posts along the thread...get everyone to know the various interactions of the rules. This has the drawback that if someone has a special talent that causes some kind of quirky effect capable of twisting the storyline, the suspense of this character's storyline is muted. Everyone knows what this character will end up doing, so it becomes less a case of "What will happen?" and more a case of "When will it happen?".

    The second is using multiple GMs/Moderators. These are not quasi-players who run around playing NPCS to push the storyline, these are purely moderators at the beck and call of the players for mechanic resolution. The recommended ratio is one moderator per five players, plus a lead moderator; a twenty player game ends up with five moderators. Personally, for this sort of game I use 1990's technology; two way radio headsets. I've got about eight of them on the same frequency for really big games. They allow the moderators to keep in regular contact, explaining changes to the setting across the team very quickly (without the need to physically move together and lock up the players access to moderation staff).

    A lot of lead GMs don't like having team of assistant GMs or a moderation staff, because it takes some of the control out of their hands. But in this sort of game, I consider it mandatory.
