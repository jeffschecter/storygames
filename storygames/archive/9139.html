\n Last week we had two gaming newbies at the table, so I made a game package inspired by the things they like: Firefly, World of Warcraft, and Laputa (Castle in the Sky). The system is my homebrew fusion of TSOY, The Pool, and Mouse Guard. Our first playtest went very well -- the newbies are coming back for more! -- so I expanded the PDF a bit, added artwork, and here it is.





[Lady Blackbird PDF Link] 3.7 megs

The PDF has a setting guide, starting situation (Poison'd style), pregen characters, and an airship data sheet. It's presented "oracle style" with plenty of suggested bits for you to fill in as you play.

Let me know what you think. I had so much fun making this, I might keep expanding it with more ships, NPCs, locations, etc. We'll see...

    \n For other ideas on how to do Sorcery in TSOY-ish style games, I'd like to mention my character's summoning ability (which dovetails perfectly with this system).

    When I wanted to summon a creature, I had to determine what kind of creature it was and go about finding expensive materials to "pay them" with. For a spirit of an ancient air whale, I had to lace our airship-to-be with diamonds throughout, which became its circuitry. For a confidence spirit, I "gave" it all the beers in a tavern (had to discretely leave after that, imagine the patrons' surprise and anger!) This was all in-game description.

    Then, the storyteller roleplayed with me a bit, taking on the role of the spirit as I explained my dilemna. When done, I rolled my summoning ability. The number of success I got gave me free dice later on for any roll where the spirit's blessing came into effect. When doing some mad piloting, I called on the air whale. When bluffing my way into a compound, I called on the confidence spirit.

    When all the dice were gone, the pact was over and the spirit returned to the spirit world. They basically used me or something I focused them into as a host to explore our world again. However, can you imagine the sticky situation I was in when the air whale departed and the airship began descending!

    \n Here's a thing we do that didn't find a place in the text:

    Ask questions -- lots and lots and make them pointed toward the things you're interested in. Like, Cyrus gives Naomi an order within earshot of Lady Blackbird, but the Lady's player doesn't register it right away. Naomi goes to do the thing ordered. So I ask Lady Blackbird's player, "How do you react when the Captain orders your bodyguard around? Is that okay with you?" And then, when it's totally not okay, "What do you say to him? What do you say to Naomi?" and a few more like that and everyone is yelling at each other and rolling dice to impose their wills.

    Also ask questions like "Does anything break when you do this crazy maneuver?" and "The fire probably spreads out of control doesn't it?" and "Are you going to just stand there and take this crap?" and "Do you two end up somewhere quiet together? Does something happen between you?"

    Keep that going at a steady pace and the game flies along pretty well.

    \n I know some people are running this soon, so here's another couple things we did that aren't spelled out in the rules.

    When it's character vs. character, each person rolls their dice. Most hits wins. Ties mean a stalemate (and you escalate!), with the option to commit more pool dice and re-roll. Sometimes you might roll dice for an NPC (a boss could have their own pool if you're so inclined) but usually they're just obstacles.

    If you set up something that gives you an advantage in a future situation, you can call on that thing as a Trait with tags. Like, in our game, Cyrus and Kale stole a bunch of guns from the armory on the Hand of Sorrow. So we wrote on Cyrus' sheet: Guns > High quality, Imperial. The plan was to use them as trading goods when they got to Haven. In a negotiation, I could say, "Sure, I'll hook you up with the pirate clan, but it's gonna cost you. How about those shiny new guns back there?" and Cyrus could erase the guns from his sheet in exchange for help. Or, if there's an obstacle and it comes to dice, Cyrus' player could call on up to 3 dice from the Guns trait to assist his roll. Generally, these advantage traits are temporary and less important than PC traits. There shouldn't be a lot of them floating around, or they'll overtake the game. Just reserve them for special situations when you really need to highlight an important advantage, not for every little thing. (For incidental, minor situational advantages, just give a bonus die.)

      \n Here's the current "Running the Game" text. Thoughts and suggestions welcome.

      Listen & Ask Questions, Don't Plan
      When you\u2019re the GM, don\u2019t try to plan what will happen. Instead, ask questions\u2014lots and lots and make them pointed toward the things you\u2019re interested in. Like, Cyrus gives Naomi an order within earshot of Lady Blackbird, but the Lady\u2019s player doesn\u2019t register it right away. Naomi goes to follow the order. So I ask Lady Blackbird\u2019s player, \u201cHow do you react when the Captain orders your bodyguard around? Is that okay with you?\u201d And then, when it\u2019s totally not okay, \u201cWhat do you say to him? What do you say to Naomi?\u201d and a few more like that and everyone is yelling at each other and rolling dice to impose their will.

      Also ask questions like:
      \u201cDoes anything break when you do this crazy maneuver?\u201d
      \u201cThe fire probably spreads out of control doesn\u2019t it?\u201d
      \u201cThat sounds like a bold plan. What\u2019s the first step?\u201d
      \u201cDo the two of you end up somewhere quiet together? Does something happen between you?\u201d
      \u201cDo you know anything about the Black Wind Pirates? What are they like? Is it normal for them to be this far into the Empire?\u201d

      Keep that going at a steady pace and the game flies along pretty well.

      Part of the job of the GM is listening to what the players say, catching it, turning it around and looking at it, and seeing if there\u2019s anything else to be done with it. The GM is there to listen and reincorporate, play the NPCs with passion, and create interesting obstacles.

      Say Yes, Look For the Obstacles
      By default, characters can accomplish anything covered by their traits. They\u2019re competent and effective people, in other words. It\u2019s no fun to ask for a roll when there\u2019s no cool obstacle in the way. Just say yes to the action, listen, and ask questions as usual. But also, be on the look out for the opportunity to create obstacles as the action develops. Because you\u2019re asking leading questions and listening closely, they\u2019ll be all over the place, so it won\u2019t be too hard to spot them.

      Obstacles can be people (pirates, goblins, imperials, citizens, nobles), weather, monsters (sky squid, flying eels), situations (fires, falling, being shot at, chases, escapes) or anything else you can imagine.

      If a character tries something not covered by their traits, that\u2019s an obstacle right there: lack of experience and training. Lots of fun things can go wrong when you don\u2019t know what you\u2019re doing! So go ahead and call for rolls there. Also, players will sometimes try things they\u2019re bad at so they can fail and add dice to their pool. It\u2019s a fine move for them and it gives you the chance to create more trouble, so everyone wins.

      Limits
      Some people feel better when there are hard limits. If you need them, you can use these.

      Maximum no. of tags on one trait: 12
      Maximum no. of keys per character: 5
      Maximum no. of secrets per character: 7

      Using limits will naturally give players some harder choices to make, so only use them if you encounter an issue that will be solved with limits or if the players request them.

      Conditions: Conditions effect the fiction, which might have a mechanical effect. If you're Injured, for example, that's an obstacle if you try to run somewhere quickly, so you'd probably roll for that, when you wouldn't otherwise. Of the GM might increase the ob level for a roll due to a condition. Or, the GM might escalate or push harder because of certain conditions being true.

      Traits and Tags: Remember that you can only apply the tags from one trait when you roll. So when you give yourself a new Trait, it is only worth one die until you add tags to it. Sometimes you want to improve something you're already good at instead of adding something new (and not being very effective with it).

      Limits: No, there's no limit to the number of Traits you can have.

      But it also points to the possibility of an incremental design/layout process wherein each step along the way is also a playable game. Geiger Counter has evolved and is continuing to evolve this way. The alpha draft was about as long as Lady Blackbird (8 pages) and the beta draft was about twice that length (16 pages).       And incremental design is potentially more manageable for most people than sitting down and writing 100 pages. You start out writing less than 10 pages for a very specific audience of people you know. And then can expand from there to make the game playable by other audiences or to add more functionality or tweak things. I don't think this necessarily means you end up with 100 pages, but I could imagine a version of Lady Blackbird that was 24 pages, say, including the intro scenario, a more thorough explanation of using the mechanics, and a bunch of other hot shit. I'm not saying John should necessarily aim for that. He has no responsibility to me or anyone else and the game is hot, hot shit just as it is. But I think it's only natural, in some ways, for him to continue to add to or edit the document as he continues to play the game. And this is a lot easier to do when you're working with less than a dozen pages as opposed to 100-some.

      \nCliff Notes:
      PCs:
      Snargle (me), A gnomelike creature in old-school sailor suit
      Kale (Chris W-The New Guy) a thief and clockwork expert from a swiss family
      Naomi (Chris P) a Samoan bodyguard ( a bit like a cave-woman, actually) secretly in love with her charge, Lady Blackbird

      NPCs:
      Vance: A Han Solo Type with a fake eyepatch
      Lady Blackbird: A cheddar knight disquised as a peasant

      The crew starts in the Brig, which is an enormous metal cage with an equally gimormous combination lock, like something from a steamtech bank vault. We know the Owl is tethered outside, and that we are somewhere down in the lowest part of the Hand of Sorrow, below the rewinding facilities for the clockwork stormtroopers. we can hear some clanking around on watch outside our cell.

      The initial breakout:
      Snargle gets all chaos-warp shifty and goes "form of...a snake!" and squeezes throught the tiny opening in the cell, with Naomi pushing his bottom side from within the cell. The effect is something like using a pastry bag. In any case, Snargle pops out on the other side. Now what? Seconds to go before the clockwork guards notice!

      Kale's player starts decribing her semi-detachable index finger, which is also a sort of safe-cracker/lockpicker leatherman tool. Semi-detachable, because it's held in place to her hand by an extremely thin ( spider web sized), but nigh unbreakable, chord made from the whiskers of space walruses.

      ( This was about the point that the rest of us realized that Chris W was a ntatural at this sort of thing, despite his protests that he wasn't _really_ a gamer).

      Anyway, the finger gets passed to Snargle, having been programmed to open the lock, based on a back and forth convo between Kale and Snargle, describing the mechanism.

      Just as the mechanism is releasing, the clockworks show up. Snargle attempts to trip them with the finger as a trip line ( still attached to Kale in the cell, but botches it. Clockworks proceed to pummel snargle with rifle butts. At least it wasn't a pistol-whipping for a change! ( In joke. Never mind)

      Naomi uses her neanderthal-like strength to force the cell doors open faster, then jumps out and squishes the clockworks quite thoroughly with her bare hands. Like, seriously, flattens their heads together like a sammich.

      A quick rest and recovery, and then the crew is off into the bowels of the ship to try to secure some fuel. This then involves the hijacking of an internal supply train, multiple failed magic-use rolls by Kale, and multiple failed moving manuever rolls by Snargle, with more pummelings involved. Also, an attempt to surrender by a surrounded wounded, trapped, angry Snargle, which fails, so he whips up on his would be captors instead, aided by a quick rescue by Naomi and Kale.

      Finally, we seize the train, smash through the doors to the gangplank/cargoplatform where the Owl is located, shoot and run over clockworks in the process, and shoot our way away from the Hand of Sorrow. Pwew-pwew-pwew, crash, crash, crash--WooHaaa!!!!

      Um, then I get the real-world afternoon, post-lunch sleepies. We do quick refreshment seens tha are vague to me ( well except for Chris P's "Naomi gives Lady Blackbird a massage" refreshment scene) and we call it a day.

      \n The game went great. My wife played Lady Blackbird, my 11 year-old daughter played Naomi the bodyguard, and my 15 year-old daughter chose Snargle the goblin pilot.

      The players were a little lost about what to do, so to start things off I had Cyrus order his first mate, Kale, to pick the cell lock. After fiddling with it a little, he said it would be easy, but it would set off the alarm... (Thanks for the tip John!) The players then debated various plans before proceeding. Lady Blackbird used a lightning spell to disable the alarm, then the mate unlocked the door. When a couple of guards came with their meals, the bodyguard took them out with much alacrity (and 11 year-old glee). The lady and the goblin put on the uniforms and escorted the others out of the brig. They neatly bluffed their way past the first pair of soldiers they came by, then found the ladder to the upper decks. Once they oriented themselves, Cyrus and the first mate (NPCs in this game) split off and went forward to disable the wireless and perhaps sabotage the piloting controls, while the PCs went to the rear deck to free the Owl.

      Along the way back, the PCs came across the entrance to the engine room. Naomi punched out the burly chief engineer and took his coverall. She then watched out while the Lady and Snargle sabotaged the boilers. Then they headed up to the top rear deck to find the Owl guarded and still chained to the Hand of Sorrow's yardarm. The players were dithering about what to do, when the ship's general alarm and battle-stations sounded. (Presumably due to actions by Cyrus and Kale.) The now alert guards questioned the group as to what they knew about the alarm. Thinking quickly the PCs explained that the prisoners had escaped and they were sent to secure the Owl, while he and his men were to search the engine room and the maintenance ducts for the escaped prisoners. My wife rolled for Lady Blackbird's bluff and command and got 7 successes!

      While the others rush aboard, Naomi snaps a chain link and frees the Owl from the yardarm. Snargle gets the Owl up and running in no time flat (6 successes). The narration is the Owl slides off the back of the Hand and dives straight down to "jump start" her engines. As she levels off just above the Fog, they see two uniformed figures fall from the bow of The Hand of Sorrow. Snargle guns the engines to reach them before they hit the Fog, while Naomi rides up top to snag them as they come down. I decided both actions would require 5 successes, but that any piloting successes over 5 would reduce the difficulty of catching the men. The rolls were successful and the session ended with Cyrus and Lady Blackbird dancing a merry jig aboard the Owl.

      The only PC injury was to Snargle when my daughter failed the roll to sabotage the boilers. I ruled that Snargle burned his hand severely in the process, but managed to do the deed.

      I think next session will start with Cyrus collapsing from a grievous injury to his side incurred during his escape from the Hand of Sorrow...

      \n I played Lady Blackbird last Wednesday night. It was only a short sessions - about two hours - but everyone had a blast. There were four players - three played a number of story-games before, the fourth had only played D&D (religiously for the last twenty years). Snargle was the only character not played (he fell into the background).
      \n
      \nI started the game by para-phrasing the first page, giving the back story and then let the players read their character background. Each player instantly got into their characters and grabbed hold of one or two of the keys (Brad, playing Cyrus focussed on his hidden love for Lady Blackbird, and his expectation for orders to be followed; Jason playing Kale focussed on always obeying his captain's orders; Brett's Lady Blackbird was an arrogant, aloof noblewoman and parogon of the Empire, Nathan's Naomi was devoted to Lady Blackbird and ruthlessly dispatched any agents of the Empire she encountered). At the start of the game we had some discussion about how gravity worked and came to similar conclusions to those John has now described (the corrosive mist was at the "bottom" somewhere). We were unsure if the worlds were "real" worlds, or flat-topped mountains / chuncks of rock. We kind of left it undecided and it didn't come up in play.
      \n
      \nThe characters escaped the brig thanks to Naomi being an ex-slave and knowing how to escape her confines (we forgot that she could destroy things with her hands!) and then explored the ship. The highlights were discovering a tranqualiser gun ("for hunting giant squid!"), setting the ship's kitchens on fire, and Naomi being shot with the tranqualiser gun! She suffered the "tired" condition because of the tranqualiser and "angry" at Kale for shooting her! There was already tension between Naomi and the crew of the Owl as the bodyguard didn't think the smugglers were looking out for her Lady in the way they should. Cyrus took advantage of being an ex-officer by knowing the "typical layout" of such ships as the Hand of Sorrow. Lady Blackbird ordered people around and refused to get her hands dirty, much to the frustration of the other characters!
      \n
      \nThe big finale for the episode (because, by the end I really could see us playing again, just like a series of steampunk Firefly) came when the characters destroyed the Hand of Sorrow's engines (in a giant explosion - of course - that ripped a hole in the side of the ship) and they dashed across the deck as it tilted - Titanic style - towards the depths of the blue. Characters tumbled across the deck, Lady Blackbird fell and both Naomi and Cyrus dived to save her. Naomi prevailed and Cyrus went over the edge of the deck, just scrambling to find a hand hold. Kale got into the Owl (tied to the hand of Sorrow and about to dissappear into the depths too), released the restraining ropes and started the engine. Naomi and Lady Blackbird scrambled on board and, with a lucky roll Kale brought the nose of the Owl up under Cyrus to rescue him (he could have teleported, but this was cooler). The episode ended with the characters salvaging the Hand of Sorrow's "Emergency Bouy" (jettisoned in the event of an accident, it contains the ship's log, manifest etc) which they will sell to the highest bidder at some point in the future (or be hunted by the Empire!). The Owl dissappeared into the blue, off to their next adventure. Roll credits.
      \n
      \nWe had a great time. The conditions were role played and Naomi's "tired" made things tougher for her (I raised the difficulties) during one scene. We had a refresh scene where the tired condition was removed, and because Naomi and Kale argued about the incident I also removed the angry condition.
      \n
      \nI kept asking questions and whenever the dice were rolled I left the players to describe what happened. When they were searching for something I asked "what are you looking for?" before they rolled, so we knew the broad paremeters for success and failure. I also made difficulties by saying things like "getting three successes will achieve X, but four successes will get you Y". Each player took to this very quickly, including the "newer" player. I didn't push the XP element so players concentrated on taking extra dice for hitting their keys. In future games this will likely change as the players consider the long-term "game plan". I really like the idea that gear and found items (and conditions) can provide temporary bonuses like tags - that will be somthing that I will include in future games. Likewise, the "exchange" of Keys - buy off an old one and take a new one so characters keep about three.
      \n
      \nGreat work. Loved it. I gushed about this game on my podcast - www.herebegamers.com . I will be running this as part of our Indie Games on Demand at Gen Con Oz this year.

      \n To me the setting does not make much sense, but I just try to find some kind of comfort zone to be able to play it:

      So I postulate that there is a general gravity field that creates the atmospheric gradient: lower layers are dense, corrosive vapors, higher layers are not dense enough to fly, and beyond that there is only space, which irrelevant for the game, at least initially.

      Because of the general gravity field, the worlds are not spheres (otherwise people would fall off, and there is gravity in ships as they travel between worlds). Instead worlds are flat and float simply because of the weird low density of their rocks, or due to some magical essence that they contain (much the Myazaki's explanation of Laputa's castle in the sky).

      One thing that bothers me is that there is no night in this setting. This is specially true if you have flat worlds, but still the case with spheres, since anyways there is an atmosphere continuously illuminated by a star. I thought about putting the setting in a gas giant, and have the planet rotation create the night and day, but then this system has no center anymore. So I guess one has to just accept that there is no night, like some polar summer. Obviously light intensity could go dimmer as you move away from the star, but that's hardly night and day, just some polar summer to polar winter transition.

      Finally there's the issue that in a flat universe the star would be hidden by clouds and worlds, besides having a continuous sunset mood. You could have the star at the center and the atmosphere wrapped around it like some hollow world, but that would make quite some disturbing skyscape. One option would be a whirlwind that causes gas rotation around the star and give some curvature to the atmosphere. The star would then be easily visible and kind of fixed in the sky.

      Nothing is really satisfactory when you think too deep about it, just hope that your players are not too much into physics while playing lady blackbird

      \n Start with easy questions. Concrete stuff works well: "Does that bit of the Owl break often?" "Have you run the comm system for a ship this huge before?"

      Question anything that hasn't been made explicit yet. Don't worry if the PDF or some other group says otherwise, it's your group's Wild Blue.

      As a vague guideline, ask a question per roll. Don't stick to this if it doesn't work, and don't always ask the person rolling. Just an idea to keep you asking.

      One of my personal favorite questions, when someone does something another character might object to: "Did you see that?" (or it's more general cousin, "What are you doing about that?").

      I might suggest that Lady Blackbird and Vance should go to people that can play off each other pretty well, but I wouldn't push anything too hard. Let people choose, it'll work out.

The poor ship is now low on fuel, busted and leaking, and crippled, since one of the turbines on the outside have been broken off.

He comes to pick them up in his own ship, the Sow (yep, as in adult female hog) and it's a very warm reunion for Cyrus and Ivan: when shown to Ivan's lounge Cyrus kicks the door open, pulls both pistols and starts shooting.       \n I ran Lady Blackbird this week. I am 5 session into a LB campaign in my regular gamenight, but hadn't really thought of the system as something to run before.
      \n
      \nI had finally managed to bring my work gaming buddies together for a night of chili and gaming at my house, and one of us told me he would bring his girlfriend (who is also one of my colleagues) along to play. This led me to wonder what game to do as she had earlier told me she did not like Scifi, Fantasy, or even games...
      \n
      \nAfter some discussion with my partner, she suggested that I run Lady Blackbird (A printed copy has been lying in the magazine rackin our bathroom so she had been leafing through it). Boy, was that a brilliant suggestion.
      \n
      \nShe came into the game very uncertain nervous, and without a clue to do, and towards the end of the night she mimed the strangling motion of Naomi as she closed her hands around the Captain of the Hand of Sorrow!
      \n
      \nThe combination of the incredibly easy to grasp, yet empowering rules, the universality of the story and how the keys constantly give the players something to do with their characters just worked for us. Hopefully we will find out what happens to the crew of the Owl after they escape from the Hand of Sorrow in the next few weeks.
      \n
