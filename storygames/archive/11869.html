\n Another month, another thread.

Last night, Burning Boston. Joanna, the blind Puritan girl with unshakable faith has, since day one, had the belief that "God took my sight for a reason."

Last night, Joanna was standing beside her brother Sam as he leveled his rifle at their mother Amias (who turns out to be a sorcerer who uses a mass grave to draw power from) and gets ready to pull the trigger. Amias responds by casting The Fear, which forces everyone to make Steel tests, which they fail.

Everyone, that is, except Joanna. The Fear effects people who can see you.

Amias starts casting another spell to put the whammy on Sam, taking complete control of the situation. Joanna prays to be delivered from this evil and gets a Miracle. All magic in the room is cut off, Sam takes his shot, and Amias falls dead.

I guess God did take her sight for a reason.

(There was a lot more awesome stuff too. Sam got baptized, Zeke became a cold blooded killer, it just rocked. For a session that started rocky and slow, it ended up being one of our best.)

    \n Playtest for Peng Lai, a game of mythic Chinese adventure by Josh "Skull" Dixon.

    The Chosen of Heaven, a hero branded by the mark of a star and blessed with magical powers, is traveling with his entourage through bandit-infested woods on a mission to recover a treasured heirloom. His balladeer, Pu, is playing loudly on his Pipa to draw the bandits out. The bandits set up an ambush, but half the party notices at the last second and hides. Pu is left standing by himself s the attackers jump out of the trees.

    Pu pretends to accidentally break a string, and slings his Pipa to pull out another. The bandits demand his surrender, and move to capture. Pu cheerfully complies, walking forward to meet them with his arms outstretched. He bows his head to the nearest brigand, and intones, "Let me express how great an honor it is to be allowed to surrender to you."

    At that moment his companions strike! In a fluid motion, Pu whips out his lutestring and garrots the bandit, holding him helpless as he grins and addresses his companions: "And now I shall allow you the honor of surrendering to ME!"



    (They did. Though it might have had more to do with the Chosen of Heaven battling the bandit leader with lightning coursing from his blade.)

    Posted By: MatthijsPosted By: KripplerLast night of Gothcon I playedDen Yttersta Domen(The Final Judgement) a game about the second renaissance in the solar system after most of humanity kills itself because of soul plague.
    \nThe site has plenty about the setting, but nothing about method of play, system, techniques... Could you say anything about that?
    The system had three parts.

    1. You have a hand of tarot cards and stack them plus your skill value to win conflict resolution (and you can add X cards of a different color if you have value X in a Knowledge). You can also choose to give up but add a condition to the victor. Conflict resolution hardly ever came up, my strict priest tried to arrange a party to delay the trade negotiations while not letting anyone know he was the one throwing it (and the GM gave up with the condition that zealous agents that had infiltrated the mansion were informed) and he tried to see what another character was doing on a hand computer while she tried to hide it (he used discretion and the knowledge arabic to know how the keyboard was set up).

    2. Everyone had 6 keys (of which one was their religion). If you played on it hard in a scene you got a token. If you gave another player a token and told them what key it was for they were forced to follow it (or buy off the key, the only thing you gained from buying off a key was it couldn't be triggered so the scenario was essentially it was interesting to see how many of your beliefs you lost).

    3. If someone does something awesome anyone says awesome and gives them a token (not one of their own, just a new one). You can spend the tokens on adding facts about characters or the world in general. These are the same tokens that you gain and trigger the keys with.

    I think it mostly was the great scenario and the way the characters were set up that did the work (apart from the players), I could ask the writer if he could give me a copy for me to translate it and put up.

    \n Played the Dragon Age TTRPG with some vet-but-rusty friends and the nine-year old. Contrary to the hate-thread expectations, we had really good time. (Number crunching is fine for theory threads, but in actual play the system really works.)

    Spent the first hour or so explaining the setting from "at first, there were no humans on Thedas" to "and now King Caillan is kinda bored and... sort of hoping for a Blight or something to fight".

    Character generation was 'standard', but a comfortable familiarity for the vets and instantly grokked by the kid.

    I had to step out of the room when we got to setting Goals and Ties, and when I came back, the kid was SUPER excited about her "Main Goal", which was for her (city elf rogue) character to find his dalish elf mom. She then went on to explain (breathlessly) how that was WHY he'd learned to be a scout instead of a normal thief, because he thought of what SHE would be like, and tried to be like that, but it was sad, because city elves and dalish elves hardly ever see each other and why would she LEAVE. (She then went on to draw pictures of all the characters while we played. In color. They weren't half bad.)

    That she (and the other players) grokked the setting that well in short order really pleased me.

    For "Ties", the players had decided that the mage in the group had been sent out to help track down an apostate mage-on-the-run-from-the-Tower, and she'd recruited or been assigned the others help out. I went back to my experience with the CRPG and tied all that into Jowan's escape from the Tower and off we went.

    Then we played, fought some demon dogs, and enjoyed the stunt system for the fluid action it imparts to what might otherwise be a pretty staid combat system -- getting to describe what your stunt actions looked like was fun for the players and me. We used a grid, and I was pleased by (a) the way it informed play and (B) not having to fuck around with flanking and opportunity attacks and all that yutz.

    Good times. We're excited to play again.

    \n Played my second game of It Wasn't Me!, the one-hour murder mystery game from Norwegian Style.

    Turns out Macklin killed his father (Neil Armstrong) outside of a circus, using a huge penis-shaped wrench. Something about being jealous of his android father and mother. Typical.

    The book is worth buying just for this simple, player-driven game that would work very well with non-gamers. When other players accuse you of lying, your name goes in the hat to be picked at the end when the murderer is uncovered. So of course, the more your name goes in the hat, the more likely that IT WAS YOU.

    \n To pass the time before Doctor Who came on, I played The Azone Butterflies with (mainly) non-gamer friends.

    I explained the rules by memory, and didn't get them quite right. The players did not find the setting convincing; it might need harder selling - or that might be regular non-gamer resistance.

    The coolest bit was when one girl described her warrior's darts - each is dipped in a different poison and she doesn't know what effect it will have until she uses it.

    \n Rivers and Lakes one-shot.

    The plot revolved around escorting an imperial princess to her intended husband after rescuing her from mysterious green-robed assassins.

    Near their destination, the blind master notices someone sneaking towards her room at the inn. Just before one of the local heavies causes a scene, he's brought down by a poisoned dart.
