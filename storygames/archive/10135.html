    \n Pretty screwed.

    My advice would be to do character creation/world creation tonight, and not worry about getting any play done at all. Getting characters created together is really important in BW, because otherwise you could easily end up with a really incongruous group, and suddenly you're running three seperate games.

    Ideally, you'll want to prep with characters' Beliefs in mind, so you can challenge them and stuff.

    I'd also really encourage you to only use the basic rules from the book. Don't worry about "Fight" or anything in your first session. Just get used to the basic dice mechanic. I played a six-session game of BW without more than the most basic mechanics, and it was fine.

    \n BW won't work without buyin. Audible your campaign to something else. Your players have Flags up that they want something else, the way I read it.

    \n I've played a handulf of BW campaigns over the last couple years. I think all the advice about player buy-in is good, but, as you say, your group *is* committed to playing a long-term campaign, so maybe if your first couple sessions are off the hook, they will get all into it.

    I'll second the idea that you should leave the more advanced sub-systems alone until everyone has grokked the basic dice mechanics. So I'd avoid Fight, Duel of Wits, Range and Cover, and Sorcery. (In fact, my most recent BW campaign did just that, and it went better than the previous one).

    Circles is a pretty important rule, especially in a "city of thieves" game like yours. It's easy to use, and I think it is a good way to convey some of the cool stuff about BW without you just preachin' it. So maybe throw some stuff at the characters that would make for a good Circles test. And, since your players aren't used to the game yet, tell them what you're doing. The fun part is making the test and having the player (not the GM) explain who the NPC in question turns out to be.

    BW handles social encounters well, even if you don't use Duel of Wits. So pick one of the players who came up with the most background, and throw them up against one of his relationships, maybe in an adversarial way, and let them use social skills to get through the encounter. They have to convince someone of something (Persuasion), make a deal (Haggling), stir a small crowd (Oratory), or something like that. Make sure to nudge them on using Wises. ("Dave, doesn't your guy have Irate City-Guard-wise? That would give you a bonus die here.")

    Definitely make sure you DON'T have any preconceived cool plans for what will happen next from scene to scene. If you're just as surprised by the outcome of things as the players, those might be good "a-ha!" moments for them about how the game works and how they might get into a BW campaign.

    Good luck!

    \n Hi,
    With the information from the character building session, I have a lot of optimism about your game. Right away you have the makings of a fun few scenes with two player characters that want the same thing--to be at the top of the Thieves' Guild. What's neat about this in a Lankhmaresque setting is that it's reminiscent of Fafhrd and the Grey Mouser's first meeting, where I think they were plotting the same heist. So make being named the Guildmaster's #2 guy a point of the first game. The guildmaster says, 'Impress me." At this point the players will bombard you with questions about how to impress the master and about the city itself--what is in the city to be stolen or kidnapped or freed or whatever. This is the opportunity for the players to use -Wises to create facts about all those things. I'm hopeful that the players will be able to each come up with a caper. You can resolve each caper as a linked test of three or four rolls, with the most impressive thief being the one with the most successes in the various tests. And, all is not lost even for the loser, because you could use the duel of wits as a final opportunity for both thieves to make their case to the master--giving the winner of the capers a bonus to his body of argument. For the third character, the escaped POW, how cool would it be for him to have been imprisoned and escaped from the thieves guild itself? The way to impress the guildmaster may be to bring back the escapee 'undamaged'. In BW demos like 'the Sword' and 'Quest for the Holy Grail' (full disclosure--I wrote Quest for the Grail, so I like to plug it ;-), BW Downloads the action is based on PC-vs-PC conflict, and the scenarios practically run themselves. Although continual PC conflict won't contribute to the campaign game you're looking for, an initial conflict between eventual comrades is common enough in the action genre.
    I think the Duel of Wits is great even for players new to BW. It allows PC-v-PC social conflict that can actually be resolved without one _player_ having to give in. Alternately, with the PCs teamed up against an NPC it's a fun way of seeing what direction the game is going to take because the NPC's decision is not preordained. I agree with delaying Fight! but DoW is what sold me on Burning Wheel--the first time I played it.

    \n Old habits die hard, Brian. :-)

    Several things that helped me over the hump:

    1) Paying more attention to good adventure fiction. Seriously! Read The Three Musketeers, and you'll see that D'Artagnon never actually just *fails* to do something. Rather, his story is constantly made more complex, the tension ramps up, and the story goes on. I think this has a lot to do with old gamer habits that have nothing to do with how storytelling works: we've come to treat rolls as win/lose propositions, while in stories it's rarely so clear-cut.

    2) Be strict with yourself about negotiating complications prior to the roll. This is BW RAW, but even the BWHQ guys play fast and loose with it -- because they know what they're doing. Until you're totally comfortable with the whole "either the hero gets what he wants without complication, or his life is about to get even more complicated," absolutely, positively always prenegotiate. I find that, by doing so, it forces me to really, really focus on what any given roll or conflict is really "about." Otherwise I do what you do: oops, you fail, tough luck.

    3) In BW at least, look to the character's traits for inspiration on good failures. If they fail because of a character trait, that helps reiterate the character and the player feels like the loss is arising from principled play. That's a tough one to explain without a whole essay. I can start a new thread if you need me to unpack it.

    4) Say Yes if a complication doesn't immediately and obviously jump to your mind. Really! If you don't have a likely failure candidate in mind right at the beginning, it probably means it's just rolling for rollings' sake, not because there's any intrinsic tension to the moment.

    p.
