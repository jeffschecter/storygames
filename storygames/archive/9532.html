\n Over in a thread about Death, I made the following comment:\n
Posted By: deadlytoqueI think what I had hoped to do was make some comparisons to games where death is not at stake to games that "transcend" death by allowing character continuation.

For example:Ars Magicaas-written does not include ghosts. A character's play ends with death; but the character'simpactdoes not, because during their lives, characters create artifacts, texts, and spells, as well as teach apprentices and ponder magical theory. So, "the character's story continues after death" in a sense.

I guess what I was getting at by cross-posting that here is something like "if a character makes real impact on a game-world (shared fiction) during life/play, then what are the possible implications on play of the dead character?"

I guess that's a little farther afield than what you had originally meant by this thread, but I think it's potentially valuable. If you play a "generational" game, likePendragon, then in what sense are you playing a series of characters, and in what sense are you playing a "meme", mutating through generations? In games like that, ideas (spells, tomes, instructions; or inPendragon, specific traits and tendencies) are transmitted between individual characters, forming a continuity.

Sorry for how sloppily-presented this idea is. It makes sense in my head.
\nSo, what I'd like to discuss is how realistic this is. In a game where you play multiple characters who are inheriting traits, setting, or other trappings from each other, are you in some sense playing a meme? What mechanics would strengthen memetic play? Is it better to spell such play out explicitly, or just let it happen?

    \nI dig this. I like the idea of a whole campaign being nothing more than a retelling, with potential for "transcription error". I think it would be fun to create some kind of structure that exists at the start of a campaign and each session of play mutates that idea somehow, and each session is a generation. It would be exciting to see where the idea ends up. So if the first session began with "power corrupts", then at the end of the session, the players would weigh in on how that idea had been modified or embraced, and maybe after five or six generations, you end up with "power must remain in the hands of the wise"; and then five or six more turn the idea into "the wise cannot be trusted, unless they are checked by the mighty". Something like that.

    It the original form, I was thinking of the "meme" in a less-specific way; in Pendragon, for example, you play a bloodline, which is not a meme per se, but within the context of the game, it is a concept which spans generations and can be seen to be a cultural entity that can be seen to replicate. Also in the sense that in Pendragon traits like "Hate Saxons" or "Piety" are literally inherited. Similarly in Ars Magica, the PCs are the residents of a Covenant and a Tribunal, and those bodies are made up of memetic ideas that can likewise be transformed as the Covenant (and its residents) age, and are passed to the next generation of apprentices.

    I wonder in what way the memetic elements of "bloodline" or "Covenant" can be extracted mechanically for a "meme-playing game"?

    \n[self-pimping]

    This is pretty much exactly what Agora: how shall we live? is about. By default, it's gritty scifi, but it's trivial to turn it to fantasy if that's your thing (and the book will probably have guidelines for doing so). But you play a leader (or series of leaders) of a group of refugees from a galactic apocalypse struggling to rebuild civilization. You have four ideals, which are ideological statements used as traits, that define what you and your people believe; those ideals get slowly mangled through play, and when you hit endgame you're a different people than when you started.

    [/self-pimping]

    \n I like the idea of literally playing a character who was influenced by your previous character. Not a whole lot more to say about that particular aspect of it, but yeah, awesome.

    On the other hand, I think a lot of people already kind of play a meme. I know I've been guilty of it: a character leaves play for whatever reason (maybe because that game is over), and you replace them with the same general character. My big, tough, stupid sword-wielding guy gets replaced by my big tough gun-wielding guy. Or in the next game you play the big tough guy that sounds a lot like my guy. I always feel a little cheap when I do this, but it's also resulted in some cool characters. It's a cool idea to make this an element of the fiction, but I think it happens a lot around the table.
