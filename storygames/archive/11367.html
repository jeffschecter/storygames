Anyway. I was proceeding from the assumption that a "story game" was one that modeled story over physics. Drawing from the example of the above-mentioned thread: carry: a game about war would be a story game, while Recon would not. To put it another way, I would consider a game that's more concerned about where the bullets go and how much damage they do to be outside the realm of a story game, while one that's mechanically interested in why the bullets are flying in the first place would fit comfortably within my definition.

I just don't get the sense that story is their first concern. Certainly there's nothing in the rules that directly address matters of story or even roleplaying, as far as that goes.     Posted By: Paul T.A "story game" is any game that allows people to create some kind of narrative together
    (emphasis is my own)

    Though I think I pretty much totally agree with you in spirit, I have to say that, as far as definitions go, the definition of "story game" must explicitly depend upon the rules of the game. After all, a story game is a game whose central, defining characteristic is that it involves a story, just as Risk is a tactical war board game, and thus centrally involves elements of simulated tactical warfare with figures on a board.

    Rules, as the limitation of possibility, are central to any game. I can play Risk with my friends and concurrently create a shared narrative ("I declare war on the United States of Europe following the underhanded betrayal of its leadership regarding the treaty of the previous fall, and furthermore declare that henceforth my Neo-Roman Empire will..."), and it is indeed the outcome of events in the game that "allow" the narrative to take its form, yet the rules themselves do not delimit the game space in such a way as to force a narrative to come into existence.

    On the other hand, a game like DitV compels the creation of a shared narrative. If the group of players actually follow the rules, as published, and do not simply abstain or refuse to act, then a shared verbal narrative will inevitably arise. It seems to me that this is the key defining aspect of a story game: its rules, by constraining possible actions in a certain way, compel (and do not merely allow) the creation of a shared narrative.

    This might sound like nitpicking, but I think it has fairly significant ramifications. Most notable, a simple "toolbox" of rules is not necessarily enough to make a game a story game, unless the rules also somehow consistently drive play towards the creation of a shared narrative.\n
    Posted By: Paul T.[Story games are] a broader category than "RPG", but there are also many RPGs that are very poor "story games" by this definition, as the tools they provide (or the way they are typically played) do not reliably produce good stories.
    \nWith my slight change of emphasis ("allow" to compel), I think this phenomenon is well accounted for. The problem is not precisely that many RPGs fail to reliably produce good stories (though this is true), but rather that whether or not a given RPG does indeed produce good stories is merely coincidental, since the success or failure to do so it not directly implicated in the rules. In other words, most RPGs have no explicit rules about story creation, and thus any story that arises is not (directly) attributable to the game-- it is, at best, a graft by those players who, like us, enjoy meaningful stories.

    This is why, for example, I don't consider Diplomacy a story game; although it is certainly possible to create a story while playing Diplomacy, the former does not necessarily stem from the latter. Thus, I think it is useful to think of our hobby as having multiple aspects. On the one hand, we have story games proper, and generally (I think it is fair to say) we derive the most enjoyment from these sorts of games. On the other hand, there are games that are conducive to Narrativist play but which are themselves not strictly story games (I would include here DnD). I play all sorts of games, and I enjoy playing DnD in a Narrativist stance, but I also recognize that there is a categorical difference between DnD played in Narrative stance and a story game whose rules compel the creation of a shared narrative. (I imagine that the same happens for, e.g. a wargamer: DnD is conducive to tactical wargaming, but for the "full" or "pure" wargame experience, most wargamers would instead be most strongly drawn to a game like Warhammer 40k).

    That's my two cents. (Sorry if I sounded critical, Paul; I didn't mean to. I just wanted to highlight the difference between a game "allowing" for or being conducive to story game behavior versus a game compelling story game behavior, since I think it is a significant but sometimes underestimated difference).

    \n Actually, Jason, I don't think your definition is bad for designers. Players have to come to the table to "story game", that's for sure. They can do that with anything ranging from My Life With Master to Monopoly, if they want to. I don't think that's the issue The question is, how can rules reliably help these players with this pursuit?

    For one, the rules focus of a game determines how much of the handling time is spent with various activities. D&D spends a lot of time on combat. That can be a central part of your story, but for my personal preferences, I like something that leaves more of the handling time for character interactions outside of combat.

    Also, in D&D, characters can die in combat even if there's no story-related reason for it. Again, sure, it can fit with your story (particularly if you're telling war stories). But for me, that's unsatisfactory and leads to me investing less time and energy into my character's personality and more into keeping them alive. So I prefer games where I don't have to worry about all of my investment vanishing due to a few bad rolls, which allows me (notice: "me", not "everyone") to create deeper stories.

    So yes, players need to be on board with telling a story. And with sufficient commitment, they can do it with any kind of game, or even without any games at all. But rules can help structure this whole experience in one way or another, thereby possibly facilitating or hindering the kind of storytelling a particular group wants to do.

    Posted By: JDCorleyThis leads to the conclusion that story games are only those games that produce a story when the players of the game hate stories and do not wish to pursue them and expend no effort to pursue them.
    \nFrankly, this misrepresents the issue. As Christian points out, the issue is how rules affect the play experience. I find it surprising that you claim that what qualifies as a story game is unrelated to the rules. I, like most everyone on this site, fully agree that players can create a story play experience without reference to the rules-as-written (e.g. in Chess or Monopoly or whatever). But does that mean that suddenly, magically, the rules don't matter?

    The truth is that rules do shape the play experience, which is why many of us constantly work on designing rules more conducive to creating a story gaming experience. Christian's example of DnD pinpoints one way that DnD is not conducive to story gaming behavior. DnD is not terrible, but also not great,* at providing a conducive environment (as it were) to creating a story game experience, while Dogs is, in my experience, reliably successful at doing so.

    But perhaps I should approach the question in a different way. One way to reliably create better stories is to work with your gaming group to learn techniques for better story gaming (or to find a new group that has those skills). If I understand the OP's intention properly, this is not aspect of story gaming we are concerned about here. We are concerned about the second aspect: whether rules have an impact on the reliable creation of meaningful stories.

    My own opinion, which I shared for the sake of argument, is that a story game needs requires development not just regarding (1), but also regarding (2). Indeed, insofar as a game is about the constriction of possibility through rules, (2) is central to the "game" part of "story games." In other words, my idea of what a story game is involves both player-behavior and rules design-- if either is deficient in a way that reduces the reliable creation of meaningful stories by those involved, then that game is, to me, not a story game.

    So, responding to Sam's original post, I don't think that Recon is a story game, though it can be played in a way that results in an interesting/meaningful narrative (just as any, imo, almost any activity in life can be).

    \n Right. It's more a question of fitting the right tools to the right people.     However, I have a question for the theory-heads out there:

    The conversation here is interesting to me, because I think a term like "story games" depends a lot on the context where it is used. Stephen seems to be trying to define the term so he can differentiate story-empowering RPGs from less story-empowering RPGs (that don't help him reliably create a good story).

    On the other hand, I often use the term when talking to non-gamers, which means I am trying to define it so as to distinguish games with meaningful and interesting fictional content (like an RPG) from games that do not have anything to do with stories whatsoever (like Scrabble or Checkers).

    But, now, here's the funny thing:

    The Story Games Codex defines the term as follows: "A story game is a game in which the players create fiction, and the fiction can in turn influence the play of the game."

    But I've played quite a few games (and designed some) where the fiction can't influence the play of the game in any measurable way. For instance, consider a game like this:

    Choose three random words from a dictionary. Now start telling a story using those words! Each player, in turn, writes one sentence, contributing to a growing story.

    Not a great game, obviously, but one can imagine a more evolved ancestor of this game being a lot of fun (perhaps Happy Birthday, Robot could be a close-by ancestor). Now, I would definitely consider that to be a story game. Is it not one, by the definition from the Codex? (Not that it matters, in the large scheme of things--it just seems like it could be an interesting point of theory to discuss: the effects of the fiction on gameplay.)

    Is that because this definition is more interested in differentiating RPGs from other games? Or is it simply incomplete? Or does the fiction of a game such as hypothetical game above affect "game play" after all, just not in a clearly mechanical sense?
