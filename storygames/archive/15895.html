\n I\u2019m new here to story games but I\u2019ve spent some time lurking in the concerning skeletons thread and loved some of the ideas people came up with. So I was wondering what you think are the essential components to a good (concerning) goblin?
\n
\nPersonally, I\u2019m not such a fan of making simply \u201csavage humanoid\u201d goblins, which to me just seem like bad racist caricatures of non industrial human societies. For me a good goblin is summed up by being \u201cgreebly\u201d. What does greebly mean you may ask? I\u2019m not sure myself, but I am certain that goblins are greebly, and the more greebly, the better.
\n
\nWhat are your thoughts?

    \n Goblins are certainly greebly. They are wicked and cowardly, but not savage. They delight in mechanical things, especially weapons and engines of destruction. They are green-skinned, skinny and knobby. They have enormous flappy ears and great wide mouths and are all bald as lords.

    I do not know if there are lady goblins, but there are certainly no innocent goblin children; they are born cruel.

    Goblins are cunning, and sometimes clever, but never wise. They have no aptitude for magic or war or animal husbandry. They love fire, and worked metal of all sorts, and gems and gold. They are greedier than dwarves, but cannot hold their drink.

    \n I like my goblins not exactly green, but jaundiced. Thin and bony, but with muscles like steel wire. Filthy claws, naturally. Narrow, pointed teeth like nails. Large, nocturnal eyes.

    Their expressions are inscrutable, and they think very, very quickly, if not always deeply. They appear fiercely loyal, so much that you always think they're up to something. They unpredictable in that they will one moment be craven, cagey, and/or cowardly, but the next moment throw themselves into danger with a disturbingly child-like glee.

    Unlike their cousins, the elves, they embraced steel and learned to shape it, specializing in contraptions and machines that aren't particularly safe or aesthetically pleasing, but are certainly effective. Their cat-like curiosity and their quickness of thought leads them to ever more unlikely designs and experimentations. They bring to their craft the same child-like glee mentioned above, even as it threatens to blow up in their faces.

    And, for the love of all that is holy, do not EVER allow them to taste human blood. They go fucking apeshit.

    \n Can I interest you in...

    The Goblin Tarot Companion.

    (There's a complete deck of Tarot cards to go with this, when it eventually get's printed)

    Otherwise there's the hundred or so goblin posts I've made on Google+ over the past year.

    In a nutshell.

    Goblins are a short lives race with a very fast breeding cycle. They tend to last weeks (often dying in battle or in accidents) and they become considered elders if they survive months. In the course of their lives, they might sire a few litters of a dozen or more children.

    Some few goblins have managed to find magical artifacts that prolong their lifespans to years, they are considered virtually immortals by the hordes around them. Such goblins become heroes to their people.

    Decades ago (or more), goblins stole a human child and convinced it to be their king; this is why the goblin king looks nothing like the goblin hordes. Because he lives so much longer than them, they were able to convince him that he lives forever. He exists as a figurehead, only fed information by a cabal of bickering goblin courtesans.

    I could easily write more on this topic, but I've already written 160 pages in the companion book referenced above.

    \nI suppose I approach goblins in two very different ways. On one hand (my more biological er hand), I think of goblins as hyper aggressive, flappy eared, magpie like, slightly green chimpanzees. Not quite intelligent to make their own complex tools, they're more than happy (and sneaky) enough to steal them. Unlike chimpanzees, they breed fast and have the physiological capacity to speak a simple chittering language.
    \n
    \nOn the other hand, I approach them as more feyish - a physical manifestation of vindictive bad luck. This is more in line with Simon C's suggestion that goblins come from discarded semen (which I think is an excellent idea). A merchant's roof collapses on her, and everyone thinks its bad luck. However, children playing near the site swear they can see tiny saw marks on the timber, and hear chittering laughter coming from near-by houses. Villagers talk that at twilight, squat figures dart in alleyways.

    \n Not nearly as fun/amusing, in my long running D&D campaign, goblins were:

    Natives to another world of dim light and other factors for which they were more suited, kidnapped and enslaved, brought to this world by your "classical anicent evil empire of bad ass sorcerers" to be miners and fast-breeding slaves for jobs even those evil megalomaniacs didn't expect humans to do. The evil empire is long gone but the goblins remain.

    Goblins breed faster than this world's ecosystem expects, and so have a "lemming like" instinct yielding up hordes every now and then.
    Goblins hate humans as having condemned them to this world, enslaved them and treated them as subhuman.
    Goblins have a hive-like mentality that is engineered to the survival of the group as long as they are in a group - where they are community-oriented, share property, clever with their hands and good with tricks and traps, but in a bizarre trait inherited from their years of slavery deathly afraid/respectful of magic (shaman always respected.
    Isolated and alone, goblins are cowardly and terrified, as goblins are never ever naturally alone and miss their packmates and comforting background noise of constant high pitched goblin yapping.

    \n Riffing off adamwb:

    Goblins are darkness personified. They abhor light and see perfectly well in the pitch black. In dark, forgotten places that have not seen light in 111 years, introducing a torch or other light source will create goblins, who will attempt to extinguish the brightness back to black. They are immune to fire. Any flame they touch goes out immediately.

    Most of the time, adventurers do not see the goblins before the ambush. They fall out of dark ceilings and other impossible places, since they were created by the light hitting there for the first time. Groups of goblins focus on the bearers of the brightest light sources first, ignoring others if they can. Their single-minded purpose is to extinguish light. Once the area is perfectly dark again, they will settle down and fade back into the shadows, becoming ethereal shadow stuff. If someone lights a torch again, they come back out.

    They are immortal, but spend most of their lives in shadow where we can't see them. They can be defeated in combat, however, and if so, they will not return until the place has seen 111 years of straight darkness. They take double damage from spells and effects that beam bright light on them. Intense light spells that usually inflict no damage do harm goblins, similar to throwing burning oil on other creatures.

    \n A friend of mine wrote a book and podcast novel about goblins based on the Rosetti poem: Goblin Market

    I'm of the opinion that when the Industrial Revolution came, goblins moved from fruit sales to other businesses, like banking and manufacturing. Things got really complicated when they started working for Edison; their gremlin cousins just couldn't seem to resist messing with everything - especially vehicles. The goblins were blamed for a lot of it and ended up working in postal and telegraph offices trying to eek out a living. It was a tough time and very unlike the good old days when you could just kidnap children and ransom them. Or possibly eat them - the sources are unclear on this point. Of course, things got better over time and they found newer and stranger technology to bend in directions they were probably never meant to go, whether for good or ill.
