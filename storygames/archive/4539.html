\n In a recent thread began by Tony LB, we were discussing Altruism when (I think inevitably), the Game Theory applied to the mechanic proposed came up. The thread is here: http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=4503
\n
\nI've seen this conversation coming off on the horizon for a long time now. Lots of "story games" have mechanics that, if played "selfishly" may not work precisely as intended. The question of whether or not you can have a cooperative "game" is an interesting one. Perhaps these are not "games" but something else. And unless you see that context, it may be impossible see how Game Theory supports these mechanics working.
\n
\nAssuming they do. I'm going to play devil's advocate here quite a bit. Please understand the following in that light, and try not to let the more potentially incendiary parts of this ignite. Consider it rationally.
\n
\nEven with a liberal understanding of how Altruism works in Game Theory, it's contentious as to whether certain sorts of mechanics can incentivize thematic play, when the potential for Gamism exists. That is, if we have measures in a game, won't players tend to see these as something to measure themselves against? And, if that's the case, won't they fail to function as intended in certain cases?
\n
\nThe classic example that I can think of is Bonus Dice from Sorcerer. Ron will maintain steadfastly that they promote narrativism, and not a clawing to get them to succeed in Gamism fashion. But, the argument goes, if the reward of getting bonus dice is character success, then for it to be a reward, the player has to be invested in character success. And if the character is simply being authored by a player who is as interested in character failure as success for dramatic reasons, why would he be invested in the character coming out on top? If not to prove their own prowess somehow?
\n
\nHow are bonus dice different than, say, situational bonuses in any other game? Well, the first difference is what they are rewarded for. And that's cool narration, according to the rules. As opposed to situational bonuses which are either rewards for good tactical play, or simply simulative.
\n
\nBut doesn't this, at the very least, create a conflict in the Creative Agenda, with one side of the equation pointing to narrativism, and the other to gamism? Ron will point and say, look at the actual play. No gamism going on there. In the context of the entire game (which cannot be ignored) the mechanism in question works as designed.
\n
\nAnd from what I've seen I'd be forced to agree. But here's where things get really potentially flamey, so please bear with me.
\n
\nAn argument here could be something like, "Well, the utility of being a member of the Forgies to those who are is higher than playing these games honestly and letting them break down." That is we can ignore the in-game factors, because social factors that outweigh them. We play against our self-interest in the game, for the larger self-interest of acceptibility in the community in question. Or, perhaps, the games aren't working at all, and we're lying about the reports of success to gain this social acceptibility.
\n
\nLess contentious is that we've just subconsciously decided to play a particular way that we're expecting the rules to produce, and getting the result despite them. We have to consider this possibility, because we often claim that's exactly what's going on when players tell us that the game didn't work for them as designed. "Oh, you're playing with pre-concieved notions of how the game should work... set those aside, and it'll work fine." Well, don't we, too, have preconcieved notions of how these games should work? For Sorcerer, isn't it all of Ron's work explaining to everyone how it should work?
\n
\nAs for our play with others outside of the social network, or of an understanding of "how it's supposed to work" aren't we simply transmitting that to them in play via techniques? If you run a game, and expect that it'll go like X, won't that tend to make that happen, system or no?
\n
\nSo there are two general questions, really:
\n1. Are systems actually doing a lot to produce the sort of play they're intended to, or do they tend to just produce what we expect them to produce, using techniques not specified by the system in question to get the sort of play we're expecting? To what extent do they reliably produce certain sorts of play? I asked this question on the Forge a while back, and I think I stated it badly.
\n2. To the extent that mechanics can produce expected behaviors, shouldn't we expect the mechanics to do more or less as Game Theory would predict? Or are those models simply too simplistic for the actualities of play? Given that RPGs are about fictions, are the benefits of play the same sorts that exist when there's no fiction being created? Are the rewards (can they be) substantively different from those in another sort of game, such that we can be incentivized toward them? And, if so, how do we make that distinction function without it the incentives being confused for the ones from traditional games?
\n
\nGiven that Game Theory does take altruism into account, perhaps it's possible to actually make mechanics where other values than display of tactical prowess are rewarded. Certainly such rewards exist; I have a friend doing psychology research into such things as how good storytelling ability makes one more attractive as a mate (so, oobviously, it's not only tactical ability that gives us life rewards towards pur essential instincts).
\n
\nBut, again, that means that we're seeing that the people playing will have one or more of multiple reward demands from a game. If they come to a game with a particular demand, won't the mechanisms of the game work differently for those players? Again, if Narrativism supporting games only work for people who want Narrativism... then how do we know that it's the mechanisms of the system that are causing the behavior? As opposed to the drives of the individual playing? To what extent?
\n
\nIs there any way to objectively test? It would first require understanding a player's biases?
\n
\nActually this seems to be precisely what System Does Matter says, systems don't create behavior, they simply support particular behaviors. It admits that one can play a system that's well designed to support one mode, using another mode, and that this happens. And then the tool analogy comes in: you can use a tool that's poorly constructed for a particular sort of play, but why would you want to do so?
\n
\nBut that's the point here... if in fact systems do not create behavior patterns, then we can't say that Game Theory is at all at work here, correct? And if that's not applicable, then what are we left with to discuss just what a particular system does?
\n
\nIt seems to me we go around and around. We claim we can make better games using the right mechanisms, then we discuss what they promote, but then we point out that we're not creating behavior, but supporting it. So... what's the calculus of "support"? See if you can try to answer that question without delving into "Well X gets in the way, so we get rid of that." How is Y supporting the sort of play that we want to see? If not in a way explained by Game Theory?
\n
\nI'm sure that this is probably way too abstruse (despite me fighting, believe it or not, to make it as accessible as I can), but I think that there's an issue in here somewhere worth discussing, and I hope I'm not the only one who sees it.

    \n You're asking a lot of questions, but here's my two cents on the issue, coming from a pretty gamist perspective. Take Dogs in the Vineyard, which I played long before I started thinking about "story-games" and collaborative gaming.

    I didn't understand a lot of the system when I started. I came to the game and saw that I had all of the little bonuses with die values attached. Big gun, sharp-shooter, etc. These are designed to promote narrativist gameplay, since you can only bring in your bonuses if you can narrate them into the story. So for me, the game became "how can I involve X item on my character sheet in this conflict so that I can win." The fallout associated could always be rolled back into more little dice, so it didn't seem to matter if I dragged in all my dice through a convoluted narrative.

    And it wasn't a satisfying way to play. The fact that the game could be... well... "gamed" didn't mean it could be done in a way that was at all satisfying to a gamist (ie me). I actually really didn't enjoy it, although I didn't want to say it to all my friends (that did spend a lot of time talking game theory and checking out indie games and such) since they thought it was the bee's knees. I knew I was getting something wrong, and I kind of wanted to play it again, if only to figure out how they were enjoying something that was so absolutely dull.

    So much to say this: a story-game can be abused in this fashion if they just don't "get" the story-game perspective, and it will be lousy. Just because a mechanic allows, or even promotes a certain style of game play, that doesn't mean that it's how it will be played. In the end, however, I wonder how many of the gamists will adapt to a different game style by learning to use the mechanics as intended (which involves some fairly abstract shifts in perspective) or just discard the game as unsuited to their native paradigm.

    \n Rewards. If you don't want the reward that the game offers, and pursue a different reward, the game doesn't "work." Players are very used to the idea that rules offer weak or contradictory reward cycles, that need to be overriden by a social-based or individually-supplied reward in order to play well. When faced with a game that does provide a reward system that supports a particular kind of play effectively, many players either can't seem to notice it unless it's shoved in their faces mechanically (and it may come across as constraining) or it becomes obtrusive by its failure - the game does not deliver fun. They'll continue to play with whatever socially-mediated reward schema they're used to: for many groups, a sort of brute Gamism of tactical victory over situation, for others, a devotion to exploration-for-its-own-sake.

    \n I think Game Theory is a perfectly fine tool for analyzing games with thematic elements, etc., etc., etc. ... as far as it goes.
    \n
    \nGame theory really has no problem whatsoever with handling things like thematics. If you get X amount of utility from addressing a premise ... that's measurable. It's fine. It's no big deal. Game theory handles games where people aim to lose, or where they aim to break the experience of playing the game ... all that stuff. It's just curiosities. It doesn't break the assumptions of the math.
    \n
    \nBut, unless you delve quite deeply indeed into game theory, the tools you're bringing to the table probably do have a few in-built assumptions: the primary one being that people know what they want, and that what they want does not evolve over the course of (and as a result of) the game.
    \n
    \nFor checkers, or chess, or go ... that's a solid assumption. Measure of victory is, in fact, in-built into the game. But even when you have people pursuing something other than the in-built strategy (i.e. a Washington Square chess player who disproportionately values moves that shake up the board and intimidate his opponent), if those tactics are static then you can create a measure for them ("A sudden gasp and the loss of blood from my opponent's face is worth half a pawn in positional value") and apply pretty standard game-theory to analyze what strategies that will lead people toward.
    \n
    \nGame theory becomes far more complicated when you remove that assumption: When you assume, contrariwise, that people will change their value-sets mid-stream, and that those changes can and will be motivated by other factors at the gaming table. When our chess-shark isn't just valuing shock, he's wisely calculating that he can rewire his opponent's goals and values (by making him more cautious, making him over-value conservative structure and retaining materiel) and benefit from that. That gets heavily recursive very, very quickly. It can still be expressed with math, but it's very complicated math.
    \n
    \nIt becomes a step more complicated when you assume that people will derive value from the mere act of causing someone else to change their goals and values, even if that change has no other benefit.
    \n
    \nIt becomes yet another step more complicated when you assume that people will derive value from the experience of having their values change.
    \n
    \nYou can easily end up with a twenty page set of equations and corrolaries, all to describe the content of an actual play fragment like "And then I just knew that I couldn't try to kill him any more. Something had crossed over in my guy, something he'd been driving toward for a long time without knowing it. I love when that happens."
    \n
    \nI personally don't think that the twenty pages of equations is always the best way to approach that actual play. Sometimes the best way to discuss desires and feelings and principles is in the nuanced, imprecise, fuzzy, information-rich language that was made for them.

    \n Mike, interesting post. From my studies of game theory as a math undergrad in college, the big challenge in using game theory as a model for anything was defining payoffs. By defining payoffs in various ways you can obtain very different results from otherwise identical models.

    If we have a game in which various choices of the various participants distribute red and green jellybeans, there are extremely different results if you put that game in front of a population that likes red jellybeans versus one that likes green jellybeans versus one that likes lots of jellybeans versus one that likes getting 9 jellybeans versus one that hates red jellybeans. Since game design takes place in an environment where you know nothing, or perhaps less than nothing (given rampant misreporting, Tigger-ism, and simple lack of motivation to introspect), about the preferences of the people who will one day play it, game theory is extremely difficult to apply with any degree of meaningfulness.

    \n Mike,

    What you're talking about is related to the issue I've been thinking about lately. It's called The Narrative Wall. I coined this phrase during my nine town Dogs in the Vineyard run. What I frequently found while GMing was that I would hit a place where there were plenty of mechanical options available to me to keep a conflict going but exercising them would be dishonest to how I felt about the state of the fiction and so I would Give.

    Most often, but not always, that would be how I felt about a character. A player would raise with specific words or actions and I just new that hit the character right in their weak/soft spot and they'd cave. Sometimes it would be something just so awesome I had to Give or shoot my own story aesthetic in the foot.

    So I started thinking about other games. In Primetime Adventures I spend budget as the Producer based on my personal investment in a conflict. But I *could* just blow maximum budget every conflict but I don't because of The Narrative Wall. All your stuff about Sorcerer is absolutely correct and it HEAVILY relies on The Narrative Wall. (Side note: I don't think this is related to character success. I prefer it when players are totally committed to their character's success and leave trying to make them fail to the role of the GM. I greatly dislike it when players want to see their characters fail. Its one thing to enjoy character fail as part of thematics, it's another to be invested in trying to make it happen and flip flopping back and forth mid-play. This is a totally separate issue I feel from trying to "win.")

    But that raises the question: How much, when designing, can you RELY on The Narrative Wall, and how do you playtest that. If someone where to play Dogs in the Vineyard and sociopathically just escalate every conflict to Gunplay just because they could I'd say they were missing the point of Dogs in the Vineyard. Same goes for blowing budget in Primetime Adventures. So, if I've designed a mechanic that I feel needs to be attached to the heartstrings of the player's commitment to the fiction to work, when I get playtest reports back how do I weed out, "No they didn't get it." from "No, this mechanic doesn't actually attach to the player's emotions they way I want it too."?

    \n Here's the thing.

    The reason game theory and utilitarianism focus on selfishness is that selfishness is by far the most testable and extensible way of testing relationships. This is because we can swap the specific flavour of utility easily when we reduce it to self-interest. Self-interest can express itself in theft or charity and all that is requires to maintain the integrity of the model is tweaking the folk description. That's why the system in the other thread is just as testable with torture/murder as it is with gift giving. It doesn't matter on whit whether or not something is "rational" in the folk sense of being the decent or reasonable thing to do in the context of a real-world narrative.

    If your model doesn't work to increase utility among participants, in many cases you are left with an appeal to sentiment. "X allows you to do this, but you *ought* to do Y because the non-technical text tells you to."

    Many of you are familiar with this line of reasoning. When it appears in the form of something called "Rule Zero" or "The Golden Rule," a lot of you talk about how that sucks and is a sign of bad game design. When it appears that the system rewards behaviour contrary to its stated mode of play, it's called "incoherent." When this appears in a scenario it's called "railroading." When the GM takes matters into her hands to correct for it, we hear about "force."

    If you want a system that does not provably manipulate utility in its simplest form (plastic self-interest) to support its subject matter, you want to make a further appeal to the folk psychology of gamers, maybe, or if you're more sophisticated, you can speculate based on the results of games that have been tested. Unfortunately, you can't apply this argument directly to an RPG because we can't perform rigorous experiments with them. But I don't think this is really as well thought out as just *making sure the system works in the first place.*

    If you don't want to do that, then basically, you've identified yourself as a System Doesn't Matter gamer in possibly the purest form, because you're saying you don't really care about what the system does when stripped of its pretenses (though some may conceal this fact by arguing for an incoherent definition of "system" that includes them convincing the participants above games that they *ought* to play it as such and such).

    If you do want to do that, the System Does Matter. You want to provide utility rewards for certain things and structure the fluff/instruction around giving those things a certain narrative feel. You want to make sure that those dice symbolize Altruism in *this* particular game.

    Now there is altruism and punishment as separate things with their own characteristics and controversies, but I seriously doubt it's honest to invoke those things merely by pointing them out and then assuming your games will use them in specific ways, as opposed to some narrative flavour of plain old selfishness.

    \n It's that people will develop play relationships with systems that are not necessarily related to the intended design. Some people get into a boat they don't really want to use as intended. This is not necessarily because the boat is badly built or the designer explains things badly.

    The fact is, people have complex relationships with RPGs. I think theory often promotes a certain rhetorical trap with agendas. You describe your agenda, enter into a social contract and so on, but people do not have relationships with games that can be reduced to social contracts that define what they will derive utility from. The subtext, of course, is that if you freely agree to these things and there's still a poblem, the designer can wash his/her hands of being to blame.

    But the designer cannot reliably demand adherence to an agenda. People don't have hardwired patterns by which they derive utility from any kind of fiction.

    This kind of reminds me of my prof's response to Pascal's Wager: "What if God exists, but punishes people for believing in Him?" People approach games with perversity as well as reason. This is not always negative, as these contrarian relationships often lead to good play.

    The best thing we can do is define what we're making when we design a game and fulfill that definition. Creative agenda doesn't really matter at this stage. All that matters is (from a game theory perspective) whether we design it to deliver straightforward rewards (the ugly boat), or inspire the group to develop its own utility game based on the situations generated by the system (the leaky boat).

    Then players will do whatever they want.

    I think this last thing scares people a lot. In RPG theory, the solution has been to use language to take command of the player experience. Consider the theory definition of "Narrative," which is much, much more limited than the real thing, and even more limited than a "dramatic narrative." It's easier to design a game that fulfills RPG theory Nar goals than one that supports all the traits associated with true dramatic narratives -- things like immersion, for example.

    Game theory allows us to design without relying on sentimental signifiers (Humanity, Violence, Evil) so that player perversity is possible. Without that, it's still possible, but requires more robust support in the non-technical text -- "the gilded bucket." Some people find that *more* satisfying, in fact.

    Here's an idea: Gamers *love* to strip a setting of system, but why not do it the other way around? I don't mean GURPS. GURPS talks about Strength and Guns and things. See how a system works as a pure mathematical object driven by unvarnished self-interest and ask yourself how many manifestations of that self-interest you want to support.

Then you can say if a course of play doesn't have sufficient story reward then maybe mechanical reward is needed in addition.

      \n I think people are having trouble with the degree of abstraction I'm talking about. Self-interest is not the same as the moral quality of "selfishness." Rational action in the sense I'm talking about is not "rational" in the folk sense of "Rational people eat food, 'cause otherwise they'll die." It's much more basic. It's about maximizing utility. Utility is essentially whatever floats your boat, as it were. It really doesn't matter what floats your boat. As I noted in Tony's thread, the "altriustic" dice-passing game can become the "sadistic animal torture" dice-passing game with no modification at all. When economists talk about the rational economic actor, they are using far *less* abstraction than I'm talking about.

      At this level of abstraction it is basically impossible to act against one's self interest barring error or some phenomenon where the player isn't really making the decision. We simply say that people do what they want for whatever reason they want. Even external coercion is irrelevant at this stage, because we don't care if that determined how the player formulated that want.

      We play for that abastraction and play with it. We see what's rewarding and what isn't. *Then* the sentimental, narrative elements have a role to play.

      They will indeed change what happens in the game. Fine by me. System Doesn't Matter, then. That's true. I'm sure it's painful for some people to get here by the intellectual route they expect will end with showing that it *does* matter, but in point of fact it shows that the raw system is a starting point for the manipulation of signs -- the precursor to the real game as it's played.

      This is an acceptable outcome. It just hurts to read if you design games and like to take credit for other people's fun. I know I do. But at this level, I really don't care about that. I care about something doing what it's supposed to do, instead of trying to talk the players into doing the heavy lifting for me. After all, that very problem is what people use to condemn Vampire. If you don't want to look at the stripped gears and want to appeal to pure sentiment, and textual propaganda about how the game ought to be played, your game has *exactly* the same issues that are often identified with Vampire.

      If we strip it down and add signifiers later we know exactly what the reward mechanisms are for playing with a rule elated to that signifier. Players can follow them or not -- their choice. When we insist on entangling our design decisions in narrative signifiers we don't necessarily know what's responsible for player actions. The players don't necessarily know, either. But we can *explain* how we attached signs to rewards and how we expect those processes to turn out in play and we can even give advice about manipulating those signs.

      We also know about the strategic options of these "pure" games. We can't disguise the lack of choice with different narratives or by chucking a few dice on for stunting. It means that the dice-passing game, with only two options and some obvious optimal strategies, is exposed as New Clothes. It's naked; there aren't many choices inside the system. It begs for narrative to save it. But we can add templates/classes to the dice and all of the sudden, we have more choices to make.

      (And of course, you playtest. One thing people forget about game theory is that it involves experiments.)

      Once again, not everybody cares. Sometimes systems that come with few choices apart from of the narrative are faster and simply chuck a sign a certain way. WoD combat is like this. There are a few resource based strategies, but the idea is to just max out dice with them. D&D has more complex options afforded by templating. Preferences vary, but once we take away interfering signs, we can see how the systems act in a petty straightforward way, desin accodingly and advise players that a game was put together to act like *this*, even though you'll go for *that.*
