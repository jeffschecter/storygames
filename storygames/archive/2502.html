\n Kick the tires on this one, will you? I dreamed it last night and woke up sort of excited about it.

Bare bones:

So you have two guys in a conflict. They each pick a number between, say, 1 and 50. Then they take turns guessing. First one to guess the other guy's number wins. Not too exciting.

But! One guy has an advantage. He gets to make the other guy pick between, say, 1 and 25. Or two advantages, and it's between 1 and 12. Or three, 1 and 6. Etc. Both sides will invoke these advantages to reduce the number pool they have to guess from.

OK, so that's the core conceit, but the really cool part is that the "number list" is really a list of physical, mental, and emotional characteristics a person might possibly have. And when you force your opponent to limit it, you will have powerful indicators of what they will pick, based on the situation in which the conflict takes place. So yeah, there are twelve possibilities left after you've invoked advantages, but you know he's scared and exhausted.

Or! I might use this for the diagnostic mini-game in Medical hospital, where it is the clever diagnostician versus the insidious disease, which exists on a list of possible candidates. In that usage there would be some currency passing around as well, probably related to the declining health of the patient.

    It's slightly easier to find the first one (a binary search algorithm still tags it, and has twice as many chances of a random hit) and then there's a (probably brief) follow-up where you're trying to tag the second one.

    I have this sneaking suspicion that binary search is not, in fact, the ideal way to go about finding the number. I suspect that a lopsided-search could get you the first number sooner, at the cost of getting the second number later. Like, if you guessed 35, and were told "Between" then you can now concentrate on whittling down the numbers from 36-50 first, and the numbers 1-34 later.

    I do think that you'd want to bring the total range down a little bit to compensate though, yeah. I'm with Russell in that regard, and the two numbers would only make for more potential tedium.

    \n Thanks guys, we should stop talking about numbers and start talking about categories. Example:

    I have a list of 36 disgusting diseases. I am the dying patient, you are the brilliant doctor. You have no advantage over me - list of 36 diseases, take your best shot as I slowly die.

    Instead, you have one advantage over me - "It's a parasite!" you exclaim, and now I have to choose from the 18 or so that have a parasite vector. And you guess. But you know I am a lion tamer, so that informs your choices.

    You have two advantages over me - "It's a parasite, and it's vector is cat shit!" Now I have only 6, three of which are only found in domestic pets.

    So as the dying patient I must bow to causality a bit rather than fight tooth and nail to screw my opponent, and I see the weakness in that. But the above is the sort of interaction I imagine.

    \n I think I figured this out in a fun way, at least for my diagnosis game.

    There's a simple card-based conflict, matching suits, whatever. The doctor can bet up to six currency \xe2\u20ac\u201c when you correctly diagnose the illness, whatever remains will be doubled. Others can place side bets for \xe2\u20ac\u201c or against \xe2\u20ac\u201c you.

    If you are playing the patient, choose a condition and note the pathogen, vector, symptoms, and clue. There will be 40 and they look like this:\n
    Melioidosis
    Pathogen: Bacteria (There are 6, all but a very few have only one - yay meningitis!)
    Vector: Animal, Contact (there are 6, they can double and triple up)
    Symptoms: Fever, Swollen lymph glands, Skin lesion (There are 18, each illness has three)
    Clue: Patient recently traveled internationally (There are 8, each illness has one. Maybe I'll call it "etiology")
    Notes: Melioidosis, also called Whitmore's disease, is an infectious disease caused by the bacterium Burkholderia pseudomallei. Melioidosis is clinically and pathologically similar to glanders disease blah blah blah
    \nAdd three bogus pieces of information in any of those categories, however you like. For example, you could choose Calicivirus (Virus, Food or Contact, Vomiting, Diarrhea, Cramps, and Patient lives or works in squalor) and add \xe2\u20ac\u0153Bacteria\xe2\u20ac\x9d as a bogus pathogen, a Rash as a bogus symptom, and \xe2\u20ac\u0153Patient routinely handles animals\xe2\u20ac\x9d as a bogus clue. You\xe2\u20ac\u2122ll want to carefully place these to mask the real illness.

    In the initial diagnosis scene, if you are the patient player you will reveal the symptoms and clues through a roleplayed intake interview.

    In each diagnosis scene, including the first, if you are the doctor you will attempt to acquire a single piece of evidence related to the illness. If you win the diagnosis game, you get to request one of the following:

    1. The pathogen of the illness.
    2. The vector of the illness.
    3. Whether a single piece of information is, in fact, bogus.

    For example, if you know that the patient has a faver, fatigue, and has been vomiting after eating an extravagant meal to celebreate her return from Peru, you might ask if the whole fancy meal thing is a red herring.

    At the conclusion of any diagnosis scene, as the doctor you can elect to announce your diagnosis.

    If you choose to wait, one point of currency slips away forever.

    If you choose to announce your diagnosis, no currency is lost. If you are correct you double the remaining currency. If you are incorrect the patient has a crisis and a trauma scene ensues. You can continue trying to reach a correct diagnosis in future scenes \xe2\u20ac\u201c if they survive the trauma surgeon\xe2\u20ac\u2122s tender mercies. So rockin', any thoughts? I appreciate the comments so far.

    \nBruce Schneier has already described how two people flip a coin over the phone.

    There's a pretty good rundown on E2.

    Unfortunately "one-way hash functions" are a bit of a hand-wave there. If you're looking for something practical that's not strictly one-way, a common trick is to take advantage of the fact that it's quicker for humans to multiply large numbers together than it is to factor them.

    So Alice picks two six-digit numbers and multiplies them together, sending the result to Bob. Bob then makes a guess about the qualities of one of the two numbers Alice chose, like "The larger number is even" or "The smaller number is odd". Alice reveals the two numbers and Bob can verify that they multiply as shown.

    If you were doing this over the Internet, you'd probably use md5sums or SHA1 checksums.

    There may even be neat arithmetic tricks you can do with a pocket calculator that result in effectively one-way functions. I'm thinking something like Alice punches in a long number and pokes the Square Root button twenty times and sends Bob the result.
