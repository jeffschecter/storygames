\n In my admittedly limited exposure to story games over the last year and a half, I've encountered some real backlash from other parts of the gaming community. I read posts on RPG.net about BW being an "anti-GM" game, about "hippie" story games that sacrifice fun for design, and poo-pooing GM-less games like Polaris because they have "no structure" (which is pretty funny if you think about it). I talk to many "normal" RPG players who either won't try story games or they have toyed with it and have found the experience either unsettling or found that it didn't work well with their group.

But on the other hand, I've been at the table fighting my own historical tendencies to railroad games and watching players either fall flat or not be happy when asked to share the burden of creative decision-making. So there's obviously something there.

Which makes me wonder why organizing and running a game is seen as some sort of mystical art? And not just games either. I was doing research into storytelling techniques and kept encountering books telling the reader to "make sure they were the ones telling the story" and to actively keep others from interfering with "the story". In the books I read there was no thought of collaborative storytelling and it made me a little sad.

So I ask: why do you think collaboration is threatening to so many people? GMs fear their loss of control and the investment they have made in their worlds. Players rue the ability to just show up at a game and be entertained for several hours. But it does appear that many gamers want something different and they can often run into trouble with it.

Is this a group dynamics issue? Or a player buy-in issue? Or something else?

    \n Collaboration without structure sucks donkey balls. Seriously, I see this all the time. A whole bunch of people say, "Hey, wouldn't X be cool? Let's go about doing X!" and they all sort of do stuff towards making X happen, but in the end it just sort of flounders and dies. And then everybody just gripes about how X would have been cool, if it had happened. They had no structure on which to make the collaboration work.

    Hands down, the easiest structure for a group is a dictator. Introduce a dictator into that mess of people wanting to do something cool, and all of the sudden like magic things start happening. The dictator doesn't even have to be that talented for this to work. He just needs to say stuff like, "We meet on Saturday at 6pm" and magically, people show up at 6 (some people can't make 6, but consequently, they aren't there and the people who are there have fun). Especially in our everybody's-equal society, though, people are very hesitant to take on the role of dictator. They don't want to tell people what to do. Alternately, they are afraid that they will screw up and people will mourn the missed opportunity (X would have been cool if it happened, but it didn't and it's Joe's fault). So there are relatively few people who are willing to even try being the dictator. Then those few pick up a few extra tricks and techniques, and compared to the folks who are scared of taking up the position, these people acheive mythic status.

    Then there accretes this sort of social feedback thing, where players show up to game and it's the GM who (a) juggles and directs the attention of the group so that the players feel they are included and (b) metes out approval for their creative contributions. So the GM becomes a source of attention and approval. That is potent, potent shit, especially when it works around to feedback status. The GM makes us feel good, so we empower the GM some more to make us feel good again. Over the extreme long term, some players who are not well-served by a particular GM fall out of the group -- they find something better to do -- and those who are well-served by the GM stick around. The GM and the games that the GM run become tailored to the group of players. You get these very tight groups that "play well together" and who treasure the feedback loop of attention and approval that they've built up over the years.

    Games which propose a different structure for creative collaboration, then, can sound like they are attacking the cult of the mythical and magical dictator-GM, which might be the only way players have ever seen collaboration work. They can sound, in fact, like a direct threat to the hobby as they understand it (not to mention a direct threat to the authority and benefits that the GMs are now accustomed to getting). So they have a kneejerk reaction, and don't try the alternative structure at all.

    \n I like a lot of Joshua's points above, but I think he gets a bit mean about it in the last paragraph with the whole "cult of the mythical and magical dictator-GM" comment. Fact is, even some groups that do try an alternative structure find that it doesn't work, and you can't lay the blame for that at the feet of some kind of reactionary, soul-blighting, shameful flaw in the people involved. And that's if there's even any blame at all in every situation like that, which I think isn't true.

    It's closer to what he said in the paragraph before: sometimes you get a group dynamic that has been well-established and has all the kinks worked out, and everyone's having a great time with it. Once you're at that point -- which, let's be honest, is a point that you should want to be at -- you're golden. You've got something wonderful that you should treasure.

    Which isn't to say that you shouldn't try out new stuff, but by the same token you are not obligated to try new stuff. You're not a bad person if you don't: hell, there are lots of reasons why a group that's got a good thing going should just stick with it and not branch out (take, for example, those groups that can only play infrequently and would like to get the best possible experience out of the fewest number of sessions). And if you do try something new and say "whoa, this doesn't work as well as what we were doing before," it's not because you're some kind of jerk who hates new things: the new game just didn't measure up to your old one. There's nothing at all wrong about trying things and deciding which one you like best. It's chocolate versus vanilla versus strawberry, that's all, and while it's awesome to have a favorite flavor, it doesn't make you better or worse than other people who have other favorites or even people who think all three are pretty good.

    \n I think organizing and running games is seen as a mystical art because in many ways it was. For years in this hobby, good GMs had a number of techniques at their disposal for all sorts of things, from creating scenes to generating situation, making players care about their NPCs, etc. They may have picked them up from reading widely, from keen observation, through mentoring by another good GM, or simply by instinct. I'm not saying all the techniques were the same or even similar. But they worked.

    For a long time, that stuff was a GM's "art." It wasn't really recorded anywhere in the games. Either you learned how to do the stuff that entertained your group and were a good GM, or you didn't and weren't.

    Part of the trend for the past few years has been for some designers to take some of their techniques and encode them in their games to make them more accessible. You wind up getting stuff like Kickers, Bangs, Relationship Maps, Say Yes or Roll Dice, Let It Ride, and many others. They're all techniques that can be used to make some games better.

    I'm not saying it's the cause, but I could imagine that some of the resistance might be akin to the way stage magicians feel when the tricks of their trade are revealed. GMs, especially, don't like the idea that their art can be reduced to some rules and guidelines.

    But more generally, I suspect the resistance comes from a few things:
    1. People in this hobby identify very strongly with the games that they play. Raising the possibility that their game is lacking something can immediately put some people on the defensive.

    2. Related to the first, for some reason, the first reaction people have to any suggestion that their gaming could be better is that you are calling their gaming inadequate.

    3. I think that for some people, when you suggest a particular new game might have tools that will help them create story (or similar claims), it is perceived as implying that the person is incapable of doing it without help.

    Posted By: buzzI'm thinking you could also cite various sports that put a player in a "captain" position, e.g., the quarterback in football. However, I think there's generally an expectation that the person has to earn their keep. If said quarterback keeps calling lousy plays and can't hit the broad side of a barn, he's not quarterback for long.

    Is the same thing true for RPGs, though? Or do groups end up sticking with DM Joe because they had fun with him once, and hope that it'll happen again at some point? 410th time's the charm!

    \nWhat I see happening, and what I think is the real problem with the "GMing is a mysterious art" idea, is that when push comes to shove you can find a lot of players who hear what goes into GMing a traditional game and say "oh HELL no, I'm not going to do all that, that doesn't look fun at all," and so even if you really want to bench DM Joe after the 410th time he fails to deliver the goods (or even after the fourth time, as is more likely), you simply don't have anyone else who's willing to step up and take his place.

    The wrong part isn't that they're playing a game with a GM, it's that the way they think of the GM role (as a highly specialized and labor-intensive job which is often also characterized as thankless, grueling, and difficult to master) actively pushes many players away from even trying it, which is often disastrous for the entire group. If the idea that "this is really hard" is deeply ingrained, you can't even get 'em to try a more collaborative setup, because they'll see things like framing a scene as "the GM's job" and flip out because obviously that's too much work and too difficult and they're going to suck at it.


    Honestly, I often get that vibe from some of the discussions here: any time someone advocates a collaborative story game entirely on the basis of "because GMing a game is too much work," I cringe a little inside. In many cases taking on the traditional GM spot is a lot of work, sure. If you don't have a fair amount of free time or energy to put into running a game, you probably shouldn't commit to doing it. But it's FAR from being impossible, or painful, or insanely demanding, and there are absolutely ways that some of the work can be distributed among the players even without going the fully-collaborative route (often ways that are extremely rewarding!).

    Hell, if a junky "Be a Better Manager in Seven Days" self-help book can improve how you run your game (and I totally believe that it could), it should be perfectly obvious that GMing a game can't be all that scary. I'm a big advocate of coaxing people out of their defensiveness by demonstrating that their fears and misconceptions are neither universal nor eternal, so that they can get to the point where they're willing to convince themselves to try something new. Perpetuating the "GMing is REALLY HARD" mystique works against that, I think.
