\n Hi, I'm Sam and I like to PBeM.

A few years back it was possible to go to pbem.com any old time and catch any one of a dozen-plus games of various types. Sure, like most PBeM games they would fall apart within a few turns, but there were still people out there trying to give their roleplaying love a literary spin.

Things have changed. First pbem.com went belly up and for a while there was no central place for people to go anymore. Now there's PBeM2, which is actually a lot more sophisticated technically but provides the kind of clearing-house venue pbem.com used to. The problem: it's empty most of the time, unless you're really into Star Trek gaming and, well, I'm not.

What happened? I tend to think MMOs happened, or at least World of Warcraft. The thing about PBeMs is that they require some degree of effort and a full engagement of the imagination to be successful, much as tabletop RPGs do. Put this up against an MMO\u2019s snazzy graphics, ready-made gaming world and built-in reward system and there\u2019s no contest; most folks will gravitate to the MMO every time just because it's easier to get one's kicks out of it. Plus, isn't everybody doing it?

This isn\u2019t to say that MMOs are any less fun or worthy of our affection. I\u2019ve spent my time in Azeroth and I can safely say I enjoyed myself. But I don't play WoW anymore and have seen my attention wander back to PBeMs. Why? Because as freeing as an MMO can be in terms of the heavy lifting of the imagination, it is also creatively stifling: one can never go beyond the limits of the game\u2019s design.

The great thing about fiction is that it can go anywhere and do anything. It\u2019s not constrained by budgets or rendering capability or the like. WoW can only do what it\u2019s programmed to do and no more. The NPCs never say or act any differently than their allotted roles allow. The world may expand as new products are released, but it never changes. A PBeM, on the other hand, has all the freedom it wants.

But as I say, the old world of PBeM is pretty much dead. Unless you\u2019re a dyed-in-the-wool Trek fan or a perv interested in writing interactive sex stories, you\u2019re out of luck.

How long has it been since you PBeM'd? Or have you ever? If you have, under what circumstances can you see yourself returning to it? What would your preferring setting/game be? How would you gather a dedicated player base? And if you haven't, what would get you to try it?

In fact, I might just start doing that, and try running some again.

    \n My most recent PBeM experience was the OD&D game I ran on twitter for about 3 months earlier this year. As always with such things, it hit a wall and died away. One of the conclusions that I took from it was that the PBeM feature of "always on" is in the long term a bug. Because the game is never "not in progress", there is never an opportunity to step back and rebuild your creative juices. I'm currently pondering what strategies could be employed to avoid this kind of burnout.

    Obviously there are success cases, as people have mentioned above, they just seem to be more the exception than the rule.

    \n In the long running game I mentioned, turns come only on Monday, Wednesday and one on the weekend with semi regular breaks for holiday weekends and special stuff the GMs want to go off and do. Also there are established protocols for players to have their characters written into the background for a hiatus and then spring back into the narrative when real life let's them come back to the game. That regular but not always on posting schedule helped much to avoid burnout on both the GM and player side.
