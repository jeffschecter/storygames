    Posted By: MatthijsPerhaps I want to use Society of Dreamers with intent? That is, try it out as a real chaos magic ritual, instead of just a game pretending to be a chaos magic ritual? Summon a mnemosite, something like that.
    I believe it is possible for you, with the right group, to actually summon a "mnemosite".

    The group would probably have to be really fired up on the ritual, and you had to work in unison to create the kind of psychotic state that enables your collective minds to produce such a phenomenon.

    And what then; what will happen if you succeed? What will the "mnemosite" actually be? What will it do to the group, and to individuals within the group? Could the game/ritual produce something harmful, or do our brains have failsafes to guard against such results? Groups have been known to do terrible things, when caught up in group-hysteria or group-psychosis.

    So read about magical practice, and other ritualistic phenomenons, and see if there is some way of doing such a game/ritual, a way that promises to give you what you want from the game/ritual.

    BTW:
    What would the participants act as;
    - themselves?
    - symbolic archetypes representing parts of themselves?
    - fictional characters?

    \n "Vanilla Magick" would be an awesome name for a roleplaying game actually!

    But more on topic, I think in a certain sense all of us are here because we've had some sort of experience with roleplaying games that struck us as very important, useful, or otherwise positive in some way. That experience transcended the mundanity of everyday life, and rose to the level that we want to roleplay again so that we might achieve that kind of experience again.

    I know this isn't exactly the "transcendence" referred to in the opening post, but it is a more practical goal, and it's something that's been done lots of times and could be done many times more, just by making a really good game.

    That said, if what you're looking for is a more mystical experience, then I'd suggest designing a game about real mysticism. Religions have been working on mysticism for thousands of years, so that's a good place to start researching -- pick whichever religion seems least evil or most awesome to you (depending on your point of view), and try to look at it as inspiration for your game. Remember the principle of the Fruitful Void, so that you design the game around mysticism rather than on directly top of it -- putting in some sort of direct "transcendence" meter probably wouldn't work -- and then see what happens. It might be just what you're looking for.

    \n If we're talking about the same thing, then I can say, yes, I have.

    Strictly speaking, it can't be explained. That's the problem. It's abstract. The best I can do is throw metaphors at you and hope that one will stick. (This is, by the way, why most mystical/occult stuff sounds like gobbledygook: it is.)

    If I tell you that the specifics of what you actually do don't matter, all that matters is your will to produce the intended effect, and your willingness to abandon yourself to the process of producing that effect -- does that help any?

    What if I tell you that the things-you-do actually do matter, because they establish a pattern of symbols, and in a universe of metaphor (such as the one we live in) the symbol IS the thing?

    And if I tell you that those are both true?

    EDITED TO ADD: See, I'd tell you to leave belief systems out of it. Belief systems attempt to codify the abstract in order to produce certainty and syntax. This is a bad idea for a number of reasons, chief of which is that it's silly.

    But then I'm also the guy who's gone on record multiple times as saying that Crowley was a hack. Maybe I'm just a "hipster magician" who's suspicious, though. For serious, though, if you want a book recommendation, try something by Carlos Castaneda. The Power of Silence is a good one, as is Journey to Ixtlan.

    EDITED ONE MORE TIME TO ADD: It's also possible that I'm the wrong guy to talk to, as I'm not particularly fond of chaos magick, or ritual magic in general.

    Posted By: MatthijsKayfall, Eero and others: I'd be happy if you could give me some pointers to good techniques. It's kind of hard to find good stuff (there's a lot of "Buy Our Self-Help Course" or "Join our religion/magical cult and learn the secrets in just twenty years" out there).
    \nI think reading a mystic book can have a positive effect, but like Marshall said, it won't teach you everything about mysticism the same way a book about Civil War history would teach you about Gettysburg. Learning mysticism is a bit like learning a language or learning to ride a bike, in the sense that it takes a lot of action and habitual practice to get a real sense of it, only the action is mental and spiritual rather than physical (though practical real-world behavior can and should play a part in it).

    I would suggest starting with the Persian mystic poet Rumi, whose works are widely translated in the West now. He's one of those guys that people talk about a lot, and write cheesy guides to, but actually he does have a lot of valuable things to say. Another Persian mystic poet, named Hafez, is as good or better than Rumi, in my opinion, but his work isn't quite as easily available in the West. You could also try reading a translation of the Dao De Jing (also spelled Tao Te Ching), or investigating Zen Buddhist Koans. William Blake is a famous Western mystic, if you prefer something a bit closer to home. Personally, my favorite mystic work is called "The Seven Valleys," by Baha'u'llah, which is also written in the Persian poetic style, and like many of these other works, is available free online. For some context on important books from another culture, I think "The World's Religions" by Huston Smith is a good choice (not free though).

    When you read any or all of these, take them little by little. Don't try to read them the same way you'd read a novel, for leisure, as they're not made for easy multicultural accessibility as such. Also, don't expect quick improvement in your mystic perception in ways you can easily notice. Instead, you'll probably get incremental enlightenment, which will be so slow that you won't even notice it until after studying for a while you'll realize there is something different about you but you can't put your finger on what it is or what caused it. You'll wonder if you would have gained enlightenment without reading the books, but as you continue reading and reflecting and talking about what you read with people, your insight will be clearer and clearer.

    All mystic books tend to have contradictions and paradoxes and things, which is by design. They're trying to achieve a certain effect when read, referring to things which are by nature non-material, and not necessarily even "abstract" like psychology or philosophy would be. I would say that "understanding" is involved, but it's not the sort of understanding that you can explain; it's the sort of insight that lets you just know the truth when you see it. Like, imagine one guy who says, "I just lost my girlfriend -- what did I do wrong? I'm never going to fall in love again!" and another guy who says, "I just lost my girlfriend, and I'm better off without her; I miss her so much, but she's just not good for me." The difference between the first and the second is the ability to see inside oneself -- insight -- and gain enlightenment about one's actual practical reality just by looking at it, without need for philosophizing or whatever, and I think that's what mystic texts are trying to train, each in its own way.

    Ideally, I think an RPG about mysticism would have some of these elements and goals as well, but represented as game mechanics rather than cryptic words.

    \n I think that roleplaying in general has the potential to become, and the kind of deliberate induction that Matthijs was describing in that Forge thread definitely moves towards, what Charles Tart called mutual hypnosis. From a piece in the '92 Whole Earth Review, which also has a good bibilography of the academic literature up 'til then:
    \n
    \nCommunication alters consciousness, and altered states affect communication. What happens when you connect these variables together in a positive-feedback loop? Imagine for a moment a uniquely state-active way of communicating, and a state of consciousness uniquely sensitive to communication.
    \n
    \nThe term for this is mutual hypnosis. The experience ranges from something like a vivid fantasy all the way to an empathic/psychedelic trip complete with vivid multisensory imagery.
    \n
    \nOver at The Mule Abides, I wrote some about "better RPGs through hypnosis?". Not explicitly stated there are:
    \n- these days I feel like my gaming benefits enormously from years of experience as a client in non-hypnosis-using psychotherapy, learning to identify unconscious thinking and not let it run the show; for me, the transcendent potential of making a RPG more primary-process-driven is not at all worth the pitfalls of moving in the other direction.
    \n- the mask idea was, or was rumored to be, one of the techniques of which would-be professional GM Captain Commando was talking about here: "Why I can't reveal my special techniques now is because they may be difficult to take seriously until successfully used."

    Posted By: MatthijsOkay, so Marshall, what you're saying lines up pretty well with what Alan Moore sometimes says - all stories are real; it doesn't matter what god you worship (he uses a sock puppet). The universe is a metaphor, thing.

    It seems to make sense to me intellectually, but I have no idea what to do with it.

    \nYeah, I like Alan Moore a lot (although I'm pretty sure the sock-puppet snake god thing is an elaborate joke that got out of hand). Scott Adams says that the definition of "smart" is "thinks just like I do, only moreso," so, yeah, I think Alan Moore is "smart," generally.

    As for what you do with it... You just, like, do it, man. Y'know? It's like walking. You just, like, do it.

    The trick is breaking out of the day-to-day, rational frame of reference. It's a fundamental problem, and has been throughout the history of mankind, but people do it all the time. There's, y'know, dreaming. Also, are you a sports fan? You ever have one of those moments where the spirit of the crowd kinda becomes its own thing and displaces the personal for a bit? That's an example, too. It's an implementation that I'm not entirely comfortable with (I see it as particularly dangerous), but it's a thing. Then, of course, there's all the historical uses of hallucinogenics and psychotropics, but I'm not a fan of those methods either, as they can do serious injury.

    And, of course, there's rituals. That's all they are (at least from one perspective; when it comes to magic, you change where you're standing, and everything changes -- you get whole new values for the set of "true things"). The trick is to give the ritual itself enough authority and resonance that it gets you caught up, despite the resistances of your rational pieces.

    Erlend,
    I'm a big fan of Castaneda's books (although not the man himself), but I want to point out that the stuff in them is mostly (say, 90%) diametrically opposed to ritual. So it might not be of use to you. But, then, it's 100% occupied with the issue of altering perception, so it might still be.

    Also, it's almost certain that most of the events in the books never happened. But the philosophy is still useful.

Looking round for the least worshipful object he could find, he hit upon the comic figure of Mr Punch, and forced his mind into believing that it possessed divine powers. The experiment succeeded. He came to experience \u2018a large share of the feelings a barbarian entertains towards his idol\u2019, and for a long time he was unable to look at Mr Punch\u2019s grotesque features without a feeling of reverence.\u201d
