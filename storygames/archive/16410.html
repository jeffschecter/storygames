\n So I was browsing Kickstarter after re-looking at Dog Eat Dog and wishing time would go faster so I can play it, and I ran across Zombicide, which looks to be a fun cooperative zombie-kill-fest of a boardgame. I click play on the video, and I begin to notice something as I watch the characters get named off: everyone who survived the zombie apocalypse was apparently white (there's one girl who is questionably asian). I found myself suddenly turned off by this. I actually sent a message about it to the Kickstarter project owner, but no idea if there will be any response (and I doubt it will be to suddenly announce an extra character to the game). I did mention I thought it was a good idea for there to be more than one female character, but the whole thing left me with a sour taste in my mouth, especially while having DED's theme of "whitewashing" still fresh in my mind.

For demographic purposes and full disclosure, I am a straight white male. I just don't think it's okay to make games only for straight white males.

Have you guys had similar experiences of balking at a game's lack of inclusivity and/or stereotypes? (Note, this is in reference to the game materials themselves. If you happened to play with a sexist/racist/etc GM, that's appropriate as well, but try to include how the game itself encouraged the behavior in that case. Otherwise, off-topic.)

You feel distinctly out of place if you try and make your character a person of color - the people the game defines as your family and home community obviously don't look like you, for one thing.

    \n

    On top of this, you don't see a lot of cultural inclusiveness in the setting. There are ostensibly different human communities that have plausible and humanlike cultures, but basically everyone you meet talks the same and acts the same, except for Sten (who is an exile and possibly some kind of eunuch, from a cartoonish alien culture that runs more like a hive than a human society) and Zevran (who is a sort of racist Frenchy assassin/prostitute) who are frankly rather embarrassing caricatures.

    \n It's interesting to note that even when the designers don't go too far with oversexualization, the players will.

    Look at Skyrim. The female figure is relatively reasonable for a fit, 20 something athlete. The breasts aren't huge. And the skin textures are pretty mottled and dirty, which plays down the sexy and keeps things somewhere almost resembling reasonable. Sure, the Foresworn female armor looks like something out of Xena and is pretty silly bare, considering the climate, but it's an exception compared to a pretty reasonable majority. And the looks on the average townsperson are rather boring and normalish - big-lipped button nosed cuties are unusual and generally make sense with who the character is.

    So, of course, about a week after the game came out you start seeing mods that make the skin tones blemish-free, that make every woman in town look like a super model with a personal makeup assistant, that give every woman in the game huge bazongas, that change all the armors and clothings to make that Foresworn fur bikini look downright sensible in the arctic climate.

    I mean, not that I have any of those mods installed. That would be insensitive. I just happened to notice them available on the Nexus and Workshop. Of course.

    I guess my point is something about how sex sells, though I guess that exact phrase isn't perfectly applicable when these are free community mods.

    I used to always play female characters in MMOs and other avatar-customizable games. After a while I started trying to figure out why this was, and I realized it was because it was impossible to make a character who looked like me. Even when you could make a character with a "slightly yellow-brown" skin tone, it'd be impossible to get the build right, or the hair and eyes, or whatever. One thing I liked about Mass Effect is that I could finally make a character who looked (more or less) like me. The Sims, also.

    \n I'm proud to say that the illustrator of my frpg Fabula did a very good job with regards to genders; he portrayed women and men in even portions, all of them in sensible attire. And still he made them look fierce and inspiring. Good job!

    Later I have become concerned that my game is a bit lacking in colors; there are no figures looking asian or african there. The concern is informed by the fact that I live in a land with a growing percentage of the population being of different origins, and I want my game to reflect that;
    - pictures of figures in the game-book is placed there to inspire the game-play, and to make all players feel excited when reading it. And I want them to feel excited by the right reasons; to feel welcome into this world of fantasy.

    So, to rectify this, I have started to rework the setting of the game, to cater for such an understanding of what a human being may be in the world of Fabula. I find it a welcome chance to brush up the setting, and to make it more interesting. And that is my main point here; reflecting the variety of human figures in the real world, by helping players to portray a variety of characters in the game, really makes a game more interesting!

    \n Just thought I'd provide these two blog links. Both of them focus on women, but I think the general thrust of them can be applied to any inclusivity issues:
    http://www.sarahdarkmagic.com/content/look-sexism-fantasy
    http://www.pelgranepress.com/?p=3501

    It shows a pretty startling trend, but actually one that does seem to be, by baby steps, improving. (Especially from the link in the pelgrane press article) At the very least, we aren't seeing much Frazetta or John Norman's Gor anymore. I know that is maybe a somewhat pessimistic front on the whole. But I have a belief the society will turn it around. Whilst sex does indeed sell, I think that artists, especially in our field, have a bit of an obligation to avoid that tactic. Sadly, it probably needs an active, full-scale consumer move to make them change it.

    The problem I found I have personally been drawn into when currently working on my game is an interesting deconstruction, I feel:
    In creating a game about Divination and Prophets, the general base setting thrust is one of a roughly Greco-Roman one. Heavy migraines, blurry events, that kind of 'traditional western' schtick.
    When I realised where this was going, I thought it would be a neat idea to include maybe some alternate depictions of how other prophetic cultures could be run under the system. Onmyoji, especially, I felt would be a pretty fun twist, especially with their history regarding them as relatively recently being relegated from court status(The major school collapsed when Shinto and Buddhism were seperated, but most of the practises live on in a mixed form with modern Shrine Shinto) and so on. I thought the best way to illustrate these would be by detailing additional cities in the setting, and chose Osaka. It is a place I have visited, know fairly well, and love absolutely.

    Now the problem is is that with just Greco-Roman and a little bit of Japanese as a side appendix, it almost begins to look like tokenism, which is really unintended. The reality is I don't feel I know another /city/ well enough to detail an additional culture in the same way. The option I could take, of Druidic practises, is not only messy and poorly recorded, but also makes it instead begin to look like euro-centrism. The option I settled on was a bit of a disclaimer in the book before the Osaka section with a paragraph effectively saying what I have just said above, and encouraging players to do the same very slight hacks to their games if they feel it will more closely represent the culture they want to use.
