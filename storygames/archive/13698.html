    My college friends and I had a Greyhawk campaign that went on for a very long time- mostly in college and the years after, waning in frequency for a decade or so after that. It went on long after the setting was officially supported, and I occasionally had to look for specific supplements that I wanted, mostly as something to rest our own world-building on.

    I expect I was just using Greyhawk because it was the original setting, and I'd had the materials since I was a kid. I'd run several campaigns using those modules, and it built up a lot of history in my mind, sort of like a comic book universe (criss-crossing storylines of heroic characters, parallel worlds, etc.).

    \n The vast majority of playing and/or DM'ing occurred in the Forgotten Realms. We started with 2e, but migrated to 3e and 3.5 when those came out. All told, I have - I believe - 15 years of experience in the Realms.

    I've always enjoyed the Realms, but I understand that there is something of a stigma associated with it - something about how everyone just wants to play uber characters and rehash Drizzt. Fortunately, I furiously curtailed that sort of thing in my games, as none of us were really interested in solo play wankery.

    The thing I loved about the Realms was that there was just so much to it. Tons of material everywhere. Lots of bits with which to fiddle. None of us were terribly beholden to canon, so we'd screw with the history and setting all the times. It was great fun, I have to say. That's part of the reason that I've set my Burning THAC0 campaign in the Realms.

    I've also played: Greyhawk, Spelljammer, Planescape, Ravenloft, Darksun, Dragonlance, and Oriental Adventures

    Most of those, though, were only played a few times here and there. Still, I have small opinions about each of them:

    Dark Sun is neat and difficult; I have a GM who converted Dark Sun to Burning Wheel, so now I play in a Burning Dark Sun game (as Boulder the cave troll - love it).

    Greyhawk was too generic for my liking. Not enough flavor.

    Spelljammer was simply bizarre, and I didn't particularly enjoy it.

    Planescape is cool business. I love the factions a whole lot. This is another setting that is a ripe candidate for conversion to a story game.

    Ravenloft was just lame. I never dug into the setting very much at all.

    Dragonlance was even lamer than Ravenloft. The Dragonlance novels were better than the setting, and the novels are fit only for kindling.

    Oriental Adventures was cool, but it was quickly supplanted when I discovered L5R.

    \n Oh man.

    I ran (who was always the DM? this guy!) games set in: Forgotten Realms, Ravenloft (yes), Birthright, Dark Sun, and Planescape. We were actually really bad at the epic long-running game - it was always more like, roll up some characters, play for a couple weeks, not be able to to get together for a week or two by which time I had a new idea for something else I wanted to run, so time for new characters!

    Notable things: our Birthright games were centered all around the mass combat rules with those crazy cards. We basically went from battle to battle with almost no game in-between, except for making rolls on those seasonal tables. It was cool because it meant I could play one-on-one with my buddy when our other friends couldn't come over. We actually used the Birthright mass combat rules in other settings for the occasional large-scale battles!

    I was totally into Planescape, but the players weren't (in the sense of "i haven't read it and i'm just experiencing it through your explanation"), so while I really enjoyed the game we started of it, at some point the players told me they didn't really understand what was going on (which was fair, b/c I was holding all this "critical information" back in order to have a big reveal later).

    I ran a lot of Ravenloft modules, actually, and they weren't really connected by any continuity. I still have fond memories of the one that came with an audio CD of the creepy sound effects in various rooms, that was neat.

    I remember all of us really liking Dark Sun, and I know we played it, but I dont' have any strong memories of what happened in those games.

    Forgotten Realms was kinda our default setting, but only in the vaguest sense of I viewed it as the default setting, so if we didn't specifically play in a different setting, we were in the Realms in my head.

    I can go into more details about any of these, of course, but thats the executive summary!

    \n Most of the time, the 2E campaigns we played were based in our own, original worlds, simply because we wanted our own particular mythologies. Of course, they mixed in healthy doses of elements from the various other campaigns (I ran one which included races from all worlds, even the undead PC options from that Grim Harvest box).

    I did end up playing in a year-long Dark Sun campaign, which sucked for the most part. Even starting out at 3rd Level, characters dropped like flies because the GM made it impossibly difficult for us to ever get water. Of course, we kept playing, making new characters as old ones died, because we were entranced with the possibilities of natural psionics, half-dwarves, giants, thri-kreen, and defilers. I think I had over twenty characters in that time span.

    We also ran a fairly short Ravenloft/Masque of the Red Death campaign, after watching a bunch of the Jeremy Brett Sherlock Holmes episodes. That was very much a bunch of fun, but in the end, we figured that Call of Cthulhu worked better as a system for those kind of adventures. But it fully cemented by enjoyment of Gothic Horror (hence my hack Castle Blackbird).

    \n Games that never got off the ground:

    Ravenloft (2nd ed)
    Dark Sun (4th ed)

    These each had promise, but failed to go more than a session or two for whatever reason. Maybe my own failures as GM.

    Played for a while:

    Greyhawk (3rd ed). Sucked. Lame prescripted adventure in lame setting. I was apprenticed to Tenser, but he never seemed to do anything useful.
    Spelljammer (4th ed). All the cool bits of 2nd ed Spelljammer kept around, embarassing bits dropped by the wayside. Still allows for some epic and surreal moments, though, like the insane beholder or the aboleth fight on a space whale carcass. Started as a World's Largest Dungeon game, but has been a Spelljammer game for a year or two now.
    Al-Qadim/Planescape mashup (4th ed). When two friends were bored, I offered to run a game at the drop of a hat. That game wound up being set in the City of Brass, where efreeti enslave humans and other mortals. The PCs were part of the Resistance/underground railroad. They were also level 1, and so killing efreets was right out. I as GM stole every cool idea I could from Al-Qadim or Planescape or the 4th ed Manual of the Planes.



    I'm a big neophile. When I game, I want the story to be something interesting and new. So weird settings like Dark Sun, Planescape or Spelljammer appeal to me. They're strange, different from the default D&D. Any weird thing you can think of can fit in somewhere, and stuff that wouldn't fit a normal setting work just fine. Greyhawk or Forgotten Realms, in contrast, are way too bland.

    \n Ah, I love me some 2nd Ed D&D settings! A lot of these I would run or play at the drop of the hat using FATE or Burning Wheel.
    \n
    \n- Birthright
    \n
    \nThis was one of those great ideas that so did not fit D&D, the world was very D&D it had cool monsters, tons of places to go adventure, lots of meaty kingdoms to really root for... but the game was really focused on being the leaders in charge of the kingdoms and rules for that kinda sucked. Looking back if we had skipped the king part and played adventures our campaign would have rocked. A few years back I ran Burning Wheel game of Birthright that was pretty rad though.
    \n
    \n- Dark Sun
    \n
    \nI love me some Dark Sun, I ran several campaigns of this throughout high school back in the day, this setting is responsible for my love of a good desert setting and I wonder how much my love of Dune is due to Dark Sun. We played games ranging from magic users on the run from the Sorcerer King, to escaped slaves living in tribes in the wastes, to a very traditional adventures roaming the tablelands looting ruins, fighting monsters and doing odd jobs for merchant houses. I remember the first published adventure "Freedom" was pretty cool too and I ran that twice as a launching point for 2 campaigns. Looking at the 4E book makes me want to run a Burning Wheel or FATE version of Dark Sun.
    \n
    \n- Dragonlance
    \n
    \nWe played and ran in this setting a lot when I was a teen since me and my friends all loved the novels back then, but it was never as awesome as the worlds we made ourselves or the other settings out there.
    \n
    \n- Planscape
    \n
    \nThis may be one of my favorite settings out there, something about the infinitely weird and bizarre planes of existence filled with every heaven and hell, the factions of philosophers with swords, the amazing art, the way belief shapes the cosmos, the awesome maps and diagrams of the planes, setting games in the city of doors where the whole multiverse is just a portal away... so much awesome. I ran some of my longest running D&D campaigns in this setting and we still didn't do all the cools things in this setting. Plus the Factol's Manifesto is one of the best game books out there.
    \n
    \n- Spelljammer
    \n
    \nRan a nice and bloody campaign about Drow pirates raiding the space ways when this first came out and then a few years back ran an updated version of the setting using Savage Worlds that drew heavily on Babylon 5, Firefly and Eberron as source material that I would like to explore again using FATE one of these days.
    \n
    \n- Ravenloft
    \n
    \nThis was one of my friends favorite games so we played it a lot, the game never was the gothic horror it promised however and was more a D&D game in which chances are you die sooner than latter and there are a lot of undead, still fun but not what was advertised. I still remember going through haunted houses like dungeons... though some of the published adventures were better at the horror than others and the rest tried to in the background or story but then still provided you with a dungeon map to explore and loot.
    \n

    \n You know, I ought to write about my experiences.

    Al-Qadim: like so many, I bought along with several supplements and totally geeked out on the world. But never played in it.

    Dark Sun: My first campaign was mostly about running and hiding and trying to stay alive. My second campaign was more fun. We started at 6th level and did the normal adventuring stuff just with Dark Sun color. We had plans to eventually take down the Dragon, but we never made it past 12th level.

    Forgotten Realms: Almost all my AD&D experiences went down here. Spent years in college adventuring in the Dalelands. Fought the Wizards of Thay. Traveled to Waterdeep. Met Elminster.

    Lankhmar: I ran three campaigns in Lankhmar. I got the one with the really HUGE map of the city. Each campaign had a lot of the same motifs: guard duty, tax day, thieves' guild, city-under-seige, etc. But each time, we had a lot of fun.

    The Ravenloft: Like most of you, the mists came and got us, we fought a bunch of undead, then ended up back where we were. I was a little disapointed with the 2e incarnation of this setting. I never did get my Gothic Horror itch scratched the right way.

    \n What I remember of D&D play (pretty much all of which was 2E) was a mix. We only knew D&D, and we didn't pay much attention to boundaries or strive for coherence. We mixed 1E rules with 2E rules and Basic rules, used whatever add-ons we liked, made stuff. Much the same with the settings: we mixed and matched however we liked, without too much regard for purity of sources.

    That said, I'm pretty sure most of our D&D play was Forgotten Realms (in the "default D&D world" sense). We'd include elements from other settings, but never too full-on. However, the more I learned about Forgotten Realms, the more cramped it felt to me. It seemed like every bit of it was full of powerful NPCs (and the NPCs, when statted up, were way beyond the power levels I had in my head!) engaged in important, world-shaking plots. It became more and more difficult to figure out how a new bunch of characters could fit into this enormous power drama. After all, if the characters were anywhere near the power level necessary to even interact with people like Elminster, how would you explain that none of these people knew about them? It was just too much to work out: they place felt cramped with powerful characters and existing plotlines.

    As for the other settings, Dark Sun somehow completely captured my imagination. This is remarkable, because I've never read any of the books (maybe never even seen any of the books!). But the concept of a dark, alien, desert D&D was very cool. Then again, Dune was one of my favourite books at that age. I think some of my friends told me a little bit about it, and it sounded cool. (Has anyone done Dark Sun Apocalypse World yet?)

    Planescape and Spelljammer sounded interesting, but I could never bring myself to play in them. It was just so far removed from reality (or any fiction that I enjoyed), that I couldn't imagine them in play. Too much power and weirdness and magic and crazy lasersharking, for my tastes. That level of gonzo, I couldn't handle, I suppose. That said, I never tried them. I suppose in a properly limited campaign frame (i.e. cutting down the elements in play to a specific, small segment) they might be interesting!

    The others didn't interest me very much.

    This thread is making me think about published campaign settings in a different way, however. There is something, perhaps, to be said for the effect produced by all the players reading various bits of the setting before playing. It kind of simulates the "rumours your character may have heard about the world" effect, doesn't it? Like how most of the players in Forgotten Realms have heard about Elminster, but one might just know he's some powerful wizard, while another knows some stories about him, and what he's most likely currently up to (ruling Waterdeep, or whatever).

    Home-brew campaigns rarely had that whole thing where you look at the bigger picture. Like how some people above are mentioning that their characters were hoping to eventually become powerful enough to face [some entity in the setting]. It's harder to do that when you're learning about the whole world together, for scratch.

    Hmm! Interesting.

    \nGreyhawk:

    Well I started in Greyhawk and never really left it. My group really loved the Gord the Rogue novels you see and they gave me a really good flavor of the world without proscribing too much. Plus the Gazetteer and that giant hex map just excited me to go and explore. Pretty much any campaign I run is set here. I made a version of Greyhawk that I consider mine and that never really happened with the other game worlds.

    Forgotten Realms: The Realms is my favorite world that I never play in. On some level it always felt like Ed Greenwood's and R.A. Salvatore's world and not mine. I loved reading the novels and the Realm's supplements but I always mined them for ideas and items to import into my Greyhawk campaigns. I can play in the Realms fine but GMing in just never gelled for me.

    Dark Sun I think my experience with Dark Sun is best summed up by a rant by my friend Steve. "I was a high level cleric raising the dead and smiting evil and now I'm starving in the desert wielding a basilisk thigh bone with a thorn on it! I hate this game!" It just seemed to be punishment for punishment's sake really. None of us really knew what to do with it and the high fantasy to gritty fantasy transition was too abrupt.

    Spelljammer Loved it. Absolutely loved it. I've run it as both a dedicated space campaign and as a palette cleanser between dungeon delves. I don't know why swashbuckling in space works better than swashbuckling on the ocean but it did. I think it was the freedom to just make up anything. Its like Star Trek. Every planet can be a complete change of pace and theme. Plus, when it became implausible for yet another 20th level necromancer to appear on their home planet I could bring one in from space.

    Ravenloft This was very similar to Spelljammer in that I used it as a palette cleanser in between dungeon delves. I think if I went back to it now, I could probably craft a campaign within a realm but back then the supplements were one module in this domain, another over here, then the mists send you of to a third. It was too disjointed to sustain as a campaign. We loved it for a change of pace though. I DM'd a very high level high magic party and even they shivered when the mists came down. They knew I took the gloves off in Ravenloft and they loved it.

    Planescape This set never worked for me. I really liked the freewheeling nature of the Outer Planes. It was like Spelljammer for me where the Outer Planes were a blank slate with a few big personalities and general trends and tropes. Planescape drastically changed the feel and flavor and I didn't feel it was for the better. I did love the idea of Sigil though.

    \n What, no one remembers Council of Wyrms?
