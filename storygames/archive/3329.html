\n I've completely given up trying to read all the GC games. This year, I haven't looked at a single one of them. However, I know there's always something in there that amazes me. So, people who know more than I do: Could you point me towards games that fulfill one or more of the following criteria...
\n
\n* No resource management. (So no stat buying, no fiddling with currency etc).
\n* Rules affecting narrative structures and flow in unconventional ways. (That is, not just "round after round" or "scene after scene" play - I'm thinking stuff like playing out scenes backwards in time; having several parallel stories; surrealistic jumps etc).
\n* Clever ways of presenting information. (Cards, flowcharts, etc).
\n
\n[EDIT] Oh, yeah - it's completely OK to pimp your game here, as long as it fits with the above! [/EDIT]

    \n You'll be wanting Jason Morningstar's Grover Cleveland, which ticks all the boxes:

    *no resource management - all you have to keep track of are your traits and the various secrets.
    *unconventional narrative structures - sure, you take it in turns framing scenes on a round by round basis, but whenever you switch scenes you also switch round who is playing who, so everyone gets a turn playing Rose, the protagonist.
    *presenting information - there's a lot of evocative flavour in the form of situation elements that you pick at the start of the game and tie to the various face cards on a funky situation sheet. When the card crops up in play, so too does the element.

    I'd pimp my own game, Lost Days of Memories and Madness, but it's all about the currency, so I won't bother!

    \n While I'd like to suggest my game, Know Thyself, it's not firing off enough on its own to even consider how it applies to your criteria. That said, I suspect the final version might.

    But I will suggest A Penny For My Thoughts by Paul Tevis. In his game, there's practically no resource management, only a literal fiddling of currency as you hand coins off to other players. There's no sense of linear time in either game, but especially Paul's as you're re-enacting moments of your forgotten life. The character sheet for Paul's game is a questionnaire you fill out during play as you remember more about your character. There's also weak character ownership elements in the game, because the actions you take during your memories are dictated by others (though you have control over the environment and all "NPCs").

    He's working on it right now as an ashcan for GenCon, and I know it's seen some improvement since the Game Chef edition because he's part of the game design group Mike Sugarbaker & I started up, Master Mines.

    \n Matthijs, try Iain McAllister's game, Stitch. It's a time travel game, told against a time limit. A little bit of stats, but not much, and it's used in a clever way with the clock.

    You could also try my game, The Saltwater Rose, if you'd consider a play-by-post game. No resource management; presents information on a forum, which is pretty unconventional; completely fucked-up narrative structure, since you're encouraged to tell stories which are inconsistent with each other.

    And I'll second A Penny For My Thoughts, which is a remarkable little game.
