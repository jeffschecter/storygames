\n I'm been working on my own table-top RPG similar to D&D off & on since 2000. I'm debating if it's worth finishing(near completion) and putting in the additional effort to publish it. So I wanted to ask here among more experienced players if it seems like a good gaming engine. I tried to design it so that it was a table-top first, but could easily be converted to a video game or even a card game without changing the gaming mechanics. It's kinda are to explain how it works, but my legal advice was not to disclose my formulas or algorithms.

The game features 5 playable races, each with 5 sub-races. It has 5 attributes, divided into 3 categories of: Physical, Mental, and Spiritual. There are 120 skills to build, with additional skills that can be had if the player joins a profession guild(joining a guild is not required). There are 16 profession guilds, and 10+ artisan guilds. There are 300 spells in the game, all of which are unique. The game features weapon/armor breakage mechanics, but not required. It also features environment mechanics, but not required. To also features mass army battles/territory/town mechanics, but not required. There are also item & spell creation & improvement mechanics, but not required. There are also detailed/specific item value mechanics never used before in a table-top game, but not required.

All in all the game is massive, covers everything I could think of. What makes the game really different is that skills are leveled up individually based on success, rather than the whole character. There is no character level, but a character's fame is the closest thing to that. The way the formula works, is that the easier it is to succeed the attempt, the less XP for that skill you get. The harder the successful attempt, the more XP for that skill you get. I choose skill leveling for a few reasons. One that it didn't make sense to me for a character to level up, and improve skills never/hardly used. Another reason is that an highly experienced character can play with low experienced characters and be close to equals by simply using skills that are less developed. For example, the experienced character is highly skilled in edged weapons, but could switch over to blunt weapons with new character/players for more balanced gameplay without having to make another character to fit in with the group power-wise.

My fears about the game is that it may be too complicated for beginners. The skill formula is slightly complicated I feel (although my wife had no idea what she was looking at and couldn't even begin the equation). Also, I think tracking various skill levels might be over-whelming for some. I tried to design it so that various gaming mechanics would not be required in order to play, but be available at a whim for more experienced players looking for more of a challenge.

So has anyone here ever played a table-top RPG that used skill-leveling, rather than character leveling? Was it fun? Do you think a majority of players would like it? I would like to hear some feedback on what I've described thus far, and I can answer questions if you have them. I am also open to suggestions.

EDIT: FYI, I am 35 and I don't have anyone that can play test this game for me. Nor do I know anyone that has any D&D experience that can give me some sort of feedback on what I've designed. So I came here.

    \n I'd say there are more players than you believe that would love your system... but the actual problem is that there are less GMs than you think that would be able to run your game. And sadly, that limits a lot the audience of your game. So unless your game comes with a carefully planned step-by-step fool-proof guide to masterfully GM it without too much prep nor railroady modules, that also allows GMs to sit back, relax and enjoy the game, I can't say I'd be willing to GM it.

    I played 3.5 and Pathfinder, and certainly loved some aspects of it's complexity, unbalanced here and there as they are. The game is certainly more enjoyable when you can relax and play your character instead of taking care of the details of the math. Even hunting for loopholes in the rules, spells or the system to game it has it's appeal. But GMing them requires a level of knowledge of the system that makes me feel quite insecure about GMing them, even when I've been GMing other games fo years and hacking and designing my own for almost the same time.

    Now, relax, the kind of GM friendly game that I describe above is actually attainable, probably without altering your system. Perhaps some of the accounting should be handled digitally but the actual GM advice needed passes trough clearly stating the philosophy of your game to the possible GMs, what kind of atmosphere you're envisioning for the game and exactly what things should the GM be doing to attain it. Also, check all you mechanics to see which ones could be oversee by players instead of the GM, and try to make everything at your hand to take the load off the GM.

    For example, collaborative world-building is an incredible tool to bring players into the setting, make them invest on it from the start and have everyone on the same page about the whole campaign from the start. They don't have to work on all the details, that's for the GM to bring up on them later, but some basic outlines are enough to inspire the GMs to give every player what they expect from the game.

    Allow me to go back on "Clarifying the game philosophy to the GM and the players". Is the game supposed to be lethal at low level or any level? Are the PCs strongly protected from harm but still under fear of losing their investments? Is it a full Power Fantasy where PCs can face anything and only strategy and luck decide if they overcome the challenges, but come out mostly unscathed otherwise?
    Is it "all of the above", as it happens with D&D in different levels and for different classes? Every question here identifies a different game philosophy, which must be clear right from the start as they define different ways of playing the same game.

    For example, if the game is lethal, players will enjoy the thrill of facing the unknown knowing their characters can die in the most unexpected way and moment. However, spending one hour or a full session to create a character that dies on the first ten minutes of gameplay is frustrating, specially if you managed to flesh it with an specially interesting background or other features.

    If the game is a Power Fantasy it's the opposite case. Here PCs are supposed to be legendary heroes and can only die at the last battle in a glorious moment of sacrifice that will be remenbered though the ages. Here, a full session of character creation is too little time for creating these greater-than-life heroes.

    D&D works on lethal mode on the first 5 levels (unless the GM carefully balances the encounters and protects a bit the party or the players learn to play in full coward/paranoid mode), then goes to a middle ground where the PCs certainly have unique supernatural or super-human abilities, and in later levels goes into a place where only mythic creatures and gods can prove to be a good fight.

    GMing for every kind of philosophy requires a different set of tools, procedures and social skills. For a lethal mode the GM needs to be more in control of the creative agenda, for most of the fear comes from having the players not know what they are facing and have them feel unable to face the challenge directly from the start. For a Power Fantasy the GM needs to make the players feel more confident of their skills by showering them in minions to kill by dozens, letting the players know that she can only stall but not stop them, though all she needs to do to "win" is to stall them long enough or make them use up their resources before the final battle. This prompts them into thinking better strategies to save up time and other resources, bringing interesting scenes to the game.

    As you can see, there are a lot of these tools for GMing engraved in the system, and you must let the GMs know exactly which are which. Having modular rules is a bless but it's better to know which of those rules will produce the atmosphere the GMs are looking for.

    \n The types of features that you quote for your game are very common in high-end mainstream commercial rpg design. This means that you're working in the exact field where the competition has high levels of rules quality, player base, writing quality, production quality and marketing. Most successful games in the category also enjoy a significant history in the market and a very active on-going publishing program. Even worse, it's a type of game where adoption threshold is high, so people are less likely to switch to a new product for minor reasons. All that means that even assuming that your game is brilliant for its type, you're still down in 4-6 crucial categories of competitiveness already before you even have a product, not to speak of entering the market.

    (For comparison, most independent game projects tend to be highly divergent from traditional mainstream game design. One reason for this is simply observable in the above paragraph: when you're doing something unique and different, you don't need to compete directly with the established high-end product. In that situation a more economical product is entirely feasible.)

    All this makes the overall publishing prospects of the game pretty grim. You haven't really discussed your goals for the game here, but you mention that you're considering publication. If you are, I would recommend doing a conservative digital strategy where you make your game available for free download, and see how many people you can get to play it by discussing it a bit on the gaming forums and linking people to it. That's a good, simple starting point that doesn't require much in the way of product development, and it'll help you to gauge the interest of the scene and the quality of the competition. If the feedback at that point is promising, you can always move on to new steps in developing the product.\n
    So has anyone here ever played a table-top RPG that used skill-leveling, rather than character leveling? Was it fun? Do you think a majority of players would like it? I would like to hear some feedback on what I've described thus far, and I can answer questions if you have them. I am also open to suggestions.
    \nBurning Wheel uses individually leveled skills, and it also has the other bullet-points you mention. More significantly from your viewpoint, it's a very successful indie game made with a small, dedicated team in a subgenre that has all those challenges I mentioned up there. There's nothing more difficult than getting a dedicated D&D gamer to read a book and decide to commit to a new campaign, yet BW seems to consistently achieve this. If I were writing a game of this type, looking for success in publishing it, I would look very carefully at Luke's recipe. (For the curious, I understand the recipe to begin with demoing your game for a couple of years in a metropolitan area, just to get the necessary grass-roots buzz and playtest polish.)

    The Shadow of Yesterday is another successful example of a game with similar features (including individually leveled skills, although this time with build points), again published independently. It is also a worthwhile subject of examination because of how the creative and economic goals of the project were so different from Burning Wheel's, and how that influences the continuing development of the game. By contrasting the two projects one can get a good sense of what it takes to develop artistic and economic success, and what kinds of publishing strategy one might choose to use.

    I should emphasize that every bullet point you mention for your game has been done by dozens of titles in the history of rpgs, the above two are just a couple of picks that strike me as ones you might want to look at for reasonably recent comparison and examples of how to go about publishing that sort of thing.\n

    \nA small sermon on the matter, if I may:

    That advice is legally sound, but practically stupid. It's stupid because your fundamental goal as a designer surely is to have people see what you've done - that is definitely your goal as a publisher. In the modern world it is very easy to publish, very easy to be pirated, but extremely hard to be noticed: your problem as an independent creator is not that somebody might steal your work, but rather simply that nobody will care even when you make it freely available. If you can fix the latter problem, then you're already in the most successful one percent of creators. Ten thousand independent creators languish in obscurity for every one that ever had to practically worry about plagiarism or piracy of their product. If we drop the piracy concern, then it's like a hundred thousand or a million in obscurity for every case of serious plagiarism.

    The scenario against which secretiveness is supposed to protect you is the one where a competitor steals your brilliant ideas and beats you to market with a copycat product. This makes sense in mobile phone development or the aeroplane industry, but applying that to a field like game design is just ignorant. Anybody looking for an easy score is already working in other fields, anybody interested in making a name for themselves is not going to touch anything with the stain of plagiarism, and anybody struggling to actually create anything knows the horrible truth: ideas are cheap, and everybody has them.

    The real work is always in product development, and why-ever would you want to develop based on stolen good ideas, when you could be developing your own legit good ideas. (If you don't have good ideas, good luck with that development work.) It's just a category error to worry about plagiarism in this context like you had some state secrets there. The absolute worst that might happen is that you'll stop publishing your thing, and ten years later you're pathetically looking at somebody else publishing something vaguely similar, and you'll be uncertain about whether they might have read your thing at some point. Who cares, everybody takes influence, and if you didn't work to bring your thing to market then it's good that somebody took those ideas and made something of them.

    This concern over secretiveness would otherwise be merely an amusing quirk that some people bring with them to the publishing arena, except that with hobby publishers it's a pretty crippling handicap to try to follow that corporate product development secrecy protocol: if you're not showing your stuff to anybody, then you won't get feedback, won't get players, won't get buzz. What you will get is surprised when nobody knows and nobody cares about your product later on, when you try to sell it. (I assume that selling is a feature in the plan here, as otherwise the secrecy thing makes even less sense.)

    And this cannot be emphasized enough: the social censure against plagiarism is absolutely savage in the creative industry, including the hobby publishing industry. It's a thousand times worse than any digital pirate ever faced. A publisher would basically have to be insane to grab your stuff and blatantly plagiarize it for their own product; they'd have to be willing to sacrifice their good name and reputation as human beings in a community of peers and colleagues for the dubious advantage that your ideas might provide them. (Dubious because it'd have to some friggin' amazing ideas to offset the sales lost due to customer boycotts.) Maybe if you had an anti-gravity generator instead of some dicing mechanics, that'd make sense. Maybe.

    \n Hi, Xarcell, and welcome to Story Games.

    First of all, listen to Eero (the post above me) - he says most of the important stuff I thought while I read your post, and he's very knowledgeable about both game design and the publishing industry.

    So, in short, I agree fully with Eero. But here are some additional thoughts:

    1. Eero is entirely correct about concerns of plagiarism. It's simply not worth worrying about: most of the highly-successful indie RPGs published in the last half-decade or so were developed "in public", with most (and often all) of the game available for free. In this world, getting people to *hear* about your game is much harder than anything else. (And I've never heard of any game getting "plagiarized" and that becoming an issue in some way - people reading along, have you heard of such a story? Maybe it happened back in the 70's, but I haven't heard of that ever being an issue in more recent times.)

    However, it occurs to me that maybe what you're trying to protect is whatever elements allow the system to be converted "into a video game or card game". I can't say without seeing the details, but I suppose it's possible there is some brilliant "technology" there that's worth protecting. Is that what you meant?

    2. For a lot of people, particularly in this community, this kind of thing draws a particular response: \n
    It's kinda are to explain how it works [...]
    There are also detailed/specific item value mechanics never used before in a table-top game [...]
    My fears about the game is that it may be too complicated for beginners. The skill formula is slightly complicated I feel (although my wife had no idea what she was looking at and couldn't even begin the equation).

Surely there are some gamers out there who might be excited by complexity alone, but most people, as far as I can tell, see something like this and cringe. I'd encourage you to take a look at other games out there - there are lots of free ones you can take a look at.     \n To expand on one of Eero's points, the way to get Burning Wheel traction was tireless going to conventions for years on end, individually converting people, while also doing tons of local playtesters. That's one way to get people to pay attention, and it's also a way to iterate and get a better game.

Something that seems marginal may turn out to be a total rockstar. Mechanics only exist in a greater context: talking about your mechanics with people and letting people see them will certainly help you get feedback.

    \n Right, considering that context in light of how others go about the rpg hobby...

    I think you should get into the scene and play for a bit. Like, play roleplaying games for a year or two, and then see what you feel like about developing and publishing your own game. It doesn't matter what you play: Pathfinder, some indie weirdness, your own game. Just play, and see what it is about roleplaying that actually matters to you. It seems to me like utter insanity to contemplate publishing when you haven't been playing for 20 years; that's like planning to open a chopper shop because you used to ride a motorcycle 20 years ago, despite not owning a single 'cycle. Would you buy a motorcycle from somebody who doesn't even ride?

    Regarding your workflow question: separate your writing work from your book layout concerns entirely for now. Write your manuscript on parchment with a feather quill if you feel like it. Many, many people with entirely professional workflows choose to write in something like Notepad (heck, I do that myself often enough). Mark tables in whatever manner works for you while writing. Then later, when layout becomes a current concern because you've actually finished the game, you can see about importing your manuscript into a layout environment. We'll help with the practical planning when you come to that point.

    (You can save work in layout by doing your manuscript in a sensible style - consistent styling in a word processor compatible with your layout software being a dramatic example. However, at this level of operation I don't recommend worrying about detail like that. Just get the manuscript done any way you can, that's ultimately the only thing that matters.)

    I feel like your approach to all this is entirely jumbled up: you're planning writing a game without playing them, and publishing without having written one, and layout without a manuscript in hand. Slow down and progress logically! I believe that if you'll actually play games a bit and see what people actually do in the rpg scene, you'll gain a more realistic picture of what you might have to offer in terms of game design and development. I understand all about being in love with your own ideas, but as others have said, it is frankly pretty unlikely that your game is anything special for most gamers - we're smart people actively engaging in the hobby, you need to get up pretty early in the morning to bring a surprise

    \n I'm making this a separate post because I do have advice to offer about layout, but I wanted to emphasize that it is an entirely secondary academic concern at this point. You're too early to be worrying about that.

    When you have a finished manuscript that you want to turn into a book (note that this is not a mandatory thing - you could publish as e.g. a website instead), you'll want to have it run through a process of layout. For this you either get somebody else to do it, or you learn how to do the layout yourself. If you choose the latter, you need to choose the software you'll use to do it.

    I should note that a common mistake is to use a word processor such as Microsoft Word at this juncture, perhaps because people in corporate environments often create little memos and such in Word. It is not impossible to make a nice book with Word, but it is an up-hill battle that requires expertise. Unfortunately, the person most likely to use a word processor to do layout is very likely one who doesn't have much expertise with either layout or office software. I would advice forgetting this route and instead opting for a dedicated layout software.

    A common suggestion in this regard is to use Scribus, but that's mostly because it's free - Scribus is not very good, frankly. If you can hack the programming, I suspect that some variation of LaTeX (Lynx, for example) might serve better. Scribus will accomplish a book, but it'll be through tears.

    The ideal choice would be Adobe InDesign, which is likely the best software for the purpose on the market right now. However, it is quite expensive. An interesting hack for experienced users would be to install the 30 day free demo and do the layout before the time runs out Of course piracy and student licenses and such are also options.

    (Another similarly priced product of similar quality would be Quark XPress. The points that apply to InDesign apply to that as well, except that Quark's not as well known and popular among the small press hipster crowd.)

    I suspect that the best choice for a newbie book designer at this point is to pay for an affordable semi-professional software - there are a few options that people tend to recommend when the matter comes up. Such a software might cost e.g. $50, and apparently they do the job well enough when it comes to a simple book design. I can't recommend any specific softwares myself, as I haven't tried these out, but I'm sure you'll find some suggestions if you want to find out more. There's a thread on this forum every year or so about the subject, the various options are discussed in those pretty thoroughly.

    I should note that I haven't used Microsoft Publisher myself, so can't compare it to Scribus and InDesign/QuarkXPress (with which I am personally familiar). People tend to put it down, though, so it's difficult to say whether it's a better choice than Scribus. I have difficulty imagining that it would be worse, though, so if I had to pick one of those, I'd go with Publisher

    Why would HTML to PDF pose issues with Typography?
    \nThe short answer is that whatever software is used to do the conversion will have to do the actual text-flow decisions, and handle the kerning logic. If that software has high-quality typesetting features, then the typesetting will be of high quality. However, this is practically speaking very rare in this kind of software. What's worse, it is unlikely that the software will allow the designer much control over the text flow, so they can't do manual corrections to the typesetting of the pdf.

    As for whether one is bothered by the kind of quality loss this kind of process represents (mis-hyphenation, unoptimal linebreaking, word gaps, kerning issues, etc.), that'd likely depend on the purpose of the file and one's expectations. For me this level of quality - comparable to what you'd get by doing layout in e.g. Word - would be fine for a free guerrilla product, while completely unacceptable for a quality product. It's the difference between professional and amateur quality, pretty much.\n

      I'm been working on my own table-top RPG similar to D&D off & on since 2000. I'm debating if it's worth finishing(near completion) and putting in the additional effort to publish it. So I wanted to ask here among more experienced players if it seems like a good gaming engine. I tried to design it so that it was a table-top first, but could easily be converted to a video game or even a card game without changing the gaming mechanics. It's kinda are to explain how it works, but my legal advice was not to disclose my formulas or algorithms.

      The game features 5 playable races, each with 5 sub-races. It has 5 attributes, divided into 3 categories of: Physical, Mental, and Spiritual. There are 120 skills to build, with additional skills that can be had if the player joins a profession guild(joining a guild is not required). There are 16 profession guilds, and 10+ artisan guilds. There are 300 spells in the game, all of which are unique. The game features weapon/armor breakage mechanics, but not required. It also features environment mechanics, but not required. To also features mass army battles/territory/town mechanics, but not required. There are also item & spell creation & improvement mechanics, but not required. There are also detailed/specific item value mechanics never used before in a table-top game, but not required.

      All in all the game is massive, covers everything I could think of. What makes the game really different is that skills are leveled up individually based on success, rather than the whole character. There is no character level, but a character's fame is the closest thing to that. The way the formula works, is that the easier it is to succeed the attempt, the less XP for that skill you get. The harder the successful attempt, the more XP for that skill you get. I choose skill leveling for a few reasons. One that it didn't make sense to me for a character to level up, and improve skills never/hardly used. Another reason is that an highly experienced character can play with low experienced characters and be close to equals by simply using skills that are less developed. For example, the experienced character is highly skilled in edged weapons, but could switch over to blunt weapons with new character/players for more balanced gameplay without having to make another character to fit in with the group power-wise.

      My fears about the game is that it may be too complicated for beginners. The skill formula is slightly complicated I feel (although my wife had no idea what she was looking at and couldn't even begin the equation). Also, I think tracking various skill levels might be over-whelming for some. I tried to design it so that various gaming mechanics would not be required in order to play, but be available at a whim for more experienced players looking for more of a challenge.

      So has anyone here ever played a table-top RPG that used skill-leveling, rather than character leveling? Was it fun? Do you think a majority of players would like it? I would like to hear some feedback on what I've described thus far, and I can answer questions if you have them. I am also open to suggestions.

      EDIT: FYI, I am 35 and I don't have anyone that can play test this game for me. Nor do I know anyone that has any D&D experience that can give me some sort of feedback on what I've designed. So I came here.
      \nI suggest you start an online game via google + to playtest your game. You may also want to check out all the different online rpg communities to see what audience your game might appeal to. There are different communities with different interests favoring different kinds of design. Knowing if you fall into a particular group is important because marketing to the wrong crowd or building a game based on advice from a community that doesn't share your goals can steer you in the wrong direction. But above get several months if playtesting in. It sounds like that is the area you really need to focus on first. Running games online is pretty easy.
