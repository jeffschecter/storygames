    Bruce, I do not think you are getting it right. I say that because I think you've got a characiture in your mind of what Narrativist games must be like.

    Bruce, all this talk about noir makes it impossible for me to stay away.

    I'm currently making efforts to write a game that should follow the narratives of 50s film noir. It's called One Can Have Her and started as a 24hour game, but I've rebuilt it twice since then. Right now I'm dying to playtest the latest version, but can't find the time. Perhaps you're looking for something already published, and then I'll disappoint you.

    Anyway, the thing that got me started was the endings of most noirs, where either everyone dies or maybe someone survives by doing some pretty questionable stuff. I'm using the prisoner's dilemma for this, and during endgame each player gets to choose if their character will rat on the others or not. If you're ratting, but no one else does, you get to decide what happens to your character and the femme fatale as an epilogue, while everyone else dies or goes to jail. If more than one character rats, everyone dies. If no one does, no one gets the girl, but instead continue some mediocre existence.

    That's a game mechanic for emulating part of a narrative structure, I would think. Another thing I've got is that each character has aspects (which can be anything from skills to belongings or personality traits) that are connected to "moral" values (you can have the aspect "boxing" connected to "physical health".) I don't know if "health" would be a moral value, so perhaps I need a better term. Anyway, each value can be "high" or "low".

    When you enter a conflict you have to use an aspect, but can activate more to get an advantage at the die roll. After the conflict, at least one of the corresponding values you've used has to switch from high to low or vice versa. That means that you can have a high physical health, go boxing and lower it to "low", or the other way around. This gives the player a chance to say what the previous scene and conflict actually meant, story-wise. Perhaps the beating was what the guy needed to sober up and get a grip, so physical health goes "high", for example.

    I started thinking about this after reading Robert McKee's book Story. He says that the only reason to include a scene in a movie script is to show a value switching polarity, otherwise it should be cut. We don't want to see the romantic couple having a high love, and then another scene with high love, followed by another scene with high love. But if we change the middle scene to a low-love scene, perhaps they're fighting, suddenly the story's a lot more interesting.

    Also, the character sheets look really neat after you've filled out what happens to the values in different scenes, with zigzagging values all across them. Makes it easy to see where the action has been.

    Is it something like that you're looking for, Bruce?

The other narrative thread is: my friends and I discovering not just the original films, but the works of these two academics and the strangeness (and untimely, mysterious demise) that eventually befell them, and recently the strangeness befalling us.
