    \n Each game has its strengths and weaknesses. I recommend being quite explicit about ways that things are different and to make an active effort to habit break as appropriate.

    My Apocalypse World game fell a bit flat because the players felt like it was their job to cooperate with each other. The monsterhearts game we ran right afterword worked better because players were in eachother's business and at eachother's throats. It can be really helpful to say, quite explicitly, "I know you guys are used to having the party stick together. But that's not what this game is about. There is no party--you can act at cross purposes and it will make the game better, not worse"

    With fiasco, the issue is pacing. characters used to combat, skill checks, and long negotiations will have trouble with the scene pacing structure of games like fiasco. Fiasco in particular because players are either supposed to shape the first half or the second half of the scene, but there's a subconcious tendency for everyone to shape a whole scene. I recommend bringing a timer to your first game, and explicitly timing the setup and the results separately.

    \n In my experience:

    * Show, display, and demonstrate other styles of gaming, other games, other techniques, but without being pushy. It's not a conflict of "trad" vs. "indie", it's just you showing people what's out there, neither better nor worse.

    You might offer to run a session (or more) of a particular game. You might say, "Hey, here's a cool technique I like to use as GM. What do you think of that?" You might relegate more authority for certain things to your players when GMing a trad game. You might bring a Fiasco playset and show it to some people, or the Lady Blackbird booklet, or whatever you're excited about, before or after the game (but without distracting from it). And so on.

    One good technique is to say that you're running a bit of a more oddball game and invite everyone, then see who shows interest.

    * Try to find what makes a particular game that's NOT in line with your preferences cool. Is there a set plot on rails, while the characters try to have cool accents and be badass? Then ask yourself: "Can I accept what I cannot change, and find the fun or the challenge in this other thing?" Join that game, accept that the plot is something you will experience passively, and put your character-acting skills to the test!

    If the game is all about clever character builds, then ask your fellow gamers what they're excited about and what their tricks are, then make it a challenge for yourself to come up with a better one, a more badass, optimized character.

    If the game is about crazy risks and just trying to survive, adopt that mindset and jump into the fray.

    If you still can't enjoy the game, have a chat with the GM and/or group (depending on the game and play culture) and ask whether they're open to adding something or taking something away. If they resist, gracefully say that it was nice playing with them, but this game is not quite what you were looking for, and bow out.

    But don't try to force people to change, and don't play a game while secretly (or openly) grumbling that you wish it was something else.

    \n The opening post claims the two options are to (1) encourage GMs or (2) adjust preferences. Given those options, I would suggest a mix of both.

    1a) Pitch an indie game to a GM. I would recommend an easy-to-learn 1-shot. Fiasco qualifies, but is also a radical departure from traditional resolution, so Lady Blackbird might be an easier change of pace. Something tightly constrained, like Penny For My Thoughts with its narration prompts, might also be a nice alternative. If the group likes more crunch and rules traction, Dogs in the Vineyard is an option, though you'd need a more hard-working GM.

    1b) If your club GMs don't want to run weird new games, then you can instead suggest your favorite indie techniques be ported into trad games. Fanmail, metagame Fate/Hero/Story points for rerolls, flashback/recoup scenes, tracking relationships and folding them into rolls, giving players moments of authority over specific chunks of world/plot/backstory -- all of these can easily be tacked onto most trad games. I have a friend who runs an entire track of "D&D with a little indie" at cons. All you need to do is identify an overlap between the stuff you like and the stuff that'll sound good to the GM.

    I recall folks telling me that the GM technique of "I need to do a thing to the story or your character now, but here are some points in return, which you can later use for important stuff" has been an easy sell.

    2) Trad games often contain multitudes, so see if you can find an aspect within them that you like, and if so, push for play to emphasize that. As a player, I've certainly turned dungeon crawls with a side of intrigue into intrigue with a side of dungeon crawling. This is extremely dependent on the group, though, so if you and the other players have completely different taste, it might be a lost cause.

    \n catty_big,

    It's a pretty natural place to be in, and we've all been there. (In the city where I live, there's a huge gaming community organized through Meetup.com, and I've given up on interacting with them because they have a very specific approach to gaming I don't like. For example, I've had people tell me, "It's an indie game! That means the rules don't matter!" So I do very little gaming. I'd rather play and have fun occasionally than waste lots of time playing games and feeling unsatisfied, and that's OK by me. Quality over quantity.)

    You should keep in mind that you expecting a bunch of gamers somewhere to be familiar with certain games or styles of play isn't entirely reasonable. It doesn't reflect on them as people in either case. They may be awesome gamers and good friends, or not, either way.

    Here, I'll try to offer practical advice!

    Things which have worked for me:

    1. Only run games, don't play them.

    There are lots of games which have cool "indie" stuff in them but it's not strictly "visible" to the players. You can play something like Apocalypse World or The Shadow of Yesterday (or even Dogs in the Vineyard) with 'trad' players who don't realize they're doing anything different. (On a sidenote, while there are fanatics on both/all sides, most people just think of games as games and don't see these lines at all. They just play a game and they like it or they don't! No point trying to make them take sides by labeling it as belonging in one camp but not another.)

    A simple example: play D&D, but ask the players to set important goals for their characters, and tell them that it's a major source of XP in your game. When the player says, "I want to kill the Duke of Westerness!", you say, "Cool, that's worth 5000 XP. Pull it off, and you get 5,000 XPs." To the players, it will feel like D&D. To the GM, it feels very different: you're not writing a "plot" anymore, but prepping in reaction to the players' ideas.

    Another example:

    I sometimes prep for games as a GM by setting myself a challenge. It goes like this:

    I don't get to do any prep (beyond a basic outline of premise/setting) until the characters have been made, including not just stats but also as much as I can get - backgrounds, histories, beliefs, relationships, etc.

    Now, I can start to do some prep. But! I'm not allowed to create *anything* that's not coming directly from, or closely related to, something a player came up with. I can't create new material of my own -- that's not allowed!

    So, for instance, I don't have a villain in mind, I don't create one. I wait for the players to describe their characters. If someone writes a backstory where they were abandoned by their stepfather when the war broke out... now I have a potential bad guy, and something about a war (I can ask for more details: "Whose side was your stepfather on? Are they still around?"). Then I use those elements to create something - this is now the direction for the campaign.

    2. When you play games, find the fun in games which are not normally to your liking. Embrace the fun of min-maxing or dungeon crawling or railroading (or whatever), and play with that. Just like you wouldn't sit down to a game of Chess expecting a dramatic masterpiece with a plot reversal... know what you're in for and play that when you feel like it. Appreciate it for what it is, and don't bring in other expectations! Leave those for other games or other groups.

    3. Find ways to see which players share some interests with you. Invite them to play something unusual, see who shows up. Start a bi-weekly "experiments in gaming" club, where you take turns running one-shots. Host a dinner party where you try out a new game. And so on. Chat with people about what they liked and what they didn't, what excites them, that kind of thing. You'll see who's interested and who's not.

    You won't get as much by talking or debating - people get riled up about terms like "trad" and "indie" - but just play a game with them and you'll see their actual reaction, instead of some ingrained response.

    4. Some of my best experiences have been playing with non-gamers. No like-minded gamers around? Then find like-minded people first, then see if they're potentially interested in some games.

    I game with people whose company I enjoy, and who are friends, creative, and fun. I start them with something easy and light which excites them, without too much explanation. "Hey, you guys want to try an unusual kind of game on Friday night? I'll make some snacks, come over at 8pm."

    Keep it simple and short, and you'll see if they want more. Eventually you'll have your own gaming scene.

    5. More and more people are playing online. If your local crowd is completely unsatisfying for you, post here (or wherever, G+ is good for this, for example) and find some people who want to play these games over Skype or Hangouts. There are lots! You could easily find people to play a game a week or something similar. There used to be something called "Play Now!" on Story Games... it might be still around, or over on G+.

    6. You could also try online gaming communities, like Meetups or conventions, gaming clubs, game stores, local Universities (which often have a gamer's club), game design groups, G+ groups, and so on. They'll help you hook up with someone in your area.

    Just posting, "Hey, I live in such-and-such-a-place. Is there anyone here in this area?", can be really helpful. Try it here on S-G, try it on any other forum/site/social media thing, and you'll probably find someone with similar interests.

    These are not in order of preference; just a bunch of things you can try!

    Good luck in any case, and let us know how it goes! I used to entertain my friends in New York by sending them e-mail accounts of horrible gaming experiences I had in the place where I live now. It made it a little more fun: instead of just being frustrating, it turned into a funny journal kind of experience and gave me an excuse to explore my own preferences and to stay in touch with friends!

    \n Yeah, I don't recommend lying to your players or misleading them intentionally. "Tricking" people into playing a different type of game than they signed up for is probably a recipe for disaster, and dishonest.

    But, if you're running a game as the GM, aren't you allowed to use whatever techniques you like? That's the way it's worked in every trad gaming group I've seen. It's considered the GM's purview to adopt whatever techniques he or she wishes to adopt.

    For instance, there are all kinds of subtle ways to change your GMing into a more player-driven mode, like the XP goals I outlined above. Here's one of my favourite ways:

    You want the players to frame scenes, not you. You can have a huge conversation about the concept of "scene framing", and see how it goes over. (And it might go over well! But it might not, as well.)

    Or you can do this:

    --"Ok, Lucy, what is your character doing next?"
    --"Uh, I don't know."
    --"Well, you said earlier that you were looking for the treasure map."
    --"Oh, right! I need to get that map!"
    --"Who do you think might have it?"
    --"I suspect the Duke knows something. I'd like to go have a talk with him!"
    --"Ok, great. Where do you think you can find the Duke right now?"
    --"Last time he was in a tent, on a hunting expedition. Maybe he's out in the woods, still, waiting to catch the boar?"
    --"So, how or when will you approach him?"
    --"I'll try to show up when he's having dinner, after the day of hunting. He's more likely to be at the camp then."
    --"Ok, perfect: so, you ride up to the camp around nightfall. The soldiers are heating up pots of soup over the fires, and you can smell some fragrant spiced food from the Duke's tent. He's there, having dinner. Uh, who do you think the Duke has dinner with?"
    --"Uh, probably his advisors. Oh, and didn't he have a young page, as well, the one where we didn't know if he was a young boy or a girl posing as a page?"
    --"Perfect, so you walk into the tent. The Duke is sitting at his oak table, which probably took eleven men to lift and several horses to drag, surrounded by his advisors. The young page greets you. What do you say?"

    I like this approach. There's nothing un-trad about it, but who's framed this scene? Effectively, it's the player. The GM hasn't really contributed anything here, except some colourful description: the player is the one who came up with the scene.

    I love doing this in "trad" games. The game flows effortlessly, since everyone around the table is effectively contributing to scene framing.

    \n If you're running Dogs for players who are used to investigate-and-conquer type play, here's my recommendation:

    1) Emphasize that their mission is to put things right, and that does not come with a boss monster labeled "defeat me and now everything is right". The players define "right", not the GM.

    2) Hand the players everything they need to know about the town very quickly, to make it clear that they aren't being challenged to uncover anything.

    3) Do not have a badguy. The greater an NPC's sin, the more likable you must make them, and the more sympathetic their motives for sinning must be.

    4) If the players are too casually violent, provide some horrified NPC reactions. They can kill Greedy Jed, but don't let them feel good about it -- his wife should arrive at just the wrong moment.

    If you can pull these off, then the players can roll dice to get their way all day long, and the moral issues you like will still emerge.

    Note: you can probably find more tested advice elsewhere from folks who've played Dogs more; I don't claim to be an expert, this is just what I'd do. Ask @jenskot !

    \n Catty,
    Yeah, the trick is going to really come down to: if they have participated in the Internet phenomenon of Indie vs trad gaming. I have found that players who have not read/posted about this topic are very open-minded and willing to try most things.
    But, if thy are aware of this false dichotomy, then they will be resistant to any attempts at subverting their perceived, preferred style. I have seen this on both sides of the fence. I have a pair of friends that I love playing with and who love playing with me and when I say D20, they literally shut down. They ask me to get back to them when I am not paying a trad game. And I know trad gamers that are the opposite as well.

    The best techniques to reach open minds is show, don't tell. Be the change you want to see. Run the games you like and you will find a group of players with similar interests. Once you get some regulars, you guys can take turns GM'ng/MC'ing/facilitating/etc.

    As to playing in other people's games, let them know if there is something that is a deal breaker and then play the game with the group. They can't give you somehting they don't know you need. But you can't expect the whole group to subvert their game to cater to your play style. It will be a compromise for sure. Many so-caled Trad GMs drift into indy territory naturally when they need to be creative or do something that is not covered by the rules. The main barrier to making this work is making sure the introduction of new rules/techniques are not abused as a way to create a "win button" for the traditional encounters. Show the GM the trust you want to receive, and you will get trust back. This will translate into being open-minded about requests for better support for your play style.

    I have mostly just built my own group from non-gamers/ex-gamers rather than try and integrate with existing groups, so I have not had to deal with this as much. You might want to try that instead of trying to change the group you have found.

    Good luck, sounds like a tough row to ho...
