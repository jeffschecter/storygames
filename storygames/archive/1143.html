The recent Exalted thread and 40k thread both talk about this; in a certain design family of games, it's unlikely that you'll play with all the rules all the time, for whatever reason; it becomes an important and overlooked player skill to select which rules you will use and when you will use them.

So, I'm wondering, what are your experiences with this sort of thing? How can we take advantage of it? Clearly we already have in certain ways; the pattern of 'zoomed out' and 'zoomed in' conflicts in HQ, Agon, &c. is a good example. I'm wondering how much farther we can push it.

    The sci-fi game I'm working on these days, Sufficiently Advanced, has a good amount of this scaling or zooming in its conflicts. The next version I put out will have a section talking about starting small-scale conflicts to resolve larger-scale ones. For instance:

    Let's say you're involved in some psychohistorical maneuvering against a particular society. The timescale here is "years," and things are painted in very broad strokes, with no ability to single out individuals. To further your goals, you might start a cognitech research race, with a timescale of "weeks." If the other side is doing too well in the research race, you start a biotech offensive against them, taking out their most intelligent researchers, with a timescale of "days." The other side might counter with a public relations smear campaign, with an "hours" timescale, to which you respond by sending in negotiators, who get into a debate with a timescale of "minutes," and when someone punches one of them it shifts to "seconds." Each sub-conflict gives an award to one side in the larger conflict, depending on who wins. (I'm still working out the details.)
