\n In another discussion (on keys and aspects):\n
Posted By: KayfallAspects, at least to a certain extent, take control of your character away from you and give it to the GM. It's not just the temptation of the extra Fate point that may make you want to take that treasure, it's the cost of one of your own Fate points to not do that thing the GM has asked you to do. Now, many players choose the aspect of Greedy because they want to get into this sort of trouble, so when the GM offers them this kind of Fate point reward, they'll happily take it. These players may also feel perfectly happy about paying a cost to resist their own aspect, because again, they willingly gave up that much control to the GM at the outset when they chose that aspect in the first place. These players will willingly compel themselves, and have fun doing it, even though it's a responsibility first given to the GM. But some players aren't comfortable with compels at all -- they feel as though everything pertaining to their character should be under their control and theirs alone, and the GM doesn't have any right to touch it.
Players feeling that their character should be spared the influence of other players (or a GM) ...
- could they be said to be 'broken'?

Is it in fact possible to maintain such a stance, or is it an illusion?

I believe the very idea of playing a character is to have it interact with the fiction. The fiction has to be built by the players, and thus we all have characters influenced by each other. Why then believe that an illusion of character-independence is possible, or even desirable?

    \n There really is a degenerate case of this preference, and I have seen it cause problems. Not horrible train-wreck problems, because it's rather well catered to by a particular set of GM skills and assumptions, but definitely inter-group problems with players who didn't recognize or share the assumptions that it runs under.

    There are players who prefer to come up with their character, and play them out along more or less the lines they envision, without any real deviations from the envisioned personal character story and certainly not with genuine risk or the possibility that their character will be meaningfully changed in ways they didn't plan. It's the player-side version of the railroad-y "my story" GM, and it's at at least somewhat common.

    Some sets of play preferences don't include much collaboration or challenge. They look "broken" when your idea of what an RPG is involves those things as basic assumptions.

    \n Thomas,

    The paragraph you quoted there is basically just saying I want to have the right to make choices for my character, and the whole compel mechanic seems to take some (but not all) of that right to choose away from me. It's about a preference for keys over aspects, as game mechanics go, not at all about whether other people should be able to influence me or not. I mean, if I wanted to be free of influence I'd sit by myself in a room and roll dice and decide what I want them to mean. The whole point of a roleplaying game is that you interact with each other, have fun, and discover new things about yourselves, the story, or whatever theme you're investigating.

    Take any of Vincent Baker's games as an example of games that influence you a lot while at the same time leaving you with complete control over your character's choices -- you can play the game, and if you want, you can put your character's life on the line in the attempt to get what you want. If you die, no one can say you didn't have a choice, or that you didn't know what was at stake, because the decision to put your life on the line was totally up to you. In another game, where the GM can suddenly ask you to roll some dice, then say, "okay, you failed your observation roll, and got hit by a trap for tons of damage, so... you die," I don't see the fun in it.

    The problem I was having with Fate aspects was somewhat similar, except the GM doesn't necessarily kill me, he says, "you've got a Daredevil aspect, so you have to jump over that chasm there," or "you're Greedy so you have to steal from your best friend now," or "you believe in 'Turn the Other Cheek' so that means you've got to just stand there and watch someone kill your family." I prefer a system like keys in Lady Blackbird, or Beliefs in Burning Wheel, where you select a theme for your character and the system rewards you if you act on it, and it rewards you if you act against it too! The choice is totally up to you to interpret what your theme means and what acting on it might look like. But by all means, put my character in a tricky situation and give him a difficult choice! Influence me as much as you like, just don't *be* me or tell me what my character should choose.

    Then again, maybe one game might be designed around the idea that nobody owns any one character, and everyone has input about how any given character should ask, then it could be different. I was speaking from my own experience, not from some sort of all-knowing perspective on How Games Shalt Be Played.

    \nCalling them "broken" may not be a good start.

    Put them in situations where they see what you consider positive interactions happening around them. Specifically in the context of aspects and compels, don't force compel them as GM. Have some situations come up where they can self-compel. Ask them as a positive thing about the situation rather than stressing that you are the GM forcing them to do something.

    As an off-the-cuff example, say they have "Ladies Man" as an aspect. You've got a sexy female villain, of course. Have a scene where the two characters are in a bar and notice each other. Ask how the player wants it to go. Point out his aspect and suggest that it might make things interesting. Find a compel that is interesting -- maybe their eye's meet across the room. she signs "call me" and mouths a number, does he write it down/remember it? -- and be clear of the consequences -- when he looks up she's gone and he can't trace her. But hey, he has her number! Later on, you might find that the player is a bit more eager both to use the aspect proactively -- maybe I can pay a fate point to charm this NPC with some quick flirting-- rather than purely mechanically -- +2 on contacting 'cause the ladies all love him. Over time he might be a bit more open to compels because he sees they aren't just a way to screw him -- to make his character stand idle or do stupid things.

    Which I guess means that I'm saying that it isn't broken players, but broken GM's that are the problem with compels. And that the solution isn't really change the players, but making the compels more interesting.
