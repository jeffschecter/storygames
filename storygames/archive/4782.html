\n
\n Suspension of Disbelief is the willingness of a person to accept as true the premises of a work of fiction, even if they are fantastic or impossible.
\n
\nMy Suspension of Disbelief is broken when encounters in an RPG seem to be "on hold" until the player characters show up -- or even worse -- when players take the time to make camp, sleep, memorize spells, and then the next morning move along in the adventure only to find encounters where it seems like no time has passed at all!
\n
\nMy friend finds it hard to accept games where you roll to "hit" with a massively damaging energy weapon with an area of effect, but your damage roll is so low that the target keeps merrily running around.
\n
\nWhat sort of things break your suspension of disbelief when playing a tabletop game?

    \n Seriously? Almost nothing. I hate the term Suspension of Disbelief. I think this largely has to do with the fact that I define genres in terms of scope of conflicts and possible resolution and not in terms of "tropes" like most people. I also have a willingness to not know what kind of story we're telling until it's been told and reflect on it.

    So I'm likely to say in *retrospect* of a game, "Well that was all very gothic" or "How noirish."

    Otherwise I tend to setup a game in terms of conflicts, emotions and a little dash of atmosphere. So, I guess perhaps I get annoyed when things fall out of those established ranges but that's much more about me connecting with other players on a emotional level than not "buying" something that happens in the fiction.

    \n What Jesse said. I don't find that suspension of disbelief, per se, is much of an issue for me. Sometimes I will start at something particularly jarring (strange ideas about human behavior, emotions, and relationships, especially - if I can't map the behavior of a human being to my understanding of how human beings behave, it bugs me), but I tend to just incorporate it and move on. The game is about the relationships and the conflicts, not the details.

    However, a clash of visions among players can be really frustrating - if one person thinks we're playing a swashbuckly adventury sort of game, and another is busily drawing up detailed plans and empire-building, that's a pain. Not because of SoD, but because I don't know what I'm supposed to believe.

    \n And to be helpful, I can willingly suspend my disbelief easily. Also, there are times where I'm like, "WTF, that TOTALLY wouldn't happen that way". Then I make a conscious decision:

    * If the GM looks like he can "fix his description"or change the encounter in minor ways by adopting a suggestion, and the suggestion wouldn't disrupt anything, then I make the suggestion:

    "Actually, wouldn't the pirates keep two guards or so outside, as they'd be on the lookout for us since they know we're on their trail?"

    * If it looks like my suggestion that would re-engage that Belief Engine would disrupt the game, or is just untenable for the way things are going, then I *believe extra hard* (no shit) and just let go, thinking, "Yeah, it just happened that way. The pirates didn't consider posting guards because they were probably distracted or something".

    There's one woman in my group who has to take characters with one of the following traits or "drawbacks" every time she plays:
    * Young
    * Foolhardy
    * Brash
    * Man of Action
    (or the equivalent)

    Otherwise, she picks apart an adventure. "I'm a 4th level mage, but why in God's name would I descend into this cavern when I know kobolds are there? I would rather alert the guards, and come back with a company of at least 50 men, or perhaps just hang back and let them take care of things. Treasure? I can't spend treasure if I'm dead!" etc. CoC games, particularly: "Why would I explore this cursed house? I'm just a reporter!"

    But the funny thing is, if she knowingly chooses one of the above traits (and she often does), she will make a comment like "If I wasn't foolhardy I'd totally not go down there with just 4 people. But since I am, I'm totally charging ahead. To the caverns!"

    Note, that this is interesting: That same player, when we play PTA, that alone is justification enough for all suspension of her disbelief. She *thinks* in TV terms, having been suckling on Buffy, Angel, Firefly, ER, etc etc for years. So even if she is not "foolhearty", and I introduce a situation where a normal sane person would never take a plunge (ex: Chasing after a killer without first contacting the police, the FBI, the CIA, and 10 mercenaries), she'll totally say, "Oh, this is totally the scene where I single handedly run after the killer by myself". She treats everything as an archetypal scene from a show or movie (or what a Movie Person or TV Person would do), and even though it sounds a little weird when she says it, that's her totally wearing that cloak of suspension of disbelief.

    So for her, thinking of the RPG as a modern TV serialized show helps her totally suspend her disbelief. Since some great PTA experiences, I've actualyl found myself even running traditional games more like TV shows with that group (calling scenes, etc).

    -Andy

    \n Honestly, it depends on the game/setting.

    In Faerun, for example, a group of four peasants deciding to throw off the relative safety of mundane life to deliberately pursue a peril frought life of hunting inhuman monsters is fine and dandy. In Harn, this same scenario is patently absurd.

    In Champions, for instance, donning bright colored spandex and apprehending villainous evil-doers by way of flexing your own superhuman muscles is the order of the day. In The 23rd Letter, this same scenario is utterly laughable.

    For me, suspension of disbelief seems to get shattered when elements that are completely at odds with the established tenets of a given setting, genre, or style of play get introduced into said setting, genre, or style of play.
