    The type shows up because its a powerful type.

    Seriously, it shows the very ambiguous ties in the American psyche between religion, violence, individuality, and redemption at the barrel of a gun. It contrasts the peaceful face of Christianity's midwestern exterior with its past of brutality and repression, and does so in the context of fringe elements of the occult, secret combinations, and the kind of full-bloody heroic spirituality that scares and compells most North Americans with its horrific and yet foundational world view.

    It also shows up often because its such an easy type.

    A priest who kills! It's such a simplistic juxtoposition that, like Snake Gandhi, it doesn't take a lot to get some kind of juice out of it. He has faith and yet he uses a gun! Oh nos! It's also, as some have pointed out, a motif that has been done in enough in movies and novels to have some cultural capital attached to it, without having been quite as overdone as the vampire with the katana (and being less rediculous besides). So, when looking for an easy thing...

    Plus, it's easy in an RPG context. Real faith isn't something many RPGs deal well with, and power from faith has mostly only been tangible in games in so much as it lets you kick more ass. So, having a priest who kicks ass and thus lets you be religious and still "get to the point" (killing shit and taking its money) is a real sweet spot for a lot of RPG play.

    In a related note, what about the position of priests in CoC or Conspiracy of Shadows games? How often does the type fall into either "saintly one who resists the evil with their inner brightness and inhuman purity" or "scary fuck who may or may not be corrupted but has to be played in the TV series by Anthony Hopkins because he's just so damn intense and excorcist style scary?"

    Well, trying to come up with archetypes for religious characters off the top of my head, drawing mainly from Christian themes:

    The Martyr - sacrificing themselves for something bigger

    The Fallen - used to have faith, but has become corrupted or disillusioned
    The Disciple of Love - turn the other cheek, be nice to each other, follow the example of Christ

  • Iso
    September 2006
    Brand--

    This is interesting, let me see if I can articulate a little more clearly the 'world' evoked by the PwG (here I'm going a little academic, so some might want to tune out). The PwG seems to owe a lot to a frontier mentality, a world where the next town over may be too far to offer any help or aid if things go bad. The preacher comes in, too, without clear ties to any of these other towns, he is 'of the wilderness,' touched by its danger and anonymity. He clearly has ties to the Old Testament prophets in this regard, but unlike them he does not simply speak of God's impending punishment, he inflicts it himself.

    Structural variations abound--the way in which the 'postmodern' PwG emerges from the anonymity of the modern city, in which his wilderness is the collapse of community, for example. But the core remains--from outside, from nowhere in the sense that he lacks a community capable of naming and positioning him (he has no papers to be put in order), he speaks in God's name, he invokes and brings about the judgment.

    When 'secret murderin' powers' come into play, he acquires a second layer of complexity--because he is unfixed, because he has no earthly authority that certifies his powers as holy, they intensify the ambiguity already at work in the type--namely, is this man really holy or does he draw inspiration from something more diabolical? This seems to tie in with your earlier point about occultism and the PwG and summons to the foreground an anxiety about our knowledge of divinity. It's veritably Kierkegaard-ian, is this true faith or madness? Just writ in big letters.

    More powerfully, by firmly binding his spiritual and worldly power, the type pointedly raises questions about the moral ground of any power. Do the people he judges obey out of love/fear of the divine, or simply fear for this particular man here?

    Of course, the question can only be asked or answered if the community subjected to the PwG *buys* the faith upon which he depends. Otherwise, the powerful question disappears and he almost immediately becomes a sort of diabolical power. Which is all to say that the PwG can only be a PwG in his own world, otherwise he becomes a madman. The MwG is the correlate of the PwG only for someone to whom the M is a real moral authority. An MwG *can't* be a PwG for me. Or, rather, as myself--I could totally see how an MwG could work for me if that were properly prepared for me by the game setting.

    Roleplaying wise, from the 'inside' of the type, there are at least two ways to experience the PwG as a character--as a fun chance to be the moral authority, to just take for granted that you are right, come hell or high water. The second is to play it as a pointed question, to stress the ambiguities, to feel them out. Of course, you can also play it at least one more way if you are willing to push the double-consciousness: to play the character as moral authority 'from the inside' while being sure 'in your own mind' that they are quite the opposite.

    A range--the second seems the most intense and honest since it frames the dialogical component of the rpg, that the player is conversing with the character, engaging in a sort of running free indirect discourse.

    Ian,

    I'm agreeing with much of what you're saying. I'd add to it that there is actually a lot more dialogue going on in the structure than just that. We're getting into real carnival of voices stuff here, with not just player and character interacting, but multiple levels of player, culture, other players at the table, character, and the surrounding culture going on. As multiple folks have pointed out on this thread the plethora of ideas enmeshed in the archetype are not always easily unpackable -- from ideas of masculinity, priesthood, faith, authority, and violence (all of which are pretty central to the American psyche and held in a stern and endless war with secularism, individuality, responsibility, and feminism) it quickly becomes not just one voice, but many and all talking from different angles.

    The reason I brought up the Mullah in the first place is because we'd been talking about non-white PwGs, and my point there was that the Mullah is an interesting case in that field because in many ways in its home field the violent mystic/preacher has a long and very similar backgrounding in some eastern countries. The Persian folk-loric emphasis upon the true visionary who comes out of the hostile wilderness uncontaminated by decadent civilization, with a burning vision of world truth that he then imposes upon the world with either prayer or sword (usually both) reads very much like the PwG, but just with cool pointy toed shoes rather than cowboy boots.

    Which is where it becomes interesting, in that whole cavalcade of voices, that your first response to the MwG was "bad man sending his minions off to die for him." Because that is very much one perspective on the issue, one of the voices in the world that is right now speaking very loudly and with a whole lot of guns on its side. But there are reasons why a lot of folks are drawn to Osama, and one of them is because he fits an archetype from their PoV â€“ an archetype of the desert priest who fights against power no matter the odds because God has commanded him and he must obey. Combine that with a little bit of hashassin mythology, andâ€¦.

    That's about where it hits the exploding point too. See, no matter how conflicted the North American view of Christianity and all its divisions and indecisions, no matter how much we might be compelled and fascinated by the image of the white man with the gun and the authority of God, its still something that is an "our problem" in a lot of the voices going on. But change "white man with a gun and the authority of God" to "brown man with a gun and the authority of Allah" and all of a sudden you're facing a different beast.

    It almost doesn't matter how structurally the same they are, how much the frontier town, desert nomad background, hostility of the world, fighting the savage Indian/Indian, secret killing powers and division between secular life and religious vision are. In all those ways the two really are very close to each other. But change "white" to "brown" and "God" to "Allah" without changing anything else, and you're going to be playing a different game. Not because the structure is different, but because you've flipped the simple switch of "us" and "them" for a lot of those voices that cluster around the table.

    Or, for that matter, leave "white" but change it to "woman" and "Hecate" and push it to the same extreme and see where you end up. But even there, with all the friction between man and woman, pagan and Christian, those that say there is a war and those that say there isn't... I don't know that it would be the same. After all, the cradle of the best and the worst isn't at war with Hecate.
