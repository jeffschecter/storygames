\n
\n There's this pocket universe I can get to through the grimy closet my water heater sits in. And in it there are these medieval guys who make these crazy little gadgets.

They are tiny potential energy engines. Each one can lift a gram of material up a centimeter, once per second. Then it starts over. That's all they do, forever - apparently they are magic. The guys that makes them wear intimidating felt hats. It takes one specialist twelve hours to make one, so they aren't really that cheap. They say they can gang them together, but individual gadgets can't be made to lift more than a gram, more than a centimeter, faster than once per second.

Anyway, all they use them for is clocks, and to be fair they've really helped out - it's changed the economy and they've got some very precise ocean navigation with longitude and all. But they know I'm from somepace else and expect me to come up with hella useful things they can do with their gadgets. I'm no engineer! So I'm asking you, Story Games - what sort of useful cool labor-saving devices can you suggest to my new feudal friends?

    \n Okay, here's my take (having thought all afternoon about how to save your poor brother): At 98 \xc2\xb5J / sec you're talking about just too little energy to be worth much of anything. Energy-wise, as has been pointed out, you can get the same effect with springs, water-wheels, or just a peasant giving a big god-damn shove once a day. If you're giving them something that you can use with that amount of energy then you're fundamentally not finding a use for these devices but rather showing them how to use energy in a new way ("Look ... it can push a magnet back and forth in a coil of wire, and produce electricity ... almost one one-thousandth as efficiently as a guy with a hand-crank, and for only a hundred times the cost!")
    \n
    \nThe perfect timing (and to a lesser extent the perfect 1cm distance) can do useful things ... in clockwork. Which they've already figured out. No dummies, your otherworldly medievals.
    \n
    \nI concur that a kilogram lifted a centimeter is a much more workable quantity of energy ... at that point you can use one of the damn things (a workable investment, if the technique can spread from the smelly magic-geek to other magicians) to do all sorts of useful things that depend upon the thing's unrelenting stamina. One millijoule / second doesn't sound like much, but it really starts to add up.
    \n
    \nPumping systems seem an obvious first application: One of these would run a small pneumatic cylinder that could keep a village cistern pretty much constantly full. If you want to introduce some new technology, another pneumatic-mousetrap could probably run the evaporator cycle for a water purifier (or a still, or a refrigerator if your nobles want to get all decadent). Pure water lets your peasantry get acquainted with the notion of not being sick from something or other every god-damn day of their lives, which in turn tends toward higher productivity.
    \n
    \n... right before it tends toward revolutionary uprisings. Which, face facts, is the only way your brother's ever going to be released. Why would they release the hostage that lays the golden eggs?

As long as you're assembling the 40 made per day into the framwork, the overall construction will not take much longer than the time to construct the individual engines. The 4000 engines would be arrange 50 by 80 each pushing simultaneously on a gear. These don't have to be incredibly strong, because they each only have to bear 1/4000th of the force. The resulting 50 drive gears on each have to be constructed, but that's a specification that I think they can reach.
    \n
    \nIf they're the dimensions of a mousetrap, you put them each up to down right next to each other on your frame. This means that it's about 2 inches thick, 75 inches wide (assuming 50 @ 1.5 inches wide each), and 80 inches long, assuming one inch between each row of mountings. If gears that small can't be accomodated, perhaps you do the dimensions the other way, and get 120 inches wide, by 100 inches long. Or, with some creativity, this can be done in three dimensions with layers of drives (if the frame is too wide for roadways and such). Perhaps four of them 60 inches wide, and 50 inches long, 3 inches per layer, so 12 inches thick. Most optimally it would be like 30x50x24 in.
    \n
    \nLet's call that approximately another 200 KG for framework. So we put our 300 KG engine in a 100 KG carriage (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ford_Quadricycle), and put a 100 KG man on top. It should go about 3 MPH, top speed. Put in an engine 4 times as large, and you'll get up to 10MPH, likely. That'll take only a bit over a year. Ten years, if you only have 4 magicians working on it.
    \n
    \nSound far-fetched? Well, here's the thing. Yeah, it's still not very efficient in terms of production costs and times as compares to, say, Ford's first vehicle above, and it's power to weight ratio is low, sure. But it's got a titanic advantage over Ford's machine.
    \n
    \nNo fuel.
    \n
    \nMike

    \n Gears are not the way to go. Arrange a bunch of these things radially around a flywheel. Use escapements with friction pads to transfer power to the flywheel. You can put as many flywheels in parallel on a single axle as you like, for insane amounts of torque. Step-up gearing and belt drives let you run any kind of pump or mill. Most profitable use of pumps is for deep shaft mining (water removal, ventilation). Drive a bellows and you've got a blast furnace for advanced steel alloys (more efficient to skip the flywheel arrangement here and drive the bellows directly). Draw some copper wire and wind the coils for a dynamo, then use the electricity to do whatever the hell you want.
    \n
    \nMan, this is negative entropy in a box. The only limit (and I mean the only limit) on what you can do is the rate of production of these devices. Unless magic is a consumable resource, that is; sub-industrial-scale magic use might not have been enough to deplete the resource noticeably, but build enough of these gizmos and you'll find out the hard way just how much magic there is in the world.
