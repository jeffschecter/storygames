\n Looking again, with renewed interest, into the game Dirty Secrets.
\n
\nI was intrigued to read that Seth based his Demographics on data from the American Census Bureau (I'm not sure I am saying it right). He also suggests that different places will have different Demographics. Meaning, different categories.
\n
\nApparently, I'm a citizen in a country that is renowned for its statistics and census. I think we're being very thorough. You can see for yourselves : this is the form for the 2006 census. It seems we have a law, here, to make us hold a census about every 6 years or so. The next one is planned for 2011. For Marital Status, it'll (probably) have a category for "Legally married to someone of the same sex". And there is talk to change "North American Indian" for "First nation".
\n
\nNotice that the word "Race" never actually comes up. They just ask "is this person..." and then give a number of choices. They are: White, Chinese, South Asian (e.g., East Indian, Pakistani, Sri Lankan, etc.), Black, Filipino, Latin American, Southeast Asian (e.g., Vietnamese, Cambodian, Malaysian, Laotian, etc.), Arab, West Asian (e.g., Iranian, Afghan, etc.), Korean, Japanese, Other \u2014 Specify.
\n
\nNotice also that Aboriginal do not answer that question, they skip it entirely as they have their own questions specific to their status, and that they are themselves broken down into several sub-categories (Inuit, North American Indian, Metis) and they make the distinction whether they are registered and whatnot.
\n
\nA question that is not to be neglected, especially if you live in the province of Qu\xe9bec, is the one about the languages you know. English and/or French. And others. Which did you learn first, which do you use most often at home, which do you use most often at work?
\n
\nHow will this affect play? I'm not sure yet. I would probably add a category for Language, especially in Montreal.
\n
\nIn any case, I though it was interesting. I offer it to you, hoping you'll find it at least a little interesting as well. I don't know anything about United States census habits, so feel free to bring up comparisons with Canada. Other countries welcome as well.

    \n There are problems with methods, ethics and motives of StatsCan. The data is not false, per se, just problematic. Also, I was being glib. I'll talk about it.

    Beyond basic demographic facts, I personally wouldn't use any of it. Quantitative data at the national level just isn't a very fruitful measure, unless you're either Health or Revenue Canada. Moreover, with that kind of data, you can find pretty much whatever you're looking for (this problem isn't limited to quantitative data, just especially true.) There are other and more localized research centers and universities that produce good data on topics that might matter to anyone.

    \n Dirty secrets is a game that emulates Noir fiction. The action occurs in "your home town, last week".
    \n
    \nOne player plays the Investigator.
    \n
    \nThe others rotates the position of the Authority (GM). When you are not the Authority (and not the Investigator), you are an Advisor. Anyone can narrate anything. The Investigator has jurisdiction ofver the character of the investigator, and the Authority has jurisdiction over everything else. Jurisdiction comes with a right of veto. You can also appeal anything you don't like.
    \n
    \nEvery Characters is described through a set of demographics.
    \n
    \nSex: Male, Female.
    \nAge: Any
    \nRace: White, Black, Asian, Latino, Middle Eastern, American Indian, Pacific Islander.
    \nClass: Rich, Middle Class, Poor.
    \nLegal Status: (Ex-)Convict, Citizen, Private Investigator, Police Officer, Federal Agent.
    \n
    \nThese have no mechanical effect on the game. Conflict resolution is done through a game of Liar's Dice.
    \n
    \nWhat it does, is make you take a look at your own judgements and prejudices. At the end of the game, you are asked to place your characters around the board, categorized as Whit/Non-white, and as Rich/Middle Class/Poor. The game itself makes no judgement, it just makes you think.
    \n
    \nIt is, however, a feature geared towards the United States.It was suggested somewhere (perhaps not in the book, perhaps on a thread, somewhere), that players should adapt the demographic to adapt them to the realities of their country/home town.
    \n
    \nI'm interested in what you have to say about the "methods, ethics and motives of StatsCan". Please elaborate!

    \n Without getting into too much detail, StatsCan is a government bureaucracy that collects national-level statistical data. It's a government-run, thus it's policies are subject to partisan political bickering and that small mindedness that comes with it. I have a lot of contempt for demography in general, but the work that goes on at StatsCan is tedious and overly bureaucratic - it lacks praxis beyond providing numbers for various medical, financial and administrative institutions. It's ethically irresponsible because these data do a kind of violence to the lived experiences of Canadians; while they have 'scientific' data at their disposal, in my opinion, that flavour of data shouldn't be used for policy decisions. Additionally, statistics can be made to show whatever the fuck you want. "Too many immigrants? Not enough immigrants? What do you want the numbers to say?" &c. Not to mention that the follow-up surveys have some silly and problematic questions on them.

    Honestly, I'm not an expert on StatsCan or the Census. I avoid quantitative methods and government work like the plague.

    I would change the 'race' category to either nationality or ancestry; there should be five or six income brackets, and/or job + income; legal status should reflect things like immigration status and aboriginal status. Like you mentioned, languages spoken should be on there too.

    \n Jumanji I totally would worry about what your Stats Canada lists. I'd worry about which of those are a thing in your home town right now. The American Census categories are useful in this fashion because they weren't created out of anysense of completeness or political correctness. They were created because they were all "things" (as in "it isn't all that uncommon for someone to get beat up for being one). Even in America which of these are "things" where you are not only will vary, but it also says something about which "things" you recognize in your town and which you don't.

    If Seth were to do a new edition I'd encourage him to do more than just add Immigrant to the list. I'd recommend different "list-of-things-packages" that could be used. Imagine, for instance where instead of race and age you had sexual orientation and religious practice. Trying to do ALL would just get confusing. The nice thing about the limited number is that they can be largely positioned in play on a simple axis which drives home the way we demographically sort people. But I definitely think there is room to swap other categories to steer the focus elsewhere.
