\n If you want a successful RPG you need to build a culture around it, or find an existing one to target, which means establishing or adopting a set of traditions, touchstones, and values.

D&D did this accidentally. Now the d20 is a symbol for 'gamer' and the concepts of Class and Level are commonly associated with the hobby. Hack and slash. Kill things and take their stuff. Murderhobo. Minmax. All cultural conceps which emerged from D&D.

Vampire did this accidentally by being a perfect fit for an existing culture (Goth). It has waned in popularity in step with it, and at this point may be all that remains of it.

The Forge had a culture which valued game design. But because of that no single game which emerged from there became a cultural foundation, as Forge culture was literally antithetical to it.

Fate and Apocalypse World are the most popular 'Indie' systems, and each of them has recognizable traits and values which form a their own cultural group.

And I know it's a cultural (particularly value) thing because RPGs are the only game hobby where people take offense to a game designer telling them how to play the game they designed and new game concepts are treated as some kind of attack. It's about values, not systems.

This is relevant to the question of how to manage online RPG communities (which I have been investigating) and why the 'Indie' industry missed the mark. It all comes down to culture.

And that's why I'm not marketing my game to current game cultures. They're already established, and I'd spend as much effort tearing down what already exists as I would building up what I want to see.

    \nAs a publisher, I can say that the marketing challenge is totally doable. I mean, RPGs are the best games fueled by imagination; provide enough material to keep players going, or just pointers for players to find more reference material and you're done. The product is great; what you need to do is make it more accesible. You must think of the worst case scenario of course, that you're selling it to people who:

    -Doesn't read too much or doesn't want to read too much to play.
    -Isn't familiar with other systems, techniques or procedures from any roleplaying game.
    -Will definitely feel cheated if you explain that they will need more than one book from your collection or some hard to find props to play (like d20 dice in my country).
    -Definitely don't want to deal with too much math nor complicate game mechanics.
    -Doesn't have too much time to invest in this hobby (YET, perhaps once you get them interested they will make some sime)

    And at the same time, to people who do, which is easy if you put some more optional rules. So you need streamlined or minimalistic mechanics, quite well explained. You need to convey a lot of info in the least space possible -for which I believe comics are the best: a lot of the techniques are more easily explained through visual AP examples. You need to make prep really short, fun and an integral part of the session, so the DM doesn't have to spend time out of the session and people can start playing right away. You need to keep the amount of mechanics to the minimum possible and recycle them to solve anything that could come up in the game.

    For the rest, the actual culture is already there. You can perfectly sell this as a formalized way of "play pretend" and every single person in the globe will get what you mean, everything else, they will acquire it through playing.

    Here's another: "The Upgrade"\n
    The Upgrade is a "jeepform," a type of improvisational LARP developed in Scandinavia.

    The backdrop is that "The Upgrade" is a reality TV show; two players take the role of the show's producers, while the others are divided into "couples" who have been invited onto the show. Each couple is split up, and each matched with someone from another couple. The conceit is that, at the end of several episodes, every player will announce whether he or she wishes to stick with his original romantic partner, or "upgrade" to the person with whom he or she was paired during the show.

    The game uses several techniques common to jeepforms, including "inside outsides" (character monologs), presaging and post-saging (other players seizing the action and playing out either a past event for one of the characters or a potential future event, with the main player of the character then required to modify his or her conception ofthe character accordingly), etc. It is played in "episodes," beginning with the producers and participants talking about "tonight's show" and what "the audience" is about to see, then improvising events that were filmed and presented to the broadcast audience.

    As is typical with jeepforms, it places a strong premium on eager improvisational roleplay, with the "producers" shaping the experience but not with as much authority as conventional GMs, and can result in much hilarity.

      As for other connection/Lonely Fun stuff in general ( for other games and hobbies), the two big ones I note are:

      1) Places to discuss "How do I do this thing better?"
      2) Places to show off and brag about what you've done

Once you can show ( visual) stuff, it's easier to connect with people outside of the hobby. It's part of the reason I think both costumed LARPs and minis games are easier to begin explaining than RPGs/SGs.

      \n I can't help but notice that the smaller-press games which have caught on the most have a shareable DIY component to them.

      In the OSR, people share dungeon modules and random tables, and other similar things which are *required* prep for play. This creates a community where people are interested in each others' stuff.

      Apocalypse World encourages people to a) use the same names in their game as their neighbour, and b) to write up hacks and playbooks to share with each other.

      Fiasco has people everywhere writing up "playsets" and sharing them.

      IaWA oracles were pretty popular for a while, too.

      I initially saw playbooks and playsets as a limiting factor - why do I have to create new books and sets just to play this game? There are lots of games where I don't need to create anything before playing (I don't character templates for Primetime Adventures, right? Just an idea to bring to the table).

      But I suspect it may have to do something with the popularity and the community aspect of a game, when you can share house rules and prep materials. Making and sharing them gets you to daydream about play and to talk to other people about their own daydreams.
