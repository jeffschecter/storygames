\n I've bought a couple "traditional" RPGs lately and while a some have some innovative ideas and seem to be "aware" of what they are, what they're doing, and how they're doing it, others seem to be stuck in 1996. To sort of explain what I mean by aware with a comparison, a lot of story games I have (like DitV, et al) seem very aware of themselves in that the design goals seem more transparent.

So when I pick up a more traditional type RPG, one with a strong GM, Rule Zero, and all that, these days I'm looking for one that "knows what it is" if you get my drift. Some of these games make design assumptions that are unspoken and it sometimes drives me nuts - why does a traditional RPG designed and published in 2008 sometimes still look like something from the mid-90's?

I should give an example of a trad game that I think pushes the envelope a bit - Weapons of the Gods is one. I'm not sure about it's level of "self-awareness" (I have to go back and look at it) but the Lore Sheets is a pretty neat idea that I hadn't seen before, and it builds more group interaction into the game.

This is an idea that's hard for me to express - can people think of recently published traditional-type RPGs that are actually doing something new and building on and incorporating advances in design, and not just doing the same thing everyone did back in the 90's?

    \n I think all of Steve Kenson's stuff for Green Ronin (Mutants & Masterminds, Blue Rose, True20) is very self-aware, pushing things a bit, which is why I am attracted to his work more than a lot of other d20-based material. The same goes for a lot of Malhavoc's stuff too (Arcana Unearthed, Arcana Evolved, Iron Heroes, etc.).

    The loresheets in Weapons of the Gods were written by Rebecca Sean Borgstrom, who wrote Nobilis, which explains why they're so progressive. Her work on Exalted too is really visionary, especially Exalted: The Fair Folk, which is just... out of this world. She switches the normal combat attributes for these underused emotional stats called Virtues, creating Shaping Combat, which is basically a battle of wits where each side tries to describe a story in which their opponent suffers unfortunate setbacks. And that's not the least of it. Probably one of the weirdest things White Wolf has ever published.

    Speaking of White Wolf, some people saw the road-to-humanization in Promethean: The Created as a nod to progressive stuff like Keys in The Shadow of Yesterday, but I'm not sure, since 1) I'm not that familiar with the game and 2) when I was flipping through it, it didn't seem all that progressive.

    Some mainstream games have seemed intent on stripping themselves of any progressive leanings in subsequent editions. I think Ars Magica is the most common suspect here. I was one of the first games to promote troupe-style play, but that continues to be de-emphasized.

    \n I'll give you an example:

    Mage: The Awakening went through many, many design cycles, leading to the design of what, were you inclined to sift through everything, three different RPGs, one of which became a shiny blue book. Central to all of these was the basic cast-a-spell power trait, which was called Gnosis, then Arete again for a while, and then Gnosis once more. That trait, along with the magic system in general, was changed several times to support not just particular crunch effects like how you would set some dude on fire, but the symbolism Gnosis would have in play. For a while, Gnosis was straight-up good, to the point where it was briefly Arete again and was a measurement of human excellence, so mages were "overpeople" in fact, as well as self-opinion. At one point, Gnosis side effects were more severe, symbolizing the opposition of a hostile cosmology (and at one point, the cosmology was *very* hostile, to nearly Kult proportions). Playtesting and the desire to give players more flexibility in defining the meaning of Mage-hood led to a subtler set of effects. At one point I argued for a complex system balancing Gnosis and Wisdom, but (like many of the things I proposed), it was too complex to use.

    So in this case, you have several iterations of design devoted to seeing how a trait supported story goals and the kind of message it gave by operating a certain way -- but *also* (and this is where commercial games differ from many indie stream designed) ways for GMs and players to conceptualize alternative meanings. This ends of being incredibly rewarding for other parts of the game, too, because we can talk about in-world groups' reactions to the way game systems work. So we have the Guardians of the Veil's quasi-religion telling them that magic means a certain thing, and the Silver Ladder saying another.

    However, this kind of thing doesn't have a high profile on reading, because it's not meant to operate that way. It *plays* certain way, but nobody really grandstands about how it all works to impress you with the Big Ideas. We do our best to make sure that the virtues of it come out in play. Certainly, it's in the nature of hype-driven online discussion to talk about whatever is immediately chucked in your face about something. If the game did that with Gnosis, there would probably be pressure to make Gnosis a but more radical in its effects, but that doesn't do it for playing. That puts a lot of the setting narrative or high-concept parts a bit more front and centre, but that's not necessarily where the design and development efforts are going.

      Posted By: chearnsYou know what I really appreciate, when I look back on games from the nineties that don't seem to know they were made in the nineties, instead designing for the naughties.

      1993 -Underground. Containing specific rules to empower the players to come up with their own goals (the example they give is "eliminate the AIDS problem in Los Angeles county"), and to figure out how to tackle the issue by altering the current state of society. The players suggest storylines that will allow them to change the social landscape (examples given: break into the Super Bowl transmission in order to raise education levels, or help an honest investor take over a crooked corporation in order to raise wealth levels). It has stats for where society is now along seven parameters, and where they have to end up for the desired change to happen.

      1994 Mind's Eye TheatreThe Masquerade(2nd Edition). Conflict resolution. Right there on page 83, player A wants to hit player B, player B wants to escape. If player A wins during the resolution mechanics, their goal happens, if player B wins, their goal happens (and the loser loses whatever resource they bid). I know I know it's a LARP, but the rules in this game work just as well for TT. I'd also point at the Influence rules, that give players a mechanical way to be all intrigue-y (something that for me was really lacking in the actual VtM rules).

      1996Dragonlance Fifth Age. The players are the only ones who engage the resolution mechanics. You want to hit someone? Roll to hit. You want to not be hit? Roll to avoid being hit. The GM never engages the resolution mechanics. Also, no real advancement (your attributes and skills (such as they are) do not improve over time, although you do get more competent). Finally, during random character creation (that I swear is totally fun, seriously, I could sit around making Fifth Age characters all day), you are mechanically encouraged to be a good guy.

I'm most interested in seeing if it attempts to do something differently, something that hasn't been done before, or something that has been done before and does it better (like, really much better). Several new (released within the last 6 months) products I have bought recently are indeed "new" but as far as the mechanics go, don't really do anything different than similar games produced five or even ten years ago. Some seemed to attempt an advance, but failed.       Tristan (above) asks what's stopping anyone from using a different system, and the answer is nothing at all. However, I like to see mechanics that are connected, somehow, to whatever it is the setting is trying to evoke; mechanics which also evoke the setting, theme, genre, what have you; or mechanics which advance play at the table toward achieving the experience the game wants to provide. So sometimes, when a game's setting is cool but the system is pants (especially if it lacks that system/setting meld-mechanic that I'm looking for) I wonder why they bothered with a system at all. Pure setting books don't sound like such a bad idea to me really, in this case (for example, I really grooved on the Iron Kingdoms GM's Guide, I think it was, as it had mostly setting info and few rules)

      That means that I often try to run games with cool settings, but lousy systems, as written, because I'm hoping that whatever the designer felt the system was doing for the setting may still come through. My friends ask me why I don't change the system if I don't like it, and my answer is that I don't want to (I feel it should be working right from the git-go). I'm often disappointed that I need to hack, patch, or otherwise modify some game systems to get them to do this. As a counter-example, a game I didn't feel a need to do this with at all was Feng Shui. It promised a Hong Kong action movie feeling, and the system delivered.

      So while the mechanics may be secondary for some, for me at any rate, I want to see them supporting and encouraging the type of experience the game's setting, themes, and tropes propose to provide. I strongly feel that any new game that isn't a "universal" system (GURPS, Savage Worlds, etc) needs to have a good system/setting meld if it wants to capture my interest. If that meld isn't there, and the system is flat, uninspired, broken, or otherwise not at the same level as the setting, I wonder if the game wouldn't have been better off without it.

      That's how I feel.

      \nThe mechanic that I think of needs to be explained in the context of the setting, and it makes it difficult to sum up shortly, but in the end it's quite simple.
      \n
      \nIn R\xeave de Dargon, PCs are reincarnated. Not from a theological point of view, with gods or karma or whatnot, but because Dragons dream the worlds PCs explore. And dragons dream of the same characters again and again. The game implies that dragons are a metaphor of the players, GM included. Though to the denizens of the dreams they are more like the mythical creatures one would expect. So in a way they are like gods, except they don't have much control over their dreams, and when nightmares happens out of this lack of control, it may cause a mass wake up of the dragons. This is only a background story, the game itself doesn't deal with those cataclysms, instead they provide cultural specificity of the setting like : "why do most NPCs hate magic users ? " "because they're considered to be the reason why dragons woke up in previous eras"
      \n
      \nSo PCs are reincarnations. As a result, one part of the character sheets is devoted to what the game calls "archetype" which is a record of the highest level of competence the character reached in previous incarnations for each skills. (I know I'm getting tedious there but wait it all gets clear at some point)
      \n
      \nThe game has two types of scenarii (= mini campaigns). Typical ones which could be compared to D&D; (looking for a treasure, save an area of nasty nightmare creatures etc...), and archetype dreams which my group really was delighted with. An archetype dream is a premise sequence where every PCs would have the same, or almost the same dream. Say we dream of a sumptuous palace and are present for a crowning. Usually the dream had sth a little weird about it, for instance instead of a court crowning a prince, they would crown a stray cat. Then the GM would explain that this dream the PCs had is very obsessive, is actually a memory from a previous incarnation and if they manage to re-enact the scene they dreamed in all its tiny details they would earn experience points = they may improve skills and get closer to their archetypes.
      \n
      \nSo the rest of the scenario would be spent exploring how to make a cat crowned in the very palace the PCs dream of. On one hand it's the horror of "my god, we have to try and guess what the GM has planned for us to do ?" On the other hand many comic and heroic situations can be forced this way, like say the players exhuming an old law book of the kingdom where it is stipulated that a cat may become king if a grotesque set of conditions were completed...
      \n
