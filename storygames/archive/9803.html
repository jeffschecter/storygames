    \n BGG is far more systematic and complete in its indexing and rating of board games than RPG.net is of RPGs. It also has a fantastic user community. They're definitely more focused on quantitative rather than qualitative analysis of games, though - a bit more Web 2.0ish to misuse some jargon. More community feedback mechanism, less articles and reviews. Not sure how their approach maps onto RPGs, since RPG quality is considerably more a matter of taste than board games are (I think, anyway. Let the flame wars commence.)

    In any case, it'll be really interesting to see how it pans out and works in practice once those pesky "users" are added and start creating content.

    If nothing else, BGG's buy/sell/trade functions are pretty awesome, so if all else fails, there's at least that.

    \n On the boardgame side, BGG has a fantastic database, collecting basic information, reviews, session reports, weblinks, user-generated files (player aids, for example), and the like, as well as community functionality (forums -- both general and game specific, "Geeklists", etc.). There's been significant extension of the site to handle all the variety that RPGs encompass (four editions of AD&D, with large numbers of RPG items per edition (AD&D 2nd edition, for example, has 158 items linked)), systems, genres, etc.), and much of the work during this "Open Beta" involves front-loading the populating of the database in a controlled (and therefore not really "open", but I quibble) manner. Once it is "in production", I'd definitely encourage everyone to take a look, and see if any further additions/corrections are necessary. Much like BGG, the data is live, and corrections are accepted (and encouraged!) on an ongoing basis.

    It also has an administrator with a rather, um, coarse sense of humor, as Eero has pointed out. Nice enough guy, otherwise -- honest.

    My link to this is that I'm participating in the Open Beta, and have done my part to catalog my RPG collection (small though it is, I still managed to add a few things).

    For those of you who are really concerned, I'd be willing to look over entries for your stuff to make sure there is nothing obviously wrong. Just whisper me, and I'll take a look. Or, apply to join the Open Beta process, and get your hands in it yourselves.

    \nInitial population is by beta people, yes. When that's over, it's still a living database, open for new submissions and corrections. When listing descriptions, we are being asked to limit our descriptions to publisher blurbs, or text from the material itself (back cover text, for example), and to explicitly cite the text as such. This is to guard against plagiarism -- taking descriptions from other RPG-oriented sites, for example. I've seen some descriptions "in the poster's own words", but it's not common. But again, there is (or will be) opportunity to correct/augment such descriptions. And plenty of game-specific forum space to provide as much commentary as you'd like.

    It's true that much of the work going on there is from the bigger publishers: D&D, WoD, CoC, etc. (OK, I've just reached acronym overload...must rest a bit.) But, well, there is more material there, so it does make sense. Like I said, I made sure my collection of indies are there (all 9 of them, but I did what I could), and yes, I used back cover text or publisher blurbs because I've only played one of those 9 so far (I'm still new to this stuff). And I've entered 7 session reports (that site's term for Actual Play) chronicling a 5 episode (+ pitch, + pilot) PTA series I played over the past 7 months or so. And I've made sure that there were front cover scans of the texts so there'd be representative images for those 9 games. Well, actually, eight of them, as my copy of The Secret Lives of Gingerbread Men is on loan. But that's what I could do. And I did it.

    I agree with you on one point: I'd like to see more indie game people involved, too. Which is why I'm here talking about it. If you can't, or won't, that's cool. But maybe others will still find some enthusiasm for the process, and help out?

    \nI just wanted to jump in and say that I've been following the thread here (and in about 30 other sites which seem to be discussing RPGG!). Good to see level headed people ;)
    \n
    \nI've been heavily involved with both the RPG Geek open beta and the closed beta (and the test period before that). I'll say that this has been a labor of love - well over 1000 hours of volunteer effort has gone into this - no compensation and no other benefit other than helping to create what we think will be a great asset to the RPG community. The layout and hierarchical structure of the RPG Geek differs form the BGG in many ways - the team has learned a lot over 10 years and a number of database experts as well as RPG notables and knowledgable people (game designers, sourcebook writers, editors, librarians, etc) have been consulted. The early period of this (Before the closed beta) was weeks and weeks of build up and tear down as ideas were tried. We believe we have a structure that will, in fact, hold up to the tens of thousands of RPG items out there. In the past 3 weeks we have added nearly 1000 beta users who have dilligently been adding games from their collections (we ask that the people have the book/legal PDF to enter data though we know some lesser known gaps are being filled in without that until we get someone with the knowledge and books to flesh it out completely). Last I counted, there were 5500 items (books, miniature sets, map collections, magazine entries, etc) grouped under 1500 RPGs.
    \n
    \nThe indie RPG scene has been represented - in no small part to the efforts of some users who posted above. I can say that, personally, I hadn't heard of most of these games though you can bet I now have The Shadow of Yesterday on order (used) and will be tracking down a copy of Spirit of the Century simply based on the listings I saw recently at RPG Geek. My wife will be sending you guys a letter complaining about the draining funds ;)
    \n
    \nI would personally love for more of the indie scene and story games subculture to be represented on the RPG Geek. While the vast majority of entries right now is weighted towards the heavy hitters, you can be sure we are looking to really cover the full gamut of RPG games and feel that our hierarchy won't give unnecessary weight to the fat cats (i.e. D&D, CoC, etc).
    \n
    \nPlease consider joining up to help with the effort and get more of these great games represented!

      \n Bret & Graham seem to be saying the same thing, and if it helps at all, I agree with their understanding of the structure.

      There are additional levels: items can have versions. One example might be the Runequest 3 Deluxe Boxed set vs. the later RQ 3 Deluxe paperback book.

      Also, items can optionally be grouped into Series and Settings. Example of a series: the Giants modules for AD&D. Example of a setting: Forgotten Realms.

      Also, RPGs can be connected to Systems.

      There is a guide to data entry that should definitely be read before entering data.

      The most confusing element of the nomenclature, to me, is that an "RPG" is what I would basically call an "edition", while a "family" is what I would call an "RPG". For example, Dungeons and Dragons is a Family. Each major edition of D&D/AD&D is an RPG within that Family. D&D 3.0 and 3.5 are also independently tied to the D20 system.

      \n here is an excerpt from the data entry guide:

      --
      The [family] and [rpg] entries are not tangible items. An [item] is a tangible item. [item] entries are something you can buy, touch, smell, burn or sell. In terms of the popular Dungeons and Dragons 4th edition, an [item] might be the Player's Handbook or the Monster Manual or some adventure module or boxed set or even a set of miniatures. The [rpg] is the basic way you group these items together as a single entity - these are products that are designed for use together. An [rpg] entry is not tangible, but it is crucial. So, the [rpg] that owns those aforementioned items might be "Dungeons and Dragons (4th Edition)". Nothing about an [rpg] entry implies a rulebook - a rulebook would be an [item]. We add the edition in (parens) so that you know what edition of the rpg you are playing as the Player's Handbooks and Monster Manuals have come out for numerous flavors of D&D. A [family] is an even higher level but looser grouping. It is optional but works well when you have a number of [rpg] entries that are related (such as the various editions of Dungeons and Dragons released over the years).
      --
