    \n Immersion has always been a tricky thing for me and my group. It happens in fleeting moments, usually when we're moving fast and furious through a scene and don't have too much time to think. When something happens, you just blurt out what your character would do or say. Then someone cracks a joke or references something outside the game and the immersion flits away.

It means that you've transcended the rules of the system and actually managed to step outside of yourself for a moment. It can never last, but those times are some of your truly special role-playing moments.

    \n Wel, I hate the common definition that says immersion means when a player is playing the character and not thinking meta at all. So maybe this question isn't for me.

    For me, immersion is about being in the fictional space and connecting to the fiction on a personal level. It's a step beyond suspending disbelief, which is forgetting about some construct to enjoy the fiction.

    I hate the common definition because I get immersed regardless of whether I also am thinking about things on a meta level. When I'm worried what might happen to a family connection I have to a character, even though I might be the one making them suffer because there's a mechanical advantage to it. Immersion is what RPGs are ALL about. If I wasn't immersed, it would feel like a board game. But it doesn't. I'll hesitate on getting that mechanical advantage (or whatever) because I'm immersed in the fiction.     \n The functional definition I use is "Immersion in x means you can manipulate x subconsciously, reflexively, automatically without resorting to reflection or conscious thought." Some have found this definition useful, but others have not. So "Immersion in setting" means you can introduce setting elements without hesitation or contemplation; "Immersion in system" means you can roll the dice and manipulate the mechanics reflexively; "Immersion in character" means you can speak for and act out the behavior of the character completely automatically - "think as the character."

    Strong immersion in character can have the side effect of a partial sense of loss of self and identity. This experience I'd describe as "transcendental" and feels similar to meditation.

    As others have said, there are other definitions out there and its not clear everything falls under the umbrella and whether there is a tight relationship between them. For example, some describe "zoning out" while reading a book, or watching a movie as a kind of immersion. I'm not sure if these experiences are really covered by my definition, and feel like very different subjective experiences to me.

    \n Over the years of reading RPG forums, I have seen "immersion" used for several different things. I think they break down into: actor-stance-only play, theatrical-play-only, noticability of system consultation, consistency of the shared-imagined-space, just plain old engagement, and any combination of these.
    \n
    \nIn actor-stance-only play, players play their character's solely based on the personality of the character.
    \n
    \nIn theatrical-play-only, players restrict their real-world interactions to in-character speech and gestures as much as possible.
    \n
    \nWith unintrusive system consultation, the players either know the relevent rules well enough to use them smoothly and unintrusively or they minimize or eschew existing rules to avoid consulting them.
    \n
    \nBy consistency of the shared-imagined-space, I mean how much the fantasy spun by the group lives up to a particular player's expectations (whether that's "historical accuracy" or "true to Tolkein" or even internal consistency).
    \n
    \nEngagement is the common human experience of enjoyable concentration that humans get when engaging in an interesting task. Any game or activity that pushes the right buttons for an individual can have this and this is what we're all looking for.
    \n
    \nI think the primary misunderstanding is mistaking one of the first four for the last one.

    \n Jason,

    I think I've already said this to you, but -

    Immersion for me is when I am playing a character and the character's emotional state and my emotional state align. Immersion is hard thing for people to define precisely because it involves internal emotional states, things that we, as humans, aren't particularly equipped to describe.

    ---

    To be a giant nerd for a minute, think of your brain as a computer. It runs an operating system that controls your shit, and your personality is a program it always runs. Trying to identify with the emotional state of another person - empathizing - is something you do all day, and you do it by setting up a similar program in your mind. It's a simpler and cruder version of your own personality program, but it resembles it. You run this off to the side and look at it and go, oh, ok, I know how to react now.

    Playing a role is even harder, because you don't have as clear a picture of what that person is - they're imaginary. Still, same thing - run the program and look. Immersion is that point when you've built that program up to a good level of detail and suddenly, your main focus is that program instead of the you program.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarYeah, I get that feeling too, Levi.

    So what are some of the most common definitions? I'm really a babe in the woods here.
    \nI'll take a shot, but I'm gonna actually steal wildly from that arcticle by Matthijs to do it.

    The following are not intended to be distinct; they all are possible components of how someone might define Immersion. They're totally opinion, to the point of being a shot in the dark.

    Immersion (Catharsis)
    Players with this goal wish to emotionally engage with the same "hard stuff" that the character does; they wish to feel the hardships of the character. The enjoyment here actually comes after-the-fact; after engaging with such stuff, the player feels a sense of release sometimes described as "cleansing". It can be compared to watching and engaging with other tragic media.

    Immersion (Play)
    Players with this goal are interested in simply being highly engaged with the whole experience of play. Losing track of time, generally "being in the groove" or "being in flow" with the gameplay are rough ways of putting it. Engagement is enjoyable partly because while in it, the player feels proficient with the methods of playing, is getting and giving pleasing amounts of attention, activity, and whatever else it is that they also want from the game.

    Immersion (Kairosis)
    Players looking for kairosis want to identify with their characters emotionally and personally at the moments of intense change or revelation. The payoff here is in the actual stirring of emotions in the player because of the intensity of the emotional content attached to the character. This differs from catharsis in that the result may or may not have specifically pleasing after-play; the reward is the emotional rush on the spot.

    Immersion (Limited Kairosis)
    Some players want the emotional rush of Kairosis, but have a specific range of preferred emotions - often, specifically positive emotions. A player that engages with their character most intensely when that character is about to obtain some form of victory, or when the character is speaking boldly, might be seeking a limited form of Kairosis.

    Immersion (Kenosis)
    Kenosis-seeking players wish to simply "let go", abandoning specific goals and concerns as a player, and let the game carry them, acting naturally within the game. While this appears and is externally identical to engagement, the desired internal, personal payoff is the difference; the player isn't looking to 'be fully on', but to 'simply let go'. It's possible that the two are actually different degrees of the same thing, but I honestly don't have a damn clue about that.

    Immerision (Fiction)
    Players that want situational immersion are deeply engaged with the fiction of the game. They may or may not be deeply engaged with their character, and are not necessarily engaged by the various activities and mechanics of play; they are "in the story" in the way that a moviegoer might be swept away by the fiction of a film.

    Posted By: cydmabThe functional definition I use is "Immersion in x means you can manipulate x subconsciously, reflexively, automatically without resorting to reflection or conscious thought." Some have found this definition useful, but others have not. So "Immersion in setting" means you can introduce setting elements without hesitation or contemplation; "Immersion in system" means you can roll the dice and manipulate the mechanics reflexively; "Immersion in character" means you can speak for and act out the behavior of the character completely automatically - "think as the character."
    \nI think this is an excessively burdensome definition that often leads to strawman, "That's impossible!" arguments. After all, people don't apply this burden to acting by saying that the Method must be reflexive. Quite the opposite; attempts to jump to reflexive emotional states *as* the character are often discouraged.

    I've read Johnstone's discussion of masks/the mask state and in my opinion, it's pretty much the opposite of immersion, because it strikes me as a New Agey way to enable bad acting. (Read through Stanislavsky, the type of bad acting is pretty apparent -- it's the kind of pre-Method "just be the character" indulgence that ends up being artificial and selfish from a performance perspective.) This doesn't apply to the cultural rituals that mask material discusses, but since few of the people doing masked Improv have been shaped in the long term by a culture with those rituals, that doesn't matter much. For the most part archetypal techniques (which masking is a vague subset of) are, IMO, really about narrative, not character. For instance when you look at commedia del arte you quickly realize these characters are the set of guys that cause a basic pattern of things to happen. As characters, they're actually quite feeble, though they do lend themselves to personal projection about the Real Meaning of Columbine, etc. But to my mind, that isn't immersion -- it's just disinhibition.

    The last time I gave this a lot of thought I produced two posts on the subject:

    http://shootingdice.blogspot.com/2006/03/immersion-and-method.html

    Discussion about the technical issues around reflexive response can be found here:

    http://shootingdice.blogspot.com/2006/03/immersion-fu.html

    The discussion of immersion has advanced quite a bit since then (when there was a more strident attempt to discredit the whole style), but I think the essentials still hold true. Strident immersionists and non-immersionists need to get past strawman-perfect immersion and examine it in terms of technique.

    These days, I think the two pillars of immersive play -- the "next level" -- have to do with examining the role of power and inequality at the table, not for the sake of rejecting it, but using it wisely, because the great challenges of the form are not so much in feeling/being, but exploring the tension between that and the play world, generating narrative (used here, as always, in its real sense, not as "address-premise") from that key interaction -- and accepting the risks that come with it.

The same happens with imaginative components of the fiction: I see the scenes more clearly and as having more powerful imagery. I wouldn't necessarily say I become the character in any way, but that I can tap into the character almost at will. Play can be enjoyable without it, but the truly great characters, whom I can still remember (and feel) to this day, have always had great moments of immersion. It is very much the elixir I bring back from the visionquest and has this aspect of alchemy to it.

    Denotation: Intense absorbtion into a topic with neglect to other duties or potential distractions.

    \n I've only seen one definition of immersion that encompassed what I've seen described that way; it was Mendel's. To paraquote him, immersion is narrowing of views. And a view (to him) is basically all the parts of a game you can see that happens during a game session, including things like talking, gesturing, the mechanics, characters, the story and so forth. (Maybe I'm expanding that definition a bit, but it suits this purpose.) So wherever you focus (i.e. narrow your view) on one thing, you become immersed in that thing. It has some non-intuitive consequences, like suggesting that you can be immersed in mechanics. This starts to make sense when you consider the way some people play D&D: anything that isn't dice rolling is considered silly and unnecessary. And other groups only care about cool things happening - a good story - but don't particularly care about the characters. They want to immerse in the story. And other groups want to stay deep in character. And still other groups want to socialize. Under this view of immersion, doing anything to the exclusion of everything else qualifies.

    The primary objection to this view that I've seen is that it includes lots of things that aren't character immersion. And I see the problem (losing specificity), because then you can't say "immersion" and mean "being my character" (or something similar.) I propose a simple solution: add some additional qualifiers. "Character immersion," for example, or even "emotional character immersion." The way I'm looking at it, immersion is a big enough tent for us all to coexist under, so long as we put up some signs to help us find our way around the inside.

      \nMy thought on this is when my imagination of the game space is coming automatically and reflexively, if someone else says something that nullifies what I was thinking about in the game space, it causes a "hiccup" where I typically have to consciously rationalize the difference between what I instinctually thought should happen and what the other person, with authority, says will happen. Per my definition/axiom of immersion, by definition having to work through consciously a paradox is a break (even if temporary) in immersion. One classic case of this is some types of personality mechanics where I intuitively decide my character will do x, but then someone else invokes a personality mechanic requiring the character to do y.

      However, another example for me would be setting immersion. If someone says "you enter a bar," I immediately begin fleshing out details of the bar in my own mind. What it smells like, who is there, what kind of furniture is present, etc. If I imagine there is a fireplace there, and then later learn from someone else with authority that there is no fireplace present, it'll cause a hiccup.

      There are a couple ways to minimize the chance of these hiccups. One is to operate with as thin a conception of setting (or character) as possible. With a thin setting, it becomes feasible to communicate every detail into the shared imagined space, and there is less chance of conflict over details if there are no details at all. But this is less satisfying if the whole point of the exercise is to explore the setting or character.

      Another solution is "don't ask, don't tell" - we each flesh out the details of the setting (character) in our own way, and try not to waste too much time communicating those facts to others. In other words, allow the individual imagined spaces to differ from the shared imagined space. If we do have to communicate something, try to make the details additive and not prohibitive (add a fireplace, but don't try to assert the non-existence of one).

      \n Well, the reason I use the term is that, from reading about and thinking on immersions compatability with story games, its not so much that immersion is impossible with story games, is that some story games (and traditional GMing) require regular and common breaks from total-stream-of-consciousness play. So even after extensive practice, there will be "hiccups" - practice can reduce the hiccups to literally one-second or less of conscious thought, but if you have to break immersion once a minute for a second each it puts an upper bound on how deep immersion can go. It's still entirely fair and reasonable to say "I immersed while playing story game xx" but even with tremendous practice there's only so deep a state you can go when doing that.

      In other words, it helps (at least for some people) to try to keep the time between hiccups as long as possible. In a LARP you might aim for hours or even days to achieve very deep levels of immersion. In a table top setting, half an hour between hiccups is still preferable to a few minutes.

      One technique that some have suggested elsewhere is to make full use of scene-breaks. During the scene break, do all your director-stance and/or typically-incompatible-with-immersion stuff: edit characters, change characters, change scene, discuss administrative concerns, discuss the last scene's themes, discuss what the next theme should be about, talk about the local sports team, etc. Maybe resolve the conflict for the next scene with a fortune-at-the-beginning mechanic (This is something I'd like to experiment with more). Make heavy use of the scene breaks, so you don't have to do that stuff during the scenes.

      I've been partly implementing this technique in Polaris, in the sense of making sure to make full use of between-scene time to discuss themes, setting, direction of the story, etc. out of the way. Then practice with making scenes longer and longer.

      This is all related to the observation that in many immersion-focussed traditional games, the players will do extensive setting work and talk to the GM about themes and stories and such BETWEEN SESSIONS, and then religiously avoid anything vaguely related to director stance during play. So maybe a compromise system with long scenes and long scene breaks could work as well.

      \n To expand on my model of immersion slightly:

      1. "Immersion in x" (x includes character, setting, system, etc.) means being able to reflexively, unconsciously, automatically generate elements of or work with x.

      a. Immersion in character means reflexively think and do what the character would think and do
      b. Immersion in setting means reflexively generate setting elements and imagine the setting
      c. Immersion in system means automatically manipulate system elements.

      2. What I will call "total immersion" is when you have immersed in x on everything you are doing, and not consciously reflecting on thinking about anything, (Perhaps this is a form of flow?)

      3. What I will call "deep character immersion" is total immersion, plus the ability to "think in character." You can consciously reflect on things, but only from the character's perspective.

      (If you think 2 and 3 are "impossible," then think of the definitions as platonic ideals that one can approximate, even if one can not achieve)

      So, in terms of whether immersion and engagement are compatible, it depends. Immersion in x is certainly possible. However, total immersion might be impossible/hard depending on what you mean by engagement. By definition, when I'm total immersed or deep character immersed, I'm not consciously reflecting on what the other players are doing. I might very well be IGNORING some things other players are doing. If engagement means paying attention to everything at the table, then I rarely engage while total immersed.

      \nIt seems a lot of the discussion has focused on the vocabulary of role playing and how FIE interacts with those structures. It might be helpful to understand these concepts from the greater human perspective and then bring it back to gaming. Here is my interpretation of what these things are and how they are different and interrelated.
      \n
      \nEngagement: Being intellectually invested in what you are doing. This is like the difference between a good lecture and a bad one. If you are engaged, you are paying attention and actively processing what is going. If asked to participate you could give evaluative opinions on what is happening.
      \n
      \nImmersion: Being emotionally invested in what you are doing. This is like being at a sporting event where you deeply care about the outcome. Your emotional state is an extension of the activity and can be directed back into the activity.
      \n
      \nFlow: Minimizing the awareness of distractions and routine tasks in the pursuit of an engaging activity. This is like having a great conversation on a long, easy drive where at the end you can remember your conversation, but realize that the driving part was done pretty much on automatic. You can be in Flow all by itself, but it is just zoning out, and that really isn't much fun for most people.
      \n
      \nMy thinking is that Flow compliments both Engagement and Immersion, but Engagement and Immersion have a more complicated relationship. Engagement and Immersion both require a concentration which can be disturbed, Flow obviously enhances this by reducing the impact of distractions. Engagement and Immersion are both types of mental investment, and while I don't think that they are incompatible, I do think that they are competitive with each other in that each is a distraction to the other. The more you Immerse yourself emotionally, the harder it is to Engage intellectually and vice versa.
      \n
      \nI think that this model is able to accommodate many, if not all, of the concerns raised so far in the thread and can be useful for understanding game design and actual play experiences.

      \nI'm coming at this thread as someone who finds character immersion relatively easy to maintain once it's established, despite hiccups, but who has a hard time getting there in the first place. So I'm interested in refining and broadening my techniques for becoming immersed.

      From this angle, I think it's useful to keep I = (having an intuitive / unconscious grasp of character, setting, story, or rules, ala cydmab) separate from F = (flow, being seamlessly absorbed in the world, story, character, or whatnot), rather than making I a subset of F. Yes, it's probably an artificial distinction. What I normally call immersion -- the sense of being in the character and feeling or thinking some of the same things as your character does as events unfold -- is both F and I, and having an intuitive grasp of your character (I) basically means that your character's actions are flowing seamlessly (F).

      But here's what makes distinguishing F and I interesting to me regardless: if I'm trying to get to FI, I can approach it initially either through F or through I. On one side are techniques for building up a stronger intuitive sense of your character / setting / whatever you're trying to immerse in: e.g. using imagery, symbols, situations, and props that you already have a visceral connection to, picturing things carefully to enhance their emotional bang, etc. Also, things that help immersion from this perspective don't need to happen at the gaming table -- I suspect that's what's going on with writing things in your character's voice outside of game, talking to your character in a trance outside game, etc. In these techniques, you get an intuitive grasp of what's going on first, and once you engage that in a game setting, you get flow.

      On the other side are techniques where you cultivate flow and hope to develop an intuitive grasp of character / setting /story / whatever from that. Csikszentmihalyi has a list of conditions that help enable flow, but IIRC, the biggest factors in Finding Flow are being in a situation that's both highly challenging but within your abilities, and having continuous immediate feedback from the situation. So if I'm having quick-paced conversations in character about something significant, that fits both conditions well, and when I'm having those conversations, sooner or later I'll stop thinking about what my character should say, say something I didn't expect, and feel like I've jumped into either the character's or the story's head.

      Some techniques work from both the perspective of cultivating flow and cultivating intuitive connection to character. For example, if I can use the mechanics to make my character do something surprising to me, that's great for initiating immersion for me, though potentially bad for maintaining it. The reason it works is that it gets me out of the rut of analytically deciding what my character would do based on x belief or y background. Once the character starts acting independently of me, I often find myself grabbing hold of the character intuitively and from there jumping into her. But the other thing that goes on when a character acts unexpectedly is that it makes the character more challenging and interesting as a puzzle to interact with, and that facilitates flow.

      \n One reason I split my immersion in x from total immersion is to highlight two techniques in my mind. I suppose it'd help the discussion though if I said what those techniques were though

      The first is "stock characters." Take a very simple character, and master it so that it's easy to reflexively, intuitively determine what the character would do. I Immerse in Character with a potentially complex character. This generates a stream of reflexive impulses about what the character should do. This method never gets me to Deep Character Immersion, but allows me to take directly into account what the audience expects or what the story requires the character to do.

      \n Here's what immersion means to me: it's when I experience something without consciously paying attention to the interface between the part of me that experiences things and the thing I'm experiencing.

      At first, I typed "...without being consciously aware of...", but that didn't seem right. If I'm immersed in a book, at no time do I ever "forget" that I'm reading words and turning pages. If I'm immersed in a movie, I'm still aware that I'm sitting in a movie theater. If I'm immersed in playing a character, I still know that I'm "just" sitting at a table with my friends throwing dice and talking. It's just that I don't have to pay attention to those things, just like in the "real world" I don't have to pay attention to making my body walk around and breathe.

      What breaks immersion for me is when the interface demands too much attention. Interestingly, that means that sometimes things can be too good for me to get immersed in. Especially with books, I've read things that were so well written that I kept stopping and noticing how good a particular bit of prose was or how well an exchange of dialog gave insight into the characters' personalities. I couldn't get fully immersed in the fictional experience because the damned interface kept impressing me so much. Doesn't mean I didn't like the book, just that I enjoyed it in a different way than the ones I've been able to become immersed in. Really badly written books interfere with my immersion too, but I seem to be much better at ignoring bad prose than good.

      As far as games go, I find familiarity with the system is a big help for me. Rolling dice and adding numbers and pushing tokens around or whatever don't interfere with my immersion at all, as long as I don't have to stop and think about which dice to roll, what numbers to add, and where those tokens are supposed to go.

      Once I know the system well enough, another thing that's a huge help to me is a consistent level of interaction with the game mechanics. It doesn't matter at all how simple or complex the mechanics are; if I'm constantly using dice and fiddly bits throughout the session I quickly stop noticing them. It's games where you don't use any mechanics for a long time and then abruptly have to stop and switch into dice-rolling mode that really kick me out of immersion.
