    \n Some thoughts:

    * Explain thing in popular culture. I like to say "we'll take turns making wacky scenes for our characters, kinda half-like we're writing it, and half-like we're acting it out."

    * Never mention the term "roleplaying game," or other jargon that the book doesn't use.

    * Don't refer to other games. It's at best useless, and worst confusing.

    * Don't shut down their ideas. You'll honestly have more baggage than they will, and you don't want to push that baggage onto them.

    * Encourage them if they suggest they're playing it "wrong."

    - Ryan

    \n One thing I have picked up for playing Fiasco specifically with non new gamers: The set-up process can go real real slow with players who aren't used to making selections like that - thus dampening enthusiasm - and people will tend to pick random stuff anyway if they're not invested in the fiction yet - which brand-new gamers may not be.

    Instead, you can just determine all set-up stuff randomly. It's way faster, and generates it's own laffs when people see things appearing on their cards.

    (Also, Fiasco will not suffer a whit from re-rolling if someone really hates what lands on their card, but I find that people new to RPGs fret less about playing characters or the other gender, or characters with romantic entanglements. )

    \n I've played with non-gamers a ton and I love it. They'll be great; don't overthink it. Do what you'd do when introducing any game to anyone - facilitate strongly, lead by example, trust them to be brilliant.

    Download the play mat and, when explaining the game, cover each step on it in a general way so they have some context about the game's ebb and flow. Make sure they clearly understand the Establish or Resolve dynamic and why you'd want only one color of dice. If someone is itching to go first, let them, and go last yourself. If there's any hesitation, you go first and model what you want to see.

    Brian's right, don't be shy about re-tossing the dice if you don't like the look of your initial setup roll.

    \n - I have learned to answer I'm-not-sure-if-I-get-it questions with:
    'Even if we screw-up a rules procedure, this isn't the kind of game that breaks when you Do It Wrong. It will still work out fine.'

    - If somebody gets stuck on developing a scene, it's ok to just give them a white or black die and have them invent a super-short 'something good/bad' happens, and move on to the next player. "We see my guy accidentally lock himself inside of the walk-in freezer. Cut" They'll have plenty of opportunity to do more, next go-'round. It's ok to be a supporting character if being a major one is too intimidating.

    -You can also give players an early heads-up that the game has a built-in ending-generator, so they don't have to fret about inventing a tidy outcome.

    -If the players do wrap things up neatly before everyone has played 4 scenes, it's ok to just say 'The End', instead of forcing more unwanted scenes on players. Then go straight to the Aftermath.

    Posted By: gravityFiasco goes to the heart of what RPGs are all about - collective storytelling and imagination.
    \nThis pains me a little because it makes Fiasco sound like Once Upon A Time or something. Definitely play it as written! Thing is, as written it leaves a huge amount of latitude for individual groups to fashion an experience well suited to them. Stuff as simple as who gets to talk and when is deliberately ambiguous. What constitutes a positive outcome. How much control and authority anyone has over a character. Who has the right to edit, and when. Almost always these decisions are made organically, in play, as players negotiate the game's bones, which are fixed - what relationships exist and why they are in crisis, who establishes and who resolves, what happens to disrupt the status quo, and how the whole thing turns out. Every group decides for themselves, for that session, and there can be a lot of variation.

    Posted By: Jason Morningstar
    \nThis pains me a little because it makes Fiasco sound like Once Upon A Time or something. Definitely play it as written!

    We do play it as written (except this time we were one more player than recommended), but as you say Jason, there are much fewer procedural and technical rules specified in the rules set. In my experience, this is great for introducing adult non-gamers to the hobby. 1) Most non-RPG people I know are not into complex rules. 2) There is less time spent on teaching how to play. 3) Play flows more organically according to the personalities and preferences of the people around the table.

    I know that if I'd brought Burning Wheel to the session last weekend and tried to explain the rules and procedures, I'd have been met by blank stares from the new players. When we laid Touring Rock Band on the table, read the theme text and quickly went through how scenes, acts and dice worked, I was met by exclamations of joy and excitement.
