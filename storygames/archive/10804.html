\n Like, seriously. What are the uses of a GM screen?

I've played plenty of games with a GM, most of which involved keeping certain things secret from the players, and I've never used one. I ain't saying they're useless, but I am saying that I've never missed one.

    \n When I played a lot of D&D I used a screen because I'd usually have a map of the dungeon with all the encounter areas and traps and such clearly labeled. As making your way through the dungeon by wit and skill was a large part of the game, players seeing that, even by accident, could deflate the fun fast.

    That was about the only time I can remember using a GM screen and feeling like it was really needed for what it was. The maps were so visual, and so easy to read on even a glance, that keeping them where I could see them and the players couldn't was an issue. Games where the information is mostly secret notes and such is much less of an issue, as its harder to accidentally see something that will spoil the game with that sort of information.

    I also, when I used to play really robust games, would put a GM screen down flat so everyone could see the handy reference tables on the back. Then I figured out I could do that much cheaper with my printer, and that pretty much killed the screen for me.

    \n I use a screen for 4e, but nor for rolls. There's a lot more excitement with the rolling when everyone can see. When the monster is attacking the guy whose in single digit hps, it's much more dramatic to have everyone waiting for that die to stop than doing it behind a screen and just announcing.

    In short, I use a screen because it makes the game more fun for my group.

    \n I haven't used a screen in a while, but when I did, it was for the reference, not the "hidden secrets".

    I found a tri-fold vinyl screen from Great White Games (now Pinnacle, I guess), that had some excellent features:
      \n
    • It folded on the short edge. That is, all the panels were in "landscape mode", making it much lower and not as much of an obstacle.
    • The panels had clear into which you could put whatever you wanted.
    • The panels had pockets on both sides, so you put things the players needed on the reverse (I usually had at least one map there). For new games, a "cheat sheet" in large type also helped.
    \nI see that Pinnacle still sells inserts for this type of screen, but it doesn't appear that the screen itself is available any more.

    \n I use a screen, but I have it set BESIDE where I sit. It isn't folded out "wide", instead it is set with the panels each set at 90 degrees to the central panel. By setting it up just to my right, I have the benefits of a secret space to store notes, without putting up the divider between myself and the players.

    It conceals notes - stuff like investigative flowcharts and social links between groups. I don't roll behind it, nor do I hide behind it, but it is a very nice tool to keep some materials secret from the players. I clip papers to the screen and have my notebook on the table behind the screen.

    This has caught on in most of the groups I play in also, with most of the GMs using something similar, even those who don't normally use screens. The laptop GMs also do the same thing now, placing the laptop to the side, putting a screen around it, and putting paper notes over the keyboard of the laptop. The Lacuna GM uses his mostly so he has a list of Jungian archetypes and a photocopy of the Mentors on it as well as a few scribbled notes about what he sort of has planned.

    Reference style screens (like the classic D&D DM screens) I tend to lay flat on the table so everyone can see the tables.

    \n GM screens are great because:

    * setting physical stuff up for your game is great
    * having a place to keep all your notes and such where they are distinctly yours is great
    * having a physical marker of a person with a special role is great (like the Investigator's Notebook in Dirty Secrets)
    * limiting the space you take up at the table is great - otherwise all your junk will sprawl out and ruin everyone's fun
    * having reference material close to hand is great
    * having something to hang pictures on where everyone can see them is great
    * having a place to put the group's name cards is great, say, in keeping initiative order

    Basically there are one billion great things about GM screens and they are objectively terrific in every way.

    If you have a GM, that is.
