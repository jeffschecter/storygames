    \n In Norway, "role-playing game" is called "rollespill". That means "role game" and/or "role play". It's the most common term used to describe, for example, improvised plays/sketches in school (it's actually part of the mandatory Teaching Plan/L\xc3\xa6replan to have "rollespill" as part of the Norwegian curriculum).

    When telling people I'm making a "rollespill" about the Holocaust, I've had no adverse reactions. When I tell English speakers I'm making a "role-playing game", some get very upset at the idea of playing a game about atrocities. That's why I'm no longer calling "Kristallnacht" a role-playing game in most contexts; it gives the wrong impression to many people. It has few, if any, game-like elements, and has much more in common with theatre than with D&D; however, I see it as a natural development of a branch of the role-playing medium.

    \n There is an additional layer of complication when a story game is derived from or influenced by a type of game other than a strategy/board game.

    For example: many theatre or improv games and literary games and some kinds of parlor games and word games are structured play and the people who create/play them consider them to be games, but they may not be competitive or use resource management.

    I feel like these kinds of games are sometimes looked down on. The best example would be the unfortunate way that the phrase 'parlor narration' has become a kind of critique or apologetic admission.

    I tend to like story games that have literary/ theatre/ parlor/ word game inspirations because I perceive them as being more direct paths to creativity than resource management/ strategy games.

    There's a neat phrase in the wikipedia entry on violet spolin's theatre games: "structures designed to almost fool spontaneity into being" - I think this is a great partial definition of what a story game could/should be

    However, as Timfire mentions, some people want the strategy game experience when they hear the term game
