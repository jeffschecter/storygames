\n I had the idea, earlier, of writing a Monster Manual for indie games. It would contain all the monsters from indie games.

Now, don't get me wrong, this is a bad idea, which I'm not going to do. But out of interest: what would go in it?

There are some ground rules, of course. They must be monsters, not merely bad NPCs. And they must be mentioned in the text: you can't have the dragon you played once in In A Wicked Age.

There are borderline cases, too, like the demons in Dogs In The Vineyard. Let's avoid those, at the moment, and think of specific monsters.

So far, I've got the Shab Al-Hiri Roach and the Space Squids from Lady Blackbird. What else is there?

Graham

    Solipsist has Shadows.
    Sons of Liberty has Englishmen.
    Urchin has talking cockroaches.
    Don't Rest Your Head has weird monsters.

    The Shadow of Yesterday, as we know, has no monsters.

    \n There's a wererat in It Was A Mutual Decision, I think, so that's definitely in.

    Oh, I should explain. The idea was prompted by writing monsters for Cthulhu Apocalypse and wondering whether I could include The Shab Al-Hiri Roach in there. So I might email one or two authors and ask if I can borrow their monsters. But that's not the main thing.

    I'm generally interested in the general idea of monsters in indie games. There aren't many. I can't decide whether this is a trivial observation (after all, indie games are generally about collaborative world-building, with little room for statted-out monsters) or whether there's something deeper going on.

    In any case, if you've got more indie game monsters, I'm interested.

    \n Tangentially, I've found a book that's served this purpose in a good number of the games I've run this year.

    ISBN 978-1-4351-1504-0
    "A Field Guide to Demons, Fairies, Fallen Angels, and Other Subversive Spirits"
    Carol K. Mack and Dinah Mack
    Copyright 1998, but republished in 2009 by Owl Books

    First, it's listed as Reference instead of Fiction. Second, it's devoid of numbers or statistics. Third, each entry includes a story, Lore, and Dispelling & Disarming Techniques.

    For instance, when talking about kappa, there are two included stories from villagers who encountered them and lived to tell about it, as well as what the kappa are famous for and what can be gained from them. It goes on further to give suggestions on how you might actually gain their favor.

    My favorite part of that entry is "Often it challenges passersby to a game of pill-finger, whereupon it grabs at the victim and pulls him into its home. The Kappa always wins."

    Simple and effective. Don't arm wrestle a kappa.

    I don't know if any of this helps you, but if you put together something in this style I'd certainly buy it. It's what I thought you meant by the thread's title.
