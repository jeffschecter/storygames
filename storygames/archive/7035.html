\nIn our continuing efforts to make it easier for publishers to submit their product (especially to save postage time and money), we'll be accepting submissions for product that was released after May 1, 2008, at Gen Con Indy. The ENnies booth is #1504. Just be sure to bring the requisite six copies (one for each judge, one for admin) and a submission form (new forms will be going up on the site shortly) by the end of the day Saturday so we can get it ready for the judges to pick up Sunday morning before heading home. Submitting early also offers the advantage of giving the judges plenty of time to really dig into your product.
\n
\nAlso, I was thinking it would be cool to set up a "Lot of Indie Games" as one of the lots for the Silent Auction at the ENnies ceremony. If anybody would like to donate product (books, accessories, CDs), merch (T-shirt, buttons), a gift certificate for the Forge's, IPR, or Ashcan Front's or anybody else's booth, or some other product or service (dinner with Ron Edwards, or whack Darcy Burgess with a whifflebat, for example) to put together a big package, that'd be awesome. It helps raise visibility of the products, and is a small buy-in to show support for the ENnies for those publishers who may not have submitted their product for this year's awards (I'm looking at you, Luke Crane), but still want to show support of the ENnies.
\n
\nWith regards to judge candidate eligibility, I think we're reaching the point now where we will let the fandom voters decide whether they think someone's involvement with a publisher constitutes a conflict of interest. Obviously, the owner of a publishing company, big or small, or active freelancers can't participate, especially if their product is submitted. But a peripheral freelancer or a playtester wants to throw in their hat, I'll decide if their involvement warrants outright elimination, or if we simply have them declare their involvement on their nomination form (question 2) and let the voters choose.

    \n Hi all,
    \n
    \nWith regards to the dates, those were put up on the site ages ago and I confess, with the exception of the voting periods (which are practically carved in stone), I've been running about three-five days later than indicated. Originally I wanted to have the judges elections happening at the same time as the product voting, but a lot of factors conspired against it. So instead the judge elections for the 2009 Awards will be held August 4-10th, 2008. That way we can announce the winners at the Gen Con ceremony in Indianapolis, and send the poor suckers brave volunteers home with a box or two of product. Sure do hope none of those elected fly in- last year each judge brought home a huge box of product- well enough stuff to keep them and their gaming group occupied until Christmas!
    \n
    \nMy apologies for not coming begging for product on SG. I'm so used to The Forge being The Place To Be in terms of indie publishers that SG wasn't quite on my radar. I'll try and make more of an effort, but just a reminder, I may not have time to go by every single third-party message board out there to troll for submissions. Your best bet is to drop off stuff at the ENnies booth (#1504) at Gen Con Indy if it was released after May 1, 2008. For stuff released later on, I suggest getting together with other small-press guys and combining your shipping efforts- perhaps once around the beginning of December so the judges have some stuff to read over winter holidays, and then once more just after GAMA, and once in May.
    \n
    \nI'm about finalized the judge candidacy self-nomination forms. I'm going to put them up on the web site by the end of the week, and will be posting the information for the judges as it comes in.
    \n
    \nI agree with CW about the personal statement. Those first 250 characters that will be going up on the web site beside your mug (and your top 3 board aliases) are what convince the voter to click on your name and get to learn more about your platform.
    \n
    \nAnd since these are fan awards, we can't have publishers as judges. And if an indie publisher wants to be taken seriously as a professional (as they should), they can't be a judge. At the ENnies we treat all publishers and submitted entries with the same amount of respect, so if I'm not going to allow Wizards of the Coast staff to submit themselves for candidacy, we certainly can't consider indie publishers. It'll have to be enough of an olive branch that I opened it up to playtesters and peripheral freelancers. Now, if you've got a game you've been working on for years that hasn't been published yet, you might still be eligible. And if you haven't published anything in 2 years, you might be eligible. But let's face it- having a publisher as a judge takes away the whole impact of these not being "industry" awards. And it creates a sense of impropriety, unfairness and blatant conflict of interest.
    \n
    \nFinally, there are plenty of indie fans out there who haven't published anything themselves whom I am certain could do an excellent job of being a judge.
    \n
    \nSo to answer someone else's question about how to nominate someone for judge, they do it themselves.
    \nOnce we get the questions out, interested individuals email a 250 character statement, a photo (no more than 150 pixels tall or wide), a larger statement, fill out a form and answer the questions. When the information is complete, their info goes up on the web site. After that, they can campaign all they like on the boards and IRL. Voting goes live the day after the product voting closes.
    \n
    \nHope this covers it all. If not, or for pressing matters, feel free to email me at denise@enniesawrds.com.
    \nSlainte!

    \nOh, I love being called out! Just ask The Pundit. It's been too long, too long.

    Denise,
    The ENnies, as best I could tell in the years I participated, are primarily a popularity contest, and secondarily a potluck recognition of quality.

    My products are controversial niches of niches. Critics love 'em, fans hate 'em. So the popularity contest part gets me every time.

    I produced one book in the alloted period, a piece of historical/mythological game set in an obscure period of Japanese history. I sold at most 500 copies of said book (all direct, too). To say it's an obscure title is being generous. Submitting it to the ENnies for the sake of form seems counterproductive. It's a waste of good books and just clutters up the judge's schedule.

    The potluck isn't to my taste either: The ENnies are driven by publisher submission; it's a self-selected mass of games. Each publisher brings a dish, but if everyone brings potato salad, all we're going to eat is potato salad. Submission isn't based on quality game design. It's based on who pays attention to awards shows. Our esteemed and overworked judges make selections from that game mass. They're the true heroes. Even so, they could be selecting from the best Green Ronin, Goodman Games and Mongoose have to offer, but they'll still be missing out on lots of good games that have gone unsubmitted.

    I love awards. I think they're great. Critical acclaim is sweet. If you want to give my games awards, I will gladly accept them.
    However, I have no plans to submit any further BWHQ titles to the ENnies as I don't think this awards program fits with how I view my games.

    Thanks,
    -Luke

    \n Just to point out: nomination takes two people, IIRC. Looking over the actual winners of 2007, I only see SotC with a silver. Meanwhile, M&M Ultimate Power raked in four gold and silver; and Ptolus--a mega-module, basically--landed three. RE the latter: categories like "best cartography" and "best adventure" skew strongly towards modules, I'd bet; and two art categories, a mini category (typically all-about-the-look, with value-add a close second--i.e. cheap AND pretty), and a production value category skew towards publishers with the money to by the year's best art, full-color print runs, and top layout artists and editors.

    All THAT said, indie can roll with the top publishers--one look at Burning Empires should put to rest any notions that "indie = cheap"--but few soloists can expect to hang with WotC, WW, or even Mongoose. By soloist, I mean someone who writes the whole book, with only minimal editorial support (and few or no contributing authors), and who must have a catch-as-catch-can, low-price, or public domain approach to artwork. This is understandable, even, as a soloist is probably working for a living, paying bills and mortgages, and just can't risk their 401K on thousands of dollars of artwork, professional layout (and advertising support) services, and a series of editors and proofreaders. And, frankly, it doesn't hurt at all to have legions of slavering fans who'd "beta" or playtest a nascent product over and over again just to see their name "in lights."

    'So what?' you might be thinking (thanks for reading all the way to the third paragraph!). Well, I'll summarize my thoughts with a metaphor: few Golden Globe darlings go to the Oscars... and even fewer Sundance winners. Small-press, independent publishers probably should look to other venues for accolades and awards. This point goes above and beyond "popularity," though that 's a major issue. Rather, I think most of the marginalization can be attributed to the nature of the market's most-common offerings, to the up-scaling of gaming materials in general over the decades, and to a categorization scheme which most-rewards those games with the most capital investment in production and promotion/marketing. The indie publisher can only really hope for best writing, rules, and (maybe) setting, as I see it (if you're like Ron, Rafael, or Luke, you get a crack at best supplement, too!).

    Posted By: Thunder_GodWhat is this Grassroots campaign?
    \nI believe it involves their efforts to get supporters to post ads at local gaming shops.

    \n Yes, that's the basics of it for now.
    \nPut up the poster at a FLGS and we'll include a link to the store (or if it doesn't have a web page, include its mailing address) as a thank you for them for showing their support of the ENnies. And we'll enter the name of the person who put up the poster in a draw for some ENnies swag. Your name is entered once per store, and we reserve the right to ask you prove that the poster is in place (a cellphone pic works great).
    \n
    \nWe're also generating "nominee" graphics that can be put on product displays, and will generate a similar thank you program.
    \n
    \nIn an effort to reach out to retailers, I also hope to get a system in place where stores can contact the ENnies to get in touch with the publishers in order to get them to run game days or provide prize support- but that will have to wait until after the Awards.
