Kind of halfway between "Gaming Links" and "GameCraft" and "Little Ideas" topics, so I'll drop it in this topic for now.

I dunno if any of you had, read, or heard of ARIA: Canticle of the Monomyth. It was a remarkable sim-exploration of creating a character framed in its society, and then take it to the macro level with concepts of player-character bloodlines and nations.

Unfortunately, it was a muddled mess, and the authors never turned up to any event to promote or explain their product or how it worked, so it died a cold, frozen death.

There was a companion book called "ARIA: Worlds", which was 'the reference' for creating a fantasy world. Turned out that all it could really help create was Western-style worlds where a lot of default Western-style things were taken for granted. On top of that, the worksheets in the back (Impressive, mind you) totaled about 25 pages of navel-gazing solo play. I could easily imagine that a crafty GM would spend all this time fiddling with making this new, awesome perfect world, then not want to use it for fear of wrecking it by putting it into play.

In any case, Troy "Power 19" came up with some setting-related questions on his blog recently. At first glance, they look like an excessively long list for creating bottom-up setting. But looking deeper, I can totally see where they run parallel to the efforts behind that Masterpiece/Crap (I firmly believe that it was at the same time "Incredible" and "Useless") ARIA Worlds. But in this case, the setting generation questions are actually useful for producing a gaming-ready setting, without this sort of fanciful "I'm going to create my own little world, and hope that the players 'get it' " wistfulness of ARIA or other ground-up worldbuilding efforts.

http://socratesrpg.blogspot.com/2006/12/what-is-setting-part-3.html

What I'd love to see is a collaborative action by a game group to go through the above questions, to get some total buy in. Or maybe the GM comes up with the above, runs it by the group, revises it, and then goes.

In any case, in my regular Thursday group we've got this upcoming year-long GURPS game (loosely based on Firefly) on the horizon with a semi-collaborative setting, but there's still a lot of open questions out there (is the tech important or not? What is there to do, really, if the players want to be proactive? etc). I'm definitely wanting to run these questions by the group and see what shakes loose.

EDIT: Uh... I want to discuss, but there's really no questions in my above ramble, is there? I guess if anything else, I'd like to hear people's thoughts, and perhaps a critique of questions or question categories? Or if nothing else, just give the blog post a read. :-)

    Shreyas: "[setting] becomes problematic and inhibitory to strongly characterised play if it exists prior to character."

    Err, really? And I say this as someone with fairly good credentials as an advocate of the argument that "fetishistic setting detail is bad for you" (viz. http://www.story-games.com/forums/comments.php?DiscussionID=222). I've had cases, both in writing short stories (unpublished and probably unpublishable) and in playing RPGs, of (1) coming up with character concepts and then creating a setting around them, (2) inventing or reading about a setting and coming up with characters inspired by it, (3) coming up with characters pretty much independently of setting, based on broad genre assumptions ("D&D" or "superheroes") and shoehorning the two together (hello, many iterations of D&D!). I've had successess and failures with all three methods --yes, even the last one, which seems destined for crap but actually works surprisingly often.

    I think the best practice -- and the actual practice more often than people realize -- is iterative between setting and character: a strong idea about one element logically (or illogically and intuitively) leads to another strong idea about the other element, which in turn has logical (or intuitive) consequences about the first element, and so on back and forth. Only in practice much more chaotic than that, usually. People can spin entire rich settings from the starting point of "Whatever we do, I want my guy to have a mohawk and a laser sword" and can come up with dynamic characters from a seed of "Gee, it says here that the Thing-Smashers of Whateverplace have the Yak-Riding and Obscene Limerick skills at 11, that sounds kind of cool."

    Now, big fat sourcebooks (or canonical novels, films, whatever) full of setting detail lovingly laid out with no regard for their potential to generate story later, yes, that's a problem, and a distressingly common one. But surely a properly designed setting is pregnant with potential conflicts and characters, not at odds with them.

    Shreyas, at this point I'd suggest that you might be stuck looking at things through the Fantasy Setting prism. Let's take one of the things you talked about for a second, "opposed by external forces." I don't think I really stated they have to be external- just something contained within the Setting. For instance, pretend for a moment the Setting for our game is the Human Mind. It's an insane mind. Trapped inside the mind is the real person desperately struggling to get out. Let's do a very quick and dirty Jumpstart on this:

    -History
    = What's happend to cause this person to go insane?
    = What has happened to him in the past that the players could draw upon to create in-game challenges/content?

    -Geography
    = Where can he go in his own mind?
    = How is the physical space in the mind created?

    -Authority
    = Are there other personalities in the mind?
    = Do they oppress the character? His loved ones?
    = How can authority figures in the person's past come into play during the game session?

    -Social Situation
    = What's it like right now for the real person trying to get out? What's he going through?
    = What does he interact with in his own mind and what is their state of affairs?

    -Mythology/Religion
    = Is the person religious?
    = Does his Faith act as an achor to reality for him? Does it give him hope?

    -Enhancements
    = Off the top of my head, can't think of any that would be useful.

    -Inhabitants
    = Who else lives in the person's mind besides the real him?
    = How does he view these others?
    = How they view/treat him?

    -Where the PCs Fit In
    = As mentioned above, he is fighting to escape the Setting. That's not a question, but you get the idea.

    -Dynamic Forces
    = Drawing from the Inhabitants, Authority, Social Situation, and History, what is making it hard for the character to escape?

    -The Mutables
    = What can be changed about the Geography, Authroity, Social Situation, Inhabitants, and Dynamic Forces in play? (probably all of it in this case)

    Now that's just right off the top of my head with no system of any kind attached. The opposing forces are internal as far as the Setting is concerned. It's all in one person's mind. I'd hardly call that "Western Fantasy." The questions I posed under each catagory are just rephraisings of the origonals specific to the type of game I'm creating here. The answers to those questions would help a designer making such a game create the Setting for his game.
