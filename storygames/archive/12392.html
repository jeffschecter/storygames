\n Over in another thread, Thunder_God had this to say:\n
The Israeli culture, especially the con-going one, is not too keen on following mechanics. They like "just playing", or when there's a conflict just rolling some dice and the GM telling them what it means (at least this is how it was several years ago).
\nAnd that got me to thinking, because not only have I run games for folks who like this style of play, but I also like it myself as a player.

For this thread, I'm gonna call that "primordial gaming". And by that, I don't just mean games where the players may not have an involved idea of the game's mechanics, but more specifically games that may have little to no written mechanics, or perhaps just a thin spine of mechanics with the GM and/or players expected to negotiate/create mechanics as needed during play.

What I've noticed is there isn't much written down anywhere about this sort of gaming, yet it does seem to be popular enough in the wild. Tracking down the game Vulpinoid mentioned a while back ( [The Saga of] Raven's Nest) showed me one classic example of this. Similarly, I've a number of second hand accounts of Prof. Barker ( the Tekumel guy) running games in this fashion for a very long time. I'm sure a number of other board members have participated in similar kinds of games themselves. Some of y'all might've even had fun with it!

I'd like to open up discussion about this sort of gaming, specifically talking about what actually goes on in this sort of gaming, what sorts of procedures/methods are supportive of it, and how to play like this successfully.

I'd also like to hear some ideas about how such play could could be described in text in a fashion that would make the style worthy of being put into a for-sale product. One of the things I've noticed is that this particular style seems to get glossed over in the historical record of game design, which seems a shame to me.

I suppose I'd better start some discussion, too.

One thing I've noticed about this style of play generally is that the GM is more like a party host in some regards, than a keeper of canon or mechanics keeper. By this I mean that while they are engaged in traditional GM duties, they're also in many ways responsible for enabling connections between the other players, and there is a lot of back and forth riffing on ideas thrown out by the players. In many ways, this seems core to what the GM does in these games, especially when the "adventure" or setting is being created on the fly, or based on just a core nernel of situation.

    \n I return to defend primordial gaming from it's detractors. It struck me only a moment ago that nearly every game of Paranoia I've ever played has been primordial - as a GM I refuse to conform to few (if any) of the rules as written. I allow the players to role their own dice (because everyone likes that) then interpret the result for maximum immediate entertainment and potential future entertainment (the introducing of new elements to the situation, for example). As anyone else found this?

    I'll concede that, were I not even-handed or had a very different notion of fun than that of my players, the whole game would fall apart. I think primordialism requires a great deal of awareness as to social undertones and a readiness and capability as a GM to negotiate and reason with players to forward the narrative. That said, I think that primordial play requires the players to be ambitious in their fun-seeking; characters (like all good protagonists) have to be pro-active and ready to leap on subtle hints and nudges from a more passive GM-figure and other players.

    I've been chewing through Keith Johnstones' Impro for Storytellers - I think a lot can be learnt from improvisational theatre and applied to storygames of all kinds, but its relevance has particular potency here. Johnstones talks about Referee figures in Impro-shows, giving out situations, guiding scenes away from the overly farcical or predictable and "honking" boring actors off-stage. Things are at their best (just like in all sports) when the Referee has no need to interfere. The point is, in primordial play the GM should be more like the Impro-Ref - A guide who holds the reigns of the game but loosely with nearly all play-agency attributed to the players; the GM only stepping in where necessary to guide the creation of new scenes, arbitrate player-conflict (not necessarily character-conflict) and keep the action tight and meaningful. "NPCs" should always be played by players, perhaps with a little more guidance.

    I'd like to see a Primordial Primer. Ideally the introductory part of the book would be "GM" advice followed by a larger section to give players the tools and expectations to be 'pro-active' in play. There's more to be learnt from Impro: character motivations, scene motivations, timing, flow, agreement etc. The book really should be about equipping players with that agency that's essential to play. As for the rest? Well, I'd dearly love some tight scenarios focused on character interaction but also some short "one-shot" scenes that can be played as warm-ups or as cut-aways in a longer session. (I was recently playing Dark Heresy and the GM "cut-away" to a short, primordial dream sequence with an entirely different tone and focus - very refreshing.)

    \n I think systems (as a combination of rules-as-written and pragmatic choices made by the play-group) can't be designed, only directed. Sometimes the direction is very strong (crunchy, rules-heavy games) and sometimes very subtle, but a system definately has more to it than just the text of the book.

    In my mind evolution in this fashion cannot be designed - it's organic. The play-group makes choices, comes to understandings and, either explicitly or implicitly, develops 'house rules.' Evolution has to be unique to each group; this might be irresponsible as a designer but otherwise the beauty of primordial play is ruined.

    I think there has to be very little technology involved - the dice is there to prompt the GM (a good role, good things happen; a bad role, bad things) and always offer the player a chance of a great victory or loss. A GM, a dice (a d20 is only 'better' than a d6 in that it offers a greater range of outcomes; middling-fair etc) and a very simple system of modifiers are all that are needed for the mechanical side of SYSTEM. Everything else is elementary... primordial.

    Archipelago II is a system that's core has a certain sense of the primordial to it - "Yes, if; No, but; No, and.." (etc,) cards taking the place of the GM.

    \n I think a design that facilitated this style of play would look a little like the principles for Apocalypse World. Here's a game I wrote just now as an example:\n

    Master of Illusion

    \nWhen the characters aren't doing anything, choose one of the following:

    Threaten something they care about
    Offer a clue to a mystery
    Hint at a greater threat
    Introduce immediate danger

    When a character does something chancy, have the player roll a die, then choose one of the following:

    They get what they want, but with a price
    They don't get what they want, but they do get something else

    If they rolled a 6, add:
    They get exactly what they want
    They get what they want, and something else as well

    If they rolled a 1, add:
    They don't get what they want, and now they're in even worse trouble
    They get what they want, but it lands them in even worse trouble

    When a character is seeking information information, have the player roll a die, then choose one of the following:

    They get a small clue, enough to lead them further
    They get nothing about what they wanted, but they spot a different opportunity

    If they rolled a 6, add:
    They get a major break
    They get nothing about what they wanted, but some other advantage instead.

    If they rolled a 1, add:
    They get stonewalled: Nothing
    They get nothing, and they land themselves in trouble

    In all cases, always:
    Misdirect: Don't tell the players which option you chose from the lists. Pretend something in the fiction made it happen.
    Make them Work for it: The more the character cares about something, the harder it is for them to get it.
    Cut to the Heart: Actively frame scenes towards what's interesting. Cut past boring parts.


    This game would make me furious, but maybe it's to some peoples' tastes?

    I can think of a whole lot of advantages for this formula.

    1. Easy to cope with new/departing players/characters. No complicated backstory or ongoing arc to to slot them into.
    2. GM planning is simplified by the formulaic structure and expectations.
    3. Player system mastery is not particularly necessary, but genre savvy, "canon" knowledge, and other more generally FUN skills are helpful without being overpowering.
    4. Secure in the protection of formula, players take more "risks" with their characters.
    5. Because characters (as they relate to the plot) are largely defined in terms of their skill set, archetype/tropes, and a few colorful quirks, you get some nice effects
    -- easy to model in many game systems
    -- as long as spotlight is well-managed, character development is largely a matter of coloring in the established outline, which means it is both optional and non-destructive
    -- as long as players stay within the bounds of the formula, they have a LOT of freedom over character's internal states and behavior. All the plot-relevant stuff is system, all the
    character-relevant stuff is not.

    Note that last one! I think that's where a lot of the emotional juice for some players is: they can let the "game" part play out pretty much on autopilot, while all their creative juices go into various forms of scenery-chewing, clever quips, and social-level goofing.       \n From my experiences, one of the best ways to promote a kind of "primordial gaming" is through the notion of Mechaphors (as coined by Daniel Solis).

      Dice and randomisers are great for restraining creativity and heightening the tension in an event resolution, but if they can be linked into the experience at a more fundamental level, they almost disappear. Players have less of an impetus to "game the system" if the mechanisms are more transparent. In such cases the randomiser and the mechanisms involved actually take on more of a facilitating role rather than an obstacle role.

      That's just my 2 cents for the moment...

      \n I think it absolutely HAS been, successfully. P-gaming is all over out there, but it is the "gaming that dare not speak its name" on the internet.       People who know a lot about P-gaming:

      1. Online rp-ers
      2. SOME diceless rp-ers - the Amber DRPG used to be fertile ground for it, but it's increasingly a hardcore-only canon-argument club.
      3. CoC players and other highly genre-driven play communities
      4. "Pulp" and superhero gamers of SOME varieties

      I think the association of P-gaming with munchkin/killer GM is like a lot of gamer identity politics: driven not by the style as successful play, but by the common (and often spectacular) failure modes of the style.

      \nI wanted to touch a little bit on this, because I think Jesse is onto something important here, and I'm trying to sort it out in my own mind, particularly the bolded part at the end.

      I think a lot of times, p-gaming is associated to an extent only with a certain sort of Power Fantasy, generally of the I always Win (right now!!!), because my character is Awesome!!!! sort. I think Jesse correctly points out that this is far from the case, provided horrific plot circumstances and outcomes conform to player expectation ( much of it undoubtedly related to the inspirational source material being used).

      So what forms does this take? And how does it work with indie-think?

      Player initiated horrors visited on their own character
      Not everyone does this, and not everyone does it overtly, but it does seem key to some sorts of P-Gaming. When it does happen, or is risked, players seem more comfortable with it.

      There do seem to be two or three versions of this, however.

      Temporary and for drama
      The player puts their character out there and fully expects the Gm ( or perhaps other players) to clue to this and make or suggest vile nasty outcome possibilities. OTOH, they expect this to be at most temporary in application. They expect their character will have some chance to reverse the badness, either through their own actions or opportunities provided by the GM, or the actions of other PCs.

      It's okay to Die Heroically as part of a Big Showdown
      Even TPK is okay, as long as there is a point in the story that might as well be labelled "The Big Showdown!!!". P-Gamed Cthulhu seems to follow this formula pretty regularly.

      "Gritty" P-Gaming means a single main character can buy the farm ( or come to another bad end) before the Big ShowDown, but no more than one or two per session or story chunk.Boromir, Boromir, Boromir. And probably Gandalf, too, at least at Moria, for a short while. Also pretty common in Cthulhu.

      The Big Showdown and Almost-TPK aka The Sole Survivor and the Sequel/EpilogueDepending on the genre expectations, it may be fairly common to end a Big Showdown with a sole survivor. This is probably especially true for war settings or cthulhu-esque adventures. Heck, it's almost an expected end state in those. In more heroic gaming, particularly of the Fantasy sort or the Pulpish kind,there almost has to be a survivor to pick things up and recruit new allies for another go at the baddies who have temporarily won the day. Especially important in the game that is expected to be ongoing.

      Rollercoaster Ride Failure and Success
      In P-Gaming it seems very important that failure must be followed by success in relatively short order. I'm tempted to say that no PC should fail more than twice in a row without at least one small success in the meantime, no matter what.

      (Unless of course we're in some sort of situation specific game where a character failure is a success at the player level. Yes, I know that sounds weird, but think about Soap Operas. In that circumstance, the Good Character that falls for the Villainous Seducter's adances, for example, has "failed" at the character level but quite possibly succeeded at the player level.)

      Of course, I should probably re-iterate at this point that I think all of those sorts of things are incredibly common in groups that are P-Gaming successfully, but not inherently definitive of the thing.

      \n As far as publishing goes I'm going to go out on a limb and suggest that a lot of John Wick's designs I think are very Primordial. They have a core mechanic that sort of represents the thematic pulse the game has and then usually a die system that is designed to be flexible enough for the group to sort of make up their own rules for applying it.

      In Houses of the Blooded for example in the text there are some rather specific situations where when one person makes a wager the other person has to make an equal number of wagers. I'd call those specific case rules except I've seen John and others on the forums talk about other cases where they've required players to do this. So the examples in the books aren't really rules but rather example cases where during play testing that dice trick was applied. If there's an underlying "rule" for when you make players match wagers and when you don't, I don't know what it is.

      Similarly I've been following his live-journal where he writes about the Changeling game he's running. At one point the characters were trying to kidnap a sleeping vampire. So John picked up 4d10 and for every action the characters took he rolled. He told them that if any of them come up 10 the vampire wakes up. Doing that in game with me would cause me to leave. That's not in the rules. That's not how the game works. But everyone as his table must have been fine with it.

      So yeah, I think you can design primordial games. I think most of Levi's games may work in this space as well. Lots of gears and widget you use to build your own gears and widgets as needed.

      \n I'm just reading through Apocalypse World now, and it's basically this style of gaming but made functional and provocative.

      You still have the GM as a sort of ringmaster orchestrating threats and challenges and making trouble, but with clear guidelines and procedures for doing so, such that it's a) not exhausting to do, and b) never needs you to pull your punches against the PCs.       As a GM, it's your job to decide when to make a roll for outcomes in a chancey situation. It's also your job to make the final call on what those outcomes mean. Some possible outcomes are almost guaranteed to go outside of what folks really want out of the play. Ditch those ones. Take 'em right off the table. You should be left with just a couple now, probably of the Yes/Yes And/No/No But (and related) varieties. Let a die roll randomly decide between them.

      As a GM, you should always state outcomes tied to the die reult before the roll is made. This can be as simple as a 50/50 chance, or can be bit more complex, involving multiple outcomes and weighting of results.( Weighting results??? This is simply saying that, in yourr opinion, some outcomes are likelier than others. "On a roll of 1-5, Thing A Happens. On a roll of 6, thing B happens". It works pretty much the same with multiple outcomes)

      As a player, you can decide if circumstances are chancey too and worth a die roll. The GM is the final voice in whether to go for a die-roll or not, however. If you don't want to step out of character to do that, just pick up the die and make it obvious non-verbally that you think a roll should be done.

      As a player, you can also apply this to other players. Just slide a die in front of them. That's a good enough non-verbal indication that you think a roll should be made. It's up to the other player to then pick up the die on their own or to have the GM indicate that they think a roll should be made.

      As a player, you're within the rules to suggest different outcomes to the GM, even if your character isn't directly involved. You're also within your rights to indicate outcomes you don't want at all. Whether you do this directly (out of character) or indirectly (through in-character asides) is up to you. As a player, you can do either and switch back and forth between methods during the game based on personal preference and group preference.
