The Two-Hour Game

Jason_Morningstar
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games
I think we should all design roleplaying games that can be played - from friends showing up at the door to finding their coats and sending them home - in two hours. Here are some reasons I think this would be a good idea:

1. That's the upper limit for a well-designed board game and presents no barrier to entry in terms of commitment.

2. Easier to round up some dudes for two hours of fun then, say, ten solid hours of fun. Two hours after work. Two hours on Sunday evening before some TV show everybody watches together.

3. Thinking like a non-gamer, I'll try pretty much anything that leaves me time to do something fun afterward if it turns out as goofy as it sounds.

4. Simplicity is essential. SImplicity is good.

5. I think it's a tough design challenge.

What do you think? What are the precedents out there? Would this be a useful exercise or am I missing some salient point? Have you played focused, two hour sessions successfully? Andy, when will the Web site for the contest be up? (I kid)

  • yukamichi
    April 2016
    The Kancolle RPG! Based on the popular Japanese browser game of the same name, where players control fleets of anthropomorphized WWII-era warships.

    Characters are all pre-gen (based off of the historical ships available in the game) with the option to customize their abilities and equipment as an ongoing part of play. Content of individual scenes is mostly determined on the fly through random tables with a mix of player input through arbitrary keywords scribbled on cards each round, sort of like charades (you can write "cat" on the card and the player who pulls it or the GM can try to work a cat into the scene somehow). Pretty heavily structured with a limit on the number of scenes per game, and combats cap out at 2 rounds, so things don't bog down. There's a lot of resource management and minimal improvisational RP based on the random scene prompts (and you can really play without any "RP" at all), but it bills itself as an RPG and I won't argue with its definition.

    My take on it (I can't say for sure but I think it's a reasonable conclusion to draw) is that since it was a tie-in to a popular franchise, a lot of the mechanics had to be made very simple so people with no RPG experience who were fans of the video game could still get into it easily.

    This topic interests me greatly! I spent some time last year experimenting with fitting things to that time slot, without complete success.

    Microscope and Dungeon World are my current preferred options for two hours or less, though they still feel a bit like demos rather than full games at that length.

    There are games that work well at one hour or less: Kaleidoscope, Cheat Your Own Adventure. But whilst those are great, they also blur the line between an RPG and a parlour game.

    Ironically, I think there are some games that work well for a quick one-shot but are a bad fit for less than two hours. Fiasco, for example, is great for about 2.5 hours. But because it has a defined number of scenes and because the mechanics only make sense in the context of the end-game, it seems hard to make it shorter than that. (I'm sure you can with experienced players, but I'm most interested in short games for demoing to novices.) I don't think you can meaningfully do a one-hour demo of Fiasco (or, say, Intrepid or Witch: The Road to Lindisfarne) in the same way you can do a one-hour demo of D&D. But I'd love to hear that I'm wrong!

    I would love to hear more opinions on this.
