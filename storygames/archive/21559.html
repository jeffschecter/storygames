\n I'm doing some genre research for a game I am working on. I'm interested in Pre-Tolkien high fantasy like Lord Dunsany's, "The King of Elfland's Daughter", William Morris' "Well at the End of the World", and Hope Mirrlee's "Lud-in-the-Mist". It doesn't have to actually pre-date Tolkien; it just has to have that ethos. I know about Neil Gaiman's "Stardust". I'm interested in others. Short stories would be best, but whatever you can name would also be grand. Movies that you can recall in this traditon would be awesome too, even children's literature of this kind would be wonderful to hear about. And if it is available on the Guttenberg Project, well, that's a trifecta.

    \n The original mythologies from the cultures on which Tolkien drew can be quite fun. Norse myths especially are great high fantasy: gods, giants, dwarves, men, the whole enchilada. Neil Gaiman recently wrote a collection, but you should be able to find free versions online too.

    The Gospel of Loki, by Joanne Harris, is a very good novel-length adaptation of a lot of the myths.

    \n Historical romance in the vein of Walter Scott is a core antecedent as well. Not fantasy proper, though.

    That being said, the following are all relevant (I'm curating the Wikipedia lists, mainly):

    Alice in Wonderland
    The Chronicles of Prydain
    Three Hearts and Three Lions, Broken Sword
    Once and Future King
    The Dark is Rising
    The Neverending Story
    Chronicles of Narnia
    The Satanic Mill
    The Princess and the Goblin
    The Brothers Lionheart
    Gormenghast

    Elric of Melnibone
    The Chronicles of Thomas Covenant
    The Dark Tower
    Earthsea
    The Saga of Recluse
    The Deed of Paksenarrion
    The Book of the New Sun
    Dune
    Amber

    The first list are pre-, concurrent or otherwise unrelated to Tolkien; the second is clearly post-Tolkien, but might be nevertheless relevant to your interests here in various ways for how they play off him.

    In general, you should probably consider the genre of "fairytale fantasy" while looking for pre-Tolkien fantasy of the sort you mention - that's probably, most of the time, the genre in which you'll find the likes of Dunsany. For example, Wikipedia's list has pretty much all of your cornerstone examples. Admittedly Wikipedia is rather vague about separating fairy tales proper from modern-age fairy tale fantasy novels with original content, the sort we'd call "saturomaani" in Finnish.

    \n I\u2019d consider fiction outside of novels/prose as well \u2013 Wagner\u2019s Ring Cycle of operas is maybe the most obvious precedent for LoTR-style high fantasy, while plays like A Midsummer Night\u2019s Dream give a great and decidedly non-Tolkien view into Renaissance-era fantasy tropes. If you want visual references, I\u2019d look at Pre-Raphaelite and Golden Age of Illustration artworks. And for my money, there\u2019s no better explicitly non-Tolkien fantasy novel than Susanna Clark\u2019s Jonathan Strange and Mr. Norrell, which also takes the premise of \u201cdistinctly English myth\u201d and runs with it in an utterly different direction.

    \n Hm, check out Joy Chant. All of her stuff is good and different from all of her other stuff, but for something in the pre-Tolkien vein, look at The High Kings. Also, Le Guin and McKillip are good authors to check out. (McKillip's Forgotten Beasts of Eld is very good, as is her Riddle-Master trilogy, which also has a pig stampede! She's written a WHOLE lot more than that, moving forward, never back, but these are good to start with. The Sorceress and Cygnet duology is good as well.) I'm also fond of Tanith Lee's 5 book Flat Earth series (in my opinion, 2nd book's the weakest). Oh, and read the Mabinogion in any reasonable translation.

    Diana Wynne Jones is definitely worth a look -- check out her Dalemark Quartet (the fourth is on the weak side, having been written many years later, but the rest are rock solid) and maybe Fire and Hemlock (contemporary retelling of Tam Lin, but good of its kind). Ellen Kushner's Thomas the Rhymer is good as well, very much about stories and storytelling.

    Oh yes, look at Catherynne Valente's Orphan's Tales duology. Very good build effect. Jo Walton's Lifelode is good and very different, and Greer Gilman writes in the language of dreams. And for high fantasy of economics, check out Max Gladstone's Craft books. And if we're mixing peanut butter and chocolate, definitely look at Alaya Dawn Johnson's The Summer Prince.

    If you want to look at comics as well, Ursula Vernon's Digger and Mike Carey's Unwritten and Lucifer are well worth checking out.
