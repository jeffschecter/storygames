\n Okay! I've given WFRP3 a thorough reading, made a couple characters, messed with combat and the dice and stuff. Neat, neat game. The dice system is very smart; Ralph's already done an outstanding job of laying out its strengths. It's definitely fussy with all the bits and bobs; I'm curious to see how it feels with other human beings managing those bits.

So anyway, a LOT of nu-skool design touches really jumped out at me. Here's what I've got so far.

* Progress tracks: As they're applied to the Party Sheet, it's pretty much Lacuna-style Static. Players do unspecified things, GM makes a cryptic note about it by increasing a counter. I've been thinking you could draw some incredibly interesting conflict-y play out of simply re-engineering the Party Sheet mechanic. Like...right now, basically it's a peer-pressure-juiced punishment -- dick around too long, and you'll be responsible for inflicting stress and fatigue on everyone at the table. But ... what if you had OTHER effects fire off at specific Party stress points? Everyone gains a Fortune! Everyone gains a free Maneuver! The party may call for an Interlude/Recess RIGHT NOW. Every player may now submit a new Goal for this Episode. Stuff like that.

* Goals: Yeah, these are totally Mouse Guard style goals. It's too bad there's not some economy facilitating them... Thinking the Party Sheet gets fed a Fortune every time a player pursues his stated goal. Now it's Artha!

* Say Yes/Prenegotiated Consequences. Holy shit, it made the big time. I'm pretty jazzed about the way they've sort of stealthily slipped this in. This aesthetic is even present in the dice vocabulary: crossed swords aren't "failures," they're "challenges." I think this works really nicely as-is: hand out misfortune, apply a Condition, inflict some in-the-fiction consequence, but make sure the player buys in before the roll. Yessss.

* Yes and/but; No and/but: This is lovely. I'm glad there are already boon/bane complications cooked into the action cards. It's mechanically sound; it probably really pops when you tie the mechanical consequence hard into the ongoing fiction.

* Slow, steady Advances aren't really doing it for me. Ralph mentioned there's a way to individually reward players but I can't find that yet. I assume that's in the Tome of Adventure?

Stuff I wish there was a bit more guidance on:

* How to build Progress tracks. I get why and when, but the actual budget for them is hazy. In all the examples, they typically tie the track length into someone's stat. I think that ought to be mechanized, even if it's as light as "the GM decides which stat builds the Progress track." Right now it kind of isn't, so it feels arbitrary.

More later.

    \n The GM's flexibility in awarding Fortune dice allows quite a bit of steering this way or that for game flavour. It could be "you get them when you're good at narrating exciting results by reading the dice types and where success/failure boon/bane comet/star came from" or "you get them when you hit your goal/key etc.". Ditto the extra XP beyond the one per session. The fact they go into the Party Pool is a nice way to avoid that being more player neutral than handout things that "free form" sometimes feel.

    The mechanic of Fortune cycling "per game session" struck me as an interesting nod to the real world, whatever else is going on, in the real world you show up to have fun, here's three Fortune dice to have fun with this session and XP to advance with, beyond that it's up to you.

    The flexibility of time to conform to the narrative flow was one of the toughest things for me to get my head around till I saw the sidebar in Tome of Adventure being clear that "in a wide-lens" mode, 1 round could be 1 day and "close range" could be same building, with medium same neighbourhood etc.

    I can see doing a quasi-Mouseguard duel of wits with the progress tracks for a social conflict, the mid-point being "a concession" and the end-point being "argument conceeded". So to win hands-down you need to advance to end-point without foe advancing to mid-point etc., the rules pretty much imply that I think.

    \n Okay, so we made three characters and had a VERY quick throwdown. Stuff that jumped out at me:

    * The Party Sheet may be even more of a killer app than the dice system. We've arrived at WFRP3-style results via other die methods before, so it's novel but not revolutionary to load them all onto funky custom dice. We do like them, though, and got pretty facile at building the pool quickly. But the Party Sheet! Yes! Making everyone decide the character of their party is huge GMing help. My guys chose Glory Hounds, so now I know that if glory's on the line, they'll go after it. I pay Fortune into the party's pool when they do that, but even w/o the scooby snak they're still ready and willing to play toward their Party Sheet. It's neat.

    Socketing abilities into the Party Sheet is hot, but also MMMMAYBE broken. Need to play it more. There are some, uh, really awesome talents that don't exhaust and provide staggeringly useful benefits. I'm sure it's just a matter of GMing toward that, and fiating up the challenge a bit to match the talent.

    * Action card selection is painfully vague. The FAQ -- which we didn't really study 'til after the session -- clarifies that you can play "reaction" cards any goddamned time and they're not counted as your action. Gosh that would have been nice to include in the rulebook.

    But worst of all is the utter lack of guidance regarding action card Traits. Sometimes they mentioned a rank -- that one is explained in the character creation rules. Sometimes they mention an Order or a College; we have to *infer* that you're stuck choosing from the correct Order or Cult -- there is no mandate. Worse still are some of the traits attached to the action cards from the Adventurer's Toolkit. "Ancestor" and "Way of the Sword"? No rules I can see. We're just guessing that the High Elf Swordmaster is the guy who gets access to Way of the Sword cards. Ancestor, I have no idea. Have to look back through the professions and see if one jumps out.

    * Does anyone know what a character's "maximum Fortune" is? Can that number increase? I'm *assuming* it's 3, since that's the top-of-session refresh. But in the Fortune rules, when you pay the party off the Party Sheet, if you already have "maximum Fortune" you leave your chit on the sheet (thereby making future recharges happen faster). So, WTF.

    * All in all there's a surprising amount of procedural looseness to the game that isn't apparent from reading. Took us 30 minutes to figure out that "Weapon Skill (St) vs. Target Defense" doesn't mean you match up, say, your St vs. his Ag. The phrase "Defense" is defined only in reference to NPC critters and armor rules. Other looseness: How much damage you actually do in a hit -- soak is implied but never defined, and the fact you add your St and the weapon's damage is also sort of obliquely referenced. Or the fact that exhausting a talent means putting precisely 4x markers on the card, which is mentioned only once. It all added up to kind of a frustrating first go.

    But anyway! More nu-skool touches and upgrades in WFRP3!

    * I'm thinking there's a lot of cleverness to be had with the Progress Track mechanism, but it's ultimately very fiat-y. Not terrible, and a possibly useful tool for GMs to illustrate progress, but the Progress Track mechanism is very much an invitation for DIY wannabe game designers. I'd like to see Progress Track recipes posted up somewhere.

    * I think there's a lot of potential uses to passing around arbitrary stress and fatigue markers -- manipulating that economy is both RAW and probably the right way to address individual players. It appears that ALL fortune markers go straight to the Party Sheet, while individual Fortune Dice can go straight to a player. That's sort of interesting, since the die is (slightly) less useful than the marker.

    * The story/encounter mode split is nice, although I wish there was a bit more structure to how and when a rally phase takes place. There's also some weirdness to some of the rally phase effects: Like, under what circumstances would you "change the initiative order" during a rally phase? I must've missed something. Thinking about a MG-style Player Turn that's possible during an Encounter Phase -- like, amongst the standing choices (free maneuver, recover from damage or stress, etc.), you can also "pursue my written goal" (and earn a Fortune for the party sheet!) and open up like a little side scene for yourself. Maybe at the cost of a rally phase afterward? Not really sure.

    Kind of running dry at this point. I'll need lots more play to see what the game does well.

    \n The whole "these are rules for playing a game" versus "these are gamelike tools for facilitating an experience" cultural divide looms fairly large in WFRP3. At least in my own head, I get slightly stressed by the "gamelike tools" approach. Clear, unambiguous procedures are awfully nice to rely on.

    But at what cost?

    Buh-buh-buuuuuuhhhhhh!

    I don't have an answer to that but it does seem to be The Big Question in my head right now.

    \n Well, I've gotten in a couple honest-to-goodness WFRP3 sessions now. Game's pretty cool but all the oldskool tradness is hard work! I'd almost forgotten about shit like prep time. Thankfully the game doesn't require that I set target numbers as well, or I'd be a basketcase.

    I've noticed that a really common dissatisfaction I'm having with dice interpretation is when you end up with a single boon or bane and have nothing designated to spend it on. Many cards, and of course the two default choices, require 2x symbols. But getting 1x is pretty common! That got me thinking about narrative authority a bit.

    Next game I think I'm gonna do this:

    If you succeed and end up with a single boon or bane, narrate to us the boon/bane element. How did you end up advantaged/disadvantaged beyond your intent? Get a Fortune die for your next roll if you narrate in your stance as well.

    If you fail and end up with a single boon or bane, I will narrate to you how you are now advantaged/disadvantaged beyond the failure itself. Grant a Fortune die to your next roll to reflect your slight advantage.

    Mmmmaybe pull a MG-type trick in which a fail + bane = a new complication for you to deal with (choosing from wilderness/city, weather, monsters or men).

    Anyway, just my latest thoughts.
