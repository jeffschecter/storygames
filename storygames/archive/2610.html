How Can I Be Less Offensive With this Bad Idea?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nJames_Nostack \n\n\n
\n
\n\nMarch 2007 \n edited March 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n As an example of solitaire or pseudo-play I've been mucking around with using D&D; for a game set in a fantasy version of Medieval Europe. This impacts SG's recent discussion on racism, but this time in terms of religious bigotry. I'm wondering: is there any way to handle imaginary counterparts to real-world faiths in D&D;'s "divine magic system" that isn't horribly offensive? In the racist art thread, we talked about ways to break down stereotypes and deconstruct prejudices in fiction; I'm wondering how to do that here.
\n
\nThis all started when I was like, "Oh, if I'm doing medieval Europe, I gotta figure out about how to handle the major religions of the era. Oh, and then assign domains. And then... let's see... uh... Alignments. Oh man."
\n
\nHere's the deal: I like the alignment system. I love that Good and Evil, Law and Chaos are objectively real forces in the world, and you've pledged your allegiance to them. It's like cosmic political parties of Fantasyland, and being a card-carrying member has effects on your magic powers. I don't want to get rid of that.
\n
\nOn the other hand, I don't see any way to assign such labels, even to fantasy-counterparts of the real religions, that doesn't make me terribly uncomfortable.
\n
\nA couple of points to consider:
\n1. I'm aware it's only a silly game which will never get played, but even in theory it still kind of upsets me.
\n
\n2. I don't care that "that's what they really believed back then, so you should reflect that." If there's a way to reflect prejudice that that doesn't disgust me, great--but I'm kinda thinking that's a contradiction in terms.
\n
\n3. To me as GM, I'm not trying to label any of these faiths in the real world. This isn't Real Islam, it's Other Peoples' Imaginary Islam--specifically, as imagined by Europeans during a very unenlightened age. But honestly I don't think that distinction is particularly meaningful.
\n
\n4. It's possible that simply raising the issue this way could cause some critical reflection about religious prejudice in modern day society. A large part of the Western world's religious bigotry really got rolling in the Middle Ages. Maybe exploring the history and the mindset of the time would be helpful in combating modern stereotypes.
\n
\n5. It also proves that, wow, the whole idea of applying "Good" and "Evil" to large swaths of people is absolutely nonsensical and, in fact, downright barbaric. What's it say about us as a society that we keep falling for it, every couple generations? What's it say about us as gamers that we run around gleefully slaughtering "demi-humans" because they're all shifty, stupid, and evil?
\n======
\nI think part of the problem here is that in D&D;, and possibly other RPG's as well, there's the world of people, and then there's a kind of cosmological superstructure--the gods, magic, alignment, etc. I don't see a way to do historical-fiction in a "superstructure" game without stepping on some very sore toes, but maybe I'm missing something.

    \n Well, see, it all depends on which religions you're doing and what you want to say with them.

    Just flat faced basic Sim stuff with moral absolutism makes me want to, on first blush, make the game about Lawful Good vs Lawful Good. Really, if we're going to do the great clashes of Islam and Christianity throughout the ME, we could make one side good and the other bad, or make both bad, and these would all say... something.

    But me, I like the idea for a D&D game of two groups of people who are Lawful and Good who are going to spend 150 years butchering each other because the exact Law and the exact Good that they follow just happen to be different. Rather than a game with ugly-souled Templars fighting noble-savage Turks, or vice versa, I'd love a game where both sides were proud and noble and true and utterly unable to accept the others Law as the Law. Funny enough, this is more or less what the movie Hero did. You could argue it a lot of ways, but there is also a good argument to be made for the major characters in that movie all being Lawful Good. Or at least Neutral Good. And in D&D if you're Neutral Good you can be a Cleric of a Lawful Good god, right? Also, even if all the Clerics have alignment restrictions, the common worshiper probably doesn't. So your Sufis and Bishops may all be Lawful Good, but your common worshiper can be anything.

    As for domains, I always say break it up by groups. Different Saints and Orders for the Christians, different law schools, sufis, and ethnic sects for the Muslims. If you're only going to have "Christian" and "Muslim" and maybe "Pagan" and "Heathen" then you've got too few to cover all the domains anyway. So either have sub-sects, or let the clerics chose two domains and then take feats to gain more over time. After all if you're mister big time monotheist, maybe your god can give you all domains -- you just can't start out with all of them because you aren't perfect enough.

    \n I think where I'm coming from with this alignment thing is that, if you're taking the religions seriously enough to be like, "Okay, St. Francis is a 15th level dude and has spells like X, Y, Z" then you're immediately saying, "This theology got it right, to some degree: in the imaginary world of the game, this particular set of beliefs isn't some historically contingent institution, but rather a distillation of (game-world) Truth."
    \n
    \nAnd once you get around to saying that, in this game-world, a particular faith is true, then it becomes hard to dodge each faith's frequent, and vehement, denunciations of other faiths as not just mistaken, but morally wrong. And that's what's making me uncomfortable.
    \n
    \nFor example, when I was thinking about this game-world, the Albigensians would have been heretics led by the Anti-Christ (a high-level Half-Fiendish Blackguard). One half of me is like, "Hey, it's fun to 'fantasy-up' this historical context," and the other half of me is like, "This is completely tasteless and vile." (I happen to like the Albigensians IRL, and what happened to them was a terrible crime against the human race.)
    \n
    \nIncidentally, in the theoretical game, "Good" would be defined as, Actions which tend toward saving the souls of others. (Presumably from a Christian perspective, given the prevalance in that region of the world.) Note that this would be distinct from looking after others' material needs, which would be a more Neutral outlook, either Lawful or Chaotic depending. Also note that it pretty much would put you at odds, to some degree, with all other religions, because they're takin' souls which ought to be coming to your doorstep.

    \n I thought it might be worth pointing out that D&D's definitions of "Good" and "Evil" are pretty specific and don't necessarily line up with what those terms mean in neither everyday speech nor moral philosophy. I've always thought these were an attempt at genre emulation, particularly the notions of Good and Evil found in Tolkien. (Law-Chaos attempting emulation of the other strain of fantasy morality, a la Moorcock. D&D, in trying to be "fantasy," dumbly combines both tendencies, like Nuts & Gum.) Point being, attempts to map real-world morality to the D&D alignment system always come off as just goofy, and lead to bizarre arguments. "Good" in D&D is some special metaphysical property, possession of which has verifiable supernatural effects.

    Ideas:
    a) Just jettison the whole alignment system, and you're instantly that much closer to dealing with "real" morality. Alignment-based effects are either removed from the game or based on ad hoc judgments.
    b) Redefine "good" and "evil," "law" and "chaos" as seems appropriate for your setting.
    c) Replace the default alignments with one or more sets of other diametric opposites. This allows you to communicate what conflicts you want the game to be about. You can be pretty politically incorrect here, but at least you're putting the potentially offensive worldview on display, and can maybe deal with it in a tongue-in-cheek fashion. Nicely, you can declare that alignment-based effect simply map to one of these.
    d) To echo Brand's comments, if you replace "gods" with "saints," you've got a setup mechanically identical to D&D's default setup. (The old PC game Darklands used this approach. Actually, the setting you're describing sounds a lot like that game, so you might want to look into it.) If you're keeping "Good-Evil," the evil "gods" are obviously demons, their clerics witches.

    If you're really concerned about sensitive exploration of these issues, D&D is the wrong tool for the job.
