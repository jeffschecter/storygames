\n Hey all, I've been working on a cyberpunk game and have been running some Night's Black Agentswhen I get the time. I dig the system, but there are a few things that I wanted to simplify for this new game I've been working on. Love the investigation aspect, running a structured mystery and all that, but I found myself wanting something a bit more fast and loose when the PCs were doing other stuff. I ran a session of Blades in the Dark, and found that I enjoyed the countdown clocks on Conditions so came up with this. I've seen a bunch of threads about GUMSHOE so I was hoping for some feedback and any thoughts. Here's what I've got so far:



Players show what skills they have by highlighting them, then they distribute points among the highlighted skills.

Spending points buys you an auto success. If you have no points you can choose to either take a Condition, or choose to forego a Condition but the GM will escalate the danger in some way. A Condition can be either mental or physical, and suits the situation, are removed when they're addressed in the fiction.

The set-up in the game is that in the far future law enforcement farms out cases they can't solve to consultants at various agencies due to a general lack of manpower for various reasons. PCs work for one such consultant agency, and the game is set in Korea, where they've figured out how to implant skills by using "mind maps" to map neural patterns that correspond to the learning of certain skills.

Of course, in order to implant the memories they've got to screw around in there, take out existing memories and use existing strong pattern maps (though temporary maps are possible) so once you wake up from one of these you're essentially a different person with different memories and different wiring, and skill varies too, of course. So PCs are able to go to Memory Lane and mess with the memories or get skills implanted they need, but there'll be consequences that should add a fun angle to things I think, and should allow for consequences during Investigative scenes as well. I really wanted to add a dimension of having to explore and get to know your body and add an additional personal level to mysteries so I'm looking forward to playtesting.

Any thoughts would be much appreciated!

    \n I love the way you've adapted the mechanics to thematically meld with your setting. I'm also working on a system inspired by GUMSHOE, but it still retains the fundamental idea that you automatically succeed at certain things, without having to spend any points.

    Regarding your system, I have a general distaste for "new danger appears" mechanics but I know I'm in the minority there. I'm uncertain of the necessity of "effect". You liked the countdown clocks, but one thing I always liked about GUMSHOE is the freeform problem solving, from the "asking questions" nature of investigation to the flexible application of skills. You think about how you're going to overcome your task, rather than rolling dice and waiting for the wheel to fill up.

    But, if you're trying to do something very different with GUMSHOE and are mostly looking for adjustments on mechanics, I'd say that you have too few "highlights" and "points" to spend. Also, I'm not sure why one would choose to Fail? Essentially, you'll only attempt that action if you want to succeed at it, unless there are situations where they are forced to make such actions.

    \n I'd be uncertain about the "pushing themselves" when finding clues. Unless the nature of the setting is that the cluefinding is universally perilous, it seems very contrived to insert danger into locating clues.

    Actually what I meant about "a new danger appears" mechanics, again, I know I'm someone in the minority with regard to this, but I don't like threats inserting themselves into play where they don't belong. Again, they feel kind of contrived. I don't mind things like "move x clock forward 1" or escalating the danger of an existing threat, but when I start the scene, these are the threats. You work out how to overcome them, you don't get halfway through and then a completely unrelated action narratively somehow mechanically causes Godzilla to burst through the wall. That's a bit of reductio ad absurdum, but you get my point.

    Basically I don't like it when the mechanics throw their weight around the narrative. I like when mechanics respond to narrative events, a mechanical failure causing a narrative failure, rather than a mechanical failure causing an unrelated narrative event to occur.

    But like I said, I know that the "new danger appears" thing is an extremely popular thing in the indie community, it's just not my thing.

    \n For Medical Bay Three, the number of skills you get is limited by the number of players. You draft a set number of cards for skills, so in a smaller group you get more skills per player, and in a larger group you get fewer skills per player. But always more or less the same number of skills for the group as a whole (this is similar to standard Gumshoe's advice).

    When investigating in Medical Bay Three, the bottleneck isn't having tokens to spend so much as worrying about the Stress mechanic. Investigation while you have Stress pushes you toward your inevitable doom, so players have to ration out their clue gathering. (They can also have intra-party interactions in order to shuffle around the Stress, but that's another matter.) Nonetheless, the mechanic exists to make it difficult to just try every skill until one works, causing the players to make meaningful decisions about what skill to use in each situation.

    The thing that struck me as most similar to your game was that when you don't have the appropriate skill, you can still buy a success by taking on additional problems. (The specifics are a bit different I guess.) For investigation, this means that the player can always get the clues they need, rather than relying on fickle dice rolls, but it isn't a simple gimme every time. I think that is important and useful.

    \nI'd like to get away from the "unsaid" part. Why not have general skills succeed all the time? At least at some level, such as with Cthulhu Dark where the d6 roll determines the range from barely successful (success with cost) to successful with bonuses. I realize there has to be some tension in the game through uncertainty; I'm just not sure if the uncertainty has to be through the actions typically resolved through general skills (fights, chases, etc.) As the GM, I want the characters to win these events, just as I want the characters (and players) to have the information found in the clues. A good compromise is a limited number of guaranteed successes through spending some number of points from a finite pool but I think Gumshoe should take a leap of faith and treat general skills similarly to investigative skills. The hard part, perhaps, becomes making that interesting. I sometimes think that would be getting too far away from Gumshoe as it currently is. However, as others have pointed out, you can guarantee success with general skills in Gumshoe for some finite number of times by spending enough points. I suppose the design philosophy may be that players should save points for when success is vital--but, in a fight or other contest, when isn't it? Even if the success is vital for the character concept. It makes no sense to struggle in fights with underlings (by conserving skill points) and then become more competent when facing the prime antagonist. And, in any case, pools are quickly depleted. That's why I ask about conflict vs task resolution. Since I am proposing general skills always are successful, why not cut to the chase and resolve the entire conflict at once? At that point I throw up my hands and go back to the rules as written (more or less!). Again, I like the compromise in the OP, which basically comes to three options: succeed (by spending a point), succeed with conditions, fail (which I think I would change to "fail on player's terms". Currently, it seems to really be "fail with conditions" and so is too similar to "succeed with conditions.") I think this is a good mechanic, and one I would use for resolving complete conflicts rather than single actions.

    I had refreshing points set-up so that they could choose 3 skills, but had to play out a personal scene to get the refresh. I designed a second page for the character sheet where they can write in lost memories, and new memories. Right now I have it so they can either:

    1) Gain a pool of 3 points they can use as they like (even temporarily use a skill they don't have on hand as long as the memory referenced involves the skill) by tying the memory to the case at hand.
    2) Refresh 3 general skills by showing how the character recovers, blows off steam, or leans on a vice or source of stability.

    I enjoyed having everything player facing so I didn't have to roll and could focus all on the story, even if I didn't prep probably as much as I should have! I also made up a cool map to use after the game. Didn't get a chance to use it so far, but next playtest I'm hoping it'll add in an extra fun element and I'm going to write up some fun rules to generate an adventure by rolling randomly on the map, making a pattern on the map, or using key locations for DaVinci Code-style adventures where clues lead them to different locations, or they can see by where they mark on the map a pattern that begins to emerge.
