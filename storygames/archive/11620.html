\n I've been toying with the thought of starting my own publishing company, focused on translating indie games to the French language and distributing them, mostly in the Canadian province of Qu\xe9bec, and possibly in the rest of the Francophonie as well.
\n
\nProblems:
\n
\n1) I've noticed that those games that are already getting translated (D&D;, L5R, WFR, etc.) are far more expensive in their translated version than in their original English translation. Example: D&D; Player's Handbook: E CAN$38,95; F CAN$49,95. I'm unclear whether that's due to the costs of translating, or rather if it's due to transportation (they seem to be mostly translated and printed in Europe, I think). In any case, I would aim to offer translated games at the same price as the original version.
\n
\n2) However, I still want to make enough money to cover costs, and allow for some expansion (translating and publishing more and more games).
\n
\nI'm thinking of focusing on games released under some kind of Creative Commons, at least at first, perhaps that would help. However, it might also be a bit limiting. Anyone has some insight to give? ('I'm think Eero in particular might have some ideas).

    \n Well, I'm almost certain that the business model under which those trad games are translated and published has almost nothing in common with what you would be doing, so I don't think that they are very compelling evidence for where your price point would fall. If you want to strive for a similar price point with English-language games, and that is backed by your business model, then why not.

    Which costs would you cover with your profits? Costs of licensing, printing and distribution, obviously, but would you also try to get some money for the translator and book designer, or would this be more of a hobby endeavour from their viewpoint? Considering that most indie designers do not accomplish that last step in their native market to a very appreciable degree, it seems like quite a challenge.

    There is one benefit in a CC licensed game from your viewpoint, and it's the fact that you can publish it in the Internet, which would cut down the hard costs considerably. If you're not interested in that (want to make a real book and get money for it), then a CC-licensed game makes no appreciable difference for you, provided that your publishing plans are at least passably serious. My experience is that it's not too difficult to talk indie designers into allowing you to translate and publish their games, especially if you give them a contract that respects their creative rights. The licensing fees are also probably not as high as you might imagine.

    My own experience in a nutshell: making translations of indie games is easy, and even the tiny Finnish-language market is enough to cover the hard costs with a bit of marketing effort. However, if you want to make significant pay for yourself, then you're in the same situation the designers themselves are - not many of us do make enough money from selling our games, so why would you be an exception? But barring any unrealistic hopes of a salary, there is no particular reason why anybody couldn't translate a bunch of indie games and publish them.

    \n If translating the games will be just a hobby, i.e. you won't be paying translators or you'll be doing it yourself, I don't see why it shouldn't work, inasmuch as you'll be able to make some money out of it. I definitely second Jason's suggestion that you contact the original designer/publisher. Most would love to see their work get translated, as it's more money for them; I know quite a few people who got reasonably big breaks in translation through just asking publishers.

    However, if you expect to pay translators to do all or some of the work, I imagine the cost will be prohibitively expensive. I'm a professional translator, and I charge somewhere in the region of 17 or 18 US cents per word. This is legal translation, which is a bit harder than roleplaying games, so the price might be a bit less than that for your project. But not much. This also explains why for example you have to pay around twice the price for non-English language versions of D&D, to answer point 1).

    \n For indie games the licensing fees for the Francophone area would probably range from nil to a thousand dollars, with the average somewhere in the lower parts. It depends a lot on who you approach and what sort of deal you offer; a game with no commercial showings in the English sphere should be quite cheap, and a roaylty arrangement (otherwise a lot of work and angst, note) would ensure that you'd only have to pay according to your success.
