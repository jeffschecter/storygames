\nBone White, Blood Red, my Game Chef entry, requires leather cords and a bunch of beads of specific colors. These aren't easy to get, which means it's harder to play the game than if you just download a PDF, print out character sheets, cut out cards, and grab your handy dice.

I can figure out how to get the rulebook published, but would you buy a set that includes the beads and cords, in addition to the rulebook? Or maybe just the beads and cords, and buy an electronic version of the rules?

It'd probably be more expensive than if you went and hunted up the beads yourself, but would save you some time and energy.

If so, how much do you think you'd pay, beyond the price of the rules, for those? Do games which require specific items for play actually sell well, or does it doom a game to no sales?

Let's hear your thoughs.

    \n Hey Kynn! I can only speak as a consumer (having not taken the "big step" of publishing my own game yet) and as an interested party to your game (go team Unicron), so take my advice with a grain of salt:

    If a game requires props I am much less likely to buy it. This is doubly true if I am unfamiliar with the game in question (since most of my "impulse" RPG purchases these days come in the form of PDFs). An added complication, from a business perspective, is that obtaining supplies of beads and cords will most likely require some up-front capital on your part.

    With that being said in a game like BW,BR the props add so much to the general feel of the game that I can't see playing without it.

    So, here is my suggestion: get the rules out there first (in PDF or printed form) with instructions for making and obtaining the 'props' required (along with some suggestions on easy to find alternatives). Once people get interested in the game, sound some of your customers out on the prospect. If it seems like there is some interest, make a limited set of 'props' easily purchasable through your website... and, depending on the way that you are doing publishing add in the package deal at that point (obviously at a little bit of a discount).

    Neglect the previous bit of advice if you are going to be selling this at conventions. In a convention scenario I would have a couple of "prop" sets on hand to sell as a set with the game. I think that it will help draw interest (you will need a couple for your demos anyway).

    \n At the local big chain bookstore there are several books on teaching yourself how to juggle. Of the books available, I'd pick up the book that comes with the three juggling balls.

    Your game has weird stuff that I need to purchase in order to play your game. Chances are, I won't be able to get the weird stuff in the store where I purchased your game. D&D used to come in a box that contained dice. Now you've got to purchase the dice separately, but they're in the same store as the books. Some miniature wargames come with rules and minis in the same box; those that don't probably have miniatures to purchase in the same store as the rulebook. So yeah, if I came across your game and I've got to go someplace else to get the weird stuff needed to play your game, I'm not getting your game.

    As a consumer, those extra bits are things that are supposed to come with the game. If other games that are of the same type are going for a set price -- let's assume $25 -- your game should go for $25. Look at board games. A game with a board, dice, player tokens, and four sheets of counters can go for the same amount as a board game with a board, two dozen action tokens, dozens of meeples, sixty resource cubes, a 110 card deck of bridge sized cards, and a 55 card deck of smaller sized cards. That first game is far cheaper to produce than the second game, but they both might retail for $45.

    \n If the game is that fun, I will pay a lot for it. My friend is currently putting together a rather expensive RPG, where it comes with:

    1) Three small rulebooks.
    2) About 100 glass beads, of 4 different colors (red, yellow, blue and clear).
    3) A small treasure chest-like wooden box to keep them in.
    4) Some colored cubes, handmade.

    I'm pretty much first in line to throw my money at this game as soon as it comes out. But again, that's only because I played it, see that it works, it's fun, and hot. I could gather all the above supplies myself, and probably already have them: Poker chips in 4 different colors, A 1d6 to replicate the color cube, and just buy the game in PDF format. I might have done that to save money, if I weren't totally in love with this game.

    Put together the pieces, and sell it as a package, and some people will buy it no matter the price. Of course, a LOT of people will buy it if the game is really that good, and there's a lot of people who will back up that fact.

    \n Kynn, I think it comes down to whether your game is going to be played primarily by gamers or non-gamers. Be honest with yourself about this, not wishful - most story games are for gamers.

    Why do I bring this up? Because gamers, even the ones who aren't (loudly!) cheap, will always have a tendency to think, "you could just go get some cheap such-and-sos and use those." Whereas a lot of non-gamers don't much want to use their imaginations when it comes to setting things up - not that they can't, just that they won't.
