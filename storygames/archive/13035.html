Old Post (2010)...
The Hole gallery (104 Greene St. in Soho) just called and woke my ass up at 8 a.m. on a Saturday,
because they have a show that's supposed to open tonight.

So now I gotta go help hang the art, then stand around telling you not to touch it or steal it from 6 to 9 p.m.

The artist is Mat Brinkman, but the press release also promises "an advance previewing of selected work by various artists from a yet to be released Necro~Demonic Dungeon~Crawl~Warfare Boardgame. "

The gallery doesn't usually provide booze, but they're tolerant if you bring your own. So bring me some too.

Given the amount of trendy-cred surrounding Brinkman and the Hole, you can expect some good Lady Gaga-esque people watching.

    \n The opening was a success.

    The art will remain on display until October 23. Worth seeing if you are anywhere nearby.

    The images above don't do this show justice...
    the art consists of many many many black-light-poster sized drawings,
    with charcoal backgrounds so thick-- they look like black velvet paintings.

    It's so easy to cruise thru most gallery shows in seconds, but this time, there's as much to see as you have time to look:

    A felled ogre who who clearly hit the ground with a thud.
    Upsidedown skullbats.
    A many-legged 'grubman' with eyeballs that glint like the old Carrion Crawler illustration.
    A pull cart fashioned from spine, ribs and longbones.
    Crude hammers, a "gore mask"

    Of Indie Interest:
    Amid the works priced in the vicinity of $3000 (plenty of which sold instantly)...

    One wall consists of several dozen pieces-- priced at $100 each.
    Those are the illustrations from the aforementioned "Necro-Demonic Dungeon Crawl Boardgame" by Mat & friends.
    The proceeds from that wall will be used to fund the manufacture of the game (which does not yet have a title).


    As for the "Lady Gaga-esque people-watching" I promised: there was some.
    But it appears that a late-80's Desperately Seeking Susan look has returned,
    meaning that little black skirts, MC jackets, crucifixes and heels-- somehow don't add up to 'goth'
    for the first time in 20+ years.

    I want to emphasize that this is the bigtime--
    The women who run The Hole gallery are the same ones who have presented shows by
    Shepard Fairey, Bjork, Michel Gondry, Yoko Ono, Fischerspooner, Keith Haring, Ryan McGinness.

    All I knew about Mat Brinkman-- before today-- was the comics I had seen in Kramer's Ergot.
    I didn't know if his dungeon-y imagery was hipster-retro-slumming, or heartfelt.

    After today's rush to hang the art & the subsequent arrival of the crowd--
    we had time to converse,
    and I am please to report that the artists' are genuine gaming-friends.

    Of the game topics we discussed, my favorite moment was when one of the collaborators
    mentioned "Rifts". Now there's something that would blow the art world's mind.

    \n This is a good time for the interface between art and gaming, especially if you live in NYC! (Better still if you didn't already have a game scheduled during the Brinkman opening, alas.)
    \n
    \nCasey Smith's Doomslangers exhibit opens at the Allegra LaViola gallery on Oct. 23; on Oct. 20 they're doing the last session of the game that feeds into the exhibit.
    \n
    \nOn Nov. 6, also at the Allegra, I'm helping to organize a panel discussion on the topic that'll cover, among other things, why some artists are genuine gaming-friends and others find it attractive for hipster-retro-slumming. The blurb goes:
    \n
    \n
    Art and games are both forms of ritualized human creativity. When Marcel Duchamp gave up the former to pursue the latter, he famously said "I have come to the personal conclusion that while all artists are not chess players, all chess players are artists.\u201d The interface between art and games is especially provocative for many artists whose imaginations were shaped by the 1974 publication of Dungeons & Dragons and the uniquely free-form and collaborative genre of role-playing games that it inspired. This panel brings together studio artists and role-playing theorists to explore the ways in which making art is like and unlike playing a role within a game.
    \n
    \n
    \nArtists who'll be on the panel include Casey and Ryan Browning of the Doomslangers, Chris Hagerty and Timothy Hutchings from NYC, and maybe some video-conferencing or video presentations from folks who can't be there in person. Suggestions for others are welcome!
    \n
    \nRole-playing theorists is, so far, a fancy name for me doing my schtick about the mismatch between RPGs being a performance art and the role of professionalism in RPGs being limited to writing sheet music for others to play. Suggestions for others here are also welcome.
