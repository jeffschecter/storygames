    \n I use MediaWiki with the semantic plugins. The latter is crucial if you assume the wiki will get complicated. My wiki is not publicly editable.

    I can't recommend the semantic stuff highly enough, though it takes some getting used to, and is probably more than you are looking to do. The basic idea behind it is that when you link to other wiki pages, there is some notion of why that link exists. You can then mine that information using queries on other pages. A typical example is something like a character page that has some mention of the character's birthplace, with a link to the page of that place. In standard MediaWiki markup, you'd do this like so:

    Grog was born from the [[Plane of Clocks and Owls]].

    The semantic markup would look like this:

    Grog hails from the [[birthplace::Plane of Clocks and Owls]].

    Note that "birthplace" is just a tag you make up. You can use any tags you want. The payoff comes on the page for the Plane of Clocks and Owls. Maybe it has a "People born here section". Rather than manually link Grog there, you can set up a special set of markup to say "list everyone who has a 'birthplace' link to this page", and the wiki will update the list automatically, any time someone adds a "birthplace" tag like Grog's.

    As an example of this, look at how this page on my blog renders, then click the "view source".

    Not everyone's bag, but you have a campaign with lots of politics, NPCs or similarly interlocking pieces, it can help. It does require a little discipline (and, I wish I had been using it from the beginning).

    \n I've used wikispaces in the past. It's free.

    I've had mixed results with game wikis. I was in a Burning Wheel campaign where we decided it was good to have everyone add whatever they liked to the setting info on the wiki.

    Which seemed smart, because I figured it would serve as "flags" - if someone stuck something on the wiki about a sacred mountain or a tribe of dwarves, they probably wanted to have that stuff in the game.

    But it turned out to be information overload, with some players posting tons of stuff (way more than the game could handle), and assuming everyone had read it, and some players posting nothing and being irritated that there was all this "other" stuff going on they knew nothing about. ("Who the hell is this chieftain, anyway? Did we meet him already?" "No! He's on the wiki under 'Tribal Leaders of the Red Wastes'!")

    In another BW campaign, we made a wiki, but the rule was that NOTHING could go on it until it had been encountered in-game. That worked better, but it squashed some of the excitement the earlier method generated.
