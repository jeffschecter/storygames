\n Hi, Nathan P. I'm taking your recent reply to another thread to avoid further derailing Graham's thread. And, this thread is FAR too much about Nathan than it should be. It's meant to be more general. I'm not aiming to pick on you or Annalise.\n
Hey Matt and Jason - I hear you and respect your opinions on the matter. And I speak only for myself on this, but here's why I'm publishing Annalise the way I am:

* I want to showcase a number of different artists in the book. I haven't been doing too well with this because of my personal slowness in getting everything together, but between Jenn, Elizabeth and a couple of other artists that I'm working with right now, there's a ton of amazing art inspired by the game, too much to fit in one book. If a potential customer is struck by one persons art over another, I would like them to be able to have that.

* I like giving people choices. Most people seem to buy the red cover, but I've received compliments on the white cover as well - since I can provide both through lulu at no increased cost to myself, why limit that choice?

* Cutesy names create atmosphere, and the production of the game is all about atmosphere. The system itself is relatively content-neutral, so I'm relying on the book itself to convey how I like the game to play without dictating a specific set of actions to players.

*Other than the Eternal Tears PDF edition (which I think is fairly clear that it's a PDF version of the game) and the Interim edition (which is always available through lulu), the different Edition names are for convention releases - the Seven Sins edition was a short run of hand-made books for DexCon, and the Unbidden Guests edition is what debuted at Gen Con in 2008. I think it's a more significant marker of where and when the game was released than "1st edition" "2nd edition" etc.

*Honestly, I'm pretty sure that the atmosphere (including cutesy titles) is part of how the audience for the game self-selects. Sure, it rubs me the wrong way when someone who doesn't understand tells someone else something about the game that isn't true, and when I see untrue statements about my game posted on the internet I feel like it's within my rights to correct them, but I guess that's what I have to deal with.

*Finally, none of these version are revisions, they are all the same, finished content. It's a choice of production and presentation, or depends on when and where you encounter the game, but nobody needs to buy it twice.

Just wanted to throw out some reasons for choosing this path.
\nAll of the above reasons are, of course, your decision as a creator owned publisher. Therefore, anything I say is just peer-to-peer advice. It's not "Oh you suck and are doing it wrong!" Your goals might be (probably are!) incompatible with the goals informing my advice. Ok.

Oh! I should explain what my goals are: I am deeply unsatisfied with the level of success of Indie RPG publishing, pretty much across the board. (I count exceptions on one hand.) I think we can all do better, and I think our culture might be preventing success to some degree. I think that, at least for now, we're too focused on the art and publisher choice and ignoring customer demand. (I think that "customer" tends to be a dirty word, for example.) Broadly speaking, I think publishers in indie RPGs should shift from innovation to increasing sales/acquisitions of published games.

I think this for two reasons, neither of which is to poo-poo someone creating something artistic and valueable to them as a creator. I think this because the endeavor will be increasingly dissatisfying for people, and the micro-industry will hobble along when it could instead stride. OK, maybe not stride. I'll settle for amble.

So, Nathan, all of the your points above make sense to me. I get what you're trying to go in and of itself.

But, I think it's a bad idea.

You have a relatively new game. Even if you had a larger customer base (say, 1,000+ people), you would need to establish your brand. Well, your brand is Annalise. That's it. You can buy Annalise. Or you can buy the Annalise PDF. The end.

Why? Why establish that brand? Because of competition. We used to say there is no competition in indie RPG publishing. This is (and was) nonsense. Of course we compete. The market is absurdly small, and thus has all kinds of fucked up quirks in terms of a market. But, there's competition there.

So, why didn't more people buy Annalise so far? Because in the extremely short amount of time people devoted to assess your game, you confused them. They weren't sure whether it was this or that, and why are they different and so on. And, they moved on to something else to evaluate and, probably buy (and hopefully play!).

It's possible brand split among various titles, of course. But, doing so requires resources we don't have. It costs either time or money to explain to people that Halo 3 is slightly different than Halo 3: Anniversary Edition. (Ok, not a great example; you get the idea.)

Well, we don't have money, obviously. No one's marketing anything by spending any significant money in the indie RPG scene.

What we do have is time. And smarts. One of the best marketing techniques indie RPGs have mastered is viral marketing online and word-of-mouth among bleeding edge fans. People take this for granted. That's easy to do because it's unstoppable. If Gamer A tells Gamer B something factually wrong thing about Annalise, you can't really stop them, can you?

Well, by then it's too late. You should have never had that problem -- you should have better informed Gamer A.

And, you do that by avoiding confusion, particuarly for your new game. Person A gets it. They then become evangelists. That is, without paying them, you turn them into representatives for you.

The more we as publishers complicate that, the more we are likely to suffer.


The money shot: The biggest problem I see among individual creator-owned publishers is that they don't appear to make good choices about what not to do with their games.

We don't make good, hard choices. My opinion is that we do far, far too much in the interest of experimenting and having fun and feeling cool among our known pals, and far, far too little creating and selling games that people actually can 1) find 2) buy and 3) play.

For example, should you simplify the game line, or should you release a multiple art editions that make you and the artists very pleased? Well, if you ask me, you should simplify because the customers fucking matter. You and the artists can have fun later. Do that in 3 years when you sell 1,000 copies.

    \n Matt,

    I think there is are a couple of splits in the community/niche/whatthefuckever between folks who want to publish for success in terms of traditional goals (sales, exposure), those who follow secondary traditional goals as their primary goals (acclaim, name recognition, status), those who are publishing under arthouse goals (artistry, self expression, boundary pushing), those who are publishing for advancement goals (pushing forward the state of the art, trying new publishing methods for novelty), and those who don't exactly know why they're publishing, they're just doing it... well, because.

    Thing is, in an Indie scene, all of those are fine and noble goals. I'm not going to go against anyone who publishes for any of those reasons. (Well, maybe the last one....)

    The problem that I see, that lines up with some of what you're talking about, is that folks don't make hard choices to push forward what they actually want from their publication. I see a lot of muddled ideas; a lot of folks using techniques or methods that would encourage one kind of success that actually inhibit them because they're not suitable to what the actual publishing goals are; and a lot of folks trying to have their cake and eat it too.

    That kind of muddle doesn't help anyone. The first hard choice I think most publishers need to make is to figure out what their goals for publishing actually are. I have a bad feeling a lot of folks are skipping this step. And if you skip that one, its almost impossible to make the rest of the following hard choices on good ground. Its also hard to get good advice from the community, because unless you can explain to them what you want and why then the advice they give you may be completely unsuitable for your goals*.



    *Of course, even if you do explain what and why clearly, folks will still give you bad advice because a lot of the community is muddled too.

    I did the design and you did the marketing
    \nThis is a very interesting point, Eero. That is, I suspect I lack the time to do someone's marketing for them currently.

    However, your suggestion raises an interesting idea, and also highlights one of the key problems for creator-owners.

    The interesting idea is: People arrange partnerships where one creates another markets. Or, possibly, a cooperative where individuals or teams create, and a co-op markets and sells. Very interesting indeed.

    The problem is one I've thought about a lot over the last year. It goes like so:

    The ethic in creator-owned RPG publishing (aka Indie RPGs, Forge games, etc. etc.) is that there is a high value and esteem based on individuals owning and being passionate about what they create. There is strong support for creator's making their own publishing descisions, owning intellectual property, conducting revisions or other material, and so on.

    However, there is nothing in the philosophy about creator owned publishing that says the creator must also be the marketer (among a number of other parts of the process).

    But, in practice, this is exactly what happens. It's a mountain of wasted (and often repeated) effort. Everyone's out there, generally excited as hell about the creative process. Very few are as excited about the marketing process.

    The "one man band" syndrome is probably preventing greater success.

    \n I very much agree, Matt - I've been thinking of things in those terms as well. Specifically, my own experience as artist (which is not limited to just indie rpg publishing) indicates that I (and I suspect others) do not perform optimally as my own marketer. It's simply not psychologically feasible for some reason. Of course this sort of thing seems obvious when we consider how culture industry in general is set up: there is no facet of it in which the artist actually is his own promoter. I wouldn't be surprised if my sentiment were pretty universal, as the sort of attitude you need to create art is not very compatible with the sort you need for marketing it. The marketer needs to have some distance from the work, and he needs to derive his satisfaction from publicity and audience contact instead of the work itself, as the artist does. This way he doesn't just lose interest the minute the work is finished, as happens to me when I work on my own projects.

    I also very much support the idea that you don't have to be a one-man production team to be independent. It's pretty simple to just buy the marketing work, which is in fact not that unusual in publishing industry. Of course it's much more difficult to make this happen at the level of commitment and flair we're used to operating on; at least when you do your own marketing you know that the message is solid, you know you can stand behind every word and you know that the thing will get done to your satisfaction in terms of schedule and quality.

    It'll be interesting to see if we could have a marketing co-op of some sort develop for the express purpose of fulfilling Matt's new, brave vision. It's tricky to some degree - IPR is already structured towards this sort of direction to such a degree that I'd consider any effort that did less somewhat dubious. Might just be an issue of leadership - perhaps IPR fails in this regard because it's too conservative in its marketing initiative, who knows. It might be that I for one wouldn't even consider seriously any outsider marketing that didn't put its money where its mouth is - and if you're going to buy a block of my stuff for the purpose of remarketing it, how are you different from a distributor or retailer, exactly?

    Then again, a good question regarding marketing: can it even be done at a serious level without having it coordinate with the creative stage? Productization seems to be in equal amounts about creating and marketing - how would externalized marketing really work for an artistically independent creator, or would it work at all?

    \n Keith's right. Currently, there is no viable option that I know of that doesn't involve working for existing RPG publishers under ... goofy conditions.
    With maybe one or two exceptions, either no one HAS the resources, or no one is willing to RISK the resources to form a sustainable operation. (And I'm not even saying full time job type operation.)

    Think of it. It would mean that someone has to do quality control. Right away, BAM! Flying right in the face of one of the hallmarks of creator owned publishing. You design what you want.

    Well, if you design a game that too few OTHER people want ... you don't get to sell out after all.

    It's the creator owned paradox. To date, we've let the market, such as it is, decide what games are "worth it." But, one side effect of that is we do a very passive, slow job of saying, "Look, frankly, this game over here isn't even commericially viable in THIS teeny market." Yikes.

    If I had the resources (i.e. the money) I probably would not risk it without some revolutionary structure OR crazy insane inventory of smash hits with repeat sales.

    \n So, just to clarify, we have four case studies in terms of "games with funky edition names." These are (Chronologically in order of publication, alphabetized for tie-breaking):

    Polaris
    Bliss Stage
    Annalise
    Mist Robed Gate

    Polaris has sold, and continues to sell, very well.
    Bliss Stage sold out it's entire initial print run relatively quickly (~250 books in ~4 months, if I recall correctly), and continues to sell PDFs at a steady clip. Moreover, it has recruited more than its fair share of relatively hard core evangelists.
    Annalise sells slowly, according to Nathan.
    Mist Robed Gate sold out its entire initial print run relatively quickly and has not been reprinted due to monetary constraints on the publisher.

    So I'm not seeing strong evidence that, in terms of actual practice, there's any correlation between weak sales and funky edition names. If anything, there's a slight correlation between funky edition names and good sales, but I'd probably put that down to dumb luck rather than any real phenomenon.

      \n Ok. So, you're saying that some people will read it the first way I posed, and some will read it the second. That helps clarify for me, thanks.


      I think that, broadly speaking, people who would conclude "I do not need to do any marketing at all" have a poor understanding of what marketing means. Not doing any marketing at all means literally not connecting with a single customer and making a sale.

      Now, sure, people might be doing not-very-good marketing, and selling to customers. That's ok. Marketing is a resources hog, and requires a lot of time and effort, neither of which a lot of designers have a lot of.


      So, pretty clearly, I'm coming down on that question like so: Everyone must conduct marketing. It's totally necessary to have any sales at all. And, of course, that means that people are indeed marketing, with various tactics, with varying levels of success. Cool.

      Unfortunately, what I think is happening, again broadly speaking, is that we indie publishers aren't doing very good marketing. In fact, I think it's pretty terrible, and I certainly include my efforts over the years in that.

      I find it disappointing that our community would generally view, say, 500 sales in a year or two as a really good success. I view that number of sales, personally, as a failure. I think we lack larger ambition as a cottage industry. And, I think we're generally risk adverse (i.e. we can't afford losing, say, around $1,000).

      That's understandable. I'm pretty risk adverse myself. I don't have the immediate resources to lose $1,000, or especially $5,000 in an effort to quadruple that 500 sales into 2,000 (which I'd start to call a success). So, I'm a pretty noisy critic not willing to do much more than complain.

      Now, it may boil down to something ugly. It may be the case that moving our "success meter" from 500 sales to 2,000 is actually impossible. That is, that such a market for indie RPGs doesn't exist realistically. What's the limit of the market? Who knows. I sure don't. But, I go on the hunch that 2,000 as a general number is reasonable and possible.

      And, I think it means we don't see enough success stories who move the "success meter." Too few people are out there challenging the idea that we should think larger than our current scale. Our current scale is, oh, anywhere from 50 sales to 500 sales is "good." Whereas, I'd much rather see 1,500 to 2,000 sales as "good" and under 500 as small potatoes, even in our niche area.

      What I'm suggesting is that our cottage industry is generally happy with its success levels, while I'm not happy with the success levels. And, I understand why publishers are happy. Of course people should be happy with their own self-defined goals. If your goal says 500 sales is "very good," that's fine. I'm not trying to rain on your parade, publishers, if you're absolutely pleased where you're at today.

      But, I'm eager to know if there are other people who aren't happy with that success metric. People who are really self-analyzing to figure out what they're doing wrong when they can't reach that 2,000.

      I'm one of those people. I think I did a lot of things terribly wrong in creating and marketing my games. I have some ideas about what I think could be done to start confronting this, but I lack the resources to make it happen currently. I will say I'm eager to find like-minded people, because I'm increasingly convinced that practical solutions will require shared resources, team efforts, or even outright partnerships formed.

      \n Finally! This is a topic that I have been harping on privately for a couple of years now. Nice to see someone else bring it up for a change.
      \n
      \nWhen I began working on a revision of The Outsider Chronicles, I was fortunate enough to have Universalis' other creator, Mike Holmes on my Yahoo group. One of the things that he kept asking me is, \u201cWho are you making this for?\u201d
      \n
      \nThat question confused me at first, but the answer finally came to me. I was making the game for myself. It was to satisfy my own ego by showing other game publishers how clever I could be. However, that was not my stated goal. I actually wanted a commercially viable game that would sell well. I hadn't considered what sort of audience I would be selling it to.
      \n
      \nSo, in order to accomplish this, I decided to learn what I could about Marketing. During this time I came to the conclusion that I really didn't know what I was doing when it came to making a product that had a chance of being a commercial success. In fact, most small game publishers don't really have a concept of Marketing.
      \n
      \nFrom my own observations, many publishers that reach for the brass ring figure that if they publish something that they like to play, then everyone will like it, too. There is nothing wrong with this assumption, but it does lead to products that do almost exactly what a lot of other games are already doing. This leads to these publishers going after the same audience over and over again, slicing up the pie into ever thinner pieces.
      \n
      \nUniversalis was my first experience with an Indy game. It was different, it was innovative. I had more fun in that half hour demonstration that I had in two years of D20. I throroughly believe that if it were ever seriously marketed, it would definitely cause a ripple in the gaming industry.
      \n
      \nHowever, as Ralph has pointed out, his day job does leave him living quite comfortably and he has no incentive to do such a thing. Designing and publishing games is a hobby for him and he does it for the enjoyment of it.
      \n
      \nMany of the Indy publishers that I have met seem to fall into this mold. They are doing it for their enjoyment, and the accolades of a small circle of friends and fans.
      \n
      \nI, on the other hand, am working two jobs to keep my bills paid. If I can use what I have learned to draw a lucritive income from my own designs, it would make me one of the happiest men on earth.
