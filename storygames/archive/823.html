[Drill/Kata/Exercise/Spacemonkey] Insignificant Choices, Significant Input (ICSI)

Josh Roby
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
Play goes around the table/circle/what-have-you.

When it's your turn, you declare what you're doing. ("I'm leaping from building to building, chasing the Gutbelcher!") You only get to say what you're doing, not why. Be descriptive and evocative, but don't be expressive.

The person to your left then explains why you did what you just did. ("Ever since the Gutbelcher roasted your parents with his terrible breath, you've been on a quest for vengeance!") They don't get to amend what you do, they only explain why you're doing it. That person then segues into what they're doing. ("And thus I, the Gutbelcher, laugh at your feeble attempts to catch up in my wake of destruction!").

And then the next person explains the significance of that ("The Gutbelcher has always had more talent for destruction than real creativity, and so destroys that which others create...")

Go around and around until you're bored or dizzy or done.

Point being: Separates the choices you make from the significance of the things that you do. Underscores how much you do not control "your" character, for whom you make choices, and actually control the character of the person to the right, since you're narrating all the whys and wherefores that undergird the character.

    Thomas, it seems to me you're conflating the message and the medium (which, if you're on the ball when you create/write/act/sing/whatever, you fuse together so they're complementary and nigh-inseparable). Certainly the what happens is necessary and important. It's the medium that signifies the message -- but it is not the message itself. To get totally semiological, it's the signifier, not the signified.

    I'm not talking about the relative merit or worth of one thing over the other. I'm just drawing a distinction between the two things, trying to display how they work differently even as they work together, so that we have a better understanding of how the unit as a whole works.

    (This is totally me being hair-splitting with language, I feel I ought to point out. I'm not using "significance" as a synonym for "importance." I'm using it to mean "thing being signified".)

    Certainly the what happens is necessary and important. It's the medium that signifies the message -- but it is not the message itself. To get totally semiological, it's the signifier, not the signified.

    No, what happens may be either the signified or the signifier, depending on your reference point. The question of signification (in the semiological sense) doesn't really need to be raised here, though. Going down this track gets us further away from what the exercise actually does.

    Thomas: The whole point of it is that by giving the action a why, a reason, it becomes significant. I think you are right that you need both an action and a why, but that, in game, the significant actions are significant because of an implicit why. In other words, I may not say that "I'm shooting this guy to revenge my family" but the action lets everyone infer that nonetheless.

    The "show don't tell" adage does not demand a story have no why (that would be kind of silly), but encourages you to show the action in a way that makes the why clear without you having to explain it. In other words, to portray the case so well that the why is 'written' in the action itself. It's not a hard and fast rule. Its value is heuristic--most often, if you *can't* show without a lot of telling, you are showing badly, leaving too much undeveloped.

    This, however, is an exercise, and as such it highlights one side of that equation for instructive purposes. Just like doing katas in martial arts--it isn't the katas themselves, which are abstracted from application, but the way in which after the kata you are better able to execute them in a more dynamic, 'real world' scenario.
