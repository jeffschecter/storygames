\n I was pondering this last night, and I started to wonder if anyone has already worked out a good solution (or knows of a game which has).

Assume a basic model of a game where characters get into conflicts with each other. However, either player can choose to risk losing something they value in exchange for a bonus in the conflict.

So far, so good, right? Sounds pretty familiar.

Now imagine you have two characters fighting over something valuable. Luke Skywalker has come to rescue R2D2 from Jabba the Hutt, and the fight begins! Jabba wants to keep R2D2, because he knows R2D2 has the plans for the Death Star

It would be pretty cool for either of them to be able to say, "I care about keeping R2D2 out of my opponent's hands so much, that I'm willing to risk R2D2 being destroyed in this conflict." Right?

Except now we have a problem: if I care more about keeping R2D2 out of Jabba's hands than I do about it surviving, I can say I risk R2D2's destruction, and get a bonus in the conflict... but if R2D2 is destroyed, then I have invalidated Jabba's goal.

Jabba's player would be quite correct in saying that this doesn't seem fair. For example, if the system is set up so that I lose whatever I risked when I defeated in the conflict, then Jabba has literally no chance of winning: either I win and take R2D2 away from him, or I lose and R2D2 is destroyed.

Is there a clever way around this? Some combination of negotiating variables or smart math to make it possible to threaten things you care about, even when your opponent cares about them too?

(It's tempting to just have them both say, "Hey, we care about this", and both get the bonus. Except the problem is that now R2D2's destruction is either certain or much, much more likely, depending on how your system is set up.)

    \n The problem I see is that we have fictional concerns (what we want for R2) clashing with game mechanics (players risk loss for bonus). To me, this is intractable, and you simply need to declare which takes priority, fiction or mechanics.

    If we're committed to playing a game where we always have the option to risk loss for a bonus, then we manhandle the fiction into a configuration that supports that. When you mention that you could simply blast R2 to keep him out of Jabba's hands, then we're obliged to invent a reason why that doesn't happen. ("Blaster-dampening field!")

    On the other hand, if we're committed primarily to seeing how established fiction leads to new fiction, then the dice outcomes are only as binding as the fiction allows, and if Jabba wins the roll, and wins R2, and my response is to shoot R2, then, well, that sucks for Jabba, but that's his loss for not taking precautions. Now he has to decide whether to kill me in revenge. This is a bummer if you thought the die rolls would settle the matter, but it's still interesting fiction. If you parse the dice as simply establishing a new menu of options, then it's no problem at all.

    Can we not play a game where we can decide what the stakes are, dice off, and then let the victor carry away their spoils?
    Of course we can -- that's the first option I mentioned. I just didn't think you'd committed to that, because this:\n
    It would be pretty cool for either of them to be able to say, "I care about keeping R2D2 out of my opponent's hands so much, that I'm willing to risk R2D2 being destroyed in this conflict." Right?
    \nonly strikes me as cool in a fictional sense. I don't see any upside, gameplay-wise. At least, not in the context of "risks for bonuses".

    In my opinion, the only time when it's interesting to destroy something instead of losing it to an enemy is when that's a meaningful choice, and for it to be a meaningful choice, it has to either:
    1) erase some chance you still had to own or profit from the prize despite the opponent winning it, or
    2) cost you something valuable

    This is a frequent occurrence in board games, where you (player 1) put down a railroad track that will (a) make you money and (b) connect places on the board, and now I (player 2) must decide whether to (c) destroy the track to eliminate your profits, or (d) leave the track in place so I can take advantage of the connected board. By playing the railroad track, you are gambling on whether or not I have a bomb to destroy the track, and/or a track of my own to replace it with; or, in a game with public info, you are not gambling on whether I can do this, but whether it will be worth it to me to spend my turn doing that instead of building factories or something else.

    If you were positing some scenario like that for destroying the prize, then I'm sure I'd have some ideas. As is, though, with "risks for bonuses", it strikes me as an ill-fitting non-sequitur. I thought the whole fun of "risks for bonuses" was "how much might you pay to get what you want" -- if so, then the prospect of your payments earning you nothing (not even increased odds) would defeat the premise and ruin the game.

    \n Game idea:

    On your turn, you have three options: Find, Claim, or Enjoy.

    Winning: Game ends after 10 turns. Player who has Enjoyed the most by game's end wins.

    Find: gain access to a new Resource.

    Claim: take hold of a Resource, such that you can Enjoy it on your next turn. You may Claim only (1) a Resource you have found, or (2) a Resource someone else has Claimed (see "When one player Claims another's Claim" below).

    Enjoy: use a Resource that is currently your Claim. You may not Enjoy the same Resource more than once per Claim. (You can Enjoy it, lose it to another's Claim, re-Claim it, and Enjoy it again.)

    When one player Claims another's Claim: the player currently Claiming the Resource has three options: (a) hand the Resource over, (b) destroy the Resource, (c) attempt to maintain their Claim, thus triggering a Conflict.

    Conflicts: when you try to maintain your Claim against another's attempted Claim, bust out cards / dice / etc. and add Risks for bonuses as per Paul's design. The challenger declares number of Risks first; the player already holding the Claim declares Risks second. Whenever one player finishes adding Risks, the other player may then declare that they destroy the Resource. All Risks thus far declared are still in play and must be resolved.

    Note: this game depends on there being 3 or more players.

    I just made this up so I don't know if it works, but the idea is that destroying a Resource is (a) bad for everyone because Finding a brand new Resource is always the slowest method of getting Resources out there, but also (b) worse for some players than others depending on whether it's already been Enjoyed. The standard Conflict would be for a Resource that has been Claimed but not yet Enjoyed.

    This is the best I can do for now at synthesizing "risks for bonuses" with "destroy the prize". I think "choose to destroy it" works better than "choose to introduce a risk that it might be destroyed by luck", but if you disagree, I suspect you can easily sub the latter in for the former.

    \n Cheating and skipping ahead to the part where I post and not reading anything after the OP.

    Take a look at conflict resolution in Annalise, by Nathan Paoletta. It's not exactly what you need, because it is a system designed for one player at a time, but check this out.

    Every conflict has at least two, sometimes more, orthogonal outcomes. For example:

    1. Jabba gets the plans out of R2.
    2. Jabba gets physical possession of R2.
    3. R2 is destroyed.

    They're set up so that one being true doesn't invalidate any of the others ("orthogonal"). For example, R2 could be destroyed, and Jabba could still get the plans somehow (through hackery or by yanking a memory card).

    Then every conflict gets its own randomized outcome. Then the player fights to modify each outcome while the GM(s) fuck with the player. This makes the player figure out quickly which outcome is most important, and conflicts tend to have nasty consequences.

    "Fights to modify each outcome" in Annalise means nudging a d6 value up or down by a point or two, or maybe picking it up and rerolling it and accepting whatever comes. That's hardly the only way to do it, obviously, but there's something powerful in "only small predictable changes, or reroll."

    \n Paul, I went back to your opening post, and I think one of the problems is that you mix an outcome (R2D2 destroyed) with a resolution bonus (i.e. getting +X for risking R2D2). And maybe the solution is to set stakes correctly and play with degrees of success.

    Pretend you're just playing a Fate hack: if you risk R2D2 you get a +2, but then you must obtain a Success with Style (3 or more over your target) for R2D2 not to get destroyed.

    The other player could say "I'm not risking the droid" and get no bonus, or again, put the droid at risk for the same bonus and same consequence.

    The bonus is *not* used to save R2D2, but to try to prevail against the opponent; hence you'd take it to boost your chances but knowing you also must obtain a higher degree of success, at that point, for the "bad thing" not to happen.

    If you get a success with style (3+ over the Target number), you get the droid and it's safe. If you get only a success, you destroy the droid, but Jabba won't have it. Same goes for Jabba; if he's the one getting a success with style, he gets the droid; if he gets only a success, then the droid is destroyed and you won't have it.

    Note that you don't get to declare R2D2 destroyed, especially if you fail. That's an outcome of your partial success. I think the fault in your initial proposition is that the player risking R2D2 would automatically destroy it in case of failure. In case of failure with the dice (or whatever resolution), well, the player doesn't get to decide what happens - because otherwise he gets to invalidate the other player's success.

    Now: can the two players risk *the same thing*? Probably not; it would make the first decision to risk something pointless, because the 2nd player would be able to claim the same bonus, hence balancing the stakes once again.

    If you try to spell it out in a 'move' style: when you risk something precious for you, you gain a proportionate bonus. On a 10+ you get what you want and nothing bad happens to your precious thing; on a 7-9 you still get what you want, but lose/spoil/destroy/etc. what you've put at risk.

    Then of course you must define the bonuses (i.e. +0 for something easily replaceable - "I risk to loose my boots", "Ok, you take +0"; +1 for something important right now - "I risk to loose my rope and grappling hook that I need for climbing the tower and I have no time to get myself a new one", "OK, take +1"; +2 for something very important or perhaps not replaceable: "I risk the sword of my father" "Ok, take +2"). And you must define what happens in a conflict not against an NPC or situation, but against another player. Probably both will roll, and the highest result will prevail (and in the degree of success, determine if the thing at risk is lost or not).

    Does any of this make sense to you?
