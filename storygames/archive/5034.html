I reserve the right to editorialise a little, prioritising threads I like, because I think projects like this benefit from someone doing that.

    In the end, after thinking long and hard about it, unless Graham's got a real winner up his sleeve, I can't see (from my limited thinking on the issue) real benefit in such a production as "a collection of threads, but in a book". But maybe we can coordinate and think more on it, though.

    \n I would certainly consider purchase of such a work, for the same reasons Noble has given: I am new, and I can't be bothered to hunt through 4812 threads to find the paragraphs of Awesome.

    Further, editorial focus would be the primary benefit to me of such a compilation. Forget paring down threads and slapping them into a PDF; I'm talking about *exploding* the thread format, sorting by overarching topics or techniques, and then reassembling the "nuggets" into what amount to advice-chunks or mini-essays by the relevant posters.

    Thus, there is a very real value-add: those who've read and participated in all these great discussions would be better able to bubble out (sort out, deduce, etc) the overarching categories--the headings of section, put into formatting terms. Likewise, such participants will be able to cull out repetition and, in general, choose some of the most succinct and useful mini-essays for each category and subcategory. In fact, that's sort of what I thought this thread was more about, not just a laundry list but also a project plan/organization.

    In my opinion, without such synthesis and organization, the project is worthless--I can read the linked threads above without having to buy a PDF. But with a solid organization, editorial guiding hand(s), and some semblance of reusability (ex: Graham's notion of a "little game ideas" section; or a checklist of DOs and DON'Ts of POD publishing; oracles and setting snippets), I'd buy it for just about any price south of $100.

    \n Another idea which may-or-may-not be worth thinking about:

    If we want this to exist and be, effectively a proper book, why not try cherry picking the best posts in the best threads. The ones which really explain something or clarify something. Most awesome threads here fall into the same three categories as 99% of the internet:

    1) Someone starts a thread by saying something really awesome. A few other people agree with him/her. There are perhaps some clarifications to, and expansions of, the original post, but really it's the first post which contains 90% of the thread's awesome.

    1a) is exactly like the above, but someone else contributes something equally awesome later in the thread. So here you have 2 really great posts and a fuckton of clarifications, agreement, disagreements and whatnot, but again, 90% of the awesome exists in two (or three, but basically 5% of the) posts.

    2) Someone starts a thread asking a question, or asking for advice about something. A bunch of people respond with good and valid advice but someone posts a really spot-on, balls-to-the-wall perfect answer, explanation or bit of advice which just reeks of Awesome and Win. Again, 80%+ of the thread's awesome is in that one post, but that post rarely makes sense outside of the context of the thread in which it appears.

    2a) is exactly like that but with multiple Awesome responses. 80% of the awesome is still in those couple of posts though.

    3) A discussion starts and people make a lot of posts, tossing ideas back and fourth explaining stuff, investigating things and discovering things. Somewhere, a conclusion is reached, and something really Awesome is born, but it's spread over twenty or so posts. There's no single posts you can point to to say "this is where the awesome is in this thread", but the thread is awesome nonetheless.

    How does this relate to G-Dogg's book idea? Well, the obvious thing is to get the book to be in some way more useful than the website. And the way you do that is to distil Teh Awesome down into easier chunks to swallow. So you find those posts which contain the majority of the awesome in those Type 1 and 2 threads, and you get the authors to tidy them up and incorporate anything from the rest of the thread they think is also Teh Awesome. Possibly you to bring in another person or two who contributed to the thread's Awesome to re-write their nuggets so they fit in or just get their permission to be quoted from the thread. You end up with a set of essays which are the Distilled Essence of SG's Awesome. It'll be better presented than a bunch of threads, it'll offer far greater Awesome Per Page than simply reprinting threads in whole.

    That would be a great book. That would be useful to all sorts of people.
