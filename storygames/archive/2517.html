\n Heya,

There is something that just facinates and boggles me at that same time. It's the facination we have with the fantasy genre and roleplaying design. I mean, how many FRPGs have come out? Hundreds? Yet, we still create, buy, and play new ones. At the moment, I have a friend Brian working on one. Judd is working on one, I think. Ron wrote Sorcerer and the Sword. Vincent has his Ars Magica knockoff and Dragon Killer. I am fighting the temptation to do one all the time.

It's not a bad thing at all. I think it's great, personally. But the question I'm interested in is "Why?" Like, why are we so attracted to and re-attracted to the fantasy genre? (No genre arguments, please). It's just spectacular to me that after 30+ years of RPGs, we still want new ones. Why is it that? Why do we (or you) like it so much?

Peace,

    My sixty-second considered opinion is that fantasy is actually incredibly easy -- of all the genres out there, fantasy is the least tied to the real world and has the lowest barrier to entry. Science fiction seems to require some rudimentary science (so you can technobabble about orbits and energy conduits and whatever). Westerns seem to require some rough historical understanding, or at least geography (We're where, again? And that's how far from civilization? How rough is the frontier here?). Superspy stuff makes you feel inferior if you don't know about the present global geopolitical situation. And so on.

    Anyway, Fantasy's easy: I have a sword, that's a monster. I stick the sword in the monster. I'm a hero!

    \n Fantasy externalizes everything and makes everything concrete.

    Rather than having bad people with shady plans, you have orcs ready to invade you.
    Rather than having this post-traumatic stress disorder, you have the ghosts of your foes literally following you around.

    In the real world, money corrupts people by distancing them from the common struggles, by getting you caught up in dreams of grandeur, etc... This is all very abstract and debatable.
    But in the Zotesian Kingdom, there is a possessed scepter. Anyone who takes it will be powerful beyond belief, but will lose their soul (your soul being a concrete currency here) and will slowly transform into a snake.

    So much of the grey area is removed. And when something turns out to be different than you expected, this is often significant - a betrayal, mind control, a corrupting spell, etc - rather than being commonplace and all-too-familiar.

    To echo Joshua's comment, it is easy... because it is concrete.

    \n I'd go beyond what Joe says, and say that fantasy implies a rule that the abstract is concrete.
    \n
    \nTelling a fantasy story, you're not merely using the structures of story to encapsulate a tale. The structures are part of the tale.
    \n
    \nWhat's the difference, really, between saying "This character is the protagonist" and "This boy has a great destiny before him"? To my mind, the one is the in-fiction version of the other. On many important levels, the people in a fantasy story know they're in a story.
    \n
    \nThat's what makes it such a draw for RPGs: You get a softer barrier between the author and the character, the player and his role. Many of the thoughts are shared. Both of you, for instance, think that the young man has a great destiny before him. And, honestly, you think it in very much the same way. That's a huge help.

    \n In addition to abstract concepts becoming concrete (as in the cursed artifact of greed example), I think fantasy is fascinating because we can express abstract actions very concretely, too: characters can make big sweeping moral statements while swinging their swords and whatnot. Fantasy weds the two very nicely, which makes a powerful way to deliver both intellectual and visceral fulfillment at the same time. Also, there are very few stories that actually get worse if you add some blood and death to the mix.

    Also, as mentioned, it's narratively convenient: you don't have to worry about a lot of verisimilitude, typical fantasy societies are pretty loose, which gives the protagonists a lot of room to flex their muscles and do impressive things without bumping into social constraints (this is a big draw of Westerns, too, I think), the characters' day to day lives are naturally simpler, and it's pretty easy for someone who's read a book or two to grasp the basic structure of quest fiction. You've got a pretty easy-to-learn structure for what the characters should be doing and tons of motivation for them to go out and do it. Nice and accessible.


    Overall, I think the biggest thing that keeps me coming back is that the fantasy genre is so good for myth-making: even if the story we're creating in play ends up rather lumpy and poorly-worded and I end up throwing in some awkward scenes along the way, the final thing we've created still has the kernel of something really potent in it (myth). And that myth is really cool by itself, but it's also a big relief to have it there, since I feel more secure knowing that I probably won't sink the whole session if I occasionally drop the ball. I think that's a big draw, subconsciously for a lot of players I've personally encountered: the sense that you won't break anything if you screw up.

    \n In addition to everything said here, I think fantasy is the mainstay for RPGs for the same reason the Superheroes are for American comics. It's what we're used to. This is very true in comics. Kids grow up reading superheroes, love superheroes, and when they get a chance to do a comic naturally do superheroes. This was super true for a long time, but it's beginning to change. More and more people are entering the American comics field that never read superheroes but were instead inspired by European, Japanese, South American or American Small press comics.These people don't see superheroes as the norm. They didn't grow up loving these characters. They never read these books. So they see no reason (or little reason) to do them. Instead they make comics about whatevr the hell they want. I think the same thing is true of RPGs. Most of us are influenced heavily by the games we played early on. Even if they arn't all fantasy most of our games have character sheets, use dice and come in manual form. Not all certainly, but most.

There are people whose first game experience is Breaking the Ice or Contenders or whatever. When they think of what RPGs are these will be the games they think of. And who knows what kind of games they'll go on to make?

As since the setting is dull and like every other fantasy setting, the only thing that is really different is the characters, so those are what people care about. Too interesting a setting may draw attention away from the characters, which in most games are the primary vehicle for the players' creative input.

    I agree with much of what Joe, Jake, and Max say, but without any value judgments. Fantasy, in the sense of swords-and-sorcery or traditional heroic fantasy, is a conservative, concrete milieu that ties into some very old motifs we in the west all learn from fairy tales. And not just the motifs, but the transformation of abstract into concrete as well; concrete thinking was the first kind of thinking, historically, and the first kind we learn as children. We learned to abstract later, first through metaphor, then through less concrete concepts. Immersing ourselves in pseudomedieval European fantasies returns us to our childhood and puts us in touch with a golden age. Westerns and Superheros do, too, but they are much newer and thus not as strong or deep as heroes with swords. Fantasy was old when Robert E. Howard and Tolkien were creating their stories. It was old when Thomas Mallory wrote about a king who had been dead for several centuries. And before fantasy became medieval, it was still an ancient core of our being. The Greek writer Hesiod was the first (that I know of) to talk about a lost age of heroes.

    \n

    we all occasionally want to get back to simpler times, and medieval European fantasy, while not universal, has the broadest appeal in western society.

    \n I decided to do a little webcrawling to find better answers than I could write myself. Most of the answers I found are about reading and writing fantasy fiction but I think they translate fairly well to playing fantasy games.

    Darcy Pattison, author of children's books, says that the appeal of fantasy is
    1. it contains a story (I don't know how this translates to RPGs)
    2. it provokes "a sense of 'wonder,' not just for things you long for, but also the means to provoke desire for something not desired before"
    3. it is a mask for the truth, and "allows a dark topic to be discussed in a way that isn't as depressing or negative."
    \nJeanne Cavellos on sff.net writes in "Beauty, Terror, and Artistic Arrest" that people like fantasy to experience a sense of simultaneous wonder and terror that fantasy delivers -- "that overwhelming sense of mixed wonder and dread we feel in the face of greatness that surpasses our understanding."

    The Locus Guide to SF Awards attempts to define fantasy and describe its appeal: "Fantasy explores imaginary worlds or imaginary premises with no appeal to the reader's understanding of the past or present world; the appeal of fantasy instead lies in psychological, social, and mythological expressions of the human condition."

    Jeff Gardiner on thealienonline.net writes in "A Sense of the Numinous" that the mythological roots of fantasy can stimulate a reader into a numinous understanding of our existence. He draws upon Frazer and Jung and argues that we perceive the world through mythological symbols and ritual, and fantasy uses those devices to reach into our subconscious.

    Finally, the library at Campbell Hall prep school in Hollywood explains to parents and teachers that, "During an often confusing and turbulent period of their lives, the archetypes of fantasy and fairy tales provide answers and reassurance." The article suggests that fantasy appeals more to children than adults and that, mid-way between the two, young adults are attracted to fantasy by
    1. rebelliousness and subversiveness
    2. a need to belong and a hand-made world to belong to
    3. escapism through the empowerment of the fantasy
    4. seeing nobodies (like them?) become heroes
    5. magic as an equalizer
    6. pervading idealism

    Posted By: Neko Ewen....LotR is one heck of a precedent too. It's probably the most widely read piece of genre fiction in the English language. Star Wars was big, but it didn't seem to redefine the genre and spawn imitators the way Tolkien's works did.....
    \nJust to state the obvious for a moment:

    Star Wars is fantasy, in all the senses we've been talking about here: abstract moral issues rendered concrete (oppressive government blowing up planets, moral corruption leading to physical ugliness and/or cyborgization), phallic weapons (that glow and hum!), and familiar fairy-tale tropes (the boy with the great destiny, the conflict between son and father).

    And because of all these elements, fantasy (and horror, as an allied genre or subgenre) engages people on an emotional level. It's not for nothing that I'm still reading my three-year-old stories about princesses, knights, and dragons in a world of executives, precision weapons, and amorphous terrorism. We evolved as tribal primates and we're best at dealing with stories couched in tribal-primate terms.

    Why ? I'd venture a guess that most of the people here were keen readers as children. I certainly was. A good chunk of stories aimed at pre-teens contain elements of magic and fantasy - greek and norse myths, fairy tales, bible stories, King Arthur, Tolkien, even Asterix and Obelix (I so need to reread those books). When I started gaming, that was where I was coming from and where I wanted to go. Then, there was a bit of positive feedback loop going on, play fantasy -> read fantasy -> play fantasy, rinse, repeat.
    \n
    \n
    \nI've played with players who hadn't read much fantasy, yet they still were totally into the fantasy tropes of D&D.; Or at least they didn't read much fantasy as an adult. And I've definitely had at least two players specifically tell me they had never read Lord of the Rings.
    \n
    \nSo I think it's something deeper. Of course probably most players have been exposed to a lot of the children's fantasy. And plenty of movies are grounded in fantasy tropes (including may so-called science fiction movies - Star Wars being a great example). I don't think I've ever gamed with someone who hadn't seen Star Wars.
    \n
    \nSo I think it goes back to the core of the fantasy tropes, especially the clear "good vs. evil." But I think it's also layered with more complex bits.
    \n
    \nAnd for me, the fact that fantasy allows me a greater range of things I can do with the game and not break suspension of disbelief is important. Magic just winds up being lots of fun to use (from spell casters affecting combat, to magical explanations for geography, architecture, and situation). In fact, Star Wars is useful to consider in this light. We don't question what the force can do (because the fantasy tropes make us accept the force as "magic"). We don't question the space ships maneuvering in space like they were in atmosphere. And episodes I-III showed us that delving into the logic behind a fantasy element often breaks suspension of disbelief (mitocholorians? What BS!). Those explanations often killed the appeal of SF games (consider all the complaints about the size of computers in Traveller).
    \n
    \nSF of course has a particular problem that often a few years of technological advance and scientific discovery blows a big hole in some very visible part of the game setting (oops, computers can be worn on the wrist, oops, cloning isn't quite so simple as we thought, etc.).
