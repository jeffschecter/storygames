Every so often, a sword is forged with nobility folded into its blade. It fights like a king. It's brighter than a stream of flowing water. It is passed down for generations, it becomes battle-hardened and wise.

Sometimes one of these weapons dies in battle, but death is not the end.

Sometimes, it does not want to quit the battlefield, and then it becomes (??? word needed here; meaning 'ghost of a sword'). Then, the fmily joins an elite order of warriors who use the unearthly magic of their ghost swords to keep peace in the world.

That's a game concept I'm putting together, and I need some terms, particularly the one for the sword ghost. It's sort of a Bleach/Jedi mashup.

    Some more thoughts...just sort of disorganised design journal stuff.

    The reiken tends to absorb and exaggerate the traits of its master. It makes them aggressive and imbues them with spiritual force. These traits linger after the master is gone; a player in this game may play a series of characters who keep inheriting a reiken from each other, as the earlier characters die in battle or retire.

    Reikendoka are instantly recognizeable as such. I think they might wear distinctive white kimono - white for funeral garb.

    Similarly, the restless dead (need a word here) and the demons of peoples' hearts (another word) are also instantly recognizeable. I have this idea that, with reikendo, one can actually enter a spiritual landscape inside a person and confront that person as a small population of people and spirits in conflict with one another. Maybe heart demons are like Final Fantasy monsters, sort of ornate and brightly painted like Southeast Asian statues come to life, all covered in ornamental appendages and extremities in unlikely places.

    It's probably possible for a heart demon to possess a person completely - this is the state that describes extreme eating disorders, alcoholism, debilitating physical conditions, and so on. I don't say 'causes' because that's not the case. Heart demon is not the best term; I mean it to be, like, a cancerous organ of the human spirit. Hm, so, I guess the restless dead are spirits who have succumbed to the cancer near-completely.

    For correct pacing there needs to be a system reason that low-conflict "regular life" interludes occur. For other pacing reasons, there needs to be some conflict arc structure that encourages players to leave story arcs unresolved, monitors unresolved arcs, and thrusts them back into the forefront at irregular intervals.

    First system suggestion: Polaris? A Mistaken-like role can mold how the protagonist and the sword shape each other, and you can alter the Zeal/Weariness scale to provide some pacing perhaps.

    Second suggestion: cut of chunks of DitV/tPK to fit. If you like the kind of exchanges that the system produces (bringing in traits through the conflict, making asserts back and forth until on side can't afford to persist), then it's worthwhile to try it out. Here's the relevant pieces of the system:

    - Stats: DitV uses different stats (base dice for a conflict) to encourage escalation between different arenas of conflict; tPK ignores this entirely.

    - Gear: Dangerous weapons (guns in DitV, swords in tPK) have an extra d4 attached, implying that their use necessarily complicates a situation.

    - Relationships: You can rate a relationship like any trait (XdY), and these only come up in situations where the relationship is in play or threatened. (It's not a freeform trait you can just bring in.) What's interesting is the way Sorcery in DitV works: you take relationships to a demon. When you actually call on demonic power (say the relationship is 5d4), your roll Xd10 (so 5d10) for what you're doing.

    - Traits: Rated XdY, brought into conflicts, earned through fallout in conflicts (and normal game progress). What might be interesting is giving traits to the sword, not just the player, and make moving some traits from the player onto the sword part of fallout - in this way, the sword can accrete more personality.

    So, here are some thoughts on a situation generator:

    The first step is, "a relationship goes wrong." This proceeds through several stages, with "somebody dies" in there somewhere, and ends in "the deceased becomes a restless ghost," and "the ghost goes mad and starts being destructive".

    When a swordsman accepts a commission, he recieves a trait (let's call it the promise) that is attached to that commission. Each time he uses it in a conflict, it gets bigger. He can even use it in conflicts related to other commissions.

    The descriptive effect of using this promise is that we learn something about the origins of that relationship's breakdown, and if it crosses into another commission, it links their causes together. So, like, maybe the shut-in who's trapped in his house by the ghost of an overprotective brother and the soccer player who is supernaturally unsuccessful in love can both trace their terrible destinies back to one day, when a post-semifinals party went slightly wrong...

    And following these breakdowns eventually leads to demons of the human heart, who are sort of like 'level bosses' in a video game sense - they are scaled based on the amount of commissions and promises that led up to them, and they appear only periodically, as a special and ricky challenge. Once a group of commissions leads to a demon and that demon is dealt with, those commissions are completed and the promise is removed from the character sheet, or maybe it turns into some other thing.
