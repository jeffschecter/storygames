TonyLB
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Game Design Help
It's a hell of a question, isn't it? :-)

I'm working on a mechanic for Misery Bubblegum where people get rid of their accumulated Angst by shifting from a mode where they can assert themselves in arenas they defined for their character, into a mode where they can only assert themselves by agreeing with what other players have said about their character. So you go from saying "I'm smart, god-dammit!" to saying "My friend Lisa says I've got a good heart, and that counts for something!" You become intertwined with other people's opinions of you, for better or worse.

So I went looking through the source material, trying to figure out "Hey, how does this Angst thing come about anyway?" And my conclusion is that the one thing I see happening most times Angst is generated is that somebody rejects your opinion of them. They say, in essence, "This thing you think I am? I'm not that thing."

Sometimes it's true ... they really aren't that thing. Sometimes they're lying ... they totally are that thing, but they're telling you that they aren't. Doesn't matter.

That exchange undermines your sense of who the other person is. And your role is defined in connection with other people (i.e. you aren't merely "A nice guy," you are part of a pair with (say) Malcolm ... you're The Nice Guy and he's The Cool Jerk). So when someone undermines your sense of who they are, they also undermine your sense of who you are. Hence the Angst.

But my question ... my question here is ... why would anyone ever do that? I mean, I get that if you hate the person and want to cause them angst then defying the image they create for you is a clearly good strategy. But why would you deliberately defy the expectations of a person you care about? Why do we hurt the ones who love us?

    Tony,

    I suspect that the problem here is that identity (as in "I'm a good person" or "She's mean") and expectations ("he'd make a good husband for someone, if he wasn't so self-obsessed") aren't usually the causes of behaviour. They are the explanations and theories that we create after the fact.

    For example, a couple of young kids (let's call them Billy and Fred) are eating from a bowl sweets, and Billy takes the bowl and won't let Fred have any more sweets. Fred tries to take the bowl back and Billy smacks him one. So, why did Billy do that?

    Because he's greedy? Because he hates Fred? Because he's hungry and selfish? Out of revenge for something Fred did yesterday?

    The truth is, we don't know, and Billy won't tell us. So we come up with our own explanation, and act accordingly (let's say, we punish Billy.) Now, it doesn't matter if Billy tells us why he did what we did, because we already know and to think otherwise would be to admit that we had made a mistake.

    So, let's relate this back to the original question. Why would I reject the expectations put upon me by a person I care about? Because that other person is wrong. Even if the expectation is flattering ("he's so clever, he'll be a doctor one day") it has consequences, it places demands upon me, that I may not be willing or able to meet. And I may not be able to tell the other person that they are wrong, because they'll feel stupid, they may even feel that I have betrayed them somehow.

    So, I sit and I seethe and eventually I blow up because they bought me a stethoscope for my birthday and I don't want to be a fucking doctor and I cannot stay silent any longer. Foolish, but it happens.

    Is this what you're talking about?

    Why do we hurt the ones we love?

    - Redirection: Some people feel negatively towards strangers or people it would be dangerous to lash out at (like a boss or co-workers). When they don't disperse or transform these negative feelings, they will often redirect them towards people that are safe (unlike their boss), like a loved one.

    - Familiarity: We often have intimate knowledge of loved ones. The more we know about them, the easier it is to strike at their sensitive areas. Intentionally or not.

    - Comfort: The more love someone has, the more trusting, open, and honest they can be towards a loved one. Truth can hurt.

    - Convenience: People tend to be around their loved ones more than anyone else.

    - Safety: Sometimes a loved one will let the aggressor get away with hurting them. This diminishes the possibility of undesired consequences.

    - Attention: Some people may use negativity as a way to connect to a loved one. If the connection grows weak, even a negative connection is better than no connection at all.

    Snap answer: reward them both. That way neither of them have an incentive to break it up.

    More considered answer: breaking up is fun too! (In a story sense.) So there has to be some variability, that encourages the build up and then the eventual explosion of the situation. I think we've been here before though.

    Another approach: if we're trying to model a real-world situation for our entertainment, let's look a bit deeper.

    Initially, the hassle from correcting someone's incorrect impression is worse than the hassle from playing along with it. So I go along with it.
    Later, the hassle from playing along is higher, but so is the hassle from correcting it - because I am complicit through my earlier silence. I choose misery and feeling like I've done the right thing (by not hurting someone else's feelings) instead of conflict (by tackling the problem)
    Eventually, I've had more than I can take (probably becasue other things in my life are also making me feel miserable) and taking on more misery from "sucking it up" is more than I can take. I have no other option than to start a fight, becasue I can no longer remain silent (this is of course a lie, but it feels true at the time).

    What does this tell me?

    1) If the misunderstanding is "triggered", I gain Angst.
    2) The amount of Angst I gain increases over time.
    3) Angst from different sources is cumulative.
    4) I can only take so much Angst before I blow up. I cannot choose when I will blow up.
    5) If I don't blow up, my Angst will drop slowly. (Either something nice happens, or I've made it through the day and get some sleep.)
    6) If I do blow up, I will lose Angst, and those around me will gain it.
    7) If I confront the problem before I blow up, I will gain Angst, as will the people around me. This will be greater than the amount of Angst I gain from letting it go, at any given time.

    What's interesting about this is that sorting the problem out early isn't necessarily the best thing to do. If I'm close to the edge already, it may be easier to take a small Angst burden from letting something ride, than a larger one for dealing with the problem all grown-up like. As long as you keep pushing peoples buttons, they will blow up eventually, regardless of whether they try to resolve their problems or not.

    Well, I would hope that the strategy of the game, in fact, leads you to those gut-wrenching decisions.

    As Malcolm, you want to achieve two goals with your opinions:

    1. You want to change Jessica into the type of person who will support your image of yourself in a relationship with her
    2. You want to prevent her from dumping Angst on you by rejecting your opinion of her.

    Whereas, if you run a middle course like "You're beautiful" then you bind both goals together in a nice little package. You present the image that you want Jessica to fulfill in terms that are aimed to make it attractive to her.
