Arkfall is post-apocalyptic science fantasy World of Dungeons/Mutants featuring Planarch Codex heritage moves.

The World Ark was a massive starship that harvested beings and shards of reality from countless worlds. Then it crashed and all that was strewn and mingled across this vast new planet. Since then, the inhabitants have bred and generations have passed. People have rebuilt and old worlds and cultures combine, clash and evolve. Everyone (including the PCs) has weird mixed heritages. Nanites are used as currency and levels of self-augmentation are one thing they can be spent on.

Some features:
- uses heritage moves to make bizarre PC races
- dead magic world, but technology emulates magic
- levelling up is not mandatory; instead of XP and coins, nanites are currency your characters can spend on stuff, including levels of self-augmentation
- includes a list of chems and their effects
- lore skill reskinned as 'culture' to emphasise that anything worth knowing on Arkfall has cultural significance to someone
- includes a few starting scenarios, quests and jobs
- includes an example move for PC flashbacks

If you read or play Arkfall or make something for it, I'd love to hear your feedback (also if you spot any typos, inconsistencies or things that are unclear). Also, it's Creative Commons (CC-BY) so if you see something you like - or don't - go nuts, use it, change it, whatever

Huge thanks to John Harper, Jonathan Walton and J\xfcrgen Mayer for allowing me to use parts of their awesome, inspiring creations.

EDIT: I've developed a few additional resources.
- Quick & Dirty Heritage Generator (mirror) to address what can be the longest part of character generation

    Some background on how this game came to life:

    I've been reading Brandon Sanderson's Way of Kings and in my home game the place we're playing in looks like the Shattered Plains, barren but with plants hiding under the dust, which is kicked up and cleared by the nanitestorms that wrack the lands.

    There are plenty of chunks of the ship that have made deep furrows and burns in the ground, and now people have made those chunks (and even the furrows) into settlements. There are world shards infusing the land, so there's an area the size of a pond, but it goes deep as an ocean and there's an ocean's variety in it too. Stuff like that, with the world shards allowing for things like a frozen city block just a few blocks down from a huge mudhive made by sentient bug people.

    And would you believe, I didn't intend to make this game?

    I had been struggling to find a one-shot that would interest my group, not be too hard to learn and be played in less than 3 hours. Then, I realised, World of Dungeons was perfect. And I could use it as a one-shot and/or extend it into a full campaign.

    I also had a campaign starter in mind, which I'm pretty sure I originally read on Jeff's Gameblog but couldn't find again. It inspired the First Generation Survivors scenario. Basically, waking up in tubes, one is open, wander out into a strange new land.

    Then the Planarch Codex heritage moves meshed perfectly with that. And my players dug into the DW monster list with glee! Having monstrous/strange heritages is a really great hook and they're playing it up even with their characters having amnesia.

    So, yeah, I cooked up this idea a few hours before my game session, adding all those inspirations together, using the Planarch Codex version of the World of Dungeons character sheet and we had a blast!

    After the session I was keen to make the PDF and after I started I discovered your World of Mutants, which helped incredibly!

    I hope people get some use out of Arkfall. I'll be using it with my home group, so I'll post any developments that surface.

    With the heritage moves, I basically just added them straight in, but they fit really well and I'm sure we'll develop new moves around them as we go.

    Already in the first session I gave 1 heritage hold as the "extra benefit" of a 12+ roll when the PC was using a heritage move in combat (so basically, the heritage move was free that time).

    I also love that WoD can be classless. So quick and easy to hack together a new special ability (or a loose group of them), than a new DW playbook. Both have their perks of course, and they're two of my favourite games, but for speed, WoD hacking is fantastic

    \n Glad you like it, Mike.

    I think having the DW book on hand is at least +1 to persuase players, because they can start flipping through and see what monster moves they'd like to have.

    I pitched the heritage moves to my group like this: "Think of some monsters you like that can do cool things you'd like your character to be able to do. Or flip through this book till you see something interesting". Then went on to explain further. But straight away everyone had ideas.

    I had a player think of Frankenstein's monster and he ended up with some flesh golem moves and coupled that with derro psychic powers. Pretty cool.

    \n I'm glad everyone's getting a kick out of Arkfall. Here are some creatures I drafted up for my upcoming Arkfall session:

    MECHSPEDITIONARY ROVER | HP 8 | attack d6 | raise the alarm; forge relentlessly ahead; fearlessly protect the masters
    MECHSPEDITIONARY SCOUT | HP 12 | attack d6+1 | detect impending danger; vanish into the surrounds; strike from concealment; call reinforcements
    MECHSPEDITIONARY HUNTER-SCHOLAR | HP 16 | attack d6+2 | unleash a plasmusket barrage; spring a trap; route the target; use scholarly data to sudden advantage

    The Mechspeditionary is a faction of intrepid robotic explorers. Originally built as simple scouting probes, later iterations included weapons, and soon, the robots emulated and outgrew their creators, but not their mission. Hunter-Scholar units, now sentient, lead the Mechspeditionary and approach all things in the light of their motto and programming: "Inquire. Explore. Master."

    FACELESS | HP 16 | attack d6 | craft a face from the dead; craft a face from the world; use the powers of a face; retreat to change faces

    There is a culture where identity and individuality are everything, and criminals who commit the worst possible crimes - defamation, masquerade and disfigurement - are given the highest punishment: they are rendered faceless. Faceless usually wander into the wastes and make a face of dust, becoming the dust itself. Some, however, retain enough control to envy the faces of others and seek to steal them. Faceless gain the powers of the dead or the environments from which they craft disturbing mask-like faces that imitate but do not capture the true essence of their source. Yet, the Faceless always need more faces...

    GNOLL TRACKER | HP 6 | attack d6 | doggedly track prey; strike at a moment of weakness
    GNOLL BLOODCLAW | HP 8 | attack d6+2 | attack with blades and guns; launch into a primal fury with claw and fang; aid a packmate in the hunt; feed on a kill and grow strong
    GNOLL RELAY | HP 10 | attack d6+1 | pass on an omega's influence; augment the pack with an omega's power
    GNOLL ALPHA| HP 12 | attack d6+2 | make a show of dominance; send the pack to hunt; bark commands to spur on the pack

    Gnolls are dangerous in a pack, deadly when running with an alpha, and terrifying with a relay in their midst. Gnoll relays have the fur shaved from their heads which bristle with sparking wires, connecting them directly to their dark masters, the distant and mysterious omegas. Alphas may lead the pack in the hunt, but omegas - through their relays - truly run things. Relays are just that, a relay for an omega's strange commands and stranger powers, able to share these with the pack. Gnoll packs following an omega's commands are unpredictable, but when augmented by a relay channelling an omega's powers they are unpredictable and a true terror to behold.

    Here's some art I found that I'll be using for the gnolls: http://www.dakkadakka.com/dakkaforum/posts/list/555905.page
