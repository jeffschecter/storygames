\n
\n

\n

\n
\n So let's say you could buy a fun little roleplaying game, but in order to play you'd need to do a little arts and crafts work - photocopying, cutting out cards, putting them in sleeves or gluing them to cardstock, laminating, whatever. The game cannot be played until you do this stuff. A nontrivial amount of effort is involved, but it is one-time effort.

What is your reaction to this, both as a player and a consumer?

Doe this directly impact your perception of the game's quality, utility or fun?

Would it impact your purchasing decision? If so, how?

If the same game was available in a "finished" version for 100 Abstract Currency Units, how many Abstract Currency Units should the DIY version cost?

Just curious...thanks for your thoughts.

    \n I don't mind some amount of DIY - it has more to do with the annoyance factor potentially involved in printing.

    For instance, I prepped Last Train out of Warsaw. When I printed the cards double-sided, the fronts and backs did not line up perfectly - they were off by about 1/8 inch. It wasn't a big deal; I cut them out based on the front. Someone might be able to game the system by noticing which backs line up with which fronts, but I'm probably not going to play the game with the same group of people often enough for that to be a real problem.

    On the other hand, I tried to prep the little booklets for Apocalypse World, and discovered I had a choice between having all the information print and having the layout work. In the end it wound up being simpler and less time-consuming to re-impose the player booklets from the player chapter, print them as 4-page wonders, and use them instead of the mini-booklets.

    I don't mind assembling, but I have to be able to complete the process without too much frustration.

    So my suggestion, which you're free to take for whatever you think it's worth - cards should be laid out with ample whitespace to compensate for printing inconsistencies, and preferably sized and laid out to make printing easy. I've had excellent luck printing cards based on Avery business card templates, for instance; I've considered re-laying out the Monts\xe9gur cards to suit Avery business cards and postcard templates as a favor to story gamers in general, just to reduce the hassle factor of starting to play.

    Also, in my notebook I found a note that says that someone at GPNW recommended superiorpod.com as a source for POD cards for games. I can't vouch for them any more than that, but if they can produce cards to your specifications for a reasonable price, you might be able to sidestep the whole issue.

      \n When it comes to pricing, it's less about percentages and more about absolutes. If you offer a DIY verson for $1 and a pre-made version for $10, I'm going to buy the pre-made version. If the DIY version is $10 and the pre-made version is $100, I'm going to look at the DIY version. In fact, I will always look at the pre-made version first. If it's affordable, bingo. If not, then and only then, will I be looking at the DIY version.

      As for level of difficulty on the DIY, everyone is going to have a different threshold. For me, if you're asking me to print and cut a handful of cards, I'm on board. If you want me to print a ton of cards, cut them out, and do some mounting, eh, I'm on the fence now. If there's more complicated stuff like lamination, glue, hordes of different components, etc. -- well, I'll play someone else's set.

      EDIT: This specifically does not include extremely simple DIY stuff like printing character sheets, etc. That stuff is just standard and takes no real effort on my part. For example, I can't see myself ever paying extra for a bunch of pre-printed player aids that I could run off myself (unless they are really exceptional).
