Deathmatch: Literary Conflict vs Conflict of Interest

Judson_Lester
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
And these two separate concepts? Or is one a proper superset of the other?

As has been raised in two other discussions now, literary conflicts consist of a character, who has a desire, and an obstacle that prevents him from achieving his desire. A conflict of interest is where two characters have goals they're trying to achieve, and they're standing in each other's way.

If I say that the goals are the desires, and the characters are each other's obstacles, don't conflicts of interest simply fall into the concept of literary conflict? As a side effect, I no longer have to worry about Characters being Furniture and vice versa, because that falls into literary conflict without such acrobatics.

Any issue with that analysis?

    I think the issue is only really relevant when combined with the RPG specific question of what do the dice resolve? Joe is trying to catch Bob. That's the conflict of interest. Now in the fiction there might be a fence between Joe and Bob which would be an obstacle to what Joe wants. But in a conflict resolution system Joe shouldn't roll against the fence, he should roll against Bob with maybe a modifer because of the fence complication. If Joe rolls against the fence it doesn't give us enough info to decide whether or not Joe catches Bob which is what we're interested in deciding.

    That's my only objection to using the term "obstacle." There can be many many obstacles within a conflict of interest. Some may be thwarted, some may cause set backs but dealing with each obstacle individually doesn't resolve the conflict as a whole. A conflict to me is about when a character has a desire *and something is deliberately standing in his way.* Not mearly passively being a problem or complication.

    They're three separate concepts, Judson.

    1) Conflicts of Interest between the players
    2) Conflicts of Interest between the characters
    3) Literary Conflicts (Character+Desire+Obstacle)

    You can do a three-way venn diagram, since they all overlap and they all have elements that are completely distinct.

    Judson, I think it comes down to definition.

    Conflict of Interest: two characters want (desire) mutually exclusive things.
    Literary Conflict: character + desire + obstacle.

    When you equate the lit-crit obstacle with another character, yes, those two things become the same.

    It's the equation of obstacle with character that is, I think, not always true. I think it's often true, and I think in games, that equation is almost always the optimal solution for increasing fun at the table. However, sometimes an obstacle isn't a character. If you're writing some existential novel about the plight of the modern man against the tide of the uncaring world, the uncaring world probably isn't a character. You can put a human face on the uncaring world -- the protagonist's boss, say -- but in doing so you're humanizing the obstacle and that may not be what you want to do for the purposes of your novel. Sometimes life just sucks for the character, and he doesn't get a human face to put on his opposition, which only makes it suck harder.

    Now, I'm not sure The Mezzanine, The Crying of Lot 49, or Waiting for Godot are really models that we want to base games off of, but there are examples where Conflicts of Interest and Literary Conflicts do not overlap completely.

    I am hard-pressed to think of an example appropriate for a game, however.

    I was specifically saying that conflicts of interest can always be expressed as literary conflicts, but not necessarily vice versa.

    That being established, I think there might be useful material to be mined from the idea of building a conflict resolution system around literary conflicts, which also imply a greater scope, rather than conflicts of interest. Benefits that I see:
    • longer framing, and natural build up in conflicts
    • play to the "the conflict every scene thing is rough" crowd
    • a complete rout of the "Furniture, or Character?" problem
    • a resolution to part of the tripartite (or even tetrapartite) "conflict" semantic issue
    Notice that I don't actually provide an idea for a mechanic here. It's quite possible that "literary conflict" doesn't necessary demand a CR mechanic - but it might suggest a situation mechanic instead. Or both.
