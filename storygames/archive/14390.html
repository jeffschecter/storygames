    Storytelling in Games: Part 1 - The Past and PresentBook sales are in decline; cinema queues are thinning; the art of the tweet is on the rise. Video games, like many emerging forms of digital entertainment, are the latest indicator that the way we consume media is changing. We want different things from our entertainment these days. Humanity is starting to move away from passive forms of recreation \u2013 instead opting to 'lean-in', participate and shape their experience.
    \nConsole Video Game sales are also in decline.

    While mobile games (which are less graphic rich, have less cut scenes, and don't strive to be interactive movies) are on the rise.

    I don't think we are seeing people move away from passive entertainment in favor of interactive entertainment (although functional literacy rates are in decline). We're seeing an overabundance of entertainment options as people generally have less and less time. We're working more, have less recreation time but more recreation options. Almost everything is going to be in decline relative to when we had fewer options to entertain us\u2026 except for maybe American Idol (close to 40 million US viewers, 13% of US TV viewers) and the Super Bowl (100+ million US viewers, 33% of US TV viewers).

    Additionally, people are consuming entertainment in ways people can't track or don't add sales. One of the reasons scifi shows do so poorly is because so many scifi fans don\u2019t want to consume entertainment the old way. So they do so in whatever way is most convenient for them, which often means the show does poorly financially. Let me stress, this isn't fans' faults. But technology and social norms are outpacing the existing business models. The same thing is happening with console video games. Their sales may be in decline, but more people are playing than ever, but they are playing used games whose sales aren't being accurately tracked.

    Different people want different things, including different types of stories, with different levels of interactivity and control. If you try to please everyone, you please no one. Instead of looking for the best way to tell an interactive story, I think the answer is finding an audience who is big enough to support you financially and wants a specific experience\u2026 and give it to them rather than telling them what you think they should want. Assuming money is a goal (which I think it is for the people being interviewed). If money isn't a goal, then that's a different and more personal conversation.

    \n Most of the people they interviewed make big-budget console games - story in those games is pretty formulaic, and a lot like how I've heard John Woo approached Mission Impossible 2 : "I've got a list of cool action sequences I want to do, how can we string them together?" Bioware and 2K touch on doing something beyond that, but still fairly disappointing - the 'bang' in these games is "do I be good or evil?", you make the decision once as you start (or you're going to get reamed game-wise), and then you get to see one of two endings. But at least there are some choices and some interesting experiments. (When you're attacked by Big Daddys in Bioshock 2 is procedural - an attempt at a 'cinematic moment' generator.)

There are guys out there that think that you can get a whole new kind of story experience out of computer games and we just haven't figured out how yet.

    \n I've never had anyone tell me a story about an awesome thing that happened when they were playing BioShock or whatever. But people are always telling me about the stuff that happens in their Minecraft games, and what's more, I like hearing those stories! It's a different kind of storytelling, for sure, but I think it's where video games are strongest.

    Stories like "I was just tooling around in my castle, and then I look up and there's a fucking creeper just standing in my living room. I must have left the door open! I back away but of course it's too late, and it just owns all my shit. I get blown down into my basement, and now there's this hole in my front wall, and mobs are just pouring in, and I'm madly trying to save all my stuff that's strewn everywhere, patching up the wall, and then there's another freaking creeper in my face, all like "HI"."

    Or like: "I'd just found this incredible vein of diamonds: Six of them, one after the other. And I'm mining the last one and I guess I got the diamond fever or something, because it's right under my feet, and the next thing you know, I'm falling into the darkness. And then I'm down in the black, three torches left, no idea which way to go to get home, and the sound of zombies all around me."

    That's the kind of storytelling I want more of. Dwarf Fortress players probably have similar stories.

    Telltale Games - Dave Grossman, Senior Designer:When you give the player agency over some aspect of the experience, you cede some of your authorial dictatorship over the story. You may not be able to count on, for instance, pacing, or the exact sequence of events. This can be daunting for someone who is used to the kind of one-way storytelling in other media, where precise use of those elements is an essential part of the craft. However, as anyone who has ever participated in a well-run session of Dungeons and Dragons can tell you, that's not the only way to tell a story. It changes from telling a story to the audience to telling a story with the audience - which can be a good and powerful thing.

    \n Stories are like personalities - they're a framework our brains construct around a series of events in order to make sense of them.

    When a series of events happen in a game, we construct a story about them. When a character acts in predictable and unpredictable ways, we imagine a personality that makes sense of it. The mistake that these designers make is to think that stories and characters are something you make before the game starts, and then communicate to the player. Stories and characters are things that the player discovers in play.

    In terms of technological capabilities, games right now absolutely have the capacity to achieve this. Probably not with fancy voice-acting and graphics, but I don't know that those neccesarily drive sales. Maybe there's market research on that, but I haven't seen it.

    Procedural techniques, such as the cellular automation that drives Minecraft, are fantastic at creating a world that reacts to the player in ways that are predictable in the small scale, but unpredictable on a large scale. The same techniques can be applied to characters in games.

    "Scribblenauts" is able to have 10,000+ unique objects in the game, each of which act in the way you'd expect them to, but also in surprising ways. A T-Rex chases and eats a sheep. A sheep goes and eats a flower. They achieved this without having to code unique behaviours and interactions for each object by using a "tagging" system. A T-Rex has "hungry" and "carnivore" and "huge" and "scaly" and "made of meat". A sheep has "wooly" and "white" and "herbivore" and "hungry" and "made of meat". The engine of the game looks at tags, and works out proper behaviours. It sees that the T-Rex is a hungry carnivore, and it is bigger than the sheep, which is made of meat. It makes the T-Rex run its "chase and eat" script, which it shares with all other "living" objects. Complex behaviours and interactions, not predicted or scripted by the designers, with simple technology. You can absolutely do the same thing with characters or other objects in a computer game, right now.
