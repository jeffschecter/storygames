\n Why is roleplaying a hobby and not an activity?

A big part of it is surely that many (although not all, I'm aware of and love many of the exceptions) have a large social footprint, by which I mean, they require a large amount of effort or investment on a number of axes which, in combination, make roleplaying games socially inflexible, compared to video games, board games, card games and so on.

The axes of the social footprint are many. A non-complete list is:

* Money. Someone has to buy the game. A more pronoucned difficulty for games with lots of supplements and high individual prices.
* Rules knowledge. Some games require participants to learn a lot of rules befgore they can effectively participate.
* Play culture knowledge. Outside of the textual rules, there's often murky stuff about how the game is approached that is part of the culture for any given roleplayer, and that simply has to be learned or adjusted to through experience.
* Time per session. Most roleplaying games require more than an hour, and often several hours, to have even just a session of play.
* Time management, longer term - many roleplaying games work best or actively require several sessions of play, which need scheduling/organising.
* Continuity of group - a roleplaying game is often difficult to adjust to the people present: more players, fewer players and different players all present challenges to an ongoing game.
* Set-up time - whether it's character generation, scenario generation, GM prep...
* Requirements of the space - nearly always, you need a table and chairs. Why not a roleplaying game you can play without a table (I once heard a report of Nicotine Girls played like this) or while walking to the cinema (Vincent had a game like this once).
* Equipment requirements - dice etcetera.

What I'd love to see is more games that explicitly went out to attack all of this stuff - to make an RPG with the smallest social footprint possible.

    \n Well, people's social time is valuable. I can't blame anyone for lacking discipline or commitment when they bail on something that's not working for them. The whole "fifteen minutes of fun packed into four hours" thing means that role-playing often demands a LOT of commitment.

    The other thing is that there probably are some aspects of roleplaying that can only be fully enjoyed by making some kinds of commitments, in the same way that one can only achieve a full enjoyment of say, salsa dancing, by putting in a fair amount of effort to learn steps and get in shape. But you also need that accessible entry point - beginner's classes that are fun right away in half an hour for the salsa, and games that are fun right away in half an hour for roleplaying. Otherwise, many people who could have been convinced to make the commitment will never get any inkling of why it could be good to make it.

    I'm also convinced that a lot of the commitment involved in roleplaying is actually unnecessary, and one can achieve experiences that are just as good while stripping out large chunks of the footprint. Some of it simply comes down to game design and social mechanics that are inefficient.

    \n Graham: while you're totally right and there are many awesome games out there that have attacked one or more of the axes I have outlined above, I don't want this to become just another SG thread in which people list loads of games which are good. I mean, you can and should do that too, especially if you can discuss specific examples of games and the innovative ways they have approached the problem. But mostly I want to talk about approaches to resolving the problem, from many perspectives:

    * not just as a game consumer (which games should I buy if I want small social footprints?)
    * but also as a game designer (how can a game be designed to have a small social footprint?)
    * and also as a games player (whatever the rules text, what things can I do in my social interactions with others to reduce the social footprint of my gaming?)

    So, you know, let's talk about all three of those questions.

    Posted By: Adam DrayWhile I am pretty sure there is a huge sucking hole in the market for low-footprint role-playing games, I am also pretty sure that it doesn't negate the market for RPGs with HUGE social footprints.
    \nYes and no. In the broader sense that all possible activities exist in competition for one's leisure time, leisure effort and leisure money, then small footprint RPGs and large footprint RPGs are in competition, just as they are also in competition with fishing, ten-pin bowling, watching TV, reading a book, going to bars etcetera.

    However in the narrower sense that some people are already willing and wanting to spend the investment required for a large footprint game and indeed prefer their games that way, and other people would much rather play games with smaller footprints and are unwilling or only grudgingly invest in large footprint ones, they are separate markets.

    At the margin, there's also a sense in which there would be competition for people who's play preferences were not so polarised, who would happily play a quick no-set up RPG on the train in half an hour but also love their eighty-week D&D campaigns.

    \n Small social footprint techniques:

    - no GM, or a GM who needs no or minimal prep time
    - plays in two hours or so, about the length of a movie
    - future sessions optional, no need for all the same players to meet again next session
    - mechanics that can be explained or (better) demonstrated in 15 minutes
    - mechanics demonstrated through play (like the training scenes in Dogs)
    - all the critical mechanics listed on the character sheet / board / play aid
    - no need to look up things in the book during play
    - little need to brainstorm things before play begins
    - fixed, pre-generated characters
    - a strong sense of "aboutness" or even a fixed premise
    - a rulebook about the length of the rules to Monopoly, that can be read aloud during play

    \n My small group (four of us, including me) have tried some experimentation with elements of this, and my large group (9 of us) may be experimenting with some of this in our Werewolf game.
    \n
    \nWith the small group, we've got what we call our 'pickup game.' Most of the time it ends up being a rules intense game, but one that can have minimalish prep time. The GM plans out large story arcs when they have time, and when the four of us are together, we just play. We figure it's as close to the sort of pickup games one sees developing at gyms and parks as we're liable to get. This lowers the social footprint in a number of ways. First, it reduces the time management/commitment aspect to almost nothing. Games happen when the four of us are looking for something to do. Second, we've already spent the money and time necessary to acquire all the gear to play, and the culture in which to play. In terms of the hobby/activity question, I think that the pickup game actually falls under activity. We could play WoW, play video games, etc, but sometimes we choose to have a pickup game.
    \n
    \nWith the large group we'll be experimenting with the continuity of the group footprint. By having such a large group, gaming becomes feasible even if as many as 5 of us are missing. This should make it more of a designated, every two week evening activity, less of a mandatory hobby type event.
    \n
    \nIn terms of money, most of the time we buy games that we can play over and over and have different experiences with. I know it's a long-run view, but I think that the money element of going and seeing a movie (around $20, depending on time of day and if you have a date), is a more limiting social footprint than buying a $20 book that I know can give me hours of entertainment. Not saying it's not a valid point, more for me it's a question of $/hours of entertainment, not just $$$.
    \n
    \nFinally, the space requirement element. The majority of the games I play in don't even have a table anymore. Players grab books and dice and sit wherever. True, we still need chairs, but that's at the same level as video gaming, watching TV, or just sitting around and chatting.

    \n Speaking as one of those busy adult-type people, I like the idea of minimal-footprint in theory, but in practice, it's actually been less viable than the higher-footprint model. Why? Because it's not a destination activity. Sure, if I've already got a large group of people together for no particular purpose, I can bring out a micro-game and get some buy-in. But... in point of fact, it's very difficult to put together a gathering of people for anything other than a purpose. We're all very busy. We have families. When I see my friends, it's usually because of some purpose - to paint the deck, move, go to a movie, have a barbeque, watch the hockey game. Playing games doesn't have the same force of gravity.

    What does is something that feels like more of a commitment, paradoxically. Sure, you never get the mildly curious casual person - but in practice, I've not found them all that engaged anyhow. But "give me your odd-numbered Tuesday evenings for the next couple months, and we'll do X" will get people to show up and engage.

    Best I can explain it is that casual games just get lost in the shuffle. They're never actually important enough to happen. When we have one of those rare times that 4 or 5 friends are visiting, we spend it shooting the breeze, talking about the kids/job/house/whatever, drinking wine and perpetually talking about breaking out a board game but rarely actually getting around to it.

    I think that generally speaking, at least in the social milieux I am usually in, telling stories is too structured to be casual. When we do engage in structured games, they're the sort that require minimal actual engagement and are mostly there to lubricate socialization and banter. I don't discount the idea that there are social contexts in which this isn't the case - I've known people who break out hardcore German boardgames at social parties - but (perhaps crucially) they tend to be younger, singler, and geekier than my circles do.

    \n This is purely a personal observation; the only hat I'm wearing as I type is the "Bruce the long-time gamer" one. Contents may have settled in shipping, etc etc.

    I think that a lot of games intended for easy, quick setup and play end up undermining their potential with exotic settings and focus for play. I've gotten best results of this sort from games set in familiar environments, with well-worn, even flat-out cliched, centers of attention. The quick start and easy play come when I can offload key parts of the explanation onto the surrounding world: "vampires, individually Rice-style fops, collectively, Ultraviolet-style schemers", for instance, given friends of mine who dig vampires, or "post-World War II espionage, with the warm cuddliness of vintage Le Carre and the secret history of weird science in a Tim Powers-esque emotional vein". (I've got a group of friends waiting for us all to be in the same state again after business trips and such for a Cold War game on the basis of that description.)

    Now I recognize that not everyone has the same mix of stimuli, and I'm not trying to claim anything like that. But I get most reliable good results when I can start off with "it's this thing you like" and best results when it's "it's these two things you like mashed together". If the stuff that's going to happen in play is itself unfamiliar, then I do at least as well to use a larger game and more elaborate setup and play.

    \n
    1. Do the members of the group currently have generally compatible schedules?
    2. Do these schedules allow most of the members to meet regularly in person?
    3. Are these schedules relatively consistent and anomaly-free, and can you expect them to be stable over a long period?
    4. Does one member of the group have the time and inclination to develop content for the game away from the table, frame scenes, and play NPCs, and do the other members want to elevate this person's authority so they can manage the extra responsibilities?
    5. Does the group want to play in a world of swords and sorcery?
    6. Are the players and GM willing to invest time in learning interlocking mechanics?'
    7. Does the group want to play a game that emphasizes tactical combat and the exploration of dangerous environments?
    8. Do most members of the group want to play characters that work together, with a GM who wants to create external opposition throughout the game?
    \nIf you have answered YES to all the questions above, you are going to have a rocking game of D&D 4e.

    Posted By: MatthijsWhen I watch a movie, I generally DON'T want people talking while we're watching! I want to focus on the movie.
    \nWell, that's interesting again, because obviously people moviewatch in different ways, and have different amoutns of actual socialisation going on.

    For example, when you see a movie in the cinema, you expect silence, because you're sharing a social space with everyone watching the movie, and even if you brought a whole bunch of friends, odds on many of the audience are strangers to you. Silence makes sense there because there's no easy way to confine the effects of your socialisation to the group without also interacting with the strangers, and since they are strangers to you, socialising with them is probably not your goal going in (cinema being a terrible way to meet people).

    On the other hand, watching a movie at home with your friends, people often do talk, comment, throw popcorn and suchlike, and this enhances rather than detracts from the overall experience.

    I also think that compared to watching a movie, roleplaying is a more directly social experience (you're usually facing each other, usually speaking words to one another as the main source of entertainment, communication as a group is critical to the success of the activity) and so therefore the need exists to allow people to actually socialise. Expecting people to not socialise in such a situation is like dropping a lump of potassium into some hydrochloric acid and expecting it not to react - in such an obviously social situation, socialisation wants to happen.

    The Big Model's most critical insight, in my opinion, is that roleplaying is inherently a social activity. I think design decisions about the amount of focus required should bear that in mind.
