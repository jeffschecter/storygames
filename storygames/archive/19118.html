\n Over in this thread I was expressing my exasperation at the fact that the folks at my club would on the whole far rather endlessly play trad games, and I'd far rather play more indie games. Various suggestions have been helpfully tendered by various folks as to how I can attempt to change hearts and minds, and reading them through and responding to them it occurs to me that before we start choosing games to wean people away from playstyle x to playstyle y we need to uncover what the different types of players' wants and expectations are, and then cater to them while at the same time attempting to subtly move them in a different direction. Obviously, all trad gamers don't have one set of wants and expectations, and neither do all indie gamers, but I think we could usefully isolate some commonalities, forex from my pov the preponderance of trad gamers like cooperative player party dynamics and clearly defined session objectives, even if there are freeform elements within the overall session, story arc or campaign, and a way of optimising their character's ability to defeat the bad guys by a careful and calculated distribution of skill points.

What, on the whole, do indie gamers want? Well, for me, player-generated content, emotional content and some degree of introspective philosophising (and no I can't really define that), a strong narrative, and hard emotional choices. Oh, and a GM who is more of an overseer than manipulator of the game world. Do other people feel the same, or do y'all have completely different wants and expectations from a game than the ones I've outlined above?

    \n To me, as basically as I can put it, the main divide between Trad gamers and Story gamers is the role of failure in their respective games. From a young age with games of other natures (chess ect.) we are taught that failure is an absolute state, Trad games build on this by having that same sentiment but also interjecting 'but you can try again?'. Indie (or story) games on the other hand take failure as part and parcel of essential happenings. Different expectations of narrative control are also big part of it, when you are playing in a trad game the GM will be asking the players 'How are you acting in MY world' whereas story games it is more like 'How do your action effect THE world', the loss of firm control on both parts of a story game is part of the fun and makes more interesting things happen.

    I think a lot of Trad gamers are averse to story gaming because the stories tend to be smaller and seem far less interesting. Take a Fiasco setting like the one in suburbia (I can't remember the name right now), anyone who has played it can tell you that it developed into it's own interesting and sometimes hilarious story but a person who is versed in trad gaming would ask 'Why should I play that when I can play a massive part in a story about the fate of the world?'. I believe that is the main problem, the idea of the scale of the importance of the world.

    I would say introducing the idea of failure being interesting and fun and leading to better things subtly into games and also introducing the idea of smaller scale games.
    I also find people who are far less interested in combat tend to gravitate toward story gaming so look out for that?
    I dunno, I'm just rambling sorry!

    What, on the whole, do indie gamers want?
    \nSometimes I'm in the mood to just kill stuff, and sometimes I like something different. When it's facilitated by the rules, I'd call that difference "indie."

    Imagine you're watching a good film like the Matrix. I like the kung fu, but I think what really makes it buzz are character beats. For example:\n
      \n
    • Neo's choice to follow the white rabbit.
      • \n
    • Neo taking the ledge to escape, followed by his surrender to the agents.
      • \n
    • Neo choosing the red pill.
      • \n
    • Cipher's betrayal.
      • \n
    • Morpheus' decision to sacrifice himself for Neo.
      • \n
    • Neo choosing to risk his life to rescue Morpheus even though the Oracle said he isn't the One.
      • \n
    • Neo turning to fight the Agent.
      • \n
    • Morpheus delaying the EMP to give Neo a chance
      • \n
    \n... these are all places where the character makes a choice, and those choices define what sort of person they are.

    Compare this to a typical Stephan Segal movie:\n
      \n
    • Segal has some really cool past, but is now a loner. He meets a girl
      • \n
    • Bad things happen to her
      • \n
    • Fight
      • \n
    • Fight
      • \n
    • Fight
      • \n
    • Big boss
      • \n
    • Victory and kisses
      • \n
    \n... Apart from his decision to fight, none of these are really character beats. It's more like watching a skier tackle increasingly difficult runs so we can watch their skill.

    Likewise consider my experiences of a typical low-level D&D session played OSR style in a dungeon:\n
      \n
    • Make character
      • \n
    • Briefed in a pub. We all know I'm going to accept the dungeon delve.
      • \n
    • Fight
      • \n
    • Fight
      • \n
    • Die randomly. Make new character
      • \n
    • Fight
      • \n
    • Fight
      • \n
    • Big boss
      • \n
    • Count money
      • \n
    \nNow, I have a lot of fond memories with D&D, but played OSR style, it's again like watching a skier tackle increasingly more difficult runs.

    Of course, you can get more of the first type of experience in D&D, too, but I think that's added by the players and not addressed particularly in the rules. So, to the degree a game facilitates character beats rather than task based success, I would tend to call it indie. And those times I'm looking for an indie experience, I'm looking for a game that delivers something with more character beats as part of the rules.

    I.e. I'm looking for a game more focused on what sort of person my character is, rather than how skilled they are.

    \n I'm really tired, but here's something.

    Trad games have a lot challenges, especially combat challenges, immersionist modus operandi (you are your character), a promise of a long and epic campaign arc, a complex system that attempts to simulate the world in detail and usually at least some lip service about acting (or "roleplaying"), traditionally supported more by group consensus than by the rules. People who like trad games should like some of these elements, I think.

    Story games have a storyteller's modus operandi (you try to tell an interesting story) and a relatively simple system that emulates a certain type of story. Storygamers like this stuff, don't we?

    For me the biggest draw in story-games was a simple system, the other big draw was that I didn't need to try to win all the time. I never liked the complexity of trad games. Also, I felt players had much more choices, they didn't even try to form a group consensus all the time.

    \n Can't we just describe what we want from a specific style of gaming? Because that's what people usually describe when they try to describe different kinds of roleplaying.

    I, for example, want to create something together with a bunch of people. It may be a story, it may be a setting or a backdrop of a setting, it may be a joke. I want to be able to contribute to other's experiences. I want to show who I am as a player through my contributions. (Playing out a role is just one of those aspects.)

    I'm not in to being immersive anymore and I haven't found any game brings that forth. Instead, you must be "invested" as a player to make those games work (Fiasco and Montsegur 1244, as examples).

    I also like competition. I like playing D&D4 as a boardgame. I like intrigues between the players. I like games like The Resistance and Diplomacy. (Why aren't they roleplaying games?)

    I like exploration. Hexcrawl could be fun. Seeing where a story is heading is fun. Unraveling a mystery is fun. Learning culture is fun. Seeing how components of a game interact, may it be story elements, game mechanics or even the participants themselves. All that is fun too. (Isn't fun an emotional response to learning?)

    Oh, and all this can be summarized as exploration, competition, expression and sensation in various degrees.

    \n As much as it is hard to believe, there are still a ton of people who have only ever played D&D and never tried anything else. Some of those people might find alternative games more suited to them, but I have to suspect that's a rather small subset.

    How gamers with a long history of "trad" games migrated away is an easy story. In the beginning, there was D&D. Even most of the early alternatives to D&D were clearly very derived from D&D. I mean, I quickly migrated away from D&D, and was always playing different games. But Palladium and Shadowrun and Warhammer Fantasy all clearly share roots with D&D. There certainly were some early examples of "story" games (Tales from the Floating Vagabond and The Adventures of Baron Munchausen for example), but for the most different playstyles were realized by playing "trad" games in different ways. As games continued to evolve and diverge, gamers could more easily split into different games that suited what they were looking for.

    I will also say I'm an omnigamer. I have four semi-regular campaigns I play in. All of those would be firmly categorized as traditional games. I also enjoy lighter one-shot games for those times we get together and don't have something planned. I can pull out Fiasco or Durance or Dread and get a game going very quickly. Or sometimes I'll have a specific game I want to run for a single session. I also find I can make my traditional games better by bringing in story game techniques. Apocalypse World is a manifesto about making failure interesting. Durance is a treatise on scene framing.

    Anyway, I'm rambling. It's hard to categorize either games or gamers into such binary groups.

    \n From an interesting side of view, what I really look for in a game is an escapist factor. I really enjoy both the high fantasy high tactical gamist romps in D&D 4th and the more overarching story-view of Microscope. On the other hand, a game like, say, Grey Ranks doesn't appeal to me so much.     So basically, there are different people that, I think, look for different things from games. Folks that want character immersion are unlikely to find it in some story games. People who look for story awareness will likely not find it in D&D. But some folks might be looking for more nebulous things that exist in the region around RPGs in general. So the best way to find out if folks might be open to whatever you want to play is ask them on an individual level rather than try and form categories.

They offer quite a bit of character control, encourage investment in characters too, but also have a focus on group based storytelling, rather than the storyteller role being monopolised by the GM figure.
