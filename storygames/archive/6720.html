    \n I fondly remember choose-your-own-adventure books. At an earlier stage in my GMing career, I often tried to use the "choose your own adventure" structure as an inspiration when writing adventures, for any system.

    "OK, if the PCs decide to board the derelict space station, then X will happen; if they land on the nearby planet, then Y will happen instead".

    I quickly discovered two things:
    1. Writing down all these if-thens is an incredibly huge amount of work for the GM. If the PCs make ten decisions over the course of the adventure, and each decision is between two options, then if there are no repeats, simple math says there are 1024 possible endings. And ten decision points with two options each is too *simple* of an adventure to be really interesting.

    Besides, they're only going to see one ending, so the other 1023 will be wasted effort. The cool thing about a CYOA book is you can read it multiple times and get different outcomes. It's very rare for an RPG group to play the same adventure more than once, so why write it that way?

    2. No matter how well you think you've enumerated all the possible courses of action, the players will ALWAYS do something else. Board the derelict space station or land on the nearby planet? Ha! The PCs will want to use the space station as photon-torpedo target practice and then siphon off the planet's entire atmosphere to sell to the Spaceballs on the opposite side of the galaxy.

    No matter how smart or creative you think you are, your players will surprise you every time. There's no way you can anticpate what they'll want to do because, for one thing, they simply outnumber you.

    So, I finally decided to abandon the CYOA model for RPG adventures and become more of an improviser. The reason a tabletop RPG can be way more fun than a CYOA book or a computer game is that the tabletop RPG is the only one that can be driven by human creativity in real-time. If you get good at coming up with interesting stuff on the fly (which I don't claim to be good at yet, but I'm working on it), then you can take advantage of this. If you're improvising, then there's no time wasted coming up with plot threads the players won't see, no getting stuck when the players want to try something different, and best of all, the story you create is influence by everyone's creativity, not just the GM's.

    I'm a lot happier since I embraced the improvisation. But that's not neccessarily the right thing for everybody. What is it that you like about CYOA that you want to see happen in your games?

    \n I agree with fnord. Choose Your Own Adventure is a very simple system. It's really just a binary set of options (although there might have been times when there were three or more choices, I only remember there being two choices). In one way of looking at it, almost all systems can do this already. In fact, you don't even need a game except to tell a player what's going on (including the actions of their character) until some juncture, present them with multiple choice options. Looking at it another way, no system does this because players usually have many more options (and in most story games, they have the power to shape the world and influence the story, taking it in directions no one planned for ahead of time). Which is completely different than a branching novel.

    I think just about any game could be written into a CYOA book form, after playing it, but there'd still be a lot of work filling out the paths not travelled. I'd say that CYOA is fundamentally solo, where the reader chooses branches (and I think most of us had a finger or two in place so we could back track and see what the other choice might have been - especially if a path leads to the main character's death). And that it takes a page from the novel, and presents the reader with such limited options, that it is its own system, best not to convert it.

    So I'm not just negative, here's some brainstorming. The way that I'd go about developing a CYOA system is perhaps something where all the players are controlling the protagonist, and if they disagree about a direction, then you branch the story, taking it in multiple directions. For a system, I'd probably want something like playing cards, or even Once Upon a Time cards, to give some guidance for the story (because improvising a story with very little input from others is hard). Although maybe this works best as a party game. If the teller gets confused, or lost in the story, then it changes to a new teller and the game continues.

    But I dunno, maybe Once Upon A Time is the closest match, because I keep thinking that I'd rather play that.

    \nI don't know of any system.

    My suggestion on a process to use - simply write a story, from begining to end, exactly how you want it to go. Finish that. Then go to certain points in it where an alternate decision could have been made. Write a story involving that option that A: is more life/failure threatening (bring in dice rolls here) or B: Leads to an alternate ending. Do not do C: It eventually leads to the exact same ending before. Cause you will suck.

    Finish that side story. Now go back to the main or the side story and again look for where a different option could have been taken. Keep doing this X amount of times - I do not know the right amount for X.

    There, a choose your own adventure!

    \n We use this structure a lot for L\xe6relyst scenarios. They're played in classes, where the teacher acts as GM, and the students work in groups of 3-5 players, each controlling one character.

    Mostly, decision points fall in one of these categories:

    1. All characters have to choose the same option, since they're mutually exclusive.
    Example: Do you want to sneak quietly up to the castle, or run up screaming and shouting to create a diversion?

    2. Characters can do different things, but they can each only do one thing from the list. Several characters can choose the same option.
    Example: Do you want to talk to the blind beggar, look under the pillow for clues, or intimidate the bird salesman?

    3. Characters can do different things, and only one thing each, but they have to cover all the alternatives. Several characters can choose the same option.
    Example: The count tells you to perform task A, B and C. Who wants to do which one?

    \n A Penny for my Thoughts splits it's scenes into 1) description followed by 2) a binary choice and it repeats 1 and 2 until the end of the scene, which ends on description.

    However, the other day I was thinking about CYOA books and I was thinking that the average game I used to play was more like a short story or a movie than anything else. Sure I had a character, but they had little ability to impact the events occurring around them, such that if there was a preplanned scene one, two, and three, there was nothing they could do to prevent scene one from leading into scene two from leading into scene three, and the end result will be the same regardless of who is playing or how they play (much like how the ending of the movie is the same regardless of who is watching). And, to be fair, I have friends who love this, being manoeuvred from scene to scene until they get to discover the GM's twist ending, and at the end they all cheer.

    However, a CYOA is not like a movie. There are set possible endings, but there is no set ending. Scene one might not lead to scene two but a different scene instead. Which to me feels a lot like bangs. Like, hey we just hit a cool choice for you to make, make it, and we'll see where it leads. Bangs are not binary (I mean, I suppose they could be, but they aren't by definition binary), but they have that same aspect where upon your choice matters, and it impacts the story.

    \n I liked those old CYOA books, too.
    \n
    \nYou could always draw out a binary tree as a kind of game board and have players move their pawns along it, having each section be defined when players come to it.
    \n
    \nIf you wanted, you could throw in some stakes setting rules.
    \n
    \nOr you could do like Ron suggests a few post up and play it in reverse. Have the players start at the spread-out end of the binary tree (one of the endings) and describe that ending (presumably a bad one) and then move their pawns (or dice, perhaps, that rotate to show a change in score and potential) as the players move either toward the head of the tree (the not-yet-created beginning of the story) or back towards the endings (defining the choices they made to get to one ending the first time and then creating a second choice to move toward a different ending.)
    \n
    \nThe game ends when all the possible endings are mapped out or when the players find a mutually satisfying one.

    \n I remember two games that used a CYOA approach to adventures:

    Chapter 2 of the D&D module Palace of the Silver Princess was a "programmed adventure".

    ICE's introductory RPG Lord of the Rings Adventure Game (not to be confused with Decipher's game of the same name) and its two adventure modules "Darker than the Darkness" and "Over the Misty Mountains Cold" were playable pretty much out of the box, with virtually no GM prep, thanks to compact CYOA style (and inevitably railroady) sequences.
