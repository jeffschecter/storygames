    \n 1. When I was working on Classroom Deathmatch, and immediatly afterward when the VT shooting happened, I had a lot of peole tell me that a game about students murdering each other was in really bad taste, and that I shouldn't publish the game in light of tragedies like the VT shootings. But I think that's the wrong way to think about it. One of the primary responsibilities of art, even the kind of art we practice as designers and players, is to examine difficult subjects and to explore hard to understand topics. I feel like the very best role playing comes when players put themselves in difficult situations and force themselves to ask tough questions and make hard choices. Using a game to examine difficult subjects like school violence, betrayle and murder (to use Classroom Deathmatch as an example) is far preferable to pretending like these subjects don't exsist. Instead, we can understand these subjects betetr through exploration via imagination.
    \n
    \nIn games I play I always try to examine and confront subjects that I find difficult or controversial. By putting myself in alien or uncomfortable situations I can explore points of view that I hadn't considered before and try new things that I otherwise might not try. This is less role playing as wish-fullfillment and more role playing as exploration, I think.
    \n
    \n2. Yes. Those are the games I prefer.

    Posted By: asdfff
    1) Controversial topics that come up in-game. Your thoughts, please?
    2) Would buy play/buy an inde game if it happened to be based around a controversial topic?
    \n1) Racism, abuse (physical, mental, sexual), infanticide, slavery, lynching, religion. Bring it on. I really enjoy playing controversial, dark themed games. I like historical games. I read more non-fiction than I do fiction anyway.

    It also depends on the group of people. I like and trust the people I play with, and I'm comfortable with my own threshholds to be able to articulate when I don't feel safe or comfortable with the course of action.

    This is the first game I've ever designed. I didn't set out to write a "controversial" game. Steal Away Jordan didn't start out as a game about slavery. I set out to design a game with an interesting premise, that would tell a compelling story. When I took it to GenCon I was a little surprised (albeit pleasantly) that my game caused discomfort. Several times people mentioned discomfort as white people portraying black slaves. My pat answer has become, "well, I play white characters sometimes". There's more to it than that, really. I think it's a good thing for people to play outside their comfort level.

    My game's not about relieving or causing guilt about being who you are. Slave narratives are interesting to me, and one race's history is really everyone's history, and should be appreciated and embraced as such. If you learn something about yourself or other people in playing a game with a difficult historical theme, that's a bonus, but not necessarily my intention.

    2. Not only would I play a game, I'd write another one like it. I look forward to playing Grey Ranks, and Carry, and even not being the GM in my own game one of these days. Bring on the history and dark stuff.

I say and do things that I wouldn't normally do. I'm forced to look at issues, both familiar and new in different ways. It's very exciting.
    \n
    \nOne of the things that I've discovered over the years is that if I have the option to build a character for a game, I will invariably build a character that represents an aspect of myself that I am comfortable with. And this will often be the same character that I played in a previous game, with a different name and set of physical features. But still the same character. With the same approaches to solving problems and the same moral compass. If I take this option away from myself, if I play a game where I don't get to create a character (Classroom Deathmatch for example) or a use random modifier's to create a character, I end up with something new and unexpected. I end up going in unpredictable directions. I find it really interesting and really rewarding.
    \n
    \nBeyond that, conflict builds character. Addressing a difficult theme or subject often creates tension and conflict between both the characters and the players. I like that. I like to argue and disagree. Again, it takes games in interesting directions, and lets me and my fellow players explore ideas and emotions that we might not otherwise get to see in the game.

    \n Jake: Novelty, gotcha. And to be very clear, because I can see how it might not have been, please don't read my previous post as adversarial. I'm ambivalent on the subject and am looking for various perspectives on it.

    Some random thoughts:

    * Being "forced" to do anything, particularly during a period of social relaxation, doesn't strike me as a positive.

    * Addressing "issues" via an RPG seems extraordinarily dangerous. Frankly I don't believe a single RPG designer ever -- and I mean like ever -- has been a particularly qualified resource, commentator or critic on any subject other than game design. I don't really care what celebrities have to say about issues, either. Personally, if I wanted to explore an issue I'd want more reputable resources available to me.

    * I totally get that using an RPG as an exploratory tool can have certain benefits beyond merely reading about a subject (and dealing with the editorial bias/agenda of the writers, etc.). I'm not so convinced that even the more "serious" RPGs out there have actually accomplished the goal of being a valuable exploratory tool, despite the seriousness of their subject matter.

    * I'm personally not (yet?) convinced that RPGs have any business being anything other than a pleasurable pastime, although I acknowledge that "pleasurable" is highly subjective. I also get a huge kick out of exploring new kinds of conflict, so in that way I totally agree about the value -- to me -- of playing outside my comfort zone.

Does anyone really believe that profanity is shocking? When you watch an R-rated movie about criminals or cops or soldiers, do you feel that the writers are trying to shock you with 'bad words'? Personally, when I'm writing, I use profanity when I think it's supposed to be used, because that's how some people talk. For a game about junkies and disgraced FBI agents who fight serial killers that are possessed by demons... Well, it just seems pretty logical.

    To clarify, I'm not saying that your dislike of the game is unjustified, I'm just saying that it's erroneous to ascribe some kind of intent to shock people. I think trying to be shocking is goofy. But it's also goofy to say 'gosh darn it' when someone has torn off one of your legs. Which happens a lot in Dread: The First Book of Pandemonium.

    Let's look at the game from a different angle. Right on the back I say it's not so much a game about slavery as much as it is a way to construct a slave narrative. There was lots of laughter at the table, and several characters had positive outcomes. You can play sentient human beings and have fun with this game, I assure you.

    But I get that it's not everyone's cup of tea. I just want to put it out there that 400 years of slavery in the Americas produced some people who did amazing, joyful things, and this is playable in Steal Away Jordan.

    I'm also curious why it would be more enjoyable to play slaved automatons (albeit sentient) over actual people? What makes this more inviting? What hits too close to home playing a 18-19th century person?

      \n What Graham and JBR said.

      But also, for me it's not just the topics, but the treatment. To use fiction as an example: I can handle the main characters suffering a lot if the suffering is ultimately meaningful and they respond to it heroically, that is, they sacrifice for the sake of others and to do the right thing and it comes out OK in the end. That's my personal preference in story. So I love Lois McMaster Bujold and Jim Butcher, who beat up their protagonists unmercifully but have upbeat endings. Futile and ultimately meaningless struggle, on the other hand, I just don't enjoy. Nor do I have any patience with passively suffering idiots, or selfish bastards who are just out for themselves and never face any consequences or gain any kind of insight or empathy.

      In other words, in my fiction, movies and games, I like to get my view of how the universe should be reinforced, even if I'm aware that that's not how the universe always is. This can involve suffering and the existence of sexism and racism and slavery and other dark and evil things, but I want to see those things as being ultimately less powerful than the alternatives. It sounds like Steal Away Jordan supports that, which is great. I'm not sure that Grey Ranks does, given where its particular story stops.

      And all of this, just to reinforce, is my preference - if yours is different, good for you, but we may not enjoy the same game.

      EDIT: I just realized that a briefer way of saying this is "comedies (in the traditional-dramatics sense of stories with a resolution that involves restoration), not tragedies."

      \n

      Ack.

      \n

      Anyway, yeah, re: the "slavery is so dehumanizing and cruel" thing, you know, it was indeed, but it was real, too. It's a thing people did and had done to them, through most of human history. People like you and me have lived their lives using and owning people or being used and owned by them. American Black slavery is recent enough to horrify, and its scale was huge, but it wasn't different in kind than the sort of thing that's happened all along and still happens today...

      \n

      It seems like it's one of those things like Nazism where we turn it into a matter of angels and devils, a war of light and darkness, and forget that everyone involved were human, and it's that very forgetting that can allow it to happen again, without folks noticing, cause they don't see angels and devils doing it, but people...

      \n

      That's why I think a game which turns slavery into something that happens among and between humans instead of a mythic good-and-evil situation is inherently a valuable & good thing.
