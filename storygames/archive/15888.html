    \n Hey Mr. Harper, lemme play devil's advocate for a sec:

    AW always struck me as too superheroic for a gritty apocalypse. The characters are too effectively badass, frequently doing stuff that a regular person would only be able to pull off occasionally, if ever. Everyone tends to have a +3 in something, a special ability that lets them use their +3 stat for other stuff, and with a little creativity it's not hard to use your best stat to solve most of your problems.

    I honestly don't think dropping every single stat by 1 (so adding up to -2) would make the game less playable or fun. For me, anyway.

    \n@Dionysus: Nope, they don't have to be unique. Unless you want them to be.

    @David Berg: That's a perfectly reasonable POV, David. I hear you. I think a lower-stat approach could work, and it's something to keep in mind. We decided that Regiment characters should be effective (not quite as effective as AW characters, due to the differences in how our moves work), but very fragile.

    I would not characterize Regiment characters as "super heroic" at all. Like Jason and Matthew said, the battlefield is a rough place to be. Unlike AW, you will likely play a string of characters during a Regiment campaign -- as your guys bite it and replacements filter in.

    But I appreciate the devil's advocacy! Despite my argument on this point, we are very open to new ideas and changes and I hope that more stuff like this comes up during playtests.

    \n I've played The Regiment twice now and, in one case, more than half the PCs died or were critically wounded (all but 1, actually) in the first session of what could have been a longer campaign. It definitely doesn't feel superheroic, IME. The heroic + fragile thing stands out, from John's comments. You can attempt heroic things, but heroic things in war often get you killed or messed up, and that feels pretty spot-on.

    Also, unlike in AW, the characters only have so many ways they can go about achieving their objectives and most of those involve combat of some variety (i.e. it's a game about war). If you're smart and lucky, you can conduct combat under more-or-less your own terms, but it's still pretty dern lethal. If you're unlucky or boldy push forward (which you have to do sometimes, due to the situation and the mission), it's a deadly shitstorm.

    I honestly disagree about lowering stats and making harder moves as the MC (since moves always follow from the fiction and kinda are what they are, in most cases). If things aren't tense enough (which wasn't the case in the games I played, but sure, it could happen), the way to up the stakes is to make the fictional situation more desparate. "You're up to your chest in a swamp, you've lost the rest of your unit, and you hear small arms fire coming from out of the jungle to the southeast." Then the more desparate fiction will naturally suggest harder moves to you or make the characters attempt more reckless things.

    \n +Intel is a prompt. It's special insight into the battlespace. In a way, I think it's the Regiment's equivalent of opening your brain to the world psychic maelstrom. The GM should use it as a tool to hook players into what's going on in the world outside the immediate present. It can be used to throw in plot hooks.
