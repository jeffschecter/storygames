\n John (jenskot) recently posted about the D&D Red Box, which leads you through a short D&D adventure, teaching you the gameplay and the rules along the way:\n
Posted By: jenskotI really liked the D&D red box! It's a little "hand holdy" in a choose your own adventure kind of way but I think it's a good start and understandable as it's meant to be a solo exercise.

If you choose:

"hiding in the back of the wagon until the fight is over"

It tells you:

"Hiding from danger is not the sort of thing that most characters in heroic fantasy do! The Dungeons & Dragons game is about playing a hero. Though hiding from the goblins might seem prudent, it's not the heroic course. Besides, the goblins are going to steal the wagon, so they're bound to find you sooner or later. You might as well face them now! Though the odds seem steep, these are only goblins, after all. Go back to 1. and choose a more heroic course of action."
\nIn this thread, you will attempt to do the same thing (develop an excellent "hand-holdy" guide) for an indie/story game of your choice.

You may begin now.

    \n Can I suggest that we give the players actually meaningful choices rather than ones where one of the answers is wrong (at least in the mind of the designer)? Hopefully that's not a revolutionary suggestion.

    Like: "Do you jump out and fight the goblins OR hide in the back until they come closer and then surprise them?"

    Or, alternately, maybe hiding in the back until the fight is over is a good call. Maybe you're totally outclassed (are they the goblins from the Rankin & Bass Hobbit cartoon, maybe?) or maybe your character is a coward or wants to learn the location of the goblins' lair.

    Also, introductory content doesn't mean it has to be relatively unimportant fictionally. Instead of some goblins attacking a wagon, what about fighting a dragon to save a city? What about rescuing your sibling from the lich lord? Just because the players are new at this doesn't mean their characters have to be relatively powerless and green.

    \n Speaking to J. Walt's points, steal from videogames:

    Skyrim has you fighting a dragon pretty early in. With help, sure, but it makes it feel much cooler. Maybe your starting character is a member of the city guard destined for bigger things.

    Metroid always likes to start you with all kind of cool abilities, then take them away a little ways in. Start as a big important adventurer killing a lich. The resulting magic implosion sucks the very life from you, leaving you a scrawny first level adventurer. This one's a little more divisive, but it can work.

    \n I am fascinated by the idea that you could have a "guide to playing" that specifically outlines options which are not good for the game, and then tells you why they shouldn't be exercised when you choose them.

    I can imagine an Apocalypse World guidebook for the beginner MC which says things like: "Have the villagers slaughter the Hocus's followers," followed by, "Slaughtering the Hocus's followers in this scene is a terrible idea. Have you forgotten your principles? Always be a fan of the characters! Now, turn back and choose a different option."

    I don't know if it's stupid or brilliant or both.
