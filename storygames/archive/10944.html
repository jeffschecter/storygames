\n I was talking to a few of my friends (including Sage, who is here) about the merits of GM-less games. I like both, but it is my theory that a game without a gamemaster, while superficially increasing the creativity at the table by potentially drawing from multiple pools, has a limited creative vista because the stories in such games tend to necessarily be predetermined by the rules in order to guide the player narrative. A game with a gamemaster, while superficiallly decreasing the creativity at the table by drawing from only one pool, has an expanded creative vista because the stories he can tell are not necessarily limited in the same way. An easier way to explain that is via Contender, which is about a specific kind of story, so all the scenes created by players go into that story. A boxing game run with a gamemaster in a different system could be about a lot more than the kind of stories told in Contender. Adding a human storyteller is what provides for that flexibility.

Anyway, to demonstrate my argument, I noted that GM-less games tend to be competitive, OR tend to basically be mutual games of one-on-one in which what happens to your character has no bearing on what happens to mine, OR must violate the Czege principle. If we're a team, and I narrate a challenge for us players to overcome, and then take part in overcoming it, bam, yawn-zone for me. This perspective is because I like games with cooperative team-play, which is what traditional games with a gamemaster tend to be.

My friends said that not all GM-less games are competitive or joint one-on-one sessions, but when I asked for examples, we came up with none. So: got any?

    \n Engle Matrix Games can be competitive but they don't have to be.

    For example: In 1998 I ran a Gen Con game "Romeo and Ethel the Pirate's Daughter" From "Shakespeare in Love"

    The players championed characters in the made up play (Romeo, Ethel, the Pirate King, Caliban) or a real life character (Shakespeare, the theater owner, the dog (Will you've got to work the dog in. People love the dog!) The game was about the story of the play and the story of the play being written. This meant that everyone won if the story was fun, which it turned out to be.

    This worked because each turn players make up whole scenes - saying what all the characters do (rather than just their own) and are given a "To Happen" roll byt a referee (which can be any of the players). Players put out different versions of what happens in each scene and which happens is settled by a dice rolling contest. Scene builds on scene to tell a full story.

    Like I said, these can be competitive but I use the same approach to do murder mysteries (we all win if we find out who done it) horror games (we all win if we save the world) and many other stories.

    One could debat that they are GMless because the role of deciding to happen rolls is still present but there is no one who is the authority on what is true in the game. All the players share that power.

    Personally I like a good competitive game but I don't think they make good stories. Stories really need more collaboration than that.

    Chris Engle

    \n"a game without a gamemaster ... has a limited creative vista because the stories in such games tend to necessarily be predetermined by the rules in order to guide the player narrative"

    Not in Universalis, which is a completely setting-less system. The players negotiate the basic rules of the setting at the start, and then create the world as play goes on.

    Like Ben, I'm unsure what you mean by "cooperative" in this context. The basic rules of fiction say that any story needs some kind of conflict. In a GM'd game, the players are generally on one side of the conflict against the forces played by the GM, which usually means the players' characters are cooperating. If there's no GM, the conflict/competition necessarily has to be generated by the players themselves \u2014 so yes, to some degree there is competition, as one player creates a conflict that the players then take sides resolving. That's really no different than a GM'd game, though, if you consider a GM to be merely a player with greater story-power.

    To get this, it isn't necessary to make the players take sides for the whole game. In Universalis, players aren't bound to the same character: during every scene players decide which characters to play. Some of those may be heroes, some antagonists. You might be playing one of each at the same time \u2014 after all, that's what an author does when writing a book. The ultimate goal is a good story, not necessarily success for one side.

    \n re: timfire

    I fully understand that a competitive game can have a very cooperative atmosphere, in which everybody is cooperating to create a cool competitive environment, but that's not how I am using the terms. . . in fact, I don't think it's at all useful to use them in that way, because we all know what a competitive game is (despite the fact that we cooperate in making it happen), so the definition of a cooperative game should follow logically from that. Getting tripped up in the meta-cooperative nature of the game is where the definitions would be confusing. Heck, a cooperative game can have a meta-competitive atmosphere if the players start arguing with each other. That isn't the level I'm examining, though.

    So, for clarity, I am defining a cooperative game as ones in which the players (and their characters) are on the same team working towards a common goal, and in which the conflict is generated by a source outside of that group. I am defining competitive games as ones in which conflict arises based on the players (and their characters) having different desires and advocating for those desires. In short, my cooperative/competitive distinction is based on the source of conflict in the game.

    I'd be happy to revisit the definition if necessary.

    - - -

    If you're happy with those definitions, I am interested in hearing why you think the observation about creative vistas is utter bunk. It is the larger point of my discussion with Sage, and he thinks he is right, so I am interested in the viewpoints of other people who share his stance. Merci!

    \n re: timfire

    For three reasons, I am not sure it is fair to call it narrow. You could flip the argument and use "competitive" to refer to a single play style (the conflict comes from resolving player disagreement) and "cooperative" to refer to anything else. To me, that illustrates how there are fundamentally two sources of conflict in a game, and identifying the source tells us whether the game is competitive or cooperative. It is therefore not narrow to describe games within that dichotomy any more than it is narrow to use "GM-less" to describe games without a gamemaster and "not GM-less" to describe everything else. And, secondly, I think team-style play fits into many games, including D&D: I've run it in d20 Cthulhu, In Nomine, Lacuna, Burning Wheel, Cat, Dogs, Vampire, Secret Lives of Gingerbread Men, GURPS, Dragonquest, etc. And, third, cooperative play also describes games like My Life WIth Master and Carry, to an extent insofar as both can have dramatic conflicts even if the players are all on the same side and all agree completely as to what they want to have happen. and neither can be described as D&D-style team-play despite being eminently cooperative.

    That said, I think I grasp what I am hearing you say, and I am open to alternative, broader definitions of cooperative play that also contrast with competitive play.

    Posted By: phargleI am very curious about GM-less games that are neither cooperative or competitive. I can't even envision such a game...
    \nHere, let me write you one right now. It's just for two players, because it's an example, but this kind of thing works on a much larger and more complicated scale too.

    - One player plays Batman.
    - The other player plays Robin.

    Batman's player is allowed to narrate Batman beating the crap out of stuff and generally being badass. Batman, however, is not normally allowed to talk. He moves silently and effectively, leaving behind a wake of unconscious goons. Batman's player is also allowed to narrate bad stuff happening to Robin, such as him being captured or falling off a building or being mind-controlled by a hypno-ray. That kind of thing. Batman's player also has three tokens and can spend a token to have Batman say something dramatic, but no more than a dozen words (frex: "I'm the goddamned Batman").

    Robin's player is allowed to narrate Robin talking, but Robin can only be described doing badass stuff that somewhat less badass than what the Batman just did. If Batman takes out three goons in quick succession, Robin heartily punches a goon in the face, yelling "Take that!" Furthermore, Robin's player introduces new antagonism into the fiction by saying: "Holy _______, Batman! [Crazy new antagonism]!" For example, "Holy frying catfish, Batman! The Circus of Crime is attacking Gotham City's Christmas Day Parade!"

    That's the whole game.

    And what you see in these sorts of setups, which, like I said, can be much more complicated (involving dice, stats, etc.), is that each player is responsible for providing antagonism for the other players, by they are not actually competing, mechanically. Batman puts Robin in trouble but then is most likely at least partially responsible for getting Robin out of said trouble. And Robin ups the ante or introduces new problems, but then both heroes have to face those problems together. So what a lot of GMless games do is make the players collectively responsible for both antagonism and overcoming the antagonism, but then dole out specific instances of responsibility such that you're avoiding the Czege Principle problem of resolving your own antagonism. There's an avalanche that affects all the players, say, but I'm chosen to play the avalanche in this particular case, even though I ultimately don't want it to kill us all.
