    \n Hey Paul,

    Thanks for giving it go, warts and all. Agon does indeed have some bits that need some polish.

    Definitely don't worry about NPC armor penalties. I've experimented with a few different ways to handle armor so you don't need to remember penalties for the PCs, either. The current one is:

    Everyone has a d10 Positioning die by default (it's not based on Athletics). Each piece of armor you have lowers your position die by one size. So, one piece = d8, two pieces = d6, and three pieces = d4.

    I agree that resolving ties is sometimes pretty fiddly. If you want an all-purpose system, it goes: Aggressor wins > highest Fate wins > dice for it.

    Don't worry too much about "broken" character builds. The heroes with +4 bonuses or really powerful combos will be taken care of by the rest of the PCs. If the rest of the PCs are too intimidated to mess with that character... well, there's your leader. It helps a lot if the other players really understand their role in the hero band -- namely to fight to earn the right to be remembered forever. If Aleta is shining a little too brightly and hogging the spotlight, they need to do something about it or accept being second best.

    \n Okay, first turn is under our belts. Some thoughts, and a procedural question.

    * The note about 3 obstacles = 1 session is dead on. This caught me by surprise! I think Battles run way, way longer than I expected. Fun and all, but yeah...it's gonna be an hour before you come up for air again.

    * I think maybe one of my players isn't feeling the Glory chase.

    * I think maybe one of my players has figured out how to optimize the Glory chase: Blow all your Divine Fortune at every opportunity to get the high roll, call as many Interludes as you want -- all that extra Strife is just incentive for the Antagonist to throw down with 2d12 challenges. So it's actually in the players' interest to give the Antagonist more Strife, since DF is usually enough to succeed at any challenge (and it seems to come down to luck when competing amongst yourselves for highest roll).

    * The combination of one Glory-hound and one Glory-meh player did not produce fireworks. I think despondency in the Glory chase kills the fun. Looking for comeback ideas on this one, since it appears to be a "rich get richer" type of situation.

    * The players don't yet have a firm grasp of how awesome Oaths are, but I think that comes down to the spread between theorizing about Oaths (you're only going to give me your shittiest die!) and actually using them (we gotta run -- take this juicy d10 as help!).

    * The Big Bad they had to fight might have been too big and too bad for the players to really get into the positioning game on Oaths, i.e. they had to coordinate just to not die. That said, we're using the "winner gets 2 position shifts" rule -- I think the players need to cash in Oaths to make the WINNER dance like a monkey.

    * Totally screwed up the +1d6/+1d8 DF effect (just check the FAQ, oops!): I thought the + symbol always meant it added to the final result, rather than just being an additional die result. This I think will greatly reduce the Glory luckfest we were experiencing -- they were blowing all their DF on every challenge, spending DF to chase the highest roll.

    * The Positioning tiebreaker rules were a snap to keep in our heads. No biggie. In fact it was nice to give the folks with nice Arete abilities a little bennie once in a while. We're having trouble really putting the Arete abilities to good use.

    Questions:

    * We're feeling some fuzziness around skills used in Battle. There are two issues:

    1) Do you still designate a weapon in hand for initiative purposes, even when you intend to do a Special Maneuver? This was causing some confusion because I think it's not modeling anything "realistic," i.e. why does the sword guy get to unleash his War Cry before spears get to attack, even when he's waaaay back on the back line? Not a biggie, just making sure that's how it works.

    2) For the "Poison" ability, you impair "an ability the target is using in the Battle." Not sure how to parse this. Is this ability that he's used at some point in the Battle? Or the ability he last used?

    3) If you perform a Special Maneuver, do you get your Name Die for the roll if it's already tied up in your right/left hand mix? This produced a lot of fuzziness, esp. when the Name Die was the only die being rolled for defense...and then there it is getting rolled again in the same exchange for the maneuver.

    * I think I read on the Forge forum that Advantage die gets rolled ONCE per quest. True? Seems like it's way, way too risky to set up an Advantage if all you're getting is one die, once.

    Procedural rumination:

    Okay, so the Antagonist gets Strife at the start of the quest. The suggestion is that about half the available Strife get spent on NPCs. And of course there's the per-NPC strife limit. Cool, no problem. So I statted up two Big Bad NPCs and their minions. Now...do I "spend" that Strife in advance of the quest, functionally reducing my discretionary Strife pool before I even introduce the NPCs? Or do I pay the Strife out of the master pool only when the NPC/minion is introduced?

    The reason I ask is that it's entirely possible that an NPC won't get used, so prepaying really hits the Antagonist hard. OTOH if I don't pay 'til I use them, it might make more sense to not even bring an NPC into play if I want to keep more points in my Strife pool.

    In last night's session, my Strife pool was 90. Given the preprogrammed costs of the battles I have lined up (NPC + minions + Advantages), that would have left me with 40 discretionary Strife if I'd paid for everything up-front. But what I did instead was simply deduct the Strife as the battle was engaged, so I had a lot of breathing room beforehand.

    Given the non-adversarial role of the Antagonist, my gut says you prepay for the NPCs, and it's on the Antagonist to make sure they show up. But I don't entirely trust my gut yet, based on only one play.

    Anyway, fun game. Mostly fun; the one player who got shafted a lot with bad rolling feels like the Glory chase is one big random dice game. We'll see what a second round does.

    I'm not sure Strife is a meaningful check on the Antagonist's resources, unless you do in fact prepay for your Battle setups. This seemed especially true in light of the "call Interludes early and often" policy: I think they called 10 in 4 hours of play!

    \n Paul: Use advantage dice however you want with the alternate rules. And each die decays separately.

    So, you earn 2d10 advantage dice. In a contest, you use one of the d10 in a roll. So now you have 1d8 1d10 advantage dice left. Then, in a battle, you use the d10 on a roll, leaving you with 2d8. Later in the battle, you use both d8s, leaving you with 2d6. Remember that advantage dice represent something specific in the fiction! That's the main restriction on their use.

    And yes, the GM sill buys advantage dice the same way. Don't worry about decay. The GM has enough to keep track of.

    Brian: Fred's build-your-own rules are great. I recommend them. And Athletics covers running, jumping, swimming, climbing, and most physical stunts. It's still pretty useful. :-)

    \n Second session, different group (one crossover player), everything went great! Some observations:

    * Having a well-balanced mix of weapons in a battle, on both sides, means never really having an opportunity to use Tricks/special maneuvers. Everyone always had something better to do other than a Trick.

    * Swift is the coolest NPC trait ever. Combined with a mob of club-wielding cannibals and a mob of blowgun-wielding cannibals, it makes for a very, very slippery battlefield.

    * Advantages continue to elude me. Probably worth starting a separate thread to hack through it.

    * Oaths, once again, only show up as awesome once someone actually uses them. For-real question: When you cash in an Oath to make someone heal you during an Interlude, does that in fact eat up their Interlude action? Because that's an awesome double-whammy we hadn't realized 'til it happened: stealing the other guy's Interlude is almost better than getting the healing. Even if that's not RAW I think we'll do it anyway.

    * Battles are just mechanically engaging enough that there's very, very little room for roleplaying left in our head-space.     You did the Oath interlude heal thing right. The double whammy is intentional.

    Here are a few tips for putting the RP and Tricks into Battles:
    - Play out the breaks as actual breaks in the action. The two sides can shout challenges to each other, makes boasts as they strip off their armor, pledge glory to Ares, etc.

    - Have specific NPCs do things to spice up the action. Maybe one of them is holding a hostage and is gonna slit his throat. Or a group of minions are dumping oil down the stairs to set on fire. Heroes will suddenly start looking for Tricky moves at that point. "How can I stop them? They're way back there!" Remind them that the range strip is abstract, and Tricks don't have a range.

    - If you have some friendly NPCs nearby, you can kickstart the RP in battle by having them react to the heroes' deeds. "Look at Petracles! What a mighty warrior! We are truly blessed to witness such prowess in battle!" I sometimes do the same thing with a god or two who show up to watch the bloodletting. Nothing gets the in-character trash talking quite like one hero being praised above the others.

    - When someone spends divine favor, get the gods involved. The player should actually roleplay beseeching the god and you can have the god appear and interact with the hero in some way. Maybe Zeus steps in and tries to stop Athena from helping the hero, and the gods clash. "Daughter! Do not waste your affections on this weakling!"

    You can totally do bangs and hard choices in Agon. The easiest method is to have the gods give incompatible quests to the heroes. Which ones do you obey? What happens if you displease a god? There are other more subtle ways to twist things, too, of course. I once had a priest show up and claim that the heroes had been deceived by Poseidon and only by abandoning their quests and pledging their devotion to Athena would they ever be able to find their way home.

    Remember, the gods are fickle, petty, and self-serving. They can (and should) lie to the heroes and ask them to do the wrong thing. That village that just sheltered the heroes from the storm and gave them rest and food? Yeah, Hera found out Zeus has been sleeping with the goatherds daughter so she wants you to put them all to the sword and burn the place down. Zing!
