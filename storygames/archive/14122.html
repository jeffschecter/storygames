\n The game: to pair a piece of fiction writing advice, which could range from a simple 'fortune-cookie' snippet ("Write what you know") to something complex (a treatise on how to explore theme, perhaps?) and then find a mechanic, rule or technique from a story game that hits that...or invent one.

So, I might start with (from Gardner's *The Art of Fiction*, though there's no requirement to cite a book):

"...writing ... depends heavily on precision of detail."

And somebody else might say:

From p.10 of *In a Wicked Age*:

Whenever you describe something ... say exactly what makes sense to you, and then say at least one concrete detail. Don't stretch for it, it doesn't have to be startling or the coolest thing ever said, it just has to be some clear detail such as an observer might notice.

And then they throw out a piece of writer advice for us to map a technique to.

Totally fine for multiple people to map different mechanics/techniques to the same snippet.

So, I'll start, with (no idea who coined this):


"Story is character."

    \n I can't let this post go gentle into that good night - come on all, it'll be fun *and* educational - so much of the latest crop of RPG's owe a debt to Ron Edwards for looking at Egri and saying, "Hey, there's no game that does this, I'll make one" - I'm wondering if we can systematize the application of that process ... and have fun doing it.

    Maybe "Story is character" is too vague. I'll throw out some more. Can anybody think of game mechanics that go with these nuggets:

    * Write what you know.

    * Writing depends heavily on precision of detail. (There's more games than IaWA that hit this note.)

    * Write the kind of story that you like to read. (This one's also from Gardner)

    * When describing, use all five senses.

    * A story begins with someone wanting something ... even if it's just a warm bed to sleep in.
