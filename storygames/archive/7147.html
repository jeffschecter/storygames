    "Now. I would like each of you to roll two dice..."
    \nThis is a breach of protocol. The GM should roll the first dice.

    You can make the character creation process shorter by just assigning names and mentors, rather than rolling for them. If you like, roll for them before the game, put them on a list, then read off the list. This saves a little time, meaning the players need to do little more than assign scores at the start.

    I tend to use blank pieces of paper as character sheets for Lacuna. They work fine. (I also sometimes roll blank dice)

    \n Hi Ara,

    I did... last year, I think. I wanted to save time while at the table, so I made the characters up in advance. I put each sheet in a manilla folder. On the outside of the folder I wrote a title such as, "The Company Man," or "The Asshole." On the tab of the manilla folder I wrote the agents pseudonym. I then drifted the game by giving the characters what I called "Closed," and "Open" traits. The Open trait was written on a tri-folded piece of paper, it was basically a sentence to give the other players an outward conception of the character. The agents pseudonym was also written on the sheet. This was to make it easy for players to see each other's name.

    The open traits were written inside the manilla folder. Typically sideways or upside down, I may have changed my handwriting, etc. With more time I might have tried to do something more in line with the book with the bleeding text. The Open traits were things like; "I'm a professional" for the company man, or "I've got the gun, motherfucker," for the asshole. The three closed traits were things that put the player in conflict with their stated open trait, other players, the company, or the target. So the company man had; I follow orders, special agent miner is like a brother/ father to me (miner was going to be a probrable company traitor), and I'm starting to worry about the companies motives and methods.

    I gathered them and then sent them on the mission, in the somewhat standard traditional way, with perhaps one twist. The Virgina Tech massacre had just happened that week, so I set the target as the shooter. I wondered how people would play since the goal of the agents is nominally to cure criminals. Would they cure someone who had done something so despicable? The answer turned out to be yes.

    If I were to run a one-shot of Lacuna again, I'd approach it this way again, but I'd leave the stated ending out. I might still try to pit the players against a choice between healing someone they might rather see dead.

    I think the majority of folks enjoyed the game to one extent or another, with one likely exception. (Sorry Tim) What I learned is the gamemaster in Lacuna needs to push the players hard perhaps even to the point of positioning them, and to be more descriptive. Fortunately my game design is teaching me how to do description better, it's always been my most prominent weakness as a Gamemaster at a table, but not LARP for some strange reason.

    \n Hey Ara,

    You're welcome. If you do try writing traits, the most important thing, I think, is to leave them open ended so they are subject to the players interpretation. Expect them to see the traits differently than you, and to likely only focus in on maybe the one or two they feel is most important. You'll notice none of my examples prescribes any action, they're just short cuts to help someone quickly conceptualize a fictional being.

    You may already know all this, if so just stop at the period after you're welcome above. Maybe do that regardless.

    \n I second Jason's recommendation that Control should issue a scantron character sheet (PDF), #2 pencil, and 4-5 dice to each player, then have each player roll %ile dice 4 times and read them off. There are only three choices a player makes during chargen. Most of the character therefore could be done ahead of time but there's something about rolling the dice and marking your own bubbles that helps set the mood, I find.

    In "the full Lacuna experience" that's all that should be on the table: character sheets, pencils, dice. (And whatever the GM requires. I suppose.) I like to print the character sheet on blue paper, for that matter, and carry everything in a wine folder, but that could just be me.

    I encourage the players to keep the sheet of their surviving character, although so far I haven't had the chance to run a second mission for anyone. I'd certainly like to.
