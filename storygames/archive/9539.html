Do you keep character secrets secret from the other players in the game, or do you make everyone privy to the information OOC with an understanding that it is to be kept secret IC?

There are positive elements to both approaches. Not everyone wants to "firewall" their IC/OOC knowledge (thanks for that phrase Kev!), and sometimes people will slip up and say something in character that they shouldn't have (or do it willfully in the case of someone who is a jerk). On the other hand, table secrets can make the other players feel as though they are being left out of the game to some degree, especially if there is any sort of note passing or leaving the room to discuss things. In an experienced group I feel like everyone having the secret allows other players to inform their play such that it adds heightened tension and weight to scenes where that secret can come into play.
\n
I posited the question over on the Social Table Rules thread, and Jason pointed out (rightly!) that it maybe didn't belong there. So here it is.

Personally, I prefer an open game. I like to be allowed to interact with someone else's character secret during play, and I always feel a little left out when the player and the GM start passing notes to each other during the game. I also have horrible memories of the backstabby bullshit I've had to deal with in crappy DnD games, where the Rogue is stealing my shit behind my back and the LG guy who is secretly LE is setting us up for a fall without our consent.

Basically, I feel that table secrecy is only appropriate when there is competitive play happening at the table; otherwise, I think it's inappropriate and takes away from the collaborative play style that I prefer.

Thoughts?

    I'm surprised I'm in the minority with this.
    I'm the complete opposite of most that have posted here. Me and my groups enjoy about 5% Transparency. Here's what we do and why...



    #1.
    Rule Number One... In dealing with any and ALL secret information at the table each player must understand that said info should never be crucial or problematic to the game in such a way that the secret (when revealed) will break the fun of the campaign. We can't have anything that would break the game or PC group immediately. I encourage juicy secrets that would bring about lively role-playing, but it can't be so secret that it will drive the party apart.


    #2.
    All PC background secrets are kept private between GM and player.

    This even (sometimes) includes keeping character things like "Cohorts" or "Followers" (in D&D3.5) private/unknown to other players to BE another PC's character. The reason that I do this, is to introduce this NPC as an NPC first - then later when the other players do learn that this NPC is a direct stat connection to another PC we've already established that NPC as an "individual". Many times if we introduce an NPC that's connected to a PC from a stat "purchase" players tend to ignore the "importance" of that NPC since its "just his cohort" and wouldn't have any info we need or a reason to roleplay with. If established as an NPC first it creates a solid character first then is a fun surprise when realized as a stat-NPC.

    I tend to build WIKI Campaign Bibles for all my games... with that I make a section for PCs. On this section I preload all the info I can (basic name, race, etc..) for the players and make a special new page (click on the PCs name) so the player can input any background story, etc... I usually load a blank "template" of questions and such for format for each player to make it simple, plug-n-play. Generally this Wiki-page is open to all players to read and enjoy if they're interested in the creation/writing another player's done for their character. So... I also make a PRIVATE-EYES-ONLY Wiki page that each player can post private info from their background for myself and them to update. This page is linked to the player's NAME.

    Secret Player Character background, you ask? Yeah - if a player wants I welcome them to come up with their "dark past" or secrets that they're part of... from villains in their background to origins to organizations they're a part of, etc...


    #3.
    My groups enjoy keeping any info their character shouldn't know secret. So if there's a spot check... they don't want to know if their character wouldn't. If info is in a game book that their character wouldn't know, they don't want to read it and don't want other players "spoiling" it for them. This is primarily for the rush of role-playing with the true info their PC has and enjoying learning in-character.
    One "newer" thing we've incorporated by player request is to NOT tell the group aloud what spell you're casting or power you're using. Rather the player gives a fun description (sometimes a canny player can tell which spell's being cast from the description!) and at the same time hand a wipe-board to the GM to note: what the name of the spell is and any important info like Saving Throw needed, etc... Then if any other PC that has knowledge of such things wants to know what was just cast - they can roll a Spellcraft check (or whatever's appropriate) and if they get the roll we show them the wipe-board.
    So if something needs to be passed along privately from GM to player or even player to player, my group either: makes liberal use of 4-5 small marker-wipe-boards I have (I usually hand one to each player at the beginning of the game) OR we take the conversation to the hallway in front of my apt where we play. (see next point)

    \n#4.
    Hallway Conversations... I've made practice to enjoy "private scenes" with individual players to impart any info that's too long to write on a wipe-board OR to do any conversations that are private between PC and an NPC. This could be from pre-arranged meetings to gathering information in town. We walk into my hallway and role-play the scene as needed. There's an important RULE here - I do my best to promise that no single private conversation will last more than 5 minutes. And as I leave the other players I try to always leave them with something to do, consider, or a topic to role-play between players, etc... Or even just knowledge of what scene is coming next or to plan for action/initiative when I return. Many times I'll start the game in the hallway going from player to player individually for 2-3 minutes each to invite questions or to give info they should have to start the game session.
    Sometimes players will actually have hallway conversations together when info or a scene is private from another PC.


    #5.
    Blue-Books... Like Hallway conversations we keep these private to only the PCs involved. Blue-Books are scenes that we do between game sessions to flesh out any NPCs or situations that the player is interested in doing. If a player wants they can post a new page for the scene to take place on our Wiki. This private scene continues much like a play-by-post from there on. If multiple PCs are involved in the private Blue-Book and one player character needs private info (like info on a Knowledge skill they have) I will usually make a link to a new private page that details any info they have. Then they can use that info to talk from their voice and point of view if they want to share their knowledge.


    #6.
    Player Secrets from the GM.... This is a big one that most GMs wouldn't be comfy with. I invite my players to keep plans or events secret even from me as GM. If they know a battle's coming, I welcome them to chat privately to plan tactics, etc... There's two BIG caveats here: a. the secret info shouldn't be "game breaking", if the info is important enough that it may endanger the actual status of the game changning things in such a large way it cannot be secret - for example if the other players have decided to execute one of the other PCs... the GM needs to know! b. spells and effects that are game rule specific need to be told to the GM. Many times an NPC or PC may have ways of sensing/knowing what is happening, so we can't have a spell/effect secret if the character should be able to react.
    Why do I allow secrets from the GM? I really enjoy the ad-libbing for role-play and reactions at the game table. I've planned out many many of the ideas that happen from the plot, the world, NPCs, and other events - so in a way I get bored if I don't have surprises in-game. I love when the game takes a radical new direction and I have to "work" off the top-of-my-head to come up with responses. It's fun for me. The key here is as GM you really really need to know the NPCs well... you need to know their though processes and potential reactions. If you don't consider each NPC as a "real person" it's very challenging (and unfun) to ad-lib.



    so....
    FIREWALLING
    Absolutely, I understand that many/most players are mature enough to firewall info their character shouldn't react on... And even with my level of secrecy in games we STILL have a good share of firewalling that we use. But knowing that we CAN be mature enough to firewall and enjoying doing it is completely different.
    The majority of my players and myself LOVE the surprises that happen from the neatest most unexpected places (Many times I've heard player-to-player say: "holy crap! you can do *THAT*?!?!?! - awesome! heheheh...").
    Most of my players also tend to enjoy not needing to *think* what to firewall and not to firewall, not knowing simply makes it easier for them to enjoy digging into their character for that immersion flavor.


    Like I said... I'm really surprised that more Story Gamers don't actively enjoy keeping private stuff private. huh...
    Any thoughts on my notes as to why you like the idea or why not?

    \n My guiding principle is basically that the purpose of a secret is to eventually be revealed - otherwise, it's a useless bit of information that doesn't have any real impact on play. It doesn't really make any sense to me if one of the characters is secretly an assassin for the Lords of the Dark or whatever, and the whole campaign goes by without anyone ever knowing. I guess there's some additional payoff for that one particular player, but that's pretty exclusionary and self-serving, and hence I find it a tad distasteful most of the time. My tastes here translate to other media as well - mysteries are cool, but after a long enough period where pursuing those mysteries only leads to more mysteries, I get annoyed.

    So if the subject of secrets comes up, I basically tell the players that however they want to handle the transparency issue, they need to understand that the Big Reveal is inevitable - it will happen, because that's the reason why you have the secret in the first place. So then it's a matter of whether they want the transparency to frame scenes dramatically around the secret, or if they want things to be opaque so they can create a sense of surprise.

    Different secrets also tend to get treated differently - character background stuff tends to be opaque more often than plot info or other incidental stuff that comes up in the session. So, the other players might not know that Suzy used to be an assassin for the Lords of the Dark and it might not become public for a long time, but if her player decides during the game that she has a crush on Tim and doesn't want to tell anyone, that'll probably be transparent OOC info so that everyone can use that to help feed the story.

    Also, sometimes we do the Big Reveal in such a way that the information becomes transparent without it being ICly revealed to the other PCs. I'm personally rather fond of having the Big Reveal happen when an NPC who knows the secret talks privately (but the scene is run at the table, of course) with the PC about the secret. That gets you a little bit of the best of both worlds - the moment of surprise still happens, but then there's a period of time where the players can play around with framing scenes around it before their characters actually find out.

    So, yes, there's a lot of what you know about your character that other players will never find out. But very little of that has dramatic weight as public knowledge, so the main impact it'll have on play is how it informs your relationship with your character like you're suggesting. And yes, that can enrich play because it shows in your roleplay as a more nuanced personality. My preference is to be able to act on the information in play, but, y'know, I'm not the only one at the table.
