\n I've backed 12 Kickstarter projects. Some haven't reached their estimated shipping date yet; some are way overdue. Some have shipped, and made me happy; some shipped, but were disappointing, for objective or purely subjective reasons (as in, "this wasn't really what I wanted or hoped I would get"). Some were mistakes from me - for instance, backing a PDF when I should know I'm not actually into PDFs.

The ones I'm most happy with are:

Dog Eat Dog. Because I got a physical book, it looked cool when I read it, and I've played it twice and want to play it again.

Durance. Because I got a physical book, it looked cool when I read it, and I've played it twice and want to play it again.

I think both of these shipped around the expected date as well, although I'm not 100% sure, and that's not the main thing - I can wait a few weeks or a month or two.

How about you - what KS projects are your favorites?

    \n Yeah, it's basically gambling. You could just back projects that you like no matter what, but only if you're okay with losing that money in the event that the project just takes your cash and gives you nothing in return. If you have a limited budget or a limited tolerance for losing money, you should only back projects that you like AND genuinely think will be completed.

    Most of the tension I see in arguments over Kickstarter seems to rise from people not understanding what it is. Kickstarter is not a charity where you give money to feel good about supporting the artist, and it's not a store where you can purchase a product directly; you can treat it like either one for a while, but it'll only disappoint you in the end. It's a betting parlor, and whether you win depends on how good you are at figuring out the odds and how lucky the project you pick turns out to be.

    \nThat's undeniable, but what you get in return is more unique projects getting a wider airing and the funding to be bigger and better than they would otherwise. There are certainly risks - especially when it comes to computer games - but what you get in exchange is more variety and experimentation.

    Buying the millionth splat book in the vain hope that it might contain a shred of creativity is a pretty risky exercise as well. On balance, speaking as a consumer, I think the Kickstarter model suits me pretty well.
