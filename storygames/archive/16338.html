\n So my two big hobbies are story games and polyglottery, trying to learn as many foreign languages as I have time for. Naturally I'm interested in combining these hobbies. My forays into foreign language story games have so far yielded little results, mostly because I'm having trouble searching for them. I have found a little in French (and had a couple of wonderful runs of the great game Psychodrame), but in other languages, I've found little, except for some forums where mostly games in English are discussed.

So here's my question: What do you guys know about story games in other languages? Do people play them at all? Are there games written in these languages? Where can I find them?

I can answer for Swedish: There's quite a few story games in Swedish, and most can be found by asking on the forum on www.rollspel.nu.

    \n Ignoring translations, there are a few recent rpg projects in Finland that do qualify as "story games" by most measures. Let's see, in rough order of interest...

    Noitahovi (Witch Court) is a fantasy game with a light, robust and general sort of conflict resolution system. It's set in a milieu that's basically Finland in the middle ages if the Mongol horde had not retreated from conquering Europe, and if there was magic in the world, wielded especially well by women. The setting is pretty provocative in its misoandry and depiction of conquered people. The rules system is clever, international-quality Forgista stuff; to me it's mostly reminiscent of Dust Devils in terms of heft, scope and focus.

    Py\xf6re\xe4n p\xf6yd\xe4n ritarit (Fables of Camelot) is a game I designed with Sami Koponen last year. Might get it out in English at some point, but for now it's only available in Finnish in its final form. It's a lightweight, quick drama game about the knights of the round table; pretty smart in places, even if I say so myself.

    Hounds of the Sea is in English, but not available from common outlets outside Finland. It's a storytelling game about pirates, a bit like e.g. Zombie Cinema or Fiasco in terms of the toolbox and nature of play. The game comes in the form of a deck of cards that is used in character creation, story-inventing and conflict resolution. The general tone is somewhat comic, and the game is intended for pretty aimless improvisation more than focused Aristotelian drama.

    Aulos and Invitation to Love from Karoliina Korppoo are very small press, out of print Forgean story games along the lines of Once Upon a Time and such. Her game design is very focused on plotting story, so much so that there is not much internal character viewpoint in these games. Both are card-based or near so.

    T\xe4hti is a freeform roleplaying game about playing a girl band in a somewhat Cyberpunkish near-future setting. The "freeform" means that it's a fair systemless campaign resource; the only systemic suggestion aside from delineating GM and player roles is that you could use fortune cookies to gain oracular inspiration in finding a direction for tense scenes.

    \n In Italy, in addition to what Ivan and Lorenzo said, there are some other italian published story-games I can think of:
    \n
    \n- Elar, by Davide Losito. Probably the first published game who explicitly references to Forge's theory. It's a steam-fantasy with lots of steam and lots of fantasy, based on a card-resolution system.
    \n
    \n- Ravendeath, by Iacopo Frigerio. A game about revenge, that goes from Medea to The Crow to Kill Bill. It uses a board \xe0-la Otherkind (or Psi*Run).
    \n
    \n- Aegis, by Alberto Tronchi. A game that recreates the feeling of such games as the Final Fantasy series, with tactical combats and belief-driven characters.
    \n
    \n- Piombo!, by Daniele Lostia and Andrea Vigiak. Set in the Years of Lead ('70s) in Italy, supports the creation of stories about gangsters and criminals against the state and the police.
    \n
    \nThere are a lot of other good games, but they're not fully baked or published yet, such as (just to name the most complete) L'amore ai tempi della guerra (Love in the Time of War), Il posto delle fragole (Wild Strawberries), La stirpe dei re (The Kings' Lineage), Moonfolk, 21 Guns, Il rosso e il nero (Red and Black), Venus in Furs, Dawn of a New Tomorrow ...
    \n
    \nThen, we have some really good live-scenario writers, such as Susi Ansaloni, Andrea Castellani, Oscar Biffi, the Volto di Giano gruop, Raffaele Manzo and basically everyone who identifies themselves as a member of the Flying Circus movement.
    \n
    \nThere are some other indie published games, by they don't qualify so much as "story-games", and they often actively separete themselves from Forge's activities.

    Posted By: GB SteveIs there some mileage in the fact that Germany and France have (albeit small) roleplaying industries of their own? Both countries produce mainstream games in their own languages and so perhaps there is less market available for story games. In the US this doesn't matter so much because the market is just that much bigger that a small subculture can thrive anyway. The UK gets most of its games from the US and most UK companies sell more to the US than the UK (with some European penetration too).
    \nMarket size is certainly a factor, seeing as there are not only much fewer German native speakers than English native speakers, but also, role-playing as a hobby is probably less common in Germany than in the US. Also, until 2004, we were mostly unaware of all the crazy stuff going on in the US and the Nordic countries. Another major factor is that the majority of German role-players are very used to buying products in English language, so the niche for people who would buy something "because it's in German" is rather small.
