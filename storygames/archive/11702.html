\n I played Fiasco two days ago, with four other players. We had a good time, and created an appropriately twisted tale, complete with Southern accents and a baby everyone was trying to either steal, give away, or just keep secret in order to preserve various reputations. One character, a young lady who was the local crime lord's daughter, was arguably the center of attention, and pretty much every character had a crush on her, to various degrees. This was particularly humorous as the girl wasn't all that likeable--really, she had few if any redeeming qualities. The baby was hers, of course: a result of a one-night stand with a local miner.

The setup portion of the game was fantastic, and I don't think anyone had any trouble figuring out what goes where. We had plenty to work with, right away.

In terms of the people involved:

I've gamed a couple of times with the facilitator/organizer of the game (if you're reading, please feel free to say hi!), but the other three players were people I was either meeting for the first time or barely knew. So, there wasn't a "existing group" dynamic going on between us.

A few questions came up, however, and I found a few aspects of play difficult to manage, so I'd like to ask how you (Jason, other Fiasco fans) handle these problems, if and when they come up.

1. One of the players had played the game at Dreamation and remembered playing Resolve scenes by drawing a die at the start. So, a player would say, "I want to Resolve a scene; you guys Establish. I'm taking the white die, so it's a scene that will go well for my character. Let's go!"

I found this really strange--why would we want to know the outcome before we play the scene? So, during the "intermission", I glanced through the book and found that the text and examples all seem to instruct us to play out the scene first and have the Resolving player only choose the white or black die at the moment of resolution (whenever that might be during the scene). This seemed much more interesting and robust to me, so I brought it up to the group and we agreed to play Act Two that way.

Which is correct? The latter definitely felt better, for me, but I'm not sure what the other players thought.

2. When Establishing scenes, it felt pretty awkward to decide who was going to say what and when. Generally, whoever had an idea first (or whoever was loudest) tended to go ahead and Establish a scene. Once a scene was under way, things tended to go well: we would all collaborate to introduce other characters and play NPCs. But the initial framing was difficult, at least for me, because I found myself worrying about whether I should speak or stay silent almost every time. On one hand, I want to contribute to the game, to share my ideas. On the other hand, if I Established the last scene, or was a major player in it, I feel like I should stay out of the way and let a quieter player take over. But if I've been quiet for a while, suddenly I might find everyone looking at me and waiting for me to say something when I rather have no ideas at all. I found myself wishing the game made it a little clearer for me to know when to talk and when to keep quiet, just so I could jump into a role and play it to the hilt, whether as protagonist, NPC, or scene framer, without worrying about whether I was hogging someone's else's spotlight.

3. In general, the lack of strong mechanical support for scenes made it difficult to bring conflict and adversity into the scenes (and we often didn't at all--is that how the game is intended to be played, or did we miss something?). Even the Resolving player, by assigning a black or white die, didn't seem to make much of a difference. A scene would pretty much be over, and the dice tended to be assigned kind of in retrospect. When you play the game, do you explicitly locate a conflict, and discuss a little about what the black and white dice might mean (similar to setting stakes)? We didn't, which made the die choice often rather inconsequential. ("Did that scene go well for your character?" "Well, yeah, I suppose we could say it did! I'll take the white die.")

After the intermission, I suggested that we try to create conflict a little more explicitly when Establishing scenes (as suggested in the book, according to what little I read there during the intermission). However, even that didn't happen very reliably. Without a sense of clearly divided authority, everything that was narrated (except for speech and action in-character) was being entered into the fiction by concensus, which worked fine for us but often meant that disagreements between players led to conflicting ideas being discarded for a compromise or third way. Instead of creating any conflict in the story, we tended to collaborate until all the conflict had been bled out of the resulting scene. It was difficult to find a space where we could "push" against each other in the fiction. There were a few times I tried to create conflict by playing an NPC aggressively, for instance, hoping to create a conflict that the die choice would resolve. Almost every time, however, narration from other players ended up sidestepping that conflict and focusing on something else. It felt more like we were all trying to fit in a piece of the story we wanted to tell, and there was no easy way to turn that into a source of dramatic tension, so we tended to drop anything that didn't create instant concensus.

I found myself really looking forward to the Tilt and the final outcome as a point to get back on the same page and be surprised by unexpected results (which both managed very well).

3. With those things in place, the game started moving further and further towards what someone here once described as "storyboarding": collaborative decisions made about what might be cool in the story that left little or no need to actually play out or narrate what was happening in the fiction. In the second act, there were several scenes that didn't see any character-play or narration at all. There were some cool events and plot twists, but no dynamic tension at the table, as the outcome was generally already settled ("How about your character gets distracted by a maid he used to sleep with, and after, uh, events transpire, she's smoking and sets off the TNT accidentally?" "OK, great idea!").

In general, this was an issue for me: it often felt like we were creating scenes without any constraints in place, without any uncertain outcomes. Just collaboratively deciding what would happen next, and throwing out creative ideas. Now, there's nothing wrong with that, but it's very difficult to create any dynamic tension in the story while playing this way--maybe that's not part of the intended style of play for Fiasco? I'm curious.

I suspect that familiarity with the game would give us a little more to work with in terms of strategy--choosing dice based on the outcomes we would like to see for certain characters--which we began to see a little bit towards the very end of the game, but even so it didn't feel like the die choice changed anything in the scenes we were playing. It was never clear to me, as a player, what a certain die choice (black or white) implied in the fiction, in terms of a particular scene, since, with four or five narrators, it was difficult to focus in on one point of tension. One player might narrate what I see as conflict (some bad men approaching!), but the resulting narration of the scene could easily sidestep that entirely: "Oh, the character goes into the other train car, but then realizes she doesn't have a ticket on her."

In other words, sometimes it felt like we were "storyboarding" right through the game. How can you avoid that while playing Fiasco? For instance, in the second act, we ended up with four or five black dice remaining. That meant that we all knew our next three of four scenes would not go well for the main characters. So, I'm Establishing a scene for my character, and hoping to see how the other players Resolve it... but we all already know it won't go well. While I understand how this models aspects of the genre, it made the narration of the scene very hard not to "storyboard". Because, even as I'm Establishing the scene, I already know it must end a certain way.

So, overall, fun game (don't be mislead by the number of words I've used to describe the problems!) but a few points of awkwardness left me wondering if we missed or misapplied some elements of the system.

What do you think? Has anyone else found the lack of mechanical support in free play difficult to manage?

    \n Hey Paul, this is great. I'm sorry your game was rocky but your thoughtful questions will make the next one way better.

    1. You are right, the other dude is wrong.

    2. The game very, very deliberately leaves you with lots of space to sort this out yourself, based on what your group is comfortable with. If you aren't playing with established friends it's more important to either strongly facilitate or hash this stuff out early.

    3. It is totally OK to have color scenes, where at the end you need to make a more or less aesthetic judgment about positive or negative outcomes. Some groups really enjoy crafting these, others go for the throat constantly. If you were dissatisfied with color scenes, or your group wanted juicy conflicts but wasn't getting them, hopefully the game wasn't getting in your way.

    3. I've seen "storyboarding" a lot, actually. It is something akin to pre-play that is best avoided, as a general rule.

    \nThe die choice has to map to the fiction. If you are resolving and want that white die, you pick it up at some point and that's a signal for how the rest of the scene should go, hopefully after being presented with a situation crafted by your friends that you really, really wanted to go negative on (that's their job in establishing, to make the optimal or obvious choice unattractive).

If it is obviously boiling down to whether Selma lets your dude ride off with the baby and I pick up a black die, and everybody nods gleefully, we can finish playing out the scene knowing your guy is not leaving with the kid.

    \n The person or group responsible for grabbing a die get to decide where the pivot of the scene is. They do it by picking up a die. If I am understanding, the guys you were playing with were not correct.

    In my experience what usually happens, at least at first, is that a scene will continue until we can't go on until we understand which direction it needs to go. Do you get the baby?     I think the game rewards a certain flexibility - even when you establish, scenes may not go where you want them to. Sometimes an obvious conflict just blooms, and it is usually best to go with it.

    \n If I may extend some of the advice about Outcome: if a black die is grabbed and the Outcome is not negative, push harder; if a white die is grabbed and the Outcome is not positive, pull harder. If you're finding that your Outcomes are too value neutral, someone in the scene isn't pushing or pulling hard enough for his or her Need(s). (Reminds me a LOT of how Best Interests work in IAWA, actually.)

    And remember that there's a metagame to die accumulation: you DO NOT want an even mix of colors, if you want your PC to finish the game positively (and vice versa). I find that aspect of die passing and claiming to be the most fascinating aspect of Fiasco--the "killer app" that keeps it from being a slightly structured freeform system with a situation generator.

    \n Hmm, that's a tall order, Paul. I can give an example but I have to reiterate - this is all group dependent, all social contract stuff. What's bombproof for me and my crew may be shaky nonsense for yours.

    We played last night, actually, so it is fresh in my mind. It's the first scene in the game and Joel goes first. He's playing a 16 year old girl, I am playing her 40 year old manager, our relationship is "Romance - manager and employee". We don't know what's up with that but agree that it is going to be creepy. Joel wants to establish, and sets up a scene - it's closing time, there's a customer interested in a Cartier watch, and Ginette, his character, wants to leave. "I want Ginette to make you jealous" he adds. OK. We start the scene, I pull in Mike to play the customer and I collar Ginette and scold her for brushing the rich lady off. The lady is impatient and affronted. My manager tries to make nice. Ian picks up a white die and we all silently agree, it gets handed to Joel (he passes it off to Ian as I recall, since it is Act One). I know it is going to go bad for me and good for Joel. Joel pulls in Greg to play his character Benji, whose relationship with Ginette is "Family: Cousins" and whose Need is "To get laid: To hurt someone". It was that kind of game. I start an argument and lecture Ginette about customer service, but it is plain that she has me where she wants me - utterly under her sway. Benji shows up - her cool older cousin, to give her a ride home. They talk about all the fun they are going to have partying with their friends and blow me off completely.

    So in this scene Joel pretty much outlined a goal right off the bat, and we played toward it, not knowing how it would show up or if it would be successful or not for his character. By the end we had a very solid relationship dynamic and I knew my guy was a frustrated, creepy milquetoast.

    We had other scenes where nothing was spelled out, where we discovered what the character wanted in play. We had zero color scenes, because my group is obsessively conflict-oriented.

    \n Dave, everything about the game is intentionally wide open where it intersects with social contract decisions. My local crew plays one way; a consensus way emerges in convention play that's a little different, your group will have its own way. A hard-charging approach will not break the game and neither will a more contemplative one.

    Also, on pushing hard - there are no stakes. There is no opposition (more detail on pages 32-33). There's not even winning or losing, which is a hugely important and somewhat subtle distinction. I use positive and negative outcome very deliberately, because it is not about winning and losing. You can, and often do, get exactly what you want and still have a negative outcome.
