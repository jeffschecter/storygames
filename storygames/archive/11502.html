    \n The GM moves are the things you always do. Not just when they fail a roll. A failed roll opens the door for your hardest move, right now, is all.

    This isn't a huge deal, because the GM moves are things you're accustomed to doing as a GM anyway. Still, keep it in mind.

    Barf forth Apocalyptica!
    This is so, so important. I kind of skimped on that during a few scenes in the game last night, and in my memory now those moments are flat and lifeless. Where was the scarcity? The depraved practices? The twisted world?

    You know how, when you run 3:16, you need to include all those color details about bullshit military protocols, kill-crazy officers, etc.? It's just like that. It's not just "Rothchild's house" it's "A stack of shipping containers all welded together and bored out from the inside to make doors and rooms. The fancy room is covered all over in old parachutes dyed crazy colors."

    It's not a one-roll conflict game.
    This can be tough to remember if you're used to "one and done" games like PTA or BW. Apocalypse World is action-and-consequences in the style of Poison'd. Moment-to-moment stuff. So maybe you try to manipulate someone and miss the roll. The GM makes her hardest move right now. Often, this means the opportunity for manipulation is gone. But sometimes the opportunity remains. If nothing is stopping you from trying again, then you can. The failed roll doesn't automatically mean, "this is over now."

    This is sometimes where escalation happens, too. You try to seduce Roark, and it goes badly. That doesn't mean Roark can't be seduced, it means that you fucked it up and there were consequences. But then maybe he's still right there so you try again. As you approach, looking all lustful again, Roark pulls his pistol and sticks it in your face. "No. We're done here. Kindly fuck off." What do you do? Still angling for the seduction? Cool... so you're acting under fire then?

    That's just another way of saying "If you do it, you do it, so make with the dice."

    \n Well, don't forget, making moves is the primary way to make advancement. Remind them "Hey, if you try to manipulate this dude, you get an improvement circle." Hopefully the tasty carrot can lure them out of their shells.

    Also, if they do something that would fall under a move, make them roll for it. Threaten a dude, even in an implied fashion? That's going aggro, roll hard. Do something slightly risky? That's acting under fire, roll cool. If they do it, they have to do it.

    Also also, if they're avoiding making moves, they're not doing anything. In addition to making hard moves when they fail a roll, you get to make hard moves when you set something up and they don't do anything to stop it. You can't escape the harsh realities of Apocalypse World by sitting in your home and hiding all day. Announce Future Badness is one of my favorite MC moves.

    In the end, remember half your job is to make their characters' lives not boring. It's not quite exactly the same thing as "get them to make lots of moves". It's also not the same as "heap unrelenting badness upon them at every opportunity". There's potential overlap, it's true, but too much of the latter especially will get boring on its own, too. Don't forget those intermittent rewards on top of the fuckery! It's right there in the principles. They're good stuff.

    \n Okay, I'll say why "create fit challenges" isn't an MC job.

    First, it conflicts with one your agendas: Make Apocalypse World seem real. In other words, the composition and disposition of an enemy gang is determined according to the logic of the circumstances in the fiction, not according to what would make a good challenge for the PCs.

    Second, something happens when a character faces challenge after challenge, overcoming again and again. They become a hero. (Luke makes that case pretty convincingly in Mouse Guard, I think.) Apocalypse World is not a game for emulating heroic fiction. Any notions of heroism are nothing but dim memories in Apocalypse World.

    So, yeah. Don't create challenges. Make apocalypse world seem real. Make the PCs lives not boring. Follow the principles, play your threats, and make your moves instead.

    \n I think that Steve's question is key: "So how have you guys used MC moves?"

    Steve, your examples are right on. I do all sorts of just those sorts of things.

    My favorite use of "announce future badness" is the split second: "the sentry's raising the whistle to his lips, you have a split second before he blows it. What do you do?" I make that move all the time, it's a majority of my moves. It's a perfect setup move: the PC moves in response, or else she doesn't and I get to make a hard followup.

    There was this time a session or two ago when, let's see, Vega tried to manipulate Rose (something like that) and blew the roll. I said to myself, "self, obviously my move is to separate them - but I bet they're about to do that for me." They talked for ten seconds more, had a falling out, and one of them stormed out of the room. Afterward, Vega's player (Rob Bohl) was like, "so that time I blew that roll ... what was your move? You didn't say anything." And I said "it was to separate you, and I knew you were going to do it for me, so I let you." Rob wasn't wholly satisfied with this as a game mechanic, but who cares? It was my move to make as I saw fit.

    Aha, yeah, here's something you may find helpful, or at least liberating: your job as MC, and thus the purpose of your moves, is to make the characters' lives interesting to you.

    \n Here are some of my MC moves from recent games. The ones that say "what do you do?" are moves I made as part of the flow of play. Moves that don't say that are hard moves; follow-ups to failed rolls or when my plans came together without interference.

    I'm also including what my move immediately lead to in play.

    Announce future badness:
    "The sniper adjusts his aim, tracking from you over to Amy. What do you do?"
    "Yeah, whatever. I don't give a shit about Amy. Fire away." Blam. Amy takes a .308 round through the throat. Inflict harm as established. Amy is an NPC, so of course, I'm looking through crosshairs. Literally this time! Ha.

    "Crine gets into the skimmer. It's driven by this huge brute wearing a black-painted hockey mask. There are black feathers sticking out of it, like a headdress. Black feathers all over the skimmer's roll-cage, too."
    "The nomad tribes don't come this far West, do they?"
    "Never have before, no. What do you do?"
    "Back off the way I came and head down to the train station." I take quiet notice of the fact that she doesn't tell anyone about Crine and the Nomads working together, which allows one of my plans to come together without interference.

    "You catch Clarion with a far-off look in her eyes. She's opened her brain, for sure. She's whispering to herself, 'We are the lantern and she is the light.' Over and over. What do you do?" (that's also announce off-screen badness at the same time.)
    "I grab her and try to shake her out of it."
    "Okay, when you touch her, you open your brain, just like that. Roll+weird."
    (That roll is kicked off because of a custom move I have from one of my threats.)

    Trade Harm for Harm (as established):
    "You gun it and crash into them. They lose control and slam into a rock like boom! Your front-end is all fucked up, the breaks don't work, and you're still going like hell. You have a piece of metal sticking out of your leg (2-harm). What do you do?"
    "Shit! I try to pull myself free and roll clear."
    "So you're acting under fire. Roll+cool."

    Offer an opportunity with or without a cost:
    "You sneak over to spy on Rothchild and Crine, but Miller is right there. He'll probably spot you. What do you do?"
    "Whatever, I have 3-hold Hypnotic on him anyway. I don't try to avoid him, I just want to hear what Rothchild and Crine are saying."
    "Miller slips over while you're spying and tries to grope you in the darkness."
    "Not now, you jackass."
    "He gets a little glassy eyed and mechanically steps away from you. Reduce your hold to 2."

    "Pellet has crude metal shutters rigged on his windows, but they're wide open right now. You're not sure where that shot came from. What do you do?"
    "I make a run for it and slam the shutter closed."
    "So you're acting under fire. Roll+cool."
    "6. Fuck."
    "You're halfway across the room when the next shot rings out and then you're face down on the floor with your chest pounding. Blood is everywhere. 2-harm ap."

    Make them buy:
    "I don't know," Lala says. "This is the last of my antibiotics." She eyes the fresh pack of cigs in your pocket. What do you do?

    "Yeah, you want information and I want some clean goddamn food for a change. Get used to disappointment." What do you do?

    "I hear you're making the Lighthouse run. I got fuel, you know."

    Tell them the possible consequences and ask:
    "You can pull a bootlegger turn here and lose the drone in the twisty streets. When you turn, you'll be facing it for a second, though. It will certainly capture an image of your face. Or you can just keep on to the clinic and keep your back to it. It will follow you all the way there, though. What do you do?"
    "Crap. Yeah, I need to ditch the thing. I make the turn and it gets my image, then."

    Put someone in a spot:
    "The black wind nomads call up from the courtyard, 'Bring forth the murderers of our brethren and we will spare your lives!' Pellet and Chack look at each other and nod. They turn on Li. Li says, 'No. No, fuck you guys! No one is handing me over to nobody. Stay the fuck back!' She brings her assault rifle around and levels it at the room in general. What do you do?"
    "This is SO not my problem. I look around for a way to slip out of here."
    "So you're reading a charged situation, cool. Roll+sharp."

    Capture Someone:
    "When you get back, the fight is over. There are some dead nomads in the courtyard."
    "I run upstairs to Pellet's workshop. Is Li still there?"
    "No. Chack's body is. He's been shot in the face several times by an assault rifle. Li's gone."

    "You step forward to finish him off, but the look in his eyes makes you hesitate. The hands of the cultists swarm over your body and drag you down into darkness." (that's a hard move after a failed roll)

    Custom Threat Move: When Crine is hunting you and you're out in the open, roll+sharp:
    Player: "12. Ha! Suck it."
    MC: "The hunter drone passes by, oblivious, then goes into a standard search pattern over Silvertree. Looks like it's here to stay. Those things usually travel in packs, right? What do you do?" (That's also announce future badness and announce off-screen badness. Three moves in one!)

    Questions welcome.

      \n Hi Ian,

      1) So, the ambulance. First, the MC doesn't take your stuff away. That's weirdly puppet-master-y. What happens is, when someone makes a move, they roll for it, and on a miss, the MC makes the hardest move they can right then in that fictional circumstance (conforming to the agenda and principles).

      There are circumstances where "They drive off with your ambulance," would be the obvious and best hard move, so that could happen, yeah. As could, "The rocket slams into the side of the ambulance and explodes. There's fire and burning metal everywhere." Again, that shouldn't be some secret plan of the MC's to take anything away from the player. It's all action and consequences, telegraphed at every step by other MC moves (announce future badness, etc.). So, when the rocket explodes, everyone goes, "Dammit!" but they don't go, "What the fuck?"

      Second, here's a motto from AW: "There are no status quos in Apocalypse World." This means that every situation, institution, condition, or circumstance is always up for grabs. The rules don't enforce anything except the consequences of the immediate action in the fiction. So, when the Blade Brothers take your van, well, you can damn well go take it back. When the rocket explodes and turns it into burning wreckage, you can try to salvage it. Or scavenge for parts and build a new one. Or steal one. Or whatever. There's no such thing as "it's just gone."

      Also, the rules in AW are prescriptive and descriptive. That is, if you choose "get a vehicle" as an improvement option, then you get one and you detail what kind and how. Likewise, if you undertake a series of actions in the fiction that results in you getting a car, then you get a car mechanically -- no need to "buy it" with an improvement or whatever. Same goes for gangs, followers, holdings, whatever. Think of it this way: It takes 5 highlight-stat rolls to earn a new character improvement like a gang. So you can make those 5 rolls doing whatever, then buy the gang as improvement and then say how they show up in the fiction. Or, you can go out into the world and build up a gang by taking actions (and making rolls...), then you also have that gang mechanically, same as if you got them with experience.

      \n 2) The mob. This has two parts.

      First, looking through crosshairs.
      The GM is looking at this NPC, Compton, and she's like (to herself), "Compton, your shit is about to get fucked up. Kiss your ass goodbye!" But then, how? How is Compton gonna be torn down, humiliated, murdered, and buried in the poisoned ground? It's an ongoing question. And then, while mulling this over, a PC strolls in and is like, "You know what? I am gonna tear this fucker Compton down." Oh, the smile on the GM's face. Whew! There's a load off the mind. She has to dish out cruel fates for, what, 30 NPCs? But now, for this one, Compton, she can sit back and fly easy. Here's a friendly neighborhood PC strolling in to do it for her. Now it's time to enjoy the show.

      So that's the main thing.

      Second, the Hocus and his move.
      "When you speak the truth to a mob, roll+weird..." is what it says. It doesn't tell you what a mob is because then you'd use that instead of thinking about it in terms of your specific circumstances. Also, much like "a charged situation," a mob is a thing that can spring into being conceptually when it's named, even if you didn't think it was there already. This is a mob? Yeah... I guess it is, now. And you have truth to speak? Fuck yes it is totally a mob. Or a mob in the making -- a little pocket of mob-ness inside the crowd. And remember that first part about looking through crosshairs. The GM is already trying to come up with a way to ruin Compton's life. And you say you want to incite (part or all) of his gang into a mob with your truth-speaking? Yes, yes, a thousand times yes.

      This doesn't automatically violate "make Apocalypse World seem real," either, no matter what happened in a previous scene. There are no status quos in Apocalypse World. Compton having a good hold over his gang before doesn't mean he has good hold over them now. Especially not with a wild-eyed hocus shouting Truth in their faces.

      I mean, this whole moment of Frenzy-making is, like, the whole deal for a Hocus. It's the very exact thing that makes his life not boring. It's the one sure-fire thing out of all the possible moments that is going to electrify the game for that player's character. It is a golden opportunity, in other words.

      Why on Earth would that get blocked, or nitpicked, or shut-down? "Make Apocalypse World seem real" is not a tool to enforce continuity for the sake of continuity and it is especially not a tool for denying players full expression of their characters. "Make Apocalypse World seem real" means, when the Hocus decides to Frenzy the mob of the biker king, GM, step forward with your principles to wrap all that up in a nice warm blanket of interesting fictional causality so that it's cool and exciting. It's an opportunity to contribute -- to barf forth apocalyptica, to make everyone human, to respond with fuckery and intermittent rewards -- to make the player's character's life not boring.

      Make sense?
