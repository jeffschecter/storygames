\n Todd L is going on about his experience with a game of Fiasco from a year ago or so over on this thread over here: "What's the Best Way"?

Shit is still getting sorted out, and likely about half a dozen good topics will emerge from that all along the lines of "Lines/Veils", "Cultural Appropriation", and "Not Being an Asshole". This topic is to address one interesting bit that I actually have implemented with success in my games.

I've never really had to work the "No Jerry Lewis 'Chinaman Impressions, please!" at the table, because most folks I've gamed with find it equally as abhorrent. I also don't specifically say, "No puppies will be boiled in this game, that's DEFINITELY a LINE for me, guys!" and so on.

But hey, in gaming with strangers, it has happened once or twice. It's usually disarmed right away with a "DUDE. SERIOUSLY." Applied with the same impact and force of a Cesar Millan "TSST!"/ear flick. Breaking the mood like that has... (remembering) yeah, always ended that sort of thing: Sheepish apology, "No problem", re-description, game back on track. Cultural dumbness averted.

So how do you use immersive moonspeak at the table?

* Substitution: Todd mentioned in a whisper that one of the players, after fumbling with well-meaning ching-chong'ing (boo!), started to basically speak in Spanish to the other players when the character (the Chinese bride) spoke in Chinese.
I've never done that: I speak Japanese and a little German, Russian. I never thought of speaking in one of these languages to convey the feel of someone speaking to you in yet another language... but it seems more respectful.

Here's some that I've actually done, usually with Japanese or Chinese characters: Most of them involve stepping back the "true immersion" in order to quickly convey something usually in third person.

ONE PERSON ONLY SPEAKS ANOTHER LANGUAGE

* "I'm talking to you earnestly, waving my arms because it's a crisis, and of course you don't understand me."
* Same, with a die-roll mechanic: "I'm talking to you earnestly, waving my arms because it's a crisis, and...
[ROLL]
"...oh, it looks like you got a partial success. You pick out that I'm talking about a Crisis, in town, involving our mutual friend Cyrus. But you can't quite get anything beyond that."

MANY CHARACTERS SPEAK THE SAME ASIAN LANGUAGE

* Ancient Old Proverb Man
So at my table, if you want to play the role of the proverb-spitting wise master (hell a trope in asian stories as well), you don't squint, you don't talk like Jerry Lewis or whatever. You can drop real proverbs. You can make up proverbs. That still obeys the trope without Going There.
Another option on the same front:
"I relay a common proverb from our own remote village that involves... uh... birds? That basically means 'Don't disobey your elders unless you are sure you are in the right.' It's one that these guards won't understand, but you and I do."
Okay! Maybe not as good as knowing real proverbs, but many proverbs on the net are bullshit* anyway. If you can learn some real ones in prep for a long game, great! I'll drop fanmail or give you bonuses for authenticity. But that length is not needed.

* Actual Speaking
I don't know a lot about Chinese, although I do know that there are several names for people in power (their given name, their public name, their "throne name", their polite name used about them after their death, etc).
Japanese has several forms of speech. Friendly, Plain, Polite, Humble, and basically Super-Humble (this last one only in like samurai settings and the like, not used today). There's even side-forms of speech, like "Yakuza-speak" which has its own words and even grammar. It's really very interesting. But no one is going to learn graduate school level Japanese in order to play 5 sessions of L5R.
But people at my J-themed games get the "forms of speech" 20 second spiel, above.

So in my games, the players pretty much default to their character types, and don't have to keep track of what they say to whom, it's assumed that they do it properly to form: When they speak to a lord, they don't roll, they simply use Humble Speak without declaration. If they want to give an impassioned speech inviting battle with an enemy to a lord's council, and the character is a lowly merchant or squire or something, they'd roll.

But if they want to do something Telling or out of the ordinary, then they describe using another form of the language.

* "My samurai... well, you don't know if it's just because he's drunk, or because he's indicating something, but he's talking to you very friendly-like. Not using polite form, like he always does in public."

* "Menou addresses the children using Humble speak. They're probably very confused, perhaps can barely understand, right?"
"Yep. No one ever talks to kids using Humble speak. They actually start to laugh at you, in a warm and friendly way though."
"Cool. I don't pick up on it, because Menou's never left the palace. She keeps using the most humble language she knows."

* "Jasper stops talking in broken speak like a typical foreigner when you come out of the restroom stall: "Seriously, you have to shut the fuck up about that company, or something's going to happen to you. Got it, Hiroyuki?" It really freaks you out, actually, because not only is he speaking fluent Japanese, he's got a perfect Yakuza accent. He's obviously been running with them for years."

CONCLUSIONS

So, those are a few of the ways that for campaigns modern and ancient, using foreign (most notably asian, since that's where all the shitty stereotyping usually comes from) accents or playing in foreign-seeped settings, we can utilize language to great gaming effect (either as background or as tools of immersion) without resorting to "pretending to speak a language that you don't know" aka "Ching-chong Ting-tong Ling-long".

If others have had success with real table techniques, I'd love to hear about it. Please post!

-Andy

    \n One aspect that's hard for many people is foreign names. When I play stuff involving China, I tend to push people to go ahead and "translate" the names into English, except maybe for surnames. So you have a character who's like: "Ox-Strong Lu" or "Elegant Hua." That's a lot easier for most folks than "Lu Zhongniu" or "Hua Xiuqing."

    And then I also try to stick with common surnames that people already know how to pronounce in English. "Han" or "Lu" or "Li" or "Huang" or "Ren" or "Wen." Nobody is named "Qiu" or even "Zhang" (one of the commonest surnames). Yeah, it's limiting, but most Americans don't realize that "Liu" and "Lu" are pronounced very differently. Likewise "Zang" and "Zhang." Or even that "Beijing" is pronounced exactly like it looks, with a hard J.

    \n Matthijs, as best I understand, it is about avoiding insulting, hyper-stereotyped gibberish speak.

    In my experience, respectful attempts to add atmosphere with accents has always been appreciated, even when those accents are far from accurate.

    But when done in a way that smacks of insult, they can be hurtful.

    I have a friend in a game right now who is doing an amazing job of playing a pesudo-Japanese character for humor without being disrespectful. Basically he's doing the "as a player I'm choosing to have my character misunderstand what you just said and interpret it incorrectly" thing as his primary humor angle, which works whenever you have cultural differences between characters, whether those differences involve an accent or not. Plus, he's very much a, what do you call it, Japophile? He has learned some Japanese and is reasonably well read on Japanese culture, both modern pop culture and older mythology. So he's obviously dripping with respect as a player, even as he uses his character for culture-driven comedy.

    I think respect is central to the whole thing. If you respect the culture you are portraying (or pseudo-culture) then it comes out in your portrayal. If you don't have much of an understanding of that culture, then that will show and will likely come across as disrespectful.

    \nYeah. Not sure about your-abouts, but here in the US, when there is Asian RPG Playing going on, sometimes racism/stereotyping raises its head. People doing "fake Chinese/Japanese accents" mainly because they have seen it on TV, less so because they think it's funny (typical "Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by stupidity.")

    Also, recently, we've been more sensitive to it due to big dumb stuff, usually involving the catchphrase "Ching Chong" (see Rosie O'Donnell and "Ching Chong Ding Dong DANNY DEVITO"). Which goes back to basically shitty racist Hollywood of the 40s-60s where the Chinese stereotype was played up (see the Jerry Lewis clip above): Basically like blackface, but with Chinese instead of black people. At the time it was more China than other asian countries (save for war posters you might see about Japan from the WWII era). So to make a long, complicated history short, basically if someone squints their eyes and talks like a Hollywood 'Chinaman from the 50s or uses "fakey Chinese language", most folks in the know would react just as pained as if they were watching a white person dance around in vaudville-era blackface.

    I'm sure Europe and other countries have a different take on the issue. For example, in Japan, you can easily do a "Chinese person accent of a Chinese person speaking Japanese" in an inoffensive way. But you can also do a "Comically Chinese person accent speaking Japanese" in an unrealistic and a more-or-less offensive way (basically add "-aru" to sentences, something which Japanese people know Chinese people don't actually say, but are unsure where it started).

    Anyway, in US, it's bad. There's too much real baggage and real history behind it.

    Like, if I was to do a "stuffy German banker accent in English", or a "French poet at a cafe", or a "British gentry at a garden party", "Scottish dwarves on the cannon squad", no one really would bat an eye even if ridiculous. But for other countries with history of real racism and especially Hollywood culture exploitation (Black, Chinese) the game is much different. Is there anything like that in your country, out of curiosity?

    EDIT: As I understand it from my French friends, it may be the case that *Polish* people might fall into this category in France? Based on lots of recent political rhetoric portraying the biggest thread to French economy being the (non-unionized, skilled, and foreign) Polish Plumber. Their equivalent of our Chinese factory worker or South American day-laborer.

    So in the end, it's not just "country/culture stereotyping", and not just "avoiding ridiculous accent", it's avoiding both when the culture in question is a minefield of exploitation and historic racism, while still leaving it open for gaming possibility. (ex, few would say, "It's racist to try to roleplay Chinese people during the Three Kingdoms era", but pretty much everyone would say "It would be racist to roleplay Chinese people during the Three Kingdoms era while speaking in ridiculous "Jerry Lewis" accents and squinting our eyes a lot." There are a couple of cultures that would have this over it, including African cultures, possibly South American. Seems like a minefield, but that's Hollywood's fucking legacy (plus political policy, wars, etc).

    \n Wandering slightly into the territory of when things are and aren't offensive --

    In response to this:\n
    I know certain overseas Chinese people who have openly laughed and been incredulous at stereotypes and racist language--one finding the concept of "chink" incredibly laughable because, as she put it, "I'm a chink? What? My civilization and culture is one of the oldest still surviving on Earth, and my country one of the largest." Like she was immune to belittling in this way.
    \nPeople tend to find racial (or other) stereotyping/slurs/whatever offensive if and only if they think there is some real way they may suffer harm (physical, social, economic) because of their race/ethnicity.

    White people in America are almost never in danger of suffering harm because of their race, so anti-white insults tend to provoke laughter rather than offense. This would change if you put a white person in a context where they thought they *could* suffer because of their race; if a white person was desperately hoping for a position in an organization, and they thought they might not make it because of diversity commitments on the organization's part, and they overheard people of other races on the search committee using one of the few available white ethnic slurs, which they'd usually laugh off, they'd get very bent out of shape very quickly. Similarly: white guy, alone at night in a high-crime minority neighborhood, the white guy fears being attacked; if he sees a bunch of tough-looking young men pass him and utter an ethnic slur, he's not likely to laugh it off.

    So yeah, what I'm saying is that the likeliness you'll be able to laugh off an offensive ethnic slur or stereotype is inversely proportional to how likely you feel it is that you will actually suffer harm because of your identity.

    The comedian Louis CK talks about this in a bit called "white people" where he talks about all the wonderful advantages he gets by being white: "You can't even hurt my feelings! What are you going to do, call me a 'Cracker'? [in a mock-wounded voice] 'Oh, that hurts, you shouldn't have called me that; it takes me back to the days when people like me owned land, and other people...'"

    This is basically an elaboration of what Andy was saying about some stereotypes being more charged with ability to wound than others. You are still treated differently for being Black or Asian in the U.S.; people can still mistreat you because of those things. In *very* recent history, you could be horribly mistreated for those things (lynchings in the South; internment in concentration camps in the West). Until those things are long gone in the past, this will be an area where you can cause people pain.

    That's why this is something worth worrying about in those cases, where it might not be worth worrying about in others (offending Scandanavians with Viking stereotypes is comparatively unlikely).

    \n I use quite a bit of glossolalia in many different types of games, actually. My current D&D campaign especially seems to have a lot of this, gypsies and goblins and whatever talking in nonsense syllables and with arbitrary accents. I think I also know why it doesn't bother me there: when I GM a game like D&D I don't usually perceive the communication at the table to be about the objective in-game reality, but rather about the perceptions of the player characters. There is nothing more appropriate to driving home the stylistical themes of the game than to gloss foreign languages into vague gibberish when the communication about the game content is overwhelmingly about private character perceptions, anyway. It's not the GM's racist parody you're being given, it's your character's actual, often very parochial, viewpoint.

    In fact, for a game like D&D where passing information from the character viewpoint is a great big part of the game, it's definitely a technique I support and recommend to gloss a lot of the character observations into a more vague and less photo-realistic image of the world. It's simply more interesting for the game's challenges when instead of saying that "17 stirges attack you" you merely say "a bunch of stirges are flying around, one of them flies up in your face and tries to bite". In this particular context dingdong-languages don't seem ridiculous at all to me: your character is a stranger in a strange land, he's meeting some fearsome, inscrutable natives, he can't parse their speech and it's all dingdongdingdong to him. It's a perfectly appropriate way of passing on limited information, as the player can still sort of pick up hints about the content of the communication from your non-verbal cues, the length of the "speech", recognized names in the midsts of the babbling and so on. A language minigame of sorts.

    I should also say that doing the dingdong thing is not entirely trivial when you have to do lots of different languages. Even for these purposes it sounds quite stupid if you don't have enough of a language-like pattern to your random babbling, and then the syllables start repeating in an annoyingly unlanguage-like manner. I'm not a very conscientious GM, so I don't practice this or whatever, but I do sometimes improve my expression with difficult languages by constructing my dingdong-ese by reading the syllables out of a phonebook or similar in the appropriate language. I notice that especially in fantasy contests, when I don't do something like this, all of my babbling starts sounding vaguely German sooner or later for some reason; keeping a list of random Chinese words or whatever nearby keeps my syllabary and accent properly flavoured.

    I can appreciate that if the game runs on a different communication mode where the players are supposed to share an undiluted view of reality with each other, then simply making less of an effort in maintaining verisimiltude in this one thing is going to be jarring. In a game like that (a more "story game" game, if you'd like, as it's less concerned with character perspective and more concerned about shared, firm fiction) I don't even attempt to fake a language I don't know well, but will rather just tell the other players what is happening, just like I would for anything else that's not direct dialogue. "The Chinaman babbles at you excitedly, but you can't make out what he's saying" is a much more stylish choice most of the time than getting randomly thespianic about describing the event just because that event happens to be speech-related.
