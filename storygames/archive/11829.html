    \n\nTeataine \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nMarch 2010 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n1. Who is this person?
    2. What does he or she do? What's her or his speciality?
    3. What does he or she want? What does she or he care about?
    4. Optional: Where, what and how is your character doing now? Why is that a problem?

    1. Narrows down from "anyone" to some specific subset of people. His name is Bob. He's an orc. He's a shaman.
    2. Determines competence, effectiveness, influence. What does the character have the power to do.
    3. Determines positioning, why is she doing what she's doing, what are her drives and motivations.
    4. Kicker.

    IMO that's pretty much all you should ever need to know about a character pre-play. During play you can tackle on new traits, motivations, detail relationships and history etc.

    \nEveryone I've ever played with has gone the opposite direction. A comment like "I want to play a game about zombie doctors!" turns into a discussion about what system would be best. And a person generally doesn't make that kind of comment unless they are already inspired towards certain kinds of characters with certain kinds of relationships. Maybe a zombie doctor, a ghoul psychiatrist, and an unethics board that's out to get their licenses revoked.

    I have trouble seeing how it could be otherwise. If system matters, but there's no One True System, then system can't be the first choice made; it must be dependent on some other source of inspiration. That doesn't necessarily mean characters, but it generally seems to be that way in my experience.

    \n Characters must be defined 'in situ;' by the conventions of genre (setting and mood being genre's constituent parts) and format (the format, in this case, being roleplaying, as opposed to film or literature). If you flesh out a character beforehand it's likely the end result will either be, at best, a figure out-of-joint with the rest of the imagined space, or a character so wildly inappropriate for the mode and mood of the narrative that the whole house comes down!

    In my mind, characters must be developed in step with the world they populate.
