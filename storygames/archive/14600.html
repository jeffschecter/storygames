\n I'm running an old-school D&D style adventure game. Back when I ran D&D in high school I would have pre-scripted grandiose campaigns with heavy plots of tragedy tied into the dungeons and adventures. I railroaded the players because that's all I knew.

So, I don't want to do that since it's 2011 and I've been playing Indie games for years. I still want to run an old-school game full of adventure, but how do you do that and still maintain a story without pre-plotting everything?

How I typically prep games is through Bangs and Relationship Maps. That seems a bit off for an adventure game where players will be going into dungeons.

I suppose I could prep Fronts like Apocalypse World/Dungeon World but I'm not convinced that's the way to go with old-school play.

What tools techniques do you use to keep an old-school campaign fresh in the long-term and allow players to co-author the story?

    \n Have you seen the free Matt Finch booklet on old school gaming? If not I can try to dig out a link, it should be legitimately free online.

    The classic modern paradigm for old school gaming (odd as that sounds) is sandbox play. That means no pre-prepared grandiose campaigns. Active PCs operating in the world drive what happens. That's the old school paradigm. It's not that different to the story-games paradigm. The OSR is much closer by way of example to storygaming than it is to say 1990s style metaplots.

    Personally I see absolutely no reason you couldn't use R-Maps, Bangs, Dogs town creation rules (that's a really good idea there). As Ry says you just don't decide what will happen.

    The point of old school play is how open it is. The techniques you know and love should work just fine.

    What kind of system do you want to run this with?

    Two things: old-school D&D, as re-imagined (revised) by the Old School Renaissance is both (a) plot-less and (b) very close to a lot of indie gaming. Here's a post I made about paralels between the GM-ing style of Apocalypse World and Jeff Rients' (a prominent OSR blogger) GM-ing style. Old-school D&D is picaresque, it has a long series of events that aren't necessarily tied together in any larger plot. It's also about money. Here is a Grognardia post about picaresque in D&D and here is a post about locale and plot. Zak Smith also wrote some smart things on the matter but his blog is hard to navigate so I can't find the important bits now.

    "You're drinking in the tavern, when suddenly you hear screams outside. The goblin horde is attacking the village!"
    That's totally a bang in terms of D&D play. The players cannot not do anything about it, whether it's bagging the tavern's profit and running through the back doors or fighting the invaders. I'm simplifying and using a dumb example but there you have it.

    R-maps are totally useful in any game. Since D&D is more about exploring dungeons, the wilderness and raiding tombs, you will probably get more mileage out of R-maps of factions, monster groups, cults, curches, kingdoms, dungeon inhabitants etc. rather than direct R-maps of complicated relationships. Do not make D&D about interpersonal drama or emotional story or something like that because it's not about that. That isn't to say it can't spice play up. Dungeon World uses the Apocalypse World inter-player relationships (Hx aka Bonds) that are set up at the start of play. Someone in the party owes you a favour, someone in the party is laughing at you, you really really like someone in the party. That kind of stuff.

    I don't see why Fronts wouldn't work. Oldschool D&D is location-based and locations are basically geographically-defined situations. Situations have fronts. Dungeon World has fronts. Maybe not in the direct sense of AW, but still. The orc horde beyond the mountain pass in a Front. Write a couple of questions about it and make a countdown clock for it. The ancient cult at the bottom of the catacombs trying to raise a sleeping demon is a Front. The greedy town official making deals with the drow to replace the mayor is a Front. They threaten resources, law, goodness, stability, justice...

    Sandbox works with player-authored goals (Beliefs etc.). I don't really see why it wouldn't. Since in a sandbox campaign the GM is basically just sitting back and acting as a referee/intermediary between world and characters, reacting to their actions ("go wherever, do whatever"), if the players/characters don't have goals or beliefs to act on then they'll just sit around waiting for something to happen...it's really interplay between putting some pressure on the situation through "Fronts" (and kicking things off with a "Bang") and the players setting some long term goals for their characters.

    Another thing to note is how the picaresque theme changes and disappears with time.     \n Yeah, old school D&D is really surprisingly compatible with Forge-think. I mean, I'm as orthodox Forgista as you can get, and I still get pleasantly surprised about how people who have no truck with that scene at all (a distaste, even) will come up and triatribe against premade plots and for cohesive creative agenda (under a different name, but still) like it was 2003 all over again. It's not all like that (I've been disappointed by Frog God Games, for instance, as it's so close to 3rd edition D&D in approach), and some of the writers could stand a slight bit more experience outside the D&D box just to give them a bit of depth, but there's enough of it to make that whole scene quite agreeable to my own play preferences.

    Odds are that if you're at home with Forge philosophy, then something akin to my ponderings on how to play D&D might suit you; my basic precept on the topic is that D&D is a gamist fantasy adventure game where a challenge is established essentially by negotiation (the player chooses to open that door in the dungeon, basically) and then resolved in a way that priorizes a sensible fiction over explicit mechanical procedures. I wrote about this a few years back as a sort of summation, and have since played along these lines quite a bit to great success (he says after an 8 hour session and a shower). Should really write a book on this just to bring everybody up to date on how to make D&D rock.

    And no, I don't find relationship maps particularly exciting in this context, although I could imagine using them with a sufficient basis on other things. More important to provide player freedom, learn to offer challengeful choices when they arise in the fiction, referee a fair game in a trusted environment and lastly, provide a segmented, complex challenge battleground such as a dungeon module or similar. That last point is really the least important, I think, but when present it pretty much does everything a relationship map might do for your game prep.

    \n Here's something I've done a couple of times, and it's worked for me. At the beginning of the first four sessions, each player makes up two of the following things*:
    -- their family members
    -- one friendly NPC
    -- one unfriendly NPC
    -- one outside threat to civilization
    -- one nearby town/nation/province
    -- one quest for their character
    -- one wilderness area
    For the first set of items in a session, the player makes up an item and then the two players to his left add a detail about it. After each player has made up one item, you reverse direction and the two players to the right of the originating player add details about his item.

    This gives the GM a ton of fodder to work with, all of which the players are familiar with. Yet the GM has free rein to interpret things however he'd like, and the players have no control over the details. Old school enough for me.

    I've found that the following workflow works pretty well: players rough out their characters, then they do the exercise, then they detail their characters while the GM roughs out an adventure based on their leads, then the adventure begins.

    *No duplication allowed until the very last item (which must be a duplicate).

    \n I set out to hack 4E to make running an old-school sandbox with a heavy taste of "frontier expansion" possible.
    \n
    \nMy techniques are pretty simple:
    \n
    \n* Draw a hex map
    \n* Populate the hex map with interesting areas, the difficulty of which is not related to the PCs
    \n* Link those hexes to each other, creating relationships across the setting, ending up with a spiderweb
    \n* Hand out rumour package based on the hexes
    \n* Make PCs
    \n* Ask the PCs which rumour they want to explore
    \n* Adventure!
    \n* When the PCs return to rest & refresh, have the NPCs react to the player's decisions, reflecting the changes they made in the setting
    \n* Next session: ask the PCs what their Quest is - "What are you doing today? That should be your Quest, since you get XP for it."
    \n* Adventure! in setting that's been changed by the player's choices; like a spiderweb, touching one strand will send shivers throughout the whole
    \n* Repeat until in-game time threshold is met
    \n* Progress powerful NPC organizations as in-game time passes
    \n* Repeat until the PCs meet their player-defined Goals and pass to paragon tier/name level
    \n
    \nHere are some of the hack's changes:
    \n
    \n* I threw down organizations with goals on the map. These organizations ("Lairs") do things and get more powerful over time.
    \n* When the PCs spend money in town, their connection to the town grows. Some classes can increase their connection when they refresh their daily powers; other classes weaken the connection (Wizards, Warlocks).
    \n* When PCs deal with threats, gain levels, hire hirelings, and spend time in town their influence over the town grows.
    \n* When PCs spend money in town, the town grows.
    \n* When the town grows the PCs gain access to better equipment, better henchmen, and better hirelings. The town also learns how to defend itself. After a certain point the PCs have to set up trade with other towns or clear hexes and form new towns in order to keep the town growing.
    \n* When the PCs return to town to rest, they have to engage with the townspeople; this gives me the chance to show the consequences of the PC's actions in the setting. eg. Martial classes have to carouse; an NPC might seek out a PC to buy him/her a drink, saying "Now that the east road is free of gnolls I can get my grain out without having to pay for extra strongarms! You've saved me!"
    \n* PCs are created with Goals that give them XP; these Goals determine what a PC needs to do in order to level up (train, research, gather followers, etc.) as well as the benefits for hitting Paragon Tier (the Paragon Path). In order to reach Paragon Tier ("Name Level"), the Goal must be met.
    \n* I brought back the wandering monster, slow healing, and training times and costs. That combined with active NPCs ("Lairs") means time is valuable.
