\n I had this thought from another thread about what players do in between playing, and it got me thinking about what would be necessary to keep any player from spending too much time on their action so everyone else can keep playing. I was initially thinking of how to keep mechanics going so that players wouldn't have to spend more than 5-15 seconds thinking about what they do, but that got discarded quickly in brainstorming.

So here's the end result. You take any game, be it D&D 4e, Dragon Age, Dragonlance 5th Age, A Dirty World.

You have either a representational battle mat with hex or square grids, or you have an abstract one which functions like Final Fantasy, where everyone lines up against the opposition.

Players control both the protagonists and the antagonists in battle. The only turn based aspect is protagonists one turn, antagonists the next turn.

Everyone just goes whenever, there's no initiative order, they move their character token to whomever they're attacking or whatever they're doing, and do it. Everyone acts simultaneously, if there's something planned, you don't have it screwed up by someone else moving before it's your turn. Things also just play out quickly. It needs some sort of mechanic where the players can determine if they hit and what the result is without having to consult with anyone else.

Next turn, the players control the antagonists in much the same way. If there are 3 players and 7 antagonists, then a rule that after you've acted a second time, every other player has to get their second action in before you're allowed your third would be reasonable (by this I mean a player's action is controlling a character to do something.)

As a result, the players know everyone's stats, and they can strategise however they like. They can make the opposition pushovers, or they can make them very nasty. The important bit is they get the battle they want. If they want to dazzle, they have stupid opposition and mow them over. If they want to fight for their victory, they have the antagonists always take advantage of weaknesses, team up, split the protagonists up, etc.

The referee may or may not be necessary in this situation, it's probably useful to have someone arbitrate in situations of disagreement, and also to make a decision on how much XP is given out. A Dragon Age XP system makes a lot of sense - if the battle was easy, not much XP, if the battle was challenging, either due to luck of the randomisers or because the players played the villains to the fullest extent, and they nearly TPK themselves, there's a ton of XP.

Obviously it's combat focused, but with an abstract layout, or potentially Diaspora's social combat setup it could work just as well for emotional interactions. It works just as well to be playing A Dirty World and land an attack by taunting a character with the revelation that you killed their parents.

    \n It sounds to me like there are two principal differences between this and what conventionally happens.

    One, you discard initiative order completely. I don't see any real benefit to this, and it really screws with some characters. If it's important to your character to act first, or to have 4 actions in a round where most people have 3, then this removes that detail without any real benefit. I mean, each character in the conflict gets to act a certain number of times per round; I don't see any real advantage to saying that it happens whenever someone shouts the loudest that that's what the character does instead of in the order prescribed by the rules.

    Two, the players determine the actions of the antagonists as well as those of their characters, and control how difficult the battle is by deciding how competent the antagonists are. The problem with this is, at least in my experience, the players who are likely to be the most annoying when they are bored between combat rounds and who are likely to spend their downtime surfing the 'net or flipping aimlessly through the rulebook instead of considering their next action are also the ones who are likely to twist this as much as possible to their own advantage. "Oh, the Big Bad thinks it's really warm after Joe threw the fireball, so he takes off his armor to cool down!"

    I think if I found conflict resolution that complex to resolve, and the group wasn't interested in complexity, I'd find a different rules system altogether.

    \n Well the obvious reason to act before other players is to buff them or de-buff or otherwise hinder the enemy before your ally engages them.

    Acting more often than your enemies or allies is also a big part of several game systems, most notably HERO, as well as in initiative countdown systems like Feng Shui or Deadlands.

    I dunno, this sounds like a train wreck to me, sorry. Jettisoning initiative might work in games where tactics are highly abstracted, like Apocalypse World, say, but it's the main thing that makes tactical tabletop games like D&D playable at all. Lose it, and you're bunch of kids screaming at each other over whose plastic army man shot first, which I didn't like even when I was little.

    The other thing to keep in mind is that those Final Fantasy games that sometimes seem as if they have no Init system all do, it's just transparent because it's all managed by the console. They mostly utilize a countdown timer and you go when it ticks off your turn. None of them let you just bash away, unless they're action games like God of War or an FPS.

    I get what you're saying, and it may work to have everyone jot down their moves in advance then reveal them so they trigger at the same time, but that can also result in some very weird outcomes that I think I'd have trouble with as a player.
