\n With "Storming the Wizard's Tower", Vincent Baker came up with a very simple but very interesting rule:

Under certain conditions in play, one player (sometimes the MC, sometimes not) can ask one or more questions of another, who must answer honestly.

This made its way into all kinds of Apocalypse World moves, of course.

I think it's pretty cool: yeah, this should be a foundational technique for all roleplaying, and yet before StWT/AW, I don't think I've ever seen it explicitly called out like this.

I was just reading through the rules for a game called "Dreamwake", and I found a neat little tidbit which uses this technology. In Vincent's moves and rules, it's usually the player who wishes to ask some questions who initiates the mechanic, in hopes of getting answers. (One notable exception: when you open your brain in Apocalypse World, the MC can ask you questions. But, then again, the MC can *always* ask you questions in AW.)

Here in Dreamwake, there's a little mechanic called "Clarity". When your character is in a scene, you can call for a "moment of clarity". The action stops, and you tell us what your character is thinking and feeling. Every other player can ask more questions, until they're satisfied. You must answer them fully and honestly.

So we get a little clarity, a little insight into what your character is thinking and feeling, hoping, planning. In exchange you get a bennie of some kind - an advantage for your character going forward.

I thought this was pretty neat: I think there's some unexplored technology in this space.

    \n This is something that I see a lot in character-focused adventuring party games: a player needs to interrupt the mission or whatever to grab a little spotlight time to show the group something important about their character. "Call for a moment of clarity" sounds like a nice formal option to replace the vagaries of conversation. In such situations, I imagine "you can ask more questions" would primarily be code for "you should ask questions, or at least try" -- getting the whole table to engage in the moment of character exploration so the spotlight player isn't talking to themselves at everyone else's sufferance. "Answer honestly" backs this up: if someone invests in your character moment, you cannot pass, lie, or play hard-to-get with them.

    Without knowing how Dreamwake uses it, sounds like a cool technique to me!

    \n This is a ritual phrase I like Archipelago: "Describe more" (can't recall what's it's called exactly)

    If something sounds interesting, any player can use this phrase to make the narrator describe more details in the scene; either it's a happening, character's appearance, or an item. This can be put into a structure, where you describe loosely up until someone actually says "Describe more".

    "And so I slayed the dragon and..."
    "Describe more."
    "[A story is told about the fierce fight between the knight and the dragon.]"

    Instead of having a benny or something similar as a mechanical reward, the reward is to actually be able to hear more about it for the listeners and, for the narrator, to have an eager audience.

    David_Berg & traviseneix ... concerning limiters to verbose/loud people:
    - you are limited to 1 Clarity per scene
    - you can "charge" a maximum of 3 Clarities in total (if you spend them, you can then make new ones)

    - also, everybody has access to other Ritual Words (a bit like the Clarity one) to PAUSE the game and clear the air calmly or to SHAKE the situation and make something happen ... in my experience these work well to both make yourself be heard even in the presence of "louder" Players, and move things along in case of "verbose" ones.
