    \n Is this Wormwood like in the Bible?

    It's kinda a strange thing to call a planet, if you're Chinese. Chinese placenames don't usually work like that.

    A simple rendering of "wormwood" is bi-syllabic Chinese (any place name is likely to be bi-syllabic) is \u87f2\u6728 (chongmu). But it's ... a really weird name.

    You could also use "rumu" which is more literally "worm-wood" (the above is more like "crawly-thing wood," but it's more fancy-sounding, too). \u8815\u6728.

    A slightly more nice-sounding name might be something like \u8822\u8349 (chuntsao) which means "stirring grasses" but the "stirring" has the feeling of insects stirring under the earth at the beginning of spring.

    \n Another option is to go with the plant names: \u82e6\u827e (ku'ai) or \u82e6\u607c (kunao). I would append -qiu (\u7403) to any of these, which means "ball" and is the suffix for planet names. (Earth is \u5730\u7403 "earthball")

    So, to summarize:
    \u87f2\u6728\u7403 (chongmuqiu)
    \u8815\u6728\u7403 (rumuqiu)
    \u8822\u8349\u7403 (chuncaoqiu)
    \u8822\u7403 (chunqiu)
    \u82e6\u827e\u7403 (ku'aiqiu)
    \u82e6\u60f1\u7403 (kunaoqiu)
    \u82e6\u7403 (kuqiu)

    The last one is nice. It just means "bitter world." It summons up an image of hard scrabble settlers roughing it in a hostile wilderness.

    \n Ku, bitterness, is an amazing character with a long, long history of terrific connotations in Chinese. Plus, as I recall, it's used in most Chinese translations of the Bible for when the star Wormwood turns the waters to bitterness.

    Du, poison, is pretty good too, but not as cool as Ku.

    \n Notes on pronunciation: First syllable is "ku," which is pronounced like "coo... coo..." the sound you make at a baby. Second syllable is "qiu" which is pronounced sorta like you were going to say "cheese" and then go surprised midway through: "chee...oh." Compressed into one syllable.

    \n Andy: Here's some pointless language geekery.

    Actually, \u661f means "star" in Chinese, too. It's sort of confusing, actually. \u7403 is planet and \u661f is star, but since the ancient Chinese thought that the planets were stars, all the in-system planets have the suffix \u661f, instead of \u7403.
