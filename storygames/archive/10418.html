Posted By: Wordman:
Posted By: Simon Rogers"Beta" is perhaps a better name for it.
I've been thinking the same thing. Not so much the actual word "beta", but the concept of beta testing software is reasonably well-known and, importantly to this particular discussion, provides the end user with a reasonable expectation of what he or she is getting.

Now, I've never produced an ashcan, but I've run a number of beta tests in my life. It's not exactly what you expect, so maybe some guidelines from beta testing might be of some use to those who actually do make ashcans. (If not, well, electrons are cheap).

First, what does "beta" even mean? There are many definitions, but two are fairly common. The "alhpa" stage of software is when all the features are written. This is "functionally complete". It may not have seen much testing, but every base you wanted to cover is covered. At this point, testing is still mostly internal. Some companies release alpha software, but not many. When all theseriousproblems you know of are handled, you release "beta". The expectation is that you may still need to change stuff, but the final result will at least recognizable what is in the beta.

In order of importance, the rules of thumb for beta testing are:

One: Find ProblemsThis sounds obvious, but is easy to forget in the middle of it all.Everythingyou do has to be filtered through the lens of "is this helping me discover flaws in my product". This is much harder than it sounds.

Two: TriageVeteveryproblem you hear about, with the realization that many of of themare not worth solving. The quickest way to kill a beta is to treat everything as important. Many things will not be. Some things will be, but fixing them will do so much collateral damage that you are better off not bothering (for this version, at least). It's rare, but you may also run into a deal-breaker issue: a design flaw so jarring that you need to head back to the drawing board. (This is rare, but every time I've seen it happen was because of misunderstanding what the user's needs were.)

Three: Keep Costs DownDon't print documentation if electronic will do. Don't burn CDs if downloading will do. Don't pay bribes unless you have to. Don't advertise unless you have no other choice. Don't spend time with people who aren't helping you if you could be spending it with people who are.

Four: Control the ProductThe type of control here refers more to control of the product's destiny than its content; that is, managing the "intellectual property rights" and market share. How this is done will beradicallydifferent depending on the product and circumstances. For some products, this means super-secret betas with NDAs signed by everyone. For others it means massive public betas for maximum exposure. If there is an art to beta testing, figuring out the right mechanism for control of your product is it.

Five: FinishThe beta is useless if it never ends. A common mistake is to set a "finished condition" based on the problems found, such as "when all issues of Medium severity or higher are fixed". Done this way, either the beta will never end, or it will end only because issues are managed artificially to meet the condition by a certain time. Generally, a timed deadline works, and everything else doesn't. The goal isn't a perfect product; that doesn't exist. Instead the goal is to make the the product the best that you can make it in the time allotted. If you aren't proud of the result, no one else will be either; have the decency to kill it and move on to the next thing.

Of all of these, most of the techniques used in the software industry would work for RPGs. The big exception is #4, as the mechanisms for controlling software different than those controlling RPGs. (You can, for example, make software expire, a trick not really tolerated in the PDF world.)

In any case, the point here is that when customers sign on to a beta (whether voluntarily or paying for the "privilege"), there is a clear indication of the quality they can expect and what they are in for. This thread suggests that (right or wrong) that same expectation isn't universally held regarding ashcans.

Posted By: David Artman:
Thanks, Lester, for that very useful parallel. I tend to apply a SLIGHTLY different metric for Alpha/Beta:
* ST - System Test; really only applicable to modular or generic/universal systems; ready to play, but no guidance (maybe this is the "tools" stage of a computer product?)
* Alpha - Full system and playable content; in an RPG, that would mean all system components and enough of setting to get started.
* Beta - Full system, full (mostly) content; just looking for the silly shit; in RPGs, this is typos, weird tweaks to rule combinations, maybe points-cost balances.
* Gold Master - This Is What I'm Shipping--is it broke? (This would be a proof stage for a publication.)

I might be skewed some, though, because my system is a generic one, intended for LARP. I'm at the stage where Unreal Editor is built and the core scriptable elements are all in it: it's not a "game" at all, because there's no levels, no goals, no story wrapper. And I want to hold there and let users play with the editor because it's generic and must support as much as possible. I would never claim it's "done" at this stage, and I don't charge for it because [mooted] I want folks to stress test it and, as such, I want them to be fully aware that it could break under the load.

Anyhow... very interesting assessment--how else do you y'all think RPG text and related products might be similar to or differ from software, vis a vis development and lifecycle management?

    \n I'm pretty dumbfounded that people can't find points of comparison in David's post for games. We're not talking about software vs. rpgs. We're talking design process and production methods. A lot of the current fashionable software design processes were ported over from the automobile industry. If the software industry is smart enough to learn design processes from the automobile industry, I think we can find parallels here in our biz too.

    I've had a lot of luck using things I've learned from software design in working on my game, specifically some video game design processes I've seen. Like John, I've been thinking of my game in terms of Alpha (internal playtesting) and Beta (external playtesting). And the five rules of Beta Testing are very much relevant. Triage is something I think about more and more, especially as feedback and suggestions about my game may be legitimate concerns but otherwise alter my vision of the game.

    One developer who I think has an interesting method of design is Tarn Adams - the Dwarf Fortress guy. He starts with fiction, and then reverse engineers his game so that the fiction is something that could feasibly happen in his game. This is another thing I've adopted in my own, admittedly sparse, designs.

    \n So it looks like we can safely map to PC gaming pre-release terminology, perhaps thereby avoiding accusations of selling broken or incomplete games (should there BE a charge for a "Beta").

    A couple of obvious knock-ons, now:
    1) How can we manage doing "open beta" releases--which ostensibly are full games, ready to play--and still protect revenue once the actual publication/product is released? As mentioned above, it's easy to "patch" pre-release--send out new beta pubs--but how do you "unpatch" to make those possessing the beta buy? What's to prevent someone with a beta from "self-patching" the last nuanced changes for Gold Master/Final Release?

    2) How do you manage "Revised" or "V2" release prep and delivery? Specifically, how do you prepare (and test) and market a Revised Edition without making the First Edition seem like a bait and switch (or a "sold beta")? Are there responsibilities to the buyers of the First Edition that must be met upon release of the Revised (if so, what; if not, why not)? Finally, is there a quantitative or qualitative line that is crossed where a Revised Edition actually becomes a Second Edition?

    As should be obvious, I have moved to life cycle management issues; things we deal with from post-playtesting to post-new-edition. (I have ideas for dealing with both questions; I'll hold off until we see what others think.)

    \n As I went back to correct mistakes in Maid after publishing (the few rules mistakes and many grammar mistakes/clarifications), I adopted a methodology similar to OS release codes.

    First number represents the edition.
    Second number represents major updates, in my case updates that fixed formatting or rules-based mistakes.
    Third number represents minor updates, in my case updates that fixed grammar or other minor stuff.

    So Maid 0.9 was the one we used to get printing estimates. Version 1.0.1 was the version we printed at GenCon last year. Now we're on 1.2.2.
    Between 1.0.1 and 1.1 were major grammar and replay format/content changing. 1.2 was fixing the last unclear/errata rules bits and some formatting bits, proper creditation, etc. This is the line that's been in print since about March or so.

    Soon, thanks to some vocal folks on the mailing list, we'll be moving from 1.2.2 to 1.2.3 in a few months. There's always a grammar mistake that can be corrected, a rule that could be better clarified, etc.

    \n
    \nI start my initial design with 1.0. Whenever I make a change to previous text more substantative than "typo" fixing I save to a new file with a new decimal (1.1, 1.2, 1.3). Whenever I make a change of such impact that it involves rewriting substantial sections of the text I bump to the next version (2.0, etc.). Typically Alpha 2.0 or 3.0 will be the first version that is "playtestable".
    \n
    \nWhen I reach a Beta Stage, I start the numbers over at 1.0. So my file name is Beta 1.0 instead of Alpha 1.0. Typically I'll create a whole new folder to save my Beta files to.
    \n
    \nFor Universalis between Alpha and Beta versions there were probably 8 whole number versions.
    \n
    \nFor Blood Red Sands I think there were only 3 or 4 whole number versions but over a dozen decimal versions.
    \n
    \nFor Robots & Rapiers I'm on Gamma Version 1.1 which is what I decided to call my current "back to formula" rewrite of that game.
    \n
    \nSo definitely not software standard, but it helps alot to keep me organized.

    \n Still thinking in terms of driving quality into designs (and stemming more from my hardware development experience than software), I am wondering if there are analogs to these concepts for RPGs:

    Regression Tests. A basic suite of tests that confirm you maintain a level of quality, since bugfixes frequently introduce new bugs. Maybe have a collection of successful actual play writeups and confirm that they would still be possible under the changed rules?

    Formal Methods. Using mathematics-style proofs to verify properties about your design. (Say, prove that your "endgame mechanic" will eventually be triggered, or prove that there are no deadlock situations).

    Random and Directed-Random Testing. Feeding automatically generated input into a design to find problems and stress conditions that human testers might not think of. (Usually requires a large number of tests, and some sort of "checking" mechanism to detect problems). This is a powerful technique but usually requires automation, so I'm not sure how you could take advantage of it in RPG testing.

    Coverage. Systematically tracking which parts of a design are stressed, both for an individual test and for your entire testing effort. (As a simple example, maybe give playtesters a checklist of subsystems and have them record each time they use a particular subsystem, so you can see which combination of subsystems were used during that session. You can then look at the cumulative results of all your sessions and direct future playtests to focus on the systems that haven't been getting enough attention).
