\nYou're looking at the Rustbelt ashcan, as will be featured at the Ashcan Front at GenCon (booth 2039), with all its rusty goodness. I won't be there personally, but Paul Czege's going to be representing it on my behalf, because he's awesome.
\n
\nThe Rustbelt is a game of grim misadventure in a place where the apocalypse is now, desperation and hardship are the norm, and the Rust eats not only the abandoned machines and ghost towns, but civilization and even the laws of nature. To give you a Hollywood-style mashup, it's sort of Sin City meets the Wild Bunch meets Mad Max.
\n
\nThe book itself is half-letter size and saddlestitched. I did the cover art, photography, and design myself. There's no interior illustrations because, unfortunately, there was not enough time.
\n
\nGlued inside the back cover is an envelope containing a 6-track CD of original music based on the Rustbelt, for purposes of audio illustration. I recorded, performed, and wrote (except the lyrics to one of 'em, written by a friend) it all myself. The music is sort of rootsy and bluesy, with a dash of industrial for good measure. The production is ramshackle, rough, and crude for maximum rustiness. The music sounds like rust looks.
\n
\nPrice tag is gonna be $10.
\n
\nWhaddya think?

    Thanks! I'm really proud of both the look and the CD. And we can arrange something after GenCon, I'm sure.

    Lance,
    I suppose I could slip it into copies I sell after GenCon, but it's too late for me to do that now; the books are already in Indianapolis by now, and I'm in Oklahoma.

    And the locked door is a bad example (for some reason, everytime I need an example, I think of a locked door), but the issue is how prepared the guy who locked it was. Which makes it a Savvy check, unless perhaps he piled up a bunch of heavy furniture and shit against the door, which would make it a Tough check. And his effectiveness in this can be represented by a heavy lock, a reinforced door, bars, a barricade, whatever makes sense. You're allowed to handle this stuff abstractly. In fact, in the rules for unopposed (i.e. against a target number and not a person) rolls, the GM decides the difficulty (in keeping with his job to provide fit opposition) then describes why it's that hard. In other words, the difficulty and the locks/bars/blockade/whatever associated with it don't even really exist until they become relevant.

    Does that make sense? Sometimes (a lot of times) I'm not good at explaining rules issues.

    Dave,
    Thank you for that! And, everyone, I forgot to credit Dave with the editing, for which he definitely deserves credit. Once he was done, I really felt proud and confident about the text.
