    \n Hmmmm. I've got them all, but I haven't gone super-deep into SSS. Anything specific that you're looking for?

    BoL focuses a lot on "previous lives your character has had" and they come up in every roll. "Farmer: 1; Poisoner-Assassin: 2; Soldier 1; Scribe 2" etc. It is also the one that feels the most "Conan" of the bunch, but that was specifically what the author was aiming for. The art is fucking magnificent as well, it's like French Hanna-Barbara.

    SSS (again, haven't read through it front to back) offers a simplistic adventure game experience (well, they all do to some degree, but SSS really focuses on it) and has a sanity mechanic. It's also cheap as hell for the print version. It features no setting that I can tell.

    JotSS is loosely based on the system behind Dead Inside and Swashbucklers of the Seven Skies. It's got a great, comprehensive magic system. The rules system is the "wordiest" of the bunch, involving lots of abilities where if you describe them well you can use them. It's got a great setting attached, too.

    I would say that SSS focuses on the traditional adventure game the most while keeping things really simple. BoL focuses on being the Conan RPG, and does so very well with few rules. JotSS is a more "leaning towards hippie" game with a wordy rules-system and a unique setting.

    Posted By: shreyasHey Jesse,That's a really interesting thing you're looking for. Can we talk a little more about it? I love the idea of slick, fast-moving maps and minis board components.
    \nSure, using the 3.5 SRD I've (mostly) already mostly figured out how to do it on the player's side. It looks something like this:

    Dump non-Human races because I like Humanistic fantasy.
    Pre-build all the classes by filling in the appropriate feats for the class's task, dump all other feats.
    Fighter is a sword and shield guy.
    Ranger is an archer guy.
    Rogue is the sneaky/two weapon guy
    For Clerics dump Domains
    For Wizards dump Schools and Familiars.
    Strip down the spell lists to combat essentials.

    For general combat rules:

    Keep Flanking Rules
    Use 4e style Defenses
    Use 4e style cover & concealment
    Use 4e grappling.
    Use 4e style opportunity attacks.
    Basically strip everything down to 4e like essentials.

    The problem is, that I haven't figured out how to do that with Monster and Encounter design. Like if I want a 5th level Naga Priestess how do I get there in under 15 minutes? If I want a interesting 7th Level Necromancy flavored cultist acompanied by 5 4th level giant spiders how do I construct that quickly and efficiently.

    I generally don't like locking my imagination in at levels. It's one of the things I liked about 3e (I could scale creatures and apply templates so if I wanted 3rd level characters fighting Nagas that were usually 7th level I could jigger that) and 4e is much more forgiving in that you can throw a 5th level monster at a 1st level party and they have a fighting chance. But doing all that scale work is exhaustingly complex.

    I want something where I can pick a "level appropriateness" and then assemble powers from a budget or something like that. It's like I'd like "Monster Powers" separate from the monsters itself or something like that. So if I want a 1st Level Beholder I just run down the chart of 1st level versions of all it's abilities.

    Posted By: Christopher KubasikIf you could write down a list of "sweet spot" items you're looking for, what would that list be?
    \nSo, you know how when you think back on D&D what you remember is mind-boringly long combats where there's a lot of sitting around waiting your turn? In my experience 3e and 4e aren't like that but you have to set the game up correctly. When you don't do dumb things like tell players they can't help each other when it's not their turn, you get this fierce energy at the table. People are shouting at each other, "Okay, yeah, use your fireball this turn! No wait! Next turn, the rogue needs to get out of the way first! Oh crap, but that will provoke an opportunity attack from the Ogre! Fuck! We're boxed in!"

    You also need to have a *little* more at stake than *just* the fight. And I really do mean a *little*. I remember running an adventure where the players were going up a mountain to a Temple to rescue a woman who had been kidnapped by Naga. Along the way there was a rope bridge and the canyon below is obscured by mist. Well, just BELOW the mist is a giant spider that has spun a huge web across the canyon. Halfway across the bridge, it breaks and the characters have to make saving throws. Those who fail end up in the web. Those who succeed end up on basically a rope ladder with a giant spider climbing up behind them.

    Similarly the end fight in the temple opens with the Naga Priestesses dumping the woman the PCs are there to rescue into a pit with snake god. So they have to fight the encounter on two levels. The Snake Priestesses and their guards in the table and the giant snake in the pit which is about to kill the objective of the scenario.

    But it took me like 5 hours to create that final fight. I had to apply the Cleric Class to the Nagas. I think I also had to scale ALL the Nagas up or down a few levels because this was a 10th level encounter and Nagas by default are a little lower than that. I then had to find a Giant Snake and apply the Infernal Template to it. It was a great encounter but it took me forever to put it together. And that was just ONE encounter in a 13 location map.

    \n Old-School Hack is something of a cross between Red Box Hack and Danger Patrol -- or maybe that's just me. Anyway, there's a visual component and the potential for very simple use of minis (basically "My guy is here"). Space is divided into arenas, and there are different kinds of them: tight arenas don't give a lot of space to maneuver, hazardous arenas have environmental dangers, etc. Weapons have properties that key off of those arena types, so light weapons are better in tight arenas because they're more agile, while ranged weapons are better in open arenas.

    Everything else is dirt-simple and a little bit of a parody, both of which are fine by me.

    Personally, I found the 4E Monster Builder to be pretty awesome, but I think that was back before they updated/worsened it (much like the old, offline version of the Character Builder is better in just about every way than the new, online-only one). I used it to gin up a fair number of monsters from scratch or modify existing ones without much difficulty. It did/does require being able to eyeball them for balance, though, which isn't always intuitive.

    What about Savage Worlds? Rules-medium, makes allowances for minis but doesn't require them, has a few good fantasy-type settings (Sundered Skies and Hellfrost, specifically), very well-supported (if you care about that sort of thing)... seems to fit the bill.

    Honestly though, to me, that cool naga fight you described sounds like it could happen in just about any system, really. I don't see the need for breaking things down in five-foot squares to make that scene work and be as exciting as it was. In our long-running, highly lethal, bordering-on-abusive 3.5 game a few years ago, we had some epic, epic fights, but I can only think of one where minis and precise positioning really made them exciting. (The one exception was trapping a vampire in the middle of all of us and exposing him to sunlight.)

    \n I guess I'll be the dissenting voice on Savage Worlds; every single time I have played it I get the same D&D flashbacks of "mind-boringly long combats where there's a lot of sitting around waiting your turn" Jesse is complaining about. I don't get why exactly-- it looks really appealing on paper, but under three different GMs the game just grinds to a complete screeching halt every time the maps and minis come out. The last time I played, it took more than two hours to resolve a battle between four PCs vs 8-10 mooks & 1 Wild Card. I've only played in modern or supers settings so I don't know how well it handles fantasy/melee combat, but I suspect it would be even more of a whiff-tastic slog, because melee defense values are much higher than ranged (the target number for melee attacks involves the target's Fighting skill, but the TN for ranged is a flat 4, which basically means everyone who can carries a gun or shoots beams and I'll stop here before I rant about how bad Necessary Evil is as a supers ruleset).

    I want to second the Empire of Dust recommendation (assuming you can either groove on its Thundarr the Barbarian/Thundercats/Final Fantasy groundbound space opera vibe or just reskin everything, but there are definitely minotaur-men and various monster-critters even in the default setting), I ran that once and thought its maps&minis combat was great. It is definitely not a game for everyone, but if you like the idea of a skill-and-feat-based rules-medium system without HP attrition-based combat that can quickly or even randomly generate combat encounters and battlefields with destructible terrain, check it out. The game is technically out of print (which is a shame, since part of the charm is having the actual tokens for "stealthed/invisible", "on fire", etc), but IPR was selling the PDF last time I checked, and Clint Krause may still have a physical copy or two left.
