    I read somewhere here that there was evidence that most adults have difficulty with or are unable to do things like maintain the multiple PoVs and lines of thought required to play most RPGs. ...
    Sounds very strange to me. Most adults are highly capable of maintaining multiple point-of-views in real life. "Evidence" to the opposite should be thoroughly tested for verification.

    Practicing different POVs is very close to the hearth of social behavior; understanding your own position in different social environments, and showing empathy for the positions and emotions of others.

    My experience with players suggest that an overwhelming majority of adults find role-playing games easy to understand and play. The portion of adults interested in playing them is much smaller.

    \n The whole thing may be a bit simpler than all that.

    A kid is playing imaginatively with toys alone. The context of the Czege Principle is based on people playing together.

    In the first context, the kid is likely modelling what occurs in some source material. Of course there's no problem creating and resolving a situation where Han Solo runs into Boba Fett!

    The context of the situation in the second is that people are presumably trying to play as a group. At that point, it's simply an issue of being more interesting with more interaction between players, and following the Czege Principle points to a cool place to encourage that interaction.

    Also, I just don't buy the age thing. If you gave the lone adult Han Solo and Boba Fett toys ( and that was something they had some cultural familiarity with), I'm pretty sure they could handle the interaction, conflict creation, and resolution just fine.

    \n Is this in the same age range where children are engaging in parallel play? It sounds like it might be. And if so, then the conclusion the OP is making - that this means the children are capable of RPG play in a way that adults are not - is the exact opposite of what the research shows. Children under a certain developmental age are fundamentally incapable of protracted social play at all. They just can't really play with others.

    But maybe I'm wrong and the phenomenon they are talking about goes on well past the transition out of parallel play and into social play ages.

    (I find parallel play particularly fascinating as a gamer, because I look at a lot of what happens in multiplayer video games, especially MMOs, as being adults engaging in parallel play. Which, broadly speaking, I consider a bad thing. It's a case of repeatedly encouraging and reinforcing nonsocial behavior.)
