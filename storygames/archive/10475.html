    \n Hmmm... First thing that comes to mind is "don't overprepare". Part of the fun is letting the environment build out in play. As examples, I've found Tomb of the Iron God, or most of the levels of The Darkness Beneath published in Fight On to have just the right amount of detail. A map, some random encounter tables, a few pre-placed major adversaries, and a generalized notion of "what goes on down here." I'll specifically site the most recent level of TDB (The Lower Caves) published in the most recent issue of FO as being too overprepared for my tastes.

    Additional things that are useful are the usual list of NPCs names that you'd want when playing any game, as well as a few random tables of "dungeon dressing". I've gotten tons of mileage out of "Dungeon Detritus" in Fight On #3.

    One important thing to keep in mine when rolling on random tables: it's totally in the DM's purview to tweak, modify, or reroll results. The use of random monster, item, event & treasure tables is to spur your imagination during play, not to hem you in.

    As to house rules, here's my current doc for the most part. Being a soft hearted GM, I'm also using the Death's Door table (also from FO 3) with the following variant: roll 1d6 + current HP value (which will be negative, so you effectively subtracting here) + level. So basically provides a death save if you've gone a couple points negative, but no joy if your paste. On the other hand, if your tastes run more hardcore, then no need to provide such weasel room.

    ...the most important thing about house rules I think is making sure that they are seasoned to YOUR taste. About the only essential one is to decide on some ability check mechanic. The two most popular are some form of d20 vs stat (which I went with) or 1d6 + stat mod. The first makes stats matter a little more, the second is more derivable from the OD&D RAW.

    Another thing that occurred to me as I run this style of game is the important of leaving the player's to work out their own procedures & pecking order. In running at GPNW I saw everything from every man for themselves to a formal caller structure appear spontaneously at the table. Be sure to inform the players they are responsible for their own mapping, and let them work it out from there. Above all, resist the urge to correct their map unless the mistake is such that would be blatantly obvious to somebody who is actually standing in the imaginary location. But dimensions that are off a little, shapes not quite right, etc, those are all part of the fun.

    Feel free to hit me up with questions if any specific ones occur to you.

    \n Oh, I'll also mention that I've decided I'm unimpressed with the "mode switching" Elf as provided in S&W core. I'd suggest using the more Moldvay like Elf from S&W White Box. I'm actually fiddling with my own Elf that takes inspiration from Moldvay .

    A few more random thoughts. I've been using ascending armor class, but in the future I'll probably switch to descending armor class, with attack roll being 1d20+BAB+opponents AC >=20. To make this work, you have to increase BAB's by +1 over what is in the book. The reason being that when using none S&W material, you are constantly having to mentally convert between ascending and descending armor class. The alternative would be just to go through your materials and pencil in the AAC beforehand.

    Oh, and I totally think Fighters should get their first BAB bump at level 2, rather than 3.

    And experience! I've come round to the notion that the Greyhawk derived XP awards listed in S&W Core are far too weeny at low levels. I've switched to the original LBB system as summarized here:

    http://www.philotomy.com/#xp

    By the by philotomy's site is essential reading in fueling the thought process of putting together your own house rules.

    \n I run a Twitter game that is based on Wilhelm's rules (although I ignore a lot of the mods, except the Fighter gets BAB bonus at 2nd level thing). It's fun!

    GUIDELINES

    Don't be afraid to give the PCs unbalanced challenges they can't beat through combat.

    Don't be afraid to kill PCs if they engage said unbalanced challenges through combat. Corollary: Make it clear that this is an opportunity for heroism, but there is no guarantee that their characters will be the Great Hero. They might be the second guy on the left in the painting of the Great Hero. They might be the guy that gets eaten by the bear. There might not be a Great Hero.

    Give more XP for loot than killing creatures.

    Make the players think about encumbrance. Especially the 'Gold is really heavy' part. Corollary: Hirelings are fun (I don't know why).

    Give the PCs as much and as clear information as you can. The game is more interesting when they are able to make informed decisions than when they just blunder around. Corollary: Clear information only extends to things it would be possible for the PCs to know. I like that there's no way to directly identify cursed items, magic items and potions without USING them.

    RULES
    I used a 2-out-of-6 or 3-out-of-6 chance "skill" system when characters attempt non-combat actions. These rolls are modified by stat bonuses, working together, and clever/fun narration. The rationale is that the PCs are competent adveturers. I've found that players usually latch on to one or two things their character 'does'. Hey, roleplaying!

    I use the S&W Elves, but they change randomly at the start of every day. I find this makes them weirder and more chaotic. I like it that way.

    \n I dunno, char gen in OD&D is so quick that I think it's worth making part of the experience. Hand somebody a blank index card and 5 minutes later they have a character. What *is* worth having is several copies of the "shopping list" (general equipment + weapons + armor) so that somebody can equip their character without needing to look at the book. And the fact that your character is on hastily scribbled index card emphasizes the fragile nature of characters in OD&D.

    On the other hand having a small selection of pre-gen'd characters can be useful in two regards:

    a) as NPCs... if players happen to run into an NPC party in the dungeon, or go looking for a hireling, etc.
    b) the guy that steps up to the table when you are 30 minutes into the dungeon & wants to play. Hand him an equipped pre-gen ("you wanna play this dwarf?") and keep going.

    \n This mechanism, under the name "Ritual Casting" is actually one of the house rules included in Supplement VI: The Majestic Wilderlands.

    This particular book is a packaging of one guy's campaign setting which grew out of the original Judge's Guild "Wilderlands of High Fantasy" setting. It's got lots of his own house rules, classes, etc, along with a map and a gazetteer.

    Anyway, in these rules, MUs are allowed to cast spells from their spellbook as a "ritual", taking as I recall 10 minutes. MUs can only uses spells of 1/2 their maximum spell level, rounded down, as rituals. So a 3rd level MU could start casting 1st level spells as rituals.

    Clearly this hasn't lead to anarchy, as the guy has been running this campaign for decades. It certainly makes spell selection less critical (memorization is now for combat spells, and low level utility spells, especially information gathering ones become used more frequently).

    One thing to be careful of is boosting the amount of healing a cleric can dish out. That'll definitely change the delving endurance and survivability of characters. Maybe you want that

    Thanks for the reply, Wilhelm! I enjoy your proposed Thief class, by the way I plan to use it if any of my players ask about that, noting the Thief's absence from the core rules.
    \nI'm flattered! If you do, let me know how it goes.

    I think the main impact that ritual casting will have will be to remove time as one of the resources you are budgeting. The effect of ritual casting still goes on in standard play, once the characters are out of the dungeon, they can cast Detect Magic to identify items, healing spells, comprehend languages, etc at their leisure, but the the number of spells they can cast per day determines the downtime that must be spend to accomplish that. That may be and issue, depending on what they are up to. With ritual casting, it no longer is.

    For my own part, I prefer emphasizing the ability, at least as implied in OD&D and Holmes, for even 1st level magic users to prepare scrolls. The costs involved are a great sink for the treasure that they should be hauling out of the dungeon, and it provides a similar "spell multiplier" effect. I don't require Read Magic to cast from a scroll (which is one possible interpretation of that spell, per S&W and OD&D). If you've found my Thief class, you've surely already found my house rules, so you can see my thinking on that topic.

    One thing I'm still working out is that in my own conception I dislike the idea of "scrolls" for Clerical spells, since they are never written down, but simply impressed upon the Cleric's mind by divine power. But the OD&D rules expect Clerics to be able to tap a similar spell multiplier effect by advance preparation. I've been pondering the notion of "charms" or "fetishes" taking the similar spell storage role for clerics. My main issue at the moment is that such items would seem to be tied to particular god, and only usable by clerics of that faith, but that hampers the ability to provide such generic items as treasure, which is also an expectation of the OD&D environment.

    \n I can't figure out why people like the variable damage dice. 1d6 for all weapons is really cool if you ask me.

    Anyway, on-topic, I've got a house rule for magic users and clerics. They have to prepare their magic ahead of time, "hanging" spells or praying for benedictions or whatever, then they can use them at any time. It takes a full turn per spell to do this. They can load a number of spells equal to their level at a time. They can also just cast a spell immediately after preparing it, so it's not necessary to guess what crazy shit you're gonna need.

    Partially this is a color thing, because I think the idea of memorizing and forgetting spells is kinda silly. But it's also a gameplay thing. Way I see it, time is the primary resource in 0e. As time passes, you face wandering monster checks. And, since wandering monsters don't have gold, they aren't worth XP. Which means they're just a drain on your gold-finding resources.
