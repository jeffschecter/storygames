    \n I find it funny that people keep recommending Toon, as Tomas' statement in the other thread was predicated on his dissatisfaction with Toon, and I wrote SAN! because I wasn't satisfied with Toon. You might ask why. Here's a thing off the top of my head: hit points. Hit points are not funny.

    After a few playtests of SAN!, one of my players remarked that, yeah, it was funny, but you could do funny with any game. I disagreed. I told him, "Calculating your THAC0 is not funny."

    There are specialized pockets of funny, and you need specialized games to hit them. There is a particular one that SAN! hits squarely. It's not the Three Stooges/Keystone Kops/Buster Keaton kind of funny, though.

    However, the principles of SAN! are pretty flexible, and can be applied to other kinds of funny. I wrote another game called OBLITERATI that's more of a comedy of manners, using essentially the same techniques, but with different aims (I also stripped down the rules quite a bit). I posted about it awhile back, and the rules are on my LJ here. I don't think it'd be too hard to make a version that is dedicated to slapstick. (I don't have time to work on it right now, though)

    Tomas,
    Are you looking for a game in which the players collaboratively create, develop, and enjoy a humorous, fictional situation involving the following?

    1) Ordinary folks-type protagonists, usually down on their luck, with whom we can identify
    2) Protagonists engaged in an activity with an objective that we can relate to, usually work, courting, or enterprise
    3) Adversity derived principally from sheer bad luck, as though the universe were out to get the protagonists, that, combined with clumsiness and/or poor planning, leads to humorous accidents that threaten the above objective
    3a) Occasionally an antagonist, often an authority figure, providing additional adversity. He has a personal grudge against the protagonists, or at least seems to.
    4) Bickering and conflict between protagonists, due to the tensions resulting from above adversity
    5) Violence carried out in an absurd manner and/or with absurd results

    -Marshall

    \n The basic rules are open-source - Wushu

    It's designed for any over-the-top genre, from movie-style Martial Arts or Gun-Fu to Swashbuckling adventure to Pulp.

    Players describe what their character is going to do - but here's where it's different - they get an additional die to roll for each action they describe - so there is incentive to create strings of movie style action. Mooks (faceless npcs) are handle abstractly as a Threat rating that has to be reduced - which also allows complete freedom in describing how that happens. It would translate exactly to Slapstick. Here's the explanation from the rulebook:\n
    Every player gets one die in their pool just for doing something. To get more dice, they have to add details to their description of the action. These "Embellishments" can be anything from additional actions to technical details or dramatic flourishes. As long as they're entertaining, and appropriate to the desired tone of the game, they're worth an extra die.
    For example...
    A player who shoots someone gets only their default (1) die. A player who
    shoots someone (1) while jumping down a flight of stairs (+1) and landing in a
    summersault (+1) gets 3 dice. A player who shoots someone (1), then ducks
    under a punch (+1) and leg sweeps the guy (+1) so he can shoot the goon
    behind him (+1) and then knocks out the first guy with a boot to the face
    (+1) gets 5 dice.
    A player who kicks someone gets only their default (1) die. A player who
    kicks someone (1) in the throat (+1), making their trachea collapse with an
    audible "sssssnap" (+1) gets 3 dice. A player who kicks someone (1) so hard
    that they crash through a window (+1) and fall screaming to the street
    seven floors below (+1), crushing the roof of a taxi (+1) and causing onlookers
    to run screaming in all directions (+1) gets 5 dice.

    \n Okay, here it is. Anyone got a name for it?

    WHAT YOU NEED
    3-5 people;
    Pens & paper;
    A big bowl of dice, in two colors. It doesn\u2019t matter how many sides; it\u2019s an even/odd mechanic, so as long as they\u2019re not those fabled d7s or something, you can use any combination of sizes. It also doesn\u2019t matter which two colors, but I\u2019ll be referring to them as white and black.

    GETTING READY
    You need to decide who will be in charge of antagonism & adversity. This can be one person, or you can share the responsibility, whichever you feel most comfortable with. This person is hereafter referred to as the Antagonism Player, or AP.

    Now it\u2019s time to make your PCs. If the AP is just one person, he doesn\u2019t make a PC, but he can make an Antagonist Character (AC), which works the same way. Give your PC a name, and write it down on a sheet of paper. Then come up with some traits that describe the PC, in terms of appearance, personality, ability, whatever, and write these down on the sheet. Create your characters out loud; that is, as you write down \u201cBaggy trousers\u201d as a trait, say out loud, \u201cMy guy wears baggy trousers.\u201d Just come up with enough traits for everyone to get a good picture of your character; you can add more whenever you want.*

    Now you need to decide the situation. The PCs need to be working toward some sort of identifiable objective that is accomplished through smaller, accumulated tasks. Building a house, courting lovers, selling vacuum cleaners door-to-door, etc. If you can\u2019t decide on one, just put a bunch of ideas into a hat and draw one at random. The AC, if there is one, needs to be working against them in some way. ACs are usually authority figures (the boss, a stern mother-in-law, a cop, etc.), and they should be played as though they have a personal grudge against the PCs.

    LAUGHTER
    During the game (including chargen!), anytime your contributions cause someone to laugh, claim a white die from the bowl. If they laugh so hard that they fall out of their chair, do a spit-take, or spew fluids from their nose, take five white dice. White dice are good, so you want to be at the top of your funny. In-character quips, sound effects, miming, and facial expressions will be good ways to do this.

    SCENE FRAMING
    Next you have to frame your first scene. Everyone participates in this, contributing an element at a time to the scene, until everyone feels like you\u2019ve got enough. Again, you can add stuff later when you want,* so don\u2019t sweat it; just get a good picture of it.

    Later on, you can call for a new scene when it\u2019s appropriate (like if you need to change locales).

    TASKS, PROGRESS, AND FRUSTRATION
    Once the scene is in place, players controlling PCs will take turns around the table. On your turn, you can either attempt a Task, try to overcome a Setback, help someone else overcome a Setback, or vent your Frustrations. I\u2019ll explain those other ones in a minute; here\u2019s how Tasks work.

    Describe how your PC performs some small task that contributes to overall progress towards the objective. The AP then describes how something goes wrong. Then it\u2019s time to roll dice.

    For each of your traits that is involved in any way with your description of the Task, you may take one white die from the bowl; hold these dice in your hand. If you do this, put a check mark next to that trait on your sheet; you won\u2019t be able to get a die from it again this scene. If another PC has traits that are involved, that PC\u2019s player may let you use them for dice as well (these traits are still checked off). If this is the first time the Task in question has been attempted in the way you described, take one white die from the bowl, and add it to the dice in your hand. If you feel like you need more dice, you can take some of the dice you\u2019ve earned by making people laugh.

    The AP gets one white die if this is the first time the misfortune/accident/whatever he described has happened. If his AC is involved, he may get dice from the AC\u2019s traits just like PC traits. He may also use dice he\u2019s earned from making people laugh. Finally, he gets all the black dice that you currently have (if you\u2019ve just started, you don\u2019t have any yet).

    Now that you\u2019ve both got your dice, roll \u2018em. You\u2019re looking for even-numbered results. If you\u2019ve got more evens than the AP, you win; you make some progress despite the misfortune, and gain a Progress point (make a hashmark on the bottom of your sheet somewhere). If the AP wins, then you fail to make any Progress, and, if he can think of one, he may inflict a Setback on you or one of the other PCs as a consequence of what just happened. Also, if the AP wins, your PC gains some Frustration: take a black die from the bowl.

    If you tie, then the thing that went wrong doesn\u2019t cause any Setbacks or prevent Progress, yet. Instead, it triggers a chain event, which the AP describes, and you describe your PC\u2019s reaction to it, and then you roll dice again. If it ties again, then there\u2019s another chain event, and so on, until you either win or lose.

    All white dice that were rolled are now returned to the bowl. Black dice are returned to the player. I\u2019ll tell you how to get rid of them in a minute.

    Once that\u2019s done, your turn is over. In fact, any time you roll dice, that\u2019s the end of your turn.

    (continued)

    \nEXAMPLE
    The PCs are building a house. It\u2019s Ted\u2019s turn. Ted\u2019s player says that Ted is nailing a doorframe together. The AP says that, as Ted reaches back to swing the hammer, the hammerhead flies off and hits PC William in the head. If this is the first time that someone has attempted to drive nails with a hammer, Ted\u2019s player gets a die for that (and, the next time someone drives nails or tries to assemble a doorframe, they have to think of another way to do it, or else receive no die). If this is the first time that a hammerhead has flown off, the AP gets a die for that.

    Ted\u2019s player aslo gets a die from, say, Ted\u2019s \u201cBig and strong\u201d trait, and he checks it off on the sheet. Let\u2019s say that Ted also has a \u201cWhistle while you work\u201d trait, and he\u2019s whistling while he works, so Ted\u2019s player can get a die for that, then check it off. Ted\u2019s player and the AP take some dice from their earned-from-laughter pile. Ted has not been Frustrated yet, so he has no black dice.

    They roll. Let\u2019s say the AP wins. Ted\u2019s player takes a black die from the bowl. The AP says that the hammerhead knocks William unconscious as a Setback. The AP should feel free to add sound effects, and the involved players should feel free to roleplay responses. That\u2019s how you\u2019re going to get your extra dice, after all.

    If Ted had won, there would have been no Setback or Frustration, and he would have made Progress somehow. Maybe William ducks at just the right moment, the hammerhead ricochets off a conveniently placed tree, and lands back on the hammer\u2019s shaft just in time for Ted to drive the nail. Why not? It could happen.

    If the rolls had tied, then maybe William gets hit with the hammerhead and stumbles over to knock the can of nails into the air, and the nails are now falling through the air (pointy end downward, of course) toward Ted, who, let\u2019s say, shrieks and tries to take cover under the doorframe. This is the first time that nails have fallen through the air, so the AP gets a die for that, plus the same black dice. This is the first time that someone has taken cover under a doorframe, so Ted gets a white die, plus, let\u2019s say, one for a \u201cHigh-pitched shriek\u201d trait.

    If Ted loses this roll, then he probably ends up with a bunch of nails sticking out him (Setback). If he wins, then the nails probably land in the doorframe in neat rows, nailing it together (Progress).


    SETBACKS
    Anytime a character suffers some consequence that would prevent him from attempting Tasks, that\u2019s a Setback. Being buried by a pile of rubble, getting your jacket caught in the door, being glued to the ceiling, etc. You deal with Setbacks just like you deal with Tasks: say how you\u2019re doing it, the AP says what goes wrong, you claim and roll dice. The only difference is that you don\u2019t get Progress points for success; the reward for winning is overcoming the Setback. If the AP wins, he can inflict a Setback on someone else, or he can change the one that currently affects you. F\u2019rinstance, if you were hanging from a balcony by your beltloops, maybe you fall, break through some floorboards, and are now lodged there, with your legs sticking up out of the floor.

    If you have suffered a Setback, you can\u2019t do anything on your turn except try to overcome it. If your buddy suffers a Setback, then you probably want to use your turn to try to get him out of it. Because, let\u2019s face it, you\u2019re going to need all the help you can get if there\u2019s going to be any Progress.

    If you can\u2019t think of a way to deal with a Setback, you can scratch off a Progress point and cut to a later time (but same scene), and you\u2019ve overcome the Setback in the interval. Don\u2019t worry about how, it doesn\u2019t really matter.

    WHY, I OUGHTTA\u2026
    The only way to get rid of Frustration (black dice) is to take it out on other people. Describe how you\u2019re going to take it out on one of the other PCs. If this would not have any lasting consequences (i.e. wouldn\u2019t cause a Setback), then return one black die to the bowl. If it would cause a Setback, then return three black dice to the bowl; then, if that PC\u2019s player wishes, he can describe something going wrong or his PC resisting or retaliating, and then you both roll dice as normal. The loser suffers the Setback, which is defined by the winner.

    THE ENDING
    The game comes to an end when a PC hits 10 Frustration or Progress Points. When this happens, total up everyone\u2019s Frustration and Progress. If total Progress meets or exceeds total Frustration, then the PCs attain their objective. Otherwise, they don\u2019t.

    *FOOTNOTE
    I say \u201cwhenever you want,\u201d but, not really, because your fellow players need to be okay with it. In other words, don\u2019t be a jackass about it.

    The title is supposed to be utterly ridiculous and nonsensical at one level, but on another level it's poetic and evocative. Mercury" into Japanese and back into English, it's "Silver water rough adventure"? That makes sense in Japanese, but not in English, until you look at it a certain way. And when you look at it that certain way, it's poetic and evocative.

    I'm inclined to think that anything that forces you to look at things in a manner you're not accustomed to is a good thing.

    The style of humor that SAN! is made for is one such thing. It's made to capture a particular brand of humor that I first encountered in an improv troupe I used to be a member of. It's a kind of humor that requires you take a broad, general view of things, and let your brain draw connections and relationships between things that, to "rational" approaches, are clearly unrelated. (On a related note, see "the Psychic Game," created by two of my friends from that improv troupe, which I've posted here)

    In one session, I played Jimbo the Barbarian, while my friends played a used car salesman and a sullen goth girl. We started in the city. What's Jimbo the Barbarian doing in the city? Who knows. Clearly a savage man-ape sent after me by my nemesis, the Skull-Faced Man (I didn't even know who the Skull-Faced Man was; it just came out). That sullen goth girl? Clearly a priestess of dark gods, not to be trusted at all. The sewer? Clearly one of the Skull-Faced Man's dungeons. One of the great things about that session was that the sewer did turn out to be one of the Skull-Faced Man's dungeons, as well as a sewer. And the sullen goth girl turned out to be capable of summoning the spirits of the dead.

    The interesting thing is that today's ultra-violent Horror films (and video games) are Slapstick "without the joke" - at least the conscious joke.
