\n Hi!
\n So, I have seen a couple of threads lately (well for years, but it took til now to articulate what I am saying now), that went something like this: "I used to play X, but then I evolved and now I play Y" or "I used to play games of X playstyle, but now I only play Y playstyle."
\n
\n I think this is a fallacy and toxic to our hobby.
\n
\n Think about it, it's like if I said, I used to listen to classical music, but I have since evolved and only listen to techno. Now there is a lot of negative connotations wrapped up in this statement:
\n1) Techno is objectively better than Classical.
\n2) I am objectively better than anyone who listens to Classical music.
\n3) Everyone who listens to Classical music is objectively less evolved than Techno fans
\n4) That there is music that is objectively better than other music.
\n5) That this is not open to discussion, because I have already declared my superiority.
\n
\n Yet, some of us do that all the time. I used to play D&D, but now I play Sorcerer. I used to play Gamist, but Narrative play is much better.
\n
\n So, do you think I have a point, or am I full of baloney?
\n
\n So, do you think we can change this, or is it an immutable part of the industry?
\n
\n Can't we all just get along?
\nDave M

    \n I don't hear any objective claims in "...but now I only...", so much as a personal preference. I used to play D&D 3.5, but now I only play D&D 4E. I prefer it. I no longer have any reason why I would want to play 3.5. Maybe you do, but I didn't say anything about that, I only said what I do.

    As far as evolution, as Ben and Jeff hinted at, I see an irony in its application here. In biology, where it started, evolution simply means "a change in alleles over time." Only if you also use the idea that "evolution always generates progress" do you get to the idea that something "more evolved" means "something better." "More evolved" really just means "more changed." For instance, Neanderthals had more muscle mass than us, could run faster than us, and by all appearances, had bigger brains than us\u2014maybe, proportionally, only the same size as ours, maybe just plain old bigger. After all, the main evidence of cultural adaptation in the Paelolithic comes when the Ch\xe2telperronian toolset combined elements of the Neanderthals' Mousterian toolset, and our own Levallois toolset. But we didn't do that, the Neanderthals did. So, in just about every way we can imagine, Neanderthals seem "better" than us. But, evolution meant our survival and their extinction. Best theory I've read says they had some tiny number fewer births, so we out-bred them. Evolution means change, and while it does mean better adapting to the specific, current conditions, the one better adapted to the current condition may still have a lot of disadvantages overall. Every so often, evolution means that things get worse. Evolution does not mean the same thing as progress.

    That said, using the term "evolution" for games still irks me, because games don't procreate.

    \n As far as I can tell, the only people who are making claims about "superiority" are over on therpgsite, where terms like "swine" are used to refer to those whom they regard as claiming superiority over them and their "badwrongfun," which is an overheated way of interpreting the "system matters" game design camp. Meanwhile, over on the Forge, Ron Edwards is bending over backwards to accommodate designers with all sorts of different play and design preferences and has pretty much stamped out invidious distinctions of exactly the sort that Dave is suggesting are pretty common. Over here on Story-Games, you occasionally see people making reference to the "conventional Story-Games wisdom" in the context of evaluating games and their worth, but that's usually a straw man, since game preferences on S-G seem to be both catholic in their breadth and fickle to the point of volatility: what's the new hawtness, dawg?

    There's some conceptual confounding in the way the OP is put together, too. The thread title implies that games don't evolve, but the post itself makes it clear that Dave is talking about claims about a gamer's personal "evolution" as a player of games. But it's quite clear that games do "evolve" in at least a metaphorical sense: we can speak of first-generation RPGs, second-generation, and so forth, implying that later games are responses to and developments of earlier games. It's not genetic, it's memetic, though: the evolution of ideas rather than organisms.

    And while, all other things being equal, one gamer's personal play preferences may be as "valid" (meaning what, exactly?) as any other's, it seems clear that unless you are playing solo, it is inevitable that play preferences will either be compatible or incompatible with other's play preferences, suggesting an "ecology of play" in which people adapt their behavior and their understandings of that behavior in order to achieve the highest degree of possible compatibility (e.g., by changing their expectations, by rationalizing their own and other's behaviors, by checking out or quitting the game, by designing a heart-breaker, by talking it out, and so forth). And if there's an ecology of play, then some preferences will be more "adaptive" than (or "superior to," if you like) others, in the sense of generating an optimal experience for the player, under given circumstances.

    One final thought: while we're taking care to distinguish between the evolution of games and the evolution of gamers, it's also possible to argue that they "co-evolve," in the sense that certain games are only possible once a population of players exists that is sufficiently familiar with the basis of its design to play it. For example, you can't go straight from minis war-gaming a la Chainmail to Capes, can you? You need the intermediate games, and the people who've played those games, to get there.

    Posted By: Ben LehmanP.S. Evolution isn't linear. It's adaptive. An important analogy here.
    \nExactly. It's the difference between better (objectively better, improved, superior, etc.) and better-suited (properly adapted to get the most use out of a particular niche/situation). It's amazing how many people assume that evolution is about becoming better, when it's really only ever been about becoming better-suited.

    So yeah, your tastes can evolve. It doesn't mean that techno is objectively better music than classical, it just means that it's a better fit for who you are and what you want than classical was. And whatever musical genre you switch allegiances to next year won't be objectively better than techno, either...just better suited to who you are next year. (Oh, okay, probably it will be objectively better than techno -- how could it not be? -- but I'm trying to make a point here.)

    \n To begin, I believe that some games are better than others, even though this opinion may be harder to support because it is hampered by the incredible openendedness of the roleplaying hobby and because it is based upon personal conviction. While this belief is, at its root, based upon opinions that others will disagree with, I would say that discussion will help find middle ground. If anyone wants to discuss this in more detail, just say so, but right now I'm trying to stay within the scope of this thread.
    I think that one thing that makes it difficult to say that some games are better than others is the incredible diversity possible in RPG's. A few inexperienced roleplayers can have a miserable time playing Dogs, while some players more versed in RPG's can still make something fun and beautiful and epic out of a game of D&D (which seems to be something of a codeword for "games that aren't narrativist.") In a movie or a book, people will have a more consistent experience. As Luke said during a discussion of the "Fruitful Void," the key to roleplaying is that it is that "fire" that players bring to the table. Ultimately, roleplaying is what you make it, and that makes roleplaying games incredibly hard to quantify.
    While roleplaying is what you make it, I would say that a gamelist is superior to others when it is more likely to yield a meaningful, challenging, emotionally involved experience as opposed to an experience that I would say is less edifying and more empty. And here is where opinion rears its complex head. Some might say "But I don't think that's what roleplaying is about." If anyone disagrees with this statement, let's first compare notes on what a good play experience is. I'm sure that even though I love the tiny Dogs and someone else may have a library of core books, we can still agree on what a good roleplaying experience is.
    With that said, I think that all gamestyles and game lists evolve. Evolution just means to change over time. However, I also think that some playstyles do adapt, or become superior to others.

    If you play Call of Cthulhu and have fun with it, awesome.
    I have no right to look down on you for that.

    I do judge gamers based on the amount of fun they are having.
    If you play Call of Cthulhu and have a shitty time with it, I will suggest Don't Rest Your Head, and even offer to run it.
    If you refuse to try something else, even though what you're doing now isn't very fun, I will think that's stupid.
    If you are doing X for fun, but it's not actually very fun, and you refuse to try Y, then that's stupid.

    Everyone could benefit from knowing more.
    If you've only ever played Call of Cthulhu, I'm going to suggest you try Don't Rest Your Head, because it has stuff you might like.
    If you've only ever played Don't Rest Your Head, I'm going to suggest you play... well, probably not Call of Cthulhu, but perhaps Unknown Armies.
    Not because you should change your fun.
    Because it's always a good thing to explore, broaden horizons, dabble, learn more, and see whether any of it "sticks".
    And if it doesn't stick, I have no right to judge you based on that.

    That's kinda my view.
