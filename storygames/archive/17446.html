\nNOTE: This is a brainstorming post, and as such it is not my usual eloquent and well edited fare. It could even be complete nonsense. I've been warned.

Most of my design work consists of testing single rule 'nanosystems', one of which is the following: Characters are described as a series of premises, and are given a chance to react whenever a situation arises which would disprove or contradict those premises. So for example, if you have 'Fast as a Bullet', then if someone states that a normal person running has caught up to you, you can roll to counter such a statement. Basically, it's all about testing premises, and discovering they exist through attempting actions which contradict them.

So far so good, except we run into trouble when we use premises which can't be disproven, like 'God is watching over me', or aren't well defined, like 'I will not harm the innocent' (but what is considered innocent?), or deal with internal states, like 'my girlfriend hates me'. And the reason I'm concerned with this is because the system breaks down when you cannot test a premise.

But then it occurs to me that this issue already exists in RPGs, it's just simply ignored because the players have already made assumptions about things. It's the reason the existence of the Invisible Choir is accepted in Unknown Armies (despite the lack of 'facts' to support it), and Vampire players are more willing to accept Supernatural vs Scientific explanations (which results in a lot of fun on my part).

But where this really gets important is in mysteries, and since all RPGs are mysteries this is really important. If you can't test or assume a premise, you can't have a mystery, and so much is possible and assumable in your typical fantasy setting that you're left with very little you can actually test (no thanks to my 7th level spell "Destroy Physical Evidence"), but honestly I'd love to be able to defeat a dragon by taking a series of actions to discover HOW to do so.

And it's not as simple as just making perception skill checks, but that's another whole discussion. Questions are great when you already have a working model of things and a set of axioms and assumptions to draw on, but those things cannot be expanded upon without experimentation. Which reminds me, I've yet to find an RPG that has any decently engaging experimentation rules either.

Finally, I'm well aware of formal logic and the issue of proof. You can't prove pigs can't fly, only show that they haven't yet. So the final sticky issue is how much is enough evidence for proofieness? I'd love to be able to numerically rate a premise in how proven it is, but what would be the best way to do so?

And ultimately, I'd like such a system to eliminate the issue of 'metagaming' with information the character cannot know by making it 'unimportant', and give the player the chance to engage their own logic skills in play.

    \n What you are describing is what the Bayes Theorem is meant to answer:

    P(B|C) = [ P(C|B) * P(B) ] / P(C)

    OR

    P(B|C) = [ P(C|B) * P(B) ] / [ P(C|B) * P(B) + P(C|~B) * P(~B) ]

    OR

    P(B|C) = [ P(C|B) * [ P(B}C) * P(C) + P(B|~C) * P(~C) ] ] / P(C)


    P(B|C) = probability that you can run faster than a bullet given that you just got caught
    P(C|B) = probability that you would get caught if you could run faster than a bullet
    P(B) = probability that you can run faster than a bullet (regardless of whether or not you get caught)
    P(C) = probability that you will get caught (regardless of how fast you can run)
    P(C|~B) = probability that you would get caught if you could not run faster than a bullet
    P(~B) = probability that you cannot run faster than a bullet = 1 - P(B)
    P(B|~C) = probability that you can run faster than a bullet if you don't get caught
    P(~C) = probability that you won't get caught = 1 - P(C)

    Solve one of the two equations above for the one you want, fill in the rest based on your intuitions, and calculate.
