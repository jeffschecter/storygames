    \n Levi, have you considered WordPress? It is easy, easier than a piece of cake while walking in the park, and BAM! there's a barrel full of a fish and a shotgun. It's traditionally used for blogging but, especially in the recent versions, has become a whiz-bang general purpose CMS, supporting even moderately ambitious presentation plans. And that will improve - full-service CMS is the direction Matt is taking the software in.

    Updating it is easy, so security concerns can be readily addressed via patching, and the community is supportive and quite large. Customization is super-straightforward, and it's well-written, so if you have just enough PHP-fu to cookbook code, you'll find that you can dive right into the guts and make it do whatever you like. The best part is that unlike Drupal and friends, WordPress actually does something right out of the box - it's a product, not just a platform. That initial hurdle is what kept me away from other CMSes.

    Please feel free to contact me if you're interested in getting sold on it/having me help you set it up.

    \n You can arrange it so that they can. Each user has a role, and each role has certain rights associated with it; a Author can normally write and edit its own content, where an Editor can edit anyone's content, and an Admin can do all that and also manage users. I'd recommend taking a look at wordpress.org's resources, maybe starting at Working with Wordpress.

    I mean, you're starting with a wiki, and maybe a different wiki platform is what you want. If you're looking for something similar, I've used and can recommend MediaWiki. Not that you needed me to say that, I guess, seeing as how there's Wikipedia as a testimony and all that. I've also been involved in the administration of an Atlassian Confluence wiki at work, which I liked less. But hey, maybe you'd be well served by something completely different, right?
