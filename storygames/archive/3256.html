\n So I'm listening to the most recent episode of Have Games, Will Travel, and having to rewind it over and over again because Paul's conversation with Joshua BishopRoby is touching off all kinds of ideas in my head about my game, Misspent Youth.     \n Almost right. The thing you do to pass the Scrip isn't your thing, it's incorporating someone else's thing. So when someone else tags my Thematic Battery of "Young" they pass me the Scrip and they take the XP. What I find this does is dramatically increase the amount of player-to-player interaction, as opposed to the more common player-to-GM interaction.

    Scrips are also the only way to end scenes. Which is mind-bendy but cool. That why folks were glowering at me; I was turning into the only person with the power to end the scene.

    \n In my game before I had essentially stolen PTA, where a resource gets put out there and people give it to one another for being cool. The problem I've found with that though is sometimes it logjams and people forget.

    So in the new system, there'll be a number of ways you can claim that resource for yourself, and taking those points and using them is important for the end game of each game session so people will really want them. I have also stolen spolight characters, and spolights will be the only ones who can give out points for stuff they personally like.

    So the issue now is coming up with triggers (a la TSOY Keys). I had the "my trait gets in the way" but had forgotten my idea for the other one, which was, "I help you by pointing out your trait in my RP." I am probably also going to add, "I did something cool based on this trait."

    \n I have totally swiped the Thematic Batteries idea for the Pentasystem, but have kind of combined it with Situation Engineering (another FLFS thing) and TSOY's Keys.

    Characters have "enmeshment" with attributes of other characters, the setting, the situation and so forth. It's along the lines of X's attribute Y and how I feel about it. Part of the initial phase of the game is, cooperatively, making sure that every character is thoroughly enmeshed (the parallel with situation engineering).

    The carrot for this is that whenever an entity your character is enmeshed with comes up in a scene, you get 1 XP. If the specific attribute comes up, you get 2 XP, and if the attribute is set up to be potentially changed by a conflict, you get 5 XP (like Keys).

    You can also use your enmeshment to get rerolls (or give them to other people) in conflicts where they'd be relevant.

    Finally, each enmeshment has an associated goal or dilemma, which again is like a Key. You get XP for trying to resolve it and failing, and for finally resolving it.

    The format goes something like:

    "I feel jealous towards Jim because of his popularity. Goal: Make Jim look bad in public while I look good."

    I'm tinkering with rules where Jim can refuse to admit to your character's perception that he's popular and that can go back and forth as well, eventually resolving as either "Your character is imagining it", "Jim is in denial" or "Your character got over it without the issue ever having been settled".

    \n Two answers, Rich:

    a) When you just start out using scrips, most of the passes are, as you say, "short-hand desciptors [that] pigeon-hole the PCs." My set of convention characters has a Patriot, and so players are always shouting "For Queen and Country! For Queen and Country!" and passing scrips to the Patriot. However, once you get into the game and start really developing the characters, the references that accompany passes get less obvious, less reductive, and more textured. Which means it's hard to describe it in a single line in this thread -- but if the PCs had uncovered some atrocity commited by British troops, the scrip might get passed to the Patriot to accompany nothing more than a signficant look.

    b) You have three thematic batteries and you can swap one out between each situation. Or more, if you really want. So if one is getting old or just not working for you anymore, it's time to find a new facet of your character.

    Okay, three answers:

    The thing about scrips is that they don't just continually bring up already established facts about characters. After the characters are established, scrips work both to reinforce what's already there but also to challenge what's been established and cast those things in new light. They end up working like in Dogs, where the GM is always asking, "You believe that? What about now? What about in this situation? What about when this is on the line?" Because thematic batteries are so short -- a word or a phrase -- they are continually open to interpretation.
