    \n I think "hidden moves" is a really solid idea - - the way I like to do it is, I keep the basic moves sheet in front of me (but in sight of all) and whenever a player says they're gonna do something that sounds like a basic move, to me, I hand them the sheet.
    As for playbook-moves, it seems like folks have those in front of them enough, and they picked them out deliberately, such that they can ask "hey, i want to do X. how do i do it?" and they they just declare when they want to use the move, and then i help them make it actually get into the fiction properly.

    I like the d20 idea. I'd be careful, though - - mathematically it's gonna be pretty different, as there's a big difference between the odds of rolling a 7 on 2d6 (it's the most likely result) vs. rolling anything on a d20: all numbers on a d20 are equally possible. But maybe some math went into deciding on 9-17 as the "qualified success" result...

    \n Something I did for a recent DW game that worked like gangbusters: I had 7-8 custom moves for the game, like stuff that happened when you braved the Winter King's storms or were touched by the wraiths. I printed those out on their own little sheets of paper, and when the PCs did stuff fictionally that triggered the move, I slapped it down on the table.

    It got everybody really interested, immediately, in what was about to happen.

    You might suppose this technique would detract from being absorbed in the fiction, but it didn't, possible because it was all custom moves, and not the standard ones.

    Posted By: Zac in Virginiabut yeah, the math don't sound too good.
    \nIgnoring modifiers the math is pretty spot on.
    7-9 on 2d6: 42%
    9-17 on 1d20: 45%

    10+ on 2d6: 17%
    18+ on 1d10: 15%

    Doing a linear transform to remap the rest of the points (equivalent to the multiply modifiers by 3 I mentioned above), shows that the error stays under 0.03 except for target numbers 1-3, 18-20, where it stays under about 0.08. However, the d20 is incapable of emulating the ends of the 2d6 probability, because of the inherently differently shaped density function.

    \n Your numbers are a little off, but the idea is right.

    +1 on 2d6 changes failure from 42% to 28%, middling success from 42% to 44%, and strong success from 17% to 28%.

    Oh, I guess that means on d20 adding a modifier doesn't change the odds of a middling success at all until you run into 1 or 20.

    The odds of failure drop by 14%, almost 15%, which shows why using +3 with 1d20 about matches +1 on 2d6. Similarly, use -6 instead of -2, etc.
