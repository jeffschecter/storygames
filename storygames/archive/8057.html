    \n It's a Finnish tabletop rpg written by Ville Vuorela of Burger Games, based on the novella "Roadside Picnic" by the Strugatsky brothers and the Tarkovsky movie. Players play stalkers - people entering twisted Zones in the hope of finding artifacts to sell, and also negotiating the social jungle surrounding the areas (corruption, official investigators, etc.) Think a scientific variant of D&D, basically, with lots of social aspects included and with a strong emphasis on really playing your character as a persona. What sets it apart from others is that it's damn well written, and has a very smooth yet surprisingly reliable diceless mechanic where the GM grades idea qualities and situational role-playing, and then counts their multiple against a target number. This makes it very smooth to play, as long as people accept a very old-school idea of absolute GM authority.
    \n
    \nAs the game is in Finnish, so are its home pages. But you can find the author's comments here, by scrolling back and/or using "search" on it: http://www.burgergames.com/notes/
    \nMy own review in English is here: http://jiituomas.livejournal.com/53066.html , and there's also a set of actual play reports in English there in my blog, starting from August 27th 2008, all tiled "Orchard: Session ...".

    \n According to Boris Strugatsky and his literary agent, S.T.A.L.K.E.R. videogames are not based on any license and that is why they do not refer to Strugatskis anywhere and the title is spelled out abnormally. Of course, their link to Roadside Picnic is obvious to anyone who has read the book but the developers cannot (and to my knowledge have not) say so.

    However, Stalker RPG has an official license from Boris Strugatsky and can (and does) use direct references and quotes, even if the setting has been edited, or "modernized" as I like to call it. For every game sold a few coins go to Strugatskis as royalties. Stalker RPG has no official connection to the S.T.A.L.K.E.R. videogames, although I would be lying if I claimed I had not played (and liked) them.

    \n Stalker is a very interesting game, design-wise. I'll be reviewing it one of these days, when I have an opportune moment. It'll be interesting to see how the international scene likes the game - heck, if Ville gets it done and printed before Gencon and my own plans don't go astray, I can take a bunch with me there to promote and sell - it'd be great to get to sell Stalker at Gencon. Let's look into that around June or so, it'll be no problem to carry a couple of suitcases worth (my suitcases will otherwise be empty anyway on the way there, so as to have space for stuff on the way back), if you want to send some to a fulfillment service over the Atlantic or something. (Unless it's easier to just print there.)

    Theory-wise it has considerable similarities to Dread (the Jenga one), and it really is surprisingly coherent when you get right down to it - lays it out very clearly how the game should be played. I'd characterize the advocated playstyle as vanilla simulationism, and the mechanics certainly don't get in the way of that. The most important techniques the game uses are GM prep and constructive dialogue (really, the method used to resolve conflicts is all about this), everything else flows from the basic split of player/GM roles. The thing I especially like about the conflict resolution system is that it's non-random and completely based on the narrated reality all players have at hand; gives some real zing to player decisions, and practically forces players to think in terms of the fiction instead of mechanics. Lots of GMing advice worthy of Epediah Ravachol, too.

    One thing I'm going to write about in a bit more detail when I get around to reviewing the game is that Stalker is written by an adult, and it's very gratifying how non-nerdy it is even while the game advocates all-out adventure content. It's like the polar opposite of the American violence fantasy overload I suffered last summer. I remember how reading Stalker then helped me to put all the plastic ultraviolence to perspective - the game is so down-to-earth and serious that this modern "OMG! Ninjaz!" thing is completely anathema to it.
