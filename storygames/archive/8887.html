\n I've got this idea for this dungeon game, which is actually just taking some stuff from some really old games of mine and making it, y'know, work.

So, one of the big things is, equipment is pretty much disposable. Your stuff wears down and breaks, or you'll drop it while being chased by ghosts, or it'll be stolen from you by competitors, or you'll discard it to make room for something else. Mostly, your pack is reserved for treasure; as far as your useful equipment, you're going to be carrying that in your pockets and in your hands.

One of the best ways to survive will be to use stuff that's in your surroundings. Like MacGuyver. Only, I have no idea what sort of rules to use for that. I'm shooting for a grimmer and grittier mood than MacGuyver, but MacGuyvering is MacGuyvering, pretty much.

How would you handle it? Have you seen any systems that do, or do something similar, or could? What about that Ars Magica thing I hear people talk about, could it be hacked for something like this?

None of the system is set in stone yet. Although I think I'd like to use d20s.

    \n What I'm familiar with goes something like this:

    GM establishes what's in the room.

    GM establishes the problem the problem that needs solving.

    Player requests more info about what's in the room, as player comes up with embryonic ideas about possible solutions.

    So, at that stage, where the player is trying to find out from the GM whether the chair is the right size and shape to plug the hole that the rats are coming through, you could introduce some mechanic that determines the setting content (in this case, chair shape) relative to player points or scenario difficulty or some other dimension you'd like to control/manipulate.

    Alternatively, that jiggering of setting content could be done pre-play, which allows a different experience of the setting in play (which you might want), but makes for more work on the GM's part.

    \n Here's one thing I've thought about. What if the things you can improvise are based on your skills? Like, if you've got the Electronics skill (or whatever), you can dissassemble this remote control, this rotodial telephone, and this toaster oven, and build stuff out of it.

    So, abstract "components" from things that your guy knows about? Could work.

    I also thought about going with the "what actually works" kind of thing. Like, if your guy has the Chemistry skill, he can do things like make explosives out of household chemicals. Thing is, should I tell you, through my game, how to make TNT and thermite with shit that you can find around the house? I mean, I could, but should I? Seems a dangerous rabbit hole, there.

    \n David,
    One problem is that the problem that needs solving sometimes isn't going to be something the GM thought up (or rolled from a table, or whatever). Most often, it's gonna be that there's another PC who might kill you. So you've gotta kill him back. All you've got is a car battery, some bleach, some nails, a claw hammer, and a phone cord; what do you do? You could just try to beat him to death with the hammer, but that's dangerous; you're pretty likely to get killed in direct conflict. So, better idea is to nail the phone cord in front of the door as a tripwire, such that the guy trips and falls onto some nails. And, as a fall-back, pry open that battery, hold your breath, dump the bleach in, and get the hell out of that room and any rooms it ventilates into, while it releases tasteless, odorless, poisonous chlorine gas.

    The easiest way really does look like "Player says what he does, GM decides if it works," but I don't know how to keep that unbiased.

    A. I want to reward player ingenuity, by having their clever plans actually have some effect
    B. I want the players to be constrained by their characters' abilities and knowledge bases.

    The problem here is reconciling those two.
