    \n Unfortunately I don't have APs posted anywhere, yet.
    (there is one, but it's italian only and needs polishing... I'll work on that)

    If you have any specific questions, ask away
    But in the meantime, the gist of it is:

    - every piece of your chess army (your Retinue) is actually a person (one King, one Queen, one Knight, one Bishop, one Rook and a faceless population of Pawns) (K, B and R each have 2 pieces on the board, as usual, but both pieces represent the same person)

    - every time you make a move, the move is tied to a fictional scene set in a specific time and place and cast of characters

    - every time you make a move, we also discover something personal about the human representing the piece you just moved

    - each move has different fictional consequences depending on its type...
    --- Positioning is when you do not capture
    --- Advancing is when you capture
    --- Conquering is when you capture an enemy King or move your own King across the middle line

    - you can only make a certain type of move if the fictional situation allows for it
    Example...\n
    if you move Zusanna, your Knight, into a scene taking place in the City's docks then you can not use the Knight to perform a move that would influence a different scene taking place in a high luxury condo... not until the scene at the docks ends, allowing Zusanna to appear in a different scene
    \n

    The game uses as a base normal chess rules, with a couple of elements taken from Chess2 (by Sirlin) and then building a structure that is natively geared for 2-3-4 players and fiction driven.
    This basically fucks up all usual chess conventions, allowing expert chess players to profit from their skill while still letting chess-newbs to enjoy the game and even win it.

    All while telling a story of scheming, manipulation, maneuvering, conquest and human lives sacrificed for all of that.

    \n Thanks
    Chess2 introduces 3 things to the normal game of Chess:

    1) a new victory condition (move the King across the middle line)

    2) a new bluff mechanic that makes Pawns incredibly valuable (and allows me to introduce treachery and betrayal into the scheming)

    3) various new armies with special rules and stuff

    I only use the first two elements, as I wanted KotC to be as simple as possible; so if you know chess, you know what all means, with no weird new things to add and remember.
    And if you are new to chess, the only thing to learn are the very simple classical rules (that are fully explained in my rulebook, it takes less than 2 pages, pictures included).

    @CarpeGuitarrem
    Yes, I reskinned "dueling" and "dueling stones" into Treachery and Maneuvers.
    Color aside, the mechanical part is the same.
    This way the player can make a move and describe the events as they unfold and the defender can optionally say "But treachery was afoot!" and use that mechanic to describe betrayal, double-crossings, unexpected reveals, etc. to retroactively inject plans and preparations and changes of heart.

    @Paul_T
    Nope, I just happened to use it more often than the others
    At the end of the rulebook there are 3 chessboards ready to be printed and used to play:
    2 players (just your average chessboard)
    3 players (the 3way you see in the pics, based on normal squares)
    4 players (a modified version of a 4way board)

    Each has the midline clearly highlighted, to help players see and evaluate the situation.

    \n Thanks David for the link
    I already knew it, but I chose to not use "matian chess" because I wanted the core version of the game to be as simple and familiar as possible, so that placing the armies on the board and moving them would appear obvious to anyone, just by knowing the very basic and classical chess rules (which I provide in the book).

    That said, exactly because KotC works with "basic" chess, it should be easy to adapt it to any sort of chess-variant
    3d/star-trek chess
    weird-board chess
    fantasy chess
    5 way chess
    etc
