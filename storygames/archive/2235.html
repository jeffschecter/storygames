A discussion of RPG carrying cases

Quintin Stone
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Play Advice
Years ago I bought a file case to carry around my papers and books. (Pictured with Optimus Prime for a sense of scale.)



After a while I realized it had two drawbacks. First, it was a bit small. With folders and papers, I could fit only 2 or 3 standard-sized RPG corebooks. Two, it didn't have a good way to carry pencils or other small items. Yesterday I got a Rubbermaid file case of the same style at OfficeMax. (Picture below is clear with a green lid, but mine was black.)



I was happy because it was everything I wanted. It even had the same visual style, down to the inverse ribbing on the lid, my old one had.

The first time I opened it and let the lid fall of its own weight, the lid fell off the hinges.

The second time, the outer two hinges (of four) snapped off and flew around the room.

What a bummer. When I got it home and showed it to my wife, she told me she had the same design and it had the same problem. Sure enough, when she pulled it out of storage, the same two hinges were snapped off of hers. A return trip to OfficeMax confirmed this is a design problem. Several others had broken hinges. Another Rubbermaid case of the same size but different design even had snapped hinges. So I returned the Rubbermaid and got this instead (also pictured with Jetfire for moral support):



The mack daddy of RPG cases? It's even bigger than the Rubbermaid one. It measures 10" deep at the base, a foot tall. It even has rubber wheels and a telescoping handle for rolling. No worries about the weight of the lid snapping the handles because it only opens about 80 degrees. That's a bit of mark against it, though it has tabs to lock it in an open position so you can access the interior without it falling closed. It's a bit bigger than I wanted (a bit of a monster honestly). I certainly won't be running out of room any time soon.

How common are cases like these among gamers? Or do you find that you just don't carry around enough books/papers make one necessary?


P.S. Hopefully Andy will verify that I am not a marketing plant.

And in that top plastic piece you can store pencils and dice and stuff, too.

    Yeah, and after Mark C and others saw MINE, they bought ones themselves. So it's pretty much a plague that's spreading up and down the East Coast. AND NOW IT'S ON THE INTERNETS!

    As it turns out, it's not too useful for single gaming sessions, it's just too big (and I don't play games that require a library for reference). I much prefer a smaller one. I usually bring my briefcase to my regular gaming group, but I too was considering getting one of the top "small" cases like the one that started this all for use with my weekly gaming group. The "fit two or three core books, a file or two, and a small set of dice" works perfectly for single session/weekly games.

    For years I've carried everything in a book bag - he kind that has gone out of style to be replaced by backpacks once again. It's got places for writing implements, pockets perfect for dice, and different compartments for books. It's a little hard on perfect-bound books, but I'm not too concerned.

    I usually carry around about five sets of rules. But these days that's not too much. Most of the space is taken up with scenario print outs for stuff I intend to run. I don't know that I'd bother to carry around any rules set that required a lot of space these days, like the other say above. If I can't play the game entirely from memory 99% of the time, then it's got too much stuff in the rules. I want games that play on principles, not on detailed rules for how to do just X or precisely Y.

    I was looking at a SRD game called "Basic Fantasy RPG" yesterday in PDF format. It's something between Basic D&D and AD&D1 boiled down to essentials. It was proffered as an "easy" version of D&D, suitable for kids. And all I could think of looking at it was, "Man, there are rules in here for grenade scatter" (which, given potion throwing is key to the whole fantasy genre, no?)

    The point is that the book could have gotten rid of half of it's contents, and still been way more complex in terms of having to look things up than, say, DitV. Kids can play The Princes' Kingdom, because they never have to read anything, for instance (my boy is 6, and just learning). They can understand how the game works from just the few mechanics that get used in play.

    If more people thought this way about play, then those wheeled monstrosities wouldn't be neccessary.

    Mike

    January 2007
    Years ago back in college, I started using a Rubbermaid file case frequently for gaming. Over the years, I've gone through a variety of ways to organize my game materials for portability (even if the portability is just to get from one room to the other in the house).

    I've used backpacks (and in high school, I even used a ruck sack for a while - loaded with 50 lbs of books and lead miniatures). Back packs are handy in leaving your hands free, but when stuffed full, often damage the corners of books. Also, they aren't very usefull for organizing your books while playing.

    The file cases are great, you can mix books and hanging file folders. The lid is usually tall enough above the books that a small bag of dice will fit in it (the cover compartment really doesn't hold enough dice for my tastes). Before I lost it when a brief case got stolen, I had a bamboo cigar tray that I used for dice rolling. It fit into the case also (usually holding some pens and stuff also). I use a file case today with folders for PCs, NPCs, dead characters, blank sheets, reference materials, campaign notes, etc. It also sometimes will have some game books in it. The file case sits near my chair (living room play space), and open or closed, serves as a bit of a table for a book or two that are actively being looked at.

    I've used a brief case when playing a game that had but a few books. I rarely play games that would really fit well into a brief case though. Most either have too many books, or don't even need as much space as a brief case. Plus, I currently don't own a decent brief case.

    For the past almost 5 years, I have been almost exclusively playing at home, and my needs of transport have been minimal. It's looking like I will soon have to play outside the home occaisionally now. I'll have to figure out how to reasonably transport my game materials (let's see, need Arcana Evolved, PH, DMG, MM, Ptolus, current module, character sheets and other notes, a notebook of supplemental rules and setting material, at least a few other books - probably won't all fit in one of these file cases).

    Frank

    When I look at a bag the very first litmus test is whether it has hard dividers for file folders. (And messenger bags always fail the test.) I never carry 8.5 x 11 hardcover core books these days. It's always file folders of prep notes and/or printouts of rules documents and/or small softcover rulebooks. So the bag must have hard dividers to protect this stuff from flopping over and folding and basically getting all trashed down into the bottom of the bag in transit. And it's never a thick stack of this stuff either. My current bag is a Japanese Dr. Ion bag I bought from J-List. It's supposedly A4 sized, but actually just a bit larger, so it just barely crunches the tabs on file folders when I zip it shut.

    And despite the litmust test, it doesn't have file dividers. But it does have the next best thing: a full size surface back pocket into which I was able to slip a stiff piece of cardboard that gives the whole bag structure.

    That said, I'm pretty much constantly looking for my next bag. Any recommendations on a slim profile bag with file dividers?

    Paul
