\n Just like this thread, what needs to be done to make a MC-less Monsterhearts? I wouldn't recommend it for campaign play, but would it be feasible for one-shots? The starting questions can be open to everyone. Scene framing can be done by rotation or another means. NPCs can be played by anyone who's character is out of the scenes. But what about Hard Moves, Menaces, Threats and that air of mystery that having a separate mind controlling the wider world (even if they are just making it up on the fly) can bring?

    \n Scene rotation can be akin to Remember Tomorrow. When it's your turn, you frame a scene as the MC about someone else's PC. For the duration of the scene, you're MC. You can ask other players to play NPCs, if you want. You're in charge of hard moves while you're MC. Menaces, Threats, and whatnot can either be ignored or can be communally tracked. My impression is also that Menaces and Threats are most important for long-running play.

    \n Seconded. The RT model is golden for this.

    Your three options are :
    make a scene introducing something /event / threat / NPC.
    make a scene where your character sets out to gain some strings/make a move on/towards some NPC.
    make a scene where a threat/NPC goes after someone's PC.

    Rotate turns. Leave any unresolved Threats or NPCs in the middle of the table for further use.

    \n We're thinking about something similar for IndieCon. That'll be a three-session game of Monsterhearts, with the MC rotating for each.

    To answer your question, then: rather than rotating the MC once per scene, I'd do it once per hour. That way, each MC gets charge for a nice period of time and the game has a natural progression.

    If you're looking for truly MC-less play, then I'd suggest that everyone does the descriptive stuff, everyone does the scene-framing and hard moves are mandatory on a failed roll.

    \n Graham makes a good point. You could try a Primetime Adventures-inspired approach where one (or more) characters just take a backseat in certain sessions while their player(s) takes on the MC role.

    Approaches where you assign NPCs, scene framing, and hard moves to players would probably work fine as well. You just pass NPCs around if they end up in scenes with a PC also played by the same player. No biggie.

    It'll probably feel different but it'll still work, especially for a shorter arc.

    \n Cool.

    I feel like my somewhat bitchy questions were answered, directly or indirectly. I think Vincent is spot-on: "all you have to do is want to do it."

    Harlequin also taught me something: "I imagine this is for groups who just don't have someone who gels with the MC role."

    That's cool. I just think the game is ideally played as its designed. That is, with someone as MC who has a creative vision about the situation and characters and is committed to it. That's what's good about an MC/GM. For a long time, for example, I was excited about playing Burning Wheel and Sorcerer but not about GMing them. Then, for both games, I got a creative vision for GMing and am now excited about expressing that through play and seeing how the characters mess about in that. For both games, also, I think that came from playing them as a player, getting my rocks off (to some extent), and then thinking further. Perhaps an MCless group that plays would then spawn an MC or two.

    If you don't have that, though, yeah, there are ways to work around it and play the game and have fun.

    I ran a bunch of Apocalypse World, because the game made me excited to play and I knew I would have to MC to get it to the table. However, I wasn't as excited about the MC role as the player role, and I think my MCing suffered for it. I burnt out on one game and dropped out. The second game went well, but it would have been better if I had come really raring to MC.

    ~~~~~~~~~~Eight months pass~~~~~~~~~

    semajmaharg, Alberto and I took a crack at MC-less Monsterhearts last weekend and were quite happy with where we ended up (at least for one-shot play). I've written up my take on the approach we ended up following for general interest. It's a one page Reference Sheet designed to be used alongside the MH Player Reference Sheet (and replace the MC sheet).

    Monsterheartless - a GM-less variant of Monsterhearts

    I should mention that we didn't try out the Story Moves. They're my attempt to better permission/structure the Principles for mcless play. The Play Advice is my attempt to write down the 'unspoken' rules that we were using (we were aided by the fact that we all had MH MC experience).\ufeff

    One thing to note is that I don't include mention of Threats/Menaces in this doc as we only had a single session to develop it. One approach, in line with lumpley's advice (above), is to have clear single ownership of them (either by having one player do everything for them or using Archipelago-style aspect ownership). NPCs would be shared in a general pool until they become a Threat, at which point they become owned by the player who owns the Menace. Under both approaches NPCs should still be treated as stolen cars and the players should be fans of the PCs. Another approach would be to avoid single ownership of anything (Microscope-style). Longer term MC-less play needs some trying out to figure what works (at least for me).

    semajmaharg also had the idea of using 'The Blood of Misty Harbour' Web of Secrets approach in some manner to support multiple session play.
