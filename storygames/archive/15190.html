    \n Well, I've done both. We've 'made' a map of the emergent dungeon with a bunch of tiles, and as monsters were revealed, used D&D minis to mark where they were hanging out in the dungeon. When it came time for the good old hack and slash, the players loved waving the minis about with gusto.
    \n
    \n In fact on a failed discen reality or spout lore about the denizens of the dungeon, I got the player to reach in 'lucky dip' style into a pillowslip full of random minis and used that monster instead.
    \n
    \n 'Oh, so you thought you knew the rumours about the tribe of orcs dwelling within the ruined keep? Huh, guess someone was spinning you 'tall tales' for as you peer over the crumbling wall you see what seem to be a hideous conglomeration of ettins languishing in the bloody charred mess of a repast of various goblinoids....'
    \n
    \nI also wholeheartedly endorse jumping around gesticulating wildly too (Luke Crane style) its awesome.

    \n I've toyed with the idea of using Old School Hack style "arenas" to indicate different zones of combat without going whole-hog tactical grid, but I haven't tried it yet.

    For example, I might arrange some index cards on the table with evocative labels (for example: The Worm God Altar, The Purple Ebony Field, The Arched Entrance and The Tunnel Entrance) and then use minis or tokens to indicate the general layout of the heroes and monsters. This might also be useful for range and such (if you're in the same arena then you're in the melee so you'll need to move one over to take a ranged shot; weapons with reach let you cover the whole arena, that sort of thing). Might be a fun, and very compatible with wild gesticulation!

    \n My thinking is, make a map as you play. Use the map as a reference about who's in what room/passage/area and let the fiction determine who's close enough to act on what. Be sort of loosy-goosy about it though, I don't think you gain anything by trying to get to precise and niggling over feet per unit of fiction.

    \n I use hella game aids. Maps, tokens, props, lots of standing and shouting and drawing and carrying on. My players like to doodle things, so when I describe stuff they doodle it and there it is! We keep all the doodles around to remind us of various elements. It kind of replaces the front sheet at times, because it gives me neat shit to say without much work (or words, even!).

    That being said, I don't use this stuff as a tactical aid. It's all just there to establish the relative space of the game and give us neat stuff to say.

    "Am I close enough to the chandelier *points to map* to jump and swing on it?"
    "I don't know, are you?"
    "Yup!"
    "Rad!"

    That kind of thing.
