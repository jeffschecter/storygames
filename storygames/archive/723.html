    Well, Lisa, I don't see anything terribly surprising in what you experienced. I wouldn't find it surprising in an RPG store, or a grocery store, or at the beach or on a ski-lift or ... anywhere.     I only had to have that lesson repeated a couple hundred times before it finally sank in: people who will take the first step are rare. If you are not one of those people then good conversations will be rare (because they will occur only when you encounter one of those people) But if you are (or become) one of those people then (for you) good conversations will be common.

    I agree with Tony, but amend his statement as: people who will take the first step are rare these days. For the last several decades, people in general have become more distrustful of of strangers.

    I have a few other random observations that may have a bearing on the topic:

    • Social Overstimulation. Modern city life is increasingly stressful, with lots of required socialization in addition to the relationships you volunatrily choose. As a result, people in urban environments are much choosier about whom they will speak to.
    • Fear of Cat Piss Man, or of becoming him (or being mistaken for him. No gamer wants to approach a stranger and have their actions mistaken as creepiness, nor do they want the (repeat) experience of approaching a stranger and discovering too late that there's something creepy about them.
    • Although the majority of people are extroverts, it's my guess that gaming attracts more introverts than normal.
    • High-end autism, Asperger's Syndrome, Avoidants, Schizoid Personality, and other social disorders may also be more common among roleplayers than among the general population, at least based on annecdotal evidence. The theory is that roleplaying is an easier form of socializing than other methods. However, most of these types have trust issues, which may inhibit them from approaching strangers.

    I may be completely off here, but those are the things I thought of immediately when I read your blog.
