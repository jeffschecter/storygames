    \n I think I can offer a pretty clear explanation on my own part:

    OSR D&D is absolutely unique in my play experience as a problem solving exercise with flavourful, detailed game fiction and resolution systems where fictional positioning reigns supreme in determining victory and defeat. It is the only game (understanding that of course there are the likes of e.g. T&T that also do this - I'm lumping them all together here) where I've seen gamism become truly complex, intelligent and adult in terms of challenges and their background.

    In most games that purport to offer challenges, it's probably a simple rules-mechanical system that we're discussing. In last week's D&D session I modeled the likely socio-psychological dynamics of the den of bandits we were about to ambush, and then constructed a ruse of war optimized to make it compelling to the specific audience. When the plan inevitably went extinct on contact with the enemy, we improvised like crazy, and managed to pull a victory of sorts, although my own character died to totally accidental combat attrition in the process.

    The above sort of content is not available in other games. If it was, I might be interested. (And yes, I am interested in various clones and such, there are plenty of new ideas and content out there for this style of play.)

    It should be noted that somebody who's played different games than me over the years probably experiences all this very differently. For example, if you've started roleplaying with D&D in your teens, you probably have a very different relationship to this whole challenge-based gaming thing than I do. It suffices to say that my own early gaming was relentlessly artistic/immersive, so truly successful challenge-based gaming worthy of the name of wargame is pretty fresh for me. Not that I understand how somebody might not like this even if they did play it a lot in their teens, though - seems to me that it is very much my mature intellect and reading background that makes the game interesting, as I can draw upon everything between the heaven and earth to interact interestingly with the other players.

    As for the mechanics and procedures, the game has plenty of these, and they work very well to achieve the above goals. I admit that the actual game texts are generally pretty sucky, but if you can get over griping about that and instead learn by mentoring or by reading very widely and experimenting on your own, it seems to me that it is reliable to discover an excellent game beneath all the cruft.

    \n Sure!

    We're playing Dungeon Crawl Classics, in the funnel. This means that we each have 4 0-level characters on a dangerous adventure. "The funnel" refers to the idea that the survivors become our PCs, but having 4 0-level characters on an adventure that will kill them is a fun way to play even if they won't.

    We've followed some bandits to their hideout in the woods, where something has gone clearly wrong. We don't have the details yet but it looks like some terrible monster has massacred the bandits and moved in. We'll deal with that later in the evening. For now, there's something weird in their yard, a tree stump with a squirrel on it but it ain't no normal squirrel. We investigate, and it's some kind of classic D&D monster where a tentacled tree stump beast uses a squirrel-shaped lure to draw larger animals in as prey. Of course we find this out by getting too close to it. It kills, what, 3 of us? 4? Kills and eats us. We figure out (at some cost to life and precious lantern oil) that it doesn't mind being set on fire, and we waste many arrows and darts discovering that it'll take a lot more than our weapons to kill it. One of my characters, a dwarf, was its last victim, before we figured out that it was slow-moving and that as long as we kept a safe distance we'd be okay. Tragically, this character was the one who'd been carrying all our extra gear, since he had the encumbrance capacity and all. We had a quick debate about whether to try to dart in and grab his pack while the thing ate him, and decided it would be comical and not a good idea.

    The fun in that game was high-color tactical play, where we (the players, not our characters) use our wits, our characters' surroundings, and our characters' meager resources to try to put off as long as possible the hilarious inevitable.

    You know the kind of Odyssey of the Mind-esque puzzle game where you dump out your kitchen junk drawer and have to use its contents to build a tower that will hold an egg 12 inches off the table? It's fun in that same kind of way. The characters and all the fiddly little game mechanisms are like the corkscrew, the rubber bands, and the packet of coffee stirrers you have to build the tower with.

    A question about "story"

    Since the characters are disposable, the tales formed are about surging the adventures. But the stories aren't driven by the characters. (Though I suppose this could happen.) it isn't "This duke took my sister and I'm getting her back!"

    Instead, it seems the richest route would be to depend on the World for the story: things happened in the past, and by digging through the details of dungeons, curses, whatnot, the players -- across multiple characters -- begin to piece this together. This, in turn, leads to new places to explore.

    Or am I wrong about this? How much is Character Story Driven, if any, if at all? It seems such thinking would muck everything up.
    \nThe basic premise of the game is that the DM shows up with a mapped dungeon (or hexmap, or any other location that is unknown and unfamiliar to the players/their characters). The players explore this location, using their characters. The game doesn't care who your character is -- every element you have on your character sheet is a potential resource for exploring the location. Treasure is there to give you a reason to explore, traps and monsters are there to make it dangerous, and the town (or other civilized areas) are there so players can trade around the rewards they have collected from exploring.

    That's the basics. What happens to the campaign after many sessions of play is up for grabs. At the start, PCs are interchangeable, ie the dungeon doesn't care which PCs go exploring. Players might get attached to their characters after many sessions, but the game doesn't. Whether or not the DM does is up to the DM. Maybe the game turns into a series of character-based quests or becomes political intrigue, but that's all add-on stuff that the DM (or the group) creates.

    So, the basic "story" is dungeon-driven: some people explore a dangerous location.

    \nOh, it's not really that bad. Of course it depends on the skillz, but I'd say that a moderately experienced DM (even just a half dozen sessions under his belt) can run your basic dungeon after a half an hour of prep. He just needs to download a small dungeon, do a quick overview (really, this is where experience mostly counts - an experienced DM can run a standard dungeon with no prep at all, just reading as they go), and then start play.

    If you want to set up a sandbox campaign, the initial setup for what I'd consider a minimal beginning would take an hour if you know what you're doing. if you don't exactly, and need to read your initial adventure locations carefully in advance, then it might be something like two hours. A properly seeded sandbox will then run five session on its own before you need to pay it any more attention.

    The above minimal numbers assume that you're not creating any original material yourself, and that either somebody is telling you in detail what to do (where to go for good 1st level dungeons, how to draw a map, that sort of thing), or you already know yourself. Really, the skills required to actually DM are much more of an issue than the time-investment, I think; you can't just spend more time in prep to compensate for lack of inspiration, for example.

    Of course it seems to be something of a trend for DMs to be too ambitious with their campaign design. You can put hundreds of hours into it if you want to create everything yourself, and want to prepare massive detail. However, nothing in the game requires this, it's all additional investment that hopefully pays off for the DM who wants to do it.

    Also, I don't blame anybody who thinks the above numbers absurd. The fact remains that the texts of the game are generally somewhat sucky, and they make things seem more difficult and time-consuming than they really are. I think you're better off looking for some OSR blogger's tutorial on "how to start my own sandbox" than the Mentzer explanation, for example; it's likely to be much more concise and elegant.\n
    For all players, though, the actual length of play looks like an issue: relative to my own fascination (to find out who these characters will become), play moves slowly and it will take a great many sessions to get anywhere of note \u2014 or that's the idea I got, anyway.

The first three sessions or so of a new campaign are likely to go by without anything but simple dungeoneering, and it might go on much longer, all depending on where the players want to take it. Our big campaign in 2010-11 (a hundred sessions total before changing life circumstances ended it) took about ten sessions to mature into a fully-fledged sandbox thing, for example.

    \n First and fore-most (for me) is the nostalgia factor. As I
    type this I have, in front of my laptop ( a computer more
    powerful than the ones that controlled the first moon
    missions!) the Holmes edited Blue Book Dungeons and Dragons.

    That's where I started. Discovered in the back inside cover
    of this venerable tome was a computer punch card - back when
    computer information was stored on card-board; more
    specifically, the holes in said cards. On the back of this
    particular unpunched card is the hand written introduction to
    the first adventure I went on. It was penned by a friend of
    the family called Speedball (along with all that his nickname
    implies!). Frankly, they are a poorly written mess - both the
    punch-card introduction and the Blue Book rules BUT...

    ... I would rather run that version of D&D than any other
    that came after it! And not just for nostalgia's sake. There
    is an "openness", a "permissibility" to the rules that only
    years later do I recognize. Example:

    "You are sure to encounter situations not covered by these
    rules. Improvise. Agree on a probability that an event will
    occur and convert it into a die roll - roll the number and
    see what happens! The game is intended to be fun and the
    rules modified if the players desire. Do not hesitate to
    invent, create and experiment with new ideas. Imagination is
    the key to a good game. Enjoy."

    THAT should be the OSR mantra! THAT should be Rule 0!
    However, the 96,473 rules that were added in "Official TSR
    Products and Supplements" (accept no substitutes! - Gygax
    actually said that in 1st ed. AD&D) rule-lawered the fun out
    of the game for me.

    Don't make me read "The Complete Book of Elves" . I AM an
    elf! I have attribute scores! I'LL tell YOU
    what an elf can do/know/sense/resist if you just pass me the
    d20.

    And that, Charlie Brown, is the true meaning of OSR (at
    least for me). That, and the beer and pretzels.

    \n Regarding the rulings not rules issue, I think its intent is exactly what Eero, Judd and Vincent have described. Here's the Dungeons & Dragons Fantasy Adventure Game Basic Rulebook, p. B60 (Tom Moldvay, 1981):\n
    Dungeon Mastering as a Fine Art
    The success of an adventure depends on the DM and his or her creation, the dungeon. The DM should have the dungeon carefully mapped out before play begins. Even so, a DM will quickly find that it is impossible to predict every possibility. After all, there are several players, and only one DM! It is not unusual for players to find a solution, or pose a new problem, that the DM has not even thought of. It is very important for the DM to be flexible.

    It is important that the DM be fair, judging everything without favoring one side or another. The DM is there to see that the adventure is interesting and that everyone enjoys the game. D&D is not a contest between the DM and the players! The DM should do his or her best to act impartially when taking the part of monsters or handling disputes between characters.

    "That's not in the rules!" The players will often surprise the DM by doing the unexpected. Don't panic. When this happens, the DM should just make sure that everything is done in the order given by the outline or sequence of events being used. Minor details may be made up as needed to keep the game moving. All DMs learn how to handle both new ideas and unusual actions quickly and with imagination.

    Quite often a DM can decide on a solution to a player's actions not covered by these rules. Other times, a problem may have no simple solution. One quick way for a DM to decide whether a solution will work is by imagining the situation, and then choosing percentage chances for different possibilities. For example, suppose the DM is running a combat that is taking place on a ledge next to an unexplored chasm. One player suddenly decides that his character has no chance to survive combat. The player announces "My character wants to jump into the chasm to escape!" There may be a chance that he will fall to a nearby ledge or land in a pool of water at the bottom of the chasm. The DM thinks about the dungeon for a minute, and remembers that an underground river flows through some of the lower dungeon levels, so there might be a pool below. Even so, the character will fall 60', and a normal fall will do 1d6 points of damage per 10' fallen. This character has only 7 hp, and seems likely to die even if the water cushions his landing and reduces the damage. However, there should always be a chance to do something nearly impossible. A player should have, at the very least, a saving throw or a stated percentage chance of a miraculous occurrence saving the character. The DM answers: "Looking down into the chasm, your character can estimate that he has a 98% chance of dying, no saving throw, if he jumps. If you decide your character jumps, roll percentage dice. A result of 99 or 00 will mean that your character lives, but any other result will mean that he will die in the attempt. Do you still want to jump?"

    "There's always a chance." The DM may want to base a character's chance of doing something on his or her ability scores (Strength, Dexterity, and so forth). To perform a difficult task (such as climbing up a rope or thinking of a forgotten clue), the player should roll the ability score or less on ld20. The DM may give a bonus or penalty to the roll, depending on the difficulty of the action (-4 for a simple task to +4 for a difficult one). A roll of 1 should always succeed, and a roll of 20 should always fail.

    "The DM is the Boss." The DM decides how these rules will be used in the game. A good DM talks about problem areas with the players and considers reasonable requests by them. The players should realize, however, that the final decision is the DM's: not theirs, and not this booklet's! If a disagreement holds up play, the DM may make a temporary decision and talk it over with the players when the adventure is over. If a player disagrees strongly enough, he or she may quit the game. It is up to the DM to create an adventure the players can enjoy.

    "Everyone is here to have fun." The DM should make the adventure seem as "real" to the players as possible. All should avoid getting stuck in long discussions about rules or procedures. The game should move along with humor, as well as excitement.

    \nThat's a common misinterpretation of "Rulings not Rules." Its also unfairly reductionist to interpret it that way. Its not a statement denying narrative control, its about making a choice to entrust the smooth moderation of the scenario to the judgement of a fair-minded individual with just as much an investment in having a good time as everyone else, rather than a blind physics-simulator. Its about stepping away from 3E mindset (which leads to scenario short-circuiting rules-hacks and the cult of the character build, and something that OSR was directly contrasting when it started) to a more actual-play communal one, not keeping down players who may dare speak out of turn. In fact its arguably player-empowering since it gives them leave to try anything, rater than being limited to system-delineated options.

    Here's the actual quote from the original essay, which on a full read is obviously about avoiding rules bloat, not fiat.\n
    First Zen Moment: Rulings, not Rules
    The biggest key to understanding \u201cold school\u201d gaming is like a Zen moment: much of the time, you don\u2019t use a rule, you just use a ruling. It\u2019s easy to understand that sentence, but it takes a flash of insight to really \u201cget it.\u201d I\u2019m not saying that you\u2019re going to have a flash of liking it. You might hate it. What I\u2019m saying is that it\u2019s a big intuitive leap from modern games to understand how this works. The players can describe any action, without needing to look at a character sheet to see if they \u201ccan\u201d do it. The GM uses common sense or makes an ad-hoc ruling, and then the game moves on. This is why characters have so few numbers on the character sheet, and why they have so few specified abilities. Many of the things that are \u201cdie roll\u201d challenges in modern gaming (disarming a trap, for example) are handled by observation, thinking, and experimentation in old-style games. Getting through obstacles is more \u201chands-on\u201d than you\u2019re probably used to.

    A simple example: by tradition, many pit traps in 0e are treated as follows. They can be detected easily, by probing ahead with a 10ft pole. Thieves \u2013 if the game even uses thieves as a character class \u2013 don\u2019t spot them unless specifically checking, and they don\u2019t disarm.

    Or say for instance you want your fighter to leap from a high ledge to the ground, both hands gripped tightly around the hilt of his sword to drive it deep into the back of the goblin below, who\u2019s about to club the party\u2019s cleric from behind. What do you do? You simply tell the referee what you want to do. Then the referee will probably ask you to roll to hit. If you hit, you do damage. You might even do extra damage because of your weight behind the blow. That will be for the DM to determine. If you are a ways off the ground you might have to check to see if you get hurt yourself \u2013 and maybe not, if you roll to hit well enough. On the other hand, the referee might decide that you automatically hit the goblin since you\u2019re attacking from behind and from above, but not give you extra damage. However the referee decides the exact die rolls and numbers, it\u2019s obvious that you\u2019re going to have some sort of powerful attack here, even though you don\u2019t have a specific \u201cfeat\u201d or \u201cskill\u201d for this kind of maneuver written on your character sheet.

      \n In terms of SWN vs. Traveller. I wrote a review once (my only review ever) that described SWN's mechanics as "Red Box D&D with Traveller skills". But it's perhaps equally valid or more so to describe the system as "Traveller with Red Box combat".

      Yeah, it's got classes, hit points, and levels. The primary point of them is the skill points (and powers for the psychics) - high-tech weapons are sufficiently lethal that the extra hit points only help slightly - players will still be working *hard* to avoid fights, as in Traveller. I like the leveling system better than the rather vague skill advancement of the LBB. All experience is through acquiring credits, giving players the same adventuring motivation as most Traveller groups.

      The rest of the game - planet building, ship building, actual play - feels very much like Traveller, though the planet-building is simpler (and faster), discarding much of the boring details in favor of generating *interesting* ones which can act as story hooks.

      The biggest differences in feel are the prevalence of psychics (optional in the LBB as I recall, but a core class in SWN), and the lack of a galaxy-spanning empire - there *used* to be one, but it failed when the jumpgates died, and humanity on various worlds is crawling back into space centuries later with much slower (Traveller-speed) stardrives.

      \n On the first page of the Referee Book for Lamentations of the Flaming Princess I found this:

      "The only important thing is to have an ethic behind your decisions, an overarching idea, and not just randomly squirm around in your running of a game."

      Which immediately made me think of Agendas and Principles from Apocalypse World. (I'm not in any way saying Raggi was influenced by AW.) One more set of lessons that Vincent made explicit in AW.

      I think this goes to something I thought a lot about while I was working on Play Sorcerer: These games don't just run themselves because they're bound between two covers. You actually need to know what you want to do with the game, arcing play toward those agendas; you need to know, as a GM, what you want to bring to the Players. Without that, you're just free-floating around.

      I think something that happened with the Publishing Machine of 80s/90s was the promise to consumers that if they bought the games -- and all the additional splats/source books/adventures the games would just "work." But the truth is something the early players, adrift on their own, had to sort out: "What do we want to do with this stuff?" And then, after that, "How do we make that happen?"

      I think OSR demands and acknowledges ownership of these decisions by the people at the table. It never assumes that "buying stuff" is going to provide a good time.

      \n That's a point that I don't think can be echoed enough.

      Over on the LotFP site's forum, Geoffrey McKinney posted:

      The 1974 D&D rules have a specific flavor to them, which I will here refer to as "vanilla".

      The various versions of A/D&D through the decades (as well as their support products) have not travelled far from 1974 in terms of flavor. Thus we have "French vanilla", "vanilla bean", "vanilla with nuts", "vanilla with chocolate syrup", "vanilla with ______", etc.

      Consider: "This setting has crusty dwarves who have wars with goblins who have chests full of gold and elves living in forests with dragons flying overhead while regenerating trolls live in caves and every town has a cleric to heal people and magic-users will zap you with fireballs and etc."

      I just described almost every published D&D product. Those that supposedly "innovate" from that merely A) add more stuff to that mix ("...plus laser guns and crashed spaceships and robots!"), and/or give the above mix some twists ("In this campaign, elves live in the desert and ride anhkhegs.").

      The only two publishers that I know of who are giving us actual innovation are Lamentations of the Flame Princess and Goodman Games (with their forthcoming DDC RPG and with James Raggi's Random Esoteric Creature Generator).

      Ever since the 1970s, people have typically failed to distinguish between A) the D&D game and the sample playing pieces included with the game. Just about every D&D product is full of monsters from the standard lists, magic items from the standard lists, spells from the standard lists, and etc. I think that shows a reticence to really unleash the imagination.

      As far as I'm concerned, all of the D&D monsters, magic items, and spells are merely suggestions/options/examples. I chucked all but 6 of the standard monsters from Carcosa, and I dumped ALL of them from Isle of the Unknown. All the standard magic items are absent from both those products, as well as all the spells and even magic systems. Demi-humans aren't there, either. All gone!

      The bedrock, the basis of the game is pretty much the standard character generation system for making a human fighter, plus the rules for him to operate: to hit, saving throws, etc. And I think that's it. Even the equipment lists and prices are merely options. Everything else is wide-open for the referee to make as he wills.

      By perceiving all these options as necessities, all too many people will say, "Yeah, but without magic missiles/orcs/dwarves/you-name-it, it just isn't D&D anymore!" Which is like saying, "If it's not some kind of vanilla, then it just isn't ice cream anymore!"

      I have as my authorities the very highest: Gary Gygax and Dave Arneson.

      They write at the beginning of the 1974 D&D rules under "Scope": "DUNGEONS and DRAGONS will provide a basically complete, nearly endless campaign of all levels of fantastic-medieval wargame play. Actually, the scope need not be restricted to the medieval; it can stretch from the prehistoric to the imagined future..."

      Then, on the very last page of the rules in the "Afterward": "We urge you to refrain from writing for rule interpretations or the like unless you are absolutely at a loss, for everything herein is fantastic, and the best way is to decide how you would like it to be, and then make it just that way!"

      Wow.

      D&D can have any setting whatsoever, from the prehistoric to the farthest of futures. And YOU decide (based on your whim alone) how EVERYTHING will be.

      That's from Gygax and Arneson in January 1974.

      It's too bad that those three sentences have been basically ignored by virtually all publishers of D&D material. There is enough published D&D stuff (of all the vanilla variations) to last anyone 20 lifetimes. I for one have no desire to see another orc or another +1 sword. I want to reach back to the exhortations in the 1974 rules and have some really imaginative stuff published.

      So I am very thankful for Lamentations of the Flame Princess and for Goodman Games. They are driving the OSR truck through unexplored terrain rather than keeping to the safe, well-trodden paths.

      There is nothing wrong with vanilla ice cream. Just don't neglect all the other flavors. I occasionally play a game of vanilla D&D, but most of my D&D games are of various other flavors. Variety is the spice of life!

      I've been reading James' adventures for Lamentation of the Flame Princess and loving them. So much so I've been thinking, "I'd love to run these for my friends."

      And then I had to think, "No. This is James' take on this kind of material. What would mine be? What would I bring to the table no one else would?"

      Please note, in James's current campaign there are no demi-humans. It seems like he'll be gutting Clerics soon as well. In order to make the setting he wants (an alternate history of 17th century Europe with dark horrors, as far as I can tell.)

      So, yes. Gygax and Arneson years exhorted everyone to build their own rules and settings and situations. No boxed sets. No recreation the "Vanilla" dungeon crawl again and again. But to take the foundations of interactions with problems and challenges for players via their characters, and through color and fiction, see how it all turns out.

      \nSome Rule, err procedures(?)
      Being explicit about fiction first
      No failures, only interesting things
      Being VERY explicit about bending everything on your character sheet to your will to make the game happen

      Procedures:
      Traps, tricks, and such NEVER follow the formula from the old computer based text adventures. The PC's are just the latest level in an already deep environmental narrative so: trappings, signs, and almost to late warnings for setting up traps.
      1) Information gathering before delving. They are doing this right?
      2) Establish that this is a trap infest hell hole
      3) We are not the first ones here. Should be signs of previous successes and failures.
      4) On the GM side you need to be highly permissive in how the trap gets solved, in other words don't solve it for them

      The PCs change the environment. Constantly remind them that their narrative matters. Did they disturb a dusty room, shatter an altar by accident, leave some dead or dying henchmen behind in the darkness. All these things become new features in the environment.

      Lots of empty rooms. See the stocking rules in the original white box or Moldvay. In empty rooms drop hints of the original purpose of the room, what's it's being used for now, and what could happen. Tables are best used for these details. And get it SHORT. You have lots to do as a GM and the player's don't want War and Peace for every room. Let the player construct the narrative detail from the elements in the room.

      *I am not being very eloquent here. Don't read these as commandments please. This is how Ara runs the game at this particular moment in time. So if you ignore these don't worry about it

      If you want, but not needed, can anyone compare the fun to different kinds of fun from games of the last decade that you love, but doesn't scratch the same itch that the OSRs do?
      \nThis I can do. One simplistic continuum is character vs. adventure focus. In a character-focused game, the camera (if you will) follows the characters, whatever happens to them, whereas an adventure-focused game, characters can become unable to participate through infirmity (or death). In the one, you use the adventure as a highly malleable environment used to reveal things about the characters; in the other, you use characters as replaceable probes to explore the adventure.

      The difference comes up when the two diverge - in low-level D&D, if you run out of hit points, you croak and make another character. In Burning Wheel, if you're horribly injured, you play out Touching the Void, the tale of how you crawled back to camp with two broken legs.

      In decades past, I spent a lot of time trying to play character-focused games using adventure-focused mechanics. When I came across Burning Wheel, it blew my mind because it was the first system that really went out of its way to hit that explicitly - the GM was deliberately crafting challenges to reveal the characters, through their choices.

      So, amazing as this has been, there's something that seemed to fall by the way side in my gaming, which was the verisimilitude of the world at an out-of-character level. Sure the world is rich and detailed, but the lens of game play starts to feel like a stage the PCs live on, everything else brought on like a prop as needed, and so often created on a just-in-time basis, and so often just a shade too coincidentally appropriate or awesome.

      I say OOC verisimilitude because while the characters certainly had to take the world at face value, the players don't. The players know that if they go left, that's where the awesome stuff (both good and bad) is most likely going to be. There's no real need to do (god forbid) actual reconnaissance, because Touching the Void result of having no plan is just as awesome as Conquering Templars.

      I started to crave player planning! I wanted players to be making intelligent plans about how to deal with the reality of the world around them, at a campaign goal level.

      Right at this point the Torchbearer playtest landed in my lap, and I got what I was looking for unexpectedly. Torchbearer is fascinating because while you can structure the adventure in a just-in-time way, the rules leap out of the book and confront players with a concrete, unavoidable logistical challenge. Suddenly, players were asking me to hold off on my description of what's next, because they needed a minute to reorganize their gear and roles (who's taking point, who's got the light, etc.) So awesome!

      It's still not at a session-planning level, but anyways, that's one illustration of a difference.

      So, I was reading the Referee's Book for Lamentations of the Flame Princess. And James Raggi is explaining all this stuff to me about the need to track time (track time!) in the dungeon and how random encounters should be set up to work effectively and keeping track of torches and whatnot...

      So then, as an experiment, I start leafing through old Classic Traveller LBB Adventures ("Across the Bright Face," "Marooned," and so on...) And they all talk about the need to keep track of time, and so on...

      And this light bulb goes off. Because when I was in high school the Traveller adventures confused the hell out of me because I couldn't see how they'd be fun, but now:
      a) I see how the Traveller modules are supposed to work now; and
      b) I realize why I never could before -- because the setting of Traveller was so narratively rich for me (I wanted Dune, not Star Wars) that the module adventures seemed so lifeless and stale. But now I see how they're not stale at all, because of this:\n
      If you want, but not needed, can anyone compare the fun to different kinds of fun from games of the last decade that you love, but doesn't scratch the same itch that the OSRs do?
      \nThis I can do. One simplistic continuum is character vs. adventure focus. In a character-focused game, the camera (if you will) follows the characters, whatever happens to them, whereas an adventure-focused game, characters can become unable to participate through infirmity (or death). In the one, you use the adventure as a highly malleable environment used to reveal things about the characters; in the other, you use characters as replaceable probes to explore the adventure.

      The difference comes up when the two diverge - in low-level D&D, if you run out of hit points, you croak and make another character. In Burning Wheel, if you're horribly injured, you play out Touching the Void, the tale of how you crawled back to camp with two broken legs.

      In decades past, I spent a lot of time trying to play character-focused games using adventure-focused mechanics. When I came across Burning Wheel, it blew my mind because it was the first system that really went out of its way to hit that explicitly - the GM was deliberately crafting challenges to reveal the characters, through their choices.

      So, amazing as this has been, there's something that seemed to fall by the way side in my gaming, which was the verisimilitude of the world at an out-of-character level. Sure the world is rich and detailed, but the lens of game play starts to feel like a stage the PCs live on, everything else brought on like a prop as needed, and so often created on a just-in-time basis, and so often just a shade too coincidentally appropriate or awesome.

      I say OOC verisimilitude because while the characters certainly had to take the world at face value, the players don't. The players know that if they go left, that's where the awesome stuff (both good and bad) is most likely going to be. There's no real need to do (god forbid) actual reconnaissance, because Touching the Void result of having no plan is just as awesome as Conquering Templars.

      I started to crave player planning! I wanted players to be making intelligent plans about how to deal with the reality of the world around them, at a campaign goal level.

      Right at this point the Torchbearer playtest landed in my lap, and I got what I was looking for unexpectedly. Torchbearer is fascinating because while you can structure the adventure in a just-in-time way, the rules leap out of the book and confront players with a concrete, unavoidable logistical challenge. Suddenly, players were asking me to hold off on my description of what's next, because they needed a minute to reorganize their gear and roles (who's taking point, who's got the light, etc.) So awesome!

      It's still not at a session-planning level, but anyways, that's one illustration of a difference.
      \nWhich then led me to realize that Traveller that's already been published is something I'd love to play (and always wanted to) and now I could see how to make it world. Because it's all about the Players getting to poke and prod this strange environment that might get their characters killed. It's going to do exactly what fuseboy is talking about in spades (If I GM'd I'd have the PCs building a merc ticket, playing out the shenanigans, seeing how it rolls, and so on).

      But there's also the Traveller in my head I've always wanted. This version is all supported inspiration and genre-wise less by SF tales than by certain kinds of crime/adventure fiction of well trained men placing big bets on bad odds mixing it up at the frontier edge of an Interstellar Empire:

      A Fistful of Dollars
      The Magnificent Seven
      Kiss Me Deadly
      The Killing
      Heat

      That game might work with Classic Traveller. But that might be well served by hacking the setting and style I want Apocalypse World. But at least now I see the distinct paths in front of me more clearly than before.

      If this is on topic, could someone explain the difference between the major OSR games? I've leafed through some of them, but I don't know enough about the genre to easily spot the salient differences.
      \nSure. I'll start by providing a scheme of categorization:

      First, the textual history of D&D in a nutshell:
      - The original -74 D&D is a garage game, indie through and through; I at least get nostalgic for the indieness of it by looking at it, and it already has all the most important parts of the game. There are plenty of people for whom it is a favourite edition for the simplicity, lack of consumerism, and purity of vision.
      - AD&D is a complex and official thing, it's for those who are really into the Gygaxian fantasy world in all its complexity. Mechanically it simply has more, and most of what it has is crazy bullshit, except if you're into it, in which case you apparently like it. Others mostly mine it for ideas. The 2nd edition is largely irrelevant for the OSR, because most do not find much of utility in it; they either go for 1st edition AD&D, or the basic lineage. Still, it's essentially the same game, so you can use that instead of the 1st edition if you feel like it. It is cleaner and less mechanically insane, at the price of being less flavourful and having more new school ideology in it.
      - Basic D&D, meaning the textual lineage that progressed alongside AD&D through the '80s, is in general more streamlined than AD&D, and consequently more elegant. It apparently used to be considered the "children's game" at the time, but currently it is not at all uncommon for people to find it the most playable and useful lineage. I know I do, the Mentzer edition is the best TSR D&D with the possible exception of the -74 original.
      - Most people see a sea-change in the game when it comes to the 3rd edition, and I do think that this is justified. Probably the biggest change is the focus on formalistic character-builds: for the first time you could build a character from 1st to 20th level from the armchair, which changed a lot in how things like game balance and lethality was understood. While many OSR gamers seem to loan mechanical ideas from the 3rd edition, few specifically use the entire system for their play.
      - Everything after the 3rd edition is essentially irrelevant, it's just too different a game at that point.

      The next step from those games are the retro-clones, which are games that basically attempt to clone the original game under a different name for spurious legal reasons. Labyrinth Lord (Basic), OSRIC (AD&D) and Swords & Wizardry (-74) are the most important ones, but there are plenty of others focusing on e.g. Rules Cyclopedia (a sort of second edition for Mentzer from the early '90s), 2nd edition AD&D and so on. Playing these is like playing the corresponding TSR game, except usually they're better organized, and depending on the editor's tastes they tend to fix/streamline the most insane bits of the old rules. Usually a better choice than the TSR original, and easier to find, too.

      If you grok the differences between AD&D, Basic and 3rd edition, I think that's the most important difference in all of the above, really.

      Comparing to the above, games like LotFP (really, it's the most prominent in this category) are still largely retro-clones in that the rules systems are very closely aligned with the original textual tradition. In LotFP's case it's mostly Mentzer D&D, although simplified and streamlined. What differs in LotFP in comparison to the retro-clones proper is that it does not attempt to duplicate the literary elements: instead of Gygaxian fantasy the game presumes a sort of historical horror fantasy setting. This doesn't affect the mechanics of the game for the most part (you could play in e.g. Mystara with LotFP easily enough), but things like equipment lists, monster and magic design philosophy and such campaign-specific things are affected.

      Once we've gotten through everything above, what we have left are games that do different degrees of mechanical innovation in comparison to the historical TSR models (which in themselves have a wide mechanical scope, of course, being as how we're talking of a dozen different rules texts for doing basically the same thing). I would divide these into two boxes: in one box are the games that specifically retain the mechanical cornerstones of D&D (xp, levels, hitpoints, flat d20 random distributions, saving throws, etc.) while not hesitating to remix everything for new purposes, and in the other box are games that are "going for that old school feel" without necessarily retaining any D&D mechanics.

      A good example of the first box where you're still going with the mechanical basis of D&D is Broadswords & Bucklers, an enchanting little game about being a rogue-ish fellow in renaissance London. It has new character classes, new xp mechanics, new injury rules, a new campaign model circling around the tavern/HQ the adventurers choose for themselves... but it is still essentially D&D in that the agenda, principles, structures and procedures of play are the same. It's just very extensive house-ruling on the mechanical incidentals, one might say. My own house game sits in this same box: massive mechanical revision, yet made with constant reference to the wisdom of the ancients.

      A good example of the second box is Barbarians of Lemuria: it's still pretty old school in goals and structure and procedures, but as it abandons the mechanical cornerstones of D&D, it's clearly a much more distant cousin to everything above. The OSR scene in general is really pretty divided on whether "OSR" means D&D or this wider expansion of franchise. Traveller is an excellent example of the question - is Traveller relevant to your interests if you're interested in the OSR? For some it obviously is (it has many of the same fundamental assumptions about how a rpg is structured), while for others it's not because it's not D&D (no xp, levels, etc.) and therefore not directly relevant to their own play.

      If we wanted to expand from the above even further, the next "step out" would be games like Dungeon World, I guess: nothing in particular to do with old school D&D methodologically or procedurally, yet the literary topic is similar, and some people see a relevance.

      The above scheme is relevant for your question because the salient properties of the various OSR games are almost always of two types: what flavour of D&D does it emulate, and what literary trappings does it rely on? The reason for the existence of the vast majority of the various OSR games you'll find out there is that there wasn't yet a retroclone for that specific flavour of TSR D&D, or that the author wanted to hack an old school rules set for some specific genre or milieu (e.g. space opera, Gamma World, various historical milieus, pulp fantasy). The literary trappings thing is pretty obvious to recognize, I think, so learning to recognize the different mechanical flavours of D&D (original, AD&D, Basic, 3rd and proge are basically the main ones, as can be seen in the above treatment) seems like the best bet if you want to learn to distinguish between the different OSR rules sets.

      All that being said, though, I would like to suggest that the differences are not necessarily very important. Many people mix and match material freely between a wide majority of these systemic options, and thus their home games can't be said to properly represent any of them by the book. This is, of course, completely fine for the OSR gamer who subscribes to the pre-eminence of rulings over rules. Besides, many of the mechanical flavours that people obsess over are really minor; I wouldn't expect anybody to care about the differences between Holmes and Mentzer myself, for example, but here we are with plenty of retroclones for both varieties of Basic D&D.

      Even further, if the mechanical differences above do not mean anything to you, then it's likely that the different flavours of OSR rules sets don't necessarily carry any meaning for you. The differences only matter to people for whom the mechanical details of e.g. saving throws or healing HP matter somehow, and that's not likely to be anybody who's coming to the subject matter cold. For this reason I suggest not worrying about the different rules sets too much; if you want to play, just pick up any carefully edited rules set that roughly matches the literary flavour you're looking for from your campaign, and go play. You'll have plenty of opportunity to fine-tune once you know what you want. Just stay away from the AD&D-based systems if you want to avoid needless complexity

      \n Hi Marshall,

      In the kind of play you describe, how is XP awarded?

      I ask because one of the (I think) really smart things Lamentations of the Flame Princess does is make XP something you get for pulling treasure out of dangerous places. (1 sp = 1 XP). You get a little bit of treasure for killing monsters... but the real meal is from pulling treasure out of places of mystery and danger.

      There's no XP for roleplaying, showing up, completing a plot line, performing as your class, or anything like that. It's for putting your nose toward treasure and following it till you find the treasure and bring it back.

      I ask because everything you write in that post makes perfect sense to me. I love all the fictional stuff and adding context for the stuff outside the adventure. If anything else, it provides a baseline of fictional "normalcy" that sets both the adventurers and the adventure environments apart from famers and villagers which makes them special.

      On the other hand, in the LotFP system, there's less finagling required -- since the Players are by definition going to be predisposed to head off toward some place where treasure is waiting. (Very much in the tradition of The Hobbit, essentially!) What the fiction turns out to be is what happens when these adventurers deal with these dangers on the way to find and recover this treasure. The Players have less need to pay attention to context of the dangers since the point is to get through whatever dangers to that treasure.

      This has nothing whatsoever to do with whether or not there are many options of places with treasures to go explore. They can of course go choose one or the other, each with bits of color or whatever that might be more enticing. And add on top of this: if the GM wants, he can begin stitches bits of fiction between the dangerous environments if the Players seem intrigued with it. An idol to an old god they find in one dungeon that they really dig might lead the GM to add a whole new temple somewhere else that reveals more about the god.

      But, ultimately, either way, in LotFP they're heading into the dungeons no matter what, no matter the color, for that treasure. And that simplifies a lot of things, I think.

      Once XP awards start coming from broader/more narrative sources, the Players now are more invested in choosing which kind of story/fiction they want to become invested in.

      So, I'm curious, how is XP working in the kind of play you're describing.?

      \n Here's the bit I wrote about the "communicate the prepped materials through play" part in response to a question about how to do it other than info-dumps (in-game or OOC);\n
      Do you play Dogs in the Vineyard? You know how the GM is supposed to communicate the town's problem through his portrayal of the NPCs and whatnot? It's kinda like that.

      Like, for the KFS example:
      Before the PCs even get into the KFS, they'll have to pass through border checkpoints manned by UTA mercs (which the PCs would recognize as such), and they'll probably encounter a UTA caravan with something crazy on a flatbed trailer like an entire fighter plane. If an NPC has no reason to hide anything, then he spills what he knows. If the NPC does have a reason to hide something, then he equivocates or clams up, and the GM gives the players that look like, "oh yeah, he's hiding something" and you go into social conflict resolution (for which I have a pretty good non-rolling, non-numerical system that I can talk about if you want).

      As the GM presents all the information, it's not in his interests to hide anything unless doing so opens up an interesting challenge for the players to opt in to. After all, the GM's main job here is to present an interesting game board. So that means any investigation never gets all trail-of-breadcrumbs, you-must-find-this-clue-to-proceed like so many implementations of CoC. If there's such a clue, just drop it on the players' heads. And if this kind of poking around isn't what your players are into, it might be better to just tell them the whole situation in a meta-fashion.

      \n@Rafu
      The first part is a hard rule that your goal in any social conflict is to get the other guy to perform some action -- telling you something, going somewhere, sending his armies to the upcoming war, etc. You can't ever count on making someone change the way they view things or think or feel; that might happen, but it's not your goal.

      So, it's a problem: how do I make this person do what I need them to do? So then you solve the problem. And it's a lot like disarming traps without a roll: you poke around at it, examine it until you figure out where you can wedge a stick to block the head-choppy mechanism.

      So you have to find about the person enough to learn where you can apply pressure to get them to acquiesce. Will he respond to threats (and what kind?), or appeals to reason, or appeals to his better nature, or what? What does he value, what does he desire, what are his vulnerabilities, and how can you leverage those? The DM should probably always have at least one solution in mind, but should be open (and perhaps even charitable) toward the attempts of the players. The DM judges the success of these attempts based on his vision of the NPC, trying to be accurate and honest (and perhaps charitable). Then he communicates how effective the attempt was through his depiction of the NPC.

      And I said non-rolling, but I do use the reaction roll to determine the starting disposition of just about everyone and everything the PCs encounter. But that just determines attitude, not action (well, unless you get the attacks-right-away result).

If the GM gets to decide everything, the only player skill that is left is "influencing the GM to make decisions favourable to you".)
      \nI'd encourage trying to join in a well-recommended OSR game to try it out. I played in a game of Lamentations of the Flame Princess run by author James Raggi (with Jason Morningstar), and I thought it was a lot of fun.

      It's true that player skill is filtered through GM judgment, but that covers a lot of ground. One could say that the only important skill for Olympic athletes is influencing the judges to make decisions favorable to them. However, they exert that influence by demonstrating a variety of different skills, and it is those skills that impress the judges. Now, obviously, GMs are not world-class judges - but players aren't world-class athletes, either.

      Such judged assessments are not objective tests of skill in the way that a chess game is. However, this means that they can assess a much wider set of skills. The players can try a solution that is "outside the box", and it has a chance of working. Sometimes the GM will make bad calls, but that is the trade-off.

      \n Well, my biggest issue is that Jim is modeling a traditional adventure text's overall structural scheme in the adventure: it has a sequence of scenes that are assumed to happen in order. This is of course old chicken-noodles for Forge-heads to get annoyed about, but the thing is that I find this a very problematic structure for an old school D&D adventure as well. As you know, traditionally those adventures tend to be location-based, and while that's not the be-all-end-all of possibilities, it has the big advantage of not being a big, fat railroad.

      Now, Jim tells me that his intent was to write an adventure where the prep will "obviously" go out the window after the very first scene, being as how the players will surely do something or other that is not in the plans. Make of that what you will; my own thinking is that if this was actually what happens, then traditional prepped-plot adventure design would never had become the dominant paradigm in the first place. I would assume that if the LotFP box ever got the sort of traction for which a game actually needs beginner materials, that adventure would just be another hypocritical traditional piece of text in a hobby full of them: we saw literally hundreds of roleplaying games through the '80s and '90s that did exactly this same thing, presenting a game that supposedly has these interactions and that idea in them, but when you got to the sample adventure, it was just another bunch of prewritten plot for the GM to finagle into play around whatever the rules-mechanics du jour happen to be.

      (I am, obviously, putting the argument in pretty strong words for the sake of clarity. In reality Stranger Storm is pretty mild insofar as traditional plot-line adventures go, it's just such a disappointment to have one in the first place. Not that I can tell Jim what to do, I'm sure somebody finds it useful, etc. etc., of course. Ultimately it's no worse a sin than that Jim simply wrote the sort of adventure text he knows; he wasn't setting out to revolutionize the writing of adventure scenarios here, after all, so I'm sure he just took the structure that seemed the most obvious and ran with that.)

      I would probably have written this same adventure myself by establishing the opening scene, introducing the monster, and then providing a bunch of environmental and tactical elements, most of them in random tables, expecting the referee to run the monster's tactics and strategy according to the monster description, and the milieu according to a random jumble of inspiring and vaguely connecting ideas. This would have maintained the purity of prep in that the referee would not need to sit down at the table with a precreated conception of what the next scene after this one will be like. Even if you're prepared to chuck it all after the players go off the rails, like Jim suggests you should do, I at least couldn't help being informed by the supposed plotline after I'd been introduced to the elements of the adventure by that means.

      In summation for Christopher: the technique of GMing that I find objectionable is the preplotting of if -> then logic trees of events as a means of preparing to run the game; nobody should learn to run D&D like that, when it actually has the amazing distinction of being a game where it is not necessary for the GM to predict and control the direction of play, unlike perhaps the majority of all roleplaying games ever conceived.
