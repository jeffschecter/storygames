    \n Well, to be fair, it's less of a genre emulation thing in the way I described it. Wu xia is a genre, sure, but my point was that there's an appendix of DOZENS of very specific and wildly different play styles, settings, tropes, and visuals. Let me find a couple examples:\n
    Mist-Robed GateBased on a popular wuxia novel series, The Swordsman films produced by Tsui Hark offer classic Mist-Robed Gate action: many factions such as the black-magic-using hillfolk and Japanese samurai with opposing personal and institutional goals, catchy traditional Chinese music, extreme romantic complications and super-low-budget sets and props... There are plenty of female as well as male martial artists, making it a particularly good choice for those interested in gender in kung fu films.
    \nThat would inspire a wildly different game than, say, this:
    \n
    Mist-Robed GateThis anti-war epic may not be all swords, blood, and exotically-colored cloth-- the film's chief color is gray and its weather pattern is the kicked-up dust of battle under the scorching sun-- but it still contains the seeds of a Mist-Robed Gate game. Three brothers played by Jet Li, Andy Lau, and Takeshi Kaneshiro get caught up in different sides of a deadly political conflict in late 18th century China, when riflemen are able to (melodramatically) gun down the traditional sword-wielding warriors with ease. Amidst the carnage comes the loyalty-shifting politics of men whose self-interest and blind obedience wax and wane with each new crisis situation, eventually destroying them all in a morally ambiguous epic battle that will leave your ears ringing.
    \nThey both have two things in common-- kung fu and shifting factions-- but ultimately they're extremely different, and you've got a concise, rich situation to mine.

    The book also says you should make some snacks and talk about what you think is fun about the situation you choose, and to not rush the part where you figure out what everyone's into and wants to play. That's important for depth, and doesn't require reading more than one brief paragraph of movie synopsis.

    \n Grant, I'm a little confused. You want the players to make characters who have "depth". Cool. But, for purposes of your thread, does that mean "characters who have internal motivations, conflicts, passions and agendas" or "characters who are deeply rooted in the fiction of the setting"?

    Just reading your OP, I would guess (a), but your responses seem to indicate (b).

    Many games do the former.

    If it's the latter, I think group setting creation is the way to go. If you run games, it's really tempting to come up with piles of cool histories and cultures and tragic figures, but, as you note, no one cares. If, however, the entire group comes up with that stuff, then everyone has a stake, and everyone cares. Or, at least, cares more.
