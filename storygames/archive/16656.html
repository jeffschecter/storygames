\n Just daydreaming...

I would pay a subscription to a service that gave me:

- PDFs of the latest and greatest Small Press games
- Reviews of these games
- Interviews with the designers telling me the reasons for creating these games
- Everything I need to run a single session of these games in 4 hours
- Videos explaining how to play
- Common hacks
- Discounts to buy these games in print
- Contests to give a portion of the proceeds to future designers

Kind of like a Story Games version of D&D Insider.

Take my money!

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarFrom a designer's perspective, one fly in this delicious ointment is that Icannotwill not guarantee a delivery date for something I'm making. It'll be done when it is done, that's the incandescent joy of small press publishing but it makes scheduling and commitment to a model like this somewhat problematic (while hopefully ensuring a quality product). There's just no way I could say "in 2013 you will get 3 playtest revisions, a set of designer's notes, 4 videos and a finished game" because I don't work that way and don't know anybody who does, except bloated plutocrats and people who suck.
    \nMaking actual games available on a deadline is probably a recipe for subpar games. But you could have other components come out as regular intervals (weekly columns, every other week a Youtube tutorial on an existing game and on Thursdays some recorded actual play sessions and such).

    Meanwhile, have access to a backlog of finished games and hacks and things be part of the subscription.New stuff is added to this backlog, at irregular intervals, as it is finished. When I subscribed to Pyramid online and to D&D Insider, the new stuff each week was nice but the access to a backlog of old stuff was much nicer.

    So basically, a few regular features that can be reliably produced on a schedule, plus several sporadic projects that are published as they are finished. (Make sure that a few of the bigger projects are available right from day one, too.)

    Posted By: jenskotplaytesting and exchanging services
    \nInstead of a subscription service, you may instead have a very-low-income exchange site, much like torrent services like Karagarga.

    I'm sure many of you are familiar with the model: you get credit for uploads (of sufficient quality), and other real work done on behalf of the site (subtitling, curating, moderating, testing) and can use those credits to download certain products.

    Money could be introduced into that economy, but not necessarily.
