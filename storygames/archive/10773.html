\n I'm having a problem.

I just ran a play test of 'Hell for Leather' with a friend of mine last night. There were only two of us, so I decided I'd be both the GM and one of the players. I learned a lot, etc. The problem is, it was really good fun to be a player, and now I'm thinking I should construct the game so that the GM gets to play as a player and not just as a GM. Can you suggest a good way to switch the GM role during play? A lot of you guys seem to know what you're doing, certainly a hundred times more than myself, so I was wondering if I could harvest some of your knowledge. Have you played a game where the GM role (as adversary) is successfully shifted during play?

I'm looking for a way that this has been done explicitly, not a game where 'it kind of does that, if you think about it.' For example, the GM role, if there was one, shifts all over the place in Universalis, as narrative control is something fluid. That's not the sort of shift in the GM role I'm looking for, nor the advice I need. I'd like something that shifts the 'GM as adversary' from one player to the other, in no predictable order.

Narrative control is already fluid according to the results of conflict resolution. I need something that will shift the control of the reactionary role of the GM. Know what I mean?

    \n To follow that up, here is an overview of the GM's duties (which I want shifted). The GM...

    ...decides if a roll is necessary for conflict resolution
    ...has to narrate the effects of critical failure
    ...has to narrate the results of critical success
    ...has to 'buy' adversaries and complications for the group (using a kind of in-game currency)

    \n In Polaris, each player has a character, and the player across from you is your character's antagonist (the "Mistaken"), and the players on either side of you are the "Moons" and have their own smaller bounds of authority.

    In Annalise, each player has a character, and on each players turn you select one of the other players to GM for you.

    If you're interested in assigning duties in those kinds of ways, here's some general thoughts/suggestions:

    *always be assigned to the player to the left (or right, or whatever) of the person making the roll.
    *each be assigned to a separate player at the beginning of each session (Jim decides if rolls are necessary, Wanda narrates criticals, Steve buys stuff) and change by session
    *those roles could be mechanically determined by a resolution system (low die buys complications, high die narrates criticals, or something like that). In carry, the guy who gives all the orders in an action scene also has to spend all the fallout currency to kill, wound and injure the squad, for example. Doing exceptionally well (or poorly) could make it incumbent on you to make things harder for everybody else.

    I hope some of that is helpful!

    \n The problem with trying to "get rid of the GM" is that someone HAS TO play the antagonism.

    The only way I can think of truly creating a "GM-less" game is to design a game that's all about PC vs PC conflict, something along the lines of The Mountain Witch or Poison'd. In those types of set ups, the antagonism comes from within the group, so hypothetically you could systematize scene framing and eliminate the "GM" role. (Do any of those Nordic or Jeep games work that way?)

    But if antagonism comes from any sort of external source, then you're stuck. You're going to have to have some sort of GM, be it a static or rotating/shifting role.

    \n Hi there guys, thanks for the input. I've been chatting with my co-pilot, Joey Prince, and he's given some serious food for thought. I ended up pairing the game down to one stat: Moxie.

    Moxie is your vigour, pep, drive and spirit. It\u2019s what keep you going when all is lost. Everyone starts with a Moxie of 2.

    Each character also has a Forte, chosen from a list of Abilities: Fight, Flight, Head or Heart. A player chooses the one that suits his character the most. When he gets into a situation that calls for his Forte, he\u2019ll be more likely to succeed.

    Every character also has a Foible. From the same list of Abilities (excluding the Forte), he chooses the one that best describes his character\u2019s limitations. When his character gets into a situation that calls for his Foible, he\u2019ll be more likely to fail.

    There's more info on Cobweb Games, if you're interested.
