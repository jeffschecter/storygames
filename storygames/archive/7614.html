    \n Yes, it was a fun game, despite first-time awkwardness. It was also not only my first game of IaWA, but my first time playing (successfully) over Skype.

    Much thanks to John for running the game and to Filip for his tremendous efforts in explaining the game to everyone!

    My favourite moments were definitely the bits with the Artificial Intelligence trapped in the human body of the comatose girlfriend, especially these two:

    --The scene at the hospital where she unplugs an elderly patient from life support and watches them die. When the hacker tells her, "you know, sometimes people can die, even in The Game..." she replies, "Well, that's the user's fault, isn't it? They had to remove the safety protocols. Besides, there are always more humans around."

    --The two of them eventually making love just outside their crashed car, just off the side of the highway, and being interrupted by an emergency patrol: "Are you two alright?" "Uh, yeah, never been better, thanks! You might want to check on the car, though."

    A couple of interesting observations and a question:

    --I enjoyed the "non-action" scenes, especially at the beginning. If there were fewer players sitting around waiting, I would have enjoyed getting deeper into those conversations.
    --In general, it was interesting to see how the very intense, dramatic scenes we had really didn't have the punch they could have, because we didn't really get a chance to build up to them.

    I know that goes against some traditional "Forge" advice, but there you have it. I feel that a game like IaWA, which allows you to get to the hard and fast action very very quickly wouldn't suffer from a little bit of slower pacing to build up tension and develop characters more.

    My question is a rules question:

    --If after the first round, two opponents negotiate instead of going on to the second round, does the one with smaller dice go onto the We Owe List?

    Filip said "no", which makes sense, but the rules summaries I've seen online seem to suggest "yes". Is there a definite answer on this?

    \n Thanks to Filip for organizing us, for his excellent IAWA rules knowledge, and for staying up late with us. We got you some English practice. I would dig continuing this cyberpunk thing, finding out what happens to poor Jeff, or maybe trying some other oracle with you guys.

    I'm geeked to hear that people dug Rhiannon, the murderous secret-sadist system administrator AI. I enjoyed playing her, esp. when Hiroshi (Filip) was tricking her (and, I think, waiting for me to order a reach for the dice) and I was just rolling with it in anticipation of the fun we were going to have once she was fleshbound...

    Regarding buildup:

    Initially, the conflict that dominated was the Jeff (Paul) / Mayor (Jason) thing. The Mayor was a Game addict who wanted to carry out real-life hits on his in-game rivals. Jeff was an accountant-turned-brainwashed cybernetic assassin, made-to-order for the mayor by Dr. Fausto. Once Fausto gave Jeff the 411 on his situation, Jeff started moving his killing-machine ass straight towards the Mayor with a murderous gleam in his eye. Which is reasonable and believable, y'know, because the Mayor's done some bad stuff to him. But I had trouble with this for a few reasons:

    1) All four PCs were in that scene and the Skype play got a bit chaotic. It's really tough to kibbutz on stuff in Skype. It might be a good idea, in a large scene like that, to treat it a bit more like a classroom and ask that people wait on the GM (or someone) call on them to speak. Happily, smaller scenes worked much better.

    2) The fiction felt broken. Jeff looks like a street bum and he confronted the Mayor at an A-list fundraiser. I left this alone because I didn't really have an NPC who could influence that situation. In retrospect, I should have framed this scene with much more emphasis on how Jeff stood out. Or I should have framed away from the fundraiser and to some more believable setting (but perhaps a less interesting one -- I was attracted to the fundraiser as a setting initially.) Filip even prompted me to frame the scene. I didn't run with it.

    3) The scene contained some microconflicts that slowed things down. Everyone agreed that Jeff should confront the mayor, but there was some disagreement on how it should go down, and we diced that out some.

    4) The confrontation felt like it should have been 'circled' (IAWA page 11) and I couldn't figure out how to do that. There didn't appear to be any restraints on Jeff. He was laser-focused on the Mayor. The thing was, he confronted the Mayor on the Mayor's home turf, and I should have exploited that somehow to put him at a really clear disadvantage.

    (what were Jeff's best interests? I don't have the GT file here...)

    5) The confrontation didn't escalate things, but neither did it decide them -- not until Simon conceded that Dr. Fausto's hands had been cut off, and mostly, I think this came out because Simon had to leave and get to bed.

    In my experience, IAWA games have two broad phases. In the first phase, play meanders a bit as everyone tries conflicts on for size. Then something happens that will galvanize the game, get it all moving in the same direction. In the second phase, everyone chooses up sides for a big confrontation. We got to the big confrontation before we'd gotten into a groove, so that confrontation wasn't as engaging as it might have been. I think people might not have wanted to narrate Big Consequences before we were really engaged in the fiction.

    It seems like the lesson here is that if a scene feels broken, get decisive about it, or transform it. Change the setting, advance forward in time, remove a character from it, etc.

    I think you're right on with your comments. I can imagine that, for you, "fixing" the fundraiser situation was difficult, since at least three PCs were not only involved and active (at the "table") but also in charge of the situation within the fiction itself. It's kind of like doing some white water rafting: you feel nervous and unsure until the first wave hits and you're soaked through. Once you're wet, you can throw yourself fully into the experience, and enjoying subsequent "waves" more.


    Ralph,

    The whole "conflict NOW vs. conflict LATER" thing is a tricky one to talk about because it's gone through many phases. For someone new to the Forge or the Forge approach to gaming (that whole Story Now business), especially with older systems, the advice was "get to conflicts, as quickly as possible". That's the advice I'm referring to, usually given to prevent people from floundering around and not having anything happen. I'm talking about people with, say, decades of D&D experience and little idea of how to get an interesting situation developed, perhaps even major problems figuring out what "conflict" is at all. They need or needed to hear that because, absent a gun and a hexmap, many of them didn't know how to create conflict at all.

    For those who are playing now, and getting into games with potentially explosive Situation, like IaWA, the advice given is rightly the opposite: as the game is waving major conflict right in front of your face, the players need rather to remember to back off and "circle" the explosive points.

    Does that make sense? I'm not intimately familiar with the Forge, so I'm not trying to misrepresent anyone--it's just an isolated point of view.
