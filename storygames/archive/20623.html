\n I am about to start running a new indie game and asked the author online how long it takes to play through -- this is a game with a strong premise and clear endgame and I was hoping for an answer like "5-6 sessions unless you have really long sessions and here's how to speed it up by dropping more loot."

Instead he said that he's never played a whole campaign because it would take too long to playtest. I was kind of shocked! I'm still going ahead because my players are excited and I think it's a good game but I will have to keep a close eye on our progress.

    \n I think games are always in the process of being improved, as we see from new editions and so forth.

    There's no certain answer: sometimes a game clearly gels after just a couple of sessions, while another game could be playtested for years and never quite sit right.

    There are also many different kinds of playtesting (e.g. for gameplay vs. for the quality of your game materials vs. communication of premise vs. readability, etc)...

    I'd say that, at a minimum, you should play the game with a few different groups (let's say three different groups), until you don't need to make any more changes to be happy with the results.

    \n Playtesting is enough when it is done; there are many different factors that go into how much you need. Not needing any at all is very unusual, but not strictly speaking impossible. It is more likely that the designer is making an error in design practices if they don't playtest at all, though. (Or they're not attempting to achieve a finished product in the traditional sense, which is of course fine - it's cool to write up some ideas and distribute them for others even if you don't feel like playing with them yourself right now, as Dirk says.)

    To be more specific, here are the major kinds of playtesting that rpg design often uses, with the names I tend to use for them:

    Explorative playtest is when you have some ideas for a game and glue them quickly together to give them a whirl in real conditions, and brainstorm with the group on the flight to see whether there might be something interesting there. Later on you use explorative playtesting to expand upon concepts and explore their implications.

    Focus group testing is when you expose your game to some players in hopes of gleaning insight into how the players engage the material - how it is working for them in real conditions, and how it is failing them. The "focus group" is there in the name to emphasize the subjective nature of the creator-receptor encounter; the results you get are heavily influenced by the kind of person you use to test, which should be accounted for in designing the test.

    Affirmative testing or stress testing is when you have an ostensibly finished thing, and you put it through its paces to make sure everything works and you didn't forget to write up that equipment list or whatever.

    Theoretically you can get by without any of these playtest types, because playtesting is a development tool, not an inherent part of what constitutes the finished game. In practice most designers tend to use playtesting, and how much and what type depends on the nature of the game as well as the nature of the designer. For example, one of my games was tested two times before I accepted it for its first publication, because those tests were successful as focus group testing and stress testing, leaving me with no doubts as to the quality of the product. In another project I organized three dozen sessions of playtests, including two small campaigns and some independent testing, because the nature of the game required a lot of explorative testing to hone the details, and a modicum of stress testing to make sure everything was working together the way it was supposed to. In both cases I stopped testing because I was finished, not because I was following a best practices guideline for the amount of playtesting you need to be a good person.

    \n Interesting distinction, Eero! So, personally speaking, exploratory playtesting is an integral part of my design process and the most fun \u2014 though, curiously, little to no such testing was involved in designing Enter the Avenger and all following testing became confirmatory (it convinced me that the game as first written was finished, and a good game). Also, I've taken flak from my fellow Italian role-players/designers on occasion for my radical opinion that you can't really stress-test role-playing games, the way you would stress-test other kinds of games.

    But enough of a tangent\u2026

    I think it bears mention that, for a long time, playtesting wasn't the norm, or even at all expected, in RPGs. Some publications did result from the author's ongoing campaign(s), and were thus incidentally playtested, and D&D tournament-style modules were of course tested on the field, but AFAIK at least in the 1990s most role-playing books were created entirely as writing assignments \u2014 RPG companies didn't employ any "department of playtesting".
    There was a bit of a change with D&D3e, I think, possibly reflecting ingrained habits from working on Magic: the Gathering? I believe some playtesting was involved in the initial development of 3e, and I do remember this being touted as a feature at the time. But this was reversed almost immediately, with the first batch of obviously unplaytested "splatbooks". All in all, I think the development of D&D remained playtest-driven, but more as a sort of post-publication debugging thing which resulted in new editions \u2014 not unlike in software development.

    Concurrently, it was of course on the Forge that people started pushing playtesting as such an important part of RPG development, including a habit of crediting playtesters (which I do subscribe to). In fact, there was a time when "needs more playtesting" had become a buzzword, a code for "put more effort into it", "don't publish yet" or even "I don't care about your game if it's not a product of the culture and scene I'm part of", which I believe was what prompted Ben Lehman to write his infamous anti-playtest rant ("playtesting is not a substitute for design" or something like that). Our community, for a time, had turned "playtesting" into a barrier to entry.

    But maybe, the author of the game mentioned in the OP isn't from that crowd. Maybe they're from a background where RPGs aren't usually playtested prior to publishing, as was the case for a long time.

    I guess that the playtesting thing is just one of those things I, among others, picked up at the Forge. There are all these little cornerstones of how to go about things that I learned there. Wonderful, really.

    Regarding exploratory playtesting, I've found that it's become a big part of my design work as well. Early on I wouldn't do it as much (e.g. ten years ago, when I wrote a lot of small and tight games, I would go directly to focus group testing or confirmatory testing), but nowadays I tend to favour a quick alpha playtest draft taken to the table soon as possible, with more consideration put into it once we get some internal insight into how the thing plays. Sort of similar to the light-weight software design paradigms that have become more common nowadays.

    But then again, thinking about it, I did write Fellows of the Julenius Archive with a single focus group test and a single confirmatory test session not too far back. Definitely possible to go direct from drafting to product development for the right kind of (small) game.

    \n I now give myself at least two years to playest something, and I do it in the context of running at least one actual campaign over that time (in addition to isolated playtests of specific mechanics). I've found that to be incredibly important, especially since I want the games playable over the long haul. Things come up during the course of regular play that shed different light things than when you are just doing isolated playtests (i.e. something that seems like it isn't enjoyable or potentially unbalanced if run as a single isolate encounter or as part of a single session, might be less of a problem-or more of a problem-in the context of a full campaign). You also find way more holes in the game that way.

    I think game companies with more time to devote on a daily basis to play testing can achieve that in a much shorter span of time of course.

    \n My personal benchmark is "do you hate it yet? If not, it needs more playtesting."

    More seriously, there's no objective answer to this question. Don't let anybody tell you how to make things you love, but understand that if you are not diligent in putting out a complete and reasonably cogent game that will be a reflection on you in the future. Take pride in your work and strive to make it Pretty Good. You can't do that without playtesting, so figure out what makes sense to you. I know really smart game designers who playtest very minimally and others who have rigorous methodologies.

    I'm actually OK with playtesting being a modest barrier to entry. Finding people who trust and care enough to make a large sacrifice in time and energy on your project speaks well of your social and organizational skills, and it probably means you are reciprocating by playtesting for others, which makes you a better designer. Obviously there are cases where the barrier may be systemic and that's another issue, but if you are just Some Random Dude who can't find anyone to playtest your game, maybe look closer at why that is.

    \n When we're talking about "finding playtesters", the issue is usually finding people to do independent testing - strangers who will play your game without you being there to run it for them. This is much less important than playing the game yourself; it takes having some support skills (e.g. human insight, wide experience with different play situations), but it is well in the realm of possibility to do all the necessary playtesting yourself for many types of gaming products. Not saying that independent testing isn't valuable in its time and place, but it's not crucial. And if somebody cannot find the time nor the players to play the game with them, I don't think that concerns about product quality are really timely for the project; a game you cannot even play yourself surely isn't ready to become any sort of publication. Whatever it is that prevents you from playtesting your game, fix that first. Lacking the players to play your game with you is like lacking the pen and paper to write your game up; it is a fundamental lack in the working environment.

    (A free hint: one should not prioritize becoming a game designer without being a successful game player first, just like you have to be a reader to be a writer. In the realm of social gaming, which tabletop rpgs definitely are, this means having friends who want to play with you, and are willing to play a wide variety of games with you, including your own designs. Work on that first if it's a bottleneck, for it will surely be the best possible move you can make in improving your game design.)

    An armchair designer who does not intend to play his own game at all, perhaps because they're not that interested in gaming, would probably be better off with some other type of creative hobby. I have no confidence in rpg writing being able to give them creative satisfaction, given how esoteric a creative field it is; actual play is what gives rpg design purpose, and if you are yourself cut off from that hobby, then there is little reason for you to design.

    (The above obviously does not apply to showpiece designs that are not intended for play, but rather for other designers to read and cogitate for amusement or theoretical insight or whatever. It's a very specific field of creative writing, but worthwhile when you have something interesting to say, and want to say it in the form of a song - or a game, in this case.)

    \n I was very careful about the way I characterized the importance of local vs. independent playtesting. You are free to disagree, of course, but I think that characterizing the former as fundamental and the latter as merely very helpful and widely useful is precise when discussing the issue in general. There are specific design fields where the situation is different, but then all generalizations have their limitations.

    (If you want to say that having others play your games is more important than playing them yourself, then I will disagree with that as well. Additionally, I will see it as a pretty peculiar viewpoint. I would expect most everybody to agree that actually having a gaming life yourself is the first precept, and everything else, including external playtesting, comes after that.)

    The point I was making was that usually, when people ask about finding playtesters, and usually, when playtesting becomes a major hurdle for game development, it is external, independent playtesting that is at an issue; it is much rarer for somebody to have the problem of being completely unable to play their game themselves. It is good to differentiate which case we're talking about, and have some perspective on the importance of the problem.

    This is an important distinction exactly because of what I said: external playtesting is important, but it is not mandatory, and for some projects not even among the top issues. While I don't think that it is possible to over-emphasize the importance of playing your game yourself, I do think that it is possible to over-emphasize the need for external playtesting. As has been said by others here, the emphasis on playtesting as a best practice can become a blind doctrine more useful to excluding people than it is for designers trying to improve their work. The remedy to that is to actually understand how important playtesting is, and why, and where, instead of blindly singing its praises. It is useful and one of the few tools that give you the benefits it does, but it is not everything.

    For the record, I have authored, published and sold games successfully with insignificant external playtesting. (Zombie Cinema, to be specific, went through its convoluted and thorough development cycle with external playtesting playing a very minor role; I have no doubts about the quality of the final product.) I have also refused to move forward on projects before arranging for sufficient independent testing. The usefulness depends on what precisely is going on in your design, the necessity for independent testing is not somehow in-built into the nature of what it is to craft a game. The same or similar information may well exist for you already, to pick just one example of a reason that might reduce the necessity for external testing. Or your design might be largely conservative and consist mostly of non-interactive elements with low chance of emergent behaviours. Or the whole point of the work might be the sharing of personal experience; I definitely know that my prospective book about how I play old school D&D would not have any external playtesting!

    \n I've had success with a sort of swapping, but it's not simultaneous - people's life rhythms rarely line up like that, and if you try to find your swap partner by the sole criteria of whether they'll be able to playtest this month, odds are that you'll get someone with unsuitable creative harmonies.

    So what I do instead is that I cultivate long-term creative relationships with other people, playtesting their games as needed, in the hopes that they'll return the favor later - sometimes years later. For this to work the creative chemistry is of course paramount; if you are not actually interested in somebody else's project, it's probably too risky to put the required man-hours into the playtesting (or studio critique, or editing, or whatever it is they need - playtesting is just one thing you can do for others), and even if you do, they might be left completely cold by your next project later on in the cycle.

    This is all long-term strategy - or rather lifestyle, it's not like I plan for future favours in advance. By approaching the scene with mutualism in mind, ready and willing to see friends and colleagues in interesting people, you'll be likely to find that over a couple of years you build up enough social capital to make independent playtesting quite achievable.

    Of course all this is not much help if you need playtesting now and are stuck without external feedback. In that case the best bet is probably to really spruce that manuscript up, add candy to hook people's interest. Sort of like a practice run for the actual publication; if you can't attract any playtest heat, that might be saying something about how much other people are intrigued by the premise of you game in the first place. The logical extreme for this is, of course, an ashcan publication (a public beta release, if you will).

      \n Articulating and expressing your ideas, in writing, maybe even in a language which isn't your native one, takes a lot of time \u2014 on top of reading and playing (which is what you invested money on in the first place, if that's the case).

      I myself am guilty of trying out several games which were in playtesting at the time, but then never getting down to writing reports. As for game-texts which people send me for just a reading review, well\u2026 I usually try and read them, but then I don't usually get as far as writing detailed feedback \u2014 especially when I think I have some negative feedback to give, then it's a huge hurdle to actually write it. I mean, that's something I would have managed to do in my university student days, but now I'm simply too tired when I get to be sitting at my computer for an hour a day or so.

      That's why I really appreciated that sharing reviews became part of Game Chef, by the way! By choosing to participate I'm now seriously committing the time to write down some feedback.

      \n I think it depends on how you deal with what playtesters say. I had a lot of good positive and negative feedback at rpgnet. I just posted the pdf and got everyone's general feel and answered questions. The feedback actually lead to some rule rewrites. This was good on just the general nuts and bolts of what they saw when they read it. It was helpful to know the impression our work was getting across. Feedback seemed to dry up as soon as we defended some ideas. Might be better to just take it all and tell them you'll talk more about the choices later.

      We ran adventures at cons and then asked for opinions. Some groups we took through character gen and others we gave out characters and so we got some decent feedback on gameplay and character generation. The feedback, again, lead to some rule changes since the consensus was the character gen had math that no one wanted to do.

      I noticed some of the GMs who were not directly involved to the development of the game got much more feedback. So that may also be a factor that could help
