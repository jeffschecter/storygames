\n Been working on a traditional style rpg design and thinking about what it would be like to gamemaster the game if you'd never run a game before. A lot of attention gets given to helping players start into games, but I don't see as much for the first-time gamemaster. Many people here are probably fairly experienced, so I ask, what do you wish you had when you first started as a gamemaster? Are there things in other games that you've found especially useful? How about hazards to avoid?

    \nI wish I had not fallen for the whole "The GM is a storyteller" mumbo-jumbo.
    I found especially useful games mechanics that support some sort of player input - like Burning Wheel's beliefs or whatever. This makes it considerably easier to drive the game forward as well as takes care of bullshit starting premises like "you meet in an inn". It also makes it easier for the GM to keep track of what the players want.
    Hazards to avoid are generally not being a dick, not making the game your personal playground and not paying attention to the players.

    Otherwise I'd sum it up as: "Your role as a GM is to keep the game move forward & run smoothly. Try to do the things the game rules explicitly tell you to do as a GM and use your authority to tailor things to everyone's enjoyment (including yours)."

    \n In general, when I start considering GMing a new game, I want to know what sort of stories we'll tell. And I love games that tell me just this (DITV being one of my favorites ones for this). Incidentally, that's why I like reading APs for games I'm considering running.

    When I first started GMing D&D, I had it easy because I had experienced it quite some time as a player first. So I had an idea about what sort of things I could make into stories.

    For a newbie GM, I'd appreciate if you give me some hinds about "this is the sort of things that you could turn into a story for this game". (So you don't end up with something like "dungeoncrawl with vampires as the 'heroes' "...unless you want to.)

    \n For the first (and only) D&D campaign I ran, I spent way too much writing out a detailed history for the game and planning out where I could direct the players. I found what I had written interesting, but I never asked the players for input. During the game, I encouraged secrecy, which led to an air of paranoia and broke down player communication. I wasn't prepared for situations where the players wanted to do something that was cool for which there was no rule. Ultimately that game ended after a couple of sessions.

    At the most basic level, be familiar with the concept of "yes, and..." GM's should be incredibly empathetic with the players - know what kind of fun they're interested in having. Let them see what they want and then make them fight for it, and never put what they want completely out of reach. Let them see that they are making progress, even when they stumble. When they do stumble, make it meaningful to their character. At a more granular level, make sure that it's clear how their characters are connected to the game you're playing. That will make it easier for you to determine what they're invested in and how you can complicate their journey.

    \n The concepts of "Say Yes" (explained properly as not meaning yes you can play a space catgirl in this historical western game), "Hold Ideas Lightly", make failure interesting, get players to narrate, encourage players to come up with Push and Pull choices and actions (cf Mo from spaceandeath), Reincorporate Content Players Create.

    Encournage Players to create characters collectively, not as an exercise in combined min/maxing but in creating a cohesive reason to associate, or accepting one proposed by GM.

    Story/Conflict/Character must be woven together as opposed to "Adventure Module created in this room, each of 4 Player characters created in four other rooms, and then all tossed together". This is a shared GM/Player responsibility.

    Personally, also I wish I had learned earlier the points mentioned along lines fo the game does not belong to the GM, it\u2019s not \u201chis/her\u201d game, the players are not \u201chis/her players\u201d. There is a division of authority around the table and with it responsibility, which varies between game systems. However, everyone is responsible for making choices and adopting behaviours that support everyone else having fun. A GM can be open to Player input but still keep all authority and thus responsibility, which burns them out.

    Rob

    \n This may sound like hippie BS, but I've found it quite useful in my day job.

    Something that may be helpful is if the group at the table thinks of themselves as a tribe - a small tribe of storytellers responsible for working together to create a shared mythology. Players of the tribe have the responsibility to add details to a fairly small scope of the mythology (usually, the details immediately surrounding a specific major character). The GM of the tribe has the responsibility to challenge the other members on the details they wish to add to the mythology as well as to add details to areas where the mythology needs them but where the players may not have the responsibility do so.

    \n So, a lot of suggestions to give the players some authority over the story. Not surprising given the group I asked, and I'm in agreement. One of the flaws of traditional rpgs is they put too much responsibility for the narrative on the shoulders of the gm, overwhelming some new gms and closing the doors to great story ideas. Making this happen in a very traditionally-styled rpg is another trick altogether.

    Now, if you're asking "why make it traditional?" the answer is that this is an experiment. I'm trying to understand the strengths of that form, and there are a few. It's a very different mindset, and adapting new techniques to that mindset has proven an enjoyable challenge, though I can't see myself going back to designing that way on a regular basis.

So what are the elements of the traditional game, as you understand them, and how do we make them work?
