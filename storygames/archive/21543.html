\n I mentioned on the re-welcome thread, but I've been on the fringes of the story game loop for the best part of the last 7yrs and so I'm wondering what peeps here think have been the vital games I simply *must* play from that time period? Why?

I've made my own wee shortlist based upon a rough scan over the indy and small press titles listed at Leisure Games:

The Skeletons - Holy shit this seems to have more layers of atmosphere to it than there are layers of dust in the tomb you have been set to guard. I can't wait to find a copy and give this a shot.

Blades in the Dark - Playing the thieves guild? With cool sounding mechanics for playing through missions and cranking up the tension as things start to spin out of control? AND relationship mechanics for your ranking with other factions? AND AND meechanics for playing from nobodies to kingpins? I'm IN!

#Feminism - Yes.

Wield - Did someone say I get to actually play Stormbringer in a RPG (as opposed to Stormbringer the RPG)? I grew up with Elric so there is no way I can be without this in my life.

Undying - Everything I always wished V:tM had focused on, rather than being the unwieldy mess it was. Finding someone else loved the same parts of V:tM as myself feels really good, and I kinda want to run this both as written and as a larp.

What needs added to this list?

    \n I think reading through Apocalypse World (or perhaps Jason d'Angelo's "Daily Apocalypse" series on G+) is pretty important if you want to be "up to date" on modern games, simply because of the widespread influence the game has had on the field.

    Bluebeard's Bride is interesting for a number of reasons:

    * Unlike most RPGs (at least historically speaking), it is designed for and by women.

    * It deals with a specifically "feminine" type of story, and "feminine horror" in particular (possibly for the first time in RPG history). Themes of disempowerment and abuse are rarely handled well in RPGs, and by all accounts Bluebeard's Bride does both very well.

    * The design is an unusual application of PbtA "technology", quite different from other hacks of Apocalypse World. It's got lots of interesting bits and pieces, from a design perspective.

    * The game is unusual in other aspects, too: there is only one character, and the players portray parts of her psyche. There are a few other games which have tried to do this, but this one seems to stand out in terms of how sharp and how gameable that feature is.

    Tanya Fyans said:

    Hi @DInDenver! Thanks for the ideas. What is the hook with Microscope? Seen it mentioned but not really getting much from that or the blurb I've found beyond it dealing with the sweeping ages of mighty empires.

    \nYeah, fantasy or SF pseudo-history seems to be everybody's favorite use of Microscope, but I've found out I like it more when focusing on a smaller scale story, such as a single episode of a fictitious TV show.
    Anyway, the real hook for me is being able to tell a story in non-chronological order - "zapping" from scene to scene and from glimpse to glimpse following a certain issue or thematic highlight, while not paying much attention at all to the logistics or connectives (the game "board" takes care of that stuff by always showing you when each part fits within the timeline, all at a glance). Also, being able to gloss over those parts of the story when we can easily assume what happens, prioritizing those where I really have no idea what's going to happen - but with the door always open to go back to those moments at a later time, revisit the glossed over scenes and even retcon stuff.
