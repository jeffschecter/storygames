Cross-posted from my blog:

I'm finally posting this - it's been sticking in my craw for a while now. Over at Story-Games (not a member - you should be, you know!) I was talking about the cohorts I wanted to get together for Ludolab. In the commentary in the thread, there was this, from Shreyas Sampat (he of Four Willows Weeping):

I'm not sure I like [the Frankengame cohort] or setting design; who needs setting? I guess I see their potential for other people, though.

[OK, my browser crashed while I was writing this - I'm going to cut back to the chase with this.]

Setting design and game design share a lot of ground - I believe that setting design (i.e. worldbuilding) is the primary means by which game players become content-generators, at least in the tabletop arena.

That aside, what this quote says to me is that setting = 100% color. It's just a fancy dressing that you hang over the system, and it doesn't need to be 'designed'.

I think that's wrong on two counts - first of all, setting (any good setting, at least) involves system - physical, social, economic and so-forth. I'm not just talking about verisimilitude-rich settings like HÃƒÂ¢rn or Monte Cooke's shelf-breaker Ptolus, either. Any setting has systems on which it depends, and of which the players either inherently understand (because they're similar to everyday life, or they're a subject of common interest), or which they learn through play in order for their characters to function well. If the setting doesn't make sense, or its too superficial and static, the players will sense it instinctually.

Secondly, big-S System springs from the setting - or at least it should. No guns in medieval France, so no gun rules - this is a trivial example, but an apt one. Whether or not a game calls for a token mechanic, a bidding mechanic or a die mechanic can also be determined by setting - the conflict takes place in a world-system that is either commerical (requiring some sort of sense of the word 'purchase'), contested (requiring an escalating stake), or random.

Certainly there are games where these systems are either beside the point, or minimized because the game involves what I'd call a 'staged' setting (either the physical surroundings or the conceptual underpinnings are artificially closed). A game like Shreyas' Snow from Korea is a good example of this. But even in Snow from Korea, there are cultural elements which must be understood by the players - they are samurai in the court of the Daimyo - which come, ultimately, from the setting of the game.

So setting is important, and everyone needs a little bit of it. Certainly, the curriculum for Ludolab will be aimed at creating dynamic settings - it won't just be a course on how to draw coastlines. As much as system is a part of the setting, so is situation. Creating settings that are built on and nuture Situation explicitly are the next iteration of the idea.

    One of the elements we consider core to the roleplaying experience is generating fictional content- Setting is the initial fictional content to get us started, and provide an example of what would further fit in play. It helps us generate focused imaginary elements, whether we're talking Character, Color, or Situation.

    For example, if you're playing PTA, you still need to nail down what the series is about so that everyone can make appropriate characters, appropriate Issues, and good Situation from all of that.

    So it becomes crucial to either provide Setting initially, or tools to produce setting. You can find play clashes when included setting is either contradictory or poorly written, OR when the System doesn't support it well.

    But, all that fancy talk aside- Setting & Color are what sparks the imagination and sell people on most games. Only system monkeys like myself drool when you show me the tools, most everyone else just wants to see what they're supposed to have as the end result...

    It's easier for me to try to sell someone on playing Eberron rather than Sorcerer or GURPS, because Eberron instantly conjures an image in their head and they already begin imagining what they would do with it, whereas the latter two require system familiarity and doesn't suggest anything by itself...

    Hey Kuma,

    I agree with you. I a while back. I'm mainly focused on protagonist play (or narrativist play if you prefer), so I'm not claiming my ideas account for other creative agendas.

    For what's it's worth, if I can introduce a couple equations of my own:
    Color + Character + System = Setting

    Setting + Situation + Player = Story

    As Kuma and others have stated, I think the links between system and setting are so tight as to be practically inseparable.

    Guns are setting in a Western RPG or Napoleonic Naval RPG, and they are expressed mechanically. Trust and betrayal can also be setting in a game about honor (like The Mountain Witch), and that is expressed mechanically too. Swords, armor, magic, technology, and so forth are all just as important to setting as who the current king is or what megacorp is working on a new super drug.

    Our experience with Burning Wheel has led the Burning Wheel team to believe that collaborative setting/campaign creation is the most powerful way to create the kinds of campaigns that we want to play. It serves multiple purposes:
    1. It gets everyone on the same page and really invested in the place we've created because they own the color and are able to contribute the stuff that jazzes them.
    2. If done correctly, it creates and informs situation. Situation is perhaps the most important element of a campaign, whether it's intended to be one session long or last for years.
    3. The color and the situtation inform character. The players will create colorful characters designed to tackle the situation.

    Taken together, this stuff should really drive players to proactive play.

    Thanks Matt!

    Jihad's World Burner was an expression of it in rules form, an attempt to take what Pete and I have been doing at our tables and express it in the rules.

    HeroQuest did something similar with its Clan Generator from Orlanth Is Dead!, a supplement for the Sartar Rising campaign. Pretty neat stuff that I've been playing with in my current HeroQuest game. I was also influenced a lot by Aria: Canticle of the Monomyth, which was problematic at best but had some really interesting setting-creation ideas.

    Obviously, we don't have anything like the World Burner yet for Burning Wheel proper, so it's a little less refined for our fantasy games. For my current campaign, we generated about four pitches. I threw in one as the GM and the players contributed ideas as well. We selected one through concensus and added bits of the others that we felt would fit well.

    Then I asked a series of World Burner-like questions. The first few questions are intended to generate the color that invest the players in the game. The next few questions are about taking the color that players were the most excited about and helping it evolve into situation.

    The last few questions were about evoking characters designed to attack that situation from multiple angles. Then we chose characters and the rest became antagonists and supporting cast.
