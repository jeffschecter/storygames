\n With all the talk about the New Hotness syndrome, I figure we could use a thread that talks about the forgotten gems of the indie community! If you know of a game, discussion, supplement, or whatever that you think is great but has sadly almost been forgotten these days, here's the place to point people to it so that friends new and old won't miss out anymore. I'll start.

Something that I haven't seen mentioned in a long time is the scenario Well of Souls, created in 2003 by Peter Nordstrand and Chris Chinn. It was designed specifically with Story Now in mind: there's a dramatic situation, characters with lots of competing agendas, and no predetermined scenes or outcomes. If you play these characters to the hilt, you're going to get a powerful, player-driven outcome. It's got HeroQuest stats, but really, it can be used in virtually any game that allows for Story Now play. In fact, I would think that this scenario could be great when combined with one of the very light rules of one of the recent playsets we've seen.

So--what gem would you like to bring back into the spotlight?

    \n When I think about it now... I might be forgetting something, but honestly I can't think of too many old independently-designed games that I still play or want to play, forgotten OR remembered.

    However, I recognize awesome things about them, every game mentioned here. Also, I love the games that have come out since that basically stood onthe shoulders of developments that those older/forgotten games made.

    EDIT: Ooooh, nevermind. Here's some I thought of:
    Pretender (No Press RPG Anthology) by Kirt Dankmyer. The greatest game of Fortune in the Whatever (roll dice first, then sort them into categories, then decide what that Means) to date, possibly to be later overshadowed by Psi-Run.

    Pagoda (No Press) by Jeff Schecter. While it had holes, it was a solid game for its time of quick Wuxia action that also involved things like Animal Signs and the like.

    Exemplar, also by Jeff Schecter. Download Here. This has a revolution that no one has tried to put into games since (save for me, and I haven't fucking finished yet): What's the difficulty for an action? *The player decides*. The difficulty number is usually static, like "10". The player decides how many d10s they want to roll. If 1, then they will absolutely succeed, however their success is shaky/"just barely". More dice means more risk, but greater results. And when in competition with someone else, the more risk taken means a lesser chance of success, but the more chance that you bowl over your opponent.
    Also has some little strategy bits where you make "combos" to use in combat. Also, very streamlined characters, basically taking 6 abilities and using those instead of attributes and skills (ahh, there they are: Prowess, Stealth, Piloting, Charisma, Technology and Wits)
    It's basically an Exalted In Space.
    A previous version had "Nemesis points" which you could expel to bring you closer to doom, but it gave you a temporary boost. This was the first instance I saw of that kind of mechanic as well.
    In short: Jeff is a motherfucking genius. It's all of our loss that he decided to, like, Go To College and Stuff.

    -Andy
