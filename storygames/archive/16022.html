    \n I've always wanted to re-write the entire Harry Potter series. But from a different viewpoint.

    You see, the way the HP books are currently written, you don't find out much of anything about Hufflepuff. Gryffindors are brave, Slytherin are cunning, Ravenclaw are smart, and well, Hufflepuff are pretty much the remaining dregs...they are the magical equivalents of the "would you like fries with that?" magical crowd.

    But what if...what if, in reality, the Hufflepullfs were really the house that was calling the shots? And their whole schtick was just a cover-up because they were basically the Illuminati of the magical world.

    In my vision, Harry Potter turned out to be a full-blooded muggle with no magic ability whatsoever, and the whole series would follow a crack band of Hufflepuffs working in the background, following Harry around, secretly using their magic to keep the charade of Harry's story alive.It would be the exact same story arc as the original Harry Potter stories, except that it would be "the real inside scoop of how it REALLY happened."

    \n 1a = "getting the character right".

    This is a huge can of worms, aren't you glad you asked?

    I think it matters that the first "fandom" I was ever a part of was Sherlock Holmes. Sherlock Holmes fanficcers were writing fanfic before Doyle was done writing Sherlock Holmes stories. In this respect, when I played Sherlock Holmes in an online game, I was less trying to get Holmes "right" as I was trying to express my own personal take on a very iconic character.

    Iconic characters are broadly drawn - they often use stereotypes or broad classifications to get across their essence. Sherlock Holmes stories don't form a deeply thought out psychological portrait of a character - there's a reason there's a shitload of Sherlock Holmes fanfic and (virtually) no Hyacinth Robinson fanfic. Sherlock Holmes can be more effectively made "my own". The whole reason I am attracted to these properties is because I want to give my own take, my own version of the character.

    It isn't that I'm trying to be true to some Platonic ideal of the X-Men universe - that doesn't exist. At various times in the history of the X-Men they have conversed like over-wordy adults pretending to be hip kids, suicidal jerkoffs, and whatever kind of robot excretes Joss Whedon dialogue. There is no "center" to the portrayal, there's nothing to get "right". Instead, there are very broadly drawn characters: a power, an appearance (this is important) and a general personality trait. These make for very simple levers that a creator can casually twist and pull to put in situations of interest to them.

    So no, I don't feel that "getting a character right" is of that much interest - instead, what is of interest is expressing/developing a character, preferably through action, but if not through action, then through dialogue. I do a lot of design-in-play, I'm one of the few in my group that does.

    1b - I definitely see a strong connection between playing an established character in a game and writing fanfiction - in fact, the vast majority of the roleplaying hobby is basically doing just that, minus the game part, in play-by-forum and play-by-email games. Empirically speaking, this is actually the center of the roleplaying hobby, just by dint of numbers.

    \n Just speaking of comics, since they're multiply authored and (these days) rebooted all the damn time, I think most comic fans accept that there are different versions of their favorites. The arguments are about which one was best, or most faithful to the Golden Age Urgestalt of the character, or which one was most badass.

    Personally, I find that people who argue a lot about being true to characters can make difficult gaming buddies, because they stop the flow of the specific game to hold out for some ideal vision that doesn't mean anything to me. That's why I like to play in games that, if not wholly original, at least have a different name and a new coat of paint. That way, if I decide that my version of Wolverine (who is named something different) is less annoying than the official Wolverine, nobody can complain about it.

    \n I found the exact thread I was referring to. If you search topics for "spectre of the mushroom kingdom" it will be right there.

    So, what that thread is, is a scenario created by me and played by Joel and friends. It came about because I read Wicked and played Mario 3 in the same day. At first I was like, I could write this, but then I decided that was silly (I never really "got" fanfic, no offense meant to anyone), I should make it a roleplaying scenario instead. I didn't have enough people to cover all the characters, so I presented it as a "Play This! Challenge" on SG.

    What I wanted to do with it was retain everything about the characters that made them who they were, then fill that out with a third dimension of human motives that cast a new and interesting light on the situation, such that issues of who was the hero and who was the villain were totally up for grabs.

    It was important to me that Bowser be totally recognizable as Bowser, with all the traits that make him Bowser, even as his war against the mushrooms is recast as a struggle for independent sovereignty, and his thing with Peach being recast as desire springing from genuine respect. The same goes for the other characters, and the way that Joel and company handled it was pretty much ideal.

    It's not so much a matter of getting the character "right" as it was a matter of seeing whether I could add literary depth to something as silly and two-dimensional as Mario, and of seeing how far I could bend the material without breaking it. It therefore becomes a creative challenge, and quite rewarding if you can pull it off, even (especially?) by proxy.

    Sorry if I'm vague; I'm typing from a phone, and it's hard to be eloquent when you can only type at one-tenth your speed of thought.

    \n With all the above, I think various people will have different "breaking points" when talking about versions of characters. I can't think of an example from gaming, but Spider Man, in the Spider Man films, is not Spider Man to me... there's just a number of little things that are changed that cause Spidey's spideyness to go missing. I liked the movies and I liked all their Sam Raimi goodness, but they were missing something that was essential to my experience of the character. So, if I was gaming with pre-existing characters, I'd try my best to get the group's breaking points down, to try and discover when something was just to far removed from their conception of the character to fit into that conception. This happens to me a lot with settings. Star Wars gaming has routinely failed in my group because people don't respect what kind of content fits within everyone's idea of Star Wars and what kind of content fits just within their's.
