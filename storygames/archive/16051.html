\n So our group has recently played two sessions of Left Coast. The intention is to use this thread to discuss our experiences. How the game played, what worked, what didn't etc.

For those who don't know Left Coast is a game by Steve Hickey. The protagonists are weird sci-fi writers in 1950s-60s Generifornia, writing fiction and trying not to fuck up their lives. As far as I'm concerned the premise is awesome. Mechanically it's GMless. Some players play authors, others play NPC and others play "Creators" - which are kind of "small g" GMs. It's designed for smaller groups but we are pushing the envelope with six players.

We played out first session last week. About half the players attempted to read the rules. One of us (luckily) really grokked it from the reading (he said he read it out loud). Personally it usually takes playing through something at least once for me to get what a game is on about and generally I really only gain a good sense of how a system works after a few sessions (at least). But back to Left Coast.

The setup was really enjoyable although quite lengthy (it took about 1.5 hours, half the session). With our six players we elected for "traditional" role assignments; two Authors, two Creators and two NPC players, although other configurations are possible. We used a large piece of paper split into quadrants labelled Money, Social, Trouble and Weird. The group then went ahead and brainstormed two Authors (including name and type) and a cast of supporting NPCs for each Author. The NPCs were each assigned to an Author and to a quadrant. The whole setup was quite organic, we spent time throwing ideas around and taking on suggestions to flesh things out. The setting felt ready to hit the ground running, full of story possibilities by the time we were done.

We then launched into the our first scene. Following this each player took turns to invoke various scenes types. We found the turns are quite time consuming. Both sessions we didn't make it all the way around the table, i.e. every player did not get a turn.

Another concern is whether NPC players feel like their role is rewarding. The players playing NPCs seemed to indicate that they did. But they did say that it was more the infrequency of turns that was a bit of a problem.

There is plenty more I have to say. I'll revisit this post soon. I just wanted to get it up here and open up the discussion for members of our group and anyone else interested in the game.

    \n Hi Morgan. You're right: NPC is the term I'm using for characters who aren't the main characters (it stands for 'Non-Protagonist Character'). \n
    Posted By: MathalusI like the idea of allowing some players to just come out and say, "I will play a supporting role." Is that what this is?
    \nI'll give you two answers, because I can see two ways of interpreting this:

    1. All players are allowed (and encouraged) to play NPCs.

    2. Depending on how the group distributes the authority in the game, some players will have a more supporting role. In Ritchie's group of six, having two Authors and two Creators leaves two people to primarily focus on playing NPCs. In smaller groups, players may double-up on this positions: for instance in a three-player game you could have a player who's an Author, a Creator (for the other player), *and* an NPC Owner.

    Does that make sense?


    I'm keen to hear more about how the game went, Ritchie. What aspects of the rules do you think made the turns take so long?

    \n Steve, Going through all the multiple choice lists for each turn and Scene type is time consuming. I think we had some trouble coming up with scenes and scene goals which caused a bit of time delay. But once a scene was rolling we didn't tend to let them drag on (mostly). I think we could get better at this.

    There could be a starting starting point issue i.e. there are a lot of hooks but no bang, maybe there needs to be some sort of bang at the beginning to get the Authors' stories going, with well a bang. Turns may get faster as there is more backstory to build on, I'm not sure.

    A lack of familiarity with the rules, since we've only played twice, probably contributed to slower play but there are a lot of different contingencies for player types, turn types and scene types. I think this could all be simplified somehow. These are just impressions though because I still don't really feel like I quite have the rules under my belt. The dice mechanics seem to work quite well, there is a good balance of success and failure - rolls feel important.

    Russ, I'd prefer to let Paul cover the fiction as he took notes but to summarise our two Authors are:

    Jeff R. Howell (played by Elliot) and T. Alfred Gosling (played by me)

    Jeff is a "Real Man" type. Being bank rolled by his girlfriend's (who is in a cult) rancher father. He also has a brother, Bruce and sister-in-law, Sophie. It just came out (Secret)* that Sophie is in love with Jeff. We've only had one "Story" scene (I type of scene where the game fiction becomes about the story ficition that an Author is writing (awesome). It turned out to be religio/political yarn on a space station. We based all the characters really heavily on the NPCs/supporting characters from Jeff's life which was cool and the whole thing turned out to be an ok first try at fiction within the fiction.

There are shadowy figures on the periphery. Towards the end of last sesh his fears were confirmed, after a hasty retreat from a library scene he was accosted by a grungy hippy who wanted him to "See the truth! - Man." Now they're on the run together and he's mixed up with some fringe group who have uncovered government mind control experiments.

    \nLeft Coast: a playtest AP
    \n
    \nPlayers (six total):
    \n
    \nThe Authors
    \nRichie plays T. Alfred Gosling, a Visionary
    \nElliot plays Jeff R. Howell, a Real Man
    \n
    \nThe Creators
    \nBen plays the Weird for Jeff R. Howell, featuring The Greater Universe Institute, a strange cultish group
    \nJohn plays the Weird for T. Alfred Gosling, featuring a mysterious Agent working for an unknown organisation who lives next door.
    \n
    \nThe Audience/NPCs
    \nPaul and Euan play other characters the Authors interact with.
    \n
    \nSetting
    \n
    \nOur game is set in San Francisco in the early 1960s, or a version of San Francisco in the 60s as imagined by six guys from Australia.
    \n
    \nCharacters
    \n
    \nLeft Coast has everyone play NPCs for everyone else, and each NPC is attached to one of the Authors. The Authors split the cast between them depending on how social or introverted they are.
    \nGosling is an alcoholic shut-in, so he has less NPCs in his life than Jeff.
    \nEach NPC falls into one of four categories representing different sources of conflict in the Author's life. The quadrants are Weird, Social, Money, Trouble.
    \n
    \nCharacters attached to Gosling
    \n
    \nAlly Gosling (MONEY) - Gosling's wife, played by Elliot. Ally is Gosling's nominated Most Significant Person in his life. Born to rich parents, she has high aspirations that Gosling cannot meet. Her parents don't approve of their marriage.
    \n
    \n"Bud" (SOCIAL) - Gosling's best friend, played by Ben. Bud and Gosling know each other from childhood. Bud is a blue-collar mechanic who thinks Gosling's a good writer but is worried he will go off the 'deep end'. Because he has some enthusiasm for technology and engineering, Gosling sometimes consults him for ideas for his sci-fi stories.
    \n
    \nThe Agent (WEIRD) - played by Gosling's Creator, John. The Agent lives next door to Gosling.
    \n
    \nGosling's Mother (TROUBLE) - played by Euan. A widow, Gosling's mother Cecilia is a thorn in his side, constantly undermining him but also desperate for attention. She is always comparing him unfavourably with his dead father. She is the only person who uses his first name, Thaddeus. This character partially inspired by Livia Soprano, Tony's mother.
    \n
    \nCharacters attached to Jeff
    \n
    \nSophie (TROUBLE) - Jeff's on-again/off again girlfriend, played by Euan. Younger than him, she is a free spirit, a proto-hippie. Lately she's been getting involved in some weird group called the Greater Universe something something. Her parents are rich and give them some financial support.
    \n
    \nBruce Howell (SOCIAL) - Jeff's brother, played by Paul. Bruce is a straight-laced, suit-wearing banker who lives in a nice suburban house with his wife and two adorable children. The white sheep of the family.
    \n
    \nWesley (MONEY) - Jeff's Agent. Competent, but very money-focused compared to Jeff. Always willing to sacrifice creative integrity for money, which drives Jeff nuts.
    \n
    \nGreater Universe Institute (WEIRD) - a cultish group along the lines of Scientology or the Moonies, played by Ben. They put out good vibes and vague spiritualism. Sophie's been going to their meetings recently.
    \n
    \nSince Jeff got more Author dice than Gosling he purchases some additional characters.
    \n
    \nKitty Howell (SOCIAL) - Bruce's wife, played by Richie. Kitty is as straight-laced and comfortable as her husband.
    \n
    \nHooker J. Stephens (MONEY) - Sophie's father, played by Paul. Mr Stephens is a powerful man in Californian social and political life, a patriotic and red-blooded American. Although he doesn't fully understand why Jeff would waste his money-making potential to be an author, he appreciates his rugged individualism and the stabilising effect Jeff has on his wild daughter Sophie.
    \n
    \nSecrets and Lies WEIRD
    \n
    \nWe worked out the Secrets for each NPC between us while the Authors took turns leaving the room. Since they'll be reading this AP, I won't write them here until they're revealed in play.
    \n
    \nFrom start to end of setup was close to an hour and fifteen, pizza time included.

    \nSTART OF GAME
    \n
    \nIn Left Coast, each player in turn starts a scene, going clockwise. However, most of the scenes focus on one of the two Authors.
    \n
    \nScene One - Richie's turn. Widescreen scene.
    \nA Widescreen scene is an Author's introduction, laying out the situation he finds himself in. It is opened by the Author's Creator, in this case John.
    \nJohn did quite a nice opening bit, describing Gosling sitting in his tiny flat in San Francisco. He's at his desk looking out the window at a playground. The swing squeaks as the wind blows. The ashtray beside him is piled up high.
    \nHe hears the commotion of two women yapping as they come up the stairs. Gosling swears; it's his wife and his mother.
    \nAlly and Cecilia burst into the room, partway through conversation.
    \n"Worthless man! Not like his father," says Cecilia.
    \n"Don't you knock?"
    \nCecilia lectures him about his manners towards his wife. "Your own wife can walk into a room if she wants!"
    \nThey nag him about his unfinished novel and his heavy drinking. He dodges the conversation. "I'm going out!"
    \nIgnoring their protests he rushes past them down the stairs. As he leaves the flat he sees a man across the street in a dark brown coat and a fedora. They briefly make eye contact. This should be innocuous but Gosling gets an uneasy feeling. He gets into his car and starts driving across town to a bar.
    \n
    \nScene Two - Ben's turn (Creator for Jeff). Widescreen scene.
    \nIt's early afternoon at Jeff R. Howell's ranch, in the outskirts of the city. Jeff is writing on the back porch when he hears a car drive up.
    \nIt's a young man in a bad suit who introduces himself as Pippin. Pippin tells Jeff that Sophie has been coming to some meetings of the Greater Universe Institute and has told them some things about Jeff.
    \n"From what she tells us, you have a lot of negative energy you're dealing with. You really should come to a meeting."
    \n"Not interested." It takes a lot of time for Jeff to get Pippin to leave but before he goes Pippin hands him a business card. The card is all handwritten. Pippin drives off with a friendly wave.
    \n
    \nScene Three - John's turn (Gosling's Creator).
    \nGosling arrives at the bar downtown. It's called the Lucky Strike. The barmaid, Lulu, tries to console him with a drink after his treatment from his wife and mother.
    \nIn the midst of conversation Lulu lets slip that she saw Gosling's wife drinking up with some men at another bar. Gosling refuses to believe it.
    \nGosling's best friend Bud walks in. He banters with Lulu and sits with Gosling.
    \n"Why the long face this time? Wife, or mother?"
    \n"Both."
    \nGosling is also upset that he's having serious writer's block with the story he's writing. He gets progressively drunker.
    \n
    \nScene Four - Elliot's turn (playing Jeff).
    \nSophie, played by Euan, squeals up to the ranch. She never was a careful driver. Though their on-again, off-again relationship has recently been off, they're still 'friends'. She invites him to the new Charlton Heston movie. Jeff agrees and they head out to dinner.
    \nAt the restaurant, Jeff asks about Pippin and this odd group Sophie's been hanging with lately. She tells him they've helped her a lot with her spiritual needs and that they can help him, too. She tries to persuade him to come to a meeting.
    \nThis starts a TROUBLE conflict. Elliot gets a partial success. Jeff is able to talk his way out of meeting the Greater Universe.
    \n
    \nScene Five - Paul's turn
    \nJeff returns home to find a message from Sophie's father, Hooker J. Stephens, to come to his ranch the next day. Since Sophie's parents provide some financial support (Stephens is in Jeff's MONEY quadrant) he does so.
    \nJeff arrives to find Hooker J. Stephens taking potshots at birds. Stephens and Jeff chat about Sophie and Jeff tells him he's concerned about this group she's been meeting with. Stephens worries that his daughter's falling in with 'those damn Communists.' However, he has other business to discuss.
    \nHooker J. Stephens has been heavily involved in his friend Ronald Reagan's gubernatorial campaign. Since Reagan comes from Hollywood, the campaign has come up with the idea to fund films with patriotic themes that subtly promote Reagan. Since Jeff is a writer, he can help with the scripts, but it would take time away from his novels. Jeff doesn't want to do it, but daddy Stephens is a source of cash for him and Sophie. Hmm, looks like a MONEY conflict...
    \n
    \nHere was the first major stumbling point for our playtest. Since Stephens is in Jeff's MONEY quadrant, he was brought in to start a conflict about money. However, when we got to the specifics of the money conflicts in the rules, we realised they're incredibly narrowly defined. A MONEY conflict is where an Author sits down and writes a novel which will bring him money. You can't just resolve any old conflict that happens to involve money. We had a very long discussion on how to resolve the scene - it didn't get too heated but it was very frustrating.
    \nWe initially thought that we'd categorised Hooker J. Stephens wrong, that he should be TROUBLE rather than money because he's taking Jeff away from his writing. However what we eventually decided was to have Jeff just refuse to take the propaganda gig, which meant Stephens' money would dry up and Jeff wouldn't be able to pay his rent. He would then have to sit and hack out a quick short story that could be easily sold. Jeff could then start the real MONEY conflict.
    \n
    \nScene Five, continued - Jeff writes a short story
    \nMoney conflicts are quite detailed and involve planning out a story's characters and themes. Note this is not the story of the game, it's a story-within-a-story that the Author character writes. The players then take the role of characters in the story and act it out.
    \nElliot decides to have Jeff write a hard sci-fi story set on a space station in a society where religion has been outlawed. The two protagonists are based on his brother Bruce and his wife Kitty. As people of faith, they are on the run from an oppressive government.
    \nThe story is influenced by themes of McCarthyism, Communist societies and the Hollywood blacklists. Obviously Jeff is bringing in things from his personal life as well.
    \n
    \nUnfortunately at this point we were well over time and we had to finish the session with the story only just started. We have had a second session that continues the story, I will post the AP soon. Myself and the other players will likely have some more detailed thoughts but here are a few brief thoughts on this session:
    \n
    \n- Character creation was really fun. Everyone really got into it and we made some interesting relationships.
    \n- I was really concerned that the Secrets for the NPCs were a bad idea, however in play I think they worked fine. Having the two Authors take turns to leave the room while everyone else discussed the Secrets kept them from being too wacky or repetitive. After the second session only a few Secrets have been revealed so we've yet to fully explore how they'll go in play.
    \n- With six players, I was also concerned the game wouldn't work to keep everyone engaged, but in the first session we seemed to do okay. Second session was different unfortunately but we'll go into that later.
    \n- Only five of us had a turn; Euan missed out. He seemed okay with it but the scenes in general took a long time.
    \n- Most of the scenes just sort of meandered along without getting much of a conflict going. The final scene started with a very clear conflict in mind but it turned out to be the wrong conflict for the rules.
    \n- The games aim for a 'laid back' vibe where the players play out a scene and play out conflicts where they find them, but my initial impression is that doesn't fit a setup where each NPC fits a clearly defined conflict category, and the conflicts are narrowly defined and specific to each category.
    \n
    \nIt is a very interesting game and we definitely enoyed some aspects to it. I hope this AP has been useful.

    \n
    \nScene Five - Jeff's story continues
    \nJeff has now named his two main characters Bryce and Kathy (no relation to his brother and sister-in-law Bruce and Kitty). He has also given a name to the evil overlord figure who's hunting them down, along with all other religious people: Booker, not at all inspired by his father-in-law Hooker J. Stephens.

    \nIt has to be said we got a little out of control. Jeff started writing a hard sci-fi and ended up with a somewhat silly space opera. Choice quote: "Don't you hate it when your characters come alive on the page?"
    \nElliot now rolls dice and Jeff succeeds in writing his story. He gets a money point. He calls the story "Unicorns of the Sun."
    \n
    \nScene Six - Elliot's turn. Sidekick scene.
    \nA sidekick scene is where the two Authors get a chance to meet.
    \nGosling is feverishly writing in one of the city's biggest and best public libraries, where a lot of authors hang out. Jeff comes in and they chat about Gosling's difficulties with his novel. They are shushed by the Librarian (played by Paul) who is not, as someone suggested, an orangutan. She's a stern older lady who nevertheless is very helpful in obtaining rare books.
    \nJohn the Creator intervenes with some Weirdness and Gosling sees the man in the brown coat and fedora watching him. He freaks out and runs for the door. However, he's taken one of the Library's rare books with him. The Librarian shouts at Eric (Euan), the burly security guard, and the big man casually puts out his huge arm to clothesline Gosling.
    \nGosling (Richie) rolls a success. He gets an Author dice.
    \n
    \nScene Seven - Richie's turn. Widescreen scene.
    \nRichie nominates Ben to narrate. Gosling is running from the Library, and passes a dark alleyway. A dark figure pounces out and accosts him.
    \nThe man, played by John, is dishevelled and crazed. He shakes Gosling by the shoulders. He tells him he escaped from some shadowy agency and that they're now after Gosling.
    \nGosling is skeptical. Richie decides to play it as a conflict, ie. roll to decide if Gosling is convinced by the man or not. He gets two successes, but rather than have Gosling succeed and therefore walk away from the man, he spends one on getting the Creator (John) to reveal a Secret.
    \nGosling now believes that he's being chased by the same agency that this strange man describes, but he also learns something shocking.
    \n"They're onto you, Gosling. Have been for a long time. It's your wife, man!"
    \n"Ally? That can't be true!"
    \n"Fraid so, she's been informing on you. You can never return home."
    \nRichie spends his second success on Narrating A Fact about his Author. He decides that Gosling is now part of an underground resistance group who think there is a government mind control conspiracy.
    \n
    \nWe discuss what to call the group. Someone suggests the Pink Panthers, inspired by the Black Panthers. Euan starts singing the Pink Panther theme, but it's not the tune we all know and love. He claims it is from a TV show and it goes "Have you ever seen a panther that is pink?" None of us believe this is true but we all think it is hilarious and we spend about five minutes grilling Euan about it. "Even if that theme does exist, why would you hum that and not the incredibly famous iconic Mancini version?"
    \n

    \nJeff's been invited to Bruce and Kitty's house in the suburbs. Bruce (Paul) offers him a beer and they chat but Kitty and the children are noticeably absent. Eventually Jeff gets Bruce to spill that Kitty is very upset with Jeff. She had heard from a friend in her sewing circle that Jeff had written an unflattering sci-fi story with a character obviously based on her.
    \nKitty (Richie) returns home; she'd forgotten her coat, and she's clearly still upset. Jeff starts to talk to her like an understanding human being but Paul reminds Elliot that the game is set in the early 1960s.
    \n"Kitty, honey, you're being irrational. Why don't you have a lie down and let us men work this out?"
    \nKitty, Jeff and Bruce continue their argument and there is a Conflict that Elliot wins. Bruce, annoyed that Kitty won't play the obedient wife, storms out, leaving Jeff and Kitty alone.
    \nElliot spent his success on getting Kitty to reveal her Secret. Kitty is upset not because Jeff wrote a story about her, but because she was hurt to think he views her in a negative light.
    \nKitty is in love with Jeff, and has been for a long time.

    \n Some notes on the second session:
    \n
    \n- Some of the issues we had were not the game's fault and can and do happen with any session. Two of us were very late due to public transport problems, everyone had been busy and hadn't read the rules since last week, we'd misplaced the character sheets and relationship maps (lucky I had my session notes) and there was just generally more cross-talking and fidgeting going on. The vibe was just a bit off.
    \n- Only three and a half turns this time.
    \n- We were in a different venue and seated in a different order to last time. This meant we again skipped Euan without realising.
    \n- Scenes this session mostly involved just two or three characters and there was a lot of sitting on the sidelines, especially for John and Euan. While the rules do encourage people to jump in and contribute extra characters sometimes it feels hard to do so without stepping on a good established scene.
    \n- The 'wrong' Pink Panther song was the funniest thing I'd heard in our sessions since the time we all got chased around a forest inside a caterpillar by a demon octopus in Stormbringer (Hi, Russell!)
    \n- We seemed to have a slightly better handle on getting good Conflicts and I liked the way Richie and Elliot could spend their successes on having their Authors fail but gain a Secret instead.

    \n I wanted to clarify something: from looking at the Jeff vs. Stephens conflict, it sounds like I need to be a lot clearer about when Money scenes happen. Is it fair to say your issues with Money scenes were about when they happen (rather than about what to do in a Money scene)?

    I'll try and clarify what the rules currently are, but I also want to acknowledge the confusion you had (and that I'll have to think more deeply about whether I can make the rules more intuitive).

    At the moment, Money scenes are pretty damn specific. They happen either (a) when the Author\u2019s player starts a scene that shows the Author writing a story, or (b) when the Author is in a conflict that needs money to resolve.

    That means it doesn\u2019t matter which quadrant an NPC is in:
    - an NPC from the Money quadrant doesn't necessarily trigger a Money conflict (eg. your agent could be be arguing you need to come to a networking event, when you really need to spend time with your emotionally-distressed son) (Jeff vs Stephens fits into this category)
    - any NPC from any quadrant can trigger a Money conflict (eg. your husband can tell you that you're all going to get evicted if you can't make this week's rent)

    So why are the quadrants there, then? I'm using them as creative prompts to help the group design the setting (and to indicate what types of conflicts are likely in this genre), and I'm using them to help define which reset scenes are used.

    (Tangent alert: Here's a little bit of history that's outside this discussion but could help explain your confusion. Originally, authors used to have four different ratings (one for each quadrant) instead of a single pool of Author dice. That meant there could be four different types of conflicts. I moved beyond that during my redesign, to simplify the game.)


    TL,DR: If a conflict involves anything other than needing money to resolve your troubles, you just roll the Author\u2019s Dice (regardless of what quadrant the NPC is in).


    I'm also aware that I can get quite detail-focused when I'm looking for feedback. And I know the list of feedback-questions in the rules is pretty extensive. If it helps, basically what I need to know is this:
    - What did you like?
    - What did you not like
    - What confused you?

    Any answers to any of those three questions are going to help me enormously.

    At the moment, though, your feedback is making me quite confident about some facets of the game, and it's pointing out two parts I definitely need to work on (starting scenes and Money scenes).
