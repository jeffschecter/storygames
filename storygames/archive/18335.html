\n I've been playing Apocalypse World for the past year now (I love it) and I want to provide my players with a harsh dead landscape to painfully digest. I prefer short descriptions of brutal environs over lengthy prose, but sometimes I
find it hard to find the words to barf forth something ugly as it is beautiful. I'd like to hear how you paint your verbal canvas of destruction. Maybe we can inspire one another.

Example...

Your boots pummel against broken unmorred asphalt, your ache labored body dodging past ashened debris. You can hear the booming belch of Shit Head's double barrel shot gun, the whine of buck shot over head.. What do you do?

    \n I am always partial to a less... Mad max apocalypse. I generally run in a wintery setting.

    Your tires whisper and cough against the scarred asphault as your convoy makes it's way to Rest Stop. You can see it vaguely through the cold mountian fog. The paths in and out are barred by large make shift wooden gates, tree trunks cut to two man height and helt together by rippled metal plates. The walls of the Holding built from the nearby lumber sources.

    As you near the gate, over the rattle of your caravan jostling from the frost heves, you can just barely hear hollered voices from the watchtowers on either sides of the gate. There are men there training their weapons on you as you and your gang approach on your choppers. What do you do?

    \n I like to front-load it a bit, look through threads on Apocalypse World forums, Google Image search, Flickr search... just about any horrible thing you can think of really exists somewhere and you'll get much more detail and inspiration from the photos than you could believe. There's that dead city in China, the aircraft boneyard in Arizona, the plastic patch in the mid-Pacific...
    Almost forgot, I like to make a document with descriptions, locations, bits of business... stuff that you can then throw out when the time comes to use it. I find that having some things prepared makes even improvised GM'ing much better, because you can leverage the material you already prepared.

    \n I like to describe dead places by evoking what's been lost. So I litter them with traces of nice things. A few marble steps suitable for a palace, leading to a chasm. Or a swing set, with the chains nearly rusted through, except for one, which has completely collapsed, leaving its swing hanging awkwardly. If your scarcity is food and water, evoke those with shambles of restaurants. If your setting is plagued by disease, show us the ruins of a hospital. Or if natural splendor is more your thing, throw in some splintered redwoods, befouled streams, and animal corpses.

    \n Just have The Road next to you as you play and randomly read a bit of prose (either to yourself or out loud for instance apocalyptica).

    With the first gray light he rose and left the boy sleeping and walked out to the road and squatted and studied the country to the south. Barren, silent, godless.

    Nights dark beyond darkness and the days more gray each one than what had gone before. Like the onset of some cold glaucoma dimming away the world.

    The road was empty. Below in the little valley the still gray serpentine of a river. Motionless and precise. Along the shore a burden of dead reeds
    On the far side of the river valley the road passed through a stark black burn. Charred and limbless trunks of trees stretching away on every side. Ash moving over the road and the sagging hands of blind wire strung from the blackened lightpoles whining thinly in the wind. A burned house in a clearing and beyond that a reach of meadowlands stark and gray and a raw red mudbank where a roadworks lay abandoned. Farther along were billboards advertising motels. Everything as it once had been save faded and weathered.

    Its essential reading.

    \n You've probably heard the (author's) rule that you should show, not tell, but this principle seems left out of most roleplaying game's 'rules for play' for players to vitalise their narration. I think this is an oft left out skill for your toolbox. Show, not tell.

    The difference between the two needs defining: 'telling' is the reliance on simple exposition...
    Rolfball is an old man
    'Showing,' conversely, is the use of evocative prose, not necessarily wordy exposition, but evocative of your setting / characters / scene / intent.
    Rolfball shuffles slowly toward you, his angry, broken form consoled by an achingly dark heartwood cane, gripped by swollen-jointed hands; translucent and liver-spotted..

    Both showing and telling convey the same information \u2014 Rolfball is old \u2014 but the first bit of description simply states it, whereas the latter never actually states that Rolfball is ancient at all, and yet nonetheless tells the group that he is 'old' and ends up opening all sorts of provacative questions for the game.

    Why is 'showing' with your descriptions better? First, you are fueling mental imagery for your roleplaying friends. Second, showing is interactive and participatory: it forces the group to become involved in the story, inferring facts (such as Rolfball's age, status and intent) rather than just taking your narration in passively.

    So my hints for barfing forth would be to show the scarcity, in all that you narrate, evoke the paradigm that there is no status quo in Apocalypse World by showing your players rather than just telling them.

    \n Generally speaking, "show, don't tell" is even important in RPGs than it is in other media, I feel.

    Rather than focusing on purple prose, I'd recommend looking for highlighting post-apocalyptic details with character action. What happens when [some typical everyday occurence] takes place? How do people react in a way which shows us how much the world has changed?

    Someone ran an AW game where the apocalypse was caused by some kind of plague. In that world, people might be carefully segregated into the "clean" and the "unclean", forced to sit downwind from one another, work together but sleep in different sides of the river, and so on.

    And whatever you come up with, emphasize it only if a) the players find it interesting and investigate further, or b) it applies directly to a character's goals or one of your Fronts.

    Edit: I also think it's a good idea to take a cue from Vincent's rather spare sprinkling of imagery in the book itself. There is no careful description: just a scarcity of important details. E.g. "Armour made out of old Stop signs riveted together." "Feral dogs with bone eyelids that go click-click-click." etc.
