\n Reading another post (in the "Where Are We Now" thread (title guessed, I'm lazy), someone mentioned that design could now look to make games that don't emulate or attempt to engender the effects of other media. It only just occurred to me (in the magic hours of an all-nighter) that I'm not sure if that is possible.

I suspect that one of the cores of "game qua game" is an exploration of the existent, a simulation, "corner case" experimentation, a case study, and the like. We (well, y'all) make "story games" which seems to imply an adherence to narrative (NO! NO GNS!!! The original meaning!), building towards theme, cooperating on a final product (be that as it may)--something with a product that could parallel the products of every other art.

I'd like to invite some brainstorming on what a game without emulation of other media would "look like". Is there a space outside of the Venn diagram of movie, novel, parable, fable (painting, music, sculpture, economics, logic, math) that gaming could populate? I've seen games described as "jam sessions" and "cooperative writing"; I've seen games that seem to value the artifact more than the engagement (think minis); I've seen games built around resource management challenges. What's outside of these media? Can gaming find a place to stand that can not be described as "well, it's like ____, but you do ____"? Sculpture can not be so-described; it has what one might call a "non-referential definition".

If it's any consolation... I got nothin' either. I suspect that gaming (in every sense of the term) is a second-order (derivative, reactionary, combinatory, analytic) activity. I do not say that to be pejorative, but merely because I've been a game theorist (by avocation) most of my life, and I can't even come up with one example to start the ball rolling.

    \n I don't see this overwhelming tendency towards emulation, myself - sure, there are many games that essentially draw their structural inspiration from prior models, but then there is also a strong tradition of games starting with Dungeons & Dragons that have nothing whatsoever to do with other narrative forms.

    This isn't even anything correlating with "story games", as it is quite possible to make dramatic and plot-driven games that do not conform to other storytelling modes. Practically any story game predicated on adventure gaming models of play remains fundamentally unable to map itself onto movies or novels, simply because the formats are not the same. Sorcerer is a simple example, or take Shadow of Yesterday for even a more striking one: one just might mention movie logic in a TSoY rules-text when justifying dramatic coordination or other features that this style of game shares with other forms of drama, but the dramatic structure itself is something that only coincidentally resembles pre-existing forms. Sure, one can postulate a literary form that produces structurally similar stories (some kind of ensemble cast slice of life periodical from the 18th century, maybe?), but the truth is that such resemblance is incidental, not derivative: the dramatic model of adventure roleplaying originates in the social and practical necessities of traditional roleplaying with its adventurer parties, weekly sessions, player-character correspondence and other such unique features, and not in simply copying other arts as you claim. Whatever commonalities there might be are no more than the commonalities between literature and cinema.

    To emphasize, I do think that there are many games that get their essential inspiration, dramatic forms and even procedural frameworks from other arts. These range from the obvious (like Primetime Adventures) to ones that have organically embedded these assumptions into their structures (Spirit of the Century, say). But games built like this are by no means everything out there.

    \nArt already encompasses the width and breadth of humanity. The human story has already been told so many times and so many ways that there really isn't anything new to tell. That isn't to say we shouldn't try, or that telling these stories again is bad in any way. All art is derivative of reality (and how we view reality can be derivative of art).
    \n
    \nIf instead you look at the mode and method used to tell these stories or the experience of participating in these stories gaming is already it's own unique story form. Unlike TV, novels, music or theater, the only audience is the actors, the only actors the audience. It's a performance that isn't intended to be viewed by others. Though it certainly be.
    \n
    \nWe use language to describe things and the power of language is the ability to construct and deconstruct symbols. We describe gaming experiences like other things because we know they are a shared experience, so we use them as a symbol to convey our meaning. That doesn't necessarily make gaming derivative from other forms (any more than all art is derivative of what came before), it's just us using language to convey our experiences to other people.
    \n
    \nGaming is still relatively young, I very much believe that someone will come along at some point and show us a completely revolutionary way to think about the experience or apply it to something no one even knew existed. Until then, I think it's more than okay to borrow from various art forms to find new ways to tell stories. Borrowing and telling stories are two central themes to the human experience after all.
    \n
    \nFor example, I think a potentially interesting concept would be to borrow from Epic Theater. The idea is to create a process that distances the audience from the action to allow for greater objectivity and contemplation. Purposely inserting elements that break the illusion. It might be too delicate a balance to try to maintain, encouraging roleplaying while also drawing the player away from it. Some of these concepts are already present in games, or used by groups, but a game designed to call them out and why could be interesting.

    \n To answer your question, I think its possible just a lot harder. I think games are their own thing as a type of medium whereas, games can also be translated and expressed by borrowing metaphors from other media. Moreover, I would say that this is true of all media. Particular media (books, video games, movies, photography, &c) all have particular untranslatable, non-adequate and singular qualities, yet we can only talk about this qualities using metaphors from other media. That is to say, when I talk about photography, picture and film don't come out of my mouth. So, to me, emulation in games (trying to be like a TV serial is a common one) is the same as dancing about architecture or writing about music. Often a fun, productive and creative project but ultimately when you dancing or write you're translating buildings and sounds from one media to another.

    So, in this sense, it's impossible for RPGs to be like other media but also impossible for RPGs to be expressed in terms that aren't always already metaphors from other media. No matter how hard you try, your Buffy RPG is never going to capture the feeling, tone and style of a Buffy episode. But also, when you're designing or playing a game, the way you interpret the narrative, characters and so on, is always going be with a conscious or unconscious reference to all those Buffy episodes you watched.

    In a more practical sense, this came up in a design thread over on Barf Forth Apocalyptica. I was musing about XP mechanics and I was asked what genre my hack was trying to emulate, as there are a number (almost all of them) AW hacks that are geared towards other media, genre or other RPG titles. There's two hacks about just supernatural TV serial dramas (Monster of the Week and Monsterhearts). I didn't have any answer. None? I mean, it's influenced by the all the stuff I like: Aeon Flux, Crystal Castles, William Gibson, Jean Baudrillard, Liquid Sky, Fashion and War photography, &c. So, it's less a project of emulation than bricolage.

    Which is just what I mean: RPGs exist with reference to other genres, media, &c as the basis of the their intelligibility but even RPGs that are deisgned to 'emulate' are not reducible to those genres.

    Moreover, I feel like RPGs already are their own genre. MMORPGs borrow so, so, so heavily from D&D et al throughout their history. MMORPGs are translations of TTRPGs and it shows. None the less, MMORPGs are their own animal, species even.

    Can we design towards what is unique about RPGs? Yes. And I agree that it's a conceptual space little explored. Should be explored.

    I don't by the sculpture non-referential definition even a little: it's like painting but in three dimensions.

    \n First of all, I think there is way too much smushing of 2 distinct terms here, genre and medium. Genre and medium are extremely distinct concepts.

    Genre is a set of shared tropes. Those tropes can be repeated across multiple media. Sharing those tropes does not make the media the same thing.

    The Lord of the Rings books are not the same thing and the Lord of the Rings movies, even though they were trying to tell essentially the same story (Tolkein hyper-enthusiasts please humor me for a moment here). Likewise, paintings, board games, TTRPGs, LARPs, and many other media have been used to create artifacts using the same tropes and "creating the same stories" to some extent or other.

    But each of those artifacts created in a different media is a different thing.

    Are movies just derivatives of novels, because they use similar narrative structures? Far from it.

    That being said, I do agree that design could focus on emphasizing the differences. Emphasizing the things that the medium does better than others.

    With larp, I already know what that is. Larp does simultaneous action better than any other media does. I wrote a paper about it last year. http://www.mortalisrpg.com/forum/downloads/2010SummitBook.pdf

    With tabletop rpg, I think that the shared narrative creation that, essentially, led to this forum being called story-games.com in the first place, is already what rpgs do best. They facilitate the shared creation of new narratives better than any other medium. Improvisational acting comes close, but I think the focus on entertaining an audience effectively limits it - the medium is almost never used outside of comedy, while rpgs can effectively work with other modes quite easily.

    I think perhaps, David, you are trying to describe the forest while, for no particular reason, refusing to use the word "trees".

    With tabletop rpg, I think that the shared narrative creation that, essentially, led to this forum being called story-games.com in the first place, is already what rpgs do best. They facilitate the shared creation of new narratives better than any other medium.
    I think you stray a bit back into the shallow waters, here, though. RPGs add a system layer to what is an ancient artform: the story circle. Hell, two guys telling a fish story by the fire do essentially the same thing, just without dice (no one would believe they lost a 2d6 pound bass!).
    Multiple people telling a story together about something that actually happened is ancient practice, yes. It's a fish story.

    Multiple people telling a story together about something that DIDN'T happen is something else. It's something that uses structures to facilitate it, because you don't have the structure of WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENED to facilitate the process.

    When you do it for an audience, it's improv theatre. Which has structures to do it well. Like "yes, and" practices. An improv group also uses things like stock characters, lines, and situations that take advantage of the fact that their audience generally changes often - allowing to fake some of the actual improvisation and, essentially, cheat a bit.

    When you do it for a small group sitting around the table where each person is both creator and audience, it's tabletop roleplaying. Unsurprisingly, some of the improv techniques work well, like "yes, and" rules. However, the reusing of characters, lines, and situations works less well, because it's an internal performance with the same audience.

    When you do it for a group that's standing up and moving around and embodying their characters more, it's larp. When you larp with a small group, some of the techniques that work well in tabletop still work well. But as the group size increases, the experience splinters more and more and the differences in the medium are accentuated.

    All three of those media are different from the ancient practice of group storytelling, because they are structured by systems/practices other than the telling of something that actually happened (which is the thing that makes otherwise unstructured storytelling work).

    And I would argue that the ancient art of fictional storytelling involves author-driven media:
    - Single voice storytelling, which is functionally nearly identical to modern story authoring, with just a few changes based on the needs of performance and memorization.
    - Drama (plays), which is driven by the authoring of a text beforehand, which is then interpreted during performance.

    All of these are different media. But all of them are related in a lot of ways, and can benefit a lot by borrowing from each other.

    So what's different about RPGs? What are the most hugely important elements of that medium?

    The different ways you can structure interactions between the participants at the table during the event. Because it's that structured interaction that's the most indicitave thing about the medium.

    I think recognizing that is the reason behind the indie game movement/revolution thingy. I think that is the primary thing that developers have been focusing on more and more for the past decade or so.

    So I would argue that, even when a game is heavily borrowing in regards to genre, if it's plowing new ground when it comes to structuring the interaction between participants, then it isn't mired in the limitations of emulation.

    Apocalypse World is an excellent example. It draws heavily on the post apoc genre. But it structures player interactions in new and exciting ways, which is why folks are so excited about it.

    Smallville did similar good work. It draws heavily on the superhero genre. It also is informed heavily by the style of TV show that Smallville is - super heavy on relationships and secrets and such. But the game didn't just say "here's some generic task resolution mechanics and some stats for Clark, now go." Instead, they created a massively fascinating system that used relationships as stats, which hugely changes the way the players think about the interactions between characters. Suddenly you have superhero characters who are strengthened and weakened by drama and feelings rather than by physical prowess and kryptonite. Being able to create a storytelling game where that sort of thing makes sense was a mindblowing challenge that the creators tackled exceptionally well by focusing, not on how to model physics in an accurate but abstracted way, but on how to drive player interactions in a way that would end up with a result that would emphasize the soap opera elements over the super powers.

    \n To return to the original question - is it possible to create an RPG that creates an output that doesn't resemble other media?

    The answer depends entirely on what scope you are willing to look at.

    If you look at too wide of a scope, I think it's totally impossible, because there are too many other media that are too much related.

    Likewise, you can't create a film that doesn't resemble something that could be done with another media.
    - You could take photos of the same things that are in the film and put them next to each other in a book or a studio. If you then added text that related to what was in the movie, you would have something that resembled the movie.
    - You could have people on stage acting out the same things that happened in the movie. You would have something that resembled the movie.
    - You could write a book about the movie. It would resemble the movie.

    Would there be differences? Sure. But you would be able to draw strong parallels between the two.

    So looking at it from that perspective - no, you can't create an RPG that doesn't in any way resemble what you could (or what others already have) created with other media.

    But that doesn't mean you can't emphasize and highlight the differences.

    Thing is, as I said, I think this is being done. I think it's being done a lot and done well. I think a lot more good work is happening in this area than is happening with larp, where I don't think enough work has been done on even identifying what the media does well. (And I agree that the use of "you can do what you can do" is another thing larp can excel at, though I think sometimes that becomes an empty void rather than a fruitful void).

    Is there more to be done on this front - sure!

    \n Here's something TTRPGs may possibly do better than any other media:

    They expand to the scope of the participant's imagination better than any other media, because the only real limitation is the imagination of the other participants.

    Sure, when you write a novel, you can decide what happens. You can go anywhere with it. But when a reader reads it, it always turns out essentially the same way (though it's perhaps a bit different if the reader is in a different mindset - and even more differences are possible if it's a choose-your-own-adventure story).

    This is one of the things that, for example, TTRPGs are much better at than larp. Larp, depending on how you do it, has a lot more limitations about where you go with it (and the more it overcomes those limitations, the more it starts to resemble other media). Having your characters who are hanging out on a farm suddenly appear on the doorstep of the castle because that's where one of the characters wanted to go is much harder in larp than it is in TTRPGs, for example.

    Sure, you can do that sort of quick jump in a movie, but again, when you do it in that media it is responding to the sole creator's imagination, rather than the participant's.

    And you can do that in a video game. But only if the castle is part of the content in the game. If the creators haven't put it there, then you can't go.

    One particular technique example of this is aggressive scene framing. Yes, even the name of that technique is drawn from other media. But I think the way it happens in TTRPGs is different than how it happens in a movie.

    In a TTRPG, it's like you're combining the responsive, dynamic creativity of improv acting with powerful director-driven storytelling techniques of film, sprinkled with the immersiveness of larp characterization, and putting the three together in a way that none of the related media does as well.

    Also, you can bash orcs and take their gold. Which is fun.
