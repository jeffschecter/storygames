    \n Hmm...that is a good question. The book isn't a lightweight, but crunch-wise, it revolves around a fairly simple core of rules. I would say the complexity is somewhere around that of Dungeon World. We were able to use them to create a wide variety of characters and different Traits and equipment for those characters. Most of play is freeform with the rules only kicking in when there is a conflict. Conflicts are technically abstract and used to determine which player is currently in control of the flow of the story.

    A majority of the book consists of source material that you can use for your own games.

    I really want to play this game. I had a long conversation with Ralph about it a few years ago, and the style of competition he described sounded unique to me among RPGs.

    Keith, what skills would you say it rewards? System mastery? Reading the other players and bluffing? Strategic management of resources (possibly including things like turns and conflicts)?
    \nA little of all of those. The primary goals are to accomplish your characters' goals for the Ordeal while retaining control of the narrative as the Chronicler. The Chronicler position is the 'football' that you need to have in order to get anything significant done. In order to get the position, you need to have your characters clash with the others in order to get control.

    \n The way you build the character seems to provide the most mechanical effectiveness. Always take advantage of one of the types of sorcery in the game. Story-wise, all of it is nasty except for Blessed magic. I had the honor of creating the first Hero in the game. I built Andru along the lines of an anime wandering swordsman. I made him using almost no sorcery, and Ralph's Iron Lord handed me my head (figuratively.)

    I had the opportunity later to redesign Andru for use later in the saga. This time, I gave him a rune-forged sword and scabbard. In Blood Red Sands, Runes are extremely distasteful since their creation involves torturing someone to death and using the position they died in as the Rune. I had conceived of Andru as being a good character, but really needed that magic. So I compromised. He created the sword and scabbard from the souls of his former lover and the best friend that she had cheated on him with. It was nasty, but that act of passion completely fit the character.

    \n There's basically no "good" magic in the setting, or in the rules (well, there's one kind, but that's not necessarily what Keith's character needed to be more effective).

    It's competitive because there's scoring and a ladder and you only get to be the main character by climbing higher than other players. Looks like the sort of competition you see in so-called reality TV: you fight to stay in the game, but every round someone has to go.

    \n The competition occurs on two levels. The first is during the ordeal. Each of the players are assigned goals that your are to have your characters fulfill in order to win the Ordeal. The goals are often at cross-purposes. The second level is the full long play of the game. You gain victory points in order to determine if your hero will be one to combat the witch king in the final Ordeal.

    During the playtest, how the character was constructed seemed to be what made them most effective. Ralph struggled somewhat to make the sorceries effective, but not so that one would dominate the others.

    As to my choice of magic for Andru, the only good magic is Blessed, and it didn't seem to fit him at the time.
