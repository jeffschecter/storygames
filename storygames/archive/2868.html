\n What follows is one of several posts in which I will attempt to convey some of the guildlines surrounding the design of adult learning. The thread collecting links for all of these posts is here. The reason for this is fairly straightforward: while not everyone agrees with this assessment, it is my belief that most RPG rulebooks are essentially teaching texts and/or workbooks or unconsciously modeled after them*. In short, an RPG rulebook's essential task is to teach the game to the person reading it.

As such, being aware of some of the rules of instruction and learning can help you deliver your game into the minds of the players as clearly and painlessly as possible.

(* - With a few notable exceptions like Nobilis, which are ((purposely)) more like a coffeetable book full of surreal short fiction pieces, with RPG rules jotted in the margins.)

What follows is a simple numbered lists of some rules to observe for Adult Learning (read: your target audience are no longer in a regular classroom environment). I'm simply going to emphasize the key bits in each point and leave this post fairly simple. If folks want to discuss how to apply a particular rule to a game's design, ask away!

During the process of learning, the learners should work harder than the person teaching them the material.

1. Adults bring considerable experience with them. Therefore, they wish to speak, participate, and contribute to the proceedings. They dislike long stretches of one-way communication.

2. Adults have something to lose. They have a strong need to maintain their personal self-esteem. Therefore, they should be listened to and we should set up the material so they will be successful at learning the tasks we're setting them.

3. Adults want tasks that focus on current situations and tasks, rather than material that may be useful in the distant future. They desire a strong "how-to" and "applicability" focus. They become restless and disengage if they feel their time is being wasted. Further, an adult will "lose" up to 90% of their \xe2\u20ac\u0153trained and gained\xe2\u20ac\x9d knowledge if it isn't used regularly (within 30 days of either first-training or the last use of the skill).

4. Adults see learning as a means to an end, rather than an end to itself. They must know what there is to gain from a piece of information or mechanic, and they must see progress being made in their understanding and practice.

5. Adults are accustomed to being active. They should be given an opportunity for active participation whenever possible.

6. Adults are accustomed to being self-directed. A group leader (whoever that ends up being, either the GM, another player, or the text) must consult and work with adult learners rather than be too directive.

This one's short, and doesn't have a lot of examples from current games, but I wanted to get it out here for discussion, since it's low-hanging fruit for me in terms of getting it posted.

    \n Workbook pages, but without the little lines to write on. \n
    Something like:Characters often want very abstract things but you as a GM can only manipulate concrete stuff. Your job is to find concrete things that can anchor the character's abstract desires. A young noble's aspirations for his family name could be embodied in an heirloom sword, for instance. Here's a little exercise to do. Think of one of the characters in the game. Pick one thing that the character wants, and then think of an object that is closely associated with that one thing. How can you make it complicated for the character to have that one thing? Do this for each character, and you have half of a game situation right there.
    \nThe key is to make it a short, concise demonstration of a skill (or trick) that can be repeated later in other contexts.

    Alternately, you can totally set up a book with the expectation that players and GMs will use little notebooks for writing things down as they read. Think self-help books.
