The Love-Hate Syndrome

shreyas
May 2006 edited May 2006 in Story Games

So like I've been thinking about a certain behavior pattern I've been observing with gamers. It's pretty visible in forums like RPGnet, and I spent a few years watching it at my college gaming club, too.

Namely, there are games we like a lot - like my friend J and old D&D (I have forgotten the edition), or myself and Weapons of the Gods, or Jonathan and Nobilis.

But of course, there are parts we don't like, and react to really negatively. Almost violently. Like, every time I hit a mention of "Kung Fu Marvels" in WotG I have to stop myself from closing the book in disgust. I've come close to crossing bits out of the book and pasting in Post-its with less distasteful text. (Jon, I'm totally curious about your gripes, we should discuss them some time.)

What's up with that?

Somehow it seems diffused in the design community, though.

What's up with that?

    In my head, I just imagine that the designer ran out of steam. I see them working and working, and then they just can't quite go anymore, but they still feel driven to address one last issue. So, they wave their hands vigorously as to how they might, kinda sorta, start to address it with the mechanics so far and call it a day.

    Somehow it seems diffused in the design community, though.

    I suspect because that people who actually produce stuff are more keenly aware of the 'real' sweat that goes into a project, as well as the temptations and necessities that demand somethings just not quite be done as well. And, of course, because designers see gaps in design as challenges that they can solve. When you don't have a fix-it mentality, a hole is just something that lets all the rain in.

    Let me point out, however, that I've also seen this syndrome with people who are absolutely, 100% totally sure that they know what a game is trying to do ... and they are absolutely, 100% wrong.

    Which probably has something to do with the designer-user conflict--as a user, we just want the game to do what we want. As a designer, we are willing to concede that we might need to make the system that does what we want.

    I suspect that game hacking is so common among gamers because of the frustration with what the rules don't do, of how they fail to meet the expectations we (rightly or wrongly) put upon them.
