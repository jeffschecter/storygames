\n I've been thinking about electronics and role-playing lately, what with a player at a recent game who was twittering, and Graham's thread about laptops. The issues there revolve around distractions at the table, engaging players, and useful tools for complex mechanics. And that has lead my mind to...

Gaming online. I don't do it and I don't want to do it. One of the joys of playing these games and sharing these stories with people is being able to sit across from a real person and interact with them. They get to see me laugh and cry and be wowed and I get to see the same from them. Then as a bonus, we get to check up on each other outside of the game. "How are you doing? How did your job interview go? I know someone who can help you out! You should come by for stew later. Yeah, lets go to the farmer's market on sunday." ETC... This is a good thing for humans to do, and I believe that it strengthens our communities (not just the gaming ones, but the ones we live in) and improves our social skills.

I realize that a lot of people seem to have no other option but to play rpgs over Skype or Google Wave or whatever because they don't live near gamers or are far from them. Is this true? I guess that I'm lucky, as I live in Oakland and there's lots of people to game with here. But is there really no one to game with in our small towns and in the more rural areas? Isn't this a great opportunity to introduce people to gaming? We don't really need existing gamers to game with, necessarily. We can always show the people around us how cool this stuff is and see if it takes. Yeah, it's more work, but it's also more rewarding I think.

I think I had more to say, but I can't think of it right now.

    \n I live in Louisville and there are plenty of roleplayers around to sit at a table with and have fun. I even occasionally DO sit at a table and have fun with friends here in Louisville. Face-to-face is still my preferred way to roleplay.

    But I roleplay most of the time these days over Skype. Not because I have few local options, choices, or compatible players with whom I enjoy socializing. I play most of the time over Skype because I WANT to roleplay with Rich Rogers and guys like Daniel Perez, Judd Karlman, Ryan Macklin and Ogre Whiteside.

    We experience many of the less-than-desirable side-effects described above. Scheduling snafus, bad connections, missing visual cues, etc. But I'd still rather put up with that stuff than to not play (and record podcasts) with those guys.

    Plus, in my experience, I find that with practice the side-effects become manageable*, and it isn't any more of an impediment for me than the process of gelling with a new group face-to-face, or learning a new game, or adjusting to the social issues that go along with all that.

    So to sum up, I do it because I want to. Because it's the way to play with the people I want to play with. In a perfect world, we'd all be able to get together face-to-face every week. But we can't, and online play is a pretty good alternative. For me.

    *except for the scheduling thing - it's harder to be accountable to one another when it's Skype, for some reason, and that never seems to improve much.

    \n I've done my share of both tabletop and online roleplaying, and when it's good, online roleplaying is fun in a way that face-to-face roleplaying just isn't: it's easier to imagine the faces of characters than when you're looking at players, describing small actions sounds less silly than pantomime looks. If you have to run to deal with something while two other people are talking, you don't miss anything, and can respond as if you were in the room if you're a quick reader: all the little life interruptions break up the narrative in real life, and you have to keep pausing or repeating/summing up for people.

    When it's good, face-to-face roleplaying is fun in a way that online roleplaying just isn't: you get there a little early, have tea and snacks, chat about your day. You sit somewhere more comfortable than a computer chair, like a couch or the floor. I get to rest my head on my fiance's shoulder (if he's playing). I get to look at some of my favorite people while we do something fun together. There's less pressure to say something exactly the right way. You don't have to worry about whether everyone's speaking in past or present tense. Sometimes you get up and figure out how really complex actions or fights take place. There are hugs hello and goodbye.

    People flake, it's life. I've been flaked on in both face-to-face and online games. I've had people leave in the middle of both. I've had GMs sleep through and miss both.

    Edited to be less ranty. I will say this much, though, as regards the crap about how playing face-to-face makes us better, more social people or whatever.

    I had a roommate who was kind of a big oaf. Nice guy, loved to roleplay. Very creative, but not very good at putting together his thoughts-- the kind of guy who'd have a great plan if someone needed busting out of jail, but if he had to use his considerable charisma score he'd just say "I make a really awesome speech" and roll the dice. We started playing online together after he moved away. Three months of online play and he was totally thrilled at how much easier it had become for him to actually speak in character, to know what was going on in his character's head and what that character would actually say. It also made writing up papers in college (why he moved away in the first place) a hell of a lot easier, as he was more comfortable defending his opinion.

    * I can easily record the session for future use
    * I can mute my mic if I need to (I friggin love that)

    \n I just posted this on Praxis, and then went, "Holy crap! This is precisely one of the ways online gaming can be completely superior to tabletop."

    There are a lot of friendly people willing to playtest something on a slow night on IRC.     \n Oh, for sure. The main difference is that things go much slower online than they do in person: online, a group could get through one round of It's Complicated in roughly the same amount of time it's taken to get through the whole board in person. If mechanics are broken, you'll find out from either type of play-- if anything, playtesting online is superior because the only way to communicate the rules is through text. If something in the playtest doc wasn't clear or needed more explaining, I'd clarify or expand in chat, and that would get copied, pasted, and edited into the text of the game that night. There's none of the problem of playtesting the designer's vision or intent instead of the product as it is written. The text isn't written from the standpoint of "play the game and write down what we did," as that can lead to a text that doesn't jive with the experience at all, but instead you play the game as written and then compare that to your goals.

    It also reduced the production time of the game, because the text was mechanically bulletproof when the game came out of playtest-- it wasn't "Great, I've got the mechanics solid, now I just need people to playtest the text." Writing and revising happened simultaneously, which for certain made for a better text.

    Some games wouldn't benefit from online playtesting in the same way-- Mist-Robed Gate, for example, with its wordless entreaties and knife ritual. You could play it online, but you'd want to test the visceral impact of the physicality of the game before it went to print. I wouldn't ever suggest only playtesting a game online, due to pacing issues and if your game has any visceral, physical components, but I think 80-90% of playtesting can be done online to the betterment of your game and the ease of implementation. It does take a couple of things to make it work well though:

    You have to have a playable text done before the first playtest.
    You have to update that text after every session and get it back to your playtesters before the next session.
    You have to be willing to stay up late if that's when people are around and eager to play.

    If you're willing to do those things, online playtesting is amazing.
