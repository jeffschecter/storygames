\n So there are more than a few games out there that follow this identifiable structure, respectfully stolen from the worthy Primetime Adventures (I think). It goes like this: (1) frame a scene, (2) roleplay to conflict, (3) engage mechanics to resolve conflict, (4) narrate resolution of conflict. There is never more than one conflict per scene.

These games usually have a fixed budget of adversity available to the GM/Producer/Antagonist/Whatever, so there are really only X number of conflicts that can have meaningful adversity, and since there is one scene per conflict*, this budget becomes something of a game timer. When budget is expended, the session is over.

*Yes, sometimes there are non-conflict scenes. In my experience this does not make budget not a game timer.

This also tends to make (but hardly guarantees) that each scene is "about" one thing, typified by the one conflict. I would say this effect works about 70% of the time, and is highly dependent on player skill to frame and find a meaningful conflict that expresses the main thrust of the scene.

Because the mechanics are engaged so sparingly, the dice or card mechanics become highly erratic. It is quite possible, even likely, to get an 'improbable' result, and since there are no more conflicts, there are no additional rolls or whatever to mitigate the occasional outlier. This results in play that can have some pretty profound and violent swings. The situation in the fiction can change in a heartbeat.

There are also no 'follow-up' actions, and players cannot respond (mechanically) to conflict results in that scene; they must wait until another scene where they can enact a response. Which can mean that they have to sit on the results, stew a bit, fully experience the consequences, and formulate a really good response for when it's time. It can also mean that they end up feeling rather deprotagonized.

In most of these games, there is no other mechanics to ensure that this series of scenes ever stitches together to create a meaningful narrative. There is no rising action, there is no climax, there is no denouement. Each scene, no matter where it lies in the series, is treated in exactly the same fashion. This again falls to player skill to turn the sequence of scenes into a story, looking at the dwindling budget and saying aloud, "Okay, big finish, everybody!" or something similar.

This is what I see in these one-conflict scene structures. I'm sure there are more implications that I'm not seeing. Despite some fantastic play under this structure, I'm not sure if I'm really sold on it. What do you see, what makes this structure work for you, or what makes this structure not work for you?

    \n Thanks for responding, Joshua.

    I'm not going to quote because that would get huge fast. So instead:

    Narrative Structure: I think you're missing what I'm talking about, here. I want rising action, climax, and denouement for the story told in the session, not per scene. I want, as a rough example, three scenes of rising action, then two scenes of setbacks, then a big epic scene of climax, and one or two scenes of denouement. Sure, those individual scenes will have little epicycles of rising action and climax (which these games already do well, yes), but I want them to work together to make a much larger structure with a definite (although not necessarily predetermined) shape.

    Budget as Timer or Pacing: See, I wouldn't call budget a pacing mechanic; it's a game timer. To me, a pacing mechanic would inform the pacing of the game in a mechanical fashion. It would be something like "When you have five budget left, you do this-and-such." As they stand, neither PTA nor Shock: do this. There are 'things that you can do to make your game rock' and exercise player skills to that end, but those are not mechanics, and they are not mentioned in the game texts to my knowledge. Instead, when budget runs out, you're done -- whether or not the story is done. If the story is not done by the time budget runs out, you as a player have failed to exercise your player skills effectively. To me, this feels a lot like the game rules failing the players, especially when the book does not even mention the possibility of this happening.

    For instance, PTA has this whole section about Act structure, but it's not linked in to the Budget pool. If there was an explicit link between the macro structure and the budget pool, budget would be upgraded to a pacing mechanic in my mind.

    Follow Up Actions: I think here we're talking past each other. There are opportunities to reroll, certainly, but a reroll within the same conflict is different from one conflict happening and then another conflict happening right afterwards. I'm not even talking about dice probabilities, here, I'm talking about how many options I have as a player after dice have been rolled in the scene. It's a matter of how rigid the scene structure is, and what is allowable within one scene. If there's a big social throw-down and I come out on the bad end of it, I might want to have my character pull a gun and shoot somebody (maybe not even the guy who beat me down in the social conflict) -- but resolving that conflict is not an option in some of these games -- notably PTA.

    I think I should point out, I'm not saying that this structure is necessarily a bad thing; I'm just saying that it exists. I find it leads to some strict screen-time economy in PTA, for instance, and results in something very much like a television show. (Where 'pull the gun' is the next scene, after a commercial break or something.) I found it out of place in Shock:, because in my experience speculative fiction has tons of one-upsmanship, immediate responses to actions, and back-and-forths. To me, speculative fiction has a lot of immediate escalation, which Shock: does not have a lot of support for. This bit is, for me, a question of selecting appropriate mechanics for the kind of game you want to participate in.

    Granularity: Again, this is one of those "this is an effect I see, not a criticism I'm making" -- games that make a lot dependent on one die roll (or one die roll and one possible reroll) result in big, sudden, shifts in the fiction. I find this entirely appropriate for both PTA and Shock:. It would be out of place if you were trying to reproduce, say, Lord of the Rings which has, like, three big monumental shifts in the entire book series.

    (As an aside, I tend to think this should be accompanied with a warning sticker, lest players expect unsurprising results from the mechanics; it can be really disconcerting to realize how fluid the game you're already playing really is. But that's a very minor issue.)

    FitM vs FatE: (That's Fortune in the Middle, ie rolling dice in the middle of the conflict process, and Fortune at the End, ie rolling dice at the end of the conflict process.) Joshua, I think this is a red herring, but it's quite possible I'm wrong. I'm looking at this more in terms of, "What can I do as a player in one scene" and not so much "How do I roll dice." What do you see that I'm not?

    \n Side note on pacing effects in Dogs: When the Dogs first run into the town people in conflicts, the GM thinks up their traits on the spot. This puts him into an advantage in the beginning, since he can tailor the traits in a way to make the NPCs highly effective in this conflict.

    Later conflicts are against NPCs whose traits are set and known, which allows the players to plan and set out stakes that are not as easily won by the npcs.
