\n I have been working on a custom Fiasco playset with a gothic setting. Think Dark Shadows (the original TV show, not the Johnny Depp movie) and you've got the idea. I may have bitten off more than I can chew because it involves stunt dice, but I went ahead and made the stunt dice an optional rule. I also tried to keep the basic rules for using the stunt dice as succinct as possible, cutting out a bunch of the fluff and either removing it totally or putting it at the end as an appendix for those looking for more details.

I've already gotten some great feedback on an earlier draft of this playset over on Boardgame Geek; I've put a lot of that feedback to use during my last round of edits. But I would like even more feedback if anyone is willing, particularly those of you who have more familiarity with Fiasco than I do.

Here's a link: https://docs.google.com/open?id=0B0Ut5mF-spZyZ1pnTXUxRFFxOTg

(This is my first time using Google Docs, so I'm hoping I set the permissions correctly. Also, you'll notice there is no art -- sorry, but I am not artistic!)

Next up, I'd like to try something much simpler. I am thinking a 20-year high-school reunion. Does anyone know if such a playset already exists?

    \n The Christmas message (rather than address) was televised from 1957 having been on the radio before then. There wasn't one in 1969. I think if you want 50s to 70s it's probably best to say so. The UK did change massively over this period so there's going to be a very different feel. Pretty much nobody in 1970 had servants outside a very few aristocratic families but my mum remembers eating with her grandfather's servants in the late 40s (and he wasn't that posh).

    The "Bayside Pub and Inn, established 1843" doesn't seem a particularly old building. 1743 or 1643 sounds more like something a pub would boast about. Also, it's usually either a pub or an inn (the words are pretty much synonymous now) and Bayside isn't an English pub name (see here for some ideas).

    \n Graham, great feedback.

    Re: cars -- I think that's a hold-out from my trying to be really ambiguous with the timeframe so that players had a wider range on when to set it. Now that I've narrowed it down, that car thing may not make much sense. I want the village to be very remote and behind the times relative to other villages of the time -- but that may be pushing it. Perhaps I could instead make a statement about a few homes finally puchasing televisions? "It\u2019s 1965, and the times are slowly catching up with the quaint English village by the bay. Several homes now own televisions, and the maidens hemlines have begun to rise just as their neckline begin to plunge."

    Re: psychiatrist -- Doesn't seem that modern to me, but I'm open to other ideas. Do you think "psychologist" would be better? I admit to being strongly influenced by the Dr. Hoffman character in the original Dark Shadows, my main inspiration for this playset.

    Re: harlot vs. prostitute -- I considered that, but the character doesn't necessarily have to charge for her services. Would something like "tramp" work better?

    Re: sabre -- D'oh! Thanks!

    Re: gramophone vs. phonograph -- My research (OK, I looked on Wikipedia ) makes it sounds like those two names were used almost identically for two devices by rival manufacturers. Do you think "gramophone" was more appropriate for the U.K.?

    Re: Expose myself to the village maiden -- Yeah, I don't love the wording on that one. I really want a Need that reflects a caddish character trying to seduce an innocent girl, which seems appropriate for this type of story. What about "To expose the naive maiden to debaucherous pleasures"? That's perhaps a bit broader than just "seduction," but it's close enough and doesn't make it sound so much like I just want a character to be a flasher!

    Thanks again!

    Tramp is 30s slang really and besides in the UK it means a hobo. Floozy is better if you're still keen on this but there are possibly better ways of introducing sexual tension.
