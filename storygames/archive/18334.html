    What are the odds of rolling 2d6 of different colors and having one dice result beat the other's? What if it's the best of 2 or three dice against a single opposing d6? And against the best 2 or 3? Are the odds affected when the same amount of dice are added to both sides?
    \nOdds of one die beating another? It's 50/50, but you only win if you don't tie. The odds of tying are 1/6 (you roll whatever the other guy did) and the odds of not tying are 1 minus that. So the odds are 0.5*(1- 1/6) = 0.5*5/6 = 5/12 ~= 41.7%

    What about best of 2d6 vs. 1d6? Let's pretend you roll 2d6 one at a time. Then, the second 2d6 only helps if you didn't win with the first. So your odds of winning are 5/12 + (1-5/12)*P(winning with 2nd die given that you didn't with the first). Unfortunately, our P() here isn't 5/12 because knowing that our first die didn't win tells us something about what the other die is (it's more likely to be higher, in fact, it's the best of 2d6!). Hmm, that's some symmetry. Let's call this P1 = probability of beating the best of 2d6 with 1d6. Let P2 = probabilty of winning with best of 2d6 vs 1d6 (what we're looking for). So
    P1 + P2 + P(tie) = 1
    and, from above:
    P2 = 5/12 + (1 - 5/12)*P1 = 5/12 + (1 - 5/12)*(1 - P2 - P(tie))
    so what's P(tie)?, which remember is tie in best of 2d6 vs 1d6. P(tie) = 1/6, because whatever the result of best of 2d6, there's a 1/6 chance of rolling that on the other die. So
    P2 = 5/12 + (1 - 5/12)*(5/6 - P2)
    some algebra yields:
    P2 + 7/12*P2 = 19/12*P2 = 5/12 + 7/12*5/6
    P2 = 12/19*(5/12 + 7/12*5/6) = (5 + 35/6)/19 = 65/6/19 ~= 57% (doesn't quite match anydice, did I make an arithmetic error somewhere?)

    You can extend this to 3d6.

    The odds are affected when you roll the same number of dice on each side. Since you are taking the best of multiple rolls, the probability of rolling high increases. Since it's more likely to roll high for both of you, you're more likely to tie. You still have a 50/50 chance when you don't tie. Maybe I'll get to calculating this later.
