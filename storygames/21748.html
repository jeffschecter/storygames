    \n In his book Persuasive Games, Ian Bogost talks about a concept called procedural rhetoric; like verbal and visual rhetoric, it's the idea that one makes arguments through the use of procedures. I think it's something that's heavily at play in most non-abstract games (that is, any game where the elements involved in play can reasonably be said to correspond to objects or concepts that exist outside of the game), and a huge part of how roleplaying games produce meaning.

    The procedures we follow when playing games make claims about all sorts of things: about how much it hurts to get shot in the leg, about what you can expect from being more or less attractive than average, about the benefits of fostering positive relationships with other people, about how characters' flaws shape the stories they participate in, about when uncertainty serves to create dramatic tension, etc...

    So the way that overall meaning in games is constructed is through an arrangement of statements like, "These things are like this," and "Doing this produces this result," "A corresponds to B in these ways," and such. There's still a necessary level of subjective interpretation that needs to be done on the final arrangement of those statements, and different kinds of procedures can be more or less geared towards being easily interpreted.

    But just like words themselves generally mean very little on their own, constructing significant amounts of meaning often requires stringing lots of these procedures together, and what they ultimately mean is highly dependent on which ones are present and how they relate to each other. Like, the rules for alignment don't really mean all that much when they're just sitting in the rulebook (though they do, I think, make strong suggestions about what they might be used to mean, particularly when we take a holistic look at their appearance in the rules), compared to when (and why and on whom) the Paladin actually uses Smite Evil.

    \n I don't really understand the question - are you really just asking about how a game carries meaning?

    If so, I like the way Yukamichi breaks it down - roleplaying games carry narrative messages, but they also carry meaning in their structures, so it's in a sense a wider and more varied medium than storytelling.

    As for how we can perceive that meaning - how is it possible to tell what a game is about - I'd say that it's no different from the way we evaluate e.g. novels: you just have to experience the thing, understand it and then verbalize that understanding. As we know from general art theory, the work in itself is not about anything per se, but it is possible for it to be pretty consistent about producing a similar understanding for many different people; this understanding is what we usually mean when we say that something is "about" something else. The meaning is not inherent to the pattern, but neither is it independent; you need both the work and the audience to have the meaning emerge in the audience's understanding of the work.

    \n I think there are two ways a game can be "about" something, in the literary sense you're using to analyze Little Red Riding Hood.

    The first way is just like a story:

    You sit down and you play through the game, creating a story. That story may have themes, subjects, morals, and so forth, just like any story.

    You might say, "We played Archipelago last night. My character was a young woman, who tried to escape from a wolf. Clearly, the wolf was a metaphor for a predatory man."

    The second way is via fictional content, fictional and procedural constraints, and play procedures.

    The game might be said to make statements about the people playing, the way they engage with roleplaying and with each other, and the fictional material.

    For instance, if I write a game where the only way to win is to lie to your friends, we could say that my game is, at some level, "about" lying, uncovering lies, or something similar. (Given a specific game, we can talk about this in some more detail; in the abstract it sounds very vague, but with an actual game we can get to a more specific formulation.)

    The relationship of the way the game tells the participants to interact with each other and the rules for what happens in the fiction all limit the scope of stories which may be told, and that implies (or even forces) themes to emerge.

    For instance, in D&D, stories which recreate aspects of the Hero's Journey are part of the fabric of the design. If you wanted to tell stories about hubris and how the once mighty all fall, with the characters being aging heroes fighting a hopeless battle as their skills and abilities degrade... you wouldn't be able to do that without redesigning the game.

    As another Monsterhearts gives the characters a limited palette of abusive, manipulative, or coercive ways to gain and exert influence over each other. In this sense, it's often said to be "about" dysfunctional relationships. However, it also tells us to create teenage characters with monstrous aspects which exaggerate young peoples' feelings of alienation, angst, anger, and similar "teenage" problems. The MC is instructed to make "all acceptance conditional"; between these various design elements you end up with a game which is clearly about teenagers struggling with their identity and treating each in dysfunctional ways. The game is "about" the high school experience, told through a metaphor of monsters and body horror.

    Many of the specifics (especially the "sex moves") make strong statements about the nature of sex and teen relationships, much in the same way as Little Riding Hood. For instance, the Mortal's sex move tells us that, at least for this character, consummating love is a self-destructive thing and the person you trust will inevitably turn into a monster (and probably hurt you).

    However, the game also shows us an inevitable avenue for hope: play long enough, and the PCs will gain "growing up moves", which allow them to create functional relationships for the first time. There's a strong and clear theme in there, as well.

    All these things constrain the kinds of stories we can tell. When the rules and procedures constrain the fiction sufficiently to create stories which reliably produce these themes, we can say that a game is "about" those themes, much like Little Red Riding Hood.

    \n So we're interested in theme specifically, and how games don't have a literary theme? There's other sorts of about-ness in the world as well, but that does narrow it down a lot.

    I pretty much agree on that point - games are about many things, and different games are different, but a literary theme is not even the most common thing for a game to be about. Taken as a whole over the entire field of rpgs one could say that roleplaying games don't have a theme, any more than football does.

    There are a whole bunch of games that indeed have a "proto-theme", a premise that the game invites the players to explore. If that doesn't turn into a theme, the fault could be in the game, or it could be in the players - I have certainly seen people play games about some blindingly obvious topics, and failing to produce a story that has a theme. Zombie Cinema, for instance: as wacky as it seems, about 10% of the people who try it seem to be able to play it through without noticing that the game invites them to depict and judge human morality in a crisis situation. For those players Zombie Cinema can truly be said to be about nothing at all, as there really isn't much left of it once you discard that basic premise. A bit of dice rolling and menacing growls, that's it.

    Furthermore, there are even games that do have a literary theme baked in, precisely as much of a one as a fairy tale does. Paranoia was already mentioned, it doesn't exactly shy away from its literary theming. Call of Cthulhu is another example. Exalted counts as well, and so does Pendragon. Dread, Dead of Night, Puppetland, most superhero games... Any games that have their own in-built story that comes up strongly in play count in this regard, I think. Dungeons & Dragons is one of those games that might count or not: its story is in most editions indubitably one-note and very clear, but it is also very realistic to play it while ignoring that story, so any given play-through may be narratively independent (telling about something else) or narratively meaningless.

    But those exceptions aside, yeah - lots of games out there that don't have a literary theme, or that theme is very secondary to what goes on in the game. Apocalypse World definitely included - it's a carefully constructed narrativist game; those aren't supposed to push their own themes.

    Also, my own experience about that premise-turning-into-theme thing: while you say that it's rare and barely perceptible, my experience is that I'll get it over 90% of the time when playing a game designed for it. The afore-mentioned Zombie Cinema, for instance: give me good players and it's gonna be 100% of the time, until we grow bored of doing it. I don't know if our difference on this is because of different play experiences or a difference in how we interpret theme out of a story - could be that you require the theme to be presented very explicitly before you acknowledge its presence in a story?

    Call of Cthulhu is about how seeking and seeing supernatural horror leads to madness or death. The mechanics (a) for when you lose Sanity and (b) for how outclassed you are in combat against monsters, both show this.

    D&D is about how troubleshooting and overcoming physical danger is the path to personal betterment. The system for how you get and spend XP shows this.
