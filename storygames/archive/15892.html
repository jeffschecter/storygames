    \n I'm having trouble with this one too. It seems like an animal obstacle is something with an agenda, and an agenda leads to conflict.

    Maybe coming up against a nest (that hasn't hatched, or that is empty for the moment) and the conflict is navigating past it in such a was as to not leave a trail?
    Or harvesting honey from docile bees?

    I think the deciding factor between an Obstacle and a Conflict is how hard the other side wants to fight, no? I mean, a fail at an Obstacle just means complications, right? A fail at Conflict can be Death.

    \n There's a lot of great stuff here!

    I have one tenderpaw in my group who's belief is "I'll never leave a creature in distress behind" so lot's of lion's with thorns in their paws type stuff

    Some animal obstacles that I've used

    A young newt is drawn to the heat of a patrol's campfire/town celebrations
    A townships grains have been overrun by pests, can the patrol concoct a scent to drive them away?
    an apiarists honeybees have escaped and they are angry! Can you calm them down long enough for the apiarist to get them back in their boxes?
    A gopher has been terribly injured by some kind of large animal and is now wandering disoriented about the town, scaring its residents
    some migrating birds have left a mess of guano near town; go harvest/clean it up!
    A young waterfowl has been caught in a fishing line

    \n When you run a game like Mouse Guard you can't mandate what the characters will and won't do. All you can do if offer a situation and then follow in the direction that they decide to take it. If you offer an open-ended situation and they decide to solve it with violence, then that's what you do.

    \n First and best advice at all times, every time: Look at your characters' BIGs for guidance.

    On that note, totally out of context, some broad animal hazards I'd be okay resolving with a roll:

    Herd of elk/moose are passing through, threatening (a merchant caravan/grain fields/children mice at play/stream paths).

    Something has nested right in your pathway, and you can't tell when the mother will be back.

    Burrowing animal has undermined a settlement but has no interest in the mice at all.

    Anthill has popped up and the workers are busy denuding a farmer's field.


    Honestly, though, I'd be pretty okay resorting to a single versus test OR a fullblown conflict. The conflict mechanic is really there to build tension around a particular moment of play, to drain off excess artha, and to allow for a compromise result.

    \n On the one hand, particularly in the context of fluency play, don't be afraid to do anything with a simple obstacle. Some are still learning the game, so the simple mechanics will take longer, but also this should also force some good roleplaying because the dice are giving you 'less'.

    On the other hand, use complex obstacles! It's a great middle ground that's not any more complicated than simple obstacles, but can give more shape to the encounter. Or Twist into complex obstacles: the Raven is trying to steal the mail and (players fail) does. Now you have to track him down and get it back to complete the mission. Just don't do the same thing over again.
