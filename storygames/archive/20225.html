\n I'm planning a short-ish campaign of Ryuutama and thinking about a general theme to base the game around.

The idea of "everyone goes on a journey once in their life" can easily be interpreted as a coming-of-age event (Bildungsroman), dealing with a mid-life crisis, or something similar (This isn't the only way of interpreting this part of the setting, but it's the one I'm going with.)

The coming-of-age story is a person against self type story, where the characters have to understand the constraints society places on them, accept them in some form, and then take their place in society. That's really good, but it's all internal to the characters. What can I do, as a narrator, to prompt that internal journey?

The obvious thing to do is to present situations where something's gone wrong because someone is rebelling against society's rules, and the PCs have to deal with the consequences, perhaps putting things right. That's sort of DitV territory, but without the PCs having any mandate to step in and change things.

So, any other suggestions?

    \n The player has to want to play a character that changes. It's often hard, maybe harder than agreeing to play a character that is flawed, because in longer-format games you only start "feeling" your character after a couple of sessions.

    My first instinct would be to get the players' buy in - make sure that they want to start with flawed characters and play through a story of overcoming that flaw (specifically, "Avatar: the Last Airbender" comes to my mind as an example of such a story, where a group is on a quest but also each character deals with their individual issues).

    [Then I would grab Burning Wheel and ask the players to create characters that have a completely reasonable Belief about not wanting to follow a rule - maybe "I'm just a kid, someone else will take care of it" (assuming responsibility?) or "It was just a joke, don't take it so seriously!" (compassion?) or "You can't tell me what to do!" (learning to cooperate?) - and then plan a game that challenges those Beliefs. I don't have Ryuutama yet so I don't know how it works there. However without player buy in, even mechanics may have no bite, like TBZ Fates.]

    \n Mixing several of my favorite approaches to character change, I would have each player choose 2-3 issues for their character (aspects of their character that stop them from being who they want to be) and then reflect those issues as recurring problems along the journey.

    This works even better if you can have the issues provide a both a penalty and benefit (the later representing the better person peeking out from under the issues). Then as the reflected aspect is overcome, the character can have the penalty ease and ultimately disappear. Not sure how Ryuutama would support that, though.

    \n Another way to go (from a mechanic I've never implemented) is to ask two questions:
    1) How most people view them/think they're like
    2) How they view themselves/What they're really like

    The implication is that there's a "BUT" between (1) and (2).

    If you want to take it even further, you could ask a third question:
    3) How they view their ideal/adult self/what they wish to become

    \n One traditional way to do this in fantasy fiction is metaphor. You're already using an actual journey as a metaphor of self-discovery; various encounters during the journey are built as metaphors of parts of the self.
    For reference, some games which explicitly embrace this concept are The Dreaming Crucible by Joli St.Patrick and Heroine by Josh Jordan, both fashioned after such (pretty unsubtle, which is why we love them) coming-of-age fantasy tales as The Wizard of Oz or the movie Labyrinth. But, really, almost anything which is about one main character on a journey ultimately comes down to this, especially when fantasy elements are involved.

    \n The coming of age story I think I should be promoting, especially given the Japanese-based society, is one of people seeing that social ties are more important than selfish impulses. I'd like to see something about people taking their place in society, taking on responsibility, and being rewarded for it. That's quite at odds with the Western (especially US) ideal of individual fulfilment.

    I like the idea of having the players define something that's stopping their personal growth, or its expression, and revisiting that occasionally.

    I was listening to a podcast the other day that mentioned Pendragon's opposed trait pairs. I don't know if that's something to implement, so that people can see how their character is acting and regarded. Perhaps it would be something for a second game, once I've seen how Ryuutama works in practice.
