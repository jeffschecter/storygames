\n Slight issue that comes up from time to time, as I'm prone to run LOTS and LOTS of Japan-themed games.

I like games with large pools of characters the PCs interact with regularly. In English, it's easy with a group of English-speakers to remember who is who in a pool of Western names (at least, after a few minutes). Even with non-western names, it usually comes down to just a few such characters, so they too are easy to remember.

But what about an all-African, all-Aztec, all-Chinese, all-Indian, etc game? What tips and tricks do you use to remember the names yourself, or to get other players to remember them?

Thoughts:
1) Get character sketches. Grab portraits of actors from IMDB. Print them out on some cards or peices of paper, and leave them in the middle of the table. It's easy to remember who "Govinda the Sellsword" is if they mentally associate it with a portrait of Hrithik Roshan.
Pros: Visual-learner people can remember easily.
Cons: LOTS of setup/work.

2) Refer to people by titles rather than names. In Japanese-themed games, this is easy. Most often, their name is already immediately associated with their profession, so "Genta the Toymaker" can be called "The Toymaker", "Osaki the Shamisen Player" can be "The Shamisen Player", etc.
Pros: Variance, you can mix it up with some people known by their name, some people known by their profession.
Cons: Eventually, if folks slack off then everyone will be known as their profession name, which carries no cultural flavor. ("Yeah, that magistrate guy, I go talk to him. First I stop off at the seamstresses' and talk to her about her husband, the barrelmaker.")

3) Name variance: We tend to do this subconsciously, but yeah. Don't start everyone's name with "A", rather mix it up, even if it produces some real-variant results. Also, in Japanese games (and probably other cultures, China?) you have lords with last names and stuff, and then you have middle/lower class people without a legal last name, just a given name. So the variance in names might be easy to remember, or be a trigger in recalling those names:
Okiku
vs
Watanabe Shogoro
vs
Ryouji
vs
Harimaya Tozaemon

What do you do? Any tips, or any stories about successes/failures to share?

-Andy

    \n Ganakagok has this problem; character names are often barely pronounceable clusters of consonants. Often the problem gets solved by referring to a character's distinct identity (the "worried chieftain" rather than Agonaq), and I've noticed that players tend to select names that are authentic-sounding but distinct and easy to remember (like #3 name variance above). So one character might be Maluq and another be Viqlak, instead of Anuquranok and Qurakanoq. Also, the relationship map tends to stay on the table during play, and so there's an easy reference right there.

    \n One thing I did for my Edo-period Japan game was to teach my players Japanese.

    ...

    OK, not exactly. What I did was explain what sounds they could expect to encounter in the Japanese syllabary and how those sounds are romanized, then we all repeated them a couple times. That way the sounds they were hearing and the names they'd see on paper weren't totally alien to them.

    This was done in conjunction with the other methods you listed above, particularly number 3 since we were doing an Edo-period game with more excuses for name variance.

    All in all, I think it worked out pretty well.

    \n My solution is three-fold: suck it up (that is, use those names until you start remembering them), don't care (call them A, B and C if you can't think of anything better) and don't use too many names (only give names to the PCs and NPCs with speaking parts). Works for my style of games enough to never make names a problem. To illustrate, last weekend we played a playtest session of a fantasy game involving mongols that conquered Finland, more or less. The fabulous names of the NPC stuff consisted of Kublai (male), Gengissa (female), Temujin (female) and Shangri-La (a castle). This degree of idiotic simplicity is, of course, comedic at first, but nothing says you can't just ignore that and focus on what you're actually doing instead of worrying about secondary stuff. I'm completely serious in that if I had needed to name another person, it'd probably have been another Kublai or Gengis or Temujin simply because I couldn't remember any other Mongolian names at the time.

    Names do, of course, have poetic value. It's just that not all styles of play utilize that value. Some do: I actually spent a minute forming up the name for my important NPC antagonist in our recent TSoY campaign, settling on "Nadir" as a suitably fantastic ("nadir" is an uncommon word in Finnish, so it almost sounds like a real name) yet adventurous choice. In the same campaign I've named characters based on product information labels from stuff lying around the room as we play.

    \n For Chinese names, I tend to insist that people translate given names into English and only use Chinese surnames ("Blossom Xu" instead of Xu Hua) or go by nicknames ("the Mountain-Squatting Vulture") because otherwise things tend to dissolve into stupid. I do the same thing with uncommon place names. Beijing or Shanghai or even Dalian are probably okay, big cities that people really should recognize and know vaguely where they are (like knowing roughly where Marseilles is), but otherwise it's Three Steams ViIlage etc. Province names stay in Chinese. It would be weird to translate Yunnan into "South Clouds Province," I think.

    \n In a peculiar bit of synchronicity - I, Colin C, did precisely the same thing as Colin C, above. When we ran a game with Japanese-named characters, I broke out the common elements of the names, so that the barely-memorable syllable clusters could instead be treated as larger, consistent chunks. Even when it comes to shorter names, I've found it it's easier to get a non-Japanese speaker to pronounce, and remember, "Mae" + "da" than "Maeda", and then to leverage that familiarity to make "Imada" and "Maebayashi" easier next time, and then "Hayashikawa"... No one was tripping over names, and everything went really smoothly.

    The same trick has stood me in good stead in dealing with phrase-length Sanskrit-derived Indian names, both in-game and IRL. Rather than having to stop and grapple with whole names like "Venkateshwarlu", a little practice makes "Venkatesh" (really "Venkat" but I've seen it set this way enough that the larger unit has taken on a life of its own) jump out, leaving the totally manageable "-warlu" as a novel element.

    Posted By: DanielSolisAs a corollary to Andy's idea of using photographs, would there be any utility in a game including preformatted mini portraits with blank labels beneath them for this purpose? You could print them out as needed and write notes on them when important details come up.
    I've certainly done this in an old Victorian Age: Vampire campaign - I gave the players a book of mini-portraits early in the campaign, but with only a few names filled in (their sires, the Prince, and so on). As they met other characters I'd turn to the appropriate page, and they could write in the name (plus any other details they wanted to remember) and use it as a reference throughout the game.

    It was a fair chunk of prep work, but I think it was worthwhile. And it had the effect of maintaining the illusion that London was an (un)living, breathing city full of NPCs with their own agendas and histories, because clearly I'd worked out complex character relationship maps way in advance.

    In reality, I'd thrown in a lot of portraits without knowing who they all depicted; when I created a new NPC during the game, I'd just picked an unused portrait.

    Posted By: AndyAh, what I was more interested in than the actual complication of the names, was remembering who goes with which name.
    So the foreign-ness doesn't matter. Misleading OP/title, don't you think?

    So: Make your r-map explicit. Actually draw the thing on a big sheet of paper or a whiteboard or what have you. Have an appositive phrase labeling each node ("Bob Smith: the man with the plan"). This is very low-maintenance. If you use a piece of easel paper and those artificially-food-smelly, colored markers, people will occasionally be motivated to stand up and write a new thing next to a guy who's getting developed ("So, Bob was confident about the plan, but his execution? Is awful. OK if I make him 'the incompetent man with the flawed plan' instead?"). At some point, I really ought to take a picture of our easel paper for the Fiasco game I facilitated a couple of weeks ago \u2014 it's a pretty sweet game artifact.

    Reward your players for taking good notes. Offer an XP bonus for a beginning-of-session "last week on..." that has these details; offer a smaller bonus to players who remember campaign details and explain them for the benefit of players with faltering memories. This was a huge help to the longest-running D&D campaign I played in, and as a bonus we have an insanely detailed campaign chronicle (those of us who took notes did so in character, by the way, including in-fiction (bad) poetry, (bad) maps, and (bad) sketches).

    Cool side-benefit: Years later, the GM asked for copies of those notes, since he wanted to run more stories in the same game world and figured he'd benefit from all the world-building work we did collectively. 200 pages of reasonably well-organized manuscript sprung instantly to his fingertips just from the two conscientious note-taking players now on the West Coast \u2014 it's quite possible he got another hundred from the folks still on the other side of the country.

    \n I've anticipated this a bit in Underground Railroad by giving a naming schema which uses familiar words and makes it easy to pigeonhole people, once you're used to it.

    Characters' names in UR are not just arbitrary labels like they mostly are for us, but follow a pattern which tells you about a person's religion, affiliations, stage of life and even personal history. And because they are ordinary words, not arbitrary foreign syllables, hopefully they are a bit easier to remember. (Plus I suck at making up arbitrary foreign syllables that sound good.)

    Of course, I come from a country that's so nomenclature-challenged that we call our two main islands "the North Island" and "the South Island".
