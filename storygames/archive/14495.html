\n Burning Wheel Gold got me thinking about this. Obviously free is pretty awesome, but that's only free on PDF (I'm pretty sure SJGames stopped doing free copies of GURPS Lite), and printing costs money any way you look at it. So, talking about physical products that are very good deals, let's list them.

So far I'm aware of,

1) Savage Worlds Explorer Edition - $10 for a digest sized, full colour 160 page rulebook
2) Reign Enchiridion - $10 for a digest sized, black and white, 232 page rulebook
3) Burning Wheel Gold - $25 for a digest sized (I'm assuming, based on the PDF), black and white, 600 page rulebook

Honourable mention for Hero System Sidekick, which by my estimation was the first, but is no longer for sale.

Anything else where you are thinking that the designers are almost producing a loss-leader?

    Whether this sort of thing makes any sense commercially is, of course, an interesting question. My own sense is currently that it would make much more sense to sell your game at $30 or $40, dropping the 20% of the customers who were buying it just for the price point, all the while taking the money from the actually motivated customers. Or even better, set up some price discrimination system that allows people to pay you more. Customers are so rare for roleplaying games that letting them go for just $5 or $10 is maybe not very smart.

    \n Jason's asking you, I think. You started the thread about physical products and their price points, after all.

    And no, of course value is not equivalent to page count. Price, however, correlates with page count to a degree due to various practical reasons we all know. Of course if we were seriously talking about "good deals", we would need to adjust for the quality of the content itself as well. This in turn becomes a highly subjective exercise, one I've fiddled with a lot over the years as I've sold indie games to a discerning public. If I've learned anything doing that, it's that such a great degree of value is generated by subjective demographic match-up of product to customer that it's almost impossible to create a game that would be objectively a bad deal. Even the worst case scenario where your game is outcompeted by a very similar but better product will often find surprising traction among the specialized clientele who already own the better game but desire further reading material for the nuances. I think that it's fair to say that a discussion of "good deals" is necessarily going to be somewhat tongue-in-cheek, using simplistic measures such as how much money was put into the physical production values of the thing.

    Solar System's ISBN is 978-952-99414-5-2.

    \n There must be many different ways to measure "value for money", I can definitely see an \xa385 Great Pendragon Campaign as having it. IFF you actually go ahead and play it, there's enough material in that tome to last ten years of regular gaming. \xa385 is a bargain for that amount of supported play.

    Cheap does not necessarily mean "not a lot of money".

    --

    I sell my game While the World Ends for \xa35 (\xa32 PDF), is it a deal? Maybe not, it's a small B/W booklet, no illustrations save for some diagrams explaining how to play, and it's not even written in proper English, but some sort of Swedish Chef bastardization of the language. OTOH, it delivers reliable, interesting stories and exciting play, even if you only play it once it's only 0.31\xa3/h and player for entertainment, I'd say that's an awesome deal.

    --

    Once I bought games according to the metric implied by the OP, Deal equals Page count divided by Price, double the Deal if the book is in full color. However, I realized that the Deal factor of a game showed little to no correlation to how much fun we had during actual play. These days I base my purchases on other criteria, and I'd say I'm the happier for it.
