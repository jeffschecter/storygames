Tom Moldvay & Frank Mentzer took D&D, a game made by designers (not writers) written for gaming hobbyists, and made it into something that anyone, regardless of experience, could understand and enjoy.



D&D Basic Rulebook (Otus Art), edited by Tom Moldvay, 1981:\n
FOREWORD

I was busy rescuing the captured maiden when the dragon showed up. Fifty feet of scaled terror glared down at us with smoldering red eyes. Tendrils of smoke drifted out from between fangs larger than daggers. The dragon blocked the only exit from the cave.

Sometimes I forget that D&D\xae Fantasy Adventure Game is a game and not a novel I'm reading or a movie I'm watching. The original D&D rules are a classic. They gave the first gaming system for fantasy role playing and, in my opinion, are still the best set of rules on the market. When I revised the rules I tried to maintain the spirit of the earlier rules.

Those rules were written for people with a background of gaming experience. This revision was designed to be easily read and used by individuals who have never before played a role playing game.

In the half\xb7dozen years since the original rules were published, the TSR staff has answered thousands of rule's questions. The answers helped find problem areas in those rules, areas which could either stand minor improvements or were difficult for novice garners to understand. This revision was aided not only by the collected gaming experience of TSR personnel but by the gaming experience of the thousands of players and DMs who sent us letters in the mail.
\nD&D Basic Rules (Elmore Art), edited by Frank Mentzer, 1983:\n
Dedication

This game has undergone a startling metamorphosis from its earliest forms, written for hobbyists, to the current revision, usable and understandable by nearly anyone. The original flavor and intent has been carefully preserved. With the greatest admiration, respect, and thanks, this edition is dedicated to the president and founder of TSR Hobbies: E. GARY GYGAX.
\n
Do we need modern day versions of Tom Moldvay & Frank Mentzer to do the same for popular Indie Games?

In some ways, early D&D was quite similar to modern day indie games. I wonder if they could use the same solutions.

    \n There's a potentially huge market here. When I was doing the afterschool class with Tavis, the children ate this stuff up with a spoon. They literally could not get enough of collaborative wish-fulfillment and challenge-overcoming. They talked about their characters all the time, told each other stories about the world, and a few of them got so pumped up they ran the game on their own even though we hadn't given them any rules with which to do so. There is a huge unmet need for social, structured, imaginative play.
    \n
    \nThe difficulty from a Forge-ite's standpoint is that these kids have about 10 seconds worth of attention. They are not going to read a rule book that is 20 pages long. They will not read a rule book that is 5 pages long. (We got D&D; down to a single double-sided page and it was too long.) So spending all your time on clever design might get you status among designers, but it means shit for the actual audience (kids, or more meaningfully, their parents). If you absolutely have to have a rule book, it's gotta be something like the monster manual with crazy-cool pictures of monsters and treasures on every page, so that a kid will want to study it lovingly even if he or she has no idea what it means.
    \n
    \nBut the main thing is marketing and outreach and figuring out a way to crack the market to begin with, rather than coming up with some brand-new crazy design.
