\n It occurs to me that the genre of utterly mad shit in space is under-represented.

Stuff like Metabarons, The Airtight Garage and related comics, the covers of early Iron Maiden albums, anything from those old Heavy Metal comics. Mad shit. In space. Like a gonzo Doctor Who, but who rides a space chopper instead of a police box and carries a massive sword instead of a screwdriver. He fights space barbarians who ride star dragons. Illustrated by Simon Bisley with a soundtrack by Judas Priest. Foreword by Michael Moorcock and Lemmy.

The nearest thing I can think of (not counting the mediocre but beautiful Metabarons RPG) is to take Octane and put that in space instead of post appoc America.

Any other game I ought to take a look at before I think any further on this? Or do I just break out the complete Rob Halford and work from there?

    \n The problem with settings like Metabarons (the comic, not the milquetoast RPG based on it) is that it's filled with mad chaotic shit that you can't control.

    Like, in one comic this dude is fucking this chick, then she gets her chest sliced open by robots to remove her heart-bomb, then the guy kills an ambassador, and then a race of hairy dudes show up, challenge the main dude to like a game of chess, and when they lose they activate bombs which entirely annihilate every member of their race from every corner of the universe: all of their stars go Nova, all of them kill themselves.

    And this was like in four pages. This is what the Metabarons (a universe of crazy shit) is like. The true Metabarons RPG would look like this:

    1: Sit in a coffee house.
    2: Take two hits of acid.
    3: Start talking.
    4: Roll a 1d20: Whoever gets the highest number gets to narrate for three minutes. Then roll again.
    5: End abruptly.

    At the very least, it would look like TOON with Character Death.

    It's something that I can recognize as a genre, but that I otherwise want to totally stay away from.

    -Andy

    \n Has anyone ever read DR and Quinch? It was a reasonably early piece of work by Alan Moore and appeared in the British comic 2000AD. It had tonnes of mad shit and it was definitely in space.

    For example, in one episode DR and Quinch use a time machine and nuclear weapons to rearrange to continents of earth into an insulting message about the Dean of their college. The point is that while there is a load of mad shit going on, there is alway a central conflict which is coherent. It gives a bit of structure to hang the lunacy on.

    \n I have two different answers to this dilemma, one based on form and another on substance.

    The easier way is to play Torg, with every cosm as fair game for character creation. In fact, requiring that everyone come from a different cosm is probably key. There actually is a space opera cosm that was added late in the game's life, which is a good starting point, but letting flying psychic starfish, jut-jawed two-fisted archeologists, cyberCatholics and free-market capitalist ninja clans run amuck in it is what adds the proper fever-dream flavor.

    The harder but I think more spiritually faithful way is to play WTF (TLDR optional) and gently reskin everything as taking place on different planets and in the deeps between the stars, because in that Jodorowsky-influenced school of beautiful space madness there's always a lot of Jungian shit going on so the pilgrim's-progress premise of WTF will honor that beating heart of crazy. Plus the players arguing out of character about how to define the rules they're playing by and even being able to roll to depose the GM and elect a new one provides a straight-edge alternative to Andy's timeless resolution mechanic above.

    Please tell me more about Star Wars Lucha Libre. Is it Lucha Libre happening in the Star Wars universe? Or Lucha Libre in our universe but involving Star Wars characters?
    \nIt was all inspired by THIS, that was pointed out by Joel Shempert on G+.
    I was so inspired, I made little mini playsets for the different characters, filling in the details and making them full-fledged octaNe characters with a few rules tweaks (the various styles are in Spanish, and there is no Magic, only Fuego...because...well...Lucha Libre.)

    It is basically a reskinning of the original Star Wars movie as an El Santo y Blue Demon-style adventure set in 1970s Mexico, and based off the feel of the poster. I had to fill in a few gaps (there was no image for Juan Uno, and they didn't have a name for the Mexican version of Obi-Wan...so of course he would be played by Ricardo Montalban).

    ...and then I had to draw everyone. Of course.

    Haven't had a chance to play it yet, because things just keep getting in the way. But here is the link to the main parts of the character playsheets. It leaves out everything about the rules tweaks, since I don't want to distribute the main rules for octaNe anywhere. But SHOULD you be interested, and have a copy of octaNe, I could briefly explain what the tweaks are in a whisper.
