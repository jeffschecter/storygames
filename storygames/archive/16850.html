\n After working on this thing I've been giving a lot of thought to the possibility of visual-based roleplaying.

I'm a very visual person. When designing, diagrams and concept art are my key tools. I'm drawn more to comics and animation than the written word. Since almost all RPGs depend heavily on verbal narration, the concept of a wholly visual-based RPG (game where the story is told through images rather than words) is extremely intriguing to me.

To my knowledge, there has been some work in this area.

- Primetime Adventures instructs the players to use the language of film and camera work to narrate ("Okay, close up shot of Dirk slowly raising his gun...")*
- Pixel Bitch uses clipped images from magazines to simulate the graphic adventure genre.

Are there any other examples of this kind of thing? What works, what doesn't? Where else could we push this idea, and what problems would we face**?


* At least I think. I haven't picked up my copy in a while.

** One obvious problem is that verbal narration is easy to generate on the fly by human players. Images aren't. It would be stupid to have to wait 20 minutes for one player to draw a picture of what's going on; that's why you generally need to either rely on pseudo-imagery (like in PTA), cut'n'pasted images from other sources (like in Pixel Bitch), or find a way to everyone else is still active when one player is creating an image.

    \n Wushu Open also has that very cinematic game play style. The players provide details and increase the size of their dice pool. Often this is done with a director's flair using camera angles and special effects.

    One visual element that I'm notorious for is hand gestures (even when I'm playing via Skype.) Technically, this is even faster than speech for some people. While I'd suggest something more elaborate than scissors-rock-paper, it might be possible to make gestures a meaning part of the game mechanics.

    Also, you could bring in visually rich gaming elements like tarot cards or casting runes. Do: Pilgrims of the Temple is a good example of a game that brings in multiple visual and tactile components; a bag of stones, letters, passports, and the very personalized trouble marker.

    I am a huge fan of portrait photography, and have parlayed my habit of staying up way too late browsing flickr accounts into a large collection of potential character portraits. I find this resource extremely useful for games like Apocalypse World, where you have a large number of NPCs who may appear briefly, repeatedly, etc. -- assigning each NPC a photograph makes it a lot easier for players to remember who they are, and also (in the case of an interesting photograph) can easily kickstart some basic characterization on the part of the MC.

    On the other hand, our group has gone back and forth on using these photographs, in part because they do tend to replace actual verbal description of what characters look like -- which, while it might be good for the OP, is something we are not quite as comfortable with.

    The whole 'a picture is worth a thousand words' conundrum is an interesting one, because ultimately there is just no way you are going to actually displace verbal description as the #1 medium of roleplaying -- in our case, there was a concern that allowing us to avoid rich verbal description of character appearance might in turn atrophy or limit or discourage rich verbal description in other areas of the game.

    You can't find an image for everything, after all -- and imagine a group of NPCs where four of them have super-awesome photographic portraits and the fifth just has a verbal description. Or a game where all the characters have vivid portraits, but there are no pictures of any of the locations. Consider, also, the difference between me saying "he is a handsome, dark-skinned man with a severe look" and showing you a picture of an actual person that I happen to think is handsome and severe-looking. Part of the genius of words in a 'shared imagined space' is that they allow every player to translate them into their own personal-visual world. I might think that this portrait is of a super-hot dude, and you might think he looks like an ugly jerk with bad taste in clothes.

    Given how potent visual images are for human beings, I think introducing strong visual elements is not without pitfalls.

    \n Daniel, I agree with you mostly on one point: that words allow each player to have their own visual interpretation of the shared imagined space. That is one of the reasons a visual RPG has to focus on playing to the strengths of visual art rather than just trying to translate concepts from verbal roleplaying.

    One phenomenon worth looking at here is the "jam comic", a genre of activities which when you look at them are basically visual story games. Players collaborate and use rules to decide who gets to draw what panel, and sometimes what they should depict. I had an idea a while ago for a jam comic/story game hybrid that went nowhere (mostly because I was looking at the problem with trad-gaming lenses), but now I'm thinking of giving it another go.

    the concept of a wholly visual-based RPG (game where the story is told through images rather than words) is extremely intriguing to me.
    \nEverway used (many beautiful) images as oracles.

    Dixit uses (many compelling) images as fodder for a Balderdash-like creative bluffing game.

    In old-school D&D, the players produce a map as an artifact of their exploration. In new-school D&D, visualization tools (maps and figures) are required in order to disambiguate the scene.

    In How To Host A Dungeon, the player creates a map to record the story of their play.

    Every game that John Harper has run for me has featured significant visual communication, everything from well-chosen found images to sketching. His Stranger Things game used a set of interlocking city geomorphic tiles as the play environment (I think Tony Dowler drew those.) Older versions of Danger Patrol required players to make cardstock playing pieces to represent their heroes on the threat display. I always decorated the hell out of these and got a lot of fun doing so.

    Last year at GPNW I met someone who was doing / had done a kickstarter for a big deck of nicely illustrated fantasy NPC portraits. I wish I could remember the name of them! Drawing these guys at random could be the basis for a game.\n

    I am a huge fan of portrait photography, and have parlayed my habit of staying up way too late browsing flickr accounts into a large collection of potential character portraits.
    \nI totally do this as well, especially in a scenario where lots of characters are going to be met very quickly. Like in the Roach, players meet chancellors and professors and boosters and they all have the ornate WASPy names that are largely indistinguishable to me. I wish the Roach came with a set of NPC portrait cards.\n
    Part of the genius of words in a 'shared imagined space' is that they allow every player to translate them into their own personal-visual world...Given how potent visual images are for human beings, I think introducing strong visual elements is not without pitfalls.
    \nThis is really interesting to me. I think the "personal-visual world" is a bigger source of pitfalls; I find that it takes a lot of communication overhead to keep everyone on the same page, or at least on compatible pages, and I really appreciate anything that increases the sharing level of the imagined space.

    With the picture of the super-hot / ugly jerk, in my experience there's not a risk in that misunderstanding. Everyone at the table (assuming they're all sighted) have the same objective facts. The subjective facts (hot or not?) are still up to the player / character. I'd say that subjective facts and judgments of the situation aren't really part of the SIS.

    (I don't mean to be argumentative here or to say that you're wrong. I'd just never thought about the objective/subjective divide in the SIS before and wanted to get it off my chest.)

    \n I didn't end up getting the opportunity to run it but I brought 14 nicely-painted PC miniatures up to GPNW intended for World of Dungeons. My idea was not to use them much for map/spatial/tactical activity outside of marching order and to get a general sense of where people were at, but primarily for character creation. The tiny details of the miniatures were to serve a function similar to Everway's Vision cards or Apocalypse World's name/look lists where creative constraint hopefully spurs narrative detail - starting situation stepping stones. What does it mean that this warrior's shield is blue with a heron? Or that this wanderer has a low-slung mustard-yellow satchel? What might be in it?

    Not as much application to game design here, maybe, but more to GM-craft.

    I think about this topic a lot and even more so since I recently got a tablet.
