    \n Daniel, there's some great APs on the Forge. Try my game [Best Friends] At Dragonmeet and Ron Edwards' game [Best Friends] Larceny, hatred, and supportive actualization (and I think there's another thread on that game, so seek that out too).

    Best tips, from memory, are:
    * Use two friendchips, not three
    * The character creation is great fun. Linger over it.
    * Encourage your players to frame conflicts against each other, not against NPCs. Use phrases like "You'll put out the fire anyway. The question is: do you make yourself look better than someone else as you do that?"
    * Remember that the players are actually friends: they don't just hate each other
    * Frame aggressively. Fade the scene as soon as it's dull. Loads of this game, for me, was down to scene framing.

    Graham

    \n Graham is pretty much on the moeny with his comments. The most successful and fun games of BF I've played in are the ones where the conflcits were totally keyed into the relationships between the characters (a game about strippers in Atlanta and one about the wives of Roman senators and generals being particularly memorable). When it starts to drift into conflicts being about things other than stuff going on between the characters, then the game is less successful.

    Grahams comment about framing conflicts against each other and not NPC/situations in particularly good. Even if an NPC is the focus of the conflict, the conflict itself should be between two or more of the friends. Sharon wants to seduce Jim. Why? Because it wwill make her look cool in front of her friends. But where's the conflict? Between her an Tracey, who also has affection for Jim. Jim himself is pretty much immaterial in the conflict, its the push/pull between the two girls and the small war that they are having to win over Jim that's key. He has no decision making power, it's all about the girls.

    Cheers
    Malcolm
