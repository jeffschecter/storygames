\n Hi all,
\n
\nI'm preparing to GM "How we came to live here" for the first time and I got slightly stuck. I have some ideas on how to challenge Skill and Strength in a conflict, but (as an Outside or Inside Player) how would you challenge a hero's Faith and, especially, Spirit?
\n
\nFor example: Faith could be contested when an NPC tries to seduce a hero (fidelity), or when an NPC tries to convince a hero to commit a crime (tradition), but if the hero is defending themselves verbally, wouldn't Strength (outspokenness and general self-esteem, assertiveness) fit better? (I guess I could explain during scene setup that in this case the hero is outspoken enough to defend themselves and this is not the issue of the conflict; the issue at stake is whether their will is strong enough, in which case this becomes similar to Pendragon's emotional traits: roll to see if your character values his chastity enough to spurn the advances of the sorceress).
\n
\nThe only two ideas I have for challenging someone's Spirit is:
\na) have a hero loose their way, or even better, have them attempt to find someone who got lost/was kidnapped, and create a conflict around whether they can keep up hope (or love, or passion) long enough to find what they're looking for,
\nb) if a hero is a sorcerer/witch, have the success of their magic rely on the strength of their negative emotions (revenge, jealousy, anger, envy).
\n
\nHas this come up in any games that you played? What conflicts did you run and what pools did you use for them?
\n
\nThanks,
\nJane

    \n Hi Jane/Katarzyna!

    In my experience, the process of determining the relevant pool can be pretty organic. Conflicts will emerge and it will either be obvious which pool is involved, or there will be a short discussion:

    ME: This is totally a Strength conflict! He's resisting the magical stones!

    OUTSIDE GM: Right, he's resisting magic, fool, and that's a Spirit conflict!

    Now either could be correct, but after a arguing and negotiating one is chosen by the GM, hopefully without too much consideration of the character's numbers. Many conflicts could be easily assigned to two or even three possible attributes, so it is best to be flexible and go with the most colorful choice in the moment.

    My advice, based on playing a bunch, is not to worry too much about which pools will be involved, but rather to create situations (and conflicts) you and your friends are going to find interesting and challenging. The back-and-forth between player and GM about which pool to use is fun and a nice part of the game.

    \n Also, keep in mind that whichever player sets the scene gets to choose the pool. That means Hero Players choose the pool on their turn, while the Inside or Outside Player chooses it on their turn. Naturally, Hero Players will try to use a big pool on their turn, and the Inside and Outside Players will go for a smaller one.

    \n Sure!

    Let's take a look at the list of attributes for both pools:

    SPIRIT
    For women: Love, Revenge, Jealousy, Joy
    For men: Anger, Passion, Tenderness, Envy

    I see lots of potential conflicts here. Spirit is the pool of emotion, anything that makes a character run hot can be a Spirit conflict. Try getting a man angry. For both men and women, challenging or threatening someone they love, and certainly jealousy and envy offer up plenty of conflict ideas.

    FAITH
    For women: Kinship, Tradition, Secrecy, Fidelity
    For men: Loyalty, Camaraderie, Tradition, Respect

    Conflicts for faith involve community. Tradition is a great one, try to get the characters in a situation where the traditions of the village conflict with what they want to do. Kinship for women is also easy to bring in, threaten family members. For men, loyalty and camaraderie come in when they are working with other men in a team.

    Does this help a bit more?
