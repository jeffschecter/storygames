    \nCampaign Summary

    My goal stylistically for the campaign is to strike a nice balance between role-playing/storytelling and a good, old fashion dungeon crawl. I expect there to be a about a 40/60 split between the two things. That doesn't mean that every session will have that ratio but rather the ratio should hold up over the course of the whole game. Some sessions might be 90% dungeon crawling while another session might be 90% social interaction outside the dungeon or with denizens of said dungeon. Hopefully this kind of game appeals to you. If not, let's talk about it.

    I'd like people to post their ideas about characters they are wanting to play. What I'd especially like is for you as players to connect your characters together in a way that makes sense. Why are they traveling into the Stoneheart Dungeon? I can't see them as a collection of strangers who just happened to bump into each other and say, "Whoa! Let's go dungeon delving!" So, how do they know each other and why are they doing what they're doing? Which brings us to setting....

    It makes it easier on you guys to come up with cool things to do and reasons for doing them if you have some setting elements to hang your ideas on. I'm going to give you some of those. Please, if you have any ideas that go beyond the ones I list, feel free to express them. The setting as it stands right now is sketchy and only a loose framework to get you started. I'd like to see the setting really develop during play with most of it coming from the ideas of the players.

    Setting Elements

    * Abysthor, High Priest of Thyr - Received a vision from his god and disappeared into the Stoneheart Dungeon some 15 years ago.
    * Thyr - God of Justice
    * Muir - Goddess of Virtue (Paladins)
    * The worship of Thyr and Muir have slowly decline over the last decades. They have lost followers to the newer gods who are not quite so strict in their worship requirements. The Stoneheart Dungeon used to be a burial place for their heroic dead but with the decline of their sects, the place has fallen into corruption.
    * The tomb of Alaric, Paladin of Muir - A long dead hero with his tomb located somewhere in the Stoneheart Dungeon.
    * A tribe of Gnolls - There is a tribe of Gnolls living in the area of the Stoneheart Dungeon somewhere.
    * A young dragon - A young dragon has been spotted in recent years and has probably established a lair nearby.
    * A beholder - Rumors of a beholder lairing in the area have cropped up lately.
    * Cult of Orcus - Evil followers of the demonlord of the Undead have taken up residence in the Stoneheart Dungeon and are desecrating the burial sites located there.
    * The city of Bard's Gate is concerned about the increased activity of monsters and evil men in the vicinity of the Stoneheart Dungeon. The location is only a day or so travel away in the nearby Valley of the Shrines in the Stoneheart Mountains. This kind of activity rarely bodes well.

    Other than these things, the setting is fairly wide open. Feel free to add stuff or expand things already listed.

    Characters should be made with the Standard Point Buy system with 28 points. All characters should start at an Effective Character Level of 2 and should have the standard wealth of characters at that level, which is 900 gold pieces. You can have any items that you can afford with 900 gold that are listed in the DMG. I am not banning any sourcebooks. However, if you have a race or class you'd like to play that isn't listed in the PHB or DMG, then you need to ask me about it. I'm fairly easy going about that kind of thing as long as it isn't too bizarre/powerful.

    \nHouserule - Drives & Conviction

    Drives

    Player Characters each choose 4 Drives. At least one of your Drives must deal directly with another Player Character whose player is at the table. Drives change over time; you will have many opportunities to change and redefine your character's Drives over the life of the campaign.

    Drives define your character's motivations and connections to other individuals. These are the things that are most important to the character, and they help the other players understand your character. The GM will also use them to create situations that are relevant to your character.

    Make sure your characters\u2019 drives relate to other characters (both those of other players and those taken by the GM) and to the overall theme of the game. Drives are the first and biggest input into a session, so this is absolutely essential to a good game.

    Drives are the most powerful tool both you and the GM can use to tie your character into the story. You should make sure that at least one (or better, two) of your character\u2019s drives relate to another player character or to the main plotline of the game (this last must be done with the group\u2019s help, of course).

    You may choose multiple Drives of the same type.

    A character's Drives should come from this list.

    Duty: A duty is something that your character feels obligated to do, for whatever reason, but does not truly enjoy. \u201cMust\u201d is a great word to describe a duty. Examples: I must take care of my friends; I must never let my brother down.

    Hate: A hate motivates your character through his loathing and longing to destroy something or someone. Your character will do what he can to harm, hinder, or otherwise damage the object of his hate. This attack need not be overt, by any means - one could hate an organization and infiltrate them to undermine their operations from within, or be a false friend to your enemy's son. Examples: I must destroy the vampire that killed my wife, I will bring down the corrupt city watch, I will get even with King Adstone.

    Love: A love is someone or something that your character cares deeply about. Your character will willingly make sacrifices on behalf of her love, and will help and protect the object of her love. Of course, just because you love something doesn\u2019t mean it loves you back. Love can also inspire jealousy. Love can be directed at an individual, a group, an activity, some abstract concept (art, for example), one\u2019s self, etc. Examples: Bahamut is my reason for living and I live to serve his divine order; there is no higher calling than to see what's over the next horizon; I would follow our commander to the ends of the earth; My land is a place worth fighting for.

    Atonement: Your character has done something very bad and now works to help others so he or she might be redeemed. This Drive can bring the character's motives into question - are they still working for the good or have they gone back to their old ways?

    Dream: Dreams are long-term goals with many steps and possibilities along the way. The player of a character with a Dream should also think about what would happen if the dream was fulfilled, or if they were forced to abandon the Dream.

    Fate: A Fate is the most unusual Drive from a player's perspective, because it motivates the player and often not the character. A character who is always getting into trouble despite his best efforts has a Fate. A character who does good despite his instincts because he is put in situations where his morals come out is also driven by Fate. Having a fate means players will try to find ways drive their character towards the Fate even if the character themselves drive away from them.

    Fear: A fear is something that terrifies your character and motivates her quite strongly out of aversion. Examples: I never want to split the party; I am terrified of enchanters and the danger of being mind-controlled; I will do whatever it takes to keep Gondor from facing war; I don't want to ever be sober; I need to be in control.

    Self-Destruction: A character with this drive is having trouble coming to terms with some part of themselves. This may have to do with their social status, race, class, appearance, past, or emotions. Regardless, the character puts themselves in harm's way and sometimes burns bridges.

    Self-Worth: For this character self-worth is a real battle. This can work well for characters that start in a "sidekick" role, unsure of how they contribute to the group.

    Temptation: This character walks a fine line, constantly tempted by something that's pretty much universally considered bad, such as a violent temper, abuse of power, drugs, gambling, and so on. The character struggles against this temptation but can't be completely removed from it.

    Conviction and XP:

    After a Scene check the Session sheet. For each Drive on the Session sheet that was addressed in that scene, award each character in the group 1 Conviction point and 100XP.

    Uses of Conviction:
    Pts Effect
    1: Roll twice, take the best result (declare before roll)
    1: Re-roll any die (declare after roll)
    1: Take an extra move-equiv action on your turn
    1: Get 1 more use of a x/day ability (including spell slots).
    1: Change an existing Drive.
    2: Take an extra standard action on your turn.

    You can also use a Conviction Point to establish a small fact about the setting. If you need 50' of rope and forgot to buy it in town, spend a point and have the rope. Want to know a barmaid in the next tavern? Spend a point. Spending points in this way cannot allow you to bypass an obstacle or challenge but can give you a bonus by having the correct equipment or there being something advantageous in the environment around.

    Thyr - God of Justice
    Domains: Law, Good, Healing, Knowledge, Protection
    Typical Worshipers: Humans, Royalty
    Favored Weapons: Light or Heavy Mace

    Thyr is the god of wise and just rule. He is normally depicted as a wized king seated ona great throne holding a rod of kingship in one hand and a chalice of peace in the other hand. His principles are Justice, Order and Peace. He represents proper and traditional rule and as such was once worshiped (at least with lip service) by all human royalty. He is the embodiment of the enlightened human caste system where each person failty has their place in a lawfully ordered society aimed at the good of all people. The noble eagle and the lion are his sacred creatures.
    \n

    Muir - Goddess of Virtue (Paladins)
    Domains: Law, Good, Protection, War
    Typical Worshipers: Humans, Paladins
    Favored Weapons: Longswords or Bastard Swords

    Muir is the sister of Thyr. Where he represents Law and Peace, she represents the martial valor necessary to make that peace a reality. As such, she is the goddess of paladins. She is often depicted as a dark-tressed maiden warrior in shining mail with an upraised sword. The tenets of her worship are Honor, Truth and Courage. A great order of paladins known as The Justicars are sworn to her service. She expects self-sacrifice, humility and charity as well as unswerving loyalty. Her standards are extreme and she quickly turns her back on any who fail to live up to them. The falcon is her sacred animal.
    \nThat's what I have on the pantheon. Orcus is around in the module as well.

I think that should probably take 3 to 6 months of 1 time a week play depending on how quickly people shoot through things. If everyone is very focused then it will go faster. If everyone is more relaxed and meandering, then longer.

    \nAs pointed out above, it comes from Complete Scoundrel. Skill tricks are bought with skill points, and are cross class for every class (so, they cost 2 points each). You can only have one skill trick for every two levels of character class.

    On p 87 of Complete Scoundrel, Hidden Blade basically allows me to hide an item on my person using Sleight of Hand (a dagger in Haakon's case). If an opponent cannot spot the hidden weapon, I can draw it using a move action and attack that opponent with a standard action, and the opponent is considered flat-footed against that attack (which works nicely with sneak attack. I just like the image of the dagger popping into Haakon's hand and plunging into an opponent's chest before he knows what happened. There is another skill trick called Mosquito's Bite where the opponent won't even realize he's been hit until after his next action. He has to act like I missed.

    \n Haakon's 3x3 Card

    Allies

    Gaerik Battleborn -- An old dwarf warrior that is now the de facto leader of the dwarves living in Bard's Gate. Once feared for his axe, Gaerik is now a canny merchant whose trading company often hires adventurers to guard his caravans. While wealthy enough to be one of the rulers of Bard's Gate, Gaerik despises human politics.

    Grilk -- A rather dim half-orc who came to worship Thyr as a way of seeking justice for the wrongs done to him as a child. Grilk is happiest when someone can give him orders, and likes to "make 'em all better!" (Healing is his domain.) His friendship with Haakon seems unlikely, until you see the two of them drinking at a tavern. Often they're the only two left standing at the end of the night.

    Olhilda Sigbj\xf6rnsdottir -- Haakon's wife-to-be for the last 10 years (dwarven courtship rituals are complicated.) Haakon's marriage was arranged before his falling out with his family, but Olhilda has convinced her family to not dissolve the agreement yet. He has some time to prove that he is still a good match for her. Olhilda is quite a beauty for a dwarf, and works with clockwork mechanisms in her father's shop.

    Contacts

    Doraedim Mythlord -- An Elven bard/rogue who runs a gambling establishment in Bard's Gate and often passes along interesting tidbits of information to other people... for a price. Everyone who wants information from him, however, must play a game of dice with the elf, "double or nothing." It's rumored that Doraedim is a master at swapping in loaded dice for this game, but if you accuse him of cheating he will never sell information to you again... and will sell information about you to others at a discount.

    Chalgzk -- Perhaps the unluckiest gnoll in existence. Chalgzk was born with a clubfoot and a disfigured face, which led to him being outcast from the local gnoll tribe. He found "employment" for a time in a traveling carnival freakshow. It wasn't much, but it meant regular food and shelter. At least until he was "set free" by well-meaning townspeople. Free to starve, Chalgzk became an enforcer for a small gang... until that gang was arrested for smuggling. Chalgzk now holds the unenviable position of the lowest member of the Bard's Gate underworld... but he hears things. He makes a living doing dirty jobs that no one else wants, and most people underestimate his intelligence due to his race and appearance. Haakon has found the gnoll a useful resource on occasion.

    Harold Westingham III -- A rich human merchant who is constantly paranoid about the safety of his treasures. He often hires Haakon ("the little man" he calls him) to test the traps and locks that he purchases to keep his vault safe. Why the ever-paranoid merchant trusts a greedy dwarf is anyone's guess, but Haakon has managed to resist the temptation to steal anything -- so far. Harold owns a large amount of land outside of Bard's Gate, worked by tenants, and is concerned about the increase in monstrous activity.

    Enemies

    Svala -- A female human barbarian who worked with Haakon on an earlier job. Haakon was inexperienced at the time and did not properly disarm a trap that went off in Svala's face, scarring her. The rest of the party left Svala for dead, but she recovered and has been hunting down the party members ever since. Haakon is the last one left.

    Alfr Arnsson -- A dwarven transmuter who seeks to become Olhilda's husband. The son of a rich family (second only to the Battleborns), Alfr was delighted to hear of Haakon's disgrace and is working behind the scenes to make the young dwarf an outcast. While he considers himself a ladies' man, with rich clothes, heavy perfume, and oiled beard, (Alfr is something of a dandy, for a dwarf), he is held in contempt by many dwarven females for his male chauvinist ways and foul temper.

    Laerad -- A mysterious human druid in the wilds outside Bard's Gate, Laerad appears to be an old man, with a long white beard and tanned weathered skin. He wields a staff of ash and commands many animals and plant creatures. He likes to appear as an old hermit to travelers. Those who respect nature and offer friendship to the "hermit" can gain his protection. Haakon called him an "old fool of a vagrant" and booted him out of the camp. Now Haakon often finds his gear attacked by small animals, scorpions in his boots, and other natural "accidents" happening whenever he ventures out of the city.

      MOHIEL NYSTEDI
      Female 1st Level Aasimar Cleric of Thyr
      Speed 20\u2019, 9 Hit Points

      Background Mohiel hails from the small farming village of Wortham, about three days travel from Bard\u2019s Gate. Mohiel was born different, having porcelain-fine skin that contrasted greatly with hair and eyes the color of dark honey. She was raised in a farm family but was the object of scorn and suspicion, both for her looks and for her ability to learn quickly. There were none in the village her age that were her equal, and even members of her own family were in the habit of belittling her. Her unhappy life took a turn for the better when she caught the attention of a cleric of Thyr who was passing through the village on his way to Bard\u2019s Gate. Perhaps Thom Wilborne recognized her celestial heritage on sight, but for whatever reason he made arrangements for Mohiel to be tested for service in the name of Thyr. Mohiel passed the tests easily, and for the next eight years of her life she trained to become a Cleric in service to Thyr. Those years did much to heal the emotional wounds she had suffered in her childhood, and she made a handful of friends among the people of Bard\u2019s Gate. She also made a few enemies \u2013 someone as striking and capable as she seemed to ruffle the feathers of many traditionalists and chauvinists in town. Finally the High Priest of the Temple of Thyr considered Mohiel to be ready to serve the church directly, as an adventuring Cleric, and his charge to her was not easy: Her task was to purge the Stoneheart Dungeon of the Cult of Orcus. She felt daunted by the enormity of it. She didn\u2019t even know where to start, or if she was even capable of putting her Clerical knowledge and abilities to that sort of practical use. Yet, because of the eerie dreams and visions which visited her from time to time, she felt that this duty was somehow ordained for her, and she resigned to do it.

      Drives
      Duty Purge the Stoneheart Dungeon of the Cult of Orcus. She truly doubts that she is capable of fulfilling this duty, but it is what her superiors expect of her. There may even be an element of fear at play here, as well.

      Self-Worth Mohiel suffers from nagging thoughts of inadequacy stemming from her depressing treatment by family and villagers during her childhood.

      Love Mohiel has strong sisterly feelings for Zaxa. A strange dichotomy is at play within Mohiel\u2019s heart. On the one hand, she can identify with Zaxa \u2013 as a woman who doesn\u2019t fit the mold, and even as a woman that is shunned, avoided, or ridiculed by \u201ccommon\u201d folk. On the other hand, Zaxa seems utterly lost and fascinated with dark, evil things \u2013 Mohiel believes, perhaps foolishly, that Zaxa is in need of redemption and that such redemption can only come about through compassion.

      Fate Has dreams or visions that indicate she will play a central role in restoring the strength of the Thyrrian faith. Her own struggle with self-worth causes her to dismiss these \u201cdaydreams\u201d. What she does not know is that she is a blood descendant of Alaric the Paladin, who himself was a Planetouched. The branch of Alaric\u2019s line that Mohiel comes from has faded into obscurity since his death, and she is the first of that line for the Celestial blood to resurface.

      \n
      \n
      ActivationTo activate a scroll, a spellcaster must read the spell written on it. Doing so involves several steps and conditions.
      \n
      \nDecipher the WritingThe writing on a scroll must be deciphered before a character can use it or know exactly what spell it contains. This requires a read magic spell or a successful Spellcraft check (DC 20 + spell level).
      \n
      \nDeciphering a scroll to determine its contents does not activate its magic unless it is a specially prepared cursed scroll. A character can decipher the writing on a scroll in advance so that he or she can proceed directly to the next step when the time comes to use the scroll.
      \n
      \nActivate the SpellActivating a scroll requires reading the spell from the scroll. The character must be able to see and read the writing on the scroll. Activating a scroll spell requires no material components or focus. (The creator of the scroll provided these when scribing the scroll.) Note that some spells are effective only when cast on an item or items. In such a case, the scroll user must provide the item when activating the spell. Activating a scroll spell is subject to disruption just as casting a normally prepared spell would be. Using a scroll is like casting a spell for purposes of arcane spell failure chance.
      \n
      \nTo have any chance of activating a scroll spell, the scroll user must meet the following requirements.
      \n[ulist]
      \n[*]The spell must be of the correct type (arcane or divine). Arcane spellcasters (wizards, sorcerers, and bards) can only use scrolls containing arcane spells, and divine spellcasters (clerics, druids, paladins, and rangers) can only use scrolls containing divine spells. (The type of scroll a character creates is also determined by his or her class.)
      \n[*]The user must have the spell on his or her class list.
      \n[*]The user must have the requisite ability score.
      \n
      \nIf the user meets all the requirements noted above, and her caster level is at least equal to the spell\u2019s caster level, she can automatically activate the spell without a check. If she meets all three requirements but her own caster level is lower than the scroll spell\u2019s caster level, then she has to make a caster level check (DC = scroll\u2019s caster level + 1) to cast the spell successfully. If she fails, she must make a DC 5 Wisdom check to avoid a mishap (see Scroll Mishaps, below). A natural roll of 1 always fails, whatever the modifiers.
      \n
      \n
