\n So, I had this little idea a wile back after reading a thread on the Forge about a dude who was burned out on prepping for D&D. See, the thing is, I like prepping for D&D. I love doing the math on all the NPCs, working out neat little tactical challenges, interactions of moster abilities and scenery, planning cool ambushes and neat descriptions of the backdrop and the bad guys. But it is frustrating, doing all this prep and then not using it, or worse, railroading the PCs into your dungeon just because you've done so much work on it. So I thought of this idea. The players have bought into the idea of dungeon delving, by virtue of playing the game. They want to be facing level appropriate challenges with a focus on combat, or they wouldn't be playing D&D. But, if they're good little protagonists, they also want to be making choices about their characters' goals, and having those choices impact on the direction of the game. They don't care if they're plumbing the Caves of Chaos, or storming the Castle of Night, so long as they're doing it for some purpose they can all get behind.

Introducing: "Conflict resolution dungeons!" The PCs want to free the Kingdom of Lancre. The Tyrant Wyrm wants to stop them. Conflict! How do we resolve this conflict? A dungeon! It can be any dungeon. It can be a random dungeon. You could have prepped it years ago, so long as it's the right level. All you have to do is work out some reason why this Dungeon will resolve that conflict. Maybe the Tyrant Wyrm's enemy lives at the bottom of this dungeon, and if you can contact her, you can convince her to depose him. Maybe the dungeon is the back way into the Wyrm's lair. Maybe there's some artifact of great power down there. It doesn't matter. Maybe the PCs don't care. Ideally, they can come up with this themselves. They'll say "Let's find an artifact that can destroy the Tyrant" and you'll say "Great, there's one at the bottom of the Caves of Chaos" and bang, you're on the way. Sometimes though, the PCs will want to take on a problem that's too big for them. Simple! Make it a multi stage conflict. First, they must take the artifact from the Caves of Chaos, then (when they're suitably leveled up) they can creep through the Howling Mines into the Tyrant Wyrm's lair. Levelling becomes not just a grind, but a means to an ends.

How does this sound? Is it dumb? Or is it a cool way to a more player-driven D&D?

    \n Thanks Mike!

    Yeah, I don't think this is railroading, but I can see how it might look that way. A good point was made, though, that the dungeon encounter is unlikely to be related to the stakes of the conflict. Your players will be all "We want to drive the orcs out of North Woods" and they'll end up fighting Beholders in some cave somewhere. Sure, they'll accomplish their goal, but it might feel a bit weird, especially after a few sessions of this. I can see that being a problem. Here are some possible solutions:

    Prepping a lot of different scenarios: You've got a handful of options to choose from, so you can choose the one that matches the stakes as well as possible. That's not an ideal solution though, because with PCs levelling like they do in 3rd ed. your prep only has a usefulness window of three or four sessions. A whole bunch of wasted prep kind of defeats the purpose. Modules could definitely help with this though.

    Adapting your prep on the fly: It's easy to substitute orcs for gnolls, bugbears for ogres, and so on. It's less easy to substitute Mind Flayers for Beholders, or what have you. If you can pull a fast switch of the bad guys, this would help a lot. It's kind of defeating the purpose though. Maybe you could prep a few different alternatives, like writing "6 Gnolls, or 10 Orcs" in your dungeon notes, or noting the stats for an NPC as various different races. It might be a cool way to get really unconventional NPCs - an Orc Chief who was originally an Elf Princess, for example.

    Getting really good at Bullshitting: "Umm, yeah, well, the orcs are being led by a Beholder, yeah! and, this Beholder is taking orders from the main Beholder council! If the Beholders survive, they'll keep gathering orcs, no matter how many you kill. But if you can take out the Beholder Council, the orcs will scatter!" This is probably the best option. Actually, it might be best to let the players help with the bullshitting, so everyone's on board. Sometimes it's going to feel really forced, and then you can go with one of the above options, but I think you can go a long way with this option, and even get really cool convoluted plots into the bargain.

    Posted By: Simon CThanks Mike!

    Yeah, I don't think this is railroading, but I can see how it might look that way. A good point was made, though, that the dungeon encounter is unlikely to be related to the stakes of the conflict. Your players will be all "We want to drive the orcs out of North Woods" and they'll end up fighting Beholders in some cave somewhere. Sure, they'll accomplish their goal, but it might feel a bit weird, especially after a few sessions of this. I can see that being a problem.

    \nI think the best way to handle that is to just leave it up to the players. A "dungeon" is really just a set of challenges (monsters, traps) standing between the players and a plot device (the +5 Mystical MacGuffin, the cryptic clue uttered by the dying bad guy). If you really want to make things player-driven, have them come up with the plot stuff and you just concentrate on crafting a challenging dungeon (You are the Dungeon Master, after all! ) for them to find it in--perhaps reserving the right to throw in some plot twists, just to keep them on their toes.

    So it could be something like:

    PLAYERS: "We want to kick those filthy orcs out of the North Woods!"
    DM: "Ok, how?"
    ROGUE: "Well, I poke around the village for a while asking questions and eventually meet this old ranger who's been spying on the orc camp. He says that every night the shaman leaves the camp and heads off towards the mysterious caves deep in the forest."
    CLERIC: "I seem to recall a legend about an evil relic, the Tusk of the Orc God, that disappeared near here about a hundred years ago."
    FIGHTER: "I bet it's in that cave!"
    DM: "Ok, but if the orcs have this Tusk thingy, why are they living in a camp in the woods instead of in the cave?"
    ROGUE: "Obviously they don't have it. Someone--or something--living in that cave is using the relic to make the orcs do his evil bidding."
    CLERIC: "We must find this relic and destroy it!"
    FIGHTER: "No, you idiot, we give it to the orcs in exchange for leaving the North Woods in peace."
    ROGUE: "We can argue about that later. C'mon, guys, let's go check out that cave!"
    PLAYERS: "Yay!"

    Now all the DM has to do is pull "Cave Full of Mosters #3" out of his notebook (or whip one up using the Five Room Dungeon Model and off they go. No railroading, no spending half the session trying to motivate the players into going into the dungeon, just "What do you want to do? How is going into a dungeon going to help you accomplish that? Ok, let's go!"

    \n Something that's been brought up that I think is really interesting here, is the interaction between Characer Level and Dungeon Level, as a way of framing conflict difficulty. Obviously this is a bigger deal for D&D than other games, so I'm going to talk about D&D.

    I touched on this a bit in the first post, but I think it could do with some elaboration.

    One of the other problems that people commonly encounter with D&D is that levelling up makes the world all crazy. It has to keep getting more dangerous, or else the PCs aren't being challenged. Conversely, the players never really experience an increase in power, because they're always fighting level appropriate challenges.

    The obvious solution, using Conflict Resolution Dungeons, is to tie the scale of the conflict to the level of the dungeon. Small, local problems are solved by level one dungeons. World-shattering conflicts require level twenty dungeons.

    But that's not easy to do. It's hard to be consistent with scale like that. It becomes really tempting to just make any conflict around the right level for the PCs, which removes the incentive for levelling up. Is it possible to create some hard rules about dungeon level vs. conflict level? What about dungeon length? How many encounters makes a dungeon? Is it reasonable to one day save the world by slaying a single dragon, and the next day restore peace to the Kingdom by slogging through miles of Umber Hulk infested corridors?

    One thing I like about the idea is that it in no way prohibits you from making a specific-use dungeon if that's your wish. If the PCs are building up to a climactic show-down, there's nothing wrong with prepping a specific series of encounters for the final conflict. I think the game would even benefit from that.
