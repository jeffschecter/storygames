\n Dave's quote from the Awesomify Traveller thread made me think about a wider question:\n
Posted By: Dave HallettMy problem back in the old days was that it was very difficult for the GM to provide enough detail about anything beyond a starship interior and maybe a spaceport (though most of those were depressingly "standard" IIRC) to make the game evocative enough to appeal to me. Particularly given the PCs freedom of action (and the games where you didn't have that didn't feel right, somehow). 11000 worlds, etc.
\nI think my problem with the old-school games (defined here as those based more on miniatures rules than narrative structure) is that the sheer number of rules and the fact that all movement and combat was square/hex/inches-based made me spend so much time trying to understand and follow all of it that is became a rules-focused game rather than a story-focused game. I felt like I had to be able to map something and add encounters to it to even play a game of Traveller (or D&D or Gamma World). I feel like Traveller in particular was all about deck plans and encounter tables and not enough about story arcs and even *adventure*.

One of the things I like about relatively rules-lite games is that they abstract a lot and trust you as the gamer to fill in the blanks. Even a game like BW throws away encounter tables and GP-counting and abstracts things in a great way with Circles and Resources Tests. I find that incredibly freeing as a player and a GM.

Is this just an issue of "Chris as a scaredy-cat 13 year-old" vs. "Chris as a creatively-burgeoning 38 year-old"? Or is there a truth here that more abstraction equals more creativity?

I have to admit I'm not totally up on a lot of the hardcore RPG theory, so this may already be a heavily-treaded path. But indulge me if you will. I'm asking this because, like many of you, I look back to those games and want to play them and want them to *work*.

    Posted By: Andy...however, too much abstraction and you are forced to creatively come up with the rest. This is very fun (for some)... andVERY TIRING.
    \nVery good point, Andy. Playing or GMing off-the-cuff can take it out of you, even though I enjoy it a lot.

    When I was taking improv earlier this year, the instructor always did several things.
    1. The start of class was all about warmups. Get used to the space around you. Get into the improv groove.
    2. The games were all intense but short. Improv folks weren't expected to do any long form work until they had been practicing for awhile (like months or years).
    3. A minority of the class was usually performing for a majority. That gave everyone plenty of rest time to enjoy others' contributions and recharge the batteries before the next insanity.

    An interesting parallel, perhaps. Maybe abstracted story games really do need things like solid buy-in, shorter sessions and defined mental breaks for players *and* GM. And you are especially right about GM-less games. Everyone at the table needs to step up for those or they fall flat.

    I think we assume these things sometimes, but I for one certainly run into players or groups who come to the table and expect to be entertained. They aren't used to putting out a lot creatively and aren't always ready for it when it comes. Even with experienced folks, you give them the spirit stick and you maybe get a sentence or two before they're saying "roll the dice!!"

    This is a real strength of the "all creation all the time" mode, in my opinion. Everyone is so engaged on every cylinder that you get a satisfying evening of roleplay in like two hours rather than four or six or eight; you can play the short form and it doesn't feel like an abbreviated form. That means, among other things, you can have a "let's hang out" evening and incidentally play games during it, rather than having a whole evening set apart for roleplay. It's good.
