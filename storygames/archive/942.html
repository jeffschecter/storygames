I've been thinking about Thomas' thread about chaotic play: where a subset of the gaming group can play the game without needing the whole group. It's particularly suited for online play, where you could have a large gaming group, and the story progresses by small groups playing together in short bursts.

How about everything in the gaming world has traits? Those traits can be anything - for a character, a trait might be "I'm a good shot"; for a bag, it might be "Made of leather". Each trait has an associated number of dice.

Each player has a steady of supply of dice which they can "spend": they can create new traits, create new items or add dice to existing traits.

(This is very like Universalis, of course).

You can also attack traits. To do this, you pit a trait under your control against the trait you want to attack. Then you make some sort of dice roll between the traits to determine who wins. If you successfully attack the trait, you may rewrite it or remove it.

So, for example, let's say you've built a house with traits of "Sturdy", "Pleasant" and "Surrounded by grass". My character is an ogre, with traits of "Stupid", "Strong" and "Huge". Even though you're not currently signed into the game, I attack your house's "Sturdy" trait with my "Strong" trait. When you next sign in, you find that your house is now "Rickety", "Pleasant" and "Surrounded by grass".

You'd need some kind of bullshit call, of course, so that I can't attack your "Surrounded by grass" trait with my "Stupid" trait without a very good reason. So perhaps you can query the attack, in some way, and your query gets emailed around some other players to see if they agree.

This is all a bit thrown together, of course. But any comments / expansions / other ideas so far?

    This seems to be the common theme with all of this line of thinking...moderation.

    That is, who decides what can be used against what reasonably? I see three options that have emerged:

    1. Anything goes. The problem with this is that we're all, I think rightly, concerned that play may become too "chaotic" if it goes like this. If one player steps out of a sense of complete co-operation, the floodgates could open, and then it's all over. In any case, this would probably feel as flimsy as most freeform.

    2. Hard-coded moderation. Where X can affect Y, and such rules, and nobody can say no to this. The question is, why not simply play a MMORPG? That is, what's unique about TT RPGs is that there's a judge who makes calls on what's suitable, and that opens the world up to larger possibilities than an MMORPG can deal with. So this seems like throwing the baby out with the bathwater.

    3. Participant moderated. This is problematic, because, by the definition of the idea, players with an interest in any particular element may not be present to call BS. And having a GM is likely not possible, unless there is a corp of GMs (and even then what if one isn't around). Now we've god Moderated MUDs (MUSH, whaever), essentially.

    For Uniwiki, essentially the central authority is between all players moderated with Coins, and was somewhat of a hassle in an essentially asynchronous environment. Though not a completely unusable format.

    This seems to be the main problem with this concept is that none of these solutions is optimal. Does anyone have an option that's outside of these three, or a modification of one that would work really well? One that allows for participant judgement, but doesn't require a GM, or asynchronous communication, or a group of privileged individuals who might not be available?

    Perhaps you could recruit neutral third parties somehow? Who would get rewarded for making reasonable judgements? The difficult part here is determining when somebody is actually neutral. Though that might be possible to set mechanically.

    Of course, that would be rather asynchronous: sometimes a ruling mightn't occur until 24 hours after the event. Perhaps you could use some sort of dreamlike setting to assist this: an Alice-in-Wonderland setting, where you think you attacked a house but, then, you go back the next day and it's all the same.

    Or perhaps the ruling wouldn't actually get overturned but, if the three players decide the rule was used badly, the player who was affected gets some resource to spend. Perhaps they get to change a trait back.

    (None of this is very satisfying so far, I have to admit.)

    The problem for me, so far, is that there's no reason for players to do anything except for go round bashing each others' traits.

    There probably needs to be some sort of relationship map. Perhaps characters need objectives as well as traits, in some way. Perhaps other players could be rewarded for responding to other people's objectives: a sort of fan mail thing or payment for doing GM duties.
