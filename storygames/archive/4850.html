    \n There must be elements that you can rewrite for flavor that interact with the core mechanics, and the mechanics must be able to handle all kinds of tasks/conflicts. Then there are often already freely definable elements (freeform traits, definable skills, create-your-own-power guidelines).

    SOTC has aspects that are freely definable, and the stunts are those rewritable elements. Most of the time you can just rename them.
    TSOY's keys and secrets are rewritable elements while the core resolution is universal. Its skills are definable elements.
    Beast Hunters' domains and tattoos are rewritable while the traits are freely definable.

    In all cases, the core resolution system stays intact while you can completely change the flavor and color of the game.

    \n I think there are many games that invite / require the players to explore and fiddle with certain mechanical elements before play can be fully realized. One traditional way to do it is to have parts of the game world that are never given mechanics (there's this ancient vampire clan, but we're not going to publish official rules for them). Another is to leave holes in the setting and mechanics for players to fill in (tales are told of the far western lands, where people breathe fire). Another is to have guidelines that allow players to create their own setting elements and give mechanical weight to them. Another is to create a general genre-specific set of mechanics, but allow groups to layer different color on top of it (I'm thinking about My Life With Master and The Mountain Witch here, which don't have setting in a traditional fashion). So, yeah, lots of options.

    \n After Gen Con 2005, Ron drew up a diagram of various narrativist roleplaying games with interconnecting arrows, and circulated it a bit among some of the designers whose games appeared on it. After Gen Con 2006 he updated it somewhat. It's an ontology, albeit one without much general public value, as the arrows between the boxes lack descriptors that would tell what relationship is being indicated. You have to intuit the relationships from knowing the games and knowing how Ron thinks about them.

    But an observation I've heard made about the diagram is that the games on the left branch tend to inspire being hacked for different settings (Sorcerer, DitV, TSoY, Riddle of Steel, etc.), and the games on the right branch tend to inspire designers to adapt their mechanical concepts into designs for new games (MLwM, PtA, Universalis, Capes, etc.). From what I recall of the diagram, this was a pretty accurate observation. But what I'm not remembering is Ron's underlying organizational principle behind the first fork in the ontology. It may have had something to do with the distribution of traditional GM authority. Which is rather interesting to consider. Is hacking, in the traditional sense, an extension of traditional GM responsibilities?

    \n Systems are more hackable than games, IME -- and, yes, there is a difference. To paraphrase Steve Jackson, systems are sets of rules that one uses to do things and games are things that you do.

    A game is self-contained and, thus, must have a built-in premise and defined goal set. Many things marketed as games are, in fact,.systems (or, unfinished games, if you like). SotC is a good example. It's a system. Part of a game. In order for one to play SotC, however, you need to add a premise and goal set of your own design, thus turning it into a game.

    The thing that makes systems more hackable than games is that, out of the box, systems are more widely applicable to different situations and premises rather than being confined to one specific situation or premise. It's the difference between, say, Burning Wheel (a system) and The Mountain Witch (a game).

    \n All systems are hackable.

    Whether or not they will be *treated* and *perceived* as hackable depends largely on the community that grows up around the game.

    Fate co-opted the Fudge community in large part, and the Fudge community is built around a game that has been defined from the get-go as very hackable: in fact, that's the point.

    But there are other factors going into it. Creators can:

    - Put obvious flags into their game showing where it's hackable

    - Explain why their game is built the way it is so the reader who wishes to hack the game can proceed with confidence that he understands the effects his hack will have on the system

    - Respond to proposed hacks on message boards in a way that says "Hey, you've done a cool thing with my game, rock out, here are some more ideas for what you can do with it"

    - Etc

    All of this ultimately though is about creating the culture around your game. Is the culture one where the creator and/or system are held to be an authority, or a collaborator?

    Hacks arise from collaboration.

    Posted By: iagoAll systems are hackable.

    Whether or not they will be *treated* and *perceived* as hackable depends largely on the community that grows up around the game.
    \nAnd then the question becomes "why does a hacking-oriented community grow up around this game and not that one?"

    Which I think really is a different question altogether. I've seen (and done) a lot of D&D hacking in my time -- when I started playing D&D back in the early 80s it was the only game in town. If you wanted to play, say, a sci-fi game or a western game you could go out and buy one, but getting your group to play it was by no means assured. It was much less risky to just mess around with the D&D rules until you'd mangled them into something reasonably sci-fi- or western-like and then pitch it to your group as "this is pretty much just like D&D except..."

    I don't think there was anything in the D&D rules themselves that made it particularly hackable; what made it hackable was its ubiquity and familiarity.

    Maybe there's something like that going on with other "hackable" games. Maybe the game you're most likely to hack is the game you're familiar with, the game you keep going back to, the game you want to keep playing so much that you're willing to turn it into another game in order to do so.

    What factors do you think enhances a game's hackability?
    \nSystems that are extensible in hierarchies of optional complexity without compromising their root mechanics.

    For example, the d20 system's basic mechanic has many, many extensions, but it's possible to ignore most of these in favor of fundamental mechanics without seriosuly contradicting the results one might get from the extension. You can use the complex rules for Computer Use, or simply mandate a simple check against a DC, for instance.

    Virtually every successful game -- espefcially those that are commonly accused of incoherence -- make use of this feature. They play best when the players understand this and can make informed choices about using the system, and game designers are generally bad at explaining this feature -- in my opinion, because of a misplaced sense of authority over the text.

    This is in terms of gameplay, though. There's a whole sub-hobby of hacking systems without any realistic prospect of playing them (whether the hobbyists will admit it or not) but I really don't give a shit about it.

    \n I think Situation is an important part of what makes a game hackable (or, really, hackworthy, since everything is hackable). Dogs in the Vineyard is a really good system for playing out a certain situation, i.e. independent people who are empowered to make life-or-death decisions for a whole community that is in crisis. That's a pretty specific situation, but it's easy to come up with multiple examples.

    It helps that this situation is one that is really common in popular culture; there's always a hero or two coming to town and cleaning things up. I think the color and even some specific rules might need to be tweaked, but the situation is pretty similar whether your character is a Dog, a Jedi, a Vampire Archon, a Norse warrior-priest, a superhero, or whatever. You can plug all that specific stuff in, then let the V8 game engine that Lumpley built drive it down the road.

    For folks like me who love to create but suck at creating mechanics, this is hot stuff.

    Don't Rest Your Head is another game which posits a very specific situation and backs it up with some really clever mechanics. It would be pretty easy to change the setting significantly, or do a complete rules mod. I think Bailywolf on RPG.Net came up with a very neat version of the game for Vampires; sadly, I haven't been able to play the original game yet and I think it's usually a good idea to try it "out of the box" before changing everything.
