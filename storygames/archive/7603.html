\n Hello!

I need some help. I'm designing a system that's kind of like the Pool (well, Anti-Pool, strictly speaking). It's close enough in important bits that we can pretend that's what it is.

I'd like to refine a mechanic where each character has a secondary Pool called "Desperation Dice" or something similar.

The idea is that you don't normally roll those dice, but in desperate situations, you may choose to. You have to have your character do something desperate to roll them. In every other way, they act just like regular dice.

My question is, if I put that in just as written (above), will it be enough? How can I make this work?

I want:

1. The dice are rolled only rarely, and in desperate situations.
2. The player really does need to come up with something pretty drastic or desperate to roll those dice.
3. Rolling them is exciting, but nerve-wracking.

Any advice? Questions that might be helpful?

Thanks in advance, story-people.

    \n A mechanic like this depends on how creatively synergistic you expect the group to be. If you are assuming that the group is creatively synergistic then I think the mechanic will work just fine. You've assigned narrative meaning to those dice to underscore certain fictional elements. The creatively synergistic group will understand that. They will know that when they want to say something there is the tool to say it and they will reach for those dice only when they want to say that thing.

    However, the less creatively synergistic the group is the more this risks devolving into comedy or awkward situations. A player who doesn't see the set aside dice as a narrative tool will just hear, "Act Desperate = Get More Dice." And so they will start grandstanding in inappropriate times and places when they just want something. "I"m DESPERATE for her love! Give me the dice."

    In that case the mechanic usually requires more "crutches." Like some kind of large risk or counter weight associated with those dice.

    That's been my observation anyway.

    Jesse

    Darcy and Graham,

    You've actually reminded me of a mechanic I used to use with the Pool which I called the Doom mechanic. In it, each character had a particular Doom: a dark fate they faced. The Doom rating started at one. Any time you wanted, you could add your Doom rating (in dice) to a roll, but if the three lowest dice rolled were equal to your Doom rating or lower, your Doom would immediately take place.

    However, this particular game is intended to be a little more light-hearted/friendly, and I definitely don't want character death to be an option.

    This is giving me an idea, however... I will report on it once it's "congealed".

    In the meantime, it creates two followup questions:

    1. Could adding in-game effects to rolling the dice (as in the examples above, where rolling a "1" means character death or a similarly serious consequence) actually _detract_ from the in-fiction context? For instance, could being so focused on the die results draw attention away from the fictional conflict and the character's desperation?

    I mean, if just rolling that die means I risk death, I might not be too concerned about what I narrate to go along with the roll.

    2. What kinds of characters (or players) should have more/fewer Desperation dice?

    Posted By: Paul T.Levi,

    That sounds interesting--can you explain what you mean? Do you mean that the characters would lose and gain traits as a result?
    \nMaybe? Typically by way of a resource, mind... Ah, hell examples needed. Let's look at a couple of games.

    In DitV, you gamble with fallout at stake - when you push, your "raising" with the possibility for more of it. Fallout can require you to change your traits.

    In Hunter: The Reckoning, you wagered Conviction when you used a power. If the power failed, Conviction was lost. If you succeeded, conviction was gained. Get enough conviction, and it translated into trait change.

    I'm not saying that either of those mechanics is the one you want. I AM saying that "gambling your character sheet" is a good way to get the feel of desperation; when you have the option (but aren't required) to put permanent change "on the line" in a contest, it creates intensity of a sort - if you can fine-tune that to get just the right kind of intensity, I suspect you'd be golden.

    And no, I don't quite know how. All I know is to wave, generally, thataway.
