Splitting BIG games into Little games

Andy
January 2006 edited January 2006 in Story Games
I'm a slow reader, it's something I lament. it affects gaming a bit, in terms of my "reading the rules to being able to turn around and run a game" ratio.

Here's something that I was thinking of the other day:

Traditional Games:

Everyone has a basic understanding of the rules, but the GM/DM is expected to know all the rules, back and forth (and usually they do). The other players can sort of get by with a limited knoweldge of the rules, but usually pick up on this or that over time, or get involved with the rules to maximize the effectiveness in play or whatnot (Iron Heroes, The Riddle of Steel, etc).

"Story Games" (for lack of a better word)

Everyone HAS to know the rules. maybe at first, one (GM) or two people understand them, but in games like Polaris, Capes, Dogs, PTA, Sorcerer and the like, there's not much rules to begin with, but not knowing them after the first session or two, or actively not learning them, will absolutely dent your ability to get into the game.

Problem is that both of the above methods tends to cause a lot of "downtime" between Purchase and Play as the one person reads the game back and forth, and either rolls from there (Trad) or brings everyone else up to speed (Story).

What about making a game (the assumption here being that the game is going to be pretty lengthy in terms of rules, somewhere at or past Dogs in the Vineyard), that starts off with a distributed ruleset? Like, it comes with modular pamphlets or whatever, and it's expected that say 2-4 people will accept the role of GM over one part of the rules: The GM for chargen, the GM for conflict, the GM for combat, the GM for the story setup/scenario (the "traditional GM Storyteller role") and the like?

It might take a bit of creative thinking to make the game in a way that can be split up like this (and surely there may be some crossover between roles... but it would also be interesting if there wasn't), and can function well if one of the players doesn't show up that night (but I figure by "session three" everyone will be pretty well up to speed anyway).

Is this even worth thinking about? It started as an idea on how to basically go to a gamestore at 5:30PM and be able to play it as it was meant to be played at 7:00PM.

-Andy

    I sense a deadfall here, which is that compartmentalising the rules means that your ways to make them interact are limited (or obscure), and if your rules interact well, then no one will actually know what is going on until they've learnt the whole game anyway.

    I suppose that you could design a game that compartmentalises to the point that different rules modules don't interface at all, but that sounds suspiciously like playing several unrelated games simultaneously to me.

    Re: Kafka Game

    That would indeed be awesome -- but it would be a one-play game. Which isn't necessarily a bad thing; How to Host a Murder games are all consumable games, and they sell like gangbusters.

    As for making a "regular" game that has distributed knowledge of the ruleset, I think that's very different from a game with distributed responsibility of the ruleset. Divvying up responsibilities is easy stuff (although what happens when the combat GM has a character that gets into combat?). Divvying up knowledge is pretty much only useful for that start-up from purchase to play -- and I'm skeptical of the real utility offered, and the resources you'd need to invest as a designer to make it actually work.

    I played an RPG-like boardgame recently, Bruce Glassco's Betrayal at House on the Hill. This uses distributed rules. Everyone plays a character who goes into the haunted house. After a bit of exploration, though, one of the characters turns out to be the traitor.
