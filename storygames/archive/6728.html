    \n I'm a designer of RPGs since the late eighties. The FRPG Fabula is one classical game I have designed. "Livets H\xf8st" is a modern game I have designed. I'va had players telling me both these games have changed their lives. Currently my greatest ambition is to design games that connects to players in a deeply personal way, both in my classical and modern design-endeavours. I purpose to reach that ambition by utilizing all that I have learned about game-design, all the tricks and tools of my trade, and I continuously strive to sharpen my tools.

    TOUCH
    One tool I have used a bit is touch. By positive touch; a handshake or a hug, or joining arms in a ring, we activate centers in the brain that regulates feelings of safety and happiness. I have used this to make players feel sympathetic towards eachother, and to make players feel sympathetic towards a NPC.

    Negative touch does the opposite, I know, but I have not tried it (in games, that is). I believe it may be used to good effect though, in a restricted and careful way. It is conceivable that by placing a fist on the shoulder of a fellow player, he/she may feel threatened. Without being actually violated, the player will respond instinctively, by sweating and feeling uncomfortable. He/she will want the fist removed as fast as possible. That is an effect that may be useful in some games.

    \nMEMORIAL-PC

    In my game Autumn of Life the characters die off, one by one, from an early stage in the game. This had to be, but I also wanted the players to keep on playing. The solution was to make them play what other characters remember of their own character. A dead character is brought to life in the game through comments on "what you and me experienced back then" or "what I did once" or "how we quarreled once" or "all of you remembers how I used to fidget in situations like this" or other reminiscing comments.

    The game itself is mostly on the past of the old (and dead) characters, building a fable on their lives and friendships. The memorial-PCs works as a very effective and meaningful tool in this game. Nearly all the game-scenes are framed as the funerals of the PC's, so the memorial-PCs fits like hand in glove with the game.

    \nENCOUNTER-CASCADE

    I made a combat/war-module for my classical FRPG Fabula some years ago. The module introduced a very effective tool for simulating the chaos of a battlefield; the encounter-cascade.

    It is fairly simple: you make up a long list of simple and threatening situations arising in the beginning of the battle, in the midst of it, and in the final throes of it. Make each situation-description not more than a single line, but make one or two sides of typewritten pages of them (a LOT of situations). Follow the list from start to end, to play out the battle.

    Example situations:
    A man comes towards you, SCREAMING, his wooden axe over his head! (peasant, Combat 8+3 Axe, no armor)
    Something MOVES in the doorway behind you! (small angry boy, Combat 5+1 knife, no armor)
    Two men in red coats close in on you, they are fearsome! (your own bloodied knights, Combat 20+6 sword, +1/-7 shield, scale mail -2/+7)
    A woman attacks you, kitchen knife in one lame hand (bleeding, crying, desperate, no combat, no armor)
    etc.

    When the characters get into the battle, you switch from normal play to this list. You start going around the table at high speed, throwing encounters at one or more players all the time. They must respond at once, or be dumbfounded, or stricken by enemies. They are in "battle-mode", and must make split-second decisions regarding life and death of any man/woman coming at them from the shadow/smoke/chaos of the field. They may choose to utilize some weapon. If so you let let them make a simple skill-check, gives them the brutal consequence, and goes on. On and on and on you goes! It will feel like an eternity for the players, and the sheer amount of fatal decisions they must make, will wear them out. In this instance tired players are a very good thing. It will help them step into the shoes of a real soldier.

    The GM goes on like a crazy clockwork of doom, until the battle ends and the characters are running for their lives, or taking control over the battlefield. Then you go into normal modus again, and try to give the players pause to evaluate what the battle did to their characters (injuries are noted, shock declared, fatal misjudgement taken into consideration, both mental and physical fatigue make their bodies shake, moral mechanisms activates, etc.).

    Most players feel like they've been in some hell when this tool is used, and have an easy job in simulating the traumatic after-effects of the battle through their characters. It is a radical tool, for radical situations, and it makes an impact.
    _____________________________________

    A VARIATION
    As a variation, you may build out your list in the midst of the battle, by asking the players "What do you see"? they must answer this as fast as any thing you throw at them, and you will make their description into an encounter at once. You do not slack on the rythm to do this, but goes on like before.

    This can only be done when the players are warming to the speed of encouters, to ensure they are on their toes and ready to answer anything you throws at them immediately. Then most players have no problem describing some brutality or tragedy or threat on the battlefield, and you will be pumped up to take it directly into play, and resolve it as fast as anyting premade on your list.

    \nFABLE-TALK

    In my game Muu (classical RPG, poetic fable, 1989) both setting and character-identification were reinforced by the use of a special, poetic way of describing everything. By using a simple structure focused on the natural elements of the setting, the poetic descriptions managed to weave a fable of harmony and bewildering wonder which both gave the setting depth, and forced the players to look at the world through the eyes of their muu.

    The simple language-structures of the game empowered the "muuleader" (GM) to use this "fable-talk" effectively, supporting the muu'ishness of the game. As the muus are beings with no language themselves, the control exercised by the game over the descriptive language used, are supportive of their alternative way of thinking and looking at all elements in the setting. The game is deeply immersive, to the point where it may be described as an "interactive harmony-motor" (really; the game plays much like a meditation). The fable-talk is at the hearth of this game-experience.

    Examples: there is muu (the character, nameless), and allmuu (all characters together, the total population of muu) and bigmuu (a pole dirven into the ground, with a stick fastened abeam). There is also Great Warm Muu (the sun) and Cold Muu (the moon). To follow up this the elements of the natural setting is described in the same way: tree, alltree (forest), bigtree (the great tree allmuu is living under), water, allwater (the heavens), prickly waters (a brook), etc.

    The game was written purely on intuition, in one single night. I've never written anything like it before or since, and have seen no RPG-tool remotely similar to the "fable-talk".

    \n "The tools for change" is the title of this thread. So I am trying to post tools of my own, that I perceive to be effective instruments for influencing people on a deep personal level. The changes purported by these tools are not always immediate, and most of them are subtle (as in: this challenges me, I must think about it ... and maybe it will make me slightly wiser, or openminded, or tolerant, or slightly calmer).

    Here's another tool in the series:

    THE MUUSONG
    As previously stated I made the roleplaying game Muu back in 1989, a poetic fable with meditative qualities. Muu is a being with no language. They communicate on an empathic level (allmuu will feel what every single muu is feeling), and by shared dreams. However; the muu has a voice, and may sing.

    The song is a very strong myth in Muus World. To sing demands thorough preparations by the muu, and the right conditions. If the conditions are there (moonlight, singingwater, nightingale ...), the player may choose to have his/her muu sing. If this option is chosen, the muuleader immediately asks the player to stand up, turn around, go to the corner of the room, turn, and walk up to the table again. As you come back to the table, the muuleader tells the player; you will immediately sing a muusong. The muusong is kind of a hum/troll-song. Not a big song with words, but a small song with sounds. Very small and strange and easy. Do not think. Go do it right now.

    This ritual has, without exception, made for the most marvellous muusongs. Most people are not very used to singing, so they perform with nervous, highpitched voices and clumsy rhythm, and that is spot on for the muu. After all; muu is a figure which seldom use its voice. So the fragile and clumsy song of the player mimic the muusong perfectly.

    The fragile songs made by muuplayers, are among the most beautiful and rewarding experiences ever given me in a roleplaying game. And the best thing about it, is the unanimous praise for the singing each and every singer gets from his/her fellow players. It is touching to the point of tears.

    \n I'll try to speculate a bit here now ...

    AS-IS CHARACTER

    You play a character named after yourself. You make a list of your family and friends, with notes on their psyche/behavior/quirks, and how you relate to them (friends, lovers, cold, warm, practical, formal, pet-name, etc.). Try to be truthful. You hand the list to the GM, for her to use as NPC's in your game. All players do this. In all other respects you make your characters as normal, and you play the game in the normal fashion.

    Your real life relationships will be treated as true for the game-world too. Your family and friends will be played by the GM, and she may use them in any way she want, even maim or rape or kill them. And she may change the relationships due to the events of the game.

    \nEXPERIENCE POINTS

    Tell the players they should dive into their character and his/her relationships from time to time, by inviting other players to explore the emotional landscape of the characters friendship. You will give any player inviting to such interaction an "experience point" at the end of session.

    The XP can be used by the player in later sessions, to make some connection between his character and a person/place/phenomenon. This connection will always relate to the past of the character, and may be helpful (or harmful) in any way.

    When you have given a player XP, you may rule that the next XP will be given for 2 emotional interactions, then for 3, etc. Maybe you will see, eventually, that the players do this for themselves, without the XP as motivation. It may change the way they play the game. By exploring themes like these, the players may actually get some new insights on friendship ...

    BTW: I am aware of the parallel between XP in this sense, and XP in classical RPGs. An odd coincidence, I assure you ;-)

    \nGAMIST-SLAVE OF IMMERSIONISM

    Playing a classical RPG you will adhere to these extra rules:

    1 - You will not act as yourself in the game. Your values don't enter into it.
    2 - The character-skills also symbolize your characters compulsions. Highest skill is the the strongest compulsion.
    3 - Any action or deliberation or statement should be expressed entirely in-character.

    4 - Make yourself an easy way to check compulsions (f.ex. with the normal die for the game).
    5 - Always check the strongest compulsion first. If the check succeeds, then act/deliberate/state a meaning/intent based on it.
    6 - If the strongest compulsion fail, then check the next in line, etc.
    7 - Go with the consequences, no matter what they are. You are an immersionist-slave, and will not surface until the game is over.

    Have a nice game!

    \n Inspired by Jackson Tegu and his game "Housebreaker" in the Roleplaying Poem Challenge:

    WE ARE YOU

    You gather to play in the house of a friend, and you will all strive to become this friend. Your friend may participate in this game too, playing him/herself. You will all dress up in his/her clothes, and will be one particular part of your friend. One may be his anger, one his love, one his nervousness, one his singing, one his inner cook, etc. Try to be something you have seen in your friend, something you may delve into with some seriousness.

    You will all live in this house now, as your friend. Move about, do the chores and live the homelife of your friend. When you meet in a room you may have dialogues between you, symbolizing the inner dialogues of your friend. Try to include your friend in these dialogues a lot, but not all the time (he/she has subconscious parts out of reach).

    When your friend decide to leave the house, he/she will tell you so and you will follow him/her around after that, silent, until all players have been gathered. You then go to the door, and open it, and the play ends.

    Ok, it ended up being a strange little game. Hope you get some fun out of it!

    \nINTENSIFY RELATIONSHIP

    To intensify relationships between PCs and NPCs, the GM step into the NPCs shoes, and start relating to the PC. Make statements like: "You know I love you" and "As your father it is my duty to tell you ..." and "We are family, so I will stand by you".

    Follow up such statements with advice and actions which are contrary to the PCs goals, or which may threaten them. The NPC may expect the PC to stay longer than possible; "I am so glad you have come! You must celebrate midsummer with us! Or course you will! It will be soo nice"!

    To do this is fairly normal among people, and it works (in games too). Lost sons coming home will often be met with statements which hook on to their consciousness (the bad one), and will regularly be expected to stay for ever.

    For players strident enough to make resistance to the fact that their PC has family-ties, you may use shame and social pressure to reign them in. For people to break with their family is fairly common, but not easy. Don't make it easy for the PCs either. The player should feel that the PC is risking something of great importance if he breaks with his family (or friends, lovers, partners).

    This slightly dysfunctional family-communication may be used to hook on to the PCs, making them vulnerable in new ways. Vulnerable PCs are only possible if the players themselves are vulnerable, so such interaction must be played out with gusto and realism, directed at the player. Make the player relate to the NPC, and the field is set for exploring family-ties and conflicts on a deeper level than normal in RPGs.

    Such exploration is a field for rich and rewarding gameplay, as it takes place in a sphere of life all of us relate to.
