\n You're at a local convention with 150+ other gamers.
\n
\nEveryone lives nearby\u2026 loves playing games\u2026 maybe not everyone likes the same games as you and you probably don't want to play with everyone there\u2026 but there are dozens of people that would be perfect\u2026 the gaming group of your dreams!
\n
\nHow do you find them?
\n
\nWe run a local all-day gaming event 3 times a year called Recess in NYC. It's amazing and gets better every time. And although we have a good success rate of helping people meet each other to start new gaming groups, I feel like we could do more to help.
\n
\nUsually you play 2 four hour games and attend 1 special event. If you play with someone you think rocks at one of your games, you exchange contact information. But you are only exposed to 8% of the people attending the event. Granted, people who sign up for the same games are more likely to be compatible but there are many missed opportunities.
\n
\nWhat can we do?

    \n Tag clouds for people and/or for games. List the sorts of games/things you'd be interested in playing. These might be specific games ("I'm starting up a Nobilis campaign. Want to play?") or they might be theme/tone/mechanics ("I'm into weird indie games that push boundaries, how about you?" "I like tales of tragic romance, good intentions gone wrong, etc." "I don't want to play anything with classes nd levels.")

    As people interact, they can discuss each others preferences, and why they agree or disagree. And they can look over games that they might play, in order to find ones that might be appealing.

    Posted By: jdfristromMy usual problem is
    - times I can play
    \nThis is a huge deal, too; it honestly doesn't matter if you like someone, live close to them, and love the same kinds of games, if your schedules can never match up.

    Whatever you do to encourage gaming groups to form, you should look for ways to get people to: 1.) seriously think about what nights and times they could get together and game, and 2.) share that information with other people.


    You might also consider giving the people who go to these all-day events some kind of advice about how to find people who wouldn't go to those kinds of all-day events. Maybe do some little seminars, a GM's conference, that sort of thing; see what the people in your community already know about how to introduce people who have never played before to gaming, good games for beginners, and so on...the usual "how do I find people to play with?" stuff, so that people who have friends/family/co-workers who might be interested in gaming can bring 'em into their home games.

    I mean, I suppose it would be nice if everyone in the world actually enjoyed spending a full day in a room with 150+ strangers, but that's hardly realistic. But if some portion of the 150+ attendees each go out and find a couple of people who weren't at the event to play with, that's a serious win for your gaming community, even if none of those new people ever show up at Recess in NYC.

    OK, so, it looks like there are a number of issues involved here:

    \n
      \n
    • People need a certain amount of geographic proximity.
      • \n
    • People need a certain amount of temporal overlap.
      • \n
    • People need a certain amount of interest in the same games, and game-play styles.
      • \n
    \n

    Crucially, you are trying to get people who match in these three ways to meet at significantly greater-than-chance. It's like making a dating site.

    \n

    I don't think I have much to add beyond that, but I hope that stating what I see as the components of the problem will help. The only bit of suggestion I have is making really targeted events\u2014be they a certain game, or a panel on a certain thing, or whatever, and having a sign-up for people who want to meet people through it, that asks for time and place. That way, you can assume people have self-selected into the third point, and you can relatively easily sort based on time and space. That leaves you in the awkward position of brokering matches, though, which might not really be great.

    \n Collect the information about what, when and where people want to play, put it in a single doc, and send it to the participants after the con. Kind of like one of those info directories for professional organization meetings.

    Hold a short mixer during the con. Figure out a set of times, and a set of areas. Put up posters with the times around the edge of the space. People go to their preferred type. Mix. Then half-way turn the posters over to show the the areas. People go to their areas. Mix. (I don't think game types would work well for this.) The times/areas could have overlapping categories. "Weekends, weekdays, 9-5, biweekly." "Burrough 1, burrough 2, on the X line, near blah-di-blah campus."

    Treasure hunt! Everybody writes down three times, areas and games they want to play. During the course of the con they have to find 3 or 4 or whatever people who match. No fair writing down names of people you already know well. No more than one name within when/where/what, but bonus points for finding one person who's a match for more than one of when/where/what. The person who gets the most points wins a year of gaming!

    At the start of the con, make everyone construct a hat representing their preferred gaming style, location and time. Then everybody has to wear their hat all weekend. Okay, this one's maybe not so good.
