    \n The Adapt Press chapter is really the chapter on indie games in disguise, but it's placed in the d20 section instead of the indie games section. The indie games section covers Evil Hat, Catalyst, and Cubicle... so it's not really about indie games all that much, which is strange. So, we're in there, just hard to find in the awkward organization. I initially thought there was nothing on indie games at all, which is easy to do, the way the book is subdivided.     \n P.S. I'm also writing a history of indie games for the Magic Missile anthology I'm putting together, so obviously I have some strong opinions about how to portray this stuff. Shannon picks up on some important points (like the connections to Costikyan, Stafford, Tweet, Laws, and Wallis; the Forge Booth) but misses some others (the critical importance of the internet + direct-to-customer distro, the heightened role of women in indie games, the building of personal connections through playing and supporting each others' games, no mention of Vincent aside from a brief note on Dogs, would have been great to include sales numbers since most indie folks publicly release those, no real attempt to talk about trends in design and play, the rise of local indie games meetups, Jiffycon / Go Play, etc.). So, overall, a major step forward and it's great to have an outsider's perspective on how this history looks, but there's still room for an more in-depth look bringing in more insights from folks who were directly involved.

    \nPaul: Exactly, I said the same thing in my initial response to the book. Sales-wise, Vincent Baker is a medium-sized RPG company, but he's also just some dude. And since it's all direct sales, it doesn't show up on any industry spreadsheets. Likewise, MLwM is never going to show up on anybody's industry figures (it's never even been on IPR) and neither are the dozens of games that have been directly influenced by it (like, most RPGs with an endgame). So, only part of the picture, definitely.

    It would also be interesting to know what indie RPG sales+play+awards look like as an aggregate, not as individual publications, because that's where our influence truly is. One dude putting a free PDF up on the internet may not seem like much, but it's much more significant when considered in the context of everything else that's going on.

    \n Just gotta jump in to say no! It's not true. I'm not a medium-sized RPG company. My books' sales, title by title, are pretty good for the industry, as best as I can gather from talking to my better-connected friends - pretty good, not stellar. But since my catalog is tiny, my overall sales are tiny too.

    I can be a little bit concrete.

    Dogs in the Vineyard's lifetime sales are about 10% of Deadlands 1st Edition's.

    Apocalypse World has (probably) sold better than any given recent Ars Magica supplement.

    Pretty good! Not stellar.

    (Again, these are based on conversations, not serious number comparisons. If you have better data than I do, I'd love to hear it and I'll cheerfully correct myself.)

    -Vincent
