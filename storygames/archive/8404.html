    \n I believe several of the writers are professional academics, and in academic systems, it's important to have a lot of published articles. Written works also have to fit into specific academic formats. Which explains why academic articles are often hard to read, and why it's not always clear what's so important about them.

    Note that writing about role-playing theory is not the same thing as writing an academic paper. It's quite possible to write about theory in an engaging and useful way. But a paper for an academic journal is a very specific thing - and, sadly, criteria for publication do not include "engaging" or "useful". Making your paper a fun read is completely optional, in other words. (Just like making your rules text useful for people outside your circle/sub-culture is optional, not required, in indie publishing).

    \n I've only skimmed it so far, but I think academic articles can be both scholarly enough to get into a peer-reviewed journal (in most any field) and also be exciting and informative to read. Especially in an interdisciplinary journal like this one, in which the contributors come from a variety of different backgrounds and research perspectives, it's important to ensure that articles are accessible to colleagues and interested parties from outside your discipline. While it's not necessarily the case that they'll be instantly accessible to the educated masses ("New York Times readers," as my boss likes to call them), it's one thing to use academic language and jargon to make it clearer what you're talking about (frex. "Defining 'roleplaying' is assisted by Wittgenstein's notion of 'family resemblance' (Footnote), as there are a number of distinct traditions of practice that are all considered to be 'roleplaying' by their practitioners.") and another to make what you're saying seem more high-falutin' than it actually is, as if you're trying to prove your chops as a scholar or show that roleplaying is something worth studying seriously.

    However, I'm loathe to criticize the IJRP as an endeavor, because -- in the desert of academic roleplaying studies -- it's a welcome addition to the annual Nordic convention books and... well, that's about it. Otherwise we're forced to squat like vultures around the game studies / ludology / virtual worlds academics and piece together stuff from various forums, blogs, and five-year-old Forge articles. So I'm all for criticizing the articles individually, yes, but I also think the folks responsible for putting this together should be encouraged to keep doing it and make it even better, since it's definitely a worthwhile and difficult project.

    \n Having read it, I'm unpersuaded by the first article. It draws a definition of "roleplaying game" intended to cover everything that's commonly called an RPG. That's rather difficult, when you're including console RPGs.

    The definition it ends up with, broadly, is that a RPG has: an imaginary world, which you're free to explore; players, who affect the evolution of the game world; characters, that develop; characters interact with the world; there's a narrative element.

    It's a difficult definition. It seems to rule out Polaris and The Shab Al-Hiri Roach (no GM), but rule in Grand Theft Auto IV and Bioshock. The Game Master criterion is particularly difficult: it rules out GMless games and, for console games, requires inventing a digital Game Master.

    I find the writing rather difficult, too. The conclusion doesn't actually summarise the article: it seems to be a continuation of the discussion. It's also hard to understand what the article is about from reading the abstract.

    The second paper looks quite good. It defines different sorts of roleplaying games, and the tasks within them, quite successfully.

    \n I think Jonathan's comments correspond most closely with my own view, since they emphasize the fragmented character of academic scholarship on (tabletop) role-playing games, which is nowhere near disciplinary status, and probably never will be. But given that you can study whatever you want, why wouldn't you study something that fascinates you? That you love? And why wouldn't you encourage the development of an academic community pursuing similar interests? Jared's snarky comment about the masturbatory nature of such efforts reflects the scorn of the real-world practitioner for the ivory-tower theorist, who is always behind the cutting edge of practice and so seems hidebound and bumbling.

    From an institutional perspective, it seems to me that those of us interested in studying RPGs as academics need to acknowledge that the recent surge in interest in video games as a social and cultural phenomenon is our best bet as a point of entry for finding a scholarly audience. For example, I'm going to a popular culture studies conference in February where I'll present a paper called "The Ironical Rhetoric of the Real in RPG Play" (which I also submitted to Solmukohta/Knutepunkt); the call for papers for the conference listed "Tabletop RPG Design/Theory" as a potential topic within the domain of video game studies.

    Given that, it seems that one of the things that IJRP has to do--and that you see it doing--is stress the equivalence of on-line games like WoW with table-top RPGs in order to convince that larger scholarly audience interested in videogames of the relevance of the journal's work to that audience's project. Because the currency of scholarly communication is attention.
