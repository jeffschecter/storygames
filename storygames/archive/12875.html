      \n Okay so we got a first session under our belts. Initial impressions:

      * Some poor organization in the rulebook. The fact you're supposed to keep a running log of short-term memories, for example, seems vital and also not well placed in the rules.

See the first bullet -- I would have liked to have seen a recap of the various ways data can be used in the Data chapter, at the very least. I'm still working through my post-first-play readthrough.

Flow is something that exists and is measurable in the game fiction but it's also a mechanical thing, and sometimes it's hard to see how the one relates to the other. Most glaring case: Ghosting! Okay so Ghosting is largely about avoiding the Aggregate's sensors, right? So if you're busy avoiding the Aggregate, why does it cost you flow to do so?       * More on Ghosting. Okay so it's both sneaking and stealing. The various effects you can buy have to do with stealing, not sneaking so much. There's a lot of interpretation involved with mapping the "you steal X but..." results over to "you are sneaky but..." results. We had one very simple thing we thought Ghosting could do, but had no idea how to do it: How would you use Ghosting to set up an ambush? I want to death a user but I want to get the drop on him so he can't get a swing back at me. Possible?

      * Not really clear on the practical differences between cultivation and printing, other than the fact you need plans and a printer to do matter printing (but one assumes it's much faster than cultivation). Time pressure seems entirely arbitrary and narrative, so...we're noodling on that one.

As long as it includes 3 of the following; person, group, place, thing, or action, you're good to go (usually I just write a note of something cool that happened to my character and almost always it includes 3 of these without trying).       In terms of a functional RPG with only four player characters and the possibility of a hundred+ NPCs in a MRCZ, we opted for the abstract route.

      \n Okay, sorry. I thought you said upthread that this was theoretical.

      It sounds like the ghosting stuff worked fine for what you wanted. He took a stab at it and got a marginal victory. You're going to find that a lot of FM challenges end in marginal victories. So he left a clue behind. Not a giant arrow with this name on it, but a clue as to who did it. If he had won a total victory, there would have been no clue. It's stretching the Ghosting mechanics a tiny bit, but it seems reasonable. You're a good GM. I'm positive you were salivating at the thought of that clue.

      Personally, I would have just used a wetwork challenge to resolve the whole thing. The ghosting-plus-wetwork challenge sounds like you're parsing too fine for FM. Challenges are meant to be broad.

      As for your players whining about the fiction, let me see if I can help.
      Every user is tagged with a flow rating. Everything they do on the station affects that rating. How is it tracked? The user reports his activity up to the Aggregate. Aggie checks the numbers and posts new totals in his database. Everything you do is reported, even sneaking around. Is it consciously reported? Hell no. That's tedious. Your key just tracks what you're doing and communicates with Aggie. It all happens in the background. Neat and simple. The little world goes round.

      If your would-be assassin wants to really go into stealth mode, he can engage with the fiction and make nice-nice with a switcher. A switcher will give him a new, temporary identity. Under that guise, he can ambush and murder -- and provided he's successful -- no one will be the wiser.

      But using ghosting alone? Way too offmarket, my friend. It's just not enough to slip through the seams of station life. If you want to survive on the donut, you're going to have to think like a freemer.


      And yes, regarding your last point, the game is challenging your assumptions.
