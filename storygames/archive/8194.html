    \n For me it's usually after I've finished some other little project, and I realize it's my brain's response to a problem I was having with something else. So what works for me is stepping away, disengaging, and working on a different, focused, little design project.

    Example: I'm hip deep in Medical hospital and have a complicated set of rules for social interaction that aren't fun and don't work. Along comes Game Chef, I drop Medical Hospital and rapidly design a game that's all about social interaction and has almost no rules and totally works. And I'm like, "huh".

    Sometimes I feel like Perfect is doomed to walk that lonely road of non-existence. I took it off the market in order to release a better 2nd edition. I'm constantly going through these two opinions about it: "It's a focused game and it does what it does really well, but that's it" / "it's incomplete and there's a serious problem I'm hearing about over and over again". Its strengths are also set-backs, and I can't decide whether that means I pitch it as such or I take the time to "fix" it.

    Whereas I sometimes feel like Perfect might be heading in this direction, I have a forever-doomed-to-be-incomplete game that I am positive about. It's called Boulevard. It's a game about punks and outcasts and low-lifes, and how their sense of community gets shifted, eroded and flipped upside-down by people gaining and losing momentum. It was this really cool concept in July of '06, and then I've failed to get a firm grip on it ever since.

    \n Well, it is a stupid theme, yes, and I did not believe I would have anything more to contribute in this stupidity, but then it hit me ...

    Fabula

    - my classical fantasy role-playing game is one I have chosen as a life-project. I have worked continuously on the game since 1995. I published it with a major company in Norway in 1999, but have done even more work on it since then.

    This fall I made a major upgrade of the game, tidying up the method for the 10th time (or something). And still I'm not satisfied.

    Next spring I will end 14 years of continuous professional play with the game (1995-2009). I had a weekend with eight 18 years old players a couple of weeks back. Most of them have played with me since 4th grade, since they were 9 years old. One of them told me: You have been like a second father to me, Tomas! The statement baffled me, of course, but then I went all soft inside, and a bit proud.

    Gaming with youths and having such feedback has been a motor in my development of Fabula . It has given me tons of happiness and insight (in both gaming and men). I wonder what it will do with the game when I end my regular professional use of it. Somehow I can not imagine not working on this game in the future, so I'm only wondering HOW I will continue working on it.

    I feel privileged having such a project to work on. It is highly meaningful, especially when looking back on the great experiences Fabula has given to players.

    I wish for all of you to be blessed with such a life-project!

    \n For me it is a project called "Future Perfect", my big trans-humanist, "what does it mean to human and why are we so special in the Universe" game that I cannot seem to nail down.
    \n
    \nNot that I am a game designer. I am a setting designer at best, but I really want to get a solid game of this played at some point...I just keep changing systems or updating the setting informationt o gel with new ways of looking at the likely path of next-level tech and such.
    \n
    \nFuturism is MUCH harder than fantasy, to me, because I want it to really feel realistic. I want fantasy to feel allegorical - and that is much easier. This is why my abiding love in fictional life is sci-fi and I end up running fantasy games that treat magic like an allegory for technology and consumerism.
    \n
    \nIf and when I get the demon trapped on paper (or PDF), I may publish it - which means I need to pick a system that is cool with others using it to publish vanity press Magnum Opii.
