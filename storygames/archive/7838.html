\n I know this is a story/narrativist games forum, but I think you are probably the best option to answer some questions that have been perplexing me.
\n
\nAlso, sorry if this is old hat for you, but it's about GNS, hopefully with some practical fallout.
\n
\nFrom my reading at the forge, I gather that according to the GNS model people play RPGs with three distinct aims:
\n
\n- gamist approach: the aim is to take advantage of the rules to "win" the game with clever chargen optimisation and tactics. Taken to the extreme the game becomes like a computer game or chess.
\n- narrativist/story based approach: the aim is to create/experience interesting stories. Taken to the extreme, it becomes collaborative fiction creation.
\n- simulationist approach: the aim is "immersion", feel/experience what the PC is supposed to feel/experience. Taken to the extreme it should become a second life?
\n
\nAll games contain elements of these three poles (often with a randomizing engine to add uncertainty), but according to GNS thinking it is not possible to satisfy a group of players interested in different approaches but playing the same game (hence the utility of a social contract).
\n
\nI realize this sketch is very rough and probably full of mistakes, but lets move to the point that interests me: I think I pretty much understand how the game system can support a gamist or a narrativist agenda. The gamist agenda is well supported by a clearly defined set of rules that provide interesting tactical choices for the players (think of chess or various computer games). I also think I see how the system can support a story/narrativist agenda: it should provide means to manipulate elements of the narrative structure (bangs, stakes, kickers, keys, outcomes, etc) to facilitate/improve shared story building.
\n
\nWhat I don't understand is how a system can support the sim agenda. I realize that many games set out to do just that, but in fact end up serving one of the other agendas. That does not prevent you from having the "immersive" feeling, but it's purely accidental. Two examples from personal history:
\n
\n1 - When I was a kid I loved to play Civilization. Although I realized that it was pure number crunching and that there was no ghost in the shell, I liked to feel myself like a potentate who is outraged by the backstabbing of other leaders. I would take revenge on the Chinese who failed to honor a peace treaty and go at lengths to destroy them even though the French where more of a strategic threat, simply because they had not attacked me. This way of playing was much less efficient but personally also more enjoyable, since I was emotionally invested or at least felt some meaning in doing what I was doing, instead of just winning the game. So even in a purely gamist game (a boardgame, really), I could feel some kind of immersion (I am the leader, and I have my reasons to do what I set out to do)
\n
\n2 - At some point in the nineties I played a short written story where the reader is the hero and must jump from paragraph to paragraph according to his choices to push the story forward. The theme was about a guy who comes to realize that he has become a vampire. I was incredibly struck by the idea of understanding that you crave for blood and need it to survive, and that you are becoming something profoundly unhuman (yep, vampire stories are great metaphors for teenager troubles). Again I don't think that the game system was particularly suited for a sim agenda. I would not choose a paragraph based on what would give the character more chances of survival (gamist), or create a more interesting story (narrativist), I simply tried to think about what I would do if I was that character, and then pick up the choice which came closest to it. I wanted to be that guy and feel his angst.
\n
\nEncouraged by the experience I tried to play Vampire the Requiem, but it was a disaster. I was totally uninterested in combat/intrigues/powers, I just wanted to experience how you would feel to have your identity irreversibly changed without your control, and could not do that with that game system (or at least with the gaming group I was in, or both).
\n
\nSo my point is that I see games serving a gamist or a narrativist agenda (with skills/powers sets, life points, etc for the former, and narrative flow tools for the latter). But are there systems or tools that really serve the sim agenda and improve your immersive feeling? From what I gather the only good tool for that is providing a coherent setting that somehow facilitates suspension of disbelief, and a challenge that forces a reaction from the PC so that you get things moving.
\n
\nAgain sorry if this is old for you but I would like some clear input on this issue, if possible.

    \n I'll try to help, because I do consider this an important topic for many gamers. However, it's proven very tricky to discuss online, which means a lot of people just want to shut down those conversations immediately.

    I'm far from an expert on the Big Model, but the G/N/S part of it is probably my least favourite part, although I'm totally down with the concept of Creative Agenda as something that ties everything together into a great game. So, with that caveat, and realizing that someone more knowledgeable might (and should) step in to correct me at any moment:

    It is going to be difficult to answer this question without unraveling a little more about what exactly it is you enjoy about the "immersive" feeling. The concept of feeling "immersed" is not actually strictly linked to the Simulationist agenda. It seems to refer to different things for different people, and is a very personal experience.

    Rather, it seems to me that for individual players there are a number of play techniques which they find help them immerse, or prevent them from immersing. These are not specific to Creative Agendas; it's about what you do and say at the table, and whether it helps you get that feeling or not.

    For instance, some people might say that they get "more immersed" if, in their game, people talk about their characters in the first person ("I open the door and..." as opposed to "my guy opens the door"), and if people act out dialogue (especially in funny voices) as opposed to just talking about it ("I chat with him for an hour and try to convince him to sell me his shoes")/

    My own experience has been that I can play and design games that are "immersive" in the way I like by identifying those specific elements, while still using a huge variety of other techniques (especially techiques common to "Story Now" Narrativist games).

    So, it sounds like you want to enjoy the feeling of dealing with the life choices of a vampire (for example), from the perspective of the vampire--through his or her eyes. It might be helpful to consider specific things in games you have played that help you do that, and things that distract you from it. (When I say "specific things", by the way, it's usually better to start with stuff the players in the game were doing, as opposed to stuff written in the rulebooks.) For example, it sounds like Vampire's "combat/intrigues/powers" did NOT help you immerse. If you can figure out why, for you, that was annoying, you can start building a list of criteria.

    With that criteria, you should be able to narrow down the choices significantly. I haven't played as many games as most people here, so they'll be able to advise you better than I on specific games.

    \n Yeah, you can't discuss simulationistic games and why they are such without understanding the concept of simulationism, first. And based on your short description you'll still need to hone your understanding of GNS to get there.

    Alternatively, you can just read some coherent simulationistic games very carefully and contrast/compare with some other games that you know aren't simulationistic. That way you'll get a sense of what an author trying to transmit a simulationistic agenda will focus on. Some such games that I like are Dread (the jenga one) and Dead of Night, which both I find very coherent and clear in their simulationistic agenda. I'd quote other titles, but the fact is that the great majority of sim design is very incoherent and much of the time you'll end up finding another type of game in there. For example, I'd suggest Solipsist, Primitive, Vampire: the Masquerade or Planescape, except that I can easily imagine how somebody so inclined could read any of those and end up playing them towards some very different creative agenda.

    Also, checking out the games Jesse mentioned might be useful in that they are very good examples of how story-focused a simulationistic game can be. The sort of understanding you display is very typical of the so-called GDS or threefold model, which is an older rpg typology that is simpler to understand as a totally separate thing from GNS. Threecorner categorization of games depends very much on teleological sorting of GM motivations for play, which pertains to how you characterize narrativism above: what you describe is not actually narrativism, but dramatism, which is one of those threecorner categories. Thing is, a great majority of modern simulationistic games is actually simulationistic-dramatist - sim in GNS terms, dramatist in threecorner terms. So what you actually describe above are the two main technique families of simulationistic gaming.

    But anyway, that's all rather fiddly and will probably require a long thread of discussion to sort out, if you're interested. Personally I recommend checking out good simulationistic games and comparing them to others to see how they differ. Better yet, go play with a solidly simulationistic crew of hobbyists.

    \n The issue of rpg theory with the GNS and all that stuff is a bit difficult, mostly because it's a complex topic which requires quite a bit of commitment from all parties to really trash it out. If you're interested in a recent effort at learning GNS, check out this thread from the Forge. In general, consider taking the in-depth examination of the topic there; I have no doubts about my own or Jesse's understanding of it, but there are others who are used to explaining GNS there as well. Or I guess we could do it here, but that stuff does steal all the attention in any given thread.

    You discuss immersion a lot, which reminds me of some discussions we had with Ben Lehman a couple of years ago. Ben claimed immersion is a technical agenda, by which he meant that it's a powerful, flexible tool that helps players get what they want from a game. It is, however, not the thing you want, but just a tool that might seem like the ultimate agenda because of how powerful and useful it is.

    The tricky part that was already touched upon up-thread is that immersion means many things to many people. Another viewpoint, which I think I saw Ron Edwards or some such advocate at one point, is that immersion basically just means being really invested and interested in the game. I have no difficulty with accepting both of these viewpoints and more, as people really do use the word to describe all sorts of things about their experience.

    But that's just immersion. What Jesse is shooting at with his questions is the more fundamental issue of what you want out of roleplaying. If immersion is "getting really into it", then you facilitate it best by playing with a rules system that directly provides things you are interested in. If immersion is a powerful tool that you have to learn to use with exercise to improve your roleplaying experience, then the tool will only provide satisfaction if you use it towards your genuine interests. That's what Jesse is driving at, above.

    Other than that, though, if we're just interested in how games facilitate immersion, that's a pretty straightforward question for ludography. Historically, at least the following methods have been used explicitly for the purposes of immersion:\n
      \n
    • GM fiat or GM-controlled mechanics that remove mechanical choices from the player experience, letting them concentrate solely on the fiction and their character in it. The same ideal can be seen in larps that strive to have everything look as authentic as possible, so as to let the players ignore the real world in favor of the fantasy.
      • \n
    • Information control techniques to unify what the player knows with what the character knows. The Finnish rpg Myrskyn aika did this by partitioning the setting background materials into easily compartmentalized sections, for example, with the intent that players would only read the parts their characters would know about.
      • \n
    • Mechanics that strive to emulate the fiction. One of the more impressive examples of this is the above-mentioned Dread (which is not only a simulationistic game, but also a highly immersionistic one, too), which strives to make the conflict resolution process stressful for the player as well as the character by using a jenga tower as the resolution mechanism.
      • \n
    There are all sorts of other things, too, I'm just drawing this stuff from my own superficial understanding of the topic. Immersion-focused hobbyists could probably list all sorts of techniques that are used to facilitate it in games explicitly made for the purpose. The difficulty there is mostly in that most immersionistic games I know are Finnish; Dread is pretty much the only English-language one that has convinced me to any degree.

    What you were (are?) after is a very specific sort of immersed experience (I really hate "immersion" as a gaming term since everyone means different things by it, but what the hell, I'm stuck with it). When you played Vampire, the guy sitting next to you might have been completely immersed in the sense that he was highly engaged in the material the GM was serving up (involving supernatural troubleshooting, I gather?) Your dissatisfaction has nothing to do with system, setting, or even character, it has to do with what the game was about. You were in a game that was not about the stuff you were interested in. It wouldn't matter if I pointed you to 50 places in the game text that incorporated things you were interested in - the GM (and other players, I presume) focused elsewhere.

    This is one of the great strengths and dangers of big sprawling flexible games like Vampire (of any edition). "Incoherent", pshaw. There has to be some campaign and character planning. "What are we going to do in this game?" is a key question. (And one the game text repeatedly reminds you to ask at every stage of the pregame process.)

    I assume the GM never promised you that you would be able to explore the things you wanted to explore. If they did, they're a filthy liar and a terrible GM. I assume that there was only a vague sort of conversation about what the game was going to be, and you ended up assuming one thing and the others ended up assuming something else and everyone was scratching their head at each other going "what the hell is that person doing?" Tightening up that conversation is the only way to get the material you want to engage with. Now, once the material is there, there may be other barriers that other people have identified in this thread (player vs. character knowledge, system that you don't understand, etc.) But those are individualized and different for everyone. For some people, adding up a stack of d6s doesn't break their "immersion", for others, touching a die does.

    \n I know a lot of people who regard immersion (in the sense I think it is being used here) as their ultimate goal in roleplaying, to the extent that they are happy if a lot of the game goes on in their own head, or in conversations away from the table, since 'living the character' is more important even than interacting with the other characters. (This sort of play can make NPCs more important than PCs in some cases, if the other players are not as focused on this level of immersion).

    For them the key techniques seem to be

    Story focused on personal traits

    Stories should focus on the character's personal dilemmas and beliefs, but not necessarily to challenge them. A story focused on the character slowly falling in love with someone and then getting married is as much of interest as one about their loyalty to their master being tested.

    Unobtrusive mechanics

    Tactical play, multiple combat options, detailed systems and the like are often contrary to immersive play, as Alex said above. The more the mechanics pull you from the fiction, by making you make choices based on rules systems, the less immersive you are. Mechanics can be detailed and complex as long as your choices can be purely story driven and still make mechanical sense.

    Realistic mechanics

    I think this is the Sim bit, it's breaking to immersion to suddenly be confronted by rules that allow (or forbid) something that it seems clear ought to be possible for your character. Basically you want the rules to support what you expect to happen, fantastic or otherwise. As with Dread that simulation can be of a movie trope, rather than GURPS-style crunchy reality, but they are still simulating something clearly. Loose and vague mechanics are not an aid to immersion (you can see this in some LARPS, where the rules just don't tie up with the story ... e.g. no touching, or no weapon props or whatever).

    Detailed cause and effect

    I link this to the above. What I mean by this is that it helps immersion to play your story in detail, with the cause and effect of character actions clearly detailed, so that you feel you are interacting closely with a solid and external world. When you describe opening a door you want to move on to a description of what is outside, not to skip ahead to some other scene, because that breaks the causal link in your head between what you are doing and how that feeds back to your character. This often turns into an obsession with detailed physics (you can spend an hour working out how to cross a chasm), because it feels real to you, and you (the player) experience the character's trepidation at dangerous physical risk.

    Often story-focused mechanics break this link. If you say to players "frame the next scene" or "tell me where we are now" or whatever, they get jerked out of their immersive state, because such questions often need to be answered as players, not characters. Even if the end point is the same (e.g. "I go to see my contact the fence") the process of getting there is important.

    Saying "Okay, we cut to the fence's shop, where I am arguing with him" can break the immersion, whereas saying "I make my excuses at the party, leaving early. Then I catch the bus across town, and walk the last bit in the rain, because I can't afford a cab. When I reach the fence's shop I start banging on his door, demanding he come and see me." doesn't, because the detailed chain of cause and effect is unbroken, and you know how your character got where they wanted to be. The more you step out of the fiction to make choices which skip the detail, the harder the immersion is. (I've seen this problem with detail-light fiction mentioned before in other contexts, so it's not all about immersion).

    Character advocacy

    By this I'm trying to imply a non-actor, non-director, non-pawn stance where you expect to advocate for your character (not advocate for their failure, or for what makes a good story). This is a key element that makes some (but no means all) Narrativist games anti-immersion, because they expect you to play with more of an eye to a good overall story than the in-character choices made at each step. When I play games with immersion focused players, nothing ruins the mood more than asking "where do you want the story to go next" or "What do you want to happen to your characters", because they don't want to choose that plot direction, they want to get their incrementally by in-character choices. meta-thinking is not something they like (even when it creates a better game).

    Limitation of knowledge

    This is related to the advocacy technique. Just as a player wants to make choices from a character POV, they also often want to limit their knowledge and story contributions to things that their character knows and has control over. So most immersive players are happy to describe, for example, what their room looks like, because it's in their character's control, but not what the King's throne room looks like, because it wasn't in their control. This can also apply to vast areas of world creation, so long as they feel it was in their remit. So it isn't un-immersive to detail massive backstory, or even the culture of your entire race, provided it's *yours* (i.e. no other player's culture), but even the slightest bit of setting creation outside your characters remit is jarring. (There can be exceptions of course. If a player is really *seeing* the fictional world in their head they will often add details external to their characters because they have already visualized it before it was described).

    \n (Just a quick note before I begin: I'm Simon's friend, and I was invited to join this thread to give my perspective on things)
    \n
    \nFirst of all, GNS copes, as Graham points out, really badly with immersionism or deep immersionism as creative agendas. This is mostly because Ron doesn't believe such an agenda can or should exist, and has at least a couple of times claimed that an immersionist agenda is impossible, that all immersionists are just fooling themselves.
    \n
    \nBut let's put that aside.
    \n
    \nDeep immersionism, as I have defined it, is having as the ultimate goal of roleplaying to completely immerse in the character, to the point where the experience becomes something akin to selfhypnosis, hypnotherapy, or Jungian role-playing. The idea is to identify completely with ones character, so that it's never "he" or "she", but "I" who feels, says and does things.
    \n
    \nMany people shun away from this style of play, declaring it either "dangerous", "psychotic" or simply "pretentious". Be that as it may, it's still a valid agenda and there are techniques to help achieving it.
    \n
    \nThe reason why we deep immersionists want to immerse so deeply, is different for each individual. Personally, it's Katharsis. I have a certain amount of angst, anger etc in my life, and by playing out and living those feeling but inside a fictional character, I come out relieved, happy and ready to get on with my life.
    \n
    \nAnyways, on to techniques:
    \n
    \nHituro has made some very good points, but I'd like to make my own list of techniques here...
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \nPortals:
    \nIn order to immerse completely, it is useful to have a distinct door, threshold or, as I usually call it, portal. A portal can be something simple, like a special music that's always played when we are playing and are in character, or more complex, like spending fifteen minutes at the beginning of every gaming session with our eyes closed, answering questions in first person about our characters. It can be objects - when I get into my character, I have this old lighter that my charatcer also owns, and which serves as my link to him. Grabbing that lighter is a portal. Portals are a systematic approach to entering the semi-hypnotic state, and should remain fairly constant throughout a campaign. There should also be some sort of "panic button", some kind of signal to the GM that we need to abort NOW, because things are becoming a bit too... emotional, or whatever. Think of them as the "safe words" used in an entirely different sort of roleplaying...
    \n
    \nHidden mechanics:
    \nOpenly rolling dice, adding numbers and checking tables are things which require active participation in the "real world", therefore hindering the deep immersionism; from being your charatcer you are suddenly turned into a nerd sitting round a table with other nerds. Not very immersionist. So; if there are game mechanics, they must be designed in such a way that they don't seem like mechanics to the players or their characters (since, for the purposes of deep immersionism, they are one and the same). A character might think "and then I shot him", so verbal commands and communications work well. The character, unless also a roleplayer, is unlikely to think "and then I attack for +3 in DAM against his DEF". Game statistics in the traditional sense should, therefore, be eliminated from the social interaction. There can still be such a system, but if so, then the GM must hide it completely from the players.
    \n
    \nThe story/narrative is not important. The characters are:
    \nWe cannot always be the masters of our own destinies. A lot of the time, stuff happens around us that we cannot control. The same should go for the characters. I don't pretend to have a lot of say in the "story" of my own life, and neither do most of my characters. Even talking about the events around the gaming table as "stories" is anti-immersionist, because the lives of real people just don't work that way. More on that later. For now, I'll just say this: It is important that the characters/players be allowed to be and act as themselves and as they see fit, but it is NOT important to keep the "plot" non-linear. Railroading is by no means impossible here. Sometimes, it's even good to railroad, because the focus then becomes more about the characters in themselves and their reactions and interactions, and less about the Big Plot.
    \n
    \nNon-episodic gameplay:
    \nNot may of us live in episodes. Things happen, and one thing seldom waits for another to finish. It's all mostly a big jumble, with a thousand different things to think about and try to do. In traditional roleplaying, there's the Adventure, and there's the Campaign. The Adventure is more or less complete in itself - it has a beginning, a middle, and an end, and a bunch of npc's which are only relevant for the duration of the adventure.
    \n
    \nI propose a non-episodic style, where adventures do not "begin" or "end"; but where thing simply "Happen". The characters/players may be asked to do things by different people, but they don't form queues. Things don't wait for the characters. It all happens continuously.

    \n There are some other aspects to consider, as well. As a counterpoint:

    For instance, many people who consider themselves "immersionists" aren't looking to be hypnotized or experience anything first-hand, but are just looking to enjoy a character's perspective, how the character changes, etc.

    For instance, in some typical Forge/indie/story/hippy games, such a player might enjoy a situation where:

    They are trying to rescue a priceless, ancient painting from a raging river. They know that they can gain a bonus to the roll (or whatever mechanism is being used) by crossing off another element on their character sheet. And the elements they have are: "Linda, someone I adore and wish I could be with" and "I am committed to overturning slavery".

    The player crosses off the slavery thing, and rolls to save painting. The player then realizes that, wow, his character cares more about a dead piece of art than he does about the lives of the slaves in his country! And this realization is fascinating to her or him--watching the character grow, and experiencing these choices, and how they transform the character.

    Or maybe the player realizes that, no, both those things are way too important to the character, and narrates instead how the character stops struggling, sits back, and watches the painting sink in the river, a tear rolling down his cheek. So, in this case, the rules and structure of the game are pushing the character in different, unexpected, interesting directions, and that's what this particular player is into. He or she might not get that thrill without those rules or mechanics.

    I don't know if that helps show how the "immersion" thing can really, really vary. (For example, some people can get very "immersed", even trance-like, when playing a game of chess.)

    Also, your comments about the vampire and your interest in dealing with the vampire's choices, and nothing before or after the "interesting part", sound well suited for a lot of what is considered "Narrativist"-supporting design, as opposed to Sim. I don't know if that's actually the case, since I don't know you, but you might want to check that out as well.

    Overall, though, as others have said, looking at real play experiences you've had (whether good or bad) and sharing them with others is probably the best way for you to get some feedback and opinions.
