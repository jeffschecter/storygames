\n I was reading Help me figure out how to run Game of Thrones, and I have a very similar dilemma. I'm running Cold City right now, for a group of people who probably haven't played anything since 2nd edition AD&D twenty years ago, and it's going fairly well. I've asked for their preferences for the next game, and as a group we're leaning towards playing something A Game of Thrones-y, but no one wants to play in the established setting. So, AGoT with the serial numbers filed off. I threw them a quick survey about what they want to see in their game, and their preferences are (in order):

1 Politics/Relationships/PVP
2 System Simplicity
3 Character Growth: Personality & Motivation
4 Player Narrative Control/Authorship
5 Setting Exploration (West Marches-alike)
6 Character Growth: Power
7 Detailed, Tactical Combat

So you can see that D&D might be the exact opposite of what they want. I had considered BWG for this, or maybe REIGN with Keys added on, but I think those might stray from their preferences (high preference for system simplicity and low preference for tactical combat). That leads me to Smallville, Houses of the Blooded or FATE. If your group gave you the above criteria, what system would you use? Why?

    \n The thing that makes Smallville best is when you play with antagonists as PCs (like Lex in the early seasons of the show. He's totally a PC and antagonist.)

    This is why it would work well to play a Game of Thrones style setting.

    Here's the thing, though. It's really really hard to make a tabletop game feel like the novels. Because you have so many different threads going on at a time. So only like 2 or 3 important people are usually at the same place at the same time. And sometimes just 1. The opening situation of the first book, where tons of important people have all travelled up to Winterfell is an intentional exception.

    I think it would be much easier to go for an hour-long drama TV show with a tighter cast of important characters version, but using a similar style of heavy politics as the core. In fact, the Starz show Camelot might, despite it's many flaws, serve as a better model of what you can do with a small group of people playing a small central cast of main characters.

    Another possibility would be to come up with some way to have each player be in charge of multiple characters. Or some similar way to ramp up that "cast of thousands" feel that the source novels have. I certainly wouldn't want to burden a GM with trying to portray such a huge cast of important characters without assistance.

    \n Some more specific reactions about BW:

    1 Politics/Relationships/PVP

    The BW character creation process isn't hardwired to create PC/PC relationships. The system has space for them in the BITs, though, but I think the players and GM need to consciously decide to use them for that purpose.

    I'm currently playing a mission-style BW game. To give that game some unity and inward focus, our GM required that one of our Beliefs be targeted on another member of the party. This has really spiced our play a lot and I think it's a best practice for relationshop-driven BW games.

    2 System Simplicity

    Ook. While it's true that you can prune subsystems to make BW play quite simply, note that BW has substantial non-optional complexity in its character creation system, and really rewards system mastery during character creation. To design exactly the character you like, you will have to dogear the hell out of the Character Burner.

    I know a number of players who have been frustrated by the work necessary to perfectly execute their character concepts. And even though I'm not one of those kinds of players, I had to play a few sessions before I really knew my character's capabilities. If you're going to run BW for your gang, you should take it easy on them in the early going and let them feel their way into the system.

    3 Character Growth: Personality & Motivation

    BW is really super excellent at this, with clear processes for modifying BITs. Some of those processes involve the group reviewing the character, and awarding or subtracting BITs. I really enjoy this group process because it's a nice prompt for retrospection and celebration of the game, and gets everyone at the table involved in the character's growth and evolution -- traditionally a solo activity. Players who demand more autonomy over their characters may not like this, of course!

    \n BW will give you belief driven gameplay. Fights both physical and social that take up a lot of space, though you can do unimportant ones rather quickly. I've played it PvP and it works well. However, there is very little braking mechanism for character death. If two PCs decide to duel to the death, one will very likely leave on their shield (I speak from experience here!). Pragmatically, setting up new lifepaths that fit Game of Thrones might be a huge undertaking. I'm not sure how much of the Man lifepaths are useable, but there are certainly holes to fill to fit the setting of Westeros.

    Smallville will give you relationship driven gameplay. Conflicts will focus more on roleplaying and what the characters want, but they rarely get what they want. It's very good at keeping characters (players too) at odds with a constant flux of unresolved issues. Characters will change and grow quite often, trending upwards, but with a lot of detours. PC death will never happen without buy-in from the PC. You could make some simple tweaks to change this to better fit the feel of GoT, but I'd still favor player buy-in for writing their characters out of the story. As a reader, constantly feeling that a character is in peril can drive you to find out what happens next. As a player, it can drive you to only making the safest choices to gain the most mechanical power and become a burden and hindrance to telling an interesting story. Pragmatically, it would be pretty easy to adjust the lifepaths to better fit the setting. You probably want to remove access to abilities and maybe gear. Though you could get away with keeping both, just tone down the setting effects to be less flashy than the superpowers they model, and go more for the mechanical feel they add (e.g. Invulnerable becomes Tough as a Khal, Super Speed could become Always Nearby or Quick Foot). You could consider hacking in a system for mass combat, perhaps taken from Reign, to allow for more unpredictable and decisive outcomes from army battles, and make them less about the values and relationships of the characters calling the shots.
