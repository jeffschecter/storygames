\n I've noticed a lot of people calling the process of working on their game "writing a game," but I consider the rules design portion and the act of putting those rules in a written form completely separate.

This is where I find myself in trouble, too. I'm a competent writer (ie, I think I can convey my meaning reasonably well) I just struggle with, first, determining the right voice for any particular piece and, secondly, using that voice.

Basically, I write one way and I have a hard time deviating from it, even if I know that it isn't necessarily appropriate for the material I'm presenting.

How can I improve this? How do I develop a voice in my writing? Are there articles, books or exercises that might be helpful?

    \n I'd love to hear some examples that apply specifically to nonfiction but in any case I'd suggest Exercises in Style by Raymond Queneau. In his famous work the French novelist and poet presents the same series of events in 99 distinctively different styles. Following Queneau's lead should at least help you out of your rut even if you don't find the exact right tone for your piece right away. For me, a healthy dose of playfulness in the process often opens my eyes for possibilities I didn't even consider before.

    \n Imitate other styles. This is similar to the Exercises in Style (there's a comic version of that too, which is pretty awesome) approach, but if the main goal is (at first) to simply break away from your dominant style, then find a bunch of examples of very distinct styles and then try to imitate them in a short piece of writing -- you could use a small section of your rules/game text (ideally something where style can manifest strongly.) The more practice you have intentionally writing in a different style, the easier it will be for you to find and execute your own game-appropriate style.

    \n Peter Elbow's book Writing With Power mostly deals with finding an authentic voice. Elbow's own voice isn't for everyone, but I suggest you give him a try. Another fantastic book is Lynda Barry's What it Is, which is half comic book, half memoir, and half writing class/workbook (yes, it's 15 pounds of creativity in a 10 pound bag).

    Read widely. If you mostly read RPGs, try to find some fiction or nonfiction writers with styles you respond to. Write lots and revise more. Find a sensitive critical reader who will read your stuff and cultivate that relationship.

    EDIT: If it ends up being too painful, consider collaborating with someone for whom this part of writing comes easier. You do the crunchier stuff, he or she does the squishier stuff.
