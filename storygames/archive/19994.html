    \n Dungeons and Dragons, a sonnet

    The world's described by a Dungeon Master,
    Adventurous characters are controlled by you
    Face monsters, challenges, try to get through.
    You each choose one: fighter, rogue or caster
    The DM decides, if you want it to go faster
    Or you can roll dice to measure your kung fu
    A thirteen or more on d20 will do
    To strike, or heal, or avoid disaster
    After you win, you obtain a reward
    Treasure, or magic, or advance your skills
    All should add up to around a plus one
    On your d20, however it's scored
    There's other rewards you can get, more frills
    But you'll find poem alone will be good fun
    Until the fancy books you can afford

    get on my level

    \n It seems both a general shame and a detriment to the article to not include some actual text from the games you are discussing; after all they're some of the few games short enough that their full rules text could be included, or meaningfully excerpted. Especially if this is an audience of poets or poetry-readers, I doubt the article as-written will give them the slightest idea whatsoever what an actual game poem is like (while providing dozens and dozens of notions of what it is 'like'.)

    It's particularly weird with the quote from the newspaper review of Marc Majcher's book. It's like if I was reading an article about imagism and the author made room for a paragraph-long quote of somebody reviewing Pound's "Cantos" but didn't include a single line from the work itself. The newspaper reviewer's reaction would certainly be a lot easier to evaluate (as funny, as understandable, etc.) if we knew what some of Marc's games actually looked like.

    I feel like you could easily expand each paragraph into several by treating on some concrete examples and quotations for each. Or if space is an issue I think it'd be better to try and provide at least one example early on to give the reader some kind of anchor.
