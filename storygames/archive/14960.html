\n Some of the best layout advice comes in short, pithy sentences. What are your best tips? They needn't be complex.

My best one is:

Use a grid

Or more subtly:

If you don't know where to put something on a page, divide the space into equal increments (2, 3, 4...), then use those divisions to place it.

    \n\nEero_Tuovinen \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nAugust 2011 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Consistency begets pattern; difference begets contrast; having both at once is harmony. Pattern without contrast is redundant, contrast without pattern is chaotic.

    Use external patterns to group together things that are internally similar (to be considered together, that is) and external contrasts to separate things that are internally different (and therefore to be considered separately).

    The text you're working on has an internal structure; your task as a book designer is to make the internal external, so as to obviate the need for the receiver to actually read to perceive the structure. Denying this need of externalized structure is denying the need for paragraph breaks, capitalization, word spacing, all of which were adopted historically in the interest of visualizing internally pre-existing structure. Your job is not to invent structure, but rather to display it.

    If you don't know that you know how a given technical facet of the work works, then you don't know how it works, and your ignorance will ruin the project. Naively going into color printing with RGB-mode graphics is basically the personal sin of intellectual laziness, of assuming things about the universe you have no right to. Be vigilant of what you do not know.\n
    Posted By: mattasticPosted By: RogerIf the average person notices how nice your layout/typography is, you're probably doing it wrong.
    \nHow is this doing it wrong?
    The untrained eye, when presented with the task of evaluating a layout, will look at the layout. The trained eye will look at what is being laid out. Having an untrained eye commend your layout indicates that you're pushing the visuals over the content. The best feedback you can get from an untrained eye is a lukewarm "I guess it's nice enough" or "I don't know, I see nothing wrong in it". If they're being amazed at your gloss, then you've progressed from book design to being a graphical artist yourself.

    \n My advice to people learning layout/typography? Understand that graphic design is a professional discipline that takes years to master. Design is not knowing how to use InDesign any more than carpentry is knowing how to use a hammer. Competence takes time. Excellence takes an order of magnitude more. Read some good books on the subject. Practice a lot. Find people you trust who are professionals to critique your work. (Don't bother listening to the critiques of people who don't have the skills you're trying to acquire; they may be well meaning, but they don't know what to look for or how to judge.) Know that for a while you will suck. That's fine. Keep practicing.
    \n
    \nAnd for the love of all that is good and holy, never use Papyrus. Not even for your Ancient-Egyptian-themed project. Especially not for that.

    \n Those would be sane defaults for your justification settings: word spacing can be as low as 80% of a normal space or as much as 133%, space between letters can scale 3% larger or smaller, and individual glyphs can scale 2% larger or smaller. If you really know what you're doing (I don't) you can fine-tune those even more, but this is a very sane standard for general layout which for some reason is not the default in InDesign. With these values you can usually turn off hyphenation, justify, and still avoid any painful spaces or crammed lines.

      Posted By: David BergDoes anyone have advice on where to steal workhorse typefaces? My design school classmates hooked me up with a lot of good ones, but no Akzidenz Grotesk!
      \nIf you're fine with stealing, it's basically a googling job - just pick the typefaces you want and crawl sufficiently over the Internet, and you'll find the files in some half-forgotten open directory somewhere, or in a live torrent. Just remember to verify that there are no limitations on the use permissions, or crack the font before use. Also, accept that you don't have entire certainty over the pedigree of your font files, and it is therefore entirely possible that the files you use will have kerning issues or missing glyphs. In addition, you will find it difficult to find a specific version of a classic font, so this is probably not the best course if you care about the differences between Adobe and ITC Garamonds, or whatever.

      Posted By: David BergPosted By: sageA block of text set on a page with even margins on every side is never a good idea.
      Can you elaborate?
      Sure!

      I'll also backpedal a little: A block of text set on a page with even margins on every side is never a good idea in print. On the web it's alright.

      First off, equal margins don't account for the physical structure of your book. Depending on the number of pages your interior margin may need to be larger to allow for the binding. You'll also need space for the user to hold the book, usually the outsides and/or bottom.

      Page layout is also a way of orienting the reader on the page and within the text. The amount of space helps guide them from page to page and helps build a visual memory along the lines of "oh, I know this was on a right-hand page somewhere about here."

      There are also some combinations of ratios that are just more visually pleasing, just like some musical notes form chords.

      Lastly, in my opinion, margins are opportunities to use to distinguish your book with art, notes, or anything else. To serve that purpose they probably shouldn't be uniform.

      My personal preference is to set the text as high and tight as feels good leaving generous bottom margins for holding and page numbers, with generous outer margins for notes, art, etc.

      \n Good layout is a craft, like building a table. You measure, align, fit, and join according to rules and structure.

      Every layout problem has been solved, by experts, thousands of times over. You never have to invent anything. Study the masters, learn the solutions, and apply them.

      When in doubt, take a ruler to a professional design, measure everything (especially text block size to page size ratio and number of characters per line) note down the numbers, and copy it, exactly.

      Sage is talking about the canons of page construction. Study them. Use them.

      \n Alternately, don't.

      Here's the thing, for the past 5+ years, pretty much everything I've picked up for gaming texts has been in electronic format. And, frankly, paging through a book that was laid out for print adds pretty much nothing to the experience for me.

      I would absolutely LOVE it if more authors would put out plain text versions of their work in Word or Open doc format. Just a few tables. A 2 column format if possible. No fancy fonts or anything. No full page images unless they're useful play aids like maps or pictures of specific NPCs that I might want to show the players. Just the important content.

      I know. Everyone freaks out about putting their content out in Word format because OMG SOMEBODY MIGHT CHANGE IT. But that's exactly what I want to do. If an NPC in a module hasn't been named and I want to give them a name, it's way more awesome if I can make that change right in the electronic version I have, so that it'll be right there in the reference copy I print to bring to the table.

      I tend to print things out in booklet format on the fancy printer at work. I make notes and changes and then print it out again fresh for the next session.

      I know, it's kind of a threadjack. So if someone wants to have a big debate about this, it would probably be best to take it elsewhere. But I thought I'd just throw that out there for all the book-focused people to consider.

      In other words, I don't want layout and typography. I want content. Just thought I'd mention that, in case anyone cared.

      \n Regarding interior margins (aka, the gutter, which is not to be confused with the inter-column gutter):

      The classical method of determining page margins is to divide your page into a 9x9 grid. The top and inside margins will be one unit, the bottom and outside margins should be two units. If these margins are too generous, use a 12x12 grid.

      To check if you have generous enough interior margins, print some sample text and stick it in a real book that is similar in size and shape to the one you hope to produce.
