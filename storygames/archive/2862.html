    \n Jason, the main difference between improv and tabletop is the ability to consider and take action on that consideration. You are able to accept meta-input, you are able to think out loud, exploring multiple actions, all without negatively impacting the game experience. None of this is true in improv. If you're thinking about plot, you're not thinking about what's going on in front of you, and that's when really bad things happen.

    Like I said, there are things that can be done to ameliorate that. HOWEVER, I have not been able to try any of them out. I really think the path is to import short-form techniques (which at their best are rituals with varying input) to a long-form setting. This sounds very much like what Paul is doing with the Soap Opera form.

    \n To me the main difference between my (half-dozen sessions of) improv experience and tabletop play has specifically been "buy-in".

    When you stand up to do an improv skit, you are basically drinking the kool-aid for the next few minutes and agreeing to work within a very specific framework. It's very constraining for a reason. Example: "Ok, Tony and John are on a rowboat moving away from a burning ship. Tony, you can only use sentences of five words. John, sentences of three words. Go!!"

    I find it very hard to get that sort of buy-in from a tabletop group for a scene much less an entire session. Players have agendas, others want to tell a certain type of story, and still others are basically there to be entertained. And just like one performer who "doesn't get it" will often cause an improv scene to fall flat, it only takes one gamer at a table saying "wait" to knock the group off-balance.

    Now I listen to AP reports and podcasts from (specifically) Remi and Jason's group, and they are focused like a fricken laser beam. They have a small group with what seems to be enormous buy-in. They appear to self-enforce setting and they are all prepared for the envitable cockpunching. But tell me if I'm wrong here.

    But I don't have a regular group and my game sessions are often ad-hoc with a few reoccuring players and whomever drops in at the store. I also play a lot at minicons.

    So, at least in my experiences, buy-in and perhaps ritual are keeping me from having an "improv-like" experience at the table. It's not so much a concept of plot and characterization...or maybe we just haven't got to that level yet:)

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarIn our first improv class, we were told to religiously avoid plot. This might be reasonably good advice for beginning improvisers, but it is anithetical to the tabletop experience, where you are typically chaining scenes together into an extended narrative, often linear.
    \nSure, but I'm wondering if trying to avoid plot at the scene level can be useful in tabletop play. One incident that keeps coming to mind was the infamous "Flaming Taft" game at GenCon last year. Remi and Joshua had one scene that was totally plot. They knew what it was going to be about, it was amazingly relevant to the overall story, and they went hard for it. To me, despite how relevant it was, it wasn't all that satisfying. Later, I started a scene where I had no idea how it was relevant to the plot. I just knew I had my girlfriend written down on the sheet, and I wanted to see what happened in a scene with her. We played it slow, just reacting to what was going on, until Judd realized he wanted in on it too. Even then, we didn't drive toward a conclusion, but once we figured out what needed to happen, it became amazingly relevant to the plot. That, to my mind, was much more satisfying. (And to other people, given the comments we got afterward.) We just took it line by line and reacted to what the other person was doing. The result became plot, in so far as it defined where future scenes would go, but it wasn't driven there.

    That's been my experience with improv as well. You frame scenes with the knowledge of what's gone before, but they work best when you don't let the plot drive them and instead just react to what the other person is doing. I've been doing a lot more of this in my tabletop play (it works especially well in PTA, which is what we're playing right now) with similarly pleasing results.

    \n Not to toot my own horn, but my Game Chef game, Young Adult RPG, very consciously uses reincorporation as its central reward and worldbuilding mechanism. It is, however, a heavily-constrained, non-campaign game that is Not For Everyone.

    Talysman: I'm going to ignore your comment and try to prove to you why this thread could be useful to you.

    Take what we're talking about above, Reincorporation. The decision to conciously reincorporate an element from earlier in a game is an effective technique that can be used no matter what your play style is. Reintroducing an element increases its power, in any game. Familiarity breeds engagement, engagement breeds good gaming, whether it's engagement in the character, or engagement in the dungeon encounter. Reincorporation makes better games where you're experiencing AND creating, instead of one or the other. Seriously, I'm not talking about super-immersionist actor-play here. I play almost exclusively in a directorial-style and the techniques of improv have been nothing but a boon to my play.

    The most important skill in improv is not acting, but active listening.
