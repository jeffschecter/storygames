\n In play, I always have to access, process and deliver information really fast. In particular if I'm not playing freeform. So while trying to learn D&D4's standard format for adventure modules, I happened to glance past that old chart of map symbols which appears to have been standard in D&D for decades now, and a Little Idea formed.

When preparing for play, so you use any kind of shorthand? What kind - any abbreviations keep coming back, symbols you find useful, positioning of text and information layout? Would it be worth it to try to set up a coherent system for this kind of thing?

    \n I use a mnemonic for organizing encounters... TRAP.

    Threat - something the players are facing
    Reward - something the players get if they succeed
    Asset - something that can be used
    Problem - something that complicates

    T - Ogres, L 8 enc.
    R - Diamond tooth filling
    A - Ruined wall, half-height, provides cover
    P - Pile of corpses, mildly undead, grabs legs

    \n Exactly!

    I often find that I resort to filling a single piece of paper with lists and grids, even when I have a perfectly good published scenario to work with. Kenneth Hite blogged something tangentially related a while ago - game designers seem to have nearly stopped elaborating the fantastically useful invention of randomized tables very early in the development of roleplaying games, to focus on macrodungeons and campaign settings.

    I tried a different format once (digs in digital thingies - three years ago!) but it didn't work out that well; in actual play, the momentum of the players' decisions kept getting tripped up. Not sure why; I think I decided at the time that it was the content and how I used the framework rather than the framework itself that got in the way, but I never got around to playtesting it more. It went like this:

    What's the element about
    Three possible scenes
    Three things that can happen

    Resulting in notes like these:

    The Evil Uncle (world-famous archeologist)
    Scenes:
    The return of the evil uncle!
    The evil uncle repents
    Treason of the evil uncle
    Can:
    Talk about the fabled land of Agartha
    Save one of the character's life, if he himself perishes in the attempt
    Kill one of the characters, if he himself perishes in the attempt
