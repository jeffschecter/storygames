\n Take a skirmish miniature game like HeroClix or DnD Minis.

Let's say the rules say:\n

* You get XP for each enemy you defeat, using your own attacks - these XP carry over from scenario to scenario
* You can move any character around the battlefield using the Teleport power
\nNow, there's this specific tournament scenario, including a bottomless pit in the middle of the battlefield. If any character falls into the pit, they are instantly killed. The object of the scenario is to defeat all characters in your opponent's army.

Alice is the referee, and has interpreted the rules such that if any character falls into the pit, nobody gets any XP for it.

Beatrice and Catherine are playing the scenario. Beatrice's army includes a character who can Teleport their enemies within 10 squares. This character is a valid model published by the same company who wrote the base rules and the scenario.

Play begins, and Beatrice moves her army within range of Catherine's. Catherine takes some potshots at Beatrice's figures. Beatrice spends this, and each subsequent turn, Teleporting Catherine's characters into the bottomless pit, thus yielding no XP, but eventually easily winning the scenario.

Catherine complains loudly at the unfairness of this tactic, and how Beatrice isn't playing the game right, citing the lack of XP reward as an indicator that Beatrice is just doing it to mess with her fun. She petitions Alice to either remove the pit from the map, make it impossible to Teleport someone to the pit, or reduce the range of Teleport, or something. Beatrice defends herself saying she's just trying to win the scenario, and that seemed to be the easiest way to do it - the lack of XP doesn't bother her.

Alice is mysteriously quiet on the matter and just silently points to the rules.

The scenario starts up again, Catherine changes her tactic slightly, but Beatrice easily wins using the same tactic as before. More complaints ensue. Rinse, repeat.

Who is at fault?

Alice, for refraining from house ruling the scenario or the rules to ensure Catherine can use her army on equal footing?
Beatrice, for exploiting the unintended (?) consequence of the Teleport rules and the bottomless pit?
Catherine, for complaining at the unfairness of it all?
The game itself, for failing to account for the powerful combo of Teleport and pit?
The Creator of the universe who allows such sorrow to occur, yet does nothing about it?

Weigh in.

    \n
    \nWhat did the designer of the scenario have in mind when they wrote the scenario? Were they envisioning the pit as being a big decisive feature that players were supposed to manipulate for victory? If so, then Beatrice figured it out, and Catherine didn't. Beatrice wins. One might argue that if Alice had to interpret the rules for the impact of the pit that perhaps the unnamed designer should have done a better job making it clear what the scenario was really about, especially if its a tournament. One might not highlight the issue to the players as a way of rewarding the studious and prepared...but one should certainly highlight the issue to the refs so they know what's going on.
    \n
    \nIf, however, the designer never even considered the impact of the pit of death and how it would interplay with powers, skewing heavily the actual value of a figure relative to its pointed value...then its a poorly designed scenario and the designer needs a kick in the butt. If it was clear that the designer hadn't properly thought through all of the implications then Alice, as organizer, should have stepped in and made the necessary rulings. Since this is a tournament scenario she would have to do so in conjunction with all of the other referees for that tournament.
    \n
    \nAs with so much in life, the problem begins and ends with incompatable expectations.
    \n
    \nCatherine had the expectation that the tournament would be balanced based on the point value of the armies. 1000 point army is a 1000 point army.
    \n
    \nBeatrice had the expectation that the tournament created a special situation that rendered the Teleportation power vastly more useful than its mere points would suggest...and that this was by design, so she used it.
    \n
    \nThe designer had the duty to make the scenario's expectations clear (especially in a tourney) to the referees, which this example does not give enough information to determine if that was done.
    \n
    \nAlice had the duty to discover what the scenario's expecations were before refing a tournament. The example suggests ("mysteriously quiet") that Alice did not do this but is inconclusive. What effort did Alice make to coordinate with other refs at the tournament to make sure all were interpretting the scenario's expectations the same way...an important factor in a tourney environment.

    \n I want to bring up something that I came across recently in my studies.

    Humans like to ascribe responsibility. More than that, they do it in order to decide who is to blame.

    But sometimes someone's responsible, but we wouldn't say they are at fault, and we need to blame them.

    I think that's a bad practice, and one we're all prone to.

    Who's responsible though? All of them. Beatrice for doing performing an action, Catherine for keeping to play even though she's not having fun (or so she says), and Alice for not making a decision, or trying to mediate.
    The creator of the game is actually less responsible, unless this is an official venue, like a tournament, where the rules must be followed. He has the initial responsibility, but that's the thing, permutations are endless. If they hears of this issue and doesn't weigh in on it, then the creators get another share of responsibility, but still less, because it's up to the players (including the referee) to judge for themselves what is fun, what isn't, and how to deal with it.

    Heck, when I meet a game I don't enjoy, I stop playing it. I don't blame others for having fun, or for ruining my fun when I play with them. I'm a big boy, and if something is an activity I do for fun, and it isn't, I don't do it.
    No one is forcing Catherine to play with Beatrice. And if Beatrice isn't having fun because of Catherine's complaints, no one is forcing her to play with her either.

    Edit: If it's a tournament, and not merely a tournament scenario played outside of a tournament, then, I would only consider assigning blame to the one who came up with the pit, not specifying it more clearly. I would only assign responsibility if it's an oversight, in a way. As for this scenario, I don't think Beatrice is doing anything wrong, the goal is not to get the most XP, getting XP is a way to help one achieve the goal, of victory.

    As for how it changes the above: By signing up for a tournament, you sign up to face things that you will not like, or are not ready for, and try to eke out a win. As I made clear in the other post, in Magic: the Gathering or Warmachine tournaments, there are things I'll play against though I'd be rather unwilling to do it outside of a tournament. When I sign up for a tournament, I sign up to meet opponents who will do everything they can (legally, and in the spirit of sportsmanship*) to win.

    * No cheating. Combos/loopholes are kosher.

    \n Adam, I agree in principle, and I also get where you're coming from (the MUSH scene, which I was heavily entrenched in for a while). In the context of a large multi-player game, there will definitely be a layer of player norms which play a large part in how the game is "meant to be played" from an emergent play perspective. You'll note I'm also removing all prior history in this thought experiment, with regards to earlier rulings, player relationships, status and notoriety, etc.

    The reason I started this thread was not to show how the Myers experiment was valid and how his conclusions were sound, but how you cannot start name-calling and assigning blame ("griefer", "scrub", "dick" etc) without considering the underlying design principles of the game and the responsibility of the game's designers and operators. I saw an awful lot of "well, player so-and-so is obviously to blame" in the accompanying thread and it made me concerned, especially given the context of this forum.

    Also, I think there's a big difference between a sandbox-style game (like a MUSH or a Play-by-Forum community) and a very structured and mechanically completionist game (like EVE Online, many MUDs, HeroClix, or Chess). The first style relies on social rules to create any type of consistent play, while the second type can (at least seemingly) be played exactly as presented without worrying about social norms and unspoken rules of engagement. Tabletop RPGs straddle these two types, which is what has led to the development of theories and techniques for managing social contract, etc. Where CoH/CoV falls, I think, is more nebulous and subjective. Obviously Myers seems to think it a mechanically completionist game where, as long as he's not cheating, his objective - which must be met, at any cost - is the fictionally supported goal of eradicating villains. His opponents seem to describe the game more as a sandbox-style game where the objective is to get mechanically more powerful by fully utilizing the reward system. And in all this, NCsoft is taking a hands-off approach, refusing to properly describe the intention behind their game's objective - presumably to cater to all possible viewpoints of their subscribers simultaneously.

    \n Let me riff off Quintin's post.

    Yeah, that teleport-to-death power is clearly a winning combo. For purely competitive, non-social play such as one might expect in the kind of tournament you describe, this is broken design. In complex games, emergent play often produces unexpected stuff like this. (Look at the history of restricted and prohibited cards in Magic: The Gathering for a case in point.)

    So the scenario designer deserves some of the credit for this situation. They have produce a scenario that allows a combination that always wins, putting all the emphasis on character creation (pre-game strategy) instead of character play (in-game tactics). That is very likely not what they meant to do.

    The referee Alice deserves some of the credit for this situation, too. A scenario that allows one specific power to win through what seems to be a rules "loophole" doesn't seem fair. Alice obviously saw the need to change something (she ruled you don't earn XP for exploiting the the teleport-to-pit death), but missed the point: the loophole is a clear-win strategy, which is generally poor game design. Alice should have either forbidden the teleport power, forbidden teleport into the pit ("it's a no-teleport zone"), removed the pit, or changed the effect of the pit (say, it does 1d10 damage instead of instant death). This requires a lot of Alice (she has to think like a game designer for some of those choices). If Alice didn't want to tread into that territory though, she shouldn't have changed the XP award rules for the loophole. It's also shitty that Alice refuses to rule one way or another. It's interesting to think of all this from a game design perspective.
