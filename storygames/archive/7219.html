\nFor my Ashcan, Kagematsu, Paul was interested in trying his hand at the cover artwork.

    \n As the artist I'm allowed to try and influence the judge, right?

    John's is great. I think he was sharp to recognize that the woman's kimono was too bright in the line art version, and his correction for it is brilliant. The head and neck of the bird brings the viewer's attention back to the interaction between Kagematsu and the woman. And I got a grin out of seeing him cover up what I presume he thought were weaknesses in my art: the three "threat" thumbnails, and the fade thing I did to the scabbard (which I did because when I pencilled a full length scabbard it threw off the composition of the whole piece). It's cool to see how a little confident disrespect on the part of a colorist can bring a piece of art to a different level.

    I like Steve's aggressive use of red. I have a sense that the concentric shapes of the various red shades are compression artifacts. But it does give a frenetic intensity to the piece. Steve, was all the "pulsing" of the red intentional, or accidental?

    Anna is clearly the master of dimensionalizing figures with digital shading. If I'd had time to try my hand at fully coloring the art for the ashcan I would have tried the kind of digital shading that she's done. The woman's face and jaw line and Kagematsu's hand are particularly awesome.

    Jasper, you did your coloring physically, with actual markers? I really like the hand colored aesthetic for an ashcan. And I really like the complexity of the background color. When I was doing research for the line art I found a lot of hand colored black and white photographs of geisha and samurai from the mid-1800s, and they had a similar "hand tinted" aesthetic. It's cool to see a tinting of Kagematsu's clothing that reminds me so much of some of my reference sources.

    Jonathan's seems to almost hint at being "animation cell" art. Digital font stylings on a layer over blobby, distorted kanji, over my line art, over washy abstract staining. I almost can see them set as separate layers in a shadow box with an eighth inch space between them, so they shift relative to each other depending on the viewer's angle of perception. And that's pretty cool.

    Paul

    \n I used PSPX to do mine. I used the magic wand to select the background and then filled it in red. I airbrushed some other colours over the red. I widened the selection area a bit and smoothed round the edges of the people. Next I applied the topography filter to the red area which uses different intensities of the main colour to indicate pixel intensity. That's what created the shapes. So it was on purpose but I didn't really know where I was going.

    And then I was completely defeated by the house and just blotted it out.

    And then I spelt the name of the game incorrectly but that's in keeping with my record on these things, "See Dracula" for example.

    My approach to design is to play around with things to see what happens. And then a proper artist comes along and moves a few things and it looks wonderful. It's a real skill.
