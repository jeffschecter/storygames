\n How would you fix this game?

The Quest for the Jewel on the Glittering Path

Your objective is to assemble the Jewel. You don't know exactly what the Jewel will look like, but you know it'll be really cool. You know it's composed of various geometric solids (pyramids, spheres, cubes, etc.) and many colors.

The game rule is that, in your quest for the Jewel, you must stay on the Glittering Path. This is a big part of the fun of the game, because when you stroll along the Path at a decent clip, its many colors and facets glitter in a truly breathtaking way.

The Path is composed of tiny Gems of all colors and shapes. No two Gems are exactly alike. The right Gems can be combined to form certain geometric solids, and the right combination of solids and Gems will ultimately produce the Jewel. You can take any Gems you want.

As you prepare to step onto the path, the game tells you what you can expect to find. Certain sections of the Path will be focused largely on specific geometric solids. When you reach the "Cube" part of the Path, for example, you will see a big chunk of a cube in warm colors ahead of you, and many gems of red and orange in the Path nearby. Eventually, your progress on the Path will take you past the cube, at which point you will either have completed its assembly, and have a big piece of the Jewel, or you will have failed, and will need a substitute geometric solid for your Jewel.

Time to play!

You step onto the Glittering Path and look over the millions of Gems before you. You pick one up. One face is dull blue, and rather squarish. Another face is sharp and red. This might be part of a cool cube, or a warm triangle, or a key joint in the Jewel! You pick up another Gem, and it's just as promising. In fact, every Gem looks just as promising. But now you're standing still, and the Path isn't glittering. You select a bunch of Gems and press forward. As you get up to speed, the Path glitters again, and is awesome and blissful, but you keep passing Gems that look like they might be important! Also, the Gems you are carrying threaten to slip from your arms, and you need to divert your attention from the blissful Path in order to hold onto them.

As you approach the warm-colored chunk of cube, you slow, and search the Gems in front of you for reds, oranges, and yellows, and right angles. A few Gems are obvious and perfect fits, but many more are partly red, and/or fairly angular. And mixed in are plenty of Gems of uniquely cool attributes that might make valued pieces of the Jewel! Now you're stopped again, and your arms are filling up with slippery Gems, and the Path no longer glitters.

What to do? At some point, you probably do a cost/benefit analysis. You weigh the importance of strolling and watching the Path glitter against the importance of grabbing every cool Gem you can and maximizing your shot at assembling each geometric solid. You do the best you can.

Still, though, every time you select a Gem, there is some frustration. Who knows for sure whether this Gem will actually help me? Would another Gem have been better? How soon will I find out? Have I slowed my pace on the Path for nothing?

    \n Hmm. Well, think about chess. You're not looking at piece for its fixed characteristics. You're looking at it's position relative to other pieces, what it can do now, and what it can maybe do in the future. You're looking for patterns which you internalized during prior play. And each individual decision isn't a binary right or wrong. It changes the landscape of the relationships between the pieces.

    So you have to make the choosing of a gem more complex. It has an impact on your ability to choose other gems, or on your ability to recognize the value in other gems. Maybe certain choices make other gems glow, providing information about their value to creating the jewel, or maybe certain choices make other gems fall of the path into an unavailable storm drain. Maybe certain gems are only useful to the jewel if you choose them in the correct sequence. Then maybe it becomes a game of skill.

    Still doesn't sound like a fun game though. Making the complications compelling is the art of game design.

    \n We unconsciously recognize good games by the learning opportunities they represent. Chess is about pattern recognition, prediction, and visualizing and understanding the way the pieces project force within the space of the board. We're drawn to the game because we recognize that memory recall and finding intersections in patterns are useful mental skills.

    I'm going to abstract this out to help me understand what's going on here.

    A Gem offers two types of information, A and B. (Actor & role.)

    One objective is to select a set of A's that meet criteria X (Actors never appeared together.). Whether a given A meets criteria X is a challenge of knowledge and memory. This knowledge is valued by the social group. It is something most players will have and share to some degree. Remembering it is neither trivially easy nor impossibly hard.

    (In case I'm losing anyone: Knowing who actors are and what movies they've been in is valued, as it's handy for hanging out and talking about movies. Watching movies is common, though different people will have seen some of the same ones and some different ones. Remembering who was in what movie can be easy or difficult.)

    The other objective is to select a set of B's that meet criteria Y (One of each). Whether a given B meets criteria Y is obvious. This adds complexity to the selection of Gems: not every Gem whose A meets criteria X is automatically viable for selection.

    A and B share a common topic. Any A can serve as a specific example of the B it's paired with. As such, past associations are invoked, aiding recall and lending familiarity to the goal.

    (Oh, that actor also played bad guys! I remember now, that dude from the Professional! That's Gary Oldman! So, we're assembling a movie with Depp as the hero and Oldman as the badguy, I could see this being like The Devil's Own, sweet.)

    Paul, do you think that sums it up?

    Testing a counter-example:

    Each Gem has a different atomic symbol on it and is in the shape of a different state of matter. You need to collect a liquid shape, a metal shape, a gas shape, and a salt shape, using only elements with different numbers of free electrons, to make the Jewel.

    Unless you're playing with a very geeky group, you'll have trouble remembering Sodium's electrons, and you're unlikely to get applause and "oh yeah, that's right!" when you do remember. Unless your group enjoys talking about elements, they won't care at all about the info for its own sake; they'll value it only as a means to an end (the Jewel). Even leaving out group dynamics, racking your brain over the periodic table is less appealing to most players than racking your brain over movies.

    In the end, instead of winding up with an evocative combo of actors and roles, you have a hydrogen-based liquid, an iron-based metal, an argon-based gas and a chlorine-based salt... which I guess you could imagine as a corrosive, steaming mass if you really wanted to.

    So the appeal of the "faces & roles" solution isn't inherent to the formal process of the challenge, but rather is contingent on the values and associations it taps from outside play.

    If I have that right, I'll have to ponder whether it's possible to enjoy the glitter of a Path made up of actor faces.
