    \n Hey Ryan, here are my thoughts.

    I'm not at all fond of the way the way you are positioning this.

    The world needs more guides and filters, and I'm all for that - for instance, I think the "best of" StoryGames shout-outs are great, and we need more attribution of excellence to the good stuff out there. And in a sense, this is kind of what you're offering. Only the flavour isn't to my taste at all. I'll start with the name.

    The name makes me very uncomfortable. Calling it The Cull is totalising and eliminativist. The The makes it totalising; it suggests that there is a clearly right set of content that should exist in the storygames/indie community, and this is going to be the source of it all. The Cull makes it eliminativist, and carries some chiding alongside; it suggests that the indie community is composed of a lot of stuff that basically shouldn't still exist, that should have been removed (ie by the community) but wasn't, and that you're stepping up to the perform the tough job that the community didn't have the stomach for.
    ...

    I've just noticed, that not only is it called The Cull, but you in your "About" page you refer to the activity you are engaged in as "Culling". Given that, everything in the previous paragraph goes twice. Reviewing or reflecting or opinionating on something is a very different thing from culling. It's a great mindshare technique to associate your brand with an activity (ask Google or Hoover), but I would ask you to consider the problematic freighting of this term.


    The other thing that makes me uneasy is your positioning of what the blog is for - namely, to direct the wider interest to the right stuff. I honestly don't believe that this sector is mature enough for anyone to take the perspective of objective "consumer interest defender". We're a bunch of smart excitable people with opinions. Heck, even Vincent (who's done the rounds and earned his chops) prefaces anyway with the notion that this is his particular perspective on what he (and some others) want from roleplaying.

    The thing is, you can justify all kinds of brusqueness and ungenerosity in the name of the supposed consumer/greater good.You seem to have distanced yourself from the effects the Cull may have on the people you review. I can't shake the feeling that you're posing it as some kind of supreme natural selection process: the pressures it imposes might lead to better work the authors. But if it does end up as a discouragement, well, that was never the primary concern, which is the future of the species sector.

    As I think I've made clear, a lot of this comes down to positioning and your longer-term objectives for this. Insofar as we need more sifting for the gold in the community, I'm grateful for your efforts. As I look through the content of what you've done for SoK, post-recommendations from others, I think this could be a useful resource. You'd have to ask Judd what impact, if any, it's had on him. But, as you're going to be moving onto other things, and other people, I figure this is the time to query a little bit what this is supposed to be about. Is this Ryan's Faves, Tips and Advice on Improvement, or The Long Overdue Correction in the Indie RPG Market from the Invisible Hand? Because it's screaming the latter to me.

    \n So I'm going to say what prompted my initial post.

    I've been toying with the idea of a podcast, not because we (you know, us) need it, but because I think it would be a fun thing to do with my friends. I was never very serious about it, and I'm very busy, so it would probably never happen.

    As soon as I read The Cull, any possibility for me ever doing a gaming-related podcast flew out the window.

    When I was a kid, the adults (the squares) would tell me my shitty garage punk sucked. It did, but they didn't know what they were talking about so I told them to fuck off. Now I'm a jazz musician, and I teach jazz and blues guitar as a profession. If Thurston Moore told me I sucked, I'd probably be driving a desk. If Thurston gave me a negativity sandwich: a very positive statement first, then a constructive suggestion on what could make my weaknesses stronger, and finished with an encouraging statement that will inspire me to keep creating...then well, much more shitty music, but at least I'll learn with experience.

    If some gaming square (who plays a particular game I don't care for in a manner i don't enjoy) critiques my podcast...well, middle finger.     I'm trying to answer this question: "When I eliminate the stuff that's boring, or meandering, or unrelated to gaming, what remains?" I'm going to approach it from my own perspective without spending a lot of time on what that perspective is (i.e. what I said to Jason about credibility, above).

    As Graham pointed out, it's much easier to focus on the negative. That can be a good thing - it's easier, when combing through a large volume of things - to eliminate those that meet definitive, easily identified criteria. I can't go through everything looking for ways it might be worth keeping; that would take eons for me, although a really nice guy might be able to do it. But as Levi pointed out, I don't need to present what goes on in the abbatoir (that's why I reformatted and took a lot of that stuff out).

    I'm calling it The Cull because that's how I'm going about it, and I'd be disingenuous if I Culled and then called it "Diamonds in the Rough." When people see the Cull, and that big ol' public domain axe, they know exactly how I'm going about the blog's purpose. I'm putting on my totalising, eliminativist hat (crown) and separating out the wheat from the chaff.

    But I understand why that would be uncomfortable. However, if you put up something in public and claim to have achieved some purpose with it, I think it's natural for someone to come along and, finding some places where you missed that purpose, point at the places you really nailed it.

    Posted By: Alex Fthe indie community is composed of a lot of stuff that basically shouldn't still exist, that should have been removed (ie by the community) but wasn't
    But that's not the only reason blogs exist. If you're reading to keep up on what your friends are doing, or looking back on how things came to be the way they are, or what your favorite designers are/were working on, that's a whole other purpose. You won't find that other purpose in the Cull.\n
    Posted By: Alex FThe other thing that makes me uneasy is your positioning of what the blog is for - namely, to direct the wider interest to the right stuff. I honestly don't believe that this sector is mature enough for anyone to take the perspective of objective "consumer interest defender".
    I disagree. While blogging is new, and so is gaming, looking critically at a text and whether it achieves a given purpose isn't new.

    "Does this block of text address its topic?" i.e. "Is it about gaming?"
    "Is this block of text too short or too long, given what its topic is?"
    "Does it stay on topic or meander off?"
    "Do the notes which follow this text address its topic, another relevant topic, provide insight that's related to the topic, and so on?"
    "Is there anything else in this text - such as humor or use of examples - that helps it stand out from drier examples of the same topic?"

    These are questions I feel qualified to answer - and if you disagree, and think I'm missing something crucial by using my method, that's fine. You're welcome to tell me what I missed, or you can do all the work I've done in parallel using a different methodology. If you decide to go through the full first season of Sons of Kryos, I'd be surprised if you didn't think Episode 8 was the best one, that many of the segments were only concerned with things happening at the time, and so on. I'm not going to claim that my opinion is the only one possible - not at least until I see a few more gold idols.

    \n Hey Brennan - I'm trying to do something more like Clem Greenburg as portrayed in Pollock (the Ed Harris movie). He (the real guy Clem Greenburg) wrote art criticism for a column in the Nation.

    If you looked at Clem's notes on Pollock you'd find several pieces that say "Same as #12" "Not as good as Clyfford Still" or "You can tell that by the time he hit the lower half that his arm was really tired."

    He might like Pollock, he might be friends with Pollock, but his job as an art critic is to tell people whether and when Pollock created something worth looking at.

    Before Clem meets Pollock, Pollock paints. Maybe it's a personal expression, but in time if it's held up by painters as this massively influential work that is worthy of painters' attention, that will probably draw Clem's attention. Clem's going to criticize it because he knows how and he's filling a need (there are thousands of painters in America). If Pollock paints better because of that criticism it's a great, but unintended outcome. If Pollock doesn't paint because Clem's out there, that's a sad unintended outcome. If Clem's readers find great art as a result of his column, he's getting his intended outcome.
