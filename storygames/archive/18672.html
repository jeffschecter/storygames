    \n I'm pretty sure that "challenge-based play" is just what I've been calling a certain type of gamism in an attempt to avoid unnecessary GNS terminology. I've used it in a pretty obvious way, it seems to me: play where the creative agenda is concerned with determining and resolving challenges. It's a wide conceit, of course, but it's worked for me because I've utilized it in an array with other concepts like "adventure gaming" and "D&D" that, when brought together, slice down into a pretty specific sort of game. Of course the "challenge" is not just any creative challenge in playing a roleplaying game, but specifically the sort of challenge where it is entertaining to see whether somebody might succeed or fail with it; I wouldn't call it a "challenge-based game" if we played say Dogs in the Vineyard and everybody found it very challenging to portray their characters credibly.

    As for Apocalypse World, I can't really parse what using a creatively well-defined game like that "for" some completely different purpose would mean. Would it be like taking the dice mechanics from it and slotting in e.g. a D&D challenge framework? If we're just talking of the rules mechanisms (ability bonuses, 2d6 rolls, comparison against pre-determined "move" arrays), it doesn't seem to me to be a difficult proposition. If we're talking about the entire game, then the MCing routines and a bunch of the specific moves used in the game come in the way, I would expect.

    Probably the deepest single issue that would come up would be in the fact that a lot of the choices in AW are either arbitrary or obvious in tactical terms; this is because they are choices about the value-set, and not about challenges. A different game, such as D&D, might deal with this sort of thing by having the value-set (the mission parameters, in other words) be very clearly pre-established, so that during execution of the scenario there isn't that much ambiguity about it. AW, on the other hand, has plenty of crunch that specifically attempts to question who your character is or why they do things or what they should even be doing. Of course a lot of this could be stripped out by rewriting the moves to be more relevant tactically, and less relevant thematically.

    \nThis is a thing a lot of people have trouble with. It can be confusing if you're just looking at the moves and trying to do what they say.

    In every AW-based game that I can think of, when you get a 7-9 or 6- result, it's NOT that some totally new bad thing happens (that would be zany and random!), but typically that the PC suffers whatever danger or peril or choice or whatever that is ALREADY HAPPENING. So, if it's clear in the fiction that the monster and the PC are battling on the edge of a cliff, then it's totally cool for a failure to mean you fall off the cliff. As a fan of the characters, I might say "roll Defy Danger/Act Under Fire to see if you can grab hold of something as you topple," but you wouldn't necessarily have to do that, depending on the context. But if the cliff was never something that was clearly important in the fiction, then it's not really fair for the GM to suddenly introduce it. Likewise, I could have a totally unseen and unmentioned trap open up beneath the characters feet, but only if we're in a place where traps like that are obviously something that we all might suspect from the tone of the fiction, like a dungeon or someone's secret chambers or something.

    Basically, the risk is usually the most obvious thing and only in very rare cases a danger that hasn't been discussed in play before. Even in the case of the trap, it's possible that the presence of traps could have been hinted at or explicitly warned about earlier in the game. If the dungeon is called "The Mechanical Mazeway of a Thousand Deadly Perils," then I can probably make trap moves whenever I want!

    \n Yes and no.

    When you are designing a dungeon, you can put whatever you want in it.

    When you're running the dungeon in play, you don't get to add things. You can define, and invent details (because now that PCs are inspecting the map you made, they will probably want details), but you can't just add 20 wraiths.

    Kind of the point of the original D&D rules is that the DM has a dungeon, that is "real" in that it's already described and written down before play begins and that doesn't suddenly change in the middle of a game. Random tables produce spontaneous results and all, but those are also written beforehand. The DM may have to define aspects of the world in more detail for the players than is written down, but can't just make shit up and add it on the spot.

    This kind of breaks down once characters are off the map, ie in civilized areas and whatnot, and obviously not everybody played D&D like this all the time, but OD&D is pretty much Dave and Gary using wargaming rules in order to do this, and this is the old-school/OSR D&D that I am familiar with.

    So in using AW rules to do this, obviously some things are a little different, but not horrendously so.

    Different from normal AW:

    - Play a game about exploring an unfamiliar place. So, don't use the first sessions stuff, don't follow the PCs around in their life. That stuff is not important unless and until they go back to town/civilization and it becomes important because you are expanding the game beyond simply "exploring a dungeon." Outside of blatantly allowing players narrative authority, there's one good reason to get them to contribute setting and stuff is so they can role-play characters in a familiar environment. And these characters aren't in a familiar environment be definition.

    - You don't use principles like "draw maps and leave blanks," or "ask provocative questions and build on the answers," but you do "always say what your prep demands." I mean, you're still portraying a world and all that. It's just the primary focus of D&D is the DM's map, while the primary focus of AW/DW play is the PCs -- in AW that's the PCs and their lives in this dangerous and hostile environment, in DW that's the PCs and the adventures they are on.

    - You can still use fronts after the first session, but it's not really necessary. if the PCs have caused a ruckus in the dungeon, you might think about how the inhabitants react and how things change because of what they did, but you don't really need more than that (although it's worth pointing out that a game focused on thwarting the GM's fronts/threats is also a kind of challenge-based game).

    - Typical old-school players don't use read a sitch or read a person that much, at least not the ones I play with. If they like gonzo science fantasy settings, they might use open your brain a lot, though. They don't tend to care much about Hx (or bonds) either.     2. "I judo flip him over the edge of the cliff!"

    3. "First I cut off his hand, then I cut off his head!"

    \nThat's ALL we've done with World of Dungeons. How? The same way Eero does it. I guess I don't quite understand the confusion here. In the boulder situation, Eero might for a DEX roll. I'd do the same. Or it could be a WIS roll to spot that feature in the trees. Same thing in WoDu.

    There are two things going on here: 1) House rules, building your game as you play and 2) "Same mechanical odds". I'll tackle both.

    1) House Rules
    When you play any old-school kind of game and you feel like there should be additional nuance and rules support you add it yourself as you go. House rules are a fundamental component of this type of play. You may have noticed in Eero's posts that the "rules set" used at the table is constantly being modified to accommodate the group. They invent the Paladin class when someone does a quest for it. They sometimes have non-magical first aid, sometimes not. If they all care a lot about the difference between a mace and a sword, they add it in. But that feature didn't exist when they started playing.

    Same thing with WoDu. You have one move to start with, and then you modify and build out your actual house rules as a process of play. We immediately added Last Breath from DW, for example. I give -1 or -2 penalties to rolls under certain conditions (like using a certain weapon against armor built to resist it). We use AW-style countdown clocks for things like quicksilver addiction and demonic influence. These additions and modifications are part of the play of the game (just as they were with Moldvay).

    2) "Same mechanical odds"
    I think you're way too hung up on the "mechanically identical odds" thing. Lots of OSR-style games (Into the Odd, Moldvay D&D itself) use roll-under systems, usually without modifiers. They're still totally challenge-based, obviously. In the boulder example, just because you might "make a DEX roll" in either case, the potential risks and outcomes for a given maneuver might differ hugely based on the specific fictional actions taken. Ducking right, under the bridge and hiding behind some trees are completely different moves that put you in different positions.

    Maybe the bridge is closer. So it's easy for you to get cover and we roll DEX just to see if any of the shrapnel from the boulder hits you (d4 damage). The trees are far away. Maybe you can get there in time and avoid the boulder entirely, or maybe you're caught in the open and hit fully (3d6 damage). It really matters which one you choose! All fictional actions and their attendant risks and consequences are not identical (obviously) even if you make the same type of roll.

    What you'll find when you play this way is that fiction doesn't have to mean "-1 to a roll" to have meaning. It has much more meaning, in fact, when the details of the action affect the whole situation around the roll -- why you make it, what you're risking, what you have to gain, what position you're in afterwards, etc.

    If you ignore all of that, and reduce all of that nuance and detail down to "it's a DEX roll either way" you're missing what's fun about fiction-first play. This is why old-school challenge-based play is fiction-first. The tactical space is as deep as whatever level of detail you care to track. You're not limited by the "5-foot-square flanking bonus" set of options.

      When I'm dealing with challenges, as a player, these are some of the ways I go about strategizing:

      1. Knowing the GM (DM), understanding the way they think, reading their body language, stuff like that. They are trying to communicate certain information about the game, but their effectiveness at doing that can vary, and they are inevitable communicating certain other information, which you can also pick up on. This might get derided as meta-gaming and all, but it's still a thing.

      2. Reading the map. If we've done some exploration already, we've got an incomplete map with features on it and blank spaces. So I look at the map and try to figure out where the secret rooms are, or where links between certain areas might be. I'm also looking for the most effective route to get to where we want to go (where the treasure is), so we can get in and out with the loot while facing a minimum of hassle.

      2a. Experience with the "actual" environment depicted by the map. Once you've been exploring a place, you get a sense of what the rules are that govern it, like random monster checks, you learn how difficult the difficult passages are (climbing down caves, fording underground rivers, etc). So along with mapping comes other information that you can use to determine exploration strategies.

      3. Knowing the rules. So like, what are the rules for finding secret doors? Do we just find them when we look for them, Old School primer style? Do we use the Moldvay rules (1 in 6 chance), and if we do do we only get one roll per character or do we get to keep trying every 10 minutes? Do we get to see the die roll or is it secretly rolled by the DM? Do we use the discern realities move from DW? Knowing how the rules work allows you to judge their effectiveness in any particular situation.

      For example, let's say I've studied the map and there's a suspicious blank spot. Knowing the DM, he's the kind of guy to fill up the whole piece of graph paper, plus there's lots of wizards around here but no wizard's lab. So we want to go check for secret doors. We mark out all the places we think we should check, then we organize our expedition(s) in a way that makes the most advantage of the secret door rules. Maybe we get one character who is really good at it (they have high WIS, they're an elven thief) bodyguard them down to a specific spot and have them search. Maybe we grab a whole shitload of mooks and have them all search (the more d6s being rolled, the more chance of a 1 coming up, and mooks are expendable if we run into trouble).

      \n Regardless of how exactly one is supposed to interpret the rules where "kill stuff you own" and "1 Harm does cosmetic damage to NPCs" intersect, I think "look through crosshairs" is a good example of a principle that's great for AW and irrelevant or bad for challenge-based play.

      At challenge time, the stuff you want to attack is the PCs' resources, but you don't want to just destroy them. Killing a useful NPC because "you own it" is playing wrong and is bad for the game. Killing a non-useful NPC for whatever reason is irrelevant and is a waste of your GM efforts.

      "Look through a lens of utility" might be a good principle here. You can do whatever you want with the mayor... unless he owes the PCs a favor. If he owes them a favor, you don't take that away; you set up a threat to take it away, and you challenge the PCs to stop the threat. (This is philosophically similar to Burning Wheel's "let it ride", which is often used to mean "once the players earn something, it stays earned until separate outcomes dictate otherwise").

      The difference here is subtle: AW advises you to threaten stuff the PCs value too, but it isn't concerned with the challenge-relevant details of when/how/why.

      \n The random call / die of fate issue is actually pretty interesting in a wider context, it comes up in many different games. (I'm not that hot to trod specifically about whether AW is or isn't a gamist game - seems like a pretty academic question for anybody not specifically interested in trying to play it that way.) Some games incorporate it in their systems, while for others it's anathema, not unlike e.g. random character generation, which is a similar technical issue that runs through unrelated games. The usual split is between whether the game assumes a GM with dramatic coordination duties, or whether the game assumes a neutral referee; in the former case a game may opt to say that "there is no luck", and therefore a legitimate die of fate does not exist - if one is rolled, that's a player abrogating their responsibility to make creative choices. On the other hand, a game that doesn't do dramatic coordination, such as old school D&D, relies heavily on arbitrary dicing to randomize events that are out of the hands of the player characters, and therefore an impromptu randomization is the only fair way of refereeing a sequence.

      My reading of AW doesn't indicate that it'd be a game for random calls - it's more like TSoY, wherein the Story Guide just frames situations and outcomes to maximize character spotlight and potential for dramatic choices, and the conflict resolution system is utilized abstractly to cover the gaps. Thus the AW MC would presumably rule that e.g. a Hail Mary throw would succeed or not on the basis of his own arbitrary aesthetics, or he would deem it a move so as to incorporate it into the conflict resolution system. (Presumably the choice would depend on how exactly the event had been framed - did the player offer this fictional event as a move of his own, or was it just an open invitation for the MC to move the events along?) Not an expert here, though, so maybe there is some minor room for the other sort of technique in there that I haven't noticed.

      \n I think there's been some disconnect in this thread about fictional vs mechanical risk/reward choices. To summarize:
      AWnotG: "In AW your cleverness doesn't impact your success!"
      AWyesG: "Yes it does! Your fictional choices impact the fictional possibility space which determines what you stand to gain or lose!"
      AWnotG: "But your tactics have no bearing on your likelihood of success!"
      AWyesG: "Sure they do! Sometimes the fiction will dictate that you don't even get or need a roll!"

      I don't think the "not Gamist" description is sufficiently clear, and I don't think the "yes Gamist" response actually addresses it.

      I don't think anyone could contend that in AW the fiction is irrelevant and therefore player attempts to skillfully work with it for strategic advantage are also irrelevant. Yeah? No need to defend AW from that; that isn't the accusation that's being leveled.

      Instead, it's a question of how relevant and in what ways are what types of skill?

      There's a common situation in old-school D&D play where the players are looking at two options, and one would yield more loot or advantage than the other, but it's also harder than the other, and so players are faced with a choice like "climb the difficulty-3 hill for a better view, or climb the difficulty-15 mountain for ambush position?" In AW, analogous fictional uncertainty is handled very differently by the rules. The choice might instead be "read a situation with not much the MC can obviously do to you if you fail, or read a more informative situation wherein failure might mean the MC could say you fall down a mountain".

      These are different types of choices. Due to the leeway the MC is afforded in AW, the AW choice offers nothing so concrete in terms of probability as "3 vs 17". The choice in AW is more vague and approximate in terms of how the relative difficulties of the two options will manifest, at least until the group comes up with their own solution involving secondary rolls or countdown clocks or something. In D&D play, on the other hand, the landscape is dominated by numbers that can be compared to each other, assuring players of a certain relationship between their choices.

      Separately, I also think most readings/playings of AW render "you don't need to roll, that would simply succeed/fail" much rarer than in the more numbers-lite old school gaming I do. So rather than "3 or 17" vs "extremely specific outcome distributions A or B", it's more like "whatever makes sense in the fiction, to be determined specifically, full stop" vs "whatever makes sense in the fiction within a very wide range of possibilities, to be navigated by the move".

      I realize AW allows you to add +1 and -1 for "this is hard" or "that action is perfect", but that seems rare from the AP I've read. It rewards a kind of probability-based thinking that most groups don't bring to AW in the first place because the game doesn't otherwise incline them to use it.

      Anyone who wants to say that none of this matters is simply failing to acknowledge the importance of certain features to many Gamist players.

If people are hung up on setting specific difficulties and don't like how Read a Charged Situation handles it, it seems like that probably has to do more with their mechanical preferences and comfort zones than it does with any weakness of the AW engine in handling challenge-based play.

      \n You know, one way I've been thinking about this whole discussion is to consider game design in terms of what decisions it hands to the players, and at what points during the process.

      In good game design, the decisions are easy and intuitive, and don't involve mental gymnastics (like trying to appear impartial when you actually have a stake in one side over another, or to value two different options equally when you know one is better than the other, etc). In bad game design, the decisions are difficult or awkward: for instance, if I make you decide between rolling X number of dice or drawing cards from a deck, and there's no clear way to choose which is more suitable, but something important in the fiction depends on the decision.

      Of course, this conversation is made quite difficult when we haven't defined our terms exactly: what is "the AW engine", what is "challenge-based play", and where do they intersect? What kinds of things do we want to challenge, and how?

      One thing I keep running into is the AW MC's directive to "make moves" whenever it's her turn to talk. This is a bit of a problem for a player who is a) thinking primarily from a fiction-first perspective, and b) wants to minimize his exposure to risk. These two things don't go together well, and likely contradict each other. Maybe fictionally the smart thing to do is to sit in the middle of the corridor and wait. But now the MC has no clear way of handling the outcome: does she follow the flow of the game (and make a move), or does she reward the smart choice by having nothing (bad) happen?

      Perhaps the kriegspiel comparison is also apt here: is the referee's job to always adjudicate in favour of the most likely result? (For instance, to consider the most likely outcome and skew towards that, aiming to get as close as possible to "realistic"? e.g An army which outnumbers their enemies and has a better position should always win.) Or is the referee's job to re-create the chaos and unpredictability of warfare, to challenge the players to react to unexpected results? (Sometimes even the cleverest plan goes South because someone screwed something up, or the weather changed all of a sudden, or someone got lucky: what are you going to do now?)

      There's a lot of room here for different approaches. I'm mostly curious to hear about *yours* (dear reader), how it works and why it works.

      I think you think that the things you do when you run OD&D aren't "gm moves." But they totally are. They're just from a somewhat different list. Hell, it isn't actually very different. Let's see.

      The thief says she'll wait in the corridor while the goblins fight each other inside the room. Sounds smart! What does the GM say?
      1. "Okay. If you wait for a whole turn, I'll roll another wandering monster check. Still want to do it? Okay." (tell them the possible consequences and ask + use a location move)

      2. "If you stand in the corridor, you aren't concealed. Want to look for a better spot?" (tell them the possible consequences and ask + give an opportunity that fits a class ability)

      3. "You wait. You can hear them arguing inside, then loud crashing, then everything goes quiet. Seems all clear in there." (offer an opportunity without a cost)

      (those are GM moves from Dungeon World, also using the make a move that follows from the fiction principle, among others)

      In other words, the "GM moves" style of play is a type of description of "the things a GM does," not a separate, incompatible procedure from standard GM play.       \n It's interesting... I bet if you took successful Vampire GMs and unsuccessful Vampire GMs, a lot of them would have gotten similar Agendas from all the "GM should pursue these goals" text (e.g. "create dark atmosphere") and have a similar awareness of the Moves available to them from the NPC and faction lists (e.g. "6th-gen prince demands something of you"), with the biggest difference being in the Principles that connect the two. Coercive use of NPCs creates a dark atmosphere in a stifling way that can squash player agency. Using NPCs to offer tough choices or opportunities, on the other hand, allows the players to bring the darkness themselves by going down the shady paths you tease. Basically, the good GM has a sense of which Moves to use when, guided by Principles born of experience or intuition, while the bad GM grabs at Moves that seem fun in the moment but violate Principles that are vital to fun play.

      This probably isn't news -- my first thought when I skimmed the AW MC stuff was "great for bad GMs; kinda irrelevant for good ones" -- but it seemed germane at this point. Seeing as how I do not currently feel up to running a great Vampire game, I probably ought to go see if I can turn my good experiences with the game into a set of Principles. Once I did that, I dunno if I'd then need to look at them, but I think creating the list would be quite instructive.

      Pardon the tangent. Returning to challenge-based play, I had a thought, that what I'd really like on top of the basic AW framework is some sort of method for managing fictional data. Not stats and numbers, but truths and relationships. The kind of stuff that would support people in playing the way John does (establish all relevant fictional particulars first, then roll only if needed) when they otherwise might not (e.g. roll first, sweat the details in the outcome narration). I don't think a +2 to a roll for cleverness is specifically required, but I'd like some sort of reliable way to account for fiction-based problem-tackling. Basically, a way to make the conversation with the GM about "definitely works / let's roll / couldn't work" less a vague matter of opinion and more a matter of interacting with established facts and probabilities.

      Brainstorm:

      Perhaps there could be some sort of Apply Knowledge procedure the players can invoke?

      When you try something you haven't exactly done before, but think would work, tell the MC all of the following reasons that apply:
      - Events from earlier in play that establish that this is how things work
      - Your real-life knowledge that this is how things work

      MC, for each reason, go down this list in order:
      - Is there evidence present indicating why this wouldn't work? If so, convey it to the players and see if they change their choice.
      - Is there a specific reason why this doesn't work, which the players haven't yet discovered? If so, narrate how it doesn't work and call for an Analyze an Experiment roll; success means the players learn why it didn't work.
      - Is there a specific reason why this is dependent on luck or other factors, which the players haven't yet discovered? If so, call for a roll of whichever Move applies. Success (in addition to the attempt succeeding) reveals the previously unknown factor; failure (in addition to the attempt failing) invokes the Analyze roll above.
      - Is there a specific reason why this works and includes side-effects, either good or bad, beyond the players' intent? If so, be sure to narrate those.
      - If none of the above, narrate the attempt succeeding exactly as the players hoped.

      Rolling for multiple reasons:
      - All above rolls of the same Move become one roll. The roll result simply determines how many successes are achieved. 10+ = 100%, 7-9 = 50%. So if the players invoke 4 reason why an action should work, and all 4 are doomed for hidden reasons, one Analyze roll is made, with a 7-9 revealing 2 of these hidden reasons.

      So, um, that may be practically unfeasible, but maybe it demonstrates the sort of feedback I have in mind, and someone else will have a smoother idea...

      \n Lotsa stuff to think about here. I've kind of given up on nailing down the definitions of any broader sense of fiction-first challenge-based play, so I'll just respond based on my personal preferences. I hope piecemeal isn't annoying:\n
      It seems to me that you're concerned that in absence of detailed mechanics, the GM's heavy level of judgment might endanger tactical play, right? Like, in the right hands, the AW system can be open and fair but with the wrong MC it's essentially fiat. Is that your concern?
      Sort of. I think it's more specific than "open and fair" vs "wrong fiat". You don't need to be a bad MC in general to be bad at connecting the dots between challenge and reward. I'd rather make those connections either (a) as easy as possible for the MC to make, or (b) present in rules so the MC needn't worry about them. Judd's example MC is a great AW MC, just not an appropriate challenge-rewarder.\n
      Does it matter if the GM knows beforehand how this works? Would they have had to have made up something about the plasma rifle casings before you tried this
      It matters to me. I certainly prefer it, so as to avoid competing impulses, and also to avoid the fear of competing impulses. (Any player who's ever had a GM tell them "no, that doesn't work" because the GM was trying to keep the plot on the rails, can get understandably paranoid.) However, I don't think "knows the exact answer to this specific situation" is a hard requirement. I think that's the best option, but when it's unfeasible (in the case of "oh geez, I'd never thought about this thing that's just now coming up!"), I think there are others that suffice.\n
      or would it have to have come up in play before for it to be meaningful?
      I think the players need to have knowledge that applies but not necessarily knowledge that is conclusive. In other words, although it would be coolest if I'm putting two known puzzle pieces together (taser overloads hardware + plasma cells explode) in a way that becomes obvious once I've thought of it (overloaded cell explodes!), it's also pretty cool if I can try stuff that I'm less certain of.

      For example, let's say it hasn't come up before in play that my taser can overload hardware or that plasma rifles are volatile, but it has come up in play before that lightning can overload hardware and that tank plasma cannons are volatile. So, in that case, what I'm doing by tasering the rifle is not just applying known facts, but also applying known logic and patterns.

      Hopefully, in playing my character in the game world, I've developed a sense of how things work, and this is my metric for guessing that a taser works like lightning and a plasma rifle works like a plasma cannon.       Here they are again, in case I've buried them with too much verbiage:

      When you try something you haven't exactly done before, but think would work, tell the MC all of the following Reasons that apply:
      - Events from earlier in play that establish that this is how things work
      - Your real-life knowledge that this is how things work

      MC, for each Reason, go down this list in order:
      - Is there evidence present indicating why this wouldn't work? If so, convey it to the players and see if they change their choice.
      - Is there a specific reason why this doesn't work, which the players haven't yet discovered? If so, narrate how it doesn't work and call for an Analyze an Experiment roll; success means the players learn why it didn't work.
      - Is there a specific reason why this is dependent on luck or other factors, which the players haven't yet discovered? If so, call for a roll of whichever Move applies. Success (in addition to the attempt succeeding) reveals the previously unknown factor; failure (in addition to the attempt failing) invokes the Analyze roll above.
      - Is there a specific reason why this works and includes side-effects, either good or bad, beyond the players' intent? If so, be sure to narrate those.
      - If none of the above, narrate the attempt succeeding exactly as the players hoped.

      Rolling for multiple Reasons:
      - All above rolls of the same Move become one roll. The roll result simply determines how many successes are achieved. 10+ = 100%, 7-9 = 50%. So if the players invoke 4 reason why an action should work, and all 4 are doomed for hidden reasons, one Analyze roll is made, with a 7-9 revealing 2 of these hidden reasons.

      Analyze an experiment: only roll for this when the situation involves limited resources such as time, experimental subjects, hit points to lose in failed attempts, etc. Otherwise, you will succeed, but ask the MC what's required first.
