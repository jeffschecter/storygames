\n As someone who has never published a game before, but would like to in the near future, one issue that's been on my mind lately is layout.

Specifically, that I know very little about it. A lot of people I hear about use InDesign, but it costs like $700(!), which is more than I'd like to pay, to be honest. Are there viable alternatives (I suspect there are, but I really have no idea)? Is InDesign worth the price? Should I just try and get someone more knowledgeable to do layout for me?     \n If you're putting your first game together and you're not really aiming for a mass-market commercial product (i.e. you're competing with other niche indie games rather than with Pathfinder, Warhammer, Mutants & Masterminds, etc.), you can really do the layout yourself, even if you're not particularly great at it. You can even use (GASP) Microsoft Word and export to PDF. The layout for Apocalypse World could pretty easily have been done in Word, for example.

    That said, it is worth running your layout past people who know about layout, since they can easily point out a dozen or so things you can do to improve the readability and usefulness of your design. And it's also useful to look at other documents that you like the look and feel of and try to steal ideas from them, or from some of the classic books about layout.

    A few games down the road, it might be useful hiring or collaborating with somebody who can do serious layout. But you don't need to worry about that yet.

    \n P.S. Adobe offers a service now where you can "rent" access to all of their creative products on a month-by-month basis. You can even pay for a single month, do all the work you need to do, and then never pay them anything more. It's a bit pricey, but if you just need the software for a little while, long enough to bang out your layout, it might be worth it. That said, those programs take a while to master, so you may not be able to really get your money's worth in a month unless you have an experienced designer showing you the ropes and/or you're working from an existing template.

    \n The best way to layout a book is often a case of preference. I would suggest taking a look at some of your favorite RPG and even non-RPG books to see how other people solve certain problems and put things together, but you should also not be afraid to experiment. If funding is a concern, then you should definitely try to explore this option of doing the layout yourself especially considering it is not too hard to learn the proper skills required to do it. You might even find that you tremendously enjoy doing it!

    If you are looking for a really cheap alternative to some of the more pricey software, you can't go wrong with LibreOffice (the successor to the no longer active Open Office) which is entirely free and functions almost identically to Microsoft's Office suite.

    \n Are you a programmer? Or otherwise highly computer literate?

    Then LaTeX could be an option. It is free, and the defaults are reasonable for printing.

    It has a small and rather steep learning "bump". Then everything is easy for a while, until you want a very high level of control over the looks or fancy layout at which time you will pull your hair. :-)

    I have done all my games in LaTeX and the feedback on layout and style are usually along the lines of "very easy to read, but a bit boring". I think it is a reasonable trade-off.

    If you want to take a look at what my stuff looks like I did the layout for Andreas S\xf6lvebrings game I R\xf6tt & Svart solvebring.se/i-rott-och-svart/ as an example for a beginner level tutorial/workshop I ran on LaTeX last week. Both the game and the page are in Swedish, but get the PDF from the second link on that page.

    --

    Otherwise, Word/LibreOffice aren't all that bad, you can get them to print to PDFs. And as long as you don't overdo things it will look reasonable.

    \n I'm very glad to hear about people supporting many do-it-yourself options that don't involve using/learning InDesign.

    True, InDesign is the King of layout apps (and Quark Xpress is the deposed dictator). But many of these conversations end up usually going with many published designers insisting that InDesign is *the* application to use, that using anything less is a disservice to the quality of your product, etc. It's kinda irritating to be in a design sphere where everyone rejects traditional models of game design, publishing, distribution, but often fall back into one-true-wayisms on layout issues.

    Sure, it may not be the most gorgeous game, but does it need to be? Really?

    Really?

    If I were going to self-publish, I'd likely use the tools at my disposal to do layout*. If the game is successful enough that later on I want to go back and revise it and make it pretty with my ill-gotten gains, I'd maybe consider that. But not on initial release.

    -Andy

    * Tho in true to hypocritical form, my wife is learning InDesign for work/hobbies and so I have a backdoor into a cheap professional layout, admittedly one that requires longer time, and me doing more chores around the house! :-)
