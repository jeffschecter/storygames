\n It just occurred to me that there's some fairly significant differences here. You've got systems like Dragonlance 5th Age, HeroQuest, d20, SWD6 and such where the target number can be moved. You can easily make something harder or easier by changing the difficulty number, which is perfectly suited to the HeroQuest idea of making things easier or harder based on prior success or failure. Then you have systems like BRP, AD&D (in some areas), GURPS, ORE, where the odds are really rather fixed. In BRP you roll under your %, AD&D you have a fair amount of roll under, ORE you have a set or you don't. There are different ways of modifying this, but success ends up being much more character dependent. With a moving target number, you can make the odds fairly irrelevant. With the odds based on the character more solidly, somehow the stats end up meaning a bit more (although they allow for less GM flexibility).

I'm pretty much writing this as I think about it, and that's about as far as I got.

    \n So I had some more time to think. What stands out to me is if the odds are character based, the player can roll dice, know they succeeded and declare the result. If they have to just call out a number and compare to a target number, they're waiting for the GM to tell them if they succeeded. It takes some degree of control out of the players' hands if the odds change.

    \n One comment:

    The secret GM factor is a common feature in those types of mechanics, but isn't necessary to "opposition-based odds". There are many ways to strip that out.

    Consider this one:

    When you need to determine success or failure, you roll some number of dice. You need at least one "6" to succeed.

    When it's time to roll, the GM decides how many dice you roll, and hands that many to you.

    However: is there a way to do "character-based odds" while keeping the result secret from the player? The only one I can think of is to have the GM roll.

    \n Well what you have is if the odds are set by the GM every time a challenge comes up, it takes the power out of the player's hands. The GM could set the difficulty just high enough that success isn't possible, or always sitting nicely at 50%. D&D 4e ends up doing this by design, so your odds of success don't really change as you go up as the difficulty scales with your improvements.

    If you're rolling against your own stat, it won't scale, and as it improves, your odds go up. It is of course possible to add modifiers, but you're going to have some issues with social contract if they keep getting thrown around. It's not about who controls the math, it's that with a dice pool system or a roll under system, the GM doesn't have control, at least not in such an explicit fashion. There's also a difference in feel when you're rolling and say "I got a 17, did that hit?" instead of "5! I hit, 4 damage!" If we dip into the thread about traditional gaming, the notion of PC sovereignty comes up with that. The player decides on the action, rolls and determines the result.
