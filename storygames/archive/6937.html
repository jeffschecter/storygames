\n You know what's awesome? Cost-based resolution. Resolution systems where your success rate is determined more by what you're willing to pay for it than by your stats or what-have-you. I'm not talking about the bid & risk dynamic that you find in, say, D&D combat (if you think about it, the combats boil down to bids and risks of HP, riding on the dice)--I'm talking about where, through the agency of the mechanics, the cost of success is determined and based on that you, the player, choose to succeed or to fail.

My aschan The Rustbelt does this, allowing the players to Push on failed die rolls to get a guaranteed success, for a Price determined by the difference between the target number and the roll and negotiated between GM and player. The Price is usually some sort of harm, or the introduction of some sort of inconvenience or danger. It's really good for brutal, desperate action sequences in the manner of Mad Max or The Wild Bunch.

I thought that I invented this. HA!

It turns out that at more-or-less the same time, Robert Bohl put a cost-based mechanic in his game Misspent Youth. You can override a loss by selling out. And the way that selling out manifests in the mechanics is the shiz-nite.

Then I found out about Vincent Baker's The Otherkind. Yep, there's another one. Even though there's a possibility that success is totally impossible, you can usually take success at the expense of one or more of the other concerns.

All of these are frickin' awesome. Does anyone know of any other cost-based resolution systems out there?

    \n Mortal Coil does this very explicitly, without the use of any kind of dice.

    1. You allocate your resources toward the success of some kind of action or actions (it's really a task-based resolution system disguised as Conflict Resolution, wherein you win the overall Conflict by (eventually) succeeding at a 'tipping-point' majority of the tasks within that conflict).

    1a. Going "all-in" on a conflict exhausts your pool of resources a little.
    1b. You also have the option, after the "reveal", to reallocate your resources to give yourself a better chance of success at a task where you realize you're outgunned. This "desperate reaction" also depletes your resource pool somewhat, leaving your weakened for later Conflicts.


    Nobilis, which for me is a kind of proto-indie game (it has a whole section on Social Contracts that I read two years before I found the Forge) uses a very similar mechanic within both the hard-cover and smaller softcover "larp" rules for determining who beats who with their respective miracles.

    \n Cool! This is some neat stuff.

    I want to clarify, though, that systems where paying a price merely increases your [i]chances[/i] doesn't count (i.e., it's not what I'm talking about, even though it's technically "cost-based"). I'm talking about systems where paying a price means that you Succeed, Period. Such that you must choose: Do I get what I want and suffer the consequences? Or do I sacrifice what I want to avoid the consequences?

    The really cool thing about this sort of thing is that every resolution has the potential for addressing Premise.

    I also think my Game Chef entry from this year applies, though in a different way. When two players are opposed to one another, one player sets two possible outcomes of the conflict. The other player applies one of those outcomes to his character and the other outcome to the opposing player's character. Since at least one outcome involves succeeding at what your goal is (that's another rule), you can always get what you want, if you're willing to pay the cost. But if the player setting the stakes really strongly wishes to oppose you, he could set both outcomes as really horrible, and accept the consequences to his character, too.

    \nASCII @HACK does this, a bit, in that one wins an Objection by out-spending all opposition to your assertion. More than color, such assertions can actually provide one-off effects (i.e. "success" or avoid failure/risk).     Basically... a total riff off Universalis. With some DitV and some "trad" pay-for-effect points mongering.

    Posted By: Marshall BurnsSo, how do Pace and NObilis work? I haven't had a chance to check them out.
    \nNobilis works like this: You have an attribute rated typically between 0 and 5. This gives you a default value for doing stuff. You also have a pool of points for each attribute. These points raise your effective attribute rating (on a one for one basis, spend as many as you like at a time) for one action. So if I had an Aspect of 3 (Aspect covers whatever a real human being can do, there are three other stats for other things) I could perform level 3 and below Aspect miracles for free, or I could spend one point to do a level 4 Aspect miracle, or two for a level 5 Aspect miracle, etc.

    Uncertainty in conflict comes from not knowing your opponent's stat value, how many MPs they have and, most importantly, how many MPs they're spending in a given round. Asyou can see, if someone really wants something, all they have to do is blow all their points on achieving that something. Of course, since the points are a limited resource, doing so limits their ability elsewhere.

    There's a lot more to Nobilis (like the simplified Ars Magica style miracle creation system, which tells you what level a given miracle is) but that's the core of the system. I seem to recall Pace was vaguely similar. I also know that Greg Stolze's ...in Spaaace! is a simple, free system built along similar principles.
