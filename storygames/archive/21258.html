I'm looking for the favorite traditional RPGs of people who are mostly into indie games. So if you're mostly into traditional games but want to post some games, please let me know that your mostly into traditional games in your post. Thanks :-)

What traditional role playing games are worth checking out for someone who typically isn't into traditional role playing games?

Traditional is the wrong word for what I'm looking for. I'm looking for old or new mainstream games that are different than typical mainstream games. When I say different I mean different in design. Games that blend or have different roles for the GM and players than the typical bifurcated ones are of particular interest; however, I'm not sure that these even exist in games more mainstream than PbtA games and Fate. Also, games that have interesting design choices that can inform and inspire my own design. For example, I may not play Fate but I found the design very original and interesting. The design ideas I'm looking for would mostly be to inform my designs for GMless Collaborative Story games; however, I am designing one game in which characters' fictional abilities help them to do things in the fictional world (for example, using stats to overcome complications or challenges). This game will have some aspects of the Simulationist ethos as part of the design. However, on a whole, I'm most interested in things that could contribute to the design of GMless, Narrativist games, as that is my primary interest. I expect the amount of mainstream games that will be helpful in this regard to be very few, nevertheless they may be useful. Perhaps, Amber would be a game to look at or perhaps the design would be archaic and unuseful at this point? I'm not sure, but Amber is something different and I'm looking for mainstream games that are different. That have good and novel designs.

I'm also, interested in certain well developed, intellectually interesting, and mature approaches to genera, themes, setting, etc. Games whose overall literary qualities are well developed that have novel settings that, most importantly, align with my tastes. Paranoia and Cyberpunk 2020 look promising in this regard. I'm skeptical that Glorantha will align with my tastes although I know it is beloved by people I respect.

Thank you for all your wonderful contributions and suggestions so far. I very much appreciate it! Sorry if I wasn't as clear about what I was after as I could have been. Thanks

    \nUnknown Armies - probably the best BRP system there is out there, and this is also how you should use a setting - as stuff of inspiration for scenario writing.

    Feng Shui - involves the players more into narration though mechanics. A good example of GM advice turned into rules.

    Esoterrorists - only for the binary knowledge skills. Another example from Robin D Laws' way of turning GM advice into rules.

    RuneQuest 4ED - really nice design solutions in this BRP system.


    Swedish Games

    Mutant - Swedish take on Gamma World. I'm not talking about Year Zero now.

    Oktoberlandet - probably the best written game in the world. Written in a way so the reader interprets the text, and therefor each paragraph is different in each person's eyes. And it's gorgeous.

    Western - I'm not kidding, that it's the best produced and researched Western game that is. Horribly dry system though.

    Operation: Fallen Reich (in English) - Gorgeous game, and you need to check out the character system which is a board game.

    Kult - A classic game that was banned in Sweden, but "death is only the beginning". Life is a lie in this gnostic horror game ... but the game system isn't well suited for this game.


    French Games (in English)

    Agone - this poetic fantasy games really made my creativity explode.

    \nI am mostly into traditional games so don't know if my recommendations will land with you or not. I'll try to suggest things I think people here might enjoy. But I think if you haven't played it, you might want to check out Hong Kong Action Theatre! . It is a very interesting game where you play an actor in Hong Kong cinema, and the movies you star in are basically the adventures. So there is a whole 'star power' aspect to it that you need to focus on in order to land good roles. That concept was a little out there for most of my players, but the system is quite good and it has a lot of great information on the genre. Arguably this is less traditional than some RPGs because of the concept but it came out in the mid-90s so it still feels pretty traditional to me.

    I would also recommend Savage Worlds (which I saw others suggest). The system is nice and simple and does a good job of handling pulpy genres. It is also good because of the large amount of support material. So you can pretty much find a Savage Worlds game for anything.

    The final recommendation I would make is the Doctor Who RPG by cubicle 7. This is a solid game. I can't say enough good things about it. If you like Doctor Who, it captures the feel of it very well and has some interesting mechanics toward that end (particularly the initiative system).

    GMless collaborative indie story games:

    Games in which a player\u2019s ability to affect what happens in the game is not dependent on their character\u2019s fictional ability to do those things.

    \n This request is an interesting challenge, Jeff! I am trying to come up with something I think you'd like, but every time I do, I wouldn't really consider it a trad game.

    If you're looking for a game which is mostly "GM and character-players" but with a little tweak to that formulation, honestly I'd recommend Sorcerer. The players author the majority of the starting situation elements before play, which I bet you'd love, and then proceed to just play their characters thereafter.

    Another option would be Wraith: the Oblivion. Part of a classic trad line of games (White Wolf's World of Darkness), Wraith's conceit of multiple levels of reality requires a lot of creative world-creation (or at least world-interpretation), and I've heard of groups handing some of that off to the players. I don't own the book, so I don't know whether that's by the rules or against them, though.

    Then there's Fate, which has a trad setup plus some important meta mechanics.

    Feng Shui, Toon, and Paranoia are all trad-era but weird and different. After Brendan's description of Hong Kong Action Theater, I might put that at the top of this particular list.

    I think you are familiar with Puppetland, but I'll remind you of it anyway. It's got character-players and a GM, but is otherwise as untraditional as it gets.

    Re: Ars Magica, I get the impression that there's something special about it, but I'm not sure whether it's your kind of special or not. For those recommending it here, I'd be very curious to hear the thoughts behind the recs.

    \n The Doctor Who RPG and 7th Sea Second Edition feel like trad-scale games which have learnt from the more indie side of things. I love them both dearly.

    Doctor Who's initiative system is simple, broad and encourages the actions you'd do in Dr Who by stacking them as going first. The drama points are gained through things like getting kidnapped. There's a scaling yes, but/yes/yes, and kind of system of success.

    7th Sea Second Edition creates big pools of dice, grouping them together into sets totalling '10' or more and each one gives benefits or removes your choice of problem from any challenges. It's odd, but great.

    After falling away from D&D hard in 4th Edition, the 5th Edition's been really good. It's still crunchy, still has things like HP and initiative, but it's simplified and again takes some characterful parts from other games.

    I've only played the new Paranoia, but it's good fun. The wipe-clean character sheets are a good touch. The system's something often forgotten about as it's not really much compared to the setting.

    I've grown away from Call of Cthulhu in recent years as games like Cthulhu Dark, Lovecraftesque and Trail of Cthulhu have refined the idea of a Cthulhu game in more thematic ways.

    I'd say the only real issue with a lot of trad games is that the interface between mechanics and themes/desired behaviours can often be quite different. If a game wants to be a kitchen sink without a lot of hacking, it may seem flavourless.

    \n For Paranoia, get the "Paranoia XP" (third edition) core book. No supplements necessary (or arguably desirable, depending on what direction you'd take with it). 1st and 2nd edition cores are also legit, relevant and interesting, but XP is probably the best entry point. Supplement quality for the game is very much all over the place; most of the (useful) supplements are adventures, and if you're going to get those, then I definitely recommend 3rd edition stuff - the old things just aren't very good, generally speaking, while there's actually a lot of surprisingly legit adventure material for 3rd edition.

    For Traveler I recommend the core rules (the current commercial set - Mongoose? - is fine), and nothing else to start with. It's a peculiarly old-schoolish game, very much significant, but you'll need to study the core rules and form your own opinion to know if any of supplementary material is relevant for you.

    I believe that Amber is in the top 20 of relevant trad games you could check out.

    (Regarding your original question for the thread, I've been trying to compose some recommendations, but just like David I'm not at all sure what to say. The best I can do is probably to encourage reading widely and with an open mind, and getting used to the disappointments, because of Sturgeon's law. Borrow books rather than buying them, think in terms of study rather than collecting. Read up on all the historically popular games - they've been popular for some reason, each one. The history of rpgs already is a massive treasure chest of hidden gems, but what specifically is relevant to each of us at a fixed point in time perennially changes. Prepare to come back to a title a few years later, as your perspective will change.)

    \n I mean, the trad games that I have been most excited over this last decade have generally been either not actually traditional (just old, would be rather more precise), or alternatively they have had actual literary merit (of the sort I, as a reader, understand and appreciate). So if I were recommending games for me, I would offer something like say Glorantha or Pendragon or JAGS Wonderland or Delta Green or Cyberpunk 2020 - stuff with ambitious settings and adventures that try to engage with their literary context instead of just regurgitating it.

    But, that specific thing (literary merit) is totally something that is completely irrelevant to many gamers. Not because they're somehow uncultured or whatever, you understand, but because they're not in a place in their hobby to need or appreciate that sort of thing from their games. Paul there, for example, doesn't really care, as I understand it - he brings the jazz himself, the game contributes strictly the rules. I can appreciate that approach, but I'm totally game for the exact opposite approach as well: something like JAGS has pretty much nothing it can contribute to my gaming mechanically, but the Wonderland setting is amazingly good, so no reason why I can't utilize it with whatever game system I want (read: something proge, focused).

    So that's why I find it challenging to recommend anything. As I currently see it (feel free to ask again in a decade), all the trad games that I find the most interesting are that pretty much due to their literary content - settings and such. Rather hit and miss whether you'd care, and just like with Paul, my impression is that you sort of don't, right now. So in that sense it wouldn't exactly be wrong to say that maybe there aren't any trad games that you really "need" to check out?

    Of course you'd know that best yourself, so hey, what the heck: I recommend JAGS Wonderland to you. It's free and it's really clever, better in many ways than Atrocity Archives or Dresden Files (popular literary properties, those, not games), and it's very gameable with anything vaguely Call of Cthulhu -like. The game will not have anything to offer you in terms of hard mechanical design. So if you're uncertain about this fluff thing, perhaps check that out and see if you find a systemless rpg book to be relevant to your hobby?

    \n Everyone,

    I was asked to comment on the type of things I'm looking for in mainstream role playing games.

    Traditional is the wrong word. I'm looking for old or new mainstream games that are different than typical mainstream games. When I say different I mean different in design. Games that blend or have different roles for the GM and players than the typical bifurcated ones are of particular interest; however, I'm not sure that these even exist in games more mainstream than PbtA games and Fate . Also, games that have interesting design choices that can inform and inspire my own design. For example, I may not play Fate but I found the design very original and interesting. The design ideas I'm looking for would mostly be to inform my designs for GMless Collaborative Story games; however, I am designing one game in which characters' fictional abilities help them to do things in the fictional world (for example, using stats to overcome complications or challenges). This game will have some aspects of the Simulationist ethos as part of the design. However, on a whole, I'm most interested in things that could contribute to the design of GMless, Narrativist games, as that is my primary interest. I expect the amount of mainstream games that will be helpful in this regard to be very few, nevertheless they may be useful. Perhaps, Amber would be a game to look at or perhaps the design would be archaic and unuseful at this point? I'm not sure, but Amber is something different and I'm looking for mainstream games that are different. That have good and novel designs.

    I'm also, interested in certain well developed, intellectually interesting, and mature approaches to genera, themes, setting, etc. Games whose overall literary qualities are well developed that have novel settings that, most importantly, align with my tastes. Paranoia and Cyberpunk 2020 look promising in this regard. I'm skeptical that Glorantha will align with my tastes although I know it is beloved by people I respect.

    Anyway, some of you had mentioned that you would like a more in-depth picture of what I'm looking for, I hope the description above is useful in this regard.

    Thank you for all your wonderful contributions and suggestions. I very much appreciate it!

      \n Another thought:

      I think a lot of good stories emerge from RPG play that's rich in settings pregnant with meaningful situations; many trad games excel at providing such settings. @Bedrockbrendan 's Crime Network is a great example -- it provides a thorough survey of classic mafia content, chopped up and organized into game-usable chunks, from the characters' webs of opportunities and expectations to all the types of NPCs they might encounter. Earthdawn is another game that does a lot to inspire play through its world-describing, with a little more emphasis on the broader world scale. Comparing these two games, I suspect that crime family drama may be a more natural fit for the average player's narrativist urges than survival and quests in an Earth wrecked by giant monsters. But maybe not; gamers are weird.

      The list of trad games with great settings is probably quite long; if this is the sort of thing you're interested in, I can try to come up with a longer list.

      Apologies if all of this setting blather is old news to you. It just strikes me as a dimension in which trad games tend to excel relative to most of the indie games I know, so it might fit within the aims of your current hunt.

      If this is not old news to you, then you might already own some stuff which will give you a good taste of what I'm talking about. On the indie front, I believe The Clay That Woke was going for something similar. Shadow of Yesterday also has some very cool setting material -- world facts aren't presented in quite as much breadth and detail as in many trad games, but they're tied into the character options quite nicely.
