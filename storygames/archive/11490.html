    \n Just finished Sasha, by Joel Shepherd. Decent military fantasy in what I'd call the Honor Harrington model. Politics were very well done, though not as well as Dune or the George R.R. Martin books.

    Others...(I'm an ADHD reader in my habits):
    Olympos by Dan Simmons
    Green by Jay Lake
    Lamentation by Ken Scholes
    Rereading Thirteen, by Richard K. Morgan. Fucking amazing novel. Holy shit.
    Talk Dirty to Me: An Intimate Philosophy of Sex, by Sallie Tisdale, a musing, often poetic take on sexuality, sexual appetite, and the way we operate as sexual individuals in a sex-conflicted culture.
    Medicus, by Ruth Downie. A novel about a Roman doctor stationed at an outpost in Britain during the reign of Tiberius. It's OK, but not nearly as grabby or historically engaging as the Roman Sub Rosa series by Steven Saylor. I love good historical novels. The mention of Shogun makes me want to reread Tai-Pan again, which I still think is Clavell's best work. Plus it's one of the rare novels where the last line of the last page carries an emotional kick that ties up the entire story perfectly. That's a tour-de-force in terms of novel structure.
    Aaaand lots of comics, since that's where the bulk of my writing is going these days. Nova is my go-to these days, but I'm sampling different titles here and there for the layout and scene-framing perspective.

    I'm not currently gaming, but I suspect the comics and the writing thereof may be a huge influence once I start again. Something about working elbow-deep in the page-panel economy really focuses the mind on scene framing. Plus I find myself yearning to run a game of Darkpages.

    \n We had a boarder at our house last year, a girl in her late teens, a self proclaimed "TWI-hard"...

    She loved the twilight series and said that anything else vampire realted was either a rip off (if it came later), or trash that Twilight had improved upon (if it came earlier)...[apparently even the venerable legends, Dracula and Buffy were improved upon??!!]

    ...so naturally, we started watching "True Blood" on the only TV in the house...just to annoy the crap out of her.

    It turned out to be better than expected, and this led to reading the series of books the TV show is based upon. With a broken leg and lots of time off work, my wife got through them at a rate of about 2 books a week, with full time work and lots of other projects on my plate, I'm getting through them at about a book every two months.

    The books have had an influence on my new campaign...I've been doing it for a while, but now I'm consciously presenting supernatural beings in a more "realistic manner"...trying to delve more deeply into the social issues that might be faced by supernatural beings in a modern day setting rather than just playing them as monsters or creatures hiding in the shadows from humanity. I'm also using the TV series (because it's more accessible to new players), to give players a quick grounding in these concepts.

    Needless to say, our former boarder doesn't role-play with us anymore (especially since she moved out), because she thinks this is all a pale imitation of the sparkly pedophile immortals found in her favoured series.

    \nDangerous Laughter, a collection of stories by the great, Pulitzer Prize-winning, now criminally neglected American fiction writer Stephen Millhauser.

    His story "The Wizard of West Orange" is about a nineteenth-century inventor who creates a machine (the "haptograph") that can create simulated tactile experiences, both familiar (e.g. the sensation of holding a ball) and unfamiliar (e.g. the sensation of being held in a giant's hand). It's a wonderful reflection on the joys and perils of simulated experience, and should be read by every serious gamer.

    \n Fiction-wise, I'm reading Greg Egan's latest short story compilation, Crystal Nights

    Like all Greg Egan short story compilations (Axiomatic, Luminous, etc), it's basically like reading a tome of "shock: Social Science Fiction" actual plays written up in story form. Egan's the hardest, most interesting true SF writer of our generation, but I think his real magic lies in his short stories, not so much his longer novels.

    I've only read about half the book thus far, but I loved the story "TAP", about a chip you can implant which lets you build a new language and vocabulary you share with other people, which lets you communicate entire full sensations with others (not only is there a word for [[dog]], there's a 'word' for [[on the beach at sunset with the cool breeze and smell of seasalt, your feet covered in sand, as your beloved golden retriever greets you by licking your face]]); a murder; a conspiracy; and the effects of a language where people can understand what others are saying in a kind of sensorium virtual environment, as well as experience those very sensations as if they were their own.

    Aside from that, a manga about fight between a good high school Chinese calligraphy club, and an evil high school Chinese calligraphy club. It's as awesome as it sounds.
