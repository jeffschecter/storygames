\n I like animals. I want more animals to show up in my games! But animals tend to fall a little flat. They are furniture - sometimes scary like an Ottoman, but ... flat. Like this week's zombie game - my guy is in the Seattle zoo, and shazam! There's a lion on the loose, licking his chops! So I run from the lion. Lion almost catches me but not quite. Lion gets a tranquilizer dart in the rump and becomes zombie food. On to the next thing.

How do I invest animals with menace, intrigue, personality, and hidden meaning? Where's the fun in giant snakes, an army of furious dolphins, or a mangy, sad-eyed puppy?

    \n My animals too often act like humans, so I think my players don't like it when I throw them in.

    The "awaken" druid spell in D&D doesn't do them any favors. They're tired of kittens or lizards that come up and talk to them.

    Well, zombies are all metaphor, right? Corruption, rapacious consumption.

    Aren't snakes just metaphors too?

    Animals in games should be metaphors for parts of the human condition and the struggles we all face; they're our fears of nature given form.

    They're not just snakes, they're an embodiment of a primal fear. They're everyday life constricting around us. They're the fear of death swallowing us whole, slowly. They're death from a mere puncture that wouldn't normally do us in, striking to inject venom then backing off to let us suffer and die. They're the children of the Tempter of Eden, cursed to forever crawl on their bellies.

    They're not just snakes, dude.

    \n Hey Judd,

    Animals work best when you emphasize what they -can- do, even if they fail to do it to the PCs.

    Real world example- three guys on a boat on the river. One guy turns to the other to ask something, and the both of them hear a splash and turn around.

    They see a tiger on the riverbank, with their buddy dead in it's jaws. The splash wasn't their buddy getting pulled from the boat, it was the tiger getting out of the river after it got it's dinner.

    \n The most effective way I've found to use dangerous animals to drop them in on an already tenuous situation. I once played a Sorcerer game where the PC was a bandit slave who had just been liberated. He ends up kidnapping the lord of the land's daughter and then sets out to take revenge on his former enslaver. It comes to the point where we have this ex-slave, the lord's daughter and the bandit king out in the deep deep woods together when all of a sudden a pack of wolves attack. The ex-slave PC is the only character who's armed.

    In your case I would watch for situations where (a) real humans are in conflict either PC vs. NPC or PC vs. PC or even NPC vs. NPC with the players near by and (b) zombies are already complicating that situation when... (c) a giant boa drops from the ceiling!

    \n Jason,

    When someone shoots a snake in the face, pause for at least five seconds. Act like they misheard something you said.

    Then, really slowly, say, "You're going to shoot the snake?" Give the player a chance to either change their mind or boldly blast its head off.

    Don't have it writhe or anything. Just have it die. A little gory, nothing dramatic. Just a cold, sudden death-by-face-shooting.

    And then, an hour down the road (IRL time), go for an "oh snap" moment:
    1.) Crazy Woman Jenny agrees to help you out. In fact, she's got the necessary trinket in her cave home right now. Her pet snake is guarding it. Her pet snake that she loves dearly and would definitely, definitely avenge if it ever got hurt.
    2.) The scientist figured out the cure! All it requires is fresh mint leaves (easily obtained) and purple cobra venom. What's that, purple cobra is a near-extinct species and only one has ever been recorded in the area? And you just KILLED IT?
    3.) Everyone has nightmares of snakes sliding down their throats and getting stuck that evening.
    4.) The exact same snake, with the exact same nick in it's face, is in the next room. And it isn't dead.

    Right? If the players don't want to humanize the animals, then don't sweat it. Just humanize the world around them (suggestions 1-3). Or, play off the fact that they are bizarrely not-human (suggestion 4).

    \n The best encounter with an animal I ever ran (and I agree that they often fall flat) was with a mountain lion. The party was climbing through a rocky pass, and their archer was scouting ahead. He sees a mountain lion looking over the ridge at him. He glances back to check where the rest of the party is, and when he looks back, it's gone. He knows it's stalking him. He stands completely still, and just listens, and thinks. We played a whole conflict between him and the lion without him seeing it at all, until the last minute when it sprung from hiding, right behind him now, and he, ready for it, spun and put an arrow in its neck before it could reach him.

    The player said he'd been genuinely creeped out by the ecounter. I think it was the way I played up the animal's ability as eerily powerful, but not supernatural. There was a "man against nature" vibe as well as the awe that it's just that good.

    \n Thinking of the other thread, the snake can try a convince roll against you to make you fear. If it passes, you can choose to have your character with that fear, or suffer some small penalty if you go against that.

    Further, when it goes to convince them of fear - go into detail. Think of how a thug would try and convince you to be afraid - the threats he'd use. Then turn it reptile, cold and its origins that are far older than man.
