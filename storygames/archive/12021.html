Posted By: Thor OThe answer to keeping rolls in the game while also just giving out essential bits of information is a little something we started playing with about 10 years ago called conflict resolution.

If you've set up your game so that the players must find a clue and they are searching the room for it, it's there. They're going to find it. So ask yourself: Where's the conflict in this situation? Why are we spending time focusing on this moment? Are there guards wandering the grounds or a deadly monster lurking? Is there a vicious trap?

When a player fails a search roll, or any roll really, the answer doesn't have to be a brick wall ("no sorry, you don't find anything"). You can throw in a complication instead. The characters find the ledger just as the crime bosses' goons burst through the door, pulls the mouldering tome from under a pile of junk as the giant spiders descend from the ceiling silently on silken threads, or notice the killer trap just as they're about to pull the ancient scroll from its case.

    \n John,

    Conflict resolution as a term gets used in many different ways, so it is helpful to remember that at the core of things you actually have a conflict, a clash of important goals in the fiction, and its resolution, the social or mechanical process by which we all discover how the conflict turns out.

    This means that what makes it a conflict rather than just any situation is the awareness of the goals in tension. Take a look at 4E D&Ds combat subsystem - this is another example of conflict resolution in terms of your goal of defeating your enemies in the fight, because at the top level of combat you have everything you need to translate the task actions of attacking and moving into that ultimate outcome. But D&D lacks (although the skill challenge system was an attempt to patch this to some extent) a general purpose conflict resolution mechanic. Meaning that if a conflict does arise outside of the regime of its conflict resolution the group is faced with the prospect of letting it be handled through purely social means - often GM fiat*.

    Now more designed forms of conflict resolution often involve explicitly negotiating the goals of the conflict in question. This can be incredibly helpful, because the negotiation airs assumptions about what the group members want and where things are headed. Then the goals are set, and the conflict is resolved, quickly (analogous to the simple skill roll) or through involved back and forth (more analogous to a combat encounter), depending on the system. Often a complex process informs and fleshes out the pre-determined goals, adding new twists or consequences to the situation. Finally the selected goal becomes the outcome of the conflict.

    This negotiation is very important to general conflict resolution. Here you, the group come to a consensus that the outcomes in question will all be acceptable and interesting. Here, for example, is when you as a GM should recognize when that simple failure (even with additional problems) will be uninteresting, so it should be taken off the table as a goal. It is the source of much of its power, and also one of its critical weaknesses as a technique. After the negotiation a great deal of the group's flexibility about the conflict is lost - in a real way the heavy lifting of defining the conflict and experiencing it occurs here, in some sense the resolution can almost be an after thought, even a disappointment.

    - Mendel


    * Which in my reading makes GM fiat a form of conflict resolution, but others take a narrower view.

    Like a lot of these terms, they stem from a particular conversation, and the context is buried in forum posts now a decade old.

    \n

    The term "Task Resolution" is used to describe when only the task is being resolved. The term "Conflict Resolution" is agnostic on whether tasks are being described in the process. What you care about is, "Am I rolling because I'm taking a risk in some way?" If you're rolling whether or not you're risking something, that's Task Resolution. (Note that "roll" is shorthand here for "in some way make an impactful decision". If you're rolling to see if you pick a lock, and the result is that you'll either open the lock or have to roll again, you're not resolving anything, other than that it took a long time to pick the lock. If you're picking a lock in a hurry because the viscount is about to see you and call the guards, that's another matter. That's resolving a conflict.)

    \n

    A favorite old chestnut of an example:

    \n
    \n

    I'm the GM. You're a player.

    \n

    Me: OK, you've snuck up to the gate. There are two orcs guarding it and drinking a beer.

    \n

    You: OK, I throw a rock into the moat to distract them. (Roll, succeed)

    \n

    Me: OK, they look down there.

    \n

    You: I sneak past the orcs.

    \n

    Me: Oh, they're just looking over the side of the drawbridge. You can't sneak by without them seeing you.

    \n

    You: (maybe a little miffed)Crap. Um. I'll kill them!

    \n

    Me: They come at you all like, "GRRAAH!

    \n

    (We roll, you win the fight)

    \n

    You: OK, I go through the gate.

    \n

    Me: Through the gate? There's a portcullis! You can't just walk through!

    \n

    etc.

    \n
    \n

    Please note: in this example, I'm not just being a jerk. We just haven't agreed, as players, what it will take to get you to the other side of the gate. I'm genuinely surprised! I thought you were just worried about them sending the alarm!

    \n

    Equally plausible is this:

    \n
    \n

    ...You: I throw a rock in the water to distract them. (roll & succeed)

    \n

    Me: You did it! You walk in and see the kidnapped prince walking through the courtyard! You take him back to the king and you're rewarded richly!

    \n

    You: Oh, um, OK... that wasn't very satisfying.

    \n
    \n

    In this case, I wanted to not be a jerk so bad, I made stupid shit happen. This knife cuts both ways.

    \n

    Now, imagine a set of rules that says that getting through the gate is a difficulty 10 challenge. That means I get 10 dice of stuff to throw at you. Some of it is orcs, some of it is drawbridge, some of it is portcullis. And let's say that, whenever I roll a 6 on my dice, I get one die back, so you don't know exactly how many dice I'll have going into the challenge; you only know approximately. Or maybe every 6 I get is instead long-term consequences for you \u2014\xa0wounds, imprisonment, theft or destruction of your weapons and armor (it costs me more to take away more valuable stuff), or whatever; that way, you don't know what it will cost you to get what you want.

    \n

    So you let me know \u2014\xa0maybe just by planning with the other players in character, out-loud, or maybe by stating it directly, that you're trying to get into the castle. You and I know what the risks and rewards are, so we can play a) fair, and b) hard. I can give you fit opposition without being a jerk and you can come up with clever solutions and bold moves, knowing that they're going toward an effect.

    \nThis is why I much prefer Ron's original formulation of Conflict Resolution being about resolving incompatible actions between fictional entities in motion. It removes the need for negotiation because the people responsible for "speaking" for a given fictional entity have full authority over it. No one can say what my character does and intends to accomplish but me. And no one can say what your character does and intends but you. So we just say what they say and do and when there's a conflict (i.e. we are attempting to interfere with each others attempts to do) then we roll. Therefore, you only need to know what the characters are *doing* and the dice resolve the conflict between those actions. Thus conflict can work at any scale from, "I hit you with a crowbar" vs "I sweep kick you to the ground" to "My air strike blows up your castle" vs. "My ground forces take the river."

    ... and that's why Stakes are the most technique to ameliorate that. See Dogs in the Vineyard and Prime Time Adventures for the most basic, "here's the thing at stake" kind of stake-setting.

    \n

    But there are other ways to do it. The Anthology Engine (In a Wicked Age and Beowulf) gives one player a stick with which to negotiate. The non-stick-haver can always opt to take a hit (which is pretty serious, mechanically) instead of taking what the stick-haver wants. The stick-haver usually doesn't care, of course, what the non-stick-haver's stats are, so will usually adjust to make the negotiation more amenable to the non-stick-haver. But at any point, the non-stick-haver can basically say, "No, I'll take the black eye. I'm not giving you what you want." This is a coercive method, where everyone doesn't need to agree beforehand.

    \n

    Note the way Shock:s stakes work, by the way. I, as a Protagonist, say what I want. You, as the Antagonist, offer something that I might want more (or might not want more). That makes me divide my resources, and since I never know exactly what my resources are (since they're dice), it makes it a really hard choice. And sometimes neither, sometimes either, and sometimes both of us get what we want. This is very similar to what Ron's talking about there, but I don't think he ever implemented it in such a way that orthogonal objectives can be addressed (someone correct me if I'm wrong). This method is focused around asking for something, then giving someone else the opportunity to twist it (which can happen at a couple of other points in resolution).

    \n

    Polaris has opposing players state stakes back and forth at each other until one of them says, "You ask far too much." Then you roll to determine the final outcome, including long-term effects on the character. Again, they're not really stated "beforehand" because they're the meat of the resolution system itself.

    \n Folks try to make conflict resolution all complicated, but it's not really.

    All rpgs (or at least the vast majority) have conflict resolution. There is a system (explicit or implicit, principled or ad hoc) for deciding if characters get what they want. That system can be as simple as "yes, they get what they want" or "The GM decides based on what seems cool", or as complex as you like.

    All games also have task resolution. That's where you decide how well a character does a thing that they've set out to do. There is a system (explicit or implicit, principled or ad hoc) for deciding if they do well at the task or not. That system can be as simple as "they do as well as the player says they do" or as complex as you like.

    Many games, maybe most that you're familiar with, have explicit, principled task resolution, with complex rules, and then implicit, principled conflict resolution, with simple rules. Usually this looks like "roll some dice to see how well your character succeeds at the task, then the GM will decide if that gets your character what they want, based on what's realistic and what seems cool". A lot of games that you're familiar with probably have more explicit conflict resolution for killing things, where the rule is "roll some dice to see how well the character hits the enemy, and then roll some dice to see if that kills them".

    A common thing is to have conflict resolution rely on task resolution, or vise versa "Roll to see if your character succeeds at what they were doing, and if they're successful, they get what they want" or "Roll to see if your character gets what they want, and if they do, they succeed at doing something (you can say what) to get it".

    Some games, maybe ones you're not as familiar with have experimented with explicit conflict resolution for all or most conflicts, alongside or instead of explicit task resolution. There's a lot of design space there.

    \n From my perspective, the only important things to remember when considering this concept are:\n
    • When a player picks up the dice, the GM should ask what he is intending to accomplish with his roll. Sometimes it's not obvious. You're trying to sneak up on the guard. Why? Because you want to knock him out? Why? So he can't raise the general alarm? Great!
      • \n
    • Once the GM understands the player's intent, the GM must ask himself: Is this a valid intent? Using your sneaking skill to silence a guard before he can raise the alarm is one thing. Using it to sneak past all the count's guards, break into his locked office and find the document you need is a stretch for a single test. That's more like three separate intents, each of which would require its own discrete skill (eg, Stealthy, Lockpicking and Read).
      • \n
    • It is essential that success means the player achieves what he intended. Success has to mean something. If the players successfully silence the guard, it is not cool to have some other guards stumble upon the unconscious guard and raise the alarm. If you do so, you are shitting all over the player's success. It's a bait and switch; you've given the appearance of success, but you are invalidating the intent behind the test.
      • \n
    • However, if the player fails the test, it allows your creativity as a GM to flower. You don't have to invalidate the task--the skill that was used in the intent. The only important thing is to foil the intent. This is when some other guards find the unconscious guard. The players's characters haven't been caught, but now they're wandering through the halls with fully alert guards actively looking for them. Failure means complications.
    \nPlaying this way does not take extensive prep. It's actually pretty easy to do on the fly. You just need to think about the player's intent and twist it so the players now have an additional problem to worry about. Try it a few times and you'll find it gets easier every time you do. And it also makes GMing more fun because you'll be surprised at he twists and turns games start to take.

    You can use this technique in any game you play. The games mentioned above all have interesting takes on and variations of this technique. My personal favorite for showing how to use it is Mouse Guard.

    \n The Mouse Guard answer is simple: What hazards had you planned for at the beginning of the session?

    I recently ran a game of Mouse Guard. The previous session had ended with a cliff-hanger in the Player's Turn. A failed Circles test had led to the Moss Lord of Elmoss sending some of his soldiers to kick down the door of the house in which the patrol was staying and drag them off in the middle of the night. The next session would start in the Moss Lord's dungeons (I titled it In the Dungeon of the Moss Lord). The patrol's mission was to escape imprisonment and return to Lockhaven with their intelligence.

    In Mouse Guard, you plan for a session by creating two Hazards out of a possible four: Mice, Animals, Weather and Wilderness. For this session, I chose Mice and Wilderness.

    I decided the Mice portion would be a Complex Hazard. Here's how it broke down:\n
    1. Interrogation by the Moss Lord. Essentially they'd have to withstand torture.
      2. \n
    2. Breaking out of their cell.
      3. \n
    3. Dealing with a flame trap on their way out of the dungeon.
    \nI had guards, the arrival of Weasel slavers and a raging fire in reserve as potential Twists. Injury Conditions were also on the table.
    .
    The second hazard was a difficult journey back to Lockhaven on roads washed out by early spring rains. Flash floods and a hunting kestrel were the Twists I had on deck.

    \n Resolving the question of what the conflicting motivations and objectives are in a situation is a type of resolution that doesn't get nearly as much design exploration. I mostly blame this on the fact that it doesn't have a catchy phrase like "conflict resolution" or "task resolution".

    On another note, this scaling issue with conflict resolution is part of the subtlety with relating it with story conflicts. In practice, any of the conflicts that define the overall story of the game shouldn't be resolved by a single address by conflict resolution mechanics. This will be extremely uninteresting - after all if the story is about the conflict between the PCs and the corrupt government officers who hurt their families, then actually resolving that conflict immediately kills the further potential of that story. There may be further stories, but this one has been resolved. Instead, there is a mediation between the story conflict and the conflicts which are described in terms of conflict resolution, often socially and informally worked, although some games will use varying levels of mechanical or guideline ties to ensure this relationship.

    In any case, a conflict resolution focused game will tend to tile the story with these micro-conflicts, in much the same way that a task resolution focused game tiles the story with task attempts. It shouldn't be surprising, as these are the building blocks used in the game. But there is a tendency to assume that without ever present conflict resolution the top level story conflict won't be appropiately handled. But all you really need is a way to resolve the story conflicts at the top level and transform situations forward (whether it be task, conflict, or some other variation).

    Posted By: Simon CFolks try to make conflict resolution all complicated, but it's not really.

    All rpgs (or at least the vast majority) have conflict resolution. There is a system (explicit or implicit, principled or ad hoc) for deciding if characters get what they want. That system can be as simple as "yes, they get what they want" or "The GM decides based on what seems cool", or as complex as you like.

    All games also have task resolution. That's where you decide how well a character does a thing that they've set out to do. There is a system (explicit or implicit, principled or ad hoc) for deciding if they do well at the task or not. That system can be as simple as "they do as well as the player says they do" or as complex as you like.

    Many games, maybe most that you're familiar with, have explicit, principled task resolution, with complex rules, and then implicit, principled conflict resolution, with simple rules. Usually this looks like "roll some dice to see how well your character succeeds at the task, then the GM will decide if that gets your character what they want, based on what's realistic and what seems cool". A lot of games that you're familiar with probably have more explicit conflict resolution for killing things, where the rule is "roll some dice to see how well the character hits the enemy, and then roll some dice to see if that kills them".

    A common thing is to have conflict resolution rely on task resolution, or vise versa "Roll to see if your character succeeds at what they were doing, and if they're successful, they get what they want" or "Roll to see if your character gets what they want, and if they do, they succeed at doing something (you can say what) to get it".

    Some games, maybe ones you're not as familiar with have experimented with explicit conflict resolution for all or most conflicts, alongside or instead of explicit task resolution. There's a lot of design space there.

    Also, because I only now noticed this thread and the issue of scaling conflicts is interesting to me; I'm thinking of Chris's question about sneaking past the guard. There are different ways of finding out the exact stakes of a conflict in different games:\n
    • Sorcerer model is that the GM understands the characters in conflict as literary actors with dramatic intent. When they roll off in a conflict, that roll determines the relationship of domination for these actors in this situation. Therefore there is no need for explicit stakes-setting prior to the roll, and we can say that if the player character wins the roll against the guard, then he gets his way as regards the guard, but not as regards the queen later on: the queen is a separate dramatic actor, she has a right to defend herself from blackmail. Particularly, the issue of whether the conflict resolution only concerns sneaking past the guard or also covers finding the right documents is answered when we notice that the guard is the only dramatic actor in the situation; winning him already resolves the immediate scene.
    • Dust Devils and other narration-sharing games involve a statement of intent on the parts of the player characters (it's called "stakes", but note that it involves what the player characters want, not what the player wants to have his character accomplish) and a very Sorcerer-like conflict procedure to find out which character is immediately dominant in the situation. However, the game does not use Sorcerer-like dramatic methodology in arbitrating the consequences of conflicts; rather, one specific player is chosen to arbitrate the consequences of the conflict, which means that he gets to pace the conflict: if that player decides that it'll be interesting to run another conflict about safe-breaking, or decides to skip that and go directly into blackmailing the queen, then that's his prerogative.
    • A game like The Mountain Witch paces conflicts in a rather interesting way in that the game mechanics determine how "big" your resolution gets to be. Specifically, the rules determine whether you get to wound your opponent or kill them, and they determine whether the character gets as much as he's immediately seeking, or perhance more. Therefore the conflict is paced by the mechanics, largely after dice have been rolled. (In A Wicked Age uses this same model, incidentally.)
    • Some games use outright dramatic arcs to determine when and how much is resolved. Polaris, Zombie Cinema, Under the Bed... there's a lot of these games. Usually they use some sort of smaller conflict resolution mechanic as well in addition, but some particularly important conflicts are shunted aside and made untouchable by the general mechanic in favour of making the rules tell you "not yet" - only once the game has reached an appropriate stage are you allowed to resolve the conflict in question.
    \nAll of the above are just different ways conflict resolution can be paced. Almost all discussion of "how to do conflict resolution" is very localized in that roleplaying really has a myriad ways of handling conflicts, including some games where they're not resolved at all, but rather postponed, subsumed or not allowed to rise at all. Thus it's potentially very confusing if you mistake explanations of how to do conflict resolution in a particular game with a generic definition of conflict resolution; the concept itself is simple, but the ways to use it to benefit game design are a multitude.

    \n John, here's an example of conflict resolution with mechanically pre-determined stakes:

    D&D's combat system has always been a conflict resolution system in all editions, and its stakes have always been rather predetermined. It's a conflict resolution system because it resolves contrary character intents, and it has pre-determined stakes because the system only really works when it's an issue of who takes the field, who lives, who dies - anybody who's tried using the system knows that protecting somebody, avoiding the enemy, avoiding combat, taking the opponent alive or other such secondary martial goals are really rather arbitrary and ill-defined in the system.

    Note that individual combat rounds are commonly defined as tasks in D&D; those are not conflicts. The over-all conflict is the combat in whole, and that's what is resolved by the system.

    Also, here's an example of task resolution without mechanical pre-determination; I'll grab this from the same game just for the kicks:

    In early versions of D&D, which don't have a skill system, tasks were specifically resolved without pre-determined system and most especially without pre-determined stakes. The Mentzer D&D rules suggest that the GM might want to have a player roll an ability check of their own devising (d20 under the ability score, whatever) when uncertain as to how a character fares in a general task situation. This is task resolution because it is not concerned with character intent but rather what characters do, but it does not rely on pre-determined definitions of skills or abilities or agreed-upon stakes and pacing; all those matters are resolved by the GM case-by-case.

    Simon's right about the player/character distinction, I considered mentioning that myself. I take it from the writing style of Design Patterns that it doesn't really concern itself with nuances like player vs. character distinction, but for practical conflict resolution theory this happens to be a very important thing: many styles of roleplaying game need for the players to not completely align their characters with their own interests, or require them to limit the exercise of their aesthetic judgment over the game in certain manners. All this fails if the player always insists that his character will, of course, do what he the player wants, and the game's content should, of course, proceed exactly in the way he wants. To the contrary, almost all roleplaying games live in the space where players are willing to accept external input into their imaginings, and nearly all of these resolve disagreements by coaching the players to cooperate towards a common goal rather than encouraging them to disagree.

    Insofar as the "right" definition of conflict resolution goes, of course people can't be prevented from using words any which way. I don't even mind if somebody wants to invent a new word for the concept. The important part is that the idea behind the term gets the consideration it deserves.

If he succeeds, fine; if he fails, he can try again, with a narrower intent (Just sneak past the guards and break into the office, say) but with an added complication of some sort, either provided by him, or the other players (GM included, of course). New Target value is set, dude rolls again, rinse and repeat until success.

    Players might quickly reach an equilibrium between getting stuff done quickly, and high-control tedium. Plus, the complications can take on lives of their own, as Thor implied.\n
