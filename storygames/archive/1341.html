"I want to learn from you."

So I've not really been on this forum that long, despite Dev's patient suggestions that I join months ago. I'm very glad I did get back in the scene though, otherwise I wouldn't be immersed in such incredible ideas. Thank you all very much for being so cool.

Anyhoo, the recent discussion of awesome-o-fying Planescape and Spelljammer, combined with a recent viewing of Avatar Season 1 and the discussion of "key chains" and "making each other awesome," have had their effect on my brainmeats. It occured to me that a "make each other awesome" game could use "cues" as the basic attribute. As in, "this is your cue to be awesome in this way." Best Friends' character creation is a cool example of this in that you're explicitly telling another player that they're enviably awesome in some way.

So, I'm designing a little RPG where the players are on a pilgrimage. (I'll spare you the setting details.)

While on pilgrimage, you are learning lessons about life from each step in the journey. However, you and your companions are also learning lessons from each other. During character creation, write down what it is you most want to learn from each of your companions.

"I want to learn courage in the face of adversity from Alex."
"I want to learn to be a better friend from Leslie."
"I want to learn to kick ass from Bill."
Some further comments on my LJ post suggested some more iterations of this basic concept:

Ties to the world and scene-framing:
I want to learn compassion from the Plight of the Swamp Elves.

Giving cues to other characters' relationships to each other:
I want Sachiko to learn Humility from Dawn.

Combining the two:
I want Sachiko to learn Humility from Dawn while baking cookies.
The nice part of this is that it provides cues for awesome-ness and a system of character growth in one sentence. It can also reinforce setting by providing a clear list of lessons that can be learned on a pilgrimage. (John Wick wisely advised at GenCon that a setting is at it's core, "what's important to the people in that culture.")

For example, if the pilgrimage were meant to teach the characters the Noble Eightfold Path of Buddhism, you could limit the possible lessons to:

1. Right View - Realizing the Four Noble Truths
2. Right Intention - Commitment to mental and ethical growth in moderation
3. Right Speech - One speaks in a non hurtful, not exaggerated, truthful way
4. Right Action - Wholesome action, avoiding action that would hurt others
5. Right Livelihood - One's job does not harm in any way oneself or others; directly or indirectly
6. Right Effort - One makes an effort to improve
7. Right Mindfulness - Mental ability to see things for what they are with clear consciousness
8. Right Concentration - State where one reaches enlightenment and the ego has disappeared
And the statements would be stated thusly:

"I want to learn Right Mindfulness from Dev."
"I want Dev to learn Right Action from Shreyas."
"I want to learn Right Livelihood from the Missing King in the City of Sands."

    Seek out new life and new civilizations.
