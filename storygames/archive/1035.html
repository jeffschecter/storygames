Crypto-Hunt

I just got back from the library where I ran a sort of game for eight older teens from the local juvenile lock-up and their adult handlers. It was a very simple dice game. The topic was cryptozoology, so I gave a five minute speech, divided them into teams (they named themselves THE HOT BOYS and THE WILD GRRLS) and explained the rules. They would go around the world trying to discover weird beasts. They could stick together or split up, and to discover a critter, they needed to roll the number of team members in that location or less on 1d6. A roll of 6 meant the monster was debunked with no points scored. I had a funny map with exciting illustrations. Some observations:

1. Both teams decided that sticking together for a 66% success was better than reducing the odds but getting more die rolls. One team had a guy who went off by himself on a whim a few times, and whenever he did, his 3 team-mates in Amazonia or wherever invariably rolled a four.

2. Everybody got into it - including the handlers* - arguing about where to go next - "Mongolia!" "No, Puerto Rico!" - as though it really mattered. The discovery aspect was fun. They'd groan when their designated roller rolled a six.

3. Skeptics! None of them would entertain the notion that any of those beasts could exist. They even looked askance at the thylacine. It warmed my heart.

4. Their focus and intensity was palpable. Next time I'm totally doing a roleplaying thing.

It was super fun.

*One of them ended an argument by saying "that's where we go, or we're going home."
