\n Let's say there's this character. And you know that they want something. And you know what it is, but maybe not in great detail. Like a one-sentence blurb.

To begin with you don't know _anything_ else. You just have a vague idea of the genre and style of the game, like "1920s pulp space opera" or "prehistoric family drama, serious in tone".

What do you need to frame a good scene for this character? What helps most?

I'm looking particularly for brief, easy things, bits of information. Things that immediately give you an idea, or have in the past. For example, such a "thing" might be:

* "a one-sentence description of an interesting location", or
* "a few genre tropes: like 'the scene of a grisly murder' or 'a beautiful woman knocks on your door with a case for you to investigate', if playing a detective noir film-type game, to choose from."

An example of an answer I'm not looking for would be,

* "It helps most to sit down with the player for two hours and have them fill out this AW30M3 questionnaire I made..."

But maybe that's what you do, and there's one question on your questionnaire that works really well, and, thinking back on it, is really the key... if so, tell me what it is!

What is a key element that, for you, would help frame a cool scene right away? What's the minimum?

    What is a key element that, for you, would help frame a cool scene right away?
    \nThe thing I always tend to look for is "what changes?" This implies making an assumption about what was the same before.

    So, example:

    Character is: a scoundrel who wants to clear his name of past crimes.

    Genre is: Arabian Nights style fantasy.

    Scene that comes to mind is: A "chance" meeting with a mysterious advocate, who has an offer...

    Why did that come to mind? Because it changes something about the implied status quo in the character's blurb. Up until now, he's been suffering from a tarnished rep and an infamous name, until he meets someone who apparently wants to help him. Why? What's the catch? Now we have a scene.

    \n My thoughts, in addition to those above (repeating for sake of clarity which)

    - where (from general it's Paris to specific but open on general, it's a city bus)
    - who (from PC's, NPC's to "furniture" NPC's - you, your lost love and the man with her, the passers by)
    add
    - when (from 'this is 2 days later' to 'the night before the Germans enter Paris') - I'm a bit surprised "when" hasn't been mentioned, several game text are explicit about stating 'when'.
    - some detail for colour mood and possibly for use in scene (it's a clear night, a strong wind is blowing, the road is paralleled by a line of lonely trees).

    And either explicitly or implicitly "why are we bothering this this". What's at stake - something has to be able to change, some desire of Player for PC must be threatened or possibly achieved, some risk evoked etc.

    In V.Baker's Afraid proto-game the idea being "Players say what they intend and you move forward to where something interferes with them reaching that, they have the scene they are in effect requesting and you resolve it, or their request is met without being played and it's "so what then" or you "have something kick in door". Even if it's as simple as "we explore the tunnel on the left", "cool, after 20 minutes of passing through disused dusty passages you come to [scene]".

    In terms of picking the elements, I think for Characters this is:

    - continuity demands (a story involves reincorporating elements), particulary if the scene is a "mid to later in game/adventure/story" one.
    &
    - complication demands (if the Player created a lost love, okay I bring them into a scene but add someone now with them in an obviously intimate way as a complication) ~ this may be a new NPC, particularly if we are "early in game/adventure/story", there must be a certain amount of adversity from PC on PC struggle or from GM-sponsored NPC or "impersonal obstacle".

    In the Mythic game's terms are you "bringing in an established thread from an earlier scene or from character creation/backstory" or are you "introducing a complication to a thread" or are you "introducing a whole new thread".

    Posted By: Paul T.Can you unpack "consequential" interest a little bit more?
    What Judd is saying. The character's aren't in a vacuum; they're in a situation. i.e.\n
    • Ava has been sent to spy on Ben, but finds herself deeply attracted to Ben's younger brother, Don.
    • Ava knows that "spy on" could easily turn into "assassinate" and is afraid of getting too close to Don.
    • Ben and Don fight a lot, Ben thinking Don is unreliable and Don chafing against Ben's authority.
    • Ben needs to get Don to marry Chloe, a beautiful maiden, to cement political power, but can't order him to do so.
    • Don isn't interested in Chloe, and doesn't want to just knuckle under Ben's authority.
    • Don has heard rumors that someone is plotting against Ben, and feels the need to uncover them to earn D's respect.
    \nGood scenes can be framed between Ava and Ben, Ava and Don, or Ben and Don. But you'll have to work harder at Chloe with anybody, because she doesn't have power in the situation, and she doesn't have interests that need to be fought for.

    Now, add:\n
    • Chloe yearns for Ava, who was her former, secret lover.
    • Chloe's family is powerful but nearly bankrupt, and she can't wait long to marry into Ben's family - if this doesn't happen soon, she has another, miserable suitor that she must accept.
    \nand now the combinations with other characters get interesting.

    Contrast with:\n
    • Edgar is the court herald.
    • Edgar has excellent taste in music.
    \nEdgar doesn't hold any interesting power over the other characters, doesn't have a consequential connection to them. Edgar doesn't have an agenda. Nobody cares about Edgar - scenes between Edgar and anyone else are going to be a lot of hard work. Who wants to see Edgar talk to Ben about music when Don and Ben could be arguing while Chloe ambushes Ava with unsolicited kisses behind the curtain?

    \n I am re-thinking my answer.

    The minimum.

    I'd think you need:

    Color:

    1) The golden plains of charred earth beneath the Broken Mountains.

    2) A giant sentient generation-ship that resembles an insect.

    3) The filthy war camp where the cloned child soldiers stay in between wars.

    And you can see situation kind of creeping in.

    Situation:

    1) The elven armies are hunting the remnants of your broken horde.

    2) The ship demands blood and your ship is nearly out of suitable fuel-meals.

    3) The soldiers hear on the radio that the clones in the city have begun a revolution.

    A touch of Color and a touch of Situation go a loooong way.

    Color: Imagined details about any or all of System, Character, Setting, or Situation, added in such a way that does not change aspects of action or resolution in the imagined scene. One of the Components of Exploration.

    Situation: Dynamic interaction between specific characters and small-scale setting elements; Situations are divided into scenes. A component of Exploration, considered to be the "central node" linking Character and Setting, and which changes according to System. See also Kicker, Bang, and Challenge.

    From the Forge Provisional Glossary

    \n So I've got a character, a goal and a setting to work with already? I don't really need anything else. All you need to do from there is, as Graham says, to put an obstacle to the goal in the character's way. Or do you count stuff you invent on the fly as "things you need"?

    A good thing to have (though not strictly necessary) is a relationship. If the character has a relationship to someone else, then I can frame a scene where that relationship is likely to change in some way. If he has an enemy, I could frame a scene where he needs something his enemy has in order to reach his goal. If he has a friend, I could frame a scene where the friend and the goal are opposed somehow.

    But if this is the first scene (is it?), that's starting out with a bang, and it might be going way too fast. If it's the first scene, I'd just frame a scene of the character doing something defining for that character. If it's an action hero, she's action heroing, and if it's a psychologist, she's in session. Now reference the goal in some way, and you've got a good introductory scene (think "Priming scene" in With Great Power ...).

    \n Going back to the OP, I realize my answer was partially about "framing later scenes"..

    A big question is system-based: what do we know about the character at the moment play starts? Do they have "stuff on their sheet" that indicates relationships, other characters or simply general types (they have loads of stuff about how they fight and kill things with swords, hmm, guess we'll give them something to fight with a sword or something a sword can't beat).

    Depending on system, the expectations may be "you frame a whack of detail at them" to "you frame something very general and they take it further."

    Early on the "cool thing" I look for is to introduce some conflict/exposure that either "explains/introduces" the situation if it's not clear enough or "complicates it - you thought you knew you had trouble because the Germans arrive in Paris tomorrow, but that's not the half of it".

    Posted By: Paul T.Rob,

    Very good point on the "when", and all the other stuff is great, too. I'll be compiling this, eventually, into some format or other.

    Can you explain the three Mythic terms? Is there anything more to that advice, or pretty much just what you wrote in that last sentence?
    \nMythic emulates a GM's plotting with a few mechanisms to create scenes and events. Lists are maintained of all NPC's, keeping special onte of those considered important to PC's (family etc.), and Threads. A thread is a "storyline", a main thread being the core issue of an adventure, others being the identity of a mysterious benefactor etc., and again track is kept of PC threads such as "find my lost sister" etc..

    I think a key part of advice about threads is that even if playing with a GM and the GM is using this to spark themselves, adding new threads should be based on the players'/characters' interests. Be player-focused in the storyline.

    Overall it recommends a minimalist approach to setting up scenes and then fleshing them out in play. Leave it at "in a warehouse" and if players want explosives to have stored there check the random answer chart to see if there are explosives in the warehouse etc.
