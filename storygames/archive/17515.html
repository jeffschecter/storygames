    \n Hey Steve

    I wish I still had the actual play me, Will and Mike recorded a long time ago, but I've lost it. If I can find it, i'll upload it so you (and anyone else interested) can see the game in action.

    I definitely like the traps, that's a damn good idea that i'll probably implement.

    As for the rape, it isn't completely random. The manual doesn't do a very good job of expanding on this, but basically, the Sword's player's agenda is their own (as in, the player's own). The Sword's player can interject themselves into the other players Monologues, and even "get inside their head" by whispering directions to the player. The Sword could, for example, tell the Thief to go South, knowing a collision with the Sorceress is imminent. It could also do the same with the Sorceress in order to protect her from the rape endgame scenario. Therefore, it's really important that the players wager, through their dialog with it, whether the Sword has their best interests in mind. The game is really supposed to be built around the ambiguity of what the Sword represents within the other character's minds- how "in control" they are vs. how in control the Sword is.

    In reference to the lack of "lead up" to the rape, I guess there really isn't any. That's sort of the point though. It's a seemingly disconnected crime that happens during the course of another crime (an opportunity rape). The players are basically playing knowing it's going to happen, and rationalizing it as they go (and really, after the fact: that's why there's an epilogue afterwards).

    Does that sort of address your question?
