I've convinced my non-Indie playing group to play an Indie game FATE, and I'm not going to futz up the opportunity by slacking off. My idea for really rocking the game requires a little introduction:

The game is going to be in the style of Cowboy Bebop and Firefly. It will involve a group of smugglers that will have survived a large war amongst nations. The funky part is that this is going to be a fantasy game, with airships, steampunk, and a touch of magic. The neat part is that it will only be on Earth, and I'll use the continents as they are, but rework the cultures a little bit. The themes that I want to address will be Nationalism and Self-Exploration modeled on real Exploration. Thus:

I want to make a large, multiple-use, awesome-tagious prop. It should be about the size of a large board in a board game (think Runebound size, ~2.5'x3.5'), be parchment colored, and have a neat map on it. At certain points on each nation, there will be three or four boxes cut out. Behind it will be circular pieces with numbers on it, and they can be rotated to show (during the Character Creation Phases) the waning and waxing power of the nations in the world. Each nation will be so insular it might as well be their own worlds on distant planets. Also, it'd be neat to use it travelogue-style for the Indiana Jones style red lines. Finally, I could list invokable Aspects near or on the nations for use while in them.

Does this seem worth it to anyone else? Do you think it will be worth it after Character Creation? Any suggestions on how to build it well or modify it for peak awesometagiousness?

  • SeleneTan
    February 2006
    One possibility: buy a piece of posterboard. Print out sections of the map on separate pieces of paper, making sure there's room for overlap. Cut them out, and paste them onto the posterboard. That gets you the large map.

    For the windows... Use an exacto knife to cut the holes you want. One way to get the rotatable things is to just put them underneath and, er, not move the map too much. And try not to move them out when rotating them.
    A more secure way is to put holes in the circles and use paper fasteners to attach them to square backings. Attach the backing to the back of the map. You might need to experiment with hole size so that the circles turn but don't fall off.
    Another alternative is to switch from circles to rectangles, and have a slider. That's easier to make. Write the choices down in a column on a long piece of card or doubled paper. (You want it fairly sturdy.) Then attach another piece of paper or card to the map, at right angles to the slider direction, so that the top and bottom are open. Insert the slider, and you should be good to go. You may want to tape stuff (e.g. more card) on the ends of the slider so it doesn't slip through.

    For the color... You can buy almost any color of paper you want. If you want the posterboard to be colored, try brushing it with tea. This works for normal paper as well, but causes problems if you use an inkjet printer.

    For Indy Jones red lines: get some overhead transparencies. When it comes time for a journey, slap them down and draw on them. You can draw on them with whiteboard markers, and then you won't have to get them wet to erase the marks. The transparencies will be useful for listing Aspects as well.

    Recently in my user interfaces class, we learned how to make paper prototypes of interfaces to test them at early stages of development. Guess where I picked most of this stuff up. :) (The transparency+whiteboard marker trick is really useful when imitating displays!)

    Random idea: Consider setting meta-stakes for the group's activities at the session-level.

    "If you smuggle the ammunition into Fort Wintersong, the Protectorate will get a +2 bonus in the next Warfare contest. If you fail, you attract the attention of the War Council, who sees you as a dangerous free agent that must be dealt with."

    Stuff like that. So during play you have the normal scene-level stakes resolution, and then their actions have consequences at the session (and map-based) level, following stakes set by the group beforehand.
