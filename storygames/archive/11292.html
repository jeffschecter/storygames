    \n The resurrected god was a pretty common trope in the various popular Greco-Roman mystery cults (Mithras, etc), and it's been suggested that the resurrection of Jesus in the Gospels was either a deliberately-added plot twist to help convert Romans, or was inadvertently spliced into the story during retellings. (The third paragraph of the above-linked Wikipedia article talks about that in more depth.)

    Earlier than that, a lot of mythologies worldwide featured a god who dies every winter and is reborn in the spring; in some areas a man would be chosen as the annual incarnation of that god, treated like a king for a year, and then sacrificed. (James Fraser's The Golden Bough is basically all about this.)

    Posted By: GrahamIn Greek mythology, you have to go down to Dionysus before you get an actual death-and-rebirth. In modern stories, it hardly ever happens
    \nI think it tends to be part of the older Greek myths. I get the feeling that as time went on they (and the Romans) got more "sophisticated" by making the death more metaphorical, so you get the very common trope of traveling to Hades and back, a la Persephone, Orpheus, etc. It may be that classical writers like Ovid who passed the myths down to us also felt that the literal resurrection thing had gotten a bit clich\xe9d.

    In modern stories resurrection tends not to happen because it isn't realistic. I haven't watched Mad Men or The Wire, but as I understand it they don't have any kind of supernatural or religious elements to them. Outside the modern-realistic-fiction genre, you still find resurrection, as a lot of people above have pointed out.

    \n Is it surprising that you don't see it often in modern media, except certain forms? It is a common mythological trope. You see it in myths (Baldur, Jesus), legends (Arthur, Drake, Emperor Barbarossa), and modern media that harken to those forms (super hero comics and fantasy).

    It used to be much more common. In the Renaissance there was a whole genre of visionary infernal literature based on the concept of the hero dying, journeying through the infernal (and supernal) realms, and coming back to tell of it. La Divina Commedia and Visio Tnugdali are two of the best known.

    \n It's a common thing with plant deities, symbolic of the cycle of the seasons and that their death gives life to the people.

    John Sheridan in Babylon 5 mostly dies and is given a fixed-term reprieve.

    Some fictions you won't see this at all, because they're using death for its emotional impact so it needs to be a big deal.

    By the way, I don't think Odin counts. There's no suggestion that he actually died. He was the *god* of death, after all. He hung himself on a tree as an ordeal unitil he perceived the runes.

    Gilgamesh died and didn't come back. That was the point - he came to terms with his titanic fear of it. (And given the nasty nature of the afterlife that was pretty sensible.)

    In Indian mythology and religion, everyone all the time.
