    So, it's basically really simple:

    \n

    Make a directed graph. Make a node for each character, and connect anyone who knows or cares about each other (and because this is a digraph, remember that you can model situations where person A knows/cares about person B, but person B has never heard of person A). Label the edges with a description of what the relationship is.

    \n

    Since I've been looking at the Smallville RPG lately, I think you might also want to put institutions, and maybe important locations and artefacts there. Those are likely to only have arrows pointing in, not from, them, since it's rare for an item to think about a person. Though if you were mapping Lord of the Rings, the One Ring would very plausibly have feelings about Frodo, Sauron and Mordor!

    \n Write a name. Draw a circle around it.

    Write another name, circle it too.

    Draw arrows from each of these to the other, and write their relationships to each other on the arrows.

    Add more names and arrows, as needed.

    Hurrah!

    Posted By: Domoni don't think it really does qualify as a relationship map (in the sense of a useful rpg technique) if it doesn't have the PC at the centre of the mapped relationship. a webconnection of villains is not as useful.
    \nI disagree -- if your goal is to create a setting where there are multiple antagonists and other NPCs who keep coming back at the PCs, having a relationship map that puts them in the middle (and the PCs on the outside) can be VERY useful.

    If the PCs move against one villain, the map will help you figure out how the other ones will react to it. If you need to know which villain will do something next, you can figure it out. If you're looking for ways to do a callback to an earlier event, the map will guide you to the NPCs who will tie into it.

    Admittedly, this is using the relationship map as a GM's tool more than anything else...but that makes more sense to me, seeing as the players probably don't actually need a map to know how their characters feel about the other characters in the game (since that's a large part of what play usually entails), whereas a GM may have dozens of NPC attitudes, goals, and relationships to keep track of, and can really use a handy chart to keep it all straight and reveal potential things they might do next.

    The PCs definitely belong on there (good villains should care who the PCs are!), but they don't necessarily have to be the core of the map. Games where the PCs are actually the spoilers who disrupt the web of relationships between the NPCs are pretty damn awesome and generate all kinds of interesting things.

    edit: It's definitely worth checking out this thread about Vampire for some really amazingly useful thoughts JDCorley put together about using relationship maps.

    \nI'm glad. I like that too. Just to be clear though, it's not my map. I yanked it off a Forge thread.

    It sounds like you could definitely whip up a simple relationship map in your game to help everyone visualize the connections between the PCs and NPCs. Just write down the names of the PCs and NPCs and draw arrows between them as needed, with notes to describe the nature of the relationship. One nice feature of a relationship map is it will suggest where you can impact multiple PCs by focusing on an NPC that touches them. Also it will suggest places where you can link the various NPCs into a network that draws the PCs into the middle of conflicts.
