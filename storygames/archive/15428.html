\n Okay, first post ever.
\n
\nI used to play a lot of ADRPG Back In The Day, and the one thing that always used to cause problems was that conflict resolution always came down to "who's better at BSing the GM". Sure,a ower Ranked PC can try to pull a trick on a higher Ranked one or try to switch the conflict to another Attribute, but there's nothing there to guide the GM when deciding whether or not that works.
\n
\nProbably the canonical example is Corwin backing Benedict into the Black Road, but if Benedict is really First Rank in Warfare he shouldn't have fallen for so simple a trick - should he?
\n
\nI was thinking about some other story games and re-reading the Amber books, and it occurred to me that one thing that Wujcik kind of skipped over is that all of the conflicts in the Chronicles between major characters have an element of "who's willing to push this the farthest" to them. Most of the time nobody wants to outright kill anyone else, because, you know, family and all that. They just want to force someone to the point of conceding something. Of course, sometimes they really do want to kill or maim their brother...
\n
\nSo I swiped some ideas from other games and came up with this. I was hoping anyone familiar with the original ADRPG or the stories could provide some feedback.
\n
\nFirst, build a Jenga tower in the middle of the play area (you can probably tell where I stole this one from). In any PC vs. PC conflict, the higher Rank always wins unless the lower-ranked PC wants to try some kind of stratagem to change things up. Maybe it's a trick, maybe it's a switch to a different Attribute. Whatever. To do that, the lower-ranked PC draws a block.
\n
\nThe higher ranked PC can then spot the trick/switch the conflict back/regain the upper hand by also drawing a block. Continue back and forth until:
\n
\n1) The tower falls. The person who drew the last block loses, and loses badly. Eyes burned out and thrown in the dungeon kind of badly. At the complete mercy of the victor kind of badly. The winner gets to narrate how you go down.
\n2) You decline to draw a block. You have to concede the contest, but you get to determine the terms of your concession.
\n
\nEDIT: Addenda: the higher Ranked PC can decline to accept your terms and force you to draw a block, but if they do so, the conflict must continue until the tower falls. If no one cares to narrate chnages in strategy, just alternate drawing blocks until the thing goes down.
\n
\nI think this would add a stronger sense of suspense, remove much of the need for a GM to arbitrate every single detail of a conflict, and provide some structure to the game's resolution mechanics.
\n
\nI'm not sure when the tower should be rebuilt - for each conflict, or only after it has fallen. The latter would add a sense of rising action to the narrative, but I can see some odd artifacts from it.
\n
\nThoughts? Comments?

    \n Yeah, nice to meet you, Daniel.

    I had the exact same issue with Amber's resolution system, or more accurately lack of same. The Jenga tower idea is neat (and I am perhaps unreasonably amused that we're adding uncertainty without using dice), but I'm not sure that adding a minigame is necessarily the right answer here. I always really liked the way Nobilis handled this, by introducing resource allocation.

    If you're not familiar with it, Nobilis PCs (which are usually demigod/demiurge types heavily inspired by Amberites and Sandman's Endless) basically compare their stat ratings to the difficulty chart to see what they can perform without effort (so if your stat is 3, you can freely and flawlessly do any related task of difficulty 0-3, all day long if need be), and then also have a pool of associated points that they can spend on a not-quite-1-for-1 basis to do something more difficult than that baseline. Recovering those points is not trivial (though it's a little easier in the current edition), so there is still that element of "is this really worth tiring myself out over at the risk of getting bushwhacked later" and scoping out your opponents' abilities by how easily they can do this-or-that, and so on. There's some other stuff that draws on your point-pools as well (for stronger-than-usual defenses, piercing other people's defenses, etc) to add other tactical concerns, but that's the meat of it. I really like that method, because it allows for failure and uncertainty without making things hinge on luck or pure metagame, which I think is pretty essential to selling the feeling that these characters are a cut above mortal frailties. You only fail if you miscalculated (which includes picking the wrong fight, or at the wrong time), or choose to take the hit.

You could totally do something like the duel between Montoya and the Dread Pirate Roberts that way with information reveals, stunts, etc.

    Just my random thoughts which have been bubbling on a back burner for a while...

When you're setting your stakes, you can easily declare how much harm you're willing to risk in exchange for knowledge of your opponent. So the GM might say, "Jade, tell me how you learned that Bob is an Amberite while you trade close shaves."

    \n The problem I can see, from a design perspective, is what ties drawing the blocks to the fiction? I say, "I do a trick! You thought we were discussing this like brothers, but all the while I've been leading you to the edge of this cliff and now I'm going to try to throw you off!" Or whatever.

    Now we initiate draws. It looks like we're supposed to narrate back and forth as we draw, and you can even make that a hard-and-fast rule, but there's nothing in the mechanism of the game that ties us to that. It's like how in D&D it's all well and good and fun roleplaying and stuff if I narrate what I'm doing when I roll my d20, but, y'know, the game still works even if I don't.

    Maybe something like: If you narrate X, where X is the kind of stuff you (designer/GM you) want to see in the game, then the player doesn't have to draw at all, and it's the opponent's turn. That's clumsy and ill-defined, but it's a start. It gives people a reason to narrate, something to shoot for. There are much better ways to do it (i.e., Dogs in the Vineyard).

    Posted By: daniel_reamColin, I'm quite familiar with Puppetland, but I don't understand your reference. Could you elaborate?
    Sure. Ask everyone to say, out loud, something that (1) their character is not competent at and (2) that they want to come up. You can phrase it constructively: "How would you like your guy to grow and change?" Then, when the time comes to mine the GM for favorable tags, the GM or another player can occasionally say something like, "Yes, that's a very creative way to try to narrate your First-rank Face-stabbing into a cooking reality show, but didn't we agree that your guy is always burning the roast? Maybe the face-stabbing can happen after he has this crisis, and comes face-to-face with his flaws?" Puppetland actually gives you permission to steer the story toward your puppet's, and your friends' puppets', things they can't do, just as surely as it it uses the things they can do, because that's how you pass the talking stick. You go from flag to flag.

    Fudge, Everway, and a host of other games that preceded Puppetland didn't have its perspective on Drama as a mechanical paradigm, and that's not their fault. They had to come first. That doesn't mean that we can't incorporate its lessons into the earlier games when we play now.

    Fundamentally, the problem is character monogamy, character advocacy, and the idea that you have to protect your character to win. The whole hobby is built on a broken set of rules, where mere survival makes characters "better". That is boring and it abets the worst excesses of illusionism, and it feeds masturbatory power fantasies at the expense of a lot of other, and to my eye better, possibilities. I never really got to test it or properly develop it out though, those were just the ideas I had.

    \n I actually had a couple more thoughts about this in the shower. (No, I don't always think about Amber in the shower... sometimes it's Deadlands...)

    \n Just as an aside, I'm not sure everyone here has a full understanding of how Jenga works. Every time you pull a block, you then have to place it on top of the tower. Otherwise people could just pull from the top down, layer by layer, and the thing would never fall.

    \nTo some extent, I don't think there's any way around the "best Jenga player always wins" problem simply because there's equally no way around the "most persuasive/knowledgeable player always wins" problem. By saying "we're playing Amber" we're also saying "this is a game for people who can BS and be persuasive and sneaky and cunning". Unfortunately I have a lot of players who aren't very good at that sort of thing but would still like to play Amber. So the Jenga mechanic levels the playing field a bit, as nobody has arthritis or carpal tunnel syndrome or whatever. I like the idea of a designated puller, and I don't think it would hurt the immersion too much.

    \nMarshall, I see what you're saying. The tower isn't intended to replace the GM at all, just to take some of the "does this trick work" or "can the weaker opponent shift the terms of the conflict" decision-making out of the realm of complete GM fiat. In the case of The Guns of Avalon, Corwin arming his troops with firearms is a monumental advantage, and I would assume the GM would take that into account. You could handle this by the GM granting one side of the conflict a certain number of "free" block pulls, although I think then we're getting right back to GM fiat again.
