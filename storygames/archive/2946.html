    I'm going to give the long games fair consideration. BEcause, well, that's only fair.

    \n

    But as far as whether Game Chef games should be long or short... consider that every year, we have a brief argument about whether we should allow formatting or illustrations, since someone with excellent graphics design skills can sway judges to prefer their game, regardless of the actual design merits.

    \n

    But we never have that same argument about whether we should allow flavor text and background material. And yet we should, because obviously some people are going to be much better than others when writing background material in a short period of time. And some people, either because they are bad spellers or because English isn't their native language, are going to pick up some bias. These things are going to impact judging, even though they have nothing to do with design.

    \n

    I have some idea about how to fix these, but rather than propose them as changes to Game Chef, I'm thinking of having an auxillary challenge (no, not an "invitational") with special restrictions. Maybe I'll call it "Game Chef entrees". I'm waiting a bit before I spring my ideas on the public as a whole.

    \n
    \n

    I think good flavour text and background can make a game design better.

    \n
    \n

    I certainly disagree. I can't see how you could claim the flavor text part of a game product makes the game design better. Well-written flavor text gives the reader a standard to create a specific experience, using the rules. But it's not the game. It's decorative, like artwork. And as for background... it inspires the game design, true, but the fact that people have played DitV and Polaris using entirely different backgrounds means that the background is not part of the game design.

    \n

    But all of this is not only derailing the thread, it's missing my point. There's a difference between the final game product and the initial game design. When you first design a game, your flavor text and background should be minimal and sketchy; you fill that in later, when you know the rules work. And you may even hire someone else to write that part.

    \n

    The problem with Game Chef games being too long is that people are spending too much time writing game books instead of designing an actual game within that two weeks time. And it's not just flavor text and background; I read one game that went on for several pages describing how to decorate the room you play in for maximum impact, and several games that had a "what is role-playing?" section. It's a game design challenge judged by game designers; I think we all know how to role-play, without being told "it's like cowboys and indians".

    \n

    What I would like to do for this "Game Chef Entrees" challenge I'm suggesting is limit flavor text to one page, and require rules to be described as an outline, or as a procedure. Additional background material would be sketchy and would only occur in one- or two-sentence descriptions forspells, skills, classes, or equipment in a list. Create the seed of an RPG, not an entire first draft. I want to test some things, but I'm thinking of a five-page maximum. Imagine how much easier it would be to read and judge that.
