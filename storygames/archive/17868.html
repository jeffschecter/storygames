    \n I don't think the player moves operate well in conflict with each other.

    Take Dungeon World -- If player A is rolling hack&slash and player B is interfering, player B could roll a 10+ (which gives a -1 to player A's roll). Player A could then roll 10+ despite the -1 and, thus, get the opportunity to deal damage to player B. That means that player B suffers harm despite the fact that player B rolled successfully. Nothing else in the game works that way.

    Alternatively, it could be hack&slash vs. defy danger or hack&slash vs. hack&slash. But, in either case, what happens if both players roll 10+? If you strictly follow the language of the moves, they can't both succeed.

    The issue is that the results of the moves are meant to create a single outcome. Competing sets of outcomes gum up the system.

    I think the best way to do PvP is with a custom move, and further to treat each PvP as a high stakes unique situation. Hack&slash and "dealing damage" is for fighting goblins and stuff like that. If you are dueling your best friend and fellow PC on a live volcano, the move should be

    When you duel your best friend and fellow PC on a live volcano, roll Dex
    on a 10+ you get both of the following on a 7-9 choose 1 on a 6- your friend gets both:
    -- you are safe from lava and rockslides
    -- your friend's life is in your hands

    Or whatever. The point is that PvP should be a special situation -- the basic moves don't really apply. But the mechanics of "player vs. MC" should work the same in PvP as they do with the other moves. Anyway -- that's how my group has done it.

    This is AMBayard in 2013 -- after this post, some people disagreed with me . . . and the next post followed.

    When one PC succeeds in a Hack and Slash move and deals damage to another PC, it means that the victim PC is taking harm even if the victim PC did not fail a roll. And, that's weird. It's weird because the moves are designed with consequences built in. The moves assume that the adversary is an NPC and not another player. If you defy danger in response to GM danger, you defy it; if you blow your roll, you get hit. The GM never rolls. The moves do not seem well equipped to deal with a situation when the adversary is ALSO rolling dice and might succeed in dealing damage to you whether or not you are successful. For that reason, it's problematic to treat PC on PC violence as if it's any other situation. It's not the same.

    I think the solution Sage provided would work. Everyone makes a call and decides that one guy is Interfering and the other guy is Hack and Slashing. Fine. But, that decision really does give an objective advantage to one player or the other (see Hotar and Pol). The player with the low STR would much rather make the Interfere move, but why should he get to? What if his manner of interference is "hitting the guy with a frying pan"? You guys talked about "who goes first" and who is "defending," but from the point of view of the players, they are BOTH reacting to stimuli the way that they always do -- sequence doesn't matter. They are just making a move. The question isn't "who goes first," it's "which move is arbitrarily assigned to whom?" And, somebody is getting screwed by the mechanics.

    But, still, I'm not saying it wouldn't work. It would work. It's just not fair to somebody.

    There are probably other solutions too. I was thinking it might be a good idea to say that a player cannot deal damage to another on their own roll. Like, if player A rolls a 10 in a Hack & Slash against player B, player A should still "succeed" but the GM should narrate some fictional advantage or benefit. The only time a PC should take harm should be as a result of their own move (which is how it works in every other case in the game).

    So, in the case you proposed several posts ago where a Hack & Slashing PC rolls a 7 and exposes himself to an attack, only at THAT point would the GM ask the other PC to roll a damage die to determine the harm.

    The idea of having a special caveat for a player versus player situation is not new. Parley has a player versus player exception, and there might be other ones. I think it would make things easier in Hack&Slash.

    \n@AMBayard: Okay, I see several gaps in understanding here, mainly regarding the fiction-first nature of the games.

    First, the GM doesn't "assign moves arbitrarily." Moves are triggered by specific fictional actions, not assigned. And the GM makes principled (not arbitrary) decisions according to her agenda and principles. The situation is not "we're both rolling hack and slash!" There are specific actions being performed in the fiction, and those actions trigger moves (or not) according to a fictional sequence. If you don't know the actions and sequence, the fiction is too vague for the mechanics to kick in.

    Second, the Wizard doesn't "get to" roll H&S when he tries to punch the Fighter, just because his player wants to. Fiction comes first, and the fiction triggers the move. Them's the rules. I'm sure you've seen the example of the Fighter who wants to H&S the Dragon using a normal sword. He doesn't automatically get to roll that, since the fiction doesn't match the trigger for the move. Same thing with the untrained, unarmed Wizard taking a wild swing at the Fighter.

    Here are some illustrations:

    Case 1 -- How it normally works -- Typical Player vs. Monster situation

    GM: Goblin soldier swings his knife toward your throat! What do you do?!

    Hotar is clearly at a disadvantage given the situation. Pol just needs to do damage to Hotar and technically he wins (it doesn't really matter if Pol gets knocked out as a result since he isn't the one trying to do something). Hotar needs to deal damage to Pol AND not take damage in the process (because he's the one actually trying to do something other then fight). So, if they both decide to throw a punch, Pol wins, even though they both take the damage and fall over unconscious. Given that logic, Hotar probably should defend himself rather then throw a punch.

      Ok, but this means the GM's interpretation sets up a contest in which the deciding factor is player B's defending skill, not player A's attacking skill. As player A, I wouldn't be happy with that. It comes back to jockeying for GM's interpretation being beneficial to what you want to happen. In that case, seizing initiative and going first can be actively harmful to your ability to win, because then the other player knows your plans and can react accordingly, effectively nullifying whatever advantage you were attempting to achieve.
      \nIf you'll recall the setup here is the emperor hands them both a sword and says go! That's a pretty contrived setup, and it's contrived to create maximum difficulty in starting off the conversation. In that instance, the GM needs to hear from both players to see if a move triggers.

      Why is it harmful to your ability to win if B defies danger? If he B gets a 10+ they get what they want, in this case deftly rolling out of reach. If they get a 7-9 they stumble, hesitate, or flinch and the GM offers a worse outcome, hard bargain, or ugly choice. On a miss, the GM makes a move. B's defy danger looks full of risk to B.

      Seizing initiative is irrelevant given this setup. In another situation, say the one with Holgar and Pol above, the person who throws the first blow might be able to trigger hack and slash just fine.

      Holgar: If he jumps in front of me I'm smashing his head with a mace.

      \nPlayer A rolls a 10+ and Player B rolls a 10+: Player A and Player B both deal their damage to each other. If one of them chooses the extra damage option, he deals +1d6 and takes his enemy's damage again (obviously this is only a good choice in certain situations). The other fictional effects take place at the same time. In the fiction, both characters surpassed each other's defenses and guarded themselves from counter-attack.

      Player A rolls a 10+ and Player B rolls a 7-9 (and vice versa): Player B takes Player A's damage from B's roll. Player A takes Player B's damage from B's roll. Player A deals damage to B from A's roll. At A's discretion, B takes 1d6 more and A take's B's damage again. The other fictional effects take place at the same time. In the fiction, A has made a clean attack while B has made one that opened him up at the same time.

      Player A rolls a 10+ and Player B rolls a miss (and vice versa): Player B takes A's damage, plus d6 if A decides (and takes B's). In the fiction, A has made a clean attack and B has made a misstep.

      Player A rolls a 7-9 and Player B rolls a 7-9: Both deal damage, both suffer damage. In the fiction, both have made attacks that open them up to counter attacks.

      Player A rolls a 7-9 and Player B rolls a miss (and vice versa): B takes A's damage. A takes B's damage. B suffers whatever else. In the fiction, A has made an attack that gives B an opening, and B has made a misstep.

      Player A rolls a miss and Player B rolls a miss: Both have whatever the GM decides.

      The thing to remember here is that your hack and slash roll isn't making you invulnerable or something. It's saying that your attack didn't open you up to any extra counter attack. So when two players roll at the same time it's like

      Also remember that one roll covers one attack, which can be a longer exchange in the fiction.

      So in the fiction the mutual 10+ case might look like: Cassius and Thelian spring to action. Thelian swings high, clearing Cassius' guard and scoring a solid hit. At the same time Cassius swings from the side, getting in under Thelian's arm. Cassius is now stumbling back a step and Thelian's shield is drooping.

      The mutual 7-9 case looks like: Cassius and Thelian spring into action. Thelian swings high, clearing Cassius' guard, but it leaves him off balance. Cassius's swing from the side also strikes home, but the follow through gives Cassius a strike at his open shoulder. Both of them take advantage of the opening and deal the counter attack. We apply the HP damage all at once.
