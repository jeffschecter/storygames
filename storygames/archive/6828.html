\nKNRPG Productions is proud to announce the release of The Bizenghast Adventure Game!

It's a brand new RPG based on the awesome Bizenghast manga series by M. Alice LeGrow. Here's the promo blurb:

When life ends . . . adventure begins!

The Bizenghast Adventure Game allows you and your friends to embark on chilling adventures in the Bizenghast universe, based on the smash-hit Tokyopop manga series by M. Alice LeGrow. Inside you\u2019ll learn how to create your own supernatural guild, explore haunted vaults, and do battle with unbearable entities.

Other features include:
- An exclusive look at the world of Bizenghast, including never-before-revealed secrets about ghosts and the afterlife.
- A complete role-playing game using the fast-playing Epiphany Engine game system. Designed from the ground up with beginners in mind.
- A dynamic character creation system. Customize your character with psychic powers, special skills, and magic spells.
- Fantastic new illustrations by M. Alice LeGrow.
- Ideas, advice, and more!

Check it out here.

This also the first in a series of KNRPG titles to use our brand new Epiphany Engine game system, which blends new-school and old-school sensibilities. Next up is Empire of Dust, a tactical, sci-fi epic, which is slated for a Gen Con release.

    \n Sure guys! Thanks for your interest.

    I'm sorry about the Bizenghast website, I'm not sure what's up with it. She might be making changes or something.

    Here's the skinny on the setting:
    The players take on the roles of agents of the afterlife. It's their job to patrol their assigned territory, capture ghosts (by binding them to a vault), and resolve their issues (by going into said vault, which contains an alternate reality dreamed by the ghost). It's sort of like Ghostbusters meets The Cell. There are a variety of different types of agents, including: guildsmen (artificial constructs of the afterlife), lost souls (ghosts who hunt ghosts), and human agents (everyday people who are contracted into the guild).

    Epiphany Engine:
    Epiphany Engine is a system that blends a lot of new-school and old-school ideas. So, it's fairly traditional in format: attributes, traits, equipment, task resolution, etc . . . but it's got a lot of underpinnings that give players a bit more control over things than the typical trad game. Design-wise it's in the same family as Spirit of the Century or Burning Wheel.

    The Bizenghast Adventure Game uses a stripped down version of Epiphany Engine that's meant to be very accessible to new players.

    The coolest features are:
    - Banter: Dialogue is an intrinsic, tactical part of combat scenes.
    - Dynamic Character Advancement: Your character advances on the fly. Any stat roll you make could result in an epiphany, immediately raising your stat.
    - Death Control: There are consequences to your character getting hosed, but character death is always a choice.
    - Unique Traits: Unique traits are a special kind of trait that give your character a specific, unique role in the setting (only one PC can have a given unique trait). This is basically the "meta-plot" killer. Your PC gets to be the cool protagonist who shakes things up and breaks the rules (rather than some Mary-Sue NPC).

    Does that give you a better idea?

    \n Sorry it took me so long to respond. I'm pretty swamped at the moment.

    System:
    The basic resolution system is based on rolling equal to or below your attribute rating on a d20 (attributes are rated 1-20). If your roll matches your rating, you immediately raise the rating by one (this is called an epiphany). It creates this cool, dynamic character advancement scheme that is really fun work with (since any given roll can advance your character).

    It's fairly traditional, task resolution type stuff. The cool bit comes when you add in all of the interactions of the various character traits. Character traits are basically little modules of setting + character detail + rules exceptions (like a more potent, setting heavy version of feats in D&D3). Unique traits are particularly cool, because only one person can take them and they tie you into the setting in a very specific way (sort of like a more focused version of "lore sheets" from Weapons of the Gods).

    In play:
    Bizenghast plays like a traditional rpg. GM, players, etc . . . Most of the cool things about the game-system are subtle tweaks to the traditional formula. A lot of the design is focused around creating/inspiring interesting situations (everything from the guild creation rules to the character traits push toward this idea). The game is all about action, adventure, and horror. As written, it's a fairly light-hearted game, but it's also capable of being very, very dark. One of the sample vaults, for instance, contains the ghost of a girl who was killed when her abusive father pushed her down the stairs. You have to deal with her angry, confused ghost from the perspective of one of the dolls that "lives" in her dollhouse. I guess I would say that it contains a pretty heavy dichotomy between light-hearted and dark/horrific themes.

    Does that help a bit? If you have more questions don't hesitate to ask.

    Edit: It's also great for a Buffy/Persona 3-style game where you have to balance you "normal" life with your "go-adventuring-in-the-dreams-of-ghosts-that-you've-hunted-and-captured" life.

    \n I guess it's just a matter of personal preference. I LOVE games with linear die mechanics. Mostly, I love the transparency of it.
