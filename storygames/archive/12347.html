    \n For me, it was "Go Play D&D", as I managed to play or run four distinct different and wonderful types of D&D.

    1) Jonathan Tweet's early stage "Indie D&D", which couples very cut down d20 style mechanics (interestingly jiggered so the DM never rolls any dice) to an OD&D ethic, along with some collaborative world building elements (only one person can play each class, and you are in charge of developing the color surrounding your class).

    2) Tony Dowler's Apocalypse D&D, which is a sweet merge of AD&D with the Apocalypse World "roll to see how many things you get to pick from a list" mechanic. Lots of a fun, produces a nice gonzo, pulpy feel. Reminded me of a more structured T&T.

    3) I GM'd "The Spire of Iron & Crystal", a tournament module for Swords & Wizardry, which is a straight up OD&D clone. The party got to play around in a weird science-fantasy wizard's tower, with strange furry snake-rats that could swim through crystal (and liked to be sung to when they weren't chewing off your leg), a crystalline tree that almost turned the thief into fertilizer, and mysterious teleport pads that split the party. I had a great time, and the players seemed to as well!

    4) D&D 4e, using Lee Short's (soon to be published) Dungeon Construction Kit. DCK enables "pick up and play" 4e, by using decks of cards, map tiles and an encounter book, as well as rules to allow the players to pick their risk/reward ratio. It produces very satisfying encounter based play, and could be used in a rotating GM format to let everybody at the table get in on the action. I'm very excited to see this reach finished form.

    I also played in another Lee Short demo/playtest, of his Tarot based Amber game. The Tarot is used both for generating situation, as well as conflict resolution, where the nature of the cards being played help drive the fiction, in a very interesting "Nine Worlds meets Everway" kind of way. My impression is that it would make for some great long form, slow burn play, and I'm interested to see how it develops.

    Oh, and how could I forget! Johnzo Aegard powered Danger Patrol, in which D4N-G3R, Prototype Automated Patrol Unit; Steve Gogetter, The Most Interesting Alien in the Solar System; and Colonel Tommy Eidolon, Ghost Explorer saved the Solar Regatta, and prevented System-wide war between the ever suspicious Crimson Empire and the insidious Venusian separatist forces of M.E.D.U.S.A. We never /did/ figure out what M.E.D.U.S.A. stood for though.

    Overall, a fantastic weekend as always. Kudos to Ping & her subalterns for enabling GPNW to level up year after year!

    \n FRI-01:In Fiasco, Matthew S-B fell in with the wrong crowd at Narc-Anon when he sponsored Gagan, who stole his mason jar of Confederate gold coins. Gagan did quite well for himself as a prosperity gospel preacher in the aftermath; that reincorporating little creep stole all the inspirational speeches that my sweetly maternal madam made during the game. As for me, the insurance wouldn't pay out and in the end, I couldn't even get a job at the movie theater. Eric Logan's brothel-mopper suffered worst, though, when the Roosterman made him into the new Little Peep.

    SAT-01: Geiger Counter: Ichor Falls, I started as a mere shift supervisor at the Ichor Falls call center, but I didn't look back when I was offered a full manager role in the call center of the Menace. You give me an endless supply of burning Amish child-slaves, and I guarantee you, I will push your metrics SKY FUCKING HIGH.

    SAT-02: In Danger Patrol, the D4N-G3R prototype multitasked the ladies. His head would chat them up at the bar while his body busted leading-edge moves on the floor. Erstwhile lavender alien Steve Gogetter Cyrano'd him through this, while Colonel Tommy Eidolon was off doing real work, decoding the enigmatic Stygians' plans for the solar system...

    SAT-03: Fiasco II, which I think fizzled a bit due to a setup that made two cards address an identical situation. We had one pair with a Need to get laid right now by anyone and another pair with a Need to get away from a former lover. We collapsed these situations and effectively moved them between the same pair of players, cutting a card away from a third player and leaving only a weak McGuffin as a central conflict point.

    I find that Fiasco is an extremely challenging game to play well, its setup doesn't take the table nearly as far as the setup for other oracular games like In A Wicked Age, and so you gotta be good at engineering the situation.

    SAT-04: NASCAR Roach. Jackson was the finest beat poet that the world of stock car racing had ever seen, but he did not serve the Master, so at the season-ending awards banquet, I cut him into little pieces and fed him to David Hasselhoff, who had been crucified inverted for the occasion. It is not easy to serve two gods, but Johan Skorpion, the Fastest Kraut in NASCAR, did his best.

    SUN-01: Scandihoovian shorts with Chris Bennett. We rocked I Didn't Do It It Wasn't Me for a couple hours. Dolly Parton's death brought many, many things to light, but no one was surprised when it turned out that Chris, her plastic surgeon, brought about her death because he was crumbling under the workload she required of him.

    SUN-02: IAWA. Jem smashed Steve's dark altar and became the oasis god; Charlton, who wanted to sleep with Jem, got mopey and wrote poems. Blake switched sides about a dozen times, and in the end was torn apart by the spirits of those he'd wronged.

    SUN-03: Montsegur. As always I could write a book about this. I think I want to codify a few rules tweaks to get the Cathar stuff out earlier, because people really stumbled with it. I believe the long description of Montsegur could be simplified and replaced with a tight paragraph, and the cathar reading moved in earlier.

    These are just quibbles. The actual play was white-hot and proved the game's considerable replay value to me. Here's everyone's fates:

    Bernard was put into a coma during an Act II sortie. His page Amiel sat vigil over him -- until a scene in Act III when the gallant knight suddenly sat upright and spoke in the voice of God that they all must flee, flee, flee. And then he died, but his corpse did not rot.

    Phillipa starved herself while trying to nurse petit-Esclarmonde, her infant daughter, over the winter.

    Esclarmonde passed the baby onto Arsende, who was also pregnant, hoping that Arsende could nurse. Arsende's pleas for mercy were rejected by the Count of Toulouse and she was burned.

    Garnier and Esclarmonde tried to escape. Garnier claimed it, leaving Esclarmonde to burn.

    Bertrand feuded with Amiel all game, then just walked right onto the pyre at the end.

    Amiel was dragged away from Bernard's corpse by Crusaders. He tried to recant, but he was incoherent, and he burned.

    Pierre Roger, murderer of the Inquisitor, didn't get much of a trial before he burned. Neither did Raimonde.

    Cecile, desperate to die before her Perfection was tarnished, had tried to lead the female Believers down to early judgment at the end of Act II, but the Crusaders just laughed at her and sent her back up, telling her that didn't want Montsegur's pagan hags -- they wanted its gold. Guided by some terrific little scenes framed by Seth and Darrin, Cecile reconnected with her humanity during Act III. She recanted before the Inquisition, stole Phillipa's three-times orphaned baby, and walked off into the spring day. Happy ending!

Fun as hell.

    Sunday morning I played Love in the Time of Sei\xf0, run by Matthew Gagan, accompanied by Joel Shempert, Jackson Tegu, and (I think) Matthew Sullivan-Barrett. Awesome, awesome Norse blood opera. He did a great job, as did all the others. Jackson's princess worked the sweet spot between dutiful and rebellious, tearing the King's heart apart. And Matthew Gagan made the Knight a tormented, lost soul who perceived his folly in the moment before he was torn apart by an enraged bear.

    Sunday afternoon was IAWA with Johnzo, another blood opera using the God-Kings of War Oracle. My war-sorceress Sethirae (or whatever her name was) got her ass handed to her early in a conflict with the gore-stained priest of the wasteland devil dogs, and resorted thereafter to playing everyone against each other, and yes, shifting her loyalties to fit the circumstances of the moment. What a killer game. I only regret I didn't have much creative juice left in me by the end of it. But hey, I got to witness the apotheosis of another character while having my magical strength broken at the altar.

    Kudos to the organizers and everyone who made it such a fantastic weekend. Everyone made it a fantastic weekend.

    \n After dinner, I played Poison'd. Amusingly enough, Twyla gave me shit for not raping the governor's daughter (before I married her to the captain), but my main goal was to have my guy make a deal with the devil. Which he did. Then he came to his senses and regretted it. It made me smile.

    Saturday morning, I played Fiasco with James Brown and Adam. Hilarious hijinks in gangster London. I got to use my accents!

    Afterwards, Matthew SB asked me to play Serial Homicide Unit. This is a cool game, I like it. Instead of an accent, they made my character a stutterer! And then Kiera played Wilson's character's wife ragging on him for a scene, and I had to pick my jaw up off the floor.

    Then I showed up late to the lottery, I think because of lunch at the Honey Hole (which has amazing sandwiches), and Jackson shanghaied me into Apocalypse D&D. Hands down the most laugh-inducing game of the con. I cast sleep on myself in the middle of a fight, and when I tried to Spout Lore about a purple worm, I spouted vomit instead. "That's my Exorcist impression. I call it my Prismatic Spray!"

    Then I had Sage GMing again, with his cowboy Lady Blackbird hack. I got to do a bad-ass Southern accent this time, and shoot one of the other PCs.

    Sunday morning I played Mars Colony with a fellow Vancouverite I didn't know (Kynnin, you can whisper me your email if you're reading this), which meant we could use certain political terms without having to explain them. We used Ping's loaded dice, so our story turned out to be ridiculously optimistic. Aside from being an interesting two-player game, I think Mars Colony might be better than Shock: for times when Joe or Daniel want to play a game as an excuse to do world-building.

    Then Kingston shanghaied me into his Series of Unfortunate Events thing, and I got to do another British accent and get into mischief. He's got a good idea here, I hope he does something with it.

    Remember Tomorrow was the only game I signed up for more than ten minutes in advance, and the only game I played all weekend that I'd played before. With Adam facilitating and not playing a character, we managed to get over the learning curve and just have fun this time.

    That was my weekend. Totally exhausting, but somehow not long enough. Thank you everybody I played with, I had a lot of fun.

      \n I played in the same Norwegian session as Dev, and I'm not sure what happened qualifies as "fun" in my personal idiolect, but it gave me a lot to think about, which is almost as good.

      I had a personal issue with the "Forgotten Memories" scenario: I have NEVER had a good experience with the "So, hey, let's have a roleplaying game where you play *yourself!*" setup, and usually excuse myself from such games, but I figured I'd roll with it for the sake of the game. Dev, I think the thing we didn't get about that one is that the point of that setup is so that the memory will have some emotional resonance for the person whose memory it is, but that's about as far as it goes. Towards the end of the game, instead of playing the memories fairly straight, we started embellishing them with meth labs and dead bodies, and while I think that might have been a little bit gonzo for what the game designer may have intended, it kicked up both the story factor and the engagement factor for me.

      And I have a different issue for the "Stoke v Birmingham 0-0" scenario, in that I personally am not a soccer fan. I suspect that if the scenario were "Red Sox at Orioles, 1-0 in the 19th" I could have engaged with it a lot more directly - as it was, I was trying to use my own great affection for baseball to get into the character's head, and kept on running up against the fact that baseball games simply cannot end in a tie. But I can also see that ending in a tie really connects to the theme of the scenario.

      But (as you can see) I've done a lot of thinking about why the games didn't work for me, which is interesting and satisfying if not actually *fun*.

      \n I actually ran the two Norwegian games. Sorry they weren't necessarily everyone's cup of tea, but I'm glad you guys at least seemed to get something out of it. I think that might have partially been because I didn't explain the game well enough (I think doing it right after Stoke, where we were trying to be as bland as possible might have had an effect too...) and maybe partially because it was somewhat difficult to tell from the text how things should be going. My impression is that it is supposed to be very driven by the Prompter and that the Storyteller shouldn't do too much but talk and wait for the Prompter to tell him/her what to do, which wasn't quite happening so much at the table, so I think I need to maybe emphasize that more in my preamble. That being said, although if I had to do it over again, I probably would've run Until We Sink as the second game, it did give me something to think about and I would like to get the chance to play it again and apply what I've learned from this game. Thanks to everyone who gave it a try! I enjoyed playing with you.
