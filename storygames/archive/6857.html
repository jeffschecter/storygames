\n Not that Night Below, although I ran that while I was in the Army, and it was great.

Here's the setup I want. You're adventurers, and you're deep beneath the earth. You should probably start at level 5, just to account for the fact that you're miles underground, and have disrupted the lives of countless beings behind you. There were plenty of bad dudes, sure: this isn't revisionist D&D.

But, there was an earthquake, or maybe there was something terrible that you couldn't fight or understand and you ran. Whatever happened, you're lost miles underground and the way you came in - you don't even know where it is. And there's miles of pissed-off creatures behind you, anyway. Your adventure is getting home, and encountering horrible foes and unlikely friends along the way. I'd definitely have strange underground communities that need help from heroes, and man, do they have a bone to dangle: you need them to help you get out of here.

The focus would be on resource management, horror encounters, mental stress, xenophobia, and bonding with each other and, again, unlikely friends.

    \n The "Linked Portal" ritual provides a great setup for this kind of campaign, too. I really want to run an adventure that starts with the PCs finding the portal sigils of a lost temple or fabled Dwarven city, and teleporting "blind" to the linked portal with no clear plan for getting back.

    I think the idea of running a whole campaign that way is great though - lots of opportunities for weird politics, fantastic locations, and plenty of horror. My one question is what you'll do about replacing dead party-members. Recruiting new members from subterranian races could be cool (it'd be fun to be the one Drow or Duregar in the group, or whatever), but once the natives outnumber the aliens, the purpose of the campaign becomes a bit diluted - "wait, why are we looking for the surface again?"
