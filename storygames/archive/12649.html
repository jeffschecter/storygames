[Dresden Files] Resisting Compels

\n
\n
\n
\n\nBrendan_Conway \n\n\n

\n\nAugust 2010 \n edited August 2010 in Play Advice

\n
\n
\n I was running a moderately persistent Dresden Files game over a couple of weeks, and I encountered a few issues. I took some time from when the actual sessions took place to mull these issues over before writing anything up. Part of the problem I've had in writing this up is that I'm not sure exactly how to put my problems into words. It's just a feeling of dissatisfaction, a feeling of \u201cI know this could have been better\u201d that permeates my memories of our games. Hopefully, the source of my feelings will become apparent as I discuss what happened, but the title of the discussion captures my feelings somewhat: I think everyone resisted compels, and the complications and interesting difficulties that compels represent, on a player-level.
\n
\nI'm not going to discuss the city that much, because I think our city was great. Had plenty of built in conflict, some really cool points. The only issue I saw with our city was that the characters maybe should've been more directly attached to certain of the factions, and thus been more connected both to one another and to the city, but I'm not even sure that was a real problem. So I'm going to jump straight to describing the characters. These included standouts such as a former Marine, now working as an EMT, who had been infected with a demonic virus, and the only thing keeping him alive was the healing effects of the Hospitaler tabard he had recovered from the Middle East, which also allowed him to shapeshift into a towering, super-tough Crusader Knight form; an ex-Warden who had made an oath by her own magic and had broken the oath in order to kill a monster, thus losing her considerable power in evocation, and being left only with her rather poor skills at thaumaturgy (which had, of course, since improved tenfold); and a novelist who had taken it upon himself to do what Bram Stoker had done to the Black Court, by writing novels about monsters and how to kill them, and who carried on the tradition of monster-hunting in the mean time.
\n
\nWe were hindered by a couple of different factors, including the fact that not everyone could be there consistently, and we had to do each session as a full story, instead of splitting up stories over multiple sessions. We also wanted to experiment a bit, so some of us had many different characters to test out different builds and abilities, and we rotated the GM position a bit, too. All of this could easily have contributed to the dissatisfaction that I later felt upon looking back.
\n
\nTo try to put it into words, I think that one of the main roots of my dissatisfaction came from an odd tension between success and failure. None of us wanted to fail. Ever. The characters we designed were all optimally built to take advantage of the rules and powers as written, so that they would be as great as possible. For example, the ex-Warden, who could've been very interesting as a character struggling to use her Thaumaturgy, which she'd never been good at, a character so used to having immense power through evocation, and now having none of that power, was instead designed to optimize Thaumaturgy, specifically in terms of magic items. So the ex-Warden had a number of different hyper-powerful enchanted items which gave her many evocation effects at very high strength, meaning that she didn't feel weakened in the slightest.
\n
\nThen, one could add on top of that the problem of supernatural powers. That EMT I previously mentioned could shapeshift into a super-tough knight form thanks to his tabard. This meant that he had both Item of Power and Shapeshifting. Most of his power, then, was invested in a source that was two steps away, if you will. He both had to have the Item, and he had to shapeshift to get to his Supernatural Toughness. The problem of this, however, was that it meant without his tabard, he had just lost out on most of the refresh he had spent. As a result, I hated the idea of taking away his tabard, because it would so utterly de-power him, and I was almost certain that the player would not enjoy it. Similarly, it was very difficult to me to figure out when or if to have his catch be hit, because it was, I believe, a +0 refresh catch, and when it did hit, it was the same thing; he would be losing out on both Supernatural Toughness and Inhuman Recovery, meaning that he'd be losing out on 6 refresh instantly, without any way to resist. The player would not have enjoyed this in the slightest, as he would have been losing the thing for which he had built the character.
\n
\nHe had made sure to set up his skills so that in his non-shapeshifted mode, he was very non-combat oriented, while in his shapeshifted form he was a combat beast. This meant that in physical combat, he was unstoppable. With 8 boxes of stress, 2 points of armor, and Inhuman Recovery on top of that, along with a Great (if not Superb, I don't fully remember) Weapons skill to provide defense in melee and a Good Athletics to provide defense from ranged attacks, it was nigh-impossible to actually cause him any degree of significant harm in physical combat. I was trying not to think of it in terms of causing him harm, too, but the fact was that physical combat with this character involved became an incredibly screwy affair thanks to his suite of powers. He could take tremendous amounts of damage, and he could dish it out with relative success, as well, because he took Claws to represent the sword that he would always get with his shapeshifted form. Everyone around him, then, in physical combat would feel nigh-useless, because nobody else was anywhere near as effective. The options that seemed open to me then were to give other characters their chances to shine in non-combat scenes, but it just wasn't the same. Non-combat scenes, even social conflicts, were never quite as thrilling, and because this character could shift his skills around through shapeshifting, he was often quite capable of ensuring that he at least wasn't useless in non-combat scenes. Furthermore, those non-combat scenes were often just cases of \u201cLet the best guy do it,\u201d meaning that the character with the best skill in the appropriate area would do it, and everybody else would just ride on his or her coattails. So I couldn't overcome the problems of this Knight overshadowing the other characters simply by granting more non-combat scenes to the others.
\n
\nThe problem wasn't just restricted to this character, as well, so it wasn't just a matter that could be solved by restricting this particular build. The pure mortal novelist monster hunter felt utterly useless in any conflict with a monster, which felt vaguely appropriate due to his pure mortal status, but also felt very odd, because he did not have an area in which he could truly shine. Or at least, he did not seem to.
\n
\nI think that what this all brings me to is a particular mindset that seemed to have infected us. It was a mindset which we could not escape, even as we told ourselves to avoid it. We were in a very combat-focused, character-optimization based mindset. Players cared about their characters and their characters' success first and foremost, with everything else being secondary. Furthermore, players were not proactive. This meant that players mostly reacted to the story as being told by the GM, and did not engineer events in their favor with any degree of regularity. The only times players would attempt to steer events was when those events were related to combat; the novelist, whose Performance skill was at a big fat 4, never ever used that skill, though he did use his Resources to buy some flamethrowers when the characters were taking down a batch of Red Court vampires. I didn't know how to get him to a place where he might attempt to wield his Performance as a weapon to his advantage, and so it sat there, a disused, sadly interesting piece of his character that never saw any play. I, as a GM, attempted to compel the players, but these compels were often weak in how the players dealt with them, not least because the players never had an attitude of embracing the compels. Compels were always seen as a negative, as a \u201cAlright, you're screwing me now, but at least I get this fate-chip to fight back later\u201d, and the buy-out was always on the tip of the compelled player's tongue. Those compels that might lead to combat were also often utterly unimportant to some of the characters; where these compels should have been quite consequential, the fact that these characters were so damn good at combat meant that they didn't care about entering into a new fight, because they new they'd get out pretty unscathed.
\n
\nAnd not once, not a single time, did a player compel him or herself. To me, this seems like one of the biggest tragedies, as I love the stories I've read and heard of players asking for a compel. Indeed, in one session, I hinted I wanted a compel, and the GM denied me. I was playing a young girl who had been raised by the Summer Court, and who was now in college near the city. She had just been playing her violin at the site of a Faerie attack, and in the aftermath, the police had arrived and had begun to question everyone. My character's Trouble related to her being the child of two worlds, and belonging to neither, as she didn't know the social practices of the human world, having been raised in the Summer Court, but she most certainly didn't belong in the Summer Court. I suggested that this might be a prime time for her Trouble to come in, as the police could question her, and she could answer very awkwardly thanks to her lack of understanding of human social behaviors (like lies), or even blurt out the truth, which might get her into trouble quite easily. The GM said \u201cNah,\u201d and it never happened. That's the only time I can think of during all our play when a player asked for a compel.
\n
\nSo here's my question: How does one avoid this type of play in a game like Dresden Files? In other games, you literally cannot load your character in such a way that you can focus on character optimization and your own success, and you're enticed by the game's mechanics into causing trouble for yourself. But in my experience with the DFRPG, the players had no such enticement. They optimized the hell out of their characters to make them combat machines, and the more interesting, less optimized characters like the monster-hunting novelist got screwed, as a result. Nobody ever wanted a compel, because compels were seen as bad things, and Fate Points were never truly needed; either the player would have plenty of fate points, or his or her abundant supernatural powers would more than make up for the lack of Fate Points. How does one get into a mindset where one starts seeing compels as cool ways to build up the story, as things you want, regardless of any trouble it might bring to your own character? How does one escape the mindset of thinking that one should optimize one's own character, and let the GM cause trouble for one's self, instead of at all seeking that trouble?

    \n I think one does it explicitly. "Hey, guys, the Dresden Files game was really unsatisfying for me because none of the characters had any interesting weaknesses and everyone was super-optimized. Nobody ever failed. In the books, every time Harry goes into combat it's a close thing, and I like that tension, and I don't like feeling like combat isn't dangerous." And then you talk about what you think is fun, and why you think it's fun. What you're talking about here is a play style difference, and so it's not really about what's right and what's wrong, but about differences in tastes.

    Basically, there are some games that support a play style where the player makes the baddest-ass character he or she can, and the GM challenges that character and that player through the mechanics. It sounds like the majority of the group you were playing with tried to play in that style, and you had trouble because the Dresden Files game doesn't support that play style well (which I can't really comment on - I haven't seen the books for long enough to actually read them, only say, "ooh, pretty, want") and because you want less optimized, more vulnerable characters.

    So what I think might work for you: be explicit about what you want to see more of. Find players who are interested in that style of play, and try a game that's not about power and winning. (Seeing how those players take to Fiasco, for instance, would be interesting.) Play games with them. Then, next time you try playing a Dresden Files game, make it explicit that characters are supposed to be humans with weaknesses and frailties and traits that can be compelled.

    \n
    \nThinking about the lack of compels by the players, perhaps the fault lies in the character's aspects. The Fate standard rules stress that it is very important to pick good aspects. Of course basic Fate doesn't have a lot of supernatural powers. But what I'm trying to get at is, the reward for spending fate points invoking aspects should be versatile and interesting enough that players will want to have fate points handy to use them, and therefor want compels to garner fate points. The essential idea of losing a fate pool in DFRPG is that you're trading off versatility and humanity for power. Also it sounds like your characters don't rely often on maneuvers or descriptive combat that helps take advantage of tagging aspects so perhaps they're more enamored with their powers over their aspects and don't think to use them. Or did they take that out of DFRPG? I can't remember. So I guess the point of that paragraph is to point out that you can optimize your character by picking really good Aspects. And the compel system becomes apart of that optimization, that helps create a better story.
    \n
    \nEvery system is going to have its twink aspects because no system is perfect. It does sound like your human character needs a few more non-combat challenges, or some combat challenges that require some non-combat tasks within them. (Your Templar and Ex-Warden holding off monsters while she completes finding and scribing down the clues from an ancient parchment before the rising sun causes it to burn away, or something).
    \n
    \nAlso how much Fate play does your group have under it's belt? Perhaps the players aren't used to a more dynamic give and take between players and GM? There is usually more to learning how to play a social game like an RPG than just following the character creation and combat rules.
    \n
    \nBut ultimately discussing your dissatisfaction with the game with the other players is really the only way to go in the end.

I am never quite certain of when to allow aspects to be used, when it's too much of a broad stretch to use those aspects in particular situations, and so on.     \n I don't think it should really be up to the GM to determine when to allow Aspect use or not. I think that sort of thing needs to be about the group as a whole's threshold of credibility.

    Moreover, it sounds as though their Aspects were sort of boring\u2014and that *is* a problem. See the guidelines for writing good Aspects and really, really make sure everyone's are interesting.     \n One of the foggiest areas in FATE is the fate point economy. Much as each game tries to pin down exactly the right number of fate points, the fact is that usage depends heavily on play style and consequently the rules of each game (in this specific regard) mostly tell you how the designers and playtesters play.

    To keep the economy moving, players need to get low on fate points. Once they are low, they will start eying the compels. They will even start offering them. But before then there is no pressure so the question is how do you get there? There are two obvious ways I'll tell you about anyway.

    1. Start with fewer. This is what we did in Diaspora -- start you with an insufficient number of fate points. This will frustrate tables that really sling them around, but for us and in that gritty sf environment, it worked.

    2. Overmatch characters in combat. Because combat can end with a concession, you shouldn't feel restricted to finding encounters that you think they can win or even barely win. Instead design combat encounters that you think will kick the SHIT out of them. They will burn fate like crazy to keep that from happening and even if it does they can concede out of it in an entertaining fashion. This is our approach in Soft Horizon, where we deliberately tune tests to be extremely hard, which forces more aspect usage which forces more detailed narration explicitly relevant to the character concepts.

    Posted By: Saint ErebusSo, here are some follow-up questions: Is there any way to provide some incentive to help players get into that mindset? Or is it something that's either going to happen, or not? And is it possible to play an optimized character, but also play such that you want to fail sometimes (which is how I would interpret Harry as having been "played" in the books)? That would be my ideal, I think.
    \nI think the first step has to be discussing it with the players and getting their buy-in. If they *prefer* the heavily optimized, invulnerable play style, trying to give them incentives to change will just frustrate them and you. But if they buy in, the best incentive you can give them is screen time. If the result of combat is a foregone conclusion because the combat monster character is that well optimized, don't play it out - just summarize it. Focus on the situations where you have doubt and hard choices. Reward players whose characters have vulnerabilities by giving them opportunities to be vulnerable, and don't hose them completely if they do show vulnerability.

    It might help if you played a few other games with that group. I've found that In A Wicked Age really confounds and frustrates optimizers, although it doesn't do much to encourage vulnerability. And Fiasco is pretty much concrete proof that flawed characters are interesting characters. What you really need to do is break the association between character success and player success and the association between character failure or weakness and player weakness.

    And you have to accept that some players just won't care for that. That's fine - figure out who you play with when you want character vulnerability, and who you play with when you want adversarial powergaming. (I have one friend who is a brilliant wargamer. I have learned to not invite her to story games, because she tries to optimize, and there's often nothing there to optimize -- but that doesn't stop her from spending 45 minutes to an hour trying.)

    As far as mechanics go, what I found with Spirit of the Century was that it usually took one player to start poking at aspects and getting some pluses from them. Once the other players saw that engaging with the system and mechanics in that way made it possible for them to all do cool stuff, they dove in -- but it took three or four sessions before they really started getting into it. They never really got into compelling themselves, but they reacted positively to being compelled -- the *character* was usually frustrated by it somehow, but the *players* found it entertaining.

They start with a map to embody the tactical vision. Characters are materialized on this social/non-combat map, it helps with getting every players on board.     \n I agree there are a lot of different issues here, here are some that I've had in my Dresden experience (it is my only FATE experience, because until that presentation, I didn't really get FATE.)

    * Aspects like "Ex-Marine" that are only invoked for good things are a fate point drain. If that aspect never causes a problem for a character, it is nothing but a drag on fate points. That is fine, so long as you have some other aspects that are only compelled, for bad things, and you gain fate points. Not every single aspect has to be both positive and negative, though that does make it easiest for everybody.

    * The Dresden Files advice is to buy your refresh down to 1 or 2. I think it should be more than advice - it should be a rule that you buy it all the way down to 1. That was you are always looking for fate points if you want to do anything.

    * Compels on Aspects should really fire up the players. This is what the Trouble aspect is about. Trouble aspects are basically the player saying "this is the kind of tavern and old man with crumbly map that I want". One from a game we're just designing now is "Baby Mama Drama" - the character's biological clock is ticking and everything to do with babies is enormously compelling (no pun) to her. So if a con artist fakes being pregnant, comes to that character bawling and sobbing, "I...I..g-g-g-ot nowhere else to turn!" then the character will be all "of course I'll obtain that satanic occult book from its vampiric owner for you dear, have some tea and don't worry". Compels are ways to get characters in trouble. If they're not enthused about a compel, they should pay to resist it, yet another fate point drain. At some point they should be out.

    * I'm confused about why the combat-monstar character is not being challenged in combat. The Dresden files version of FATE especially scales up to godlike badasses really fast. Is there some reason you don't have a villain at their level of combat? Like, if you knew they wanted to get into combat, and were uber badasses, how about getting an uber badass bad guy? And compel "Ex-Marine" with an old buddy who this guy put in the hospital in a coma, and the hot red headed wife of the buddy who needs comforting, and there you go.

    I'm also curious as to what the other players think of the game.

    \n Characters don't have to be weak or challenged to be interesting. Sure, that's one way to do it (a very popular way in fiction) but it's not the only way.

    Powerful, badass characters are often great in an RPG because they are consequential. Their actions shake the world. Status quos are destroyed and the powerful are torn down. This can give the game a lot of momentum. If the PCs are constantly obliterating powerful bad guys, what happens in the power vacuums that result? How do all the other characters in the web react? You have instant fodder for session after session of play.

    If your players like to play invulnerable badasses, try going down that road with them. Instead of trying to think up challenges to turn the tables on them and make them vulnerable and weak, embrace their awesomeness. How does the world react to the sudden appearance of these dangerous characters? Who's first to try to cut a deal with them? Which groups gang up for self-protection? What other 'good guys' are suddenly knocking on their door asking for favors and help?

    Exploring consequences is what's fun in gaming (to me). Exploring the consequences of strife and failure is one way to do it. But the consequences of power, responsibility, and changing the world can be really fun to explore, too.

The fate chip is more just icing on the cake, or it can be thought of as some quantity of future narrative control, guaranteed to the player to reward him or her for the complication. That, actually, pretty much sums up what I think I need to talk to my players about: fate points given for compels are rewards, not compensation.     Posted By: Saint ErebusHow does one escape the mindset of thinking that one should optimize one's own character, and let the GM cause trouble for one's self, instead of at all seeking that trouble?
    \nAdversity has to come from somewhere. Almost all of the time, it's going to come from the GM. That's just the Czege Principle in action.

    It follows that if there's insufficient adversity in the game, the problem probably doesn't lie with the players.

    "Submerged" is a super-heroic power level. The GM has to rise to the challenge with adversaries of similar stature.

    Off the top of my head, here's some things I would try:

    * Ex-Marine and EMT goes to White Council: The ex-marine and whoever else is around get summoned to White Council. Maybe they're accused of something they didn't do or something they did do but for a good reason; happened to Harry in every other book.

    The ex-marine walks in and Ancient Mai's wardhounds start growling at him. They can sniff out the demonic virus in his blood. They're getting closer now, baring their teeth. What do you want to do, ex-marine? Really want to Hulk Out and defend yourself in the middle of the White Council? And what are your friends doing?

    * Oathbreaker: The ex-warden gets attacked by a black wizard and his pet demons. Not even a very competent wizard. Now he's dead and the real wardens are not in a real forgiving mood about one of their own who has left the flock. Mixes well with the ex-marines White Council problems above.

    * Drycleaning Extra: It's the ex-marine again. He's hanging around, minding his own business, and a couple of Knights of the Cross show up. Hey, thanks for saving the Holy Tabard for us, but we're going to need it back now. Want to pick a fight with two guys on a Mission From God?

    * There's no business like it: Great news -- the novelist has had his movie rights picked up by a big studio. They want him on site to check out their progress. Wow, their monster effects are great. Fantastic. Way too good to be fake. Do you want to take out the monstrous star of your own blockbuster movie?

    * Frickin' Fanboys: Everyone loves those monster-hunting books. Some kids love them way too much. Now they're starting to turn up torn to shreds with a blood-soaked copy of your latest novel in their pocket. Anything you care to do about that?


    PCs need someone to throw rocks at them. Super PCs need some pretty big rocks. The duty rests squarely with the GM.
