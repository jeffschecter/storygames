\n So I'm thinking about making a game of courtly intrigue, love, pride, jealousy, hatred and ... more intrigue, in 18th century Sweden. The game is a big 'maybe' at the moment, and probably won't happen at all, but I've got some design goals and I can never resist thinking about mechanics.

So I've got this thing I've been contemplating, called Metaphors. Basically, the GM can set up a Metaphor for a relationship, for an emotion, for someone's happiness, or similar. F'rex (is it still hip to say that?), my character is setting up a play of some sort. The GM now establishes the play as a Metaphor for my relationship with Katarina. So if our relationship suffers, the play suffers, and vice versa. I like these things in stories, and it seems like a cool tool to get some subtle storytelling going (I don't have to tell everyone that my relationship with Katarina is in a bad state; it's enough that I have a scene where I argue with the actors) and let details like plays, fashion, masque balls, music and paintings become important.

The thing I'm worrying about, though, is stretching the credibility where everyone suddenly starts caring about this play I set up, because they want to save or spoil my relationship with Katarina. What do you guys think? This game is nowhere near playtest ready (this is more or less all there is), and it might not happen at all, but I like this mechanism. Will it stretch credibility? If so, what can I do to fix that?

I've got a few ideas, but I want to hear what you have to say first.

    In all seriousness, I think the idea is great... but the suggested genre/setting, not so much. This sort of close coupling of otherwise (seemingly) unrelated things befits a land of dreams or faeries or gods. It's just too surreal, to my mind, to take this coupling--more so, the awareness of it by the characters--into any kind of "realistic" or historical setting. Sure, with this example (play = relationship) you can come up with "logical" reasons the characters would think impacting the play impacts the relationship (see above).

    But suppose we couple, oh, a small garden plot and the Swedish King? Perfectly valid metaphor in literature--as the plot blooms, ripens, and eventually fades, we readers can see a similar, resonant arc in the King's health or reign or love life or whatever. But in an interactive fiction, where the characters themselves know/see that coupling? I fear you'd get a comedy of errors, as assassins poison rutabagas and diplomats help weed the tomato rows and courtiers... uh, wax the cucumbers? (Nice for the little garden's owner, though, I suppose--celebrity, volunteer gardeners!)

    In all seriousness, I think the idea is great... but the suggested genre/setting, not so much. This sort of close coupling of otherwise (seemingly) unrelated things befits a land of dreams or faeries or gods. It's just too surreal, to my mind, to take this coupling--more so, the awareness of it by the characters--into any kind of "realistic" or historical setting.     \nIt's been done many times as a-play-within-the-play. But it would work just as well if it were a shared dream world, jockeying for position in the king's court through indirect means (parties, gossip, fashion, etc.), the telling of stories (like 1001 Nights), or a meta-game like the gods playing chess with people's lives.

    But I think you would want to limit it to two game worlds running in parallel - with the players competing in both and the result in one explicitly linked to an attribute bonus of some kind in the other (rather than linked both ways). For example, for the more realistic example of courtly intrigue, throwing a lavish party might give you a die of King's Favor that you could use in any other situation you wanted (and the actual effects of which could be easily described if necessary).

    For the play-within-a-play scenario, it would work in a similar manner, but at a dramatic level that doesn't need to be explained: a character whose counterpart in the internal play is successful in some way would receive a bonus of some kind in the "real" world. For example, if a character within the play seduced someone, it might lead to the corresponding outer character receiving a bonus die that could be used on his or her next personal interaction with the opposite sex. In this case, you'd probably want to exaggerate the action in the internal play as much as possible (murder might only signify a drastic reversal of fortune - or an attribute going to zero temporarily).

    \n Very cool!

    I suggest you call them "mirrors".

    I really like the "sacrifice" option tied into the equation.

    For instance, it could be set up like this:

    The two situations influence each other, but are not necessarily the same. So, perhaps, the actions of other players affect both "mirrors", but your own actions can only affect one "mirror" at a time. So, your play and your Katarina might both improve because of good influences, but you still have to choose which one you want to concentrate on more. If you're lucky, both might do well.

    In addition, if one of the mirrors gets close to losing it--the show is about to fail or you are about to lose the girl--maybe you can sacrifice one to get a bonus to getting the other one. But it only makes sense to sacrifice the one that is in better shape. For instance, things are going well with the play, but your relationship with Katarina is slipping, and is about to disintegrate completely. You can sacrifice the play to get a bonus or a second chance to get the relationship to work.

    In this way, you can get four possibilities (both succeed, both fail, one succeeds and one fails). I don't know if that undermines your whole premise, or if it sounds interesting.

    Hopefully that gives someone some ideas.

    This whole metaphor idea can be played as a figurative literary sort of thing (perhaps with connections made at random!), or more like something reminiscent of "Say Yes or Face the Dungeon": you want to achieve some goal in the fiction, and you use some metaphor for that goal to carry out your attempt.

    \n I actually really like the term "Mirrors". It feels just right. As an added boon, when you talk about sacrificing one of the two, you can talk about "breaking the mirrors". That's cool.

    Some more about the system: I'm planning to keep it diceless, similarly to Nobilis. Either you do a thing, or you don't. You dont fail to do things, but you can be stopped by some other player interfering. There will, most likely, be a mechanic for sacrifice. If someone's doing something you don't want to happen, you can sacrifice something to stop it from happening. But you don't start with things you can sacrifice -- you have to win them through play, and there'll be different bonuses attached to them. For example, you can, through play, earn a Mark of True Love. That'll give you immense power to help the person you love, and it's also a thing you can sacrifice.

    So all I need to do to connect this to the Mirror mechanic, is to say that you can sacrifice one part of a Mirror that contains an aspect of yourself or a relationship with you (like the Mirror between the play and my relationship with Katarina) to save the other, and this breaks the Mirror. Cool!

    \n I think the key would be something like this:

    Sacrificing one side of the mirror only makes sense as a last resort. For instance, gameplay is about players trying to get two things into play, each reinforcing the other. Sacrifice is only an option when you _know_ that you're about to lose one or both.
