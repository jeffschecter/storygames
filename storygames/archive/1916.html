Dragonmeet: Sales, Post Con Thoughts and Suchlike

Malcolm Craig
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Meetups / Conventions
[Cross posted at The Forge]

Dragonmeet, in London, is a very friendly and successful one day convention that runs on the first Saturday of December. With 600 - 800 (approx.) attendees, there's a goodly number of people there playing games and browsing the trade hall.

This was the first year that The Collective Endeavour had a presence there. Indeed, this was the first con that we would be at as a group to promote indie games in the UK. As well as demoing our own games, we were also on hand to talk about the full range of indie games that were being carried by the excellent Leisure Games, who had the stand right next to us. Brennan Taylor also kindly provided us with an Indie Press Revolution banner and postcards.

Our stand itself looked excellent, with the Collective Endeavour and IPR banner providing a backdrop and the tables dressed with appropriate red and black cloths, with handy clear racks for the books. Our conscious decision, taking note from The Forge booth at Gen Con, was to really engage with con attendees, standing in front of, rather than behind our stand, offering 15 minute demos and taking time to discuss games and gaming with people who came to the stand.

The result? An excellent first outing for The Collective Endeavour. Our sales were way beyond our expectations and the buzz around the stand was fantastic all day. Out of all the stands in the trade hall, ours definitely had the most enthusiasm and get up and go. People were enjoying demos, enjoying taking and buying games. There were also many opportunities to direct people to Leisure Games after they asked about (and this is just a small selection) My Life With Master, Burning Wheel, The Princes Kingdom and many others.

In conclusion, Dragonmeet was a great success for all of us. On a personal note, I'd like to thank Gregor Hutton, Andrew Kenrick, Matt Machell, Iain McAllister, Joe Prince, Cat Tobin and John Wilson for their hard work and enthusiasm throughout the day and in the run up to the convention.

    The a|state CDs have all the pay-for PDFs you can buy at RPGnow and DriveThruRPG, as well as wallpapers and all the freebies available from the website. I think it's pretty handy and you get a good deal for your money. People seem to like the free stuff being all in one place on a CD and are willing to pay for the convenience, and while they'd maybe only buy one or two of the PDFs for sure online, they figure that the ones that were borderline for them to buy individually are worth it as part of a bulk deal. [In fact, Malcolm, thinking out loud, is it worth making a bulk deal of a|state stuff for sale from RPGnow and DTRPG for people new to the game?]

    They look nice (Paul did a great cover and CD booklet, as well as printing on the CD itself) and for people that don't have accounts online or internet connections it's a good way of getting support material.

    The Best Friends CDs had the RPGnow/DriveThru version of the game on them (includes wallpapers, playsheets, richly linked PDF of the game, and the text as an RTF file), as well as extra wallpapers, some advert PDFs, an MP3 of the Sons of Kryos demo (I figured some people might not have web access), and the 24 hour versions of Best Friends and 3:16. I also put some other stuff on like a high-res Kill Bargle video and PDFs of the free games from my website. They came in pink CD cases (mostly) and each one had a different circle-triangle girl drawn by me on the front, so they're kinda cutesy and unique.

    They were aimed at people who either picked up the book and thought "what the hell, give me the CD as well", or for folks who don't like paper. For me, they were quite cheap to make (as I've already done the hard work assembling the stuff for online retail earlier in the year), and I was pleased that my small run of CDs ran out at the con.

    I'd recommend it to any Indie designer to make up a few for sale at cons, they're extra income and there is a market for them.

    Now: what's the next step?

    Well, that's the big question, isn't it? :-)

    In actual fact, we have pretty solid plans about where to take this, and the excellent first time out at Dragonmeet has only reinforced this. In the interim, the plan is to grow the CE website as a community and some good discussion going on there. There's already a couple of game sin develoment being posted about, so that's a great start. The site is also being used to set up small indies games meetups throughout the UK, with Matt machell taking the lead in this and organising one for the Midlands.

    As far as conventions go, the next one we will have representation at will be Conception in Bournemouth in January, followed by Conflagration in Glasgow at the end of February. Then there is Conpulsion in Edinburgh in March, followed by the re-vitalised (hopefully) Gen Con UK in Reading and the new UK Games Expo over the course of the summer.

    Our aim is to make the CE stand a feature of UK cons, where people can pitch up, try demos, talk about games, get advice on publishingor any other game related matter and, yes, maybe even buy a game or two. As with anything, we can't stand still and hopefully we'll see other indie publishers from the UK joining us on the stand as things progress.

    With the website and our success at Dragonmeet, I think we've built a really solid base for future success.

    Cheers
    Malcolm

    There were some people that picked up almost one of everything, but most picked 2 games or so I think. Dead of Night, Covenant and Cold City were quick out the traps in the morning. I certainly felt that the first people to make a bee-line for the booth had heard of them online and just wanted to buy them. Best Friends and Contenders were slower to get going at first -- Best Friends really started selling well in the afternoon (hats off to Graham for running his game of it, and I figure that the demos helped too -- none of the 4 sucked which was great).

    As for the demos? Well, the other guys won't say anything publicly negative about the BF ones so I will. One of them was 40 mins long, which is way too long. I should have been more on the ball about that, but guys, you should also have booted me off. So something for us all to learn from.

    On the other hand two of the demo games couldn't in any way have been done in 15 mins. I had 8 players on one and 7 on another and you can't pregen characters. I think Swansong is maybe another that needs some explanation time too.

    I've also had some feedback that some of the congoers wanted more than just a "bang!" 15-min demo (they felt those are too rushed in the more laid-back UK environment). I'm sympathetic to their argument, and perhaps a 20-30 minute demo wouldn't have killed us.

    The pressure on keeping demos short at Indy is that there is a lot of pressure on table space. I felt that there was less of this on our tables.

    I also ran a Best Friends demo at the end of the con where we kept the players at the table and got Joe to do a Contenders one to the same group. In hindsight maybe it would have worked better if we'd split the large table into 2 Contenders demos and got them all slugging it out.
