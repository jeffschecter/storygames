\n Hey all,

So i'm about to get a Burning Wheel game underway, for the first time with a totally new group in a new town in a new house at a brand new dining room table. It's exciting. My players are all college kids and while most of them have roleplayed before, it's only really been D&D, and pretty lame D&D at that (i know their old DM, he's pretty dry).

So i want to impress, create some atmosphere, really pull out the fireworks. I've been working on a playlist for the game but i'm coming up a little short. I want about 9 hours of music i can put on shuffle, turn down low in the background and not worry about. Fantasy, but not cheesy.

I read the suggestions in the BW books, and while I love Fire On High's "Blessed Black Wings" as much as the next guy, i think it may be a little heavy handed. I want something Passionate but understated. Vocals are okay, but they shouldn't be super dominant. Big Wagner-esque orchestration is acceptable only in small doses (suggestions with strong actiony content should be noted as such, i'll make a separate Fight! playlist).

So story games, help a brother out. What do you listen to during a (fantasy) game?

    \n Hmmm. It's hard to rec music, because for me it mostly serves as a distraction.

    Themey music (like, "oh, it's a combat scene! cue the music!") tends to get in the way.
    Classical music can put me to sleep.
    Music with lyrics distacts me.

    However, I do use music a lot in gaming, I use it turned down low, basically the equivalent of "white noise", to keep me focused. For this, I recommend:

    Drum and Bass, namely the Artcore 2 and Artcore 3 albums. Stuff that's mellow and without lyrics.
    Ian Pooley

    Some space music is ok. I subscribe to www.hos.com, so I've got a bajillion albums and sets at my beck and call. Problem is you want something with movement, because something too soft just makes you want to lay down and sleep (or ME, anyway).

    One classical-ish album I like is a compilation called "Music for Concentration". It works. Crazy.

    Windham Hill '94 is a great collection of chill ambient/celtic tunes, with only 1-2 tracks that are perhaps too mellow.

    Other than that, I pretty much roll the extended soundtracks of the anime HACK//sign (there's like 4+ albums in the set total, but it's good stuff). Sometimes I drop Twelve Kingdoms. The music soundtrack to Persona 3 is pretty good, too. But again, this is fantasy-ish music that rolls in the background to act as white noise to focus attention, I can't recommend 'cranking it' to create mood.

    But if there are others that are all OCB and easily distractable like I am, the above may help.

    -Andy

    Posted By: Kevin Allen JrWhat do you listen to during a (fantasy) game?
    \nHere's what I did (my current games move too fast, or require too much attention for music to really help - I tried it last with Dogs and I would just forget it or it would loop forever). Get great movie soundtracks, for medieval fantasy, Conan, Gladiator, Thin Red Line (yes, really), Harry Potter and the Prisoner of Azkaban, are just some choice material. Unless you are running these settings, avoid famous soundtracks like Star Wars or Lord of the Rings. Too familiar and connected.

    Cherry pick the tracks and either remember, or rename them for a theme. Stuff like "dark and moody" or "spooky" or "combat scene" and "emotionally dramatic". Then play just that track when you need it. I avoid any modern music for fantasy games,* it just takes everyone out. I avoid classical most of the time because they have their own thematic thread that isn't always good for the right mood. Cinematic scores are written to be in the background, to underscore what is going on, and to reinforce a specific feel for the given scene it was written for. In short, they're perfect for RPGs. I will also get into instrumental stuff, particularly a modern spin on traditional sound (like Cheb i Sabbah or Dhol Foundation for combat with an asian feel) depending on the genre. But Hollywood soundtracks are filled with appropriate sounds if you're going for something with a Western civilization feel. And with more foreign films getting higher quality and coverage in the West, those are good sources for non-Western stuff too.

    *The exception is usually end credits, maybe opening credits. Many shows often have some modern song over the credits, even if it breaks genre, with only the lyrics fitting some theme of the show or movie. I've done this for Exalted and Werewolf and it was fun. It's also a good way to say "We are playing now" or "We are done now".
