    \n Hope this hasn't come up before.

    Dave Morris and Jamie Thompson, goddamn kings of the British choose-your-own-adventure book scene back when it was a scene, and writers of the recently resurrected Dragon Warriors RPG found boxes of unpublished material long thought lost in the back of a cupboard (no, honestly) and are putting it up at their blog here.

    This includes stuff from the last 6 Fabled Lands books that were never published (Fabled Lands was a sort of perpetual world adventure gamebook fantasy series. Each book covered a different land in a fantasy world and you could just wonder from one to the other, doing quests, being a merchant, exploring. The technology it used - if that's the right word was genius. Keywords to track changes in the world due to your actions and clever little intersticial paragraphs to introduce context-appropriate random encounters)

    It also includes notes and stunning concept art for an MMO they were developing a long while before WoW happened - a phantasmagorical, John Carteresque exotic fantasy setting called Abraxas. It looks beautiful. An interview with them regarding the fate of the MMO over here.

    \n I blogged about the Indie Saga Series thing I'm running at Modern Myths Comics and Games. Here's what I have before-the-cut:



    So this is the first in a hopefully-regular series about the Indie Saga Series1

    Hey there, everyone. Welcome to a forest floating in space, peopled by slight, hollow-boned humanoids with a culture steeped in poetry and ritualistic torture and murder. This is no forest moon of Endor we're talking about here. This planet, Yzalyx (a name that is extremely likely to undergo linguistic drift in-fiction), as well as its sisters, Lorax and Zisa, were created using Emily Care Boss's Sign in Stranger \u2014 part of it, anyway. At the start of the game you do an exquisite-corpse-like thing, where you write a thing, fold the paper over, and hand it to the next person. We then picked a world that seemed the most interesting, and played a session of Vincent Baker's In a Wicked Age.... to see a story in the prehistory of this culture.

    Today, I've got the details of the worlds we made, with the one we wound up gaming on (Yzalyx) getting more detail than the others.

    I played this session with the manager of Modern Myths Comics and Games, Jim Crocker, as well as two dudes who just wandered into the store, Seth Ascher and Joshua Birk. I had the sense that Seth and Joshua weren't super-familiar with indie RPGs, and I know Jim hadn't played the game beforehand2.

    more

    1: In brief, I run indie games, one after the other, but building up the same world. You can see more info on my Story Games post on the subject.

    2: It's important to note that this store didn't have any copies of IAWA before the game started, and Vincent, hearing this, set something up special to make sure Jim had copies to sell when the game happened. Also, Jim offered to pay for all my printing costs. He rocks.

      \n If you have kids that watch Cartoon Network, you probably have seen at least the commercials for this, but "Adventure Time with Finn and Jake" premiered this month. There's been two episodes this far and one of the stories is available free on iTunes. Why would I think this interesting? I dunno, it's a very strange cartoon, but what I think is neat is the old-school D&D feel to it. It definitely has some roots in D&D (which the author claims in an interview), and refers to magic-users, etc. It's not at all an D&D adventure though, just has that surreal look (almost like Erol Otis had a hand in drawing it) and the elements of adventure. My son and I love the show ("Mathematical!"), but everyone else in this house not so much. So, that's a recommend from me for "Adventure Time".

      \n [D&D for Kids]\n
      Posted By: John Harper[D&D for kids]
      Man, how many hours of game design have mediocre D&D products inspired?
      \nFar, far too many. Seems like any time WOTC/Hasbro comes out with some "new idea" for D&D, it spawns homebrew games that then try to do the "new idea" correctly one thousand-fold. And yet, these "new ideas" tend to be based of things that indie and story gamers started already. There was one called RPGKids from www.newbiedm.com that pretty much covered the concept, though without the cute art.

      Maybe what we need is a kid-friendly dungeon-delving RPG that is actually presented like a real game, instead of a poorly thrown-together converted Word-doc (he says, looking very sternly at WOTC), with decent production values! Even kids know how to tell the difference between something cool and something their parents say is "cool" (but is in fact shoddy merchandise... kids have a "Detect Shoddy Goods" skill of 11, a la Toon).

      Now that'd be something that really hasn't been done yet. (at least, I haven't really seen anything like it, in this nation...)

      \n [Elfstar 1] Wow, such emotionally resonant fiction out of a Chick tract. Even the "Come to Jesus" part was fitting in context!

      I noticed one of the commenters is incensed, in detail, at how "wrong" they're playing D&D. By contrast, I thought it was neat how the author managed to recontextualize the LUDICROUS Chick depiction of D&D into something that made more sense to how the game is actually played. I wonder if the commenter got how A) the rules infractions were deliberate, part of a social power struggle and many, many people have played this way and called it "D&D."
