\n The "go aggro" move in AW has caused a lot of people grief.

Why is that? The description of the move ("go aggro") is not a terribly clear fictional trigger. What does it actually mean?

The explanatory text says:\n

Going aggro means using violence or the threat of violence to control somebody else\u2019s behavior, without (or before) fighting. If the character has the drop on her enemy, or if the enemy won\u2019t fight back, or if the character is making a show of force but isn\u2019t disposed to really fight, it\u2019s going aggro.

[...]

If the target forces the character\u2019s hand and sucks it up, that means that the character inflicts harm upon the target as normal, determined by her weapon and her subject\u2019s armor. At
this point, the player can\u2019t decide not to inflict harm, it\u2019s gone too far for that.
\n

It occurs to me that violence OR the threat of violence covers some pretty different territory. We are instructed to use "seduce or manipulate" if the character isn't actually planning to hurt their target, just bluffing. (Not sure why this handled with +hot instead of *hard* in this instance, but that's neither here nor there...)

One possible interpretation of the move is that the acting character is already in motion, carrying out this violent act. (Like firing bullets at someone - covering fire - to force them to keep their head down.) In this instance, it's really clear why the victim has the choice to suck it up or give in.

"Go aggro", here, means quite literally to behave in some way like a violent maniac. This suits Apocalypse World quite well!

And the back-and-forth is satisfying: one player starts the action, the other concludes it.

However, what if it's just the *threat* of violence? I've always had a problem with this.

"I stick my gun in his face and I tell him to hand over the money!"

Go aggro, right? It's "...using [...] the threat of violence to control somebody else\u2019s behavior, without (or before) fighting."

But what if we're playing and the MC says, "Whoa there! I don't have the money!" (And it's true.)

Now what do we do? By the rules, it's "too late" - if I rolled a 10+ and Dremmer can't hand over the cash, I can't decide NOT to shoot him.

This feels awkward in play. I'm tempted to carry out *threats* of violence through regular roleplaying/conversation, and only bring in the "go aggro" move when the PC actually commits to acting with violence, as the move implies.

(In our example, then, there would be no roll. We'd just play it out, with the PC having the option to just "inflict harm" - pull the trigger - if they don't like the NPC's response. After all, they maneuvered themselves into a position where his life is in their hands. Alternatively, we could use the "seduce/manipulate" move.)

Am I making sense, or is there a better solution? How do you handle this?

(Also, if you like my suggested solution, how would you recommend deciding when to just play it out and when to bring in seduce/manipulate? I'm not sure where the line would be drawn there.)

    \n I'm not saying it's elegant. It isn't. But it does work. Paul, I think you're involuntarily being all "Zeno's paradox" about it and making it not work by overthinking it, really.

    It's not that the game "forces" you to pull the trigger, it's that the moment you roll+hard you're committing to pulling the trigger.
    The move is about getting what you want by murderous violence\u2026 The moment you roll, it's a statement that successful murder sounds like a favorable outcome to you, even if there's something you'd like even more. Reaching for the dice is saying one of three things:

    1. There's this thing I really want, but I'll settle for retaliative murder (the default stakes) if I can't have it.
    2. I mostly want them dead, but I wouldn't mind settling for X instead.
    3. I want them either dead or dead, and won't compromise on it.

    On a 7-9, you fail to murder them - maybe you missed, maybe you flinched, maybe they dodged or ran very fast - but you still get something out of your earnest murderous attempt, like having them out of your way, maybe even permanently.

    On a 10+, and that's the catch, it's not you but the MC (or other PC) who has a choice between handing you their head on a platter (successful murder) or that other thing you might have wanted (if any). That is, you win and the other side has a choice between meeting your arbitrary, un-negotiated demand, or conceding the default stakes of murderous violence (injury and probable death). I trust an MC to choose according to agenda and principles; as for another PC-player, it's only fair it's their choice.

    Now, for the special cases discussed in this thread (have any of these actually happened in your game/s, or are we practicing the fine art of debating hypothetical scenarios?):

    * If you tell a guy to flap his arms all the way to the moon, and you mean it, then I assume it's #3 - you want them dead or dead, right?

    * If you demand money from a broke guy (what do you mean by "money" in Apocalypse World, btw?), that's the same as the above arm-flapping scenario and you should know - because that's what honesty demands and we aren't playing gotcha with hidden information in AW.

    * If you think you're going aggro on them and you roll 10+ and earn your successful murder and now you realize you don't want them dead: cool. It's just a do-over. Turns out the fictional trigger wasn't there after all. Nothing happened.
    It's clear from other parts of the book that AW assumes these things do happen sometimes, and it's no big deal, and it doesn't need to be specifically regulated or anything: it's just players making a mistake with the rules and then realizing they did and fixing it. This goes for most games.

    \n I've been thinking about it further, and I'm coming to the conclusion that it really depends how you infer the use of "moves" in a PbtA system.

    Vincent has stated recently that AW is not a "fictional triggers" game. You have moves, and you make them when you want to.

    Taking this point of view clarifies a lot of these issues.

    As Rafu says, above: that's the design of the rule, and when you use it, that's what you're signing off on. The move says something about the nature of Apocalypse World - it's only when you're already committed to hurting or killing someone that you get to roll dice.

    The move isn't triggered by the character's actions; it's also up to the player to decide whether the circumstances and consequences are appropriate and worthwhile.

    If taken in this spirit, the move seems to work just fine, I think. (As long as we're willing to be smart about choosing options, and use some of the tricks mentioned above.) To "go aggro", I need to have a character threatening another character with physical violence, *and* I, as a player, need to be ready to commit to the consequences of the move. Both conditions must be met, in other words. The fictional situation must be right, but the player must also be choosing to make the move.

    This really makes sense when I consider some of Vincent's other comments (e.g. "Of course they still roll if the victim is helpless; otherwise you're taking away the player's chance to, for instance, earn XP from a highlighted stat" - not a verbatim quote, note).     From that point of view, a character pulling out a gun and threatening their victim *is* the trigger for the move, and we must "make with the dice".

    If you approach the game from that latter perspective, as I apparently have, that's when you run into this trouble. Under the former paradigm, however, this issue doesn't even come up.

      1) Player decides he wants to Go Aggro. He says "I want to go aggro." "Oh, but what do you do?" "Oh, ". Player intent --> appropriate fiction --> move. The key here is that we reach a meeting point of player mechanical desire and character action, "to do it, do it," concurrently.

      It should look something like:

      Player Triggers Move ---> Reconciliation of Player Desires, Fiction / Consequences
      Fiction Triggers Move---> Reconciliation of ...

      Reconciliation --> Does Fiction Match Player Expectations/Desires? If (N), Proceed to (1). If (Y), Proceed to (2).

      (1) Adjust fiction or player desires to suit desired fictional consequences. Loopback to Reconciliation (communicating adjustment w/ MC).

      (2) Establish fiction, subject to agreed-upon reconciliation w/ player desires. Proceed to (3).

      (3) Move happens, as appropriate.

      Character intent is kind of irrelevant: it might create the fictional requirements, or not, depending on the move. In Go Aggro, the requirement is that the character be committed to violence to change a behavior: it doesn't require that commitment be carefully cerebrated over at length. An unrestrained impulse fits the bill, too. All it asks is that the character resort to aggression.

      The only question here, specific to Go Aggro, is what specific fiction piece has to be made harmonious with player desire in order for us to proceed with the move. And, the key to go aggro is that, as others have stated, it doesn't depend on a character action, so much as a particular fictional fact: that this character has committed to violence. We can argue all day about whether the "action" is "swings his fist" or "commits to using his fist," the key is that there are no more checkpoints for changing his mind: player and character have reconciled that there are no more turn-offs or jug-handles: this character is going to hit somebody.

      It actually doesn't matter whether we describe these things as verbs or intentions, as the moves require them to commit to acting on those intentions - they belong to the same category of fictional elements, separated only by the brief passage of fictional time and the question of whether they are successful in their goal. It's like if we went backwards in time 1/20 of a second before someone took a swing, and grabbed a quick EEG. The initiation of violence is there, in the brain-pan, already existing in the fictional universe. Just because we don't see the fist moving yet, doesn't mean "he's gone aggro" isn't a fictional fact. If we go back another 1/20 of a second, to before the motor cortex has engaged, and jump over to the cerebral cortex (or let's be honest, this is AW - the fucking limbic system is driving everything) where the character has committed to using violence? That's all part of the same move.

      I don't think character "intent" and "commitment" are the same thing. Character intent is a sub-category of commitment: "My character is going to shoot that guy if he gets out of the car, 100%, no doubt," can be a reflection of the character's motives and decisions, or it can be a reflection of his current state of fear and jumpiness. What matters is that the character (and player) aren't choosing the two dozen options that involve not shooting the guy (e.g., running away). Whatever decisions and intentions that character has made, he's committed to using violence.

      \n So, now I'm thinking about how player-facing "moves" are engaged in a game (whether AW or another PbtA game; many will have different needs, of course).

      I've seen different groups do it differently, and I think there are a variety of functional ways to do so. Here are some (all?) of the ways I've seen:

      ---

      First, the preamble. Cosmetically (who talks?):

      A. Players describe what their character says and does, and perhaps their intentions. It's the MC, however, who says when a move should be rolled.

      "Ok, sounds like you're going aggro. Wanna roll it, or do something else?"

      (Vincent had a very nice, economical phrase for this earlier in this conversation: "Cool. Roll it!" That's only three words, and gets us on the same page. Nice.)

      Some people like this approach because they find it immersive, and because it demands that a fictional description for the action happens, every time.

      I cringe a little bit at the lack of player empowerment here, but as long as the decision is seen as open to discussion (i.e. the MC is in charge of saying it, but really it's up to group consensus), it can work for many groups. Feels very familiar for "traditional gamers", as well, if they're coming from a culture of playing games where the GM always calls for rolls.

      B. The player is in charge of announcing when they want to make a move (generally, although anyone can speak up if it seems like they clearly have met the requirements, and have forgotten).

      This is very practical; generally it's the player who's making the decision, so it's the most direct and efficient way to get from point A to point B.

      However, it's also easy for some groups to skip out on talking about the fiction. AW's moves cover the needs pretty well (e.g. 'go aggro' requires you to specify what you're threatening your enemy with), but for some groups that's not descriptive enough, and they feel like they've fallen into a trap, and their gaming is now less detailed.

      (I've seen several groups complain that when they play AW they fall into a pattern of just saying "I go aggro with my gun!" and "I manipulate him, with the promise of a nice meal", skipping much of the narration, and they feel like the game's gotten a bit "thin".)

      Vincent covers this distinction nicely in the thread I link below.

      ---

      Ok, so that's who talks. Fine. But how do we decide when a move is actually to be made? When do we reach for the dice? Whether it's the MC, or the player, or anyone at all, what are the criteria for the decision? That's the real meat of the question.

      I have seen three different, somewhat overlapping ways:


      1. The option to make a move is purely the player's decision.

      I want to 'go aggro', so I do. If necessary, we then ask for additional information ("How do you do that?"), but it's very much my option and my prerogative, as a player in the game. The moves are, effectively, my "buttons" in this game - the things I can do, and doing so is my right.

      I may have to convince others that it's appropriate to the fictional circumstances in some occasions, but the default assumption is that I make my moves when I want to. The fictional description here is not *structural*: it's your responsibility to do so as the player, but it's for our pleasure and interest, not because the game demands it.

      The basic flow of play is:

      I pick a move; the others might ask me to tell them about what my character is doing in the fiction to justify that move.

      If we're talking in terms of IIEE or something like that, its position is fairly loose within the resolution process.

      A perfect example of a move which operates this way by default is "read the sitch". Trying to adjudicate that move by method 2 (below) is messy and awkward.


      If I had to guess, I would say that this is closest to Vincent's vision for AW. (See apocalypse-world.com/forums/index.php?topic=8689.0.)

      What's the catch? It's "if you do it, do it": that suggests that there is a "fictional trigger", and I cannot introduce that fictional material (e.g. threaten someone with a gun) without rolling the move. That complicates matters a little bit, or, at least, requires some clarity for what the "fictional triggers" for each move are.


      2. Moves are strictly triggered by fictional events and/or circumstances.

      This is typically easiest to see with custom moves: "When you drink Dremmer's moonshine, roll+weird..."

      Moves with mechanical or procedural triggers are right here, too ("When your Hx reaches +4, reset and...").

      In this view, every move has a strict "trigger", and when that thing happens, you roll the dice. When X happens in the fiction, we make move Y.

      With Dremmer's moonshine, there's no wiggle room: either you're drinking it or not, and that's that. It's not up to anyone in particular: when this happens, we roll the dice.


      The challenge here is, of course, that not all fictional triggers are as clear as taking a sip of a drink. (And even there we might have some debate about whether just having a little drop does the trick, or whether you have to down a whole cup.)

      In addition, some moves may or may not suit all situations. This depends on how the move is designed, of course. Do the outcomes suit the situation? Do the player decisions? How flexible to interpretation are the outcomes?

      This option has, generally, the clearest position in play in terms of IIEE.


      3. Moves are applied by the group to give the desired outcomes, or force the desired choices, in a particular fictional situation.

      In this view, the move is a procedure we throw into play when it gives us what we want.

      The fiction we're imagining moves forward, and we invent various details and narrate various actions, but we can occasionally go to one of these processes to "disclaim responsibility" or shake things up.

      The basic flow is that we learn the structure and outcomes of the moves, and then call on them when they seem like a good fit for what's happening "on screen". Do the outcomes and choices of this move suit what's happening?

      The moves are almost an artist's brush, in this view, used to punctuate the fiction and to emphasize certain moments in specific ways.

      A perfect example here from AW is the harm move. Most people (from what I've seen) call on it opportunistically. Essentially, it's "Hey! It would be fun to roll for harm right now. Let's do it!" On the other hand, if it seems like it might get in the way or draw attention away from the action, we might skip it altogether this time.

      I find a lot of Monsterhearts' moves work best this way. Here's an example I posted in an earlier thread: one person has dropped their glasses and is scrambling to find them, half-blind. Another person comes forward, and steps on the glasses, crushing them. Although it doesn't really suit the description of either move, we found that "lash out" and "shut someone down" were both great options here. Calling on either move gave mechanical weight to the moment and punctuated the story in a powerful way.


      The issue here, of course, is the lack of clarity. It's a slippery slope from here to just deciding arbitrarily when to roll any given move. Not only in terms of when to call for the move in the flow of play (IIEE, etc), but also whether we do so at all, and which move. That's not going to help the group find their groove or avoid disagreements - at its worst, taken to an extreme, it's unprincipled and unpredictable play operating purely by consensus.)

      \n A clarifying note to keep Story Games accessible to people who don't know jargon: "IIEE" is an old Forge term for "what we must establish before we roll the dice, and what we establish afterwards". For instance, if we "roll to hit", does that mean your character has already swung the sword, or just that she intends to?

      (This matters if, for example, we want to know if another player or character can interrupt the action before it happens.)

For instance, we certainly need some kind of threat or attack to be made, but we also need to know that the player is committing to the potential outcomes, or the flow can be broken and we need a do-over.

      -"I threaten him with my gun!"

      -"I got a 10+"

      -"Well, that's how the move works, sorry. Should we replay this scene?"
      -"Yeah, ok. But I still want to threaten him, I just don't want to be committed to shooting." -"Uhhh..."

      \n We're having a bit of a discussion about move design on G+, and Johnstone said the following:

      "For me, one of the great things about PbtA moves is when they have clear triggers and you can go from description to the dice very quickly. If there's a discussion about "do you have leverage?" or "are you persuasive enough to get them to budge?" that feels too much like setting stakes or discussing what success looks like, at which point you might as well just switch to playing Blades in the Dark (in my opinion)."

      I thought that was quite interesting.

      It pretty well nails one of the things I was trying to get at in this thread: go aggro operates in this funny double space between, "As a player, this is a move you can make when you're willing and able to hurt someone, and there's something you want them to do", and "You're going aggro on the dude? Sweet, roll it."

      I can imagine that, after a few awkward interactions, the players simply internalize the use of the move and avoid making it when it's not appropriate. However, I'd love to have a clearer condition for triggering the move, making play smoother.

      The same thing can happen with the "manipulate" move (i.e. the discussion about whether you have leverage or not), but that's not as much trouble, since it's easy to simply say the move hasn't been made yet, and go on with free play.
