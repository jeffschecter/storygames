  • Ben_Lehman
    February 2006
    Two weeks --

    Well, it summer vacation had just started, and Jasper and I went over to Eli's house to hang out. We had been playing a trans-Dimensional TMNT game on and off, and Eli really wanted to play it again, but we couldn't find Jasper's character sheet. We made a new character for Jasper (a cyborg cat) and Eli found his old guy (an armadillo) and away we went.

    The basic pretense of the game was surprisingly like Dogs, and it was inspired by The Homeward Bounders and Quantum Leap. They were dimensional travellers who went from world to world, either solving the problems of the world or not, and moved on. There were three other forces at work in the world (as I recall: Humanity, Mechanization, Authoritarianism, and Selfishness), and from time to time they ran into agents of those.

    All I needed to figure out for each world was the color and maybe something wrong, and the guys would take it from there. It was pretty easy.

    Like I said, it was the start of summer vacation. We were three fourteen year olds with nothing to do, and Eli's mom would feed us for as long as we wanted to stay there. We fell into a pretty steady cycle of sleep, play, eat, play, sleep, with me calling for breaks every once in a while to think up new ideas for worlds. Sometimes, other friends came by, and we had them bring in guest characters (natives of the world) for a while, and then they'd leave, and we'd move on to a new world.

    It was pretty fun.

    yrs--
    --Ben

    My longest single session without breaks other than for ordering food was a Cold Iron game in college that was about 20 hours or so. 10+ of it was a single encounter, and the reason it ran so long. We sort of didn't realize how late it was and how big the encounter would be. By 1:00 AM (our usual latest ending time), it was too late to stop...

    So the motivation was simply to finish an encounter. The structure that supported that play was a combination of low prep (especially back in college, I often did little or no prep for a session other than possibly pick a module from Dungeon Magazine to use as an inspiration). This particular encounter was not associated with any module (basically a random encounter - though I didn't use random encounter tables). The PCs had lots of buffing magic which can prolong encounters in Cold Iron. I suspect it also would have ran a lot faster if we weren't so dead tired.
