Andrew Kenrick

Over on the Forge I started a thread about an idea had for character creation in my dystopian game, Yesterday's Tomorrow - the idea I had was for the game to begin with the characters being interrogated, and their answers to form character creation, informing attributes and so on.

Is this something that could work? Or am I being too ambitious?

Paul pointed me at Dread, which apparently also uses questions to shape a character, but I was wondering what other games approach character creation in a different way? Best Friends immediately springs to mind, but what others?

    In Crispin Glover is Here to Kill Me:
    Starting with the person to the right of the heaviest smoker in the group, each player will give a short overdubbed narration of their character, as seen through the eyes of Crispin Glover, as he watches his prey drink, smoke, and have a good time. This will establish the baseline of the character and cannot be denied through later narration.
    So, basically, your character is as dynamic as the game makes them except for one piece of narration that you give at the beginning. But it has to be through the eyes of your assassin.

    It's an interrogation. Of course there will be lying, either witting or unwitting, on the part of the person being questioned. But the answers, whether true or false, will establish the at-least-superficial appearances of the characters. The point of the game-mechanics can then be to determine who is allowed to resolve the contradictions in the fiction (i.e., introduce "proof" that someone's lying).

    So an exchange like this...

    "So, Mr. Guildenstern: Were you aware that Etheline Rosenkrantz was an active member of the conspiracy when you made contact with her?"

    "No! I only requisitioned her from the secretarial pool for her stenography skills!"

    ...helps set up a kind of 1950s Organization Man kind of ambience and with respect to the characters establishes that Rosenkrantz is at least suspected of being "an active member of the conspiracy" and Guildenstern may or may not be her accomplice. In terms of "character attributes," it also establishes that Guildenstern is at least a minor bureaucrat or administrative functionary, while Rosenkrantz has mad steno skillz.

    In a heavily narration-based game, such free-form attributes may be all you need.

    I'm reminded of Kirk Mitchell's game about the Inquisition, which was I think supposed to take place wholly within the torture chamber.

    Probably don't need it, but another data point is that I run a HQ scenario I call OtherWorld at cons where the idea is to create characters completely in play. We start play with a blank character sheet, and then I say, "Three figures come over the horizon...what does your character look like?"

    And we fill in the character sheets using the "As You Go" method of chargen from HQ. Basically players make up their characters in response to what's happening. What's interesting is the diversity of characters you get, given the same stimuli given to a group of players.

    What makes it work is presenting the players with the situation, and then having slots for stuff on the character sheet. The players come up with answers to the implicit questions like "What does my character do for a living?" that the Occupation Keyword represents, and "Where does he come from and look like?" from the Homeland (and Species) Keywords.

    So as long as you have a system with some structure that's asking questions, and a situation that gets the imagination going, I've yet to see a player who hasn't been able to come up with a very interesting character in only one scene of play.

    So, no, not too ambitious. Looks like you have precisely what you need already.

    Mike
