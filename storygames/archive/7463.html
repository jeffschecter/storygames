\n I am working a lot with 4E right now and really enjoying the new system, but there is one part that bothers me: Minions. I like the idea of these 1-hit characters that are fodder in limited numbers but can be dangerous in groups. My big problem is differentiating the minions from the main characters/villains during play. Too many times lately I've fought my way through a group of nobodys only to find one of them really hard to kill ("That's not a minion!") for no real reason, or some cool villain dropping in one shot ("Wait, that guy was a minion?")

Is there any clever way, without just coming out and saying it or making the characters diffferent colors, of differentiating between minions and main villains?

ME

    \n Mearls and company covered this during one of the seminars. They recommended making it explicit to the players but dig suggest using rhetorical device to do so, e.g., "These 3 orcs have tattered armor and dented weapons." My group has found that way works best. Also, make sure you use different counters for the different classes of monsters in a given encounter.

    \nTo summarize, what Justin said! Either clearly mark minions as minions or describe them in a manner than makes it very obvious. The DMG also covers this in that D&D; is a game made up of exceptions and "gotcha" scenarios where you thoughts things were one way but because of a power, feat, trap... the normal rules don't apply. In general the designers advise that you always tip the players off and warn them instead of surprising them. Describing a demon's 10 foot aura as potentially damaging, using PCs passive skill checks to auto detect traps, and so on. Since it is a resource game, they want to avoid PCs spending resources in situations where they may feel cheated. Damn, I just spent my daily power on a bunch of minions! Crap!

    \n There is no "supposed to" or "not supposed to". That's been left to individual DM choice.
    \n
    \nBut the way the designers do it is to make sure the players know who is and isn't a minion. As Justin notes, Mearls uses narrative description to highlight the difference between scrubs and non scrubs. But simply placing the minis / tokens down on the table and saying "these are minions" is just fine too.
    \n
    \nThe game was balanced with the idea of players being able to optimize minion attacks vs. non minion attacks, so playing without that knowledge will ramp up the difficulty signficantly. Especially when you start getting into the higher level minions who can actually do some scary stuff.

    \n Related from DMG 4E, page 26-27:
    \n
    \n
    DMG 4E, page 26-27 wrote:
    \n
    Informing Players
    \nAll the information the players need to make their choices comes from you. Therefore, within the rules of the game and the limits of PC knowledge, Insight, and Perception, tell players everything they need to know. You don\u2019t have to reveal all aspects of a situation or hazard in one go. You should, however, give enough information that the players know what\u2019s up and have an idea what to do\u2014and what not to do.
    \n
    \n\u201cGotcha!\u201d Abilities: Pay attention to monster abilities that change the basic rules and tactics of combat, and give players the cues they need to recognize them. Describe the ability as it might appear in the game world, and then describe it in game terms to make it clear.
    \n
    \nFor example, if the characters are fighting a pit fiend, whose aura of fire deals fire damage to creatures within 5 squares, you might tell the players (before their characters come in range), \u201cThe heat emanating from the devil is intense even at this distance. You know that getting within five squares of it is going to burn you.\u201d
    \n
    \nGame States, Conditions, and Effects: Since PC abilities can sometimes hinge on a game state, condition, or effect that affects their opponent, make it clear to the players how their enemies are doing. Be descriptive, considering the source of the condition, but also be explicit.

    \n OK, cool, works fine for me--back to the OP, then? I can now offer ideas:

    * Get some of the smallish, cheap plastic poker chips (I'm sure there's 1" diameter ones) and stack the bosses (or various classes) on top, leaving mooks without chips. Three or four colors should cover all of your needs, not to mention being able to trivially stack up to three, for "ranking", in addition to color. Note that this might not be as cool for doublebase, quadbase (but are there even any large/gigantic minions?).

    * Trick from the Pirates TCG: use colored braces rubber bands to indicate specialness/uberness--just hang themon whatever's convenient: head, horns, weapon, upraised arm. This technique is also cool for marking and cursing.

    * Again, influenced by Pirates TCG (and a bit of Mechaton thrown in) use TINY d6s to indicate whatever. You have both color and numbers 1-6 to use for differentiation.

    * Legos, man... Legos. Use small square plates in a manner similar to the chips above (and morre scalable, if there's larger minions or classes). You can use Pick-a-Brick of Digital Designer to just buy the ones you know you'd most-often use (or to color customize or whatever).

    * For that matter, as soon as you introduce using alternate figures in general (i.e. other than actual minis), then I can verify that Icehouse pyramids offers a LOT of flexibility, once you have about three or four Treehouse sets (link). And the bonus is that you can use small beads or tiny dice UNDER the transparent pyramids, for mark or curse.

    * If you're definitely going with minis, maybe try using those little colored dot stickers, or even Post-It flags (link) stuck to the base or to the figure back or wherever.

    And I'm spent, though I'm sure there's a bajillion other, cheap ways if you wander a crafts store with an open mind....

    \n I've been "playtesting" 4e with my group several times (whenever we fall below critical #'s for regular game and once when scoping out next 4e campaign) and have been not naming "here's a minion" but describing creatures in ways pretty clearly dileniating them, "these are decrepit looking skeletons, but that one looks fierce and deadly", "the bandits look like ill-kempt rabble who are only really a threat because of their numbers and surprise, it's that halfling slinger who ordered the attack who looks deadly", "these kobolds keep looking to the ones in the ornate armour for reassurance".

    That's been fine with group, the ones most up on 4e are quickly identifying the minions.

    I don't however reveal when the perception check notes the bushes are concealing something that the something is a minion. The one nod perhaps to simulationism is that you need to get a look at the creature/see them in action/interaction to assess them.

    I would also not be above a tricky villain "dressing up some minions as menaces" but still let Perception or other checks reveal the hoax.
