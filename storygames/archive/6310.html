\n Quick question for you. I've cross-posted this in a few places, but especially wanted to get some more small-press type feedback.

Are you entering in the ENnies this year? As a new judge this year, its been curious to see the companies that have and have not entered (thus far, I should say). I know some publishers see a benefit or do it for fun, and some don't see the point. I'd just like to hear your thoughts on it. I'm an ENnies judge, not the organizer, so you aren't going to hurt my feelings. I'd just like to hear some honest feedback about if you are entering anything this year, why or why not, and what might happen to change that.

    \n I can't really speak to this year, but I chose not to submit Push 1 (a collection of short essays and games) last year for a number of reasons:

    1) There wasn't really a good category for it, aside from "Game Supplement" which didn't really fit.
    2) Small press games that do well in the ENnies have generally been those that look and, in some cases, feel more like traditional roleplaying products.
    3) The cost of submitting is pretty high, contributing 6+ free copies of a product for an outside chance of making the finals.

    That said, I suspect that small press, non-traditional games stand a good chance of doing fairly well this year, since it's the calm right before the D&D4 deluge. Also, I think non-traditional games are increasingly becoming more visible and known among mainstream roleplaying audiences, with more traditionally looking/feeling products like Burning Wheel/Empires and Spirit of the Century leading the way. Honestly, even though I don't have a product to submit this year, I think the success of small press games in "mainstram" roleplaying awards like the ENnies has and can continue to be an important part of that process.

    \n I don't have anything to submit this year, but having had an incredible experience when I did submit to the 2006 ENnies, I have a couple cents I'd like to toss into the discussion. The judges for the ENnies are selected by popular vote. These folks are folks that people listen to, in and out of the context of the ENnies. When you submit to the ENnies, you put your work in front of these judges. Even if you don't win, even if you aren't nominated, if any of these five judges like what they see, they'll talk about it and people will listen. People who you may never have reached otherwise. It's far better to win over a judge than it is to win the ENnie itself.
