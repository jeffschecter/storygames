\n So, first post. Sorry it's about something I did rather than adding to the existing discussion, but I don't have anything else to contribute to the discussion just now, and I don't think posting just to post is a good idea. That said, here goes: I'm writing a wuxia genre Apocalypse World hack, and I'd appreciate any feedback. So far all I have is the basic moves, none of which are wildly original, but which I think have some interesting twists. It's playable at this stage, something I am immensely proud of as a newbie* game designer. Here 's a link to the gdoc.

The biggest twists on the basic mechanics so far are based on strings, from Monsterhearts, and power, from the first version of Just Heroes.

In the first case, I took the idea of strings as points representing a social hold over other characters pretty much wholesale, but I've expanded on it in a manner that I think is pretty significant. In order to represent the networks of relationships that are so important in Chinese culture as a whole (at least according to my reading) and in Confucian philosophy in particular, I'm adding the option to spend five strings to establish a permanent "tie." A character can use strings on anyone they are connected to through the network of ties, though each degree of separation increases the cost of a string action.

With the power mechanic, I used the concept to points gained by specific actions and used both to improve short term circumstances and gain advances, but adjusted the advancement mechanic. I modeled my implementation on Hx in Apocalypse World, with the stat I'm calling cosmic breath added to certain rolls, but resetting and granting XP every time a fourth point is earner

Altogether, while I know I don't have anything terribly original yet, I think I've got a solid platform to build more content on.

Opinions?

*The only other thing I've done is Adrift, an AW powered storygame which I worked on with this site's own inestimable Dani and which needs more playtesting.

    Without a read, I can still offer you one piece of advice, though: Be very, very careful about using strings in a Wuxia hack. Strings work best when the PCs are constantly in conflict with each other, which may not be as common in your hack as it is in MonsterHearts.

    \n So, "Go Aggro", and it being a basic move says that AW is a place where people can use violence to get results, but also that using violence to get results is, on some level, ceding power to the person you're threatening. It says that using violence to get results is going to be complicated and messy.

    When I was reading your moves before, Sway with Threats was unfinished and it looked like it was going to come out close to the "Go Aggro" move. Since then, you've clearly made it your own thing: Whereas the use of violent threats in the Apocalypse World is likely to get messy, the use of violent threats in Wuxia World interacts with cosmic breath. How do you see that working in the fiction of the game?

    I think Horn Head is absolutely right about choosing basic moves to shape the world/setting you're going for. For instance, my own hack is thematically similar to Avatar and Fullmetal Alchemist: Young people mastering their skills as they explore a world suffering from war.

    Here's my own list of basic moves:
    Stone Fist: Train and Practice, Fight in a Stone Fist Stance
    River Serpent: Dance Past Danger, Act with Stealth, Fight in a River Serpent Stance
    Dragon's Belly: Bare your Soul, Fight in a Dragon's Belly Stance
    Paper Crane: Spin clever words, Fight in a Paper Crane Stance
    Eye of Heaven: Study a Moment, Study a Person, Fight in an Eye of Heaven Stance

    Notice that I've dropped Go Aggro and Seize by Force (which don't really fit the way violence works in my world), while still having a bunch of moves that communicate how violence does work (different martial styles, which mean that there's always a form of combat tied to a character's good stat. Also, my version of AW's special moves are triggered not by sex, but by characters dueling each other)

    I've also ditched Open Your Brain while adding in Train and Practice and Bare your Soul. Because OYB is about learning new information about the world. Because this hack isn't one where the characters learn about the weirdness of the world. Its one where they learn about themselves and about each other--and so my basic moves reflect that, while directing the characters to participate in the sorts of scenes that occur regularly in the fiction I'm trying to emulate.

    The point here is that your basic moves help define your world. When the players aren't sure what to do, this is the list they'll be looking at (especially if you use highlighted stats). So choose a set of basic moves that gets you what you want.
