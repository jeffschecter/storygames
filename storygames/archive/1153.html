    August 2006
    Podcasting is fun, inexpensive, effective, and laborious. Expect to spend three times as much time preparing a podcast as it is long, minimum.

    You're going to want a decent headset mike at the very least. A mike with a pop filter and a good headset work even better.

    On Windows, use Audacity; on Mac, use Garageband. Both software suites are free, easy to use, and give you all the functionality you need.

    Okay...

    You're not talking about podcasting.

    You're just talking about recording and playing back. That's a WHOLE HECK OF A LOT simpler.

    First of all, you don't need a computer. All you need is a digital voice recorder. There are a number of them that will record to an SD card. A one-gig card will handle a whole day's worth of gaming.

    I've got one in my camera that I've used to record sessions, but you can get dedicated devices for cheap:

  • Vaxalon
    August 2006
    Yeah, definitely. You can do everything you need with a crappy condenser mic (like they have on digital voice recorders) but if you want to record a game session for public consumption you really really ought to have decent gear.

    To do it RIGHT, everyone would have their own headset plugged into a mixer board and recorded on a separate track so you can independently boost people who can't be heard as well. You can get away with just recording it all onto one track but then you'll have to make sure that all the people around the table know that they have to talk into the mic all the time and that's a drag.

    And uploading to a website isn't QUITE podcasting. You'd also either have to learn RSS to code up the XML feed, or else subscribe to a service like libsyn.com that will do it for you (they're real real cheap).

    The book I cited can tell you about all that business.

    By asking here.

    I've recordedsessions of our games with my iRiver 795, a little mp3 player with a built-in mic. They're starting to get hard to find, but you can pick them up for about $75. (The 795 is the 512MB model; there are similar 700-series models with different amounts of memory.)

    You can find links to all of our Polaris sessions, which I recording with this handy-dandy little thing, here: http://www.havegameswilltravel.net/index.php?post_id=42877. Note that Session 1 isn't an actual session, but a recap of the session we didn't record. Session 2A is the first one that uses the iRiver.

    I edit using Audacity, an open source program that's fairly simple to use. All I do with those recordings is Compress and Normalize to fix the levels.

    Does that help?

    --Paul

    The iRiver's native recording format is an mp3. You do need to use their program to move stuff on and off, as it doesn't support the USB Mass Storage protocol, which means you have to use a Windows machine to talk to it.

    I spend spend essentially no time at all fixing the recordings. Unlike Matt Snyder's Nine Worlds game he ran via Skype, I don't edit the sessions. As I said, I run a Compressor filter and then a Normalize filter to even out the highs and lows in volume, but that's it.

    No one in my gaming group ever has a problem with being too quiet. And the iRiver's mic is pretty sensitive.
