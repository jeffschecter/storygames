    \nIvan: What parts in particular are you missing? This version of the text is mostly about conveying the rules to an outside audience and less about being evocative in every paragraph, which I thought was more important for people to actually play the game. The evocative stuff is likely to creep back in in later versions.

    Dave: The game doesn't actually subscribe to a strict Buddhist or Indic cosmology, but a mixed or "mythopoetic" one. The "saints" are meant to imply Catholic saints (likewise "demons") and the character descriptions are intended to feel alien and strange, even in a pseudo-religious context. If I had to explicitly describe the theology of the game, it proposes that there is a higher, stranger spiritual world that all human religions describe only partially or imperfectly. In that sense, the Buddhist elements are most like those in syncretic religions such as Yi Guan Dao or Cao Dai, prominent but not the only thing going on.

    P.S. Thanks for the typos. I'm collecting them (which'll teach me to make corrections in InDesign).

    P.P.S. Since I didn't actually answer your question: Those Who Tarry are irreparably destroyed at the end of the game, sacrificing themselves to bring about the world to come. Honestly, I feel like this is a logical though pseudo-Christian-y (martyrdom) extension of bodhisattvas' dedication to saving others, given that this is a very millennialist and apocalyptic take on things.

    \n@jw Thanks for clearing that up. I see where you are coming from a bit better now. It also got me reading the Wikipedia summaries about traditional teachings on the kalpas and the cycle of creation and destruction, which is an aspect of Buddhist thought I hadn't previously encountered in any details. There's some wild stuff in there.

    One interesting thing to me is that the Judeo-Christian take on this would be that it's all more or less literally true, whereas in the Buddhist perspective, the whole thing would likely be a way of talking about the nature of experience in a gigantic, somewhat impenetrable metaphor that fries your brain if you think about it too hard. And these can both be true at once. I like that! I also like the use of 8, and the fact that The Assassin who destroys the world is a bodhisattva. (Arguably he/she should really be a wrathful manifestation of the Buddha-nature, but that's being picky. It's the right idea, to me at least.) I'd still make a pitch for including hungry ghosts; they just seem such a good fit for subway stations. But whatever. Great job. My only problem will be finding people crazy enough to play it!
