For all that people get worked up about GNS, Creative Agenda isn't that fucking important. It's important that everyone around the table is pushing for the same kind of thing, but if they're not, it doesn't necessarily mean your game will explode. It doesn't matter that much WHAT you're all pushing for, really, or if there's a goddamned name for it, as long as you are pushing for something all in roughly the same ballpark.

As far as I can remember from my lurking days way back in Forge history, Creative Agenda came about because of clashes in play styles. Some people were pushing for X and other people were pushing for Y, and it wasn't a difference they knew how to fix. Some groups seem to mix X and Y and Z in their games and no one gets frustrated, bored, or mad. But other groups do implode when these "agenda clashes" occur, and the theory sprang up to explain what the fuck was going on.

In the end, the GNS categories might turn out to be incomplete or totally fucking arbitrary. Me, I think they're descriptive. Whatever. It doesn't matter. They're just some labels people slapped on different kinds of play they were seeing. They tried to use these labels to predict different things about these kinds of play, and use those predictions to make different kinds of games. To that end, GNS theory has been pretty successful.

What happens if we can't identify GNS in actual play? Nothing. Nothing at all. Is your game fun? Yeah? Awesome. No? What are you pushing for in the game? What makes you happy in the game? What about all your friends? Are you all on the same page? No? That's likely the problem right there. You're on the same page, but the game still explodes? Do the rules support what you and your friends want out of the game? No? Maybe that's the problem. There: that's all you need to know about Creative Agenda theory.

    \n I think one of the issues that comes up with GNS, or socket theory, or any other theory that has genre types or labels that can be attached to things (in other words, most theories) is that they're awfully easy to tie into identity politics of the pernicious sort. Even when (usually when?) the original thrust of the theory wasn't about that at all.

    I think it was McLuhan who said "People these days want roles (things to be) instead of goals (things to do)." And I think a lot of GNS talk falls into that kind of category. Rather than talking about games, and the games we play, as ongoing processes -- things that we're doing that are in motion and fluid -- we find it easier to talk about them in terms of set categories and roles. Thus we focus less on the ongoing decisions and overall arcs of games that influence agenda, and want to label the game as THIS THING and then make every moment of the game fit into the role of THIS THING THAT THIS GAME IS.

    If you let GNS be genres that talk about the overarching goals and trended patterns of play, they're perfectly useful. But the "what role is this game/this player/this one moment" game is often much less useful. At that point it isn't about analyzing and understanding, its about finding a box that the game can be put safely into so that it can be contained and quantified.

    Which is something we've been on about for years, really. From the "There is no "narrativist gamer" damnit" discussion to the "Narrativism is a way of playing, not a text without players" and on and on...

    \n My perspective as a player and aspiring designer is to play games that support the kind of play I enjoy. I know I like fairly deep exploration of character, especially when I'm reacting to some kind of morally charged scenario.

    As a kid, I loved playing my demon-summoner in rounds of Everway; that gave us our first crack at in-character vs. out of character information, and we grew because of it. My goal was to try and portray someone good who used highly suspect means to do those good things, and it created some nice tension and distance among the PCs. But that was me being a Prima Donna - it was all about *me* contributing this to the story, and the other players more or less playing X-Men with their cool powers.

    In college, I played a campaign of Exalted from start to finish as an Abyssal, one who grew disillusioned by the infighting between Deathknights and decided, in the last scene of the last session, to turn and join the Solars on the other side of the battle so that at least my guy would be fighting for something that someone *else* believed in, too. I had an audience of one - another player who was running an Abyssal, and had to settle for getting my jollies that way. That game, honestly, was mostly mind-numbing combats and railroaded "plot developments", and it was only us Abyssals that seemed to have any emotional investment whatsoever in the game, so it was only when the action was on us did it seem like any kind of story was going on.

    That's what I was looking for, and I rely, in part, on GNS now to help me find the kinds of games that might support such play. Crunch gets in my way, but arguably there are different kinds of it: there's mechanical crunch, yeah, but I'd say there's such a thing as setting-crunch, too: a measure of how much intricate detail is provided in the rulebook to explain the setting the developers envision.

    These are the issues that I have with game design, what I'm looking for, and what I'm trying to avoid. GNS has helped me a lot with that, as have various "hippie" techniques like player authority and conflict resolution, which aren't part of GNS per se, despite their proximity to that community.

    \n Adam,
    It's pretty straightforward - I look at the attention to tactics, to "getting it right", and to theme or story, and then see how the game goes after those things. I care about these things in the order of N, S, G, with the latter two running a distant second. A little flavor or a well-written motif that makes something feel very [whatever the genre or setting is] can be a lot of fun, but it doesn't compare with interesting mechanics that cover escalation of conflict, personal motivations, moral dilemmas, etc. Stuff that doesn't reference at all *who* the person is or what they care about, well, such rules are things I'd tear right out of the book. At this point, I'm starting to care a lot less about Ron's (once-upon-a-time) apparently ironclad insistence that Premise had to be a ... thing, in our games, if we were going to do Story Now. The way that whole essay is framed (and yeah, it's a few years old) is such that you must have a Premise, capital P, like it's some kind of separate thing.

    I think my problem has been that I've been misreading Ron. The idea is probably more like "put a moral conflict or quandary right there into the setting or situation, or make rules that create characters who get into such situations", and then character creation, followed by play, are when the players do things to indicate their response to that quandary. Premises are rarely specific as-given in the game text; "what will you do for power?" is incredibly! generalized, so it doesn't get in our way. Ugh, I'm glad that this thread has helped me to see that. It didn't help that the Forge has that term "Timid Virgin", which is a fine concept but puts a huge emphasis on getting Nar play "right", which then sets one up to try and read the mind of an internet article-writer and yearn for some exact photocopy of whatever it was he "meant".     We can see agonistic play as it happens and we can see mimetic play as it happens; can we see thematic play as it happens?       One of the things that makes this tricky is the story-logic, rather than physics-logic or fairness-logic, that is used in many Story Now games. We derive different amounts of meaning based on our investment in something; if we are told by the protagonist (in a bit of time-hopping voiceover) that he is dead now, and relating the end of his life to us, then we are biting our nails waiting for the moment when BAM he dies. If it were, then individual sessions of the game would only be pleasurable and exciting as development towards that goal, but this is clearly not the case - the game is played as one-shots plenty enough, campaigns are left unfinished and in general people play the game in units of individual sessions, not campaigns. I get many more actual play reports about individual towns (and how awesome their stories were) than I get about complete cycles of individual dogs; I get the latter as well, but obviously not as many of them. Compare with traditional fantasy adventure rpgs, for which the reports are about long-term developments that take several sessions to play out; players expect such a campaign to only really begin around the third session or so.

      This is totally a matter of individual experience, for all I know the people you play with genuinely consider the campaign cycle primary in the game and don't care that much about the individual stories of the individual towns. Just discussing my experience here. If you'd like, you can think of it this way: the game's town content "lures" us to play the game for long enough for the character arc to emerge, at which point we start caring about that more and more, so that in the end we have a solid story of an individual character instead of an unrelated set of town-stories. Which you consider more important is unimportant in the sense that they both are clearly rewarding, and in harmony with each other.
