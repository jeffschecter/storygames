So first, a little background on me:

When I walk into a game, I generally have a character idea that I can summarise in a few words... "flying poet-baker", "joan-of-arc mechanic", "charming Mentat", &c. It does me precious little good to hang Flags, situations, links to the larger setting, and stuff off such a character; I will often forget that such things are even there and when encountering them in play, they come as a surprise, and sometimes my interest has shifted away and they slide off unnoticed.

Then these characters gradually crystallise in play, or play doesn't last long enough for the character to harden and start to 'work'.

So this has led me to turn away from games with a lot of character generation, or to get skittish around it. Ask Jonathan how long it took me to write my Power of Brilliance!

Can I get chargen to work for me?

    Historically, (and I may be inventing a lot of this history, in which case please consider it a charming alegory) chargen served a strategic purpose. In particularly hack-n-slashy games, where a lot of the meat of the game was overcoming a challenge, you settled up front what your character was and was not. So mechanical balance was really important, in order to provide the basis of the strategic challenge at chargen, and as the foundation for the challenge of play. You could even say that a lot of the diseases we see in older fantasy games are a natural and necessary result of framing the games that way.

    But the analogy I'd make is that, for there to be a _*game*_ to it, you do need some kind of challenge. That could be a strategic challenge, or it could be a narrative challenge. And laying out, committing to "enough" of your character ahead of time is a challenge in and of itself. Then the character you've committed to is the foundation for narrative challenge as the game goes on.

    Chargen is by no means the only way to establish challenge in any RPG, but it's a means that's tested by time, and if you take a game that builds it's challenge on chargen and then strip that out, you're changing the activity from game to toy, IMO.

    There's long-since been a DIP (Develop in Play) vs DAS (Develop at Start) argument between gamers -- once that like many rpg-related political arguments, tends to only get acknowledged by one side (in this case, the DIP guys).

    Personally, I tend to like a lot of DAS--it sets up situations and reactions, and front-loads negotiation rather than forcing you to alternate in narrative play between "narrative play" and "negotiation" (and I do consider it a weakness of games like PTA, at least for me, that they often require a lot of negotiation during narrative play) or mix them. But it's nice to have -some- in-play development; ideally, pre-"game" development should provide vectors which in play continue to move (like Fate in an ideal Everway game) rather than nailing you down to something that may end up being less than ideal in play or slowing down the start of play.

    In a similar way, I far prefer pre-written larps to "players create their characters" larps -- because the former allows a huge amount of frontloaded situation, allowing a 4 hour game with enoumous numbers of things getting resolved and multiple plotlines to climax. By contrast, a "player create their characters" larp will tend to have the first 4-20 hours of any given player's play involve meeting and developing situations with other players (admitedly, such a game will usually be at least partially "adventure style" so situations will be introduced externally...but you still lack the richness of having established complications with other primary PCs), and only then can you really engage in the kind of play you can get in a pre-written larp right away.

    Naturally, a tabletop rpg, having a much faster information velocity (ie, as soon as you say something it's part of the game text, whereas in a 20 person game, something might only become part of the text 15 minutes or an hour after you start it because of the need for the information to hit everyone it involves directly), can bootstrap this stuff much faster, but having at least -some- info frontloaded can be very useful.
