    \n I would have "yes" and "no" only.

    Logically, "and" and "but" are synonyms. Both introduce an additional statement with the same truth value as the original clause. Linguistically, they're not: "but" implies a contrary expected emotion between the two clauses.

    My preference is to separate the y/n from generation of additional elements. I'm skimping on the skill rolls (instead, I'm having lots of "let it ride", lots of using passive values (in 5e, that means "assuming a 10 was rolled"), lots of "play it out instead of rolling", lots of "paying in time instead of successes") but have put encounter checks (whether or not monsters arrive) center stage. I usually use a 1/5 chance but there can be advantage or disadvantage on that roll.

    \n If you have tested the mechanic that you are going to implement with these dice go ahead and buy nice engraved dice (I personally hate to use homemade stuff that wears out quickly and looks amateur and I'm completely unable to craft high quality dice so I can understand that others prefer getting high quality dice instead of crafting their own).

    This being said, I strongly recommend you to try first the mechanic you are going to implement with these dice because you may regret expending too much money in such dice after a while.

    Some years ago I would have advocated for the 6 outcomes (that I discovered while reading FU). There is some elegance in the fact that they are a product (i.e. {Yes, No} \xd7 {But, ___, And}) that fits perfectly in a a D6.

    Nowadays I must recognize that, as "universal outcomes" they might be more granular than needed (what's the exact difference between Yes and Yes And?) because it is often difficult to come out with 6 different outcomes that make sense for a given situation (as @2097 and @Paul_T have already expressed).



    For a while I've tested a simplified version that later appeared in Blades in the Dark. Roll Xd6, keep the highest result and read:\n
    • 6 - Yes
      • \n
    • 4,5 - Yes, But...
      • \n
    • 1,2,3 - No, And...
    \nAs you can see, the "Yes, And..." has disappeared because you are already getting whatever you want from the situation. The "No" has disappeared to avoid "lets keep trying" situations, every roll must have consequences. Finally, "Yes, But..." and "No, But..." were too similar to keep both.

    The focus is on maintaining the things in movement and avoiding outcomes that lead to dead ends. The 50%-50% Yes-No balance is maintained but there is a little negative shift to compensate the "keep the best result of a D6 pool" that works well in practice.



    More recently I've arrived to the conclusion that tossing 3 coins to get the following 4 outcomes is simpler and as universal as the {Yes, No} \xd7 {But, ___, And} method:\n
    • HHH - You narrate your complete success
      • \n
    • HHT - The opposition narrates your partial success
      • \n
    • HTT - You narrate your partial failure
      • \n
    • TTT - The opposition narrates your total failure
    \nHere, the fact that you get to narrate the 2 outcomes that are "twisted" in your favor helps a lot to find a good continuation for the story.

    If you don't feel like tossing coins you can always map the outcomes to a D6 with similar results:\n
    • 6 - You narrate your complete success
      • \n
    • 4,5 - The opposition narrates your partial success
      • \n
    • 2,3 - You narrate your partial failure
      • \n
    • 1 - The opposition narrates your total failure
    \n


    So, basically, there are many variations (FU, Blades in the Dark, The 3 Coins System...) on the basic {Yes, No} \xd7 {But, ___, And} idea and you may regret buying expensive dice that implement "the wrong one".
