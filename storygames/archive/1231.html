  • Vaxalon

    The secret is to integrate the dice into play.

    Here's an exercise: Play one game of Dogs where EVERY interaction must be in the context of the conflict mechanic, part of a see-and-raise. It's not easy at first but it can be done. Sometimes the stakes get kind of silly, but it trains you to look at every interaction as a possible diced conflict. Once it becomes second nature to pull out the dice, the "bump" won't seem so jarring.

    In the latest revision of Verge (updated last night!), a player just says "Conflict!" when he can't get his way through negotiation or at an acceptable token cost for an "override."

    This happens when one player has made a change to the setting (as recorded on paper on a big crazy network of setting elements) and the player who owns the setting elements that are being altered raises an objection. Then the player suggesting the change can force it by paying a token cost, which the protecting player can raise with his own tokens. At any point, though, the changing player can just say "Conflict!" and rely on the dice mechanics for ultimate resolution.

    Essentially, once negotiation and token tricks fail, the player who doesn't like it must "say yes, or roll the dice."

He came from a mostly Call of Cthulhu/Unknown Armies "avoid the dice" kind history, and we'd find lots of verbal sparring and blocking which was really frustrating to me, because Dogs relies strongly on "Say yes or roll the dice" to prevent blocking.

    I usually look at a situation like that and ask the player, "Do you want to make that a conflict?" and take the last exchange as the initial raise/see or whatever fits the game. And, if the player says no, I usually cut the scene, since it's clear it's not going to really go anywhere. It's a good way to avoid falling into the classic "party argument" habits from games without social resolution.

    Usually, I find if someone isn't willing to cut the scene, but also unwilling to engage system, it's often a habit picked up from avoiding system, and they will often try to smooze or use force of personality to get what they want. They want something, but they're not willing to put the money on the table, as it were.

    I think Warren and Chris are totally right about scene framing and how this interacts with conflict resolution. The more crisp and targetted your scene framing, the less "wandering around free play" there is, and it's a lot easier to target the conflict and dive right at it. I struggle with this a lot, myself. I have old-skool skills that say, "Let play develop organically, then, when conflict naturally appears, engage the resolution system." This play style can work with a good CR mechanic, but in my experience, a game like Nine Wordls, Dogs, or Dust Devils plays waaaaay better if you play with really sharp and aggressive scene framing.

    Think of free-play as this rolling wavefront (this is Ron's analogy, btw). The game rolls along, people say things, the GM cuts from location to location, etc. As you go, conflicts arise and "pop" out of this rolling wavefront of fiction. Then the wavefront has to pause (this is the jarring transition), you have to pick up on the conflict that popped out, take out your dice, etc. Depending on the mechanic, the fiction wave might start up again as you engage the mechanic (like in Dogs) or it may not. Either way, there's a feeling like you have to "get rolling again" and out of the freeze-frame that the conflict put on your wave.

    (Quick aside: I've seen some players who get very impatient with this. They sort of bounce in their seat like, "C'mon, c'mon... let's get this over with and get back to the game. When to a player who is used to meaty CR methods, the conflict resolution part is the game. This can lead to some friction.)

    Think of scene framing like making a pizza. We make the dough and sauce and put the toppings on and bake it, all so we can eat that pizza with those toppings. Will it be good? Will it be bad? We have to eat it and find out. Making the dough is not eating the pizza, but we have a goal in mind when we're making the dough. We know that soon we will be eating the pizza. Just making dough and slicing pepperoni isn't the point, and we're not going to keep doing that over and over, hoping a pizza will just happen. We have a goal and a method to get there before we even start.

    Conflict is eating the pizza. By framing the scene in a way that points to conflict as our goal, then we are already most of the way there. Conflict happens inside the scene, and is relevant to the parameters of the scene -- "the stuff inside the film frame" as Ron likes to say. The specific nature of the conflict might be discovered as the scene develops (like suddenly deciding to add pineapple to your pizza), but conflict with the scene elements is already a target for us when the scene is framed. We don't have to wonder if we're going to eat pizza at all -- that's why we're in the kitchen in the first place.

    Those metaphors got stretched a bit, so I hope that all makes sense. "Free play with popping conflicts" is how a lot of my play ends up going, so I sympathize with the transitional gear-grinding issue. There are a few good ways to deal with it, as mentioned in the thread already. But another thing to try is to ditch the idea of free-play completely. Try some hardcore scene-framing, targetted at conflict, embedded in the parameters of the scene. It works like magic in PTA and Dogs, and I imagine the same goes for Dust Devils.
