    \n One of the things I did with my GC entry "Home Sweet Home" and am doing with Bullseye is exploring alternate [working term] "fluid attributes". So, for HSH, is replaced hit points with, essentially, faith points. Conflicts arise that put your faith at risk and the damage comes off your faith points. If you run out of faith points, you retire the character, i.e., character "death". For my Gen Con Bullseye game, I'm doing 24 and using Time as the fluid attribute. So, the PCs can't be hurt or killed, but they can run out of time before the suitcase nuke goes off. Playtests so far have proven very exciting, as changing the focal attribute changes the focus of play as one might expect. In the latter example, the PCs don't worry about getting killed, they worry about taking too long. This leads to more exicting attempted maneuvers.

    \n My crazy idea is to
    1. Gestalt every class with the Monk. This includes all NPC classes. [i]Everyone[/i] knows Kung Fu.
    2. All PCs and NPCs heal all nonlethal damage after any fight.
    3. Introduce a conflict mechanic: Say Yes or Kung Fu Fight. PCs can set any stake that does not alter their character's effectiveness. (So no staking "I find a +5 sword"). Stakes are resolved by whoever is the last man standing. If PCs disagree, they fight each other, too, until everyone standing agrees, or "gives".

    So, want to win the heart of the princess? She'll love you forever, but only if you best her Iron Kick. Want to convince the town guard to let you through the gates after dark? Shame them and their pathetic Tiger Stance, and they'll be too dishonored to stand in your way!

    \n Thanks for the compliment! I find Iron Heroes to be a particularly appealing D&D variant for the kung fu idea. Regarding long battles, I agree it can get kinda bad at high levels.

    My other crazy D&D idea isn't quite so radically crazy.

    1. A mystical explosion rocks a major town, destroying a city block and everyone inside.
    2. The party are spirits of people who died in this explosion who have refused to pass on, and through shared strength gained the power to inhabit the corpses of those who died in the explosion.
    3. Every character has an "agenda"; the thing that needs doing that makes his spirit cling to the physical plane.
    4. No member of the party can find rest until every partymember's "agenda" has been settled.
    5. A player gets +5 (level dependent) to any d20 roll (skill check, attack roll) that directly advances his personal agenda.
    6. The PCs do not have their original bodies, and the bodies they do have access too are obviously dead. This complicates interactions with humans.
    7. Anytime a PC is reduced to 0 hp, he is destroyed, but will awake in a new body back at the graveyard within minutes.

    \n I once ran a D&D campaign set in an all-humanoids city. It was D&D meets Thieves World with liberal splashes of Vlad Taltos. The founders of the city were several "evil" races who were tired of being attacked by adventurers every time they went out looking for food, so they went to the ass end of the world and founded their own village. Of course, their city turned out to be right on top of some ruins and became an important port, so it grew. Then the drow came up and claimed a portion of the city. And a mind flayer started a gang. And that gang ended up fighting another gang of orcs who inhabited the flooded portion of the city and ran around with boots of water walking...

    I guess most of this has been done before and can be found within d20 Modern, but this was 15 years ago, so it was pretty unique stuff then.

    \n For those with a Pyramid subscription, Steven Marsh's The Last Year Of Your Lives campaign idea always struck me as very, very awesome. In short:
    * Something happens to you & your friends (the group)
    * Because of this, you folks become truly unkillable. Period.
    * ...Except that in one year's time, to the day that this happened, you all will live no more. Period. No resurrections, reincarnations, take-backs, mulligans or do-overs.
    What do you do with this year?

    I mean, dude, what do you do with your live, when you have something better than most D&D-style immortality *and* you know you only have one year left to live? Dude.

    Posted By: Judd
    - It might be neat if you could get rid of hit points as a resource in order to DO something but I'm not sure what or how.
    \nFeats/powers. Bonuses to a roll, up to some cap. Adventure-style edits and declarations. Hit points are the degree to which you can say what happens to your character, usually in a specific fashion. Alternate uses would be different ways to influence your character or the world. You can take cues from any other game where similar things are permitted.

    I think D20 Star Wars let you spend hit points for force powers, and they had super-cool alternate HP rules I've seen in a few places: Hit points gained from increased levels specifically have nothing to do with physical integrity. Thus, when you lose them, you still haven't been "hit," and you can recover them swiftly between scenes. If there had been more ways to spend them for luck and bonuses to rolls and the like, I might have been happy with it as a representative of Star Wars gaming...

    Spycraft (and Stargate, based on it) had stunt dice, which were a resource that were used to do all kinds of interesting things, like confirm criticals and define gadgets.

    Someone who's played Buffy could say something about...whatever those points were called. Fate? Cool?
