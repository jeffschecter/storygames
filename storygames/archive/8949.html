\n I came to a realization lately, after listening to The Walking Eye podcast play my game, Misspent Youth. It confirmed something I'd been thinking about for a long time, that I've changed as a GM. I recently tried to run Mouse Guard and had it reconfirmed for me.

I can't be the guy who is responsible for saying what's next all the time. When I'm one of the people who gets to do that, I'm super-excited and contributing lots of cool shit. But when I'm in a situation where I am always the one people are looking to for "what's next," I get drained.

Thank god for Primetime Adventures. I had run a bunch of games as a GM--including my own--and wasn't having any fun but whenever I ran PTA I enjoyed myself immensely. PTA was like my touchstone to go back and back and back to and wonder "Why do I like this and not anything else?" It showed me how to make my game the kind of game I will enjoy GMing, for which I am deeply grateful.

I finally figured it out. PTA requires, by the rules, that the other players participate in saying what's next. There's a world of difference between "people ought to participate" and "it's your turn to say something." In the latter case, occasionally, if I'm gifted with players who will support me, I have fun. In the former, I always have fun.

GMless games are good for this, too, but I should note that I actually prefer games where there's a GM in this PTA style. I love taking a seed from people and expanding it into a scene. I love cutting between people, picking moments of high tension and zigzagging. It's electrifying.

I'm enjoying GMing again in ways I haven't for a long time, because I know what I want. This is exhilarating.

    \n GMing as a facilitator can in many ways be much more relaxing and fun. We all know it requires more and different input from the players, and on paper it seems straightforward enough. What I've noticed is the KIND of input isn't like being a "mini-GM" and isn't like being an inordinately proactive player, it's very important not just to state facts (like a traditional GM) or posit reactions, but to state facts that other players can build on.

    This may seem simple to a lot of people here, but that middle ground is not much seen in traditional games. Once this skill is mastered, Rob's preferred style of GMing can really flourish. When I play a lot of these games with traditional gamers, it's not usually old biases or bad habits that cause the game to fall flat, it's their inability to build a story from all directions (player > Player and GM > Player), and not for lack of trying. Once that's mastered, it's awesome to see the energy at the table skyrocket.

    \n I have a question. Are we talking about simply having pro-active players or are we actually talking about the scene framing architecture of Primetime Adventures. I ask because there's a difference between how things are done in games like My Life with Master, Sorcerer and In A Wicked Age, vs. how things are done in Primetime Adventures, Capes and The Shab-al-Hiri Roach.

    In the first three games the GM retains total editorial power over scene framing. Players can *request* scenes either explicitly (I want a scene where Jon and I are talking) or implicitly (I'm going to go kill that bastard Robin). Without proactive players (through their character's declared actions) the games don't work but the GM still retains full editorial power over scenes. In other words the GM is free to say, "While you're waiting for Jon to arrive your mom calls you" or "On your way to kill Robin ninjas attack you."

    That's NOT true in the other three. There either is no GM at all and players must frame their own scenes or, in the case of Primetime Adventures, the GM MUST abide by the player's request after they set Focus, Agenda and Location (although he can "spike" the scene if he wants to). In other words the best the GM can do is say, "While you and Jon are talking your mom calls" or "In the middle of your duel with Robin, ninjas attack."

    That's why I actually DON"T like PtA's formalized structure because 98% of the time I'm fine with whatever the player says but 2% of the time I want to "insert" something or "spike" or "spin" the situation to help lend a certain focus and I can't do that effectively in PtA. As such I much prefer the "respectful editor" role of Sorcerer or In A Wicked Age...
