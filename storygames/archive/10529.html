\n So, you have an idea for a game that has grown past a thought in your head or a single sentence on paper. Where/how do you record your game going forward - as part of a larger stream of notes in a pocket notebook? In a notebook of its own? A Google Doc? A website? A blog? How do you get the ideas out of your head so you can start thinking on the next step of design?

I'm a player first, game designer last, but for me things tend to start in my moleskine, and migrate to Google Docs. How about you?

    \n Find your own way, of course, but here's mine:

    For each nascent project I have a big, unorganized text file that is my initial dumping ground. I have one for every little potential idea, and the ones that accumulate cruft become going concerns. The rest percolate or die.

    I also have a little Rhodia pad with me all the time, and sometimes I'll make notes in that between computers. It all gets typed into the dump document eventually, but for the most part I use a text file.

    Sometimes I'll work on a particular element on its own, in its own file for clarity, and then cut and paste back into the dump document.

    Eventually that shapes into a loose draft, and when that happens I start numbering versions. Once I have a version I want to share with friends I put it up as a Google doc for comments. This is the point at which a project will probably get its own folder, with a sub-folder for art and another for layout files.

    That is my sloppy process. It has not served me well, particularly when a project approaches 40 numbered drafts. I could use some friendly version control.

    Hope that helps!

    \n I have ideas all the frakkin' time.

    If one sticks around in my head for a while, I throw it onto my computer, usually in a text document. Sometimes it's a snippet of fiction, sometimes a funky rule, whatever. I throw it in a text file, on my computer under 'blank shield press'. I brainstorm into that text document until it "feels right", and I pound out a first draft into Word.

    At this point, the project gains it's own folder, and *everything* to do with the project gets tossed in there. I version the Word document with a very abstract process. If it feels like I've made significant change, I'll increment the version number. Once the game is "done", I make another folder for layout, and butcher the crap out of document. It gets cut into raw text documents by chapter/sidebar/appendix/whatever feels like it needs to be on it's own, and those go into the layout folder. At this point, the base text becomes officially obsolete, and the InDesign version becomes the "real" version.

    This gets particularly messy with something like Blood and Bronze, which has several differnt release formats, so I don't necessarily recommend it, but I can't think of a better way of doing it. 'cause there's just no way to get away from making changes to the document in the layout stage.

    \n I have about 13 or 14 notepads filled up with gaming ideas. I just scribble everything out as it comes to me, complete with questions to myself and other notes. The other day, I found my first few notes on Beast Hunters; that was funny to look back at now.

    Once I have enough handwritten to know where I'm going, I write up a complete alpha draft in Word. Usually in one big push of a few days without much of a break. Nowadays, I keep it on a thumb drive to be able to write at different locations.

    I used Google Docs for a while, but somehow reverted back to Word. Partially because I can't access Google sites at work.

    \n I do most of my thinking and talking about games (online) while at work. If I hit on something I like, I copy and paste the chatlog with the date it took place into a googledoc for future reference. Related chatlogs go together, and if I ever write anything more formal about the idea, it goes at the top above the logs.

    If I ever get far enough to share the rules with somebody, googledocs makes it wonderfully easy. Just copy the rules portion to another doc - leaving the chatlogs behind - and voila!

    I did try doing a design on LiveJournal once, but it didn't really take. You get better feedback if you direct your questions to specific people.

    \n I make blog posts with noodling about various parts of a game. When I've got enough various parts, I write it up in earnest in Google docs. When I've got enough for a playtest, I start porting that text into InDesign, but invariably realize there's stuff I need to add and change in the process.

    My file tree looks like this:

    Documents
    *Game Designs
    **Folders for each game in progress
    ***Layout files
    ***Art

    For games I've released, the interior folder for each game looks different:
    **Folder for released game
    ***Obsoleted files
    ****Layout files
    ****Art and diagrams
    ***Current files
    ****Layout files
    ****Art files
    ****Print PDFs
    ****PDFs for sale

    I hope that makes sense!

    \n I find that writing some stuff clarifies my thinking and makes me think seriously about what I'm doing. It makes me go down avenues I was filling with handwavium.

    Anyway, what I do is I open a Word file and create a bullet list of stuff. At first it's just brainstorming a bunch of ideas. I then chop, rearrange, and refine the ideas, putting them in an order that makes sense and condensing them. As I start to get ideas for mechanics, I begin to refine further. Misspent Youth was a bullet list of rules systems for about 2 years.
