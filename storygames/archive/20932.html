    1) Depending on the jurisdiction it may be entirely legal to download that PDF and/or make copies of it for your friends, no matter what the rights-holder thinks. For example, this is the case here in Finland. Is copyright as a moral concept more expansive than the law of the land, requiring of us actions and forcing limitations that the law does not?

If our hypothetical rights-holder has abandoned their property, there is nobody else who even has the authority to pursue their rights in court. Is copyright so important that, contrary to law, we are in fact all responsible for upholding the commercial rights of other people where they won't do it themselves?

    \n I have no bone to pick with that position, Jeff. We can wrangle over it for the sake of philosophy, though, if you want. A bunch of facile rhetoric incoming!\n
    Most indie RPGs are self published and the authors deserve to be payed for their work.
    \nI disagree about authors deserving to be paid for their work. If that were true, either the amount of the payment would be negotiable ("You get one euro for your game - it's just not very good"), or anybody could make a ton of money by pushing out trash games. If it's the former, then that statement just doesn't say much - sure, maybe everybody deserves to get paid, but if what they deserve is 30 cents per head, who cares?

    This isn't merely facile rhetoric, but a fundamental philosophical problem for society: who gets to decide what you do for living, and who gets to decide how much wealth doing that accrues to you. Most people nowadays are pretty keen on an individualistic market exchange society of some sort, so the answer probably won't be "the board of Indie RPG Critics" or "everybody gets paid, no matter how sucky they are". Rather, I think most of us here are pretty happy with the idea that the market decides how much value your work has, and nobody is actually entitled to being paid for their work just because they wanted to make a roleplaying game that nobody asked them to make.

    (I also disagree with the idea that it's OK to rip off non-indie RPG designers, while self-publishers have special entitlements. We all play by the same rules, far as I know.)

    So if we're all agreeing about the market being the one to determine who gets paid, how does that work in practice with digital piracy? The short answer for me is that it does not - there simply is no feasible way to make it work, long term. The system will continue functioning on sheer momentum for a while, but in the end it's going to boil down to either a police state or a new social contract over artist rights, one that minimally legalizes non-commercial digital distribution.

    (The practical reasons for my belief on this matter are complex and have to do with economics, information theory and all sorts of assorted philosophical bullshit. The important part is that I do not believe in the practicality of trying to regulate an old-style copyright market in a world of digital freedom. Either you curb the freedoms, or you accept free exchange of information.)

    Thus, my argument for the moral folly of anti-piracy: the anti-piracy ethos only makes sense if traditional copyright is a system worthy and capable of being saved. If it either should not, or cannot, be saved, then it's still cool for a person to personally hold onto the copyright values, but it becomes rather morally suspect to try to force others to do the same with peer pressure or legal action or other such techniques of moral persuasion. A person arguing for copyright in a world where copyright has become untenable is essentially just causing grief by postponing an inevitable change; worst case, they encourage the perpetuation of copyright-friendly repressive structures that attempt to force what can only be forced at a far too high a price to society.

    (Price to society? In my eyes, the choice is not between piracy and copyright so much as it is between a world where copyright is essentially unforceable, and we gain all the benefits of free information, or a world where everybody's computers are registered in a Big Brother system of digital validation, with regular policing of unauthorized hardware. That's what it takes, technologically speaking, to stop non-commercial digital piracy, and I don't think it's worth it. Any half-way measures are just causing grief for no good reason to cherry-picked people who are all acting out of natural, often laudable impulses.)

    Were one to take my thesis at face value for the sake of the argument, namely that copyright has become dysfunctional and needs to be discarded, then what about that artist who may or may not deserve payment; how does society decide on that, then? I believe that pre-orders will be the short-term solution, but 50 years from now it's all going to be back to patron-client models, like it was 500 years ago. It just makes the most sense for society in a free information environment to pay the artist in advance or at the time of publication, instead of trying to enforce an a posteori monopoly for the artist to make their money afterwards.

    Ultimately, though, that's a problem for the artists and audiences of the future to solve, because we (you) don't owe them (us) a living.\n
    I also think it's practical issue, I want some of the ideas found an indie games to become more widespread in the role playing game market.
    \nI don't feel much need to argue with this practical observation. I don't think it's quite that simple, but the basic principle is somewhat sound: pay a person, and they will work for you. We are in rough agreement on the practical consequences of giving people money.

    Specifically, the reason why I don't think this has much to do with the problem of piracy is that if you just think that people should give money to artists they want to support, surely that then also implies not giving money to artists you don't want to support. And if this is the case, we're basically just saying that you should only pay artists when you feel like doing so, and this needs not have any particular connection to whether you consume their art or not; if you don't pay, surely it is because you do not want to, at this time, support this artist. Makes sense to me, but that means that piracy is no longer a moral or social issue - it's just a case-by-case preference. "I like this artist enough to check out their stuff, but not enough to pay them money to patronize further production." Fair, fair enough.

    Eero,

    The reason I asked about copyright was not a matter of copyright law (which I don't assume you to have any kind of expertise handling, and is in trouble, anyway, due to digital media and globalization - as you say, it may soon become outdated).

    Rather, it's about the higher-order concerns, such as you are discussing here.

    Like you, I value free access to information, for the benefits it grants to society, civilization, and perhaps even the human race. If I was elected World President and asked to decide on this issue at this very moment, I would be very tempted to adopt a fully libertarian stance on information - free and available for everyone.

    However, the impact of copyright protection on creative endeavours is enormous. In this context, it affects the level, quality, and availability of the products, their development, their distribution, and their advancement.

    It's not hard to what would happen if our dear Jeff suddenly become a billionaire. Perhaps he would offer to pay $1,000,000 (or some other hefty sum in your preferred currency) to the best 10 games anyone could produce for his game club. This would create a revolution in game design, playtesting, online discussion, and, likely, evolution in gaming practices. People could afford to hire playtesters, experiment with new mediums, organize events where players test rule variants, pay "performers" who learn to play games at the highest level of skill, produce YouTube videos teaching people to play Rolemaster or Aftermath, and so forth. We can only imagine what developments story games would undergo (much like how Kickstarter/crowdfunding technology has really upped the ante on game product quality in the last five years).

    This is, in a sense, what happens when copyright is enforceable on a certain medium. When a creator has a sense of security in her product become monetizable, she is able to leverage a whole other level of commitment to excellence.

    Sure, art and craft will always be around. We'll always have people playing instruments, competing in sports, making paintings, and (hopefully, but less certainly) designing story games.

    However, laws like copyright protection have a powerful influence on the scope and quality of these activities. A perfect example is the recent boom of the Chinese film industry - for decades, there has been no way to enforce copyright (or no interest in doing so) in China, and, while many independent films were made, the Chinese are hardly seen as world players when it comes to the silver screen. However, in recent times, they have started implementing copyright laws with regards to film, and now the Chinese film industry is roughly *tripling* (!!!) in size every year. We're seeing bigger budgets, better effects, bigger productions, better actors, etc, etc, etc. Some people are saying that by 2025, Chinese cinema will surpass Hollywood in every measure (profits, budgets, etc).

    Give people the idea that a certain creative work can be reliably monetized, and that field grows quickly, both in size and in quality. This is, effectively, one of the things that copyright laws do for us.

    I'm not sure where that leaves us, but it could explain Jeff's attitude here - as you point out, saying that "creators deserve to be paid for their work" is a somewhat self-defeating statement from a logical standpoint, but as a sort of voluntary moral stance, it makes total sense. In theory, we could agree to support a certain form of art financially, and if enough of us do so, the whole level of the artform rises up, evolves, develops.

    Is there, then, some benefit to this kind of law to balance against the values of free information?

    I'm not sure what the answers are - and I do like seeing the success of crowdfunding platforms for games, even if it leaves less serious designers like myself in the dust - but I will mention that there may be other possible outcomes than the two extremes you posit (either a tyrannical police state or total piracy).

    We know as game designers that all kinds of different incentives can be designed into a system, and if you set them up right, sometimes you can get the effect you want without the consequences you don't want. For instance, in a lot of art fields, there are effective ways to compensate people for their work without limiting their work's impact. For example, many countries collect a tax which they then redistribute to artists according to the impact of their work.

    For instance, until recently the radio music system in North America was quite reliable. Union-like organizations collected a pool of money every year (as a general tax, or a tax on certain types of profits), then tracked which radio stations played which songs, and how many times. Then they used that data to redistribute the money to the artists who were being played the most.

    It's not too hard to imagine some system along these lines working for other media (radio, after all, is hardly as popular as it used to be). It works (though not extremely efficiently, granted), and it doesn't limit anyone's access to the products. None of the people involved have any incentive to circumvent the process - if I run a radio station, I have no reason not to play whatever music I think my listeners want to hear. Listeners aren't limited - they can listen to any music they want - and artists are still compensated.

    Perhaps in the future we can find new, better ways of accomplishing this kind of thing? The availability of high-quality art (craft, sport, etc) is a public good in itself, and worth thinking about.

    I'd love to hear your* thoughts on all this. My ideas here are fairly idle thought, not terribly well considered; I'm just brainstorming a little on the topic, based on some basic knowledge.


    *: Applying equally to Eero, Jeff, or any other reader.

      As it happens, I think that the legalization of non-commercial copying is actually one such compromise. I mean, the various pirate parties of European politics could be demanding the complete abolishment of copyright law, but that doesn't seem to be the general demand. Instead, what they (we, I guess I should say) demand is legalizing non-commercial digital distribution - essentially, to stop hunting private individuals engaging in non-profit cultural activities. This would still retain a large sphere of cultural industry for exclusive commercial exploitation, as commercial pirate products would remain as illegal as ever.

      (For those who track my earlier argument about piracy having to be legalized because enforcing the laws against it is impossible, you might be wondering why I seem to be in favour of retaining legal limitations for commercial piracy. The reason is simple: unlike with non-commercial piracy, I still retain faith that it is somewhat possible to effectively police commercial piracy. Money leaves tracks, and is ultimately spent in society, and all in all it is no more difficult to police commercial pirates than it is to watch out for illegal drug sales, tax evasion or human trafficking. Difficult, sure, but not outright ludicrous in a technical sense. Easy enough that I'm willing to try, still.)

      Aside from that, it seems to me that if there was willingness, copyright could be moderated in all sorts of ways. As a student of history I suspect that this is one of those things where a revolution is largely brought on by the inflexibility, greed and blindness of the current ruling elite: the culture industry as a whole is entrenched in defending copyright to such a degree that making significant concessions would be considered a defeat - as it would surely be for the owners of the largest IP portfolios. Thus, when compromise is made impossible, we might end up with the entire system getting discredited over time.

      As an example of a compromise that would be fully within historical precedent, imagine if the mandatory licensing scheme from the radio medium was generalized over all arts: anybody doing distribution has a subjective right to distribute any published work they want, but they are also obligated to keep records of what they distribute, and they pay a fixed fee (set by whomever, not so important here) for the privilege to a central organization that then takes care of distributing the money to artists.

      The most significant features of the mandatory licensing scheme are that the licensing fee is low; that it is fixed; that it is mandatory (no opt-out for individual rights-holders). This basically makes a service akin to Netflix both immensely more powerful in terms of selection, and also much, much cheaper. It reduces the profits in certain media fields compared to current conventions in how they sell things, but it still retains the concept of copyright. It is a compromise, just like it was a compromise when radio play was facilitated by curtailing the copyright severely.

      Heck, one could just have Internet providers cover the licensing fee for their customers, on the assumption that everybody does a little bit of distribution on the side. It'd be like the old cassette tax we used to have here in Finland. I've seen this suggested now and again, but of course it would be politically difficult to set a tax that officially condones piracy.

      I can imagine how revamping copyright with something like mandatory licensing would cut the piracy movement at the knees, while still retaining a firm concept of copyright - or I guess it would be "payright" at that point, as you wouldn't have the right to set your price or refuse to sell anymore, once you'd made the initial decision to publish.
