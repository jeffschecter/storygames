    \n Unstore should probably have a consistent way to contact the individual authors, considering that it's supposed to be just a standardified sales interface for individuals instead of a store. The commerce is between individuals and Vincent as the Unstore maintenance man doesn't really take any responsibility for anything except the website tech. This doesn't really work if the site provides a means to send money to somebody but not even minimal indentity information for them, though - it's a pretty empty statement that Vincent's "not responsible" if he's the one hosting Paypal buttons that send money to mystery destinations without providing any disclosure about the identities of the publishers involved. What if somebody sneaks in a bum Paypal link that does really send money to an Internet scammer?

    Of course Paypal sales imply a contact, as Paypal accounts are associated with email addresses. Unfortunately Unstore seems to use "hosted" Paypal buttons, which means that the account address is not obtainable from the system. It's up to luck and a given publisher's diligence whether a way to contact them exists for the Unstore customer.

    As for Graham, it's inconceivable that he'd be ripping off Rasmus here. (The sole reason this needs to be said is because of how Rasmus framed the OP.) Much more likely that he's on a safari somewhere and hasn't just read his email for a few days, or he's managed to lose the order. This is generally the case with indie fulfilment, you just need to find some way to contact the guy to remind him about the obligation.

    \nThis has happened to me as a publisher: after sending two parcels that got lost I sent an insured one, and even that got lost, recouping some of the expense Ultimately we just recompensed the customer with our apologies, I think, as we couldn't get the mail to go through. The customer of the time resided in France, and I have in fact discussed this with other publishers and discovered that holy moly, the French post office is apparently so unreliable that if you have enough French customers you might actually have to have a separate policy for what to do about those lost parcels. I've no idea what they do there, do their post office eat books or what.

    My general advice is that if you don't get something you ordered from the Internet, most definitely be in touch with the seller at first opportunity. As a direct-order publisher and occasional retailer myself I know that 95% of the time the reason for the delay is either that they (I) forgot to send it, or that the mail monster ate the package. Neither of those problems is actually going to resolve itself unless you contact the seller and ask about it. I at least won't bite if you do!

    Anybody know how two of the same item to the same address fail to show up?
    \nIt does happen. It's a nightmare.

    Reasons I've found in the past: differences in address formats between countries, customs returning packages for a variety of reasons, packages being lost in a workplace's internal mail.

    Reasons that we'd never know about: packages being left on doorsteps and being stolen, packages being left in strange places by a well-meaning housemate or spouse and found months afterwards.

    Add it all together and, eventually, you stop worrying about it and accept that sometimes packages need to be resent. Hopefully only once, sometimes twice.
