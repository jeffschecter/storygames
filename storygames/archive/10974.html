\n With the holiday season approaching, my gaming wish-list starts to grow as does my desire to see what other people are playing and enjoying.

So what games outside of the story-games community are you digging on right now and why?

I'll go first:

Malifaux, a table-top miniatures skirmish game featuring a bizzare mashup of steampunk, wild west, southern bayou, goblins, wizards and undead. The sculpts are incredibly cool, and the game is diceless, using a unique card system where cards dealt from a deck as randomizers (my 10 attack beats your 8 defense) can be modified by playing cards from your hand (I play a 12 twist of fate and defeat your attack) as well as dealing cards to cast spells and a ton of special abilities depending on different symbols on the cards.

I had the chance to play in a demo at CharCon in October and fell in love with the game. It is a really sweet, elegant system and a hellishly fun game to play.

    \nMalifaux seems awesome! I hope to take a look at a demo at the LGS, I'll see if they're running any.

    Tabletop:

    * Pandemic. Jason's notes on his expansion are totally spot on. Unlike every other game, ever, where the expansion is nothing more than some length/complexity monster (or totally throws the game off by not allowing for essential/core card ratios, like BANG!), the On The Brink expansion is flat-out great. Every single role and rule is excellent.

    * If you like word games, check out Bananagrams. Totally awesome word game. I hate scrabble, but this is fast, furious, and totally fun. It's like the fun that comes from Apples to Apples, as you build a pile of bizarre words that shows off your thought processes a little too much. Each game lasts like 4 minutes or less. Totally hooked our group.

    * Trans-America is also another hot standby for our group. The play, the speed and all are infective. We got this as a group after playing some Ticket to Ride, and honestly have not gone back to Ticket to Ride in months.

    * We've also played a lot of Shadow Hunters, which is similar to Are You a Werewolf but with some tactics and strategy.

    * Although I have not played it yet, Shane Jackson swears by the NEW EDITION of RISK. I have not played it yet, though. Apparently, it's looks like Risk, but it's actually fun. There's new play options and objective cards, so instead of "beat the world", there are specific meetable goals (on random cards) and starting conditions which you can use to play limitless goal-oriented games.
    Here are the rules. The new version is called "Mission Risk": http://www.hasbro.com/common/instruct/Risk_2003.pdf

    Console/PC:
    I've been playing these recently:
    PS3: Trine (demo): A highly graphical puzzle-y game, reminiscent of Thee Vikings.
    PS3: Assassin's Creed II: Bought on a whim, loving it a little too much. In the first one, nothing you did other than killing dudes mattered (collecting flags, etc). Now, everything you do other than killing dudes matters (enhance your estate, find the conspiracies (at least 2), help your catatonic mom).
    PC: Left 4 Dead 2: It's good, but after sooooo much L4D I'm not sure if it's "$44 good". Plus, my PC is a little behind the times.

      \n
      \n Board game wise, my current jam has to to be Britannia (the Fantasy Flight edition, not the older ones). Covers all of British history from the Roman invasion to the Norman invasion. Pretty much requires four players, all of whom control various peoples who come on to the board at set times and have particular objectives they're looking to accomplish. It looks like a war game and combat is the main way you interact with others, but playing it like a straight up war game will make you lose; it's much more about knowing what areas you have to hold and when to score the most points for your side as a whole, because any individual people you control is unlikely to stick around for the whole game (with the exception of the Welsh, and, interestingly, the Picts).
      \n
      \nRules aren't that complicated, but the richness of the timeline means that once you're playing with people who have a sense of what's going to be happening, it becomes quite deep; as the Romans, my main job is to get north as fast as possible to put the hurt on the Picts so that when my Scots show up, they aren't just thrown into the sea, but I have a very limited window to beat on the Picts, because next turn, the Saxons and Angles and Jutes are showing up and I have to keep them and the Irish from burning down all of my lovely cities...
      \n
      \nAnd all the time, you have to be able to figure out who is scoring better than they should be scoring in every phase of the game, so you know who you should be ganging up against [hint: it's not always the person who has the highest current score]. Very nifty game, and if you are a history nerd, lots of emergent history potential. The Saxons killing William the Conqueror, anyone? Harald Hardrada, King of England?
      \n
      \nOn the videogame front, L4D2 has been everything I hoped for. I have to disagree with Andy, since I feel it is an enormous improvement on the previous game. Scavenge lets you actually get meaningful play time in even if you only have half an hour, and the new Special Infected make higher-level versus play much less static and boring than it became in the original. After all, cornering has become a really bad idea (Spitter + Charger, anyone?), and enough of the major events require you to run through never-ending hordes that everything just feels much more dynamic. Have definitely gotten my money's worth.
