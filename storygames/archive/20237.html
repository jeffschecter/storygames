\n Here is the current revision of the Compass I'd like to share with you.


The Compass is a tool for players to give each other non-binding feedback within game play.
Each player gets a token they can place on the Compass. At the end of each scene, all players are free to move their token to a field that resonates most with them. This a simple informal way to inform and inspire each other where the story or the game should go.
Additionally, a concentration of tokens on a specific field indicates that there might be a need in the group that needs attention.
If players feel something important is missing on the compass, they are free to add an additional field that represents what is important to them.

Feedback & experience so far has been very positive. Let me know if you think something like this could be useful in your game.

    \n Paul: Exactly. However, basically the spaces can mean whatever you want, as long as you all agree on it.

    Here is an explanation of the specific spaces (again, feel free to use whatever you want, of course):
    \u2022 I'm ok - Choose this if you are overall content with how things evolve for you as a player as well as the overall
    story.
    \u2022 I need break - This you as as the player expressing a need. Feel free to get up and leave the space if you need
    to. You should feel comfortable being involved in the game.
    \u2022 Speed up - You would like the overall pace of the game to go faster. Less time should be wasted in details for
    you and the story should move forward.
    \u2022 Take more time - Storyplaying is rushing too fast for your taste. You would like to take more time and pay
    attention to the details.
    \u2022 Roleplaying - You would like to spend more time roleplaying, immersed in the scenes immersed in-character.
    \u2022 Plotting - You would like to spend more time scheming plots, advance character development and talking
    about where the story is potentially going.
    \u2022 Action - You would like to see more action scenes.
    \u2022 Dialogue - You would like to see scenes based on intense, interesting dialoues.
    \u2022 Stability - At the moment, continuity within the story without too many suprises or abrupt incidents is
    important to you.
    \u2022 Twist - You would like to see a game changing twist in the story.
    \u2022 Intimate - At the moment, you would like to see scenes with a focus on a few characters in a more intimate setting.
    \u2022 Epic - At the moment, you would like to see things happening on a grand scale, at least relative to your story.
    \u2022 Light - Within the story margins, you would like to roleplay scenes a bit more light-hearted. Perhaps, you
    prefer less dramatic things at stake in the scenes.
    \u2022 Dark - Within the story margins, you would like the scenes to get grittier. Roleplaying should be probably more
    tense or dramatic for you.

    \n I had a person in my life that I had trouble standing up to. I didn't dare to speak up when they were cruel. So I wrote "arguing notes", pre-written little text cards that said things like "Maybe we should slow down right now" or "I don't know if it's your intent, but you're making me feeel really bad".
    However... the tiny window of "being too shy to speak up but being brave enough to pass a note" is such a sliver. Formulating the phrases and putting them on paper helped me become braver and speak up more often. So I threw the notes away and started speaking up -- I don't always dare to do it but in those cases, I would've had a hard time with the notes as well.

    \n The "I'm okay" space seems like dead weight to me, and I don't like the shadow it casts on the other spaces. Just because I'm itching for an intimate moment doesn't mean I'm dissatisfied with what's happening now. For me it would feel socially safer to put my token somewhere if I didn't have to imply dissatisfaction. A space that says "surprise me," or "I'm good with whatever" could work, but I think it would work better without the neutral option.

    Recently I've been recently doing this type of thing (less formally, and less frequently), by asking "what do we want to see more of?" I've had better results framing this as a positive ("what would be exciting to you", rather than "what do you wish would change") since people are reluctant to complain or to seem like they're complaining.

    Love that this enables that negotiation quickly and quietly between scenes.

    \n I agree with @Potemkin. Besides, there are two different things in play here: one is "how did you perceive the last scene?" and the other is "in comparison to the last scene, how would you like the next one to be?".

    So, if I put my token in Light, I'm saying that the last scene mostly felt Dark to me and now I want something lighter. Because of this, maybe the fields could be clearly paired up and perhaps people could have two or three tokens to communicate their feelings about multiple pairs.

    I can see this being useful for playing online, because of the lack of face-to-face feedback.

    \n But the previous version had them paired up, Ricardo.
    Maybe there's some benefit to decoupling them.
    Such as when Lojban (a dorky language) was being developed, originally most descriptive predicates were comparative. "blanu" which means that something is blue, for a while meant "something is bluer than something else". you'd say "lo kanla cu blanu lo tsani" which would mean "your eyes are bluer than the sky". The idea was scrapped and now blanu just means "is blue". I'm happy about that change: predicate relationships don't have to be binary, not everything is a contest.
    (Lojban is pretty good about distinguishing between scalar and polar negation, as well.)

    Potemkin: imo, it's not only about intimacy but having a reminder on the table that is present all the time and that doesn't interrupt the flow.
    \nThe point of having a tool that redirects the tone of play is all about interrupting that selfsame flow. Flow ebbs, time "off the ball" and specific breaks are useful; checking with players to reaffirm the direction of flow and consolidate progress feels a whole lot more natural to storytelling, conversation and human sociability than pushing a token about to ask for a loo break.

    Apologies if my tone seems belittling at all: your invention is lovely and I'm sure many people will find it useful, but I'm not one of them. I mostly want to hear the players own vocalisations of what they need and I'm content to pause the flow to find out.

    Perhaps it's a culture-thing? I'm from that stock of hard-bitten British grognards who've been mercilessly hardwired to grab any shed of player vocalisation they can. Subtly and sensitivity aren't virtues that characterise many folk I play with. Game-mastery is all about managing multiple voices for me; your tool would be more effective for my table just as a list of options players can quickly choose from, but I wouldn't be worth my while to insult their intelligence to that degree.
