    \n The changes with the Forge booth policy have been a major cause, I think: the majority of indie publishers are really small-scale hobbyists for whom it's not reasonable to pay more than a couple hundred dollars over personal expenses to participate at the convention. Those same people also mostly participate for social and creative reasons, which are also curtailed if there's no Forge booth to be in. The more serious folks have formed their own crews when the Forge started curtailing the booth a few years back, but others have simply stopped going. Stopping is especially easy when you've already done the Gencon thing several times and are not getting commensurate benefit out of it once it becomes old hat as an experience.

    Meanwhile I understand that there are more up-and-coming conventions in the USA that are friendlier towards the indies to begin with (more room, less bureaucracy, smaller expenses, better crowd), so people are going to those instead of Gencon. I'm no expert on the USA con scene, but I know that I myself can't really be bothered to go to more than a couple large conventions per year, so any competition will cut the Gencon crowd visibly. In fact, now that I think of it, one might say that I myself belong in this population: with Essen Spiel to concern me in the fall I don't have the motivation to worry about Gencon as well.

    I'm not aware of outright changes in the Gencon policy, but perhaps somebody else is. It hasn't been particularly easy for the smallest indies to participate ever, really, so I suspect that this is more to do with the natural order reasserting itself than any particular change in the nature of Gencon itself.

    \n Gen Con makes for a very expensive lemonade stand.

    One of the hot topics on the GPA mailing list is, of course, organizing for GenCon. For those of you that don't know me, I am on the GPA Board of Directors. From what I can gather, Gen Con isn't against Indie publishers. The are for making a profit, though.

    Space on the dealer floor is now being sold by the foot, as opposed to the preset islands they've had in previous years. And there is that booth sharing fee now. The high prices and the arrangement makes it very difficult for small publishers to go alone or to group together with other publishers. If you treat your game design and publishing as a hobby job, any of the major conventions are going to be inhospitable.

    However, indie publishing did not start with the Forge, nor will it end with it. The Game Publishers Assocation has been booth-sharing before the Forge, and we are still continueing the practice. The vibe of the GPA is quite different, though. You won't read any discussions about theory, or much in game design. The emphasis is on running your publishing company like a business, and sharing information so as to avoid very expensive pitfalls. You can read more about us at http://thegpa.org.

    \n I started off at the GPA booth for the first two years Gen Con was in Indy. Prior to that I slummed at the Jolly Rogers Game booth. The GPA was a good way to go.

    Being a Hoosier the East and West coast cons are in the unaffordable range for me. I've never been to Historicon but would love to go - for instance.

    Gen is a profit making show for me because we sell stuffed animals. Without the plush we'd lose money. My games have never made more than a third of what the plush makes. Usually a fourth.

    I can see how the "big shows" cater to our egos more than economic realities. I want to sell to stores. I think I will meet game store people and game distributors there and suddenly I will be found! Well I have met store and distribution people there but it has not created a Holywood moment. Publishing the Forge way (small independent, usually POD) doesn't fit the three tiered model of business. I've never thought the three tiered system was bad or evil - just different. Most things don't fit it and those games that do are not necessarily good - but then again are not automatically bad either. It just comes down to Gen Con being a land of dreams - which begs the question "How much do dreams cost?"

    And none of this touches on the small cons - Camp Nerdly, Go Play Northwest, Forge Midwest, etc - these are the places to go if you want to play a particular kind of game and just have fun. Nothing can approach them for quality of play.

    \n Just came back from PAX. It was a mixed experience.

    I think PAX can't be beat for people who want to sell games and I love that they have such an amazing opportunity. But for people looking to play RPGs (beyond demos), it's a different story. There are plenty of positive things to say about PAX (the staff was the nicest con staff I've ever encountered) and I'm glad I attended but it is no way a replacement for Dreamation if you just want to play indie RPGs. Not even close. I would encourage people who don't sell games not to get sucked in by the hype. I'll go into more detail later this week.

    That said, I love Gen Con. But it's important to note, I don't sell games. I think we need to separate which con is good for what based on what people want out of conventions. Selling and just playing games are VERY different agendas and often I see the signals being crossed.

    Also, I've been going to Gen Con as a person mainly wanting to play a great variety of games with a strong diversity of people for 10+ years. And Gen Con rocks for that (with a few exceptions).

    And it is important to note that indie does NOT equal forge games or story games (and the GPA was around before the Forge). I play plenty of indie game at Gen Con every year. When I read Story Games, it sounds like there is this huge exodus from Gen Con. But when I attend Gen Con, I'm too busy playing amazing games to remember what the fuss is about. Last year Gen Con was amazing. Probably my favorite Gen Con experience in the last few years.

They kept that going all con long, trading out employees in shifts every few hours. This is best video I could find of it, but I thought it was an amazingly indie thing for a major game and major company like Harmonix to do, trusting just on the quality and rep of their game and making sure everybody got a chance to try it out.

      \n FWIW, The BWHQ crew entertained the hell out of a whole lot of people. Folks would march right up to the booth, "We want a demo." And we did our best to meet the demand, but ultimately we were overwhelmed. We could not entertain everyone who wanted to be entertained. Even so, everyone was patient and everyone was enthusiastic. Some folks had the game, some folks had only heard of it. Everyone wanted to know more about it.

      Scores, perhaps hundreds, of people were demanding to play our crazy, overblown roleplaying game.
      And from what I saw next door, IPR, Vincent, Joshua, Eppy and company were no less busy. Hell, even Jared ran demos until he couldn't talk any more.

      This isn't a "sales vs play" thing. This is my observation about how much people wanted to play games.

      I doubt PAX will change its entire structure to make that happen. Tabletop at East was infinitely better this year precisely because it was not intimate. The large open space allowed you to find a game almost anywhere.

      \n
      \n

      I'm not proposing that they do that. I'm just noting a difference. Another difference: PAX is the size of 80 Dreamations. I'm not expecting similarities, but I do want to be clear about the differences.

      \n

      These are folks who love games. They want new ones. What we do is make new ones. They love us. Profit.

      \n

      Note the startling lack of question marks.

      \n

      Now, I do think that we can probably do some surreptitious quiet space making that could make some more intense games happen, or maybe hook up with someone over at the attached hotel and play over there in a more intimate setting.

      \n

      But it's all very, very promising.

      \n I think it's important to try and shape the con you attend into a good place for what you want to enjoy. If the con doesn't support this, you may have to move on or make your own.

      The Forge Booth made Gen Con a vastly better experience for Indie publishers, and now that the Forge policies are changing, the Gen Con landscape is changing too. It sounds like some of Gen Con's policies also aren't great for the small publishers who want to set up booths. That's too bad. But it also sounds like there are opportunities there. One of the thing I love about Gen Con is the million or so folks running their own flavor of tabletop fun. I could wander those rooms and watch games all day.

      PAX, on the other extreme, is practically new ground for Indies. Sage helped get an Indie room off the ground at PAX prime last year, which changed the whole character of the con from an indie gamer point of view. I know I wouldn't have even gone if there hadn't been that indie room. PAX is a great opportunity to make a new home for RPG-ers and con goers of all sorts.

      And there are a growing number of small local cons offering vastly different experiences (Jackson's art-installation RPG con anyone?).

      And micro-cons, and birthday cons, and so on. It's a hard field for Gen Con to compete in.
