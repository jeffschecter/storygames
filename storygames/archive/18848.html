\n Yeah, it could be the next J.K. Rowling book but it isn't. I opened this thread to discuss a bit about something that started on this other thread.

I just found out why (design-wise, not color-wise) there are games that some people will understand, run and enjoy better than others. See, you've probably heard before of the Theory of Multiple Intelligences, but have you ever thought how deeply it impacts our hobby?

I've often had trouble playing 3.5 and PF because of the amount of rules and fiddling around with numbers I've gotta make there. I'm an artist, so I'm more visual/spatial oriented. Our gm feels at home with the system, since he's more logic/math oriented. On top of that, he works as an accountant and he's used to memorizing legal stuff and relate it to those numbers, so in the end he actually relaxes playing PF, while I get a bit stressed about that part of the game.

He can set a bunch of numbers on a spreadsheet and see a living town in there, where I only get a headache. But I can draw a map of that town and bring it alive in 3d, while rolling dice to come up with numbers just when needed. He can pull a combat without miniatures but you'll realize he's thinking verbally and not spatially, just using concepts like "flanking" or "behind you". I can pull a combat without miniatures (actually I prefer those) and track the action in the three axes of movement, visualizing the whole thing in my head like a movie. And the faster the action, the better.

No wonder why you can find so many different systems, mechanics and procedures, and think "it's a matter of taste" or "this game isn't for me" and start hacking away something other people can run as it is, or never get a game and hate it despite you love the genre used. Genre and Color is a matter of taste, while easily, only 30% of the issue is that you're used to other mechanics, and the other 70% of it is because you think in a different way than the authors of the game.

I'm wondering what would happen if this concept were used widely enough, until you could find labels on the cover of RPG books with something along the lines of "suggested for visual-spatial thinkers"

Also, 9 types of intelligences and their combinations! You could find out easily what your kids most developed type of intelligence is and educate them properly to take better advantage of it. What do you think? And how are you thinking it?

    \n Just reading about Multiple Intelligences for my Education class. As already mentioned, it's mostly bunkum.

    People do have certain learning styles they're better at. Some students are more likely to learn when information is spoken aloud, while other are more likely to learn when information is depicted in an illustration or diagram, and so forth. But that's a secondary concern to the information being taught. So someone studying geometry, even if their favored learning style is auditory, is more likely to learn a geometric concept from diagrams than from a spoken lecture. Likewise, a student who learns best from pictures overall will still have an easier time learning vocabulary from a lesson that focuses on writing and speaking than they would from a lesson that sought to impart vocabulary pictorially.

    What does this mean for gaming? Mostly that it doesn't matter if certain mechanics are easier for people who are good with arithmetic while others are easier for people who are good with spacial visualization. What matters is which rule best fits the thing you're trying to do.

    That's most evident in games that use abstract positioning systems, such as the FFG versions of the Warhammer and Star Wars RPGs, and 13th age. They use a system that's designed to make things simpler by taking out all the inch-by-inch movement systems that games such as D&D use. In practice, they're usually more complicated and clunky--because positioning is a fundamentally spatial concept, so using a spatial system (even one that on paper is more complicated) is a better fit than an system that relies on narrative description.
