\n Hey. So I spent the last four years writing RPG books for White Wolf. Lots of them.

I had the privilege to meet some of the Story Games massive at Concrete Cow 8.5 back in September (Jenskot may remember me), and there I mentioned to one or two that I was writing my own game.

So I wrote my own game, and it is called MSG, and I managed to get it pimped at io9.com, which meant that I had over a thousand hits on the free version that I posted up for promotional purposes (which is going to be around until November 25th).

So I wondered if anyone here had seen it, and what they thought about it.

    \n About the "setting": there isn't one.

    The thing about those bogus SF elements - and they are bogus, yes, completely - well. The thing is, the game is a straight list of things that companies really do. The bogus SF stuff is there because it would be too true to life (people don't realise, but all that stuff about massacring unionized workers, offering amphetamines to help workers get through overtime, the terms "stabilisation consultants" to refer to paid killers and the term "parallel market" for the mob are all things that happen today). The SF stuff is there to push it into satire. I don't think I was mercilessly mocking anything. I was pushing it just the tiniest bit.

    FWIW, the reaction I got since the io9 plug has been massively positive, more among non-gamers than among gamers, interestingly.

    \n I've taken a look at the game. My initial impression is generally very positive. There are things which aren't 100% and where the game needs additional polish: Some of the phrasing doesn't quite read as smoothly as others and the layout needs some TLC, but for a playtest edition these are expected.

    I'm really digging the core mechanic: The game runs on tokens, rather than dice. In order to resolve conflicts you blind-bid a number of your tokens against someone else. Whoever blind bids the most wins the conflict, and loses all the tokens they bid. The loser, gives all the tokens they bid to the winner. If you have a big conflict, where both sides want to win, you're looking at a lot of tokens lost by the loser. If only the winner cared and bet a lot of tokens, the loser probably ends up handing the winner only one or two tokens. That's a really damn elegant system, and one I'm really liking. The emergent behaviours from that seem really interesting in terms of driving those conflicts.

    I like the setting/tone a lot. It's like Transmetropolitan, Sinister Dexter or Eastern Standard Tribe. I think the humour works very well for me as well, but then, I'm English and dark humour is part of our national make-up

    I don't say this often, but this is a playtest release that I'm really looking forward to actually playtesting when I get a chance!

    \n So I've just finished the Executive Edition and it's in proofreading now.

    I changed a couple things, and changed some of the rules (for example, in playtesting, we found that Bidding to be the Company was unnecessarily complex for most players, so that got moved to the Variants section, and we moved Perks to the main body of the text). But I'm largely happy with it, and I'm particularly pleased that the people who grok it the most are non-gamers. A chap on another site who was somewhat bemused by the game said he thought it had about as much in common with an RPG as the Sims has with a first-person shooter.

    He was being critical, but he actually understood.

    2120 people downloaded the free version. Hurrah for the Mainstream RPG Writer's Vanity Project!

    \n I like the idea of the brand being made up of many separate companies with completely different aims. That alone justifies practically anything that you want to do.
    \n
    \nIf you want to carpet bomb a whole town because a few workers have unionised, you can bring in your private police force to deal with any whining survivors. And although the cost of calling an air strike is astronomical, you can justify it because it's your brand's air force that profits.
    \n
    \nI'd like to see a relationship map showing how the companies support and oppose each other from a complicated example. Players could work out how to play the companies off against each other to their own ends. You could engineer a Catch-22 situation where it's more profitable for your air force to bomb itself.
    \n
    \n
    \nThe bidding for turn order in the demo did seem overly complicated to me as you may as well pick at random and go clockwise, at least for the first game. You have a number of ways to score soap but what's to stop players from coming up with a TLA and referencing their USP every chance they get? I haven't actually played so maybe it comes out more naturally than I'm imagining.
    \n
    \nKeep it up Wood, I found the writing style amusing but I'm English too!
