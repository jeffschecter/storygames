    I'll get back to one of the core dysfunctions of blorb. Maybe old Forge heads will get a kick out of this:

    In a traditional 90s game the player is tied up with a bunch of rules and restrictions for what they can do, both in character creation mode and the playing-the-game mode.
    The GM otoh just gets a bunch of peppy advice, sometimes advocating straight up unblorby or antiblorby actions. The thesis is that this is a dysfunctional power imbalance. So there are two ways out of that bind. One is to hippiefy. Let the player loose! The other is to blorbify. Tie the GM up the same way the players are tied up.

    The purpose of this isn't primarily fairness but the idea that interacting with entities is more engaging ("tangible") when they haven't been arbitrarily injected or manipulated. I.e. when they are part of the game state and not just smoke & mirrors.
    Obv blorb vs unblorb is kind of a non-issue when it comes to a tight chamber drama where there are pretty much no entities except the PCs themselves.

    Let's look at this:

    In a traditional 90s game the player is tied up with a bunch of rules and restrictions for what they can do, both in character creation mode and the playing-the-game mode.
    The GM otoh just gets a bunch of peppy advice, sometimes advocating straight up unblorby or antiblorby actions. The thesis is that this is a dysfunctional power imbalance. So there are two ways out of that bind. One is to hippiefy. Let the player loose! The other is to blorbify. Tie the GM up the same way the players are tied up.

      July 23
      I think "rulings, not rules" can definitely be hardcore blorb; I've seen games like played this way.

      The key is the "patch and codify" loop Sandra often talks about; the group treats rulings as legal precedents for future rulings, and it forms a strategic landscape for play.
      "Patch and codify" is a fallback; ideally, you use a rule; if one does not exist, make a ruling and a develop a rule if it seems the occasion will repeat.

      "Rulings not rules" allows anything from having a very simple skeleton of rules that you (the referee by themselves or the entire group by group consensus) build upon on the moment as seems appropriate, to assuming that every ruling is henceforth a rule. With the latter approach, the rules/rulings might or might not be written down, but my impression is that they usually live in memory, aside from tables and such which are often written down.

      One end of the "rulings not rules" spectrum is quite close to blorby practices, or maybe even there, though I would say that there is a philosophical difference even then: Whether coming up with rulings on the moment is a good and welcome part of play, or something one should, for the most part, avoid.

      The other end of "rulings not rules" is quite far from blorb.

      My personal view is that this is not a deep issue, but rather different solutions to the same problem. In a convention game, in sporadic online play with strangers, or with a shifting or casual group of players, going full blorb might be less ideal than when playing with a fairly stable group, especially if there are plenty of player-facing rules and procedures that are codified as rules. Hardcore blorb also assumes an effective means of saving and retrieving information.

      The benefits of blorb involve less negotiation about rules during play (since the ruling has already been made and codified, after some play at least) and, at least for some people, a more solid and objective fictional reality. Depending on details, hardcore blorb might also allow elaborate rules-level play (character optimization, using clever interactions between the rules to advantage in play), should the group enjoy such, whereas "rulings not rules" is quite incompatible with those.
