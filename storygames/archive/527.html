Setting Without The Crap

shreyas
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Play Advice

So, lots of games do a lot of work to present you with these bits and pieces of their settings that aren't useful at all to me. I'm gonna use Exalted as an example because it's got a setting I know a little about. But the question applies more broadly.

If I flip to a randomly selected page of the setting section, I find dreary facts, the sort that you would discover in a third-rate self-guided tour book: climate, predominant crops and trade goods, the appearance of inhabitants, distinctive details of dress, and so forth. It paints a picture, but if you're listening to a tour guide, it's the picture that you tune out.

This is strongly at odds with the way it paints its characters, as a sort of confetti mess of unrelated but consistently exuberant concepts - a crippled war hero who runs his corner of the world from his rose garden; a vanished, demon-worshipping, nameless queen; a relic hunter with jewel-beaded hair, searching out the treasures of her past lives; a dragon who has gone mad and wanders the earth as a beggar, forgetting his position and power! These paint a picture I pay attention to, one with energetic people that have complicated, over-the-top lives. More importantly, these vibrant, dynamic parts are what define a setting for me. They're the bits that matter.

What I'm uncomfortable with is that I don't have any procedures to make that second thing. I can make the first lickety-split, by just taking one such guidebook and sprinkling liberally with Asian Buzzword Seasoning (or whatever works for the setting you're in), but it's a lot harder to make characters and mood, and it's mood that I'm really after.

What I wanna know is, how can I give people the tools to build the setting that I want them to build, without actually doing it? That is, to lock down mood and color and even situation structure (this, I think we know a little more about, so please don't focus on it to the exclusion of other things), but leave the dreary details of proper nouns and defined numbers to them.

    Shreyas, I think what you're trying to get at is the difference between evocative and factual description. The latter tries to convey information by, well, actually conveying the information that you'll need and then making you apply that information through a process of reasoning to create a setting. The former, rather, evokes the reader's existing knowledge, so that even if you're not giving them any additional information, you're informing them by telling them what parts of their existing knowledge to draw from.

    (Can you tell I've been looking at role-playing from a cognitive psychology perspective?)

    I think, then, that what you might want to try is figuring out what you want your players to be able to know about your setting, and then figure out what real-world context they'll know those things in. If you can find ways to remind them of this - the formal term being 'evoke those schemas' - you'll be getting their whole set of associations with whatever they already know, from characters to mood to plots to, well, you name it. Then you need to find ways to shape and guide them so that they can discard the inappropriate bits of those models, and to show them how to combine the different models that you're using.

    This may just boil down to what Ben was saying, but hopefully some idea of why this works might help you figure out, concretely, what to do.

    If you can come up with a specific example of what you want to convey, I'd be happy to kick around some concrete ideas; I have no idea how usable this notion is in the abstract.

    Shreyas, let me suggest this: there are two approaches to published-setting-creation. The first is to present a long list of facts and expect the players to create connections between them.     This is a little whacky, but I started thinking about structural anthropology (Durkheim, Mauss, Levi-Strauss). One of the core ideas there is that most societies can be understood by determining the oppositions that define them, that the oppositions create a conflict that things like stories, religion, clothing style, how you cook your food, etc. try to resolve.

    Now, if you take for granted that most rpg settings are little more than stories bulked up with fact steroids (climatology, geography, etc), could you provide players with societal conflicts? This flies in the face of the keep situation and setting separate, with setting being a constellation of 'ur-situations' which setting mediates. In other words, you establish setting-specific obsessions that in play and character creation get developed in certain ways.

    In structural anthropology (esp. Levi-Strauss) the tension emerges because the opposition can't be resolved conceptually but that doesn't stop people from trying and asserting to the contrary. So, maybe imagine somethin like this for setting creation:

    1. Select set of oppositions (law v. chaos, sun v. moon, dirty v. clean, dry v. sticky, etc)
    2. Correlate oppositions (law and moon v. sun and chaos, etc; don't end up with just two sides but a set of two sided correlations)
    3. Select mediation zones (clothing, war, hut building, travel, etc.)
    4. Provide sample mediations (different sorts of legal business associated with different phases of the moon, warfare carried out in light of day, law at night, twilight seen as dangerous time, when law and chaos meet, law seen as something to be carried out in secret)
    5. Encourage players to work with sample mediations, allowing mechanical elements to do so--some way to 'invoke' the setting by way of the oppositions (a simple-minded example: it is night time and players 'invokes' the lawfulness of the night to add dice to their roll to apprehend the man who killed a child; allow them to narrate why this is so--i.e. "our elder gives us a holy whomping stick to bring justice" or "the moon follows the killer, a silent accuser, and sows guilt in the guilty") (another imagined invocation by players wearied by a long hunt: "those who travel by night do so in justice's name, and so rest houses dot the landscape, giving succor to we who right the wrongs")

    I feel like I can go on and on, which makes me wonder if this might be a nifty little trick.

    Is it possible to give players a methodology for building the relationships between things and then, after that, constrain the building of those things to guarantee that they're things that fit the aesthetic? How?

    This seems twice as hard because there's defining the relationships between setting-things, then building setting-things into that framework, two challenging tasks. But here's what comes to mind. (Tell me if I'm getting close.)

    First, let's define relationships between setting things, in abstracted terms. Let's pretend the only things that matter to our setting are Justice and Fairness. Maybe we need things like:

    • an impossible, potentially religious ideal of fairness
    • a logical, academic sense of justice
    • a real-world backdrop of justice as implemented
    • two corruptions of said backdrop, one internal and one external
      ** the internal one must come from the opposite of the fairness ideal
      ** the external one must comlement the extreme of the fairness ideal
    • both must interface into sets of guidelines and lines of duty, from the larger community into the family
      ** these must contradict each other

    So this is part of a map. Now let's provide some core seeds into the setting, maybe like group activities to fill in the blanks to help build things out. So perhaps like these:

    • Fill in the parens: "There is a terrible (accident). Many people are unable to help, and (innocent party) is in grave danger. A person of (which of the above affiliatons?) is able to intercede and save this person; however, this draws the ire of (a member) of (which of the above affiliations?)."
    • Choose one: "Between the internal corruption and external corruption, there is a covert alliance / overt alliance / morbid history / glorious history / brewing potential."

    And then, filling in more setting-things into the framework. Quick version: everyone gets a pool of Setting-Thing points, and they buy factions and elements and histories into the game, and it is cheaper to buy a fact into a node that is supported by more of the relationships we've clarified.

    Add color, bake, and you're done! Except maybe Shreyas came up something that looks like the Wuxia Champions, where as I've come up with something more Gunslingers in the West. But, aside from color in itself, it's quite likely that our settings are pushing the same core aesthetic drive.

    Shreyas asked:

    So, first, you asked for an example - let's take mythological Polynesia. I don't know this place from a cat's behind, so I'm not gonna get myself all silly-looking telling facts about it, but I know how I want it to play - There's lots and lots of water, but a sense of "it's just over there", a sort of vacation effect since we are temperate people telling stories about Paradise, and a tangible awareness of the odd spiritual forces that run under the world.     And now, here's the tough nut to crack: Without giving players knowledge, how can I stop relying on their knowledge? I guess that's a roundabout way of saying, "I have these schemas and I don't want to endanger people who don't; can I build them into a game in a structural rather than declarative way?"

    The way I think about this is that it's like playing a backwards game of Family Feud. You have to come up with the objects and events that will cause people to make backwards associations to a certain world and how it works.

    For a mythical Polynesia, for example, I'd start by associating things that "seem" Polynesian to me. Long and lazy days, jewel-like lagoons, tall palms and other lush vegetation, roughly carved stone statues, things built of natural materials, tribal structures among the islanders, complicated rituals involving naming and menstrual purity and keeping down the souls of the hunted beasts. While I don't think those are the only things you could choose, those are the things that evoke my own Polynesia schemas, based on things like the Mutiny on the Bounty and my vague memories of Margaret Mead and The Golden Bough. You could do an informal survey of a few people to see if you're drawing on the appropriate source material, and if you're drawing on source material that's popular enough that players could reference it either explicitly or unconsciously.

    Then you want to arrange those things in some way that evokes the kinds of stories you want to tel. Like Vaxalon and Isbo were suggesting, this has to do with the kinds of conflicts that you're setting up. The "game elements" I've listed above are nothing more than static information, by themselves. The question becomes how you can arrange these pieces of information, which evoke certain schemata, into dynamic situations which also evoke schemata. For example, if you were going for a horror feel you could write about why the seemingly-happy inhabitants don't go out of their houses after dark, and introduce the various rituals that they use to protect themselves. If you were going for more of an island-paradise feel, you could have the conflicts be about things like who gets to be in which ritual - conflicts which pretty clearly indicate that the scope of the necessary conflicts are pretty narrow, giving a feeling of contentment.

    Then you can start building concrete conflicts on top of that, using the kinds of things that other people have suggested. Modeling conflicts concretely is a good way to support players who don't have the appropriate schemata, because everyone can reason from examples. You can use some of the principles of case-based learning to do this effectively, by providing a series of cases which slowly build up significant differences from each other. So you might begin with two cases which are somewhat similar (such as a conflict between two PCs and a conflict between a PC and a villager, but about the same topic), then extend further examples to have one or two major elements changed in each further example. This will allow people who can't use their schematic reasoning to use more inductive methods to figure out what you want just based on examples.

    Hey Shreyas!

    So, schematic vs. inductive reasoning:

    Schematic reasoning is when you reason using prior knowledge that you already bring to the game. For example, the classic schema that's always used in cognition classes is that of "going to a restaurant." You only have to evoke the schema in a very bare-bones way (for example, sitting down to eat, paying, and leaving) for everything else, from the presence of waiters to the etiquette of tipping, to come to the forefront of the reader's mind.

    Inductive reasoning - a term which I'm using informally, not in the inductive vs. deductive logic sense - is when you're presenting a series of examples and asking people to reason, from those examples, about future situations. Basically, you're presenting everything you want players to use in their reasoning in the material you provide, which makes this a lot more time-consuming than schematic reasoning but also more accessible.

    I wouldn't throw out case-based reasoning either, though. It can be badly done, but when it's well-done it's nothing like fact-diarrhea. Imagine that you want to educate the person reading your game about the kinds of conflicts that you're trying to design. You could try to evoke the kinds of conflicts using a schema that relates to real-world or media conflicts that players may be aware of (Dogs does this really well). Or you could present a series of examples of different kinds of conflicts that you can have (an approach that Vampire often takes, where you get a list of "here's a conflict, and here's another, and here's how you might apply them in a new setting or in combination"). Or, if you took a case-based approach, you would actually use specific cases to illustrate the underlying principles of the conflicts you want to get players to introduce. This doesn't have to mean a lot of random setting material that's thrown at you! In fact, it means exactly the opposite. Every single example is meant to introduce a new element, and to illustrate the ways in which players can combine and reuse those elements. I'm not sure how clear that is, but that's the best I can do right now.

    In other words, it's kind of like concrete examples which build on each other, but you know, you'll actually be able to find relevant research on the topic if you're searching for "case-based reasoning" rather than "um, examples and stuff."

    Really, these techniques are often at their best when used together. I'm just trying to analyze my own process and separate out the different cognitive techniques that are relevant, in the hopes that it'll be useful to you.

    JD:

    Using these techniques, particularly the schema technique, can actually help you make a game more versatile with less fact-diarrhea. What it sounds like you're trying to do is to help players reason in original ways about the setting. ("I want to do this . . . but with PIRATES!") A mental model or "system understanding" (not in the technical sense of role-playing system, but in the sense of how different parts of the diegetic reality function) is much more flexible in terms of original reasoning than fact-based understanding. The presence of those facts may possibly be helpful, but it's even more helpful to be given tools which tell you how to figure out what any possible set of necessary facts could plausibly be.
