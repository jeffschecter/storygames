\n There's a developing fashion, I think, to release different versions of your game under cutesy titles.

For example, to publish my game "The British Pulp Exploration Company", I release "British Pulp Exploration Company: First Voyage" at GenCon. Then, next year, after a bit more playtesting, I release "British Pulp Exploration Company: The Early Years". The year after, it's "British Pulp Exploration Company: The Power And The Glory". And so on.

So here's the plan.

When someone publishes a game like this, let's ask: Is this a finished game?*

We'll probably get a happy, dismissive answer. Hey, my group's had a lot of fun with this game, so I just wanted to publish it and share the love, you know? And if people want to play it and give me feedback for the next version, that would be awesome.

Then let's ask: Are you publishing this as a finished game, yes or no?

And if they still don't answer, the default assumption must be that, if money's changing hands and there's no warning the game is unfinished, it's a finished game. So then we can go ahead and review it on RPG.NET, under the title "The British Pulp Exploration Company", and say it looks a bit unfinished.

What do you think?

(Although I'm using my outspoken voice for this post, these are opening remarks about something we ought to discuss, rather than an invitation for a fight. It's just clearer this way. Otherwise, every sentence starts "Sometimes I wonder if..." and "Does anyone else think that..." and everything gets muddier.)

Graham

* Note: for ashcans, the answer to this will be "no", and that's fine.

    \n Hey Matt and Jason - I hear you and respect your opinions on the matter. And I speak only for myself on this, but here's why I'm publishing Annalise the way I am:

    * I want to showcase a number of different artists in the book. I haven't been doing too well with this because of my personal slowness in getting everything together, but between Jenn, Elizabeth and a couple of other artists that I'm working with right now, there's a ton of amazing art inspired by the game, too much to fit in one book. If a potential customer is struck by one persons art over another, I would like them to be able to have that.

    * I like giving people choices. Most people seem to buy the red cover, but I've received compliments on the white cover as well - since I can provide both through lulu at no increased cost to myself, why limit that choice?

    * Cutesy names create atmosphere, and the production of the game is all about atmosphere. The system itself is relatively content-neutral, so I'm relying on the book itself to convey how I like the game to play without dictating a specific set of actions to players.

    *Other than the Eternal Tears PDF edition (which I think is fairly clear that it's a PDF version of the game) and the Interim edition (which is always available through lulu), the different Edition names are for convention releases - the Seven Sins edition was a short run of hand-made books for DexCon, and the Unbidden Guests edition is what debuted at Gen Con in 2008. I think it's a more significant marker of where and when the game was released than "1st edition" "2nd edition" etc.

    *Honestly, I'm pretty sure that the atmosphere (including cutesy titles) is part of how the audience for the game self-selects. Sure, it rubs me the wrong way when someone who doesn't understand tells someone else something about the game that isn't true, and when I see untrue statements about my game posted on the internet I feel like it's within my rights to correct them, but I guess that's what I have to deal with.

    *Finally, none of these version are revisions, they are all the same, finished content. It's a choice of production and presentation, or depends on when and where you encounter the game, but nobody needs to buy it twice.

    Just wanted to throw out some reasons for choosing this path.

    \n I agree with Matt and Jason. It's annoying to try to divine the publication status of a game from its name. This is especially annoying when I end up retailing a dozen copies of some game that is actually an ashcan that will be republished in a year. This is even worse from my viewpoint as a retailer than just publishing a shitty and unfinished game - at least in that case I can just sell the shitty and unfinished game instead of having to sell something that has a "best before" date and shouldn't even be in retail.

    On the other paw, I like the idea that an artist can organically and honestly express his development in the publications he makes, instead of trying to artificially keep up a veneer of productization. Obviously I don't find my own decision to call my Solar System booklet the "2008 edition" annoying; I find that the choice to do that helps me emphasize the idea that the game is an on-going process and tradition, one where somebody might or might not publish further editions one day.

    As far as I know, Ben Lehman invented the funky naming convention. I did not have trouble with Polaris in this regard because Ben was very clear to me that the onehundredone brothers edition or whatever the first one was was already a finished game. (I also think that we didn't know to be afraid of unfinished work in 2005.) With Bliss Stage Ben screwed himself quite nicely by naming the first edition "Ignition Stage" and declaring that he would be publishing a more comprehensively illustrated color edition in half a year. I think I bought into a preorder at the time, too. It took me about year and a half to figure out that Ignition Stage was actually the end-point of Ben's design process and therefore could be said to be the "complete game". I have to imagine that others have suffered from an equal level of confusion, which must have been a part of why the game hasn't sold as well as we expected after Polaris. I'm still a bit leery about buying a large stock of Bliss Stage for retailing purposes, as Ben might get inspired to finish that new edition at some point.

    In summation, I don't hate what Ben and others do - I think it's nice and sort of fun that they can exercise their independence by reflecting their individual, unique creative process in how they publish. I do this too, nobody can claim that it was smart to publish my new edition of The Shadow of Yesterday with a new, different name. I imagine that I'd be annoyed with myself as a customer, but still I gotta respect my own freedom to mess up however I want. I'm not paid enough for this to make me optimize my marketing, and I imagine that the same goes for Ben and Nathan and others as well.

    \n Just as a point of clarification:

    I don't think we'd release a new "edition" of Mist-Robed Gate with a new cover and new layout and art unless we were also releasing new content.     \nYeah, finished, hand-made. And the distinction is rather important because most people purchasing an RPG these days expect a tidy little perfect bound book, not something that looks like a medical file.

    I'm 50% sure that for every Graham that's confused by the title, there's someone who sees it and thinks, "Unbound? No thank you. I prefer my games bound." I much rather Graham didn't purchase the game than sell it to someone who's going to be pissed because it doesn't fit next to Dread on his bookshelf.

    I'm 75% sure that for every Graham that's confused by the title, there's someone who sees it and thinks, "Unbound? What's this mean? I must investigate further." And as this someone investigates further, they can't help but to learn more about my game. Which is a win for me and a win for them.
