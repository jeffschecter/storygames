    Posted By: bunnypunditI'm not sure if I see the difference between furry-fandom and non-furry-fandom, whatever that is. I have yet to meet an official and stamped member of the former, though.
    \nFrom what I've seen and heard, furries are a very fractious fandom, and there's not a lot of consensus even among them (especially among them) what exactly it entails. However, among English-speakers who use the internet, you really can't use the word "furry" without people assuming you mean stuff that fits into that fandom. Mouse Guard is at best on the fringes of what would be considered furry, and Yuuyake Koyake is even further out, more a kemonomimi game than a furry one. WRT Robert's question, my perception is that furry stories tend to involve furry characters exclusively. A setting with one or two animal-type races but plenty of humans doesn't really qualify for the label, and IME furries usually turn their noses up at manga kemonomimi style characters (who have the ears and tails of animals, but lack fur, snouts, reverse-articulated legs, etc).

    I think you've pretty much listed all of the story games that can be considered even remotely furry. You're trying to find the minute overlap between two small niches, and while I wish you the best of luck, the only actual furry RPGs I know of are all traditional ones: Ironclaw, Albedo, World Tree, Furry Pirates, etc.

    Surprising how nobody seems to have answered the question yet. The Questing Beast is essentially Pool with layers of rules for the specific genre. It's modestly furrified - characters are specifically described in animal terms and get attributes based on what animals they are, there's some discussion of why to color the story with animals, and so on. I find it an ambitious effort at making the Pool act as the system for a quite specific genre; especially the rules for chronicling your character's chivalrous exploits are sure to be important if the players can buy in to the aesthetics of the system in the first place. I'd say that if a group can get behind the lightweight, fiction-first set-up of Pool, they'll like TQB's implementation as well. For those who are looking for intricate and complex system, though, the game might feel like a disappointment.

    \n Trollbabe really puts in high relief what the furry genre lacks. There's a distinction between character, which is how a figure acts within a story, and characterization, which are the superficial traits of the character. In Trollbabe, you play a half human, half troll woman in a world where trolls and humans are in conflict. The nature of the trollbabe automatically puts you in place of making character decisions, with the characterization of horns, strength, and gender being characterization. In furry stories and games that I know, you play a samurai with bunny ears or a supergirl with fur and a tail--all characterization, no character. What I would recommend is taking any of the existing furry games and then changing the world so the nature of the PCs puts them into an existing conflict. Eg the Cat clan vs. the Dog clan, etc.
