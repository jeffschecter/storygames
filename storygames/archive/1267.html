Situation: a stab at the hidden gorilla

James_Nostack
September 2006 edited September 2006 in Play Advice
Folks have been talking about "Situation" lately, and how to build 'em. Here's what I'm thinkin', and I'd appreciate feedback:

A good situation is: a status quo, a threat to the status quo, and a reason to care. (I'm aware that somebody, maybe Ron, defined Situation as the intersection of Character and Setting. This isn't helpful to me conceptually or practically, so if I've gotten it wrong, someone should step in.)

A kicker in Sorcerer meets the definition: here's what the sorcerer has been up to lately, here's how things have changed lately, and here's why drastic, immediate action is required. A town in Dogs in the Vineyard is a situation: there's a utopian community seething with resentment, demons find an opportunity to cause harm, and the Dogs care because it's their job. Clinton's AG&G Oracle isn't a situation; it just provides some randomized, evocative images, which players then use to create a "backstory in motion."

It's easy enough to adapt this to non-RPG narratives--Star Wars, Watchmen, the Iliad, etc.

I suspect that a good situation involves a threat that really hammers the issues implicit in the status quo. The reason to care--i.e., the spur to action--can work on two levels: why the character's got to do something (which is usually a question of plotting & character development), and why the audience should give a damn (which probably involves emotional pandering, to a greater or lesser agree).

Rip this to shreds. I'm sure it's too simplistic, but hopefully it's a baby step in the right direction.

    James, you seem intent on this status quo/context being changed thing. While, yes, a lot of stories and situations do incorporate this motif, it's not a required element. There is no big status quo/context being threatened in Slaughterhouse Five or Persuasion. Redefining situation in terms of conflicts (which are themselves defined as character + desire + obstacle) gives you a constellation of characters, desires, and obstacles some of which may be the threatened status quo, but not necessarily.

    FLFS Situation Engineering goes something like this:
    a) gather player flags (such as thematic batteries)
    b) create three conflicts using two or more flags each (loyal + colonist = starving colonists plead with PC to steal military supplies for them)
    c) figure out five "cogs" (characters, sets, props) that are necessary for each of the three conflicts (for the above: starving colonist, food, supply depot, guards, colony)
    d) complicate the cogs by overlapping them (the starving colonist from one conflict is also the resistence leader of another conflict)
    e) assign one complicated cog to each PC as a foil
    f) write up the cogs with stats and stuff
    g) write a short paragraph (~200 words) to describe everything going on, including the way the PCs are made aware of the situation

    For what it's worth, Judson, Sitch Engineering was my attempt to take Dogs Town Creation and make it more universal. The same procedure can be used regardless of setting to create many-layered situations that directly engage the PCs. You just need an appropriate input for the flags -- which can be lots of things.

  • Josh Roby
    September 2006
    Oh, James, I'm going to have to violently disagree with you on "situation for all media is essentially the same."

    In novels, film, narrative poetry, and things I'll call "planned works," situation is a rather cynically constructed juxtaposition of elements that the author(s) plan to use to forward their predetermined purpose. Consider Gulliver's Travels -- there is nothing in Lilliput or how Gulliver gets there that does not directly serve Swift's purpose.

    In roleplaying and other "unplanned works" like improv and television serials, situation is a juxtaposition that the creators expect to yield interesting fiction. Touchstone here being Battlestar Galactica or Buffy, where the creators certainly had an idea of what they wanted to see to start with, but certainly did not plan out the entire scope of the series before they shot the first episode. The idea here is to seed the situation with enough 'oomph' to provide drama, comedy, and excitement for as long as possible.

    Note of warning: Babylon 5 is a novel expressed as a television show and constitutes a notable exception to my point, here. On the other hand, Great Expectations is often considered a novel but is, in fact, a collected series of chapbooks and is closer to an unplanned work.

    In planned works, situation is a pretext. In unplanned works, it's a playground. Very big difference.

    "It's stuff waiting to happen."

    Yeah, that's a good definition for situation. Stuff waiting to happen.

    Everybody thinks in terms of conflict, because it's pretty definitional of drama. Want dramatic play, have conflict. Thing is, not all play is all drama all the time. Some of it is just noodling around with setting elements or whatnot. Exploration with an intent to create theme, even if it's done sans conflict, is still thematic play.

    So, let's say you have a player come up with a ritual in the game related to the central religion that's been set up as a key part of the situation (oh, let's say we're all cardinals in the vatican or something, making religion pretty central to the situation). The nature of the ritual created will be a statement made by the player. Like if it's some devil-worshippy thing, the player may be saying "look at how corrupt the Vatican is!" or "isn't it fun to consider this sort of juxtaposition?"

    The point is that resolving bangs or other conflicts is not 100% of the value of RPGs. It's key to pacing and keeping drama part of the equation. But there's a whole lot more subtle stuff going on than just the conflicts.

    So I think what you're doing here, James, is talking about how to get drama into play through embedding it in the situation. Which seems like good technique to me. It's just not the sum total of situation.
