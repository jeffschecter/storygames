    Elektra
    Changeling MUSH

    Anybody who knows me even slightly knows that I am a disturbingly huge fan of irony. Elektra was a childling (a kid) redcap (monster) who was raised by a shadow court (evil) sidhe (noble), rebelled and then set herself loose on the world. I played her on at least four different Changeling MUSHes, at different stages in her history, which means I got way the hell more authorial control over most MUSH characters, as I was able to "write in" what happened between games. Elektra was totally built exploiting as many rules as possible, and was a holy fucking terror for anybody who dealt with her. Playing her was always an experiment in seeing how much I could get away with -- and she got away with a lot. Her volume knob was stuck at 11. The thing of it was, though, that all her volume knobs were cranked up high. Even when she was biting off gangbanger's hands or throwing cold iron dust into the noble freeholds' balefires, it was this four-foot high kid that was bringing down the high and mighty or making adults blanche and fear.

    And her hair was fire. Like Hades in Disney's Hercules movie, except I did it first, thankyouverymuch.

    Karl Vicente Ricardo ("Kavri") Tan-Yan-Doo
    D&D20 Modern

    Kavri was the son of my Shadowrun character, a Filipino-Chinese physical adept named Jose Tan-Yan-Doo. Jose didn't get much development. (In the game, we all played children of our Shadowrun characters. The system was mostly D20 Modern with some D&D 3.5 thrown in.)
    Kavri was also based on a character concept I'd come up with several years back and meant to write a story for. I never got around to it, but I toyed with the idea of making him in D&D and experimenting with a Fighter-Druid combo. My GM let us use the Unearthed Arcana Gestalt classes, which let you basically advance in 2 classes at once, which made the concept more viable. He also let me throw in lycanthropy, which was part of the original character concept. I like experimenting with different kinds of characters, and Kavri was an interesting challenge.

    I had fun playing Kavri for several reasons. One was that my mental picture of him involved being outgoing and laid-back, which is not true of most of my characters. Also, I kept thinking of ideas that fit with the general concept but hadn't been fleshed out before. e.g. I decided midway that he's looking for a girl but is intimidated by girls who are too tall! (He is not tall. Filipino genes.) Then other background explanations got nailed down mid-play, like why he was a lycanthrope (artificial insemination baby; his two fathers didn't check on the mother well enough...) and why he tried not to show it. (his fathers didn't like being reminded of his birth mother!)
    Also, the game had lots of fun tactical situations, and I got to use Kavri's various abilities to good effect.

    Edit: I'd like to add that for the game, I jettisoned whatever backstory I had. I only kept the core of the character--personality, ethics, abilities, looks.

    My GM actually told me that he really liked how Kavri turned out. He hasn't done that before.

    Zebedee James Johnson
    aka "ZJ," "Z-Dawg," and "StreetJarl"

    Werewolf: the Apocalypse LARP (Mind's Eye Theater)

    He began as a poor inner-city black kid just trying to do the right thing. When I stopped playing him four years later, he had become a fierce warrior, leader, and father.

    Highlights of playing him:
    • learning by example to become a killer;
    • befriending a pathetic geek of a kidnapper who really just wanted a werewolf to bite him so that he could be one too;
    • adopting an inner-city street gang lifestyle as the only human environment that would accept him anymore;
    • falling in love with another young Garou and fathering a Metis child who died after four agonizing weeks;
    • countless clashes of wills with other Garou, culminating in his rise to Alpha of the Sept;
    • almost being killed by a powerful enemy, and waking up to the loss of his left eye;
    • finding a healthier (yet tempestuous) love with a Kinfolk woman, having a daughter; and
    • Being captured by the risen zombie of a police officer he had killed, who held him in Attica State Prison for months before his rescue.
    Why was he so much fun?
    • Mostly, pure escapism. Where I am rational, he was driven by passion. Where I am a wimp, he was a tough guy. Where I am intelligent, he was dumb as a brick (and all the more lovable for it).
    • The soap opera of relationships in a long-running LARP remains one of my favorite reasons to use the form. I've never before or since played out a romance in-game, let alone two. Add in the rivals, mentors, friends, charges -- it was remarkable.
    • Empathy. I'm a middle-class white guy. Pretending to be an inner-city black kid got me reading about real-life race and poverty issues. I have a lot more understanding and respect now for social issues that I dismissed before out of ignorance. The same goes for vicariously becoming a gang member, teen father, and inmate of the prison system.

  • rafial
    February 2006
    1st ed AD&D

    This was during my first disenchantment with D&D. I had mostly stopped playing in favor of Champions and Traveller. I wanted skills. I wanted point buy. But I had a friend who was trying to learn to be a GM, and he wanted to run D&D. He'd run a few short lived and disastrous games previously, but he was starting up again, with a limited set of players, all who I liked. So I said, yeah, sure, I'll play D&D.

    Only as usual for those days, I didn't want to play one of the standard characters. I was reading "The Gateless Gate" at the time, and grooving on it, so I had this vision of a wandering Zen monk in my head. Only he wasn't martial arts monk guy ala-AD&D. He was just a guy who wandered around and wanted people to just get along. Plus AD&D Monks were impossible to roll up at the time...

    So I rolled up a fighter. And I said, he's just this big dumb farmboy that got sold a bill of philosophy by a wandering monk. No, he doesn't know martial arts. No, he doesn't have any kewl powerz. So he made himself a staff, and went out into the world to spread the world. I called him Joshu.

    He fought (when he had to), with a staff. He never wore armor. He never threw the first blow. He hooked up with a Paladin of Bridget, and a couple other characters I no longer remember (I think there was a mage?). He and the Paladin had long, loud, acrimonious debates about theology. They explored a cave system full of kobolds. He almost died fighting some sort of flightless raptor bird in the dark. Against all odds, he survived first level. The campaign lasted several months, and was a resounding success.

    I was still disenchanted with D&D, but it was very freeing to realize that my character choices didn't HAVE to be dictated by what was "best" under the rules.

    This is a hard one, as I tend not to continue playing characters I don't enjoy playing. I'll go with:

    Theodore Carver (aka Brother Carver)

    Wraith: The Oblivion LARP

    In life, Carver was a drug addict who espoused a new New Age philosophy every time he woke up. In death, he's a jaded, self-appointed, would-be messiah. He's an experience junkie who has seen it all. He's come up against some of the largest antagonists in the setting and sees himself as having come away unscathed (though he's wrong about that last bit). Now he's setting his own personal challenges, both for himself and others (whether they like it or not, as long as he thinks it is what they need).

    He's engaged with the setting and its assumptions to a greater degree than any character I've ever played.

    He's examining and challenging what it means to be a wraith. He plays paternalistic mindgames. He's changing assumptions about good and evil.

    ...and, when I don't feel up to roleplaying that sort of intensity, he has a built-in pressure gauge. He can have one of his infrequent flashbacks to his life and seem very confused for a bit.
