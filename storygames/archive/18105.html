\n I just found this today over on DrivethruRPG and I am charmed.
I'm not a good enough writer to put in a full review, but I am kind of excited by this game.

A refreshing look at dungeon crawling, where the mazes are on derelict star ships and forgotten cities of an ancient civilization long obliterated my a deadly mutagenic plague. The remnants of the ancients, hideous monsters and horrors, roam these old places, resenting intrusion. Great treasures of unknown technology and value are scattered about for the taking.

Whether I play as written or use another set of rules, this is an awesome game and setting. I kind of think I like the idea of using some kind of AW/DW/OSH hack like I put together for my Fallout game that didn't happen.

I don't know that old school dungeon crawling quite qualifies as story game, but the idea could be, I suppose.

    \nOne of my favorite old-school dungeon crawlers, almost a retroclone years before retroclones were cool, is a little game called Monsters and Mazes by John Garwood. It's got a full set of procedures for generating dungeons down to the last dead end corridor, and I loved the hell out of it.

    So I knew if I was gonna do a dungeon game I had to include some actual generators for designing the maps, but I wanted them to be a bit quicker and easier to do quickly. The first playtest dungeon I ran I think took me all of an hour or two to prep. Easy peasey.

    Can you (you=anyone) tell us more about the cool, genius bits of this game? It sounds really interesting!
    \nWell, I wrote it, and I can't say as "genius" was necessarily a goal, though I am quite proud of the results.

    My favorite things about it are these:
    * Really easy to pick-up mechanics that feel old-school but without the counterintuitive bits that make real old-edition D&D a struggle to learn
    * It's SF, which frankly always interested me more than fantasy ever did
    * It's still D&D gameplay, which means it still has dungeons and monsters and loot, which I like, instead of throwing them away because they aren't "serious enough" for SF.
    * Tables. So many tables. There are enough tools in this book that as a DM you could literally never have to come up with an idea of your own, though you'll probably want to flesh the results out a bit more.
    * Some of the monsters are just friggin' wicked.
    * I finally managed to find a way to elucidate why and how free-roaming sandbox gaming works in a relatively straightforward way.
    * It's house-ruleable as hell. The simple mechanics and straightforward class design means you can literally design new classes and other bits for it in no time flat, and there's even notes in the book on how to do this.

    H&H is probably my favorite game ever, of course, because it's a game I wrote to be the game I wanted. But it seems so far like it's going to be a lot of other people's favorite, or at least a favorite.

    \n The system and sector generators are sooo cool. Honestly. They're full of stuff about generating possible plot hooks that are actually relevant to explorers, giving cool details and backstory for prior civilizations and such to riff off of.

    The dungeons, as mentioned, include some pretty simple and straightforward rules for generating a bunch of different types of dungeons, from space stations, to pod colonies, to abandoned ships (hulks).

    I'm also proud of the ancient loot system, where you can actually generate ancient weapons and armor with unique stats and silly names for out-there technologies.

    The GM advice focuses heavily on the idea of premise vs. plot, that a DM's role is more of a scenario builder than a writer, and the importance of not wasting time trying to create linear stories and instead setting up worlds for the players to muck about in at their whim. I think it's a well articulated explanation of that style of play, but it might come off a bit ranty at points.

    It's also got a lot of cool stuff about alternate rules and stuff in the DM section.
