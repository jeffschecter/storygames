    Woah one Andy K is calling out another Andy K! Show down!

    Actually I've been meaning to make a post about this game, and the wider problem I have with historical games, but this thread will do.

    The game was a few weeks back at a convention, the GM was fairly experienced with Heroquest and obviously quite passionate about the time period. This proved to be the downfall of the game. I'll just add that my problem was not with The Game itself, per se, but with the game we played, so take it with a pinch of salt.

    Hope it doesnt come across as a whinge, but I think it might be symptomatic of a problem with many historical games - too much history!

    The GM spent half an hour describing the intricacies of HeroQuest, before breaking down every painstaking historical detail and context about 13th century Lithuania for another half an hour. An hour down, and we only just began.

    The characters were all interesting. We were all nobles dispatched to broker a peace on the borders between the pagan lord and his teutonic order neighbours. Good setup, ripe for conflict.

    Except everytime someone asked a question about the game or suggested doing something, it was accompanied with about 10 minutes of historical detail and context ("obviously this is all in vain as in 50 years time so and so invades ..." or "well you know that what really happened was ..."). Needless to say this got tiresome, and this coming from me, an ancient history graduate!

    This came to a head later in the adventure when one of the other players, who was clearly digging the detail and the period, announced that because of the historical context of the adventure, "even though it would be 50 years too early", he was going to try and ride to Poland to get the king to come and save the day. The rest of us just rolled our eyes as this prompted a wave of "well this could happen early if this or that happened, but in reality blah blah blah."

    Finally, because it was late, the adventure wrapped up really unsatisfactorally with one of the players doing something I hadn't realised anyone could do - firing an arrow into the sky that turned into a bolt of lightning to smite the baddie! I knew it was mythic, but my character sheet didn't suggest anything magical!

    Anyway, so gripes aside, I'm sure the game itself is good when run properly. But, I think this is a wider problem with historical games - there is a tendency to treat history as invioable canon that has to be adhered to at all times, to an extent that wouldn't be expected of any other genre or setting. I've certainly found people reluctant to play historical games because the history has a tendency to get in the way. Anyone else found this?

    It's inspired me to make a Roman story game that somehow precludes this sort of play, but not sure how yet! Maybe just a big stick ...

    Our group has run a number of pseudo-historical games, and I think we've addressed this problem in different ways. In the first- a "weird WWII" game with classic horrors influencing the events of the war- our group's ground rules were that anything goes as long as it can plausibly be denied, covered-up, or otherwise glossed over by historians, leaving our modern-day understanding of history intact. With that we were able to have zombies running rampant in Normandy, Cthulu-esque horrors in Dresden, and Japanese gods at Manzanar.

    The other game- set in a turn of the century, post-Martian invasion America- was rich with historical elements, but ran with the the agreement of the group that history had diverged with the invasion. So while WWI still occurred, it began because of the race to control captured alien technology.

    I suppose that's a long-winded way to say that it really comes down to agreement among the GM and players about how history is to be used in the storytelling.

    I think another issue here is tied into Jesse's musings about games that excite you around the game vs. those that excite you through the game. Oh, and the old issues of metaplot.

    Historically most historical games have had systems in which the excitement about history had to come around the game, because the system didn't provide a lot of the details of history through the game. You'd do you mass of research, set up your intricate maps, plot out your million historical NPCs, and then hit the ground with a game system that was mostly about how much damage your weapon did and how many feet you could move in 3 seconds. Which, for the record, isn't always bad, as some historical players have tightly focused games of small unit military history. But, for many it does put the emphasis of the system on things that aren't many historical players main goal.

    Now a game like Heroquest can do a good job of fixing this for people with a specific set of historical goals. People who want to explore the tensions and conflicts of history through the individual human angle should be able to get a lot of mileage out of HQ through, rather than around, the game system. Really, for many historical periods it should be possible to hard-code a lot of the conflict into the character sheets.

    Playing the Albigensian Crusade? Set up characters with high scores in their "faithful Catholic" and "devout Cathar" traits, combine it with relationships between all the nobles going back to the early Franks, give Arnaud-Amaury a "callus bastard" of 15w4, tie the players to both Carcassonne and the French King and.... let it go. Players acting on the traits on their sheet should be able to make some real statements about history, really get in and feel the passion and tension and brutality that lead to the slaughter, the inquisition, and the breaking of the Occitan realms.

    It seems to me that is one of the places where Andrew's GM went wrong. He didn't use the system to try to get at the important parts of history (possibly because those decisions weren't the important parts, in his mind) -- he tried to get at that all around the game. His excitement had nothing to do with playing the game, it had to do with talking about the setting and so his emphasis wasn't on ways to bring out the setting in the game, it was just on talking about it and getting excited about it almost seperatly from the game.

    As for the whole 'well that wouldn't happen until 50 years..." Yea, that sounds much like the problem that everyone has with metaplot. Can you change it? Can you not? All the standard answers apply the same here, I'm thinking.

    I'm finding this Brand-Max dialogue kind of fascinating, because for me the whole idea of history is to impose themes/narratives on seemingly unconnected facts. It is precisely the sort of history books you read in high school that like to reduce everything to conflicts between X and Y, and the sort of premises being described as 'trite.' And while real historians are not usually quite as reductive, they are mostly seem to be engaged in the same activity -- making stories out of real world events. Meanwhile, another impulse exists -- to faithfully represent what it was really like, through exhaustive reference to primary sources and a rigorous denial of the human desire to frame the facts.

    So really, it's like we're just transposing the same two impulses -- both to my mind completely 'historical' -- into roleplaying.

You can't extract meaning without details, and it's impossible to isolate details from the context that gives them meaning. That context will always be constructed, it doesn't matter if you're standing in the middle of history or standing outside it.

    Which is where I have a problem with the quoted bit above. You are after the fact. Suggesting that this makes that type of roleplaying more plausible is disingenuous -- it's just as hard to have a roleplaying game where you pretend to be 'in the moment' of history as it is to write a history book that completely avoids framing 'moments of history'.     Comrades!

    Coming belatedly to this fascinating discussion, let me just make a few points:

    1. The problem with historical gaming is often that for some the emphasis is on the former, for others, the latter. There are those who enjoy the intricacies of verisimilitude, and don't mind accepting a degree of railroading of themes, outcomes, etc because it still gives them a greater feeling of immersion than watching the History channel. Then there are those who are in it for the gaming, and for whom, as has been said, the key thing is that a historical setting can offer colour, perhaps in a new and intriguing palette. Neither is right, neither wrong. However, the first variety really does require a high level of 'buy in' that you are more likely to find in an established group that has made a consensus decision. A con game, where players ought to be able to just pick up their characters and play, and where there will generally be a finite time slot, frankly suits the latter. IMO, of course.

    2. Ironically, I didn't get to run Mythic Russia at that con, in part because of other commitments and in part simply because I wanted to allow myself _some_ time to play and chat. However, I did write and run a big (41 player) Mythic Russia set freeform (LARP) game, Rising Tsar. Each Russian character received a single side of paper with a bare-bones outline of Russia in 1382 and another on 'Russian Ways' (superstitions, ways of address, etc) really just to ease them into character. OK, some characters with particular specialisms such as clergy received more, but even then I kept each such source to a single sheet of paper. Then, after a brief introduction, the players were unleashed. They really were amazing, and while on the one hand did work themselves into a Russian idiom, they also came up with far more weird and wonderful stuff than I could possibly have scripted. The moral, I would suggest, is that very lightly sketching the outlines can be at once stimulating and permissive for the players but nonetheless still give a game a very different feel evocating of its setting. After all, while what happened in the game proved very different from how events unfolded in the real world, (a) the players seemed to have fun and (b) it was still very much within the dramatic context of the real world challenges facing Russia at the time, something they all seemed to take on board.

    3. I'm not in any way criticising Andy's GM, who I know is a keen HQ/MR GM -- and, as a university lecturer, I'm always keen to see people reading history! All I would say is that my own experience of teaching and hopefully enthusing is to start with very broad and colourful brush strokes and then, as far as the needs of the class or lecture allow, find a way of tailoring the paedagogic lessons you want to impart to the interests and enthusiasms of the audience. Some will appreciate detailed analysis of historiographical debate, others want to hear all the gory rumours. What is entirely certain is that trying to force-feed detail into a class that hasn't already been primed to want to learn it, is as futile as, say, trying to force a gamer who wants to play a game instead to enjoy listening to an episodic lecture...

    All the best
