In another thread, Brendan mentioned:

I want to see the episode where, since they can't run the same mission twice, the characters have to sneak into the traveling facility to warn themselves before they went on that disastrous mission last week.

What other classic time-travel tropes are there? What not-so-classic but really-really-cool time travel stuff do you think would be fun to do in a game? What are interesting limits and paradoxes beyond the obvious (meeting yourself, altering history) and what are your favorite ways of handling those things?

Full disclosure: I'm running a Nobilis game where time travel has somehow become a major theme, and I would love cool new tricks to add. However, I also think this would be useful in general, since doing new things with an old genre (like the time travel story) is always good.

I'll kick off with a couple of classics:

- The PCs are stranded in the past (or future) due to a device malfunction.
- The PCs are accidentally sent to a time other than the one they intended to go to.
- The PCs encounter and aid themselves.
- The PCs encounter and hinder themselves.
- An apparent deus ex machina occurs, which the PCs later go back and arrange to happen.

Other ideas?

    In the finale to my Stargate game, the PC's chased after rogue time travellers into the earth's past, but weren't able to stop the rogues from changing the future. So when the PCs returned to the present, the galaxy was over-run by Mongol aliens. Fortunately the Golden Khanate, had discovered a way to combat the forces otherwise laying siege to the PCs original timeline. So they stole the information, went back to meet their earlier selves who were about to head to the present, and gave them the info, with instructions to contact a strangely cooperative alien a year prior to when they first encountered him, to have him construct the weapon. Then the PCs who stayed stopped the timeline change, and went to the future to see if things worked out. Fortunately things did work out, and quite a few other things had changed, the longest lived PC recieved a note from his other self, written to him by a third self, who (presumeably) was from his own future. The note told him to go to another point in the "objective" future, but no reason was given. When the PCs went forward again, the found themselves greeted by strange ships, apparently expecting their arrival, greeting them as fellow time travellers.

    Yeah, the time travel hurt their heads too.

    In short, time clones can be fun (if your cosmology works that way), and the best deus ex machina aren't blatant, but subtle. It's best to look at the events so far, and look for any lucky breaks.

    For more bizzare time travel tricks, take a look at Primer. "Sure you can drug your earlier selves and take their place."

    Simon Hawke's Time Wars series of books started off with temporal terrorists, who were trying to alter the past. The first several books had the heros dealing with them. It was nice because it kept things from being too easy, with the heroes having access to future knowledge and tech, but the terrorists having it as well.

    Later in the series, a "timestream split" occurred, which created a parallel timeline. In the temporal physics of the series, that was considered an unstable situation -- it could last for a while, but eventually, one timeline or other would become the "real" one, and the other would cease to exist. This led to a full-scale time war, with each side trying to manipulate events in the other's past so as to cause more timestream splits (the idea being that destabilizing the other "branch" of the timestream would make their own be more likely to be the one that survived).

    At some point in the series, there was a villain who believed that a stable timestream split was possible, where both timestreams would continue forever, and was trying to cause one.

    Two things.

    1. One way you could play "time-cop" stories (see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternate_history_%28fiction%29 for a good summary of what has been done with the "time-cop" trope) is kind of the reverse of the usual player/GM relationship to plot. Usually the GM has a plot not known to the players and must (to some degree) wrestle them to keep them on track - at least, that's one model of GMing. With a "time-cop" story, the players know what the plot is supposed to be - and the GM throws them curves as they try to get it to be like that.

    2. Has anyone else read Connie Willis's "To Say Nothing of the Dog?" Great (and hilarious) time-travel yarn, except really it's a historical romantic comedy with time-travel as a background. In her setting, you can't land at a place in the past where you're already present and you can't land within a certain distance (temporal or geographical) of a truly pivotal historical event - for example, nobody can get within years or hundreds of miles of Waterloo, because relatively small changes would have made the outcome of the battle quite different (apparently). When I say "you can't land" I mean the universe, or the laws of physics, or something prevents it - you can try to land there but you will be "deflected". So you have to make your way through time and space the old-fashioned way at least part of the distance to get to any really significant, pivotal event, and this gives plot room.
