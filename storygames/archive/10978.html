    \n I only played like twice, and it was your standard mystery ending in violence that is probably 98% of RPG play. I understand that the setting had plenty of Romantic Fantasy tropes, and I recall some solid advice about how the character arc differs from your standard "action hero mythic journey" and what characters can do besides kill stuff and the sort of emotion-centric buy-in that everyone at the table needs to have, but beyond that, it was pretty much D&D (and we didn't have any sailing or pirates). That is, you could take the advice from the book and pretty much just use D&D. Mechanically there's little in there to encourage or promote Romantic Fantasy beyond what any other game offers.

    \n I do not know Blue Rose, so I can't tell what is wrong with the game, but it sounds like the author is giving the players a method of play with too narrow a scope; he is stuck in the old mechanics-thinkinig.

    I've had great experiences with my (unpublished) game Romanse! (Norwegian for romance)

    The game focus on friendship, romance and intrigue, with violence limited to the rare duel for honor. It's all about dancing, conversations, etiquette, gossiping, social and political alliances, and flirting (of course). The characters can be declared "social dead", but no physical death is possible (all duels are as much a duel of wits as of swords).

    My idea is that "mechanics" don't suffice in making a great game of dramatic interaction, so I gave Romanse! a method (mechanics AND storytelling/leadership in balance, with hard rules for both) that really help the players into the romantic mood and moves. The lack of violent solutions is one of the premises in it. Wanton murder is a killer for romantic notions, so I kicked it out. Defining dancing and conversations, and other central interactions, as tough conflicts with hard consequences of skill-use is another premise. No need to fight for excitement, when dancing gives it to you.

    \n I've only run a single game of Blue Rose even though the World of Aldea sourcebook was one of very few settingbooks that actually got me interested. Anyway, in preparation for it I got myself a Mercedes Lackey book for cheap, read it to confirm my old prejudice (and add some new ones!) and then made pre-gens for my game. All in all it turned out pretty good, but as you say, I wouldn't contribute it the system in any way. Most important was probably talking about the tone and feel of the game/the session before hand:"It's kinda Disney actually, but without the silly and more melodrama..." (or something along those lines)

    When I made my pre-gens I took a look at what generated Mary-Sue points and put as much of that in there as I dared, right down to basing all the characters names off of my own and having everyone look younger than they actually were (which culminated in Wilhelm claiming that his 30 year old lady noble, leader of the group, only looked about 15...!).

    All in all I'd say it was a pretty decent stab at the genre, which it wouldn't have been if there was no player buy-in. Basically it's just like the old "anime game" discussion: it wasn't romantic fantasy through system at all really, it was romantic fantasy 'cause we said so and tried to follow the norms of the style.
