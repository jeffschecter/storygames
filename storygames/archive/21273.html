\n D&D has ruined me. My playstyle has evolved, but it's forever moored to the traditional wargame. As one of the unfortunate few whose first exposure to D&D was the unfortunate 3e, I've internalized a number of a bad habits, many of which I've curbed, many more of which still afflict me to this day. In particular, one annoying habit is calling for a roll without clearly understanding or knowing the consequences at hand.

In the majority of cases, behavior is exhibited with the players going along without a roll until uncertainty or danger arises, in which case the rutted neural pathways activate, and I blurt out, "Roll X," not really considering the roll itself. What are the consequences for failure? Does it make sense for this to have a binary resolution, or should it be a gradient level of success? What is the appropriate difficulty? Have I accurately communicated potential risks and rewards to the players? Is this scenario appropriate for a single roll resolution, or should it require multiple rolls?

(A rather silly set of top-down concerns, overall, as the most important metric is whether we're having fun, and by all rights, the "sprinkle enough rolls throughout the game to keep things interesting" works for the majority of games and players.)

I'm curious for the rest of you: what are those old habits you've developed as a gamer and how do you modify, overcome, or ameliorate the negative effects of them?

    \n I'm a strong believer in the word NO.

    As a GM and player I played way too many hours of guaranteed success. Because of this I do not ascribe to "the rule of cool." I may allow "cool" so long as it doesn't break anything else. I often say no.

    Sometimes players run up against it and are frustrated. But I'm too loyal to "challenge aesthetic" to back down.

    Oh, and death always has to be a possibility, for the same reason.

    \n I developed some bad habits as a designer.

    \u2022 Rely on dice rolls as uncertainty. I'm having a hard time designing without it. That's why I summed up this list of uncertainties so I can look at them and choose another way. However, that's not everything, and it's also the structure of the game that affects why we choose to use dice rolls. We design to lead up to dice rolls, and if we design for that process, it's not just change the dice roll for something else.

    \u2022\xa0Thinking of dice rolls as challenges. This fell flat when I designed a game where the heroes succeed with everything. I had to fall back and really think about what kind of challenges there are out there. I found five.

    \u2022 Designing with rewards in mind. Dice rolls are a type of rewards, and it's the most addicting one based on the random outcome of positive results. We have XP, artha, and some poodle points in fanmail, and it's hard not trying to design with that in mind. I prefer nowadays to design interactions that are fun in themselves rather then having to rely on rewards, but it's hard. Really hard. I blame D&D for this.

    \u2022 Thinking in terms of GM-less and dice-less, when it should be the other way around. It happens because "roleplaying game" is not a genre in itself but a subgenre. This becomes really apparent when some people have to describe their games as of what they don't include: dice, game master, acting in character, etc.

    \u2022 Having characters with a certain type of stats. I had trouble breaking away from using attributes or skills, because - again - roleplaying games has a clear process that leads up to using stats (with dice roll).

    \u2022\xa0How roleplaying games are written need to go through a reform. For example, starting with character generation isn't a smart thing to do because the reader doesn't know what a roleplaying game is and how the character is used. I'm stuck on this point for now.

    \u2022 Writing roleplaying games as a game engine instead of a game. Typical roleplaying games are "Put whatever you want together to make whatever you want", but what's really the game part is the adventure + rule system. Before I realized that, I wrote games like D&D.

    \nI don't like there to ever be "one" predetermined way to solve a problem. I also don't like the unwillingness to change and improvise and take things in another direction that the players are going. If the players are solving a solution in a reasonable way, the solution should get solved. The game isn't about simulating the real world where there are 12 ways to solve a problem, but the GM has chosen the one way that the players must solve it, and that's that; it's about having a compelling game with a compelling interaction between the players and the world. It's not how things work in real life, it's how things work in a movie. Sam Harris explains it like this:

    "The sweet spot for fiction is something that has enough verisimilitude to be credible and yet it's a heightened reality which makes it much more interesting then just finding out what happened to an ordinary person on an ordinary day."

    If you lose the heightened reality and the interest what's the point? More than a single hint should typically be unnecessary if the GM has started the premise accurately and isn't in love with their creation. After all, the GM has one mind and one perspective and the amount of times that that one mind and perspective should trump five different minds and five different perspectives is fairly rare. So I would say offer a hint\u2014and maybe repeat this process once but no more\u2014and make sure you stated the premise of the problem correctly and that all relative information has been restated, but if there's no progress in the story at this point then be willing to kill your darlings and adjust, because in all probability it's not your players it is you that has miscalculated. If the process takes more than two or three minutes, I would say you're doing it wrong. IMO, there's nothing worse than a GM who falls in love with his creations; for example, a GM who advocates for their NPC's instead of seeing them as a tool to create a great story and a great interaction.

    \n@ValyrianSteelKatana I don't think your problem is particular to 3E, so much as D&D as a whole, and trad games in general. Unless, perhaps, I miss your point.

    Anyway, I picked up bad habits, too, and once had a hard time letting rules go. Those games taught me well, so well that I've come to be an excellent career bureaucrat. But I've had some success moving on and recovering from gamer baggage.

    Anyway, some things those games have taught me:\n
    • They taught me that survival is the first order of business.
      • \n
    • They taught me that success, at basically any story-based cost, is justifiable.
      • \n
    • It taught me that knowing the rules was the best way to defend myself against a hostile GM.
      • \n
    • Fellow players taught me that one of my objectives is to 'keep up' with the power level of the party. If I build a sub-optimal character, I'm playing wrong.
      • \n
    • It taught me that the 'proper' way to GM was about mechanical challenges, rather than figuring out how to just tell a cool story that engages the characters/players.
      • \n
    • Adventure modules taught me that A happens, then B, and then C. In that order, without deviation. And if the players don't follow in that order, then they are wrong.
    \nWhat got me out was a few years of Primetime Adventures 2E, A Penny For My Thoughts, Ribbon Drive, 1001 Nights, and games like this. Games which simply don't allow you to think about the game in terms of survival, and where success is mechanically governed by the game design, and not by a game master.

    Essentially, my advice would be to try to remove those things you're struggling with from the equation, and you'll be forced to engage and learn new habits, and that can help you break out of those old habits.

    Also, read Play Unsafe. It was also helpful for me.

    Good luck!

    \n I've been finding that anytime I run a game that kind of differs from D&D, but isn't extremely different, we'll hit a block sooner or later. Like, if I host Archipelago, then okay, it's tricky for everyone to learn this new thing, but it's obviously such a different thing that nobody will try to turn it into D&D. But if I run something that has a GM, one character per player, and rolling dice and comparing to stats to check for success of an action, but differs in some other small way from traditional D&D-like games \u2013\xa0like, other players get some input into your character's history (as in many PbtA games), or character death is unlikely, or you as a player are expected to welcome complications or failures for your character \u2013\xa0sooner or later, we'll get stuck.

    So, I have been trying to figure out a way to systematically identify all the axes in which any given game I might run for my group differs significantly from D&D. It turns out there are MANY such axes, so I'm not having much luck. Maybe it'll be sufficient if I can just think about every new game I pitch, "How is this UNlike D&D?" At least then everybody gets a heads up.

    \n I think that, for me, the strongest elements which sticks is something I like to call the "GM Mystery Cult".

    This is an expectation or a tradition common to a lot of gaming cultures I've seen where the GM of the game takes the role of a magician or presenter.

    This means that the GM plays the game but must not ever reveal how the trick is done or show doubt, uncertainty, or waver in any way: the best GM is one who smiles mysteriously at anything that happens and says, "all is proceeding according to plan" (not unlike a B-movie villain).

    I much prefer a more democratic and more transparent style of gaming these days, but sometimes I notice that I hold back information just out of habit - the very idea that hiding information is the foundation of good GMing is seated deeply in there somewhere.

    It always surprises me how much conscious effort it takes for me to keep enforcing my newer habits in practice.
