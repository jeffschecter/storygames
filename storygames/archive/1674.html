    Here's what I like best about my game, Hellball.

    New ideas for task resolution
    Just because I've never seen it before doesn't mean it hasn't been done, but I think I invented something cool. There is no declaration of intent. Players roll dice in very task-oriented situations. The highest roll narrates first (and it has to be relevant to what he was doing) and he has veto power over anything that is narrated after (till the next die roll). Then the second highest roll narrates and can't contradict what the guy before him said, and he can get vetoed. Then the third goes, and so on, till everyone who rolled narrates.

    Essentially, no one declares intent, but the winners have the authority and power to ensure their intent is done anyway.

    It also handles multiple, orthogonal conflicts pretty elegantly.

    A slick tactical system for playing "hellball"
    The athletes in the game have to play this almost-rules-free football variant against demons. I've always hated movement systems in tactical games. Take D&D, for example. One person moves. Then another moves. Then another. What if you wanted to intercept and tackle someone on their way across the field? With D&D movement rules, they could just go around you (avoiding threat zones and thus avoiding attacks of opportunity).

    I solved this with the combination of an initiative system (worst roll acts first) and an interrupt rule (anyone who hasn't gone yet can interrupt anyone in the midst of their turn and take their own). So Bob is about to run the ball down the field for the goal. He can run right past you, just a few squares out of reach. When he has taken, say, half his movement (he's counting out the squares: "Okay, I run 1, 2, 3...") you can interrupt there ("Wait!"), and take your turn ("I run two squares over to you and perform a tackle maneuver on you. Pick up some dice so I can flatten you!"). When you're done, if he didn't get tackled or held or something, he can finish his turn.

    Two GMs, sorta
    I have one main GM, "The Devil," who provides adversity and plays all the bit parts. I have a typical player roll, playing "athletes." I added a third role, "Coach," who has some GM authority but is basically there to help the athletes. He gets a sheeted character (Coach) who interacts but does not play hellball. There's a mechanism to give him some bonus dice that he can dole out for or against his athletes. (He can use them against them to teach them a lesson if they refuse to listen, for example.) I'd love to see how this plays out.

    The rest isn't that surprising: Attribute (Body or Soul) + Skill for both sides, opposed rolls, with a clever little twist about eliminating opponent high dice by taking a punishment (Dave Cleaver gets credit for that). There's a fixed skill list but there are also some freeform traits represented not in numbers of dice but as a virtual die result you can just bring in (and reroll for later, if you want).
