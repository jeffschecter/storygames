    \n What about having it be a mix between a choose your own adventure and a custom move. When the character figures out that its a trap, give them three options. Each option is a custom move with outcomes related to the way that the player chose to handle the situation. Kick it off with announcing future badness. For example, "You start to turn the door knob when you hear a -click!- What do you do? A) turn the knob back to how it was, tie a string to the door and pull it the rest of the way open from a distance, or C) try to break the knob off. Each of those is a move with a set of outcomes. As you design more and more traps, you will develop a small database of potential custom moves for any one type of trap and it will become increasingly easy to design traps for your game.

    \n I think that traps are all about announcing future badness and avoiding announcing past badness. For example:\n
    Posted By: KripplerWhen you step on the pressure platea big fucking axe blade springs out from the wall and you take 5-harm.
    \nThis move announces past badness. You just did something and took damage. Ok. You don't need mechanics for this.

    I think the fun comes when you know the trap is there and have to do something about it. Once you realize that there is a trap, then discerning reality might tell you what type of trap or ways to disarm/avoid/break it. Players should get to pick a course of action. Once the trap is triggered, a custom move should give them some option in how they react to it and what happens. For example:

    "As you walk down the tiled hallway, one of the tiles depresses and you hear a ~click~. What do you do?"
    When you take your foot off the pressure plate, roll+dex. On a 10+, choose 1. On a 7-9, choose 2.
    -You avoid stepping on the other pressure plate
    -A big fucking axe blade springs out from the wall and clips you, take 1 harm
    -The other blade hits you hard, take 4 harm
    On a miss, choose all 3.

    or

    When you put something heavy on the pressure plate, roll+int. On a 10+, the weight allows you to move your foot without triggering a trap. On a 7-9, you add the weight and take your foot off the pressure plate. On a miss, you hear a second ~click~. Now everyone with you gets to roll!

    I have a Thief in my party, and I love the interchange over traps.

    Traps are a threat. I normally write down a line or two about them, like "Scything blades: when you step on the wrong tile take 1d6".

    Traps are part of how you reveal how cool your dungeon is to the players. Activating a trap is usually a move you make as DM when appropriate. If walking into the room activates the scything blades, then you make the appropriate move when someone walks in the room.

    But this is kind of a dick move to spring on the players without any context right? That's why you've been showing them that your dungeon is a labyrinth of fiendish Dwarven Traps all along through Spout/Discern results, showing future badness, and so on.

    The Trap Expert move is just like Spout/Discern, except its focused on traps. Because of the hold mechanic, on a 10+, it can be used to guide the party safely through a whole section of dungeon. On a 7-9 you have to choose carefuly where you use it. On a -6, it's a golden set up for the DM.

    One great way to use traps (as said above) is to provide a set up and then let the PCs find their own way out, like turning the door knob and hearing a click, above.

    \n Tony, as always, is smart.

    I've dealt with something similar, with ambushes. My general feeling (not sure this passes the AW/DW bullshit test, so it's not in the rules yet) is that most of the time, the players have to know what's hurting them.

    If the Thief figures out there's a trap first: done. Now you deal with making moves, spring the trap as a consequence, etc.

    If they blunder into a trap, the goal is still to get them making moves, but it also has to be worse than if they had used Trap Sense or some other ability to spot it first. So you announce it in a really impending way: you hear clicking gears, the floor starts to shake, every hair on your body stands on end, whatever. Now they're still making moves (maybe custom moves) to deal with it, they just start at a disadvantage.



    Now I'm really spitballing, this is the kind of thing that usually turns into an annoyingly long email to Adam, but I'm kind of thinking of all traps, ever, as the Trap Move:

    When a player character triggers a trap, announce its impending badness, then ask them what they do. Use the trap's moves as appropriate. When a non-player character triggers a trap, it just happens, use the trap's moves freely.


    Traps could be written up a little like monsters:

    Dwarven Boulder Crusher
    A classic of dwarven engineering, usually used to break down quarry rock it can also break down bone, armor, and just about anything else. Two huge teethed rollers crush anything that falls into their pit. When used as a trap, it's usually rigged to a nearby trip wire or pressure plate which starts the rollers. Sometimes a tarp is laid over the pit to disguise it, but the sound of the rollers spinning up is usually a clue.

    Level x?

    Vibrations - When the rollers reach full speed the floor shakes like a dancing bulette. Everyone nearby must Defy Danger.

    Shrapnel - When something big falls into the rollers, it sprays bits and pieces at high speed everywhere. 6 damage to anyone who gets hit.

    CRUNCH - When someone falls into the rollers, they take 10 damage and end up beneath the rollers, in the junk chute. If a friend drops everything to save them, the friend rolls+Str. On a 10+, they both end up prone next to the roller pit. On a 7-9, the friend chooses: follow them in and split the damage, both ending up in the chute, or give up and let the trap happen as usual. Anyone who falls into the rollers can jam them temporarily with an item. This holds the rollers for a bit, but destroys the item.

    \n That's a neat trap, Sage. I made up a couple of different traps and found that they worked great with the thief's special moves. It felt very dungeon crawl-y, more so than any game of D&D I've ever played. The traps became relevant and interesting instead of dumb obstacles that slowed down the game. It did give the thief way more screen time than anyone else but we worked that out over time.

    One of my traps caused a huge flood of water and people caught by it got a special move:

    In a raging torrent, roll +Con

    10+ You are shaken but safe

    7-9 Choose one:
    You don\u2019t lose a valuable item
    You don\u2019t take damage
    You don\u2019t take -1 ongoing until you warm up and rest

    All three on a failure and you end up in Tanat Czichamot's reflecting pool.

    This started out as a "In a slab avalanche..." move, but never got used when they were in the wilderness, so I re-purposed it. One of the things I like about DW is how easy it is to recolor stuff and use it in a new way.
