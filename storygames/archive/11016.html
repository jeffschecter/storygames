\n So something I've been toodling around with for a while now is a fun and simple RPG that is a cross between Red Box Hack 2 (because I love the simplicity and beauty of its system) and, I dunno, some of the dungeon generation stuff similar to Tony's How to Host a Dungeon. RBH2 with the old classes added back in plus a bunch of adventure generation tables, essentially.

Primarily the new thing I'm hoping to bring to it is an escalation of talents and powers, mapping to four different game booklets, harkening back to the Basic/Expert/Companion/Master sort of thing. The idea is that players pick their classes and have a small pool of basic talents to choose from, while the GM has a basic toolset for a simple dungeon. Once they graduate to the next booklet (four levels or so), the players have a newer set of more powerful talents to choose from, and the GM has a toolset for a wider countryside and some regional intrigue. The third booklet will elevate the next set of powers, and the GM will have an even bigger scope, etc.

The talents I've been brainstorming have a little less "oomph" than the RBH2 talents, but it's pretty important to me that they continue to have a strong impact on class flavor as much as mechanics/combat. I really like Eric's insight that the class talents should really be about what sets that class apart from the others, not just about what that class happens to be better at.

So I've been brainstorming a lot about what sort of things "define" each class, and I'd love some help with that. In fact, I'd love some help with any or all ideas regarding the whole thing - I'm pretty new to this whole game design thing. I plan to release the game for free when I'm done, in the same spirit as Eric did with RBH2.

The Fighter - warrior/barbarian type, who strongarms his way through adventures.\n
  • skilled with weapons
  • natural leader in fighting
  • lover of life
  • understands danger
  • physically powerful
  • resourceful
\nThe Magic-User - studious mage, who manipulates the world at whim.\n
  • strange and mysterious - big hats
  • knowledgeable
  • "connected" to something greater
  • pouches of components
  • books and staff
\nThe Cleric - monk/holy man, who is a calming or inspiring presence to all around.\n
  • spiritual focus
  • power against undeath
  • clout with the Gods concerning healing and death
  • ascetic (unusual restrictions)
  • charismatic and inspirational
\nThe Thief - clever cutpurse, who can do sleight of hand and climb any surface.\n
  • hands are always busy
  • hidden weaponry
  • ability to climb anything
  • magical and mundane "gadgetry" - always able to pull something out of their pocket
  • naturally able to avoid authority
\nThe Dwarf - stocky underground type who knows stone, axes, and hammers.\n
  • Stubborn and naturally resistant
  • Crafty - in the artisan sense of the word
  • Devoted to heritage and history
  • Lover of precious metal and brews
\nThe Elf - woodsy and fey, the elf can both fight and do magic.\n
  • Stealth and mystery
  • Power with the bow
  • Understanding of nature
  • Touched by immortality
  • Companion to animals
\nThe Goblin - Mischievous and hungry, the goblin is troublesome but lucky enough to get out of it.\n
  • Small size
  • Mount-friendly
  • Knows dungeons
  • Tricksy
  • Good at minioning
\nI was really struggling with the halfling as a class, I liked the idea of the third non-human being small-statured, but I honestly couldn't think of very much to set him apart. I decided it might be interesting to swap him out with a Goblin class, because there's so much more to work with there.

Anyway, any ideas on cool flavor I might have missed that would go well with any of these classes? Any brainstorming on what would make a cool talent? I know that Yet Another Quick-and-Dirty Fantasy RPG isn't exactly breaking new Story-game ground, especially when it's just a tweak of an already existing system, but any thoughts or encouragement would be greatly appreciated.

    \n If you're thinking of combining this with dungeon generation, how about talents that affect generation?     Holy cow, coming from you, that's high praise! Thanks, you've been a real inspiration for it.

    The big goal on my end has been to create something that's intuitively and graphically understandable, I've really been approaching it from a graphic design perspective; which is a great method for those who want to maintain system simplicity.

    The game's still very Red Box Hack-ish at it's heart. The biggest changes (besides going back to D&D-ish classes) is a more detailed (and optionally card-based) combat system and a much looser Awesome Point system swiped from PDQ#.

    \n Hey I think the problem is fonts. I opened up your pdf in Adobe Acrobat and found that your file has a lot of embedded fonts. If you don't have those fonts installed in your system already, then for some reason it slows down the graphics processing in Preview a lot (but it doesn't have the same effect in Adobe Acrobat for some reason -- it's strange, some files like this one open faster in Adobe software, but most others open faster in Preview; I don't know why). I de-embedded all the fonts from the file, which only reduced the file size by about 0.6MB, but it had a huge effect on the speed of loading in Preview. Now I can load it and read it just fine. Have a try at altering the fonts you use or changing other aspects of the embedding settings.
