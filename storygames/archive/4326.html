Yeah! Editors! When I went through the development of Shock: 1.1, this was a big deal. I knew I had to get a real editor \xe2\u20ac\u201d someone who was going to invest time and energy into my text in a way that I couldn't.

\n

Rich Forest agreed to do it, but my constant refiddling prevented me from working with him or even communicating clearly. I have no doubt in my mind that he's a good editor, though.

\n

I eventually wound up using two separate editors. Ben Lehman knows the game about as well as I do and helped me say how the game works, since he knows how it works. This is a big deal! Thor can edit Burning Wheel not just because he's an editor, but also because he knows the system intimately and knows what Luke is trying to say.

\n

And speaking of Luke, he said to me once, "Have a rules editor and a text editor," and it's a fine, fine idea. So I got myself one in the form of my brother in-law.

\n

My text editor was Cauley Green. Read his writing. He writes good words! And then he puts them in a pleasing order! He's also excited about editing more games. He's value-added in a significant way, too: he's interested in fiction-generating games, but doesn't have any serious history with them. If you want someone who's a non-gamer to be able to read your text, he's an excellent test subject who will give incisive and helpful feedback.

\n

I'd also suggest that material change hands for any editing you have done. It can be money, services, whatever; an agreement without, in my experience, tends to make the project drift down the priority scale. So I advise that you pay Rich, Ben, Cauley, Matthijs, whoever you use. You have money and/or talents that your editor probably wants.
