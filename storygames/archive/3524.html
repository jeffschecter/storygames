\n So, I have this idea for a new game that I'm really jazzed about, and I think it'd work best as some kind of GM-less type game, but I have relatively little experience with such things. Basically, I've played The Shab-al-Hiri Roach and read Polaris, Contenders, Drowning & Falling, and Cranium Rats, and I really want to avoid being too derivative of any of those (though a Roach-esque approach to scene framing seems appropriate for my purposes).

I know GM-less games are pretty diverse, but I'd appreciate pretty much any insight into how they're put together, what works and what doesn't. In particular, I'm wondering about games where players each have a primary character versus games that essentially divide up different aspects of GMing/storytelling among the group.

    Well, here's how I've distributed authority in my games, Under the Bed and Shock: Figure out what authority is needed. Make sure those authorities don't contradict each other within a single player. If they do, give one authority to one person and a different authority to someone else.

    \n

    For instance, in Shock: you are only responsible for one of these at a time: circumstance and unlikely saves; driving the world against the Protagonist; the Protagonist's struggle against the world.

    \n

    In Under the Bed, there's a sort of light competition that happens, and it's encouraged by the person who just had to do the hardest thing now getting their chance with the stick. But they're not responsible for determining the actual degree of the challenge, except in the most abstract way.

    \n

    So my recommendation is, figure out what authority is required, then distribute it in the way that works the most smoothly to generate interesting conflict while avoiding conflicts of interest.

    One thing that I think gets missed a lot by people new to the concept of GM-less games (which is nobody here, I know) is that really the term GM-less is misleading. What you often have is a game where all participants are GMs, or at least are expected to perform some or all of the duties and wield some or all of the authority (as Josh was saying) that a GM normally does in a more traditional game. This is kind of the approach I'm taking in The Year We All Died (since we were just talking about that over on the forge). In that game each player has the same authority, powers and responsibility. Although the kind of things you have authority over are a bit different from what a player (GM or otherwise) might in another game, this is still very much a game where everyone is a GM.

    Jake
