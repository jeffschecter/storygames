    \n I have photography business cards, in the hopes that someone with game designer business cards would want to take mine and get some art from me.

    The first time I picked out photos for my cards, I picked out the ones I thought were the most awesome and most representative of how awesome I was; I only included a couple shots of stuff like food, kid pictures, normal portraits.     \n After seeing friends or strangers with

    Calling Cards
    Business Cards
    Game Designer/Game Business Cards (that is, just says their name, the product, a URL and an email)
    Game ***Player***/***Gamer*** (Asterisks = "!!!!!") Calling Cards

    ...well, I want some too.

    Here was me at some Cons this year:

    "Oh, you want to talk to me about a potential project or event? Cool! Oh, shoot, you have no notebook and pencil. I've got my business cards, but they're for my Real Job, so if I give you this card you'll get home and won't remember why you wanted to talk to a Technical Engineer at a major computer storage company. Here, take my biz card, I've got a pen, and on the back I'll write "Maid RPG", and my REAL email address for reaching me, and perhaps a note about what we were talking about".

    After doing the above like 10 fucking times per con, I said "fuck it, I'll design some calling cards".

    It'll list some projects I work on by URL, my email addy, and somewhere on the front or back a square area or line where you (or I) can write down what we were talking about, so you remember what you wanted to contact me about when you get back to wherever your home/computer is.

    The alternative is tiresome and repetitive. Plus, I can decorate it with pretty things like ancient sumerian temples and hello hitty reliefs.

    Hell, I'll even dig up those old Victorian social rules about which corners to bend on the calling card to indicate what the importance of the situation was. Some of that shit needs to come back.

    -Andy

    \n If you were a freelance game designer it'd be utterly vital that you had cards. But since many/most for-hire guys I know actually do normal non-nerd work as well, they typically go for the understated "writing" or the slightly swankier, yet enticingly ambiguous, "writing and development." Give it a non-gamer and you can do the handwave thing and say "oh, you know, creative ideas. That's what I develop...maybe into screenplays or novels."

    On the upside, designing and printing cards that say "game designer" appears to be *the* most efficient way to ante into the designer status game. No need to have actually designed a game. No need to keep up with a blog! Genius.
