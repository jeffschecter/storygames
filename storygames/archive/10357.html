How do I make conflictless solo scenes interesting?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nSimon_Pettersson \n\n\n
\n
\n\nSeptember 2009 \n edited September 2009 in Game Design Help
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n So I have a game and sometimes there are solo scenes, that is, scenes with only one character in them. In these scenes, the character is often building or scavenging something (it's a post apocalyptic game). Let's say the character builds a car. This car will have a mechanical effect later in the game and these scenes are meant to be some slow buildup and establishing scenes. There's a roll to see how good a car you build, too.

The problem is, it's very easy to just say "I build a car" and then roll the dice and end your scene. In fact, it's very hard to sit and describe how you're building the car with the other players just listening. That sort of monologueing doesn't come naturally.

Think of those montage scenes in movies where you show lots of clips of the car getting built, until it's finished. How can I get that in a game? I suspect I should get the other players involved in the process, but how?

    \n I specifically have a number of scene types in the game I'm currently working on.

    Vague scenes allow players to gradually build up their resources. Minimal risk for minimal gains, these are your montage and background building scenes.

    Unfocused scenes allow the players to start applying their resources to the story. Moderate risk for moderate gains, these are your story building scenes.

    Focused and visceral scenes really kick the game into high gear when conflict is nigh. High risk for high gains, these are your climax scenes.

    Players can choose the type of scene they want to engage in, and in each type of scene, the other players can contribute to the scene framing. The more dilemmas they throw into the scene, the harder it will be for the active protagonist, but if they succeed, it will prove all the more spectacular.

    Just some ideas...

    \nGM-Free games usually aren't, really. Instead the duties of the GM are either shared by all players, or replaced by rules mechanics that drive play instead. I think the advice you got about the GM asking questions is a solid one. Perhaps you could find some way to reward the other players for asking sensible questions instead? Something like a small reward whenever the controlling player chooses to answer a question because it makes their scene more interesting. You'd probably also need to limit the amount of questions to keep the ball rolling though.

    I like the concept of scenes with specific purposes myself. The game I just finished working on used a similar mechanic to help build tension and mistrust amongst the players. Works pretty well.

    Posted By: Simon PetterssonThink of those montage scenes in movies where you show lots of clips of the car getting built, until it's finished. How can I get that in a game? I suspect I should get the other players involved in the process, but how?
    \nWell, why not have all the players collaborate to describe the montage scene verbally? This is a fun writing exercise to do in general, and it works just as well with more than one person: just take turns contributing a single image or moment of action. Try to build up a slow but steady rhythm, go around the circle once or twice or until the primary player decides to cut the scene... and then roll.

    You could weight the descriptive responsibilities somehow too, so that the primary player does a mini-monologue and other players contribute single details -- or borrow a page from Archipelago ("describe that in more detail") and have the other players call out questions that the primary player responds to in order to build the scene. This way the other players control the level of detail and where that detail is focused, as well as providing the primary player with constraints & prompts to play off of in their description.

    \n Does the thing they build give them generic bonus dice or are more like a trait - bonus when pre-described conditions are applicable?
    \n
    \nIf they were a trait-like, you could use this scene to build in more specific "parts" of a thing: "I build more armor on the car so it could steamroll fleshy things." "I work on a nitro-booster so that we could get some extra speed when we need it" or "I paint it the war-colours of my tribe so everyone would know who they're messing with." This way, at the end of the day, you get a thing with some feeling.
    \n
    \nOf course, to work properly, this sort of thing would have to double as some sort of suggestion system... "I want to steamroll fleshy things" "I want to get into a high-speed chase" "I want to boss people around" and such.

    \n Montage scenes aren't just about getting the car built. They're about the character putting everything they have into building the car. So, if it's a technical character, you'll see them scanning through technical manuals to get everything just right. If it's a weapons-based character, you'll see them mounting rocket launchers on the car.

    And you've got to struggle to get the car built, too. There's got to be heat and physical work and grinding mechanical things.

    I think you're right. There are actually conflicts involved.

    \n Simon, are you familiar with Mechaton? In Vincent's mecha battle game, different dice are associated wit different stuff. Red dice are weapons (I think) and blue dice are targeting equipment (I think) and so on. If you had something like that, players could roll before or after their description and then use the result or the things they wanted to add to their vehicle to provide some structure for describing the improvements they wanted to make.

    So you could do like:

    [rolls, get a certain result]

    "Okay, well that 4 on the yellow dice means I get an upgrade on my engines, so... Hans crawls under the rig and finds that the turbo booster has fried completely, so he takes a wrench and carefully takes that piece out. Totally blackened. Could have killed them if they'd tried to punch it. But replacing it is going to be no easy matter. He looks around the garage trying to figure out what to do. Then he remembers there's that old junked street racer at the dump, one that looked like it might still have something useful in it, so he walks down the street..." etc.

    Otherwise, coming up with something unstructured, without any sort of guidance on what kinds of parts or what features the player works on... yeah, I can see that being really hard.

    \n I'd go for colour and reflection: I want lots of details and I want to hear what the character is thinking. Is he humming a tune? What tune? Does that tool he is using remind him of someone or somewhere? This could even lead to a flash back scene. Or is he worried about a coming confrontation? A simple rule for encouraging colour is to give a bonus for each of the five senses that are involved in the description.

    You could also go for conflict and building tension. You could take this so far as to cross cut between building the thingy and using the thingy. Or make it a race against the clock, cutting between the approaching doom and the construction work.

    Posted By: JDCorleyIf you want to skip over them quickly then you have created good montage scenes.
    I disagree most vehemently. Boring montage scenes are boring. The endgame of Fiasco is awesome, and it's just a structure for a montage scene. (Basically, for each die you have at the end of the game, usually about 4 dice, you get one "cut" in a montage that tells the epilogue of the story, constrained by the events in the fiction to that point plus an oracular paragraph about how good or bad the outcome is for your guy.) The trick is to have something to say for which a montage is an appropriate expression. So sure, "I build a car. I roll a 20. The car is cool." is boring. But:
    Car-builder player: "OK, I'm building a super spy car. I roll a 20! So this car... [points left]"
    Player 2: "That car is a junkyard Camaro, but under the dust, the hood decals are still mint."
    P3: "It makes a sweet roar when you finally get the engine back to life!"
    P4: "I look at my watch - it's midnight, and you're installing a nitro bottle, like you don't even know what time it is. I get on the other creeper and kiss you under the car."
    CBP: "And together, we install the sweet James Bond rear-facing machine guns?"
    P4: "And the oil-slick generator!"
    P3: "Awesome! The ejection seats, right? Test 'em with mannequins, whole team is there the next day, high-fives all around!"
    P2: "Bitchin' Camaro! Scene?"
    ALL: "Scene!"
