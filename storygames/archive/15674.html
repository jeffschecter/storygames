    From the infancy of the hobby, the entire concept of character leveling itself is a part of that gold mine. You see it in a ton of games apart from Prince Valiant. Legacy stuff from Pendragon or Microscope, writing a piece of history in the Dictionary of Mu, making maps in Geiger Counter or On Mighty Thews; there are hundreds of examples where either individual creative input or tactical prowess is "stamped" and given a sort of permanence or reality.     \n I am not the biggest Skyrim fan, but my experiences with it and other sandbox games are informing how to prep for a dynamic sandbox game.

    Things to keep: Random events can make interesting emergent stories, unstable situations made more unstable by player interaction, keeping a consistent world, and repeat after me...emergent stories, don't aim to tell one, just let one happen as the players interact with the game and the setting...oh and maps, and history behind everything is cool.

    Things to change:
    the world moves at its own speed, sometimes the player will have no choice but to be swept up in events. Example: The Civil War in Skyrim, add a damn timer to that, have things happen beyond their control, see how they react to it.

    Focus, make sure everyone is on the same page of what they will be doing. Be upfront with what interest you, the GM, and have players tie in their motivations to it. Seriously, if you're into exploring caves and have elabrate structures to explore and delve, and the players want to run a blacksmithing operation...there is going to be some dissonance. Skyrim is a game, so it can get away with forcing you down a path.

    Also...consequences, when I sacrfice people to Dark Gods, damn it! The game world should respond with more than quest complete, here is some token XP, and YET ANOTHER magical artifact.

    Finally, the world exist as its own entity. It is not leveled. Sometimes, discretion is the better part of valor.

    \n@Josh : Here's how I do it in our current Stars Without Numbers game.

    There are a bunch of opportunities on the table, most in the form of in-fiction missions being offered by various factions. They look like this:

    Recovery/Rescue
    Lost Vessel: Ithaca
    Last known location: Neptune, Triton orbit
    Reward: 15,000 credits
    Contact: Issac Njembwe, Oasis, Mars
    XP: 2,000

    Privateer Charter: Terran Mandate
    Wanted: Vessels for active combat duty
    Reward: 25,000 credits per deployment
    Contact: Major Katherine Ford, Terran Mandate, Paris, Earth
    XP: 1,500 xp per combat deployment

    Scout Report: Mining Surveys
    Universal Dynamics Corp.
    Wanted: Survey scans for uncharted areas
    Locations: Asteroid Belt, Mars, Jupiter
    Reward: 5,000 + credits (each)
    XP: 750

    There are 21 of those in play right now (organized by location and faction). SWN has a faction mini-game that the GM plays every in-game month, which determines which factions prosper and which suffer, and those outcomes make new opportunities appear and others vanish. Plus I just create some new ones when I get a new idea.

    I use the news feeds to keep the group up to date on solar system events (the cold war is about to get hot, so there's lots of crazy reports about warships being mobilized, for example). This is usually just a short snippet or two whenever they have some down time in transit or at port.

    My NPCs are also free to take on contracts, so I sometimes slip tidbits into the news feeds about another ship completing a job, or getting blown up, or whatever, just to keep the world feeling alive. In the last session, the PCs ran across an NPC freebooter crew who had accepted the Terran Mandate privateer contract and were flaunting their new status.

    Maybe that covers it? Ask questions and I can say more.
