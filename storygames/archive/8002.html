\nI'm going to post a couple of "under the hood" posts about different systems. If you like the format, I encourage you to do the same!

I'm reading through Dogs and trying to get a better handle on the system, and a couple of questions came up:

1. In some situations, there is no advantage to escalating (except inflicting more serious fallout).

For example, let's say we start talking, then go into a fistfight, during which I pull out my gun and hit you over the head with it. Since I've already rolled all my Stats, and my dice for my gun, I gain absolutely nothing by escalating to gun-fighting. (I'm pretty sure I got that right.)

My question: is this a quirk of the system, just an odd thing, or is it a deliberate design feature? If it's deliberate, what's the idea behind it?

2. You're more likely to get experience from talking than you are from getting into fights or gunplay.

Again, quirk of the system or deliberate design? If deliberate, why?

Thanks!

(I've only played Dogs once so far, and I'm getting ready for a more serious game, a prospect I'm pretty excited about.)

    \n Getting more experience from talking is deliberate design, I think I remember Vincent saying somewhere. Well, he might not have said it, so much as grinned slyly when someone mentioned it.

    I can't speak to original motivation but I can talk about my experiences playing. I find that "rewarding" talking with more experience encourages two things:

    1) Players start out talking more often but inevitably end up escalating to violence. Without the encouragement to talk, players might just start out with their guns.

    2) Players are encouraged to take tons of Fallout while talking. They grin like they're getting away with something by taking 6d4 Fallout in the early stage of a conflict, before they start escalating to shooting in the face. The Fallout ensures that characters change.

    \n I don't know what Vincent's intents were but in my opinion:
    \n
    \nTalking fallout is great. It encourages people to talk first instead of going strait for violence which will almost always inevitably come. But talking fallout means taking the blow in talking conflicts. Taking the blow means selling your opponent's action. So if I say, "admit you are a sinner." And you take the blow with 6 dice for 6 talking fallout, you have to show how my words words affected you in a meaningful manner. Now that might mean the obvious and you admit you are a sinner. It also might mean that you become full of doubt, "maybe I am a sinner?" Talking fallout doesn't kill your character. But selling taking the blow in a talking conflict is insanely powerful. Admitting you are wrong? Changing your beliefs? Agreeing to actions you don't want to do? You decide how to sell it but these are no small consequences.
    \n
    \nI have mixed feelings on escalating. It gives you more dice to stay in the game but your opponent also gets to escalate as well. It's a great way to hide dice. So if your opponent sees you have a bunch of crappy dice (all with a value of 3 or less), they may raise you with a low value, say a 1 and a 3 for a total of 4 which allows them to save their stronger dice but also protects them from you since you don't have any single results that can see that raise with only 1 die, thus reversing the blow. BUT if you escalate, you may end up with dice that can indeed reverse the blow, giving you a huge advantage.
    \n
    \nMost people I know do not like this, but I do! I like using the Escalate rules from Afraid (horror version of the Dogs rules). When you escalate, not only do you get to roll more dice but you get to ignore someone's Raise! It makes a huge difference and has led to some of my favorite roleplaying moments. Before, sometimes we would escalate, roll a few extra dice and depending on what we rolled it didn't have very much impact. But ignoring a Raise and then attacking right back... that's pretty sick and tends to sell the fiction better for us.

    Posted By: VincentUnpopular Ideas for 2006 #1
    These aren't dangerous ideas - in fact they're quite safe - but they're good ideas and maybe they've fallen out of fashion.

    1. Have stats
    Yes, I mean strength stamina willpower charisma education and savvy, or whatever combo your game calls for. Probably your game calls for some.

    Know why? Because stats imply arenas of conflict, and they differentiate both a) the characters, in terms of which arenas of conflict they're fit for and b) the players, in terms of the details and wrangling of your reward rules.

    Remember how important the fitness of a character is? Stats are a clean, easy and functional way to make fit characters.

    But check this out, let's call it 1a:

    1a. Make your stats tricky
    That is, don't name your stats after the arenas of conflict in your game - in fact, don't line them up 1:1 with the arenas of conflict in your game at all!

    It probably doesn't make any difference to the fitness of the character, but it makes a huge difference to the details and wrangling of your reward rules for the player.

    I'll offer Dogs in the Vineyard as an example. Here's my character:
    Acuity 3
    Body 2
    Heart 5
    Will 3

    In terms of character fitness, is that functionally equivalent to these stats instead?
    Just Talking 8
    Physical 7
    Fighting 5
    Gunfighting 6

    Yes, it sure is.

    But see how much difference it makes to me, the player, to be manipulating my character's fitness in pairs instead of singly? I want to make my character a more fit fighter, for instance - so do I increase her Body, thereby also making her a more fit athelete, or her Will, thereby also making her a more fit gunfighter? Dogs' split stat makes its reward rules more interesting than they would be if I just slapped a +1 on my favorite way to win.

    \n I think it also serves to underscore the decision to use guns. Since you don't need to go to guns to get all your dice, going to guns is a more revealing decision about your character and how you feel about the situation at hand.

    I'm personally a little tired of design trends where everything is tied together in mathematical cycles. Mathematics is not to sum total of design. I don't think Sorcerer would be better served by giving demon's Need Points. And as they use their Powers it costs them Need Points. You can convert your own Humanity into Need Points by narrating some horrible thing you're doing to feed the demon.

    Simply giving demons Needs and Desires and talking about how they serve the fiction *is* design. If the group applies that design badly, that's a failure of the group, not the game. Dogs in the Vineyard similarly draws all kinds of dark circles and underscores around guns that I think it would actually be counter productive to force the issue through guaranteeing dice when you go to gun play.

    \n I may be mistaken--but I doubt I am--when I say that threatening someone with bigger fallout dice is a very clear mathematical benefit of escalating, even when it doesn't get you more dice to use in the conflict. The reason: duh! The other person can't just casually take the blow--so if they think the conflict isn't worth being badly hurt or dying over, they either have to give or burn higher dice when they block.

    Of course this does depend on the mindset of the opposing character. If it's an NPC, you're really at the mercy of the GM in a way, because the dice ultimately don't tell you how effective this threat is. (I mean: yes, the available dice will determine how badly the NPC is constrained should they decide to stay in the conflict. But the dice don't provide any guidance on whether the NPC is of a mind to stay in even if they're going to have to take the blow against gunfire.)
