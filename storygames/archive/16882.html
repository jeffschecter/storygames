\n Has anyone tried creating a story game that has the complete absence of a modern concept, theory or technology? I recently got back a book I loaned a friend on Time's "100 Ideas that Changed the World, history's greatest breakthroughs, inventions and theories", and I've been thinking about what if something was never invented or that concept was never thought of. To be clear I am NOT talking about a simply going back to a time before there was space travel or television or the Internet or whatever, but something more complex, sort of an alternative world where some important concept or technology doesn't exist and anything leading up to it or related to it, also doesn't exist.

Basically I'm thinking on the lines of the movie "The Invention of Lying" where because no one had ever thought of the concept of lying. It wasn't just that people were 100% honest with each other; there was also never any stories, no religion, lack of hope/faith, etc. It was really a bleak world but the writers were so clever to take every single instance of anything that could be based on the concept of lying out of that world to start out. To a less dramatic degree I've played a few Zombie games where zombie lore doesn't exist in that world, so when things start happening, people have no reference of what is going on. Obviously like in the movie, during the game we learned what a zombie was. As a side line, I don't like zombies much, however "Zombie Cinema" is VERY good.

However, what I am looking for is if anyone has ever tried to remove completely from a game world a core concept or piece of technology. Maybe it's the modern age and no one has ever thought about what it would be like to fly? In fact people don't even look up at the sky as there are no birds and no stars at night? No one has ever tried to build wings, there are no stories of angels, no stories of trying to fly? I hope this makes sense and I hope this is posted in the right category. Thanks.

    \n While I can't think of any games like that, I have a thought: you might want to take a look at some older science fiction novels. Many of them imagine the future as an extrapolation of the time they were written, but end up missing out on certain major advances that are now common place. For example, Neuromancer managed to envision a completely networked world, but missed out on the advent of ubiquitous wireless networking, and so cyberspace works in a very different way than it actually does in the present-day real world.

    It's not an RPG (yet) but you might benefit from checking out Chrononauts, by Looney Labs. Your FLGS probably has a loaner deck, if they sell Looney Labs.

    It's model is that one Lynchpin event being flipped keys other, Ripplepoint events to be flipped, sometimes due to odd logic. Perhaps you could begin with that basic mechanism, generalize it (and expand it to MUCH more history than US) and move from there to how one engages it in an "RPG-like" way.

    Or, you know... play Universalis. It's trivial to make one or more Tenets that change major aspects of the world you're going to play in. As you play, you can expand upon the Tenet(s) when you bump up against something you hadn't spelled out in setup. The beauty of doing it in Uni is that the Tenet(s) have actual mechanical weight, as they provide Coins in a Complication over the state of the world. (Or is that only Facts? If so, then establish the Tenet(s) as the "Lynchpin(s)"; and any Ripplepoints discovered in play, as Facts.)
