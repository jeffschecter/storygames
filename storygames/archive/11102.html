    \n Context is in this thread here. Everway was mentioned as a "game I'll never play" and then a side discussion started, defending it. I made this thread for that discussion.

    I can only speak for what happened when I played, although I don't imagine my experience was unique. The cards worked, more or less... the numbers didn't. I fight a monster. I have Fire 4/Earth 5 and it has Fire 4/Earth 5 (or whatever it was - it's been years); we fight forever and nothing happens. We couldn't make sense of the stats in the fiction, couldn't figure out how to make monsters, and couldn't figure out how to make fighting already-made monsters interesting. The only drift I can envision, beyond "don't fight monsters!", is to abandon the numbers entirely and consult the deck as an oracle, but then there's no good system for negotiating whether your interpretation or my different one goes into the fiction. So, Graham, my position is actually that Everway is doesn't work because it's a terrible, boring system that fundamentally fails at providing a framework for adding collaboratively to the fiction, beyond "cuz the gee-em sez", which is axiomatically awful, and because #ihadabadgame. Please tell me why I'm wrong. I would like to be wrong.

    \n I agree with Colin's point about Everway's overreliance on GM fiat, and if I were to try to play it, I would hack the shit out of it so as to (a) give players the chance to interpret the cards more often and (b) regulate and systematize when you consulted Drama, Fortune, or Karma at all.

    That said, the GM advice in Everway already seems to regard them as integrated, in that everything is GM fiat (Drama) until the GM doesn't know what the hell is going to happen next, at which point players start describing what they're doing and the GM compares the relevant character stats (Karma) to determine what the likely outcome is, throwing a card and reading it (Fortune) to confirm or deny that judgment as desired. But that means that there's lots of room for people to get creative paralysis, and it puts a lot of pressure on the GM to get it right, be creative, acknowledge player input, and all that.

    I'd say that the first step to running Everway in a fun and engaging way while still playing more-or-less by the rules-as-written would be to engage with the system on its own terms, rather than as a defective version of another game. So if I as the GM throw a monster at you, I have to know why it's there. If it's just there to be a brief moment of adversity, then it gets resolved Dramatically. "Here's a monster! It's big and scary! What do you do?" "I swing my sword!" "My God! You've cut its goddamn head off!" Boom. Easy.

    If, conversely, it's meant to be a significant part of the encounter, a true obstacle, then the player has to put in the effort to overcome the monster.

    "I swing my sword!"

    "Your sword rings off its scaly hide and it lunges forward! You are equally matched in strength and endurance with this thing! If you try to match it blow for blow, you may manage to kill it, but you'll be exhausted and easy prey for the next such mindless monstrosity to come along. What do you do?"

    "I maneuver so that the pit is behind me and then, when it lunges forward, I leap out of the way, spin, and use my shield to push it into the pit!"

    Here, at this point, the GM can either say, "My God! Its scream echoes for what seems like half an hour as it falls!" (Drama), compare Air scores or whatever to see which opponent is cleverer to justify saying "My God! Its scream echoes for what seems like half an hour as it falls!" (or "My God! You're too slow! Roll up a new character!"), or throw a card to shape the judgment. Suppose I throw "Nature (Life Energy)," which reminds me of the tenacity of life. If I'm the GM, I say, "My God! It's hanging by its claws from the lip of the pit! What do you do?"

    And play proceeds from there.

    The trick is that something needs to happen in play to provide sufficient narrative detail that in juxtaposition with the cards gives the interpreter something to work with. Neither is wholly effective by itself.

    Posted By: Zac in Davis
    You could say that slaying the monster so quickly was just to establish mood, or tension, or (if the player leads it) to point out what a badass hero the PC is.
    \nThis is exactly right. All of that falls under "needs of the story." But notice how it's always co-constructed by player and GM. A generic model looks like this: (1) GM highlights situation elements (some of which have been introduced by player), (2) player acts on behalf of character in response to situation, (3) player and GM consult the system to resolve the action, and (4) the output of the resolution provides new situation elements, and (5) the cycle continues.

    I should note that my game Ganakagok resembles Everway in its reliance upon an oracular tarot for resolving actions, but Ganakagok has an explicit and extensive game-mechanic for awarding the right to interpret the card. This mechanic draws out step 3 so that everyone at the table can potentially intervene in the on-going action to shape the resolution.

    But of course that's not a complete model; it just covers action resolution. There are other ways of introducing elements of the situation; Colin's examples of asserting facts about his character underscores the point that character generation is one of those ways. Everway's Vision cards provide another technique for this: they have pictures on one side and a set of questions about the picture on the other. I can imagine a game where as a group you pulled a card and answered the questions about it to create the starting situation, gave players each a card to come up with their character (per the rules), and then started play from there, without any need for the GM to "come up with an adventure" or any of that bullshit. You would just respond to character initiatives.

    So one of the things I'd do to update Everway is build in collaborative situation generation. Easy. I'd also like a GM budget for adversity or some other mechanism for putting limits on my ability to just throw shit in your way until I thought you'd had enough, because I'm a wimp and would go easy on you. A pacing mechanism for the narrative, a la Inspectres, or maybe just a tool for ending an adventure and seeing what the consequences were.

    \n In my experience, Drama resolution can also involve consensus building among the group about what should happen next. Like, when I was running Danger Patrol the other day, something happened and I -- as GM -- narrated a fictional consequence: "So what if the dark portal's energy has leaked into the bodies of the lava fish, turning them into voidsharks?" And John said: "Okay..." and I read this as skepticism and said: "Is that not cool?" and he said something like: "No, that's totally cool. I'll never reject voidsharks." Basically I was checking t make sure that the other players thought that voidsharks made sense in the story (since that was a pure Drama resolution decision on my part), that I wasn't taking the gonzo past the point where nobody could be invested in the fiction anymore (since that happened to some players when I ran Danger Patrol before).

    You can also have Drama resolution in which the other players say "Yes, but" as in Polaris or in more informal ways. Like if John had said: "Okay, but the dark portal has actually turned into a giant voidshark itself and spawned pups" or whatever, that's still part of Drama resolution but takes it beyond a decision made by a single player. You even get this kind of thing in Fortune / Karma resolution when players say: "Can I fork in my Tracking skill?" or "Don't I get an additional +2 for Underwater Combat?" or whatever. It just takes a different form in Drama resolution.

    So don't necessarily fall for Everway's GM-centric approach to resolution. You can definitely inject some dirty hippiness into that and share control of resolution.

    Posted By: jaywaltIn my experience, Drama resolution can also involve consensus building among the group about what should happen next.
    \nYes, absolutely. Of course, it's worth it to distinguish between the "problem-solving" or inventional side of Drama resolution ("What could happen here?") and the the "decision-making" or deliberative piece ("What actually does happen here?"). A good DM (i.e., Decision-Maker) is always listening hard to get a sense of the fictional possibilities that intrigue the players most. Then your system tells you how to decide among those fictional possibilities, and negotiating-to-consensus is absolutely a legitimate way to do that. It's also a particularly fragile one (i.e., easy to break, game, or manipulate), and one that seldom leads to surprising or unexpected results. Which may be what you want, depending on the game or sub-system of the game you're in.

    So formal techniques for managing Drama, as in Polaris, strike me as good and interesting design, because they make the thing that's going to happen anyway a part of play rather than an obstacle to it. You could do something like that in Everway, too, where you let other players offer suggestions about what could happen and the GM gets to decide which one happens (or vice versa).

    \n Bill, sure. However, I think this oft-repeated conception that consensus "seldom leads to surprising or unexpected results" or is "particularly fragile" doesn't really match with my recent play experiences. Often, I think people have this mental image of consensus as the entire group sitting down, negotiating out a detailed solution that everyone is totally satisfied with, and accommodating even the slightest bits of discomfort that other players might have. How often does that actually happen? I mean, in the games that I play in, there is a shifting but unequal power balance at the table, where either the GM, the current spotlight player, or the first player to offer input has significantly greater say in the final outcome. Building consensus in 90% of cases allows for variation on the most authoritative player's suggestion, not putting everything up for grabs. Which is to say, you can totally drop in with something unexpected or dramatic. In fact, in many emo/genre-porn-tastic styles of play (*cough*DangerPatrol*cough*), those types of dramatic declarations are MORE likely to be accepted by the group. Also, I'm not sure I see the fragility of it (though like all interactions based on informal power relationships, they can definitely be abused), but maybe you're talking about something else here.

    Posted By: jaywaltBill, sure. However, I think this oft-repeated conception that consensus "seldom leads to surprising or unexpected results" or is "particularly fragile" doesn't really match with my recent play experiences.     \n I ran Everway for a pretty long time. 30 sessions? Maybe something like that.

    I did what the book says to do regarding resolution (drama/karma/fortune, pick and mix to taste) and it worked great for us.

    Let's say we have the 5/4 PC vs. the 4/5 Demon. According to the numbers, the fight is a stalemate. Except we're not comparing numbers, we're playing a roleplaying game. Our characters go into action and someone (the GM by default here) makes a judgment. Is the PC being all ass-kicky against the demon? Cool, that's Fire, yep. Is the PC just trying to hurt the demon? Okay, Fire vs. Earth and... yeah, a 5-5 tie.

    "You smash the demon with a spinning kick, and your foot bounces off like you just kicked a stone wall."

    The player, at this point, never, ever says "I kick it again." Clearly, the demon is too tough. The karma resolution has told us that, all things being equal, this character can't hurt this demon with brute force alone. Time to try to maneuver it into falling into the magical vortex, or maybe go grab that weird looking spear you saw in the throne room, or... whatever. The player does something to change the situation and try to get an advantage. Which puts us back into the freeplay + karma/drama/fortune cycle.

    Or maybe the player just comes up with some crazy awesome move that no one knows how to deal with. I usually go for a card draw at that point, then lay the card out on the table and "interpret" it out loud, which usually means the players jump in with their own ideas. "The picture shows a demon-looking thing dying. Come on! That is too perfect."

    Dang, I have to run. I'm happy to answer questions.

    Colin:

    1) This does not seem like a serious question. 9001? Pffft. The game has a guide that tells you what the numbers mean. You pick numbers for things according to that guide.

    2) Eh? It's a roleplaying game, right? No one says, "I Fire up that demon." What does your character do? What's the situation? Based on those very specific things in the present fictional moment, the GM decides which resolution to use (D/K/F) and then says the result of the action. Fire 6 guy says, "I kick the demon in his stupid demon face." I look at the numbers and see a clear winner. "You smash his stupid face real good. Black demon blood flies everywhere and the creature howls in pain. It's clutching its face and sort of half crawling around on the floor, blood everywhere." The situation has changed, more actions happen, and so on.

    2b) (Because someone will ask) The demon isn't instantly defeated because I decided to use Karma as my guide this time and the PC is only 1 point higher. So it's a decisive blow, but the demon is still alive; still a potential threat. That's how I chose to interpret the resolution for this specific moment. "1 point higher means you wound them" is not a hard rule. Everway GMing is about judgment calls like this.

    3) Yeah, the GM said there was a crazy huge god-killing spear hanging in the throne room when the PCs were in there earlier. The PCs are in the abandoned keep of an ancient hero of legend, so you'd expect to see stuff like that. The GM says what's in the world around the characters. How is that a sucky thing? That's like every game ever.

    4) If you don't agree about the interpretation of a card, you discuss it like adults. The GM has final say. If that causes strife, either don't show the fortune cards to the group (just look at them yourself and cock an eyebrow dramatically), draw a new card, or play with different people who aren't dicks.
