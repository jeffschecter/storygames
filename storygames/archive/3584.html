\n So I've been pondering the Campaign Mode for Sons of Liberty, and how much I want to constrain and flavor the three different stages of the campaign. The levels (sessions) in the first stage should feel different than levels in the last stage.

There are little nearly-rules like "nobody dies in the first stage" but I can't seem to articulate enough of these or enough powerful versions of these to really flavor the different stages appropriately. I thought about having a little short paragraph that is read at the beginning of each level, with different stages having different readings, kind of like Radio Lightning in Grey Ranks.

Then I thought: what if, at the very top of the game, to open the Grandmasters at the Lodge of the Americas phase, everybody around the table performs the 'secret handclasp' of the Sons of Liberty and gives passwords and counterpasswords to each other, flavored appropriately?

So you start off the Intolerable Acts levels by clasping hands with the guy to your right, saying, "If we do not hang together," to which he responds, "we shall surely hang separately." Then you go right into creating objectives.
Then, at the end of the campaign, you start off the Fires of Adversity levels by clasping hands and saying, "The States do not have to win," to get the response, "The Empire just has to lose." And bam, objectives.

Here's the question(s): first, is this hokey, or would it lend a certain air to the 'secret meeting of the grand masters'? Then, secondly, would starting off in this way color the objectives (which you create literally moments later), and thereby color the whole session where you pursue those objectives?

    \n I'm not so much after a ritualized opening as I am after some mechanic or procedure that will lend a different vibe to different levels without making massive changes to the basic mechanics. Which is, admittedly, a very tall order.

    There's a Comedy Sportz / Improv game where the team does a two-minute scene and then gets suggestions from the audience for three different emotions (or styles of plays, or kinds of movies) and then they redo the same scene in each of those emotions, in one minute each. I want to produce something like that: where you're doing the same stuff, but you're being sneaky and surrepticious, or you're being flashy and ostentacious, or you're desperate and reaching.

    \n Yeah its a bit hokey. But hokey can be fun. It can also be powerful. Little rituals like this can be very useful if all the players buy-in.

    When I got my MBA the program began with a three day retreat. There were lots of little games and puzzles that we were told would teach us something profound about ourselves and how we communicate. And they did, precisely because the moderators said they would and got us to believe them. The first game involved putting puzzles together as a group where everybody had everyone else's pieces. You weren't allowed to speak or otherwise request help. You were only allowed to silently help others. It made a simple puzzle mind-blowingly difficult and required a mind shift to effectively communicate. That one puzzle game framed the rest of the weekend.

    When they work, rituals act on the subconscious parts of the brain. Lots of magical rituals begin with something to phsychologically engage the participants in the results of the ritual about to be performed. Often this is some form or payment for the services of the spiritualist. Other things like candle lighting, ritual washing, repeating key phrases, act to seperate the ritual from everyday life. To make the ritual special.

    If you want the players to think like they are in a "Secret Meeting of the Grand Masters," then by all means make them start off with behaving like they are a bunch of Grand Masters at a Secret Meeting.

    Of course, rituals only work if everyone believes they will. But, people who don't believe in them will just throw them out anyway. So, my rec would be to push for the game you want and include them.

    \n How ritualistic is the rest of the game? I worry about patches of ritual in games that aren't, on the whole, ritualistic.

    (This, pretty much, is my gripe with the salute in Beast Hunters: it's the only bit of ritual in the game, so it feels a bit out of place. To me. In my personal opinion).

    There's a bit of a fad for rituals in indie games at the moment. You see odd patches of rituals appearing in, say, Game Chef games, but they're not worked into the game as a whole. For example, in Troy Costisick's game "Hunter Rose", there's a sudden moment of ritual where you toss rose petals in the air and whisper "Death!". And you think: that's a weird bit, where did that come from?

    That said, I like the idea, especially the phrases. I'm just not sure how well it'll fit with the rest of the game.

    Graham

    \n Thanks for all the comments, guys.

    For the "This is maybe sort of hokey, and I might not like it" folks: does it make any difference that the "ritual" is performed in-character, rather than out-of-character? This is opening the scene where you play the Secret Grandmasters of the Sons of Liberty, and you're greeting each other as you arrive at the secret meeting location. Handclasp, sign, countersign: now we can begin. Change anything for your sensibilities?

    John suggested whispering. That might be useful for the first stage, but I think it would be counterproductive for the others. Perhaps I can think of things to flavor the other two stages in similar ways. Thinking. (For reference, the stages are: The Intolerable Acts, where the Sons are engineering the revolution; the Shot Heard Round the World, where the Sons are rallying colonists to the cause; and the Fires of Adversity, where the Sons have to keep the revolution going just... one... more... day!)

    Graham, me personally, I don't really see this as a ritualized opening, any more than the GM saying, "So you all meet in a tavern..." This takes place already inside the game, rather than as the transition from non-game into game-space. And the game, where you're playing pseudo-masons, has a goodly portion of ritual to it. So to me, this 'fits' but it fits inside the game, rather than necessarily fitting with the zany mad-libs-and-rummy game mechanics. Dunno, however, if the two would still clash.
