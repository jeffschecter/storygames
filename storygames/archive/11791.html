Happy Birthday, Robot! is a storytelling party game designed for young and new players. Perfect for gamer parents who want something light and easy to introduce their kids to the hobby.

In this game, you and your friends roll some dice and create a new story about Robot. Robot's story is different every time you play. Even Robot can be different each time! But the story always begins the same way: "Happy Birthday, Robot!"

My goal is to raise $1000 to fund the printing of 100 hardcover, full-color books with art by Rin Aiello. You can help by pledging your support on Kickstarter. Donate $10 to reserve your PDFs. $30 for the PDFS and a signed/numbered book shipped to you as soon as they're printed! Just click on the image on the right or go to the following URL:

http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/danielsolis/happy-birthday-robot

The response so far has been overwhelming. Over half-way to the goal the first 24 hours. With your help, we can take it over the finish line.

Thanks!

    \n Think I have the google doc version already. I remember commenting on the lovely bubbly robots in it. Need to look at it again, actually. It should be on my digital book, I'll dig it out later tonight.

    Hm. Ways to reward folks who post stories... it's a tricky one, really. If you offer something that takes too much of your own time or money to prepare you risk being snowed under if a ton of people submit stuff. I guess you could offer links from Robot's website to their blogs as extra advertising, or allow them to use an official robot logo or something. Maybe you could run a competition where the best X stories each get an illustration and are featured in a free-to-download companion PDF? Or some other prize, maybe? I've seen very reasonably priced runs of enamel badges around the place, but that might prove pricey due to needing to order in largeish quantities (I think 75 pieces was the minimum at the place I looked at.) Still, you could sell the excess to make up your expenditure. Or you could get a cheap button-badge press and make your own. I think Cafepress makes them too.

    I'm currently making a brass figurine / trophy that I may at some point use to start a competition or award of some sort, but I realise that kind of technology isn't within everyone's grasp. I got lucky since the college I'm studying CAD/CAM at let me use their 3D printer to create a prototype for free, but I doubt I'd get away with that twice. Got to pick it up tomorrow and take it around the caster's on Thursday to see if I can get a mould made and how much it's likely to cost. That's probably a bit grand though, considering.

    Oh! Here's an idea! You could perhaps turn the winning entry into a 'digital' Kid's book, in the form of an animated slideshow and voice-over to read the story, just like you might see on a kid's TV program. That'd look good on YouTube too. All you'd need is a few slow zoom-pan effects on static pictures and a gentle-voiced, versatile actor to read out the narration. It'd make a good advertising gimmick too, and you might start a craze of other people doing the same thing!

    (Incidentally I think you should make an actual children's book too.)

    On the face of it though, you might not need much incentive. People do plenty of crazier things already. Maybe if you put up a 'virtual kids book' somewhere and invited other people to use your game to do the same, it might just become the next internet craze.

    \n Thanks so much for sharing your thoughts. Believe me, I've had the same thoughts, too. I talked to Brian Pedersen directly about this arrangement because I was concerned about how it might look muddling the for-profit/not-for-profit motives. It's certainly a gray area to which I'm sensitive, for personal reasons. For what it's worth, here's a general timeline of how I came to approach Brian with the idea.

    * My initial modest fundraising goals are more than met. I'm happy and comfortable with those numbers you see in the widget on the original post. Heck, I'm overwhelmed and humbled.

    * There were 70+ days left before the funds would actually go towards producing the books, so I wanted to maintain a bit of exposure for HBR in the meantime.

    * At that time, the newsblog Purple Pawn posted an article about HBR's quick success and about this new charity called Kids Need To Game.

    * Both entities seemed like a natural fit for one another, so I emailed Brian about whether he could use some copies of the book. He said sure, that it he'd only need three at most. Unfortunately, that's when I realized his charity is based in Denmark. Eep!

    * I still wanted to send some books to KTNG, but I didn't want to lose my shirt with shipping costs and so forth. The way the math worked out, it would take about five print+PDF pre-orders to cover the costs of producing and shipping one copy of the game to Denmark. (Along with some simple custom dice I planned to throw in as a surprise bonus.)

    * I asked Brian if he was okay with that kind of arrangement. He said yes, so my own anxieties were relieved enough to go forward.

    So now that brings us to the present, where we're only ~3 pre-orders away from covering the costs of the last of three books to be produced and sent to Denmark. To be honest, I'd have sent those books to KTNG even if I had to pay for it out of my pocket. At least this way, it's getting more exposure for Kids Need To Game over a longer period of time.

    Is this arrangement the most altruistic and selfless of all possible options? Definitely not, and I acknowledge that.

    However, I hope any distaste for this arrangement would be channeled into making your own donation directly to Kids Need To Game. As I said, Happy Birthday, Robot! has been more successful than I could hope for and I genuinely appreciate all the support my friends and fans have given to the game. I'm presently looking for ways to send out free or discounted copies to other domestic charities and to classrooms (like the class at Derrick Thomas Academy mentioned above.)

    Thanks again for voicing your concerns and giving me an opportunity to address them openly.

    \n Daniel, I agree with Chris (and you), it's a bit heavy in the panda department. I think it needs richer color and more emphasis on the robot, like the illustration you did in the video.

    I really like your choice of typeface for the title, it's playful like the subject matter of the game. One thing about using a typeface that is hand-drawn (and this is more of a personal preference), when you use it on more than a word or two it starts to look like a font and not a something hand-drawn because there are multiple instances of the same letter that are "drawn" exactly the same (down to the imperfections). It's especially noticeable on words with repeating or adjacent letters like "Happy" and "Robot." It wouldn't be difficult to replace a few of the more obvious letters with ones that you have done. It's nit-picky, yes, so feel free to ignore the feedback.

    Overall everything I've seen on this looks great. I'm really excited about it. good luck!

      \n Now that we've reached 100 backers, it's time to lift the curtain on something we've had in the works. When this project first kicked off, Evil Hat Productions (www.evilhat.com) offered to sponsor whatever amount we fell short of our fundraising goal by. Thanks to your enthusiastic generosity, that just wasn't necessary. But Evil Hat has stayed interested since that point, looking at ways we can work together to make sure Happy Birthday Robot reaches as many fans -- and kids -- as it possibly can.

      Today, I can confirm that with Evil Hat's help, Happy Birthday Robot's first printing will run over 500 copies, making it possible for the game to have a broader reach -- getting into game stores, schools, and libraries as well as direct to fans on the web. Once the book is released, copies will be available for purchase through Evil Hat and through Evil Hat's distribution partners (Indie Press Revolution, Alliance, Esdevium, and others).

      As far as branding goes, Happy Birthday Robot will still be under a "Smart Play Games" label. From one perspective, Evil Hat is stepping in as an "angel" partner, covering any remaining financial risk and making sure that the book stays in print. From another perspective, Smart Play Games is hiring Evil Hat to do the publication job, handling all that messy business stuff so I can focus on supporting the game and the fans.

      So that's the big surprise! Happy Birthday Robot will be on game store shelves, thanks to the help of Evil Hat Productions.

      Happy Birthday, Robot!
      Robot giggles and buzzes his antennae while waving his hands.
      Robot's frightening red gaze terrified the tiny kitten to death.
      Robot mourns, calling out for help and animal control but Tom arrives instead.
      Robot's antennae droop when Tom begins to roughly examine kitten's stiffening corpse but good.
      Tom had never liked Robot's fluffy little cat and was secretly quite pleased because dead kittens cannot meow.
      Suddenly, the ancient voodoo priestess arrived to thwart Tom and revive Robot's kitten with magic.
      Robot grabs Tom and lets Priestess start the ritual but she needs blood.
      The Priestess pulls out a gleaming knife and Tom struggles against Robot, but Tom remains captive.
      She pricks her finger with the knife, much to Tom's relief and winces as kitten reanimates.
      Kitten's unholy mews fill Robot's joyful receiver when Priestess turns and says, "Robot, cake time!"
      Zombie Kitten and Tom join Robot and Priestess for delicious birthday cake.
      Kitten developed a taste for brains.
      Kitten liked smart people.
      And to eat them.
      Also cake.
      THE END.
