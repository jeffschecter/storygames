\n Jungle Queens are an archetype of Good Girl comics. They are the female equivalent of Tarzan, raised in the savage jungle, embodying the Noble Savage archetype, usually with an animal companion or three. They were skimpy bits of leather. And I love them. I've got my favorite, Miriam from Cavewoman, tattooed on my arm (the original drawing is by Frank "god of Good Girls" Cho).

So I'm thinking about an rpg I want to write (after I finish the three projects I have lined up already) about Jungle Queens. And I was thinking there was no way it would work, because if you have multiple Jungle Queens that breaks the Sim, and if only one character can play the JQ then everyone else is kinda fucked.

I know there are systems that attempt to resolve these sort of issues. The Buffy TVS system (which I haven't read) apparently does this, allowing both Buffy and Xander as characters, with the Xanders getting some sort of compensation for not having superpowers.

But I had a different idea. Most Jungle Queen stories have the same essential plot. A group of civilized people become stranded in a Lost World. They are unprepared to deal with the dangers of the jungle (such as DINOSAURS). They are rescued by the Jungle Queen, who agrees to lead them to safety. Thus a trip is called for. Along the way, the Jungle Queen tries to make the civilized people aware of the dangers they face, but they won't listen. So they die. Generally one or two members of the party befriend the Jungle Queen and she saves their bacon and keeps them alive.

So, here's my sketch of an idea: A game where the players play members of the lost group of civilized folks, and must travel the length of the Jungle to Safety. No matter how skilled they believe they are, only the Jungle Queen can save them from danger. No-one plays the Jungle Queen, and the players must ingratiate themselves with her, or die. When danger erupts, the character with the lowest affiliation score with the JQ must narrate how they are killed, while the other players may choose to narrate how they save themselves, or how the Jungle Girl saves them.

So the real game occurs between eruptions of danger, as players attempt to get the JQ to fall in love with them, or respect them, or whatever. Anyone have any ideas about how to make a system out of this?

    \n Gah... I cannot find the thread. In any case:

    The simplest case might be a game for 2 players. Maybe your game is a game for two? Why not be a game for two?

    Player 1 is the hero
    Player 2 is everybody else

    (You could also do something I've heard done with It Was A Mutual Decision, where "Player 1" and "Player 2" are groups of people working together to decide how to act.)

    If you have more than 2 players, you start dividing up "everybody else."

    Polaris uses this division:

    One player controls the hero
    One player controls everything that opposes the hero
    One player controls friends and family of the hero and male NPCs of no particular affiliation
    One player controls business relations of the hero and female NPCs of no particular affiliation

    The main job of the latter two players is to use their NPCs to make impossible demands of the hero, break the hero's heart, and generally raise the stakes and bring the emo.

    Think about what the main forces in your game are. A game in development I've heard about occasionally here is called, I think, "High Planes Drifter":

    One player is The Drifter
    One player is The Devil, controlling forces that try to make The Drifter self-destruct
    All the other players are The Man, controlling forces that try to make The Drifter conform to society. These players must decide as a group how the things they control will act.

    Interesting thing here is that most conflicts are between The Devil and The Man, with The Drifter siding with one or the other. If The Drifter wants to exert his own will in a situation, he needs to buy his way into the conflict and then hope that luck goes his way.

    I think you can see how this approach can work, and hopefully determine whether it fits with what you're wanting to do.

    Posted By: PartheniaThe term Jungle Queen clearly has different connotations for you and Miles than it does for me. As does the fun in the idea of a Noble Savage.     \nYeah, people who don't actually read the books. (I'm fighting the temptation to go off on a rant about this critique of Tarzan I read in an English class years ago, written from a multiculturist perspective, in which the author admitted to not actually reading the books and relying mostly on second hand accounts of scenes from the films. Let's just say I find that sort of criticism deplorable.)

    Tarzan was not raised by natives. He was raised by a species of highly intelligent apes similar to (but not) gorillas. Tarzan is, of course, far from the first character with this origin. Romulus and Remus, the men credited with founding Rome, were said to have been raised by wolves. "Raised in the wild by animals" is an ancient trope found in the legends of every culture. The "feral child" trope also became very popular in the early part of the 20th century, with the explosion of mass media and photography, that allowed for the sensationalization of stories of feral children, both real and hoax. That is where Burroughs got his ideas from, the headlines of papers and mythology. And he set the story in Africa because a) there are gorillas in Africa and b) Africa was still thought of as "that huge unexplored and untamed land over yonder."

    Which is not to say that the Tarzan stories aren't chock full of rather blatant racism. The African natives that live near Tarzan are, in the early stories, portrayed as cowardly, callous, cruel and ignorant. When Burroughs moved to Hollywood, he was exposed to more cosmopolitan perspectives than those he was surrounded with in the whites-only Ohio ton he grew up in. That's why the later books feature more heroic African characters, and tend to portray the white explorers as the cowardly, callous, cruel and ignorant ones.

    But there's nothing inherent in the concept of the Jungle King/Queen that implies racism. Mowgli, in Kipling's The Jungle Book (a major inspiration for Tarzan), is Indian. While he is portrayed as a boy in Disney's animated The Jungle Book and most of us think of him that way, an adult Mowgli -- such as that portrayed by Jason Scott Lee in the live action The Jungle Book (1994) -- fits the Jungle King archetype perfectly. Likewise, there's absolutely no reason why one couldn't have a African native Jungle Queen. All she needs is a backstory that explains how she got lost in the jungle and raised by [insert animal here].

    The idea of the Noble Savage is something altogether different. That's a product of philosophy, strongly associated with Rousseau. I'm sure I don't have to explain how Europeans developed the handy concept of "the savage" to justify imperialistic conquest, slavery and all that other bullshit. The concept of the noble savage was a reaction to that, a de-legitimization of imperialism achieved by asserting the moral superiority of the "savage," an inversion of the moral order asserted by the conquerors. It was an early baby step in the long and arduous (and ongoing) process of recognizing universal human equality. But it also spawned a stock character of fiction with so many popular examples that it's never, ever going to go away. That concept of the noble savage, while patronizing when applied to real people, does embody a set of virtues that are valuable and important (living in harmony with Nature, generosity and selflessness, innocence, honesty, natural health, disdain of luxury, moral courage, innate and untutored wisdom and intelligence) that are entirely appropriate for fictional characters, especially characters in the sort of poetic mythologies created by role-playing.

    I seriously can't understand why someone could get offended by that, unless they were...I dunno...not really examining it. In which case, their offense is not exactly sympathy inducing. It's really no different than people who claim D&D is Satanic.
