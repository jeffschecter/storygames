\n I'm a 4th Grade Teacher in Nashville, Tennessee and I've recently begun working to incorporate story gaming into my classroom. On Mondays, during a study period at the end of the day, I've been picking 4-5 kids to participate in a G/PG Rated version of Everyone Is John and they really loved it. One student even told me he got his mom to GM a game with he and his two little brothers. Two weeks ago I introduced them to Happy Birthday Robot! which they loved and is now a Center during my Guided Reading block. I also threw together a Fiasco Insta Set Up for Dungeon Delve and used the Soft Tilt and Aftermath (with editing for language of course) and they enjoyed it a lot.

Further, I've found Vincent Baker's philosophy of play outlined in the MC chapter in Apocalypse World to be reflective of the current movement in education to have the Teacher act as a facilitator and guiding hand in the background while Students are allowed to explore, discuss, and critique concepts. I have also been reading over The Second City Guide to Improv in the Classroom which has created some even stronger connections in my mind between story gaming and formal learning.

That said, what experiences have any of you had incorporating story gaming techniques outside of gaming? (particularly education, but I'd love to hear about any uses)

Also, what systems or story gaming techniques do you see as being appropriate and beneficial to upper elementary students? (think 8-10 year olds) I work with very low socio-economic students with, what I would consider, sub-literate homes. So anything that would provoke and encourage deep analysis of texts through gaming would be appreciated.

    \n I'm planning to try The Quiet Year with my 10 year old. He really likes games a lot. But I'm not sure if I would try it with anyone younger without making some serious changes.

    As it is, I'm not sure if it will work all that well. Looking forward to the experiment.

    For classroom use, the first obstacle is the length. It's supposed to last 3-3.5 hours, which is WAY WAY WAY too long for that sort of activity in the classroom.

    Also, it's really unstructured and open ended for unguided use by younger kids. I'd dial it in to be something much more focused and guided.

    And I'd probably work in some sort of more competitive angle. Not sure how, yet. But I think that would be important for driving engagement for younger players. Make it a bit more board gamey.

    Ruthless Diastema


    The Ruthless Diastema Games BlogCast is both a celebration and an exploration of GAMING.

    A public school teacher interested in using games and game elements in the CLASSROOM, I am also equally interested in what games can teach us and how they teach us in and OUT OF THE SCHOOL CONTEXT. We can teach AND learn from games, surely.

    Networking with others with similar interests is super-duper-duper important to me, so join the discussion. I fancy myself the FOREVERNEWB, THE SUPERFAN, THE CURIOUS ONE. There is no expertise here unless we count my open profession of passion and wonder and my preaching about the importance of experimentation and lifelong learning.

    I love ALL kinds of games but, truth be told, I have a soft soft for story games and games created by independent designers.

    My long-term goals for this venture include the following biggies:

    Offering free teacher guides that show how to use awesome games in school
    Arranging and joining gaming convention panels about gaming and learning
    Providing and encouraging professional development for teachers about using games for learning
    Working toward a TeacherCon, a gaming convention for teachers and other learners

    Join me.
