    Do you have a model of good negotiating skills in mind?

    This is going to be a very serious case of system matters.

    1. Find the actions you want to reenforce and how those things change the situation.

    2. Ditto those actions you want to eliminate.

    find a reward system that is not intrusive to the dialog, but provides positive actions with something the negotiator wants.

    develop a system whereby the other side of the negotiation has the tools to provide conflicts that are both realistic and broad enough to test the skills.

    include a reflection period where both sides talk about the encounter. double up on the reward system here so that the participants are encouraged tofigure out what they did wrong for themselves.

    this is just off the top of my head based on stuff I did when I was a manager.

    Graham: It depends on what sort of negotiation you want them to learn. Capes will teach them that conflict is not something to fear, but can be productive. That means that they will be more ready to embrace a conflict and to search for a way to resolve it.

    Will it make them any better at actually resolving those conflicts? I tend to think so ... I tend to think, in fact, that much of the problem of conflict resolution (particularly in the workplace) is that people look around for somebody to blame for the conflict, because they assume it's a bad, sinful, painful thing that could never, ever happen between reasonable, passionate, invested people. Learning to discard that misconception helps you negotiate.

    Now if you want to teach them to avoid conflict, to take the blame themselves (e.g. "I should never have let it reach this point") and to view conflict as something toxic, a disease to be treated (e.g. "Let's all just calm down here ... make sure things don't get worse, then we can talk about making them better") then Capes will not help. In fact, it will make anyone who plays it markedly less open to that method of negotiation.

    Unfortunately, I think that latter one is what companies want to teach their managers, because (though it doesn't solve conflicts) it reinforces the manager's position of authority.

    Sidenote: A lot of teaching has to do with the context that you're teaching in, and specifically why people are coming to learn. You could totally make an RPG that helped people with addiction problems to better learn how to recognize warning signs in their own patterns of behavior ... but would a player who had been ordered to play such a game by his boss at work be willing to invest?

    Graham:

    It's very hard to get people to spontaneously reinvent the wheel, so to speak. Yes, you can make a game that will teach people to (for example) negotiate - and that game can be anything from "Now move this piece around the board and memorize the negotiating rules as you go" to a brilliantly-designed system that supports the development of expertise. But just saying that people will do it, and then they'll automatically get better at it, isn't really supported by educational research and theory. The number one way to develop expertise is through deliberate practice, where you have some way of getting feedback about your behavior and what parts of it are and aren't successful. That method of feedback can be built into the rules (in a more or less cool way), or it can come from expert leaders, but it has to be there or else most people will flounder without getting better.

    Similarly, the reflection phase is actually immensely important if you're trying to teach something that you want to transfer out of the game. People that learn skills in one context (game-like or not) tend to keep those skills in that context. However, just talking about possible other applications of those skills hugely improve people's ability to transfer them. Transfer is still really hard and not very well understood, but reflection is one of the few things known to improve it.

    I agree that thor's general model could be made a lot more interesting, but as a set of design principles for games that teach skills, it's actually quite solid. I'm sure there are other models that would work too, though!

    --Jess

    I feel we're rather talking at cross-purposes.

    The perspective I was coming from was this: let's say we want to train people in negotiation skills. So, for example, I have a team of project managers who regularly need to negotiate, to persuade other companies to let them have things at the lowest cost possible and to make concessions in return. Could there be a game to train these skills?

    So, Jess, of course the feedback stage is important. We understand that, after we've played the game, we're going to sit down and talk about what we've learned (or similar). And we understand that the context is important: that if the project managers want to learn, they'll learn better, and it they don't, they won't. All valid points.

    But what I was asking was: how would you actually design the game? For example, one way of doing it would be this.

    Each player represents a country that's negotiating a peaceful end to a war. Let's call the countries, um, Alphania and Betaland. If they can't negotiate a peace treaty within an hour, the war will continue, to the detriment of both sides.

    Each side has a list of demands and a list of what they're prepared to concede, with orders of preference attached to each. For example, let's say that Alphania is particularly concerned to have control of Red Mountain, a huge mountain on the Alpha/Beta border. Betaland presently holds Red Mountain: they don't particularly want to retain control, but they don't want Alphania to take control if they're going to build a rocket base on there. Alphania also feels strongly that Betaland must not know where the Alphanian weapons are deployed.

    And, of course, there'd be loads of similar quibbles. There'd probably be a map on the table. And the players would have resources which they could agree to give each other as recompense.

    Now, to me, that's a start: it's a vaguely interesting game which is going to get people to negotiate. You could divide a training group in half and get one half to observe the other playing. Afterwards, you'd talk about people's negotiating styles.

    How's that, for a start?
