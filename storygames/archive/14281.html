\n This will be an obscure topic I'm sure, but I'm hoping to get input from folks who've put some thought into this with play experience in the various Hero Wars/ HeroQuest editions.

After looking the Hero Wars/HeroQuest 1/HeroQuest 2 editions, I've decided that for the most part I prefer Hero Wars.

That said, Augments seem to be the thing most fiddled with over the years. I'm wondering what rules folks finally settled on. Did you keep the rolling with the possibility of failure during Unrelated Actions? Did you port over the Automatic Augments from HeroQuest? Did you keep the roll, but remove failure? Or did you play them as written? (As I've found many people did after fiddling with the HeroQuest rules and then deciding they actually liked the Hero Wars rules better.)

Thanks!

    \n The auto-augments were added in HeroQuest, though rolling for them was retained as an optional rule. (In HeroQuest 1, you divide the Ability by 10, rounding up at .5, and that's the Augment. Obviously, it takes a lot of the risk -- and potential of a bigger Augmentation -- off the table.)

    The ability to fail an Augmentation roll (and so get a penalty) is in the Hero Wars rules as written. From my reading of the rules, I believe Augmentation is only for oneself, while Characters can Lend APs to each other in Extended contests. But I actually love the idea of being able to augment each other's roles.


    I'm leaning toward is sticking with the Hero Wars rules. As written, one can't just make a bunch of Augmentation rolls -- they need to be done as an action during the fiction of an Extended Contest (an "Unrelated Action" is how it's defined in the rules, but I hate that term because, of course, it's related) So, there's no endless fishing for rolls and Augments stalling things from happening... you can describe how you're Augmenting and making a roll, but other people are doing things on their turn as well.

    What I don't like about that is it reduces the chances people will be bringing in Augmentations from different Abilities--providing a broader pool of bits of fiction stuff to draw from. I especially want to make sure relationships are a key component of Augmentation. I think the key there is to make sure the starting values are relatively high enough to encourage their use on a regular basis.

    - think about the situation for a sec
    - look at my sheet for an appropriate skill
    - announce what I'm doing
    - GM says, "want to augment?"
    - we try that sometimes, sometimes we don't
    - we announce the outcome of our rolls, both good and bad, and the GM adds on to that with his own description

We augmented ourselves and Hrothgar, and then cheered him on during the extended contest. At the end, Hrothgar took Skaldi's wineskin and drained it, then sent a mute boy to hand it back to him.

    Our goal was to stake our lawful claim on Gevalti hospitality, and we did so by shaming them down off their high horse. It was pretty amazing.
    (Come to think of it, I'm not certain that we actually augmented one another's rolls. I think I got confused because I'd augment my own roll and then use said roll to assist Hrothgar - not the same thing, I suppose.)

    \n I'm a big fan of automatic augments, and I like them to see heavy use during play. As has been pointed out already, the problem with this approach is that conflicts can easily get bogged down by people trawling their character sheets for every last augment. My game Other Worlds is kind of a riff on my favourite parts of HQ (among other things!), and this is how I handled the issue:

    You can only use 3 auto-augments per conflict. This is enough to add an interesting spin on things but not so much that play slows down as a result. However, some conflicts are so important or character-focused that you really will want to take the time to savour every last detail of the situation. So, you can spend a point of metagame currency to ignore this limit for a specific conflict.

    The advantage of doing it this way is that it introduces a clear creative constraint for most situations but also puts the power to go beyond it directly into the player's hands. I don't think HQ1-style self-regulation quite works in this regard, and giving the GM some sort of 'plot/character significance' veto would be inappropriate for the style of play I'm interested in. Charging for the privilege of bringing in all these detailed augments not only limits how often it will happen but also, crucially, gives the player a kind of permission to really go to town when he does decide it's appropriate without feeling like he is doing something wrong and getting an unfair advantage. It also acts as a pretty cool Chris Chinn-style flag of interest to the rest of the group.

    I do have a mechanism for rolled augments in the form of sub-conflicts, but they're generally reserved for augments that can potentially go wrong and actively cause the player new problems rather than just failing to work somehow. 'Roll to see if anything happens' is not a meme I'm very fond of TBH.

    \n As written in Hero Wars, getting an Augment is actually doing something. (I'm addressing is the rules in the book, not the cool fixes that people have found, and trying to sort it out, digging in deep to make sure I'm not missing something before I start tinkering.)

    So, after reading all these terrific posts and going back into the rules, what I'm seeing is a lot of different little problems, all connected but discrete.

    The Task/Conflict Resolution Problem

    1) In Hero Wars, getting an Augment is done during an "Unrelated Action." This is an action, a specific something your character is doing during a discrete amount of time during the Extended Contest. You pass on making a roll against another opponent when your turn in the round comes up.

    2) I think the term here--Unrelated Action--is horrific. If my Heortling warrior is rearing her horse around to charge at an opponent as her Unrelated Action (her action, while disengaged from an opponent) to Augmenting an upcoming Spear roll, clearly it's related to the conflict.

    3) The problem, I'm beginning to see, is that the Augmentation roll, because is not cast against an opponent has slipped us from Conflict Resolution into Task Resolution. Thus, we all try to waffle around the problems that Task Resolution can engender: Rolls that do nothing or fumble that lead to a dead stop, rather than failures that are interesting and push the fiction forward, regardless of a failure.

    4) The fact that this is happening isn't that surprising given that the game was published in 2000 and the issues and properties of Conflict and Task resolution were only beginning to be nailed down. (I'm also pretty sure Laws isn't particularly interested in the distinction from the design I've seen since.)

    5) So, an example:

    The Heortling warrior is disengaged from her opponent and, in an Unrelated Action, rides her horse back for a charge against an opponent for the next round. If she succeeds in the roll it means she set up her charge properly. If she fails and gets a penalty on her roll, it means she stumbled through the ranks of her comrades, dodging them and making a bad attack. This is all cool and makes sense. It is part of the fiction and we can all see it clearly.

    But in doing this, what is she rolling? Is it a Task resolution roll using her Riding skill? Not quite, because she's rolling against the Augment Bonus Chart. It actually is a Conflict, but the Conflict is against a table in the rule book -- not a piece of the fiction.

    I'm gong to be bold and say This is a Big Fucking Deal. The Augmentation roll is a weird hybrid of Task resolution and Conflict resolution. I'm pretty sure that's what we're dancing around and trying to fix.

    6) I'd say that this, first and foremost, is where the problem comes in. And this is where the desire for Automatic Augments comes in as a fix. With Auto-Augments, we get around the problem of the Player making Task rolls anymore (which lead to all sorts of "How do I justify whiffs? What does failure mean to the fiction? Do things just stop?" problems) and we slip past the problem of Conflict roll against something outside of the fiction. Instead of a roll, we grab some quality of the Character that matters to the conflict and apply it.

    Fiction Interacting with the Rolls

    The other problem, as I see it, is what Vincent Baker refers to as the "Right Pointing Arrows" -- point where the result of rules impacts the fiction is in ways that actually turn the fiction in specific and meaningful ways. Some games have rules that do this, and other games don't. In the game that don't, you can use all the mechanics to resolve a conflict, "paste" some fictional color on top of dice rolls or results, but whatever happens in the rules doesn't really change the fiction in ways that will affect the next roll. Vincent uses In A Wicked Age as an example of a game lacking right pointing arrows in this discussion.

    1) So, let's say we don't demand that a Character remove himself from the conflict into an Unrelated Action and actually do specific activity to justify the roll for an Augment -- which, from what I've been reading from around the Internet, is what a lot of people did. The Players just called for a chance to roll an Augment, the roll was made, and then the roll either succeeded or failed, with results ranging from "Awesome!" to "Oh, fuck, I've got a penalty on my next roll."

    2) The problem is, of course, that if the Character doesn't describe what fictionally happened to make the roll, we know that something is going on, but we have no way of connecting it to what's happening in the fiction. What then does failure mean? We know what success means -- "I get a bonus!" and with that bonus in hand, we're happy enough not to think about the fact that we also have no idea what a success means. But with a whiff it sticks in the craw because we have a penalty but we suddenly don't know why. "I was using my riding skill! What went wrong?" Well, it's kind of late to figure that out without having to spool back a bit and paste on some sort of justification.

    3) More significantly, however, whether it's success or failure. Another character can't grab hold of whatever happened in a fictional context and make a statement based on the result.

    In other words, something did happen (a riding skill was made) but actually nothing happened (it wasn't represent in the fiction in a significant way. We're jumping next into the Spear Fighting roll which will either succeed or fail all on its own, independent of what happened with the Augment roll. So, we're making rolls that never stitch into the fiction in any meaningful way.

    4) An example of successful stitching:

    Same as before: The Heortling warrior is in an Unrelated Action, pulling her horse around for a charge on an opponent, making a Riding roll as an Augment for her Spear Fighting. That's her action for the round, while all the other characters are doing other things (making opposed rolls against opponents or their own Unrelated Actions).

    The Player makes a roll and ends up with a Minor Defeat -- she's going to have a penalty equal to the Augment she was shooting for on her Spear Fighting in the next round. So, the immediate question is, What Happened?

    The Game Master (or the Player?) says, "As she sweeps around for a charge on the Lunar soldier, she comes up through a line of Heortling infantry. She pulls the reigns, dodging between them, but slips off her saddle as the horse bucks her side to side. She's still got her spear, half-hanging on to the pommel, off-balance but charging toward toward that fucking Lunar to gut him."

    So, this works. We have the roll, we have the fiction in context. And, significantly, the fiction matters in the next round.

    If in the next round the Heortling warrior makes a successful Spear Fighting roll, it'll be described as pulling off the attack despite being half off balance. But if the Lunar infantryman wins, he can easily slip in, "I yank her off her horse as she rides past," since it was already established in the fiction that she was off-balance.

In an Unrelated Action, the character touches the broach his dead wife gave him before he attacks her killer -- and so on. Essentially, given how Unrelated Actions are described I'm seeing them less as Unrelated Actions and more like Spotlight Shots --where we get the camera on the Character doing something cool or emotional or specific before leaping into a Conflict resolution roll. What does success or failure mean? Exactly, as far as I can tell, what Trait or Passion rolls would be in Pendragon -- we learn the definition of touching the broach by the result of the roll. If it goes well, the Character is inspired. If he finds out, we find out that he's touching it because he's remember how he failed her.

    6) When we go into the next round and the Character makes the Conflict roll against his wife's killer, if the killer wins he can say, "And I strike the broach from the Heortling's chest as my sword strikes down and draws blood." We're building on the fiction that his built by the rules. Which certainly is how I like to play.

    7) So, I'm saying if someone is attempting an Augment it isn't just a thing that happens. It's a big deal. I'm saying that if the Heortling husband stops to touch his broach, it's not a little deal - it's a BIG DEAL. It's the moment before or during a fight when the protagonist really checks in and finds out where his heart is. It's Luke listening to Obi Wan during the final run on the Death Star. By keeping Augments something rolled, during Unrelated Actions, you're keeping the Augment attempts significant, special and worth of specific screen-time... which I think is how they were meant to be used.

    Finally, there are mechanical issues in the rules as written:

    The Problem with Getting into an Unrelated Action

    1) The rules say that to do an Augment, one must be in an Unrelated Action. To be in an Unrelated Action, one can't be in a conflict with another character for that round.

    2) The rules imply, in how they are structured and in the text, that if you are engaged with an enemy and want to perform an Unrelated Action, you're going to have to disengage with the enemy. To do this you're gong to have to make a roll to disengage.

    3) Let's think about this for a moment: According to the rules, if I'm fighting someone and want to Augment, not only do I have to give up an action in the conflict to make a roll in the Unrelated Action, but I'm going to first have to make a roll to disengage! This is not only a lot of steps but a lot of risk as there are now several rolls: first you roll to see if you disengaged (you might fail and lose a round attacking); and then a roll for the Augment (which might, ultimately, lead you to a penalty)!

    That's... kind of crazy.

    4) My guess is a lot of people simply glossed over this aspect of the rules. (I know I did. I had to go back several times, thinking each time I read the rules, "Really?")

    5) On the other hand, there's no way to get around this. Given the way Extended Contests are structure, you're either in a contested action with an opponent or you're in an Unrelated Action. There's simply no way to make a Augment Roll while you are engaged with an opponent, because you need to be in an Unrelated Action!

    6) The quick fix everyone found, I think, was to disengage Augments from Unrelated Actions. Thus, you made the Augment roll as part of the actual contested action -- a pre-roll to see if you got a bonus.

    7) But once you did this, there was suddenly no screen-time left for focusing on the action required for the Augment. Or, at least, it got really compressed. Because clearly the Augment roll was a kind of lesser roll that only prevented you from getting to the roll that really mattered -- the contested roll with the opponent. This, of course, clearly blows the significance of the action required to justify the Augment out of the water -- and it's just a couple of more steps to simply getting rid of the action completely. And then we're wondering why we're rolling at all. And then we go to Auto-Augments.

    8) Given the structure of the rules, I really don't know how to make going for an Augment enticing enough to really come into use as written. And this, again, simply renders the use of Augments during a Simple Contest impossible. One is either doing one thing or another thing as an action -- a contested roll or an Unrelated Action. To compress the time frame is to engender all the complications of the first two sections of this post.

    9) This is a huge mechanical problem. If you're a player and want to do an Unrelated Action with your Character, which requires you making a roll outside of any contested action, and I'm the GM and have a character attack you... you've either made sure you're really out of the fight (which is why the rules require a disengage roll, to really nail that down) or somehow I'm engaging you with my character, blocking you from doing the action. I just can't shake the feeling that there were some last minute shenanigans in the game text before publication... But I also see the same problems in both editions of HeroQuest, so I don't know. It's like someone just hasn't noticed that there's this problem that is a problem and needs to be addressed ratter than some sort of handwavey-it's-a-toolkit logic.

    Something interesting I noticed in HeroQuest 2, however, is that you get to make Augment rolls when you disengage or win the preceding contested roll. There might be something in that.

    Still, my mind is gnawing on all this.

    \n Hi Joe,

    I think Flaws, as written, in all three editions, are kind of goofy and not well thought out -- whether they are personality traits or relationship based.

    The key, for me, is to junk the word "Flaw" and get on with it. Is the character "Hot-Headed"? Awesome. Forget the word "Flaw," write down "Hot-Headed" and get on with it. The question is (for me) "How does a character go about doing something?" If he's in a king's court and loses his temper to strike someone's head off, use the Hot-Headed as an Augment to the sword stroke. Full stop.

    As for Relationships -- they're "flaws" when they get a character in trouble. In your example, the fact that a character might have competing allegiances on the character sheet is the point -- and it's enough, in my view. The Player has to look down at that sheet and make a decision. That's it right there.

    Later, when he's knee deep in a task or commitment to one of the Relationships, I'll have someone show up from the other Relationship and ask, "What the hell are you doing?" As far as I'm concerned that's the value of a Player writing down conflicting Relationships. He's saying, "Push me on this!." Eventually he might even forgo one of them and delete it from the Character Sheet depending on how the fiction plays out.

    But as far as the Relationships and scores go, they're all about the connection/devotion/loyalty the Player Character has to another character. The flaw--if it is one--is that in the fiction the GM can put stress on the PC by putting the character in danger or testing loyalty across a spectrum of Relationships. Being "Devoted to My Son" isn't a "flaw." Again, it's just a Trait. If your no-good son is always getting hauled into court for stealing cows, others might see a Father's devotion to his son as a flaw. But for the father it just is.

    The only time I could see using them as some sort of flaw is is we had built up to a big climax and it had been made clear over several session of play that the Player really, really cared about and was digging torn loyalties or something and we came to a big climax where he had to fight someone he was loyal too. I might give the opposition an Auto-Augment based on the PCs relationship score. But, again, I'm addressing the Character's choice of action as affected by the score. The choice is all the Characters. The devotion is still there (it's still a "positive" relationship) which is why the person the PC is loyal to is getting the Auto-Augment.
