\n 5e is something of a Swiss Army knife for me because of the broad playerbase, the (relatively speaking) lightness of the system (compared to 4e/3e), and that my familiarity with the d20 system gives me the experience needed to bludgeon the damn game into something resembling playable.

There are still plenty of wrinkles in need of ironing, some of which are on the system and some of which are on me. Some overlap between the two: combat. Once we're rolling for initiative, things like intent and stakes go out the window in favor of the detailed rules of task resolution, and fights turn into swing-hit-HP-damage fightfests. What are some techniques or rules you use to handle difficulties like this?

    \n I understand your concern in a relatively generic manner - you want to expand the toolset to enable more fluid pacing and more purposeful scene play. There are multiple useful concepts for this kind of thing, more than just one rules innovation. Let's see...

    One-roll combats: Everybody rolls d20+level+key stat bonus. PCs roll, every named NPC rolls. The side with more henchmen/minions gains advantage. The side with the single highest roll wins. All losers suffer injury equal to 1d6 per full five points in the winner's result; winners suffer 1d6 damage. Henchmen and minions take only 1 point per die, named characters take full damage. A player may choose to "stunt" to halve damage by expending an important tactical resource (a potion, spell slot, whatever); multiple characters may stunt per combat, and one character can stunt multiple times, but every stunt needs to pay more in the GM's judgement than the previous one did.

    "Winning" in this sense means that the winning side gets to hold the field, while the losers retreat. Either side may, if unhappy with the result, call to immediately extend the fight, in which case it's resolved to the end with the normal rules, starting at an encounter range chosen by the initial winners. (The initial hefty loss of resources should discourage from this most of the time, such that players will only call to extend a fight when they cannot afford to retreat.)

    Adding a quick resolution for combats, similar to how skill checks work, should help cut down on time spent in combat. This helps with the big picture, as you can skim less important combat encounters, focusing more seriously on the important ones.

    I guess I'll write up some more thoughts later, the sauna is calling me right this moment. You'll want, on top of that, to have formalistic means of establishing and resolving strategic goals, both inside and outside combat; the generic conflict resolution rules need to both lead to combat and work within combat, seamlessly. (Think 4th edition.)

    \n I'll talk about fights in particular, not conflict resolution in general or during fights. Our group has in the past, for certain campaigns, spent a good deal of effort on making combat encounters not devolve to "attack is always optimal". I don't think conflict resolution fits well without lots of hacking, but you might try some of these techniques to see if using them relieves your symptoms:

    - Mobile, telegraphed, dangerous environmental hazards. The goal is to make movement actually matter. This is more necessary when tracking squares, less when theatre of mind. All three adjectives are important, and dangerous means actually dangerous, not "you take a tiny or even medium bit of damage". Rapidly flowing lava, telegraphed huge AoE lightning strikes, that sort of thing.

    - Goal other than "kill everything", especially one that requires moving around the space. For example "corral the dragon pups" who have wandered into I don't know dire crocodiles or something.

    - Time (turn) limits to accomplish non-hitting-things goals.

    - Inexhaustible sources of foes. (Obviously need other goals than "kill everything")

    - Usable, potent, telegraphed environmental details. All three adjectives are important. Potent means legitimately more effective at achieving goals than just "use attack actions on obstacles until obstacles don't remain then accomplish actual objective". If the usefulness involves removing obstacles otherwise removable via attack actions, the overall effect (including chance it doesn't work if you have to e.g. roll STR) must be significantly better, not just on par, with attack actions, and the players must be pretty confident that's the case.

    I agree that D&D combat is conflict resolution, it's just made up of many, many small tasks to get to the end point.     \n D&D fights may be conflict resolution, but as I most often observe them they're conflict resolution with the very boring fixed intent/goal/stakes of "Kill 'em all".
    Historically, I believe this is a degeneration due partly to the influence of video-games (esp. arcade) and technically to the loss of the lowly Morale Check.
    I suspect reintroducing the Morale Check to be an important step to take: it changes the implied goal from killing all foes to routing/defeating them in a real-world military sense. This injection of verisimilitude, I expect, would force players to reconsider their victories, from the ontological binary state of "we won/we died" to the tactical, strategical and logistical space of "what did we actually get by winning" (where winning effectively = holding the field).
    That might or might not take on a moral dimension of "what are we fighting these battles for", but is definitely a prerequisite for introducing formal sub-goals to a fight other than killing. It takes an understanding of one's greater goals as a group to weigh options against each other... If the real goal is just to get past the door, I'll consider rushing toward it and/or blasting it to pieces versus killing its guardians: what killing the guards really affords me is "just" my leisure to walk up to the door, tear it down and cross - can I do that anyway? what costs me the least resources? If the actual goal is to drag a cart full of gold back to home base, at times running might be a sounder tactic than fighting - but enemies who are coming for the gold specifically? we need to fend them off.
    Of course, introducing Morale Checks for NPCs won't magically make any of the above happen, but I maintain it's a prerequisite - as are Experience Point awarding criteria that don't penalize players for choosing tactical goals other than "Kill 'em all". Then it becomes largely a matter of scenario design.

    \n I would recommend something similar to the 4th edition skill challenge rules, at least if you're also doing set-piece combats. Those allow you to rather seamlessly design encounters that have just as much or little of combat as you want.

    For example, a bar fight could involve civilian rescue as a side-show: moment to moment part of the party fights and part of the party helps civilians get out of the building. The civilian issue could be handled semi-abstractly by assigning it as a skill challenge of length Y - you need Y successes on skill checks (which skills and how difficult is determined moment-to-moment by interpreting the fiction) to get the whole thing done. Perhaps the civilians pour out of the building on their own at a rate of one per turn, but then there's also the complication of somebody getting stuck under falling furniture, and then the building catches on fire, and there's simply a lot to do on this on top of keeping a lid on the combat situation. Perhaps the opposing combatants make the situation worse unless somebody successfully engaged them in combat turn by turn. Success or failure does not determine who lives or dies among the PCs, like conventional combat, but it does determine how much of a humanitarian catastrophe this situation is, which might be something that the group cares about in the overall story context.

    The next-step problem with the 4th edition philosophy is that you basically need to do set-piece railroads if you want all fights to be dramatic in this Thunderbirds kind of way where every specialist gets to strut their stuff and the conflict has twists and turns and complex simultaneous processes. Anything like organic sandboxing is out of the window simply because those kinds of processes cannot guarantee that the fight conveniently has a pleasant balance of non-straightforward cinematic complications.

    (There are other ways to solve this than railroaded setpieces, certainly. If you want to, we can explore the options, but it's all going to get pretty far from conventional D&D, pretty fast.)

    I recommend adopting some sort of one-roll combat shortcut resolution especially if you also embrace the ideal of a complexly choreographed setpiece combat. That way you can be more flexible about minor events along the way from one set-piece to another; it won't be a big practical problem if there are extra combats you didn't plan for if they can be quickly rolled out of the way in a more freeform way.

    \n Val, I have seven sources for DCs, listed in order of preference:
    1. Is one listed in the module (if written by someone else) already? Use it
    2. Is there a rule? Like death saves 10, navigation rolls varies by terrain etc? Follow it
    3. Am I designing a rule? Do math then write it down. For example my 5e port of Veins climbing tables use DC12 to match the probabilities of the average LotFP PC to the average 5e PC.
    4. Can it be derived from an opposing NPCs stats? Do that
    5. Am I making a module? 15+4dF, write that down
    6. Is there opportunity to have success with a cost? DC 12 for the cost, DC 20 for no cost
    7. Is there binary pass fail? DC 15

    All these principles for something we almost never do. But when it does, it matters. The most common ones are 2, 4 and 7 in that order.

    \n I will say that I think there is some purpose to setting DCs, but there needs to be a clear fictional reason for setting those DCs. Armor Class is a great example of how the DC system works well, as AC is just a very specific application of the DC system (insert your AC/DC puns here).

    Oddly enough, the AC system came before the DC system, but that seems to be how things work in RPG: the specific is created, and a general rule extrapolated from this later.


    The granularity serves a purpose, and the DCs are set ahead of time so there's really no winging it beyond cover bonuses or the occasional improvised shield. Over the long term of a game involving hundreds of d20 rolls vs. AC, a +1 or +2 to AC does matter, something that has frustrated one of my players endlessly. "What's the point of having a high AC?" he griped after a group of lucky goblins assailed him with arrows. The point is that the additional 20% miss chance that the +4 AC provides is going to save you a lot of pain over the course of a game. If there are 50 attacks aimed at you each doing 1d8 damage, that's 10d8 damage you've avoided. That's useful.

    What's less useful is that same granularity applied to the skill check system. For one, how often are characters rolling to run, jump, climb, and typical adventurer tasks? Too frequently demanding a roll results in the bumbling of a Three Stooges skit; too infrequently renders the system totally moot. There's a sweet spot somewhere between the two, but it's one rendered to the sacred mysteries of the dice gods. For another, the entire reason that AC functions with this granularity is that it does not require ad hoc judgments on the GM. "Does this monster feel like AC 12 or 15 or 20?" doesn't exist, unlike in the skill system.

    The gameplay produced by this system is often dysfunctional ("the plot demands the PC succeed/fail, so I'll set the DC arbitrarily low/high") or pointless ("there's a dice system, so we have to roll the dice, right?").

    This is probably rehashing all things known to present company, but bear with my chattering. It is helpful to get my thoughts written.
