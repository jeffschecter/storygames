    A few of my lessons learned - stuff I would want to do differently as organizer, or that I would recommend to organizers of similar events in the future:

    1) Have people reserve a spot by paying, not by posting & then paying later. Easier for everyone that way, nobody gets feelings hurt or has to nag about payment. Set a date by which people need to register or the price goes up.

    2) Set a refund policy in advance - there turned out to be a couple of people who paid & then couldn't come and this was a gray area I hadn't defined. Nobody asked for money back, and I would have had no problem giving it, but that situation could have been less awkward if everybody knew in advance what the deal would be in that situation.

    3) Don't provide all the food - no matter how helpful people are willing to be, you'll lose some gaming time and its a pain to have to shop for it and transport it. I'm told big LARPs generally will provide one big meal for everyone together and then snacks outside of that. That seems about right. I'd tell everyone (who was driving) to bring snacks and water and soft drinks to spare, and let people mostly fend for themselves with respect to what they're eating when. However, sharing is a beautiful thing - it was great to have slow cookers with chili or soup or oatmeal going for people to just come to and partake of, and it'd be a shame if there was none of that spirit of sharing going on at future events.

    4) That said, I think designated breaks/mealtimes keep people on the same schedule. There were a couple of times where people who were doing their own food got needlessly left out of gaming because they were doing a meal when everyone else was ready to game.

    5) Try not to mix gaming spaces with non-gamer activities - we were using one lodge (because it had outlets & a fireplace) for everything, and there were times when the gaming was interrupted by meals being prepped or by people outside the game who were genuinely interested but not helpful to the game. In particular, I think that we'd want any games that were going to have adult themes or content to be in a place separate from anyplace that children would be. Better for everyone that way; nobody has to worry about offending or being offended.

    There may be more but those are most of my big 'lessons learned' I wanted to get out of my head.

    1) What went well at CN0 or in its prep?

    Everyone got there pretty easily, and everyone had a nice, relaxed atmosphere going. Things that needed to be done on-site (like, for instance, figuring out which games to play) happened without anyone having to go all "I'm the boss!" Everyone understood that the point was to have fun, and went along with pretty much any suggestion that headed in that direction.

    2) What do you think should be done differently next time? How?

    Assigned cabins and bunks would be helpful. I finished off a late night game of Jungle Speed, and then was sitting there thinking "Hey ... where do I go to sleep, anyway?" Turned out to be fine (there was a spare bed in an obvious place) but I could totally imagine being in a situation where I couldn't find a bed because I didn't want to be flashing a light over the faces of the sleeping people who were in the same room as the bed that was available for me.

    I understand that one can hire people to dump a cord of fire-wood just about anywhere. Hiring people to dump a cord of fire-wood along the wall of the main cabin would have given us noticeably more time for gaming, and a noticeably warmer environment. Of course, that's likely to be completely irrelevant if we run in the spring. Then we'll probably be talking about bringing enough rubber mats to make mud-free paths between cabins, and extra towels and socks for those who don't have enough. I guess the general point is "Don't merely prepare for the environment, prepare for the environment in a way that won't cost you time and comfort at the camp."

    3) What were you glad you brought? What do you wish you'd thought to bring?

    Glad I brought a decent sub-zero sleeping bag. It kept me cozy. I learned for the first time (from others) about the existence of sleeping bags that are not rated to sub-zero temperatures. Strange concept.

    Wish I'd brought a flash-light. Waking up in a dark room in a dark cabin in a dark forest is creepy. I'm used to city-glow.

    4) New games you tried, old games you tried, other things you did that you'd like to talk about.

    Got to play Shock: , for which we were all thoroughly punch-drunk ... which made it wonderful, but not (I strongly suspect) in the way it was intended.

    Got to play Misery Bubblegum, which (as always) gives me both a warm feeling of "Yay! It sorta works!" and thoughts about improvement.

    Got to play Tenra Bansho Zero, which ... huh. A lot less revolutionary and a lot more traditional than I'd expected from Andy's descriptions. I was fascinated to watch how a task resolution system where there is no mechanical support for doing the things I, as a player, want to achieve immediately fostered all of the same exact dysfunctions in me that I remember from my play of a decade ago. I am not a more productive gamer now then I was then (and I was bad then) I am simply more able to steer away from the systems that bring out my bad habits. Sorry, everyone, for the trouble I caused at the table!

    I honestly think that the emphasis was too much on new games, or old games reinterpreted in a wacky new way. Maybe that loosened up on Sunday (which I had to miss), but my sense from Friday and Saturday was that nobody wanted to bother playing a game that we were sure would rock (like, say, Dogs or PTA). I understand (and share) the desire to try new things, but the result is that this was not a spectacular weekend for me in terms of actual gaming. Everything else? Great. The gaming? Mediocre with flashes of great.

    The improv workshop, however, was pure awesome, limited only by the fact that we didn't have enough time for everyone to do as much improv as they wanted (at least not enough for me! I can't speak to anyone else)

    Sorry to toss out that can of worms, but there was one more step in the process, which upon thinking a little more about it, I think that meeting in a warmer season might help with:

    So we did have non-gamers, but because of the cold and all, we were all in one room for most of the time. We want to be inclusive and all, but a lot of the games that we were playing had mature themes and the like. On one occasion, our group was chastised for saying something that could have been interpreted as non-kid friendly. I felt bad about that outburst and wanted to do more to make the environment more friendly to non-gamers, without cramping the style of the majority of folks. Unfortunately, again because of the weather we were pretty much stuck in one area, so we just had to watch what we said.

    I think if the next one took place in a warmer climate, some folks could just say, "OK, we're getting together a game of Dogs in the Vineyard, since it will probably have mature themes let's all go outside to the picnic table in front of cabin 2 and play there". (or wherever). It will be much easier to split off and not worry about inappropriate speech or behavior if it is warmer out. Meanwhile, more non-gamer-friendly games could totally go down in the group's communal area and the like.

    So the Time of Year issue might kill two birds with one stone (warmth and inclusion).

    -Andy
