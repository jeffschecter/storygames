    The biggest hurdle for me has been getting brutally honest feedback. I think that Becoming Heroes was a strong game in many ways (stronger than the text), but most of the playtesters were friends, people who felt the social obligation to be nice, despite their best efforts. For our future games, I want a wrecking crew (or three), who will play it honestly and with integrity and not spare our feelings in responding to it.

    \n

    The next thing is help interpreting feedback. I think it's a pretty well-established adage that when playtesters (slash, reviewers) find a problem, they're usually right, but when they propose a fix, they're usually wrong. Having more experienced designers around to help one work through diagnosing what the problem actually is and how to set it right would be really cool.

    \n

    That's my 2\xa2.

    \nWe have a fairly robust gaming community here in Cincinnati; I generally don't have too much trouble getting people to look at something I'm working on - what I'd value most is blind playtesting...being able to hand the document over to complete strangers and find out if they can properly interpret the game procedures and find the holes and blindspots.     \n Feedback from experienced designers, in general, is the most useful to me. I feel like any time I'm talking design with players or people with a fondness for thinking about design who have not yet published, the feedback is only minimally useful. There are exceptions (Jamey Crook), but often when I just put my design out there for feedback the response I get makes me feel like the person providing the feedback read a different game than the one I designed. This doesn't happen when working with folks who are a little more experienced with the full process. I'm not sure how to back this paragraph in without sounding like a snobby jerk, but that's why I gave up on designing on forums a while ago.

    The type of feedback I look for is usually very specific "does it work or doesn't it work" stuff, or sometimes suggestions when there's a gap in the rules that I do not know how to plug. Or sometimes I just want to talk about an idea and talking about it lets me organize my thoughts, and when that happens I'm just looking to have a conversation rather than get feedback. That's easy to do in person but hard to do online, sometimes folks are aggressively helpful when you're not ready for that yet and just need to talk about your ideas. And I get that people think those kind of conversations are nonproductive when they lead to no game, but for me that's a design stage that exists.

    Mainly I get my feedback from talking with Jamey Crook/marhault. Since The Final Girl became a real thing I have an easier time talking to other folks who have designed games about what I'm doing but it's still awkward and I occasionally wish there were a forum that gave me the conversations I'm looking for but, as you say, it might be outright impossible on a forum. I haven't tested the waters talking design on Google+ yet.

    The best feedback I ever got was from Ron Edwards during the Ronnies, but I'm not sure how to make that happen again.

    All that said, 90% of my design happens in my brain, at the table when I'm playtesting, and for some reason when I'm taking bathroom breaks at work.\n
    Posted By: Jason MorningstarYou should already be paying for your future playtesting by playtesting for others right now.
    \nAlso I want to say, this does not necessarily happen. I think it's a great ideal for a community of mutualism and co-operation, but I also don't want new game designers to start shoveling shit and then be like "What happened?" when nobody playtests their game anyway. My name is in playtester credits quite a bit I think, and the amount of people who have reciprocated for my games has been, like, one guy. And I'm not bitter. People are busy, my design sounds boring, or whatever. It's cool! I don't always want to playtest someone's brand new game idea either. But who you playtest for has, in my experience, little to do with the willingness of people to playtest your stuff.

    I think thin playtesting is a fact of life until you establish a body of work that has people excited about your new projects, or until you become someone who is way socially savvy about connecting with a wide variety of people who are open to your stuff and willing to suffer to help you make it. I'm working on the former because I know the latter will never happen. I think if you're worried about the amount of playtesting your game has (not) received and see no way to get further playtesting in a reasonable timeline, you should:

    - Call it quits and give up.
    - Suck up the fact that you have created an imperfect thing, but create it anyway and alter your price point so that you can release it in good conscience (to the point of a free release if necessary, as Jonathan suggested). But keep running it, keep playing it, keep promoting it when possible. Eventually you can revise it, or use it as a foundation for other works.

    \n I'm not sure I'd consider myself "new" or "fledgling" anymore. I am unpublished, though. When I designed my first game for Game Chef 2009, I believed I wanted feedback but I didn't really know what I wanted (eventually I figured out that I needed to have an engaged conversation about what was or wasn't working in the game, because talking about it helped me think about it in a different way than when I was just staring at it). What has mostly helped me develop that game has been playtesting. I've also gotten some feedback about the text itself which has made it more digestible, but I haven't gotten as much of that as I would like. I think it would be really cool to get some "usability testing" on that text, where somebody reads it and records their emotional responses to it, as well as areas where they get confused, frustrated, etc. External playtesting is what I'd consider most useful (and I'd be more interested in an actual recording than an after-the-fact text report which doesn't always capture everything that goes on during a test). Having people read my text and respond positively to it would also help me keep motivated on the project.

    For this year's Game Chef, I realized that I didn't care too much about feedback for my entry since I knew it was ready to be playtested and that the text also had some issues caused by the constraints of the contest. I was gratified when one of my reviewers with a reputation as a designer said that there were novel and clever ideas in the game -- that helped my ego and motivation -- but, as expected, I didn't get much new insight into the game from the reviews (although it did feel somewhat validating to see that I wasn't surprised by any of the issues raised in the reviews). I assume I felt less need for feedback because I'm a better designer now.

    In terms of who I'd like to get feedback from, getting attention from recognized designers or community members is valuable for motivation and keeping a positive attitude. Having conversations with people who have read many games, understand design, and are conversant with the "state of the art" can be useful in actually developing ideas.

I did create a thread talking about layout stuff for my game here on S-G, and I eventually got some feedback that I think was useful, but it was not easy to get people to express their viewpoints in a way that I could understand them.

    \n I'll take you right through what I used and could use, for GLASS:

    * Initial feedback on basic systems - Saved me a LOT of time balancing and writing, having experienced LARPers discuss possible ramifications (or outright borkenness) of some Abilities. And do I have everything you think I need (put another way, can you make Your Character, or is some element missing)?

    * Playtest - With a generic, modular system, a lot of bits and pieces need to be rubbed against each other. How do Abilities A, X, and 4 interact, in actual play at full speed? Similarly, Break This System: find some wonky combination that makes trouble. It doesn't help that a game like mine has particular venue requirements (mainly privacy and safety concerns).

    * Production - I happen to be a print designer, so production questions would be limited to basic proofreading and general aesthetic opinions.

    Would I pay for any of the above? Well... sort of. I am considering running a Kickstarter to fund playtesting, so I could run "full scale" games at a local state park that hosts other LARPs. Promotion + free weekend should get me a LOT of attendees, compared to promotion + $20 or more for the weekend. And because I'm giving it away free, I could do more to "drive" the test than I'd try to do with a paying group being conscripted to "beta test" (which is the default mode for LARP rule development! ;0 ). I could, say, give pre-gens on the first evening and afternoon, then let folks build with Packages before the second night and afternoon. I could run post-apoc on day one, to test projectiles and "tech flavored" Abilities, then switch to neolithic on day two to focus on basic melee and totem magic and raw Situational Ability tests/challenges.

    Why haven't I done that already? Well, because I can't get past "break the system" requests--I got, like, about four (three of which had either barely skimmed the rules or couldn't follow their terseness--they weren't "break the system" but rather" ignore the system, violate basic rules, and think you broke it"). So I am sort of stuck between wanting to expand the rule text, but not wanting to have the ripple effect of broke rules permeating a "complete" rule document (not an SRD), but not being able to get enough eyes and crackers on the rules to find the broke bits before they permeate the rules. Rules I need to have any hope of a major play test. And around and around we go....

    \n Well, the hardest thing for me has always been being able to fix stuff that people want in the game. Someone comes along and says "this would be really cool if..." and throws out a vague idea for a new rule that I have no idea how to work into the structure of the game. So I usually table the game. Very depressing, actually.

    Mostly what I want to know is:
    Can you figure out how to play the game?
    What's the hardest thing to understand? What's the clearest?
    Under what circumstances would you play this game?
    If you do happen to be generous enough to play the game, what was the most fun part? The least fun?
    Do the rules of the game seem to match the spirit in which it was written? Does it tell the kind of story it seems to want to impart?
    Were there any glaring typographical, layout or technical errors?
    At any point, did blood begin running out of your eyes? Was this a good or a bad thing?
    Who would you reccommend this game to? Older players? Younger? Enemies? Friends?
    What kind of story did you think this game was trying to tell, anyway?
    What genres do you think might work with this game?
    What cool rules hacks have you already thought of based on this set of mechanics (that I can use and include as maybe an appendix or something?)
    Generally negative but specific layout advice (your background colors make the text hard to read. Your arrangement of materials is counter-intuitive and difficult to navigate. why did you use so many pictures of Megan Fox?)
    Possible ramifications of the things I might have overlooked. (Yes, that's stolen from the last post, but I really need to know it.)
    Technical difficulties players had in trying to acquire the design materials.
    Genre-specific suggestions for lists that I might have overlooked. (I might have just not had the space, too.)

    Just to let you know, what I don't care about is:
    The other game that you really think is cool that you'd rather play instead of this one. Really, I'm just going to ignore a post like that so it's a wasted effort on your part.
    The game you're working on that's much better than this one.
    Cool rules ideas you're using in your own game (because I really don't want to steal your stuff if we're not collaborating.) - If you WANT everyone to use the idea, cool. I'll take it!
    My general inability to be a trailblazer in a Post-Forge environment. (I'm fully aware that my talents lie elsewhere. Thanks.)
    Advice on where to go to fund my game and its distribution... unless it's really good advice and doesn't use the word "kickstarter" in any way, shape or form.
    Genre-specific suggestions for lists that cause a clear and intentional breach of copywrite.
