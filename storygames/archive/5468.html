\n This is a call to anyone out there who has in their hands some 1st Edition D&D material dealing with high-level characters.

Why?

1st Edition (and possibly earlier rules) had some really surreal/weird abilities that characters could gain at very high levels. I seem to remember something about the Ranger becoming to track creatures through water (!), and then even through the air (!). Monks could learn to talk to plants and animals and kill people with a touch (although maybe that was just something my friend made up). Elven paladins could end up riding a unicorn, or something like that...

Anyway, I think this stuff should be mined to make Talents for the Red Box Hack. I don't have any D&D material, but I thought I would throw this out there. If you know of some 1st Edition abilities, mention them here in this thread!

    \n Hey, Paul.

    I'm a fan of the Hack, and happen to have a 1st. Ed. Player's Handbook sitting right next to me.

    Actually, monks are the cream of the special ability crowd. At 3rd lvl, they can speak with animals. At 5th lvl, they are no longer affected by diseases, slow or haste spells. At 6th level, they can appear dead. At 7th level, they can heal damage on themselves. Indeed, at 8th lvl they can speak to plants. For some reason. At 9th lvl, they can largely ignore charm and hypnosis. At 10th lvl, they become immune to telepathic and mind-blast attacks. At 11th lvl, they can no longer be poisoned. At 12th lvl, they will laugh at your attempt to geas them. And, last but not least, at 13th lvl, when they become a Master of Winter (though not yet, mind you, a Grand Master of Flowers...), they can vibrate someone to death with the palm of their hand.

    There's nothing on rangers tracking people through water and air, though I personally think that's a damn cool idea.

    And, alas, elves couldn't even become paladins, let alone high-level ones. Though if they could, you KNOW they'd need to ride a unicorn.

    Aaron

    \n And, sadly, looking through the Rules Cyclopedia, there's actually very little difference between high and low level base classes. More spells and hit points, sure, though a fighter could become a paladin or avenger after reaching 9th level. Either basically gained the ability to cast clerical spells and detect evil. Although one clerical hack I've been considering is:

    Healing Smite
    Being called a healbot is demeaning. Even worse, hearing the cries of "Medic!" each time the warrior is wounded is getting old. Now you've found your calling - a divine force to be reckoned with. Wading into combat, you bludgeon evil with the best of them. After inflicting damage on an opponent, you may convert up to one point of damage into a healing aura that restores one Hit Point to a nearby ally.

    Granted, that works best with a Heavy Weapon or hitting people in the face. A lot.
