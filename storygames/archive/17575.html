\n So the The Thief has made it out of the Vault of the Still Heart. The Heart is in her backpack, wrapped in oil cloth, and she is racing across the rooftops to escape the dead-but-living cultists behind that are in pursuit. The rooftops end at a cobblestone street and a 25' gap yawns between her and the next set of roofs. World class athletes can make this jump - after years of training, running on a track, completely unencumbered. For The Thief, none of these are true. She is going to jump, so she jumps, and she rolls the dice...

This had me asking a few questions. Do you modify a roll like this or is that a leftover instinct that doesn;t belong? What is the top of the 6/9/10 scale? Does a 12+ allow for super-human effort, does the fiction dictate that, or both? I saw a few options:

1) Don't modify the roll, a 10+ gets her across the chasm. If she misses, scale Soft or Hard moves accordingly. She is The Thief after all and I am a fan of her character. I don't want to just say no, then again, what happens when the next jump is 30'?
2) Modify the difficulty with -1 or -2.
3) Change the scale. A 12+ is successful, 10/11 is partial success, 9- gets you a hard move.

How do you do it at your table? Do you let modifiers enter into your games or just let fiction dictate what is possible and what is at stake? Where do you see the top end of the achievement scale in DW?

For the record I chose option 3, she got a 10; she made it over by her fingertips and had to choose whether to drop her sword or the Heart.

Thanks!

    \n At my table I do option #1. In general, if the next jump is 30' across, you just say "You can't make this one" given your set of premises -- if it's impossible, it's not a roll.     Definitely not wrong to change the scale, though, it's just a more drastic change and whatnot. If you players and you played a lot of classic WoD, it might have simply made more sense immediately, which is fine and good.

    \n Someone (I cant' remember who) once pointed that the most basic move in AW is not "acting under fire" but the Savvyhead's workspace move.

    So I think in this case the best thing to do is to invoke those:

    * "You could try to make the jump but only if you [take some action] first."
    * "You might have a chance to make the jump, but only if you [make some other sacrifice]."
    * "It's going to take several/dozens of tries."
    * "First you'll have to figure out..."
    * "The best you'll be able to do is a crap version..." (this is like John's proposed solution)
    etc.

    If you squint a bit, you'll see that these are just standard MC moves, like "offer an opportunity with a cost" (Hexabolic's proposed solution) or "take away their stuff" (Irminsul's proposed solution), and so forth.

    The short version is that, whenever you're playing AW (or a hack of AW), and rolling the dice doesn't seem *quite right*, it just means it's time to make an MC move. Then the game keeps moving.

    "Put someone in a spot" is a totally viable choice, too: "It's too wide for you to jump. You stop at the ledge, the guards are close behind you... what do you do?"

    \n So here's a thought I'm having:

    This whole move, roll, and choose business is based on Otherkind dice. As long as you remember that, you can easily make your own custom moves on the spot.

    First, break the player's intent into two different things (if possible). Then add a third danger/harm/new problem.

    In this example:

    * You get across the chasm.
    * The Heart gets across the chasm.
    * You don't break your leg doing it.

    And then you can control the fictional "difficulty" as follows:

    Easy version
    On a 10+, you get all 3.
    On a 7-9, choose 2.
    (On a 6-, choose 1, or leave it to the MC move, as you prefer.)

    Hard version
    On a 10+, choose 2.
    On a 7-9, choose 1.

    Totally Impossible Version
    Same as Hard version, but add one more bad thing to the list of things.

    (This is very much like how seize by force and go aggro are differentiated in AW.)
