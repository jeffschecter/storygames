\n First off, a bit of context...\n
Posted by Millsy It's a shame there's not more advice out there on prepping the one-shot
\nThis quote and the overall positive reaction on my "one-shot methodology" thread are prompting me to share an observation of mine. Maybe you'll find it useful, and maybe you won't. It might even rattle some cages. In any case, please remember that while I have strong opinions on stuff, I'm not "anti-" anything. I'm just a guy with strong opinions, not a guy with an agenda.

Sometimes, it feels like I've been around on the outskirts of the industry since forever. I began my gamespotting back when I was still a teenager, not old enough to vote. I've known the days of usenet, and my active participation to hobby discussion began on the old RPG.net, back when Sandy Antunes still owned it and it felt more like a town than the sprawling metropolis it now feels like. More to the point, however, is the fact that I was around before the Forge-theory boom and the rise of story games.

In that boom, I saw a significant aspect of hobby discussion die. As Forge-theory exploded onto the hobby, discussions of GM craft withered away. Here, I'm using "GM craft" as a catch-all term for any and all discussions that were concerned with being the best GM you could be, independently from what game was used. It kind of died in two strokes, actually. The first came from Forge-theory: practical discussions centered on actual play best practices ended up being swarmed by theory-enthusiasts convinced that looking at the minute details of how to better be a GM amounted to addressing the symptom rather than the disease. The second came when story games reached critical mass, and there was enough variety on the market to suit various tastes. By then, the deeper end of best practice discussions had died down, drowned in theory. What was left were relative newcomers to the hobby (or gamers newly broadenning their horizon) , puzzled with questions of how to do this and that better. Things not deep enough for theory-enthusiasts to get into. On these questions, a bit of best practice discussion had managed to survive, until there were enough story games out there that these discussions innevitably turned into discussions of how some of these games did the work for you, and how they were better tools for the job.

It's a chicken or the egg thing, IMHO, because Forge-theory and the design trends that followed were born out of these best practice discussions. And I'm not blaming anyone specific for the way best practice discussions were smothered afterwards. I think it's only natural for a community to get all excited and obsessed once a breakthrough happens, and the state of story games today speak pretty clearly about how much of a breakthrough this all was. But it is a shame, though, because the basis for discussion about how to be a better GM and tell a better story together for people interested in doing that with traditional games is now years back, and hard to find. Sometimes, it's almost hard to remember that it's possible to talk about these things without entering the realm of design theory.

Is there a point to what I'm saying? More or less. On some level, I'm sharing my views on this because I think it's an aspect of how our hobby evolved that few have noticed, and I think it's interesting to think about it. Also, there's a bit of nostalgia there for me. These best practices are what made me the GM I am today, and it's a shame that I wouldn't encounter these discussions of I was today where I was at 15 years ago.

But in a way, this is kind of a manifesto. A lot of the drama surrounding the various design trends has died down, and I sort of feel like traditional RPGs and story games are today seen as two siblings that don't have to compete with each other for dominance. In that current climate, maybe it's time for a revival of sorts. Maybe it's time to actively start talking about traditional RPG best practices again, even if it feels forced at first. Just to build momentum, because it sure has died down by now.

And maybe we can gain from that little hiatus, as well. I know it'd be awesome to have story games aficionados ponder about how to port the best of their subset of gaming to traditional RPGs, without denaturing them or outright turning them into story games.

So there. Feel free to discuss or not, or whatever. Hopefully, some of you will find this is something interesting to think about, if only for a few minutes,

    \nI feel like I need to clarify this part. I think the human brain is lazy, and posting thoughtful stuff on the internet invites laziness unless you make an effort. At the simple level, I think best-practice discussions died because the advent of story games brought discussions of which game to pick on the foreground, and traditional RPGs gamers jumped on board as well, chiming in on what their favorite game was.

    The difference is, before the advent for story games, a request for advice on how to GM didn't bring into the discussion which game to pick. You would just go with whichever game the person was using, provided the issue wasn't directly tied to the task resolution engine or whatever. Story games, bringing in whole different ways to play, inserted the choice of game into the equation. Everyone jumped on board, and now the questions that get asked are not how can I best do this, but what game is best for me to do this. This happens even when fans of story games are nowhere in sight - which is sort of why I say best practice discussions have died down. They're simply not a way we're used to talking about gaming anymore.

    \n I think those discussion died for good reason. They were founded in the idea of "GM skill" being one thing, applicable broadly, across many different audiences, game systems, player expectations, genres, mechanics, etc. etc.

    The internet - once it reached a critical mass of accessibility - destroyed that notion. One person's best practice was another person's worst nightmare, even within the "trad" community (broadly construed). Any best practice in the modern, diverse environment is going to be local to a particular set of culture, expectations, distribution of authority, genre, and so on.

    You can see brutally divisive arguments over best practice even in communities constructed around a single game system.

    Posted By: Jocelyn RobitailleThe difference is, before the advent for story games, a request for advice on how to GM didn't bring into the discussion which game to pick.
    \nI would like to make the suggestion that, that is because, at the time, all games were functionally the same game, or less charitably, the same non-game. What you had was a strong emphasis on high-color concept, a LOT of fiction (called 'setting') and then basically a statistical model that attempted to encode that fiction in a sensible manner along RPG cultural expectations. Appropriate stat set. Appropriate skill set. Acceptable bell-curve shape to the die mechanic. Maybe a neat genre appropriate lever or dial you could set. And that's about it.

    The point is that, no matter how radically different the *fiction* was, the underlying *game* was identical across the board. So, of course, you could have broad general GM advice because at the social/creative dynamic level all games were identical. The play group literally had to forge their own local functional game that worked for them. So, swapping advice about that was not only easy, it was down right NECESSARY.

    Mark's premise is that it wasn't the Forge, but instead, the Internet that destroyed "GM craft" discussions. I think this is true and extremely troubling. This:\n
    Posted By: Mark WThe internet - once it reached a critical mass of accessibility - destroyed that notion. One person's best practice was another person's worst nightmare, even within the "trad" community (broadly construed). Any best practice in the modern, diverse environment is going to be local to a particular set of culture, expectations, distribution of authority, genre, and so on.
    \nis the biggest indictment of the hobby that I can think of that doesn't stray into absurdity (like, say, accusing all pre-First Thoughts forum games of being "identical non-games". What baloney.)

    Let's turn back to Mark's statement.

    First, let's take as given the extremely wide variety of expectations, desires, and goals for RPGs, each set by individual groups with the various inputs into those groups across this fine green globe. If we look at the history, all the way back to Usenet, then further into letters column and reports from conventions, we come to the inescapable conclusion that this was always true, that this was true the first day there were two gaming groups in existence, and nobody who took the time to think about it could ever conclude otherwise.

    Yet the only, the ONLY thing that anyone did about it for THIRTY YEARS is to write some text in some books.

    (Forge games did this the same way, which is not a surprise because the Forge was about creating and publishing your own game text.)

    That's not to say that publishing is a bad idea, or shouldn't happen. That's not to say that texts don't have something to teach about crafting your game (whether as GM or a player with another name). Discussion is another way of doing this, and some of us have taken to that, so all right. Texts and Big Internet Discussion.

    But what else is there?

    Are there conferences we can attend which are focused on improving play? No. We might go to a panel (all panels are shitty panels) at a convention, a two hour block of time based around an extremely general topic.

    I made a "GM's Conference" here in Tucson and ran it for two and a half years. It was fucking amazing because nobody had experienced anything like it.

    We had speakers and presenters who would come in with some topic, related to their experiences, preferences and techniques, and just explain themselves, say what they wanted, and describe how they sought it out. That's ALL. That's like the bare, pathetic minimum of trying to improve things. And yet there is simply nothing else like it in the hobby right now.

    Shitloads of games have a GM, and having a conference where GMs discuss their goals, desires, drives, and techniques is so SIMPLE, so MINIMAL and nobody whose game has a GM is doing anything about it.

    Fuck Apocalypse World, I mean, it's a cool looking game, but it's just another fucking text when it comes down to it. Yay, more fucking text, someone's selling me more text, wooo.

    If we really want to improve our GMing (for games with GMs) or playing in general (for any game), where's:

    * the mentoring system?
    * practice, practice and more practice?
    * the culture of criticism of performance?
    * the exercises?
    * the expectation that you will try to improve your play?
    * the systems by which you are exposed to many groups with many different goals and techniques? (player exchange? GM exchange?)
    * anything, anything, anything in the whole fucking world to help you improve, other than general discussion with the entirety of the Internet-posting hobby, and other than texts in a book someone wants to sell you?

    I am the dumbest person in the universe, and I came up with these potential bullet points in thirty seconds. Someone who is actually smart could come up with a bunch more.

    What Mark says is true, but it shouldn't have taken the Internet to get us to this point.

    When you buy a game with a GM in it, in that game, it should tell you how to find another GM to mentor you or partner with for constructive feedback. It should tell you how to give feedback, how to make it concrete and actionable.

    But let's be clear, we're in this position not because of something the Forge or Forge theory did or didn't do.

    We're in this position because it is to nobody's financial advantage to improve the quality of your play.

    Nobody gets paid when a GM does a great job.

    Nobody loses money when a GM does a shit job.

    Nobody gets paid when an Apocalypse World game goes great.

    Nobody loses money when an Apocalypse World game goes off the edge and dies a horrible shitty death.

    Nobody gets paid when a Polaris game really clicks and our four (presumably ideologically pure non-GM) players knock it out of the park.

    Nobody loses money when a Polaris game fumbles and slops around and doesn't go anywhere.

    It is to nobody's financial advantage for RPG play to be improved. Only that it be "good enough" to get you to buy the next thing.

    So RPG creators have said, meh, it's not my business. I'm in the text-selling business.

    (And maybe the discussion business, along with Internet assholes like myself.)

    I am totally down with that, you choose the business you want to be in, and texts can be a piece of the puzzle. Certainly texts matter! RPG creators have done an amazing job. (Yes, even in the 80s and 90s, with those, ha ha ha ha ha ha ha ha ha "identical non-games", snrk.)

    But the idea that texts distributed (ideally) to lots of people are going to help them reach their idiosyncratic, individualized, always-shifting goals?

    Just saying that idea out loud shows how silly it is, and always has been.

    \n To try and marry what Paul just said and JD said and directed at what I think Jocelyn is after, I DO believe that you can write generically about *game families*.

    I think it would be possible to talk about play techniques for Investigative Games that would be widely applicable across Call of Cthulhu, The Gumshoe Games, Cthulhu Dark, Chill, etc.

    I think it would be possible to talk about Encounter Based Adventure Games that would be widely applicable across D&D 3e/4e, Pathfinder, and much of the OSR.

    I think it would be possible to talk about Blood Opera Games that would be widely applicable across many LARPS, Houses of the Blooded, and certain applications of Vampire, Burning Wheel, and The Riddle of Steel.

    But I think what would be important for these kinds of discussions that books like Robin's Laws and the like don't do, is to have a touchstone of design. What key design features should/must be present to make the more universal techniques applicable and functional.

    In other words, here's a series of ideas of techniques followed by and here's the system features you should look for to support and enable these techniques.

Nearly two decades of developing my own classical game, Fabula (published in 1999, in its 8th version), with a growing focus on working out a better balance in resolution mechanics and dialogue techniques. It has made me realise that a game do not have a system, it has a method (in which the system may be said to refer to the mechanical side of it).

      But it all started back in 1989, when I had the game Muu revealed to me, in a nightly spell that may be described as a shamanistic journey. It was a game so bereft of all trappings connected to rpg's of the time, that it opened my eyes to a whole new field of the rpg-methodology; the field of dialogue techniques. I did not understand the game fully, and had no way of setting the insights and dialogue-tools of it to paper, so it was sold solely through full-day courses in how to be a "muu-leader".

I believe it is very important in our understanding of how the special trappings of Norwegian dialogue-based role-playing design developed, and possibly the special developments in Nordic live action role-playing too.\n

      Good, general GM craft certainly enables you to lead games on a very high level.

      However; the tools needed to do so is, in fact, very simple and straightforward, once you've learned where to look for them; in the field of dialogue techniques. That is actually what people of your culture, like Jocelyn, do too, when they explore and develop the craft of game mastering.

      But they have little or no "cultural capital" in your gaming-culture. The American school of though is heavily focused on the mechanical side of rpg-design, to the detriment of the technique side of it. This is evident in both classical (with GM) and modern (no GM) role-playing games. There are little design work done in the field of techniques, in your game-culture, and so it is easy for you to discard thoughts on techniques as irrelevant, or even: non-existent.

      But it will not do. The blindness of claiming that a very important field of the rpg-methodology is non-existent, cannot be maintained. You will, eventually, have to open your eyes and travel into the great field of dialogue techniques. To be able to develop the proud tradition of game design further, we all have to do this. The methodology inherent in role-playing games will, eventually, demand it of all rpg-designers. It is simple; role-playing games like ours are dialogue based, so to ignore the evident dialogue techniques of it; how they may be mastered and put to use, is not something we may do forever. Role-playing games of the future will come to be designed with the full methodology of the field in mind.

      The craft of game mastering you are invited to discuss in this thread, is situated in the tick of dialogue techniques, as applied to classical games. And a lot of it certainly applies to modern games too, where no GM is present. It has the power to inform your design of classical and modern role-playing games, and make you are better schooled designer all over.

      \n A few thoughts.

      1. Running (and playing) RPGs is (I believe) tacit knowledge. Like surfing or speaking a language, you won't learn it by reading about it, but by doing it. Reading can help, and watching other people do it will help even more, but nobody can explicitly explain and write down a clear set of instructions for it.

      2. Different games (as in game texts, or rulesets) require different techniques, approaches and tricks. A particular set of advice might be great for one game but terrible for another. There's also the question of different groups, tastes, playstyles and social environments. A set of advice that works with a specific ruleset for one group of people might not work with the same ruleset and a different group of people (and vice versa).

      3. The above makes it very very difficult to create any sort of great "GM Craft" bible or whatever. There are lots of people who've tried (and succeeded!) in making great GM advice books (Play Unsafe et. all), but they're hardly universal or infallible. I agree with 95% of what JDCorley has been saying in this thread. The question then obviously becomes how do we talk about GM Craft in any useful or productive way? We can't establish any real discipline or write a manual. We can just talk. Talk talk talk talk. Talk and do. And isn't that what we are doing anyway, at least some of our time. Trying to get better, playing, learning, talking to other people about it, asking questions, listening to panels, etc.

      [Aside: I love to "research" the practical side of gaming, I want to learn, I want to get better. Which is why I wanted to start a podcast dedicated to the practicalities of GMing, and we had a pretty good skype discussion for it with Tavis Allison, John Stavropolous and Jason Corley, sadly my recording software was shit and fucked up the audio. I want to get back to it but (a) I've been very busy these past few months (b) I need to get better equipment and (c) I'm in Europe and the majority of internet gamers I know are from the other side of the pond so it's also a scheduling nightmare. But I promise I'll pick it up again as soon as I can.]

      4. In light of the above I'm slightly confused as to why "story gamers" would be disinclined to have this conversation (or these conversations, rather). Barring Shreyas' ideological wedge and Thomas' rather hasty and generalizing assessment that "this is a forum not overly eager to discuss GM-ship" (two posts among many [also, it's not like Story Games is this immutable bastion of conviction or whatever, I've seen it mutate several times over the years, and who knows where we'll sail next]) there certainly seems both interest and willingness to discuss these things. On other sites, too, ENWorld has quite a lot of GM Craft talk for example. The OSR is 25% about DIY content, 25% about archeology and 50% about GMing tricks and techniques. I think Chris Perkins is doing a pretty good job of discussing GM Craft on his The Dungeon Master Experience column (and there are countless 4E DM blogs out there). So I agree with those who say that this supposed Death of GM craft via Forge (or whatever) never really happened or rather that its influence was very limited. There's a lot of discussion going on, all the time. If anything, I think what happened with "forge" games in the last ten years is that designers attempted to incorporate their specific techniques, their actual play, their craft into the game text itself, integrate it into the rules themselves. But as Jason said, it's still just a text.

      Also, what is Sorcerer & Sword but page upon page of GM advice on how to run a sword & sorcery game, largely applicable across different systems, with zero crunchy bits (with a D&D supplement you'd get a bunch of prestige classes, feats and magic items, otherwise what's the point?)? And yet, lots of people don't know how to play Sorcerer at all (because it's a text), which is why Christopher Kubasik is writing Play Sorcerer (another text).

      5. So, as far as I'm concerned: do we need to talk about GM Craft more? Yes. Do we have to nurture and teach and share and mentor? Yes! Do we have to listen and learn? Yes!

      But do we have to do this because we somehow haven't been doing it, or because the discussion was dead, so we have to do it again? I'd venture a bold no. The discussion is and was always there, it's just never-ending and non-progressive, because there isn't a way to record and codify it to a satisfying degree (points 1, 2, 3 above).

      \n Ok, so this whole thread has been striking home for me. Although oddly, I find myself in agreement with the folks saying there should be more discussion of techniques in relation to 'trad' games and, and I also totally agree with the 'story game' folks about a ton of these things...
      \n
      \nOne of the biggest parts of this for me is what I ultimately decided was my skepticism of 'trad' and 'story' games being totally different things that have no relation to each other at all. Apocalypse World is a 'trad' game in soooo many ways. It's got traditional GM and player authority for the most part, it's got mechanics that have a strong aspect of human judgement, it places it's focus on being in the skin of your character rather than a specific dramatic structure, it's very, very trad. EXCEPT it's also cut out a lot of stuff that personally impeded me in a lot of more trad games (and impedes me in Pathfinder). Stuff like how it handles equipment, and the thoughtful design of character abilities.
      \n
      \nCompare this too, to Greg Stolze's games. They almost always avoid a lot of the stuff you'd find in say, D&D; 3e long equipment lists and poorly balanced magic system, you can get a Stolze design up and running a lot faster, IMO, than you can with most White Wolf games.
      \n
      \nBut the big clincher for me is Classic Traveller. Sure, there's lots of little things about it that I'd probably ignore, like the detailed mathematical formulas for gravity, but at the same time, it's advice for adventure creation is *very* good, it takes pains to set out 'procedures of play' a lot, and the skills were one of the most interesting things for me. Skills, you see, looked a hell of a lot like moves in Apocalypse World. Each skill gave the player access to a little mechanical tidbit they could use to influence the game world and fiction.
      \n
      \nThe OSR folks may dismmiss 'modern game design' a lot of the time, but they've gone and went back, and emphasized the bits of the game that helped them the most, and have made up formalized procedures to help them run better, and they hate all this indie stuff, from what I've observed.
      \n
      \nBut the problem you mentioned, Jocelyn, is again a very real thing, I think. A while back, I asked for some tips and advice on how to run Pathfinder, D&D; 3e here, and I didn't get much of what I was looking for. Part of that was my fault for not asking better, but I still was sort of surprised as to how little advice I got, besides the fact that magic is way too powerful (which is true), there was very little on how to emphasize the parts that work the best.
      \n
      \nWhat I'm getting at, is the problem seems to be on of communications. Story Gamers (myself included!) love things to be codified, so we codify it. The thing is, a lot of that codified stuff could be applied to 'trad' games quite easily, and you wouldn't even need to drastically change the rules or anything. We're getting at similar issues, but talking about them in different languages. 'Rules' vs 'not rules' seems a semantic distinction me, so I put it in terms of rules as I personally process better that way, others don't. And I think this, above everything else, is the big distinction here.
      \n
      \nAnd the GM mentor thing has set off a totally different set of thoughts I'm still stewing over, but my feelings on that are still murkier.

      \n Jocelyn,

      As far as I can tell, there is GM-craft discussion happening right now in this forum.

      Spotlight Hogging (How to manage your table)

      Object Based Magic (A system-agnostic discussion of making magic more interesting in your game)

      Fantasy World Bits (A system-agnostic world-building brainstorming session)

      Find Cool Maps (Finding resources)

      Explain Bangs (Creating situations that are meaningful to your players and demand response)

      Not all of these are exactly what you're talking about, but, in general, they're about gathering tools and materials and concepts/techniques that a GM can use to make a better game, irrespective of a specific game/system.

      So I'm not sure that your assertion that these things stopped happening is correct. Some of the discussions are different. Some of the responses are "This specific game does that well". Which is true. But, as has been said in this thread, that can still be of use to trad gamers because you can learn from what a recent game does well and take that concept back to your trad style GMing.

      Right now, I'm running a game using a semi-trad combat/task system - a modified Savage Worlds hack. And I'm planning to run my players through some old skool D&D modules, with tweaks to fit the differences for the game world I'm using.

      But I've just been reading through Apocalypse World and I really like how Fronts work. They make a lot of sense to me as a way of thinking about threats to the PCs. So I'm going to use that model to write down my thoughts about how the threats in my game are structured.

      So I'm taking a technique. That technique was developed within a specific, recent game text. But I'm going to use it outside that context. I'm going to put it in my GM-craft toolbox and make use of it.

      And I think that's a lot of what is happening in recent discussions. People have made games that do certain things extremely well. So when a GM wants to know about how to do that thing, it makes sense to point them at that specific game and say "Check that out, you can learn from it. And it might be the system you want to use to run your game. But even if it isn't, you might be able to adapt a few things from there that you can incorporate into your game."

      So, yeah, pointing people at things like that has replaced SOME of the best practices discussions.

      But others are still happening. Right here. Right now.

      \nWith this I agree 100%.

      The problem is that there were a few specific key innovations in the "indie" scene that when they got scaled up and generalized they kind of "ate" the much older long standing techniques.

      The notion that Relationship Maps in the broadest general sense is an "indie" thing is absurd. Faction Maps and Plot Maps have been around forever. I think Ron once credited GURPS Goblins as being the first published text with such and idea. The specific new idea presented in Sorcerer's Soul had to do with *restricting* such maps to connections of JUST sex and family. Doing that has some unique properties other kinds of maps don't.

      Similarly the innovative idea behind Kickers in Sorcerer and Beliefs in Burning Wheel was that the GM's prep work was to be based entirely on heightening and resolving the content of those things rather than simply using a way of enticing the player into following a pathway that ultimately lead to the resolving of the solely GM created material the way Melodramatic Hooks work in Feng Shui. But those things got bundled up into "Flags" regardless of usage.

      \n One other point to consider:

      Forget about player empowerment, flags, distributed authority/responsibility, actor vs. author stance, all that stuff. Just forget about it. Because all that stuff aside, many of the most culturally important trad games\u20142nd Ed. D&D, oWoD, Shadowrun, Exalted, even GURPS depending on how you look at it\u2014are simply completely broken from a fundamental mathematical point of view. Some aspect of the basic mechanics either doesn't work, doesn't work well, or only works well a) within a very small framework within the game or b) unless players choose some commonly-chosen but broken powers.

      So sometimes I just want to say to people, "Try playing a game written by someone who checked to see if the probabilities made any sense."

      Now, a more recent wave of trad games seems to have much better math, which in my opinion is a huge step in the right direction. There are still all sorts of weird edge cases (Solo-pinning in 4E comes to mind), breakdown points (above 8th level in 3.x, arguably), and overly-effective combos. And, perhaps more importantly for the purposes of this discussion, the mechanics still don't necessarily *do* anything meaningful outside of combat. But the only solution to that problem is some amount of story game-ification.

      But I do think that the mathematical progress among the trad games points to Jocelyn being right that this a good time to reengage with mainstream games and gamers.

      Still... man, just check your fucking math, will you!!! :-)

      \n Paul B is right when he says that some of Trad-GM Craft really is about the higher/more encompassing social level stuff. I call that dialogue techniques, and it has been part of my methods for role-playing games, both classical and modern, since 1989. When lecturing on GM-craft I tell the students that they, as GMs, may be said to "play the players".

      This ties neatly into what Aronson is saying of early gaming (I know about Braunstein, and the miniature-connection):
      Posted By: Peter AronsonWhat happened during the mission was determined using a mix of the supplied rules, house rules(*) and roleplaying. It wouldn't have occurred to us to have a planned plot -- that would have been cheating!
      It's a kind of open-field tactical simulation; with emphasis on how characters behave and respond in critical situations.

      Since then role-playing games has developed into open-field simulations of nearly any human condition, including how we fares in deadly combat. The emphasis on characters has become more poignant. Some great games are going deeper than ever, into psycho-social interaction of near-reality quality. Other great games are going lighthearted, playing on genre conventions, and characterizations full of bluff and comedy.

      The open field has been undermined in some circles, but that is not what classical role-playing games are really about. Rather; it is just an example of some people failing to get the gist of rpg. The open field, as a principle, is of great importance to rpgs. It's one of the foundations. It may be perceived as left out, to good effect, in some game-sessions, but not as an underlying principle of rpg-design. There must be some measure of open field, to have players make choices that influence the drama.

      Respect for the significant differences of the main game-forms within "rpgs" of today is important, and it is mostly present in this forum. To identify classical role-playing games with badwrongfun is as wrong as identifying modern rpgs with dreary social-realism, or story-games with fluff-gaming. We know each of them has these weaknesses, but we also know that the weakness is outmatched by the vast possibilities in each of these game-forms.

      But, lets go back to the "open-field simulations of nearly any human condition". This kind of game-play, especially in sandbox-mode (the ultimate "open field"), demands different things from game leaders of today. They need tools to influence the ritual and dialogue of the the games. The players must be helped into the right mood, and helped thematize the conflicts in keeping with the game at hand, wether it's comedy or tragedy. The psychology of the theme must be worked into the interaction.

      That is why games of today need to leave the one-sided focus on mechanics aside, and strive to work out a new methodical balance of game-mechanics AND dialogue techniques. We have discussed the art of leading role-playing games (or "GM-craft") since the very start, and those discussions have all been in the field of dialogue techniques; how to ritualize the game, how to empower and engage players, how to feed scenes, how to frame choices and communicate consequences, and so forth ...

      It is possible to explore the possibilities of stronger dialogue techniques both in classical rpgs (with a GM), in modern rpgs (without the GM) and in story games (with narrative focus). All of us will be better off for every discovery made in any of these game-forms.

      From my vantage point the new awareness of a broader method is THE most interesting development in classical roleplaying games of today. And it is directly linked to all the debates on GM-craft in the seventies, eighties and nineties. I would never have been where I am today, as a GM and designer, without them. The awareness of what role-playing games really are, and what they may achieve, would be so much smaller without those debates.

      So I fully understand Jocelyns point; that he wants players and GMs of today to have those debates too, as it has been a great boon to our society, and could still be.

      \n I think the community thing may be a tangent, but my experience here in the Midwest US was that there were essentially four groups of "trad" players/GMs, in decreasing order of prominence and numbers, and generally none of them intermingled (or had the slightest respect for) the others.

      1. Mainline D&D players - this would include the RPGA-type players as well as 4e people, Pathfinders, and the like. They all recognized each other as playing the same games, and while there was a fair amount of internecine conflict they definitely constituted a single community of play.
      2. GURPSies. This was the main group of dissidents, who were down on D&D but basically liked the same kind of highly mechanized and tactical mission-based games. The "cult of the GM" was strong here, but generally this was the domain of people who liked the D&D structure of play but rejected class-and-level mechanics. Shadowrun and HERO were popular among this group too, but GURPS was God.
      3. White Wolf players - often ONLY played WW, and a fair minority were devoted to one particular game and edition. Prided themselves on "maturity" and very much believers in System Doesn't Matter in the "we never roll dice" form. Often mechanics-averse, but not always - Exalted in particular brought out system-hackers. Sometimes would dabble in other narrative-forward, atmospheric games, but often found them wanting if they were not strong-GM.
      4. Freaks - often either devotees of one particular obscure game, an earlier edition of a popular game, or creators of idiosyncratic homebrew rule sets. Sometimes would play indie or non-trad games, but usually hacked them into unrecognizable forms. Usually had their own small clique of followers, but their only commonality was their disdain for the above three groups.

      \nUSABILITY is the key to GM advice.

      That's my opinion.

      Just because someone tells me a brilliant way to GM doesn't mean I'll remember it and act on it in the heat/immersion of the moment.

      So how do we make our GM advice usable? One way is to force it, to hard-code it into the game's mechanics, to say, "roll these dice to stat every monster". Another way is to turn it into play aids (e.g. John Harper's Apocalypse World MC reference sheet) and mnemonics (e.g. Burning Wheel's "Let It Ride").

      And then there are all sorts of approaches that are in between, such as instructions and constraints with lots of room for choices within them.

      That's the project I'd like for the RPG world to be working on: how to take our thoughts on GMing and really make them matter in play. We're flooded with GM advice that we can't use, and are rightly wary of it.
