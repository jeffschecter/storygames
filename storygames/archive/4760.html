\n I've unlocked Castlevania: Symphony of the Night on my copy of Rondo of Blood for the PSP and I'm in love again with the labyrinthine castle, the unlocking of areas via item collection, the pompous interactions of Alucard and Maria, and the eventual thought of having a sentient sword floating at my side attacking my foes or jumping to my hand for ridiculous combat.

Castlevania lovers: Keep the trappings of a Castle of Chaos that reforms itself with every reincarnation of Dracula and the desperate and repeated attempts of the Belmont family and Dracula's children to destroy him once again, but help me figure out how you'd story game it.

Or! Tell me how you'd game it right now, if you had a willing group and a game you think would work.

    \n You know, it's funny... as far as video games go, I'm not all that interested in Castlevania. I love platform games, but I prefer more dynamic, high flying platforming, double jumps etc.

    Now, the story of Castlevania... the Castle that rebuilds itself, the war of perennial evil... Pure genius. You have intense narrative themes and a deep, pregenerated cast of players... you couldn't ask for a better pregenerated background.

    Themes I would focus upon, mechanically: Generational play. If I make a Belmont (and EVERY party MUST have a Belmont; I'd think of the bloodline as something like a racial template), next session I should get a bonus if I play my previous character's descendent. Belmont Jr., Alucard the IV, Sophia, daughter of Maria... the whole thing could flow into a very engrossing campaign, and death wouldn't mean the end. Perhaps instead of leveling, you run your characters until they drop; then the next generation is more powerful (because they are determined to avenge their family's death, perhaps?), and the cycle begins anew. You can have much more leeway with lethal traps and the pratfalls of the Castle of Chaos, and make for a bit more threatening of a game experience.

    Imagine if, mechanically, every time you are the last man standing, you mustn't keep going... you MUST retreat. Narrate your character training his son to take down the Castle, all the while wearing the badge of shame. The one who retreats must visit the (likely only) child of each of his troupe, telling them of their parents' valiant deeds... Add a few abilities / skills / modifiers, change the name, and narrate each of the children, now grown adults with children of their own, finally building up the courage to conquer the Castle.

    I'm totally hooked, and I'm just a non-fan babbling ideas.

    \n I ran Castlevania as an RPG twice; once with a stripped-down version of Exalted for "heroic" mortals, and once with Donjon. The first game was the standard fight-your-way-to-the-tower-and-kill-Drac formula, except that one of the characters was the date that Dracula had stood up for the prom, and he gladly suffered one of the other characters teleporting him to hell rather than face her. The second game had the characters betting with Alucard on which of the three Belmonts in the castle would be the one to kill Dracula, and then sabotaging the other two. Good fun.

    Obviously, neither game was very serious, but they were both a hell of a lot of fun.

    Jarrod, if you want double jumps, Symphony of the Night is for you. It is, hands-down, one of the best side-scrollers ever made.

    \n At its core, Castlevania is an arcade game about killing things and collecting treasure. I'm willing to bet money that the vast majority of consumers don't buy or play Castlevania for "story". The Castlevania storyline has never been especially deep in any given Castlevania release and it certainly hasn't been the framework around which any given game in the series was built. Indeed, I'd go so far as to suggest that the "story" elements of Castlevania are only apparent when the entire body of games is viewed as a whole.

    This being the case, if I were to game Castlevania, I'd use a system that rewards killing things (the primary goal of the protagonist in every Castlevania release to date) with treasure (the primary reward for killing things in every Castlevania release to date). The obvious choice would be D&D, though it handles character growth in a manner quite unlike Castlevania (in Castlevania character growth is a long, arduous, and often frustrating process). Given this, I think that I might use vs. Monsters, which retains the primary motivation of killing things and uses an organic character advancement system. Although it ditches the money for killing aspect.

    Ultimately, I'm had-pressed to fidn a system that gets Castlevania exactly right for tabletop play, though D&D and vs. Monster both come awfully close.
