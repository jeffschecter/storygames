\n I saw on Vincent's G+ this link to a short film about a map that this guy, Jerry Gretzinger, has been working on for fifty years. From there, this link to his blog. I'm sure bunches of you have already seen it. I'd like to make a multi-player, world-building/map-making game inspired by Jerry's work. Who else would play this with me?

So, if you're interested, what should this game look like?

My first thoughts are that there are two obvious things that are really captivating. First, there's the way the map grows; outward, but also through revisions to already worked area. And the other thing is the role of his deck of cards as an interpreted randomizer.

As a multiplayer enterprise, I think the game would need a system for taking turns. I think the deck could be a die-roll on a table that expands over time. And participants could normally (unless constrained by rules/cards?) work in any medium -- software or physical as long as they can digitize their changes and pass them along to the next player. I guess the random table should start with: (1) a way to draw a new card/tile/plate/page on the edge of what exists, (2) a way for revising a card, and (3) a way for extending the table itself.

I'm interested to see if this has legs.

    maybe something like this, to begin:

    Rules:

    The map is made up of three-inch square tiles arranged in three dimensions to represent a fictional world. Each tile has an x,y coordinate pair and a generation, indicated as: (0,0):0 or (1,-1):2. The x and y dimensions indicate the the position on a plane -- the surface of this fictional world. The generation doesn't exactly correspond to an in-world real thing, but it helps the players keep track of how the world has developed.

    The game starts with (0,0):0 already extant.

    The players (perhaps starting with just one) take turns playing. On your turn, roll one of the following and follow the directions:

    1-2) Randomly select an existing tile by picking from among the x-values that exist, then picking from among the y-values in that column. If that tile has any vacancies adjacent to it, create a first-generation tile in one of those vacant spots. Remember to leave some blank space on the tile. If not, make alterations to the selected tile that change things up and/or add more detail but also maintain some a sense of historic continuity.

    3) If you have a clever idea for something to add to this table, append it to the list and pass your turn to the next player. Alternatively, you may weight one option on the list by adding or subtracting one to/from the range of numbers that triggers that event. Otherwise, treat it as if you'd rolled a 1.

    4) If you know someone else who'd like to play, invite them to join and if they agree, add them to the end of the player list and pass your turn to the next player. If not, treat it as if you'd rolled a 1.

    (Edited to remove the z-axis. It was cumbersome and I figure the underground will be better handled through rules that get added over time and probably as more autonomous entities.)

    \n I got to hang out with Jerry this weekend. so I can answer a few of the questions in this thread!\n
    How do you ensure the tiles of the map fit together seamlessly?
    He pulls the surrounding tiles. While we were there, he was working N 326, with the card direction to work on a certain feature, and he pulled the three tiles that touched that corner of N 326. Sometimes the card directions are really simple! "Upgrade services", "Expand population zones", etc. So some days he pulls six or seven cards, not just one.

    Also, there is a Minecraft mod of Jerry's Map: http://code.reddit.com/wiki/help/faqs/jerrys_mod
    This looks to have some of the mechanics figured out on how to make this multi-player.
