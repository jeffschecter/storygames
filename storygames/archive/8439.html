    \n I have almost never had success introducing those sorts of things in games as a player. This may be because I have played with a disproportionate share of cynical misanthropes, though. Typically, introducing things such as happy relationships, family ties, nostalgia, and the like are taken as an invitation to stomp on the object of those feelings in order to "get to the good stuff", which was the fighty-killy-thrashy-intriguey-angsty. Occasionally, it's just been laughed off uncomfortably.

    I have had a few good experiences with this sort of thing, but almost universally they occurred with the confluence of three factors (I don't know if these actually mattered, but they correlate).

    1. Predominantly female players and/or female GM
    2. Dirty hippie games that explicitly encouraged such content
    3. Non-fantastic or minimally fantastic genres

    As a GM, I've had mixed success introducing that sort of material. Often, it seems to lead to creative differences within the group, as one or two players dive in whole-heartedly and the others disengage and/or go looking for something more boom-bang.

    \n Last year I ran a game of Sorcerer that took place almost entirely in a State Penitentiary. Lore was rituals of domination and submission. Demons were guns and tattoos and cell blocks.

    The most significant moment of the game was one PC putting his hand on the shoulder of another PC. (Humanity gain roll!)

    I still remember that moment. I love that moment. (The guy who comforted the other PC was a convicted ex-cop who murdered his own partner. He was the last guy you'd think would ever be there for someone else. It was genuinely moving to see the guy shift as the shit hit the fan and he had to make choices where to stand.)

    And it's not at all the sole tender moments I've played.


    I'd offer that in stories, tender moments need to stand out in contrast to something that isn't tender. If things are just, you know, good... then not much is going wrong. Stories are about the equilibrium of life collapsing due to one reason or another.

    So, it's hard to force such moment, I think. But I think they often emerge as characters figure out their priorities (through the players of course), or the players realize, intuitively, they need a shift in tone to make the crisis bearable.

    But no... In Sorcerer, Sorcerer & Sword, Primetime Adventures, Grey Ranks, Dogs in the Vineyard, In a Wicked Age... I've found plenty of tenderness to spare.


    I just read some more posts that were made while I was typing.

    I will tell a story.

    When I sat down to play Grey Ranks at a local con (with some fantastic players, and Denys facilitating for us new players), I said before play, "I have one favor. This is about some terrible stuff. World War II. The disaster of the ghetto uprising. Jews being sent to camps... So. Tone. If we could keep a tone for the game, that would be great."

    And early on, when someone did crack a joke (a good one! to relieve tension!) I laughed, but slapped my hand on the table and shouted (playfully, I thought) "Tone!"

    I would later make a joke myself -- and Denys slapped the table and shouted "Tone!" Which got a big laugh from all of us. But it rallied us once more and kept us on track for the setting, situation and story we were making.

    Which is my way of saying, that as a GM I run a tight ship. And as a Player I try to encourage room for the appropriate tone as much as possible. Some games should be laugh riots. Others, not. Some, a mix. But it's up to me to make some decisions about what I think is worth pushing for at the table.

    I'm willing to push for it.

    \n Sorry to have muddied the waters. Yeah, tender <> good/happy/etc. My shorthand caught up with me.

    Warm fuzzies as resolution = okay
    Warm fuzzies as *action* = not okay

    \n I cry for you, Graham! To have such a wonderful tool at your disposal, and to see it abused like that; must be frustrating.

    I've been in a lot of great interaction on love, friendship and comfort, and some pure poetry too, in both ordinary rpgs and larps. Role-playing games are naturally suited to cater for any kind of human interaction. You only need the right method to support your interaction, and the game will sing any song.

    Since I published the naive, poetic rpg Muu in 1989 (Norwegian, not translated), there has been a widening of themes to be had in role-playing games here in Norway. The landscape is still skewed towards the standard action-drama (and it will always be), but you have so much more to choose amongst nowadays (including the games from your indie-scene).

    In the Nordic larp-scene the picture is even more promising. The multitude of themes to choose from is great. No one needs to sit and cry for want of play in any part of the emotional spectrum.

    I would wish for you to have more of caring and exploring players around you, Graham, to be served with a multitude of different games (you are, in fact, through this commmunity), and to have free reins with positive co-players to discover all kinds of happy and fun play. And that goes for all of you!

    \n I really love those tender, sweet (or bittersweet) moments.

    However, I've rarely been able to find those with fellow gamers. I've had the most such moments when roleplaying with non-gamers, people new to roleplaying.

    I have two theories about this:

    1. The roleplaying culture is coming out of an adolescent mindset, and those who remain gamers into later age simply want to continue in the same vein, in the same tradition, that they are used to--the style they created as young teens. New recruits have to conform to this to play with the grognards, or they leave.

    2. Traditional RPGs (basically, most of the games on the market) have such a heavy focus on combat and life-threatening action that roleplayers become conditioned, over the years, to associate all fun with combat and danger. I;ve had many people tell me (intelligent, mature people) that an RPG without combat is like pizza without grease or sex without sweat.

    I can see how that would happen--when the systems are all designed to give you more fun out fighting and skip over everything else, over the years a certain message will set in to your brain: "this is the way it always is".
