\n Hello fellow gamers,
I'd like to know what coherent game system you did use for playing shadowrun-style (underground mercenary team goes on missions in a cyberpunk fantasy setting). I want you to talk about real play experiences, or at least about a game preparation gone pretty far, not simple ideas that can pretty much reveal shitty.
When I say coherent, everyone understands that I mean story games / indie games / systems with rules you can actually apply (cross those you don't like).

I love the Shadowrun setting, and I loved reading the 4E Anniversary book, but I can't possibly imagine play it or GM it with all these paradoxical rules and no real framework.

Thank you, Story Gamers

    \n@Wordman - I like your stuff, but somehow missed it before.

    We found existing AW classes worked fine. For example. I played a Battlebabe as an emerging Street Sam with powers equating to getting cyberware, but I could just as easily played her as an Phys Adept. It played more about relationships than jobs.

    The fundamental scarcity worked, as you noted in your text. because food is scarce even if nutritients are not. I found the same thing in my goblin hack: if the scarcity only exists for the players and their cohort, not the whole world, it actually becomes more interesting and poignant, not less.

    Posted By: mattasticNocker, why do you think Shadowrun has no real framework?
    \nI can see no good advice for the GM to make interesting missions. For a game that supposedly asks the player to beat an adversity, it presents no real advice for the GM to decide when to stop, when to push and what type of adversity needs to be built. Another example, it keep saying that the purpose is to create an interesting story, but it also advices to be fair (which is paradoxical, to me). Also, the rules for each type of action are very complicated, and the only advice to apply them is "put the rules aside when they disrupt a good story", which is not really an advice, to me.
