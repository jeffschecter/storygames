\n Two pages is a wonderful number of pages, but why a new game each time?

I'm loving this oracle game revolution, but I'd love to see some oracle game stylings to spice up other games.

For example, I had a dream last night about owl-headed, winged pumas who spoke by resonating their deadly music off of your bones. They loved starlight and ate large snakes, and gave esoteric advice. If I could paint I'd paint them.

I don't see that as a kernel of a game, but as a supplement for another game it could be a great set piece.

    \n My impression is that a couple interesting things MAY be starting to happen, and the "playsets" are a possible sign of that.

    I could be totally wrong about this stuff below. That's ok. The result is probably the same.

    So, it seems to me that the indie scene is starting to get "tired." This is not meant to be an insult, by the way. I'm counting myself among the tired. It seems like it comes in two forms.

    First, game designers -- even (especially?) the ones who've already published in the last 7-8 years -- aren't publishing stuff like they used to. It became more difficult for a variety of reasons. Like, it's stressful to run the business side of things. Or, supporting existing games takes away time and creative energy from new ones. And, damn it, it's fun to design games. So, it's stressful/tiring to not be able to design new stuff. Plus you gotta plan a publishing schedule and half-assed marketing plan and get it all done before GenCon and how are you gonna find playtesters and ... PHEW!

    Second, while the audience for indie-style games has grown, the number of available games is almost obnoxious. Sure, if you're that person in your group who loves to learn new games, collect the latest, and so on, great. But, convincing your already-difficult-to-convince-let-alone-organize-for-sessions-each-week to try, oh, 6 news games in a year? That's tiring. You know what's easier? Mashing up the 2-3 games you do like and playing a session or three with your pals. FUN! Less tiring.


    Thus, I think there are a few tendencies or results. One is that, at least in my opinion, we're getting fewer really compelling games over all. Maybe that's purely my personal reaction to things, so no biggie. The second thing is that we're getting more of these kinds of down and dirty playsets like Lady Blackbird. The third thing (the one I'd most like to see, really) is that people start "supporting" their go-to games with exceptionally cool playsets comparable to Lady Blackbird. In another recent thread, I asked for open/free games. Those alone could spawn kick-ass situational playsets easily, to say nothing of the extremely-easy-to-acquire permission to support your favorite indie RPG. Want to make a "playset" for, oh, With Great Power? You'd probably have to tell Michael Miller to shut up already with shouting "PERMISSION GRANTED!"

    And, honestly, I think this is fucking great. Look how many people posted about Lady Blackbird positively. Now, look how many already played it and had a great time. Awesome! Now, consider: How many people have playtested and posted about anything else released in the last 3-4 months? My read (again, I may very well be wrong) is that more people have already played and enjoyed Lady Blackbird than have playtested and posted about, say, Rustbelt from the Ashcan Front last year.

    In short, while I'm sure there are downsides, I think this trend is really, really fucking good. I think that because it means the hobby's become more, um, "hobby-like" than it might have been 2 years ago. Maybe Lady Blackbird later becomes a bigger thing, a more commericialized thing for John. That's fine. It's his call. But, meanwhile, I KNOW he's inspiring others to do the same kinds of thing just for fun.

    Hell, I want to do one myself. It's the first time I've wanted to create anything RPG-related in a long, long time.

    \n Matt,

    I see what you mean, oracle games rock and are a lot of fun, but I worry a little if things move too far in that direction. While I love seeing people churn out stuff mostly for their own uses and just post it as an after-thought, I worry that this could make story games (indie RPGS, whatever) more insular. It seems like a focus on throwing together new stuff, then leaving it when it gets old abandons any hope of the game having a broad appeal.

    I feel like oracle games primarily appeal to those who already have some experience with these type of games. That's great, but we need games that are self-explanatory too. I'm not arguing against oracle games, I just would hate to see it become so much of a trend that they become the standard.

    I guess I'm kind of torn. I love seeing an awesome idea summed up in a few pages, but I don't want to see every game go that way. If the majority of games move to that format, I feel like we'd be loosing some of the complexity and some of the strength of larger games. Maybe I'm just strange (or, you know, strange-er), but I don't want every game to be bite-sized.

    Basically: give my Lady Blackbird, so long as we don't abandon Burning Wheel along the way.

    \n Ryan, I have a stack of things exactly like what you are suggesting sitting on my harddrive waiting to be played (for the record: 2 "playsets" for Mouse Guard, 1 for Bliss Stage, 2 for Cold City, 1 for Dogs in the Vineyard). But I'm not going to release them until I actually try them out and have a chance to tweak them based on the results. They're also, y'know, primarily meant to be fun things for me and my local group to play, not products to be sold or even given away for free. The product part is totally secondary to the fun I'm personally planning on having with them.

    Honestly, I think the few playsets that have been posted since John threw Ghost/Echo and Lady Blackbird up are approaching the whole playset-making thing a bit backwards (no offense to Matt Wilson, etc.). One of the coolest parts about John's playsets, to me, was they were basically a window into the games John was currently running and allowed folks to potentially "play along" and see what he was doing. I can't really imagine, though, that creating playsets in isolation from actual play is really going to be that great. I don't really care how you might play, honestly (that's like hearing about your lonely fun, planning a campaign that may never happen). But I might care to hear about what you are actually playing with other people, why it kicks so much ass, and how I can play something kick-ass like that too.

    Basically, I can sum up how I feel about design right now in a single phrase: I want to see stuff that is currently generating awesome fun for the designer and co. I want that to be the reason people are sharing it with others. If the stuff you're sharing isn't generating awesome fun for you yet, I don't really want to see it unless you're asking for help with something that's not working. Otherwise, you're probably just showing it to us to get attention on the internet, y'know? And Moreno's right, that is a danger. But as long as what you're sharing is really rocking your socks in play, such that you can't help but talk about it, I think it's gold. Real, true excitement spreads. Attention-seeking spreads too, but not really in a positive way.

    Also, I think there are quite a few of these mini-supplements out there already, like Journeying West for Under the Bed and The Roach Returns containing two playsets for the Shab Al-Hiri Roach. So there you go, fence painted. Also, I'm painting the fences I see just fine enough for me, thank you. Are the fences in your life all bare?

      Posted By: Matt SnyderChristopher (and Jonathan, but you know this), you realize that Keith's game,Conspiracy of Shadows: Dirty Hands, is free, right? 'Cause, ya'll are talking right past one another.
      \nYup, I know that. And I'll be the first to admit that I responded out of emotion. Because I feel angry when I read Keith.

      Personally, if I had to name the reason why I think Lady Blackbird appealed to me I think I would name mechanics, length of product, name of designer, price, the look, and focus (not in that order and not all equally).

      Three of those are feet in the door (hey, I liked Agon so I'll take a look at Lady Blackbird), it's short so I'll read it right now instead of later, and it's free so I'll read it now instead of never.

      One of those allows me to finish reading it. It looks pretty and is therefore easy to read (I am not struggling to stay with it), heck length plays into this too.

      And finally we come to mechanics and focus. I think this game taps into something important, which is the whole focus angle. I think there's an appeal to very strictly defined situation and characters that has been more or less sidelined in RPGs but has been very functional in other story-telling mediums. Some games try to get to do this yourself with specific instructions and tools. Some games hope you do this yourself but give you no tools or instructions. And other games (most in my experience) give you tools and instructions that actively work against this. Which is why I think Lady Blackbird will see more talk and more play than, say, GhostEcho. Because it is an example of how to bring the module (as in the dungeoncrawl) to characters (which has been attempted many times (examples abound in Vampire, Werewolf, Mage, Shadowrun even, basically any module where events are treated as if they were rooms in a dungeon) but never really worked in my opinion).

      As for mechanics, lordie how I love keys.

      EDIT' Actually, were I to compare this even more with Ghost Echo I would point out that I found Ghost Echo difficult to read, without mechanics that I juiced over, and without the focus. Still read, still interested, but not as jazzed about it as I am Lady Blackbird. Meaning that, at least for me, price, name, and prettiness only go so far.
