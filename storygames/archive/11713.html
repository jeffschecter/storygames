    \n Three parallel reactions to your post.

    1. RPGs are not real life, and for the most part, they don't try to emulate it. They are closer to literature, theatre and film. Violence in art is dramatic, shocking, cathartic, meaningful or (gasp) fun. Real life is not really like Rambo or Die Hard either. Or Richard the 3rd or Macbeth for that matter. Then again, the world is an extremely violent place, we just overlook it most of the time. There has not been a day of complete peace since WW2 which means, somewere, people are killing each other right now.

    2. RPGs share their ancestry with wargaming. The ancestry is obvious, especially in more oldschool games, the apple doesn't fall far from the tree. Not much to discuss here.

    3. In many "contemporary" RPGs, violence is far from easy or it at least brings up many questions and shows us the consequences of violence. Some games put violence last. Dogs. MLwM. Doctor Who.

    \nSlightly tangential, but one of the things I like about Unsung (which I know I end up shilling for a lot) is that shooting someone isn't a Guns roll, or a Precision roll, or anything like that - it's a Guts roll. The "hard" part is working up the nerve to kill another human being. It turns out, at least with reliable weapons, that may be a big part of what's hard about it in real life - see, e.g., Grossman's _On Killing_.

    In addition to JDCorley's point, though, I think that it may have something to do with the fact that most of us *don't* use a lot of violence in real life... but, you know, the thanatos impulse is always there and especially in our (my, at least) culture the message that violence solves things is reinforced heavily. So it's tempting to play with. So maybe that's part of why so many games revolve around violence.

    As for why violence is maybe relatively easy to implement, technically? Maybe what's attractive about toying with violence is toying with its outcomes, not about trying to figure out if I could shoot a pistol straight if I put my mind to it. Heck, that I *can* learn to do if I really wanted to.

    \n Our games are set up with different consequences for violent behavior than we'd experience in the real world. Why? Because we're creating a playground where it's safe to play out those fantasies. Why do we need to play out those fantasies?

    Why do people play at all? We play to try out scenarios and get some experience with things that frighten us. Role-play is no different.

    Why do people play out violence? Violence frightens us, so we practice it. Power fantasies are an extreme form of playing out scenarios that frighten us because they let us experience what would happen if we were above it all.

    \n Man, violence is easy in real life. It's the 'Fallout' that's hard - broken bones and broken lives. Working up the nerve can be hard for some, but for others it's keeping control when they're upset. I find that some people are just looking for a reason to get violent and self-righteous, whereas others wouldn't know what to do with their first, or a knife, or a gun, to save their life. Psychologically speaking, violence always destroys people internally.

    The disconnect in most games is twofold: one, that there's necessarily no narrative consequences - i.e. being arrested and tried for assault/murder/carrying a concealed weapon/etc. In most 'modern' setting games this would be pretty boring and not address the premise of the game. Not mention the sort of people that the character would have to be and become to commit these crimes with an regularity. Two is that most 'combat' mechanics are to real violence what chess is to medieval warfare. Because the mechanics are predictable and abstract, they conceal the fact that almost all violence incidences in reality are ambiguous, quick and messy events that are largely beyond anyone's control, or are horrifyingly asymmetric.

    For many players and game designers, representing these realities isn't interesting to them. Vincent Baker includes them in his games to some degree. I agree with Adam in the sense that often our characters are toys or experiments and we play with them accordingly. I find the Hit Point style of design (even with dice modifiers) is incredibility tedious; some sort of narrative condition is far more interesting to me as a player and better represents the 'reality' of reality.

    At any rate, interesting topic!

    \n In my opinion, violence is present in RPGs for a couple reasons:
    1) Violence is a side-effect of action, which is fun.
    2) People in RPGs tend to have highly conflicting views, and one way to solve (to a certain definition of "solve" anyways) any dispute is through violence
    3) Letting proper authorities take care of things and resolving disputes through reason and compromise is often boring and/or difficult. Many RPGs carry few penalties for violence, and thus it becomes a preferable method of handling things, especially when you consider than in many RPGs the players a pre-disposed to win violent conflicts.

    \n Any sort of story [almost] involves conflict, and violence is an expression of conflict that's familiar but usually forbidden, and thus exciting to pretend about. Same reason that kids' games often involve play-violence.

    Also, physical conflict is by far the easiest kind to simulate in a game. Various RPGs have rules for mental/social/emotional conflict (like BW's 'Duel of Wits') but they tend to be unsatisfying because nonphysical damage is harder to put metrics on than physical damage, and because the nonphysical aspects are the things that _aren't_ being simulated in the game: if a PC and an NPC argue, they're using real words that are being interpreted by real minds. That tends to make the conflict rules feel arbitrary and controlling, since the simulated result might not match what happened IRL. This doesn't come up with physical combat because you're not really fighting.

    \n What Jens said, what Ken said.

    Not only is interpersonal conflict (person versus person) easy to relate to, it's easy to describe.
    Person versus themselves or versus their environment is more difficult to describe, and that's what we're doing when we role-play,
    we're describing things.

    The culture of the West (you can draw the exact lines wherever you like) puts a big emphasis on Rugged Individualism, which is to say
    that a single person fighting the odds is familiar to us, familiar down to our bones, and so we latch onto that, because it is easily understood.
    It resonates with us, as that it is the story of our culture.

    In our culture, people don't co-operate with one another except to defeat another group (with exceptions, of course.)
    Co-operation is unfamiliar and difficult to describe, so we're left with conflict.

    Why violence though?
    I think that violence is easy. Easy to describe, easy to provoke a reaction, easy to make something seem really immediate and important when there is a danger of (fictional, described) violence.

Imagined violence on imagined enemies allows a freedom of action and freedom from fear that almost can't help but be somewhat extreme.

    \n Here's an interesting twist on violence in games:

    I wonder if it's something that we can't get away with in real life, and thus roleplaying it makes it that much more exciting? And I use "wonder if" to mean "pretty sure"...

    Mainly, this example: A roleplaying game from Japan called SATASUPE ("Saturday Night Special"). It is a self-proclaimed "Asian Punk RPG", and the game has rules for basically just four things: Guns, Fucking, Chases and Drugs.

    Now the thing is, drugs doesn't get a little ruleset like "if you do this drug, you get these bonuses for x hours", it goes off into a whole sub-ruleset involving character powers, backgrounds and cinematic advantages and disadvantages to being involved in the drug culture.

    Thing is, drugs are pretty taboo in Japan: You can probably talk to 5 random people in the US and find out that 2-3 of them had tried pot (depending on where you lived, yadda yadda); but in Japan it's very rare. Also, all illegal drugs are lumped into class-A felonies: So having a kilo of marijuana is the same as having a kilo of heroin in Japanese law. If a movie or TV star is caught taking drugs (and they rarely say the drug involved in the news, just "drugs!"; could have been uppers, crystal meth, weed, etc), they're done.

    It seems that this game, with it's huge cinematic take on drugs (along with typical violent action, chases and romance) could only have come from Japan: It's not as big of a "real problem" in Japan, and thus easier to fantasize about it?

    Some thoughts.

    \n The most popular RPGs involve magic and violence. Both things that we don't get involved in very often. I don't find adventuring in a modern day world to be much fun - I know what it's like, it's too much like real life. In a made up fantasy world, with magic, on the other hand, it's something completely foreign. Same goes for a sci-fi game that involves traveling the stars - it's something that isn't part of my life, so the make believe aspect is also strong. It also helps that violence is something most people don't know much about. I always run into this with grappling rules in RPGs. It's something I do, so I always find problems, and always need to fix it. Most other people don't notice it's a problem. If something comes up involving swords or guns, I'm not going to give it a huge deal of thought, while a sharpshooter might be quite annoyed. This also ties in somewhat to where there are heavy mechanics and where there are none. If it's expected most people know it, like negotiation and social interaction, there isn't a complex subsystem, it's just a roll to see what happens, or skip the roll entirely. If you don't know how it works really, you let the mechanics decide it for you.
