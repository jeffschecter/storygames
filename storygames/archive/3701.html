    \n Graham, might it be the case that you are referring less to the games themselves, and more to the authors of said games?

    As Minx said, many independent games and their authors take their fun very seriously. In an effort to improve on old tried and true concepts, they take on a deconstructive, academic approach. Instead of saying:

    "This is the story of a farmboy who grows up to lead a nation to victory against an evil from beyond the stars, here's how you play a farmboy just like that"
    they say
    "This is a classic example of a Hero's Journey monomyth with the following 5 (or 8) archetypal acts. These are the rules to lead you through those acts in a way that optimize the thematic aspects of each one"

    And if they don't say the latter in the game text proper, they certainly do on Internet forums when they discuss their game. What I'm not sure about is whether high-exposure WotC or WW games receive the same academic treatment behind closed doors or not.

    In general, I think this is great. The irony, of course, is that while the latter approach will certainly ensure that the game is kept tight and focused around what makes the story what it should be, it can also make the story creation process - "play" - very mechanical. I think the "indie" game movement (if there is such a thing) need to avoid constructing Sacred Cows and judging those who slaughter them. If someone uses Dogs in the Vineyard to play a party of paladins delving into dungeons and recovering holy artifacts - and have fun doing it - there is little need to lecture them on the moral ambiguity lessons inherent in the application of town creation rules yadda yadda yadda.

    I'm not a big fan of the much-despised "Rule 0", but mostly because it is often presented as an excuse for producing a clumsy and disjointed rule set by placing the editing responsibility on the players. I like my games simple, elegant, and internally consistent - and if a game reaches that point there is no need to stress "Rule 0". Having said that, it still boils down to player satisfaction, and many players aren't interested in the deep philosophical reasons why odd dice faces means that the Divine Authority receives narration rights that infringe on your Soul Essence's creative agenda. Or whatever.

    Posted By: lachekAs Minx said, many independent games and their authors take their fun very seriously.
    Just for the record, while I did say that, you and me seem to understand it very differently. ^_^

    When I say "indie designers take their Fun very seriously", I certainly wasn't talking about them taking "on a deconstructive, academic approach". What I meant was more about their (perceived) design ethic: That FUN, whatever kind of fun is aimed for, is worth working very, very hard for. That it isn't enough to just shake a few rules out of your sleeves and fudge the rest, but that FUN deserves well-thought mechanics, playtesting and just plain old HARD WORK from the designer.

    That even a wacky let's-play-psychotic-right-wing-hamsters-infiltrating-gay-right-groups rpg* deserves to be taken seriously by their designer. Because they strive to make a good product.

    (And just to make sure: I'm really not trying to say that the same doesn't go for many "mainstream" designers or anything. It's just an attitude I've, personally, always felt more strongly in the Indie community, but that could just as well be just me seeing things.)

    *Not that I'm aware of such a game. Sadly.

    Posted By: Lord MinxWhen I say "indie designers take their Fun very seriously", I certainly wasn't talking about them taking "on a deconstructive, academic approach".
    \nApologies for putting words in your mouth. I don't mean to say that "indie" game designers are killjoys, but frankly, I want designers to pay more attention to the rules that are driving my stories than "Roll under Strength on a d20 to see if you can break stuff". I want them to take an academic approach. I want there to be unforeseen side effects to meddling with the system, because I want the game design to be tight and streamlined.

    It's just that, sometimes, I get the impression that this thinking is visible between the lines in actual game texts. The next time this happens I will be sure to post a better example, but for now, consider the many passages in Sorcerer that justify various game design decisions, or in Donjon where the "game currency" (1 success = 1 fact = 1 die etc) is mentioned. As a GM, I have nothing against these passages, and as a player interested in game design, I am fine with them as well, but I imagine that many gamers would wonder why such parts are necessary. Just tell me what to roll!

    Compare Magic: the Gathering rules. They tell you what you can do and how, and define victory conditions. They don't discuss how a Green Sapling Dryad deck is purposely disadvantaged against a red Lightning Bolt deck, and what steps you may take to rectify this - although this is what they really need to do in order to teach expertise in the system. Those that are interested in being experts in the system will have to learn this themselves. Personally, I would like to be an expert at any game I run for my players, because I don't want to fumble their game experience because of my stumbles, so I want my book to tell me this. My players, on the other hand, just want to know what they can do and how. Some GMs I know would be turned off by this, as well - they figure their intuitive idea of how the game should run is more important than how the game designer intended it to be run. If you're only going to release one book for all these crowds, you will have to carefully balance the language between them, or clearly separate sections (as, for example, Sorcerer does).

    With Rich's post, he's cleared up any confusion on my part as to what Graham meant (exactly what he said, duh) so if anyone would like to comment on this further please feel free to do so in a new thread KTHXBYE.

      If anything, the reality is that "Story Games" (as defined as the games we've discussed so far) are just more focused, less universally applicable, games with regards to the stories they tell.
