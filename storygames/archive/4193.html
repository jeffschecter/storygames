    \n i haven't played the game yet, but i've got a copy of it and i'm hoping to play it soon. i won't say too much because Chris Moore is around here, and i'm sure he'd probalby do a much better job at telling you about it.

    but the tag line i was told at Gen Con was "It's like the Bourne Identity with psionic powers." basically the PCs were all being taken somewhere when the vehicle crashes and they are able to escape. they don't really remember anything about themselves or who they were with or why. your "character sheet" consists of a list of questions, and these questions both imply things that are true about your character (i.e. "Why do things explode when I get angry?" and "Why do I know how to fly a fighter jet?") and create questions that will eventually be answered during the course of the game.

    \n Hey, Caesar,
    \nHere's Psi Run.
    \nYou have no memory of your former life.
    \nYou've been captured by some shadowy organization.
    \nWhile being transported somewhere by your handlers, the vehicle crashes, leaving you alive and the guards dead.
    \nThe "Crash" scene is the first scene of every game. The characters (Runners) try to avoid recapture, while piecing together flashbacks of their former life. It's easy, fast, and there's a cool mechanic where the Chasers (that shadowy organization) follow the Runners trail. There's a playtest here and
    \nhere.
    \nHope that helps!
    \nChris

    \n One cool thing about Psi Run is the emergent development of a coherent story through play - you start out knowing literally nothing about your circumstances, but by the time the game ends, you've got a very compelling rationale for why all the strange things are happening. It's essentially the "Mountain Witch Trick" covering the entire game. This isn't ground-breaking but Psi Run does it effortlessly.

    \n The game also beautifully coordinates gm, player and group input using the conflict resolution system. Aces in form and content.
