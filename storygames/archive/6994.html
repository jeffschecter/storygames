\n I was having a discussion with a friend of mine about how the trend of RPGs is going away from games with a lot of crunch (i.e. diceless games). I find myself in the middle, at a happy medium. While i love getting into a character, delving into their personal stories, and creating a great game, i also love rolling dice and using abilities based on the different systems out there. I've always been fascinated with systems and love a well crafted one.
\n
\nSo, i'd love to hear all of your opinions on the matter. Some try to use as little of a system as they can and others might as well be playing the "Game of Calculus RPG". lol. Where do you fall in this spectrum?

    Posted By: First OniI was having a discussion with a friend of mine about how the trend of RPGs is going away from games with a lot of crunch (i.e. diceless games). I find myself in the middle, at a happy medium. While i love getting into a character, delving into their personal stories, and creating a great game, i also love rolling dice and using abilities based on the different systems out there. I've always been fascinated with systems and love a well crafted one.

    So, i'd love to hear all of your opinions on the matter. Some try to use as little of a system as they can and others might as well be playing the "Game of Calculus RPG".
    \nI think your friend has a misperception. I don't see any movement toward diceless, let alone low crunch games - and the two are not the same thing. Nobilis is fairly crunchy, but it's diceless. In A Wicked Age is really low crunch, but uses all kinds of dice from d4s to d12s.

    What I do see is a movement toward focused game design. There are more and more games that focus on doing a small number of things really well instead of trying to do everything decently. And D&D4 has shown me that this just isn't a small press thing. Game design in general is moving forward.

    My opinion is that trying to use as little of the system as possible means you are playing the wrong game. Use the system to its extend. If you're not getting the results you want, use another system. The results you are getting might be close enough that "another system" involves altering the game you're using. This might mean houserules, or it might mean cutting out rules. But deciding to cut out combat rules (for example) of a game doesn't mean you are playing that game and not using the rules as much, it means you are playing another game wth different rules.

    So, the difference between "Calculus lol" and not using the system all depends on personal preferences. If it does what you want, then it's not going to feel cumbersome (or "crunchy") regardless of how detailed rules interaction turns out to be.

    \n
    \nWhat's crunch for? My aim in roleplaying is to enact and create compelling stories. I think (at least for me) this can be described as my desired end condition for a rpg game (in the mechanics level). And to simplify things, let's look at games that basically have just some kind of conflict resolution where your resources are, well your resources with which to succeed. Which usually is your character status plus some story currency. (hang with me, I think I have a point)
    \n
    \nNow if we have players and a GM, the players' desired story goals tend to align much more on their characters than the GM's story goals are aligned with potential NPC characters. And, generally the GM has a greater supply of resources (for example in Dust Devils just make up an extra NPC and you can use her stats in a conflict, though in PTA this is (wisely) limited).
    \n
    \nSo, where does the crunch get into this? It basically gives each player different tactical options within the game mechanics to achieve different things in the fictional level. If you can't for example choose to use your weaker stat against an opponent you would like to face but would like your character lose to (or if you happen to win, win with a kind of miracle), your only option in the story level is to win. That can happen if the system does not have enough stats or something like that. The same goes for GM: he has to have enough options to affect the story towards his preferences, but probably should not be able to use the system to ram his ideas over the other players (that's why many people don't like task resolution, right?).
    \n
    \nI thought of this the day after our Dust Devils game came to its end. All the player characters met their end in the final session, because they felt like that would be the coolest thing to do at the time. They could do that because they had played in a way which allowed that option: Even though their stats were pretty good overall, each of them had decided to take damage during the earlier conflicts in a way that left a stat important to each of their characters to zero. And when the time was right, they plunged to their last desperate conflicts. That was way cool. And just enough crunch to allow this kind of story-level tactical thinking made it possible.

In building my classical FRPG Fabula into a game with more facets than the average classical RPG, I try to make it more "crunchy" in several fields, to make the fields of contact "drown" the players (a kind of immersion through system).\n
