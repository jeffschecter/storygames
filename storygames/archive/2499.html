What central authority is good for

TonyLB
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games
Over in this thread I started a discussion of the ways in which central authority is a strong and reliable foundation for a social contract ... but it was by contrast with other stuff, and the other stuff proved so fascinating that we've had a fun discussion about it, which I wouldn't have missed.

At the same time ... central authority! Neat, huh? Good for a hundred and one household uses! Clean your floors, polish your silverware, snake your toilet ... or, even better, delegate those tasks to someone else!

Socially, passing the functional buck to a central judge or arbiter seems to go along with passing the social buck to the same central figure. That can be a very good thing: human beings organize well into packs. It's a pattern our instincts make familiar and easy.

Indeed, getting together a group of people for the first time, where I care about the long-term stability of the group (i.e. not convention games) I might well lean toward a game with central authority, purely to provide a structure for everyone to discover each other without risk of pushing dysfunctional relationships.

How do you use this powerful tool?

    Central authority is a highly useful tool for certain types of play. In particular, play where the focus is on the experience of being in the game world and play where the focus is on some form of mystery or puzzle solving.

    Why so? Because by centralising authority you appoint a creator of world content or a creator of the underlying mystery. As such, the other participants are empowered to concentrate on the experience of being in that world or solving that puzzle without having to simultaneously worry about creating part of that world.

    For some players this is a really big deal, as for some the act of creating world content significantly impairs their ability to then absorb into the experience of being in that world. For other players this is not such an issue, or experiencing the world is not so important anyway, and so it may be of less use to them.

    Posted By: TonyLBWhat I'm asking here ishow do you deliberately USEthe structure of central authority to make your gaming better.
    The main things I see using it for is ensuring that everyone get's their spotlight time, pushing ahead when consensus isn't clear, and handling continuity.

    1) Spotlight Time

    The spotlight time issue was something that struck me while attending a seminar last weekend. In seminars I was used to there being a moderator who would cut off people who went on to long, and made sure that other people had their turn. With a moderator, people would raise their hands and be called on. In this case without one, I felt I had to interrupt someone to be able to get my comments in. Because there were a few people who would go on, it was an aggressive environment, and some people just never got to speak.

    2) Pushing Ahead

    Pushing ahead when consensus isn't something I do a lot, but it comes up from time to time. One of the interesting things about Forge-associated games is that many of them encourage the GM to do scene framing, whereas I more commonly leave that up to consensus. The players say what they are going to do, and we skip ahead as we agree. If someone wants to not skip ahead, they generally can. However, there are times when I'll call something and skip to someone else, and so forth.

    3) Continuity

    This is acting as a consistency check on background. This particularly applies when you've got a game where there are secrets, but also works for other things by having attention to it. Finding inconsistencies isn't something easily distributed -- you need to have at least one person who both knows everything and is actively looking through it. Multiple people can add redundancy to this, but they can't split up the job. The key thing is that by having this, I can not only create secrets (i.e. reveal new background to one or all players) but I can quickly confirm suggestions from other participants as working or not working.

    I think that central authority does not have to mean just a GM. It is the notion of who has power that is important and how much authority they have to use that power.

    Tony asked what my specific experience has been (which by the way is a perfect act of central authority to direct a conversation. He starts off with agreement and ends with the corrective miffed growl at my post. It worked because the thread didn't go off topic. A+ moderating.)

    The first central authority in all games are the rules. These define what people do and how. In board games that is all the authority that is needed because they limit the range of action so much. Players move around in the world and make things happen - the rules give them authority on how to do that. RPGs give players power to make things happen as well (even in the most simulationist game). So central authority is not harmed by the idea of division of labor.

    I do Engle Matrix Games, in them the division of labor is that the players make arguments about what happens next in the world. They alone create. They can stay focused on their characters but they don't have to. As is needed for the story to work, they can say what other characters do. The rules provide a "plot track" that suggests a sequence of actions that should happen to tell the kind of story they are playing. The rule's authority is suggestive not mandated so player's range of action is not curtailed.

    One player is the referee of the game (though this role can be shared - passed around from player to player). The referee labor is to be the voice of reason. They determin the chance of success of the player's arguments. The advice I'm giving to first time referee's now is to start off giving all arguments a 50/50 chance of happening. After a couple of turns they can start using their judgement to rule some arguments stronger or weaker. Once their confidence is up they can act as a real central authority to keep games on an even keel.

    MGs are a very robust system. They work even if the players make "poor" arguments and the referee is practically random in ruling. When players make good arguments and the referee is confident in their judgement they work great.

    Unlike a narrativist RPG view of story now the effect is more detached and problem solving like. They are verstile though with the same rules working for murder mysteries, spy thrillers, action adventures, horror stories and more.

    Players who don't want to step outside of their characters in session will have problems with the division of labor because it calls on them to create. On the other hand, they are relatively easy on the referee. I've GMed games for 30 years and know how much work it is. It burns out people. This shares labor and thus saves GMs.

    Chris,

    I think that central authority does not have to mean just a GM. It is the notion of who has power that is important and how much authority they have to use that power.

    Yes, of course. Because "GM" is actually a bundle of several different tasks (creative control, referee, adversary, and so on) that can be assigned to different people, as we went into on the other thread. This is kind of what I was getting at with my question for clarification, because I wanted to know if Tony only meant games where the majority of authority-based tasks are vested in one person, or whether he just wanted to look at games that assigned a specific task to one person throughout play.

    At any given time, only one person can have authority over one task. What makes games different is whether there is a process for transferring authority: How often does the authority change? What triggers the change? Games on the more collaborative end (what I would call games with fleeting authority, to use an old classification scheme) have methods for transferring authority on demand, either informally (interrupting someone in a freeform game) or formally (winning a die roll in InSpectres, etc.) I'm sure Tony is excluding games where authority is fleeting for most of the tasks.

    I think a lot of the benefits of central authority (fleeting or fixed) boil down to "talent". To take the less glamorous task of interpreting the rules, that task usually goes to the person who's most familiar with the rules; it might shift if the rules arbiter recognizes that another player knows more about a given section of the rules, but this is an informal and usually temporary process (I can't think of any RPGs that allow formal challenges about who gets to interpret the rules.)

    Creative authority is likewise centralized when one player is regarded as having more talent or knowledge, either overall or for a specific vision ("Bill's suggestion for a sci-fi/horror game about intelligent spiders sounds interesting. Do you have it written up, Bill? GM it!") Authority over social disputes is pretty much always assigned to the best negotiator in the group and isn't related to the game rules at all.

    With a centralized GM, I find it easier to play in a no-holds-barred sandbox fashion, yet still have a unified game vision. Where a traditional board game has well-defined rules, which allows you to exploit them fully without having to worry about "playing in the proper spirit", the rulings of a GM who has a strong, consistent vision also provide the predictability that's necessary for meaningful manipulation of the game-world as an external object. (I think it's a little easier to be consistent with oneself than with a group of others.)

    This is in addition to the advantages that Balbinus described above. I'm talking about central authority as referee or judge; the GM may or may not have made up the scenario, and may or may not be playing "the opposition" (someone else could be delegated that task), but by being fair and consistent, and as impartial as possible, the GM gives everyone else the freedom to concentrate on playing their character.
