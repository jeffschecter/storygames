\n Played my first session of Dust Devils last night. It might not be our cuppa, and that's fine, but I want to make sure I totally and completely understand the intent of the rules before I make that decision.

Item the first:

Okay, so you start out with your scores, your traits, a past and present, and your Devil. (My guys all went 3 (!) on their Devils, which turns out was probably a super-bad idea for newbies.) You get into some conflicts. It appears to me that each conflict is, ultimately, an opportunity to earn chips, mostly via opposing one's own traits and Devil; we started at 0, so the chip economy started poorly lubricated.

The players pretty much absolutely refused to earn chips. Their value is almost totally opaque early on, since they didn't know how they'd work in The End (see item #2 below). Besides not really seeing the value of chips, they were also terrified of giving up 1 (for a trait) or 3 (for their overjuiced Devil) or 4 (for both!) cards, because Harm hurts so, so much. As the Dealer, I also whipped out some extraordinary hands -- three Full Houses, a couple straights, a flush. The players got their asses statistically kicked, resulting in a death-spiral of ever-decreasing-effectiveness inside the game.

So my question is this: Is this how Harm is supposed to impact the game? I'm getting that your scores act as a countdown toward The End, but it seems like Harm also accelerates the effect. True?

Item the second:

Is the point of a session of Dust Devils to drive your character toward The End?

After our session, I was strongly reminded of the play pace in InSpectres: you can't finish the mission 'til you've earned enough job dice, right? It seems like you can't really finish a session of Dust Devils 'til you've reached The End.

My impression of the overall flow of play is this:

1) Get into a conflict

2) Go against your traits and Devil to earn a couple chips, courting Harm. If you start with 0 chips, there's nothing to Stake. If you start with >0 chips, then you need all the cards you can get to double your bet.

(Rules question: At what point in a conflict can you bet Stakes? Can you wait 'til after the Draw?)

3) As you enter more conflicts, eventually start gaining Harm

4) Get into new conflicts, but with even fewer cards (thus beginning the death spiral)

5) Keep earning chips, even in the face of decreased character effectiveness. Also, as effectiveness decreases, betting Stakes becomes untenable.

6) Enter The End

7a) Hopefully armed with a fuckload of chips, narrate your final scene on your own terms, or

7b) Left with nothing but your Devil and probably few-or-no chips, narrate a spectacular failure.

I think if we knew the "point" of a session was to harvest chips for The End, things would have played out a bit differently.

That said, if the point of a session really is to aim for The End, why the discussion in the book about playing for multiple sessions? The thing about giving out 3-4 chips if you're going to play a single-session game is almost a throwaway comment, where it seems like that should almost be mandated.

Side question: What does a session of DD look like when nobody has reached The End?

And that's about it for now. I'm sure more discussion will come up as I start reading answers.

    \n To me, winning in the Dust Devils is creating a meaningful, resolved story. The better, more powerful and gut-wrenching the story, the better. It is not necessarily to beat the bad guy, get the girl, ride off into the sunset. Etc. That may be the story. It may not. For example, the racist jerk dying with a whimper not a bang? I don't see that as "losing" at all. The audience (that is, the players) get something out of it, as I see it.

    It's a game about violence (both inflicting violence and receiving violence) and how you deal with that. It's not about heroism.

    \n Wow, well...DD is definitely the wrong game for my A crowd, based on tonight's session. The B crowd may be more receptive.

    Biggest gripe is that Harm is so, so fast. The negative impact on their authorship ramps up so fast that they were avoiding conflicts at all costs, which they feel is the wrong behavior to incentivize. Worst behavior of the night was, as I suspected/feared, basically elbowing their way into a conflict only to immediately fold and net their +1 chip. Since they can't take Harm, all I can do is change the context of the conflict a bit. I can ratchet it up narratively, but Harm is still Harm, and it's not like they're going to take any more harm based on the context of the conflict.

    So, for the bulk of my group, the flow of play was to 1) Identify that the punishment for losing conflicts is far greater than the reward for winning them, 2) Entering conflicts with no intention of actually completing them, 3) Netting +1 chip and immediately folding. They get a chip and they don't suffer Harm. Teh Win! \\o/

    My players found that playing toward or against their Devil was simply not interesting; every decision came down to an analysis of resources, with absolutely no emotional investment in the flow of events or narrative outcomes. This should have been the loudest alarm: If they don't click with the fundamental question of the game, they're not going to get anything out of it.

    Ah, well. They can't all be winners. :-/

    \n Has anyone run the math on the distribution of Harm versus average hand sizes? That'd be verrrry interesting.

    Totally anecdotal here, but in three sessions of DD, I found the average total card draw is 11 cards (7 from scores, a trait, Devil 2, and a relevant past/present) and the average play is a Straight or Full House, i.e. Harm 4. I think we had a single Harm 3 conflict, otherwise it was always 4 Harm. That's a lot of risk for a single conflict! As GM, I basically went 50/50 on whether my NPCs were giving in to their Devils or working against them. Pacing felt correct, and as many NPCs as PCs entered The End.

    Granted, there's probably a disconnect on my players' part as to what the stats represent. But the net result is that an average single loss effectively cuts down their character's authorship power by a third, and accelerates future losses because of the reduced card draw. After a single loss, assuming you're drawing against damaged Scores, the average draw goes down to 8ish. Also, the winners get richer, faster: not only are they not losing cards, by drawing against the PCs' smaller hand sizes they can afford to earn more chips (and bet more!) in future conflicts. If the PCs spend their chips to heal damaged scores, which one of them did last night, it is really hard to dig themselves out of the hole (and the bad guys with lots of chips aren't having to spend them recovering!) -- there's nothing to Stake.

    I'm not sure how I'd change this. Dialing down Harm across the board would just slow the game down, probably, although it'd make Recovery scenes more viable since the odds of zeroing out a Score would be reduced. The "high action" rules from Deathwish seem promising, making the "how many chips can I earn without zeroing out?" dynamic more a game of chicken.

    Anyway, just some mathy thinking about this stuff. I don't know that it addresses the fundamental thematic disconnect with my players, but it would give them a bit more breathing room.
