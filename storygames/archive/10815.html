\n Games flaws often hide in warm fuzzy places when you play with friends.
\n
\nBut delightfully rear their ugliness when playing with strangers or when you play with friends hyper competitively.
\n
\nSmackdown vs. Raw 2010 is the best wrestling video game I've ever played (better than No Mercy or Fire Pro Wrestling). It's a great party game. 4 of us played for hours last week. Fun! Then I played online. Fuck!
\n
\nThis game is designed to simulate a wrestling match. But when someone prioritizes winning over simulation, the game breaks down fast.
\n
\nThe problem is simple.
\n
\nYou have different ways to attack. Attacks that do a lot of damage are easy to reverse. Attacks that do a little damage are hard to reverse.
\n
\nSo most people only use the attacks that are hard to reverse. They may only do 1/4 the damage of a stronger attack. But that's a small trade off for a guaranteed successful attack. Additionally, because of online lag, low damage attacks become even harder to reverse and high damage attacks become easier to reverse.
\n
\nSo online wrestling matches end up looking like this:
\n
\nPunch or kick someone till they fall down. Kick them a lot while they are down till they roll out of the way. While they are getting up, run at them and punch or kick them back down. Repeat.
\n
\nNo wrestling moves. No submissions. No holds. No suplexes. No powerbombs. No slams.
\n
\nYou aren't doing a lot of damage but in time you will win. It's boring. For me at least.
\n
\nThese flaws exist when you play with friends. But they are less likely to use them. And if they do, you can tell them to stop. Because of social pressure and earned social capitol, usually they will. Not so much online.
\n
\nI suspect some of this applies to RPGs.

    \n This is interesting because it demonstrates directly how social dynamic "counts" in game playing. The way I see it in both cases the purpose of play is exactly the same. In both cases the activity is about winning. However, what's changed between the two examples is the *win conditions*. Online the only thing that matters is the in-game score board. Did I beat you by the rules of the game hand? Yes/No. In the social environment *style* becomes part of the win condition. Winning through boring applications of the game rules becomes no win at all.

    Online: Winning = Game Points.
    Party: Winning = Game Points + Social Points

    And yes, I think this is applicable to RPGs.

    \n To build on what you are saying Jesse:
    \n
    \nIn party play, we often have an audience. People hanging out or waiting for their turn. This is also true in RPGs. And adds more pressure to the value of Winning = Game Points + Social Points.
    \n
    \nHell, this was true playing Street Fighter 2 in the arcades. You could go for an easy grab attack but everyone would start calling you cheap. Later on they fixed the grab attack (you can trigger a tech hit for less damage or land on your feet). In some ways, the social pressure also acts as a rules patch till official rules patches can be implemented.

    \nBig time. A lot of older RPGs gave that social capitol solely to the GM who was expected to drive the game forward, keep the peace, and also had the power and expectation to change or ignore rules (and sometimes add them) as needed, probably largely in response to their original nature of being pretty fast and loose among friends to playing with new people and strangers. A lot of what I see in modern design addresses this as well, but solves the strangers issue with better game design and advice on group consensus, instead of loading it onto a single person.
