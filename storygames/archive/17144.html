\n My fiancee and I are writing a game set in a tavern. It's really good! You create the dish of the day, the customers and the employees. (The employees are based on the ingredients in the dish: so, for example, the landlord of the tavern might be based on a Honey Roast Pig.) Then you play the employees through the life of the tavern. Some leave, some stay, but gradually, their lives change.

It's a lovely little game. It deliberately subverts various storytelling tropes: for example, you're encouraged not to drive towards conflict or introduce mysteries. Instead, you play through the relationships and see where they go. There is no character ownership, either: the characters are played by various players.

It's a GMless game. You write on playing cards with Sharpies.

But it's really hard to pitch. How can we pitch it so it sounds interesting? What are the bits we should emphasise? Any help much appreciated.

    \n What's the scope of the game's story? Is it one day in the life of the tavern (and it's occupants), or does play stretch over years (or some other interval) with lifer staff and long-time regulars? Is there any mechanic to help front-load motivation for the characters (and thus a direction for the players) such as keys, aspects, or needs?

    In terms of how to pitch it, an obvious strategy is to point at some other media that represents what you're after. Movies, books, other games, something that people will be more familiar with that can serve as a shortcut for explaining your game. Jim already called out Cheers, which is probably the closest thing Americans have to a common idea of a tavern. How about "it's like Shaun of the Dead, but they never leave the Winchester and there are no zombies." Are there other better references you can use?

    How about telling potential players what they can expect to get out of it- at the end of the session, what will we have accomplished? When we're playing, how do we know when we're doing it right, and when the game is done?

    Based on what little you've said so far, I can easily imagine that one answer might be "you're playing to find out why all these people are here, and why they keep coming back. What's that guys story, and why is he always sitting in the front booth, alone at a table for two?" But then, you said you're avoiding mysteries, so I'm not sure.

    \n This is what I threw together to pitch Graham's game for Concrete Cow:

    A GMless game about a tavern and the people who work, drink and eat there: each round of play concerns itself with a different part of the tavern's history, which may be days, months or years apart.

    I thought about fitting the whole 'ingredients inspiring characters' thing in there, but I think that could detract from what the game is actually about: it's a really cool idea, but it only comes up breifly at the start of every round and I wouldn't want to give people the impression that the ingredients were like traits or something that gave characters mechanical weight. Probably the only other part of the game I'd include in the pitch is the shared ownership of characters and the absence of driven conflicts, but I'm not sure how to phrase those in a cool and pithy way.

    \n Thanks guys. To answer Steve's questions:\n
    What's the scope of the game's story? Is it one day in the life of the tavern (and it's occupants), or does play stretch over years (or some other interval) with lifer staff and long-time regulars?
    \nIt plays through several evenings over the life of the tavern. That's likely to cover several months, although it could be days or years instead.\n
    Is there any mechanic to help front-load motivation for the characters (and thus a direction for the players) such as keys, aspects, or needs?
    \nNo, there's not.

    Oh, there are relationships, though. There are lots of relationships. The game is about playing through those.\n
    In terms of how to pitch it, an obvious strategy is to point at some other media that represents what you're after.
    \nI'd really prefer not to do that. Many indie games seem to be pastiches of other genres: it would be nice not to go down that road.\n
    How about telling potential players what they can expect to get out of it- at the end of the session, what will we have accomplished? When we're playing, how do we know when we're doing it right, and when the game is done?
    \nThe short answer is: play through the relationships and see where they lead you. The analogy is kneading dough for bread. Work every part of it, but don't overwork it, and you're done when it feels right.

    One of the game's central ideas is that it tries to break conventional storytelling techniques. So you're encouraged to end both scenes and the game without everything being resolved. You should leave things hanging.

    Thanks, Steve, those are helpful questions.

    \n The game isn't really about conflict. But sometimes players ask: what happens if Character A wants to do something and Character B wants to stop them?

    The answer is: you're at work. So you're expected to back down and keep things sweet. You mustn't make a fuss in front of the customers (or, indeed, anywhere).

    This means that, if you want to win a conflict, you can insist that you do so: but your character leaves the Tavern at the end of the net scene.

    That's the rule when the conflict is between two employees. If a customer is involved, then the customer always wins, because the customer is always right.
