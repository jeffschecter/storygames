    Well, the pro argument for fiddling has always been, "I just like fiddling." The con argument says that we all got that way because early bad designs sent us all off looking for better. That if D&D actually satisfied all agendas that we'd not be having this discussion today.

Perhaps a better analogy, in modeling this is called "Kit Bashing" where you take the model out of the box, and immediately start using it for something other than it's intended purpose. Or you make it once, and then disassemble it to use the parts with other models.

    I suppose that, at some point, it becomes too constraining with something creative like this to always work with what you get out of the box. After a while you become good enough at modifying things that, I suppose, kit-bashing becomes more interesting than just assembling he model at hand. I have to admit that few RPGs I've ever encountered have endured first contact with me running them intact.

    On the other hand, with a game like MLWM or DitV, I don't modify. So that would argue that I'm only altering rules when I see a problem. That's probably accurate. Certainly I prefer the rules to be as stable as possible when playing. After all, if I want something distinctly different, I can design my own game, or play something else closer to my needs.

    Here's the thing...what's the other option in terms of design goals? Design a game that isn't clear as to how it runs, so it must be kit-bashed? Why? Can't a player do that anyhow, even if it works well as clearly designed? Would a comic be sent with just a bag full of panels that you have to sort? Would a model be sent sans instructions on how to build it? Even if you knew that people were kit-bashing it? To force people to kit-bash it?

    I'm completely ambivalent on the subject of whether or not games should be kit-bashed, modified, whatever. People should do with them what they want. But a designer does have an imperative to design a game that works well as written out of the box. With as little work neccessary on the part of those playing to figure out how. Not because kit-bashing is bad, but because some people want to just make the model like it looks on the cover of the box.

  • wyrmwood
    December 2006
    I think the enforced dichotemy between relentless kit-bashing and exactly-by-the-rules play is causing more harm than good.

    It washes out the fact that all RPGs are evolving as we play them. Sometimes the players intend changes (choosing options and kit-bashing), sometimes the evolution is more subtle (misapplying a rule, and then remembering the misapplication when it comes up again). Both sides seem to want to still these dynamics - whether by changing to a different but "stable" version or by playing exactly as it is.

    Now the tragedy of this is that dynamics can do far more than a static configuration can. We're seeing the tip of the iceberg with some of the pacing and evolving mechanics being developed now. But there's much more design space.

    Why do we as designers tend to cede the crafting of characters so easily, but not the crafting of system?

    Players change the game they are playing. Its not a matter of whether we should, but the fact that we do. When we design we should try to take that into account and even encourage the kind of changes in the game that meets our goals.

    But to do this we must first understand how games change while we play them. And that means giving up the illusion that they do not.

    - Mendel S.

    I suspect that disagreement is over the importance of the game designer compared to the play group.

    For example, I've never played unmodified RuneQuest or CORPS, but I've used them in kitbashed games. I don't think this is disrespect to the game designer. I wanted to run a detailed sci-fi game based on Star Trek, and at first used CORPS as a basis. Similarly, I had an idea for my Vinland game of Icelanders in the New World, and started with RuneQuest for my rules. In both cases, I had something which I wanted to do, and to get it, I took the closest game to get what I wanted -- and modified it from there. So I picked up the game at that time not to play it as written, but to use it for something which I already had an idea for.

    This is basically the same approach that most game designers work from, taking elements from an existing game (like The Prince's Kingdom or True20, say). Indeed, I think the only clear difference is whether you package the result of your kitbashing for official publication in the end.
