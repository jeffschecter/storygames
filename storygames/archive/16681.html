\n So, over on Black Armada I'm doing a little project where I try and analyse how really evocative combat scenes in books work. The idea is that a lot of games and a lot of GMs think in movie terms, but since rp is basically word-based, it has more in common with literature than film.

Anyhoo, I'm posting here to ask if anyone has any recs for decent combat scene authors. So far I've looked at Joe Abercrombie, who I believe to be a top-notch author and especially good at writing combat scenes. I'll probably look at Roger Zelazny and George RR Martin as well, as I recall them having good combat scenes. Any more for any more? I'm conscious this list is very short (and lacks any female authors).

Let me know if you've got any recommendations!

    \n Abercrombie FOR SURE. Martin doesn't really do action sequences, they just sort of happen (which suits his story), but the Battle of the Blackwater at the end of A Clash of Kings is riveting.

    \n In Brandon Sanderson's Mistborn series, his action sequence descriptions are excellent. Particularly interesting are that they are describing a weird fighting style due to the funky magic in that world. Combat is much more 3 dimensional than usual, because many of the combatants can use magic to move vertically. Because it's so unusual, though, it may or may not compare well with more typical combat writing.

    About halfway through The Wise Man's Fear, but Patrick Rothfuss, (or maybe it was closer to the end, it's been a while) the hero goes through intense martial arts training. I remember the descriptions being particularly effective. Plus, the series as a whole is just plain the single most beautiful bunch of fantasy novel writing I've read to date.

    I'm conscious this list is very short (and lacks any female authors).
    \nAu contraire! I can think of...well, a few.

    C.J. Cherryh, one of my favorite authors. Combat is confusing, scary, delightfully plot-driven.

    Elizabeth Moon. Workman-like space opera, feels like a Traveller game about one PC.

    Other good SF ones, but not female:

    David Drake, brutal and straightforward. His 'Hammer' stories/novels are the best example of this.

    Joel Rosenberg, following in the footsteps of Drake. 'Hero' and 'Not For Glory'.
