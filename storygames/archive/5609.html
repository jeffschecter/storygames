Hey, folks, I started to write this thing over here the other day, but it was more of an article than a forum post, so I put it over on my blog.

\n

The summary is, I think that people should make things in the way that makes them happiest. Publishing for money might not be that, because you will probably have to make compromises that will make you unhappy.

\n

Check out the article if you're thinking about designing, publishing, and/or selling games. I'm happy to take questions, but I'll probably only have questions in response. I'm not really an answer-having kinda guy.

    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanDon\u2019t do anything you\u2019re going to resent.
    \nAmen. I mean, if my game's sales continue at this rate, I'm looking at breaking even by the time it's been out for a year. Which is great. After that, it's all profit. Cash money, wodie. Yes, I will have fries with that, thank you.

    But if I'd done things differently, if I'd made a game that I wasn't 100% excited about, because I was hoping for that extra $100 in sales, or $1000, I would be very unhappy right now. It's all about making the game that you want to make, that you want to play -- and if you feel that you have to spend money to get it made, keep your costs low enough that you can at least break even (or come close).

    If you're one of those people who creates games that they believe in, and you're selling thousands and thousands of copies, then you're a badass and I salute you. But I believe that it would be a mistake for me to try to design a game in the hope of matching your sales. That way lies madness.

    -- Rafael

    Ryan just reminded me: I started thinking about this a couple of months ago. I was starting to see abstract "sales" and "con slots filled", and I was grouchy about shipping books. Then I remembered that I never got to play Shock: because it was always overbooked. I wasn't saying no to people showing up to play, and it was making me really crabby.

    \n

    So I decided, OK, I'm gonna only run games with three other people. That means that I get full participation (I usually play Audience, which means I get stuff to do, but I don't get a Protagonist) and I get to have a lot more fun with the world and I get to make moral statements in the way the game's designed to support.

    \n

    All of a sudden, more fun! And more fun has led to more sales! I can honestly and enthusiastically recommend the game, and I know this is weird to say, but I understand the game better. Like, I had all these parts that theoretically did stuff, but I was so wrapped up in the cogs that I couldn't play with the whole machine.

    \n

    So, if publishing or selling is getting in the way of your play, drop them down a notch and play your damn game. Then do the next part that will add to your satisfaction.

    \n I think we're coming to a time where everyone who plays an RPG is also a game designer. Or rather, everyone who was already a game designer is now standing up to be counted as one. And it's awesome! I love the explosion of different ideas, the way everyone has their own project. I love the hacks, the house rules, the home-brews, and everything. I think that, I hope that, the distinction between "player" and "designer" is disappearing just as the distinction between "GM" and "player" is.

    I think you're right on the money, Joshua, in suggesting that not every design needs to follow the "publish on IPR, promote at Cons" model. I think we're hung up on that in the same way we were hung up on the three-tier model. Certainly that's an effective way to publish a game, but it's not always best for every game. I'm constantly designing games, hacking games, and so on, like everyone else. I play these games with my friends, and we have fun. That's my goal. A few of the things I've done I've published as free PDFs, and it makes me feel good when people say nice things about them. But if I hadn't made them first and foremost as a way to play fun games with my friends, I'd be pretty unsatisfied.

    I wrote about this same topic last year on Knife Fight, and I've been thinking about it a lot since then. I think there will always be a range of different publishing models, from three-tier distribution through self-publishing, and down to trades between friends. I guess what I'd like to see is that last model be seen as equally valid, and not just a consolation pize for people who aren't cool enough to play with the big boys and girls. I'm really happy that this is starting to happen. For this reason, The RPG bakery is my new favorite site.

    \n There are many aspects of these threads that are driving me batty. Principle among these is the use of the word "Publish"
    \n
    \nPublish simply means to make a fixed copy of your work available to others. Period. Everything written on this site is "published". Anything written in a blog is "published". Anything formatted as a PDF and posted on your website is "published". Anything you handwrite on a napkin make a dozen copies of at Kinkos and pass out to your closest friends is "published".
    \n
    \nAnyone who does any of the above is a "publisher". Period. Paragraph. End of discussion.
    \n
    \nVarious attempts by certain individuals to ascribe any additional requirements, restrictions, or behaviors around the term "publisher" is simply evidence of vested interests and personal agenda.
    \n
    \n
    \nFrom that definition there is a whole host of different WAYS you can publish ranging from copying napkins at kinkos to 10,000 unit print runs sold through distribution.
    \n
    \nWhat these threads are really talking about is simply selecting the method of publication that's right for you. Framed that way...Hells yes.
    \n
    \nAny and all attempts to define some methods as "publishing" or "real publishing" and other methods as "not publishing" can go directly to hell.
    \n
    \nSo please, for the sake of clarity (and my sanity) as we hold these discussions...lets talk about the pros and cons of different methods of publishing, whether to treat your publishing efforts as a business or not, and so on.
    \n
    \nLets NOT fall into the trap of equating "Publishing" with "Business" and lets definitely not let others attempt to equate "Not Business" with "Not Publishing" for that is the bullshit.

    \n Right before Gencon last year I had a.... moment of clarity? I decided that I just didn't want to do what I was doing . In the year since releasing Panty Explosion I had been to a half dozen cons, dealt with tons of printers, distributors, stores and individuals (often in languages I didn't understand), stuffed a few hundred books into envelops, wrote, tested and published a companion game, sat on panels, argued with tons of people and was called all kinds of names. I helped grow Portland's indie game community with Travis, Matthew, Colin and a bunch of other great people, I ran or played in two or three games a week. I posted, argued and participated on forums, chat rooms and blogs constantly, and I promoted my game in any place and way that I could.

    And looking back, it was a year well spent. But I was exhausted. I was physically and emotionally drained. I realized that despite the fun I was having, all that stuff was keeping me from doing what was important to me. It was keeping me from being actively creative.

    So I decided to just stop. I didn't go to Gencon. I stayed away from some other regional shows as well. I stopped promoting my games, and I stopped posting to (and reading) this and most other gaming sites. I let Classroom Deathmatch go out of print, and I only ordered a minimal reprint for PE. I stopped running games, and I stopped playing games (for any other reason then personal enjoyment)I let myself relax, and I let myself focus on getting some good work done.

    It's been really nice. I've put some really solid work in on my web comic (and to a lesser extent, the art for Bliss Stage), and I've been enjoying playing and working on my new game design. I feel happy and good. I don't feel like I'm missing anything. I don't regret the year of hard work I spent promoting PE, but I don't think I'll do it again.

    \n Going back to the original subject, and trying to decide why I have chosen (for example) to publish Solipsist by printing it in hardcopy and distributing it to IPR, rather than punting out a PDF, I have come to the conclusion that it has a lot to do with closure, and with making sure that the game is *finished*.

    I find it hard to finish things, and I know lots of designers are the same. Left to my own devices I will keep tinkering with a system forever, adding something there, taking something away here, and probably eventually rewriting the whole thing in some other system that isn't as good as what I started with. I've seen plenty of other people do the same, and never finish their games. For me publishing my game, in a final, paper, printed, distributed form, makes me actually finish it, polish it, and stop messing with it

    Now evidence of PDF sales and blog-released games shows that other people don't always suffer from the same syndrome. They can do something, show it to people, and leave it alone after that But I don't think I'm alone in finding the formal 'publishing process' something very important in actually making me fix and finish what I write.

    \n Quickly, and not to dive back into semantic bullshit too much...

    For me, the word "Publish" is just fine for "sell what you write".

    The kind of publishing that is not selling is, to me, simply, "Sharing".

    Which I think gets to the spirit of it correctly.

    To me, the word publish sounds like a commercial activity. A publication, the product of publishing, is something I spend money on: that's common use. Hell, I think the innate commerciality of the term is part of what's exerted a subtle psychological pressure on folks to go all the way to selling what they create.

    But when I was five, I didn't know shit about any of that. But I knew, thanks to Mom and Dad, that it was good -- hell, that it was important -- to share things. And that's what's really at the root of that thing which has been conflated with the idea of publication, the thing that people wanted to do but let all of the commercial trappings of publishing get in the way of: the joy of making something and then giving it to other people and seeing the fun it gives them.

    Which is just another expression of the businessman/philosopher-artist divide that I think is operant here. "Businessman" is to publishing as "philosopher" or "artist" is to sharing. For one, the point of the act is to successfully engage in commerce with the fruits of a creative effort; for the other, the fruits of the creative effort is where it starts and ends.

    There's a lot of happiness that can rot away inside that fruit once you add the commerce to it, if you're not careful and clear about why you're adding it.

    So, remember what it's like to be five. Remember that sharing is important and good. Remember that five was a pretty happy thing to be.

    Posted By: Filip LuszczykAbout the size of "P" in "publishing", I suppose.
    \nWell, whip 'em out and lay 'em on the table, people!

    Given that I do all the art, text, layout, and (in the case of supplements) the actual printing, cutting, and binding of my books, I feel confident asserting that my P is bigger than yours.

    ... which is precisely the publishing model that makes me happy.

    Habit, and in many cases well-meaning advice to the terrified, can quickly create a "more equal" way of doing things in any community. I'm not surprised that this community slips into it. I'm happy that we generally follow that by taking a good look at things as they are, rather than as we've grown to perceive them, and readjusting our thinking. That's why we're always telling each other that we're wrong ... because we are. We try to figure out "The one true path", not because we intend to reach and follow it and stop thinking, but for sheer love of the unending pursuit.
