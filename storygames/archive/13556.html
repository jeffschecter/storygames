\n You know how you play some games and have some experiences which inspire you and make you go, "I want more of that!"? And then maybe you refine your GMing, or your character, or try to make a game? And all the while, you've got this vision of what play should be like? Maybe it's something you've done before, but not reliably. Maybe it's something you've almost done, but never quite. Maybe it's cobbled together from your favorite bits of other play. Whatever its origins, it's palpable. Some combo of social vibe, game choices, system interaction, style, genre... you know it'll be awesome.

So, now, how do you go about taking that vision and making it happen?

I can't answer that question. Ry St. and I were discussing it, and he suggested I start with an example of a highlight from one of my games. So, here's a typical payoff moment from Delve:

With the clock ticking down, the characters face a final danger and a puzzle. The players, fully immersed, deploy their characters' resources to survive the danger long enough to use their own wits to innovate a particularly elegant solution to the puzzle.

Actual play example:
The characters decide to alter the potion and dip the rune-stones in it to create a giant "protection from curses" rune in the wall of the haunted castle. As Jan dangles from a melting portcullis to insert rune-stones, Alaric and Roderick distract the undead king who has the power to stop the rune. Alaric feints a sword stroke so the king will waste his command powers on stopping it, while hiding a holy water flask under his shield arm. The king is spritzed and dissolves; Roderick braces the portcullis as the castle threatens to crumble; Jan adds the final brick.

Everyone holds their breath and looks to the GM.

The GM describes the curse effects vanishing. The dark mists part, the cursed doors fall, and the reanimated dead crumble to dust.

High fives all around.

That wasn't the majority of my vision of "what play should be like", but it was a key part of that vision. So, Ry, what next?

    \n Quick note before I respond:
    The cut-off for "authentic" is very low. By that I mean that only total GM hand-feeding is a deal-breaker. As long as the player has some decision that didn't feel completely obvious, that's sufficient. For the players, I think it's more about having a way to meaningfully participate than about needing to overcome a genuine challenge. Not infrequently, there will be an obvious solution that is sufficient, but the players will gt a kick out of improving on it: "Not only did we save the town, but we made ourselves look fucking badass to the nobility in the process!"

    what other moments had to happen before this moment could arrive?
    \nFrom my scenario-generation procedure:

    Hook. There needs to be a moment where the opportunity grabs the players' attention. This is usually an advertised problem with at least some hint of reward for fixing it.

    This moment often comes while leisurely pondering options for what to do next. These can range from fairly low engagement/immersion if starting from indifference to super high engagement/immersion if furthering a plan the players have previously concocted.

    The haunted castle was listed on the PCs' very first "help wanted" sign as "too tough for you".

    Promise. Early in the process of checking out the opportunity, some NPC will clarify what stands to be gained here.

    When this merely confirms the players' initial guess, no biggie. When it's something unexpected and cool, the excitement can skyrocket.

    Imperial officers revealed the haunted castle to be at a point of great strategic importance for them. Their appreciation and payment for this job would be orders of magnitude beyond any village-saving.

    Lead. A second potential reward, often less well-defined. Usually the potential to find something out that isn't directly related to a scenario's core. This can hook more specific player goals than the Promise's more general offers of fame, status and wealth.

    Players often pick a Hook based on this, eager to follow up on some project they've already invested in.

    They decided to go after the haunted castle now because one of their enemies had just fled to it.

    Secret. New knowledge about the supernatural. A piece of the puzzle.

    These are always highly engaging when first encountered. Players get psyched about their potential. Then: (A) When they quickly figure out how to derive some actual use from adding this piece to their other puzzle pieces, it's a celebration. (B) When they quickly figure out how this probably gets them closer to doing something cool, there's just as much enthusiasm, with much speculating. (C) When the new puzzle piece is inscrutable and the players just stash it for later, play continues at its current level of engagement. (D) When the inscrutable piece gets poked at for too long, engagement wanes.

    When (A) happens and the new ability actually helps with the current problem, that's The Ultimate. (Why don't I try this every scenario? Too fast a rate of gaining useful magic powers utterly ruins the game's tone.)

    In the haunted castle, the players discovered a way to make rune-patterns activate: construct them out of bricks enchanted by a special potion!

    Clues and Tasks. This is the process of pursuit, discovery, complication, theory, experiment, and victory (usually). It's also the narrative in which the fiction comes alive, with vivid places, colorful actions, moral dilemmas, horrific monsters, occult plots, tense negotiations, and combat.

    Engagement is highest when the players know how to make progress toward some goal(s) and are planning or pursuing that.

    Engagement is also high when players jump at a situation that facilitates striking portrayals of characters -- moral stances, great speeches, showing off, pet causes, and well-timed visual descriptions.

    Jan looks out for the poor and hates abusive nobles. Roderick promotes free knowledge of magic and hates fearful superstition. Alaric likes scaring people first and reassuring them second.

    Relative to the thoroughness of your description of GM hooks/promises/leads and player responses, the link between "PCs choose some particular small-scale problem to resolve" and "PCs undertake to resolve a larger-scale problem in a situation that involves risk" seems a little incomplete.

    Thinking about an example from last night, the PCs were already determined to quell the menace of the Tentacled Thing Beneath the Ziggurat. They first dealt with the small problem of preserving their cultist allies' secrecy, and in the process made friends with a scout who can help chart the reach of the tentacles. So the little problem effected how they pursued the big problem, but not whether they pursued it. This is a fairly common configuration, but there are others. Looking at it more broadly, I think there are basically two requirements for the PCs undertaking a big problem:
    1) it gets on their to-do list
    2) of all the things on their to-do list, it achieves the position of either Most Fun or Most Urgent (or, rarely, Most Doable, where everything else on the to-do list is too risky at the moment)

    \n Specific is good. But your list of prerequisites is too abstract - it's concept prerequisites instead of events that have to happen.

    What I'm after is what "moments of play" are prerequisite for your payoff moments. And moments of play that are prerequisites for moments of play. That's where the diagrams come from.

    So to figure something out, you need to have a problem, and some info about it, and then get MORE information about it. So you need a moment where you learn about the problem. We infer cycle 1.

    To figure something out under pressure, you need the above, plus pressure. So you need a form of pressure. So we infer cycle 2.

    For that to be fun the players have to choose it. So we infer the Choice cycle.

    To make a choice, the players have to have some context, so we infer the Town cycle.

    To provide all this stuff, the GM has to prepare, so we infer the Prep cycle.
