    OK, get this ... my wife's a veterinarian, right? And for reasons way too bizarre to go into, often clients need to feed their pets food they've never had before. Which often means venison. So this other vet, she and her husband give up the veterinarianing (what a great word), and start a venison ranch.

    And she gives tons of this stuff to my wife, not the stuff for the clients and their pets, but the stuff for the people. Like me.

    So, it's yummy, corn-fed deer meat - not gamy and dry. You get the tenderloins and clean'em off with your bare hands and a knife, then rinse deer blood off your hands so you feel like you're really manly. Oil it up really well, but don't put any spices in, 'cause something good's coming next.

    Then you wrap it with prosciutto or real meaty bacon from the real butcher down the street, and you grill it in the back yard. The venison is really lean but the wrapping juices it up and it grills so fast that you won't dry it out.

    Mmmmm.

    What, that's not "steak" because it's a deer? OK. Well, in that case, go to that real butcher down the street I was talking about. I'm serious, the "young" guy there is in his sixties. They all wear suit pants and black shoes to work, and bow ties with their shirts, then wear these spattered aprons over that. This place has been there for centuries and gets the meat other places never even dream of.

    Get big, scary, no-foolin' beef filets from them, and rinse'em off. Then mash up peppercorns in oil, with some salt and maybe a little herbal stuff. Soak the steaks in there for a while, basically until the guests start looking woeful and you feel like a little too much beer is going down into a hungry stomach. Grill'em!

    Then grab the bottles of some kick-ass red wine that's not too woody, say a Cab-Shiraz Australian blend, or a real peppery Shiraz from coastal California, which you opened about an hour ago.

    Bliss. Eating this meat and knocking it back with this wine is like getting the dessert at the same time as the entree.
