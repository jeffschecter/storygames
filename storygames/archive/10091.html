\n
I experienced what might be my favorite D&D; moment of all time a few weeks ago. Chris Perkins' Wednesday night game is a world of small islands and large oceans, and our low-paragon group has just acquired our first ship. We routinely have sessions in which we don't roll a single die, and one such session a few weeks back involved us attempting to hire a crew. Chris informed us, through an NPC ally, that the secret to a happy crew was a good ship's cook. When all was said and done, we managed to successfully hire the best cook in the city through a series of roleplaying encounters, wrangling him away from the service of another captain. As negotiations wrapped up, I happily leaned back in my chair and announced my satisfaction at finally hiring my own personal chef -- even if he was only around to make me midnight snacks in game. What a night!
\n
\n
\n
Fourth edition doesn't include some of the mundane mechanical elements of character building that 3rd Edition did. For example, certain skills (I'm looking at you Craft and Profession) enabled a player to feel like his character had some sort of grounding in the "real world" of the campaign. Odds were good that you never made a Craft or Profession check in your game, but having ranks in that skill made you feel connected to your character's background. In 4th Edition, those skills are gone. Why? Because we feel like a character's statistics don't represent the absolute truth of a character's story. That's right -- one of the reasons those skills (and other such elements from other editions) are gone is that we felt they hindered roleplaying.
\n
\nNow if you want to say your character was a blacksmith's apprentice and knows how to make his own sword, just say so. Don't worry about feeling forced to reflect that story decision mechanically. Just write it on your character sheet. Liberating, isn't it?
\n
\n
\n
...in every RPG I've played over the past twenty years and more, roleplaying was ultimately a result of the narrative brought to the table by the DM and players, not something provided by rules. That's the beauty of roleplaying, after all! D&D; has always been a game of heroic fantasy, and there's nothing about those two words that requires rules of any sort.

    \n Here's the thing. I get what people are saying about 4E nerfing their skills and spoiling their fun. 4E is a completionist design and by leaving out a bunch of stuff about riding horses, making armor, and cooking, the rules are basically saying that PCs can't do that. Maybe you don't interpret it that way, but when a set of rules that has hundreds of pages of detailed powers leaves something out, it's doing so on purpose, and it's okay to believe the game is saying, "don't do anything not here."

    When I run 4E, it isn't like that, of course. I recognize that I am drifting the rules though. There's no reason that players shouldn't be able to write down that their characters were blacksmiths and have them be able to make swords and stuff. The player might be cheated out of a skill check though, and that can ruin the fun.

    Still, I think it's wrong to say that 4E gets in the way of role-playing. Not having certain skills on the list doesn't stop you from role-playing around all the stuff the game does spotlight. Role-play about your cool combat powers. Role-play about your race and class and alignment. Role-play about the skills you have and the feats. Role-play about the situation the DM put you in, with all of its points-of-lights goodness. The rules do help you with that, with all the game structures I mentioned. There are sections of the rules that are not mechanical. Treat them as guides for role-playing.

    There aren't procedural rules that help you role-play though other than "describe what you're doing when you use a character ability." Look at other games and you'll see procedures that get into the character deeper. I don't know that you'll find any of those rules in earlier versions of D&D though, so I don't entirely understand where these 3.5E fans are coming from, except for the skill-nerfing thing. Is removing a couple skills enough to really kill "role-playing" for a gamer? Geez, if so, house-rule them back in already. But as Jason and others have pointed out, now you're burning skill slots on blacksmithing stuff that is less likely to be useful during the game.

    \n Essentially I agree with Adam. People here know how to get to Imagination Land no matter which kind of RPG car we're driving to get there. D&D 4e is probably going to be a number of peoples' introduction to roleplaying games in general, and these new players are going to look to the rules for guidance on how roleplaying games work and how they should be played. What the skills, and some of the non-combat class abilities and feats did in 3e did, was give the players a structured way of saying "here is a another facet of your character". They helped add complexity to the characters beyond what they could do in combat and suggested that non-combat encounters were to be expected and dealt with with rules as well. My fighter has craft (blacksmithing) or profession (sailor). Why? I will grant that it's not much, but it's better than nothing. It's a rickety little bridge pointing players towards the Fruitful Void. I was hoping 4e was going to take these tiny concessions to roleplaying and expand upon them, but it took even these away for the most part.

    I think I mentioned this before, but when my group of older, D&D 3e players started playing D&D 4e, they assumed their characters were incapable of becoming blacksmiths because it wasn't in the rules. Even knowing them, I was pretty stunned at their naivety, but that's some proof right there.

    I called bullshit on the assertion that stripping away the crafting, profession, some of the knowledge and other misc. skills because I don't believe that they were something they were joyously sacrificing on the alter of the Fruitful Void. I think they were another thing hacked out indiscriminately in the name of streamlining. They're essentially claiming that it's not a bug, it's a feature. I could be wrong. Maybe there's something in the DMG that details how to handle all of this stuff.

    I need to quit with the 4e bashing, I know. I'm becoming a broken record.

    \n I think D&D in its many configurations carries with it a long tradition of separating games into Action and Roleplaying.

    Way back in the day, enormous chunks of the game were spent meticulously scouring dungeon complexes for loot and the poor misguided creatures stuck guarding it. The dungeon was the place for combat and problem-solving and trap-deactivating - in other words, engagement with the rules.

    In between dungeons, it was back to the city to rest and recharge. There was some obligatory rules engagement (healing, leveling, etc.)...but this "down time" between dungeons was when roleplaying happened. This was when you got to talk to the barkeep or the shadowy stranger, or face the stern temple priest, or go on shopping sprees, or whatever. After about half of a session of this it was back to the wilderness (with its nightly threats of wandering monsters, tracking rolls, ambushes on the trail, etc.) en route to the next dungeon.

    The tradition was established that the rules governed action, and the roleplaying took place entirely removed from the rules. You see this tradition rear its head in most discussions of social conflict on RPG.net ("keep your filthy hands off my roleplaying, you damned dirty rules"). I don't find it at all strange or incongruent that someone extolling 4E would invoke The Fruitful Void, because in D&D it has always been there in a very regimented fashion.

Does 4E suffer by giving you enough specificity in the fiction to work with if you want to do what it wants to do, but not enough sense that you can re-skin it if you don't like the fiction?
