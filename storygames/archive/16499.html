\n Here's one I've been searching for some clarification on, but no luck so far.

When you publish a print version and a PDF version, do you use different fonts?

My understanding is that you use serif fonts when publishing for print, such as Garamond, but when you put something online, for perusal on a computer screen, you go with sans serif fonts like Arial or Helvetica (or whatever).

So, when you've got a print book, and then you release the PDF, do you switch fonts?

How did that go over? Did anyone even notice?

    \n I use the same typeface in both print and PDF. I try to avoid faces with too much subtlety (and fail -- I love them) since that information can be lost. However, I also target a 6x9 page size, so the faces are fairly large on a laptop or tablet screen. If I were explicitly aiming at display on a smart phone I would choose differently.
    \n
    \nEdit: in that latter case I would use the same face in the print version. The idea of doing layout twice (and then maintaining two versions) makes me cringe.

    \n Yeah you'll find faces that have unusually thin strokes in them or unusual axes (the rationalist angle is one of the worst -- cf. Didot and Bodoni) can be hard to read at low (less than 300dpi) resolutions in normal text sizes. Faces with less variation between thick and thin will be superior (say, for example, Mendoza or April Samuels).

    \n Brad's right that it's not so much about faces but getting text to render well, instead of disappearing or becoming indecipherable smudges. The actual serifs on serif fonts are hard to see, as are their more complex curves, if you're on a lower-resolution screen. Same with thinner san-serif faces.

    An easy way to partially test legibility is to make the text really small (zoom out or reduce the font size) and see if you can still read it.
