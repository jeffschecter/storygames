\n Michael talked, recently, about a difficult experience with 4th Edition Dungeons and Dragons. A player walked out and later complained that there was little roleplaying and combat had been too hard, for which he blamed the GM.

My first experience was similar. Simon was GMing. We began with a combat.

Immediately, I had no idea what I was doing. I knew that, on my turn, I could roll a d20 to hit. But I didn't know what I needed to roll: so, when I rolled the die, someone would tell me whether I'd hit. There were lots of bonuses, which I didn't understand.

Most frustratingly, there wasn't a default "You hit something" option. It all had names like "Incandescent Slap". I generally had no idea whether I wanted to use an Incandescent Slap or a Poke Of Death. I just wanted to hit stuff.

When I did hit, I wasn't sure what the damage was. There were various formulae on my character sheet. There was also a symbol, [W], which wasn't explained.

Sometimes, I got hit. When this happened, someone told me to use a healing surge, so I did.

While I was being mystified, everyone else seemed to be having fun. They all had rulebooks and were flicking backwards and forwards, trying to get bonuses. There were things called flanking bonuses, which were good, and were apparently the reason we were moving things around a map. I was not sure how to get these. I thought you might attack while standing beside someone else, but no.

Every round, people would do interesting things. Then play came back to me. I rolled my die. Someone told me whether I hit or not. I rolled another die. Someone else told me how much damage I did.

This went on forever. Eventually, I gave up, and rested my head on a pair of batteries that were lying on the table. I was very, very irritated.

Since then, I've played 4th Edition again. I'm starting to see how it might be fun. But it really does assume lots of prior knowledge, which rather punishes new players. You couldn't just sit casually down for a 4E game. You need to read up, first.

This, I think, gives the GM much power. The GM has to know all this stuff before running the game. So, if you're fairly new, the GM will know many more tricks than you do.

So there you go. My experience was, I think, remarkably similar to that of Michael's player.

Could I ask you to post slowly and thoughtfully to this thread? My basic nightmare is that I check Story Games in two hours and there's twenty posts, most of them from Adam, talking about the minutiae of 4th Edition. But I'm interested to talk about it. Thanks!

Graham

    While not invalidating Graham's crappy experiences, I've had plenty of good experiences showing newbies how to play 4E. I've run it for people who:

    * have played D&D but not 4E
    * have played RPGs but not D&D
    * have played online RPGs but not tabletop RPGs



    The very basics every player needs to understand include:

    * rolling dice
    * initiative and order
    * at-will vs. encounter vs. daily powers
    * actions in a round: 1 standard, 1 movement, 1 minor
    * roll to hit, calculate damage (and [W] means your weapon's regular dice, so 2[W] means roll twice your weapon regular dice)
    * roles: your powers can help other people

    It doesn't take long to go over those things. H1 (Keep on the Shadowfell) has a nice, short rules summary, too.

    Posted By: GrahamAnd I'm wondering whether it automatically gives the GM an advantage.

    Graham
    \nIn my experience, it just gives the advantage to the most rules-savvy person at the table, GM or player. Here's an example: 4E uses a complicated system to manage who the monsters attack. By default, the monster should go after the softest, most dangerous target. If everyone is equally dangerous, then simply the softest target. Defender classes change the equation in specific ways. If the GM is savvy, they will go through the analysis and figure out if this new whammy the Defender put on the monster is a big enough deal to change the monster's mind. If they're not, they'll 'respect the mark' and attack whoever you want them to.

    Concrete example: the monster can attack the rogue or the paladin. The monster needs to roll a 6 or better to hit the rogue, and an 11 or better to hit the paladin. The paladin uses 'mark,' an ability that says 'attack me or take a penalty.' Now the monster needs to roll an 8 or better to hit the rogue. Baring anything else, the monster should still attack the rogue. If the GM lacks that level of rules mastery, though, they will (unwisely!) go after the paladin, 'respecting the mark' rather than doing the math. A rules-savvy player can then manipulate the battlefield in ways that would be impossible with a more rules-savvy GM.

    Now, is that fair? Well, in that anyone could have read and memorized those rules, yes. Is it reasonable? That's a harder question. It is certainly a heavy burden, and doubly so in a game that is nominally competitive. And yes, I'd say that lacking it is extremely crippling.

    ...Every time you attack an enemy, whether the attack hits
    or misses, you can choose to mark that target. The mark
    lasts until the end of your next turn. While a target
    is marked, it takes a \u20132 penalty to attack rolls for any
    attack that doesn\u2019t include you as a target. A creature
    can be subject to only one mark at a time. A new mark
    supersedes a mark that was already in place.

    So, let's put this together. A Vampire Lord is a level 11 elite lurker. Since it's a lurker, it specifically wants to avoid the defender (DMG pg. 62). It's also intelligent enough to focus attacks on the most dangerous foe (DMG pg. 41). So, the Vampire Lord is happily munching on Mr. Laser Cleric (a big threat to him) when the fighter runs up from behind, hits the bloodsucker, and marks him. What happens now?

    Well, the mark doesn't say you have to do anything at all. It provides incentives to do this or that. The Vampire has to choose between continuing to focus on the cleric while getting beat on extra-hard by the fighter, or ignoring the dangerous cleric and focusing on the tank - the hardiest opponent he faces. Which should he do? Well, you can ignore the mark arbitrarily, since Vampires are lurkers and lurkers avoid defenders. That's within your rights. You could target the defender arbitrarily. That's less reasonable - what if the dangerous cleric was nearly down for the count? Or you could do the math, in the context of the monster's role, tactics, and intelligence. And that big a differential says a lot about Graham's concerns regarding rules mastery. You may say that a player could just look this stuff up online, but that's a question of how you master the rules, not whether or not rules mastery is a big advantage.

But how do we go from there to mastery? After we get a new player like Graham laced up and onto the field, how do we then level that playing field (edit: and keep the game feeling fair)?

I find myself thinking of Pun-Pun, the kobold build in D&D 3.5 who, upon obtaining level 5, became the most powerful creature in the universe, literally having infinite stats and access to every single possible creature or player ability in the game. I'd rather just come up with a neat character, pick some powers that sound kinda neat and fit my concept, perhaps reflavour to taste, and have some fun killing monsters and taking their stuff.

    Oh, why not. A third rambling thought: even the GM doesn't need to know everything about all the "plug-ins". I know that when I've GM'd 4E, I never bothered learning the intricacies of the PC's abilities. My players would tell me what their powers did when they used them. I was just responsible for knowing what the monsters could do. This might be an effect of the balancing efforts that WotC has put into 4E, however. I never need to worry about making sure my Big Bad Evil Guy is immune to a particular insta-kill combo that the party wizard might have figured out. So, if I know more about how my particular set of monsters work, and you know more about how your PC works, which one of us is really at an advantage?

This is especially true if trust the design of 4E and it's balanced approach: I've often heard it said that not only is it harder to make over-powered characters in 4E, but it's also much harder to make underpowered characters. You can pick any set of powers and feats and still be a viable character; there are no "trap choices" that make you useless. Hrm. Again, this is all late-night rambling, but I guess what I'm getting at is this: the inherent balance of 4E, as well as the unified mechanics, makes mastery pretty much unimportant.

    \n The basics of D&D 4E are in the Quick Start Rules PDF, which is a free download. This is the ruleset that was included in the introductory module, H1 Keep on the Shadowfell. It's 27 pages, but only 15 pages are rules. The rest of the pages are pregenerated characters.

    Much of those rules are "rules by exception," too, meaning you can skim them and look them up when you need to. For example, skim the cover and concealment rules, all of the Conditions section, the Other Actions in Combat section, the temporary hit points rules, and the Skills section. Skim them, don't skip them. You can look this stuff up later when it applies to something you care about, but you have to know it exists. You should know what you can do, but you don't need to know what to roll to do it if you don't mind your DM or another player telling you. There are two pages of rules for monsters for the DM; a player doesn't really need to know this stuff.

    So about 13 pages you need to read or skim. You can skim probably 6-7 of those pages. Learn those other 6-7 pages, skim the other 6-7, look up your specific powers and skills and understand what they mean, and you're off to the races.

      \n I taught 4E to a group of High School students, most of which had played computer RPG's, but not touched a set of polyhedral in their lives. Of the 6, one had played a bit of 3e, one had played a bit of white box with his dad, and the rest had no experience.
      Started with Keep on the Shadowfell (which starts out with a fight right away).

      Here's what I started them with initially:\n
        \n
      • Core mechanic (1d20 + stuff on sheet + stuff from situation) vs. Target
        • \n
      • That if they tried something that wasn't on the card, to say what they wanted, and we'd figure it out (charging, bull rushes, etc don't need to be introduced right away)
        • \n
      • Initiative
        • \n
      • Actions (Move/Standard/Minor)
        • \n
      • Powers (At-Will/Encounter/Daily) + how to read a power card
        • \n
      • When a player's turn came up, at first, I explained the basics of the role. (marking, healing, combat advantage)
        • \n
      • Pointing out flanking opportunities (at first)
        • \n
      • When they stared at their cards, trying to decide, I have a handful of tips, mostly how power A is different from power B (reading power cards revisited)
        • \n
      \nIt was pretty slow going at first, because we were all learning together. I didn't time anyone, but I tried to push things along best I could.

      I also was learning the tactical game myself (which I think is complex, if the goal is to master it). I think that unless you're playing competitively (player vs. dm), all that matters is that everyone is close enough (I'm a fair chess player, some people I can beat consistently, but anyone serious will mop the floor with me, it's most fun, and you learn best to play with people at about your 'level').

      First session, I ran the monsters sub-optimally, mostly to contrive examples.
      Second session, I ran them normally, and the players did really well against them.
      Third session, I had to study and come up with better tactics (I could have also added monsters to the encounters, effectively 'handicapping' the players.
      From there on out, I wasn't amazing, they weren't amazing, but we kept pace with each other pretty well.

      The thing I found to be most important in teaching new players is to have the character match what the player wants to do. One of my (many) mistakes teaching this group was forgetting that a Human Fighter is as complex as an Eladrin Wizard in 4E, the fighter can't just say "I hit him with my greatsword" like in previous editions. Also, the fighter's job in 4e is to get hit. A player who doesn't want to have a target painted on her chest shouldn't be a fighter, at least at first. It's not fun for them. In later sessions, this player created a cleric, which she loved playing.

      I guess my point is, I don't think that study of the books is necessary. I do think that there is a learning curve, and to get to the 3 encounters = 4 hours of play standard, one needs system competence, and possibly mastery of their character. I also think that the "What you need to know to play" could be summed up in 2 pages (with a big asterisk that one can do other things not listed on those 2 pages)

      \n I haven't watched any other groups play and I've never seen an RPGA session in any edition of D&D, so I only have the two groups I play in as reference, both groups I've played with a long time. I guess we don't take the tactical elements as seriously as those guys. There is one guy that often takes more than a minute, but that is balanced out by a lot of 20 second turns. The DM turn does typically take a couple minutes. Another factor is that we haven't seen Paragon play yet. Soon!

      I wasn't counting roleplaying, just the moving and rolling your attack and damage dice together, which honestly usually takes about ten to twenty seconds. The roleplaying does add more time, but that's time I don't mind, and I don't think of it as just that player's turn. That's the period where we all look up from the table and engage one another, regardless of whose turn it is.

      I've read all those ENworld threads and participated in some of them, asking questions to try to understand what's making things move so glacially. We typically only have one or two combats a five hour session but only because we want the rest to be pure roleplaying. But when we decided to try a module just to gain proficiency with the combat rules, we went through Scepter Tower of Spellguard at 4 encounters (once even 5!) per 5-6 hour session night. We had four Strikers and a Leader which also helped keep things short and deadly.

      I do agree the combats take too long for my tastes. So... my house rules.

      All more evidence, I guess, that people can't talk really talk about D&D because they're not playing the same game in a lot of ways.
