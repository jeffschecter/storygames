    \n Like Judd, I don't know what to tell you. Here's an example, though, based on racism that I've personally experienced. Because I'm a privileged White male, this means going halfway around the world...

    In China, they have this term for foreigners, "lao wai," which seems pretty harmless. "Lao" means "Old," and is used in many terms of endearment or familiarity: "Old Man Xu," "old friends, "old schoolmates," etc. "Wai" means "outside" and is a general term for foreign things. If you ask most Chinese people about the term "lao wai," most people will describe it as a term of respect or familiarity. To them, it doesn't really seem to have any racist connotations. After all, it describes all sorts of people, from all kinds of cultural or national backgrounds.

    However, if you look at the way Chinese people actually use the term "lao wai," you get a pretty different picture. Now, anti-White racism in China is nowhere near as bad as anti-Black racism, don't get me wrong. There were race riots in Nanjing in the 80's when African students were viewed as taking advantage of (or just polluting) Chinese women. And there have been riots related to China's own Muslim population. But, when it comes to "lao wai" in practice, it almost always applies to people of European descent. In other cases (at least, in my experience), Chinese people tend to use more specific terms: Black people, Japanese, South Americans, Arabs, Southeast Asians, Indians, etc.

    And when people use the term "lao wai" as opposed to the more proper "waiguo ren" ("people from foreign nations) it's almost always in a dismissive or negative fashion. Part of that has to do with how unspecific the term is. It implies "I don't know where you're from, but I can tell you're foreign by looking at you." And this kind of usage happens daily, all around you, as you walk down the street. The most common being: "Look, a lao wai!" So this is a definite case where the way the term is actually used is very different from the way people think about the term or possibly even their intentions. They're not necessarily intending to make people feel like circus freaks, but that's what ends up happening.

    So, to bring this back to Jabba...

    I hate people using ethnic stereotypes to depict evil or exotic characters. Period. Equating ethnicity with evil or sneaky intentions has a long history (like the Yellow Peril), all of which fucking sucks. Also, ethnicity does not make you exotic. I look pretty unusual when I'm in China, being an ethnic European, but I am not a stranger there. I speak Chinese fluently. I can discuss politics and recent events with people and sing along to popular music. Likewise, Shreyas and Dev come from a South Asian ethnic background, but basically they're just awesome nerdy kids from Jersey. Equating or associating appearance (race, gender, etc.) with culture and personality is hella dumb.

    People are complicated. Things like race and ethnicity and gender and nationality and sexuality and culture are important components that people use when constructing their identities (or having their identities constructed for them and then impressed upon them), but no one wants to feel like a walking cliche. People constantly break with traditions and act out. There are thousands of studies of deviant behavior that talk about this. This is also how new traditions get created, because a few individuals make decisions to do things differently and the results are really neat.

    Roleplaying has the potential to celebrate the complexity and diversity of people and the world, which I find to be one of its most beautiful characteristics. Too often, though, like people everywhere, we reach for the "easy answers," the stereotypes, the generalizations that aren't actually correct. And that just sucks.

    So yeah.

    Censorship also sucks. If he took down that picture of Jabba then we wouldn't get an opportunity to talk about racism. It would just be sweeping things under the rug and pretending like everything was cool. But everything isn't cool and conversation, changing people's understandings a little bit at a time, is the only way it's ever going to get better.

    Anyway, I'm not sure if that helps any, but there are some of my thoughts. It always feels kinda weird to be a straight white middle-class American male attempting to fight racism, sexism, classism, homophobia, and bigotry of all kinds. I don't want to co-opt other people's struggle and water it down with my own limited perspective, but this shit affects all of us in the long run (like Brand said recently in his post on how misogyny hurts men too) and we all need to do what we can.

    \n I have given a lot of thought to racism in gaming as well as its next door neighbor, sexism, and its upstairs tenant, political prejudices and assumptions.

    This is as a result of sharing a birthday with Sax Rohmer, author of the Fu Manchu novels, which I have read and collected and enjoyed thoroughly.

    "How can you read and enjoy that racist stuff, JDCorley?" you ask. "You are one of those liberal mealy mouth hippies who believes in the Bill of Rights and will probably be interned at Guantanamo once the coup happens. You work with highly diverse people in a highly diverse population, you see with your own eyes every day the unbelievable intergenerational horror that results from institutionalized racism, and you know for a fact that fictions like Rohmers are part of the problem, that they perpetuate a myth even if they're presented as fiction, you know all this and you still like reading them?"

    I go further than that, I see nothing wrong with using an Asian master-villain stereotype in a pulp adventure game if the players are all right with it.

    Here are some examples. Assume I have been tapped to run a cops-and-criminals game.

    Game 1: Designed to depict a highly diverse population, scrupulously avoids stereotyping, issues of race and racism are addressed realistically. (The Shield).
    Game 2: Although most of the main characters are white, race is occasionally an issue addressed semi-realistically but not in any great depth. (Law & Order).
    Game 3: Although most of the main characters are white, racial issues are presented primarily as a complication to an already difficult situation. There is no real analysis of the concern, it is simply presented. (tokenism)
    Game 4: Race is simply not addressed and the game is played with our out-of-character middle class racial attitudes implicit in the game.
    Game 5: The characters in the game, be they PCs or NPCs, respond and appear in ways according with racial stereotypes, although they aren't caricatures.
    Game 6: The game is overt race propaganda in which the denigrated race is portrayed in the most twisted, vile ways imaginable. (such as Golden Age supers or war comics et. seq.)

    In looking over these games, I see the most objectionable game as being #4, even more so than #6.

    Why?

    Because it is unconscious.

    When I read Sax Rohmer's books, I read them not as he wrote them or as a contemporary might have read them, but as I, JDCorley, mealy mouthed hippie living in 2007 in Arizona. That is not to say that I don't have racist impulses, of course I do, I'm a middle class child of white privilege, but when I read Rohmer (or the Arabian Nights or any number of other highly racist historical texts) I try to be as conscious as possible about what it is I'm reading - to read critically, so to speak. Actually I try to do that with everything I read.

    I see nothing wrong with a game that adopts points of view that the players find repugnant or even vile so long as they do it voluntarily and consciously. For example, I don't think that #6 would be a very fun game, so I wouldn't choose to do it.

    But if you replace race with politics, I am running game #5 right now, the Project Tenebrous game I mentioned in another thread. If you are running any kind of superspy or commando game, you are doing a game in which the conservative stereotype of world politics is accurate and unquestioned. The reason spies aren't like James Bond is not because we couldn't train them to be if we wanted to - it's because the world doesn't work like the world of James Bond. Vanilla D&D is the conservative view of the Old West.

    Now I think the conservative stereotype of world politics is dead wrong and extremely dangerous, I think it's produced rivers of blood and squandered mountains of treasure, and I know a big part of its perpetuation is that it has dominated American mythmaking and storytelling for so long. That doesn't mean every time someone, meaning me, entertains the fantasy, they're agreeing with the premises. It means they're playing "what if" and watching what happens as an audience.

    This doesn't get the artist (or in the case of RPGs, the players) off the hook. They're still responsible for what they produce. I just think that you can take that responsibility.

    This is a little bit of a threadcrap because I dislike damn near all art in all gaming books ever and consider it to be a waste of perfectly good space that I could use for, say, writing notes on. But the basis of the objection is the same and actually a little stronger against written game materials than against visual materials.

    In conclusion: I blame the patriarchy.

      I have been really inspired by the treatment of race in The Shield. SPOILERS AHOY.

      Now as you may or may not know, The Shield is a cop show set in a hellhole neighborhood of Los Angeles starring Michael Chiklis as Detective Vic Mackey, described accurately in the first season as "Al Capone with a badge". He and his team of corrupt cops take bribes from drug dealers and target their competition in return. They frame people, extort people, and in the first season, they murder a police officer who is getting too close. A side note on the Strike Team: in addition to The Shield inspiring a new treatment of race, for those who haven't worked in law enforcement and who don't know a lot of cops, Mackey and his group are a bracing antidote to the authoritarian wet dream that most "bad cops" on TV and in movies are. Typically "bad cops" are portrayed as hamstrung by "the rules" (meaning the Constitution) and are praiseworthy for their brutal treatment of the subhuman criminals (sometimes the inhumanity of the criminals is outright stated, other times it's just implied). The Strike Team, by contrast, has been shaped by violence and before the first season's over, they've railroaded an innocent person into jail, arranged for several deaths, and it has torn apart families and destroyed their aspirations. Mackey is the only one confident in what they're doing - and he is presented as the worst of the group to make that decision. (Indeed, when his son is diagnosed with autism and his wife becomes estranged from him, the kitchen sink drama demonstrates that he doesn't understand that his good intentions mean nothing when placed against the monstrous mountain of bad acts.)

      But back to race. The neighborhood is highly diverse, but all poor, so the criminals are white, black, Latino, Asian, all varieties. The use of music in the series enhances this feeling - you hear Latino hip-hop and local Angeleno acts all the time. The supporting cast are equally diverse - a political kingmaker is Latino, a high-ranking state administrator is black, a respected author and professor is black, and so on.

      In the main characters themselves, although there are standout black characters (Claudete, played by CCH Pounder is absolutely brilliantly portrayed, tired, smart, dissatisfied with herself), the second central character to the series is David Aceveda, a politically ambitious police captain in Season 1, a City Councilman by Season 4, and he wants to get Mackey - not in small part because it will cement his political career, but also because he knows Mackey is part of the problem.

      What The Shield has done with respect to race is simply depict what a diverse city is. I suppose I should not say 'simply', after all, so many TV shows have 'simply' not done anything of the sort. Race is a significant issue in many shows but as far as the characters are concerned, it can't possibly be an issue even if they wanted it to be. None of them have the power to change it.

      I highly recommend snagging Season 1 on DVD and taking some notes about how they handle race. It has changed my modern-day games. (And historical ones too!)

      \n ICE, one example I can think of (she ran 6-8 simulations a year, and switched them out constantly) is a unit for the Articles of Confederation (another bit of elided US history). Each table in the classroom was a different colony on Mars who had all collectively just seceded from Earth. They had to interact in order to prosper, stay alive, and earn points, but all of their interaction was governed by a really cumbersome consensus mechanic (ie, the Articles). The goal of the simulation was to prod the students to throw out the Articles-analogue and try to put together a replacement (ie, Constitution). Which they always invariably did. To complicate things, there were a lot of little bits including big states versus small states and industry versus agrarian cultures. One of the big ones was the green-skinned martians that the colonists used for slave labor. There was nothing in the sim to provoke students to ask about freeing the martians, but they always invariably did. When ask, the teacher was instructed in the teacher guide to tell them that the martians would riot, fight amongst themselves and raid the colonists, and eventually starve. Because that was the common perception of the results of freeing slaves in the US. The students, always and invariably, try freeing the slaves anyway. At which point the rules for labor shift very slightly, but nothing catastrophic happens.
