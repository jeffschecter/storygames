\n Somehow I've never played Lady Blackbird. I need to remedy that! I might even have to GM it, because I don't know anyone else who's volunteering to. But I have a noob question.

Looking at the system ("Rolling the Dice") I'm a bit confused; the difficulty levels seem to be a lot harder than their naming implies.

For example, an "easy" challenge requires 2 hits; to have even a 50% chance of passing, you'll need to roll 3 dice. If you have a relevant trait and tag you can roll those without spending any of your pool dice; but then, if you have those the GM shouldn't be asking you to roll in the first place ("By default, characters can accomplish anything covered by their traits".) So we assume that if you're rolling you don't have a relevant trait, right? That means you have to spend 2 of your 7 pool dice to have even a halfway good chance of passing an "easy" challenge. Or to put it another way, you can afford to try about 3 easy challenges per scene, of which you'll fail about half, i.e. you'll succeed at one or two.

That doesn't seem "easy" to me. :-/ And as for a "hard" challenge, you'll need 3 hits, which means 5 dice to get a 50% chance \u2026 that's spending more than half your pool right there, on one attempt that will fail half the time.

I'm not trying to poke holes in the game! Obviously I'm just missing something, because Lady Blackbird is widely attested to be an excellent game with simple but enjoyable mechanics. So how does it really work?

    \n You're taking that "can accomplish anything covered by their traits" statement out of context. It means they can accomplish those things when there are no other complicating obstacles in the way. That is, a pilot can pilot all day long in clear weather without having to roll for it, but evading a skysquid in the middle of a thunderstorm will definitely call for a piloting roll.

    \n No, I think the assumption is that characters will be rolling their Traits most of the time.

    That line means, "Don't ask for a roll when a character is just being themselves" (e.g. the mechanic trying to repair something, the Captain ordering his crew around). They should still roll in the case of some kind of conflict, trouble, etc - and in those situations I'd be pretty surprised if a player's Traits didn't apply (it does happen sometimes, of course, but not often - only when they're really acting outside their roles).

    (Cross-posted with Mike, above!)

    When I wrote my Lady Blackbird hack, I expected characters to be rolling about 6-9 dice on a "typical" roll, and that seemed to fit pretty well.

    \n Sure, easy.

    Let's say the guards are coming to attack our brave heroes and take them back into the brig. Who's gonna do something? Well, Naomi has the Key of the Guardian, so of course she steps up. She's going stand guard between the guards and the Lady, so Natasha can (presumably) get away. (Of course, the Lady may end up doing some else altogether, but Naomi's a stubborn defender and thinks she knows what's best for Natasha...)\n
    Bodyguard
    Awareness, Threats, Defend, Disarm, Restrain, Carry, Delay,
    [Security], [First Aid]
    \nShe gets one die to begin with (1). Then she takes an additional die for being a Bodyguard (2). Awareness, Threats, Defend, Delay definitely apply as well, for four more (6). It wouldn't be that hard for a creative player to come up with an action which also includes Disarming or Restraining. So that's 6-8 dice.

    Then she can add dice from her Pool, if she wants, and possibly a companion is helping her.

    If she was acting counter to her role, of course (like trying to impress Natasha by competing in a fine arts contest? Not as likely an action for this character...) she might only get 1-5 dice to roll. But then she'll probably pitch in a few from her Pool, plus possibly helping dice.

    In the worst-case scenario, she just gets the 1 die, and then she might chip in 3-8 dice from her Pool, for a total of 4-9.

    The hypothetical single-die roll *could* happen (a character doing something really out of character and right after blowing all their Pool dice, and without any possibility of help)... but I've never seen it.

    \n It's worth stressing that it's pretty easy to get pool dice by hitting keys and by having refresh scenes, so players can and should spend them fairly freely. When I've run it, I've been pretty generous about letting tags apply, and pretty generous about interpreting whether a key has been hit. As a result, it's been a game where the characters succeed at stuff most of the time- and that's felt like part of the appeal.
