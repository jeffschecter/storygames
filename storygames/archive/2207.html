Howdy, folks.

I need a little low-commitment help from anybody who's got ten or fifteen minutes to burn.

My current game-in-development, Sons of Liberty, is a wild romp through the American Revolution, loosely framed as "Dynasty Warriors meet the Founding Fathers." To give you the basic idea of how the game plays, if you're playing Ben Franklin you can be sweeping the cobblestone streets clear of Redcoats by swinging around your electrified kite -- and if you're not cranking up the crazy to a commensurate level, you're playing it wrong. The game is design to go to crazyland and fast.

The situation creation mechanic that I'm working with uses random draws of cards to determine elements of the players' Objectives (just like a level of Dynasty Warriors, right?). Cards are looked up on a table to find words that are filled in to a mad-libs-like structure. The players fill out the blanks and then briefly play the roles of Freemasonic Grandmasters discussing the progress of the conspiracy and revolution. In other words, they apply a little human interpretation to the mad libs to knit them together. Theoretically speaking, this produces a situation in ten to fifteen minutes, and then you're off and running.

The one face-to-face playtest I've had so far worked well, but with so many (52 x 4 = 208) elements that can go into a given situation, I'm slightly worried that there may be some bad apples in the random charts that won't work well in creating a situation. That's where you come in.

I have a bare-bones web page that uses javascript (thanks, Jason!) to create Sons of Liberty Objectives, just like pulling cards. If you wouldn't mind helping me out, hit up the webpage and copy-paste the resulting objectives into a comment here, and then explain how the bits "obviously" go together in your hypothetical game. I'm hoping that this can sniff out any problem items in the lists -- if somebody says, "I have no idea how to interpret this bit here" that's a pretty good sign that it needs to be tweaked or removed.

(Yes, there are a few little grammar hiccups, too. Those aren't my concern right now, since they're more programmatic than conceptual.)

Thanks in advance, everybody!

    Here's an example:

    Primary Objective
    1. The Sons of Liberty must incite an arms cache in Boston Harbor to prevent the British install a street gang!

    Opportune Objectives
    1. The Sons of Liberty can blackmail a lawyer while the British plant clockwork armor.
    2. The Sons of Liberty can hide a preacher while the British introduce a battery of light cannon.
    3. The Sons of Liberty can contact an apprentice after the British introduce an orator.

    Secret Objective
    * The Sons of Liberty must supply a fleet while the British lead an author!

    And I get a tough one right off the bat, of course! Incite an arms cache? Well... with the understanding that the British are trying to sway the allegiences of one of the street gangs down in Boston Harbor, one can only assume that they are offering the street rats arms that they might not otherwise have access to. Our operatives, then, must somehow interrupt that transfer -- and it seems like it's a delicate one, easily incited somehow. I'm sure our boys in the field will be able to sort it out!

    There are a couple opportunities for our agents to acheive that goal that we've already identified, as well. The British are trying to manipulate the patent office in Boston to undercut colonial manufacture of clockwork armor -- they're trying to cut off our munitions before the war even gets started! We know, however, the lawyer who they're working through, and we can threaten to expose him... and he may know something about the British running guns to the street gang.

    The British are also moving in a battery of light cannon (think: clockwork-and-mirror powered lasers), which may distract them long enough that we can move in and extract poor Reverend Carter, who they are keeping tight surveillance on. He works with the street gangs, and he may know one of the ruffians well enough to give our agents an opportunity to subvert the British plans.

    The British are also trotting out that damned Tory orator Robert Montgomery, and we understand they're escorting him into Boston. His runaway son Jeremy has recently left the street gang to take up an apprenticeship, and the appearance of his father may enflame his long-standing hatred of everything his father stands for. Manuever him into an 'accidental' run-in with dad, and he may decide to help us out.

    Lastly, (and the Secret Objective is actually a bit that the Grandmasters don't discuss, it comes later) it turns out that the British are trying to encourage Montgomery to publish his speeches. They've already got the paper and presses set up -- paper and presses that our pamphleteers can certainly make use of. We have a trio of smuggling boats in Boston Harbor, and it would be so simple to pinch those presses and squirrel them away for our own purposes. If our agents can accomplish that without endangering their primary mission, all the better for the cause.

    Primary Objective

    This objective will determine victory or yet another setback on the road to liberty.

    1. The Sons of Liberty must pin down a fleet in Florida while the British improve supporters!

    Opportune Objectives

    These three objectives are means by which the Sons of Liberty can effect their plans. Choose your strategies well -- or dominate the battlefield by accomplishing all three.

    1. The Sons of Liberty can disassemble a heliograph station to prevent the British frame a mole.

    2. The Sons of Liberty can teach tax records after the British lead minutemen.

    3. The Sons of Liberty can incite a mole while the British jury-rig a judge.

    Secret Objective

    * The Sons of Liberty must charge an informant to prevent the British jury-rig an ornithopter!

    OK. The British are negotiating with the Spanish in Florida, which they've only held since 1763 (thanks, Wikipedia). The Spanish want it back (and indeed will get it back in 1783 after the Revolution). They are trying to improve Spanish support against the colonists by offering a good deal on Floridan produce, which is currently loaded on a fleet of merchant ships. There's a deadline, however, because the Spanish official they're negotiating with is about to move on to greater things.

    The Sons of Liberty, meanwhile, have inserted a mole into the negotiations. A British loyalist has discovered this and is trying to send a message via heliograph framing the mole for a crime. The British would then set up a kangaroo court with a specially selected jury to convict the mole, despite anything the judge might think or say; the Sons are trying to incite the mole to actually commit the crime on the "might as well be hung for a sheep as a lamb" principle. The crime involves fraud over the Floridan produce, and the Sons are coaching the mole in how to falsify tax records, but the current location of the relevant tax records is unknown; the British must be decoyed into leading several minutemen who have volunteered as espionage agents to the location of the records.

    The British informant who knows the mole's identity and can testify against him is himself vulnerable to criminal proceedings and, if not stopped, will jury-rig an ornithopter to fly to the island where the negotiations are taking place.

    Or something. This is moderately hard, but definitely doable.
