\n Greetings,

we all know that there are story games that can feel gimiky or that use a lot of stuff like extra cards or a gameboard.
In my experience there are often people saying that this stuff is to boardgamey or "just like a boardgame".
We all know you can theoretically roleplay the shit out of a boardgame.

So where does playing a roleplaying game start and boardgaming stop?
I can never quite define it and have hard times when this comes up even though i know how it feels to play a boardgame verus a roleplaying game.

    \n This is highly subjective, but I think when you make any decisions that don't guide you explicitly towards a "win" condition, you've stopped playing a boardgame.

    I mean, if I use my in game resources to help someone else win, or on some novel effect that doesn't get me points, I'm playing a role and treating my game piece like it has some kind of will. That's the line for me. So the game can guide you to do one or the other, but you could do either activity in either "type" of game.

    \n People get hung up on artifact use.

    Early on, RPGs looked weird to people because so much of the game takes place in the imagination of the players and the games play out primarily verbally.

    I suspect that RPGers felt a bit put upon by the reaction of other folks, and as a culture started to feel a bit both isolated and elitst about this difference people were noting.

    When people began to re-introduce physical artifcats into play, it ran headlong into that emotional defensive elitism, and the crankiness began.

    Most arguments I've seen about certain games being Not-RPGs because of artifact use seem pretty ingenuous to me. They have about as much weight as hearing someone argue that D&D is just a yahtzee variant because both have players rolling dice and changing scores on their own piece of paper.

    \n I would say it's a boardgame if the game itself take care of all the narration. When the results of the boardgame is so obvious, it doesn't need anyone to bring anything else fiction-wise.

    With this in mind, how could you change Monopoly into a roleplaying game? What happens if you remove the street names and say the participants may come up with a theme concerning buying shares in a economical system. That the players has to narrate what they buy (within the theme) when they buy it? What happens if the interaction between the players are only allowed in "business meeting scenes" or "telephone scenes"? If the chance cards needs narrative interpretation to be applied to the game?

    \n Orthodox Forge-originating rpg theory has a very simple answer for this: if your play actually happens within what we call the "shared imagined space" (or "the fiction" when we try to be transparent), then you're playing a roleplaying game. Insofar as this is not the case, you're not. If we need to get even more specific, we can look at the nature of the activity around the table: is it predicated upon (that is, dependent on as a prerequisite) "exploration" of the "shared imagined space"?

    Both "Exploration" and "Shared Imagined Space" have very persnickety definitions that tend to make it pretty obvious whether some activity is roleplaying - at least I haven't had any trouble drawing the lines in practice, and I dare say that I've considered some pretty out there forms of play and non-play.

    Other traditions of rpg thought have different definitions/assumptions (often the thinking is not codified, so it's not so much a question of definition, but rather of perceiving what a person is assuming). I demonstrated this in 2004 with a nice Ven diagram that showed the three partially overlapping dominant rpg schools of thought in terms of what they consider a roleplaying game. Aside from the Big Model definition above I'm pretty happy with these descriptions:

    Traditionalist: traditional rpg thinking utilizes a family resemblance heuristic. This means that for something to get categorized as a roleplaying game, it should bear sufficient resemblance to games reified by the tradition as important roleplaying games. Thus e.g. having a GM, being about dungeon crawling, involving characters modeled by attributes, xp reward systems - all of these and others are features that make it more likely for a traditionalist to consider your activity a roleplaying game. It's a matter of cultural history to define what is a roleplaying game as opposed to some other type of game, like defining which works belong in the Pre-Raphaelite canon.

    Immersionist: Nordic immersionism particularly seems to focus on the idea that roleplaying is ROLE-playing, and thus the topic and subject matter of rpg theory are games where individual players have individual character relationships that can be described as "playing a role". This usually, but not necessarily, involves immersion into the role on an emotional level.

    I find it intensely interesting that the stupid perennial Internet debate about what roleplaying is obviously cannot be resolved once you accept the very postmodern fact that there exist in this world multiple models of thought that are differently useful for different purposes and different people. A term like "roleplaying game" will be utilized in useful ways by people who have different concerns, which can be clearly seen in the above comparison between prominent schools of rpg theory. Thus the only people actually debating this sort of thing are the ones ignorant enough to never have encountered genuinely different needs and models of thought.

    To demonstrate this, consider the game My Life with Master: a Forgean theorist will recognize it as a roleplaying game because clearly a fiction is being utilized as a playground. A traditional theorist will have misgivings, for cornerstones of traditional design are clearly being flouted; I think he would be justified in claiming that a break with tradition has occurred and that this is "something else", although he might not necessarily do so with a game that still has a GM and players and where at least something happens in the imagination. An immersionist will likely criticize the same game for how it violates internal sanctity of characters and interferes in "pure roleplaying" by insisting on using dice and framing devices; he would likely claim that MLwM is not a good vehicle for roleplaying, and not properly a roleplaying game, but rather a boardgame of some sort.

    As an even more radical example, consider Final Fantasy VII: I have met immersionist roleplayers who quite seriously tell me that they consider the activity of playing this video game "roleplaying" in the same sense that they use for what they do while e.g. live-action roleplaying. I have no reason to disbelieve, clearly this person is just focusing on internal experience of immersing in character (or world, or plot, or whatever) as his measure of whether roleplaying is present or not.

    As for what a person should do in this sea of definitions and theories, I would suggest the postmodern approach: select models and definitions that serve your own interests, and communicate them clearly to others where necessary. For example, the reason for why I myself usually utilize the Big Model definition of roleplaying is that I've found that it is effective in capturing the feature of roleplaying that I personally find centrally interesting in roleplaying: games that are roleplaying according to this model are consistently interesting for me as "roleplaying games", and games that are not practically never are. This makes me think that there is something to this definition, while e.g. the immersionist idea of character immersion is something on which I am profoundly uncaring - it does not correlate with what I find interesting in the hobby.

    \n Lately, I've pretty much only been working on games that operate right in the middle of board games and RPGs. My personal feeling is that worrying about these distinctions too much tends to get in the way of making more interesting and provocative games. Plus, it makes it more difficult to market or explain RPGs to those who are unfamiliar with them or weirded out by them. When I tell people about my game design work I tend to say: "I make experimental board games." And that's something they can easily imagine and possibly connect to. In the end, it's all tabletop. The real divide is still between digitial and "analog" games, but even that is being bridged more often these days.

I tend to think of the one hard requirement for an RPG being that the player has one character/persona (or a very small number of them) that they control within the game world, but it's really easy to find edge cases and things to quibble about.

    \n My two questions would be:

    1) Are role-playing and board gaming exclusive? (i.e. Could there be a "role-playing board game"?)
    2) How and/or why is the distinction important?

    Personally, I prefer to define a role-playing game based on relatively common/intuitive conceptions of "role-playing" and "game". This would exclude some shared-fiction games like Once Upon A Time or the shared-creation-of-an-encyclopedia game whose name I forget. On the other hand, I don't think it's a big deal to vary in this label.

    So I would say that the two aren't exclusive - If a game involves role-playing and uses a board, then it is both. (For example, I had an article on using a board with My Life With Master - http://www.darkshire.net/jhkim/rpg/mylifewithmaster/board.html - though I haven't tried it yet.)

    Are role-playing and board gaming exclusive? (i.e. Could there be a "role-playing board game"?)
    \nI don't see any reason they should be exclusive. A board game is just using mechanics that encode part or all of the game state in terms of tokens and their spatial positions. That definitely applies to all sorts of things that are used in RPGs: from miniatures on a gridded battle mat, to poker chips as Fate points/bennies/whatever.

    For what it's worth, I'm all in favor of these sorts of things. I think that by moving part of the bookkeeping and state-maintenance of the game into physical space, they can simplify the players' jobs and let them either focus more deeply on the fiction, or make the fictional world broader. (As an example of the latter, the D&D style battle mat lets the fiction encompass more details of where people are positioned in space, which you can't really do in any detail otherwise.)
