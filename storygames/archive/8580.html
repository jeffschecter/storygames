    \n So I'm running into the same problem here that I ran into making my solo dragonslayer challenge. I'll just toss it out, and maybe someone else will have an insight.

    We need a game system to define what characters can do in the fiction, i.e. who can they reasonable expect to take in a fight? What kind of magic can they work and when? Is it reasonable for them to try and trick an opponent? Sneak past? Realize they're being lied to or ambushed? Likewise we need a system that somewhat defines the capabilities of the monsters.

    Then, presumably, we'll make a plan for success in each challenge that uses our character capabilities to hopefully beat the monster capabilities.

    Then we need a system for resolution. Most systems require some kind of player interaction, which means they won't be ideally suitable for solo play.

    I'm not sure these are the same thing in the context of a solo dungeon challenge. For example, the Engle Matrix system can be used for resolution if someone is nominated as the referee, but it doesn't give us any guidance in creating characters. D&D is commonly available and familiar for character creation, but doesn\u2019t give very wide resolution capabilities (covering basically straight up combat). StWT gives broader resolution, but still isn\u2019t optimized for solo play.

    Would it be too confusing to use a hybrid system, where, for example, we make up characters using StWT but then use Engle Matrix for resolution?

    \n Seems to me that the best systems for this sort of solo gaming are complex and well-researched. You're not going to get much of meaningful challenge play from solo execution of a rpg challenge, but what you can do is get interesting analytical discussion. This is what we see on the forums of challenge-based games: folks playing something like D&D can very well "play solo" by running simulations about fighting trolls or whatever the rules system does, which then provides strategic data: what is the minimum level for a character who can kill a troll, what is the optimal class for him and so on and so forth. That sort of solo play seems to work quite fine for folks who have that sort of investment in a given game system.

    In other words: asking "what is the minimum level of the D&D character/party that could reasonably clear this dungeon?" seems to be a valid solo play challenge based on what people are doing in different places. "Can I challenge this dungeon and survive?" on the other hand seems like somewhat pointless as a solo exercise - at least people don't seem to be doing this sort of thing.

    Of course it's valid to question whether fiction is actually being engaged in that sort of system-optimization exercise - after reading my share of 3rd edition D&D character optimization and whatnot I'd answer affirmatively to a degree.

    \n I don't know, I'm just speculating about whether solo play is even possible when engagement with the fiction is a prerequisite. D&D discussion forums sort of seem to get something like this out of speculative play, but from what I've seen, the focus is different from normal play: instead of having a specific character and specific conditions, the solo play shifts between ideas and resources and maps the space of possibilities that the rules and fiction offer in an effort to answer some generic question. So instead of a single play-through of a challenge you get an essay, essentially - an analysis of the fictional situation and the different ways that the challenges could be overcome. This is very different from the end-results of normal roleplaying.

    As a concrete example of what I'm thinking of here... well, this thread delves into the mechanics of creating invulnerable monsters in D&D by cross-breeding trolls, and the potential challenges an evil wizard would face in executing such a plan under the rules - as well as the challenges in stopping an army of such "Ikea Tarrasques" when they attack your town. As far as I can see, this sort of discussion is quite close to "solo play" of challengeful fantasy adventure games - as close as I can imagine getting, in fact.
