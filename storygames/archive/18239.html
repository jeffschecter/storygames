    \n 15 is a tall order. I've run 9 groups simultaneously, and that was bloody hard work. Now I run 7, and that's a bit much too.

    This is my best tool:
    I have a large sheet for each group, with a large square on it (representing the table);
    - outside the square are the player names
    - inside the square are the character names (in BIG letters)

    At the back of the sheet I have a list over the sessions of the group, with dates and short notes on what has happened in each and every session (single words and highlights, no prose).

    I bring this sheet to each session, and start the sessions by handing out characters and having players tell me what happened last session (that's when I note it down). Having players telling what happened helps them all remember, helps them reestablish the atmosphere of the game, and gives me valuable insights into their view on things.

    After the telling I turn the sheet, with the names up, and start the game.

    \n Now that's interesting, framing up your project as managing 15 classes.

    Having never formally taught a class before, I can imagine there are some commonalities with facilitating an RPG but also some important differences.

    The big question I have, and I couldn't quite figure it out from your previous round of answers, is: Do the events of these fifteen games impact one another? If they're totally free-standing, and the games are not really heavy on character-arc development, then I think it would not be crazy to keep notes on 15 simultaneous game-states.

    I remember doing a fun classroom RPG in middle school that broke my class up into uh...I think four groups of 5 kids each. It was a postapocalypse scenario (it was late into the Cold War) and all four groups listened simultaneously to the same prerecorded "radio transmission." Then the teacher would have us deal with a crisis, taking into account what we'd just heard on the radio and the ongoing game-state. Then we'd have to work it out amongst ourselves (creating lots of terrific psychodrama), and occasionally interact with other survivalist groups (trading assets we'd lucked into or hoarded throughout the exercise).

    That was, basically, four simultaneous games that touched each other once in a while. I could easily see running the same thing in four different settings, resulting in 16 games (grouped into four higher-order games).

    \n Wow, this sounds amazing and challenging.

    Is written communication part of this course? If so, you could create interesting "assignments", where a certain group's progress during a certain scenario becomes a challenge for another group. For example, we have a difficult situation--maybe some hostages must be rescued or negotiated out of some kind of conflict. Let's say they manage to rescue the hostages, but their captors get away free, and with a bunch of money or weapons (or some other resource).

    Once the players have resolved the conflict (to some extent), you ask them: "What is difficult and challenging about the situation now? What are the dangers? What kinds of difficult choices remain?" They write down their answers (essentially, another scenario), for the next group to deal with. Who will take care of the survivors? They're running out of water. The captors are heavily armed and heading towards another village nearby. Etc, etc.

    This way each group takes action "in the game world" and then passes on the stick to the next group of players, who must deal with the consequences.

    \n I've been a professional GM since 1995, in Oslo, Norway. I'm running seven groups a week currently, each for sessions 2 hours and 15 minutes. Some of my experiences may help you.

    Here's my penny ...

    First; use a good game!

    - It should be much more effective in feeding the fiction than most classical game are ("more effective" = faster, easier to "read").
    - It should be simple to run, as in; let you make scenarios on the spot (preferably).
    - One die is preferable to many, sketchy characters preferable to complex (all characters grow complex in the course of game-play)
    - I have nothing but good experiences sharing scene-framing with my players. Let them take rounds framing scenes. They tend to feed the game with what they are interested in, lifting that responsibility from your shoulders.
    - Make sure your game support a broad array of themes and conflicts; in groups like the ones you plan to start there will be a broad range of interests, so you need to have a wide range of "fishing gear" to get them hooked
    - RPGs need some time to hook the participants, so tell them up front that the first session will be a bit boring; making characters, learning the method. And tell 'em: "We'll have a great adventure when all that dreary stuff is done!"

    The room is important!
    - and how you use it. You need to be undisturbed (a door to close is good). You should have as small a room as possible, and as small a table as possible. RPGs are based on dialogue, and dialogue thrives in intimate spaces. Sit close, so you may use low voices too (whispers are great for atmosphere). In a big room; use a corner, with a loose wall or shelves or curtains to make a smaller space.

    Be direct, practical and positive!

    - set regular times; the same time each and every week; a must if you are to cope with the many groups
    - be very polite to your pupils, and clear in your communications
    - make a blog with background-info for the games

    - be very friendly and welcoming the first session; shake hands with each and every player (even if that feels a bit "special"), say your name and ask for theirs
    - tell the players this is for FUN, and most fun will be had if all is playing along, treating their characters with serious intent, doing their best to survive
    - establish group-loyality: make it clear that they are meant to collaborate on the challenges you throw at them
    - entice them: tell them you are meant to play non-player characters nice AND WICKED for them to meet
    - help them along! You need to be helpful to make game-play fun for them; RPGs have some codes which is hard for some to crack; give them time by being patient and helpful as game-play proceeds

    - do NOT use a GM-screen; it detracts from your communication with the players, and anything you could put on it may be put on the table, or in a chair beside it
    - make yourself a game-sheet for each group; with names of players and CHARACTERS
    - be sure to write down what happens during each session, on the back of the game-sheet
    - invite players to re-tell last session at the start of a new one

    - let game-play build and develop in liege with player initiatives, but be not afraid to make your mark on the fiction; you are a leading player, so lead by example
    - look for dramatic opportunities all the time; play the table, play the group, play each and every player
    - have fun!

    - use the alarm on your bright new and shiny smart-phone to keep time, so you may engulf in the sessions as a focused and engaged man

    Invite players to laugh a lot, and invite them to be serious and focused too. A good mixture will earn you their friendship, gratitude and respect. Use that to make the sessions a good time for all of you

    Good luck!

    \n Thanks, guys! Nice to hear that this is useful to you! I've done this for a long time, as said, and my system has been carefully crafted and re-crafted through the years. These things works for me. If they work for you too, all the better.

    Forgot to mention three important tidbits:

    - Make yourself a list of names suitable for the setting; one column of male and one of female names. Include columns of names for different regions and races, if needed. Keep these names in front of you during play. It makes it easy to come up with names for NPCs invented during the game.

    - Make a resource-sheet with useful information on the setting; names for inns, coins, wares, tools, weapons, travel-gear, etc. Include info on traditions like summer-festivals and well-known songs and tales, and info on ethics too (a list of virtues and sins). Make it a double-sided sheet to hand out to players during play. It will help them establish a framework for the setting for themselves, and enable them to frame great scenes for you all. It will help you too.

    - Make a simple scenario-sheet with 5-7 elements in LARGE LETTERS on it, for each game session. Place it in the center of the table, visible for all, and tell the players these are the elements of the scenario. Tell them they may frame scenes with these elements, if they want to, or they may leave those scenes to you. The elements may be marked by dots or numbers. If dots; you may frame scenes on any element. If numbers; you must frame a scene with element number 1 before you can introduce number 2, and then 3, etc. You may keep an element hidden (on the back of the sheet, or on another sheet), to surprise the players, if the scenario calls for it. You have done some thinking, of course, of how the elements interconnects, but you have not fixed the connections. You need to keep the the substance of each element and the connections between them open, to cater for player ingenuity. This way of doing scenarios goes very well with classical rpgs, and helps players understand what is going on. It is a simple way of increasing the pleasures of interaction for the players. You will find it makes the games much easier to lead too! And it makes for more fun, and more excitement, on your part.

    Have a nice day!

    \n The large and open scenario-sheet is a simple way of "framing the scenario".

    An example of a numbered scenario-sheet:

    1 - RONDIAN, a disgruntled guard

    2 - THE BUCKET, a hidden inn

    3 - The last words of a DYING RASCAL

    4 - THE CELLARS

    5 - ACID-WORMS!

    6 - An old and wicked DRAGON!!!

    As you see; this is numbered, so the guard must be first in line, and so on. You mark the elements as they are introduced, so there is no doubt about what is available. And the players may re-visit any element, of course, if possible within the confines of the narrative.

    The list lays out the road to adventure quite clearly to the players, and helps them envision some possible venues of play. It also helps them understand each scene in the larger context of "what is really going on here". The things you as a leader has to pay attention to, is to remind them that the list contains elements not known to their characters, and to observe the confines of the narrative (obstacles, challenges, misfortunes) as they present themselves in the course of play.

    Here's an example of a dotted scenario-sheet:

    * LAVINIA, the whore

    * AMBARIAN Rosythorn, the prince

    * LUDO, ambassador from the Kingdom of Lucia

    * THE SCEPTER, an ancient artifact

    * A DARK STRANGER

    * THE GREAT TOURNAMENT

    * Queen PARTHANIA Rosythorn

    Dotted elements makes the players free to visit any element on the list whenever they want, bar special hindrances that may be placed in their way during earlier scenes. This way of "framing scenarios" are well fitted to play on intrigues and other conflicts of complexity. As the elements are talked about you mark them with a slash, and when they are visited you make the slash into a cross (and you may keep adding crosses to mark several visits). You also make notes on the elements if necessary; a single word may be enough to help you and the players remember a secret or quality that surface during play. Arrows from one element to another may show important connections.

    Playing around with elements this way is a great way of sharing responsibility for the fiction, and a great way of keeping all players on the same track.

    And it is a ridiculously easy way of creating great adventures!

    Have fun!

    \n I've run 3 groups weekly, same setting, same time and in the course of the setting, have run about 9 or 10 groups through it, with some timeskips inbetween. I tend to do it in sets of 2-3 groups and round it up with them all meeting eachother in person for the first time (In some cases, OOC AND IC) and finding out why the campaigns had ended up conspiring themselves as such.
    What I found really useful was designing NPC/plot flowcharts. Have you ever played Chrononauts? The layout of the game has flipping of cause cards causing effects later down the line. Something very similar and ripply tends to occur if you try and run multiple groups through at the same time. You need to do very very little work beyond some simple NPCs, and you can rely on the actions of each PC group to play the GM role for you in the next session with the next group. This also gives a great way to PLAN sessions inbetween, in that by using a single planning document, rather than 15 separate ones, and marking inputs and outputs, rather than a list of actions performed by the groups, you can observe from the meta scale what is occurring to the world and how it might respond in kind.

    If you are planning on same set up and with the groups not interacting, I feel like it might increase workload despite outward appearances. Players have a tendency to be wildly varying in their actions and reactions to events, so whilst you might all start at the same point 1, group one will end up at Z, group 2 at M, group 3 at ; and group 4 at smiley kitty face, so to speak.

    Also Tomas provides some really useful framing and adventure advice that I might have to crib next time I try to run a simultaneous world...
