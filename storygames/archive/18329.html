    \nThe Shadow of Yesterday comes to mind. It's more differentiated than Burning Wheel, about the same as Apocalypse World. Slightly less than 3rd ed. D&D, due to a more unified resolution system and a degree of voluntary choice in how deep into the rabbit-hole you go. (With 3rd ed. some of the differentiation is forced.) Still, the game's got scope for characters that play radically differently to each other, and unlike D&D these differences can go into such fundamentals as what your character actually spends all their time on in the game.

    FATE-based games like Spirit of the Century are also quite reasonable in this department - about as differentiated as Burning Wheel, not quite as much as AW or TSoY. Differs a lot between the individual games, and if you can consider fictional positioning to be differentiation (that is, you're looking at more than just mechanics), then something like SotC is massively differentiated, as the whole premise of the game is to have entirely lateral character concepts.

    Of these four (AW, TSoY, BW and FATE) TSoY is most clearly mechanically differentiated, so if that's the sole gauge, then maybe check it out. The game has mechanical subsystems that individual characters commit to and that are completely unrelated to each other, which is pretty much what 3rd edition D&D does, except that D&D only gets this level of differentiation for casters vs. non-casters.

    And yes, older games have plenty of character differentiation - I'd say it's more common for a game traditionally to be built on the basis of differentiation than similarity. Ars Magica, for example, manages massive differences between characters while sticking to "everybody plays a magic-user".

    \n I find Nobilis to have very good character differentiation, first because each character is the god of a specific thing, so it's pretty easy to tell the Power of Thunder apart from the Power of Knives, or the Power of Laughter.

    Also, Nobilis has four stats that work in very different ways, so if the characters each max out a different stat, you've got one that's extremely good at being superhuman, one that's the master of his chosen element, one that's very godlike, and one that has a lot of cool magical stuff. (Speaking very loosely)

    So that gives you very distinct character possibilities without needing specific character classes.
