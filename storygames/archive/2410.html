Another haunting (yet lame) realization

Andy
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Story Games


TORG is 17 years old.

I'd played RPGs before, but TORG was the one I really cut my teeth on, right before playing regularly in a 2E AD&D game through the rest of high school. It was the one I played, ran, and bought all the books for, even the useless ones (Space Gods, Kanawa Heavy Weapons, etc)

Looking at it now, it feels like it came out only yesterday, but other things about it feel so old. The books themselves are worn and patchy. It's hard to believe that this thing came out less than 20 years ago, that it's a "classic" in this infant hobby. It just really struck me here as I was surfing on the net.

Wow. Anyway, y'all get to be a part of a mini gaming-related midlife crisis. :-)

Anyone else struck by the newness of the hobby, or their own mortality from the perspective of their games?

EDIT: Maybe later we'll have to Awesome-o-fy TORG. I smell Heroquest baking in the oven...

-Andy

    Torg and I have an interesting history. I've never played Torg. When it came out it never really grabbed me. Nobody I knew ever had it, so I never got exposed to it that way. So it kinda slipped under my radar. I think the big hair in the cover images didn't help. From what you say Andy, I think Shadowrun has the same place in my heart Torg does in yours.

    Last year I got interested in it. I heard a few people talking about it and it sounded pretty interesting. It's no secret how much fun my group and I have had with Savage Worlds and it seemed to me that a lot of the ideas in SW came directly from Torg.

    So, after a trip to Ebay, I picked up the core set (minus the infamous Torg Die) for about Â£8. I read it over a weekend, which is pretty odd for me (although this was before Final Fantasy 12 came out, so I had more time back then). On the Monday I dropped about Â£50 on a bunch of the books from another dude on Ebay. I ended up with pretty much the entire gameline, or at least, all the "World Of" setting books which is where all the crazy fun seemed to be.

    Some of that shit's awesome, like Arlysle or whatever the fantasy world is called is this planet sized hollow disk, and the sun slides up and down through a hole in the middle to make it day on one side while it's night on the other. I really like the mechanics for the differing realities and how the players can do stuff that normally wouldn't work in a given reality because it would work in their own. I think that it's a bit fiddly, but I like the principle.

    It's very interesting to me from a historical point of view, because there are a whole lot of things in there which are now pretty common in RPGs, which at the time were very new or groundbreaking. And then there are things like having to look up every die roll on a chart, which is pretty much anathema to me these days (actually, it always was now that I think about it). But then there's something like the Action Deck, which is completely and totally wonderful.

    Neat game, I'm pretty pissed off that I missed it in 1990.

Loved those old modules, especially Harlequin and later Deus. I loved the way they used to put together their adventures. They were often written as message board or BLOG posts including comments which would give you alternate perspectives and hooks to the main material. It was brilliant! We all loved those books so much, I think each player had their own copies of the main book and 4-6 of the core books.

    We've since then tried to play Shadowrun in different systems with mixed results. We tried PTA for a few sessions with pretty lackluster results (although we've played other games with PTA that were lots of fun). Closest game we've come to matching the Shadowrun feel was Burning Wheel. After playing Sorcerer (but not with Shadowrun), I suspect that may actually work really well (humanity as essence/empathy/money/fame, demons as tech, combat is lethal, you roll large fists full of dice, kickers can drive the group forward). One of my favorite things about Shadowrun was rolling 12+ d6s and smashing them on the table. Felt like bullets dropping as I empty my magazine on full auto.
