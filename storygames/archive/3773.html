\n A, somehow, followup from this thread, I'd like to adress the issue of prewritten part in the process of actual play. What I mainly said in the mentionned thread, was that people (well, at least some) bring something already written to the table (if they GM) for a game. It turned out it took around forty posts to explain what I was looking for, according to my specific needs, in that part I bring to the table (and explaining my own project that evolves around this idea).

So, here, I'm looking for a discussion, concerning this prewritten part, about your needs.
For instance, here's my mine, quoted from the above thread :\n
1. I want the PCs to have freedom, to be able to choose their own path, their own destiny.
2. In this context, I want the PCs to be able to change their "center of interest" in the course of play.
3. I want the PCs to be able to interfere with the plot ; change things.
4. I want the PCs to be in a "living world", to get the sense it doesn't live only around them.
5. I want the world to be coherent from a plotline point of view.
\nOf course, this is in the context of a campaign. So, based on those needs, I'm using a specific design that looks like this : multi plots, calendar based, relating all the events of the world, for a given time, written in a way that excludes the PCs (the prewritten part never mention them).

What I'm saying is that plot does matter ; just like systems. But not the plot itself : the way it is written, designed. Different needs bring different design. I also do believe that the prewritten part is kinda absent in indie design ; safe for exception. Dogs, for instance, uses a very powerful design for towns, that corresponds exactly to the needs of this game !! And I keep looking around and beside a couple of exceptions, I don't see much going on the subject ; not much replacing the old chapters based, linear, centered on the PCs that doesn't do what we need.

So, what are your needs ? What do you bring (maybe you don't bring anything, for a game of Capes, for instance) ? In what context (small game or campaign) ? Does the context changes what you bring ? Do you use a specific design ? Do you think it is a good idea to have a specific design for it ?

A note about this thread : I'm really looking out for a discussion, not a poll !!! Neither I am looking to talk about Avalanche (my project) !!!

    \nAnswers, not in the order of the questions presented:
    a) Context is everything. I play a lot of different games, and different games require different prepwork.
    b) My ideal prepwork is one that is exactingly presented in the game text -- Dogs town creation being a great example, but so is Roach, in that there isn't any prepwork and that's exactingly presented in the text. I am increasingly annoyed by games that don't give step-by-step guidelines on how to prepare what needs to be prepared. GMing is not a universal skill, no matter how much gamers may want it to be, and the assumption that prepwork can just be handwaved and still come out right is just ass.
    c) Generally speaking (which is a mistake), I prefer prepwork that gives me a network of people, places, and organizations and how they are problematically related. Again, Dogs, but even clearer, the situation engineering in my own game, FLFS. Player flags are very nearly a requirement for my appreciation of the generated prepwork.
    d) Now I contradict my 'generally speaking' from the prior bullet point. I prefer prepwork which works from within the game and uses everybody at the table, and does so in real time, face-to-face, and makes prepwork part of the at-the-table play experience. There are a couple tricks to learn (and those tricks are of differing utility in different games), but this is not actually hard, just different.

    \n John, your PDF sums up all the interesting bits of half a dozen RPGs I paid good money for on two pages. Consider it stolen. Sucker!

    I used to write novels and bring them to the table, then get upset when the players didn't fulfill their roles quite as well as they had in my head.

    I've now grown a brain (and it's still growing!), but what I do bring is still too detailed - fully statted NPCs with motivations, goals and backstories that are completely superfluous to the game. I would be very interested in a product that taught me how to quickly make functional NPCs for their game. Dogs kinda tries doing this, but I find pseudo-NPCs a bit too loose and I'm worried that players would think I'm defining the "perfect trait exactly when it's needed". I'd prefer a bit more structure, while still keeping the character fluid enough to fit into a wide variety of situations. Half-finished Spirit of the Century NPCs is more what I'm going for.

    I've also had some past success bringing "stuff" - handouts, maps, trinkets. The problem with handouts is that they're often a big time investment if they don't really matter in the long term. I will always keep my character's "Wanted" poster, for example, but the carefully prepared note that said "Come to the XYZ high school, we have shelter and food but need medical supplies" seemed a bit of a wasted effort on the GM's part. I love city maps (I usually print full-colour transit maps or satellite pictures for games set in modern day), mall floor plans and the likes. I find players and GM alike can easily fill in cool content and riff off existing content on the map. Location photographs and portraits are also useful for similar reasons. I will use "stuff" more in the future, I think.

    Edit to add: I think the "Next Week On..." mechanic in Primetime Adventures is one of the best tools ever conceived to structure and assist the GM in prepping for the next game. No, that's not how it works in PtA (where there is no prep, and everyone is supposed to ensure those Next Week items occur) but the same technique can be used in any game, really, to help the GM in figuring out what things the players would like to see more of.

    \n I'm not sure what someone should bring to the table, but here's what we're using in our current Principia game:

    John brought a cool map of Venice he printed out. We play with it in the center of the table.

    He also brought photos of some cool historical gun/swords.

    Brandon brought a bunch of photos he gleaned from Flickr or Venice streets, people, and cool Venetian masks. We do this a lot with our games now. When someone starts narrating anything that resonates with a particular photo, they or another player grabs a phot and puts it in front of that player. It's like having a library of extras and plot elements you can grab quickly.

    John made a list of the cheif families of Venice and wrote them on a cue card. When an NPC shows up, it's natural to glance at the card and give them a family name. This gives NPCs instant context.

    We also have cue cards for all the cheif characters, some historical, some a-historical. Some cue cards never get used, which is fine. This is super helpful with Principia because all keys are explicitly related to people, places, and things in the game. When I buy a new key, it's natural to glance around for a good NPC to relate it to.

    We also keep a relationship map tacked to the wall.

    I think John also has some notes about the NPCs he's put out into the world. If the relevant NPC becomes important, he can pull out the notes. I think these are pretty sketchy; not much more than what ability they're best at.

    \nI'm kind of amazed I never see this mentioned, but the Town Creation rules in Dogs in the Vineyard are a complete match-up to the Relationship Map in Sorcerer's Soul (a supplement for Sorcerer.)

    Both produce a set of relationships based on:
    1) Family
    2) Romantic Ties
    3) With acts of Moral Transgression Disrupting the Community and Characters

    With the explicit purpose of drawing Players into this web of relationships and problems via their characters:
    4) Without an intent to hide "what's really going on"
    5) While allowing the Players to follow up on which NPCs, incidents and situations they find most interesting
    6) And forcing the Players into having to make moral declarations of what is right, what is wrong and what do in the face of he Transgressions

    I happened to be re-reading both DitV and Sorcerer's Soul last December at the same time, and there was this, "Hey wait a minute!" moment as I flipped back and forth between the two chapters and they sort of came into focus as one image.

    Now, the DitV Town Creation chapter is much more step-by-step outliney -- which I think makes it very functional as part of a rules set. Meanwhile, the Relationship Map chapter in Sorcerer's Soul, with it's almost alchemical process of reading a novel and borrowing the map of relationships for an RPG game might seem cluttered and simply too much. However, the text of the Relationship Map chapter does a bang-up job of describing the purpose, function and actual play benefits of such a prep.

    But the key point is this: both games are arriving at the exact same destination. They're just taking different roads.

    The destination they reach is required for each of their games to run smoothly: forcing the players to make moral choices via the PCs.

    So, the Sorcerer Relationship Map (which is a specific thing, and not to be confused with the generic term "relationship map" -- which has a lot of different uses and construction in RPGs), is a vital form of pre-game prep going back a while.

    In the spirit of this thread and your interest in this topic, you might want to track down the book and read that chapter.

    \nWhat do I bring to the table? Well, all this is going to sound a lot more organized than I actually am, but let's see. I usually have some idea/premise which I think I can sell to the players in some form or shape, perhaps one in which I've already inquired for interest. Around that core and keeping in mind what it is that the players might possibly draw from that idea, I start working on the world. I draw maps (gets me thinking), define histories for locations (creates depth and purpose) and think about what might happen in the future (for player hooks, bangs, et all).
    \n
    \nThe next thing I do is I work on the NPCs based on that. If there is something that needs to be said about the setting, I make sure there is an NPC to do it. For example, if there is a conflict/issue, I want one NPC to exemplify that conflict/issue. Then I basically draw all these guys together in a relationship map, though I very rarely use an actual graphical implementation of the concept. I try to leave the NPCs in tense situations which are just waiting for some outside force to walk into it, spurring a conflict.
    \n
    \nAt this point, after weeks and far too many hours, I have a lot of stuff, much of which may not ever see the light of day in game. I accept that fact. The next step - as we go into our first session - is to turn these into GM flags, ie. I give the players the option to buy into them - to buy into the setting and the conflicts. If they like a particular thing, we'll work their character somehow into it. The ideas which the players don't necessarily take to either a) disappear, b) become part of the fabric of the world/setting, c) remain in the background until such a day comes when they can be utilised or d) all of the above. I also ask the players what kind of NPCs they'd like to see or they would need to bring out aspects of their characters and I work their wishes into everything. Sometimes it means converting existing ideas, sometimes it means all new ideas.
    \n
    \nAfter I've heard the players and seen what they are interested in, I tend to go back to what I've created and give it a thorough shakedown. Now I know how the PCs fit into everything, so I make the situations more centered on them, more dependent on them walking in, etc. This is just a final trimming, but I think it is a pretty crucial part of the process. A lot of the ideas that I find incompatible go to file to be used in later.
    \n
    \nOnwards from that, is play.

    The GM who would support these players has a difficult life. They must prepare enough detailed material that they can communicate a world with conviction and colour. This world must be full of interesting characters who are fun to meet. And it must have both interesting events going on without the PC's involvement, and opportunities for the PCs to have a real effect on it. Aspects of the world must be tailored to engage the PCs in interesting ways, without seeming too solipsistic or unbelievable. And at any moment the PCs can and will do something entirely unexpected that renders vast chunks of prep quite useless.

    I don't see a lot of support out there for such a GM. Some, but not much. Little advice on how to prepare economically, or algorithms/models that they can use. Some of the "indie" theory is good, but can be hampered by incompatible underlying assumptions. Towns in Dogs has the right sort of idea, IMO, but it's very specific.
