\n Somehow I ended up coming home with a copy of Mike Pohjola's game T\xc3\xa4hti after Gen Con. If it is yours - sorry! I am not giving it back.

It's written in Finnish. It's a full color, glossy magazine format thing, all bright colors in a luscious, saturated tropical palette that radiates fun. It's obviously about riot grrls who are also mutants. Beyond that, I could parse nothing. Naturally, the whole exercise is intensely exciting.

I asked Olle Jonsson (only one country and one entire language family away) WTF was up with T\xc3\xa4hti, and he finagled a translation of some explanatory text/commentary out of his friend Pia Niemi:

"In the RPG T\xc3\xa4hti, the main characters are the members of famous mutant girl band Evolution. It's set in a dystopian Finland, 2017, where the corporations and the leftist-green government vie for power. The game revolves around the gigs of the girl-band, their dating and recording business. Pohjola's tried to make a fun game, but the dystopian setting includes violation of human rights which affords the player to close in on more society-related themes, via the girl-band life.
Society-related themes include media power, the pressures of celebrity, and religions. Also, there is a sect called "nokia mission", and someone in the band dates a sectarian. Pohjola sees himself as a roleplaying artist. He feels that as an artist one has a duty to stand up for values that are not the prevailing ones in a society.
He has two pieces of advice for gamemakers: First and foremost: be political and make games that touch a nerve. Two: make funnier games."

(Original text here, on a page that also includes some chick making out with Admiral Akbar. You have been warned.)

So! I like Mike Pohjola's advice. I want to play T\xc3\xa4hti and see what happens.

    \n\nMike Pohjola \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nAugust 2007 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Hello!
    \n
    \nSome short answers from the author/designer...
    \n
    \n1. I've asked the publisher about shipping abroads. I'll get back to you on that...
    \n
    \n2. The reaction among non-roleplayers, casual roleplayers, and friends of roleplayers has been immensely positive. Many magazines and newspapers have written about T\xc3\xa4hti, or are going to. There's probably some tv publicity still coming up as well.
    \n
    \n3. The main "mechanic" is fortune cookies. You open one, eat it, read the fortune, interpret it in the group as appropriate to the situation. There is no ability scores or success rolls or anything like that in the game, since the focus is elsewhere. Also lacking are Forge-style drama mechanics. The structure is mainly provided by the game master, the direction and arcs by players roleplaying their characters.
    \n
    \n4. There are some additional mechanics for specific situations, these being character creation (teen mag quiz style) and exam week (solo roleplaying on paper).
    \n
    \n5. I don't think the world's terribly dystopian, but I'm told it is. The government is just the way I'd like the Finnish government to be... except for the fact that it's largely powerless against corporations, gangs and religious movements.
    \n
    \n6. Evolution is a concept band with Maoist mutants, and produced by Northern Europe's biggest media corporation. The band is huge among Maoist youth who, of course, oppose big media corporations. So there's one of the core conflicts right there.
    \n
    \n7. No one has to date a sectarian.
    \n
    \n8. It's pretty cool.

    \n\nEero_Tuovinen \n\n\n
    \n
    \n\nFebruary 2012 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n Full color POD is traditionally pretty expensive. Lulu, for example, would take >12 \u20ac per copy for something approaching the parameters of the Finnish edition of T\xe4hti. No advance costs, though - the real POD printer takes their cut as part of the sales process, so you only pay for what you sell and nothing more.

    My own philosophy with these sorts of publishing projects is that instead of trying for POD I make a short print run. It's a rare thing that you can't sell a few hundred copies of, and the print costs will be significantly less. There's a bit more work, but not much, and it's easier to get a price point where you can afford to sell the product to eg. retailers. Easier to fund convention gigs, too, if you're making say 75% off the cover price as profit instead of the 50% or less that often ends up the reality in POD publishing.

    That said, there are plenty of small print rpg publishers who make the opposite decision. POD doesn't require even a small capital commitment in advance, and it goes well as a supplementary publishing form for somebody who's publishing primarily in digital format. There's quite an undergrowth of rpg publishers nowadays who don't care to keep the options open regarding retail sales.
