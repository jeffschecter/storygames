A discussion on Game Chef 2007

Andy
January 2007 edited January 2007 in Play Advice
Another year, another contest.

This year I'm thinking of starting way early, perhaps end of February/beginning of March or so. A couple people had to pull out last year because of early spring vacations and the like, and honestly there's not much going down in terms of contests for the beginning part of the year anyway.

The contest is most likely going to return to a simple "Genre" "Words: choose 3 of 4" (or 3 of 5)" format. Most likely there will be two 'categories' of words, because unlike early Game Chef contests where there were only 12-20 participants, recently there have been 40-60 participants, and even more people have signed up on the info list than last year. So to prevent 60 games about the same topic, I figure a choice of categories or words would help break it up a little.

There will most likely not be rules about rules constraints (must not use dice, etc), but most likely there will be something like this put in as an entirely optional challenge.

There will only be one entry allowed per particpant. The contest can no longer support 2+ entries per person. People are by all means welcome to create many, but only submit one.

I'm still thinking of some ways to get peer reviewing easier to manage.

Does anyone have any questions, feedback, etc? I won't be entertaining prospective genre or word items ("Let's do Science Fiction!" etc) but I'm open to any other ideas.

-Andy

    Oh, and here's my opinion on design constraints, in the sense of which mechanics must be used or structure/functions the game must fulfill: I don't like 'em. At least, not in the way they have been implimented. I enjoy the Game Chef/24 Hour competitions as spurs to creativity, but the design constraints seem to focus attention on things that aren't important. The decision to use, say, a dice pool, or a time limit, should rise out of the focus of your game, not the other way around. Design constraints on these things tends to encourage a Frankenstein approach to game design, creating monster games with lots of novelty for the sake of novelty, but very little passion or attractiveness.

    One exception might be if there were a list of common themes that occur in other RPGs, like "physical violence", "leveling up", "flaws/disadvantages", or "niche portection", and the design constraint were "pick one item from this list to avoid." This might be interesting. Of course, a lot of people here are already regularly making games that avoid the four I just mentioned, so maybe the list should be something more like "conflict escalation", "stakes setting", "personality mechanics", or "director stance"...

  • Kuma
    January 2007
    I'll offer a different voting scheme that I was going to use for the Mix-O-Tronic Challenge that removes the whole individual score range problem: run-off ranking.
    Each judge (participant) is given four games to review and score.

    Each game is to be judged on several criteria of descending value. The tentative criteria and their positions are:

    * Originality.
    * Completeness.
    * Clarity of rules.
    * RPG-ness.
    * Fun/Cool/Wow factor.
    * &c.

    Each of the above criteria is ranked 1-4 by the judge, for the games that a given judge has been assigned.

    Each of these raw rank scores is then weighted according to the criterias importance. These weighted ranks are tallied across all four (4) of the judges for a final score in each criteria, then summed together for a Grand Total on each game.

    Games are first compared by Grand Total. If a single winner emerges, that game and designer are awarded the Grand Prize, with subsequent games being awarded First, Second and Third Place.

    Should a tie arise, the games tied for a position are compared criteria-by-criteria, beginning with the first. The game with the highest raw score in the first criteria is awarded higher place. If these are identical as well, the games are compared on subsequent criteria until a clear winner is declared.

    In the unlikely event that all of the raw criteria scores for two or more games match, the placement of the winners will be determined by the roll of a twenty-sided (d20) die.
    The up-sides:

    * Using a run-off system removes the problem of differing score ranges,
    * Weighting the factors allows you to prioritize what designers are ultimately rewarded for.

    You could possibly have two rounds of voting: one with the distributed reviews, and then take the top games and do a straight poll so that the final winner is scrutinized by everyone.

I saw a lot of people who were (A) gung ho to be part of the competition before the criteria were posted only to find out in the first few days that they had nothing that inspired them enough to pursue. I also saw a lot of people like me, who (B) came around to see what the ingredients were with no real intention of doing the contest but ended up hit with something they just had to produce.

      Now, if you pre-register cells what you might find is that a lot of (A) folks pre-register with the intent of contributing and end up disappearing, which might leave the committed one or two or half dozen in their cell with less feedback and interaction. Also, you might find that a lot of (B) folks never register at all because they don't want to commit before seeing what they're in for and drop out.

      If you opened registration to cells 24 or 48 hours (or, some other variable) after the ingredients are posted, you might find that you get more B's to register and end up with more consistent A participation. Just a thought.

      Posted By: Matt WilsonI'm confused about the groups thing. How do they work? Are people now designing games in groups?
      Nope. Those are simply feedback groups. Everyone makes games by themselves. Then they walk around giving feedback to other people...

      ...only here's the thing...

      ...they START by giving feedback to the people Within Their Group first. That's their "feedback responsibility". They can then move around to other groups once they've accounted for their own.

      This is basically to combat that "New Guy Gets Zero Fucking Feedback" Effect that we see every now and then. There's always someone with a few good ideas who needs/wants feedback, but doesn't get it. They simply fall through the cracks. Meanwhile, people are tripping all over each other to give feedback to this hot new idea they just saw from That Guy Over There.

      This is basically a way to divide up the community into smaller pieces, so that they can give better feedback to people within their regions, then move on to the whole community. So that that one or two dudes don't fall through the cracks (or at least, not as easily).

      -Andy
