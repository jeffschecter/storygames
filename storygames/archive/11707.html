\n
\n
\n
\n\nAndy \n\n\n

\n

\n
\n A cool topic came up on the LJ RPG community:

http://community.livejournal.com/roleplayers/1533696.html

What do you think? Here's the "mantra":

"Make the character you want to play and f**k game balance," is what I always say. Game balance comes from the Narrator giving everyone equal time, making everyone important, giving spotlight when players need and/or deserve it. Everything else is just math exercises." - John Wick

John's mainly talking about games with balanced point-based style character generation (so yeah, not so applicable for "In a Wicked Age" or whatever) it seems. And yeah, I find myself nodding in agreement.

Sure, there's the tales of abuse: I remember Steve D from RPGNet talking once about a player saying "For my character concept I *need* these boosted stats and abilities for my child-like ancient vampire", and after getting them proceeded to roll all over the other players and fucked up the campaign. So let's say you have that system of trust underneath.

Anyway, I agree for the most part. A player says that they want to be able to do X well, X sounds cool to everyone, but the player can't take X unless they turn their character into a one-dimensional character that can ONLY do X (because of some limitation in the long-range-view of the point-buy system mechanic), then yeah it's up to the Players to think of things they want to do that break the rules, the GM to hand them out, and then for everyone to make sure all the players get even face-time in play.

-Andy

    \n If a large fraction of the game is going to play out using a purely mechanical subsystem, then that fraction of the game CANNOT be managed by the GM's spotlighting skills -- it's by definition managed mechanically. And so that is where the "unbalanced" system risks disaster -- Judd's example of a 4e game is bang on. Outside of combat, where the GM is (largely without engaging the rules at all, though as long as things are partitioned well, she can use the rules and still succeed) handling scenes and making sure everyone gets to shine, confronting objectives commensurate with their skills. But if you get the battlemat out and run an encounter, the apprentice gets to do nothing but run or die for an hour (a real hour). And a good many games are predicated on a significant fraction of table time being purely mechanical in exactly that fashion.

    \n To get jargontastic, game balance is about making sure every player has the same opportunity for resources and positioning so that a player doesn't feel like her character is being deprotagonized.

    1. You can ensure that balance through game mechanics that carefully distribute mechanical resources.
    2. You can ensure that balance through game mechanics that carefully distribute positioning ability.
    3. You can ensure that balance through vigilant play that makes sure every player gets the same opportunity to shine.

    Game designers have control over #1 and #2 (system). Players have control over #3 (social contract). #3 is the most powerful, but it's also hard to retrofit when #1 and #2 are in your fucking way all the time. Also, if #1 and #2 don't matter, then system doesn't matter, and you might as well just freeform it and not use any set of RPG rules.

    \n Game balance has a definition already, folks. It has nothing to do with spotlight or navatarism*. Jesus christ, put down the Kool-Aid.

    Balancing a game ensures that the choices in the game are meaningful and no one choice is obvious. You can purposely create unbalanced elements, but that's something else entirely. I'm not talking about player skill/preferences, handicapping or difficulty levels. To wit:

    Imagine if Starcraft was designed so that the Protoss were stronger than the Zerg. The game wouldn't be properly balanced. I'm given a choice and there is a right answer and a wrong answer on which faction to play (and if you choose the Zerg you'll probably lose).

    In D&D, the choice between a long sword and a dagger is obvious. They are unbalanced at first glance. But factor in size restrictions, weight and special abilities (like those of a rogue pertaining to a dagger) and you see why the dagger is in the game.

    In chess, both players have the same pieces with the same capabilities and the same board layout. If it's timed, they start with the same amount of time on the clock. The only factor is in who gets to move first, which is done via a random method. Once the game starts, the players make decisions based on that initial setup. If I started with an extra knight, then the game balance would be off.

    Some games, like LARPs, are incredibly hard to balance because player skill is involved. Vampire LARP, for example, involves player personality as a deciding factory in gameplay. I can be matched in a contest against a meek and shy player, thus giving me an advantage. Or I can be put up against the Storyteller's girlfriend, at which I'm at a considerable disadvantage. Doubly so if I want to try and get her into bed.

    See?

    - J

    *I just made that term ("Navatarism") up.

    \n Adam,

    Not really. It doesn't have anything to do with feelings. It has to do with forcing optimal vs. suboptimal decisions. The bastard sword is more or less the best weapon in D&D (versatility, damage, proficiency bonus, etc.). What makes it a non-obvious as a choice of weapon is that it will eat up a feat slot for you to learn to use it. Now it's the best weapon but there's a price for using it.

    Compare this to the old old old version D&D where you had less than a 40% chance to hit with A Weapon (two-handed weapons did same damage as one-handed, maces did as much damage as swords - 1d6). But if you used flaming oil, your damage went up to 1d8, the accuracy was 50% and it was a ranged attack. We as players figured this out and threw away our melee weapons, opting for flaming oil instead. Yeah, it was expended upon use but it was still waaaay better than using a sword.

    What you do with your character is your business. Wick complains about his half-orc bard concept that always gets quashed because the half-orc gets a low CHA bonus but John plays that character because he wants to. His character is going to suck more than say, a half-elf bard. This will make Johnn sad (?) because he made a sub-optimal choice. Part of the skill/mastery of D&D is learning how to play the game well, which is something missing in many roleplaying games. "Playing well" in D&D is a measurable metric. You choose complementary feats and powers and a race/class combination that takes the best advantage of these choices. There isn't an obvious build, per se, but there are some that are better than others. Making a bad character isn't a measure of an unbalanced game. It's ok to let players play the game poorly. However some games (Exalted) have similar traits that simply MUST be taken if you're to play the game with any reasonable expectation of surviving or being successful (Ox Blood Heart or something like that? Basically enables you to survive attacks). That's not good game design.

    \n Games can be balanced in different ways though, (albeit not necessarily well). AD&D had two (ostensible) balancing factors - top heavy vs bottom heavy, and advancement rate. You could have a character that's more powerful than others to start, but it doesn't get much later and ends up weaker. Or a character that's more powerful to start but advances slower as a result. I'm not hugely keen on it, but it certainly works in some ways. It creates a game where if you go across 15 or so levels with everyone, each character type has a time to shine. Fighters, Priests and Thieves get the spotlight early on. Druids and Wizards get pretty wicked advancement from levels 7-11, where they accelerate past everything else, and then slow back down.

    It is a balancing factor, but it only works out in certain ways, and if you play long enough to realise it. If you're starting at a certain level, or at first level, and only playing a short adventure, you might as well write some classes off. A Druid gets really interesting if you can hit 7th level, and that's also when the Wizard gets some powerful spells. If you're only playing levels 1-5 there's no reason to play either.

    \n That seems to mostly be a weakness for people who game a lot with strangers (and therefore rarely know what to expect) or for people who only really like one specific thing and therefore see no use in versatility in its own right.

    I mean, in our group -- all friends who have been playing together for a while and whose tastes are not particularly rigid -- a game that we can make work for us in whatever way we need it to work is a positive boon. The more versatile a game is, the more we can leverage our understanding of each other and our current goals to make that game ideally suited for us. Rather than cross our fingers and hope that some game designer we have never met has somehow struck upon the magic formula to give the six of us a great gaming experience (which seems to be an unusually cruel thing to expect from whoever wrote the game), we can put our heads together and come up with our own magic formula.

    Which isn't to say that there's not value in tightly-written, single-minded, highly-focused games; there very obviously is. I'm just glad those aren't the only games out there, because a versatile system is awesome as hell.

    \n No, because pie does not work like that. The way you would cook a pecan pie is entirely wrong for a chocolate pie, and equally wrong for kiwi.

    The point being, pie is too important to use as a strained metaphor for game systems. Seriously, you want to mix metaphors with the dessert cart, go pick on cakes or cookies or ice cream or creme brulee or some bullshit like that, because pie is too good for this.


    If you have to think about pie just to stay interested in a discussion like this, though, I can't say I blame you. In which case, here's a slightly better analogy:

    You may think "Oh, key lime pie, I fucking LOVE key lime pie," and then when you take a big bite of it you find out it was not the particular kind of key lime pie you love. Because, duh, there are lots of different recipes for key lime pie, and if you never asked which recipe was used for THIS one, you were courting disappointment to begin with. You didn't get the pie you wanted, but at least now you know to ask for the right kind of key lime pie.

    You might also think "Damn, I wish I could get the exact pie I want, but no one serves it here." In which case you either learn to love the pie you can get, or you move to a more cosmopolitan pie community, or....

    You might get together with your friends and say, "HEY EVERYBODY, LET'S MAKE US SOME MOTHERFUCKING PIES IN HERE!", then spend some time figuring out just what kind of pie you're in the mood to eat, and then tweak the recipe so as to get the best pie you can from the ingredients you have on hand. If you had your heart set on a store-bought pie that you just had to open the box and eat, this won't work for you...but if you think that spending time in the kitchen with your buddies is good fun (plus, dude, there's going to be PIE when you're done), this'll work for you. And having lots of recipes and a willingness to experiment certainly helps.


    When it comes to games, learning to ask the right questions so as to avoid unpleasant and disappointing surprises is an ongoing process. You can't always get a game that's perfect for you and your current mood right out of a box (though if you can, you fucking win forever and ever, because seriously, that's awesome for you); by and large, though, you have to deal with whatever options actually exist in your neighborhood. And since that's the case, good communication + a versatile system + some fun "work" can get you pretty close to perfect.

    \n Well, I would submit that in gaming, there's always an X factor that can't be written down. For god's sake, just look at all the people who love PrimeTime Adventures. I've read that book, it's great. I do not doubt the many people who have told stories about how much fun they had playing PTA, and I cannot possibly claim in good faith that it is a poorly designed game.

    But it is absolutely impossible for me and my friends to play PTA and have it not be a buzzkilling non-fun catastrophe. We know what we're doing (at least, we do in general, and we certainly worked our asses off trying to get better at it), we have the best of intentions, but whatever it takes to play PTA and have it be entertaining and satisfying and a great time, we simply do not have it. The sort of fun it provides is not to our taste, I guess; whatever combination of personalities and preferences you need to sit down and have such a good time that you cannot wait for the next episode, we cannot provide, and there's fuck-all that the rulebook (as great as it is) can do to provide that to us.

    And that's just how gaming is, really, and (I think) it's part of what makes it such a great hobby; because what happens at the table is unique to us. It is ours, and ours alone. We own our successes (and our failures) in a very personal way, and I like that.
