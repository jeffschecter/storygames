The player who does this better totally wins the game (even when rules doesn't state a clear winning in Fiasco)

    \n I have one group right now who were mostly newbies about 6 months ago, and it took 4 or 5 sessions for some of them to begin identifying strongly with their characters. Rather than putting this guy on the spot by asking questions you don't ask anyone else, make it a regular part of your GMing that you ask leading questions of everyone, all the time. Don't worry if it takes him a while.

    Because... Meanwhile you are building up NPC character relationships - which takes a little time - and some of them will be with him. Some of them will lead to hard choices or emotional dilemmas, sacrifices, tests of loyalty, or emotional responses to his sociopathic actions. Use these NPCs to make him see the effects of his actions on realistic people, not just on targets fit for killing. Make what Vincent calls "PC-NPC-PC Triangles" (that's where an NPC treats two PC very differently and they have to come to some consensus on how to deal with that). Or put him in a situation where if he does his typical thing, somebody he cares about (or has use for) will get hurt, upset, or lose faith in him.

    In a similar way, encourage your other players to speak to him in character. Set up some scenes for this to happen. "Ok, I know Rayche and Blademan have different ideas on how to get the maguffin. While everybody else goes out to get supplies, you two are left alone in the camp to discuss it. Let's see that scene."

    Finally, let him "cast" his role. Pick an actor or character that he emulates, and then every time it's his turn, have him literally think of that actor in the movie scene: picture his look, picture his stance, and then deliver a line of dialog. Even players who don't bond with their characters strongly can imagine little cool chunks of roleplay, like a single shot or one scene at a time.

    \n@cupbearer11 - Regarding leading questions: Yes, and address him in character: "Blade, how do you feel about that/them/her/him?" or "Blade, are you concerned about what might happen to that/them/her/him?" is a good start.

    But to take a page from Apocalypse World, you can make these questions even more leading by giving players more creative control: "Blade, there's a guy outside who says he knows you from the war. Got a scar across his face and wears a strange blue tunic. He says you'll remember him. What's the story there?" or "Blade, while you're knocked out/asleep/drugged you have a vision from your forgotten childhood. When you wake up you realize something about yourself you have kept buried for years. What was the dream, and what did you realize?"

    \n Different players have different expectations of the game session. You can't make someone role-play. If that's simply not fun for this particular gamer, then it's not fun. Nothing you can do in game or out is going to flip a switch in their head to get them suddenly doing voices or making decisions in character. Just talk to them. Tell them what you want from the players in the game you're running, if they're not on board with those expectations, invite them to quit the group and find someone else who matches your expectations. Otherwise you're going to waste a lot of everyone's time trying to coax, bribe, and cajole this person into enjoying playing in a fashion that they clearly do not want to engage in.

    \n It sounds cheesy, but have them take that Robin Laws Game Style Quiz. You take it as well. Chances are he's way down the Tactician or Power Gamer lines while you're farther along the Method Actor or Storyteller. There's nothing wrong with any of that, per se, but if your expectations are for less Tact/Power Gamer and more for MA/Storyteller than what this player is, then you're not going to solve the issue by trying to win him over.

    I've dealt with similar issues with players at my tables before, both as a GM and fellow player. Sure, this player might be brought around, but most likely not. I've run experiments with simple in-game stuff to coax more role-playing out of my largely Tact/PG groups before, and they've fallen flat. Certain players expect certain things out of certain games. Like rewarding in-game details with bonuses, something like Exalted where a better description would get a minor bonus. After a few rounds of attempts, someone did the math and told me that the extra time spent describing the action would be better spent just pushing through as over the long term more attacks per hour was better than a 5% bonus chance to-hit. No, I'm not kidding. Similar with action points, fate points, meta-currency style incentives. That one seemed to stick, but the one player who it was implemented for simply stock-piled the things and had to be reminded to use them.

    You're free to do what you think is best for your table, of course, but if their play style is enough of an issue for you to post about it here, you should definitely talk to the player about it. And for what it's worth, I don't think it's fair to that player that you would try to push them toward the style of play you want from them. Unless this gamer is quite new to the hobby, chances are they already know what they like and don't like. If it's a huge problem, they don't fit with your table. If it's not a huge problem, you probably wouldn't have posted about it.

    \n What Liblarva says, basically. It sounds like this guy is playing a game of D&D that's perfectly in line with a playstyle that the game supports. He's doing things you don't like--but he's not doing anything wrong.

    The solution is to talk to your players. Not just the new guy, but all of them. And don't talk as the voice of authority--When your talking about play-styles and expectations, the Dungeon Master isn't in charge--they're just another player.

    If you really believe there's a role-player inside this guy that just needs to be coaxed to the surface, you're not going to do it in D&D. Take the suggestion that you run a game like Fiasco more seriously. Not as long term thing, but as a short break from your D&D game. You're the Dungeon Master, yeah? Being the GM doesn't mean the same thing in all games, but in D&D, it means you're in charge. So if you say "alright, next week we're going to take a break from the halls of the Orc king to play this game called Fiasco instead", you're probably going to get a response of "alright, sure". If you don't get that response, maybe question why you're GMing for that group at all.

    But don't expect that to necessarily fix the problem. Certain gamers see RPGs as an opportunity to stab Kobolds for their XP and treasure and see role-playing as window dressing. Some of those gamers have that attitude because it's the attitude they've been trained to employ and they're unfamiliar with other play-styles. But some of those gamers just like killing monsters and getting treasure and just don't care about XP. Maybe that's not someone who belongs in your game--but it's not because they're broken and need fixing, it's because you just want different things out of the same game, and that's okay.

    \n What is most likely here is that he has a totally different play style than you. It's important for you to understand that his play style is completely valid. I sense a bit of judgment in "How do i get him to roleplay better?" He's not role-playing at all, right?

    The easiest way to get people to do things is to ask them to do it. You say you've done that. To me, that's like me saying, "Hey, if you're gonna play Monopoly with us, you have to organize your money so your turn doesn't take too long," and the dude won't stack his money.

    Barring that, reward the behavior you want. D&D 2nd Edition doesn't really do that, if I remember correctly. I haven't played 2e since 1990. There might be XP rewards for role-playing, but they're largely overshadowed by XP rewards for killing things. Stop giving people XP for killing things--or reduce it significantly--and increase the XP for role-playing. D&D 5e / Next has very simple non-XP rewards for role-playing in the form of "BIFTs" (bonds, ideals, flaws, and traits). You role-play your BIFTs, you gain Inspiration (you can only have it once; spend it, lose it, gain it again). You spend Inspiration, you gain Advantage on a die roll. Solid mechanical reward for role-playing. Even without a wholesale switch to 5e, you could implement a simple Inspiration mechanic with a solid +4 to hit rolls, saves, and whatever else for Advantage.

    Punish behavior you don't want. I mean negative reinforcement in the psychological sense. I'd do it through the fiction, as others have suggested. If he is taking actions that fly in the face of what his character would do if he had a non-robotic personality, have NPCs point that out and inflict real consequences for that. The consequences can effect the entire party, too, which puts peer pressure on him. It's manipulative and not fun, probably, but this is someone whom you've asked to change their behavior and he refuses, right?

    I'm getting a whiff of Geek Social Fallacies here, too. You don't have to include this guy in your games if he doesn't play the way you play. You don't have to be all right with him playing the way he does. If he wants to play in this particular game badly enough, then he can conform to group social norms.

    Really, by now, you should have talked to him to find out /why/ he's not role-playing. Maybe he just doesn't feel comfortable role-playing. Maybe he doesn't know how. Maybe he isn't interested. Maybe he finds it distracts him from what he feels is important about the game (winning challenges, perhaps). Really, I don't know how you can make any choices without knowing for sure. Talk to him about it.

    Also consider the possibility that your game-mastering isn't inspiring him to role-play. As you said, "My other bud just sits there and retroactively justifies every decision." Is that two people who aren't really engaging with their characters? When the game is largely a set of combats linked together with thin thread, don't expect much more than the basic kind of role-playing. I know you're not saying you want Oscar-winning performances here, just basic character-driven decision-making. What are you doing as GM to evoke this from players.

    A lot of character-driven stuff is put into motion at character creation. Was the character as created more than a collection of numbers and race/class choices? Did players show up with characters pre-made? Did they create them at the table together, but without any discussion about character concept between players? Or did they interact during character creation to create social connections among party members? What did you do as GM during this time to make sure they understood that character role-play was important?

    What kind of hooks are you putting into play that require character role-playing solutions rather than "I hit it with my axe" solutions? Are there any social encounters that can't really be solved with combat? Have them stumble into a dragon's lair. If they fight, they will die. Make that really, really obvious up front. This is a role-playing encounter, not a combat encounter. And don't make the stakes "win or die." Make them, say, "win or lose all your gold" or something palatable. If they force a combat encounter, kill all of the characters and make new ones. Have them find an enemy that has crucial information they need. The enemy has 1 hp. If they even torture the dude in typical sociopathic tendencies, at least that's role-playing? (But I'd make it clear that any kind of torture will kill him, and that I'll change their alignments to Evil if they engage in torture, and thus no healing at the temple anymore.)
