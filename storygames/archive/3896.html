\n Over in the underappreciated games thread, Brennan said a couple things:\n
Posted By: Brennan Taylor
carry. a game about war.This game is talked about, a little, but I think it is truly brilliant, and not enough people know about it or have tried it. I think games with serious subject matter like this can be a hard sell, but they are really, really worthwhile.

Hero's Banner. This is a good game, and again, it's had some indie scene buzz, but that isn't reflected in its sales. The structure, with its tightly constrained story arc and short character lifetime, might put some folks off, but this is another game that hits its theme with a sledgehammer.
\nNow, rather than clutter up that thread, i decided to start another one, because i want to know more about these games. Both of them are games i passed on, but now i'm wondering if i was mistaken. Specifically, war movies do nothing for me. But anti-war movies can be great. In general, war as a setting doesn't really interest me, but i can "put up with" it, if it's in service of a message other than glorification of war, or the goodness of necessary war. Heck, Full Metal Jacket is one of my favorite movies.

[No, i'm not claiming my positions are inherently right, or perfectly consistent; just that either of those comes up, and it hits a hot button. I believe that war is sometimes necessary; i just don't think that necessity makes of something a virtue--it can be both necessary, and wrong/evil/sinful/pick-your-term. IMHO. I really don't want to debate this position in this thread--this isn't the place for it, for starters. I'm just letting you know where i'm coming from.]

So, it probably won't surprise you that i never even looked at Carry. I see "game about war", and my eyes glaze over. Why do i want this game, anyway, if i generally find war boring as a topic/setting? And, to answer the obvious, i *do* like the idea of addressing serious topics in RPGs, as well as discussions.

On to Hero's Banner. This one, again, i don't know much about. It looked interesting to me at a quick flip-through, so i asked the author to 'splain it a bit. However, he managed to explain it in such a way that, the more he said, the less interested i became--i don't think he could've done a more effective job of un-selling me on the game. Unfortunately, i don't remember what he said, now. So pretty much the extent of my knowledge is the above-quoted post, and what Fred Hicks said on the Game Master Show podcast. And, based on both of those (people whose opinions i've found often are close to mine), i'm beginning to suspect i dismissed it too quickly.

    \n My thoughts on this:

    I think you could play a game of carry that glorified and reveled in war if you tried really, really hard. But it's not designed for that, and the difference between your intent and how the game works would begin to show pretty quickly. Carry is a game about how war destroys people. It's like watching Come and See, which is ostensibly a war movie, but one no sane person could walk away from thinking war was cool or glorious or anything but endless horror.

    You could play a game of Heroes Banner in which there was no blood spilled. It wouldn't be that hard, if that was your expressed intention. In its full, multi-session form, it is about things like dynastic ambitions and the way fathers indelibly mold sons and the relentless forces of history. It tends toward wars and violence because its firmly set in a violent age, and is about passionate, driven, tragic people.

    \n Hero's Banner is fantastic. It let me run the trans-generational low-fantasy epic I've always wanted to. It's a game, at it's heart, about fathers and sons... families and growing up in your parents' shadow. If you're on the same page as me you're thinking "massive, unplumbable depths of intensity" and that's about right.

    The cool mechanical thing, short-term, about carry is that you're like "man, it's awesome to be an asshole" and then you're like "no, wait, I don't want to be an asshole anymore" but those big asshole dice just keep tempting you. The coo thing about carry, long-term, is that you can actually fiddle around your rewards and escape your own personality to become a 'better' or at least different person.

    By "short term" for carry I mean "5-10 seconds of play," and by "long term" I mean "maybe an hour of play." The whole game plays in 2-4 hours, unless you're Ron.

    Posted By: woodelfI perhaps wasn't clear. It hadn't occurred to me, from my general impressions of Hero's Banner, that the war thing was relevant. I didn't figure Hero's Banner was any more violent than, well, just about every other RPG. It just got tossed in the same post rather than clutter things up with two threads.
    \nIt's not really relevant. I mean, it can be, it's really up to the characters that get made. How violent it is, is also up to the players and GM, entirely. Generally, there should be some conflict with rival nations and their nobles, but it could be entirely intrigue and political squabbles.

    The game is about making choices. Your character stats are entirely defined by three life paths they can make, centered on a heroic ideal, family duty, and love. Any of those, the first two in particular, can involve violence, but don't have to. Furthermore, resolving conflict is pretty open ended - what actions are taken are left to either the player or GM to decide. It just has to fit into a narrative. Your stats don't dictate whether you are good with a sword or not, simply how much emotion and energy you have invested in each of the three paths at a given time.

    Even if a war is underway, how many soldiers kill how many other soldiers isn't important to the story. It's about what the characters do and the decisions they make.

    \n Hero's Banner is secretly the Conan game I've always wanted.

    Consider: Instead of the multi-generational thing happening, where you generate a new character that's influenced by the previous one, you make a major shift in the life of the epic legend, and list what vestiges of the old life influence the new.

    In this way, you have Conan the Barbarian, Conan the Pirate, Conan the Thief, etc etc... culminating in Conan the King. And when you're done, you have made your own honest-to-God epic tale of one figure of legend, in the most accurate way possible in gaming today, I think.

    Woot.

    \n Hero's Banner is a very good game, though the author (sorry Tim!) often falls into the trap of assuming that the reader lives in his head when it comes to explaining certain aspects of the game. That is, there are spots where how things work aren't all that clear and, as a result, these things could be potentially huge stumbling blocks for somebody who is relatively new to either story games and/or the hobby of roleplaying in general.

    If there is ever a revision, I would love to see an accomplished technical writer/editor brought on to clarify things such as how you decide which trait is the most appropriate for a given roll (the text makes it seem as though the choice should be obvious, though in many circumstance, it's anything but). In many ways, I think it's like the earlier edition of Shock in regard to the presentation and clarity of rules.
