    \n I love supporting friends when I can, so I buy their games less discriminately.
    \n
    \nBut in most cases, before I buy I do the following...
    \n
    \n1. Look and read the front and back cover.
    \n2. Carefully look at the Character Sheet.
    \n3. Flip through the book, looking at art, tables, and lists.
    \n4. Look for a list of playtesters.
    \n5. Ask a friend who knows me and has played if they think I will like it OR read multiple online reviews OR play a demo.
    \n6. Ask my friends if they will play it.
    \n
    \nHaving playtested many games myself, I know even the most brilliant ideas sometimes don't work in actual play.
    \n
    \nThat said, list of playtesters isn't a guarantee of a proper playtest. It doesn't mean AP data was used to revise the game. Take D&D; 4E, the list of playtesters is extensive. But it became obvious that some elements were not extensively playtested after it was released.
    \n
    \nI prefer games that aren't playtested to be labeled as betas. Otherwise it is easy to feel cheated.

    \n Well, this one's tricky.

    I care about playtest because it is perhaps the only way to making a truly solid game when it comes to the craft. However, I do not care about it when I buy games - I buy games for their ideas, it's no big deal for me if the execution is a bit rough. I'm easy to please.

    So I might say that I care about playtest in that I do it myself, but I don't mind how others want to make their games. I might feel pity or amusement when somebody's clearly a bit clueless about how his rough product looks like to an outsider, but I also feel sympathy, realizing that people work in different conditions and perhaps there are entirely understandable reasons for why the playtesting was omitted here. Perhaps if I had a burning need to buy more games and a limited amount of money then I could muster some righteous indignitation at being ripped off, but that's just not the way I roll; I get all the games I'm interested in, and it's no big deal if some game needs a bit of fine-tuning to make it run like it's supposed to.

    \n Last five game purchases:

    1. How We Came to Live Here: Finally bought this 2 days ago. I know Brennan playtested the hell out of this game, partially because I did layout for the playtest draft. Knowing that it was heavily revised and polished definitely game me confidence in my purchase.

    2. Lone Wolf Multi-Player: Don't know how extensively this was playtested. Probably not enough, but oh well. Bought it anyway. No regrets but it certainly could be more polished and helpful.

    3. Apocalypse World MC Handbook Playtest Draft: Lots of people had playtested this game before I bought it, so I knew it was good and also knew there were still some remaining issues. Bought it anyway and will buy the finished version as well, which will be even better.

    4 & 5. Really not sure what I bought before those.

    Personally, knowing a game was playtested thoroughly is not nearly as important to me as WHO it was playtested by. If Burning Wheel HQ puts out a game, I know it will have nearly all of the issues found and fixed. If folks that I trust are involved in a playtest and say that the game is great and the issues are being fixed, then that's great too.

    If a book has a long list of playtesters, but I don't recognize any of them, that really tells me nothing about the playtest process. I mean, D&D modules sometimes have loads of playtesters, but the modules still suck. So you take a chance with products like this, just like ones where it's not clear whether any playtesting was done or not.

    Worst of all is if people I know are involved in a playtest, but they tell me that the game really isn't that good or isn't really ready to be published but the author it going forward with it anyway. I think that's a sign that the playtest environment surrounding the game really isn't working, because the designer really isn't hearing what the playtesters have to say or the playtesters really aren't in the game's target audience.

    So I guess my real thoughts are: playtesting is important, but it's even more important if you can get your playtesters talking about the game, how great it is, and what the known problems are.

    \n Yes, I care. I am also aware that playtesting can mean a lot of different things, as a good GM can fix a wobbly game on the fly (and I've loved some wobbly games) and a lousy GM can drag down even a stable game into a lousy play experience. I'm willing to forgive various hiccups, errors, and assumptions provided that a game is clear in its intentions and operations, does some thing very well, and adds to my repertoire as a GM somehow.

    I've participated in all manner of playtests, though, and have seen perfectly good games sunk by thin testing, jealous testers, and loaded criticisms. So, in other words, there is sometimes a certain value to the experience that comes with a less-tested game, as it sometimes exposes you to more raw (or flexible, or fresh) ideas. Playtesting can solidify but it can also smooth out and dilute.*

    So deciding on playtesting versus purchase, it's a judgment call. I don't think I've ever looked at a playtester list and not purchased something because of it, though. To a certain extent, nothing has been satisfactorily playtested for my group until it's been playtested with my group, you know what I mean?

    I like it when options are presented as a result of playtesting, as Pelgrane Press sometimes does. I like it when a designer has logged enough time with a system or scenario that she can speak from experience, not just design, about the shortcomings and strengths, and how to run a game to minimize one and maximize the other.

    So, yeah, it's complicated. I've liked some games that really needed more playtesting, and I've disliked (or grown to dislike) some very tightly tested games.

    Perhaps I should start a different thread, but I'd love to hear people's takes on how they spot subpar playtesting.

    Cheers,
    Will

    *Update: You know what, I should say that a slavish adherence to playtester notes can do these things. Part of good playtesting is incorporating feedback in such a way that does not damage or diminish the game, isn't it?

    \n There's a lot of different kinds of playtesting, you shouldn't just do one kind:

    * Playtesting where the designer playtests with people they teach the game to.
    * Playtesting where the designer is physically there to answer questions and such but doesn't really participate in the playtest.
    * Playtesting where a group one thousand miles away gets the material and plays it as best they can with no help.
    * Playtesting where a group attempts to stick as close as possible to the written material.
    * Playtesting where a group uses the material as normally as possible, with their normal group dynamics and skills.
    * Playtesting with a group of people who have narrow preferences and push material hard towards those preferences.
    * Playtesting with a group of diverse people whose preferences are a jumble.
    * Playtesting with one person working through the systems of the game as a "simulator" of play.

    And so on.

    The more different kinds you do, the better results you will get. What you do with them is your decision of course.
