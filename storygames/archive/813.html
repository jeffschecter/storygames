Roleplaying Games vs Board-n-Card Games: Generating Significant Details

Josh Roby
June 2006 edited June 2006 in Story Games
So here's a little snippet of theory-thought that Thomas and I were throwing around:

The difference between Roleplaying Games and Board Games (and Card Games) lie in where the significant details come from.

In a Board Game (or Card Game) all the significant details are generated by specific procedures of the game -- a roll of the dice, drawing a card, the pieces on the board, or whatever. The players make decisions about these significant details, but they do not generate these significant details.

<em>Example:</em> I'm playing Pit. I'm dealt cards. I decide which cards to try to pass in order to collect a full hand.

In a Roleplaying Game, all (or most) of the significant details are generated by the players, and manipulated by the procedures of the game (a roll of the dice, a draw of a card). Players then make decisions about the significant details that they have introduced, or more to the point, they make decisions about the significant details that the other players have introduced.

<em>Example:</em> I'm playing Changeling. I make a character, a pig pooka with broken wings, because I want to play a game regarding impossible dreams and the frustrations revolving around them. In play, the dice inform how the story progresses and presents me with choices to make, but I make the choices.

Does that sound accurate to your experience?

    But that doesn't matter, Josh, the question here, really, is what defines a RPG, no? Otherwise why did you say board or card games? I could come up with a criteria about those that make them disparate categories as well. Like, in Card games there often are zero imaginative elements (people even forget that King means anything other than 12, effectively).

    I mean is this discussion really about the differences between "nomic-like" games (including Universalis, BTW) and board games?

    I've actually used a very similar definiton myself to set up a line of what's unique about RPGs. This is how I describe it. In non-RPGs, all decisions come down to a finite set of possible actions that a player can take based on the agreed to elements. Even in a Nomic game, where you can create new elements, you still make decisions on what happens based on the selected elements. In Nomic that list expands continually, and may become very large, but is never infinite.

    In RPGs, the assumption is reversed, and the in-game elements may do any of the infinite possible actions that they could do if they were real. The rules, then are set up to adjudicate what happens when things are attempted.

    And there are rules for everything in RPGs. Often the rule is, "It it's not a contentious sort of thing it just happens." Often followed by, "If you're not sure, then the GM just decides if it happens." But usually something like, "Then roll and compare the elements thusly, to see what happens.

    Now, looking at this, you can try to come up with counter-examples. Like in a miniatures game, you do really have an infinite number of moves you can make with a piece. But, in fact, all of these moves can be categorized into effective categories. Moving 9 inches or 9.5 inches may not matter if it's not to within firing range, or line of sight. Yes, with many units, this can rapidly mean a very high number of potential moves for any one unit (which I'd argue is one of the selling points of miniatures sans hexes), but it's till effectively finite.

    I think this is telling. Basically, in a non-rpg, you can nuance a move in play, but it doesn't make any difference to the outcome of play (other that, as in the example, players who are creating their own narrative on top of play - which I'd argue is playing an RPG simultanously or instead of the boardgame) per the rules. In a RPG, these nuances are often very important as adjudicated by the GM.

    I think this is key. In RPGs, there is some authority that gets to decide the outcome of every "maneuver." And they all *may* have an effect (though many don't). It's this responsibility to determine these things, instead of having a "hard" set of rules that pertains to only a finite set of maneuvers, that makes RPGs distinct from other sorts of games.

    Mike

    This is a good post and a good thread but as always I am marching at right angles to everyone else, tooting my horn and waving my banner and just trusting that there's someone behind me.

    I think certainly there is a distinction between application of rules and creation of anything (rules, a la Nomic, flavor text, a la the D&D dungeon crawl, a town full of miscreants a la DitV). Let me just add to that a little with my gaming group's experience with Once Upon A Time.

    My gaming group has 6 people, me and 5 others, 2 of whom were not around for this experiment. They are dead-set against narration-passing games, hate them, hate narrating, hate every part of contributing to the setting except through character (and character resource) design. "If we wanted to do that," one said cogently, "we would GM."

    So myself and 3 others played a game of Once Upon a Time. Now, I enjoyed it greatly, I loved improvising the story, going along...this was actually a game I thought might be an interesting training instrument for people who have to do public speaking "on their feet" - salespeople, lawyers such as my ownself, publicists, reporters, etc. You have to not only create a cogent storyline, and express it, but you have to angle it towards the concepts in your hand.

    In other words, you have to simultaneously manipulate things that are assigned to you by the impersonal laws of chance and design (the cards) and get your story to move along in some kind of coherent way. Get too incoherent and people will call you on it and you'll lose control of the narrative to someone else.

    I was good at this, my friend was good at this, my wife and her friend were terrible at this. They would get caught up in listening to the story and be unable to shift gears quickly enough to use their cards to interrupt the flow of it. And once they got control of the story, often with a triumphant "HA!", they would be totally unable to think of where to go with it in order to get it to the next "fixed" point, i.e. one of their cards.

    So while I disagree that there's something special about card and board games that make them more prone to one kind of activity than RPGs are (Diplomacy. You lose.) certainly there are two very different types of activity here, and it is a huge mistake to just decide that a potential player is equally good at both kinds of activity, or will equally enjoy both kinds. (There is often a big assumption floating around that all players, if properly exposed to the "right" kind of activity will always like it better, which is even worse than believing that all players will equally enjoy both sorts.)

    One curiosity: parlor narration seems to be one of those boundary situations where a game rests precariously between 'rpg' and 'board game.' Parlor narration invites you to engage in some of the rpg elements without the usual mechanical elements that let you really *play* that role.

    An idea: the 'role' of rpg serves as a frame for the player, a tool through which they can invoke an interpretation of the world. Usually, it comes with a number of mechanical supports that enable it to achieve this goal, usually one of those being a certain freedom the player has to 'move' the frame around in the setting, go here and there, interact. Board games really don't have that--the board is laid out in front of you, what you see is what you get. If you don't see it, it isn't there.

    Now, a 'parlor narration' invokes the window without the mechanics to allow the player to do much with it. They can look and respond but not really move it. There is a 'tragic' direction to it--tragic in the sense that the window gives a certain sense of necessity to what unfolds (tragedy is more than just necessity, but I find necessity alone begins to feel a little tragic). It may have a few end conditions but nothing but those end conditions can be achieved.

    I'm not sure if this is innately bad, but it does create a very particular kind of play experience. It can be very rpg-like in the right situations (players embrace it and basically go fiddling and dancing to their end, seeing what happens along the way) or very board game-like (players don't really get into the roles and just play the game out as an exercise in strategy or rote dice throwing). I suspect the way in which the game presents itself might have much to do with whether someone considers it RPG or board game...

    Like all forum monsters, Graham, it's morphed and transformed into something bigger and uglier. I'll try and tie bits together:

    Both boardgames and roleplaying games have premises -- the premise of Monopoly is something akin to "Clever investing makes you rich" while a premise of a game of Capes is "Power is fun, but do you deserve it?" Through the course of play, that premise will be addressed (via significant details). The question is whether it will be addressed in ways proscribed by the game mechanics (I got rich because you landed on Park Place, which I'd invested in) or addressed by the players themselves (I prove I deserve my superpowers because I comforted Katey after rescuing her from the badguy).

    No matter what I do in Monopoly, I cannot address the premise in any way other than how the game mechanics address the premise. I cannot show that you have to be charming in order to get those investment opportunities -- the only way you ever get those opportunities is by landing on its square (or it's auctioned off).

    Contrariwise, if I don't do anything in my play in Capes to address the premise, the game falls flat, and in Capes' case, I accrue Debt until everything I touch fails.

    The question is not about choice -- both board games and roleplaying games have lots and lots of choices. The question is whether those choices generate significant details (ie addressing premise) or whether they are reactions or expressions of significant details generated by the game rules.
