\n Say I wanted to run Dirty Secrets at a gaming convention. Say, Draconis, for example. Should I play the Investigator, since it's the heaviest job, or just play the Authority for the first scene and let one of the players be the Investigator.
\n
\nDirty Secrets is special in the there is a single Player-Character, and many Game Masters (the Authority rotates amongst them, everyone else is an Advisor until the Authority role returns to them).

    \n Here's how I did it:

    First, I advertised what the game was about very clearly on the schedule. This is a modern noir game. I then explained it in terms of authors, like the text does, and asked people what their experience with it was. There was one person who was far and away more of a fan of the genre than everyone else. I gave them the Investigator position.

    Second, I used the novella grid even though the short story grid is supposedly geared more for shorter sessions. Having a bigger grid and more crimes made it more interesting. However, I kept an eye on the time and when there was about 30-45 minutes to go, I said "okay, we're going to skip ahead a little here" and went around the table just writing in people on the grid. People were fine with this, they had gotten the hang of writing names on the grid and a bit of what that meant.

    Importantly, I have never had Crime Resolution be satisfying at any convention. It is an extremely unconventional (no pun intended) mechanic that flies in the face of most mystery solving games, due to its randomness. Letting people know about what it's going to be in advance may help with this.

    That's it!!

    \n For the three convention sessions I've run, I played the Investigator twice. The third time one of the players had played DS with me before, so I asked her to be the Investigator. I think it was easier to guide and teach as the first Authority than as the Investigator, so I'm probably going to stick with that unless no one else wants to be the Investigator.

    As far as Crime Resolution being "random", the way I approach and explain it is that we need to explain why that person had to be the guilty party. It might fly in the face of mystery solving games, but it feels more like a detective story to me.

From a story perspective, the explanation feels fake, tacked-on and perfunctory, because nobody wanted it to be them at the end of the game...

    \n I've generally run convention games the way that Jason Corley does, except I just crossed out spaces on the Crime Grid to accelerate the game at the end. If you were to get a cross-out space on a Resolution, simply reroll. For the record, I think that Jason's method is probably better than mine.

    And yes, explaining what's happening with Crime Resolution up front is probably a big deal, for the reasons that Jason lays out.

    \n I would think that the player who is adding a name to the grid...should only add the name if they can imagine a plausible chain of events that could be "discovered" that would explain that persons guilt of any of the crimes in play.
    \n
    \nAt a convention it might be helpful to have that person provide a mini "internal monologue" to share those thoughts as "one possibility among many" with the rest of the group.
    \n
    \nSimilarly, no new crime card should be filled out, unless the person filling it out can think of a plausible way for any of the names already on the grid to have committed it (even if its just hiring someone to commit it rather than doing it themselves).
    \n
    \n
    \nWith those fairly simple guidelines in place, the Crime Resolution should never feel random because we know going in that at least one set of clues reasonably pointed to that person...because we reviewed those clues when they were added to the grid in the first place.

    Posted By: ValamirAt a convention it might be helpful to have that person provide a mini "internal monologue" to share those thoughts as "one possibility among many" with the rest of the group.
    \nThat's a great idea. It lets other players have a taste of being the Investigator too. In fact you should pitch it that way. "If you win you get to put a name on the crime grid and explain, as the Investigator in an internal monologue, how they could be guilty of one of the crimes."

    It doesn't help if you are developing crimes that are very disparate or opposed, of course, but I haven't had that happen in play, usually the murder is closely related to the other crimes and they are "aligned", even if they are not technically done by the same person.
