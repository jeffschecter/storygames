\n So, I was thinking of something on the way back from yoga tonight: I've been shoveling down a huge amount of hard SF, mostly transhumanist stuff like Greg Egan:

* Societies of gender-thinking people who choose to enhance their gender/racial features, or remove them altogether.
* Cities of humans that had long since converted themselves to sentient software. 10 years to us is 8,000 years to them.
* People choosing to change their own DNA and rebuild themselves as something different (creatures able to easily process/control genetics, etc)
* Crystals inserted into the base of the skull, replacing the brain, turning people into immortal creatures.
All sorts of crazy awesome shit.

...So I'm wondering if the same could be applied to fantasy and still make sense? I'm thinking world-building here. Basically where perhaps magic takes the place of technology as the pivot point of transhumanist change. (but note: I'm not thinking like a futuristic real society where their technology ends up eventually turning the world into a magical D&D world or anything like that: SF - to - Fantasy, etc)

Does the idea even make any sense?

Perhaps societies where even every barkeep and stableboy has the ability to reshape, remake matter in any way they want?
Perhaps fantasy societies where, using some sort of fantasy genetics, could "undo" their sex and race, or join together in groups to recreate themselves as new races?

Thoughts?

-Andy

    \n You know, it occurs to me that this is exactly what Exalted: The First Age is all about. Not, you know, that it will do it with the laser focus that a Shock! fantasy game would, but I think that it shows that the concept is not only doable, but floats vaguely in the subconcious minds of the gamer block.

    Really, it shouldn't be undoable. We've already got the "any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic" and the idea that in a lot of transhumanist literature the issues aren't so much about the technical plausibility of the shock (its important, but outside of engineer wanker fiction, not the central issue). Put those two together with magic as psychological pressure manifested in a literal way, and you're set for it.

    Back when I was thinking on Age of Kali I wanted magic and science to be part of it. One represented the rising tide of science philosophy, and the other the tide of religion, and the different ways they strike us. It still feels to me like our world is often caught in the crux of dilemmas between the heart and the head, and that in the genres we're talking about "science" often stands for "the head" and "magic" for "the heart."

    So what happens when rather than following our heads to big and scary new vistas of technological magic, we follow our hearts to big and scary new vistas of mystical magic? What would happen if we got, say, Diamond Path Mormonism (when enough Tibetans get baptised for the dead....) where its shown that man can become as God, but not after death but in this life, through a combination of the Book of Mormon and the Book of the Dead. Or what happens when an elite group of shock warriors of the gods are given immortality, near unlimited power to use the stuff of the universe (essence = strong/weak nuclear forces?), and then left in charge while the absentee gods fuck off to pleasure planet? And then the newly self-appointed rulers of the universe start finding that things like age, gender, scarcity, and morality don't apply to them anymore?

    So yea. Should be fun.

    \n The challenging thing about "transhumanist fantasy" is not the trappings, but the underlying assumptions -- fantasy is often essentialist, suggesting that fantasy races have inescapable essences and that some people are elite and born to rule, or born to magic.

    On the other hand, transhumanist suggests that human existence is radically contingent, and that with advanced technology, one is free to edit or scribble over humanity as one will, and that this is an ethically neutral activity.

    In other words, you need to go from "a mad wizard created the Owlbear" to "excuse the mess -- I'm spending the year as an Owlbear for tax purposes. It has other advantages as well."

    \n I've considered making a follow-up to Sufficiently Advanced using magic rather than technology. The big challenge is figuring out sensible rules of magic that can then be followed to their incomprehensible conclusions, as opposed to starting with nonsensical rules in the first place. I probably won't end up doing it, but we'll see. All that aside...

    Let's say that there is amazingly advanced magic out there, free from the confines of science and common sense. Let's make it commonly available to all. Just take a couple spells from your favorite fantasy game and set them to "activate at will, indefinite duration." You'd get...\n
      \n
    • People who can live most of their lives in the Ethereal plane, interacting just with the folks in their clique and passing through the rest of us at any given time.
      • \n
    • Nations of liches, who use Helms of Opposite Alignment on newcomers to ensure they stay good.
      • \n
    • "Perfect" people who almost never fail... but who get aggravated easily and age and die more quickly. Why? Because they're triggering time loops every time they screw up, and going back a minute or two to fix it.
      • \n
    • People who vacation as shapeshifters, spending a year in a different body (or a string of several dozen).
      • \n
    • Swords become basically useless because everyone has incredible magical defenses. You need your own magic to get through, and enchanting a metal sword probably won't work - someone worked up a protective magnetism spell. See also "turn wood."
      • \n
    • Remember Dairen's Instant Fortress? A little cube that turns into a tower to hang out in? Yeah, everyone has one of those now. You can just walk around wherever you want, because your house is in your pocket. Cities only appear at nighttime, or in civilizations that haven't discovered extradimensional spaces.
      • \n
    \nYou need to start with a base set of magic. That base set is very important. Then you strip off all the restrictions except the most vital, and make it available to damn near everyone. The important part is not what technology or magic is available to you, it's what you decide to do with it. You're headed towards a point where you're limited not by fundamental constraints of nature, but by choice.

    My two cents.

    Posted By: noclueIs that transhumanistic enough?
    \nIt misses the "trans" part. Transhumanism doesn't deal with "is this human" by pointing out non-human things, as much as it changes humans and asks "is this still human?"

    It poses questions on what it means to be human, not by taking non-human things like aliens or fantasy races or robots and making them humanlike, but taking humans and changing them based on advanced science. A fantasy transhumanist story would approach it the same way, except with magic.

    Actually, come to think of it, White Wolf's World of Darkness is vaguely tranhumanist. People are turned into vampires, werewolves, wizards and other things, and what point do they stop being human, if any?

    You could do a clone related stories with ressurection spells or duplication spells. AI type stories with golems. Human mind storage or transferrence with memory crystals. Transformation spells for bio-engineering. And probably more with healing spells, curses, blessings, mind control, summoned servants, and all sorts of other magic. Intelligent swords! Undead! The more I think about it, the more ripe it is.

    I've been thinking about this a bit for the last couple, and it occurs to me that there is an interesting that that could be done here.     \nYou know, that's an interesting question.

    Standard D&D alignments always seemed to say to me, at any rate, that extremes of each alignment -- inevitables, fae, that sort of thing -- were always archetypical, and somehow not exactly human, and that only in humanity (or, at least, only in the demihuman races) was the human characteristic fully expressed.

    One way of exploring this would be to turn it on its head: that the conflicts between alignment don't help humanity, they keep humanity mundane. It's when you fully express Law (or Chaos, or Good, or Evil), you become more human.

    Law keeps you in balance with the universe, fully in tune with the world, unlike the way humanity seems out of place. Chaos is liberating, and only in Chaos can man be truly free. Man expresses lovingkindness in Good, and only then can he find peace. Man is bound in chains of bone and blood and sinew, and only in Evil can he transcend those limits.

    Mind you, I'd probably use different moral values. I've always found the Law/Chaos and Good/Evil moral axes were too clear-cut or had their own baggage, semantics-wise ("Transcending limitations is Evil? Hah! We know where that goes, don't we, children?").

    \n Hey all, very awesome, interesting stuff. I like the soul ideas too, but it seems to be more of an awesome background element to a fantasy world than a transhumanist campaign focus.

    Last night I was driving somewhere and I think I figured out a lot of issues with my idea, and why it's problematic.

    So, most of the transhumanist stuff that I've been reading, namely the Alpha and Omega who is Greg Egan (his novel Diaspora is worse than coffee in creating insomnia), supposes as was reflected above that human-changing "technology" is available to all (or "most", in books with classist/racial issues). In fantasy, though, the prominent paradigm (OMG I used that fucking word) is as Shreyas suggested: Magic is rare and special, and only a few can use, control, harness or understand it.

    Those two things are at odds, making the project hard.

    Well here's the thing, let's just say "shit, magic is like technology": In the "future" (of Forgotten Realms or whatever), everyone learns how to use and manipulate magic. Magic becomes technology.

    That would be a perfect transhumanist setup. The only problems are:

    1) We have no literature where the above happens as a frame of reference. Something to relate to. In SF we have tons.

    2) There's no literature where the above happens because it's not particularly interesting. The transhumanist lovers are wrapped up in SF. The fantasy lovers have no interest in transhumanist ideas. I'm painting with broad strokes here, but basically I've found a rich, untapped idea market... that won't get filled, because it's not rich, no one's interested in it. :-)

    3) The few examples we see have been geeky and/or dumb. Magitech, anyone?

    To that end, I think sometime in the next year I'll experiment a little with using Shock: for fantasy, specifically saying that on the table must be the shock of "Magic is prevalent and Everyone can use it well". And then go from there.

    Can people become like the gods?
    What sorts of marvels would a society swimming in raw magical dream-power produce?

    I think a lot of the coolness and fun will come from this formula:

    1) Defining, loosely, how magic works. But setting up some definite limitations, like:
    -- All magic is elemental based, only
    or
    -- All magic is based on dreams
    -- All magic is based on the Tarot (with extra cards)
    -- All magic is based on a collective unconscious
    -- All magic is physical; there is no true telepathy or "wishes"
    -- All magic is mental/mind based; there are no physical effects.

    2) And then further creating shocks/issues (or the like) based on the above.

    3) And then playing it out, either using the shock system or moving it to its own game system.

    -Andy
