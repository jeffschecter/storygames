    \n Hiya Brian,

    I've got two bits of advice for you. First, make sure everyone who's involved knows where the game might go. Pull the Murub card and one of the tamer cards out of the deck for everyone to see before play begins. The Roach can get raunchy, and you don't want to step across any lines.

    Second, and I can't stress this enough, the Roach is "GM-full", not "GM-less". If everyone plays crazy just because they're imagining a lack of constraint, from the lack of a central GM, then the fun level might drop a little for everyone. On the other hand, if everyone is taking the responsibility of making the game awesome for everyone else, then you can hardly go wrong. Jason might call this "I'm gonna make you awesome."

    Unfortunately, I don't know a thing about the alternate settings of the Roach Returns. But I'd guess that one setting is no more difficult than any of the others.

    -Eric

    \n The only disadvantage to the alternate settings is that there is no cheat sheet of names, places, and objects to crib from yet. Overlord also requires a bit of informed restraint, since everyone has insanely deadly weapons at arm's reach. It plays better in a subtle mode, to provide contrast. Eric's advice is spot on.

    Explain the rules as you go rather than all at once, aiming to lay out everything by the end of the first scene. Everybody is on, all the time. You have to pay attention and participate, so don't forget to take breaks. I think the sweet spot is five players. Figure an hour per Event, and the game isn't hurt by excising an Event or two in advance, based on time constraints. If you are facilitating, plan out a strong initial scene in advance to set the tone. It doesn't have to be crazy, just pointed at a couple of players and demonstrative of the system and setting. I usually try to humiliate the character I start out hating in front of a Pembertonian at the convocation, enlisting the guy I love as an ally.

    \n In my experience (reinforced by listening to the Rolemonkeys podcast), the two concepts that seem to cause the most confusion in the game are the starting relationships ("I like him, he hates me- what?") and the targeting of other players for commands/opportunities.

    For starting relationships, it's key to realize that it's just to get things rolling- the relationships can change as quickly as players want, through their interactions.

    For targeting, the confusion comes from the fact that many of the cards don't make it clear what you should do, or if you should do it to someone else at all. Often the cards are written in the Roaches "voice", speaking to the Roach-bound character, which just makes it confusing if you're thinking about a target.

    My advice is to pick a target, but don't dwell on it too much. Interpret the commands and opportunities however seems appropriate, and when/if it seems relevant to involve another PC, glare at your target player maniacally.

    \n Oh, another tip about something that I do all the time. It's helpful to fold some index cards or scraps of paper into little table-tents. Have enough of these for each major Pembertonian, as well as some extras for NPCs invented during the game. By writing the NPCs names on the cards, you can keep them visible on the table, making it easy for folks to grab them and reincorporate them into scenes throughout the game.

    Also, if you do this, resist the urge to destroy them when someone dies. The ghost of Bantam Whaley is still a perfectly viable NPC for a later conflict...

    \n After playing it for the first time this afternoon, one bit of advice I can offer is to make sure everyone understands that it's a game about conflict. How someone can mistake a game about parasitic telepathic Sumerian cockroaches that order you to do crazy things to each other for a game about cooperation, I'm not sure. But one of the players seriously said "I'm not used to this much conflict." And when she framed her scene, she wanted to figure out the conflict as we went along, and then didn't produce one at all.

    So: make sure everyone knows that the scenes will resolve in a conflict, and that it's their job to bring them about.
