    \nAs I've said in the past, I personally feel that getting nominated means more than winning, because nominations are from people who can compare all the products to one another to make that determination, whereas voting is likely only from people who own just the game they're voting for. This is not to say that their votes are worthless, but rather that it's like voting the party line on election day. You're voting for the candidate you think should win based on what you know, but maybe if you knew more about other candidates you'd vote for them instead.
    \n
    \nSo yeah, it's a great honor to be nominated. It'll be nice if we win, but I'm already satisfied.

    \n\nJuly 2009 \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n To me there are a few surprises in those lists, but I can understand why. The ENnie Awards cover a wide range of tastes so while I would have picked differently, they do reflect the hobby as a whole.

    I'm thrilled to get a nomination for3:16 in Best Cover Art. I think it's great recognition of Paul Bourne's work. Here's a guy on a laptop in Falkirk, Scotland kicking the ass of the biggest companies. His indie work sticks out: a|state and its supplements, Cold City, the Cold City Companion (baby in a jar!) and the Dossier, the classy re-done cover for Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries, Hot War and Summerland as well as 3:16 Carnage Amongst The Stars.

    It's a great cover image for 3:16, and I do hope people vote for it.

    Paul's work is amazing and his process is nuts. That cover image is actually a capture of a 3-D model that he has constructed, skinned, arranged the lighting on and then rendered.

    I'm super-jazzed for Eppy and all the other indie nominees too. It was a tough year to get stuff nominated for sure.

I owe Ryan a big one for the Best Writing nomination, as he was tasked with slogging through my hideous manuscript and cleaning it up, rather like the dudes who come in the plastic overalls and waders to clean up crime scenes when the cops are done. Yeah, he was the guy who had to scrape up the splattered brains of innocent punctuation, all wriggling with maggots amidst the chaos of sentence fragments, broken about the place like furniture after a fight.
