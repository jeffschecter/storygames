\n Hello all,

I have this game I've been working on for over a year. It's called Cheap. At first it started as something that I wanted to play one night, but after playing it with multiple groups I realized that it was something I wanted to pursue further, and I have been refocusing it, tightening it, and developing it.

The game I have created is fantastic.

Cheap is a game about killing your narrator before he kills you. Someone is using you to play out their twisted fantasies, and you have no idea how to find them. You enter the game without knowing the rules, and you're forced to go over your head to figure them out. It's a game about power, abuse, how you use your knowledge, and how you use your ignorance.

The game feels like a departure from other roleplaying game experiences, and not because it's *trying* to be cutting edge, but because it evolved into this really cool thing that's all its own.

I'm at a place where I really want to share Cheap. And I found this really awesome artist, Jesse Parrotti, who I want to contribute art to Cheap. And these decisions are leading towards binding Cheap inside a physical product and getting it into people's hands so that they too can delve in.

I want to know how one best promotes a game.
I want to know how one best promotes a game with a very volatile and unfriendly subject matter.
I want to know how to market a game without explaining the rules.
Cheap has a very intense, in-your-face playstyle. How do I communicate that without sounding like all those "Most Hardkore game Evar!!" nobs?

Cheap is a very simple game but requires prep work. That prep work is NOT playgroup specific, so it'd be easy to have someone else do the prep work and pass their materials on to you. How do I best facilitate this? One way is through supplement wheels (setting expansions in D&D, for example). I was thinking about another way: What if you purchased a quarterly magazine subscription, and each issue had the full rules, any necessary updates, and a large bulk of that aforementioned prep work? Cheap is a simple game, procedurally, and thus the magazine could be 10% core game and 90% supplementary material. What about a prep-sharing network of types? What about setting up forums where people could post their prep?

This post hasn't given that much information about the game. If you'd like more, ask away!
I'd love some suggestions, ideas and insider hints on how to market a game, and specifically this one.

    \n Joe,

    The best way to promote a game is to identify who is likely to buy it and make sure they know why they should buy it. It sounds like you have a cross-genre game. Without knowing too much, I think many different types of role players and other types of gamers may find something about the game compelling. Only you can determine what the audience is.

    Beyond that, look to build a community around your game. The thing that's great about printing background/supplement materials is that you can always invite fans of the game to submit materials or go on to forums and discuss their research. That's a Good Thing. In terms of package, I think you can sell subscriptions, but I would just call them supplements, not magazines or periodicals. You might also consider delivering them as PDFs, it's cheaper and easier to fulfill.

    I am not sure why you are concerned about not explaining the rules. I think that you can describe the game in broad strokes and that should be enough. I have bought numerous games without knowing the mechanics of the game. I think if you let them know what is great about the game (and your description is a really good start) then knowing of the rules is largely superfluous. Especially if the player is not to know.

    As for marketing a game with violate subject matter...I think you put your cards on the table and say this game is about psychological tension. The Saw films didn't try to package the movie as a comedy, you don't need to either. Instead, keep circling your message back to what makes the game great: how its a departure, how the idea is to kill the narrator before the narrator kills you. There are enough talking points in there for any banner, ad, email message, however you decide to promote the game.

    Hell..."Kill your Narrator...Before the Narrator Kills You" should sell a good number of copies from that alone, I think.

    Does the help?

    Callan, I'm confused.
    If you wanted to get out of one of the central tenets of Cheap, you can just quit playing Cheap.
    Each session opens with a sign-in/salute/opening speech. And in that, I make quite clear that if you do not find this sort of discomfort fun or engaging in a good way, it is absolutely fair to get up and leave the gaming session with no questions asked.

    I've had friends walk out of games of Cheap before, and others play through and then decide it really isn't for them. It has in no way affected our friendship, and I kept playing roleplaying games with them. Just not Cheap.

    When I say "you are forced" I mean, "the system is one that forces you to act in Pattern X if you want to succeed in game". There is no imperative to act in Pattern X in real life or outside of the context of the game, and indeed I would feel like things weren't working if that were the case.

    Dave/DinDenver,

    The GM-figure controls the Narrators. Which, in most scenes simply means they frame the scene and run NPCs. The only time when someone is actually "in" the Narrator character is during a Showdown, and then the GM does indeed control the Narrator. The sense of competition you speak of is present in the game, but it's not a players-vs-GM thing. It's a players-vs-GM's character thing.

    What's the difference? The difference is that the GM isn't fighting to "beat" the players. Since the players don't know the rules of the game at the beginning, the GM could very easily "beat" them simply by choosing to make all the variables really, really difficult and frustratingly non-obvious. The GM's job is to provide situations and play them to the hilt, not to win. The players' job is to explore and exploit the situations. There is a sense of competition, but it's focused more towards a fictional character and a system than the GM-as-person.
