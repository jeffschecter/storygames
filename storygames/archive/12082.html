\n I did a FATE game in March and I did a collaborative story telling session at the start. I think you can use it to add collaborative story telling to any traditional-ish RPG. This is basically doing a REALLY simplified version of Universalis at the start of each session:

1) Each player gets a certain number of story points that they can spend to state vignettes. A vignette can be a paragraph or two or three. It could be a continuation of the current scene or a scene in a different time and/or place. I think we used 3 points, but it could have been 5 -- I don't remember.

2) The GM can veto any vignette

3) A player can veto a vignette that involves their character

4) The GM can issue more points

Vetoing: try never to veto. Try to work with what the player is saying without changing it at all (just adding to it with a "yes, and..." technique). If changes are unavoidable, try to get the player to make the change instead of you doing it. If it totally can't work in any way, veto is a last resort. If a player's vignette is vetoed, they get their point back.

One group I did this with in March had not played any collaborative story telling games; they had only played RPGs like D&D and Dead Lands. They really liked being able to mess with the other PCs' and their stories and also with the NPCs and the GM's stories.

When we used Universalis in our earlier campaign, we did it at the start of each session and any time we would have otherwise had a cut scene.

    \n Sounds like something I did with the D&D 4E campaign I just started. I have six players, so I had them each introduce their characters, and then, each other player wrote down two questions they had about the character and passed them over. So each player now has 10 questions about their character. Once per level throughout the heroic tier, I told them they can call for a vignette, a short scene focused on their character. If the vignette answers one of those questions satisfactorily (with the person who wrote the question judging whether or not it's satisfactory), they get an extra action point.

    \n It was interesting -- the first session was with the experienced Universalis players from my earlier campaign and the second session was with the group that had not done Universalis. The first group made much smaller vignettes, closer to Universalis facts.

    The Episode 1 tab is the adventure flow. "START FRAME" is how I started the story telling. I started with that cut scene and then let the players add story. The Universalis players knew they couldn't contradict the previous story, but the second group didn't really get that (despite that it's in the story telling rules I read them from the doc), but I just glossed over that (of course) and didn't interfere with their creative flow when a player changed the knife from thunking into a door to thunking into a ninja's back.

    \nThank you. In my presentation, I focused on the thing you think is cool about your character may not actually be that cool. If two people asked the same thing, that's the cool thing that everyone wants to hear more about, so you can spend two scenes on that. Play to focus on other people's fun instead of your own and all that. We're only one session in, but by all reports, it was a hell of a session. (I never trust my own judgment of how well a game went when I GM, it's just too different an experience.)

    They've already learned that it's real easy to pace the reveals out too slowly, since they already made it to level 2 and only one of them got in a vignette. Once that sinks in, I think we'll have several big character reveals in every session. Which will be awesome!
