\n So you've got these two behemoth cons, both covering broadly similar areas of gaming (or do they?), and both appealing to broadly similar markets (or do they?), only a month apart; coming from Europe one can't afford to go to both (well, most folks can't), so, if one (forex this one) were planning to fly a long distance to attend one or the other, which one to choose? In other words what, basically, are the differences between them, and, as an entry level indie small press publisher, which one should I pump for? (Note, it wouldn't be this year, most likely 2014).

    \n Don't discount Dragon*Con, either--it's one of the Big Three. (And maybe PAX and PAX East now make it Big Five?)

    Origins is much more gaming oriented, with a broader range of games in play and promotion. Also, being the first Big Three, it's where a lot of new product lines launch, so if you're into free or cheap gear, it's the one to consider first.

    GenCon is massive, but it's also highly dominated by TSR/Hasbro (D&D). That's it real origin; and that's its biggest draw, from my (limited) experience. That said, it's also The Grandaddy of Cons, and so a lot of other major and minor and independent companies go big with promotions and organized events, including parties. (It seems that Gen Con gets the lion's share of indie scene events, but that's all subjective on my part; no stats to back it up!)

    Dragon*Con is much broader in scope than just gaming, though its gaming is Very Big (like, most of the basement of a major hotel plus half a floor or another hotel, typically). I would say, if you want more pick-up or free-play options, it's the one to attend. Plus, there's much more cosplay, parties, movies, concerts, etc--Dragon*Con in more of a "geek interests" con than just "gaming" con. Also, Cruxshadows.

    Take all of the above with a grain of salt, though--everyone's experiences differ and that affects perceptions. Your best bet would be (a) do a social poll of your acquaintances in the States and find out where they are going, then (b) carefully review all information on the web sites, to be sure you're going to have stuff to do with them at your chosen con other than play pick-up games with each other. Not that that's a waste of a con experience... but you can do that any time, without all the con expenses.

    \n My two cents are:
    Origins is the scrappy smaller convention that is expanding and proving itself. Lots of fun, but little polish.

    GenCon has long since "made it" and is more heavily retail oriented. You can more easily get lost in all there is to do at GenCon, whether you like that kind of thing is up to you.

    If you want to connect with people who are there to game, and might also want to buy what you are selling, I would go to Origins.

    If you want to connect with people who are there to purchase the latest White Wolf supplement, the newest Dominion expansion, and might be interested in buying your stuff too if they have money left over, or time to hit the dealer's room after the "games they came there to play," go to GenCon.

    Take that as you will. Your mileage may vary.

    \n I've sold at both cons and there is a lot more money at Gen Con that at Origins... so if you are going there to sell follow the money. Of course as a small press prepare to be ignored by the vast majority of attendees. Booths are cheaper at Origins but the costs are similar (cons are expensive). Both are old established cons where all kinds of games are played. Historically Gen Con was the RPG con and Origins the Board Game con. That is still somewhat true. Indie RPG stuff happens at both but there are more Indie game company dealer booths at Gen Con. Some of the big companies have skipped Origins in recent years so financially they are more tenuous.

    I go to Origins to play and run games. My brother lives in Columbus so it's a cheap trip and I get to see family. I like Columbus. If I ever left Indiana I'd probably move there. The convention center is big and well kept up. There are great food options at the North Market a block from the convention center. Ohioans are friendly and the down town is not crawling with pan handlers. Nice place.

    I go to Gen Con to sell so I'm tied to my booth much of the time, when it was in Milwaukee I attended Gen Con the way I attend Origins now. I live pretty close to Indy but due to booth work we are getting a down town hotel this year, an expensive proposition. Indy is an okay city but there is the local joke that is it India-no-place or Nap Town. Outside of the Con you might not find a lot to do, but the Con is freaking huge. There is way too much to see and do. You have to plan what you want and do it. This means missing a lot. The dealers area is huge and has a lot of stuff that you will not see anywhere else - especially from small companies. It also has a lot of non-game stuff (utility kilts, swords, and stuffed dragons toys at my booth (they pay the rent!) The food options in Indy are more than Columbus. The food court is meh but is open till 9 or so. There are lots of restaurants cheap and expensive. In the past there were a lot of pan handlers down town but I understand the police are pushing them out this summer, so they might not be a problem. They never gave me any issues in the past, they follow the rules (make your pitch and don't pursue of the person says no or walks on).

    Personally I picked Gen con over Origins for many years. It is the bigger show but it depends on what you are after. PAX East and Prime sound way cool to but are just too far away to make it. I've always heard good things about Dragon Con to and Atlanta is a wonderful city.

    I think you can't go wrong no matter which one you pick.

    Chris Engle/Hamster Press

    \n Origins is a very pleasant, laid back play-focused convention. It is in a beautiful town with much to recommend it. If you have to choose between Origins and Gen Con and are there to play and enjoy life, choose Origins.

    Gen Con is a generally unpleasant sales convention. It is in an ugly town with little to recommend it. There's a ton of stuff to do, see and buy and it can be overwhelming. If you want to meet everyone and buy everything, Choose Gen Con.

    I wouldn't recommend Dragon Con, which has outgrown its available space to an alarming degree and is in no way focused on gaming.

    Also consider an east coast convention like Dreamation, which is more accessible from Europe and offers high quality gaming.

    As a publisher consider partnering with a company or group of people buying a booth at any or all of these shows, like IPR. I think unless you have a lot of interest and awareness already from the gaming public, buying your own space at any of these shows will be a waste of money.

    \n Thanks for all the input guys. I'm beginning to form a picture of the differences between two events. I think in terms of sheer numbers GenCon seems to have it, but Origins is more play-orientated.

    @MatrixGamer I see why you've plumped for GenCon, it's a marketing decision based on the sales focus, but given the apparent WotC/Hasbro/TSR slant (not surprising gven its location), I don't think it would work for me, as for the forseeable future I'm likely to be producing quirky, off-the-wall games with innovative but at times bizarre-seeming mechanics- hence why I like this forum particularly- and I don't think the overwhelmingness would appeal to me, so probably Origins would be a better bet.

    Incidentally, I knew a little bit about Origins before becoming a roleplayer because I was on Boardgamegeek for a few years and got to know (online) a guy from CABS (the Columbus Area Boardgame Society), and formed a vague plan of maybe rocking up there one day.

    @Jason_Morningstar I take your point about trying an East Coast con- PAX East has been on my radar for a while now thanks to various posts on this forum.

    @Michael_Hopcroft The con scene in the UK is constrained by the fact that not only do the transport links not encourage criss-cross travel, they are also hideously expensive. After a period of wrangling among various parties, GenCon imploded in 2008, and was replaced by a con called UK Games Expo, which is largely a commercial venture and, as I've mentioned before, doesn't smile on small press indie gaming but is nevertheless a enjoyable con. Its focus is in fact more on boardgames than RPGs. The other major national games con is Dragonmeet, which takes place in West London at the beginning of December, and is operated by the guys behind Cubicle 7. Despite the website being legendarily slow to get updated, Dragonmeet is very well organised and very popular, and accepting of all kinds of games- I got a full table for Where The Heart Is last year- as well as having talks, seminars and a thriving trade hall. Because the numbers are much lower than either Origins or GenCon- I'm not sure exactly how many but it's less than 2'000- the relaxation factor is pretty high.

    @JDCorley Yes, we have those sorts of cons here in the UK, in fact the vast preponderance of con gaming, especially small press indie- we have a lot of home-grown talent as I'm sure you're aware- takes place at these kinds of cons. Numbers vary from 50-300 so they're all pretty relaxed .

    \n Listen to Chris, he's actually attempted having a booth at both conventions. I haven't done Origins myself, but everything I've heard indicates that it's a much less sales-intensive convention. To my understanding this does not somehow translate into "so that's where a small press publisher should be" - at least not if you've got sales goals to meet. Gencon is where customers go expecting to make purchases, which makes all the difference in finding people willing to try quirky new things.

    (I've got quite some experience with small conventions at this point. My general observation is that WotC not having a booth at a convention does not magically cause customers to flock to your booth; it just means that it's not a sales convention, and people likely did not come with commerce in mind in the first place. A commercial convention is much more expensive for you than a smaller convention, but the sales are also a magnitude higher.)

    As for Gencon being somehow narrow-minded and only accepting of big-name games, I have to say that this is not my experience in any significant way. The alpha customers of the American gaming scene go there, so if that's your target audience, then what can you do? You could avoid meeting your target audience, I guess, by not going to the same convention with them. Somehow I don't think that this would help sales any. You already made the decision of not being the most appealing game in the convention by not being the newest edition of D&D, but this does not mean that it's a good idea to go to a smaller or less sales-oriented convention.

    In general a booth at these big conventions is too much of an investment to be profitable for an indie publisher, unless you're very exceptional. It is more feasible to get together with half a dozen other like-minded publishers to share the costs and the booth: this way you'll have more product catalogue to present, and more workers to run the booth. This is what the Forge booth did over the last decade, mostly pretty successfully.

    As for what one might expect to sell at Gencon: in my experience a solid indie working from a joint booth with a bunch of other people might expect to sell a couple dozen copies of their game. If it's a particularly appealing game (the right genre, appealing pitch, the product looks solid, the booth crew is energetic, etc.), then 50-100 copies is not unrealistic. If it looks amateurish, has a dull topic and the salesperson is passive, then it's entirely possible to come out of Gencon joint booth with 5 sales or less. For multiple titles, you might figure that each additional title after the first one adds e.g. 20% to your sales total - that's a guess, but the number won't be very big, regardless, as the limiting factor in convention sales is your ability to initiate sales interactions and close sales, not whether the customer has only one choice or five as to what to buy. If I had to guesstimate the sales for an indie booth (that is, a booth selling works with no brand appeal, only the sales force and novelty on their side) at a 4-day large convention like Gencon or Spiel Essen, I'd assume 20*[1+0.1*the number of products] sales per person involved in the booth, to a maximum of 20 per square meter of booth size.

    \n I think it really comes down to your reasons for the trip.

    If you want to meet people and have fun playing games, Origins is great. Some great people go, there are fun games available and there's great food in Columbus.

    If you want to run a booth and sell games, Origins is horrible. Seriously. We only started to enjoy Origins when we gave up trying to man a booth and sell games there. We started just running lots of games instead and letting a company like IPR take a cut in exchange for selling it for us.

    I'd lay you even money that as a new small press publisher, you stand a much better chance of selling enough games to recoup your expenses at Dreamation than you would at Origins. Plus you'll meet a bunch of nice folks. Even though it's small, Dreamation is a pretty good place to start.

    Gen Con is like drinking from a fire hose. There's just more of everything. The food options suck, the town smells and it's hard work. But you can sell a lot of games there if you have a recognized name or you've managed to generate some buzz about your game ahead of time. That said, don't expect to make a profit there. It's really, really expensive for a booth and hotel/travel for your staff (you need to bring enough people to man your booth and give them breaks). You can mitigate some of that expense by going in on a booth with other companies, but even then it's rare to do much better than break even. It can be good marketing if you care about that sort of thing.

    Pax East can be a good, middle-of-the-road choice between these options. It's already big and it's getting bigger. In my experience, people at Pax East come to get demos of games. The vast majority are there for video games; however, many are interested in trying fun things, whether they're digital or not. As long as you don't mind occasionally explaining what a pen-and-paper RPG is, you're likely to get a lot of people who are willing to give your game a go. Boston can be a bit pricey, but we've always managed to successfully recoup our costs there.
