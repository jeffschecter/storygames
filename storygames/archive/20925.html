    \n In my eyes there is little reason for why the same game wouldn't work for all three of those, assuming that by "style" you mean the phantasmagoric detail and not necessarily the plot structure of each of those works. Specifically, you could easily play a drama game (as in, important protagonists making tough choices) set in any of those settings with one of the generic vehicles - anything like Primetime Adventures, Dust Devils, Solar System, Heroquest, even Fate (which we discussed at length last week) will easily adapt to all three of those settings and provide its own take on what it's like to be a jedi/hobbit/prince in those worlds when the Story comes barreling at you.

    I will particularly recommend Tales of Entropy (an upcoming story game, I think we discussed it at some point a bit) for your purposes here for a couple of reasons: it matches the campaign length you suggest very well; it is specifically designed to front-load a firm literary basis, while still making the actual gameplay all about the players and their ideas; it is easy to set up for each of those three settings, and the players will enjoy using the same system for three contrasting settings, while still having plenty of unique freedom to make each scenario an unique experience. If your plan is to play in all of those settings as a sort of a fantasy story game trilogy, then Entropy is probably one of the top picks for pushing the project to a successful completion. As a bonus, it is entirely feasible to "play the novel" with Entropy (as in, you play Frodo, I play Gandalf, and here's the One Ring, let's play to see what happens), if that strikes your fancy, instead of setting up a secondary story in the same setting.

    It is, of course, possible to get more ambitious and detailed about the substance of each of those stories, and then there is the question of player interfacing - what will, exactly, be the role of the playgroup in adding to the corpus when there already exists a perfectly serviceable novel/movie? What is it that we want to do with the far-away galaxy, Middle-Earth and Westeros?

    Assuming it was me planning to play these, and I wanted to get ambitious, while still sticking to a short campaign, here's my personalized picks for each of those stories:

    Star Wars: Star Wars is an ensemble adventure movie, so I would choose a party-based game. The story in the campaign needs to go big, epic and iconic, to match the feel of the movie. The setting is a conceptually rich space opera, so a game that can handle that conceptually (ship battles, statting up droids, aliens, whatever) is a must. Thematically there is leeway: it needs to be romantic (in the literary sense), but aside from that there is room to do stuff and still have a basis in the SW feel. Considering these factors, my choice would be Heroquest - the latest edition in particular is a perfectly streamlined princess play Sim adventure story game, and it's much more realistic to adapt it to a highly specific setting than Fate would be just for the giggles.

    The Lord of the Rings: This is a morality play; I know that many focus on the Fellowship of the Ring, but that's not really what the story is about. Instead, you'll want a high-power drama game that allows for high contrasts in scale of action (because providence will make even the smaller things matter, and has meaningful rules for the internal life of the characters. Concepts such as moral absolutes and corruption are central. Characters are defined in terms of their social position more than their skill-sets. If you weren't crunch-averse I would recommend Burning Wheel, but that being the case, a slight hack of Hero's Banner is what surfaces for me: it'll be to the point, and the epic breadth of campaign play will actually help the setting breathe in a novel way that doesn't conflict with its basic feel.

    A Song of Ice and Fire: Personally, I would probably go for a harsh challenge-game with power struggle, secrets and betrayal, but that'd quickly go beyond "story game", so let's stick to that. We want something with a grey moral world, huge ensemble cast, no unitary plot, but basically a blood opera with characters in harsh opposition to each other's interests, often in tragic manner. The obvious choice is In a Wicked Age, no question: in a campaign context it captures the necessary breadth of storytelling easily, having no difficulty with swapping protagonists in and out, jumping forward in time and so on.

    So yeah: Heroquest, Hero's Banner and In a Wicked Age, apparently.\n

    \n The One Ring is not a particularly simple game; It has a lot of different components designed for different situations. However, it's also an excellent example of controlling the way a game works by constraining the mechanical actions available to the players - when the list of things you can roll for includes "Awe", "Song", "Riddle" and "Courtesy" it's amazing how the game swings around to feel more like Middle Earth. So if you don't feel overly confident in your ability to make a game FEEL like Middle Earth, then I endorse the system as something that gives you carefully curated tools to help with that. (I fall into this category - I LOVE Middle Earth, but I'm not remotely confident in my ability to evoke it consistently. ToR made this easy, if the number of compliments I got from my players are any indication.)

    Burning Wheel is, of course, another take on the angle - you don't need to have Luke tell you that it's basically Middle Earth with the serial numbers filed off (I mean FFS, the Elven Racial Trait is "Grief") but it makes The One Ring look like Basic D&D by comparison, so it's totally off the table. There's also a hack for Mouse Guard (Which is a Burning Wheel relative) called Realm Guard that is about playing Dunedain guarding the ruins of the realm of Arnor, but that's relatively constrained in subject - if you don't want to play Dunedain rangers, the game doesn't have much for you.

    I don't have good suggestions on the others, though I think it might be interesting to try to run ASoIaF in a hacked up version of Houses of the Blooded, that'd be a nontrivial amount of work.

    \n FAE doesn't, in my experience, provide a satisfying advancement scheme in the long term. Characters start quite competent, which is generically appropriate for e.g. a Star Wars game, but it doesn't leave a ton of room for growth in power, so the advancement consists of lateral drift.

    As a result, FAE uses the other common advancement scheme: at regular intervals (by accomplishing Milestone goals), characters change and evolve, mostly by rewriting the freeform abilities (Aspects and Stunts). I like the use of Milestones as flag and pacing mechanic, but the only opportunity for mechanical power advancement is both rare and generic (basically, just add one to an Approach or to your Refresh).

    Compared to similar "growth as change" schemes (e.g. Mouseguard's), the advancement system feels (to me--ymmv, obviously) like it lacks depth.

    Keep in mind that I love FAE! It's great for what it is. But, like other lite games (and more than some), it suffers from lack of depth in the long run.

    \n Like I said in our other conversation, I think that finding a good game to play Star Wars, Lord of the Rings, and - more recently - ASOIAF is pretty much the Holy Grail of gaming for a lot of people.

    As such, it means that you'll never get consensus, and no one will ever be entirely satisfied. People will never stop looking for "it"!

    For Lord of the Rings, I think my Eowyn game is a great contender, and would be easy to make generic. It just involves writing more Key Traits (which already exist in Lady Blackbird hacks, Solar System, First Quest, and my game the Bureau, among others). There are dozens (maybe hundreds?) of them floating around the internet.

    I think Solar System/The Shadow of Yesterday is one of the best "everyone like this" games for romantic heroic adventure. Lady Blackbird and my Eowyn game are both based on that. I wouldn't overlook Solar System/TSoY for this purpose; it's almost the "industry standard" for this kind of gaming.

    The old WEG Star Wars d6 game was great for just, you know, adventure Star Wars. (Except for, maybe, its handling of the Force.) But that's a traditional RPG (just a particularly fun one), so it may not suit your needs.

    ASOIAF is the tricky one. What form does playing this take? Is there a GM? Do we jump around in time, or in locations, or not? Are characters disposable or not? Do we know ahead of time who the heroes are, and who they aren't? How long do we play for? What's the story "about"? How large is the scope of this thing, anyway?

    These are all questions which are relatively easy to answer for Lord of the Rings and Star Wars, but very confusing when it comes to ASOIAF. I could easily see arguing for either side of any of those questions, and the larger plot about the White Walkers even suggests that the story may have, ultimately, a big "Heroes against the Big Bad" story underlaying all the grey-morality intrigue stuff, which complicates matters further.

    There are some interesting AW hacks suitable for this (although I don't know of any that are completed and ready to go), like this one:

    apocalypse-world.com/forums/index.php?topic=7085.0

    Otherwise, I like Eero's suggestion of In a Wicked Age..., that would be an interesting way to do this. (Except that your story would be far less linear than in the novels; that could be a feature or a deal-breaker.)

    \n Stupid question, but isn't the thing Jeff describes just a lingering benefit, insofar as this sort of drama game cares? Sorcerer is one of the first games that had those as a central part of the game's currency, and they're in very prominent (and flexible) place in games like Capes and Solar System. The Apocalypse World "plusses forward" thing is only exceptional in how inflexible it is.

    I mean, establishing that a character trusts another, and having specific mechanics for how much they trust, and how that impacts further events - that's precisely what the Effect rules in Solar System do. (Read the Solar System text, specifically, for this: the older TSoY text has the same principles in there, but they're not generalized yet, dealing mostly with lingering poisons and such.) It's a general rules-mechanical concept for portraying any lingering benefits, which is a great strength for a certain type of story game, as you can naturally have various types of prep and foreshadowing scenes that actually have mechanical impact for the climatic scenes. Also, unlike the AW version, the significance of the lingering benefit is not mechanized in advance, so the lingering situation remains fluid: when you establish trust, for example, you do not know for sure yet whether it will actually come to matter later, and how. (For example: will their trust enable you to do something great together, or will it enable you to stab them in the back?) If it does, though, the rules will tell you how to mechanize the impact. Something like one third of my more recent SS play has been about establishing and moving Effects around, something like that is really a great boon for a classical Forgite drama game just for pacing reasons if nothing else.\n
    I just picked up your Zombie Cinema game from IPR; any suggestions on how to get the best results running it at my gaming club. Same question for Solar System. You can answer me here or send me a PM and I would appreciate it. Thanks :-)
    \nNice to hear that you got ZC; you might appreciate the way it goes about things. Those are good questions. Pretty wide in scope, but I'll lay out a few key points nevertheless:

    For Zombie Cinema, it's pretty fool-proof. The only way it can go awry, pretty much, is if the players are drama-blind and thus resort to trivial sitcom and destructive badgering of the other players instead of building up the story; the worst this will cause is just that the game might be sort of mediocre and pointless, so no big deal even then. This happens to something like 10% of random-pick game groups in my experience, as most people seem to get it without difficulty. For advanced notes on play strategy, you might wish to read my article on the website.

    For Solar System, I recommend that you plan for a short 3-5 session campaign to begin with; it's possible to play the game as a one-shot, but it's really probably only feasible once you have some experience with the system. For subject matter, there are two feasible choices for a first campaign: either play a very minimalistic game with crunch created as needed in real time (very basic crunch, mostly just Keys and Effects and some bonus dice here and there - limit every character to just one Secret at start), set in a fresh setting uniquely interesting to the play group, or play The Shadow of Yesterday - either with the original text or World of Near, as preferred. The former option keeps the game simple enough that the players can do the crunch-development inherent to Solar System without difficulty, while the latter option provides much textual support for the crunch, thus reducing the need to develop your own for every little thing you might wish to do.

    Oh, I don't know if you would really want the thing I speculate about there - when I say "challenge-game" it's just a covert (hopefully less annoying and misleading for some people) reference to Forgite Gamism. What I envision would be something like a mix of Paranoia, Diplomacy and the Amber rpg, with the player characters as political movers and shakers of Westeros, each with their more-or-less conflicting goals.     \n My immediate idea is to develop something like a Diplomacy boardgame, but with various moves corresponding to fictional prompts, which, in return, create fictional material for the game.

    For instance, we might see players A and B successful pull off an invasion of a part of the map, which allows Kingdom X to develop a solution to its famine problem. However, to do so, they have to move a unit off the "financial safety" square on the board, which then instructs the GM to make a "move" (in the AW sense) against one of their characters, threatening them: perhaps one of the funders of their expedition backs out at the last minute, leaving them without financial backing a week before they are supposed to set out.

    Having a larger-scale "boardgame" which messes with the larger setting (starting and ending wars, creating other large-scale effects, influencing immigration and trade) which then interacts with the small-scale stories of the characters at a more "zoomed-in" level could be quite interesting.

    I can even imagine some rules where a character's fictional positioning affects the moves the player is able to make on the board. For instance, perhaps you must have a character reach the Duke of Gooble, have an intimate scene with him, and succeed in a roll in order to be able to - as the player - move one of the units on the map which represents the Dukedom of Gooble. Moving that unit, then, will correspond to changes in the Situation for some of the other characters in the "zoomed-in" story level.

    \n In case the suggestion is not clear, part of what would make the game work is that "secret information" and player-vs-player competition would be happening at one level of remove from the "storytelling" aspects of the game. We "zoom out" to handle large-scale competition, and this forms the backdrop for the in-character scenes, where the players can approach play in a more typical RPG-style collaborative fashion, working together to create drama, good scenes, and vibrant characters.

    Another way to make this work is to have the players who are acting competitively removed from the "collaborative fiction" phases of play. For instance, you and Bob play out a scene in the story. Then Lucy and I get to play a token or make a move on the game board, which is influenced (but in a neutral fashion) by the scene just played out. This allows you and Bob to freely create fiction, while Lucy and I are freed to pursue competition. For the next scene, we switch roles. And so on.

    Using the "Dipomacy" backdrop for the sake of the example, we'd have a board with moving pieces on it. On your turn, you and Bob pick an area of the map where to set a scene, and play it out freely. Once the scene is done, Lucy and I can make moves on the board, but only in the place where the scene took place and adjacent territories. Perhaps the type of scene you end up having affects our decisions, too - I choose to mark your scene as "consequential", which gives me a point of some currency, while Lucy marks a developing relationship between the two nations portrayed by the characters you and Bob chose in the scene, which affects future moves in some other fashion.

    I could see such a framework in action - it would be a relatively weighty and in-depth game, with lots of room to explore and master ramifications, but it would create a world-spanning story like ASOIAF very effectively.
