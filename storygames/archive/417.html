    Oh, and the obvious way is to offer bonus dice. Sorcerer does this (and Sorcerer can be pretty task-based too) by offering a bonus die for adding something "good" to the roll (there's 5 distinct things, like "Good Strategy", "Effectiveness", "Coolness", etc).

    The game Dread by Raf Chandler got me totally into narrating effects because it has something called the "Cool die": Literally Every Roll where you spiced up your description, you get a die, which in that game was a big bonus. Like, imagine for d20 if you got a free extra 1d20 to roll at the same time, and took the higher one: That's how strong the coolness rule was. And that also made it really easy to get into narrating things, from driving a car to researching monsters to asking for directions. It seems like the players would "go overboard" with it, but there were times as well when the players didn't want the cool die as well (not often): They had just described 10 things cool in a row, and were like, "No, fuck it, this time? I Just Hit Him With My Axe."

    So a bonus die to any and every roll is a big helper. Leave it in the middle of the table as a reminder.

    The other thing is, say, when the player "misses", whether its a hit roll or a research roll, let them describe the results. Maybe turn it over to them. Sure, everyone wants a chance to look cool, but they probably also want to fail in their own way: Giving them the power to narrate how they failed makes sure that narration doesn't revert to "whiffs".

    -Andy

    Yeah, I didn't see the "Sheer Folly" thread at the Forge (can you point that out), but here are two additions:

    1) Quality of narration vs Quantity: You can't just push for Quality without Quantity. That's like the myth of spending "quality time" with your family. You should rather encourage a way to get as much quantity of narration as possible, which will also hopefully pull in quality along with it (because at least SOME of your quantities of narration will be cool. No one bats 100%, but encouraging the players to step up to the plate more gives them more opportunities to hit home runs)..

    2) Ah, yeah, after seeing JBR's comments. Some people say "Oh, but the coolness is up to the GM! Or the Coolness is left up to the players! It's too subjective! What if you have a guy who always turtles, but finally comes out to say something cool, vs the person who can come up with cool things all the time!"

    Folks who say this generally:
    A) Are Retarded
    Have shitty gaming groups
    C) Are arguing for the sake of argument
    D) Have never actually played a game with Fanmail, Cool Die, Gift Dice, etc. Because when you do, the nature of those so-called concerns melts like butter. The Shit Works if you Try It. There's no more to say on the matter.

    It would help, of course, to describe conditions on which such a cool die would be awarded, where it wouldn't be awarded, and how the gaming group, personlities of the members etc might influence the cool die. This explanatory text will help the players understand when they should award their fellow players such coolness dice.

    But yeah, I'm a big fan of games that reward you for describing things cool, or involving "tactics", etc. To the point that it's REALLY HARD for me to be involved in a game that doesn't have those mechanics.

    -Andy

    I'm thirding a Fan Mail kind of system - I used them in my WoD game for years without really codifying it - we'd use casino chips or some-such, and the person who got them could use them to re-roll 1 die per token, 1 token per roll. You could also spend your whole shebang for one big success. It was a very play-by-feel system - you could give a bunch of them if someone had a very good, prolonged, scene.

    As Storyteller, I was the only one who had an unlimited supply - everyone else used a set number in circulation. When you spent one to re-roll a die, it went into a pot that was then re-divided at the end of the session or if everyone ran out. Some people hoarded, some didn't. Mostly they flew around the table - it's not only good for the ego of the players, but it encourages a sort of karma system as well.

    Mind you, all of this means that we re-rolled quite a few dice in our days.
