    The big three themes I've seen are:

    * Key phrases (as in Polaris)
    * Giving the GM resources which he has to spend in some way (as in PTA, I gather)
    * Winners and losers in narrative games (as in the Shab Al-Hiri Roach)

    I think it would be interesting to discuss each of these: what's the purpose of using them and what effect it has on play. That sort of thing.

    Graham
  • Mike_Sands
    April 2006 edited April 2006
    I can answer for myself, anyhow.

    Three Dooms has GM spendable resource (kind of) and win loss conditions.

    - The spendable resource is there because when I am GMing (which I usually do), I find this makes my play more fun.

    - The win/loss condition is there as an adjunct to the time condition. The game basically says "you have to do this stuff by this time or you're dead." Which gives you a loss condition. And the win is just to give the winning player a chance to say "oh yeah! I was most heroic!"

    So, not terribly deep reasons, but important ones (the first, especially).

  • Bryan
    April 2006
    PEQ has spendable GM resources in the form of steel points that allow the GM to buy bad guys. As the game moves along, the GM Steel Points become more powerful. The reason that I included thembecause the game is timed as was a two-hour countdown, and I wanted to limit the GM from throwing in too many encounters to make the game unwinnable. I also included them as a tension builder. The players know that the GM has a set amount of points, and they also know they have a set amount of time to complete the game, and because the points initiate time consuming combat, especially as the game moves further along, I felt that their use would increase tension.

    I'm not sure if PEQ is a narrative game (probably because I don't see a huge distinction between gamist and narrative games), but it certainly has elements, and it has winners/losers. Much like Three Dooms, you have to win by a certain time or else you die in a nuke explosion, i.e lose. Also, the team can split up and then if their goals are different (i.e. stop the nuke or save the hostages) you could end up with some players winning and some losing. I was hoping that this would increase the tension of the game. But I also like to win games.

    I'm curious as to any thoughts on whether PEQ is a narrative game? (I'd hate to muddy the discussion here so if you could give me thoughts as whispers that would be cool.)
