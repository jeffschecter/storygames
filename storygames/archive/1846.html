From another thread:
I don't mean to threadcrap, and this might sound a bit hypocritical coming from me. But, Troy, do we really need to encourage more people not already thinking about making designs to make new designs? I think John Kim has taken the lead on this one, with the notion that, in fact, myriad new designs may not actually be a good thing. Maybe not everyone should be a potential game designer. The fact that such a large proportion of RPG players become game designers is, I think, a testament to the fact that there are a lot of terrible RPGs out there.

But there's also a lot of damn good RPGs. Just because game A is broken, doesn't mean we need game B.

I'm not saying we should stop making RPGs. Or even stop having design competitions and the like. But, "Hey, you there, with the cool actual play - make some rules around that!"... that's where I think you've crossed a line from RPGs as an artifact meant to be played several times, and RPG as something created to be played once, by one group, ever.

I think that's fine for people to create true "one shot" RPGs (that is played once, ever by one group). But I think that publishing them all could create a situation where to get the game you want to play, you'd have to pour through so many RPGs that finding the good ones might become nigh impossible.

I think that it's simply too much specialization. Focused design is one thing, but there is something to be said for the fact that I can sit down with a group of people and play Dogs in the Vinyard, because they've all played it before. So I don't have to teach the rules each time to everyone, and so that emergent properties of the game can, well, emerge. Which doesn't always happen if you're alwyas playing a new game each week.

There are loads of good ideas for RPGs in this thread. But, as I've ranted before, concepts are a dime a dozen. As Andy intimates, the good RPG is one that's been completed, and completed well, because it's been playtested, and the writers have done their homework. Let me be clear - the RPGs that come out of the Iron Game Chef competitions are crap. How could they be anything less than crap, given only a week to construct the game, and no time to playtest? The award is given to the game that sucks the least, and maybe has the greatest potential to be forged into something cool. As, for example, Jason did with Roach, or Tim with Mountain Witch.

Now, fortunately, this thread may not produce any designs that were not already planned - it's far easier to talk about ideas than it is to work on them. But...I dunno, let's just try to keep in mind that if you want to be a designer to try to set a very high bar for yourself. Only start a design if it's not just a cool idea, but you have a strong vision of what it might be like in the end, and you're willing to really put in the effort to make it not just an also-ran game, but one that really adds something to the constellation of RPGs that already exist.

I don't know if this'll slow anyone down, but I hope it at least gives them a pause to think.
Now, this isn't to point a flashlight at Mike: If the dogpiling starts, I'll jump out of your monitor like Tony Jaa and kick your head with the soft part of my inner forearm.*

What I love about recent trends in gaming and game design is that I realize that I'm of two or multiple minds about any, ANY, phenomena. And here I both find myself violently disagreeing with Mike's pronouncements, and at the same time I find myself nodding my head hard, as if I was self-rocking at a Dream Theater concert.

I'll go deeper in a post or two, but I wanted to throw that out there for discussion.

    I'll elaborate my one-off in another thread: I'd like to see a better-articulated distinction between games designed to be shared for free and games designed to be implemented as a product to be sold.

    One of the "problems" of the recent revolutions in printing technology that makes it so damn easy to publish a book is... well, they make it so damn easy to publish a book. The bar, once high, has been dropped really low, and now if you've got Word and an email account, you can publish a book. There are no gatekeepers. Which, sure, means that some books that might not have been published before are being published now, and we are the better for it. However, for every one diamond, there is a hundred roughs. As consumers we used to be able to rely on the publishing industry, composed of editors and financiers, who determined "quality" in terms of what could be expected to return the investment with profit. The only books we saw were the result of somebody somewhere deciding to put a substantial amount of money behind them to get them out there. And while not perfect, it was better than nothing whatsoever.

    So as game designers, we need to become our own gatekeepers. The decision to publish should be a real decision, considered carefully. It requires a goodly dose of humility and to be able to ask yourself, "Is this really worth asking money for?" There are a whole boatload of options besides publishing for sale, but these are not very well explored or very well recognized. There is the free pdf download, but there is also creating games to be run at conventions (Graham's Meatloaf LARP comes to mind) and games to play with and talk about with friends online and off. Hell, there's publishing the game through Lulu at cost. There's also the very real category of games that can be created in order to better understand the creation of a game, to better inform the next design. We already do this, but we have a nasty habit of then publishing them as completed games when, really, they were stepping stones to creating completed games.

    One thing that I keep coming back to as I think about this is that a good game product has more than one awesome idea. I've seen a lot of games -- both indie and mainstream -- that are little more than one really cool idea mixed in with a lot of material "borrowed" from other designs. The Fantasy Heartbreaker, the "Like D&D but Better," the "Sorcerer but with different dice," are all examples of this. I tend to refer to them as half-games or third-games. They need just that little bit more oomph to make them really cool new things that I'd want to buy. A good, solid product has multiple new, creative ideas that are themselves related to each other in new, creative ways (and are preferably implemented and presented in new, creative ways). A good product should be a knot of new, useful ideas, rather than one new, useful idea that's tied up in a lot of old, useful ideas.

    A quick test of this is whether or not the whole of the game can be summed up in a couple sentences. Sure, any game can be pitched with a tagline, but if the tagline is all of the innovative content in the game, then it's not really a viable product. If I give you money for the text transcript of what can be communicated in ten minutes of dialog, what the hell am I buying with my cash?

    Of course, it's hard to make such a decision about one's own game. It's especially hard if you're getting into this because you want to publish a game before you have any sense of the game you want to publish. I'd really prefer to see our community stress game designers as the creative folks that people want to be and emulate, and the game publishers to be the addled few who decide they're going to offer their wares for actual cash.

    Hey Max,

    ...designers buy each other's games and don't necessarily play them, which creates a small demand bubble.     Word. Back in the early 90's comic book publishers betrayed comic book fans by appealing to materialism and fetishism over solid entertainment value. And as fans started to lose interest the publishers ramped up their schedules, publishing more and more "must have" titles in an effort to extract the same amount of revenue from a shrinking customer base. And somewhere around 1993 the bottom dropped out of the market and 7000 comic book stores went under in 18 months.

    Feel the heat of your wallet and tell me if there's any denying that we're selling each other an increasing amount of product. Look into your gut and tell me the entertainment value alone of the games you've bought recently is solid enough to sustain twice as much purchasing in the next twelve months.

    Are people designing games for a small group of peers to enjoy or for those guys plus a wider market?

    A comparison can be made to the maturation of video game genres. When a genre (like real-time strategy, or first-person shooter, or whatever) is introduced, the games released have a broad range of features. With each successive generation of games in the genre the feature set achieves greater standardization, and fans who aren't being served drop off for other interests. The fanbase gets more and more hardcore. So what you get is an industry in which some game publishers (Xbox and PS3) are in a very high stakes competition for hardcore fans within established genres, and other publishers are working to create new genres (Nintendo).

    Tell me you're not seeing the rise of a hardcore "indie" fanbase with increasingly strong feature expectations. Rather than an expectation of design excellence and entertainment value, the hardcore expects specific mechanics, or a specific kind of graphical production. I dunno, publishing indie games to the hardcore expectations of traditional gamers probably does result in sales for the games in question, but it certainly doesn't help grow the indie market. It catalyzes hardcore expectations into it that hasten the drop off of those who aren't being served.
