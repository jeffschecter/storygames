\n What games (and what playstyles, if you're talking about a game that has a lot of approaches) require more/less/different kinds of trust?

Most games, it's important you don't act like a jerk.
Many games, it's important that you don't use the freedom you have to drive play towards skeevy topics.
Some games, you need to not laugh at the premise.
Some games, you need to be able to tamp down your emotional reactions.
Some games, it's important to play to fit into the GM's story.
Some games, you have to try really, really hard to act like someone you're not.
Some games, you have to be honest about what you like and don't like.
Some games, you have to listen for clues about what people like and don't like.
Some games, you have to be able to take criticism.
Some games, you have to be able to give criticism.

Which games are which, and how much?

    \n "Not so much a "gross" generalization as a "completely wrong in every way forever" generalization."
    \n
    \nI'm not so sure. I think Teataine is on to something. Several times I have endured the situation as GM where I have gone to the trouble to build something that I think will satisfy the players, only to have specific players (yes, very specific people) deliberately go out of the way to grand stand and derail the session just to show how much more clever they are. Possibly.
    \n
    \nHere's a specific example: it is really hard to run a coherent split party and make sure that everyone gets a fair go and ensuring that no one is advantaged or disadvantaged by hearing what is going on in the other half of the party. So having characters haring off out of the party and doing their own thing (for no good story reason other than they want to make their character do things) is either deliberately or ignorantly disruptive behaviour. It is, in short, childish.
    \n
    \nThe player clearly places trust in a GM in a conventional RPG. But the GM also places an awful lot of trust in the players not to bugger things up. Pretty soon you start to think about ways of controlling the wayward players, rather than trying to all share in a mutual experience.

    Posted By: RyWhat games (and what playstyles, if you're talking about a game that has a lot of approaches) require more/less/different kinds of trust?

    Most games, it's important you don't act like a jerk.
    \nI assume you're referring to in-character acting, not OoC? If the latter, I'd amend this rule to "In all games it's imperative you don't act like a jerk." I would liken this to playing a villain in a theatre or movie production. Just because your character is a jerk or has crossed the moral event horizon, doesn't mean you behave like that out of character. In and out of character separation is key, methinks.

    Games where this is imperative: Wraith, Vampire: the Masquerade, any game that incorporates player vs player conflicts. Shock would be another, just because you are antagonist to player B doesn't give you licence to be an asshat.\n

    Many games, it's important that you don't use the freedom you have to drive play towards skeevy topics.
    \nAt least not without the groups explicit consensus after explicitly explaining what you wish to do. This is a good one. I'd say that full disclosure of the topics you intend to explore prior to play is an absolute prerequisite.

    Games where this is something to watch for: Vampire, Wraith, D&D. The content here should be tailored to the comfort zone of the group. Wraith and Vampire in particular are ones where you need to be careful of this, given their premise and settings.\n

    Some games, you need to not laugh at the premise.
    Some games, you need to be able to tamp down your emotional reactions.
    \nAgreed here, the first one is setting dependent, and the second I would perhaps amend to not allowing your emotional reactions to spill out in the form of problematic behaviour.\n

    Some games, it's important to play to fit into the GM's story.
    \n

    Some games, you have to try really, really hard to act like someone you're not.
    \nWhen playing an antagonist in Shock, the Shadow in Wraith the Oblivion, characters like Mages, Prometheans, Werewolves and Vampires in world of Darkness. As the GM when running unsympathetic villain and antagonist NPCs in most games.\n

    Some games, you have to be honest about what you like and don't like.
    Some games, you have to listen for clues about what people like and don't like.
    Some games, you have to be able to take criticism.
    Some games, you have to be able to give criticism.

    Which games are which, and how much?

    \n I agree entirely, Paul.
    \n
    \nWhen you have a GM and separate players - separation of duties - you almost automatically have a power struggle. If you have a great bunch of friends all on the same wavelength the division of labour and willingness to let the other fellow do his job makes this traditional role playing incredibly satisfying. Enough to build an entire industry, in fact.
    \n
    \nBut if you don't have harmonious participants it can be a nightmare for everyone. Dictatorial GMs and plot-destructive characters.
    \n
    \nStory gaming, where the roles are often shared, goes a long way to diffuse this, I think. To talk to the thread start: trust is easier to give out when you actually share the same responsibilities and therefore can understand what the other fellow is going through.
