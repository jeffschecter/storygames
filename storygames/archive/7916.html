\n So, taking some cues from D&D 4E and Anima: Shadow of Omega, I began thinking about a playing card based RPG for an attempt at making a Final Fantasy game to my liking. Those of you that know me know that this is my holy grail of game design. I'm not sure what I'd do if I ever made this game, but I love trying.

So, I came up with a few ideas and saw that it would work well in a Solar System (The Shadow of Yesterday) framework. I've decided that each character will have three of four possible Pools that match up to the four suits in playing cards. Choosing which Pool to leave out will have a lot of nice characterization and customization in my mental picture of what's going on. But, I'm sort of stuck at the beginning.

What four Pools could I create that the absence of one doesn't make a character impotent? I don't want anything like Physical Social Mental Willpower because that lack of color is disturbing to me. Also, what does it really mean if one of those is missing?

I was thinking of something like Shadow for one of the Pools. This would give it a nice concept as being a power source and I could see making some neat Refreshes based on it. But I'm stuck.

I don't want the four elements, but if the four end up mapping in a way that allows an implication of the four elements, that would be great, because then elemental spells could be keyed in on this.

Also, I want the Pool that the player chooses to remove to be a weakness for that character. Not in a tactical sense, but in a dramatic or narrative sense. If I don't have Romance, that should be an indication.

Ideas? Please help!

    \n Final Fantasy? I'm with you. Four Pools:

    Inhuman: Many Final Fantasy characters are inhuman in some manner, ranging from catgirls to animals or monsters or living slot machines. Useful for having special monster powers and attack modes.
    Past: Most characters have a deep and convoluted backstory that is drawn in as the story unfolds. Useful for knowing exotic fighting styles and possessing special weapons.
    Passion: Often Final Fantasy characters are driven not by reason, but by simple dreams or passions taken to extremes. Useful for going gonzo on your enemies, limit breaking all over the place.
    Bishi/Moe: Some FF characters seem to mostly get by on looks and panache, never mussing their hairstyle. Useful for never making a fool of yourself and not being the butt of stupid jokes, and in general being like a protagonist. And of course, the scripted battles will all be won because you light up and your innocent magibabble chosen one powerz save the day.

    I'd make it so a character might lack more than one of these, though. Perhaps make some more and let the player pick 1-3 for his character, or something like that.

    \n A TSOY Pool isn't a D&D style "stat" like Strength or Dexterity in my mind, but more of a way of the author to ask for certain kinds of scenes to occur on a consistent basis that compliment, intersperse, or bookend the story the group is trying to tell. Given that, I think I've determined the Pools I'm going to use (for now, at least - best to get a set and try them out than to worry over it for too long).

    So, given that, the kinds of scenes (or example scenes) I want to see occur in a FF style story game are:\n
      \n
    • Questioning your loyalty to a leader gone mad with power with your compatriots
    • Romancing your love, or bemoaning your separation from you love
    • Visiting a town from your past and renewing friendships or dealing with rivals
    • Participating in festivals, contests, gladiator arenas, or gambling halls
    • Explaining to someone about your history or revealing a secret about yourself
    • Completing side quests for people, so long as they involve interaction
    \nI wanted to include "Montage scenes" - (preparing for battle, repairing a vessel, researching vital information, "level" building), but, since that doesn't involve any interaction with PCs or NPCs and is kind of hand-wavey, I'm leaving it out.

    These are what I can think of right now. Based on that, the Pools should Refresh on those actions and should indicate that the Abilities come from this source. So:\n
      \n
    • Past - Visit someone or someplace from your past. Reveal a secret about yourself. Tell your story to someone.
    • Punk - Question the government or its leaders. Defy an authority figure in most any situation.
    • Passion - Find a new love, or continue to woo one. Commiserate with your friends over lost or unavailable love.
    • Pursuit - Take on a job for someone for the heck of it. Enter a contest that you aren't already trying to win. Challenge someone to a friendly game.
    \nThoughts?

    \n This is sort of a thought dump; items may contradict each other.

    You get three out of four "main" Pools during character creation. These will map directly to three out of four suits (and are the same mapping for everyone). There may be other Pools, like a Job Pool that affect other actions. Combat may end up being a subset of normal resolution.

    When you start a scene, you're dealt three cards. Based on those cards, you'll know that you have a strong hand or a weak hand towards certain actions. Based on what you want to do in the scene, but before you use the cards for any sort of resolution, you can describe your actions (I stumble towards you, with murder on my mind, weak from a night's deliberation; I counter your every argument, my words fueled with malice and forethought) to accentuate the pull you made. As players get used to this, things that might have called for rolls in the past are ignored and you move towards drawing cards only when you say, "Oh, no, you don't!"

    The cards to which you have no main Pool for can act as helper cards but can't be the lead card. I still think you can have Abilities based on non-Pools - you just won't be able to augment them with Pool points.

    Damage in Bringing Down the Pain will actually come from an elemental label attached to your Abilities or your Pools, I'm not sure. Shadow, Holy, Fire, Earth, Water, Air, etc. These elements will have conditions that need to be met to recover from the wounds.

    When you play a card, your Ability modifier will allow you to add more cards of the same suit (or half the value of a helper suit). So, an Ability at Grand Master would allow you to add in four more cards to come to your total value. The math will have to be worked on.

    \n Well, as for the cards tell you what to do thing (which I'm trying to steal from Best Friends, if that helps), I was trying to make it be something like this:

    Card draw: 10 Hearts, 2 Clubs, 5 Diamonds

    So, you'd know immediately that some Ability based on Hearts (ex. Seduction) would be likely to win without Bringing Down the Pain, and an Ability based on Clubs (ex. Spear-fighting) would get trashed. An Ability based on Diamonds (ex. Bribery) would have an even chance. These aren't absolutes, but high likelihoods.

    Shadow
