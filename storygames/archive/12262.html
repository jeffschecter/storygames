\n This is a simple hack to Lady Blackbird to get in some of that Otherkind goodness. If you feel like the genre deserves some more screw-ups and complicated situations, give it a try.

Everything about the game remains as written; only the resolution rules are modified slightly.

1. When an obstacle comes up, the GM no longer decides on its difficulty. Instead, she decides how complicated the situation is. It is either Simple, Challenging, Complex, or A Fiasco. This determines how many dangers apply to the action.

Simple: No Dangers
Challenging: One Danger
Complicated: Two Dangers
A Fiasco: Three Dangers

You probably shouldn't roll at all for Simple obstacles, but if you need to for some reason, you can. Most tasks are either Challenging or Complicated. If you can't think of enough Dangers, or you think of more than the number you originally thought, that's fine: it's more or less complicated, as follows.

Conditions play into determining how complicated something is, of course.

2. The acting player names their goal--what they're trying to achieve--the GM and other players name the Dangers.

3. You roll the dice. Each "6" is a hit. You can spend your hits as follows:

* Spend a hit to achieve your goal.
* Spend a hit to avoid a Danger.
* Spend a hit to establish an Advantage. (See below.)

4. If you didn't roll as many 6's as you would have liked, you can flip your results: ignore those "6's"--now all your "1's" are hits instead. If you choose to use your "1's" as hits, you will suffer a Condition, however!

5. An Advantage is a one-time Tag, something that results from an action. You can use an Advantage to roll an extra die--but then the Advantage is erased. If you spend multiple hits, you can create an Advantage that's worth multiple dice.

---

So, for example, say you're attempting a Challenging task: you're trying to ram through the hold's doors with the Owl, escaping from the Hand or Sorrow. The Danger is that Naomi, who's holding on to the back of the Owl, flies off into space.

You roll your dice, and you end up with one "6" and three "1's".

You could accept the one hit (from the single "6"), and use it to escape, but lose Naomi.

Or you can use your "1's" as hits, escaping from the Hand (1) with Naomi (2), and using your third hit to establish an Advantage (it's flying like a bullet of the hold!). But the Owl is now Busted & Leaking (a Condition). However, that Advantage will give you an extra die if there is pursuit from the Hand.

That's it!

    \n I did a similar thing, referencing Lady Blackbird, Otherkind, and the Burning Wheel.

    The way mine works is when you endeavor to do something ("endeavor" is the technical term in the system for the moments when rolls are called for), the GM names Obstacles and Dangers. Obstacles are things that would prevent you from accomplishing your endeavor. For instance, you're endeavoring to fight off armed assassins who have just attacked in your sleep:

    Obstacle: you're outnumbered.
    Obstacle: you're unarmed.
    Obstacle: you're surprised.
    So this endeavor has 3 Obstacles.

    Dangers are unpleasant things that might happen whether you succeed or fail in your endeavor. To continue the previous example, there's certainly the Danger that you'll be injured.

    What you do is you roll your appropriate dice pools looking for hits. A 4 or higher is a hit, as in BW. Then you "spend" hits to "address" Obstacles and Dangers. If you do not address all the Obstacles, then your endeavor fails (thus the number of Obstacles is the same thing as the Obstacle number in BW). If you do not address a Danger, then it comes to pass. And, of course, you narrate how you address all those things.

    So, in essence, it's the same as Otherkind in that you have to balance a limited resource between success in your endeavor and avoiding unpleasant tangential outcomes.

    My favorite part of this is the whole Obstacle idea. It solves the problem for me, as GM, in arbitrarily setting difficulties. This way, I've got concrete principles to judge from.

    (Mine's also got advantages and tags and stuff, but they let you reroll misses or explode sixes instead of just adding extra dice)

    \n Paul, what I meant was two-fold.

    Firstly, the changes you made to Otherkind Dice are the same ones made in Danger Rolls, so describing it as LB+DR is actually more accurate than LB+OD. Is that distinction meaningful? Probably not, so the real point is:

    In original Otherkind Dice (link for those who need it), the results for Goal and Danger are different numbers, with Dangers having 1-3 comes true and 4-6 does not come true. So having Goal be distinct from Dangers is important, and gives you something to think about when you roll 3s and 4s.

    In both Ghost/Echo and Danger Rolls, the numbers are the same for both. Having a separate category for achieving your Goal in G/E means that failure is always a danger on the table. In Danger Rolls, the player still states his Goal, but when the GM states Dangers, those may or may not stop the character from achieving that Goal. If a Danger comes true that stops the character from achieving the Goal, he doesn't achieve the Goal. But if none of the stated Dangers would stop the character from achieving that Goal, then there is no chance of failure, and it becomes a question of how much it costs. Or, two different Dangers could stop the character from achieving the player's Goal, where it's always just the one in G/E. Do you see the distinction? It makes things more flexible for the GM. Having the die results similar for Goal and Dangers is the only thing I dislike about G/E, though I still play it the way it's written.

    I've fooled around with the OD/DR+BW thing in my own game designs, too. I really like the idea that each "success" provides a concrete result, instead of counting up successes to determine a result. Much like how an investigation roll in Storming the Wizard's Tower works -- each success gives you one clue or piece of information. So each success on an action roll lets you avoid one danger, and if you avoid them all, you do what you set out to do, exactly, and you don't have to worry about adding them up. The system I wrote was closer to Marshall's than your LB hack, but LB gives you more dice to roll than BW does, and the option to use 1s with a Condition is pretty ingenious.

    \n Sure, here's a bunch of examples:

    1.
    Okay, Joe is playing Razor. He sees his enemy Brodie look out around a corner. Razor goes around the corner and shoots him. It's early morning and Brodie has just opened up his illegal chop-shop's garage doors.

    Joe's goal is to kill Brodie, and Razor's action is to shoot Brodie. GM says the dangers are: "You fail to kill Brodie. You cause unintended damage. Brodie unloads on you and you get shot in the leg, around the knee area."

    Joe rolls his dice and gets two successes, so he can avoid two dangers. He chooses to cause unintended harm. So he kills Brodie, and doesn't get shot himself, but he hits a propane tank, it explodes, and the garage is engulfed in flames. Razor books it.

    In this instance, it might be better to have the possible results for the Goal (kill Brodie) include partial success, just like in OD or G/E. I don't really see how having a result of "the danger partially comes true and remains" would add to this example.

    2.
    Razor is on the roof of a building, being pursued by zombies. His player says he will jump across a narrow alley to the next building, in order to escape. The GM chooses dangers: "Dangers are: You fall. You don't escape."
    Joe gets one success. He can choose to jump and fall, thus escaping the zombies, or he can not fall and not escape the zombies. What does that mean? Perhaps Razor falls short of the other building, but falls through a window. The zombies can't get into this building, however.

    Back up. How about instead the GM says: "Dangers are: You lose something. You suffer harm. You wind up in a bad position." Let's say Joe gets three successes, and so he avoids all dangers. The GM can't veto his narration without a danger coming true, so he can describe his character escaping. But what if these dangers come true?
    "You lose something." Either the GM or the player picks something. Maybe he drops his backpack, maybe he loses consciousness. He probably escapes.
    "You suffer harm." Maybe he twists an ankle on the jump over. Or maybe he falls to the street below. If he's too hurt to get up, he probably hasn't escaped the zombies.
    "You wind up in a bad position." If that bad position is him on the roof surrounded by zombies, then he obviously hasn't escaped. If he jumps through a window and finds himself surrounded by gun-toting thieves, he's probably escaped the zombies.

    In this example, the player describes his character's actions, and the GM interjects with the dangers that come true. The GM can describe the dangers in great detail, or vaguely, whatever. They'll be detailed when you actually introduce them.

    How about a third version? Razor jumped, now it's my turn. Dangers are: "You fall short and hit a balcony. You fall short and hit the ground. You don't jump." Hey, that's pretty strange, isn't it? What happens if I roll no successes? I don't jump, but instead I trip over the edge, hit a balcony on the building I started on, then fall off that and hit the ground. If I only get one success, I could still jump and hit both, but probably I want to trip and fall onto a balcony. If I get two successes, only one danger comes true. So maybe I'm just standing there at the edge, having realized the jump is too far. Shit, now what? Or I could jump to a balcony across the alley. That might be a better option. If I get three successes, I just narrate my character doing exactly what I want him to do.

    3.
    And then one of us falls. Dangers are: "You die. You're crippled. You're unconscious." Damn, that's rough. Obviously the first success will go to avoid dying. No point in avoiding being crippled if I'm dead, right? If I get two successes, I've got to choose between being crippled and unconscious. If the zombies are still coming, I may not want to be unconscious. That's an example of staggered dangers.

    \n So, the challenge with taking the "Danger Rolls" approach (no character goal) to this particular hack is two-fold:

    1. It's hard to decide how to determine "success" versus failure. A minimum numbers of hits rolled is a good solution, I suppose: if you rolled at least one hit, or if you rolled at least two hits, you give up your Pool dice. Would have to playtest this one.

    2. Issues of GM control and player agency. My feeling is that the default I've outlined, which includes a player goal is more "heroic" in feel: as long as you can name a reasonable goal for your character, you have pretty good odds of achieving it. It's just a question of how much you'll suffer or how much trouble you'll cause along the way. I like this for a game like Lady Blackbird; the Danger Rolls variant gives the GM a lot more leeway to make things harder on the players, but I'm not sure that's necessary for this game.     \nGMing this Hack

    To run Lady Blackbird using this hack of the rules, here are some guidelines:

    1. Most of the time (and especially at the beginning of the game, or, later, at the beginning of a new situation), make actions Challenging: name a Danger, and roll the dice.
    2. However, upon failure, escalate the situation: the complexity of subsequent attempts will involve a new danger.

    For instance, in the case of the Owl trying to escape from the brig, a failure might bring a whole bunch of stormtroopers/guards down on the heads of the party. That means the next roll will have an extra danger, reflecting the actions of the guards.

    As situations escalate, they become more complex, and more dangers are brought to bear upon the heroes.

    3. Tremendous Challenge: Optional

    Sometimes, the players will attempt goals that are reasonable but tremendously difficult or unlikely--they'll overreach themselves. When this happens, the GM has another tool at her disposal: the Obstacle.

    The Obstacle is just a special type of Danger: unless it is avoided/circumvented, the goal cannot be reached. Obstacles are added in on top of the regular dangers present.

    Throw in one Obstacle for really hard stuff, two Obstacles for something that's practically impossible. In a really unlikely situation, you could have three Obstacles.

    Use Obstacles very sparingly--maybe just once a session or so, as seems fitting.

    That's how I would do it, anyway.
