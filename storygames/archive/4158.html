    Posted By: LiminautWhy I don 't like T&J That Much ...

    Let me give an character to try to get at what I don't like. Note to people who haven't read T&J: starting characters start with 10 pts of Qualities (non-super abilities) and 6 pts of powers. Roll 2d6 and add the total of Qualities and Powers applicable.

    First Character: Thug with Bat: +4 with a baseball bat. Now, TwB will pasturize almost every example character listed in the T&J. Most of the example characters have +2 or +0 in their base combat. On 2d6, +2 (+4 vs +2) is a huge advantage. Looking at the example characters most of them have most of their points sunk into Qualities and Powers that basically function as color. For instance, Snow Owl's flight doesn't help the guy at all in resolving conflicts (Flight at +0). It's just a way for the player to describe SO showing up on the scene.

    Why does this matter to me? When I play a system I want to be able pump the fuel lines with nitro step on the gas and GO! I feel like with T&J to play characters like in the book I've got to play three-speed Schwins. TwB can give most of the book characters a lot of grief, let alone the high-octane critters I normally come up with.

    They way I've seen T&J kind of work is by actually throwing away the system. The GM I'm playing with looks at our abilities and makes a call; no dice, no rolls. So "decribe your character and the GM tells you what happens" is a pretty good alternative to T&J as I've seen it work.
    \nHe comments that the system doesn't seem like it could take a lot of heavy lifting. PDQ, I think, is all about using your Qualities in a way that applies them to the situation. You fight on your terms, not the bad guy's. PDQ does not have a 'the terms' (combat works such-and-such, arguments work this-a-way), it has your terms and your opponent's terms.

    In this case, you fight the Thug With Bat on your terms, not by standing in front of him swinging your own bat. You make his Qualities less important (while at the same time the GM is trying to make them more important). Truth & Justice has a bit about how a fight between Superman and Batman would go down, it always makes me smile.

    The T&J game I played in had the Orange Ogre as a reoccurring villain, he WAS Thug With Bat (or rather, Thug With Supersteroids), and he would always get a good clobber in before we reworked the situation to our advantage. (Unfortunately, I played the clobberin' type, so I was usually the bait. Ouch.) There have been multiple times when playing PDQ where I said, "Well, I guess I could make this situation like *this*, I hope it doesn't break." I don't think it ever has.

    \n Hello Chad,

    I've finally figured out what bugs me about T&J. Basically, human-scale Qualities and super-scale Powers are the same scale when it comes to resolving conflicts. +4 Good with Bat is the same as +4 Super-Kung-Fu when it comes to rolling the dice.     I think that's why I like ZoZ, at least on reading. Magic is seriously magic. It's not just a roll but a catch and a cost and if you're willing to pay the cost you can do amazing things.

    \n I am far, far away from my copy of T&J at the moment so my comments are, perforce, going to be a bit general. But I know Liminaut, and I play in the same campaign as Liminaut, and I think - THINK, mind you - that I have at least a bit of an understanding of what Liminaut is talking about here.

    Here is the problem as I see it - bear in mind that it may be specific to the way our campaign is played, and being far away from my rules set I may get some citations wrong - so don`t yell (insert smiley face of your choice here).

    The big difficulty that I see with T&J comes in the stacking of multiple qualities and powers that allow you to hit some pretty impressive numbers - to the point that the difficulty numbers in T&J become fairly meaningless in some cases. This is particularly a problem in combat because it appears, from some of the material written in the rules, that as long as you have a superpower to base your attack on, you can load all the normal everyday qualities you want on it and they give you the normal benefits.

    As a sort of general example, let me modify Liminaut`s TwB character to TwBB - ``Thug with Bat and Battlesuit`` Spend a couple of points to give yourself a Good quality Battlesuit. This, if I recall, gives you the ability to take a certain number of regular qualities in addition to your Battlesuit ability (I don`t remember offhand how many). Use those quality points to buy ``baseball bat`` at whatever level. Now convert all your additional super power points to regular qualities (again I don`t remember what the point discount is for converting superpowers to regular qualities). Spend all those points, plus your regular quality points, on qualities that make you good at hitting things with a baseball bat ``Professional Ballplayer``, ``Hit things with a large aluminum bat with unerring ferocity`` etc. etc.

    What happens now is that you have a character who can lead with a superpower - ``Battlesuit`` for both attack and defense, and can stack a whole mess of quality modifiers onto Battlesuit. The character has more levels of Qualities than your average character, which means he can take more damage than your average character, and the character can crank out modifiers way in excess of +6.

    This strategy does not have to limit itself to beating people up. For example take a character with the superskill ``Super investigation`` at ``Good`` and a whole mess of investigative skills that can be stacked with it, and you have a character who will blow the doors off of any mystery scenario in one or two rolls.

    It seems that an easy solution to this problem is 1) limit how Qualities can stack with superpowers, 2) make the optional higher difficulty levels non-optional.

    Anyway, my two Canadian cents from Rougemont.

    - Shosuro-san

    Posted By: SweeneyToddAnecdotally, I have to agree that Snow Owl comes off as kind of a wimp in the sample combat. I don't know how much of that is me.
    \nOn Snow Owl, Super-Wimp

    He's going up against two opponents at once, so I'd expect him to struggle at least a little bit. As with most game systems, getting outnumbered sucks, unless there's something that lets you deal with multiple numbers easily.

    In T&J's case, that "multiple numbers" thing is all about the super scale vs. normal scale... most heroes can simply walk through an undifferentiated mob of normal scale folks with a single roll. When there's a super in the mix, it gets a bit tougher.

    Snow Owl's outnumbered for the first part of the fight, with the super foe Bob Brick having a fairly twinked out build for a minor villain: Super-Armor and Super-Strength. He's going to dish out ludicrous amounts of Damage Ranks and be able to suck up Damage like nobody's biz, so he has to be defeated more via Failure Ranks. Snow Owl does NOT act optimally for much of the fight, which helps to illustrate the workings of the system.

    But once he does gets his act together, it's pretty interesting: he neutralizes the normal-scale dude pretty much for the rest of the fight with a simple use of his Frost Manacles. Then he gets creative and whacks Bob Brick with failure results rather than damage results. He takes the day.

    On Problems For How I'd Run Things

    That said, I still think it should have been a fair-er fight than it was, and this is due in part to the implementation of Super-Strength, and whether PCs are normal-scale or super-scale when it comes to sucking up punishment.

    Super-Strength lets you add the TN of your Power Rank to damage upon successful attacks, period. This sort of bonus -- adding the TN to your damage ranks -- is something every other attack power in the game only gets to apply to non-living, normal-scale opponents. I'm comparing this to the (Something) Blast power as a baseline. That power requires you to spend a Hero Point to apply super-scale damage to living targets. I'm not clear on why Super-Strength doesn't have the same requirement, and one of my first house rules would be to require a Hero Point expenditure to treat your Super-Strength as super-scale vs. living targets.

    You then have the question of what scale PCs are by default. By watching Bob Brick beat on Snow Owl, it's clear that the default answer is: PCs are normal scale damage receivers unless they have a Power that says otherwise. Invulnerability explicitly states that it sets its possessor on the super-scale, getting rid of the extra Ranks of damage from Super-Strength (the Super-Strength writeup in my copy does not say that the extra damage is only vs. normal-scale, though that may be inferred by the behavior of other powers). Super-Armor's writeup does not explicitly state that it sets its possessor on the super-scale, though one could make the argument that it does.

    Looking at all of this together, I'd say that I'm fine with Super-Strength not costing the extra HP, but that all PCs are considered super-scale by default, or that PCs are considered normal-scale by default but Super-Strength requires the extra HP like all the other attack powers do.

    A small tweak either way, but something that I think needs to be foregrounded in the considerations of a GM prepping to run T&J.

    \n There's two other specific powers behavior issues in T&J that I want to highlight. At least one of these may have been addressed by errata but I haven't had a chance to look for that sort of stuff yet.

    Intense Training

    The math in my copy of T&J doesn't work out for Intense Training: treating Average as 1 for the purpose of calculations, you take two times the power's modifier, then add 2, to get the points of Qualities you gain from sacrificing a power slot.     Super-Speed

    Nothing else in the game offers multiple actions the way that Super-Speed does. The closest we get is a "multiple targets" thing, where the character takes a downshift for each additional target. So my immediate urge is to limit the way in which Super-Speed can take its multiple actions.
