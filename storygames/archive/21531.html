\n Over on G+, Dan Maruschak laments that many rpg groups will not or cannot follow rpg rules as written. I said\n
\u201cIn most life contexts, believing written rules and procedures is of dubious value. When you enter a new community, you\u2019re usually better to follow what others do rather than what the rules say you should do. The real rules \u2014 those that most people follow in practice \u2014 are unlikely to be exactly those that are written down.\u201d
\nto which he replied\n
\u201cWhich is why I find it so aggravating that that \u201cfollow the written rules of a game\u201d is such a hard sell. People can get no-rules everywhere else, why demand no-rules here, too?\u201d
\nIn other words, Dan is asking why people don\u2019t take rpg play as a chance to enter a well-defined magic circle \u2014 to play, for a change, in an environment defined by rules.

I think there are several reasons in play, and I've tried to capture them in a blog post.

Anyone think I've missed any? Or missed some nuance of why some reason is particularly compelling?

    \n As long as this doesn't lead to another throw down: this looks like one of those 'can of worms' topics', but here's my two-pence worth anyway.

    I find, as I often do, a big divide here between classic, rules-heavy games like D&D, CoC, RQ, etc and modern, rules-light games like Fiasco, Monsterhearts, Cthulhu Dark, etc. I think it's pretty understandable that the more rules there are, the more likely that one of the reasons you name in your blog post will come into play and cause a rule to be forgotten, skipped, bypassed, altered, etc. For the most part, I've only found rule skipping happens when time pressure applies: "Oh heck, we've only got half an hour left of this session but no-one's met the requirements to trigger the end-game; does anyone mind if we just skip to the end-game without meeting the requirements? Or fudge it so that we do meet the requirements?"

    I think this is just typical human behaviour when any social group engages with a set of pre-written rules for their own enjoyment: the rules of football are hardly adhered to at all in a friendly kick about in the park, except for the most fundamental requirements. On the other hand, I'm sure I'd be really annoyed if I sat down to a boardgame and we didn't play by the rules as written, so I guess it also depends on where your tolerances lie.

    I expect the rules of an RPG to be followed pretty closely, unless there is a clearly compelling reason not to, such as the afore-mentioned time pressure or, the other popular one, constraints imposed by the medium, e.g. playing online as opposed to playing face-to-face. This is all conscious choice rather than 'forgetting' though; I would associate the latter with rules that don't have an obvious purpose, are tangential to the other mechanics or seem to run counter to the tone of the game. The rules I forget most often are Tension in Dead of Night and conflict weighting in Hot War, both systems I love to bits, but when I'm in a hurry, those are the rules I'm most likely to forget to explain to new players or just forget to implement them after explaining them, They don't directly impact the game, in most cases, so if I forget them, it doesn't fundamentally change the game, it just means some opportunities get missed.

    \n The truism is, we as a subculture have trained ourselves into not following rules to the letter over the whole history of this hobby (and a niche of a niche amongst us have recently re-trained ourselves into doing the opposite, but that's besides the point).

    The actual question is, of course, why did we? To which the answer is, most likely, a combination of multiple factors, including the influence of real life and our broader culture/s (it is my general subjective experience that most people in most contexts do not strictly follow rules they are not rigidly bound to by restricted technical affordances).

    However, I submit my opinion that one of those multiple factors at least is poor design. I maintain that, over the history of RPGs, badly designed rules (and/or arguably badly explained rules) have been commonplace, to the point of perhaps being in the majority. To most participants in the role-playing subculture, then, oral transmission of replacement (better?) rules has been the only option - even when those replacement rules have often been reductive, including referee absolutism and other underwhelming solutions.

    \n 1. Free form play. Rules are more like vague guidelines of what we're doing and how the space even works. e.g. shooting hoops.

    2. Playing a game. Rules make the game work well. They form its structure. Dropping them materially changes the game. Everyone expects that decisions are made within the game's general framework, and would get miffed if they made a decision expecting others to be constrained to the general framework which turned out poorly because others were not constrained. e.g. playing a game of basketball while keeping score.

    3. Tournament play. Rules strictly define what is and isn't allowed, because everyone's expected to play to win without necessarily adhering to informal "don't be an ass" guidelines. e.g. playing a game of basketball between two teams that aren't all friends and also there's a ref.

    Well written rules allow #2. Poorly written rules only allow #1. Generally the existence or desire for #3 drives the creation of rules that really allow #3.

    Mutual knowledge that rules are written well, or that if they aren't it's a failure of the game and you're allowed to say "this game is shitty", is what allows a group to #2 rather than to #1.

    Traditionally, RPGs did not have rules written well enough to allow #2. It takes everyone knowing that an RPG is well written and everyone knowing that everyone knows and etc. to form mutual knowledge.

    \n I think all games need to demonstrate the value of their rules, or those rules won't be followed.

    In soccer/football, the value of "don't use your hands" is immediately obvious. It creates the unique challenges and opportunities of this particular game. "This game is, among other things, a foot dexterity challenge." You can quickly decide whether you're into that or not.

    In chess, the value of "you can only move each piece in a certain way" is immediately obvious, creating its own challenges and opportunities, related to spatial envisioning and anticipation. Maybe you want to do that, maybe you don't.

    A lot of RPG rules fall into this category of "obviously valuable", but a lot of other ones don't. A rule might be cool on its own merits but have nothing to do with why people are playing the game. Or it might be a vital part of the game but not be obvious about how or why. Or it might be merely functional and interchangeable with other options from other games which the players like better (maybe because they are better, or maybe just due to simple familiarity).

    I like to try out new RPGs and learn what unique things they have to offer, as written. But when I'm not on that mission, and am just looking to have fun with my friends in the moment? The value of your rule had better be immediately apparent, cuz I can do just fine without it.

    That last point is what separates RPGs from board games and card games. Going on adventures in your imagination is inherently fun, even without rules, in a way that pushing cards around a table is not. If you sit down to play a card game, you're a captive of its rules for as long as you're playing, so you'd better either try your best to get the most out of them, or decide conclusively that they're not for you. In an RPG, there's no such incentive. You can ignore or forget every rule and still be roleplaying, possibly even having a great time. That may not be what you want to do, but it is at least possible, and if that's what the other players want, you may find yourself going along with it.

    \n Here's a surprisingly thorough and very particularly worded definition I just found on the Giant in the Playground forums:\n

    The Oberoni Fallacy, or argument, normally is paraphrased something like this:

    \u2018There is no problem; inconsistency, loophole or mechanical issue with (whatever rule) because you can always Rule 0 the problem; inconsistency, loophole or mechanical issue.\u2019

    On the face of it this appears to be shallow and illogical statement; ignoring or changing something does not make it \u2018disappear\u2019 and we would agree with that position. However, as this \u2018fallacy\u2019 is used so often I shall explore it further by looking at the meaning and application of this in-depth, first with \u2018Rule 0'.

    `-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-`-

    Rule 0, sometimes referred to as the Golden Rule of Gaming states;
    \u2018The arbiter of the rules may change, bend, break or add any rule he thinks will improve game-play.\u2019

    We will assume is needed for two main reasons:\t
    1) To aid in moving the story along.
    2) To see to it that the mechanics of the game are not bogged down by trivialities.

    As the judge of in-game action the Storyteller or Game Master needs to have final say on what happens mechanically and thematically.

    \n For what it's worth, I feel like "Rule Zero" has undergone significant drift to become basically this power-justifying thing, and I absolutely hate it. The point of Rule Zero shouldn't be "change whatever you feel like"; it's a stopgap that's meant to address the inevitable non-comprehensiveness of a rules text for addressing the effectively limitless possibility of open-ended play. Sooner or later, you're going to run up against something that the written rules don't tell you how to resolve; at that point, you'll have to use your own judgement. I think it's a shame it's become so twisted by the "Cult of the GM" thinking.

    Then again, I'm a huge advocate of hewing as close to the rules as possible. I don't really understand the point of choosing to engage in a rules-governed activity if you're so casually willing to ignore them. I think it's a question worth asking whether so many games really have "bad" rules that require changing, or if they just have rules that people think they're above following. I think Ron Edwards started questioning that supposition by trying to suss out the difference between the stated goals of a text and the experience the rules created (System Matters and the three CA essays); the OSR movement did it too, and revitalized what was at one point a rather maligned rules set/play style.

    \nThat "thing the written rules don't tell you how to address," though, is often overblown by trad gamers. Like, sure, there may not be a rule for this exact situation, but it's not hard to extrapolate from the existing ruleset how it should apply. In our 5E game, when @DBB 's character was in a monster's stomach, the Paladin jumped in to save her, and did so by lighting up some incense to make the monster cough them both up. The GM handled the incense lighting with a Con save. Obviously not something the rules could have predicted, but a totally within-the-rules-framework solution.

    What I find when I play games with GMs who frustrate me is that they very often don't even understand the point of the game... which gets back to @DBB 's comment about the goal being most important. A good example would be trying to play Burning Wheel with a GM who refuses to set binary stakes before each skill roll: that isn't just against the rules, it undermines a whole lot of Luke's entire purpose in how he constructed the ruleset. Players need to know exactly what's at stake on each roll so they can decide what resources to bring to bear. If the GM can decide post-facto what happens, that all goes out the window. Also, without a hard and fast difficulty number, many GMs will be tempted to go with a "good enough" approach, which actually sucks.

    What I mean by the "good enough" approach (and here we're getting into "Spicy Dice Roll" territory) is that, if the Obstacle really should be 5, but someone rolls 4 successes, the sloppy GM will often let them get what they want, or part of what they want. (Obviously this sort of compromise is fine in, e.g., an AW engine game where a mixed result is part of the rules.) I often see this with 13's or 14's on skill rolls in 5E. Motherfucker, the DC is 15, so that 14 should be treated exactly like a 1.

    (To be fair, there are more principled ways to handle that specific issue: many GMs do in fact give you a partial result if you're within 5 of the target, particularly on knowledge checks. This isn't terrible, but I do wish they'd be up front about it.)

    ***

    \n I've been waching some videos about cognitive psychology which gave me the idea that it's a problem that starts with things like selective attention and such. The human brain can't process a whole book of data in an hour to play a complete game and at most we will probably spend half an hour as players to learn the rules of a game. So, what our brains do? They use past experiences to fill the gaps. No wonder why many D&D players ended up playing VtM like it was the same deal with a different way of rolling dice.

    Then we have the communicational problems: even if we understand both the spirit, meaning and applications of a rule, explaining it properly to others requires an effort in both finding clear terms to express it and don't think something is "too obvious" to be explained. That's one of the most recursive mistakes of designers and GM's: we have interiorized rules and concepts in such a way that we end up thinking everyone else knows what we're talking about, so we end up forgetting to explain them.

    This is specially true for behaviors, gestures and ritual phrases we use to trigger procedures and ellicit emotions in other players. We had to wait until Archipielago to see these turned into part of the rulebook. Of course, now it's such an obvious thing, but if you remember how confused you were the first time you saw people playing a RPG, then you will get an idea of what I'm referring to here.

    On top of that, when we reduce the RPG experience to a minimum common denominator, what we've got is that we get together to make up a fictional world where some things happen, or at least that's where our expectations are. To fulfill those expectations all we need to do is make things happen, which can be done by completely ignoring the rules! Give your brain an easier way to do things and it will go down that path everytime it gets frustrated or distracted.

    It's not like these things can't be fought. Being clearer and a bit more rigid about the core rules of the game helps, by stating most of those really helpful advice to play the game as actual rules. People will still ignore those if they want, but will definitely feel less sure about doing things their way instead of trying the designer's way for once.

    Next you will need to be as clear and concise as you can about those rules, think of what you're forgetting to explain, what you have already interiorized in order to play this game and that important new focus you're applying to your game that makes it work. If you forget to explain that people will simply use their own experience to fill the gaps and you can bet it will not always be the same as yours.

    Actual play descriptions end up being the best vehicle to explain a game, after playing it with someone who already knows the system. Examples help a lot, but will not suffice if whatever the designer has interiorized isn't described. It's somewhat frustrating that the same marvellous devices that allow us to experience this wonderful unlimited world of fiction -our minds- also hinder us when we try to convey the rules and procedures used to reach the best form for this experience. Words and other tools are there to help us, but we've got to be really careful about how we use them, when, where and what sort of things we're forgetting to explain.

      \n Can't help but see again that a lot of the disagreement here would resolve if we used the idea that I wrote above. Resuming it so it uses some other stuff written on the thread it would be:

      -Rules, mechanics, techniques and procedures are a toy, in the same way a setting is a toy.
      -The Game part in RPGs is dynamic, inconstant through the session, it may be personal, shared, implicit, confused etc. Because it's only when we set an objective or "winning" condition -while using the rules as constraints- is that the activity becomes a Game.

      Then, can the game be broken and still work? Yes, some winning conditions and rules may be mutually exclusive or generate a dissonance. Like, encumbrance rules may get in the way if the winning condition is to create an epic story where "How much weight did the heroes carry?" isn't an important question to be answered. But the rest of the rules, conversational techniques of the group etc. will often make the whole game work, because they have experience with situations like these. A few more or less constraints won't make much of a difference for them. And the broken gamepart doesn't have to keep going the whole session, it will soon be replaced by another mini-game as the session changes focus.

      Can a game be broken from the box? Sure, if the designer didn't had his goals clear or explained them badly, the game might be unplayable by RAW. Here the "game" is what the designer had in mind of course, not what gets played at the table, hence the RAW condition.

      Can there be no game while we are playing with the toys? It can certainly feel like that if the goals are implied instead or expressed verbally, personal instead of shared or somewhat undefined, like "let's have fun toying with these rules/constraints". Or if the goals keep changing before seeing completion too. Or if our efforts aren't focused on a common single part of the activity. Some mechanics and procedures that produce fiction somewhat randomly for the participants to react to, will feel like toying with rules instead of playing a game, without being less fun at all.

      So basically, everyone is right.

      \n In the similar thread I started on rpg.net, I just posted the following, which addresses some problems with my original formulation picked up both here and there.\n
      Thanks all for your responses. My blog post already captured many of the reasons you've given, at least to my satisfaction, but there are some ideas that may lead me to revise it.

      In particular, I think I muddled up this distinction:\n
        \n
      1. RAW (rules from a single game text external to the group)
      2. Explictly agreed house rules (including those cannibalised from other games or syncretised from blogs, etc)
      3. Informal and/or implicit table conventions, habits, and norms
      \nI suspect that Dan M's problem is as much with "unwillingness to do 1 or 2" as it is with "unwillingness to do 1". And I'd guess that many others who complain about (3) are similar \u2014 they can put up with house rules, as long as there's some attempt to make them explicit.

      The great strength of (3) is that most people (neurotypical ones, anyway) have a natural talent for agreeing them, negotiating them, and remembering them. The great weakness of (3) is that the resulting "rules" are extremely difficult to examine, since they're not written down, often never spoken as such, and sometimes not even recognised as things that could be otherwise.

      The weaknesses of (1) and (2), particularly (1), have been discussed at length in this thread. The great strengths of (1) and (2) is that they can be examined, reviewed, explicitly agreed, and communicated to other parties. In particular, if you want to sigificantly change your gaming experience, you probably need some of them. Otherwise, you're likely to drift back to your usual (3) fairly quickly.
      \nAny thoughts on the above would be appreciated.

      \n Ranting :
      After watching some Actual Play, I begin to notice something unsettling. Even with open-minded seasoned players, some rules are regularly distorted, namely those for character bonding. When the rules ask that each PC be linked to another, or strongly motivated to attain a goal, there is always the player who manages to negotiate (oh, subtly) until being practically "bond free". This makes those rules very unfair in practice, giving the cheating player total legal protection.
      I mean : I can make rules to encourage tone and narrative consistency and stuff, but I don't know if I want to prevent players from feeding bananas into the machine if that's all they want to do.

      \n Either the designer didn't anticipated various circumstances or scenarios where the rule produces a bad gaming experience, forgot to explain how to avoid them by using the rule or the players misread/misinterpretate the rule.

      The end line is that the rule is perceived as broken by the group, thus is best removed for that particular group to be able to play the game. If you change the rule to avoid the problem, you're still removing it -even when the replacement looks similar. You're changing the game as the designer intended.

      Anyway, I agree that the rule will always get replaced by something else. Encumbrance rules can be removed but it will be replaced by a limit of carried items (like, you only get 5 spaces in your character sheet to write down all your stuff) or the "narrative common sense" that you can't be serious about your character carrying a ton of weapons, armor and treasure and still be able to sneak past the guards like nothing without some magical aid or the best distraction ever.

      We may not always realize what we're exchanging those rules with but removed rules will be replaced instead, unless they are totally outside the scope of the things the group want to narrate, or already tackled by another game mechanic. I mean, if things will come down to "you grapple the thing/you don't", why does it needs to be so complicated?
