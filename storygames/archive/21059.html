\n A friend of mine who isn't at all hep to RPG theory but has played D&D and Fiasco with us occasionally and I were talking on our way home from a movie that used the traditional four act structure. He was frustrated at what he saw as some "false endings" and I said I used to be too, when watching other films that used that structure before I read about it and now I'm more into those films, including the one we just saw.

I quickly explained it as "introduces elements, expands on elements, introduces completely different element [which is usually where it might look like a false ending] and then brings it together". And he said "Huh. Wonder if Fiasco was based on that idea?"

I said "I don't think so, I think it's more of a two-act structure" (what I was thinking at the time was that it's more based on the platform + tilt that's used in improv theatre) but I promised to ask Jason.

So here we are. Jason... would you mind revealing whether or not you were inspired by that traditional four act structure? Thank you so much for your time and this amazing game.

    \n Thanks for asking. The Tilt terminology was obviously stolen from improv. When I was designing Fiasco I began by looking at common cinematic structures and trying to emulate them. For a while it had three formal Acts and the Setup (which, structurally, functions like an act in a screenplay), and it still sort of does - the Aftermath is just a drastically truncated fourth act in the service of cogent and efficient gameplay. I gave up trying to rigorously emulate the cinematic arc and everything fell into place.
