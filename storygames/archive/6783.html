\n The only way I've ever seen it done is for the GM to drop them in the middle of the table, or hand them around in a stack, and let the players figure out who will play which one. The most assertive players claim characters first. The less assertive players claim characters from what's left.

Is there a better way when the GM doesn't know the players?

I remember a few years ago reading a convention horror story in which an assertive player in a Start Trek game quickly claimed Kirk, and how the game played out as an utter disaster because of how poorly qualified he was to play Kirk.

Is there a better way?

Paul

    \n At a recent game, I chit-chatted a little with the players before we started and picked up some cues from their behavior. They were a group of kids (12-14?) who knew each other from before, so it was easy to see the group dynamic. An adult friend of mine joined the group, too.

    The characters were three demons and a magician - who had little control over his demons. I gave the quiet boy at the bottom of the pecking order the biggest, strongest demon, so he'd have some oomph. The most vocal of the kids got a fairly minor demon - I knew he'd be all right anyway. The adult player got the magician, because I knew he was mature enough to handle a character who was constantly losing face and getting no respect from his demons.

    It worked well; the quiet kid got a lot of action, although, of course, his friends often tried to tell him what to do. And the kids all liked challenging the authority of the adult player, who acted confused and surprised whenever they messed things up for him.

    \n Hey, Paul, it's not a bad way. It provokes conversation. It's difficult enough to get a table of geek strangers talking, but give them a bunch of characters and they'll talk nicely.

    If someone claims a character too soon, just say "Let's talk about it some more before claiming characters. What does everyone else want to play?". Make sure you've asked everyone who they want to play, including the less assertive characters, then assign them.

    I still have nightmares about a fat Scottish tosser claiming the character I wanted during a game. He did it using the words "I'll stick with this character". On reflection, I should have said "No you won't, you fat tosser, we're passing the characters round for inspection, not giving them out".

    Graham

    \n I try to set up convention games so that all the characters are equally compelling and are tied tightly together, which mitigates the assertive player thing a little. Also, I've found myself doing some social engineering, taking time to explain why the characters who were chosen last are central and amazingly cool. I run this TSoY Aztec-themed game occasionally, and there's one guy, Lord Bird, who gets picked last invariably, and he really is the most fun character to play, and very important to the game. So I make a point of saying "you made an excellent choice in picking the cowardly drunk to play. Here's why..."

    \n My brother, Bill, utilizes a character selection matrix--an idea I've copied. Players get a handout with short blurbs about each of the available characters, then rank order those characters. The blurb includes some information about ways the characters' skills and abilities can be used in the adventure--sort of like 'roles and responsibilities' information. Once all the players' choices are tabulated, characters are assigned based on maximizing 'satisfaction'.

    Another method I've used is to have interested players roll for each character, with the higher roll winning, but the players have the option of using in-game resources to modify the dice. In Spirit of the Century, for example, players would roll the four FUDGE dice but could then spend Fate Points for a re-roll or +2 to the result.

    Mel

    \n I like Matthijs' approach very much, I may try that myself someday.

    Usually I prefer characters created together by players at the game (Ditv, DRYH), but I've also done:

    With Monsters and Other Childish Things I read a bit about each character and their monster, and then let the players decide. With six players I have 3 players play monsters, 3 their kids. I was worried there might be a deadlock, but it wasn't a problem. I also have 6 kid/monster pairs so no one gets stuck with last choice.

    In The Mountain Witch I put the six ronin at the six seats and people played whoever they sat down at. Your enemy was directly across from you and your allies two seats to either side, so that was easy to remember.

    In Bliss Stage Final Act or back when I ran Junkyard Cops in Feng Shui I read the signature quote for each character and then let players select in order of arrival at the table, so that people who showed up first get first pick.

    Back when I ran Champions I did notice that leader PCs often got picked by players who were LEAST suited to playing leaders, which at least once led to a coping strategy among the other players that their smart leader had been replaced by a robot and to work around him.

    Someday I'd like to do a time travel scenario where multiple people can pick the same character and its no problem, but that would be troublesome to prep since I'd have to bring multiple copies of each character. My original idea for that was a two-part tournament in which part one would end with one hero chosen to travel back in time, and the part two would start with those characters arriving from their respective timelines, possibly some of them duplicated. My dislike for tournament format advancement meant I never got around to attempting that, though.

    \n I distributed my Dictionary of Mu characters based on their kickers, yeah, and it worked out really well.

    I was play a game with Thor and he mentioned how for one pre-gen game he ran, there was an elf who was actually the conscience of the group and he distributed him by saying, "Who wants to play an elf," and the player, wanting to play Legolas from the LotR movies, took the character thinking he would knife orcs in the eye and shield surf his way through the adventure, which he attempted to do, ignoring any Beliefs on his character sheet.

    We talked about it and how he could have handed out the character saying, "This is the conscience of the party," instead.

    Thinking very specifically about what I say and how I present the characters is really important.

    \n I've only had full-pregens to handout in traditional games - but I've run a hell of a lot of character-provided events in traditional games. Mostly I went random; it was the most likely method to avoid problems - this was a hard-learned lesson from years of convention experience. But if I usually had two female characters out of each table of six characters and if I had female players I'd ask if they wanted the female characters first (with little to no information about them).

    In most of my events, the characters had secrets, so there was no possibility of switching after the start or passing them around. But even when they didn't have secrets, I'd still go random to avoid the "I was really hoping to play a wizard" crap. My games were about characters, not classes, and it was hard enough getting people to actually read anything on their character sheets other than the stats.

    \n If I'm using pre-gen characters and I don't know the players I don't let them browse through the options.

    Either I asked them some basic questions: "Would you rather player a thinker or a doer?", "Are you familiar with military tactics?", "Are there certain types of crime that upset you?" and then make a judgement call on who gets what.

    If that's not an option I shuffle the characters and hand them out randomly. I ask if anyone has gotten a character they are unable/unwilling to play if so I try to adjudicate an exchange with another character.

    These two options have always worked well for me.
