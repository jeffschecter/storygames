    \n Well, if you really do want to be versed in the theory stuff developed at The Forge, then yes, start with the articles:

    http://www.indie-rpgs.com/articles/

    However, there's kind of this weird misconception that people *started* with the theory and built games from that. Which isn't correct. There's a reason that Ron shut down the GNS and Theory forums at The Forge. It wasn't to say "theory is done" or "the theory here is complete." It was done to drive theory discussion into the Actual Play forum.

    That's because that's how the theories were developed in the first place. Everyone took a good hard look at what they were actually, really doing, at the table and threw away all the dishonest bullshit that goes on in games. From that they extracted principles and then built games that were honed to what they, personally, wanted out of play without regard for what an RPG "is" or what other people might "want."

    So, is YOUR game built to be what YOU want out of a game? Really and truly? Then irregardless of where it "fits" with Forge theory it's already built in the spirit The Forge was intended to be about all along.

    \n outsider, you have articulated the difference between modern and postmodern games quite nicely. As soon as designers decided to write toward a theory (or theories), rather than theorizing about what they'd written, they crossed the line (I think of it as the "Dostoevsky Line"). The Forge was where RPG design postmodernism really gelled, rather than being unevenly and even na\xefvely scattered among games hither and yon, and if you're looking for games to read and play that will help you understand that transition, you could do a lot worse than Forge impresario Ron Edwards' own work (Trollbabe, Sorcerer). I found it especially illuminating to consider the differences between the (fine) pretheoretical game Mage: The Ascension and the early postmodern Sorcerer, but of course your mileage may vary.

    You don't need to cross that theory line with the Forge-inspired designers, of course. "Pretheoretical" doesn't mean "bad". But maybe you, like a lot of people who come into contact with it, will find that your successful games had things in common, that your unsuccessful games had other things in common, and that the Forge-theory helpfully characterizes some of the differences.

    I recommend, if you're launching into Forgery, that you start with the provisional glossary. It will make a little sense when you read it the first time, and more sense (though I've never gotten it to make complete sense) when you've read more of the articles and discussions. You should know that in the Forge's discussions, a few games are considered touchstones, are always used in examples, and are never listed by their full titles: TSoY is The Shadow of Yesterday, BW is Burning Wheel, PTA Primetime Adventures... it goes on and on and on. I spent months reading mentioned rulebooks in my Copious Free Time to feel like I had a chance of understanding the state of the art. (The highlights among Forgey games, in case you care about my opinion for some reason, would be Dogs in the Vineyard, My Life with Master, InSpectres, The Shab al-Hiri Roach and its silly little brother Drowning and Falling, BW's offspring Mouse Guard, Sorcerer, and PTA.) Worse: these new works are best understood in light of some earlier games that also come up a lot in conversation: Ars Magica, Puppetland, the unloved but influential Everway, D&D in both the Red Box and 3.x incarnations, The Extraordinary Adventures of Baron Munchausen... it just never ends. Good luck.

    \n It should also be mentioned that none of the articles on the Forge quite represent the state of the Big Model theory as it is today. The essay "System Does Matter," in particular, contains a few things that are outmoded or misguiding, as does "GNS and Other Matters of Roleplaying."

    This is because these articles are not and never were a presentation of This Is the Thing. They were (and are) part of an ongoing conversation that has led to these things getting hammered out. (So, why are they still there? Because the whole conversation is still there, and still continues).

    All of which is to say, yes, start with the articles. But please don't stop there and think that you've got it all.

    Another thing: there's several folks who have gone off and written stuff on their blogs that explains and re-explains some of this stuff.     \n Tim, I always figured that given the publication date of Sorcerer, and its apparent self-awareness, and the inclusion of a theory essay as an appendix in the book, that Edwards was already thinking in terms of theory when he wrote it. I stand corrected. Its postmodernism must fall into the same category, then, as that of Everway, still incoherent, but understanding that even the most basic assumptions (na\xefve "realism") of existing games were arbitrary and disposable. Still, don't you think it's interesting the Edwards chose to lead us to think that "System matters"-type concerns drove the publication, at least, of his game?

    At any rate, I disagree strongly with your contention that taking theory as a design inspiration \u2014 that is, taking it in some sense as prescriptive \u2014 hasn't led to good game design. The thank-yous of too many excellent games weigh in on the other side of that argument, for starters. And everyone on the Forge, for all they may proclaim their innocence as a way of preserving their "authorship"*, surely works in the light cast by theory. It's how they aim to improve, to answer the question "Why will this game do anything better than the game I was playing before?" And sometimes we find rather explicit acknowledgement of theoretical considerations in excellent games: I see this in MLWM's physics of relationships, and even more clearly in IAWA's direct hacking of the transcript.

    Tim, if your argument is that 90% of the time, taking theory as prescriptive leads to crud, I agree wholeheartedly. Postmodernism is susceptible to being used as a fig-leaf for lazy or just plain poor work. But it's also capable of leading us to a mature consideration of the whole medium, and of giving us sophisticated tools for making that medium go into new places in exciting ways. At the minimum, designing toward an aesthetic theory is no more likely to lead to bad results than designing without a theory in mind; at its best, it is groundbreaking.

    * I feel like postmodernism in this medium isn't especially mature yet, and this is a main reason why. The notion of authorship hasn't come under the same scrutiny as (say) rules ontology, and so an unexamined and na\xefve model persists that might see appropriation as damaging to creator-hood.

    \n Graham, by "postmodern" I mean the usual things: awareness of convention and expectation, and willingness to play with them; the blurring of distinctions among descriptive levels (meta- versus object-level); explicit reference to existing works including self-reference; the flattening of use-mention distinctions (e.g. rules that refer directly to theoretical constructs such as the transcript); creative activity often aimed toward an aesthetic theory, especially any work created with a manifesto in mind. I'm sorry if I said anything that would have muddied my use of the term; I didn't mean anything unusual by it. I guess the controversial assertion would be that Forge games are generally postmodern, right? Or that the key aesthetic distinction in this particular medium, so far, has been pre-theoretical vs postmodern? I think it would be a bit of a thread derail to discuss that, but it might be an interesting topic for a new thread.

    \n I'm seeing alot of confusion around the notion of "theory designed games"
    \n
    \nLet me simplify based on the way I see it. I'll claim no special knowledge other than someone who spent absurd amounts of time on the Forge back in the day.
    \n
    \nIdea One: There exists games numerous enough to be worth categorizing that were designed as a concious effort by the designer sitting down with the "Current State of Forge Theory Discussions" and saying "I'm going to design a game founded on the principles espoused in these theory discussions. I'm going to analyze my social contract and creative agenda and select fortune techniques and stances and various ephemera specifically to achieve the desired Creative Agenda".
    \n
    \nTo the best of my knowledge this never happened. Or if it did, extremely rarely and solely as a mental exercise. I know of no published product designed in this fashion.
    \n
    \n
    \nIdea Two: There existed a number of people who, in various places and with varying degrees of active involvement participated in, or at least followed the heady days of RPG Theory discussions -- largely at the Forge but also spilling over from RPGnet, Gaming Outpost, and long before any of them Use Net...and later out into the blogosphere. Whether these people eagerly embraced the theory's assertions, actively pushed to expand or develop them further, rejected them outright, shrugged and went on with their lives, or pursued their own variants; this period of time instilled in a generation of game designers a way of thinking about design that rejected any notion of "just the way things are done" or "thats what an RPG is supposed to look like" in favor of making specific choices to achieve specific results. That culture produced the vast majority of "indie" designs from 2001-2005. That culture has since dispersed, and broken up into smaller centers of "thinking about design" that often are very different from the more monolithic days when the Forge dominated the center of the theory discussion universe.
    \n
    \nTHIS...yes...this happened alot. You'd be hard pressed to find an indie title that wasn't touched at least a little by this process.
    \n
    \nSo Colin, I think your overall notion is pretty sound. There is definitely a large difference in how designers thought about design pre-2000ish and how designers think about design today. That line could best be thought of as a range of dates with 2000-2001 being the primitive early stages, 2001-2004 being the peak production years, 2004-2005 or 6 being the winding down years, and 2005-today being the dispersion and beginning of the next evolutionary stage.
    \n
    \nBut phrases like "decided to write toward a theory" overstates the reality by a pretty wide margin. "became generally more aware of the possibilities and began making deliberate choices to explore them" would be a more accurate depiction, I think.

    \n The Forge and games produced there are actually very structuralist, not especially postmodern. Postmodern is not the recognition of structures ("awareness of convention and expectation"), it's the recognition that all structures are inherently flawed, as they are fictional. Structuralist works aim at an aesthetic; post-structuralist and postmodern works aim at the seams of an aesthetic, trying to break them open.

    \nJesse: Yes, our game is what we want out of a game. In high school we started playing D&D. We ended up 'free-forming' and then playing a few indie rpgs. (first was 'fudge'). It didn't take us long to decide that this RP design thing can't be too hard, we are reasonably intelligent people, and we were growing frustrated with other systems. Over the course of 6 years we've played maybe half a dozen times a year while tweaking and rehauling the new system. Now that its pretty much to a place where we can play through sessions without making comments on the system. (it has ultimately faded to the background for better or worse and we take it for granted that we are using it and not something else)
    \n
    \nDuring the course of the design, I found story-games and the forge. I never devoted myself to figuring it all out, but the emphasis on \u201cwhat exactly are we trying to do here\u201d was something that stuck with me. We started out with a very broad notion of a 'mission statement' and then as we got closer to a finished product we were able to tighten up our concepts.
    \n
    \nI wanted to include theory in the manual, not because it would add anything to the playability of the game (no matter how much someone tells me why a movie is great or why certain camera techniques are great it never equates to my enjoying the movie) but rather, because anyone who is going to consider playing an indie-rpg is the kind of person who knows something about rpg-theory. I just wanted to make sure that before someone opened the pdf, they could read a sort of introduction to it which would distinguish it from all the other hundred or so RPs.
    \n
    \nNot to mention I just graduated with a philosophy degree so I figured that if I learned anything about philosophy I should be able to usefully apply it to RPing.

    In lit-crit terms, structuralist (understanding things as complex constructions of constituent parts) instead of formalist (analyzing a text based on the structure of the text). Once you stray outside of lit-crit and into broader arts and aesthetics, the distinction between formalism and structuralism is... fuzzy at best.\n

    I dunno, it certainly helped me understand things. As soon as I cracked open Egri, or rather, as soon as I checked the publication date, I was able to put Egri and large portions of the Big Model into structuralist terms, in which they make perfect sense, and stopped trying to apply post-structuralist and postmodern thought to the ideas, which is just crazy-making. It also contextualizes Big Model, making sense of later developments (roughly, what Ralph is calling the next evolutionary stage 2005-present) which start poking and prodding at the edges of the Big Model.

Big Model put forward a lot of definitions and theses; these days we're examining them and turning them inside-out and applying them to each other and seeing where things break down or hide deeper meaning. And \u2014 in a vain attempt to drag this back to OP \u2014 the best tools we have for testing what has come before is with play experiences and game designs that create those play experiences. In a real way, we shove games into the cracks of theory and see what happens.
