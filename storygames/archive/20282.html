\n Players = Two Heroes, Lt. Villain, Villain
They each choose some traits that can influence (step up/down) die sizes, some relationship NPCs and some front-loading of inter-PC relationships
They rate their effectiveness in various types of scenes (chase/skirmish, interpersonal, investigation, battle/big fight, etc.) with dice; this part is private and the die sizes are secret until revealed. The Villain side overall has slightly better die sizes than the PCs for the pre-Showdown scenes.

Scenes are played in free play until a conflict is reached, during conflicts various props are introduced on index cards, and one of them will generally be kept and reincorporated.

Once all the scenes have been played, the Showdown happens. Its mechanics are similar to the pre-Showdown scenes, though I think there\u2019s more emphasis on reincorporating old props and introducing fewer new ones. It\u2019s played in rounds, so it doesn\u2019t hinge on a single roll. Things are weighted in favor of the hero side; if they lose then the villain gets away but still doesn\u2019t succeed in their evil scheme. If the heroes win showdown early (that is, get so far ahead the villain can\u2019t catch up), then there is a final danger such as the classic fortress self-destruct, and one of the heroes may die, or a relationship NPC may die, or something like that.

So: Simulationism. Right?

    \n A suggestion occurs to me - and I don't know if I agree with this myself, but when's that stopped an idea from getting considered:

    It is possible that calling things "genre simulationism" and "system simulationism" or similar distinctions occludes more than it reveals for people wrestling with Sim. The concept of "genre Sim" seems to be really, really good for people to get a handle on what Sim is - at some level at least - but then it also immediately becomes a stumbling block in understanding that "that other sort of Sim" is the same exact thing, and that there aren't any walls between them.

    Take GURPS for example. It is possible that people could understand what it means to do Sim with it better if they could detach from the whole question of their personal relationship to mechanics-heavy point-buy games, and just look at it as a Sim game that happens to be generic (so you choose your Sim at the point of application), and happens to involve a lot of math and such as a matter of taste. I haven't played Fudge more than a couple of sessions, but as far as I understand, GURPS and Fudge deliver essentially the same creative agenda, they just use a different amount of rules to do it. Heck, Wushu probably would go the same way if it didn't have a default setting.

    I mean, mechanical weight is not an indicator of the underlying creative agenda any more than use of dice or playing cards is, right? If a Narrativist game has a fuckload of rules like say Burning Wheel does, that's not because it belongs in some special Nar box - it's because the designer and players like mechanically proficient play that occurs on multiple levels, with mechanical interplay and fictional events merging together into a kind of experience unique to that sort of game. It's a matter of taste and capabilities in execution, same as using funny voices for NPCs, not a Creative Agenda classification.

    \n I think it's interesting to compare play that emulates a well-known source or genre with play that emulates/invents a novel hybrid of sources and genres.

    When I mentioned in a chat that my Fiasco games tend to benefit from me asking myself, "What would fit in a good Coen Brothers movie here?" I thought perhaps that might be just me, and wouldn't work for others. After all, it's the complete opposite of the "constructive denial" which, at one point, was touted as being definitional to Sim. But no, Jason thought it was a useful enough approach to mention it in the Fiasco Companion, and several of my gaming groups have since gotten a lot of mileage from taking explicit looks at the source. Before my recent mini-campaign of World Wide Wrestling, we actually watched some pro wrestling so the less wrestling-savvy among us could refer to it in play.

    In contrast, "going back to the source" isn't always viable in a lot of beloved, Sim-encouraging RPGs out there. I mean, what's our shared reference for Werewolf: the Apocalypse? What are we celebrating? I've heard it argued that when a team of furry badasses with different powers and worldviews goes on a mission together, what's really being celebrated is X-Men comics, but even if that's true, that doesn't do much good for a group who's unaware of it. In that case, there's no ability to say, "my inspiration has waned; what would Cyclops & Wolverine do here?" More importantly, there's no ability to say, "whoa, that thing you did is cool for how it resembles or departs from what Cyclops would have done!"

    In some cases, I think the source material is the Werewolf books themselves -- there's plenty of stuff in there that a group can celebrate or emulate, but only if all the players develop an appreciation of it first. Some groups did this! But others just left the book-reading to the GM.

    Of course, the Werewolf authors didn't invent all their content from scratch. It's a grab bag of themes and tropes players may already have plenty of references for. X-Men plus Captain Planet plus werewolf monster movies, perhaps? How much of the same stuff gets through to different players? How much overlap and coherence is enough? If the goal is a shared standard for appreciating each other's contributions, I'd bet that me riffing off Wolf while you riff of Captain Planet is far from sufficient.

    Depending on how you view Simulationism, a little blurriness of source/reference material can be a big deal or not a big deal at all. Different theories abound...

    \n I think what I said was basically that GURPS is genre Sim, yes. It's just that the "genre" thing is usually conceived of in a sort of over-pronounced, exaggerated manner. It's treated as if if it isn't cliche, then you don't have the same creative mechanisms underlying the Sim as you do with a cliche-ridden game. This then confuses a person when they encounter a Sim game that doesn't rely on a really trope-filled literary genre as the cultural background upon which it acts.

    Try it this way: if we call "genre Sim" with the equivalent name of "cultural background Sim", and define it the exact same way, except that instead of thinking of it as "genre" you think of it as "the shared appreciation of some fictional focus of play", perhaps that would help get over the hurdle of treating "genre Sim" as a sort of subcategory. That differentiation might well be illusionary, caused by people attempting to approach understanding Sim from multiple perspectives instead of just one.

    GURPS, the way I've witnessed it, has been pretty clearly Sim, and the "genre" has certainly been heavily present. Sometimes the genre is just not quite a genre in the literary history sense. Often it is, such as in this dungeon fantasy campaign we've played. The genre of that has of course been "dungeon fantasy", that literary melange of post-tolkienist fantasy and D&D. Projecting out from that to a less "genrified" GURPS game, I don't really see anything structural changing - so OK, you play a kitchensink multiverse GURPS game with mages and cyborgs and whatnot, so it very clearly does not limit itself to the tropes of a single genre. It's still the same game about giving point scores to your doll and having it fight other dolls; shine in spotlight for using a trained skill; calculate encumbrance; build weapons with the weapon-building subsystem. All of these activities are prone to produce the same kind of Sim appreciation (celebration of the fictive content, to be specific) whether the underlying subject matter is "my character's a cyborg and he's in a war against a council of wizards" or more conventionally "my character's a cowboy and it's a Western with all the trappings". The presence of a "genre" in the latter example changes nothing for the actual operation of the creative mechanism.

    \n I'm with you on the shared cultural material, Bob - what you describe accords with my experiences of the Simmish creative agenda. The joy of it is largely on revelation and acknowledgement shared with others: "Oh, so that's how it looks like when this thing I've only ever read about is actually applied in practice!"\n

    \nThese results are important, I think, exactly because they force you to apply the cultural material at the game's focus: what does it look like in this genre/reality/subculture/place/time when this unexpected thing happens. If the exceptional never happens, the ordinary has less chance of being noticed and appreciated, because that ordinary thing only becomes relevant to anything whatsoever against the contrast of the extraordinary.

    For example, let's consider Mulan (largely because I just started reading "Judge Dee" detective novels and have China in the brainpan): Mulan is interesting for Sim purposes in that the concept of the gender role breaking woman becoming a soldier, set in Han era China, concretely asks us to apply what we know regarding the time and place to a specific subject matter. In what ways of behavior and speech does a father discourage a tomboy girl from her inclinations, considering that the father is a Confucian gentleman? How about the mother? What if Mulan does this or that, how does the world react? All interesting and relevant questions that could not occur without the 20% "off-color" material - in this case, Mulan herself. The setting only gets to truly define itself against the context of those occasional exceptional results.

    Similarly, to pick a less sweeping example, the idea that the hero might fail can be fruitfully used in many genres generally considered very formulaic and staid. Western can deal with it if the hero exceptionally makes a moral mistake; superhero story can deal with it if the hero "dies". It is not necessary to build your Sim game to leave that slim chance of protagonist failure in there (despite roleplayers traditionally having trouble orienting to the idea that they might be rolling dice without even a theoretical chance of failure), but it is one way you can go when considering the way the conventional clashes with the exceptional in your design. Of course the exceptional still needs to be constructive, so you're unlikely to see a Sim game where the Enterprise just goes Poof because somebody forgot to turn on the proton shield.

Gets into semantics, to be sure.

      \n Come to think of it, a lot of popular properties to Simulate/celebrate are pretty much already mashups.

      Star Trek was famously elevator pitched as Wagon Train in Space.

      Star Wars (New Hope) is somewhere between a mashup of genres and outright copy and reskin of several other movies.

      Games themselves are mashups. D&D is at minimum a mashup of Jack Tolkien's races and map fantasy with Two Gun Bob's barbarian stories.

      Shadowrun ( Tolkien/D&D + Cyberpunk) and Space 1889 ( late 1800s white boy imperialism meets ERB's sci-fantasy, with some Verne and Wells for flavoring ) are blatant mashups, and that's even before you hit weirder more obscure stuff like Lords of Creation ( Zelazny's Amber + Doctor Who...made by Americans!).

      I'm thinking maybe we do need to more thoroughly examine mashup as a concept and what role that plays in Sim.

      \n Yeah, I thought about this a bit when writing about the genre mashups earlier. Seems to me that there's a difference between a game with the starting point of "let's mash genres, oy!" compared to a game that merely happens to be set in a peculiar genre (or more strongly, no audience-recognizable genre at all). The former is more of a "genre mashup game" I think, as it's the players themselves choosing the genres and focusing on the implications of the mashup, while the latter is simply a work set in an exotic genre.

      "Genre" is usually understood in literary theory as a set of cultural signifiers that combine into a bunch of variant patterns, tropes that repeat from work to work within the genre. A "meta-genre" would be sort of a parent genre, I guess: we have fantasy adventure movies and then we have plain adventure movies, and basically the former is a subset of the latter. That sort of thing.

      \n Personally, I've never experienced a particularly conscious relationship of the play group to any source material unless there was a single obvious source or genre. Anything you might look back on and call a mash-up in retrospect was usually played without any deliberate intent to celebrate/emulate/manipulate/etc.

      Example: my longest running GURPS game, in high school, was a "boss and minions" game where each player played two characters. The "minion" characters were statted up with a limited budget to be various flavors of effective adventurers. My guy majored in stealth and hand-to-hand, thus I gave him a ninja costume. I also did my point-whoring with Odious Personal Habits, having the guy chain-smoke cigars, spit on the floor, and be generally rude and vile. I can't remember the other minions, but none of them had anything in common with mine, color-wise. There may have been a good-with-guns no-nonsense merc... that sort of color gap.

      Our four "boss" characters were basically bent on world conquest, helping each other to overcome the powers that be, but eventually competing to be the one winner. Here's what I remember:

      Donovan Hex: Endowed with immortality by a Greek luck god 880-some years ago because 1111 was a lucky year, Hex was basically unkillable, had no other powers, and presented himself as kind of a distinguished illuminati type. Refined manner, nice suit, tons of connections, implacable, subtly intimidating. Aimed to be the power behind the throne of world governments, and already pretty much ran much of the U.S.

      Straxus: An old level 20 D&D character who became an Evil demigod and then inhabitted various spelljammer technology before getting trapped in a ship's guidance crystal by the gods of Good. That was the backstory; in play, he controlled a main alien robot and also a few auxiliary robots, machines, and features of the crashed ship. I can't remember his exact plan for Earth, but I think his main goals were to magically escape from the crystal and restore himself to embodied godhood, which would presumably allow him to do as he wished on present-day Earth. As someone who'd gotten used to being worshipped, Straxus was compulsively imperious, but tried to fight it so as not to completely give away his true nature.

      The Virus: An artificial intelligence program with a will of its own, this creature could move through any computer systems at will, alter data as it saw fit, and control computer-controlled tech. The virus harbored some sort of curiosity about becoming a "real" being and understanding humanity, but step one was to take complete control of Earth so it could study as it saw fit.

      The Envoy: I can't remember who this character was pretending to be, but in reality he was an alien sent to prepare the Earth for conquest. I think his true form was something monstrous and insectoid, but I don't think we ever saw it until the last session. His general m.o. was to try to insinuate himself into positions of power -- kind of like Hex, but with more of a political flavor than Hex's private/business/cult deal.

      So, what are the references there? What's the source material? Well, I knew these guys very well, so I can offer a very educated guess:
      - The Minion: Marvel comics, Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles, G.I. Joe (Spider-Man, Sabretooth, Punisher, Raphael, Stormshadow)
      - Donovan Hex: Greek myth, Vampire RPG, Indiana Jones, other Marvel (Tyche, methusalehs, clan Ventrue, Nazi scientists, Kingpin)
      - Straxus: Dragonlance novels, Spelljammer books, anime where some horrible power that is imprisoned or held in check threatens to break free (Raistlin, the Overfiend)
      - The Virus: 2001: A Space Odyssey, Terminator 2, Blade Runner (HAL, SkyNet, Roy Batty)
      - The Envoy: Alien comics, X-Men (Mr. Sinister, N'astirh, S'ym)

      At no point did we ever try to communicate these influences or bridge these gaps. The Dragonlance fan and the sci-fi fan didn't read comics, half of us were ignorant of Spelljammer, and plenty of things we all knew (like Indiana Jones) were never communicated and not all that clear anyway. If we had any unifying priority for play, it was some combo of reveling in these badass characters doing their thing and seeing who, if anyone, would succeed (via inspired GM fiat). But I'm not sure what our rubric was for celebrating these characters and their doings -- individual notes of familiarity were frequently struck, to be sure, but there was never any coherent aesthetic context. "Come up with a mission and pursue it, and then see what kind of wacky approach the player next to you comes up with," was more our m.o. When would Hex bribe, hire, blackmail, or reveal that he already knows that guy in our way? When would the Virus jump into some new device? When would Straxus' clunky robot demand that foolish mortals bow before it? Who would the Minion next offend or unnecessarily chop to ribbons?

      From the perspective of Genre Emulation Sim, this is either a fringe case or something else entirely. From my own history, though, this sort of play was much more common than tangible homages like Dead of Night or World Wide Wrestling. The motives my high school group brought into play with our grab bag pastiche make sense to me, and I think the result is worth trying to understand.

      Perhaps one way is to look at play as a conversation that goes, "I think this is cool. Let me show you, so you can see the coolness in it," and then either, "Yeah, cool!" or not.

      - Our basis for decision for "Yeah, cool!" (or not) might be cultural background, or shared reference, or novelty, or just picking up on each other's enthusiasm.

      - The thing we want to show off as cool can be a direct reference, vaguely derivative, a mash-up, or as original as it gets.

      Note: the "go on adventure missions" structure was not a great vehicle for this. The GM was just going with physical obstacles a la dungeons, and the fun of having our minions shoot and sneak and slash their way forward was fine, but nothing special. We were all more psyched about playing our bosses, and that got relatively little screen time. Importantly, I think a similar problem plagues Rifts, where the typical game sees players concocting really diverse and interesting character types, only to then go out and just shoot stuff.

      \n It seems to me that this kind of play is all about throwing these disparate bits together, reveling in each one individually ("how will this character be badass THIS time?"), and then seeing whether it all hangs together or not. (As evidenced when a player feels that another player is "doing it wrong".)

      If it all hangs together in some coherent (or semi-coherent) whole, there is a certain to having pulled it off, to be able to enjoy all these different flavours of "awesome" simultaneously. If it doesn't, typically I've seen players insist that so-and-so "isn't getting it", and calling each other names, like "powergamers" or "munchkins" or "stupid dramatic actors" or some such.

      It strikes me that this kind of play is often referenced in the past tense. ("I remember this game I played in high school...") That's certainly how it is for me.

      \n My group never accused anyone of doing anything wrong, as we were completely unaware that there might be a certain something we were attempting to get right. A given action of ninjutsu or demolitions or scheming could certainly fail to entertain, though.

      Honestly, the suiting of an action to the particular circumstance of the moment was more our appreciation criteria than simple aesthetics. "That was clever!" always got kudos, regardless of flavor or flair. Whether that's an accidental result of the mission structure, a malformed leftover from Gamist or wannabe-Gamist play, or an ideal interaction of colorful competence porn with the immediate and transient medium of roleplay, I don't know.

      Again, I mention this because I've seen a lot of play like it -- Rifts, GURPS, and the more "lite" side of Shadowrun and Cyberpunk.

      Eero, would you tell us more about your GURPS game, if you feel it fits some kind of Sim classification?
      \nIt's a pretty normal game in the traditional vein, I think - it is my understanding that much coherent traditional Sim play is exactly like this in terms of Creative Agenda. The GM wanted to run old school D&D except with GURPS rules because he's got GURPS in his head and rarely gets to indulge; I'm participating because my brother is (I want to encourage his social hobbies), and he's participating at least in part because it is not old school D&D, which he doesn't like, but which is pretty common in these circles these days. The group in general is half new to our circles but old GURPSists, half familiar faces.

      So the game is a straightforward application of the GURPS Dungeon Fantasy sourcebooks, which are basically somebody's notes for applying GURPS rules to play, well, a GURPS campaign set in the dungeon fantasy genre. There's some verbiage in the books about adapting GURPS to be more D&D-like or whatever, but considering how superficial the D&D understanding is, it's really more of a thin aesthetic flavouring on top of GURPS than any real change to the proceedings. It's not the most vivacious literary choice ever for me, frankly - I'd be playing Dungeon World or something like that if I got a hard-on for D&D tropes without the creative agenda.

      In practice the game is about more-or-less prepped adventures stringed together with organic bridge pieces (that is, there's no downtime or abstracted openings, we just talk about where the adventuring party goes next and new adventures fall into our laps at appropriate junctures). The party is following a sort of a quest line, meets "random" encounters (not really random, but unrelated to the main plot) and occasionally contributes to deciding what we'll do next. It's not railroady, but neither are we trying to buckle the GM's plans for the sake of it - no motivation to, as nobody's characters really have anything more going with them than "I wanna be an adventurer". I imagine that a Pathfinder adventure path would be played in almost exactly the same way as this GURPS campaign goes.

      I'm playing a martial artist (a "monk" as D&D traditionally has it) myself, and I spend a lot of time in a given game session mostly reading the GURPS rules books (which I've never read before) and listening with half an ear as the party plans and buys stuff and so on. My character is accordingly taciturn and generally goes along with whatever, and as a semi-religious martial artist type he's disinterested in wealth - I don't even track it, simplifying the game for myself. I started with this sort of passive personal positioning originally because I'm unfamiliar with GURPS, didn't know what the creative agenda of the campaign would be, and because I was mostly there as a seat-filler, when all's said and done.

      Over a few sessions I found to my surprise that it was pretty fun to just tag along and play a taciturn martial artist with a nigh-obsessive need to prove himself worthy of his master. I understood by observation that we weren't dealing with Gamism (as the GM's pitch and such would have had you understand), but rather princess-play, a form of Simulationism focused on character portrayal: the challenges in the game are generally rather easy for the power level of the characters, the main point of interest is usually about what sort of a feat a given character can or can't pull off when its needed, and the goal is generally to portray your character in a vivacious way as a representative of their D&D character class archetype. The little satisfactions are about calculating how yes, your martial artist character can outrun the wagon the rest of the party are using on this week-long trip between cities; the big satisfactions are about putting together various combat maneuvers and finding that yeah, your character can pretty much just punch down a rock golem with one hit, thanks to the guidance of the Tao, provided he gets a half-minute of set-up time.

      In the last session of play, for example, our party were exploring a city that had apparently been hijacked by mighty magics that caused it to float in the sky, while simultaneously the residents had turned into zombies. (Later we found out that these were basically unrelated occurrences - the price of having two characters in the party with the flaw "Trouble Magnet".) I found it pretty entertaining when my character sneaked into the local church and bearded a vampire bishop in his church, in the middle of some sort of a zombie religion ritual - he sneaked in, grappled the vampire and outright grabbed the magical jewel embedded in the vampire's head, wresting it loose, before running away with a vampire and his zombie congregation at his heels. You may imagine how this would feel pretty novel for me, to do this sort of thing simply because I felt like it, with no concerns for tactics or strategy or anything - this game doesn't need that, it's not like I believe that the GM would let my character die or anything even if I take stupid risks in a game where character creation takes five hours

      (An example about how the GM deals with character death: at the end of that same session a necromancer wizard type PC burned themselves out while preventing a demon from breaching out from the nether realms. I'd previously given the vampire's blood gem to the character in question, because it's not like I needed it, I just wanted to see if I could take it. So the GM talked it over with the player, and they agreed that the character would become a vampire next. I did my darnedest to make sure we'd bury the poor perished wizard thoroughly before leaving to continue our quest, but that was just because of ironic humour, not because we didn't know that he'd be back in the next session. We'll see whether I'll need to put him back to rest as an undead monstrosity next time we play, although I imagine that I'll have to get swayed by whatever justifications the party invents for accepting the vampire, in the interest of the genre conceit of adventurers sticking together.)

      The GM supports the princess play agenda amazingly well, considering how he thinks that he's emulating old school D&D. His material is varied, faithful to the literary precedents of the genre, and he's always ready to work with the players to enable their characters to express themselves. His material is pretty interesting at times, too - in the very first session, when we found an old third reich-y strongbox with a nerve gas trap, mucho gold bars and a military pistol inside, I have to admit that I started paying attention; the setting is apparently some GURPS default fantasy setting (Yrth is the name I think?), which occurs in the context of a big multiverse that enables the GM to develop a campaign plot involving some sort of multi-dimensional Nazis (for whom the party promptly started working, because they pay better). He mixes influences skillfully, the game is still very clearly in the dungeon fantasy genre, and the individual occurrances and encounters and such are generally presented in a pleasing narrative style.

      So, in a nutshell: the players fuzz over their own characters, the GM fuzzes over his setpiece adventure ideas, and then we reveal to each other what we've got, and appreciate the results of the GM's adventure situation interacting with the various player characters.

      \nSounds right to me.

      I can also imagine "moments" when, temporarily, lightweight Narrativist or Gamist activities get everyone jazzed up and add flavor to the activity, without coming to dominate the longer term agenda of the playgroup.

      I do giggle a bit internally when you use the term Princess Play. Having said that, sometimes I really do prefer terms like it, or even some of the goofier jargon terms I've used regarding Sim play in the past. Stuff like "Verbal Costume Party" or "Advanced Play & Pretend" and similar.

      It's not because I'm hating on it. If anything, it's the opposite.

      I do admit, I might be hating n Gamism and Narrativism a bit when I do it, however.

      To me, an ongoing, successful Sim game has a parallel to a kids' Halloween Party. You dress up in your costume ( verbal creation, rather than a physical costume) and when you get to the party with your pals, the games played there are all themed to your costumes. And yeah, some challenges, and yeah, some portrayal of your character identity, but the fun is that you get to come back and do it over and over again ( unlike the Halloween Party, that happens one time a year).

      I know, that's fairly culture specific.

      But I think part of understanding the joy of Sim, and Sim generally. really des come from looking at it as Play first, and everything else second. If we can step back and do that, then we can start to delve back in and discuss, well, what supports that exactly, and how can it be formalized?

      \n Yeah, I'm with Bob on this as well - that all sounds familiar.

      Furthermore, to return to that question about genre Sim vs. physics Sim and such: when I compare my experiences in GURPS with my experiences in Dead of Night (I haven't played very many Simmy games for any length of time through the last 15 years, so the same games pop up in my discussion constantly), I don't think that they're really that different. Both have a genre context, and GURPS does certainly have more character focus than DoN, which is very focused on setting, plot twists and narrative technique, but ultimately they feel the same, the same way two Narrativist games feel the same to me despite having all sorts of differences in techniques and topic and whatnot.

      The big fat differences between those two games are that a horror game usually doesn't have a lot of that princessing emphasis going on (the characters are simple and ordinary), and that GURPS has a lot and lot of rules systematics; but as I described earlier, I think that the rules heaviness is just a presentation technique that enables the participants to appreciate the events from different angles (sort of like watching sports on-location or in the television), so it's not as fundamental a difference between the games as the character emphasis vs. situation emphasis thing is.

      \n Thank you for the fantastic actual play account, Eero. It was a pleasure to read. Do you think any coherent design principles can be drawn from your experiences with it so far?

      (And, boy, does it ever sound familiar, as well. It also reminds me of several times I've played more focused games wherein the players approached play from this angle: their drive was to portray their character and attempt various relatively meaningless goals, and so they didn't get anything from the engine of the game itself. One easy example I remember was a game of In a Wicked Age... where one player said that their character was a bird, didn't really engage with the Best Interests phase of play, and then spent the entire game flitting in and out of scenes in order to pluck a hat off someone's head or place some straw on someone else's.)

      As for the general discussion:

      I think the "playfulness" angle covers quite a good deal of what can be great fun in this kind of play.

      But what about the flipside? The very, very serious commitment to bringing forth the creative vision, the constructive denial, the immersion in the world or story or characters that comes from an incredibly powerful drive for willing suspension of disbelief?

      I was thinking about that this morning, because I just finished re-reading the novel Dune (which was one of my favourite books as a small child). Much like the Lord of the Rings, the author goes to great lengths to create the illusion of "realness" in the material. Is this related to Sim in some way (not directly, of course; books are not RPGs), or just a technique which could apply to anything?

      Here's a sample from the lengthy Glossary appended to the book. In the novel, there is a scene where a character has their hands tied with something called "krimskell", with no explanation. It has no particular importance to the plot or to the characters. But, flip to the back of the book and you can see:\n

      "KRIMSKELL FIBER or KRIMSKELL ROPE: the "claw fiber" woven from strands of the hufuf vine from Ecaz. Knots tied in krimskell will claw tighter and tighter to preset limits when the knot-lines are pulled. (For a more detailed study, see Holjance Vohnbrook's "The Strangler Vines of Ecaz.")
      \nAnd, of course, you can then flip to "Ecaz" and read about it (it's another planet, apparently, and there's a bit about it there).

      I've always been fascinated by this kind of thing, and find it extremely engaging. Perhaps it's childhood nostalgia, from a time when I so badly wanted other worlds and other realities to exist. But I can't deny its power.

      \n I also don't really see GURPS as particularly Sim-supporting, except perhaps in the way the game's text and rules focus so tightly on "realism" and proper representation.

      In other words, the text seems to outright deny being a "game", in the sense of something competitive - there is very little discussion of failing or succeeding, of winning over another - but, rather, focuses on representation. How do we present a certain world, a certain vision, a certain type of story?

      Most of the rules are all about a modeling of some sort, and, while most of it claims to be focused on "realism", there are lots of nods to "genre emulation", with special "mook" rules and "action hero" and so forth.

      In addition, there is the whole very visible "genre pastiche" thing going on, with the game advertising itself in part as a game where yes, you actually can mash up these various genres successfully!

      So, all those features could supporting a few (different) types of Sim play, or at least hint at them to the reader.

      There's not much to say for Gamist support (in terms of the aims of the text; the mechanics themselves could certainly be used in this way, but there's no attempt to aim for "balance", and, for example, character creation often forces you into an awkward position where representing your character "faithfully" forces you to make inefficient decisions, like buying lots of background skills and how raising your attributes as high as possible saves you lots of points, even if it doesn't fit your vision of the character).

      Same goes for premise-addressing: it's not clear at all how the game would help you with that, except perhaps a handful of the more unusual Disadvantages.

      Something like the "Quirks" rules is a perfect example: they seem silly from a Gamist perspective, and hardly useful at all for a Narrativist perspective, but point at portraying a certain type of character, and providing the player with a small reward for doing so.

      But to say I would reach for GURPS for this kind of play... I wouldn't.

      Back when I tried really hard to aim for this kind of play priority*, I eventually found I had the best success with a very rules-light approach.

      I used the Fudge system, in part because it allowed me, as GM, to shape the game to match my vision. There were almost no strict rules which could ruin my vision or go against it, and the players couldn't point to them and say something was wrong.

      The way it worked was, basically, to outline all possible/believable outcomes of a possible situation, and then map them to the Fudge ladder. So, for instance, if, as Eero puts it, the Phantom is looking for someone in an African port, the best outcome is that he finds him quickly and is utterly under control of the situation ("Superb"); however, the worst possible is, perhaps, that the target is prepared for the Phantom, and finds him first instead ("Terrible"). Clearly, we can't have the Phantom failing to find someone altogether, so we don't include that in the range of possibilities.

      (It might be completely different for a different character in the same situation, however, as befits that character's style and expected story tropes.)

      This worked well for story-oriented priorities as well as "realism/modeling" priorities. (For example, I had different charts and systems for unarmed combat, armed combat, firearm combat, and so forth, although all were ultimately vague enough to allow appropriate interpretation in the moment, with us describing the outcome in a way which felt right to us. But a single, static "damage scale" like a game such as D&D - or the default Fudge rules - clearly failed to generate the kinds of results which felt "right" or "realistic" in all situations.)

      I ultimately stopped playing in this style because, in the end, as GM I was the one making all the calls. I wasn't planning ahead or railroading and *still* I found that the game lacked surprises for me, and always put me in the driving seat. These days I like much more clear mechanics, which allow all the players input in some way - and therefore don't allow any one person to steer the boat at all times.

      \n I think that the difference between "genre Sim" and "princess Sim" is mainly in the degree of genrification/degenrification the game's subject matter has undergone. If you have a strong genre and it's a genre that supports the notion of princess characters, such as superheroes, then there's no reason why a game couldn't do both at once - it's part of the genre for the game to celebrate the ultimate coolness that is the Phantom (really, he's like the coolest superhero, and Defenders of the Earth the coolest superhero team in the history of American superheroing).

      However, if the game is something like say Werewolf: the Apocalypse, then its basic conceit of what it is to play is still 100% princess-y, but also much, much less genre-informed; it's gestulating wildly at the meta-genres of adventure fiction and urban fantasy mostly, but the real core is to have its own setting that supports authentic adventure drama - authentic in the sense of building from cornerstone up yourself, without being strongly pre-constrained by specific genre choices unless you choose to. Basically the same as Spirit of the Century, just somewhat less pointed on the genre - one of them's an adventure game and the other's a pulp adventure game with emphasis on the adventure over the pulp, which you know doesn't make for that great of a difference between the two in practice.

      All that is to answer David by the way of saying that "genre" and "princessing" here are functional lenses through which we get a handle on the creative, social, psychological mechanics of what's going on in a given game. Similar to how some Narrativist games are "protagonist advocation" games and some Gamist games are "mission-based", but that's not the whole story of what's going on with the creative agenda mode. I guess one could call these "creative agenda strategies" or "patterns"; they're not agenda modes in the GNS sense, but rather structures of how the game goes about satisfying the agenda.

      \n That's an interesting question about what cohesive princess play looks like. I'd say that it would, pretty much by definition, require the other players to appreciate your character. If the players are just focused on their own characters and merely allow others to have the spotlight because you got to, not because they want to, then it's by definition of the term incoherent play.

      For example, in our GURPS game, insofar as I understand, the other players have been slightly bemused about my approach of not min-maxing equipment and combining silent, seeming passivity with proactive willingness to pull the group's collective asses out of the fire in dangerous situations. After the say 8 sessions or so that we played over the spring season (the game's been on hiatus during the summer, I don't quite know what the GM is planning schedule-wise) an expectation pretty much grew for me to fuzz around in seemingly suboptimal ways in combat, poking fun at the 1 second GURPS combat round (an infinite jest if you ask me), only to pull off a brutal, tactically decisive move at the right moment. (Anything ranging from sneakily jumping down on top of an enemy to running up and delivering a Fist of the Northstar style finishing blow to simply throwing them down and sitting on them - a very effective approach in a realistic combat system in many situations, I might add.)

      The other players have certainly appreciated my effectiveness in this regard - the GURPS martial artist mechanics sort of synergize with my characterization of the character (technical details, but there's stuff like spending time in concentration to amass your qi energies before executing a high-performance feat) and make "tactical reserve" the proper tactical role for the character, so it makes for an interesting combination of seeming cluelessness and actual usefulness when the character reacts to combats and such by climbing into a tree and spending 10 combat rounds merely getting into position.

      It's worked well, because I've also found that I enjoy being sort of a hero in the group - it's a very flashy position to be "the cavalry", as it leaves my own character seemingly untouchably cool, being the one who didn't have to distract the enemy for half a minute while the monk sat in a tree waiting for his moment. I imagine that this is entirely routine for some roleplayers as a source of enjoyment, but it's been pretty peculiar for me, because it's such a... simmy thing to be enjoying. I know that it's artificial (I mean in comparison to e.g. a Gamist game that'd strive for absolute authenticity in "heroism", not consistent depiction as here), but it being emergent (there's no tactical role support in-built in GURPS or anything, this is just how the group's tactical action has settled as a combination of character builds, combat rules and player choices) and unvoiced (nobody planned for it or even described it in so many words before now) makes it interesting nevertheless as a discovery brought about by our process of Exploration - I discovered that a hanger-on character I created to fill a seat is basically the hero of the team, without whom the rest of the jokers would be thoroughly lacking in hard-hitting stopping power.

      Similarly, I think we've been pretty functional about appreciating the other players' characters: the sadistic barbarian-ranger gets lambasted for wanting to torture prisoners and such (that's basically a schoolbook example of what Simmy appreciation could look like - you acknowledge the impression the player was going for, in this case that his character is an unstable sociopath, and have your own character react appropriately); the players all joke about the cleric with supposedly high moral standards but convenient justifications for getting extremely well along with evil Nazis; everybody listens with at least some slight interest in the calculations the wizard player makes about his character sitting in a wagon during travel to read books, to get those 1 character point / 200 hours of study skill bonuses. Even the self-deprecating goblin thief gets his laughs for being small and clever and a vaguely fantasy-racist caricature - his schtick is sort of similar to mine in some respects, as his skills also work well in solo exploits, which has encouraged our characters to specifically interact some in sneaky commando stuff.

      What I said about the sadistic steppe barbarian there might be a key to understanding how two princess players cohere against each other creatively, without even particularly needing a GM in the recipe: they both understand the other's character in some manner (not important to get it exactly right to begin with, it's a process of learning as you play), and acknowledge that understanding by reflecting it in their own characterization, therefore getting an opportunity to show off their own character as well. The oft-cited X-Men example of Wolverine vs. Cyclops is basically this: Wolverine by being savage gives Cyclops a chance to show responsible leadership, and Cyclops by being a stick in the mud gives Wolverine a chance to show rebel spirit; therefore their interaction is useful for both players, and amply justifies playing together instead of everybody sitting in their own closet imagining themselves as superheroes.

      And yes, all this seems like it's basically what those wacky freeform forum rpgs are about, insofar as I understand them: having your character vision, showing it out, and if you're really lucky, meeting somebody who both understands your character and has an interesting one of their own that, when brought into interaction with yours, causes pretty sparks.
