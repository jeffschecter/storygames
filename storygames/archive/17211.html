    I've had much better success describing the aspirations of a character on one side and then the issues of the character on the other.     \n When looking at "internal conflict", it's generally super important, within the realm of RPGs, to consider how the conflict is going to be externalized.

    With Lizzie's example, all those flaws were explicitly externalized, because they affected your ability to form love relationships.

    A guideline that I heard once from John Wick is that if something isn't externalized in play, it doesn't exist. He was referring at the time to secrets, but I think it can be extended to "internal" conflicts.

    I put them in quotes because I think they only really matter/exist/have relevance when they are externalized.

    One way to generate "internal" conflicts that are likely to externalize is to ask good leading questions, as a GM.

    "What did the Baron do to make you hate him?" implies that there is something internal going on that is likely to spill out when the time is right. Plus, by answering the question, you put it out on the table for the players to know about, so they can be aware of it as existing in the fiction and can appreciate it, even if the character keeps it pretty close to the chest.

    In novels, we often see internal conflict working well because the author either give us insight as to what is going on, or gives us carefully worded hints that give the reader insights into things that the characters might be missing.

    With roleplay, it can be challenging to externalize the internal, unless the game being played has tools for doing so and those tools are being used.

    If you're making up your own tools for adding internal conflict, I'd suggest that it's super important to make sure that those conflicts are externalized, so that they can be experienced by the participants. Otherwise, they don't exist.

    Yeah, I know that they exist in the head of a player. And yeah, everyone's experience is somewhat subjective anyway. But my point is that we're trying to create a shared imaginary space, right? So it's best to make sure that things are shared as much as possible, rather than driving toward less sharing.

    \n We drew our flaws on slips of paper. First you drew one, then talked with other players about how it might fit your character. Then we drew another flaw, and repeated. You then chose the one that fit better for your character.

    Mine was something like, "You preach to others and believe they should follow your advice." We also had a positive (You learn to trust others) and a negative outcome (keep preaching) for each dilemma.

    Other dilemmas were stuff like, "You are violent toward the one you love" or "You have an addiction."

    Does that help? It was for the game In Fair Verona that I played for my book, a game that used tango dancing as the core mechanic.
