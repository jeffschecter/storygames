    \n Well, the thing is it has the problem that most D&D editions, including Pathfinder (especially Pathfinder) have.

    The way I see D&D 5 is it's sitting nicely in the realm of OD&D/AD&D exploration/puzzles/strategy gameplay. Essentially, the rules seem like a great framework for throwing the players into dangerous and complex situations, and having them figure out the best ways through them.

    Thing is, the rules don't talk about what philosophies of dice rolling to consider. You can either have a more 'indie game' approach to rolling like The Burning Wheel where each roll answers a question and you keep moving forward with the results without time spent re-rolling. OR a more retro game approach where time is an extremely important factor. Sure, you can spend all day trying to unlock the door, but your lantern only has an hour of oil left and you're a hundred feet underground and you're going to want to see things when you try to get out.

    The game could even support both at different times, but there needs to be a lot of talk about when to roll. There's even some pretty decent discussion of this in other parts of the guidelines.

    Reminds me, I've been thinking a lot about how I'd right the D&D 5 DM advice section if I worked for WotC. I should probably do that at some point...

    \n Well, ostensibly no D&D game was ever about dying in a pit trap. But yeah, if we're talking about "what is this game for" then it doesn't have a clear focus.
    It's design is primarily social. Create a platform for the brand that most people can agree "this is D&D". No matter how well designed 4e was, it sadly didn't qualify for a lot of people.
    Its design is also mechanical on an abstract level. As someone who got into roleplaying with 3e (and had a love-hate relationship with it for a long time) I feel that they are fixing a lot of the problems from that edition (mostly by applying 4e solutions in an unintrusive manner).

    If there is a platonic D&D and we ask what is it about the answer is "strategic and careful management of resources, tactical wargamey combat and invention and wonderment"*. Next seems to cover at least two of those without any real commitment. It's not focused but it's functional for _playing D&D_. Whether that will pay off remains to be seen.

    I must admit I am not without bias here. My regular group won't play 4E, and the retroclones feel to archaic or limited to them. I on the other hand like most editions of D&D for various reasons. If Next can give me something with 3e-style customization for my players but with an advancement system that actually functions and elegant solutions and tools in the 4e vein with a simple core like Basic/AD&D for a quicker and dirtier game, this will be the D&D for me. So I'm at least a little invested.


    *there's also the "telling heroic stories" angle in the dragonlance tradition but I feel by now that is considered an evolutionary offshot of D&D

    \n BReenekamp, I'd say there's no such as an "edition" that brought out such flavor, only GMs and groups that brought out the flavor they wanted from the game they played.       \n Setting the difficulty of a roll is a functional mechanic (see Burning Wheel if nothing else). If a GM abuses that to railroad, that's not the game's problem, no more than an MC pre-determining outcomes is a problem of Apocalypse World.

      In fact, one of the reasons why I feel in love with AW so quickly was because it promoted a fiction-first kind of play that I was familiar with from good D&D gaming (not all D&D gaming by a long stretch).

      If the DM says there's a wooden door, then it's DC 17 to break down. Consult the fiction. If you say there's a wooden door, but it's suddenly DC 23 because you don't want the players to go that way, that's bullshit and you should have your DM card revoked. If you put an reinforced adamantium door there instead, then the players will teleport, lockpick or burrow their way through or just go elsewhere. If you have intention to railroad and collapse the passage with an avalanche or whatever, then you will railroad anyway, DCs or no.

      That's why that sidebar in the latest doc is such absurd nonsense. They know better. But I guess Next is also build on pandering to certain demographics and one of them is the partecipationist, "we didn't even roll dice" crowd. And you know, that's fine, they can still build a good, fun, functional game behind that - those people will ignore the mechanics anyway. The important part is the presentation of such "advice" particularly for new gamers.



      On Dis/Advantage: as a DM you don't really modify DCs anymore (no -2/+2 type modifiers like in 3e) you set a DC based on the fiction. And it shouldn't be your job to adjucate _vantage all the time either. The official stance (expressed in design diaries, not sure it's in the playtest docs yet) is that _vantage is only gained through player (or monster) action. Ie. the PC gets advantage when they use a spell, stunt, ability, power or other action that grants one. Maybe you can give _vantage as a freebie once in a blue moon, but it shouldn't be the rule.

      I ran a two hour game a couple of days ago, my first actual playtest of Next. Two friends made a Rogue and a Wizard. The Rogue got shafted by the reintroduction of attacks of opportunity I think, they can't weave in and out of combat anymore as it was implied they could in the first packet. The skills are kinda messed up. I liked their original concepts and ideas for them but now they went in too deep trying to solve problems that are created by the skill system in the first place. I think that if they don't untangle this I will be running games without Backgrounds. They fought a bunch of skeletons and died twice (the first time they recovered and woke as the necromancer's prisoners, the second time we wrapped up the session because it was late).

      Overall the game feels a lot more Howard and a lot more Vance that the last two installments, which in my eyes is a success. Although to be honest, like I commented on ENworld it's more a matter of the system not obstructing such play/flavour rather than actively enforcing it.

      I think it's 3rd edition with 12 years of hindsight. The actual 3rd edition was a bloody mess. This takes AD&D/2E as a jump off point and builds a sequel with 4E's attention to math and streamlined, easy handling. It's also 4E for people who didn't like the actual 4E (meaning it has most of the trappings and innovations of 4E, just presented in a different way - I've always claimed that 4E's main problem was presentation).

      If they keep improving and fixing and expanding this, I will:
      -play it rather than 4e because combat doesn't last hours and it's far easier to keep track of, it makes my players happier with the diversity of classes and it feels it's more like about adventurers than fantasy superheroes (and I personally prefer the former)
      -play it rather than 3E/PF because combat doesn't last hours, the math is flatter and not all-around fucked, casters aren't dominating melee classes, magic items are not assumed, alignment is largely divorced from mechanics, skill points are gone, and I could keep going for hours
      -play it rather than AD&D because it's has the same kind of simplicity and speed without being a glorious mess of confusing subsystems
      -play it rather than Basic/retroclones because it has the same kind of ease to it but with far more classes, races and levels

      If they mess it up, it's not like there's a shortage of other options and games.
