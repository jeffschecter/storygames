    \n 1 - Well, several folks came up with the idea and we said we HAVE to do this. As they used to say on alt.config, "I See A Great Need".

    2 - There were 32 attendees total. For a city of Tucson's size, that's fucking awesome.

    3 - My favorite topics were:

    Berin's "Worldbuilding 101", in which he identified the fundamental question of worldbuilding: Why?

    and Thomas D's "Low Prep/No Prep" presentation, in which he showed how to use relationship maps to help figure out plot twists and opportunities.

    But really, that's like "Steak or Lobster?!" The whole day was incredible.

    4) Oh criminitly, YES, I learned tons of things, re-learned some things I hadn't thought about in a while...the most common reaction I heard from all the grinning, enthusiastic participants was along the lines of "Although I think I knew a lot of this stuff on some level, it really helps to have it boiled down, explained, and expanded on." And of course John Wick's stuff was great.

    I should note that one of the purposes of this was to get lots of styles and lots of ideas together - not every single style or idea could be used by every single participant or group in every single moment. John devoted a nice big piece to in media res, and I have a player who just won't do that. She digs her heels in and simply refuses, that's how much she hates it. So it's not a good technique for her.

    The audience was lively and challenged the speakers, asked excellent open-ended questions and the discussions were wonderful and educational.

    5) It was free, the venue was a local library meeting room, which was awesome and just barely held the number of people who attended. If we expand at all, we will have to go somewhere else next time.

    We did try to record the presentations...right now that audio's being processed, but we didn't really make recording it a big deal, so I'm guessing the quality of the audio's going to be highly variable. When it's in a postable format, I'll put up a URL.

    Really, the fact that this was the very first Gamemaster's Conference in the history of the hobby is a disgrace to the hobby. Training, mentoring, developing partnerships, this is how you get better at everything else in the world, why not GMing?

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI take it the whole thing was very collegial? Was there stuff to learn for everybody in every session? I understand that not every idea is useful to every participant, but I'd worry that some sessions would be total washes - not true?
    \nI don't think there was anyone that was a wash, at least from my perspective. I didn't hear anyone say "well that was pointless" or whatever. The audience being very energetic and interactive was a big plus. They could direct the speakers where they wanted to go.\n
    Did you know everybody who attended? How did you publicize? Who came the farthest? What are these people running for their groups, in general? What was the range of experience and play style?
    \nI didn't know everyone who attended, and some of the presenters I only knew as colleagues-in-gaming, like John and Dave. We had people come from around 2-2.5 hours away in many directions (Bisbee, Phoenix). I didn't do any kind of survey of the audience, we were running out of time, but the majority were at least D&D GMs, from their questions. I had more than one person say "You know, NPCs are so central to what everyone has been saying, but in a lot of our adventures we kinda have been skipping past them, this was great, it gave me some ideas for making them better." Of course we had a strong World of Darkness contingent, including Jess Hartley, Dave Martin and others that accompanied them.

    We publicized with flyers in our 2 gaming stores, the meetup group, and with me posting on various web forums.
