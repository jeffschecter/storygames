    \n I experimented with something I've had in mind for a while. You've seen me do the paper-texture-in-vector-shape thing before, but I wanted to experiment with it a bit to get simpler silhouettes. Think I've mentioned this before, but silhouettes are the absolute key for any character design. I'm still workin' on this skill. :P

    First I made some of the vector shapes, imagining that I'd actually cut these out of paper by hand. (I'm not really, but just building it like that.



    Then I filled in the shapes with paper textures, added color overlays and drop shadows at various depths.



    The nice thing is that each element of these characters is an independent shape. You theoretically really could make paper dolls out of these if you were so inclined. Also, that means I can move around the heads, hands, and posture to get lots of expression.

    Soon I'm going to hand draw the faces, scan them and paste them onto the heads. That way I can swap out faces as necessary. Ultimately, I see these characters (and a handful of others) as the raw material for little comic strips in the actual book. Different-looking enough that it doesn't compete with Liz or Kristin's work, but more colorful than just plain flavor text.

      Comic #1: Flying Lesson

      Elder: [Graceful, almost ethereal] The first time you fly, you will want to find your 'down.' That bond must be eased, the worldly weight released. Up, down, left or right: These are worldly ideas. The only directions that matter are forward and backward, past and future. Let these be your compass, Pilgrim.
      Pilgrim: I think I'm gonna puke.
      Elder: Yes... that happens.



      Comic #2: Names, please.
      Customs Agent: [Bureaucratic, humorless.] Name, please.
      Pilgrims: [All in dramatic poses.] Pilgrim Mitra the Sharp Feather! Pilgrim Jarak the Dragon Bane! Pilgrim Tondo the Emerald Might!
      Customs Agent: Thank you.

      \n Sure, Sam. My pitch is kinda like: it's Continuum but with space instead of time. That only helps if you've know Continuum, though.

      Basically, you play one of a handful of known humans who travel between the stars by literally reaching out and touching them. It's assumed that hundreds or thousands of others wander lost in space, the odds being so astronomical of ever encountering another human being. You trace mysteries left in ruins that are thousands of years old, built by other humans who once roamed the stars. You try to recover those lost. You try to map new trails to expand the network of familiar stars, creating a bigger web that makes the sky more welcoming and becoming lost more difficult. You try to resist the siren song of the Darkness Between the Stars, which drives everyone to loneliness and madness.

      I've got a hack forum up at the AW forums.

      Plus, it's a new version of an idea I worked on with Justin Jacobson at this old blog, when I was thinking about it as a d20-based hack.
