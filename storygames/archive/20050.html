    BeePeeGee:
    What is it, in particular, that you enjoy about separating those two things? Can you talk about that a little more?
    \nWell, it was exactly my experience in playing a roleplaying game with a child:
    This style of playing happened quite naturally: You seperate what you want to roleplay (plot, scene, resolving scene) and how you want to roleplay.
    The biggest benefit is that you truly immerse in acting the scene. The performance flow is not interrupted by dice throwing, plot clarification, skill checks etc.

    In my experience, both phases benefit from it:
    The planning get's really a focus on carving out the most fun scene, brainstorming creatively.
    The acting get's the full joy of losing yourself in the performance.

    - Introduce Castle Blackspire mystery
    - Get into Castle Blackspire (incl. troubles)

    - Wrestling with evil Baron (he gives in)
    - Flashback scene: diving into evil Baron's past

    \n The short version is that some people started becoming very excited about techniques which allowed you to determine a lot of "what would happen" in a free, brainstorming phase (e.g. "stakes setting", where two possible outcomes to a scene are pre-determined, and then we roll dice to see which happens).

    Another group of people (which included many of the Forge founders/leaders) said that this kind of practice was dangerous, since it pre-empted active play "in the moment", and, instead, allowed people to make decisions outside the play space, killing the vitality of the game itself. (e.g. In Monsterhearts, Joe - the author - writes that the rules and the dice serve to "keep the game feral". These people took the stance that such practice "tame" the game and allow people to vet all outcomes in advance, at which point they might as well not play.)

    The latter group seemed to "win out" over time, but a few people remained unconvinced and liked such techniques (and still do, presumably).

    This affected the design of many games, and the way conflicts are handled in, for example, In a Wicked Age... and Apocalypse World seem to me to be reactions to that whole discussion. (In both games, a more standard "stake setting" procedure is replaced with different ways of handling the fallout of conflicts. Both approaches were pretty unusual/innovative when these games were designed.)

    There were many, many discussions around this topic for several years, in any case.

    \n I read Paley's book A Child's Work a few years ago and enjoyed it immensely.

    One thing that Paley emphasizes is that children use play as a developmental tool, not just as a means of recreation. Or maybe more accurately, that children demonstrate how recreation can be a developmental tool. Many children (especially once they reach 4 or 5) center their play around very developmentally salient issues. I have a lot of experience working with preschoolers and they love to play about death, sickness, responsibility, loss, bodies, and other universal human issues.

    The scene-setting/scene-playing distinction is interesting, but I think that's just one tree in the forest. When you zoom out, one of the most distinctive things about children's play is how adaptive it is. Sometimes they set a scene before playing it, sometimes they improvise completely. Older children engage in a constant dialog about the constraints of the fiction/game world, sometimes implicitly and sometimes explicitly. Remember arguing about how many people can be Captain America at once? That's kids negotiating the fiction.

    One of the greatest things about watching kids play is seeing how creative storyplayers bring in children with very diverse play styles. Some kids who want to play very quiet "House" scenarios are very good at incorporating other children who want more physically active play. Children who want to build complex structures can often find heavy lifting or role-playing elements for other children who aren't as engineering-oriented. It's really interesting to watch a group with very different motivations and skill sets work together to improvise a game that meets all their needs.
