\n Hi all,

My game, Scarlet Wake, is ready for wider playtesting.

Scarlet Wake is a competitive GMless roleplaying game of seeking bloody vengeance against the motherfuckers who destroyed your life. It's heavily inspired by \u2018revenge flicks\u2019 like Kill Bill, John Wick, Django, etc. It takes a few sessions to play through a whole game, but it's very intense and fun.

I have a few small incentives so that you can get something out of helping me to playtest too: \n
  • Playtesters who give me feedback on mechanics, like what works and what doesn\u2019t, or what feels too hard or too easy, will have their names added to the Credits.
    • \n
  • Playtesters who send me a link to their actual play experience on a blog or forum will receive a free copy of the final release PDF. Additionally, the coolest actual play account will become the basis for the Example Scene later in the book, and this will be credited to you.
    • \n
  • Playtesters who post a pre-release review of reasonable size will get a free copy of the final release PDF and the physical rulebook.
\nIf you reckon you can help me out with this, please download the playtest PDF here. My email is in the PDF if you want to contact me directly, but I'll check in on this thread too.

Thank you all in advance!

    Escalating numbers of dice are a major problem for a lot of clever designs: you can achieve some great things, but at a cost - your game won't get played as often as you might like.

    I love Dogs in the Vineyard, for example, but I have had to cancel playing it many, many times due to a lack of dice (much more often than I've had a chance to actually play). (And your game requires a lot more than that game!)

    I like math challenges - if I get a chance to look over your rules in more detail, I'll see if I can offer any simplifying ideas!
    \nI can appreciate that barrier, though obviously I personally have a ton of dice.

    One of the quickest wins I can think of right now would be to remove dice for Mooks altogether. Currently, if you're aiming to gain a reasonable amount of Fuel, you'll choose to face a number of Mooks 2 above/below your highest possible die (depending on your Stakes). However in these circumstances, since Mooks are eliminated highest-to-lowest, most of the remaining die will be rolls of 1-2.

    So if I remove the dice for Mooks, and just say "Mooks deal a flat 2 damage each", and each point of your Damage continues to eliminate one Mook, then it's still a gamble how much Fuel you'll gain, but you don't need a ton of d6s. It's less of a gamble, since dice allowed for unpredictable spikes that could send you into a Bind, so I might need to reduce Fire gain as a result, but that's not hard.

    I'm having a lot more trouble eliminating dice from the Traits though. d4s you could do with less of, since as a Reaper, you'll never need more than one, though depending on how you spec your Marks and the potential for Lackeys, you might need another 5. But d12s you'd definitely need 5 for your Reaper and 5 for the Mark, and hope nobody buys a rank 5 Lackey at the same time.

    Though it's possibly worth emphasising that the numbers of dice I've listed are for the group as a whole to share... not for individuals to each bring. It doesn't entirely mitigate your point about requiring a lot of dice, but mathematically, if at least two players each bring 5 poly sets, then you have all the dice you need. But I can appreciate that I'm just justifying myself at this point because I don't have a better solution right now. \n
    The game is seriously beautiful, by the way. Very well done.

    \n Especially given that it's a GMless game, I find some precautions to avoid bleed and keep players comfortable essential:
    1. The usual 'X-Card', "brake", "cut" or other code words when players don't feel well.
    2. I'd suggest using the Microscope RPG "Palette": a list of things you would like to see / definitely don't want to see in the game (i.e. "Yes:supernatural, No:zombies, explicit sexual violence").
    3. I forget the name of the mechanic or it's source. Basically, it's about defining which topics can be played out and which topics should only be vaguely hinted without detail (i.e. "tortured should be only vaguely hinted")
    4. Furthermore, a process to make sure what mood you'd like for your game.

    For example, I could very well imagine this as a slap-stick Anime revenge trip. People could have different ideas from over-the-top humoristic to dark, realistic gritty.

    Here is my opinion on the language:
    It reminds me of how Apocalypse World tried to evoke the mood of the game by using vocabulary like "fuckery" etc.
    Personally, I didn't like it. As a reader, I'd like a tone that's a bit more natural & friendly - a tone that addresses _me_ the player and doesn't identify so much with the in-character jargon.

    One more critical feedback: ten d8s, d10s, d12s... To me that's quite a lot for a "lighter" game system. (pesonally, I've counted up to six, no?).
    For rare or casual RPG players, it might also be a turn-off, if they don't have that many dice. Is there a work-around or an essential minimum no. of dice?

    Also, it would be nice to have more detailed information how long a session ('over several nights') is expected to last: is it gonna be 2 hour sessions or more like 6 hours?

    Can't wait to try it out! Have you found a sweet spot for optimum no. of players (3-4?)?

    Honestly I think there are pros and cons to both perspectives on how much/little I do to hand-hold players and give them options to protect their sensibilities. I've actually cut sections to that effect from the rules on Ron Edward's excellent advice, and I think that for this type of game, that was the correct decision. If sales suffer as a direct consequence of that, I'm actually ok with that, because for me, it's more about the integrity of the game itself (and finally publishing it after 11 years).

    Your point on the dice is also valid, but at this point the only solution I can think of is to halve the amount needed (so, require the minimum for a single Reaper), and then have all rolls be made sequentially, sharing the same dice, so that Reapers and Bad Guys need to remember their results until all rolls are made and finally resolved. I'm not entirely comfortable with that, because it makes play less streamlined and more clunky, but maybe it's a necessary sacrifice?

    On a completely different tangent though, magic missiles hold a special bittersweet sentiment for me. They were the first spell I was excited about with my first D&D character in the first roleplaying campaign I ever experienced. They sounded awesome! I was a sorcerer and was looking forward to blasting holes in bad guys with my bolts of magic awesomeness! And then the game happened, I cast my spell, did 2 damage, not even enough to kill a single goblin, and was left to resort to firing my crossbow for the remainder of the day... That singular experience (also my last) with D&D was what got me so mad that I started making my own games, just so that players wouldn't feel so disenfranchised as I did in my first session. /backstory
