\n So, speaking hypothetically here...

What do you do if there's a great idea you want to wrap a game around, but it's not yours to play with?

Let's say you've got this terrific idea for a game set in the world of Doctor Who. But 1) there are already at least two Doctor Who RPGs out there, and 2) you don't have the rights to the IP.

How far can you go? Can you expect a cease-and-desist letter if you post a free PDF on your site? Has anyone ever done anything like this? Most of the indie games I run across are created from whole cloth, but surely someone has designed and published something along these lines...

Of course, I know that there are few lawyers here, and I'll probably consult with one before I start actual work on this pet project. But, just wondering. Thanks!

-- Rafael

    \n My Avatar: The Last Airbender game, despite being unfinished, gets the most hits of anything on my website. It's been up for over a year, maybe two, and I've never heard anything from anyone. Really, it depends on the IP holders and how big an internet suppression campaign they run. In general, though:

    1. If you do HTML and not PDF, I doubt you'll ever hear anything.

    2. If you do PDF download, you probably run a higher chance of receiving a cease & desist letter. But it still might never come. Definitely avoid distribution through another source, like RPGnow or IPR, even if you're giving it away for free. Link to it from your website.

    3. The only way I can imagine you actually getting sued is if you started selling something with someone else's IP in it.

    This is just the common practice of the internet nowadays, as I see it; not real legal advice. I say go for it, but don't sell it and, if you want to be extra careful, just put it up on a webpage, in an attractive format that's easy to print out. Or go for a free PDF and see what happens.
