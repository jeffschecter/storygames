\n Two questions:

What are peoples' favorite superhero games?

Does anyone know of a good narrative-based superhero game?

I'm mostly a fantasy guy, myself, but I can dig superheroes because they incorporate just about every genre out there. Of all the superhero games I've played (old school DC by Mayfair, the old Marvel, Aberrant, Mutants and Masterminds, and I feel good throwing BESM into the mix), all are pretty much physics-based games. But there are so many good story elements, it seems strange that there isn't a narrative-based game. Or if there is, I don't know of it. Anyone got anything?

    But my new favorite is Supers!
    http://www.rpgnow.com/product/105204/SUPERS-The-Comic-Book-RPG

    New Edition on Kickstarter:
    http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/412792115/supers-the-comic-book-rpg-revised

    Quote from my buddy that is totally my experience with the game too:\n
    I read Supers! not long after it was released, and thought "Man, this is way too light and hand-wavey." I put it down, and didn't think much about it. A few months ago I started up a superhero mini campaign using BASH UE, and pretty quickly got frustrated with some of its fiddlyness. Then I saw someone commenting about how much they liked Supers!, so I hauled it out and read it again. The second reading gave me the same impression as the first, but I decided to give it a go and converted the game over to Supers!...

    I could not have made a better choice.

    In play, Supers! is quick, fun, and evokes the feel of comic books unlike any other superhero RPG that I have ever played (and I've played most all of them).

    I strongly urge everyone to give Supers! a try, and the Revised Edition certainly looks like it's going to smooth a few rough edges off.
    \n
    Supers! is narrative, but with a system that still feels tactical in that you can usually only use a power once per round, so if you are making multiple attacks, defense moves or other actions, you have to come up with descriptions on how you use your other powers or skills help you.

    @catty_big: What do you like about Squadron UK?
    \nI've only actually played it once, but I've chatted to the designer about it quite a bit, as he's been demoing it at pretty much every con I've attended since the beginning of this year, so I feel like I've played it by osmosis. So what do I like about it?

    1. The chargen, specifically the random generator for the superpowers (which is most of it as there's no personality, it's a wham bam kind of game rather than one that supports a lot of in-character roleplaying, although you can of course roleplay a personality if you want). It looks quite complicated but isn't really, although it does take up a fair amount of time. The thing I like about it particularly is the fact that there's such a wide variety of abilities to select from.

    2. The fact that whereas most table-top supers games try to replicate the feel of the movies, SqUK- in an extremely innovative move- seeks to emulate the feel of the actual comics, and the way it does this is by having the action happen in panels, IIRC the dice roll yielding the number of panels you can create on your turn. It's a bit analogous to a hex game, so if you are ten panels away from where you want to be, and you roll a six (I can't remember the d-size offhand) that's how many panels your action happens across, and if you're within range of your target, the villain has an opportunity to have a go at you, and you to defend yourself etc. (Sorry, I can't remember the combat rules).

    3. It uses minis. Now I'm not a huge fan of minis- well, I'm a regular on a storygames forum, so it kinda figures- but I really like that aspect in this case, because again you can emulate the actions of your superhero flying through the air and landing on top of the bad guy etc. Plus they're beautifully designed. Are they blank and can you paint them? Ah sorry, once again, my memory fails me.

    4. The package the game comes in. A great deal of care has gone into this- it's not a book, however nor is it a box in the traditional sense, rather it's a battleship grey case which looks a bit like a workperson's tool-kit, and which contains the rulebook, the minis, all tastefully arranged, plus a bag for the dice. All in all a very attractive package.

    Hope that's helpful.

    \nHi

    I'm Simon Burley the author of Squadron UK. We DO seem to meet up at odd places.

    Thanks for the kind words and I hate to correct you but I don't know where you got battleship grey case from Squadron UK is a traditional rulebook. It think you may have been referring to to box I carry round for conventions. That's my own personal convention kit.

    Though the rules are bloody brilliant - the best character generation system around, panel by panel simulation of the comics, your play in this scenario directly affects your success in others etc etc. - i think in terms of this thread it falls into the physics based games referred to in the first post. SqUK and Golden Heroes and most other traditional SHRPGs assume that what you see in the comic is only a snippet of what happens. There's loads going on in the background and we play out every blow.

    Now I've speeded things up for SqUK. You won't spend your whole evening on one battle, they is plenty of time for Roleplaying. However, as we've discussed at cons, I still think there may be room for another type of game which ups the Roleplaying between battles and reduces battle times. Traditional SHRPGs are like FIFA 2013 - the match is the focus. Do we need a game like FIFA manager - where its the planning and setup that's the key and the matches take care of themselves.

    Not surprising that smallville is mentioned. All TV superhero shows have to sideline the battles for budget reasons. I'd also cite Heroes as source to base a low-physics high character game on. The powers are much broader.

    Sorry to woffle on. It IS my specialism.

    Have you found the game you want or do you think I should write one?

    \n I still love Hero System for supers play; and I think it was ahead of its time with Disadvantages, in that they give player authority over some of the world (or at least that's how I let most of them work when I GMed).
    * Awards for playing failure/negative consequences; and "keys" (Psych/Phys Lims)
    * Spotlight time mechanically generated by frequency (Hunted/Watched/etc)
    * Creation of organizations or entities in the game world (Hunted, DNPC)

    For Hero System, it's all about the initial setup and premises. If you build for "kewl powerz" and to be super-efficient, you miss out on a LOT of the system's support for 'narrativist play' (yes, a slight misnomer--work with me!).

    What I'm missing is some rules/structure for the story in between battles.

    Any ideas?
    \nIn my Marvel heroic hack, I made up some rules for 'downtime' scenes between fights. Here's what I wrote:


    --- --- ---

    Scenes during downtime provide opportunities for heroes to do many things, including (but not limited to):
    \u2022\thaving casual conversations with each other
    \u2022\thaving intense personal conversations with the other characters in their lives
    \u2022\tpursuing long-range goals
    \u2022\tinvestigating mysteries
    \u2022\tmaneuvering for advantage in upcoming encounters

    Every hero gets one scene of downtime between encounters. Each hero can buy one extra scene of downtime by spending a PP.

    The GM gets one scene of downtime between encounters. They can centre this around heroes or villains.


    Framing the scene

    The person starting a scene of downtime answers three questions:

    1.
    What do you want to do during the scene? Phrase this as the \u2018question\u2019 you want this scene to answer. (This will give you guidance on when to end the scene\u2014once the question has been answered.)

    As an example, imagine that during the previous encounter the heroes learned there\u2019s trouble the AI controlling an orbital weapons platform. One of the players wants their scene of downtime to discuss what they\u2019ll do and who will go. The question, naturally, is \u201cWhat are we going to do and who\u2019s going to do it?\u201d

    Another good example question that will probably be used quite often is \u201cHow did I survive?\u201d

    2.
    Who is in the scene? Other heroes? Any significant NPCs? Incidental characters? Just yourself?

    The GM is responsible for playing any NPCs in the scene. Each NPC should want something that
    \u2022\tcomplicates the hero\u2019s attempts to be a hero
    \u2022\tcomplicates the hero\u2019s personal life
    \u2022\tthreatens the hero\u2019s secret identity
    \u2022\tallows the hero to demonstrate, question or explore an aspect of their personality or motivation
    \u2022\tshows another side to the hero
    \u2022\tmakes it difficult for the hero to get what they want
    \u2022\tendangers someone close to the hero
    \u2022\tforces the hero into a bind

    3.
    Where does the scene take place?


    General advice

    If the scene lacks focus, remind yourselves about the question you want the scene to answer.

    The starting player or the GM can end the scene.

    Once everyone (including the GM) has had their scene of downtime (plus any extra scenes heroes purchase with PP), the GM initiates the next encounter.

    \n I generally give players a hook. "You are doing this job.." or "You are on your date with X" and see what they choose to do and how it develops, the hook method gives them something going on, but I let them direct it with their in game choices. Sometimes that flows well, other times it doesn't--but there will be fallout from those scenes either way.

    In H&S2E, I require characters to come with three Stress Triggers (called Hooks) these hooks are people who they interact with--three in total. That allows me to have people to oppose them, date them, hate them, love them, and put in danger.

    \n I\u2019ve used Everway and I\u2019ve used Fudge for supers. Fudge worked the best. We were playing a very X-Men inspired campaign but with the serial numbers filed off and names changed. For example, the Jean Grey character was called Mesmeralda and her clone was called Klezmeralda. The team was called S-Men and the professor was called Professor Shaver.

    The players were playing the junior team and we were playing c:a early New Mutants era (so Paul Smith era X-Men, with elements from the original Claremont Wolvie/Kitty miniseries), but the players were playing all original characters instead of NM based characters. The device of having them playing the junior team was good because then the senior S-Men characters could be more funny, more powerful, and didn\u2019t have to experience as surprising events\u2014it was fine that they were retreading ground from the comics. We all pitched in in describing their adventures. We had plenty of player contribution to what was happening.

    It was fun. In the end, more of a fun game, than a serious tactical super powers game.
