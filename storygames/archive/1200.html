Hi all,

During the '90s I did some development and playtesting for RPGs, and was pretty disenchanted with the whole industry. I continued gaming on a personal basis, but retreated from contributing in the larger community and industry. I went from being an avid collector of any RPG in print to focusing on the older systems I owned. Later around 2001, I started promoting gaming on a local mailing list and began designing games to eventually release. I didn't frequent Bulletin Boards, or Forums, or anything. I worked in isolation from other designers and the industry in general. So being here has been a little bit of a system shock. A lot of the indie-games-in-your-face-huwah contributed to this system shock, and the semi-canonization of certain demagouges in the gaming field resulting in the polarization of camps that were always there but now are entrenched. Reflecting on the time of isolation from these aspects of the gaming field has led me to both appreciate my retreat and to be thankful I discovered this site to be aware of community concerns. I am glad that my existing games were developed before I stumbled upon these aspects. This forum has generated ideas for other games for me, some of them even in-your-face-indie-attitude games, but I personally think the venom in some of the this-is-the one-true-way ideaologies is not generating positive results for game development.

Just my opinion.

    Gryph - I can see where you might feel that way from being "out of the loop" for the time period you describe. However, there's enormous and enormously fertile crossover between mainstream and indie gamers and games, for most ordinary definitions of "indie" (this is not an invitation to pop open that debate again). It's still fairly rare, though getting less so, for a mainstream game to pick up on a thread laid down by an indie game, but gamers are getting more highly attuned to social issues, game focus, and other indie-game booster ideas. Some indie games have been a great success in the mainstream gamer community and are highly critically acclaimed and (most importantly) played by thousands of people. So from a practical point of view your perception is dead wrong.

    What you are seeing is simple branding, just as you might in any other hobby, and as you no doubt saw prior to your withdrawal from the world of gaming. Some people get a piece of themselves tied up in the label on the package because they like what's in the package. You have done this at some point in your life, it's a natural thing and marketers pay hundreds of thousands of dollars to try to figure out how to get you to do this.

    Man, I am awash in sympathy.

    I have, through Dexcon and Gencon, met a bunch of the people in this and the Forge community and they have, to a man (or woman) been a pleasure to meet. I like them and I think well of them, and that takes the edge off the fact that I have shared (and occasionally still do share) most of the sentiments and concerns that you list. What this community is can only be described as a fuzzy and sometimes troubling thing indeed.

    But with that in mind, I can point to the two things that keep me from stressing too much. The first is, as mentioned, meeting folks and thinking of them as individuals.

    The second is, frankly, I like it better here. I like the post-diaspora atmosphere of Story Games, and while the Forges gravity can never be entirely escaped, this is a pretty distant orbit, and it works for me. So much so that when the answer to a question here is a link to something very long and involved at the Forge, I die a little inside. This isn't the Forge, and a lot of what rocks has grown out of that fact.

    Anyway, I sympathize with your concerns. We went round and round on this early on, and I still firmly feel that there _are_ some entrenched One-True-Wayisms floating about, though others will certainly argue otherwise. Thankfully, they are mindfully held, and for all that I think you will find them, I do not think you will encounter any which serve as an _end_ point to conversation. Similarly, opinions on things like the value of drift, the utility of narration, player/game types and the role of the GM are far from monolithic. (The one exception is arrogance. You'll find that here in spades. Why, I myself have arrogance enough to power the mighty computers behind this board for generations to come, and I'm sure you will find similar sentiments among the, erm, *lesser* lights.)

    But here's the flip side. People here _do_ like one another (in general) and are enthusiastic about what they do. Mostly, this is an awesome thing, for reasons which are, I hope, obvious, but hell, it also has its problems. Cliquishness and pedestals are constant threats, but perhaps most importantly, when you talk about how bad some game/behavior/theory is, you need to be careful that it doesn't sound like you're busting on the guy behind it. From the receiving end, it can feel like a fierce defense of dogma, but in reality, it's people sticking up for a friend, which may explain why the knuckles get bloodied up so fast.

    Anyway, that's kind of intimidating, and that enthusiastic closeness (coupled with numerous folks who very clearly liked it better when the club was smaller) can be off putting - it's really easy to feel like you're watching the cool kids club. I know that's a perception I wrangled with up until...well, I'll let you know when I stop. But what's important to note is that it's (mostly) not true - the community really would rather have you on the inside than the outside, and they demand no kool'aid be drunk, only that you be thoughtful and polite, or barring that, entertaining.

    Anyway, this is much farther into how I view the world than I meant to go, but I figured you should know that there's no universal denial of the warts so much as a willingness to go forward, warts and all.

    -Rob D.
