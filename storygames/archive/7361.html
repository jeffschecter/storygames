\n I need some word-smithing help, in tuning up Misery Bubblegum.
\n
\nThere is a set of questions that players answer for their characters to start the game, and it (currently) goes roughly like this:
\n
\n(1) Point to someone at the table, and tell us a dream your guy wants to achieve with them.
\n(2) How would everything go wrong if they knew how you felt?
\n(3) Point to someone at the table and tell us why you blame them for your inability to achieve your dream
\n
\n#1 and #3 seem to work just fine, but #2 isn't phrased ... uh ... quite right.
\n
\nThe general idea is this: Someone else is really important to your character. If they feel the way you feel, and they want the things you want, life is going to be really, really nice. On its own, this would indicate that the rational thing to do is, as quickly as possible, to tell them how you feel and what you want, and find out whether they feel and want the same. After all, if they don't it's no big loss, right?
\n
\nQuestion #2 is trying to get at the downside risk: What might you lose by speaking out, which you could keep by being silent.
\n
\nClassic example: "I want to tell him how much I love him ... but if he doesn't feel the same then I don't think we could even be friends any more. It would be so awkward! I can't bear to risk losing him so completely. Friends is fine. Friends is good. I can be content with that."
\n
\nThe current phrasing of question #2 is inconstant in leading people to such things. It's ambiguous. People can also answer (quite legitimately) "His role in my dream is to be the stooge who takes the fall for my crimes, and if he found out then he'd turn me in to the cops, which would ruin everything."
\n
\nWhat I'd like is a rephrasing of question #2 that, while short, is more focussed and clear. That may well involve rewrites on questions #1 and #3 as well.
\n
\nWe got a lot of smart, verbal folks here on the board. Can someone solve this conundrum?

    \n Rich: Is it possible to make it more concrete? Like "I might lose his respect" comes in all sorts of flavors: "He might look at me with kindness and pity, and I just couldn't take that from that SELLOUT!" or "He might laugh at my weakness" or "He might try to help me 'get over' my sinful feelings", etc., etc.
    \n
    \nI've been really impressed by the results when players give their characters a very specific nightmare-scenario: At GenCon, Julie played a character who had secretly written lyrics of a romantic song for the punk band she was part of, and her dream was to see the lead singer singing them, but her fear was that he would look at her while he sung it. That specificity evoked much more (I think) than saying "I'd lose my anonymity" would have.

    \n Tony:

    I think the wording you used to explain it to us was pretty successful, though I'll agree that it was a bit clunky. I remember it being something like...

    1) Point to someone and tell a dream that you have involving that character
    2) Tell us why you *know* that everything would go *horribly horribly wrong* if Person 1 found out about your dream.
    3) Point to someone that you blame for the fact you haven't achieved your dream

    I think that was a good starting point - I felt like everyone at the table grokked the angsty place you were trying to get us to go with the wording. Though, I'd like to see it explicitly stated that you can blame yourself, because I don't know that we picked up on that.
