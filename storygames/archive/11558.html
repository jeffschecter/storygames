    I mean, I personally might want to see magic-users and descending Armor Classes in an old-school game, but that's because I remember them from 1986, not because there is anything inherently fun or cool about those things. So if you're playing with a group who has no nostalgic attachment to that stuff, why strive to re-create it?

    \n I think it's not a matter of "how much", but of "where and why" - in other words, don't just punt on your GM responsibilities, but rather make reasoned choices about where and when to seek player authority. For example, a pretty old school conceit is to extend a player's character authority into determining many things about the character's background, and therefore even the society and issues of that background. If a character is an elf, it's pretty old school -natural to have that player make major contributions about what it means to be an elf, which is the same as determining what elves are like in the game world.

    Probably the most important thing to my mind is to know the time and place - I could see myself asking the players in between session about what they think about the idea of placing a large desert to the west of the basetown of a campaign, for example. That's something the characters presumably know about either way. I wouldn't, however, make that same question when the characters have just heard that the McGuffin is to the west and the characters are preparing to leave for a long journey there - how is the player supposed to answer that sort of question, except by reminding the GM that it is in fact his job to decide and describe that sort of thing so the players can react to it, not the other way around. Tell me as a player that there is a desert to the west, and I'll know to pack extra water. Don't ask me whether there is such a desert, for on what basis would I make this choice?

    Of course that's just setting canon (or backstory, as we often call it nowadays). In my experience you can be much more equal about narrating conflict resolutions and such without making a mess of the GM role. By all means let players narrate their character actions without constant affirmations from you, too - I've never quite understood the point in asking the GM whether there is an inn in town when you could just declare such and expect the GM to correct you if he wants; presumably the GM is listening to what you say, so you don't need to explicitly ask his permission for every little thing separately.

    \n Conan likes wilderness adventure, I hear. The travel from one adventure location to another is important to him, not having a social framework to fall back on.

    I like the large dungeon (done it), I like wilderness sandbox (sort of done it), I like even set-piece adventures (done it) as long as they're well designed and respect the role of the players and their characters. I'd have to say that my choice would depend largely on personal GMish inspiration - first I'd have some idea of what I'd like to play fiction-wise, and that would then determine the structure.

    One way to look at this is as an issue of initial focus: if you start the play from the dungeon door, that's a different initial experience from if you start at an inn. My longest D&D campaign used a specific inn-based procedure: the players would make certain city-based information gathering moves, and I'd give them a list of rumours from a pre-prepared list on the basis of their effectiveness in this work-mongering. The information collection would then result in a number of different types of adventure hooks, among which the players would choose which to go after. These hooks would differ in terms of reliability, level of danger, consequences for ignoring, character-based relevance and so on, so the adventuring party would have to argue among themselves whether they should investigate a potential danger to the city, uncertain rumours about an enemy cult or other priorities.

    My point in describing the above is that if you'd find that sort of initial scene interesting, then you might want to go with a sandbox. If, on the other hand, you want to ensure that the first session is already solidly adventurous and not just logistical, then you might choose to start the game from the dungeon door. A sandbox is more work than a set-piece adventure like the Fury of Nifur (at my blog) in some ways, although good GM technique can cut the prep down to a minimum - I used to think up around 20 new hooks every three sessions when the characters got back to town, but I usually wouldn't make any detailed prep for the potential adventures behind them, improvising the necessary details on the spot as the game proceeded.

    \n To reiterate and also get back on topic, it really helps if you do see OD&D as a S&S rather than a Tolkien-esque high fantasy game, arguments about influences set aside. The game will run much smoother if you assume:

    a) The PCs are rogues, not heroes - the rules expect this (XP for money, comparatively little XP for monsters killed, no XP for roleplaying) and demand it (PC death is pretty common, so nobody is going to stick around long enough to save the world even if it needed it);
    and
    b) There's a world going on entirely independently of the players, so encounters don't have to "make sense" and/or conform to any kind of plot or story.

    The key element of old school play is that it's sandboxy. Player initiative is important. A lot of DMs give their players a list of a dozen or so rumours when the game begins, so they can follow their own noses with an element of choice. Rumours might be as banal as "there's a troll living under a bridge nearby" or as deliberately weird as "an army of flying monkeys has invaded the lowlands". It's likely that at least one will be something along the lines of "you've heard talk from the locals about an ancient temple/cave network/abandoned castle/etc., etc.", i.e. a dungeon.

    \n If you feel that you and the group are clueless, I'd recommend starting at the dungeon. It's simply less hassle. Narrate: "After several hours of travel you finally find the foxhole farmer McGritty complained about. Except it's not a foxhole, not unless foxes are three feet tall and are in the habit of leaving the boots and anklebones of their victims scattered in front of the cave.     The danger in starting with the pub and the rumourmongering and all that is that the players might pick up the wrong cues about the point of play. Another danger is that the pubbery and travel might take so long that you don't have time to bite deep into the dungeon during the first session. The big benefit of starting at the pub is that it helps distill the in-character goals for the players; if you read that treatise of mine, you might have noticed how I think that player-directed motivation is a key part of successful fantasy adventure roleplaying: you want the players to internalize character motivations that they then struggle to execute, rather than just doing things because the GM told them to. But if you find that you have enough to juggle in the dungeon alone, then I believe that allowing the players to orient themselves can wait. Make the first dungeon a simple one, something where the characters can come back carrying a load of expensive furs or objects of art. Then they can waste some time haggling for them in town, hear some rumors and so on. Then, in the second or third session, you can ask them why their group is together and what sort of adventure they might be interested in next. A particularly clever GM would do the rumourmongering and orienting towards a new mission at the end of a session to have time to prepare for whatever direction the players choose.

    \n 3d6 in order is the method of character generation dictated not only by St. Gygax and G-D, but also by the sweet sisters Reason and Prudence. Deviating from this simple method is a clear sign of ulterior motives that do not befit a dungeoneer.

    And yes, I myself find that advance information will immeasurably improve the D&D challenge scenario, which often becomes immeasurably dull and simplistic by my measure. I would say that it is very easy to give the players too little information, but very hard to give too much. The more they know, the more they can prepare, and the more the eventual failure or success means to them. I've myself noticed very concretely how players given ample foreknowledge happily buzz away with logistics, tactics, resource-consumption planning and other nuances of overcoming a challenge.       \n I myself greatly prefer 3d6 in order. This is for solid game design reasons, I feel, so it's a shame that many people seem to find the random chargen just a sorry artifact of the past that should be replaced by more player control.

      The main philosophical argument for random character generation is that the random element provides the player with an incontroversible, unique mechanical landscape against which he gets to make his choices. Because it's random and created on the spot, the player can't pre-play or develop hang-ups about always playing a half-elf ranger or whatever. Instead he has to look at what he gets and compromise with the facts. This is healthier as an overall attitude in my games, as I do my best to strip the game of any notion of "character development" that does not correspond to in-character motivation; no pre-planned feat selections, prestige classes, probably not even fully public class progressions. Considering this attitude, it would be counter-productive if a player got to develop his character in a very controlled, replicable way.

      Ultimately I simply think that a player should have no reason to want this control at the early stage in character creation, and if he does (wanting to play a wizard or nothing, say), then he should cast aside these hang-ups and focus that burning passion on in-play achievement. One of the biggest mechanical differences with my D&D is probably that character abilities are much more fluid than expected; not only are there plenty of magical or otherwise exceptional opportunities for messing with them, but the abilities can also develop as they are used, sort of like Runequest skills. So if you want to play a wizard, but the ability scores didn't allow that, maybe you could make your character a wizard through play, hmm?

      The negative facet of this is simple, too - when I end up playing with a point-buy or other controlled chargen system, I absolutely require the game to also have a clear creative orientation for me to follow. I don't like it at all if a game just gives me a setting and a bunch of character options and tells me to create whatever character I want. That sort of thing works if you're a frustrated author or have a favourite character you play in every game, but if you come to the game wide open, that system leaves you hanging. I just don't usually have the requisited inner frustration that tells me to pick the most powerfullest character class out of the twenty options. Much less disorienting to roll some dice and follow their lead. Quicker, too - I remember vividly how uselessly long the Runeslayers chargen is in comparison to simplified D&D.

      --

      As for in-character communication, I'm strongly against any sort of external control in this regard. The GM can encourage colorful and fiction-supporting language by his own example, but trying to enforce anything is foolish - the players won't do it unless they feel like it, and them feeling it isn't helped by GM control. Commit to a creative fiction, and it will be expressed in how the players play their characters, including proper use of out-of-character knowledge and other such worries.

      \n A central artifact in my sort-of-ongoing fantasy campaign is called the "Alder Gate". This product of eldritch arboreal craft resides deep under a city built over a long-abandoned underground laboratory / pleasure palace of a legendary wizard. The gate takes you to the Other Side, which is explicitly not a direct part of the game environment (no scenes set in there, that is), and thus it's pretty vague what the Other Side actually is. However, many of those who choose to step into the Alder Gate also come out after an indeterminate time. Their wounds and diseases heal, their skin bleaches unnaturally white, their hair and eyes might change colors, too, and all of their ability scores are rerolled with a doubles-explode rule, and the new scores are then arranged by size to match the nature of the old array.

      (Hmm... I should publish some more of this stuff in my blog, really - I have some session reports stashed away, I wonder why I've forgotten to put them up...)

      Whether this procedure produces elves or Melniboneans or angels or demons or what is sort of a point of discovery for the campaign. At least it should be obvious to a player that undergoing the "treatment" is a carte blanche for the GM to mess about with the character's integrity; perhaps it is worth it if you don't like your current ability scores. (For what it's worth, the only guy actively seeking for this thing is a power-mad merchant prince who's convinced that the Alder Gate can heal his son's deadly ailment.) It's also pretty obvious to me that something like rolling average or bad ability scores is ultimately a completely meaningless complaint in a campaign environment that includes this sort of stuff. Even the most down-to-earth sword & sorcery fiction involves personal transformations, prophets chosen by gods and other such stuff that might elevate anybody, no matter how low their Strength score, into a Very Important Person. There is no reason to get hung up about character protagonism just because your character doesn't have high scores; it seems to me like the worst kind of simplicity if your character being interesting and important is dependent on mechanical entitlement instead of in-fiction material. If only your scores matter, is that because there is not enough fictional context for events to do anything else but roll dice against that score?

      In comparison, I don't quite understand the point of some rule about rerolling your scores until you get sufficiently high ones - isn't it much more interesting to step through the Alder Gate to get that reroll? Or find a magic item that allows you to use an ancient warrior's strength score as your own? Or retreat into a monastery for a decade to improve in a natural way? Lots of options that are open to characters if you get rid of the idea that ability scores are permanent and should only be changed systematically every fourth level or whatever. That static view of ability scores, combined with the impact they have on character strength, breeds resentment and frustration. (With good reason, too, if you ask me - the world of modern D&D is a caste society of haves and have nots insofar as ability score distributions go.) In my own rules ability scores are pretty influential in determining your success (practically all checks are ability + class level + d20 against a set difficulty), but they also fluctuate about as easily as your experience level, so it's not the end of the world if you start play with a weak character - it's a bit more challenge to begin with, that's all, but smart play will make the character just as powerful as the next guy.

      \n Another thought:

      In OD&D, if you have high stats, you get an experience bonus, meaning you learn/progress/advance faster than other characters. Stats aren't really a big issue in old versions of D&D--they don't make much difference. However, in most games, and later versions of D&D, they do make a significant difference.

      So, I was thinking, from the perspective that a randomly-generated character defines a starting position for you to play from, with its attendant challenges and advantages, reversing that could be an interesting design choice:

      * Roll stats randomly.
      * The lower your stats, the more of an experience point bonus you get.

      So, a high stat character is more effective, but slower to advance--a "safer" choice in a sense. A low stat character is riskier, but with bigger payoffs, as you accrue experience faster than the others if you survive. That seems to make more sense, to me.
