\n Here's a fun topic - what's your first choice for a non-gaming book that sheds light on games, gamers and/or gaming? And why? What does it tell (or show us) that we are not likely to pick just by gaming for a long time? What implications does it have for playing better (and what do you mean by "better")?

(We could also include non-_game_ books that are about gaming. But please be careful with that - only pick something that you really think tells us non-obvious things. History alone, with clear implications for the future, is not what I'm looking for here.)

Bonus points - link to a review or an article about the book that we can all read right now, for free.

    \n I'll start, by choosing Carl Wilson's "Let's Talk About Love: Why Other People Have Such Bad Taste", which is about Celine Dion, and snobbery, and how many people use snobbery to build sense of identity that they then defend with vicious passion.

    I choose because if you replaced "Celine Dion" with "participationist linear-plot wish-fulfilment 2E+ D&D", you'd have a book that's basically about me. Given that analogy, I identify very strongly with Wilson's account of how he equates musical taste with moral value, and how he finds it very hard to see people who like sentimental, polished, corporate music with anything but contempt. His explanation of why he and I do this (it's about social status through cultural capital) is consistent with my experience. And his progression from contempt to (tentative) acceptance is something that I'm working on myself.

    Wilson's a good writer and he comes across as open and honest. There's a nice set of response essays at the back, by many different people, which helped me think through the implications of the main text.

    This New Yorker article is a decent summary of the key points http://www.newyorker.com/books/page-turner/people-who-like-cline-dion-are-people-too

    \nMade to Stick is a book about what makes ideas catchy. Very useful when it comes to provoking reactions in people, whether it's pitching your game or your con slot or your GM content. Following its advice is often easier said than done, but when I can pull it off it's great. The authors' key acronym for the catchy idea is SUCCESS -- Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories and I forget the last S but 6 out of 7 without looking it up ain't bad. If I need to hook players' interest in some path as GM, "Concrete" and "Unexpected" are the key -- the latter arouses curiosity and the former makes it easier to envision a course of action.

    Agreed with Rickard on Understanding Comics.

    Funny on Tao Te Ching -- I feel like those ideas inform my intuitive social play, but not at all my design or analytical efforts.

    I've been meaning to read G\xf6del, Escher, Bach but the size is daunting.

    \n Focusing solely on the gaming bits -- there's the first adventure we see where the PCs succeed at a cost: One of their numbers loses her magical eyesight. The final adventure is described only briefly. It's one of those sessions almost everyone's had, the one intended to wrap everything up neatly that just doesn't click and everything about it feels off.

    There are also scenes where the members of the gaming group draw on their own PCs to help them handle situations where they feel out of their depth. It's done sparingly and subtly, and this being a John M. Ford book, I am certain there's a lot going on that I didn't catch.

    Also, with one exception that I learned the author was never comfortable with, the entire novel is a long tracking shot. Okay, that's not got anything to do with gaming directly, but it's neat.
