\n A conversation I had at Dragonmeet on Saturday inspired me to take another look at Puppetland (which can be dredged from the depths of John Tynes web site).

I was struck again by how excellent the ideas are. But I couldn't help but notice that, while the game has a character creation system, rules for interaction between players and the GM and a setting, it does not seem to have any kind of resolution system. The author suggests that one could be grafted on, if the reader was so inclined.

Some of the rules are challenging. For example, players can only speak with the voice of their characters - if the player says it, the character said it. So the only way to act in the game world is to narrate your actions in character. The GM must narrate as if he were the narrator in a story book i.e. in the past tense.

So, I was wondering, has anyone played this game? How did it go? Were you able to stick to the interaction rules? Did the lack of a resolution system matter?

Has anyone tried to use any kind of resolution system with Puppetland? Did it affect your ability to speak only in character?

    \n In the edited volume "Second Person," a grade-school teacher describes using Puppetland to improve his students' writing skills. It's pretty neat: the students start to use more vivid and descriptive language, are more able to narrate the arcs of plots (beginning, middle, end), and enjoy writing more. I don't know how closely the teacher adheres to the rules-as-written, but he doesn't say he adds anything.

    Wardrip-Fruin, Noah (2007). Second person: Roleplaying and story in games and playable media. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.

    \n It's been a while since I've played Puppetland...but now I want to, again. We just playtested my latest game last night (A Flower for Mara) which is in the Jeepform tradition ('cause, like, all the cool kids are doing it), and I'm thinking that I'm probably in the right zone to play Puppetland.

    When I ran it (which is probably six years ago, at this point), I remember feeling very naked as Narrator. No dice to hide behind; just my decisions. Now, I think that I'd flourish in that environment. I also think that adding dice or whatnot would break it.

    Puppetland was also responsible for one of my favorite gaming moments. My sister Adiel was playing a puppet who had the description "I cannot be sad". At one point in the game, something awful happened. She said, "That's terrible", and then she scribbled off a puzzle piece from her character sheet. The loss wasn't a punishment to the player; it became a way to express what was happening to the character. Great stuff.

    Also, if you can find the Hogshead edition, it's worth getting for the art.

    Seth Ben-Ezra

    \n We played six sessions of Puppetland... ohmygod ten years ago. My friend Jon, the voice actor, was the Narrator. He was a courier for Kinkos at the time, and he would drive around all day with a handheld recorder, practicing the voices of the various characters we would meet. The other player, Dan, actually hand-made our puppets for the final three sessions (a paper cutout shadow-puppet dragon for him, a crotchety old man finger-puppet for me). It was an unforgettable gaming experience.

    And the game totally has a resolution system -- it's just a really sketchy one. "This puppet can..." and "This puppet cannot..." form the boundaries of it.

    Speaking only in character seemed like a huge obstacle for about thirty seconds. Then we did it as if it was the most normal thing in the world. The one-hour time limit makes it possible. I highly recommend sticking to that strictly, no exceptions.

    \n Yup, Harper is quite correct. Technically, the diceless resolution system in Puppetland is much like the one in Amber: straight comparison between stats, with the GM as the final arbiter of the outcome. In practice the "is" lists even correspond to the four attributes of strength, speed, size and weight... It's something of a filler game for our group, since each session is only meant to last an hour and doesn't require elaborate preparation in advance.

    \n Well, as the Q&A; at the end of that online version mentioned, the game as written essentially runs on fiat, with the ability lists determining the outcome unless the players come up with some creative description or cunning scheme that's enough to persuade the Puppetmaster otherwise. Again as in Amber, resolving any conflict relies heavily on the good judgment of the GM, although the booklet does suggest just going along with the actions of the PCs and letting the "good guys" almost always win in the end, unless the players try something completely ridiculous (such as blowing up a house by suddenly declaring that it's built on a giant firecracker).
    \n
    \nTo quote a relevant bit from "Puppetmastering":
    \n"In the course of each tale, the actors will be doing their best to react to the situations you throw at them and find a way of turning each situation to their advantage. You'll have to adjudicate what happens when puppets come into conflict, whether that conflict is physical, verbal, or emotional.
    \n
    \nDon't sweat the details. If the Sally Red Buttons player says she picks up a rock and throws it at the nutcracker, don't worry too much about whether a rock would really be there, close at hand. Of course, if an actor tries to do something ridiculous -- 'It's a good thing this house is built on top of an enormous firecracker! I'll light it!' -- then you should disallow it: 'Sally looked to and fro, but there was no sign of the firecracker. That must have been some other house.'"

    \n True, Punch's dastardly plans might include planting firecrackers into the homes of Puppettown so that anyone who opposes him would have to live in the shadow of being blown away at the least sign of defiance. However, that's not really up to the actors to decide during play. Puppetland may be a storytelling game, but it's one that makes a sharper distinction between the GM and the other players than just about any "conventional" RPG. Even the online text from the old site isn't exactly misleading about the authority of that one person. For instance, from the beginning:
    \n
    \n"The puppetmaster is responsible for creating and presenting the stories of the game, and he or she serves both as referee for the game's rules and as an actor who portrays a number of supporting roles."
    \n
    \nAnd from the end:
    \n
    \n"Maker's Land may be a beautiful place, but all children grow up and perhaps it is the destiny of Maker's Land to grow up, too. Judy claims that the Maker's tear will restore everything to the way it was -- will it? That's up to the puppetmaster."
    \n
    \nThe Hogshead version just makes all that a little more explicit, with examples of narration and cut scenes that a GM might use to guide the flow of her tale.
