\n Hey all: So over the past two days I've been thumbing through my first edition, second printing L5R book thinking of throwing down sometime as a GM. Before, even though I'm a Japanophile (or at least can speak the language, my "love" is cornered to media, stationary, traveling and food), and even though I find the L5R setting not only bearable (the names and location names: BARELY. But still), but awesome, I have never run it for more than one session.

The reason, I realized, is that someone (or the group as a whole) has to set up a sandbox; a situation to which all the players jump in, make characters, and resolve. If you don't, and all players come to the table with a Crane Archer, a Crab Shadowlands Zombie Hunter, a Lion Noble Warrior, a Phoenix Shugenja, and a Dragon Shaolin Monk... FUCK. The GM will have to do some major work to get them together, do phenomenal work every second in order for them not to run and tear each other apart, and then, I dunno, present basically FedEx quests until the campaign is over. At least, that was my thought.

Recently, I've had a thought to ignore all the bizarre CCG-seeped voluminous history of people, events and shit (well, was planning on doing that anyway) and use the 1st edition as a clean slate: And have basically a campaign where most of the players are Scorpions attempting to regain their domain, with one character from another clan (for spice). The situation would be a behind-the-scenes setup and takedown, focusing on key members of other clans. Mostly social, some fighting.

I wanted to toss the idea out there, and make a thread where people in the know of L5R (any edition) could possibly toss in a one-paragraph situation.

Also, and this is a side-question: If anyone out there has 1st ed and 3rd edition, is there any reason to get 3rd edition? What is "better" about it, if anything?

-Andy

    \n Another situation:

    All PCs (except, and this might be the Andy Spice Technique: One PC is from another clan, for spice) are in the Crab Clan. The great wall protecting the territories from the shadowlands... falls. The head of the clan and his highest retainers were last seen standing atop a small mountain of zombie and ogre corpses, holding out while the Crab flee their ancestral homelands into the other realms.

    That's the setup. What happens from there is up to the PCs. There's probably problems for the PCs and the 10,000 refugees following them, being allowed into neighboring kingdoms and accepted.

    There will have to be efforts to build a second wall, far behind the first, which blocks off the Crablands from the rest of the empire.

    There will be more struggles as the Crab clan struggles to integrate (or decidedly non-integrate) into the societies of the countries that adopt them.

    The crab ambassadors to other clans regularly write the other ambassadors, keeping a running tally of Clan issues, refugee issues, refugee status, etc. Suddenly, contact with the crab ambassador of the Dragon clan is cut. Upon investigation, the crab refugees in the Dragon clan have all completely disappeared.

    If a longer campaign, then the PCs could find a way to fund and power an effort to regain the Crab ancestral homelands, perhaps making some cruddy deals in the process (sign a pact: the clan who helps hold the Crab castle for a month and a day will be allowed to take control of that castle, and its city; arranged marriages of Crab princesses, etc. Anything to get back the homeland). During the early stages of the battle, some of the soldiers on the front line return with stories; They swear they heard the voice of the Crab clan leader barking orders from the mists... and the zombies and ogres seem to follow formations that the Crab have historically used...

    \n Hi Andy,

    My advice is to avoid 3E. The first problem being that they did a lot to fiddle with the numbers, but didn't give any advice on how the TNs would change compared to the first two editions. The second problem is that the book lays out the massive timeline of L5R events, but in a very "Forgotten Realms" kind of way- all of the good color stories and such basically had disappeared. This helped contribute to a lot of problems dealing with setting and canon if only because I never kept up with the card game metaplot and the players now had tons and tons of setting in their heads which I wasn't really interested in using.

    As far as the Crab clan idea- awesome. Are the PCs starting out higher than 1st rank? Only because 1st rank really makes characters sorta competant, and they usually lack the Glory to really command others.

    The other thing to think about with ALL L5R editions is how you want to handle Honor- is it strictly based on the what the chart says, or is it based on what is "really" honorable? This can set up some interesting situations where you have bastards who order their own children on suicide missions who are super honorable and true saints with Honor 1. If you choose to go with what's really honorable, I suggest tossing the whole honor chart and doing it like Sorcerer's Humanity checks- state two or three themes of what is honor and reward/remove accordingly.

    \nI briefly ran a PbP game on rpg.net called Crosswinds, featuring the five children of Emperor Toturi- the five noncanonical children.
    \n(The wiki for it is here: http://crosswinds.pbwiki.org/ )
    \n
    \nA few things I learned:
    \n
    \nScrewing around with canon is crazy fun. Put your own spin on things, change it, spindle it, and mutilate it. If you're familiar with canon events, change a key one, and see how things change. It's fascinating for those who are familiar with it, and puts players who aren't on a leveler playing field. And you can always say, "my rokugan is like this."
    \n
    \nAlong those lines, killing major/canon NPCs is really carthartic. In your Crab example, I'd kill off the Crab daimyo, and most of his/her family. The Crab rulership stands empty, and the acts of heroes will determine the future leadership of the clan. (If your players don't want high-level adventure, they can be key supporters for a new Clan head and get intrigue there.)
    \n
    \nI find 3rd ed much more interesting than 1st- run it out of the core book, avoid supplement bloat, and keep your Rokugan simple. Award more xp than the book suggests.

    \n Here's my L5R campaign idea. It requires buy-in to one maybe-controversial idea:

    The L5R setting, at least as it has been presented to me, is really close to "Japanese Imperialism, the RPG." I don't say this in a super-negative way (basically all western-produced Samurai stuff is centered in early 20th century Japanese imperialism), although I realize it's hard to talk about facists on the internet without making people flinch. But basically, you have the "pure" lands of Rokugan and then there are the "Shadowlands" to the south and west (where Korea and China are, natch) which are corrupt and the source of all evil in Rokugan.

    Okay, so what do we do with this? Well, let's take it at face value. Let's say that most of Rokugani culture and values and such actually *come from* the Shadowlands, particularly in the court. They write their books in Shadowlands characters (kanji), they practice Shadowlands religions (buddhism and confucianism), and they even lay out their cities like Shadowlands cities (this is also historical to early imperial period.)

    So you play a bunch of hick Samurai lords going for the first time in generations to swear your fealty to the Emporer. You show up at court and find all this decadence and decay and corruption being openly displayed. The poets write poems in Shadowlands style! The calligraphers draw Shadowlands characters! The emporer himself flirts with Shadowlands religion! Maybe his favorite concubine is a princess from the Shadowlands, brought back as tribute from a Crab conquest. What do you do? Do you swear allegiance to a corrupt emporer and, if you do, what does that entail?

    \n Hi Andy,

    The Pheonix are easiest to work with. Not only do you get all the tradtiional samurai values, you get magic, which opens up to all the cliched stuff of magic, forbidden magic, magic gone wrong, people stealing ideas, etc. But you also have a neat thing about trying to make peace, which kind of opens the door for anything from DitV style "fix this town" things to major politics. You could even have a secret conspiracy to -make peace- which then opens the doors for all the usual ethical questions- is it ok to kill one person to stop a war that will kill thousands? Etc.

    The Unicorn are pretty badly tacked on, and if anything seem like the writers' excuse to shove Duncan McCleod into their faux Japan. You'd think after a few centuries where they've crossed continents they'd figure out how to adapt to the culture and politics of their home country, right? So then the question becomes are they just super isolated or are they mostly normal AND just painted as foreign (see, "racism"). Are they an "untouchable" samurai clan that no one knows what to do with? There's some possible neat stuff in that.

    But more interesting, what happens if they get enough acceptance that their internal divisions begin to break loose? You have the L5R version of arabic, western, and mongol cultures turned samurai at war with each other- you do the awesome.

    The Dragon are the hardest to work with, if only because they're the most exotified, bullshit ass clan in the game. The summary basically comes up to "Be oh-ree-en-tal, be mysterious, be zen", you know, without any actual writings on the ideas of zen buddhism. So you have a fortune cookie phrase-spouting guy with 2 swords who never has to justify doing random and weird shit. Pretty much to get anything out of the clan, you have to put something in, something that humanizes or makes them actual people.

    Maybe people just -expect- them to have answers, in which case, you have the DitV kind of thing again. Or perhaps more interesting, they're given some concepts, ideas, or koans and some physical mission and they're expected to contemplate the ideas and how they apply/don't apply during the quest. (see also- the problem with Mage the Ascension).

    But more interesting, what happens if they get enough acceptance that their internal divisions begin to break loose? You have the L5R version of arabic, western, and mongol cultures turned samurai at war with each other- you do the awesome.

    The Dragon are the hardest to work with, if only because they're the most exotified, bullshit ass clan in the game. The summary basically comes up to "Be oh-ree-en-tal, be mysterious, be zen", you know, without any actual writings on the ideas of zen buddhism. So you have a fortune cookie phrase-spouting guy with 2 swords who never has to justify doing random and weird shit. Pretty much to get anything out of the clan, you have to put something in, something that humanizes or makes them actual people.

    Maybe people just -expect- them to have answers, in which case, you have the DitV kind of thing again. Or perhaps more interesting, they're given some concepts, ideas, or koans and some physical mission and they're expected to contemplate the ideas and how they apply/don't apply during the quest.     \n My first L5R campaign (which I GM'd) revolved around a group of Emerald Magistrates on the borders of the Unicorn and Scorpion lands (though I don't think we ever went to Ryoko Owari). They slowly uncovered a plot to murder the Emperor, conducted in the spirit realm by corrupt Lion Shugenja. The climax of the campaign (which went on for about a year as I remember) was a massive battle in the spirit realms between corrupted lion spirits and honorable dead.

    My second campaign (which I was a player in) was set in the border lands North East of the Phoenix, where they mix with non-rokugani savages. All the characters were exiles, their family names tarnished by various relatives dabblings in Maho, who had been given a chance to regain their honor far from civilized lands (though they were not expected to do so). They became tangled in a plot between savage wyrm riders and the living shadow, which had great potential to allow them to redeem themselves, except that one character sold out to the shadow and murdered the Shugenja leading the ritual to cast the corrupted ones out of the Phoenix border lands. The rest of the campaign was about her disgraced friends going ronin and hunting her down.

    I also ran a medium sized and length (20-30 players, 2 years duration) monthly L5R LARP set in the lands of the Mongoose and Dragonfly (nestled between Phoenix and Dragon) some very minor clans with big ambitions. Almost all the characters in that game were from minor clans.

    Can I also recommend the L5R-2k setting that Rich Wulf wrote as a fan before he joined the company, which moved the whole of Rokugan forward into a setting of cyberpunk style sci-fi-magic with Lion mecha and flying Crab castles, it rocks! I ran three or four one-off LARPS at Conpulsion in Edinburgh set in R2k and they were great.

    Finally to make the Unicorn more cool take a look at the fiction written about them for Legend of the Burning Sands, much more interesting.
