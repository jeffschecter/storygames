\n I'm brand new at Burning Wheel (and I don't have my book with me), so pardon me if I mess up the lingo a little, or if this question is silly.

When you're a GM setting an Ob for a task, how do you know where the base Ob stops and the Disadvantage starts?

Like, if a PC is trying to pick a rusty lock, is the "rusty" quality of the lock a disadvantage or is "picking the rusty lock" the straight up Ob?

Or, I guess the more abstract question is -- isn't the GM always just deciding what part of the Ob is the base and what part is the Disadvantage by fiat? If the task is climbing a wall, the GM could say the OB is 4 but manufacture a fictional reason for a disadvantage on the spot (the wind kicks up for example) -- and with the Disadvantage, it raises it to Ob 5, or the GM could just say the climb is Ob 5 with no express fictional Disadvantage and leave it at that. (The mechanical distinction, as I understand it, is that a player with no specific skill available would prefer that the Ob be a result of Disadvantages and not a result of the base Ob because the base Ob is doubled on a Beginner's Luck roll).

How do you draw the Ob line and why?

    \n Obstacle is described by the skill factors (and to an extent by the table that describes difficulites early on). Disadvantage something which is not.

    There is some leeway with the GM's rating of the obstacle and the GM is expected to both act fairly and set the obstacle. It's pretty traditional in that way. The game also gives the GM the power to say "The task of picking a rusty lock is not approriate to getting the door open in time" and that's a judgement call too.

    I don't think there is any getting away from players (including the GM) having to make stuff up even when there are integral game numbers involved.

    The only time where the distinction of what is the obstacle and what is penalty matters is during a beginner's luck test or testing without tools. If the player reminds the GM when this is the case you can zoom in and firm up the composition of the obstacle.

    \n It's a tool for the GM to say "this is important to the task; this is just incidental". I think you've hit it dead-on with the Beginner's Luck attempts.     \n Disadvantages can be directly countered by changes in fictional positioning.

    "It's really rusty, +1 Ob."
    "I have a flask of oil, I lubricate it real good."
    "Okay, good job. No disadvantage then."

    Same goes for darkness penalties (wait until there's light), weather penalties (do it on a clear day), etc.

    Core obstacles usually can't be adjusted that way.

    "It's a very well made Dwarvish lock. Ob 4."
    "I, um, use my oil on it?"
    "It is a very well-oiled Dwarvish lock. Ob 4."

    \n You make a judgment call. Being good at these kind of calls is a key GM skill.

    The lock is stuck by rust, which makes it more difficult. This is a disadvantage. You use oil to unstick it. This negates the disadvantage.

    The lock is of a certain quality of craftsmanship, which sets the Ob. You have schematics that detail the mechanism for this brand of lock. This gives you a +1d advantage.

    Schematics don't negate the rust, and oil doesn't make the lock less challenging to figure out.

    \n Hmm. I agree with Vanguard. My inclination is that you can't fiction your way out of a disadvantage -- you have to lobby for advantage dice if you want an edge. If you're up against a test (picking a lock) and there is a disadvantage (rusty), you can't pause the test, scrub it with CLR, and then resume the test with no disadvantage. Putting CLR on the lock is part of your character's task to achieve his intent -- if you have some CLR on you, you'd just get a +1D advantage.

    If you could simply narrate clever solutions to disadvantages and negate them, it would swallow the advantage/disadvantage rules. If you could negate disadvantages by having a smart answer for everything the GM says, then the game would be about out-witting the GM. But, it's not about that.The testing is all an abstraction -- it's not literal. The rules are there so that the GM doesn't have to be a lock expert who knows exactly how rust would affect a lock. "The lock is rusty" doesn't literally mean that the lock is rusty with all that entails -- it's just code for "this lock is difficult to open -- in part because of inherent qualities of the lock, and in part because of unusual circumstances." The player can then say something like, "I put oil on it" to represent that the character has access to something that will make the lock less difficult. If it was REAL LIFE, would "putting oil" on a rusty lock REALLY make it easier to pick? I don't know. Maybe. I'm not a cat burglar from the 13th century. But, off the top of my head, oil might make it way harder -- all that greasy oil messing up the sound and feel of the tumblers -- but, whatever, it's fine -- "I put oil on it" is good enough because it's code for "my character knows what he's doing and has something up his sleeve to cope with unusual lock circumstances." So, he gets +1D.
