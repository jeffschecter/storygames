    \nWithout aiming to sound dismissive, they probably haven't thought of it because both of those games are, in terms of actual gameplay, totally unlike the JRPG sims you describe. The *World games are explicitly non-fiddly, fiction-first, open-ended games; I can't even really imagine anything LESS like the ultra-fiddly/grindy/unlock-preset-option, hyper-mechanical style of JRPGs and dating sims. (Which, incidentally, I totally enjoy playing.)

    So yes, the content of the two seems to match up perfectly, but the games themselves seem miles apart to me. That doesn't mean it wouldn't be a really interesting project to try and fit them together; there's a lot of different approaches that could be fun, the most straightforward of which would be to just play a either/or mashup of the two games (i.e. each character has both a Monsterhearts Skin/character sheet AND a Dungeon World class/character sheet, and you just switch between the two based on where the fiction is at), where you just stick entirely with the *World approach to actual gameplay and don't worry at all about replicating the mechanical feel of the JRPG genre. It would just be a sort of fictional conceit/premise, nothing more.

    But you could in theory also try to add custom moves/systems to each game in order to replicate some of the dating sim/JRPG elements (creative use of Strings/Hold/bonds comes to mind.)

    Really though, if I wanted to make a *World game inspired by the genre(s) you're talking about, I would write a totally new hack, and I'm guessing it would look almost nothing like either Monsterhearts or Dungeon World.

    \n I hope I can explain them well. I intend to make my own so you'd think I'd be able to put it succinctly.

    A dating sim typically sets it up in one of two ways: Branching storylines or stat-building. Many blend both, but they generally lean more towards one or the other.

    In a branching storyline, you're given choices about what to do/how to respond to a situation and through choosing (for example) whether to shout at someone, apologize to them, or run away, it affects which branch of the story you're shown until you've worked your way through the story to its ending. (Which may be "good endings" [established relationship with your chosen love-interest], "bad endings" [death], or "neutral endings" [friendship endings].)

    In stat-building, you typically perform activities that improve/detract from stats (for example, "Charisma" or "Polite Speech", and even your specific relationship with a character - the specific stats will vary) either visibly (you know that doing this activity has improved this stat and made you worse at that stat) or behind the scenes (often relationship stats are invisible stats - so you don't know that in spite of Haruka's stony gaze, they actually liked what you said). There are usually different "checkpoints" in the game where it will assess if you have the correct scores in different stats and then branch the storyline based on those checks. (For example: if the story is trying to find love while preparing for the Halloween dance, the first checkpoint might be "Charisma 8" and "Neighbor 4" which affects whether or not you have high enough Charisma and a high enough relationship with your neighbor with the scary Victorian house to use the house for the party. If you don't have the necessary Charisma, you might fail and the story ends, you might be branched off into a storyline where you don't use the creepy old house, or you might still have the party take place at the house but you take penalties to your relationship scores with people.) The endings are basically the same as strictly branching storylines, it's just a matter of what affects how you get to good/bad/neutral endings.

    It might help a bit if I toss a link to you to a game:
    http://www.sakevisual.com/realistair/ Free game and one I enjoyed. It's of a stat-builder type, though it does have a bit of storyline branching based on choices made.
    http://risamo.cyanide-tea.net/ Another free game that I enjoyed. If I remember correctly, it's entirely branching storyline.


    On-topic, I'm very interested in a game like what's being described by the OP.

    Yeah, I think the concept is interesting and could make for fun play, but actually trying to emulate the genre strictly seems like a bad idea for a game with more than one player. Anyways, there are a few games that follow the sort of system dynamic you're talking about (Bliss Stage comes to mind), where social play and elseworld-combat feed back into each other. If you keep that border very explicit in the fiction (didn't the Persona games feature like a literal high school that was also literally a portal to demonic dungeons?) then lots of simple MH/DW interactions come to mind. Things like 'advances in MH let you buy moves in DW', or 'Sex Moves in MH unlock unique special moves in DW' or whatever.

    But I think the main challenge is going to be making the genre work for a party of PCs, rather than a single-dude-with-agency-and-his-many-friends. If you want to have custom moves and such for a variety of interactions, that's going to be a whole lot of custom moves pretty fast -- then again, using DW's monster moves for inspiration (my impression is they can be quite free-form/fictional) could make this easier than I am imagining.

    \n I was thinking of this last night and it looks like several of the thoughts I had are already being voiced - which to me, just means that they're the obvious thought. xD

    So, yes - creating bonds between characters and getting rewarded for playing towards these bonds is pretty well necessary. I'd think that it might work with the Advancements. You can have X number of bonds to start off with (perhaps #players (including GM) - 2? So if you had a game of 2 players and the GM, that's 1 bond). Then as you Advance, one of the Advancements you can take is extra bonds. Being able to change the nature of these bonds should be thought of too - perhaps either after you've used the bond power or between sessions. I was also thinking that as you play towards these bonds, you're building up some sort of Overdrive power. And that the Overdrive power would tie to the type of bond you had with the other character. (Rivalry might let you do something more impressive than the person you're a rival with - so maybe it's an automatic success on your next roll as long as it's made after the rival's. Friendship might let you give your friend an automatic success if they're in the scene with you.) Etc.

    I also was thinking that perhaps the playbooks/skins could be created based on dating sim character type tropes. For example, the Plucky Heroine might have a move that allows her to take +1 forward on her current goal if she takes a Harm (ex: She gets knocked down and takes a harm trying to grab the villain's staff. If she gets up and tries again, she has a +1 forward to grabbing it this time). Or maybe the Gentle Healer type may be able to remove 1 harm on a 7-9 and 2 harm on a 10+.

    After thinking it over, I also would prefer to shy away from a strict harem or reverse-harem scenario. I'd, of course, want that to be one of the options of play, but setting it up so that it's the default... I'm not sure how to voice this without provoking some sort of, "Trust the players." counter-argument, but I am leery of the idea of it attracting the sorts of players who need to be the center of the story at all times. It would also limit (to one degree or another) alternate types of love-polygons.

    Just some of my thoughts - of course, if they don't fit with what is being gone for, no worries. xD

    \n If you're up to the challenge, I think it would be interesting to write an anime inspired action+romance hack in 3 modes:

    Harem mode - The PCs are all the same gender preference and are all trying to impress/protect/seduce/etc. an NPC of the target gender.
    Mixed mode - The PCs are a mixed group in a web of messy relationships with each other and with NPCs. (the most like Monsterhearts)
    Solo mode - Inverse of harem mode. There's one PC who is trying to impress/protect/seduce/etc. a series of NPCs and perhaps maintain multiple relationships among them before possibly choosing one to marry or whatever.

    And maybe a 4th mode:
    Star mode - Harem and Solo mode together. One player is the star and is the object of affection for all the other players.

    At the very least, figuring out which of these modes of play you're going to try to support and which you are not supporting will be an important early design decision. Things like relationship meter mechanics might have drastically different meanings depending on the mode.

    In harem mode or solo mode, for example, maxing out a relationship meter may mean you have permanently solidified a relationship, where in mixed or star mode, you probably wouldn't want it to be that proscriptive.

    \n Hmmm... I have a hunch that you could almost directly mash up all the rules and play "as is", so long as you keep the two "spheres" separate.

    Perhaps equate every two or three points of damage with one "level of harm", and then use Monsterhearts rules for PC damage but DW rules for monster damage.

    Let each character take two playbooks: a Monsterhearts playbook and a Dungeon World playbook, and pick half the number of moves from each or something like that. So you could have a Fae who's a Paladin, or a Vampire who's a Thief, and so on.

    I haven't thought it through entirely, but it seems to me there would only be a handful of sticking points, which would be fairly easy to smooth over (like the hit points/harm conversion).

    If that's too tricky, then simplify a little more and have two types of scenes: "Personal scenes" and "Dungeon scenes", with some way to decide when each happens. Personal scenes would be kind of like refreshment scenes in Lady Blackbird/TSoY, except they would happen more often. In Personal scenes you use the Monsterhearts playbooks and rules; in Dungeon scenes you use the DW rules. Think of it as slapping a different colour filter over the movie footage, or changing the soundtrack in the background.

    Maybe the scenes alternate, or players can take turns calling for which kind of scene they want next. (So they could, if they chose, play out a dungeon combat scene as a Personal scene, or vice-versa.) Or maybe "changing the filter" is just something you add to the list of MC Moves, and the MC brings that in whenever she wishes.

    Of course, you can still use your Strings in Dungeon scenes, though! Many of the moves and rules could be used in "inappropriate" scenes, just for fun and genre-mixing, as long as it makes sense. Why not turn someone on while they're trying to disarm a trap?

    Each character would have two sets of stats and moves and everything: so you could potentially have complex characters who are very Strong but not at all Volatile or vice-versa.
