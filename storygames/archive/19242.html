\n I find myself sitting in a weird place for the D&D plots and stories I want to tell. Bog standard FR and Greyhawk bore me to tears. But at my core I enjoy the emergent play of D&D adventures. (If not the 3.X or 4E mechanical issues. *ahem*) However it is rare in a D&D edition that within the first few years my prefered play style is supported.

Take 2nd Edition. It wasn't until I picked up a copy of Dark Sun, Spelljammer, and Planescape that I found my nitch. I'm one of those psycopaths that LIKED 2nd Editions psionic rules, bugs and all.

The same could be said about 3rd Edition. I got really happy when Forgotten Reams added details about the far south (airships!) and then that excitement was eclipsed by the Eberron setting! (Airships, Psionics, robot people! oh my!)

4E offered me some weirdness out of the gate with Warlocks, and then the new Primal world idea from the 2nd PHBs, etc. Mind you I didn't like 4E's take on how powers worked and well. (Lets simplfiy it and say I'm heavily in the 4E was a mini's war game camp but try to be polite about it.)

So here I am now looking at Next with curiosity and aprehension. Hasbro/WotC's abyssmal marketing aside I feel that my playstyle is again going to be marginalized. I do have a saving grace that Pathfinder seems to have hit my 'sweet' spot but mechanically I'm looking for something that gets away from 3.X's power creep. (Something I've recently had to deal with high level Jade Regent play.) And while I hope Iron Gods will excite (and I'm sure it will.) I'm middling in my desire. Pathfinder psionics isn't directly supported by paizo and the 3rd party product while very beautiful feels like a recanning of 3.X mechanics and though.

So what do I do? Pick up Next, try it out and try to world build my own take on D&Disms that fit closer with my more Sword and Sorcery/high fantasy weirdness vs the Tolkein idea?

Or well, rehased Forgotten Realms doesn't excite other. Are they going to give us Next Sword mages too? No?

*lament*

    \n I might be a fringe player too! For mysterious reasons, 4E seems to be my favorite (rather: it's the edition I tried to read a lot) but part of that was how they pushed a lot of less standard content as central. I loved having various Ardents and Seekers and Fightbrains at the table, and I liked having a table made up of a living tree and a rock person and a psychic crystal and this tiefling and this dragon. It just worked for me, personally. I liked the promise of a mixed-technological setting, and the intense premise of Dark Sun. Basically cosigning all of your bullet-points!

    Note, I also like hitting the straight-ahead fantasy tropes as well, what with the Dwarves of the Iron Hold guarding the Shadow Gates against the Forsaken Etc, but this stuff above is gold.

    I'm experimenting with using Planarch Codex as a base for delving into "fringe" D&D content. It's a wild premise, but part to if is that you'll explore and create new content/cultures at the table, so you're all discovering some fringe content for the first time, rather than replaying a classic trope. (IMO, Planarch Codex could fit onto a Pathfinder chassis. I'm still figuring out whether Dungeon World or World of Dungeons is a better fit for my style.)

    \n For all the things that 3e does wrong (caster supremacy, character builds, bad/opaque CR calculations), one of the things it really did right via the OGL was to provide for extensibility in many directions. I suspect if you're cool with AD&D2's mish mosh of systems many of the problems in 3.0-3.5-Pathfinder are not problems for you, so I would suggest instead of chasing after Next, look into some of the third-party stuff. Malhavoc Press did a psionics system that was pretty solid, and there are numerous setting books out there that are relatively cheap at this late date.

    If you're looking for something that really locks down the advancement and balancing system (although it's quite a bit more complicated than 3.0+ in terms of character generation), I might check out Fantasycraft, because that one uses such clear and explicit balancing mechanisms that adding in psionics or whatnot is really easy.

    \n I'm looking for the right OSR mix too. Right now I'm brewing my own, choosing to take an step back and have players as common humans with no spellcasting classes available against a whole dungeon-like world. If they find spells in any form, they can learn to cast them, unlocking the spellcasting classes later in the game. Races as well, as long as they find them and manage some degree of cultural/economic exchange. I'm expecting this will help produce the fringe effect with less material and more difficulties in the exploration, making this element more important. High casualties expected in the process, of course. I'm aiming at a lethal OSR style.

    \n I find the OSR choices while useful lack the diversity a lot. ACKS and LL with all the optional bells and whistles gets close but falls apart partly because of the OSR mentality (Class X is X because that's the way it always was.)

    True20 back during the d20 boom was close, someone noticing that 3.x games classes and options were built with predesigned pieces that fit together. And I could mod it.

    I look at classes as a strong baseline but needing optional layers that you can attach to customize it. I do like Next's approach with swapping +'s for feats and Background packages attaching to a class then the sub-classes built from there. Pathfinder archtypes are great but fall in to the 3.Xism issues.
