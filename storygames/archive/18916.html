\n Hey everybody. I'm Ben Costa. I make a comic called Pang, The Wandering Shaolin Monk. And now I'm making a game based on it. I'm hoping to get some feedback.

It's a D6 dice pool system with skills and classes, and the game is set in 17th century China during a real time period. There's no magic kung fu powers, just abilities based on historical kung fu styles. Aside from combat stuff, the player is afforded a bit of narrative authority through their abilities as well.

I would love for people to actually play the game, but even if you just checked out the PDF and gave your thoughts, that would be fantastic. Here's a direct download of Pang, The Wandering Shaolin RPG

    \n Don't take me too seriously, first of all. This is just my opinion.

    The first thing I evaluate game systems by is the character sheet. My motto is "if it's not on the sheet, the players don't have time for it."

    Also, I agree with your "keep it to a page" push as well. I'm with you.

    You've got some really good stuff in here, with players being able to author content, and some strategy to character advancement, and a game system that looks like it will flow off the page. I love the encounter and travel tables, and this document has a lot of really cool art and good layout.

    But.

    If you put it in my hands, and asked me to play it, I would be stuck at character creation. Without context, without a goal in mind, it would be hard for me to make a meaningful choice as to which character type to play. They all seem more reactive than proactive. From this reading, I can't get a good feel of what impact my character would have on the fiction.

    If I don't have a goal in mind, if I don't have a thing I feel like I need to do, then how is the game master going to offer me meaningful choices or targeted compromise opportunities? If my character sheet tells me (and everyone else at the table) that I'm currently "searching for the man with the green lotus on his wrist" then we have some ground to work from. The GM can plan to incorporate some shred of evidence, or some gossip about his whereabouts to keep things juicy.

    And they must be Player Authored, in case I didn't make that clear. This must be created by an interested player, not handed to them by fate.

    What happens if the building is burning down, and there are valuable scrolls threatened by the flames, except now I finally see him, running from the fire! What do I do? Which will I choose?

    Yes, this kind of thing can happen organically, at the table. And people who know each other really well can do this to each other with almost no thought or effort. But it's much easier with things written down. It's easier to remember and reincorporate, easier to keep focus.

    The thing about Mastery tracking, I'm borrowing from another system. There, you had to purchase things with points, and you were given different awards each night. A typical example would be a 4,3 2, and a 1. So you would have to put 4 points towards some advancement, three towards a different advancement, and so on. There was a tracking system. "Oh man, just one more session until I get Tremendous Horse-Cut Technique."

    And, more importantly, it let us incorporate that in to the fiction. "You almost have enough points to learn that technique, so next session I'll make sure you encounter a master or a school where you can learn it. And I'll make sure to incorporate that school into the current villain's schemes, to keep things interesting. He'll learn more about you, let's see if you learn more about him too."

    .. ..

    We didn't get to a playtest this weekend, which is not unexpected. I'll keep trying, and I've got some opportunities in the next few weeks. If I get an online playtest going, would you be interested in observing (during or after)?

    \n Re: this particular issue, one technique I used for this in a fantasy campaign game was, "After you advance, announce what you're going to advance next." This gives everyone a heads-up as to what the character's working on during down time etc., so that it doesn't come out of nowhere when they later become better at that skill. Sometimes the training is narrated, sometimes not, and it works either way.

    I don't know if "announce early" is compatible with Pang, but thought I'd mention it just in case.

    \n Harlequin, your playtest was very informative. I realized very quickly there are some things I should clarify or change.

    \u2022 You have to put at least 1 point in each Virtue, and no more than 5. You can have 0D in a Skill but not a Virtue.
    \u2022 I'm thinking you shouldn't put any Gongfu Points into Skills at character creation anymore. The plus and minus 1D thing already seems to take enough time, and I've noticed people seem to actually get annoyed at the prospect of further modifying their skills.
    \u2022 I need to consolidate a better list of everything you need to do to make a character on page 4.

    \u2022 The Karma Die is not an extra die. It's just one of your dice that you designate in a dice pool. If you have 1D, for instance, that's your Karma Die as well.
    \u2022 Aside from critical hits, the Karma Die doesn't do anything else in combat. My thinking was that I didn't want to bog down the pace since there's a 1 in 6 chance that something good or bad will happen, and you roll a lot in combat. But if you want critical misses to be a thing, maybe when you roll a 1, if you roll the die again and get another 1, some complication occurs.

    \u2022 Obviously, there's gray area and perhaps some overlap between some of the social skills. But I'm not sure if I think that's a problem. The difference between Bureaucracy and Enlightenment seems pretty clear to me (not sure if that was the comparison you guys meant). Normally you might get Bluff vs. Diplomacy in a game, but a pure "lying" skill didn't really fit with the Virtues, so I went with Charm/Diplomacy. The way you explained it at the beginning of the session is how I envisioned it. I can see where litigious players might get frustrated, but I don't know that I care too much :P
    \u2022 Making an opposed check is explained on page 3. Is it unclear? In that situation where the priest was trying to discreetly talks to the PCs, you could have had them roll off against you. Count the Successes, and if it's a tie, count the numerals on the Successes, and if that's a tie count the whole thing. Heart is usually the skill you use when someone is trying to use a social skill against you, but it could make more sense to use Perception in that case, or even Inspect.

    \u2022 Regarding the Ax Mastery thing, it's probably not clear anywhere in the rules, but in general you are supposed to declare your intention to use something before you roll, unless the rules state that you can use it after you roll. So for the Ax, you'd have to say I'm going to use my Ax Mastery, and then if you got two successive 4's on your Karma Die, you'd critically hit. That might seem harsh in terms of usage, as its once per session, but you can use a Qi Die to do it again.

    \u2022 As a note, I would suggest for you guys to use more Qi Dice! That way you don't have to be stingy with your abilities, or you can more likely hit an important roll.

    \u2022 In combat, I totally get that it would have been difficult to play as described in the book, since there wasn't any visual tracking of the goons' wu shu dice, and you were rolling dice for one of the players. But I personally think it adds a bunch to the combat if you track things for each individual enemy, rather than one giant pool. It makes everyone's choices more consequential.

    Also, I enjoyed how you described the scenes in terms of a comic book, and panel layout, etc. That was a nice touch. It was really fun to watch you guys play!
