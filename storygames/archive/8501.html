\n(Caveat: as I am fond of writing these days, I am trying to catch up on rpg theory, which isn\u2019t an easy task at all. So forgive me if I am wrong in my interpretations or usage of terms (please correct me) or if I am using old theories. Also, I am posting here and not on the Forge because I \u201cam\u201d here and not on the Forge.)

I was watching my 2 years old son playing, and especially \u201cplaying pretend\u201d, that proto-rpg form (I supposed this is contested), and started analyzing it in G/N/S terms. Of course it\u2019s outside the big model scope (not a rpg, not a group, \u2026), but I wanted to share that.

What he\u2019s doing now is Simulationism. He imitates, impersonates. For example, he pretends he\u2019s cooking by mixing the cat\u2019s food with the cat\u2019s water, stirring the mixture with a spoon or a pen. He wants to put the resulting mess in the oven. We indulge him, he done gloves to put and remove things from the (cold) oven. He takes a cloth (any cloth, or a paper handkerchief) and rubs different surfaces to clean them. This sort of things. He seems interested in authenticity and fidelity in his imitations. Sim.

Gamism and narrativism are not yet apparent, but seems to be fare on the horizon.

Competition isn\u2019t friendly at that age: it\u2019s pure competition, not a game. But there is already provocation: faking a conflict / aggression to get a reaction (usually to get attention). Maybe it\u2019s the start of gamism?

For narrativism, he of course doesn\u2019t \u201caddress premise in a lit 101 sense\u201d But there is sometime a story-structure to his games. Second, I may sometime detect a tension-release rhythm. Is this proto-nar?

To me it seems at least to validate Sim as an actual, independent mode.

What do you think? One can find many analogies and analysis based on child development (analogies with evolution, analysis of difficulties of certain concept, \u2026). Is this useful for furthering our understanding of our enjoyment and its different modes / priorities? Or am I only narcissist?

    \n I would be wary of reading Creative Agendas (even prototypically) into behavior at that developmental stage. Not enough social/modeling capability until around age 5 or so for most kids to engage in anything creative beyond solitary or parallel exploration with any consistent intentionality. Kids can express their own internal ideas, and reflect ideas they perceive from the world around them, but synthesis is pretty rudimentary. The skills to do that develop on a continuum between around 18 months and 8 years.

    Watch how groups of kids do imaginative play together. Synthetic exploration - where kids' models of the world and each other's internal states interact constructively and they learn what the "right" way to play is from peers - is rarely seen much before 3. Earlier than that, most exploration is mimetic - copying the world-model that they observe from a source they trust; or empirical - based on trial and error experience.
