Larry's comment in this thread about "reinterpreting" D&D made me think of markv's New Edition game, which sparked this idea:

Each player plays an editor of (original) D&D. Each editor has a separate creative agenda, and attempts to make the rules fit his/her agenda. After all rules have been edited, players switch games: Now, they play a role-playing game with the new D&D rules.

    I've run two nomic experiments.

    The latest I ran on a wiki and it went a dozen turns before the 6-8 players lost momentum or interest. Lessons learned: Nomic participants should have an agenda for the nomic to succeed. I modified Peter Suber rules slightly to account for some wiki differences. I probably still have them around if people care.

    The first I ran on a MUSH. We used the Pure Nomic ruleset ("All players must agree to any changes to the game.") and built from there. We progressed through hundreds of new rules. We coded a proposal / rule database and voting machine in mushcode (instituted by rule, too). Some initial players had gotten the impression that the goal of the game was to create a role-playing mush by committee, which was interesting. I told them that we could very easily make that the goal, by law. No one really pushed it. Most the 'let's create a new RP mush' people left and the hardcore nomic 'let's fuck around with rule systems' folks stayed on. I used a loophole to declare myself emperor for a week, then I undid the loophole with my effective dictatorial power and abdicated my position. The nomic ceased to be fun for me after I had "won" it. When my attention waned, so did that of other people, and we eventually shut it down due to inactivity.

    Talk to me if you have serious ideas about building a RPG-building nomic.

    In order to do it in an RPG, I think you'd have to make the rules a part of the fiction.

    Fighter: "We must always follow the Holy Book of Systems!"
    Thief: "Yes, but what does it say about this situation?"
    Cleric: "I have a +2 Interpretation bonus. I roll...15. Okay, it says that fighters must keep silent in all Scripture Debates!"
    Fighter: "I use the Veto point that I got from killing that last orc! The orc's head goes: 'That is Orc doctrine! Behold, the page you read from is colored green!'"
    Thief: "Okay, I roll... 2. Critical miss! That means the Fighter gets to decide!"
    Fighter: "It says that all clerics must touch their toes whenever they enter or leave a room in the dungeon."
    Cleric: "Oh, shit. Okay, I touch my toes. Let's find a new room!"

    Matthijs, I'm working on something slightly similar to this in my Gormenghast-inspired game, where you can basically make up rules and rituals which you and the other characters are bound by for the rest of the game, and anyone can invoke against you (or on your behalf).     Sneezel: "Hmm. Guess I"ll have to find another route and hope that I can still beat her."

    I hadn't thought about applying this to meta-fictional elements (i.e. to the rules of the game itself) but it would be a pretty neat thing to play around with.

    Instead of implicitly using Nomic to generate a ruleset outside of actual play, why not use the Nomic to handle actual play, and generate a ruleset as a side-effect?

    For example, Adam's first rule might be "I am an elven fighter named Goldenwind Happypants. My magic is reknowned and my sword is deadly. I have ears that are 12 inches long." Let's say it passes. Bob might then propose a rule, "There is a large dragon attacking Goldenwind. It kills him." Everyone but Bob and Denise votesagainst it. Denise hates long-eared elves. Chris proposes a rule, "Characters have hit points. When you create a new character, you must specify how many hit points it has, or default to 6 hit points. Characters with less than 1 hit point are dead." It passes. Denise proposes a rule, "I am a dwarven warrior named Grognard Deepstone. I hate elves, especially long-eared elves. I have an axe that does 10 hit points of damage when it hits." Despite including the implicit definition of "damage" as a concept, the rule passes. Ellen proposes a rule, "Grognard hit Goldenwind with his axe." It passes. There's some discussion about what this means. Did this kill Goldenwind as the combination of rules imply that Goldenwind has -4 hit points (6 default minus 10 damage)? No, the ruling is that Goldenwind has 6 hit points and 10 damage hit points. It's Frank's turn, and he makes a rule, "If damage exceeds hit points, a character dies. The following is added to the text of the rule that created the character: 'The character is dead and may not be mentioned in new rules. All existing rules that mention the character by name are considered void.'" It passes.

    I think the addition of some actual play creates a concrete test bed for play, plus a fun little game in itself.
