\n Folks over here said that I should start this thread about ripping apart character creation in d20/OGL.

Here's what I'm trying to do:

1. Unifying the experience system and character creation. Characters start out as a blank slate and then have experiences applied to them, giving them attribute modifiers, skills, powers, and such. Characters can either start out with a lot of experience or with only a few things defined. In the latter case, characters can be continually developed through flashback scenes, stories they tell about their past during play, or things that are discovered about them in play. Imagine it as super-flexible lifepath system that's constantly updating characters to reflect new things that have happened.

2. Unifying traits and relationships. Every trait comes from somewhere or, more likely, someone. Genetic traits that a character inherits from their parents; social traits gained from their community, friends, and family; knowledge and skills from their teachers; etc. Each trait is not just an ability but a tie to someone else or a group of someones, a memory of interacting with them. In this way, you could strongly hate someone from your past, but they still played a big part in making you who you are.

Here's why I'm doing it:

A. I want to be able to roleplay stories like Gilmore Girls and the new Battlestar Galactica, where it's all about character development and how much other people contribute to making you who you are. But I want actual rules that support it, because I get lost trying to care everything through freeform characterization, especially over multiple sessions. I forget what I'm doing with a character or how they relate to other characters. I need more assistance to pull it off. Whether hacking d20 or not, I think this is a worthwhile design goal, for a specific type of play.

B. I want to be able to break down general character traits ("I went to college") into things that actually have meaning, into specific memories of events that happened ("So this one time in college...") and the people who were involved in it.

Here's some of the issues I'm dealing with:

1. How do you convert experiences into traits? Are attribute-giving experiences different from skill-giving or feat-giving experiences? How do you differentiate? I don't want to quantify experiences, like, "record three applicable memories to raise an attribute modifier." That's dumb and just the kind of thing I'm trying to avoid.

2. How do you elegantly keep track of experiences when a character might start out composed of, say, 30 memories and gain at least one or two more each time you play? How do you keep track of which memories are connected to which traits? I don't want this to require a Continuum level of bookkeeping.

3. What are the real implications of saying that each trait, each experience, also represents a relationship with another person? How can we manipulate those during play?

    \n Okay, Johnathan; first idea - note, this may be no damn good; I'm just throwing stuff at you...

    First, take everything that's currently a progressive bonus. Beat it to death with a stick, and make it into a feat, so all characters are feat-based. So, for example, my "base attack progression" that's awesome like a fighter is three feats stacked up (Or whatever), and I have a tag on each one.

    When you build a character, you grab your feats, and you also create a set of tags - five or ten or however many. And you tie each feat to one of the tags. So, "I was trained by Master Yael" is a tag, and connects up to one of my base attack feats (I have three of those), as well as to my flurry of blows.

    Follow me so far?

    Okay, now for the silly shit. Each and every feat has several possible conditions - "enhanced", "reduced", whatever suits. If my base attack feat gets enhanced, I'm a better hitter. Now, thing is, feats aren't directly screwed with. Tags are. When my training by Master Yeal is on the griddle, that does stuff to a fistful of my feats.

    ...Anyhow, that's one idea. I'll probably toss a few more at you, if this is the kind of thing you're looking for.

    \n The everything's-a-feat thing is what I did with Passages. I suspect you will ultimately find it unsatisfying. Which is why I went the other route for Bullseye, which seems to be more what you are thinking. Riffing off your recent posting about this got me thinking about SotC's nifty chargen tools, i.e., the what-my-character-did-during-the-war bit and the inter-PC novels.     You have, say, ten "standard" uses to which any given trait could be put. It can be used to attack, defend, boost, compel, blah blah blah.

    Ye Olde Character Record is a series of blank lines. Under each each is a row of symbols (each symbol indicates one of these uses). When you grab a trait, you set a rating, and black out the symbols corresponding to the things you can use it for freely (using it for other stuff would cost a resource of some kind; whatever you get for compels).

    When a trait mutates, change the free uses.

    \n Another thing to consider might be:

    --You, as the player, decide what the character wants to do, and announce. "I punch my father in the face!"

    You know you need to roll a certain number to succeed. If you fail, you not only lose the in-fiction conflict or whatever, but you also suffer some kind of mechanical setback you want to avoid (you lose tokens or take harm, or whatever).

    --Each of the other players can now either invoke a relationship/trait to try to get you to do something else (giving you a penalty if you try to stick with it), or invoke a trait to give you a bonus, and thereby change that trait. Each other player is picking one trait on your character sheet and applying it towards or against your character. Like so:

    "But you have an obligation to your father because he blah blah blah, control yourself or take a -4 penalty."
    "Here's a token. +2 to hit him, but if you do, your trait will be changed to 'I think I'm better than my father' (or 'I feel pity for my father')."
    "Mary Sue is watching. If you hit him, your relationship to her will change to 'Mary Sue is too afraid of me to trust me'."

    Is that sort of like what you're talking about?

    Each player is in charge of their character's feelings and desires, and only barely in charge of what they do and where they end up.

    \n Some thoughts I had in the shower this morning, drawing on stuff from my Avatar: The Last Airbender game and some of the "lessons" stuff that Daniel was talking about before he started working on Do...

    One of my general principles about character development in RPGs is that it's way more interesting if characters becomes different instead of better, even though that strongly goes against the idea of levels and advancement in most d20 stuff. Another principle is that it's better if character development arises from what happens to a character instead of being explicitly chosen by them. As Shakespeare says in Twelfth Night, some have greatness thrust upon them, despite their best attempts to avoid it.

    So what if you start the game with a blank character sheet and all your stats at +0.

    Then, you start framing micro-scenes about your childhood, where the player declares what lesson they're attempting to learn, like "I'm going to be the best player on my peewee soccer team." Then you have a scene about that and actually play out the part where your character tries to score the winning goal in a difficult match, rolling dice for all the conflict-y parts of things. However, after the scene plays out, and the scene never plays out as simply as you might have hoped, because this is real life, you gain a modifier to the trait that best reflects what you actually learned from that experience. Maybe it was teamwork or leadership. Maybe it was endurance for the harsh criticism you received after you failed.

    Maybe you play one of these scenes for every 2-3 years of your age, up to a certain cut-off, like 13. After that, you've learned all the lessons of childhood and are no longer a blank slate that soaks up experiences like a sponge. Now, it becomes a bit harder to learn things and it will become harder still as you become older and more set in your ways. Now, your new experiences don't happen in mechanical isolation from everything you've experienced before. Instead, each new experience is a further development of or a limb branching off of one of your previous experiences. This could help eventually build the interconnected structure that Bill was talking about.

    Yeah?

    The only thing I'm not sure about it whether you actually want to play out a shitload of backstory scenes for older characters, because I want people to be able to play 65 yos if they want to. Hmm... How can you simulate all those accumulated years of experience without actually playing them out? Maybe we could do like Mike suggested and give old fogies a resource like Reflection Pool or something, that they could spend later in the game to have flashbacks. If would be kinda hilarious to have the older characters constantly talking about things that happened when they were younger.
