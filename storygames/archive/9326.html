\n So, what's in your bag?

There's the Without Peer edition of Blood and Bronze, which is an artifact in itself.

I was thinking about this this AM, when I realized I was squirreling away a sum of money to blow on this:


Basically replica gold "koban" coins, where each coin is basically "one ryo". These replicas are made out of a gold-like compound of zinc and other stuff, and is of the quality that they use in Japanese samurai TV shows and the like. They even clink like the original when you shuffle/play with them.

I've already got 10, which I bought the last time I was in Japan:



However, I'm saving up to buy 50-100 more, so I can basically use them as "Hero Points" in some Japanese-themed RPGs that I'm running. It may sound cool and all, but they come out to about $3 per fake koban. If I go through with it, it'll probably be my most expensive gaming prop.

I've seen other folks with hand-crafted maps, self-produced music collections and the like. What are you working on, or what do you have? Game-specific or general?

-Andy

    \n I love props. I think they are great. Wish I had pictures. They would do more justice. Here are the props I used at the last couple con games.

    "The Gift" - Burring Wheel\n
    • Start with an 8' x 18" table. Divided by race but everyone is very close to their opposition.
      • \n
    • The center chair on each side is a throne. To do this just get two yards of fabric for each side and drape them over the chair. For the dwarves: Crushed red velvet. Elves: Silver and blue brocade
      • \n
    • Following that color scheme, blue/red vis-a-vis pen for scripting, blue/red dice for the sides, with a giant black d6 in the middle of the table for and advantage die.
      • \n
    • Dwarves enmbolded by dwarven nog (Sam Adam's "Winter Ale" if you can have alcohol in the game, I.P.R. Root Beer if not).
      • \n
    • Refined Elves have Mirrorwine (Navarro Vineyards Gew\xfcztraminer or Sparkling juice)
      • \n
    • A standard for any game with player resources. Poker chips to represent Artha.
    \n\u201cMy Life with Joker\u201d \u2013My Life with Master\n
    • Started with an image of the Joker with blank caption boxes, stolen from the cover of a comic. Print that onto an iron on transfer paper and then iron that onto a white t-shirt (I tried purple and it wasn\u2019t legible). During mater creation, as the players create the joker (Brain vs. Beast, etc), using a sharpie, fill in the caption boxes. Once complete, put on the shirt and become the Joker.
      • \n
    • Die my hair bright green.
      • \n
    • A set of gorgeous purple and green d4s from Chessex.
      • \n
    • My masterpiece. With a digital camera, laptop and printer at the game, I took pictures of the players and printed them out. During the game, whenever the players would oppose me, I used a red crayon to draw red all over their lips and a purple crayon to draw their hair green. As the game progressed I pulled out a pair of scissors and started hacking away bits of the picture while I repeated what they just said in a mocking tone. That freaked people out.
    \n\u201cExalted Unplugged\u201d - Exalted/Wushu/Wuxalted

    The game started in the 1980s as a battle of the bands where the two rival bands \u201cWhite Fire\u201d and \u201cThe Maidens of Mercy\u201d were transported into \u201cCreation\u201d as Solars and Abyssals, both vying for the legendary artifact at the pillar of air \u2013 the Stratocaster! Hard core cheese here!\n
    • All the character sheets were built into cassette tape boxes. Name on the spine. Picture of the character on the front, stats, dice, and poker chips on the inside
      • \n
    • As the game was a late night game, a six pack of Rock Star in the center of the table for the players to drink
      • \n
    • Red and Black dice. Scarlet/Ebon and AC/DC
      • \n
    • A music sound track of 80s rock.
    \nThere is No Spoon \u2013 Matrix Wushu Adaptation\n
    • Character sheets printed on as 4x6\u201d pictures and then put in clear plastic (high tech looking) frames. Matriculated picture on front, stats on back
      • \n
    • old sticks of RAM as tokens for Bullet time
      • \n
    • Matrix score (not soundtrack) playing in the background
    \nBrain failing\u2026 more here and some things I know I missed by the noggin only stores so much.

    For the upcoming Mouse Guard game at Good Omens Con here is the plan so far:\n
    • Mouse Beanie Baby for the \u2018Mouse Ball\u2019 game (a group building icebreaker at the beginning).
      • \n
    • A large print out of the Territories map to spread on the table
      • \n
    • Obstacle cards for building the epic journey (another group exercise for skill building)
      • \n
    • Trait labels for the \u201cI see\u201d game (group character creation)
      • \n
    • Character tents with name, picture and room to put the trait label on
    \nIf there are any suggestions for MG, I\u2019d love to hear them, the game is in July.

    \n Whenever I see a cool puppet cheap, maybe* because it's a set of ten Passover plague finger puppets in May, I buy it and put it in the bag. Whenever a shirt falls apart, I cut out and sew up some blank finger and hand puppets and put them into the bag too, along with some markers. Then, when I run Puppetland, I spill out a big bag of puppets onto the table and have everyone pick one. This is so effective, so magical, at getting everyone focused and psyched up that I can't understand why I don't do it for every game I play. Seriously, time to start stitching up some FBI agents and Mi-Go and faceless horrors and whatnot.

    When I saw the tricorns on Josh's Sons of Liberty table, I got that feeling again.

    \n I'm a huge fan of props in my games.

    Whether they be water pistols to simulate guns, used liquor bottles refilled with dyed water to simulate the alcoholic spirits drunk by the characters, Forty (30cm x 30cm) pieces to a modular city for wargaming (though this often gets used to construct combat scenes for rpgs), bucketloads of figures, old and musty tomes such as a 19th century German edition of Faust from an antique book dealer (which has seen a lot of use), test tubes from my wife's laboratory days, a range of sound effects CDs (now compiled into easy access MP3 playlists before games), candles, hurricane lanterns, antique ouija boards...anything that helps to evoke a mood.

    ...but recently I've started making a lot of my own new props from resin.

    I'm trying to build up a decent range of these to hopefully provide them as accessories to the gaming community.

    Coins


    Steampunk Goggles


    Small modular streetscape pieces (mediaevil/renaissance/steampunk).


    P.S. After a decade and a half of live roleplaying I've also got more costumes than I've got regular clothing. Everything from caveman skins, through to sci-fi outfits. The most expensive gaming outfit was a kimono on which I spent almost $2000, plus it's assorted accessories which were another $500 or so...and I only cross-dressed as that geisha assassin character once (but it was worth every cent based on the anecdotes that are still told seven years later...).
