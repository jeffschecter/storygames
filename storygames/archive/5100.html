    \n Veterans of the French and Indian Wars have come down the river to get away from their bad memories.

    Probably they are French.

    There is a band of them, maybe half a dozen at the most? They are very good at violence, and like to get drunk and pass out in the streets. Even drunk they are effective killers, though they don't tend to start fights. They are just intolerably rude and self-destructive miscreants. The locals try to entice them to leave, but they won't.

    The worst thing is, when they are passed out in the gutters, their nightmares of the war stalk through the streets of New Orleans, made of shadows that bleed red wine, that drink blood and night soil. They go to the Church on holy nights and batter at the windows, demanding to be let inside, where they can wreak more havoc.

    One of the men is old and bitter, and wants nothing more than to destroy himself.

    One is young and will shun any opportunities for redemption until one is proven to work, then he will take it at once.

    And a third is neither young nor old, but if he is told about his nightmares, he will sober up and use them to raise himself to a position of power.

    \n The Fairy of the Dueling Oak.

    He's an old whithered spirit, hard as the wood of the tree he calls home and he prepares men and boys to fight to the death for their honor. Sometimes he manages to talk them out of it or end the duel before anyone dies but if not, they perish.

    Whether they die right there above his roots or, as is more likely, die later in bed of internal bleeding, he is there to gather them up and take them home. All who die at the Dueling Oak are his boys, the Oak Sons.

    It is said that he has a few daughters and that he treasures them above all else.

    (The Dueling Oak is absolutely real.)

    \n I think any source on creole (French) New Orleans will tell you the two ubiquities of the culture - dueling and colored mistresses. Sometimes both together, as when there would be a fancy ball (where the white women were not invited, of course), and any little thing would set off a duel. A few guys would step outside, poke each other, step back into the ball.
    \n
    \nThe French Quarter by Herbert Asbury is some good. Although it's mostly a later period, it still details the pre-Civil War era some.
