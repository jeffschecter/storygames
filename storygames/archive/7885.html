Posted By: Jonathan WaltonHeinsoo and Wyatt talk about why there's mechanically-determined death in 4e. Personally, I'm not convinced.
\nI wanted to discuss this article and talk about the pros and cons of various death systems here.

I think 4e's death system is rather inelegant. There are two ways you can die: reaching a negative hit point number equal to your bloodied condition, or failing three death saves once you are in the negatives. I think negative hit points are, in general, inelegant, too.

I think that 4e's goal of making death exciting and tense is only partly realized. I wish they had allowed characters to take fatal wounds that do not stop you from playing. Waiting for your initiative to come around so you can make your death save isn't that exciting.

Is death even an important part of the game? On the one hand, you need a strong feedback mechanism to reinforce good play. It's a strong Gamist design and it needs positive and negative reinforcement for certain behaviors. Is death the right "punishment" for poor play, especially when there's so much luck (many d20 rolls) involved? When a character dies, doesn't the player just make another? If you make up a very similar character as a replacement, does the loss of continuity of the character in the fiction provide a strong enough punishment?

Obviously, the 4e design makes it harder and harder to die as you level up your character. This is because you've invested more in that character and it's a greater loss.

If the design is doing everything it can to make death a "speed bump" for characters but still provide tension in play, maybe death isn't the answer after all. I'd rather see a system of penalties that provide color and quest opportunities.

Here's an example implementation of what I mean. A character "passes out" when she reaches 0 hit points or below. Enemies can perform "coup de grace" attacks and think they're killing the character, but inevitably the character wakes up after an extended rest. Any character who dies gains a limitation like the following:

Painful Limp
Effect: Your speed is reduced by 1 square.

Foggy Vision
Effect: All ranged attacks suffer a -1 attack penalty.

Scar Tissue
Effect: If you roll a 5 or below (before modifiers) on a melee attack, you drop your weapon.

The player gets to choose the limitation from a big 'ol list. A character can remove any limitation with a quest. The player and DM should agree on the quest at the time the player chooses the limitation. If the player doesn't like the quest, he can choose a different limitation.

    \n I agree that D&D4's death rules are fairly weak. I do like the "left for dead" thing where monsters stop attacking you when you go down, but otherwise they're pretty vanilla old-school. I'm pretty surprised to hear Rob say they were considered a big overhaul internally. And making a 1-in-20 roll to see if you get back up on your own is just silly.

    Poison'd has one of my favorite death rules. When you take a deadly wound, you make a bargain or die. You can bargain with a surgeon, with God, or with the Devil -- and bargains carry mechanical weight in the game. Or you can choose die, to try to become a ghost or move on to final judgment and the afterlife. All of those present interesting outcomes and story material for the character, without simply returning to the status quo of "you recover."

    I think collecting limitations can be a real issue for a game where you're going to fight and "die" as much as a D&D hero does from level 1 to level 30. By level 15, you'll be keeping track of so many little penalties it'll get pretty cumbersome, I think. Still, a collection of battle scars of some kind would be pretty cool.

    \n In terms of penalty->something else, in 3.5, I took a bunch of feats and made them "Returned Feats", things only those changed by coming back from the dead could do - because it does change you. This included some in PHB and many from splat books. Some that were about being tougher (feeling less pain), interacting with the undead etc. I then ruled that if you died, your next feat had to be a "Returned Feat". The idea was that instead of forcing "hindrances", death simply "shaped what sort of advantages" you could have.

    We also all agreed that continually raising from dead was boring story. We agreed that usually "death" was really "near death". You suffered the same mechanical effects (essentially lost XP) from "near death" but narratively you had never died.

    I see the 4e death rule as trying to (a) posit some simulationist validity [well of course if you take 50% again of your hit points you're a smear on the ground] and (b) some traditional, random but not completely arbitrary "suspense of the die roll" from the 3 fails rule. An aside on that, it's sometimes overlooked it's not "three failures while down" it's "three failures between rests", go down, fail a save, then make it and get brought up by healing, you're down to 2 failures left this round. So it adds some tension on the die roll.

    To be simpler (in spirit of not adding on too many different bells and whistles, working within existing mechanics), you could have a "near death" experience inflict a mini version of the universal penalty dying inflicts for about half the time. You can require people to "colour that" with whatever it amounts to - be it limp, ringing in ears, psych. issues.

    \n 4E has three different phases, and I think the game suffers badly from trying to map the same death mechanics to all three tiers. I think it would be very helpful to allow the rules to differ by tier, so that you don't get one size fits all death for plane-traveling hell storming heroes, and the band of kids trying to drive the rat-king out of their town. For example you could say: At heroic level the characters take a limitation as Adam suggested. When you get to Paragon you have to make a bargain with a god for your life. When you get to Epic you get your three saves and nothing can bring you back.

    Those three are not necessarily the best options, but I think a big part of the problem is that different death mechanics are needed at the different tiers. This is largely due to the attempt by 4E to be three games at once.

    Another different idea is that a different character could suffer the penalty for saving you (one of your companions deflected the killing blow). This could at least help to ward off the death spiral from limitations or bargains. Thematically it is a bit harder to work and I doubt many of the hardcore 4Eers would like it, but it would drastically change the game.

    \n As far as a quest to remove an injury being a sidetrack, I'd like to think of it more as a second story thread. I recently talked to some friends about this type of gaming, how a really good game means that there's more then one thread of the story to resolve. Mount Interestingadventure may be close to somewhere they need to go anyway, but it's not too good of an example. A better one might be the bitter old healer Plotpoint who can restore vision, but only if the character can prove he deserves it. Or the drug dealer who can fix your vision, but at the cost of a favor to be named later. Or the herb that will remove the blurry vision if taken regularly, but is also illegal within the empire. Now you've got an interesting plot that takes place in the midst of everything else.

    You could also structure it kind of like Achievements in Xbox games: they make you play a little differently. So blurry vision is a problem until you learn to deal with it, which is represented in game as making x successful Perception checks, or hitting a target at least y range, or whatever. Now the player is going out of their way to do something that still fits within the adventure. The adventure continues, but now the player is trying to use their vision more, or attack targets at the edge of their range, to remove the penalty.
