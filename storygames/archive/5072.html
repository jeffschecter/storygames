Evocative and Canonical Settings

\n
\n
\n
\n\nChristian_Griffen \n\n\n
\n
\n\nDecember 2007 \n edited December 2007 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n In another thread, I mentioned that the setting in Beast Hunters was purposefully evocative rather than canonical, and Shreyas expressed interest in talking about that, so: here we go.

To start off, here's the way I see the distinction. Canonical settings provide you with lots of information. They require you to keep up with the setting material that other people produce. There are great advantages to this: someone else is producing setting material for you, they probably put a lot of thought and maybe research into making it all click and work, you can share and talk about the setting with people not in your group, you have a feeling that this world exists outside of yourself, and so on.

Evocative settings provide you with a tone, and with snippets, but not with the big picture. They require you to fill in the gaps, but they allow you to fill them in the way you want to. The advantages I see about evocative settings are that you can take part in that bigger creative process of creating it during play together rather than learning it, you can connect dots in-game and on the fly, you can tweak the setting the way you like it, and there's no "getting it wrong" in a way (though you need group buy-in, of course).

There are reasons why some games work better with one type rather than the other.

What do you think?

    \n Evocative settings work better for me when they're halfway towards pastiche; if I'm going to be filling in the blanks (or dickering with other people about how best to fill in the blanks), being able to start from "Just like the real world, only ghosts actually exist" or "It's WW2 set in a city like Sherlock Holmes's London run like Ankh-Morpork, with wicked steampunk robot-things as soldiers fighting in the streets" or whatever tells me what ideas will be in perfect harmony and what ideas will suck all the air of the room. Really bizarre settings or settings that draw on influences and source material I know nothing about are basically useless to me in an evocative state, because I don't have the right tools to have fun with them.

    And canonical settings work better for me when they're presented in discrete, highly-factual chunks ("History" and "Religion" and "Geography" and so on), ideally with lots of sidebars, plot seeds, suggested uses, and authors' explanations that I can think about when I'm deciding what parts of the setting I want to keep and what parts I should gut and replace with something cooler. Canonical settings which are only presented through some kind of muddy, rambling, in-character story (a faux-evocative approach, maybe?) are harder to sift through and take more time to rip apart and reassemble into something fun.

    That may actually be the only way I can tell the difference between "evocative" and "canonical," now that I think about it -- with evocative settings, I'm looking for clues and hints about what I can fit into them, while with canonical settings I'm looking for hints about what I can remove from them. And really, at that point it's a distinction without real difference, isn't it?

    \n To me, playing Polaris was a real eye-opener in terms of the power of the "evocative setting". We read a few excerpts from the book, and I felt like I had a real grasp on the world and what is was like... and yet almost nothing was described in any concrete terms. It was just a powerful image, with blurry edges. If we had to summarize the information in terms of Geography, Politics, etc, we would probably have ended up with three or four lines of text, tops.

    (Note: I didn't get a chance to read the whole book. Maybe there's a lot more in there! But what we read outloud was enough to play.)

    My take on this is:

    If you're going to have "canonical" information for a setting, make sure as much of it as possible is going to work itself into the actual, real game you'll be playing at the table. If each bit of your setting is something that the players will be interacting with, you're doing well.

    \n Hi Christian,

    I've been thinking a lot about setting, mostly coming at it from the angle of context and focus.

    One strong point of a lot of canon settings is that they often give you enough information and context to hook into it, to actually care. "Aliens attack humanity" means nothing- "Cylons infiltrate your band of survivors" means something- if you've got the context. This is one of the places where I point to L5R as being a great game at providing story snippets and intriguing NPCs as a way of building a network of context that the players can care about.

    To balance that, most canon settings also suffer from bloat. This is for a number of reasons but an important design reason is simply when you make a setting for others you don't necessarily know if what you're making is going to be interesting to them or not- so you just make a LOT of stuff and hope that something sticks. (This proved to be a big design concern when I wrote Well of Souls as a HeroQuest adventure).

    Too much of this information and the group gets lost- they don't know what to do with it. You get players who've memorized the 200 star routes but can't get into the gung-ho action of Star Wars anymore. You have half the group who just looked at the pictures and the other half that have memorized thousands of pages of canon. Instead of the setting bringing the group together- it leads them apart. You've lost focus.

    Evocative settings tend to work better in this case, because the information is often vague or very light- the focus is on evoking color and mood for play- keeping it the focus. The key depends on whether players get enough from it and the steps of the game to gel into a cohesive unit and start building context for themselves. Polaris and Falling Leaves both do a good job of using the rules to quickly help the group hone in on what matters and build strong context throughout play. Games with evocative settings where the group doesn't figure out how to build context quickly fall apart- it seems a fair amount of failed Sorcerer and Burning Wheel play are examples of this.

    Honestly, I really like both. The only thing I'm not down with is useless setting- stuff that is quite tertiary to actual play. (Iron Kingdoms, I'm looking at you and your whole chapter of astronomy that made my eyes bleed).

    \n Hmm. I think I may object to one element of the definition of a canon setting - specifically: "They require you to keep up with the setting material that other people produce. " There are certainly settings for which this is true, but there are many more for which it is not, ranging from single single source material to material which has finished its run and is simply not getting any more additions. Whether it's a now defunct game line like Planescape, a finished series, like Amber or a free-standing source like Neverwhere, it can be canon, but not require anything more than a manageable core.

    I'll go a step further and suggest that there is much more range within the idea of a canon game than this pairing suggests. Sure, there is the canon game that clings loyally to the source material over all else, but I think that's pretty rare. There's the canon game with general modifications, where a few things have been changed for convenience or stylistic reasons. This can be problematic, since without a pattern to the changes, the canon becomes more a source of confusion than reference, which is why I'm much more fond of the canon with an explicit point of departure. For this, consider the now-famous example from the Sons of Kryos - starting a forgotten realms game by killing Elminster. This clearly says "Canon is a useful reference up to this point, but from this point on, we've taken ownership." and is much easier to play. A similar effect can be had with taking a single element and adding, subtracting, or transforming it in an explicit way - Playing 7th Sea but with an Africa analog, or Star Wars where the Sith are Githyanki.

    And all of those versions of playing with canon still don't cover the even broader use of canon for thematic purposes. This is what allows for mashups (like the star wars/Dune Mash up that Judd was running) or pastiche (Such as amber games with no recognizable characters from the books). If I run a game that is Zelazny's "Night in the Lonesome October" only set in 1982 Florida, with a cast appropriate to the time and place (rather than a small town in Victorian-ish England, with an appropriate cast) it will be instantly recognizable to anyone who has read the book, but I am very far removed from the source material.

    All of which is to say, that's a lot of very different ways to handle setting that would all be lumped under "canon" and I don't think it can take the strain. Now, this is not to say I think that a discussion of what makes a setting evocative is not one to be had, but I think this particular dichotomy is false, and it disregards a lot of useful and powerful ideas about setting construction and use.

    -Rob D.

    Here's a quick example: I'm reading a setting book for Space 1889 and get to the NPCs section. Here's the problem: The NPCs are actually pretty interesting.     \n AS I see it, the difference is less in what's "there" in the setting material (although highly specified setting material will tend towards being treated as more authoritative), and more in how the play group sees the material as having authority.

    The tendency has always been to use text as a source of authority ("Canon"). This is great right up until there's a conflict of interpretation or power between people at the table. Then the lawyering comes out, which can really undermine the usual, moment-to-moment system at the table. There's also, secondarily, the problem of differential absorption of "canon" creating differential authority - the players who understand in detail how the setting logic "works" have more success at getting what they want in the fiction.

    Alternatively, you have the setting-as-starting-point ("evocative") approach, where certain specific things are defined as having authority at the beginning of play, but reliance on texts after that point is minimal. This puts the focus for disputes right back on the people and their consensus system - the setting is essentially being filled in at the table through the play process.

    In point of fact, as JD says, every table plays the second way. No setting is fully determined in the text, and individual interpretations of text will vary. But devotion to the idea of textual authority is a real phenomenon, encouraged both by general cultural forces and the "gaming industry"'s devotion to ever-expanding setting source material as revenue stream. You pay for all that setting, you want it to be worth something.

    In general, I think acknowledging that the authority of text is something decided at the table rather than assumed is healthier for play, but the role of authoritative, detailed texts in creating convergence among individual imaginative content can't be neglected.

    \n The talk of evocative settings brings to mind my favorite line from Snow Crash: "To condense fact from the vapor of nuance." Basically what people do with these settings is take a mood and some images and make up the concrete stuff. This tends to make for the most improvisational and dynamic play, since everyone can simply take each other's ideas and run with 'em. (Play Unsafe's building on each other's ideas, in other words.)

    As for canonical settings, eh. They're okay when done well, but the amount of prep and knowledge required can be intimidating. Like Bob I tend to pare them down, taking some elements I like, getting a mood for the thing through genre conventions and such (humanity on the brink of extinction! Renaissance steampunk!), and grabbing maybe a few adventure ideas and NPCs.

    Continuing from the earlier quote: "[T]he human mind can absorb and process an incredible amount of information -- if it comes in the right format. The right interface. If you put the right face on it." What's the right face to put on an evocative setting to convey the right amount and kind of information? Lots of canonical settings have sample fiction to be more evocative, with hit-or-miss results. Books like, say, Polaris and Dogs in the Vineyard don't have any fiction, yet manage to convey the mood extremely well in a few short pages. Is it because fiction is too narrow in scope to pass along very useful information?

    Some other differences I can think of: Both Polaris and Dogs have setting and rules in one book and complementing each other. The Wild West feel and religious backdrop of Dogs make dramatic extremes easy, and the horror-fairy tale feel of Polaris is well suited to the direction of the rules. Both books also have a lot of play examples that show not only how the rules work, but how the rules work in that setting. Same goes for a lot of story game greats. So maybe that's one way to present evocative setting effectively, by having them work with the rules and giving concrete examples.

    \n Playing a lot of Glorantha, this one comes up a lot for people. The issues around canon seem to be around people's need for adherence, once it exists.

    Some folks want adherence by players at the table to the shared understanding of a place or culture. This restriction happens at different levels, for different groups. I think this is especially true for people who want immersion from gaming; they find that when people stray from the canon, it hurts their suspension of disbelief. But it can be insidious for more storytelling groups too, who would like to encourage creativity but can find themselves responding to setting questions in a way that means the end up saying no to contraventions of canon. If a player asks, would or could, and the answer is negative - because that is what canon says - then it stifles creativity. I have been in the situation where I, without malice, kept shutting down a Gloranthan newbie, because she would ask 'would people do this' or 'would people do that' and I would respond from the canon as to what would be the norm. Now you might say that going against the norm is interesting, but breaking taboos is often not the desire when asking. The difficulty is strengthened because other players at the table may be relying on cultural facts: who you can marry for example, for conflicts essential to thier story.

    So in some ways, the danger for settings with canon is that the detail becomes restrictive to the vision of people at the gaming table. However, even in an evocative setting we will still begin to establish facts as we play, so we close off avenues of creativity through precedent. I suspect many 'canon' settings began life as evocative settings that accreted more detail over time.

    Shorter games may win here, because you get less accretion during the lifetime of the game, and you tend to reboot the setting more often, cleaning and paring back to the bones.
