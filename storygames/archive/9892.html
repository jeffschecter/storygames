    \n Other things that help at the Embassy Suites are: being sociable; going with other people; introducing yourself to strangers; being totally willing to jump in on games; volunteering to playtest stuff; asking authors, earlier in the day, to run or playtest their game with you; buying a game and asking the author to run it for you later; going with a game you want to play and asking others whether they'd like to jump in; having a good accent.

    For example, if you:

    a. Go up to an author at the Forge Booth.
    b. Ask if they're playtesting anything.
    c. Ask if you can play it that night.

    You're almost guaranteed a game. I'm exaggerating slightly, and you should really seek out games you're enthusiastic about playtesting, but talking to people and being helpful is a good thing.

    \n Aaron, (or other newbs to the indie scene at GenCon)
    \n
    \nI went to GenCon for the second time last year, and for the first time with a focus on the indie scene. It definitely helped that I had gotten a room with Ralph and Matt Snyder, but the most important thing is to get there, introduce yourself around at the booths, and at Games on Demand. I think I played in 2 games with Ralph (an ill-fated prearranged playtest of Blood Red Sands, and a later pick-up of Reality Cops with James Brown) and none with Matt. Otherwise, I met a bunch of cool people, and played in several cool games.
    \n
    \nIf gaming is your main priority, and indie gaming is a preference, spend a lot of time at GoD, and get to the Embassy Suites early on, preferably with a meet-up already planned. The games all tend to start at near the same time, (I can't recall the general time, sorry) and if you're even vaguely shy about asking to jump in on existing games, you'll find yourself without a game if you don't get in early.
