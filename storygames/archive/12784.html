    So I gotta tell you, Arvid. Your post reminds me a whole lot of a pretty infamous post by Jonathan Tweet. It's since been taken down, but here's an excerpt posted on John Kim's livejournal (full entry here):\n
    These are Jonathan Tweet's words
    Roleplaying, as currently construed, appeals disproportionately to guys because it's mostly about the things that men evolved to enjoy: hunting and warfare. It's about a group assembling to undertake (imaginary) risks for glory and dominance. It's the same reason that team sports, such as basketball, are more male, whereas women compete to be judged beautiful and worthy (ice skating, gymnastics). TCGs are even more male-oriented than RPGs as they're about direct conflict with little to no story or personality. When I was in Finland 5 years ago, the TCG players were typified as guys without girlfriends. LARPing has more female appeal because it's more about personalities, relationships, clothing, and make-up. Finally, the quality of gamer men is a factor. A Finnish gamer I met said she got into gaming as a way to meet good-looking guys. The US gaming scene has less to offer along those lines.
    \nNow, I'll admit that this line of thought pisses me off. But because:\n
    Posted By: Arvidthere isn't any real research behind this. Just me writing from the top of my head.
    \n...I'm making an effort to not be snarky. If my response sounds snarky, I apologize. This subject is admittedly one of my Internet Crazy Buttons.

    First off, speaking as a breasted gamer, let me just postulate that one of the reasons why women aren't exactly flocking to play D&D and other more trad games is because the rampant chainmail bikini porn and deprotagonized depictions of women just don't make us feel women. Why would we want to engage in a hobby whose promotional material very clearly sends the message that YOU ARE ONLY WELCOME FOR THE TITILLATION YOUR BODY PARTS PROVIDE FOR REAL (READ: MALE) GAMERS. Granted. Some of us grit our teeth and forge onward anyhow. I'm not going to claim that I'm wearing a hat of Speaking For All Women. (It doesn't exist.) But I can tell you from talking to other female gamers that I'm not the only one that feels this way.

    Secondly, I think you'll find that the concentration of female gamers in the indie community is significantly higher than it is in trad roleplaying circles. Hell, the indie community has female GAME DESIGNERS in a much, much higher concentration than will be found in trad circles. Similarly, there is a much higher percentage of female gamers in other areas of gaming outside of tabletop. Women can be found in much higher numbers in console gaming, MMOs, and CCGs in particular. So looking only at trad tabletop roleplaying and saying "WHY DO WIMMINZ HATEZ THE GAMING YO?" is perhaps a flawed approach.

    We're here. If you're genuinely interested in understanding what females gamers are thinking, then I might suggest checking out RPG = Role Playing Girl. It's a zine that was first published in 2009, with a followup edition this past year in 2010, that is fully written, laid out, and produced by women about women in gaming. There's some fantastic stuff here written by lots of really smart people who are all women and who are all engaged in gaming. I encourage you to take a look.

    Lastly, I'll say what I always say whenever the question of "how can we make games that women will play?" comes up.

    DON'T TRY TO.

    I mean, come on. I loves me some killing things and taking their things. I loves me console action RPGs, and zombies, and dungeon-delving. Does this make me "masculine"? No. I also love relationship games, games that emphasize drama on an interpersonal level just as much. I assert there is no such thing as "masculine" gaming or "feminine" gaming. There is gaming that makes women feel welcome and gaming that does not.

    Arvid, I enjoyed your post. I think it's fair to say that D&D has, if not defined, at least heavily informed 90% of all role-playing game designs since, and starting the conversation on that level - what did this extremely influential game do to shape the future market for our hobby - is not a bad thing. As an exploratory post designed to elicit responses and further thinking on the subject, I think you're bang on. Clearly you've already elicited some pretty strong reactions and, hopefully, thinking.

    I game with a large proportion of women and have some empirical observations and anecdotes I could share. One of these is that most women in gaming do play D&D. Whether this is because the game system is, in fact, not at all hostile to a feminine persuasion, or if it is because that's how almost everyone gets into the hobby and where almost everyone remains, I cannot say for sure. But I do know that when members in our extended gaming community organize combat-heavy games of D&D, GURPS, Twilight 2000 or whatever, none of our female members start jumping for joy and shout "memememe!". But when one of them pitched a Primetime Adventures game using Vampire: the Masquerade as the setting, they all got excited, and now I'm Producing the game for three women and one guy (who ironically but entirely characteristically tries to adopt the role of "their saviour").

    I bought Apocalypse World primarily for my wife, who loves apocalyptic fiction and stabbing people in the face. She's drooling over the character books right now. She also loves zombies, and is looking forward to the new edition of Dead Weight using the AW rules (hint hint, Daniel). She played DnD 4E for about two sessions before she decided the system was fine and everything, but she just didn't care for the continuous monster slaying and wish fulfillment as a payoff, and couldn't give a crap about going up a level so she could choose between the Feat That Makes Her Kill Everything In A Five Yard Circle or the Feat That Makes Her Grapple Move Way Cooler.

    Of course, all of the above is entirely anecdotal. I much prefer to get actual females - as Anna puts it, members of the breasted persuasion - to give feedback on this stuff rather than have a sausage party sit around and idly speculate. Unfortunately, women are exactly as incompetent as men when it comes to thinking about and explaining what they like (rather than that they "just like it"), and furthermore - in my experience - they tend to be fucking sick of talking about gender and RPGs because that's all they ever get asked about and they just want to play games dammit. Combine that with the fact that there are a lot fewer of them in the hobby to being with, and you have a pretty difficult situation where it's near impossible to draw reasonable statistical conclusions. I suspect, Arvin, even if you could leverage Facebook to check for Fan status, your results would be skewed because most women who like RPGs, much like the men, only play (or even know of) D&D to begin with.

    This necessitates actually thinking critically about this issue, rather than just listening to a few loud voices due to the presence of a statistical void. And Arvin, I think you're raising some fine points, that ought to be deconstructed and explored.

      \nHi John, thanks for your observations. It's nice to have some actual numbers to work with.

      However, I'm led to a slightly different conclusion than you: that the communities of story gamers don't seem to attract a significantly larger female audience than traditional tabletop RPGs, but that the communities formed around games like LARPs and Amber DRPG do. Going back to my earlier assertion, most role-players, male or female, stick to the game and the community through which they were introduced to the hobby in the first place. Simply based on the fact that due to the lack of mainstream industry marketing most people are exposed to the hobby through friend referrals, it's easy to draw the conclusion that communities who already have a lot of female members will tend to gain a larger proportion of female members than other communities, regardless of the products they're centred around.

      Actually, at least on the surface, there are a lot more similarities between D&D and Primetime Adventures than there is between D&D and Vampire: the Requiem LARP, or even Amber DRPG. Watch an ongoing game of D&D and PTA, and you will likely see people sit around a table, talk in character, talk out of character, pass little tokens around, draw cards / rolling dice and go Oooh! and Aaah! at the results, laugh, and eat Cheetoes. Watch a game of Amber DRPG and you will see people sit around a table, talk most always in character or in narrative language, maybe get up and act out a scene spontaneously, rarely if ever break character or munch on Cheetoes. To an outsider, a game of Amber or V:tR LARP might seem closer to improv acting, while a game of D&D or PTA looks more like social night with a rousing game of Sorry.

      The play procedures of Amber and LARPs might lend themselves better to attracting women than the play procedures of D&D or PTA, but as role players aware of game design we know that, deeper down, the difference between most games of V:tR and D&D is mostly that V:tR is acted out while standing up and wearing makeup. Questions of narrative authority, for example, might be important - feminists all agree that there is such a thing as a "female narrative", to my understanding, and thus games which leave the narrative up to a single individual who is with 95% certainty male might discourage women from such games. I'm quite interested in exploring these questions further, from a game mechanics perspective.

      Yeah, let's merge a "System Does Matter" thread with a "Women and Gaming" thread. What could possibly go wrong?

      \n I love gender topics too I know I'm a bit late in the conversation but I think there's something new to add, so here it is:

      Gender is not a totally incomprehensible thing. A woman can describe her experience, and a man can understand what she says; and vice versa. Human beings have this awesome ability/feat/move called "Put Yourself in Someone's Shoes." It goes like this:

      When you want to really understand another person, roll +listening (i.e. roll 2d6 plus your listening attribute).
      On a 10+ you get it, well enough to summarize what the other person is saying in totally different words, and in such a way that this person nods his or her head and says, "Yeah that's right. That's what I think and feel." Improve your knowledge of that person by +1.
      On a 7-9, you think you understand, but when you try to summarize what you think you heard, the person you're listening to says you got part of it wrong, or missed out on something else he or she was trying to say. Take +1 on your next interaction with that person.
      On a miss, you don't even finish listening, you just nod your head and interrupt him or her as soon as possible in order to tell them your opinion. The MC gets to make as hard a move as he or she likes.

      In other words, underneath the skin and meat and all the different experiences people have, men and women are basically human, and this common core of humanity, which they share, is what really matters to us in the end. Games have to address that common humanity if they're going to mean anything, and that's why men and women both enjoy playing awesome games. Therefore, really listening to one another is not only useful, it's absolutely essential to solving real problems like sexism. And it actually, truly, definitely works.

      Also, as we have seen in this thread, there is no one way that all women (or all men) approach games. It is a mistake to draw too many generalized conclusions about all woman from what individual women say. So, we have to listen, understand, and repeat, over and over, learning new things every time until we die. Sometimes, the key to good listening is first to speak up on a particular topic -- even if you're wrong about it -- hear what people say, and then share your improved understanding. If your intention is to learn, then whatever you say will lead to something good, no matter how "wrong" it is at first.

      On related topic -- lots of men enjoy talking about women: why they behave this way or that, and so forth. But one thing that men ought to talk about a lot more is why we ourselves behave this way or that. So many of us just take it for granted that "manly things" are "masculine" because of our hormones and muscles and hunter-gatherer-genes and whatnot. I'd like to see more games that explore what men are really all about in a way that questions traditional ideas of what masculinity means -- that is, how the characteristics of a man's body change (or do not change) how he should or should not behave. Sometimes we may find that the traditional view of masculinity has advantages; often we will find something different which works better -- either way, the exploration of it must yield valuable results.

      If men really come to understand themselves better, they'll be able to go a lot farther in understanding women too.

      Actually, at least on the surface, there are alotmore similarities between D&D and Primetime Adventures than there is between D&D and Vampire: the Requiem LARP, or even Amber DRPG. Watch an ongoing game of D&D and PTA, and you will likely see people sit around a table, talk in character, talk out of character, pass little tokens around, draw cards / rolling dice and go Oooh! and Aaah! at the results, laugh, and eat Cheetoes. Watch a game of Amber DRPG and you will see people sit around a table, talk most always in character or in narrative language, maybe get up and act out a scene spontaneously, rarely if ever break character or munch on Cheetoes. To an outsider, a game of Amber or V:tR LARP might seem closer to improv acting, while a game of D&D or PTA looks more like social night with a rousing game of Sorry.

      The play procedures of Amber and LARPs might lend themselves better to attracting women than the play procedures of D&D or PTA, but as role players aware of game design we know that, deeper down, the difference between most games of V:tR and D&D is mostly that V:tR is acted out while standing up and wearing makeup.
