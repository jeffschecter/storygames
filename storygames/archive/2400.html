[Tragedy Game] What does this game lack?

Meserach
February 2007 edited February 2007 in Game Design Help
So, I've been watching altogether too much of a Les Miserables DVD (the musical), and I got to thinking about stage tragedies as a genre, and before long I'd worked up the following game.

I've been tinkering with it today, but I feel there's something missing, to really get the game to fizz and pop.

The Tragedy Game - first draft

Please take a look at it. All comments will be interesting.

Specifically, I'm interested in the following:
  1. Are players given compelling reasons to spend Life tokens to the point of death? If not, how can I encourage it (mechanically and/or narratively?)
  2. Are players given compelling reasons to want to avoid Tragedy? If not, how should I encourage this?
  3. Am I being too hard or too soft on the characters? Specifically, are dice lost too quickly, and is Consolation too difficult?
  4. Should there be a means of regaining dice, or even regaining Life tokens? What should it be like?
  5. How should I deal with characters with Causes in common?
  6. How should I deal with characters with Causes that are directly opposed or mutually exclusive?
  7. What further narrative structure should I impose? I think there needs to be some kind of opening prologue, in which people establish and demonstrate their Causes, so that there's actually something to care about when things get tragic.
Thank you for your time.

    Howdy.

    I'd suggest checking out the excellent Wuthering Heights. Its mechanics gleefully brutalize the characters in such a way that the players can't help enjoying, if that makes sense.

    Here's another thought, without actually having read your rules: Based on posts above, you seem to want Tragedy to squat in the middle of play, leering at characters, hoping to drag them in. That's cool, but then players are really working to avoid Tragedy, right? If so, well, it's not really a Tragedy in the dramatic sense. In dramatic Tragedies, it's understood that at least one person is going to die, and no one will survive unmarked.

    It's been awhile since I saw Les Mis, but to use another musical theater example with which I'm more familiar, have you ever seen Sweeny Todd, the Demon Barber of Fleet Street? Ain't no happiness at the end of that. Same with Shakespeare--- who's happy at the end of Othello or Hamlet? Not even Fortinbras, whose cause is furthered by the disarray and insanity in the Danish court! All are marked, which is probably the point in the minds of the Greek originators, since it was, in the beginning, about religion...

    Anyway, you could set it up mechanically to make it inevitable that at least one character will die, as the center of the Tragedy itself (Valjean does die, right? I can't remember...) Others may live, may actually go on to live decent lives, but will always be marked by the Tragedy they witnessed (here the ending scenes of MLwM loom largely in my mind). I also can't help thinking that it'd be cool (in a Tragical sense!) if the death of those least marked/directly involved by/in the Tragedy helped to drive things along (in Hamlet I think of Ophelia, and even Rosencrantz and Guildenstern).

    I have some other thoughts, but I think I need to read your text first. You got my brain going today; thanks!

I could structure the game so as to make Tragedy some how mechanically beneficial - he who has the most tragedy wins - but I'm concerned baout what that wpould do to identifcation. If the game , mechanically speaking, coming up with ever more tragedy to pile upon your charcaters, it might slip more readily into melodrama and even comedy, as players compete to top each other and then top themselves as agonisiingly as possible.

    Hmm.

    So instead the game is less about accumulating tragedy but is instead about the small amount of consolation you can achieve in an environment of otherwise unremitting tragedy by default. It's the game of "OK, your life sucks, but you get one moment of beauty and happiness amongst it all. What is it?"
