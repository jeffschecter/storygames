\n I'm in a weird situation. Now, admittedly, some of you might not see it that way.

Right before I got into Indie Games (specifically, those with a small time investment and with high focus on Fun Now), my wife agreed to try out this RPG stuff.

She bought a mini for her D&D character. We had a short campaign. She wasn't having much fun. She gave it one final try when I found this new group of people in the area. Jason M. ran Dogs in the Vineyard. She still doesn't like roleplaying - too improv intensive for her tastes.

I am fine with this. I would have preferred a gamer wife, but it just isn't going to happen. This isn't the weird part, though.

Her best friend, we'll call her W, went out to eat with us the other night. We were all talking about random things when my wife brought up one of our favorite TV series, Freaks and Geeks. I immediately laugh and reminisce about the final episode, wherein the Freak sits down with the Geeks and plays Carlos, the Dwarf.

W exclaims (having remembered at that point what my big (hidden?) hobby was), "Oh my god! I've always wanted to play D&D!" She later on commented that she could only do it with people she trusted. She was looking to me to provide this, which I can help her with.

This is not an issue of trust with my wife, nor is it a communication issue (at least between the two of us). It's just, well, weird at this point. I think she's afraid, deep down, that W will start liking it. However, she has given her blessing for us four (W, her husband, my wife and I) to do something together.

I guess I just had to share, was all. Anybody else had any similarly weird situations because their spouse/SO didn't roleplay?

Try to add one new person to the group she doesn't know or is not yet at that comfort level, you might as well put a potted plant at that spot at the table because she's not playing.
    \nI think the comfort thing is big with games for a lot of people (sadly, for a lot of women). My SO is really hesitant to play for fear not just of not winning, but of not looking competent or smart. She also resents the way that RPGs, and many other games the way my crowd plays them, completely absorb a person socially so that there's no room for any other sort of interaction until the game is over.

    This latter viewpoint is starting to persuade me. I mean, why are there no RPGs that you can play in 20-second increments while you're mostly doing something else? Why is there so much commitment required? Why don't (m)any LARPs have a switch that says 'no, I'm not interested in that kind of interaction right now'? That'd be a huge step. Social context, social context, social context.
