\n At Conception this year, I playtested Cthulhu Dark, my rules-light Lovecraftian system, and its supplementary rules. (Alternatively, here are Cthulhu Dark and the supplementary rules in a format that's easier to print). I used it to run my Trail of Cthulhu scenarios: The Dance In The Blood, The Watchers In The Sky and The Dying of St Margaret's. (By running established scenarios, I could focus on problems with Cthulhu Dark more easily).

So, Cthulhu Dark is really simple. It has two main rules.

1. Your Insanity starts at 1. Every time something scares your Investigator, roll your Insanity Die (a d6). If you get more than your current Insanity, increase your Insanity by 1. When Insanity reaches 6, you go mad.

2. To do something, roll one die if it's within human capabilities and another if it's within your occupational expertise. The highest die dictates how well you do.

First, I ran The Dance In The Blood. This is such a reliable scenario that it could hardly fail: if I'd run it with Paranoia, it would still have worked. The playtest was reassuring. I'd worried the players would want Call of Cthulhu-style skills, but they seemed happy with that "occupational expertise" die.

Also, Insanity worked nicely, creeping up slowly, with one Investigator going mad. The mechanic pushes everyone up to 4 or 5 Insanity quickly, then leaves them hanging around that level, until they suddenly snap.

Two rules surprised me. One, The Final Horror, felt wrong. It said: when you see the most horrific thing in the scenario (e.g. Cthulhu rising), the person with highest Insanity goes instantly mad. It felt completely unfair in play, like death by GM Fiat. Another rule, Suppressing Knowledge, felt more important than I'd thought. This said that, when your Insanity reaches 5, you can decrease your Insanity by suppressing Mythos knowledge: burning books, shooting yourself, shooting other Investigators. Everyone loved this.

The next night, I ran The Watchers In The Sky, a much harder scenario. It felt like hard work, with quiet players staring at me. Afterwards, however, they said they enjoyed it, and it was an excellent playtest: it showed up problems without totally falling apart. Again, Final Horror was wrong. Again, everyone loved Suppressing Knowledge.

On the final night of the con, I decided at the last minute to run The Dying Of St Margarets. It was a quiet slot: many people had left the convention and everyone remaining was tired. So I stripped it down and ran it in two hours: it felt like a ghost story. The players were fabulous. They'd spent the weekend playing Warhammer 40K and other traditional stuff. They took to the rules-light stuff beautifully.

One rule, which developed throughout the weekend, involved adding your Insanity Die as a third die when you wanted to do something. You get the picture: it gives you an extra die, meaning you're more likely to roll higher, but there's a risk of increasing your Insanity. The rule went through three stages:\n
    \n
  • If your Insanity Die ties with any other die you roll, your Insanity increases. It felt clunky.
    • \n
  • If your Insanity Die is the highest die, your Insanity increases. This felt like a disincentive: if your Insanity die was any use, your Insanity went up.
    • \n
  • If your Insanity Die is the highest die, you make an Insanity Roll. And that worked much better.
    • \n
\nBy the end of the convention, it felt pretty solid. I'd use it to run new scenarios. (The question of how you design scenarios is still open.)

    \n By default, there are no combat rules. The rule is: anything you meet will kill you if you fight it. You can run or hide, but you can't fight.

    If I had to do it, though, I'd have a Harm stat, which works like Insanity. Use the standard rolls for combat. If you lose a roll, roll your Harm die and, if it exceeds your Harm, increase your Harm by 1. You die when your Harm gets to 6. Opponents die when their Harm gets to 3 or 4.

    Treason! That's lovely.

    \n Colin has pretty much got it. Here's how I think of it.

    It's Lovecraftian horror. So there won't be any combat. Any monster you meet is too big for you to take down. Any cultist you meet is imbued with dark power. You can run or sneak (that's how Lovecraft brings excitement into his stories), but you'll never fight.

    Now, sure, some groups like a pulpier style of play. Perhaps Cthulhu Dark can be hacked for that.

    But those core rules focus on Insanity. Because that's the problem. Your physical well-being has little to do with it. In fact, the main horror is that you could be left alive, with your mind broken.

    \n Hm, interesting.
    \n
    \nPersonally I'd go all the way through the description of basic Doing Things before introducing the Insanity complication. And I'm not sure whether rerolling is redundant. Does applying Insanity twice dilute it? You could use it as do the basic roll then decide whether to chuck in Insanity and risk to gain. That'd cover both bits.
    \n
    \nIt does feel like it needs a bit more attention to physical conflict. Perhaps monsters might be instantly lethal, but what about those cultists? You need to at least specify. I suppose you could say that all physical conflicts with ordinary people are resolved by intimidation, and it's a story conceit that they never go as far as actual violence. So an occupation of Boxer, say, would help to make you too scary for ordinary folk to trifle with, and you could narrate how that is demonstrated.
    \n
    \nIf you do use a Harm stat, you should definitely consider letting players risk it in the same way as Insanity. For some physical tasks, perhaps it might help if you're willing to strain a bit. You could say that if they want to leap from a train or fight crazed cultists, that *isn't* something ordinary people can do. You have to have a physical sort of occupation - which you might discourage or disallow for genre reasons, or allow for a pulpier game - or, for most characters, roll the Harm die. It's kind of an indicator of non-cinematicness. So you might end up with levels of physical action: trivial, dangerous (Harm) and lethal (auto-death). You'd also need to talk about what happens if you lose a physical conflict: presumably lose 1 Harm for minor ones or die for major ones.) ...or maybe junk all that and say that jumping off the roof of a train requires Insanity!
