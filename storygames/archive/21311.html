    1) Protagonist Character as narrative Anchor
    The cornerstone, I would say, is that each Player has their own Protagonist Character to play.
    As a Player you have complete and final authority on your own PC.

    Building on this, the game tries to have the Player be very engaged in their PC.
    On the one hand, Aspects and narrative-oriented constructs (such as Ways to Action) are preferred to numerical quantitative measurements.
    On the other hand, characters themselves are created AFTER the setting Issues are established by communal accord and brainstorming. This means that PCs are tailored to game Issues, and game Issues are tailored to Player interests.
    This is further reinforced by the fact that each PC is created with at least one, and no more than three, Personal Goals.

    Long story short, each Player has their attention focused and rooted (anchored) in their own PC, both as a starting point and as a future direction.

    2) Just Play your PC mechanics
    One trait of gmLed games is that you just think about (role)playing your own PC, leaving a lot of out-character and out-fiction tasks to the GM to handle.
    In a gmLess system these tasks fall on the Players, but a bunch of techniques can be used to have them be less conspicuous and intrusive.
    I'm very reluctant to use the term "immersion" but it might be useful here, as the aim is, more or less, to help Players avoid "breaking character immersion" or at the very least to notice it less as the game organically flows in a way that Players will feel as comfortable.

    A) Fiction First mechanics
    Game mechanics attempts to be as dependant as possible by the Fiction, rather than by human arbitrary decisions.
    Instead of having Players discuss on how to apply the rules, or leaving grey areas for them to do as they please, the game mechanics try to be expressed in the most terse and unambiguous way possible ... you describe something, aka you produce a fiction, and THAT should be enough to have everyone at the table say "OBVIOUSLY this one rule applies now! OBVIOUSLY the roll difficulty is X and not Y!".
    Less negotiations = Less immersion breaking
    This shows in the resolution (Test) mechanics and their constituting components: test trigger, Ways to Action, and the overall Test procedures (how to define the Type of action, the Difficulty of the test, the Outcome of the test, etc).

    \n Strong P-to-P Conflict Management
    The presence of a small number of extremely clear and "strong" mechanics aimed to handle out-fiction conflict among Players is another brick in this structure.
    The Revise and Dissent ritual words, specifically, are built towards efficiency and expediency over democratic consensus, they cut knots rather than disentangle them.
    Again Less negotiations = Less immersion breaking.
    This "rule of the most picky player" (of Dogs in the Vineyard inspiration) is enforced so that the end result is either of high quality (subjectively for the current people at the current table) or so obviously dysfunctional that the game breaks really fast ... which is an in-built security net against abusive behaviour, where the best (and only?) solution is to stop the game and talk it out as human beings.

    \nC) First Person View
    It is strictly forbidden for Players to narrate/describe anything outside the immediate physical perception of the PCs present in a scene.
    This also anchors Player attention on the PCs, rather than let it meander towards more GM-like ways of thinking. You play the here and now of your PC. You don't (because you can't) tell a tale as a Narrator / Storyteller / Director.
    Less plot planning = Less immersion breaking
    On the other hand, the system leverages the natural tendency of Players towards connecting dots and finding patterns in specific moments of the game:

    C1 - the ninja trick
    Confused? don't know what to do next? Play a scene where your PC poses these very questions to someone! This is an explicit game procedure that helps Players turn all sorts of planning and brainstorming into in-fiction first-person roleplay, rather than having them stop the game and break immersion.

    C2 - end of session ritual
    The game procedures require and encourage Players to wrap up every session with out-fiction speculation and brainstorming. What do you think is going on in the story? Are there unresolved matters and questions? What do you think the answers might be? What is the Invisible GM up to? What are you liking or disliking?
    This all happens out-fiction and out-game, as a simple and relaxed chat among Players that comment about and puzzle over what the GM has had them play. It does not decide anything, but helps ideas circulate, gently encouraging Players to look at the whole picture (as single specific events might appear jarring or mysterious) reinforcing the illusion that there is an external logic behind all that they have played through.

    \nD) Sedimentary Storytelling
    As a function of point C, the only thing that a Player can do story/plot wise is to just add details to what is happening in a specific scene, here and now, without ever defining big overarching truths.
    Grains of sand.
    Mainly coming from the direct actions of their own PC.
    Most of the time, this will produce an effect similar to the one explained in point A but for the fiction rather than for the mechanics: "OBVIOUSLY there is a fountain on the map! OBVIOUSLY the rebels scream your name! OBVIOUSLY the bartender looks at you sideways!"

    This works most of the time... but obviously not always
    For this reason there are a few "pressure valves" in the system that are available to support this structure by injecting fictional details from the outside (outside as in not-from-the-players).
    These are:
    - Quoth the Die ... this ritual word allows Players to ask Yes/No questions to the invisible GM
    - Damn! ... this ritual word allows Players to have something unexpected happen
    - Hard and Soft Opposition ... this automated mechanism adds unexpected consequences to important/meaningful actions (aka Tests)

    They mostly work by providing "skeletons". The rules tell you, for example, that "someone in the scene becomes hostile, or arrives now and is already hostile, and ATTEMPTS to act against you".
    This kind of fictional skeleton is detailed enough that Players are only required to dress it up with obvious color, but also vague enough that it could easily (less negotiation!) fit in any specific situation.

    And this is the trick behind "sedimentary" storytelling.
    Coming up with big important long-term stuff is difficult and authorial.
    Coming up with small details that appear obvious given the specific current circumstances is easy.
    Bit by bit this activity, coupled with PC actions and external (non-Player) input, sums up to a story that is: coherent, solid, logical and yet surprising, unexpected and engaging (all also thanks to the constant micro-corrections enforced by the P-to-P social mechanics).

    NOTE:
    A surprising side effect is that this structure works best when Players are honest... as in "egoist".
    You play advocating your PC, to the hilt, unconcerned but what the other PC might think or do about it. You want to go right while the party pushes to go left? Go right!
    You don't like what Philip just described? It makes little sense to you? It hurts your vision of the game? SAY SO! Use the Revise mechanic! Advocate what YOU like about the game!

    This translates in a tight game where everyone gets as close as what they want as possible while still granting the same to everyone else, with the side effect of SHOWING (thus teaching) to everyone what everyone else truly likes ... it teaches openness, listening and cooperation as a byproduct of being "egoistically honest".
    Usually Players are either already tuned to each other tastes, and the mechanics seldom kick in, only producing course correction if and when needed to keep the harmony and focus even over longer periods of play.
    Or they have a moment of initial high usage, as Players learn to know each other, and then fade to the background for the occasional course correction.
    Or they have the game break in a way that clearly shows the misbehaviour of a Player, allowing the group to solve (or at least face) the social problem on a clean social level, out-game.

    This is the exact opposite of most gmLess structure that rely on common sense and social graces, encouraging everyone to be good and kind, to think of the others and their needs, etc... which I've often seen produce more shyness, more awkwardness, more social fear and pressure, as you don't want to impose yourself, you don't want to be the dick saying "I don't like this".
    And the more you hide your opinion and silence your critics, the harder it becomes to be honest at the table.

    I've seen how hard it is for people to say "No" even with all the tools that FateLess provides, and how the more they use it, the easier they are to use, the better the resulting game is.
    THIS also is an important piece of the Invisible GM effect, as it makes the whole game tik better, thus reinforcing the feeling that the plot is solid and meaningful, just as if a very good GM had it tailored just for you in advance.

    \nE) The Illusion of Immersion
    By far and large the idea of play being "always" immersed in your PC is utter bullshit.
    Even in gmLed games, all the time a Player is sitting at a table, looking at a character sheet, rolling dice, thinking about game mechanics, and what time is it, and if they like what Malcom has just had his PC say and do, etc.
    Then the Player also imagines themselves looking out from their PC's eyes, or following their story as a very interactive audience. A Player can prefer this part of the game, sure, but it does not mean that all the rest is not there.
    Immersion is an illusion that has us selectively focus on one thing rather than another, nothing more. Different people are able to induce such self-suggestions in different ways, depending on their personal tastes and habits and whatnot ... this is why one table behaviour can be "immersive" for one Player while "immersion breaking" for another and absolutely neutral and irrelevant for some else.

    By the same token when we play we are also always an "audience" for the other Players/PCs.
    In gmLed game you think about your PC, true, but when you engage with the SIS you automatically fill-in the blanks, or notice such blanks and feel the need to ask about them, or imagine abstractly what could be the meaning of the fiction... where it comes from, where it goes.
    You don't devote too much attention to this, you are immersing in your PC after all!, but it still happens.

    FateLess tries to exploit this by having the Player give voice to such usually unvoiced ideas and questions.
    When the game requires SOMEONE to offer an input, it uses all the aforementioned tricks and mechanisms to see that someone at the table answers.
    Chances are that someone at the table will organically have a clear idea of the answer, thus it would feel OBVIOUS and perfectly natural to state what should be already clear to everyone.
    The magic is, if it IS obvious it works as reinforcement of the fiction: it makes sense, it is logical, we can depend on it and build upon it and exploit it.
    If it IS NOT obvious (and it often is not) you just surprised everyone else, and by virtue of the social procedures it will either feel "right" and enrich the game, or feel "wrong" and trigger the editing/harmonising mechanics ending up in a "right" result.

    If one Player were always asked to provide all input, they would feel too much out-character.
    But making the input very small, and outsourcing it to whomever at the table feels comfortable answering to just this one specific thing right here and now... it feels like nothing.
    And the more you do, the more you get used to it, the more you end up contributing without feeling like breaking immersion.

    Sometimes this illusion of an illusion tears a bit. Sometimes the game needs an input and no one is comfortable giving it, and even using the mechanical tools at your disposal SOMEONE will in the end HAVE TO spell out a clear answer.
    It can happen.
    But not as often as one might imagine. And the longer the game progresses, the more the Players subconsciously arrange the dots in their mind in the shape of an answer, until even the biggest reveal of the most complex mystery investigation becomes and easy(er) answer that doesn't really require breaking immersion. You're just stating what to you, by this point, makes perfect sense.

    \nF) The Illusion of GM preparation
    Finding solace in the presence of a GM that prepares a story beforehand is, also, another illusion.
    As a Player you can't see behind the GM screen.
    You can never know if what the GM says was carefully pre-planned weeks ago, or is an inspired improvisation of the moment.

    Usually one can tell by the quality of it.
    But not always... GMs might be storytellers by vocation, but not necessarily by talent or trade... some planned stories are awful, while some improvised plots are pure genius.
    And it all usually affects the players much more by virtue of the Player engagement in whatever shit they have "lived" at the table, than because of the objective quality of the story/acting material.

    This is the biggest trick of story-games... emergent play is naturally engaging, so even if the story is utter crap the Players will enjoy it immensely more than a best seller novel in which they have little or no buy-in.

    The main difference in then in authority. You trust the GM to do their job. So you feel like a gmLed is inherently more solid, coherent, and allows for more "immersion" into your PC.

    FateLess tries to leverage this illusion too.
    Promoting emergent gameplay with the lowest possible Player effort, while shifting the perception of authority from the absent GM to the very present and very solid game rules.
    The mechanics are as clear cut as possible for usability, of course, but also because they need to inspire trust.
    So on one side of the GM screen the Players mostly do what they have always done in a gmLed game, while on the other side of the GM screen all the bits and pieces of the system try to summon the silhouette of an invisible GM ... that you can tell by of the quality of the resulting game.

    This is also the Achilles' Heel of the whole caste of cards :P
    The game rips the illusion of the GM, then tries to make you not notice by focusing your attention on the PC, hoping that with the corner of your eye you kind of see kind of feel that the GM is still there ... you KNOW it's not there, but if suspend your disbelief just a little you can FEEL like it is there.

    \nIN CONCLUSION
    The end result is a narrative structure that can only be played "to find out what happens".

    Where you first and foremost play your PC in an "all in" way (or call it immersive, whatever).

    Where both the game itself and the other Players act in such a way to make you feel the GM tasks as little as possible, while tricking you into feeling as if it was an invisible GM all along.

    Is is not 100% like playing in a normal gmLed game.
    Meybe not even 80% :P
    I guess this is subjective.

    But it is the only gmLess game I know about that even tries to capture the feeling of a classic gmLed experience. To me, it succeeds in doing so to a degree I find satisfying ... but I guess I don't count
    Maybe others here can try it out and tell me what their impressions are.

    This is an important observation and many don't think it is a option worth considering; games and audiences are not the same and different solutions work for different tastes and goals. Many designers just assume that it is an unwritten rule that if anyone's contribution to the game is even slightly modified that you are taking away their creativity or hurting their feelings\u2014Archipelago being an exception\u2014meanwhile, many other players may be unsatisfied with the story and no one feels comfortable talking about their dissatisfaction or discussing issues generally, and resentment can quietly grow toward others; for example, the most gonzo player(s), etc. This especially happens if the group are not friends to begin with and don't feel comfortable airing any dissatisfaction. Sometimes people don't pick up on social cues, even after a very long time, and they are acting with goodwill but unwittingly can affecting others enjoyment of the game; I think that having a mechanic to arbitrate can't be worthwhile for some designs. Also, in GMless games there is no GM to reign-in and adjust differences in the narrative tone and people are improving, so there may need to be subtle mechanics for making slight adjustments. It took about about nine months, playing weekly, for our group of regulars to realize that nearly everyone at SG SLC was not satisfied with many, if not the majority, of the stories we were creating, because they were being derailed and lacking in verisimilitude because of whimsical humor that drifted from the desired tone of a given story. It's not about blaming anyone, it's about everyone looking at their contributions and seeing how those contributions affect others. There are sessions where gonzo, whimsical humor and lack of verisimilitude are great, but this is not the case with every session. If there is even a little bit of an augmentation, rarely, of a player's contribution, even if it is for the satisfaction of the entire group, this is seen as a trespass by many designers. And they have their arguments, and they are designing their games for certain audience, and I'm not going to say for a second that they are designing in the wrong way; I'm just saying that there are other options that are valid. Often, the social outcomes of not having a way to slightly modify contributions creates a more dysfunctional social environment then having a way to gently do so. I might add that I'm talking solely about collaborative GMless Story Games in this regard, and that it is important that, even objectively, a player's contribution isn't "wrong," it is just something that interferes with another player's enjoyment of the game. No one is "wrong", it is just as much about the player(s) who ask the player to adjust their contributions lately, as it is about the player and narrated the contribution. Besides who wants to interfere with another person's enjoyment of the game? Why not make a small compromise if it is reasonable? It isn't about pointing out "bad" contributions, it's about sharing the story in a way that everyone can enjoy it. It's also important that asking someone "to try another way" is not used in an arbitrary way that is just enforcing one players creative taste over anothers or attempting to monopolize the story; this, is of coarse, an abuse of the mechanic. The reasonable times to employ the mechanic should be made explicit in the game. In most games, if the mechanic is used, it should be confined to when something seriously goes outside the agreed upon tone (which is established prior to the game), or seriously disrupts the verisimilitude when this is unappropriate given the story's tone. Mickey Mouse shouldn't show up in a Game of Thrones episode, for example. Everyone is different, and enjoy's sessions with different tones, so it's also important to provide different sessions that accommodate different players tastes and needs and respects those needs, so that the majority doesn't always make those with minor tastes unable to play there type of game. The "try another way" technique also helps to train people socially so that they can accommodate others tastes, and in time, is needed very rarely. There is of course, more nuance then I expressed above, but I don't have time to write an essay and refine it Anyway, my two cents.

    FateLess is a "pulp" game where the main objective of play is to "find out what happens".
    Translated: Characters are protagonists. The game can easily dish obstacles to make their lives hard and difficult and thwart their plans... but at the end of the day the point is NOT to stop them, the point is NOT to see IF they prevail, the point is to see HOW they do it.

    I haven't yet seen a single PC die in actual play.
    I have, though, seen plenty fail at achieving their Goals, undergo deep and painful changes, or get beaten up pretty badly.
    Danger is very real, game over is not.
    I don't know if this helps framing the game in the correct light.

    Anyway, coming to your specific points.

    -
    --
    ---
    Less starting Skills
    ---
    --
    -

    FateLess uses a different skill system than Fate Core, and the latest version (currently being tested) ditches Skills entirely to adopt my own stab at the theme of Approaches, which I call Ways to Action.
    But anyway, this is not so relevant... by default PCs are assumed to always succeed in any action they undertake except when:
    - they face opposition from another character (and anything different than a "Yes I'll do as you wish" is considered opposition, haggling is opposition, stalling is opposition, etc)
    - they want to Create Advantage
    - the action is so difficult it could most probably fail AND the consequences of failure are deemed important / interesting / meaningful

    There are no classic Fate conflict structures: no conflicts, no challenges, no contests ... if the fiction fits the above mentioned criteria, the action triggers a Test. That's all. It's a sort of resolution that uses a "task trigger" to then produce "conflict outcomes". It's easier played than explained XD

    Also, the difficulty of the test is set by the fiction: roll 1d6 minus 1d6 plus Rating (old Skill or new Way) vs a difficulty of...
    ... 2 is always the minimum
    ... +2 if the action is inherently crazy difficult
    ... +2 if external elements are actively hindering your action
    ... +2 if the action would directly resolve a Goal

    What usually happens, barred blind luck with the dice, is a naturally outcome of FAIL or TIE that is then modified by either spending Fate Points or accepting a Cost.

    So, as you can see, if a Player wants an action to succeed... it will... no doubt.
    But it will have all sorts of side effects, consequences and costs, which in turn have the game (and story) spin ahead meaningfully and dramatically.

    Not sure if this fits in your idea of "grit" but in general there is really no point in lowering the starting amount of Skill/Way points.

    You usually have more "high" Skills but only in specialised areas.
    You usually have fewer "high" Ways but their application is broader.
    As before, lowering these caps should not really be useful to achieve the grittiness you mention.

    That's all.

    - Damn!
    - Compel an Aspect
    - suffer a Hindrance at the hands of another Player (aka, when they Invoke an aspect to give you a -2 on the roll ... which might soon go away as I've never seen it used in my life)

    There is no such thing in FateLess
    A PC can withstand 3 Consequences (aspects) which "only" affect fictional positioning.
    (spoiler alert, in FateLess fictional positioning is effing important)
    At the 4th Consequence suffered, the PC is Taken Out... which puts the fate of the PC into the table's hands, death included.

    Consequences are suffered from mechanical effects. True. But also by simple fictional positioning.
    This means that if anyone "just" describes a drunken knight swinging his sword at you... either you do something to avoid it (and succeed) or you get wounded. Just like that. And that's a Consequence.
    Same goes for social or mental ones.
    And Consequences take time (game sessions) or Significant Milestones to heal.

    Again, it might not fit the classical idea of "high lethality" so I'm not sure if the end feeling is what you are looking for.

    Never been a problem.
    Each Aspect can be Invoked only once per Test (not scene).
    But you don't usually have tons of Fate points.
    And even if you do, it doesn't ruin the game... as pointed out before, the mechanics use the NATURAL test to have stuff happen, and the MODIFIED test only to set the final outcome.

    So yeah, you might spend a bunch of Fate to invoke a bunch of Aspects and score a WwStyle without sweating.
    But Opposition effects (think something like PbtA Hard Moves from the Invisible GM) can and will still hit you no matter what.

    The overall game structure makes the problem a moot point :P

    -- "Quoth the Die" sounds very important to me, depending on the rules or guidelines governing when it gets used. What are those?
    \nThis is the text from the rulebook...\n
    To do it you say aloud \u201cQuoth!\u201d. This calls a Pause and tells the other Players you want to ask a question to a die. Formulate a Yes/No question and make it as specific as possible, explaining clearly what you mean. Again, the only possible answers are bound to what the PCs can perceive right here and now. Then roll one die: odd means No, even means Yes.

    Feel free to add further details to weave the answer into the fiction, or to ask more Yes/No questions to produce, bit by bit, a satisfying description. A chain of Quoth is a very effective way to produce unexpected and interesting results.
    \nPause is another mechanics, here is how it works by the book...\n
    Sometimes the game conversation can get a bit hectic and the resulting fiction unclear. Or someone may try to fast-talk her way through the game. This is not fun, so at any time you can raise your hands and say \u201cPause!\u201d.

    The game must stop so that everyone has time to ask questions and clarifications, offer ideas to the table and generally clear the air. The fiction established just before the Pause must be renegotiated to account for any misunderstanding and incomprehension. Pause ends when everyone agrees to resume.

    Don\u2019t debate!
    Ask questions and offer answers, but if an actual argument arises stop it using the other Ritual Words to cut the crap.
    \nThis means the Quoth can be called at any time. And can even supersede spontaneous Player input. So if I simply say that it is raining, you can call a Quoth to have the die determine if it rains or not.

    In 90% of the cases this all happens very smoothly, as part of the normal game conversation around the table. The full and verbose text of the rules is there to help in the remaining 10% of cases, when someone is somehow doing something screwy.\n
    Resolving conflicts about how the game should go quickly and in a manner that everyone can accept sounds like the proper approach to me. How do Revise and Dissent achieve this?
    \nRevise is a straight veto, but limited to the aesthetic of a description.
    A - "...and there is a palm tree."
    B - "Revise. There is a tree all right, but not a palm, it's too tropical."
    The end.
    The tree can't be a palm. Period. We come up with a more fitting kind of tree and move along.

    Of course the game text explains this more fully, but you get the gist.
    Dissent is an actual auction meant to settle deeper divergences about how things should be in the game world.
    Players will briefly express their vision, then go turn by turn bidding Fate points, one at a time. Not everyone needs to be involved, and once you pass you are out for good.
    All bids are real... so if you bid 3 points and then pass or loose, those points are gone no matter what. This way you can't fake-bid just to troll and inflate the final pay out.
    It is possible to bid more Fate than you have... this puts you in "debt" with the game, which means that all Fate you earn from now on will instantly vanish to pay up your debt.

    In my experience, the Revise comes up as much or as little as needed to put Players in sync with each other, then unobtrusively vanishes in the background, reappearing only briefly here and there to keep everyone on track.
    Dissent is used much more rarely, but when you need it, it's there and does its job egregiously.

    Yesterday I was playing my second session of The Veil and I would have given a leg and and arm to have a similar mechanic in place... we spent ONE HOUR debating, and getting progressively angrier at each other, about how the damned Veil worked, by default, in regards of chip-less people. A nightmare
    By the time we cooled our heads and acted more sensibly, the time and energy had already been wasted, and still we only brought home a grudgingly acknowledged result.
    The Dissent mechanic would have lead to the same result in a matter of minutes and with less leftover anger and frustration towards each other

    1) What's at stake in FateLess? Is it whether the PCs can attain their goals? Is it what attaining their goals looks like? Is it who they become, or reveal themselves to be, in the process?
    \nIn FateLess you first establish the setting's themes and Issues.
    Then you create Characters in function of those Issues.
    Then you create Goals in function of those Characters.
    A big driver, the main one, is therefore theme exploration. Which in turn means setting and character exploration. Goals are there as the finer and most specific part of this path, and as such they are tools that give the Character (thus the Player) direction.
    You care to accomplish your Goals because you care for your PC because you care for the game Issues.

    In my view this means that achieving your personal Goals is indeed a BIG part of your game experience. It gives you both direction and drive. But it's not the underlying final goal.
    Just as you can and will face obstacles and challenges, but "winning" is not the point of the game. At least, not like it is in a skill-based challenge-oriented Old School design.
    It's more akin to Apocalypse World.
    Failure and death are real dangers, and you can (and should) advocate for your PC's success, but in the end you sit at the table mainly to see what happens next, to explore setting, character and plot.

    That said...\n
    "2 guards" means I can probably achieve my goal and "30 guards" means I probably can't
    \nThis.
    The level of practical obstacle, the fictional positioning of the whole situation, does make a difference.

    But you don't have to be the one setting it up, if you're not comfortable with it or prefer a different play experience.
    Anyone else at the table can define such things for you, just because they seem obvious and sensible to them.
    Or you can Quoth the die to know if there are guards, if there is more than one, if they are a few or many, etc.
    Or anyone might set such details in order to activate a mechanic (maybe Compelling and Aspect, maybe throwing a Damn! in the mix hoping to scrounge 1 Fate point for themselves).
    Or as a consequence of a roll you accrue enough Opposition points to activate a Hard Opposition effect...and then describing guards or sharks or shark-guards is just a matter of dressing up with appropriate fiction a hard mechanical fact you can't wiggle away from

    And anyway, actually achieving a Goal is fundamentally a coin toss.
    If you care about it, the only way to increase your chance of success, even to a safe 100%, is to face a lot of obstacle on the way there, to do lots of stuff, to research and investigate and plan and then take action and gather resources and prepare and... and at the end of it all if you hit a 100% chance of success and put yourself in the needed fictional positioning to achieve the Goal... well then it is well deserved, and everyone has had a blast, and the game has done its job

    On another level, as the game is focused on individuals, the other players will have no qualms in playing opposition for you.
    But even if the PCs end up together, as a team with a common goal, there is enough automation that in the game that they will face sensible challenges no matter what. A single guard can be a meaningful obstacle, and each roll can generate interesting and unexpected (and inconvenient) outcomes in addition to a mere Win/Fail result.

    Last, but not least, I have noticed in time how most people, especially those that really care a lot about "the win", tend to:
    - react badly to cheating, or what they perceive as an "undeserved" victory
    - give high value to "realism" or whatever passes for it

    So you can say there is just one guard. You can even say there are no guards at all. Chances are that, with almost no conscious effort, someone else will react with a fix for that...
    "No guards? Pfffft... more like a small regiment of guards organised in groups of three, walking the perimeter on a routine path every 30 minutes."

    The prompt here is not the "weak player" desire to ramp up drama, so common in many shared-story games. It's the good old need for stuff that "makes sense" to you. Very powerful and ingrained in pretty much anyone I know; the only caveat is you need social structures to handle divergence of opinion.

    - Dissent

    Arguments between a PC and another PC are solved by a Test, as your PC tries to overcome my PC.

    Arguments between a PC and an NPC are solved in the same way. Your PC overcomes that NPC.

    One example could be...

    A - My PC asks the girl (NPC) out.
    B - She says no.
    C - I would rather her say yes.
    B - No.
    C - Yes.
    B - Well I said no first, do you Dissent?
    C - err... no?
    B - then shut up.

    or

    B - Well I said no first, do you Dissent?
    C - Hell yeah, 1 Fate!
    B - Enough for me, "yes" it is then.

    etc...

    You don't need absolute isolation to deeply engage in your PC. You can do it and still say "oh and it rains!" without ruining your PC-first experience.

    And then of course, this game will not be perfect for everyone.
    But that's normal and ok, I guess

    \n In one of my previous games, Tactical Ops, that is the case.
    You know that the mechanical Difficulty level is X, so you narrate a fiction that will fit that.

    In FateLess it is the opposite.
    You narrate a fiction and then, through the filter of the mechanics, you find out an appropriate Difficulty rating.
    But a few considerations should be taken into account...

    Saying that 30 guards have a higher Difficulty rating is wrong.
    Because the game is fiction first, the most important question never is how much, but always how.
    Say you want to enter a building and there are 30 guards protecting it.
    What is your action?
    How do you try to solve this obstacle?

    FateLess "action system" uses what I call a Task-Trigger and a Conflict-Resolution.
    What a PC does, specifically, in terms of discreet actions/tasks within a specific set of circumstances and fictional positioning is what determines if there is a conflict in the first place, and then which the scope of such conflict might be.
    Only then you go grab the dice. And only then you consider the difficulty of the Task.

    So you want in the building, and 30 guards are spread here and there patrolling it.
    What is your course of action?

    Load your guns and barge into the front entrance, screaming and kicking and shooting?
    Ok.
    Then maybe you don't trigger a test at all, it's painfully obvious to everyone that you will be mowed down by an overwhelming number of guards. You don't roll against crazy odds; you just don't roll because it is not possible. This might be a perfect time to Dissent, by the way.

    Or maybe we are all cool with the idea that this described action might at least be possible, if not probable, and you could survive and succeed... because movies! (or maybe you won the Dissent)
    Then going by your description the Difficulty of the test is established:
    2 is the base, which already assumes that the action is difficult
    +2 if your action in inherently super duper difficult
    +2 if aggravating circumstances hinder your action
    +2 for drama if the action would, in and of itself, lead you to achieve a Goal

    In this example a solid 6 seems fair to me. Again this is not just arbitrary consensus, it is what looks obvious from the fiction. In case of problems, there's Dissent.

    But what if you instead put on a disguise and try to pass as one of the guards?
    Then having 30 dudes roaming the area might actually help your plan, as it is easier to pass as just another face in this "crowd" of people.
    So you describe how you put on your costume and how you wait for a gap in the guard rotation, then you sneak in the first perimeter and... just walk among your peers.
    This calls for no roll, in and of itself.
    I can't imagine the other Players passing on the opportunity to have at least one guard call you out:
    "Hey you! ...do you have a cigarette?"
    Or something.
    Or maybe they are more aggressive (possibly as a result of Soft Opposition, possibly because someone tried to get a Fate by calling in a Damn!)
    "Hey you! ...why haven't I seen you at the briefing?"

    THEN depending on your actions, you might trigger a test.
    But this way the number of guards has become completely irrelevant... you just need to get past this one suspicious guy.
    Although the Difficulty might still end up being 6 (or less, or more) because of how you describe your actions in this circumstance. The rule is always the same:
    2 is the base, which already assumes that the action is difficult
    +2 if your action in inherently super duper difficult
    +2 if aggravating circumstances hinder your action
    +2 for drama if the action would, in and of itself, lead you to achieve a Goal

    Or maybe you try to ninja your way into the building, sneaking out of sight.
    (assuming you previously found a way to do it)
    Then maybe the action is difficult (so you roll) but not so terribly difficult (so you don't get the +2 Difficulty) but having 30 pairs of eyes moving around is an obvious hindrance (so you get that +2 Difficulty) for a grand total of Difficulty 4.

    So yeah, numbers are crunched by the fiction. And an adverse fiction equals to a concrete increase in overall difficulty. But the actual Difficulty rating comes mainly from your fictional positioning, from your chosen action, and how you carry it out.

    Maybe you instead find a way to have a legitimate excuse to enter the building, and now the guards are completely irrelevant, be they 3 or 30 or 300

    I like to think of this as "tactical fiction".
    This is an element of Player skill very reminiscent of Old School gaming: you have a fictional goal (get in the building) and a fictional problem (a mob of enemies) and your main tool to solve it are your PC's fictional actions ... with a roll in between to shake things up a bit, according to how good or bad your idea was.
    Just an element, not a big deal, enough to add some concrete feeling of challenge to the game

    I touched on this subject in another thread...
    To me most gmLess games have a soft and optimistic approach where Players are assumed and expected to be nice to each other, to willingly go in the same direction, to spontaneously play along.
    FateLess assumes the opposite ( ) and uses tools fit for the task.

    Of course the clearly defined goal of all Players is to explore a story together. Players are never truly in competition with other Players. But within this view, Characters can merrily be at each other's throats. And by using arbitration tools, as you call them, this works even if Players behave in a mildly antagonistic way ... as I mentioned before to David, Player motivation (do it for drama, do it for realism, do it for antagonism, etc) becomes a moot point.

    It's maybe like... using arbitration tools to achieve harmony?
    This line of thought reminds me of an old (Forgite?) nugget of wisdom about rules and realism.

    The idea would be that the human brain is usually exceptionally good at discerning what is realistic, what makes sense ... maybe everyone has a different opinion, but each will instantly have it: this makes sense, this is bullshit.

    On the other hand the same brain is exceptionally bad at discerning probabilities and outcomes ... any infinite number of things might have a play in how things roll out, including self-interest, the desire to be surprised, etc.

    So the point was: don't waste rules on something you already do spontaneously and exceptionally, and instead focus the rules on what you struggle with.
    I feel this is kind of a similar direction, maybe?
