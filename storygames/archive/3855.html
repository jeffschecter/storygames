\n What are some of your favorite under appreciated games? I'm a softy for 'em, as I'm frequently told Nine Worlds is one of 'em. Here are a couple of mine:

Conspiracy of Shadows -- Keith's got a solid game here. The text is sometimes too concise. It requires you to read it, and then put pieces together in your head when you realize things like "Oh shit! Doom is awesome!" And, the conflict modifiers seem ho-hum at first, until you start to realize how they can combine with weapons (which are, like in The Shadow of Yesterday, not always literal weapons, but modifiers for situations), and then you're off to some funky damn scenes. And, there are some places where it needs examples desperately.

And Witchblood. How cool is that? It's fucking cool. I will later Frost Dance my Coke and have a nice lunch.


a/state -- I really dig this game. Hell, I even like small parts of the ho-hum system. (Penetration rules! I actually dig that. I made rules like that once. I smile thinking about it) But, that system is still pretty bleakly ho hum. But the setting! MY GOD IT'S MADE OUT OF PEOPLE! Ghostfighters, Flowghosts, Lostfinders. The races! Holy hot shit, the ekranaplane races. The whole thing gives me a hard on. I want to be a Ghostfighter so bad. Just bad ass, those guys. This game is fucking cool. I want to run it in another system. And, saying that sorta feels like sleeping around on your new girlfriend. Or something.

I'm not sure a/state is too under under appreciated. I think it has seen a pretty good following overall. But, it sure isn't talked about much where I hang out.

    \nWraith. Of the World of Darkness games, I was only ever into Mage and Wraith. I've found other games that fill the niche that Mage once filled (e.g. Nobilis), but Wraith remains in a special place in my heart. It deserved better than it got. Sure, they tried to shoe-horn the concept into the Storyteller system, when it really deserved a proper write-up from the ground up. But it was so full of amazingly cool ideas.

    Like the Shadow. All your darkest desires that you refuse to admit to anyone, now having taken form as a personality in your mind. Played by another player. With its own powers and temptations to mess with your head.

    I've divested myself of my Mage books, but I still own Wraith. Maybe one day....

    Seth Ben-Ezra

    \n I actually have a list over on IPR. Here they are:

    carry. a game about war. This game is talked about, a little, but I think it is truly brilliant, and not enough people know about it or have tried it. I think games with serious subject matter like this can be a hard sell, but they are really, really worthwhile.

    Covert Generation. This one is a cool little game about teenage anarchists. It's a true indie product, from a guy hardly anyone around here knows, and it's his only book. Because the author developed offline and doesn't participate in various indie games fora, it has no buzz, but it's fun, and definitely has a story-game structure.

    Fastlane. This is a tough one, because of the roulette wheel mechanic. You can play it with dice, but the wheel is the biggest barrier (along with the design, as mentioned elsewhere on Story Games). However, it hits its theme hard, like a sledgehammer. You literally bet your character's stats in order to perform actions. That is cool.

    Hero's Banner. This is a good game, and again, it's had some indie scene buzz, but that isn't reflected in its sales. The structure, with its tightly constrained story arc and short character lifetime, might put some folks off, but this is another game that hits its theme with a sledgehammer.

    Nine Worlds. Matt mentioned this above, but it's another indie game that doesn't get as much traction as I think it should. Tight, elegant mechanics and a truly cool setting. Play it, dammit!

    \nNine Worlds, for sure. I've played and run this game many times and it's always been awesome for me. Elegant mechanics, fantastic setting. Plus, it's not just a fun game to play, it's a good book to read. Nine Worlds holds a place of honor in my gaming library.

    Great Ork Gods. Like InSpectres, GOG is concentrated, manic fun. If you need to kick up the energy level of your group and get everyone smiling and hollering again, GOG is your huckleberry. Plus, it teaches you how to stab your friends in the face.

    Contenders. Not under appreciated, per se, but not talked about as much as it should be. My first ever game of Contenders goes in my top 5 gaming experiences of all time. That was playing the game cold, with other players who had never tried it before, and it was instantly awesome.

    Shooting the Moon. This game is tied with Don't Rest Your Head as my favorite design of 2006. It is brilliantly crafted, accessible, and may be the perfect introductory game for your friends and loved ones who want to try gaming. This is a kind of holy grail in the gaming community but for some reason this game did not get a lot of fanfare. The same goes for its companion game, Breaking the Ice.

    \n Eh, most indie games are underappreciated. Even some of the most popular count their sales in the hundreds, which is woefully insufficient to recognize their quality. Even if they get reasonably good press in the indie circle, I'd count Conspiracy of Shadows, Cold City, Dead Inside, Don't Rest your Head, Don't Walk in Winter Wood, Full Light, Full Steam, Mythic Russia, Truth & Justice, The Zorcerer of Zo and many more as under-appreciated.

    And among "mainstream" games:

    Aeon Trinity worked well and had a nicely flavoured setting. I found it much more interesting than 99% of the other materials produced by White Wolf, but it never caught on.
    Adventure!, in the same 'Verse, also got a raw deal. Although there are many good pulp games out there I love to play, it's still my favourite. The fact that it was a self-contained book only added to its value, in my opinion.
    HoL: for some reason, people don't actually believe this is a playable game. It's entirely playable, and great fun too.
    Low Life also gets no respect -- that comes with the territory when you make a game about post-apocalyptic sentient filth -- but it's probably the best implementation of Savage Worlds on the market. It had neat rules tweaks, and the adventures were way more interesting and supportive of player creativity than in other SW products.
    Masterbook, the TORG-derived "universal" system published by West End Games, lent itself well to so many genres and did such a nice job of promoting player cooperation, suspense, and action that wasn't just attack-or-defend.
    X-Crawl, a hilarious take on dungeon-crawling as an Xtreme sporting event, and Underground, a cynical rendering of super-heroes, never received the appreciation their satiric writing deserved.

    Posted By: AndyCan you provide a few examples of what you think is really striking about it?
    \nThe whole game is made up of two things:

    1 - resource management
    2 - seeking out favorable circumstances to make your resources go farther

    The most interesting thing is that learning - and using and finessing - the system is not done by addition and subtraction, or even by comparing two numbers like most diceless systems out there. It's done with a tactile element. You are physically moving counters around on your character sheet, adding them from this pool and throwing them out to that pool. That is a method of learning that has great power particularly for my group.

    Furthermore, it limits GM fiat "in the moment", and shifts it all to the preparation section, which keeps things fresh for the GM and makes decisions easy to make on the fly. You just look at your resources and move them to the right area.

    The system lends itself to minor improvisations of the sort that make comic book style (or action-cinematic style for that matter) roleplaying fun without sacrificing the basic engine.

    In addition, it's highly extensible. I added a "drama pool" mechanic that would simulate the comic book/action movie/age-old cliche of bad guys who are awesome in act 1 while the good guys bumble around more or less switching competency levels by act 3. It worked without a hitch. I've also done a stealth subgame with it (similar to combat) for a Thief: The Dark Project fantasy adaptation. For that same adaptation I simply changed the "ranges" on attributes from hugely-superhuman to a tiny-bit-superhuman and on abilities from comic-book-broad to technothriller-broad. The system didn't even hiccup.

    This is a system that you can easily change to make it yours. (You know, like you do with every other system even if you don't think you do.) The central resource allocation system is very, very robust.

      \n Since "Indie" games are virtually unknown beyond a relatively small section of the game playing public they're probably all under-appreciated, so I'll opt for a venerable system enjoying a quiet renaissance (too quiet, perhaps):

      Tunnels & Trolls.

      It's always had that "second RPG" tag and is frequently dismissed on the basis of the spell list having jokey names, to the extent that the edition put out by Corgi had the names changed. Look a little deeper. Yes, it's a simple fantasy game with no setting and some mechanical issues at higher levels (oh yes, it has levels), but the Saving Roll mechanic is a thing of beauty, a universal resolution system well ahead of its time allowing a surprising subtlety of play without having to fudge things outside of the rules. Combat incorporates "critical hits" right into the attack rolls, no extra tables or calculations, because you're comparing totals to establish who won the round and how much damage they inflicted; on top of that, damage automatically reduces the effectiveness of a monster in subsequent rounds.

      If it sounds old-fashioned and samey then that's because it has been around with relatively minor changes for over thirty years and plenty of games have tried to occupy the same ground. Greg Costikyan once mentioned it as the game he'd pick to run fantasy, commenting that it is "dumb but adequate to [his] needs." I don't think it's dumb, really, maybe a little too simple in places and too constrained by the standards of the time to look innovative these days, yet of all the "traditional" RPGs this is the one that lets me get on with playing an interesting character and exploring exciting and challenging role-playing with the game supporting me but not getting in my way.

      \n I have to second this - I had been avoiding the thread, because T & T was going to be my suggestion and it felt almost criminal to suggest.\n
      Posted By: BigJackBrassSince "Indie" games are virtually unknown beyond a relatively small section of the game playing public they're probably all under-appreciated, so I'll opt for a venerable system enjoying a quiet renaissance (too quiet, perhaps):

      Tunnels & Trolls.

      It's always had that "second RPG" tag and is frequently dismissed on the basis of the spell list having jokey names, to the extent that the edition put out by Corgi had the names changed. Look a little deeper. Yes, it's a simple fantasy game with no setting and some mechanical issues at higher levels (oh yes, it has levels), but the Saving Roll mechanic is a thing of beauty, a universal resolution system well ahead of its time allowing a surprising subtlety of play without having to fudge things outside of the rules. Combat incorporates "critical hits" right into the attack rolls, no extra tables or calculations, because you're comparing totals to establish who won the round and how much damage they inflicted; on top of that, damage automatically reduces the effectiveness of a monster in subsequent rounds.

      If it sounds old-fashioned and samey then that's because it has been around with relatively minor changes for over thirty years and plenty of games have tried to occupy the same ground. Greg Costikyan once mentioned it as the game he'd pick to run fantasy, commenting that it is "dumb but adequate to [his] needs." I don't think it's dumb, really, maybe a little too simple in places and too constrained by the standards of the time to look innovative these days, yet of all the "traditional" RPGs this is the one that lets me get on with playing an interestingcharacterand exploring exciting and challenging role-playing with the game supporting me but not getting in my way.<//img>
