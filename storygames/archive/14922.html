\n I spent several hours coming up with a big list of GM-less RPGs and story games. Right now I am not drawing any distinctions between GM-less and GM-full, one protagonist vs. many GMs, rotating GM, etc. I might also have missed out some games. Please let me know if you know of any game not in the list.\xa0I am also not organizing the games by genre or mechanics. If you have opinions about taxonomies and organization, please let me know and I will update the page on my blog.

Forgive me but I couldn't bother with all the links :-)

    \n In Panty Explosion Perfect it is suggested that it could be played GM-less.

    The Tulip Academy\u2019s Society for Dangerous Gentlemen is also GM-less (rotating scene setting privileges).

    Also, the old GDW Sci-Fi game Traveller had a suggested GM-less play style, even if no detailed advice on how to play that way was given.\n
    There are three basic ways to play Traveller: solitaire, scenario and campaign. Any of these may be played unsupervised (that is, without a referee; the players themselves administer the rules and manipulate the situation).
    \nThe quote is from the Classic Traveller reprint.

    \n In Bliss Stage, the group of player characters forms a resistance cell. The GM plays a central character in the cell: the authority figure, who issues orders and plans missions.

    If another character takes over that role, in any number of ways, then their player becomes the GM.

    \n@ben + @peter

    I added both L&L and Bliss Stage under the Rotating GM section. This is my rationale for this category:

    Rotating GM between different sessions. How do I define a session? It is a period of game-play that offers closure on a number of events. Therefore the GM has full control on the unfolding of those events and doesn't have to negotiate them with other players.

    I see it as a different social/mechanical issue than rotating GM within the same story arc. You could argue that in a game like Polaris or Shock, for example, the characters may never meet, therefore you also have different story arcs with different GMs and little GM vs GM conflict, but for the time being I left them in the main category.

      \n @ james

      Are you making a point about how you solve conflict or about who provides adversity, or is it about the source of scene progression?
      Do these two always move together or is it useful to think about them as different dimensions?

      Let's expand on these concepts...

      I can see systems where The Game provides the scenes, either via a fixed scene/act structure (eg Last Train) or via some semi-random mechanism (eg The Quiet Year). (From the point of view of players it is the same, as it is just a sequence of scenes outside their control)

      Then there are systems where the players have to make up scenes (eg Polaris) and hybrid systems... where you make up scenes within some Game-determined context, flavored by a progression of drama, an apocalypse countdown clock or something like that (eg Geiger Counter to some degree, but there must be more fitting examples)

      Then there is adversity.. and I like to think about it along these lines:
      - between players: (PvP)
      - between individual players and The Game: (P,P)vG or... PvG,PvG
      - between allied players and The Game: (P+P)vG
      - between competing players and against The Game: (PvP)vG

      Sorry for the small adversity algebra ;-)

      \n I'm more interested in the aspects of narrative authority and the stakes players have in the game; for instance, in A Taste for Murder, every scene concludes with one PC attempting to push another PC into doing something and the whole game is structured so that winning these 'pushes' gives you more narrative authority over the story that emerges during play. Even though its is a PvP structure, that structure enables you to claim narrative authority over the story, not neccessarily the victories and defeats of an individual PC, though the two often overlap.
      \n
      \nOn the other hand, Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple, which is clearly (P+P)vG, has definite win/lose conditions which are arrived at through the individual narrative choices each player makes on their turn; the authority to narrate is never up for grabs, as it is in ATfM, because you always get to choose on your turn whether to have a success (which you narrate) or a failure (which the other players narrate).
      \n
      \nFrom a game design point of view then, which types of stories are best supported by the different models? ATfM, a PvP system, is all about influence, intrigue and pushing the other PCs into accepting negative results, while D:PotFT, a (P+P)vG system, is all about co-operation, team work and trying your best with good intentions, even if you sometimes fail.
      \n
      \nSo, discussion points:
      \n- Are the themes and styles of these games embedded in their systems? If not, can I have a co-operative PvP game? Or a competitive (P+P)vG game?
      \n
      \n- If I describe a game as having PvP mechanics, what will you imagine the game is like? Reversing that, if you were asked to design a GMless game that only had PvP mechanics, what sort of setting would you choose? What sort of stories would that game tell? Ditto for (P+P)vG and so on.
