    \n I fooled around with a mechanic like this a couple years ago, but couldn't get it to do what I wanted without needlessly complicating things. I think it may be one of those things that's perfect in its simplicity already.

    What would you want different die sizes to do, mechanically speaking? I was going for creating dice that could only produce a subrange of the whole result range, and then reversing the polarity of the danger/success scales, such that low dice could give you success or danger, and high dice gave failure/safety. So d4s are great, because they almost always generate success, but they also almost always generate danger. And d8s are great, because they don't usually give danger, but they rarely succeed either. But it doesn't diverge enough without a lot of fiddly.

    \n I've been playing around with a dice mechanic that allows players to use d4, d6, d8, d12, or d20 for a given effort; the highest roll gives you narration rights, but you have to roll a prime number for "success" -- so your chance of winning narration rights is inversely related to your chance of success, since:

    d4: 1, 2, and 3 succeed (75%).
    d6: 1, 2, 3, and 5 succeed (67%)
    d8: 1, 2, 3, 5, and 7 succeed (62%)
    d12: 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, and 11 succeed (50%)
    d20: 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, and 19 succeed (45%).

    \n As you play with it. Give some thought to making LOW numbers (and hense smaller dice) better. It gives an entirely different feel to success and failure.
    \n
    \nWhen high numbers are better, then weak dice can never be as good as strong dice, but strong dice can still suck.
    \n
    \nWhen low numbers are better, then weak dice can sometimes be just as good as strong dice, and strong dice will never totally suck.
    \n
    \nDepending on what you're going for the latter method may feel better in play.
