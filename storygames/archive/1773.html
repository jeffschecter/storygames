    I wouldn't discount Universalis so quickly. Though there aren't special rules for "PCs", to say that players "don't play as characters" isn't exactly true. Players still "play as characters", they just have the option of sharing characters or playing multiple characters. (Universalis may sound like a weird hippie game, but really, in practice it feels like any old RPG.) Whether or not control of individual characters is shared between players can be decided by the group. You can leave this decision informal, or you can formalize it through an official rules-tweak (there are official rules for modding rules).

    BtI, StM, and the Roach are all short games, playable in 1-3 or so sessions. Is that the type of thing you're looking for?

    1001 Nights is a little tricky in the PC regard. Technically, yes, you play PCs. However, the game is about characters telling each other stories, where you role-play the stories. So the bulk of play is independent stories involving similar characters (within each individual story, you get cast in a role that is modeled after your "PC").

    Out of all the games mentioned, Polaris is the most like "DnD GM-less". Ben (or anyone), have you tried playing Polaris 2-player?

    What Tim said about Universalis. Universalis has rules for formally altering the rules (called Gimmicks), and there are examples of "PC Gimmicks" on the website. (http://www.ramshead.indie-rpgs.com/)

    Capes works out of the box, and would be good especially if you're looking to play supers. Though it can do other things as well, I suspect. And Capes might seem a little more traditional.

    But both come at this sort of game from the "author" angle. That is, even when playing your own character, you're thinking about their actions in terms of what effect it has on the story. If you want something a lot more traditional, then take a look at Rune: http://www.atlas-games.com/rune/

    In Rune you rotate being the GM, creating a hack n' slash gauntlet for the PCs to run.

    A "Proto-GMless" RPG was the Ergo project by Ian Millington. I can't find a working link to his stuff anymore, but here's an interview with him: http://ptgptb.org/0020/coop.html

    Hey Ronaldo, why we're here, let me ask a question:

    There are two styles of "GM-less" play. There's play where we just rotate who's the GM, so we take turns being the player and the GM. And then there's play where we both play and GM at the same time. What style were you looking for, or does it matter?

    A game like Polaris and Rune (and potentially StM) rotate who's the GM. In Universalis and Capes you both play and GM at the same time.

    I ask because I'm wondering if you're looking for party-style play, where your characters are together all the time. It's hard to get party-style play if you play a rotating-GM game, at least it would be with only 2 players. So it's something to consider.

  • Josh Roby
    November 2006
    Ronaldo, Full Light, Full Steam has rules for GMless and Troupe Play games, which work disturbingly well. On the surface, it is about as bog-standard of a roleplaying game as you can get: everybody has a character and there's an "adventure" to do. The adventure is built using some pretty well-defined rules, so a group can do it together as easily as the GM can do it in private (which I've done myself, and it really worked well as a group activity). Then when you start up play, scenes are framed with a neutral mechanic called the spoils scrip that gets passed around the table. The game as-written is for steampunk space opera, but if you got a bee in your bonnet to play something different, it would just be a matter of picking six new attributes and listing off thirty skills.
