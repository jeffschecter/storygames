\n I'm working on a game in which the players collaborate to create a list of obstacles and challenges that we (may) play throughout the session, and then pull them randomly from a shuffled stack. Not a revolutionary idea, but a new one to me from a design and play perspective.

The characters can face multiple challenges in a single scene, and other than agreeing upon the type of challenges (and the nature of the challenge environment) there's nothing to stop multiple disconnected challenges from appearing in a single scene. You might get "you lose all your stuff" and "get attacked by wolves" in the "Snowy forest" scene.

Sometimes this can seem pretty cool and action-movie-escalation-esque, especially when the challenges sort of complement each other. Sometimes though, at least conceptually, I'm wondering if it is a problem if they don't do that. I haven't come up with any concrete examples from actual play, but I could see a situation in which the challenges seem impossible or improbable to coincide, especially at a particular scene or location.

Do you think this provides interesting hooks for the players to collaborate on how these seemingly disconnected events happen (especially in a group survival vs the wilderness game), or do they break your immersion if they don't immediately line up to your expectations of the genre or narrative? Do you have any experience with this type of scenario?

    \n In my experience it is more a matter of setting clear and functional rules for Player-to-Player communication, than anything else. You can then inject a few ideas in the fiction, and most pieces will naturally fall into place simply because "it makes sense".

    Once you have such structure in place you are, in my opinion, much better off by creating "obstacle templates" rather than coming up with a specific list of things.
    Look at the MC Moves from Apocalypse World.

    What I mean is, do not write down "wolves attack you" hoping to make it fit somewhere.
    Instead write down "someone is hostile" and leave it to the Players to dress it up with a specific color depending on the specific fictional situation you have at hand... maybe wolves are ok, maybe guards make more sense, maybe it is so much cooler and dramatic if the character's loved-one becomes hostile (not in an attack/kill sort of way, possibly >_< ).

    You can then define how to use them. Can the Players have them happen too? Do they automatically happen if some other rule activates? If a dice result happens? If a pool of points ends? etc..

    I use these (and other) techniques in my gmLess games.
    You might want to specifically look into TacticalOps and FateLess.

    \n What if you combine those two ideas, and have threats/challenges created by combining items from column A and column B?

    "A" includes things that are vague prompts. ("A betrayal". "An unexpected attack." "Something ever trusty and reliably fails the group.")

    "B" includes specific fictional things. ("A wolf pack." "A preternatural hailstorm." "Doctor Fenninglass, expert on infections.")

    To create a challenge, you draw at random from one pile, then pick an appropriate item from the other list.

    e.g. Maybe you choose to draw from "A", and you get "A betrayal". The "B" pile shows three or four items, face up. ("A wolf pack." "A preternatural hailstorm." "Doctor Fenninglass, expert on infections.")

    You can now choose to have a betrayal which involves wolves... you're not sure how that goes, so you choose one of the other two, perhaps "Doctor Fenninglass", and describe how he has betrayed the group.

    \n On my first tries with a similar system it was easy to get wacky-gonzo stuff without really trying to. Playing that relaxed way is also fun and refreshing, though if it's not what you want, you will certainly need to add some limits through guidelines, and perhaps present those constrains as creative challenges.

    Now I use a combination of a randomizer and player input for many (tried and tested) reasons.

    Player input is great, it allows the GM to relax a lot and still get wonderful material for the session. It's easier to satisfy the players tastes and mood when they say exactly what they want. Whenever you say yes to whatever they want and build upon it, it creates trust and that simple recognition to their input makes them more eager and open to collaborate, increasing the game Flow. Whenever a player creates something and it's taken and recognized bu other players, they get emotionally invested on it a bit, which is key to motivate them later to interact with the fiction in a non-destructive way.

    Have players help you create a setting and you will have them in the same page as you; you won't have to shove them tons of info at the start of the game nor explain everything again and again every now and then. You won't have to drag them and convince them to hate or love a place or a character. And they will probably have better ideas that you could have ever think of.

    You can always keep the surprise and wonder of exploration. For starters, who said all they have created is the actual truth? I often state at the world creation stage that whatever they are making is what their characters know and may or may not be accurate. Also, it's best to have the players create fragmented bits of info so you can later fill the gaps through the game.

    The randomizer is there to help fill those gaps, keep some sort of coherence using a limited list of entries as said above and help players (and GMs) having a blank page syndrome to get some inspiration, to keep the game from stalling (something very important on a game where real-time player input is needed.) And also, to avoid Czege's principle.

    About you particular system, I wouldn't recommend randomizing the places, at least not by writing them in the cards and shuffling those into the deck. For me it works best to have the players mark those locations on a map drawn by them. Then I roll 2d6 over that map and use the places they fell as the start and ending of the current adventure or arc.

    The values of the dice mark how dangerous is the location, so that itself can tell what the story is about. If they are going from one dangerous place to a safe one, they are escaping some threat. It it's the opposite, they are going to confront the main threat. If both are dangerous, they are meant to escape through the worst place possible while in pursue. If both values are low they will find the danger on the road, perhaps while escorting someone or delivering goods/a message. You can interpret the rolls differently according to whatever think the players, too.

    Also, you can formalize a simple technique like "That doesn't make sense" as a rule, so players can veto a particular combination and try again a new one without feeling like they are breaking the game.
