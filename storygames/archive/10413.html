\n Do you enjoy doing hard, complex, goal-oriented work when you play? As in: Trying to grok Burning Empires, going through complex 4ed encounters "by the book", etc?
\n
\nI find this fun, sometimes; it's not necessary for my role-playing, but it does add an optional extra dimension of fun. Just like drinking wine, or pushing personal buttons, or live-action sequences can add an extra dimension. Nothing fits every game, of course.
\n
\nHow about you?

    \n I do not enjoy trying to grok a game. I gave away my copy of Burning Wheel because I couldn't get through the book. Similarily, I don't think I'd like trying to run a D&D encounter by the book (I've never tried).

    I do, however, enjoy trying to master a game. Not master the rules, but master the skills needed to run it successfully. The first time I ran Don't Rest Your Head, it sucked balls. That's because I couldn't handle it. I tried it again, and I started to work out how it was supposed to be run. That sort of work is fun to me. I expect I'd have fun trying to master Nobilis and PTA the same way, even though the rules are easy.

    So, to me:
    Struggling to understand how a game works: unfun
    Struggling to understand how to make a game work: fun

    I'm with Logos there - it takes a little mental energy to play and set up 4e, but it feels like a challenging game.

    \n

    On the other hand, levelling up a character, or for that matter creating one, can definitely feel like work, particularly if you're like me and optimization is a side benefit rather than a goal. If you are in it for the optimization, it feels a lot more fun.

    \n

    So what I guess I'm saying is, it's hard for me to use the word "work" to describe this activity, because in my own usage, the primary purpose of work isn't enjoyment. I enjoy my job sometimes, but I do it to pay the bills. So it feels like work levelling up, but not like work optimizing, because in one case the activity is a barrier to fun and in the other case the activity is an end in itself.
