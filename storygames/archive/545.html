Bad-Good-Bad value curve ... times two

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

Okay! First, the Bad-Good-Bad value curve ... also known as "the two-edged sword." This is any resource where if you have none you're in bad shape ... and if you have too much you're also in bad shape. But if you have a value that's in a certain range, you're good to go.

I like this. Those who have played Capes and used Debt know how much I like this, and probably have some notion of why. Most of all, I like how it takes the sting out of "punishment" ... you punish a player for losing a conflict by giving the character more Debt. 'course, Debt is a resource they can use, but now they've got too much.

But for Misery Bubblegum I want two of these things operating together. For instance, in a past version of the game I had Passion dice and Misery dice. The number of Misery Dice you had on hand was the difficulty you had to beat (in total) whenever you rolled Passion dice, and likewise the number of Passion dice you had on-hand was the difficulty you had to beat whenever you rolled Misery dice.

This worked ... okay ... You wanted some Misery and some Passion, but not too much of either. Sometimes you wanted to indulge hugely in one or the other, but that constrained your choices.

I really don't know what I want just yet. I have a feeling that I'll know it when I see it. Can I impose upon people to jump on board for a brain-storming session? I sure hope so. It might help me an awful lot.

    Tony.

    Here's a mechanic.

    Let's say that you're rolling a pool of dice, and you want to roll high.

    What you do is you take a number of dice equal to your attribute and sum the five lowest dice. Stats start at 1. If you are rolling less than five dice, you just sum what you have.

    So, clearly, your value peaks at 5. What I like about this is the decline afterwards is *slow* I was thinking about tying it to a mechanic where, say, "After each conflict, you can raise the attribute you used or drop it back to 1." Which clearly sucks either way. Because 1 die is worse than any large number, but large numbers are continually worse.
