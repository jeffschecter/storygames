    \n I think it's pretty easy in RPGs. I mean, you literally just say that your good and that you're doing good things and it's done.

    You skip most of the hard, boring, hurtful, psychic and social bullshit that causes people to do bad things or at least not do the right things.

    Like, if your a innocent and tireless medic, you just do that. You don't succumb to temptation, you don't worry about you're hands or back failing, or getting a bloodborn pathogen. It's much like combat in RPGs. Really combat is confusing and scary - like doing the right thing. But your character has a clear meta-perspective and intellectual distance.

    \n Eh, you're treading on shaky ground when you start using adjectives such as "better" or "more enlightened". Better how? Despite people trying to explain how all religions are essentially the same, the value of traits and behaviours varies between religions and cultures. You can actually find a codified example of this in Pendragon: Christian knights value Chastity, Pagan knights value... whatever the opposite of that is called in the system.

    To play someone who is more X than you, I guess that you have to do some thinking before playing. If they value X, what behaviours does that cause? Why do they value it? How far are they willing to go for X? (You have to think of this beforehand because otherwise in play you might slide to your "default" value of X). Also: why do you want to play someone who values X?

    [Edit: also, I guess that when playing, you need to make sure to roleplay or describe the internal conflict of the character, and how they decide to support the values they care about despite the cost, otherwise they'd just be paperthin characters.]

    For example: we're playing in a pseudo-Viking setting, where hospitality is of high value. I'm playing a local lord and by gods, I will lead my men and women by example. That means being an example of hospitality (among other things).

    What behaviours does that cause? Hospitality means I will greet anyone who arrives at my house. I will postpone taking care of current affairs (meals, talks, etc) to greet and invite the guest, as well as give orders to the servants to see to their needs. I will make the guest's safety my responsibility and my own honour. And finally, if the guest is of high enough status, if they say they like something of mine, I might consider immediately gifting it to them.

    Why do I value hospitality? Well, hospitality draws people to you and I want to be the leader of a great clan. If I'm hospitable, I'm encouraging people around me to believe we have enough of our own so that we can even share. (Good PR!) And finally, my life was saved one wintry night when I almost froze to death but was rescued by a stranger, who made me swear I would repay his deed in kind - to others. He only had one eye and I'm secretly convinced that was Odin.

    How far am I willing to go in order to be hospitable? I will take in anyone, regardless of their status (class). Now, I imagine the looks of my peers when I speak to an escaped thrall that arrived on a cold night. I will share my last food with them. Now, I imagine the looks on the faces of my family as I generously share a bag of grain with my men - but they know that it means they will eat gruel for months to come. I will insist that conflicts cease among the guests in my house. Now, I imagine being manipulated into hosting a meeting between two sworn enemies - and both insisting that I will become THEIR enemy if I do not help them eliminate the other one.

    Now I feel better prepared to play someone who's more hospitable than me

    Posted By: Nathan H.How do you play someone more enlightened then yourself?
    \nDon't talk much (easy for me).

    Observe what other characters say and do (fairly easy for me).

    When I do speak or act, do so from a position trying to maximize compassion and try to create the least harm for all involved (hard for me).

    When lost for the above, speak or act in a way that will solve problems where the most, if not all, hurt and harm will land on my character. (Easy or difficult, depending on the circumstances.)

    Break or limit the above rules only in accordance of code of conduct that the character might follow, whether its a code expressly created by some organization or community, something that's considered important to the culture (and the character sees as enlightened), or a personal code of honor that is relatively unique to the character. (Usually really hard, depending on how natural that code might feel.)

    OR I intentionally want the character to be acting less enlightened, perhaps to show how much the current situation is stressful or emotion-full or because the character might be abandoning those ideals or breaking until the pressure of holding to them as a major personal crisis or approaching change. (Usually the most fun part, so it's hard to do with restraint.)

    Being enlightened is not a mystery, and the things enlightened people do are not complex. We've got tons of religious and spiritual writings that all say essentially the same thing when you clear away the oppression, politics, and dogma that's been added to control people. It can be summed up as: Treat everyone else with the utmost respect, compassion and understanding.

    Case in point, following that general rule would still have you fit right in as 3Jane's example of an enlightened Viking if you knew nothing about Viking culture.
