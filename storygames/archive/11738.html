\nHe's the biggest, most totally ripped barbarian to ever exist and he has a ten-foot-long sword which he uses to decapitate fools. \u2014 Badass of the Week

I'm poor, so I don't get to play the latest games. I do, however, get to fool around with free demos all I want, and this is how I managed to get my hands on at least 90 minutes' worth of Darksiders. It's a tease, for sure, but it also got me thinking about characters with incredible abilities. The ultimate in power fantasy.

I was a good consumer and I bought the Epic Level Handbook back in \u2014 what was it? \u2014 2002. I looked through the book and checked out the pretty pictures and I had a vague idea of how it would all work but I didn't really know what it'd be like to wrestle with characters possessed of that much power. I still don't.

Well, maybe I do, thanks to Darksiders. The sheer strength and size and presence of the character gives the first clues. When War falls from a decent height he slams into the ground so heavily that he leaves a crater. He has the aforementioned ten-foot sword and can use it to hack and slay with ruthless abandon. Nothing stands in his way. And don't get me started on his massive scythe.

There are a couple of games I can think of that could accommodate this level of play. One is D&D, of course, and the other is Wushu. Want to kick all kinds of demon ass? They're happy to provide an outlet.

But\u2026.

Surely kicking ass is a big part of playing such a tremendously powerful character, but are there other things to do, too? I wonder because I've never actually played a character with the sheer physicality of War. I imagine it would take quite a while for the novelty of being able to lay waste to everything in sight would wear off and then\u2026 what? It's hard to imagine War from Darksiders settling down to rule a kingdom or what have you. So are we talking about the sort of game where the ass-kicking goes on forever until finally its potential is exhausted and the character is put aside? Has anyone here ever taken things that far?

    \n This is sort of why I've been really eager to play Exalted lately.

    I believe the basic idea being that you start at the world shakingly badass phase and build from there. However, Exalted doesn't really limit you to being a physical badass. I'm pretty sure there's a charm to sell someone their own grandmother if you want to play that sort of guy.

    Anyway, my point is that looking at Exalted stuff sort of helped me wrap my head around that style of game and I don't think it's actually that much different from any other game where the players have a reasonable amount of control over the story. The setting is bigger and more epic, but I don't think what you're actually doing needs to change that much.

    You're just a fantasy hero with the fate of nations, or even worlds, in your hands, instead of villages and towns.

    Oh, it just occurred to me that the setting material in Exalted may be part of what makes that work. I played an epic level D&D 3E game back in 200x and I think it failed because we had scaled up characters but not a scaled up setting to match. The WORLD needs to be epic enough to handle that shit or everything turns into a mook fight. So War is cool when he's fighting demons and dragons with a city as collateral damage, but it wouldn't be as cool if he was fighting the US Marines.

    It's like Godzilla. It's fun when he's smashing tanks and shit, but eventually another monster needs to show up.

    \n From playing Nobilis, I've learned one key rule with regards to this kind of play:

    When you can utterly destroy -- in fact, unmake -- lesser creatures, longevity in play comes almost entirely from conflict with your equals. (Almost entirely - a mortal love interest cannot simply be slain when you want to win an argument. Usually.)

    Which isn't to say that laying waste in the mortal realm isn't a fun diversion for a little bit, but... yeah, it's when you encounter someone who can match you that the player's eyes shift from joyful destructive glee to laser-like focus.

    \n So let me introduce you to Charnel Gods, Sam.

    Here's how this works. There's this world, Naur Tier. It's your basic pulp fantasy world, loosely based on Earth. The details are up to you. What matters is this: the PCs are the bearers of the Fell Weapons, the angry creations of the Old Gods, forged as a last-ditch attempt to defeat the Nameless Ones and hold the Black Gulf at bay. This attempt succeeded, but the Old Gods did not survive it. Now it's just Man and the Fell Weapons, locked in an eternal cycle of battle and destruction.

    Cycle, because Charnel Gods is a Sorcerer sourcebook. Eventually, one of the PCs will reach Humanity 0. That PC's player then becomes the Harbinger, who gets to describe the way the Epoch ends, and what the apocalypse looks like. They can't kill PCs or significant NPCs, and they have to be able to relate all the devastation they cause to a Humanity-losing action their PC took sometime during the game. It could take a couple of sessions to play out the end of the world, because everyone needs to resolve their loose ends. But the world is going to end, because that's what happens when you lose the remainder of your humanity and your Fell Weapon becomes more important to you than your soul.

    \n I think tragedy follows from extreme badassery. Most of the (good) things I've been reading/watching involving extreme levels of badass recently have either ended up in the deeply tragic or deeply horrific end of things. Laying waste to all that opposes you just ... doesn't seem to end well. You either destroy everything you love, or have someone else do that and end up destroying the world. You can't just keep going about kicking ass - the more power you throw around the more horrible everything becomes.
    \n
    \nI don't know any game that does this (though I wish I did) - in terms of other media I'm thinking Saikano, Narutaru, Elfen Lied (the manga moreso), and X-Men's Dark Phoenix Saga.

    \n Ryan will do this better, but lemme give you the rundown on Mythender.

    You play a Mythender, a powerful dude who hunts down FUCKING MYTHS and KILLS THEM. With extreme prejudice, because they'll fuck up your world if you don't. In one of the games I played, we hunted J\xf6rmungandr, the snake that wraps the world. You roll vast handfuls of dice and bring all of your might to bear on your enemy. The dice pools have names like "thunder" and "lightning" and you have kick-ass special abilities that grow over time. But as your abilities grow, you become -- wait for it -- more MYTHIC. Eventually, you turn into the thing you hate, and your friends have to hunt YOU down AND KILL YOU. With extreme prejudice.

    This game was a bunch of scribbles in Ryan's notebook last year, and STILL WAS MINDBLOWINGLY AWESOME.
