    \n There is the old Cortex base book, but that is now kind of outdated, what with the new Cortex+ based games.
    But yeah, the three main ones, Smallville, Marvel Heroic, and Leverage (and they have a new one coming out soon... Dragon Brigade, I believe?) use the same basic mechanics - roll a bunch of dice based on who and what you are, sometimes based on how well you work with others or what you believe it, add a few more dice from special abilities or special effects, take the highest two, and total them vs. an opposing roll, either an adversary or a general trouble/doom/etc. pool that represents the tension of the moment.
    Otherwise, they tend to differ quite a bit after that, as each tries to capture the feel of their particular genre - heists and criminal mastermindyness, superpowered angsty teenagers with complicated lives, and slam-bang superhuman action.

    My personal preference is Marvel Heroic. I've been playing a Smallville-Gundam hack with Joel Shempert, Mike Sugarbaker, and Mike Rude lately, and while Smallville really hits that DRAMA feel, Marvel Heroic really has that action impact that I crave with larger-than-life heroics.

    ...sadly, have yet to play Leverage, even though I'm a crazy fan for the show and have all three books.

    \n Reason you don't have Watchtower Report in print is because we never actually printed it, Jason! Not enough advance orders, probably. It's a steady seller in PDF though.

    Cortex Plus games are as Jason says all designed from the ground up to capture the property or license they're designed for. They use common language and a common baseline mechanical function, but they're not the sort of games where you can easily take one character from one game and play him in another. That's not the point of the design.
