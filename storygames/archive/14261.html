\n Over in the Star Trek thread, Ryan Chaddock asked - "Fictional positioning is a vague concept for me to parse. No offense intended, I don't see how it's not included in any and all systems. Isn't that the point?"

The best discussion of the term so far is probably here.

So, I'd say, yes, it's included in any and all RPG systems, but to varying degree. Freeform would be almost entirely fictional positioning; Castle Ravenloft (the boardgame) is almost none; and Apocalypse World relies much more heavily on fictional positioning than D&D 3 and 4e.

The way I see it, the tactical combat of TFT and D&D 3 and 4 allow for little fictional positioning because there's this near 1:1 correspondence between the mechanics and the fiction. In these games, if you say, "I jump off the rafters and use the weight of my body to drive my spear in the cyclop's eye," the GM shrugs, consults the rules if they don't have it memorized, and says something like, "Okay, you get +2 because of your height advantage. Roll your attack and then move your mini one square and take 1d6 falling damage." And then you roll your attack and do average damage. "Is he blind?" you ask. The GM says, "No, nothing in the rules about targeting his eye. Guess you just hit him on the cheek."
Compared to freeform or old-school, where the GM could say something like, "Awesome, you drive the spear through its eye into his brain and hang there - then the cyclops topples and lands on top of you." (a yes, but) or "Hmm, take a -4 penalty for aiming at the eye, but if you make it he'll take double damage and be blind."

For me, with TFT and D&D3 and 4, the tactical combat is so detailed that almost anything I want to express in fiction has a mechanical rule. "I run up and flank the goblin" is not inert narration. So I like it! For what it is.

What if you used the D&D4 skill system to handle combat, though? Then "I flank the goblin" would be the same as "I hit the goblin" and "I trip the goblin" - make your roll and add 1 to your progress.

    \n Yeah, for it to be "fictional positioning" and not just colorful description, it has to actually matter: it has to be describing a choice (since you could be describing something else instead!) that changes the direction in which the narrative was going or, at least, future possibilities.

    Getting a +1 modifier or whatever is one way it could matter, sure, but if you can just do that whenever you want -- with the GM rewarding you for describing placing your character in an advantage -- doesn't that incentivize "spamming" that interaction and doing it every single time? Walking into every bar with your gun at the ready and your back to the wall gets really dumb.

    But fictional positioning can also put yourself in position to do something else. If you've established that the people in this town like you, maybe one of them will offer you a place to hide when your enemies come looking. Honestly, I find that kind of positioning much more interesting.

    \n@Cy: Fictional positioning means that you use the fiction to inform the mechanics.
    D&D3e: If I get knocked prone by the Watcher in the Water in one round, I could fire my crossbow with a bonus on the next round. If I don't have a crossbow, I couldn't attack it at all, 'cause I'm too far away.
    If I'm facing off against some foes who don't speak at all, then anything my character says is going to be just so much colorful dialogue. If what I do in the fiction doesn't engage or inform the mechanics in any way, then it doesn't help to establish where I am/what's possible with the mechanics, and in turn it limits what the mechanics can do to change what I'm up to in the fiction. If I could, potentially, reason with the goblins, then what I say could very well matter, and quite a bit!
    I think the term literally comes from tactical combat where distances and cover and stuff matter - everything that happens in the plot (even stuff like "I charge across the field, dodging arrows, then duck behind the wagon!") is Fiction. Whenever we use an element of Fiction to determine advantage, disadvantage, triggering, etc., we have established Position.
    AW's Moves are 100% about fictional positioning. You can't just say "I seduce him. I roll +hot"- that doesn't trigger a Move. On the other hand, "I seduce him so he'll tell me where the Water Chip is", is totally fine - it firmly establishes that you are a) seducing or manipulating in order to b) get someone to give you something they wouldn't just hand over. Seduce Or Manipulate demands that you have both a) and b) before it can be triggered.
    It boils down to how we translate what we narrate in the game to what "counts", what everyone has to acknowledge as real and important. Does that help?

    \n Looking at Cy's post I'm thinking my post may have muddied things more than clarified them, since I spent so much time talking about tactical combat.

    I'm not sure fictional positioning always necessarily informs the mechanics. Like with Jonathan's example. "The people in this town like me" might have been arbitrary backstory you made about your character. And then the enemies come looking, so you say "I hide at a friend's house." And the GM might say, "Sure, he takes you in and opens a trap door so you can hide under the floorboards." Without rolling a die or consulting a rule.


    Here's why fictional positioning is so cool to me - what I came to realize sometime in the last year. I used to believe, basically, "If you don't have a rule about it it doesn't matter." Like, you can say you have a helmet in 3e, but there's no mechanic for it. So I then say, "Who cares if I have a helmet or not? It's irrelevant. It's just color." And that means that a very comprehensive ruleset like Advanced Melee or D&D3e is a good thing (even if they don't have rules for helmets) because it's more details for the fiction.

    But now I believe that if I say "I have a helmet" it might matter, whether it's because I use it to take water from a stream to a fallen comrade or because the GM is specifically about to narrate some damage to my head and then doesn't because I have a helmet. Even if the chance of something like that coming up is one in a thousand. Just the fact that it might matter means I don't feel I've wasted my breath. And then we don't need such a comprehensive ruleset, because the play's in the fictional positioning.

    Though it can lead to its own problems, like arguments about what we did or did not establish in the fiction, or arguments about what does or does not make sense given what we've established. "No, he's not going to hide you." "What? But they like me in this town." "Yeah, but not enough to hide you from imperial soldiers - they don't need that kind of heat."

    \n cy, I think those examples are fictional positioning taken to a strange extreme. It's a combination of only fictional positioning (no rules step in to say "yeah, that just happens, move on") and a GM who's the sole arbiter of fiction.

    Fictional positioning applies just as much if the rules step in and say that one alertness check applies for noticing traps in an entire area (like BW's Let it Ride) or if one fictional action covers a discrete area, or if one fictional action allows the player to ask "are there traps here" a certain number of times. The important bit isn't that we need to establish how tall the buildings are, its that being on top of a building means you're actually on top of a building.

    Basically, I wouldn't allow the Delve style of fictional positioning turn you off from the AW style of fictional positioning.

    \n Hmm. Tricky topic! So, for measuring what a character can do, the Forge glossary distinguishes between Effectiveness, Resource, and Positioning.

    Let's say I want favors from the nobility in my Burning Wheel game. Right now, my character's in the position of not knowing any nobles. So, I make a Circles test to find a friendly noble. I succeed. The test established stakes for my roll, and the success got me those stakes. I now know a friendly noble! I am now in much better position to get the favors I ultimately want.

    So, what do we call that? Effectiveness, Resource, or Positioning?

    When Positioning is completely measured mechanically ("I have +1 to get a favor, but only because I paid for the Noble of +1 to Get a Favor"), does it become Effectiveness?

    \n The way I see it this is all about those clouds-and-dice diagrams Vincent used to do. Fictional positioning is rightward-pointing arrows. Sometimes they go all the way to dice, but sometimes they only need to go to the smiley faces, because the moments of judgement are enough (or we don't even actually have rules for it anyway).

    Archipelago, as a system, has a lot of leftward-pointing arrows. I don't think it has a single rightward-pointing arrow as far as the system is written (maybe with fates, I don't remember). However, in play, it mostly consists of rightward-pointing arrows. The system kicks in when that's not sufficient and lets the players edit the fiction through that.

    Cy is right, it's not necessarily about fiction impacting mechanics because freeform doesn't have mechanics and is all fictional positioning. It's about fiction having a role, it matters. If you take away rightward-pointing arrows you are left with only dice-to-dice arrows which means you've got a boardgame or leftward-pointing arrows without feedback, which means you've got a fiction...crafting game? Something like an exquisite corpse for example.

    [[[So all RPGs (or story games) have fictional positioning, on a big scale, if nothing else.
    -"You're on a boat."
    -"I go buy fresh bread from the baker."
    -"Huh?"

    I think where it varies the most is fictional positioning "at the micro". For example in combat D&D4 has almost zero fictional positioning at the micro by default, because all positioning is governed by the battlemat and various powers, it's all leftward-pointing arrows. But there's almost certainly still some fictional positioning in there. If I'm facing a pair of kobolds and the evil guy who killed my hero's mentor, I'm probably going after the evil guy, right? Because it's what I (fictionally) care about, it's how I'm positioned in the fiction.]]]

    Fiction impacting the mechanics (cloud-to-dice) is just the easiest way to think about it.
    "If your character has the high ground, get a +2 to your roll." - me jumping on the table and now being higher than the orc is all fictional, but I got a +2, so it matters.
    But it can be non mechanical (cloud-to-smiley-faces-to-cloud).
    "I look through the keyhole." "You see the queen and her lover!" "Now I have something to blackmail her with." All fictional, but it matters, because now the story is in another place, direction, you've got a new resource, a different position, relationships have changed. Very structuralist actually.

    \nAnd Ron expanded:\n
    A key feature of the Currency concept is that a given game item is perfectly capable of being more than one of Effectiveness, Positioning, and/or Resources. I am emphatically not recommending that a game designer parse out every little detail on the sheet into one and only one of them.

    Some related points.

    1. Character sheets from different games aren't as immediately comparable as they might seem. "Native land: Perspicania," on one sheet might tie directly into attribute scores that are primarily about Effectiveness. And in some other game the equivalent notation might be specifically about Positioning, whether political or psychological.     2. I also want to articulate a subtler point about Currency which I think may be getting lost in this thread. Currency exchange may occur within a given character component category, i.e., within different kinds of Effectiveness; as well as among those categories, e.g., between Effectiveness and Resources.
