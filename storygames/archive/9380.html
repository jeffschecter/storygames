\n There's talk of Web 2.0 tool this and Kindle that, but I've grown very much attached to my 8gb Kingston USB flash drive. You can get one for about $20 these days.

8gb is a sweet spot (as of May 2009, anyway; next year, "2 terabytes" might be the "sweet spot") of price and functionality.

I can organize my entire RPG PDF library, and keep most or all of them on this thing, and still take up less than 1 gig.

I can use the rest of the space to store gaming-related media: For our Tenra game, I dumped episodes of the Zatoichi TV show and the J-Drama "Edo Assassins" (Hissatsu Shigotonin) to later watch on the PC to get some game background.

I use it to carry PDFs to work, where I print out character sheets and the like. I dump campaign notes, idle RPG design flailings, and cool concept art I find on the web to it.

To that end, I nominate the 4-8gb range USB flash drive as the single most useful tabletop gaming-related tech device.

What is your nomination?
What do you do with your flash drive?

-Andy

    \n OK, Andy--I defer. Not a big deal to me, anyway, the more I think about it. nd, yep, I use a mechanical pencil every time I play.
    -----
    Ideas:

    * Use USB key to pass detailed notes, clues, or general information to particular players (means everyone needs PC access). Could even password-protect each player's individual folder, so that the info dump could be unique for each, with one USB key, and meanwhile you can go on with running the session for the other players waiting to read their clues. Hell, I could see a LARP handling all kinds of stuff like that, including PC stat updates, with a cheap-ass key per player (surely they sell in bulk, now--can probably even get them with the LARP organization's logo on them, with sufficient quantities). Sure, this could also be done in a variety of online manners, but LARP (in particular) can suffer from lack of Internet access, but still have access to power for a bank of laptops.

    * Keep every character you've ever owned on it, so you're ready to play just about any game on the fly. I still have a notebook with all my old PCs in it, but it's a BIT too heavy to tote to every single con and session.

    * Online collaboration tools, in general, make it easy to schedule session, offload "downtime" work to outside of session time, and keep a central repository of game data. We've used a Yahoo or Google Group for just about every campaign in which I've played the last several years.

    * Another LARP idea, about passing info: a USB key could be any number of in-fiction information sources that actually must be found in the playing area. Glorified envelopes with notes in them, but now the clues can be multimedia. Further, you can make a "real life" puzzle out of finding the info, if you have a HUGE and obscure directory structure. Or, hell, encryption that takes time to un-encrypt (as the zombies shuffle ever closer!).
    ...And just WHO controls the access to the one in-game computer, and what do they want in return?

    * We've used Forums a LOT in LARP, for downtime RP and general chat. Works best for "real time" games where time between sessions is the same as in-fiction time between session, or else you get some odd continuity problems. The flip side is that folks who don't or can't keep up with the forums end up missing out on a LOT and are sort of flabbergasted by the things folks do once the game resumes. In-character forums are the worst for this sort of thing (out-of-synch play). But I've had a hell off a lot of fun winding folks up on them. It's also easy for a GM to play several NPCs at once, without the restriction on appearance and without having to struggle with being in more than one place at one time.

    * YouTube has also been a boon for the gaming and in particular LARP world. MANY LARPs now run promotional videaos, or actually document a game in progress (often OOC, bu some play with the idea that there is an in-game journalist taking the videos). Makes a good reference for GMs (maybe not for players--depends on their in-fiction access to it), and a GREAT promotional tool to attract new players. The various Flickers and PhotoBuckets of the net offer similar, though less visceral, opportunities.
    -----
    More as I think of them tonight. Really, the opportunities are boundless, both for management functions and for in-game use.

    I'll toss out several tech things I use... In order of my must have nominations:

    1. WIKI - used for campaign bible, all-purpose everything for the campaign goes here including PC doing/posting their adds to the campaign. Most now can also upload files - including excel character sheets, word docs, PDFs, sound files/songs, images, etc... Most are FREE. I prefer Google Sites, many folk apparently use Obsidian Portal (Google Sites is much better though, even though it's not RPG specific).

    2. YAHOO GROUP - or a Google Group or other free web group forum. I make a new one for each campaign, we post weekly between-game thoughts and questions and most importantly talk about game schedules. Yahoo Groups (and others) have calendars, file uploads, etc...

    3. iTunes Playlist - I make a custom playlist for EVERY campaign, simple and excellent.

    4. iPod - I use my 80gig iPod to hold EVERY file I have including music. Take back and forth exactly like a memory stick, plus playing my iTune playlists from it...

    5. the Computer - in general I've made it easy to do multiple cool things during game with my computers. From checking Wikipedia or Google Images for info or pix to displaying images on my 42" TV screen to show maps or NPC pics, etc... I also have my music playing from iTunes through my sterio. Easy-peasy. I'll toss my scanner and photoshop into this bag as well. also:
    a. my laptop
    b. my TV screen
    c. my PC desktop

    6. Wipe-boards - I bought a large magnetic wipe-board that I use as a table play surface to draw maps, etc... I also made a custom Initiative magnetic wipe-board and printed custom magnets for each PCs name, then made dozens of blank magnets (laminated) to write/erase NPC names. Pop them up on the Initiative board, move them around in correct initiative order.

    7. Beads - like the life bead stone from Magic the Gathering, I've collected dozens of colors for a full box of beads - the work great for counters when minis are needed.




    lots of goodness.

    \n My little Acer AspireOne netbook is now a regular at our gaming table because:

    a) it has a pdf copy of several of my games (I usually buy print + pdf) meaning that someone else can snag the print copy while I use the screen;
    b) I load music on it, or bookmark websites I might want to show to my players, or videos or whatever;
    c) I can take it with me when I'm out and about and work on my games whenever I have a minute;
    d) built in mic and webcam means that even though the majority of the group is in Calgary, our man in Nanaimo can still participate; and
    e) it weighs less than some hardcover game books.

    That said, GoogleDocs is also one of my favourite little tech toys because I no longer have to do player handouts at the beginning of sessions and then wait while people read them. I can just say what I was going to in a google doc and send a viewer invite to everyone and they can peruse at their leisure. Also, when I'm playing, I can do all my in-character notes and backstories and assorted wankery and share it with the GM that way. Arguably I could do this with email, but I like the persistence of GD.
