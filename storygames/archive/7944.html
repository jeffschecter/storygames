\n This is about Answerer power. How much do you have? How much is too much?

You know that example Vincent uses about one person trying to take a ring from another? Well, that's all you need to know about it. You say, "I take the ring from you", and I answer, "no way!" We roll dice.

Let's say we get to the third round and I have to Answer you. I can say, if I want, "I hold on to the ring as you beat the crap out of me."

But what if I had the upper hand during the second round, and you're Answering me now, and I win. Can you say, "I grab the ring from you, and you beat the crap out of me"?

That's interesting, because you got what you wanted in return for giving me The Stick, and thus potentially paying for the ring with two die sizes.

But, now, what if we went to the third round with you Challenging, and then _I_ won? Do I get to say, "I take the ring from you and beat the crap out of you"?

That means I get what I want _and_ I can negotiate for additional pain or reduce your dice.

This is my understanding of how the rules work. Is it right, or did I get something wrong?

    \n Paul, I think it works differently.

    That thing with the ring isn't the stakes for the conflict. It's merely what you want at the start of the conflict. It's more than likely resolved on the first die roll.

    By the second and third rounds, we'll be talking about something entirely different. I'll have taken your ring and you'll be shooting my horse or something.

    Stop me if I'm wrong, IAWA experts, but one of the things I like about IAWA is that it doesn't use stakes.

    \n I don't have my copy handy, but take another look at the outcomes. I believe it works like this: the person taking the action is the one who describes how they are trying to change the status quo. The answerer describes only how the other guy doesn't reach their goal with their action; they don't get to somehow cram in an action of their own (like "I take the ring back from you") into their Answer. If you're answering, you're not achieving, you're only blocking. That's how I understood it, anyway.

    \n Paul T isn't talking about stakes. Only what the Answerer, the person being Challenged, gets to narrate into the fiction.
    \n
    \nMy understanding is that the Answerer must describe the events to clearly describe who has the advantage in the conflict. BUT since there is no stakes, they don't have to give the ring up. Because it is not at stake. Even if it is what the fight started over. Consequently, there is no limit to how often the ring can be narrated in. There is nothing in the rules stopping anyone from repeating actions or re-introducing certain elements in the fiction. But this is all limited by group style and tastes. If your players think something is bogus, then I don't think it flies.
    \n
    \nTo contradict myself earlier, in a way, winning the 3rd round as the Answerer is great but since there are no stakes, and no stakes that stick, there is nothing stopping someone from just attacking you again. Winning the 3rd round doesn't protect you from future actions. And it should be noted that even if someone could attack you again over the ring doesn't mean you are repeating the same conflict. It's not a do-over. Time, location, negotiation details, and damage sustained have all changed. And regardless of who the Answerer is, the person doing the damage gets to describe (with GM approval and within the guidelines of the group's play style) how that damage is applied. So they could chop off your hands. So sure, we may be fighting over the ring again (which is not at stake, it is just the reason we started fighting initially) but now you have no hands!!!

    \n Paul: "I take the ring and beat the crap out of you" is a challenge, not an answer. You have to have initiative to say that.

    It might help if you reread the little side box about "if you've played conventional RPGs" and take it to heart. Like, Shadowrun. I have initiative, and I shoot you and make my attack roll. You make your defense roll and your defense beats my attack. Does that let you beat the crap out of me and take the ring? Not at all! It just lets you not have gotten shot.

    Same thing in the Wicked Age.

    -Vincent

    \n Perfect. You reroll for your defense.

    You beat my standing attack roll, so you get to answer to your own advantage. However, all that's up for grabs right now is my wrestling you to the ground and choking you into submission; you can't bring the ring into it because you don't have initiative. What's your answer?

    You have lots of latitude. You can block any, all or none of my challenge, from "I jump back, you're off balance and you don't get your hands on me" to "you're choking me but I manage to get my arm free and reverse your hold" to "you choke me for a while until I'm still, but then you let your guard down and I'm up and after you." But, again, you're limited to answering my challenge, you can't slip in a challenge of your own.

    So let's say you say one of those.

    NOW you have the stick. "How about I beat the crap out of you and take the ring?" you say. The ring's fully up for negotiation.

    Make sense so far? I want to explain what I mean by "you might say that, but the game's rules keep you from really meaning it," but let me check so far.

    Rewind. My initiative. I said "I wrestle you to the ground, get my hands around your throat, and choke you into submission" as my challenge. You just rerolled for your answer, and beat my standing dice. We're both looking at the dice, and my face is falling.

    "Yeah, crap, I guess you do," I say. Or else I say, "how about you take the ring but I'll reduce my With Love instead?" Or else I say, "no way, beat the crap out of me if you want, I'm not letting go of the ring."
