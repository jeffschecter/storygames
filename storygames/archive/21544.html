\n Hi all. We are organizing a game jam in the Seattle area. So first of all, stay tuned for info if you are in the area and want to participate.

And second of all, we would be happy (and eager) to recieve any helpful hints. None of the folks organizing this have ever done this kind of thing before (I don\u2019t think...).

We are a playtest group that works on gmLess and gmed games. Mostly one shots or short arcs.

Our goal is to lower the bar for entry into the world of game design for people who might be interested but haven\u2019t gotten over the hump and tried yet.

One thing we are looking for is a list of game mechanisms etc. we are hoping to provide something like a toolkit, or maybe a heap of spare parts that people can use to get started in the game design process.

Any other helpful hints or materials are also more than welcome.

We are all ears!

Thanks very much,
Davey.

    \nPlay With Intent
    A collection of freeform, LARP, and Jeepform techniques. I was going to start writing about safety, the importance of a clear structure of play and initiative order, and how to create a safe and creative environment, but this document says most of it in the headlines Principles and Environment.
    http://erebaltor.se/rickard/playWithIntent_newLayout.pdf

    Improv glossary
    Accept, Block+Offer, and even Status, are incredibly important.
    https://improwiki.com/en/wiki/improv/special/category/90/improv-glossary

    MDA
    A formal approach to game design.
    http://algorithmancy.8kindsoffun.com/

    Daniel Cook's blog
    Everything on his site is a should read by game designers, but this one in particular. In order to create a game - something that creates an interaction and, for collaborative storytelling games, an interaction/conversation between the participants - they need to create a feedback loop. That's how they get invested in the game/story.
    http://www.lostgarden.com/2006/10/what-are-game-mechanics.html

    ---

    Active listening is really important in collaborative storytelling games, where the participants that doesn't have characters involved in the scene/situation still wants to listen. You do that by having them affect the outcome, throw in new stuff in the scene, or assist the participants.

    The Murder of Mr. Crow does that by the Assistant, where the Detective can, at any time, ask the assistant for help if the person gets stuck.

    Zombie Cinema and Nostalgia lets non-participants vote on which one they will prefer to win.

    Svart av kval, vit av lust does hard suggestions of what the characters in the scene must do, or resist doing.

    Fiasco lets the non-participants either start the scene or decide the outcome of the scene.

    Archipelago uses ritual words to steer how someone else describes things.

    Once Upon a Time forces others to listen if they want to continue the story, and also have trigger words that can make a participant to take over the word.

    Baron M\xfcnchhausen and Nostalgia lets the participants that are not storytelling suggest challenges for the storyteller.

    The Coyotes of Chicago only have one character, but the rest are game masters with their own agenda, so they need to listen to what the other says to incorporate their own agenda with the others.

    Imagine lets everyone participate by saying a sentence, and they need to listen to actively put the sentences together to form a story.

    In game master driven games, the game master must do active listening to be able to plan ahead (the game master is always participating by responding to the players' actions, but plan ahead is different).

    ---

    By making this list of active listening mechanics above, I would like to throw in a thought that listing mechanics only blocking the creativity, instead of offering new insights. I would rather see a checklist of things to include to make a successful collaborative storytelling game, like safe environment for the participants, active listening, and feedback loops.

    \n Pay attention to Rickard's last point there: just listing different game mechanics is, in many ways, entirely the wrong way to go about this.

    A great piece of advice I heard recebtly from Nathan Paoletta: come up with a game concept and then playtest it with a simple coin flip anywhere you want randomized resolution.

    Only then, if that doesn't feel satisfying, you can try to think what would feel better. (And, as a bonus, if a coin flip feels just right, you just saved yourself a lot of headache!)
