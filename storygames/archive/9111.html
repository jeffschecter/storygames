So, you ever play Chess and Go in close succession? It's very interesting because the board awareness necessary for each game is actively detrimental to my ability to play the other game.

\n

My experience is that chess requires you to be aware of the entire board situation all the time, whereas with go you can attend to a particular skirmish for quite some time. The skirmish affects the larger board situation, but you can profitably focus on the smaller scope. It's interesting to talk about why this is the case, but for now I'm going to gloss over that.

\n

I'm aware of a lot of indie-rpg formalizations of a chesslike relationship map, but I don't know of any (off the top of my head) that work in a go-like way. Are there any?

\n

If there aren't, what do you think one would look like?

    \n Let me see if I suss out what you're saying:

    Chess is like a tightly coupled relationship map, with each character exerting a direct influence on a number of other characters. No character is ever more than a few connections away. A scene between any two characters has the potential to directly influence the entire relationship map. So the game is played in moves, each of which impacts the whole board more or less directly.

    In Go, a piece more often exerts an indirect influence that wanes as you get further away, though in theory it extends across the board. It's played in skirmishes which are primarily local, but indirectly change the overall landscape.

    I think if Burning Empires as conceptually rather like Go. A particular move, such as a color scene where you introduce a technology, may be of very minor significance in the moment, but exert a strategic influence far away. A particular scene may change the relationship between the involved characters, but have no direct impact on other characters in the phase, or across the phases of the invasion. At least that's how I see the intention of BE.

    \n Here's how I envision a Go-like relationship map: we are playing a mashup of Reign and IaWA. Each session, we care primarily about the PCs played during this session. Their actions will explicitly change the Companies and have an indirect effect on their relationships with other characters not present, but there's pretty much no problem with the scenario in which somehow we end up playing the same PCs for 4 sessions in a row. Later when Steve the neglected PC prince turns up at the table, he will e.g. find his kingdom much poorer and much larger and this radically changes the way he will be played, but play which caused this had vanishingly small need to consider Steve.

    \n In the new political-soaked Pathfinder-ish game I'm running, each player is pulling strings for different outcomes in a semi-annual meeting of the guilds. In this way, each characters' actions are tugging on different, sectioned corners of a relationship map. It's structured so that each situation has an R-Map, with a meta-R-map determining larger outcomes as they each resolve. So, more like Go, in that each chess match resolves as a single move within the larger chess match... if that makes any sense.

    Posted By: shreyasPosted By: Josh RobyI nominate IAWA and Dogs as Go-like.

    What makes these games Go-like? I'm not seeing it.

    \nThe first thing to remember is that in Go the board starts blank. If there is a little skirmish in one corner of the board, another skirmish may open up somewhere else without any reference to the first skirmish. As the skirmishes develop through play, they might end up touching one another and then their current states affect each other. Think of it in terms of context \u2014 stones only have meaning in relation to other stones nearby. A long enough game makes each stone relate, eventually and perhaps massively indirectly, to every other stone on the board. But most of play is relatively encapsulated.

    This is like IAWA because each chapter you play may open a new 'skirmish' with its own oracles and in a completely different place and time. As you play, however, some characters or locations or even items will show up more than once, carrying with them the context of their prior appearances. Now, Go has a finite board while IAWA has an infinite "board," but one could imagine playing IAWA long enough that all of the stories and characters and whatnot knit together into one massive, if decentralized, anthology of stories.

    Dogs is similar except you could consider each new town as a skirmish that is just next to the last skirmish, so there is some context (the GM pushing player statements to see how far the PCs will go), but most of the town is new. Eventually the town-skirmishes creep their way around and across the board, filling it up.
