    Posted By: Simon C

    Your "negotiated conflict" description seems confused about whether conflicts exist between characters or between players. This is, in my opinion, an important distinction. You write:

    One person introduces an idea that he thinks would be exciting or interesting, and if the others in the group agree to that idea, then that idea is accepted as having taken place. When one person wants to introduce something into the game world but another player wants something else, a conflict arises. The Negotiated Contest design pattern allows players to negotiate a collection of possibilities that are acceptable to all involved and then determine through some mechanical means which possibility is actually incorporated into the game world.
    \nThis is, in my opinion, not a useful description of RPG play.

    Role playing games do have systems for resolving conflicts between players, but these systems are typically at the social contract level, or embedded in the authorities and responsibilities given to different player roles. Some games have conflict resolution systems that partially or exclusively grant authorities to players, but this isn't the basis of conflict resolution. A small number of games concern themselves entirely with apportioning responsibilities and authorities to players. Systems that do this are not the same as conflict resolution systems.

    Conflict resolution is about conflicts between characters. It does not resolve disagreements between players over what is entered into the fiction. It determines whether characters get what they want or not.

    I understand the difference between player and character conflicts. I also understand that player goals and character goals do not always line up, and that this is largely why System Matters. That is, a game system may be designed that provides players a rational reason to want something that is detrimental to their character.

    However, fundamentally, characters are fictional constructs that only exist in the minds of the players. The characters cannot actually "want" anything. As such, all conflicts that truly exist are essentially differences of opinion as to what should be introduced into the fictional world. If there is no difference of opinion, then there is no conflict that needs mechanical resolution, whether it be a character conflict or a player conflict.

    For example, let's say the GM of a game has set up a troll guarding a bridge that a character needs to cross. The player can say something like, "Biff the Barbarian defiantly walks past the troll." The GM may respond with, "Okay. As you approach, you see that the troll appears to have dozed off. You pass without incident." On the other hand, if the GM feels like he wants a fight, he can declare, "As you approach, the troll steps forward and swings his axe at you." One response demonstrates no conflict exists, the other shows one does exist.

    That's all I'm saying there.

    I like games where the rule is that if there is an identifiable conflict between characters then use the system to see what happens. Here's why that's different than just a difference opinion.

    Player: "I shoot NPC Joe."
    GM: "Yeah, NPC Joe is an asshole. I hope you kick his ass. But NPC Joe is going to try to save his ass and that's a conflict so we need to roll."

    So the Player and the GM are both hoping the player kick's NPC Joe's ass. But then he doesn't. NPC Joe totally wins. Now both player's are upset and worked up and "Oh my god! I can't believe that asshole got away with it! Fuck, what's going to happen NOW?!"

    \n Actually, after two minutes of further thought here's a less head wonky way of saying the same thing.

    Opinions are about value judgments. If you say, "Ninjas come through the window" and I say, "No! Zombies come through the doors." Then we, in the real world, are in conflict because we are each asserting that our idea is *better* than the others. You want Ninjas. I want Zombies. One of us has to be denied what we *want*.

    When players are tasked with "speaking" for a given fictional entity then they simply have to say what they think the entity does during the given circumstances. If you say, "Joe shoots Bob" and I say "Bob hits Joe with a baseball bat." I'm not saying "I think it's better if Bob hit's Joe with baseball bat rather than Joe shooting Bob." I'm simply stating what I think Bob does in reaction to being shot at by Joe.

    What I or you *want* doesn't matter. We're not trying to "get things" out of the fiction. We're just playing the character with creative passion and gusto and seeing what happens together.

    I think most problems arise in RPGs when player's try to wrestle and shape the fiction as a whole to conform to something they want rather than just playing the character and willing to let the fiction take form on its own.

    \n OK, some more about the difference between task and conflict resolution. I see that Jesse already explained well how conflict resolution in most games is not about differences the players have as regards the fiction; the great majority of successful designs presumes that the players actually agree on the fundamental direction of play, and conflict resolution is only there to provide excitement and interesting creative constraint and interactivity. The characters fight for the amusement of the players, essentially.

    As for how conflict and task resolution differ, I can only offer the definitions: task resolution is when the imaginary character is trying to do something and we want to find out whether he succeeds. Conflict resolution is when imaginary characters have a conflict of interest and we want to see who gets their way. Note that these two are not in any way exclusive towards each other; they're different categories of consideration. It is entirely possible for the same situation and game mechanic to resolve both a task and a conflict at the same time if the players have arranged for the task and conflict to coincide; many think that the only effective and fun way of using a task resolution system is to use it like this, by ensuring that the tasks resolved correspond to conflicts.

    From the above viewpoint it's not exact to say that all roleplaying games have a conflict resolution system. To pick a game you mentioned, John, one that I know well: Call of Cthulhu does not, strictly speaking, have a conflict resolution system ingrained in the game text. The text advices the GM to introduce task resolution as appropriate, but there is no verbiage that would ensure for the task resolution to correspond with conflicts in any way. In fact, the game is notorious for how much task resolution its methodology engages in without any particular conflict guaranteed to be involved. Of course the same exact mechanics can be engaged with conflict resolution methodology; the simplest way is for the GM to ask the players what their characters want, after which he frames reasonable situations and conditions that allow the characters to try to accomplish their intents, after which one or more skill checks are made to find out whether they succeed - that's conflict resolution, because the GM has committed to resolving the character intents; it's also task resolution, as the CoC rules-set only provides task resolution and Luck rolls that might be used in resolving things.

    If we want to talk about game texts and which "have" a conflict or task resolution system, I think that it's pretty important to only consider a game text as possessing a conflict resolution system if the text actually discusses character intents, stakes-setting or whatever and then provides concrete mechanics or methodology for how to do this. All games resolve conflicts, but that is not the same as all games having a conflict resolution system. The great majority of games can be used very effectively to resolve conflicts if you know what to bring into the game yourself from outside the text in terms of GM skill or such, but that's not the same as the game text itself having a conflict resolution system.

    I should note that if a random passer-by reads the above from the viewpoint of thinking that I'm sermonizing for the absolute supremacy of conflict resolution vs. task resolution, it's going to seem strange. After all, why would character intentions be that central in comparison to what characters factually do? The real truth is that they're not - a robust conflict resolution is not an universal roleplaying tonic that everybody needs to swallow for their play to work. There are plenty of rpg design paradigms in which the conflict resolution can be arbitrary or invisible. It's just the dramatic, player-coordinating style of game that's so common at the Forge that needs robust conflict-resolution: because drama arises from protagonists in struggle, we need to know what those protagonists want and whether they succeed in their struggles. For this sort of game how you do conflict resolution is very important. For a different sort of game, such as Call of Cthulhu, it's not nearly so much: the GM can resolve conflicts by fiat, essentially, as long as the players get the chance to experience the events and direct the order and pacing of their development by having their character move to different places and initiate different proceedings.

    \n The key part of the definition, Mike, is not unilateral vs. opposed action. The key part is that there is a dramatic actor (a character for whom we posit an inner existence, to be exact) with an intent acting in a scene. You might think of conflicts as "task + motivation" in this regard - conflict only exists if we have a character the audience can sympathize with struggling to achieve something; indeed, some would say that we have a conflict even without the overt struggle, and that's certainly true of certain genres. The reason for why this is often depicted in an opposed manner is that most conflicts in fiction tend to involve several characters. Strictly speaking, however, something like whether Odysseus ever gets home might be a conflict "against nature", making for a one-sided conflict. (And yes, I would make that "Odysseus vs. the gods" in practice, too. Just an example.)

    Consider: when a character is hitting another with a sword, that is always a task to be resolved - some games might allow a player to just describe the task any which way, but that's still resolution. The mere fact that there is another character there does not make this conflict resolution. However, if we also presuppose a character intent (perhaps to kill the other guy?) and decide that the intent will be effectively resolved by this sword-swing, then that very same act transforms into conflict resolution as well. Or, as D&D has it, we have the overarching intent of killing the other guy, and this conflict is resolved by a series of tasks: hit successfully so many times that the other guy loses his hit points, and you get what you want. In this D&D example the "task resolution system" is to roll a to-hit roll to see if the task was successful, while the "conflict resolution system" is that you kill the other character not arbitrarily, but by reducing him to zero hit points, which is usually done by hitting successfully.

    I should note that this basic theoretical definition does not really comment upon the common virtues of robust conflict resolution systems simply because it's theoretical. When we get to practical application, the conflict system will look somewhat different because the given game allows and disallows different types of conflict, and handles them differently. For example, Primetime Adventures makes it seem like conflicts are about scene-resolution and static character issues because that's the way the game in question narrows and utilizes the general concept. The example of PTA does not mean that all conflicts everywhere are like they are in PTA, it just means that PTA transforms all conflicts into the sort it handles to structuralize the game in the way it does. Stakes technique is another example, people mix up the general concept of "conflict resolution" with the specific concept of "determining stakes and resolving them". The more general conflict resolution thing is something that happens in practically all games somehow; some games have a conflict resolution system, making the resolution explicit and systematic; some games use a specific stakes-setting method of conflict resolution, which is just an arbitrary technique among others for handling and resolving conflicts. Taking the part for the whole of the concept tends to make this thing more difficult to think about than it has to be.

    \n Ah, also, your example, Mike: rolling to escape prison is almost always a conflict, it seems to me; I can't conceive of the practical game where we would roll for a character to escape a prison and it wouldn't be a conflict in the theoretical sense. This is because I'm having difficulty imagining how the character would escape without this being a matter of intent for him.

    Some games will resolve this prison-escape thing by querying the fiction and breaking the conflict down into a series of tasks. For example, Solar System would have the players pick some appropriate abilities, describe how they pertain to the situation, and then make rolls to find out how the character sneaks, lies, bribes his way out. Succeed in the tasks, and the system guarantees that you also succeed in the conflict.

    Other games will leave the conflict resolution up to the GM: his job is to introduce adversity into the situation and resolve it in a way that allows the character to escape from the prison at the end of the session, fulfilling the pre-sketched dramatic arc. In this case individual task resolution cycles do not directly impinge on the overall conflict of escape, really, except insofar as they inform the narration of the details.

    Yet other games will allow you to resolve the prison escape conflict without querying either the GM or a task resolution system. Polaris, for example, doesn't have a mechanized task resolution system at all; all conflicts in it are overcome by the character when and if the Heart player succeeds in accomodating the antagonistic pressures of the Mistaken. It's also interesting in that the game's conflict resolution system is a really, really generic one: the only real conflict in that game is the one the Heart has with the Mistaken, and individual resolution cycles are just way-points in that. Something like a prison break will be resolved in that system as a minor sidepoint of a much larger story.

    Just some examples of how conflicts can be resolved. The concept of conflict itself is simple, but there are so many ways of handling conflicts as they arise in the fiction that we haven't even nearly tapped them all as of yet. For instance, I've been exploring delayed resolution myself lately: I'm pretty convinced that certain genres of fiction rely on indefinitely delayed conflict resolution, so that's something I want to put into a game in a consistent way as well. Sort of like Dust Devils allows you to put down the conflict and come back to it later, except enforced by the system.

    \n I appreciate your viewpoint, Todd. Have you considered, though, that the innovative part might still be there even if we're strictly logical about the application of the terminology?

    I'm well aware of how people want to use the term "conflict resolution" for the exciting new ways of resolving conflicts while minimizing the fact that the same theoretical functions have actually been accomplished by different means in games all along. Then you can say that D&D "doesn't have conflict resolution", and that's a firm, concrete reason for why the game's not working for you. That is certainly useful.

    However, I personally prefer to be exact in my terminology. I have no difficulty in accepting that "conflict resolution" is not a very special term by itself, but rather simply means that conflicts get resolved, just like natural language works. If I want to speak specifically of the modern, advanced techniques of conflict resolution that make recent game design exciting, I can easily speak of "explicit conflict resolution procedures" or "explicit stakes" or "mechanically regulated universal conflict resolution" or "formally paced conflict resolution mechanics" or "limited GM powers over conflict resolution procedures" or any number of other individual things that are concretely attempted by specific games. I don't see the value of mixing these details up in the very general and simple concept of conflict resolution by itself. That seems to only end up in stupid debates over whether conflict resolution is any different from task resolution or how come this game you claim to be task resolving actually does the same exact thing that other game does. Vague buzz-words give good sound-bite, but they're not going to convince anybody of the logical efficacy of your theory work.

    Excitement is all well and good, and I don't begrudge the use of a short-hand when the participants in a discussion know what they're talking about. I myself often say things like "this game doesn't have conflict resolution, which seems a shame when it's otherwise rather modern" and do not actually mean that in any exact theoretical sense: I'm just saying that this game doesn't have explicit textual procedures for conflict resolution - it's just shorter and simpler to say "no conflict resolution" than "no explicit textual procedure for how conflicts get resolved, so I guess we'll have to default to traditional GM fiat on this one".

    \n I agree with you on the rough lines if history, Todd. It's true that the way terminology is used evolves. The way I see it from the inside of the discourse is that our understanding of the matters themselves evolves, and that informs the way we use the terminology. For instance, "conflict resolution" has indeed been used as an opposite of "task resolution", and I fully understand why this has been so: when you only have examples of "implicit conflict resolution with explicit task resolution" and "explicit conflict resolution with implicit task resolution" to compare with each other, it makes sense to use those two primary terms to describe these specific system set-ups. It's simple and to the point. However, as our understanding of the entire field grows, we see that what we called "conflict resolution" is actually just one arbitrary form of a much larger phenomenon - well, the name fits that larger thing much better, so it's pretty natural to start using it that way.

    I'd compare this to how some secluded village is used to calling the animals they have "cows". Then industrialization comes and they bring in new sorts of cows from the larger world, ones that have been adapted to milking much better than their own country breed. So now they start calling those new things "cows" as well, but that's confusing, so they add qualifiers: one type of cow becomes "traditional cow", while the other is the "new cow", or whatever. Perhaps place names: "Jutland cow" or whatnot. The point is, the word "cow" has shifted from signifying a part to signifying a whole, which is a pretty natural development in how language is used. I personally believe that if we still used "conflict resolution" to mean exclusively "explicit stakes-setting with narration sharing and without explicit task resolution the way Dust Devils does it", the terminology would be much more confusing. I can't even begin to guess what term we would use for the larger general idea of resolving conflicts in general, without using stakes or narration-sharing.

    As Vernon describes it, "conflict resolution" is a good term for resolving conflicts because it's transparent and to the point: we understand what a "conflict" is in theory terms (that term has remained unchanged since Sorcerer in much of Forge parlance, despite some confusion a few years ago when some folks appropriated the term for inter-player disagreements), and we understand what "resolution" means as a natural word. This is one particular bit of jargon I'm rather happy with. The same goes for much of the GNS theory for me - the shifts in usage I see are mostly refinements, as we learn to better analyze phenomena and thus find that hey, these two bits go together or hey, actually this one bit is in truth two separate bits. So the usage of terminology changes, but the change is not arbitrary, but consistent and logical for the most part.

    --

    I should note that staring at mere terminology instead of the ideas themselves is a very confusing way of following the rpg theory discussions. Everybody tends to have slightly different meanings for the words they use, especially if they do active theory work! This is simply because of the sort of terminological development I describe above as regards "conflict resolution" - our understanding of the things we discuss evolves, and thus we reapply the terminology to better fit the situation. For example, I wouldn't swear that Ron Edwards would underwrite the way I describe conflict resolution vs. task resolution as terms, but I'm pretty sure that he would agree with me on the actual substance behind the words. Usually we don't have any significant difficulty in understanding what others mean when they talk about these things when we're immersed in the stuff ourselves, simply because we can quickly match the terms others use to the actual ideas behind the terminology. If you don't know the ideas yet, it becomes much more difficult to reconcile different ways of saying the same thing.

    This also gives rise to the frequent outsider perception that theoretical terminology is arbitrary and everybody disagrees about what the words mean. GNS for instance - I know perhaps a dozen people whose understanding of that terminology and theoretical model accords with my own (there are probably more out there, I just couldn't name them right off), while practically everybody else utilizing those terms seems to do it without really understanding the theory. This isn't that confusing from inside the discourse because I know what I know, and I can easily ignore mis-applied terminology or just ask a person to speak in plain language. However, looking at the topic from outside the discourse, it's obviously going to be hella confusing when you don't know whose explanations to believe and why these two people in these two separate discussions use the same words in entirely different ways. With inside knowledge you can just categorize superficial differences according to whether they matter, whether some usage is outdated or whatever. From the outside it's all just a big mess.

    Ultimately, insofar as I'm allowed to get philosophical about the entire prospect of rpg theory here, I have to say that all this theory discourse would probably be easier if it wasn't conducted in public fora among a shifting set of participants. That's an unique environment for discussion, resembling public political discourse more than quiet academics in many ways. Everybody has an opinion and there are no credentials or chronological development in any of it. Terminology is appropriated and mutated arbitrarily because "that's how I understand the word". It's just ships passing in the night, excepting some core circles among which the same people engage in continuous dialogue with each other, often privately. Considering how rpg theory isn't really that immediately useful for gamers (or shouldn't be as the game texts improve and you no longer need the equivalent of a Master's degree to play successfully), all this could probably be arrayed in smarter ways, too. The average hobbyist reads all about this GNS stuff or whatever and then understandably gets uncertain and apprehensive: should I know this stuff to improve my play? Should I have an opinion on this matter to fit into a community? Should I learn to do this conflict resolution thing so I can be a real boy? Can I go to the movies and enjoy the film without reading The Art of Dramatic Writing? Then knowledge is looked for in all sorts of places, all mired up in wacky Internet identity politics that dictate who you can and can't ask - sometimes it seems that nine in ten rpg discussion participants would rather invent their own meanings for the word "narrativism" than go ask Ron, who is consistently available in the Internet, what he means by the word. And then it's some surprise when nobody seems to agree about the term. All that much bull-shit from the viewpoint of actual play.

    \n John,

    Here are a couple of concepts that might help you:

    Explicit vs. Implicit

    I think what you're describing is explicit Conflict resolution, but not all conflict resolution is explicit. One of the most common kinds of conflict resolution is "the GM decides whether the character gets what they want", which is implicit resolution. In other words, we have a system for deciding whether characters get what they want, but that system is not described in the rules text or even discussed out loud. It's just how things are done.

    One of the points of confusion regarding conflict resolution (and Eero discusses this above) is that people want to use the term conflict resolution to mean only the kind of explicit conflict resolution (where we have a system for deciding if characters get what they want, and that system is written down in the rules text) that's found in some indie games. That's understandable, but it makes the definition of conflict resolution a nonsense.

    All games where characters get things they want have conflict resolution.

    Ad-hoc vs. Principled

    Ad-hoc resolution is where the resolution is made on the basis of changing, arbitrary, or unclear methods. "GM decides" can be ad-hoc, if the GM isn't clear or is arbitrary in their decision making, but it can also be principled if the GM has clearly stated criteria for making decisions. Dice-based resolution can still be ad-hoc, if the decision about when to employ or how to interpret the dice is arbitrary or unclear.

    In practice, I think most GMs are making principled decisions in "GM decides" games, though often their principles are obscure to the players.

    \n Yes, this seems like a smart direction. Both conflict and task resolution are pretty transparent as terms - the real meat is in the concepts of "task" and "conflict".

    The important historical meaning of conflict in this context is the Sorcerer conception of struggling dramatic actors. A "conflict" under this understanding occurs when a dramatic actor (a character with thematic interest) is trying to accomplish something (thematically) interesting against a source of adversity. (Sources of adversity in Sorcerer do not need to be other characters, but they almost always are; some other games explicitly only recognize character-vs-character conflicts.) This pinpoints conflict as a term that only really makes sense when we perceive the events of the game in dramatic terms to begin with. On the other hand, insofar as we accept this dramatic viewpoint as a priority, the concept of conflict is absolutely crucial to analyzing the significant structure of the roleplaying act: the concern of establishing and managing conflicts is sufficient all but alone to build a roleplaying game of this sort, as demonstrated repeatedly by modern design - many of the more lightweight games practically devote two thirds of their system to conflict management. (The same idea is present in other games, I should note; not trying to say that this was necessarily invented for the first time ever in Sorcerer - it's just where it comes into these discussions.)

    A natural follow-up question concerns the necessity of the dramatic aspect of that definition. As it happens, an useful concept of conflict is preserved by replacing it with "whatever is the focus of interest in your play". For example, you can have a challenge-focused conflict system reasonably enough: it resolves character intents just like above, but it's only utilized under the presumption of challenge instead of dramatic weight. There's a bit more to this whole thing, but that gets into on-going game design and theory; for our purposes this whole character intent resolution thing is clear enough, I believe, as long as we remember that a practical game doesn't try to mechanically apply the conflict resolution system to all character intents ever everywhere.

    Another potential complication concerns the nature of character struggle - or that of character intent, interchangeably. One might argue that a character who doesn't know what to do doesn't have an intent and therefore can't struggle, stuff like that. Some games make a big deal of this kind of thing and clearly expand the definition of conflict to essentially match the thematic forces the character represents against the adversity. Polaris is a schoolbook example of this: the conflict rules of the game are not actually predicated on character intent, but rather on what might be called the character's "theme" - the Heart player advocates for his character in an entirely recognizable way, but conflicts can happen without the character's knowledge or the character can try to act against his own best interests or whatever, really; the real conflict is between the values the character represents and the inevitable corruption and decay represented by the Mistaken.

    (Remembering our maiden/monster example from above, it's a pretty good illustration of how "character intent" starts to break down even in pretty conventional storytelling now and then: if the monster's conflict is internal and concerns its shamefulness vs. its lecherous habits, can we really say that there is some unified "intent" in play there? What is really in play are themes, personified by characters.)

    Considering the above elaborations, a pretty powerful and abstract definition of conflict recognizes that conflict occurs in roleplaying games when fictional forces clash; characters are only really the most common way for such forces to manifest in the SIS, but technically recognizable conflicts are possible without mixing characters into it as well. This is of course a rather unwieldy and possibly uninformative way of saying things, but that's anal-retentive exactness for you. For practical purposes and analysis of conventional and common roleplaying game situations it's easily sufficient to define conflict as when a character's intent goes at cross-purposes with some significant obstacle. That'll suffice for any game where characters and their actions are a major concern - and there are good reasons to think that characters are mandatory for dramatic games, for example, so that covers quite a swatch of all relevant roleplaying games already.

    \n Other ways of resolving conflicts... it's an interesting question, certainly. As established above we have many varieties of stakes-setting that explicitly determine whether the character's intent should come to pass, and then we have games like IaWA that ensure the primacy of intent by having the players negotiate the stakes post-resolution within certain parameters established by the rules. Are these two categories exhaustive? I see no analytical reason for them to be so, but counter-examples are thin on the ground if we're looking at effective and consistent conflict resolution systems. "Have the GM decide" is both effective and consistent (insofar as the GM is instructed to recognize and resolve conflicts, that is), so I guess that counts.

    Some games twist the stakes-setting procedure by having the stakes hardcoded regardless of character intent. This works because the game ensures in other ways, mostly by genre management, that whatever is hardcoded into the system will be a matter of conflict. Dance and the Dawn, Polaris, Zombie Cinema, Under the Bed and other such games that have a prefixed dramatic arc often make certain key conflicts of the story unresolvable by whatever system is mostly used for conflict resolution in the game. For example, a typical rule is that you can't kill a player character without having the dramatic arc run its course. Dance and the Dawn is a particularly clear example, because the game actually does not include a general conflict resolution system at all: the only conflict you get to resolve in the game systematically is the issue of whether your lady gets the right husband or not. Any other conflicts are subsumed to this one.

    There are also games that resolve conflicts by narrating over them with general narration powers. Your average parlor narration game, such as Once Upon a Time, does this: conflicts are established insofar as the players manage to set up a coherent drama, but their resolution is left up to arbitrary narration on the basis of general creative tension between the players. This might be more like the immersionistic case in that the conflict resolution won't be explicit and reliable because the players do not have an imperative to resolve conflicts by their narration.

    I'm pretty sure that there is more variety to conflict resolution when you get digging and find out those weird experimental edge cases that are developed in game design. It's an interesting field of design for sure, as the management of conflict is rather central to a dramatic roleplaying game. We can perhaps be pretty confident that explicit recognition of character intent and having the GM decide are the most common and successful methods overall, perhaps because of their overall effectiveness.

    \n Have you read or played IaWA? The game involves a negotiation phase after conflict resolution; the resolution itself determines which character gains primacy in the conflict, while the negotiation establishes the consequences of the events. Winning the conflict matters in the negotiation phase because the winner can default to injuring the loser, should the negotiation break down. However, it is usually more useful for the character if the player negotiates directly to achieve whatever it was his character wanted, as most of the time the characters do not actually want to injure each other so much as to gain something despite the resistance of the other characters. Considering the role of stakes in the whole matter, we can say that the "stakes-like" establishment of conflict scope and purpose happens in this post-conflict negotiation.

    As for Polaris, the hardcoded conflict I speak of is not the general conflict mechanics of the game, but rather the constraints within which those mechanics operate. For example, you cannot ask for the death of the player character before the character's become a veteran, because the most important hardcoded conflict in the game directly involves the character's life: the star knights are fated to either betray their people or die tragically in their defense, and which one'll happen is strongly regulated by the rules: you can't do the ultimate betrayal before Weariness reaches its final value, and you can't die before the character has at least some Weariness. Even when death is an option, it doesn't go through the general conflict resolution mechanics, but rather requires specific player choice. This issue is clearly a conflict, but the rules specifically set it aside from special regulation, making that a somewhat different conflict management mechanic from the usual stakes-establishing systems.

    Your analysis of granularity in the scope of the conflict is a good one, nothing to add there. You're also probably right in that a parlor narration game will likely resolve a given conflict via player fiat, although any tendency in play for conflicts to align along the lines of mechanical interaction allowed by the game is also interesting: it's an open question whether players of f.ex. Once Upon a Time will instinctively commit to championing specific fictional forces against each other, thus making the player-level competition a conflict resolution system. I've seen that now and then with the game, and it seems that the players get most excited with the game's haphazard narrative model when they can have a coherent competition that reaches into the fiction; for instance, if and when players notice that one is trying to get the established prince and princess hitched up while another wants to get the princess killed, that's different as an experience from when everybody's just trying to narrate random, fragmentary snippets. The game is notorious for its lack of fictional coherence, of course, so that sort of thing isn't exactly common.

    \n IaWA is certainly not easy to identify on the conflict/task resolution dichotomy.

    The way I see it, in that game all "tasks" have a preset outcome: lowering the loser's game attributes by a set amount (and since this eventually leads to the character's removal from the story, it's not all that meaningfully different from hit points). However, to bring this "task resolution" into play, you must have a conflict between two dramatic actors.

    Finally, the system allows the players to circumvent the outcome--instead of applying the "damage" to one of the parties, they can use that as a benchmark to negotiate a resolution to the conflict.

    Thus, any given conflict in the fiction which leads to the dice being rolled can emerge as task resolution or conflict resolution, depending on how the players decide to negotiate.
