\nFirstly, a shameless plug - as the writer and publisher of the forthcoming Gateway roleplaying game I just wanted to bring it to people's attention. There's a bunch of info you can find out about it in other places. Perhaps the best place is on our forums (malladinsgate.lefora.com). You can also download a couple of previews from the forum there if you're interested.
\n
\nHowever, the rules for this forum says that I should only post here to generate discussion. so here goes:
\n
\nWhilst the mechanics used in Gateway came straight from the heads of myslef and my co-writer, and in my experience are pretty unique within rpgs, I am not so arrogant as to assume that someone else might have done the same before me and I've just not noticed it. So here are the three things that I think are unique about Gateway and, if you are interested in joining in the discussion, you tell me if you know of anything similar already published. My background in gaming is in more traditional games, and I have written a lot previously for the D20 system and played a lot of Storyteller/ing System games, Savage Worlds, and the like, so I am not massively familiar with the indie scene. So just how unique are our ideas?
\n
\n1. Backgrounds: These generall fill the roll of skills in traditional rpgs, but are quite general in their use, and relate to what your character has done in their past, or other ways in which they interact with the world. You can use a background for a range of different tasks, including performing some kind of action, having knowledge of related topics, noticing things related to your background area, investigating or researching within your background area, making a "narrative intervention" to make different things happen in the story, and acquiring items or information. You can see this system in more detail by downloading teh first of our previews. Has one score been used in RPGs to do all of these things together?
\n
\n2. Equipment: This is perhaps the one that I think someone /must/ have done previously, but I can't think of an example. Gateway uses equipment traits to describe an item you might use. For example a handgun might have the weapon, pistol and firearm traits. These are the only game stats equipment needs. you can apply whichever traits are appropriate to a given action to gain a +1 bonus to a background roll. However, some traits block the use of other equipment traits by your opponent. For example, a Bulletproof trait on a kevlar vest might block the Fiream bonus being applied. I know of games that use equipment traits (or weapon traits at the very least) on top of otehr more traditional game stats (range, damage, etc), but I can't think of a system that uses just traits to describe equipment. Are there any? You can see this system in our second download.
\n
\n3. The third item is the biggie and won't be available until the book is released (probably next week). The system's main "engine" is called the Drama Resolution System, and works in a very different way to traditional rpgs. Rather than trying to model the physical position and competencies of individuals within a combat system, the Drama resolution system instead abstracts all dramatic situations and your actions and dice rolled influences the ebb and flow of tension and drama within the scene, recreating the feel of such scenes in films, TV shows, novels, etc. Now I know it may be difficult to discuss this system before the rulebook comes out, but I was wondering if anyone knew of games that at least attempted the same ends?
\n
\nThanks in advance for your comments,
\n
\nBen

    \nGreat post!

    Ben, for these exact reasons, I would encourage you NOT to look at any of the example games listed above.

    At least not till you have fully fleshed out your own game and playtested the hell out of it (which you may have already done). Your resolution system may end up being 75% like Dogs but the 25% that isn't may be pure gold. If you still want to check these games out, I would strongly advice you to play them rather than just reading their texts. How they sound may be very different that how they feel in play. You don't want to get to the point where your drawing of a human figure is influenced by the drawing of another person's human figure which was influenced by a book on anatomy which was influenced by technical drawings and become farther and farther disconnected from the actual source material.

    Also, what has been common place or old hat in this community for years is likely the new cool thing to most traditional gamers out there. This community in many ways is a creative think tank on the bleeding edge. You likely don't want to test your game for uniqueness here as it isn't a realistic sample of your likely target audience. That being said, this community is filled with excited and helpful people. Use us and give back and use us again! It's a fun ride.
