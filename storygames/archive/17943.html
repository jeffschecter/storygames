\n I've got that Friday feeling, so here are some thoughts I'd like to share with y'all. They've been on my mind for a week or two now, and I think this is probably the best place on the Internet to come and get these particular ideas dissected and picked apart, which they're in need of, as I'm not sure if I'm saying anything new, or even saying anything old in a new way, but I might be saying something quite interesting. So, here goes - and please, do your worstest.

I'd like to propose that there's an umbrella style of gaming I'll call fiction-oriented gaming, although you could just call it fluffy gaming if you'd like (fluff as opposed to crunch), and that this fiction-oriented gaming (F.O.G.) embraces not just storygames of the Forge-ish variety*, but also other styles of play, including conventionally GM'd freeform games, a significant proportion of LARP and a significant proportion of indisputably trad tabletop games, especially those where immersion [by which I always mean immersion in character] is valued.

Some core propositions:-
~A~ all roleplaying games and storygames involve fiction (in the sense of things imagined and unreal), but not all roleplayers are involved in actively/deliberately/consciously telling a story (or even necessarily in telling a story at all), nor does the fiction emerging from their games necessarily have storylike qualities such as theme/premise, plot, structure and so on --- so you have to be very cautious about equating the "fiction" in roleplaying games and storygames with "story" --- "fiction" and "story" are not exactly coterminous --- they are subtly different concepts, and the subtleties matter.
~B~ fiction-oriented games are games where fiction is at the heart of the gamers' gaming experience [contrast, for instance, games of pure hack-n-slash, or games heavily focused on probabalistic modelling] --- and by no means all fiction-oriented games are storygames of the Forge-ish variety.
~C~ in particular, having fiction reliably at the heart of your gaming experience doesn't necessarily mean that every player at the table needs to have some institutionalised role in actively putting fiction at the heart of the gaming experience (e.g. shared narrative authority or aspects of your character's personality/beliefs that give big bonuses / automatic effects when they're brought into play) --- it could be that all present, or only the GM, or the GM and some assistant GMs have that function; it could be hard-wired into the rules to some extent; it could be a combination of these things. It is absolutely a non sequitur to suppose that simply because one particular player, or most of the players, have no specific role in actively putting fiction at the heart of the game, then the game won't have fiction at its heart to the fullest extent reasonably conceivable. A trad GM is perfectly adequate for the task, and can bring fiction to the heart of the game while leaving broad scope to the player characters to be the main influence on what actually happens.

Reasoning:-

There are at least 5 things I can think of that you can do with the fiction in RPGs/storygames:-
(1) contribute to its creation by human agency;
(2) contribute to its creation by applying rules and dice \u2013 this covers mechanics generally, not just probabilistic modelling;
(3) explore it, in the sense of finding out more about it, pushing on its various buttons to see what happens;
(4) feel as if you\u2019re a part of it \u2013 the central case of this is immersion;
(5) use it to make the stakes of combat or other challenges seem greater.

Forge-ish games* tend to emphasise (1) above all else. I've said elsewhere that there's an element of (2) in there, and on reflection there's maybe some of (5) as well. I'm not denying, for a moment, that for some gamers (4) works best in storygames of the Forge-ish variety. But truly Forge-ish games, I contend, generally speaking aren't designed with (4) in mind. They're designed for (1) with maybe a bit of (2) and (5) thrown in. They may work great for some people for (4), but it's a side-effect, not the main purpose of the design.

But games that emphasise (3) and (4) also have fiction at their heart. Not necessarily story in any meaningful sense, but fiction, for sure. The gamers who play them value fiction over, for instance, challenge or probabalistic modelling. I for instance love to have complex fiction in my RPGs for its own sake, and I'm talking really about the complexity of the fiction, not the complexity of the rules (though there is a minor, indirect relationship between the two). And Forge-ish storygames tend to be less than optimal for (4) for many gamers, and are on the whole totally unsuited to (3), in my admittedly relatively limited experience.

The really fundamental point of this post is that whilst there are many things that tend to divide a certain segment of immersively-oriented trad gamers from a certain segment of Forge-ish storygamers, fiction-oriented gaming is a very important thing we all have in common (speaking as an immersively-oriented trad gamer myself). And that point has practical importance, because my experiences so far tend to suggest that although there may be gaming experiences that work for the trad lot but not for the storygamers, or vice-versa, it's eminently feasible for the same fiction to serve both groups' preferences at the same time at the same table - it just takes careful thought regarding how you structure the game. Indeed, I've found my own gaming experiences as an immersively-oriented trad gamer to be definitely enlivened by having a storygamer present, even though I wouldn't enjoy doing what they were doing their way as much as I enjoy doing my thing my way. And I really think that there's potential for the converse to be true too:- even though my preferred style of gaming is very individualistic and ego-centric, having a gamer like me at the table can work very well for storygamers, IMHO.

I've written up a rougher, possibly more contentious, definitely more self-indulgent exegesis on the same topic here for anyone who may be interested.

* which tend to be narrow, focused games with an emphasis on shared narrative authority because the people who play them tend to value collective, collaborative creativity, often with an egalitarian twist in the sense of power-sharing between GM and players, so that a player's ideas brought to the table won't be "stifled" by the dreaded Viking Hat GM. [I am a bit of a Viking Hat GM myself sometimes, to be honest.]

    \n@Paul_T, I think the starting point is that there are important differences in what people find "interesting" in/about/to do with the fiction at hand.     The above notwithstanding, I am optimistic about finding new ways to have the same plate of fiction feed some very different diners who appreciate it in very different ways at the same table. The storygamer is like a restaurant critic who wants to produce a polished piece as a reaction to the meal. The immersively-oriented trad gamer is more like a gourmet eater who just wants to gorge himself on the finest food available and is less fussed about creating witty, readable prose drawing on the technical niceties of the chef's accomplishments and the variety of ingredients. Then there are other trad gamers who are experimental eaters, who spend the meal experimenting with whether this mouthful goes best with this or that other mouthful, and which wine balances it best. Those are the ones more focused on exploration and less on creation or experience.

    You claimed that \xbb"Whereas immersively-oriented gamers have only the word "immersion".\xab and therefore making the term unique. It's not. In psychology, it's also called "presence". Now you got two commonly used terms, besides immersion, for something you thought was unique.
    \nCan't use "method acting" for "immersion [in character] in RPGs" because it implies rather more than just immersion-in-character-for-fun.

    Can't use "presence" because it wouldn't be understood by ordinary gamers. It's shorthand for "feeling as if you're present in the game-world" I guess, which is virtually synonymous (from the point of view of most immersive gamers' experience, maybe not all) with immersion [in character] if only because the only way for you to be present in the game-world would normally be as your character. But the use of the word "presence" would be misunderstood / is too obscure. How would you use it in context? "This game creates presence"? I suppose maybe "This game is intended to give you a feeling of being present in the game-world as your character", but woah, what a mouthful.

    "Immersion" is still the only suitable term.\n
    "...of so many gamers"? Can you give a number? My impression is that it's mostly LARP gamers that talk about immersion. Beyond Role and Play is LARP theory, for example.
    \nWell my polls on TBP have had well over a hundred people voting for options that apply only for immersion in character. Some of them may have mis-voted, but surely not most. And it's exceptionally rare for a poll on TBP to get more than about 300 votes, so if we're looking at what proportion of gamers immerse enough to care about and articulate it then... I'm guessing - 40% to 50% of gamers enthusiastic enough about gaming to use Internet forums? Maybe 10% to 20% of gamers generally? And that's only for immersion in character recognised as such by the gamer. I contend that many gamers experience light levels of immersion without ever really analysing it or being able to articulate how it enhances their gaming experience. That would be another 10% to 20% of gamers generally (maybe 5% of those enthusiastic enough to use Internet forums). So I would say, at a guess, a third of gamers experience some level of enjoyment from immersion in character, though maybe only half of those (a sixth of all gamers) could articulate it, and these proportions are higher for those who care enough about RPGs to participate in online forum discussions. Obviously, I'm conscious that this is educated guesswork, not hard facts. But the whole existence of theRPGSite is basically predicated on two things - immersion and freedom of expression without caring about causing offence. That's a pretty reasonable indication by itself that this playstyle has a committed following.\n
    What I've showed with my links is that immersion exist in other forms. It's not called "narrative immersion" in RPG, it's called ... storygames(?). It's not called "strategic immersion", it's called gamism(?). It's when you loose yourself in the area of the game. When you become part of it. It's a beautiful thing. It's the essence of that specific area of the game, and game designers - at least video game designers - are really keen on creating that effect for the players. There has been scientific research about immersion, otherwise I couldn't have given that link to the Wikipedia article. Here's an article about spatial immersion in video games.
    \nFascinating link, thank you :-)

    I think there's a massive overlap between "spatial presence" and "immersion in character" for the reasons I've already stated. Where that's not the case, we don't need a separate concept of immersion. Why not? Take "losing yourself in the story". This is surely promoted by exactly the same things that promote enjoyable "story" for that player. "Losing yourself in the strategy" - this is surely promoted by whatever makes for enjoyable strategy. There is no need for separate terms in gaming theory. Because games that do these things appeal not just to players who lose themselves in the story or strategy as the case may be, but also to gamers who game for the story or strategy without losing themselves in it. There is no material or systematically analysable difference between how such games must operate if they are to best appeal to those who love losing themselves in those games and how they must operate if they are to appeal to those who either don't lose themselves in those games or don't care whether they do or not. Counter-examples, please, if I am wrong.

    In almost the same way, for immersion-in-character, the single defining feature is that you feel somewhat as if you are your character, you suspend your disbelief in being them. Whether you do so to the point of "losing yourself" in the character or to a milder level is again more or less irrelevant from the point of view of game-design. The game designer's task is the same whether s/he wants to appeal to those gamers who lose themselves in being the character or to those gamers who like to feel somewhat as if they are their character without necessarily losing themselves in the character. The importance of immersion as a concept from a game-design, game-advertising or game-organising point of view is not the "losing yourself" but rather the "feeling somewhat as if you are your character" even if that may be to a lighter level where you DON'T lose yourself in it. It's the aim of identification-with-character* that's important in practical terms, not the depth of it.

    * "identification" in the sense of seeing the character as an extension of self, or seeing the self as an extension of the character, or seeing self and character as unified... not "identification" in the sense of "perceiving similarity between"\n
    Well, the character sheet can still say "greedy", is that what you're asking? Will the player feel greedy? Not without any reference points on how to interpret the character, in my opinion. But immersionist players I read (in LARP theory) or talked to usually say that the role grows over time, which is not that peculiar, because they will attend more reference points the more they play; when the world starts to make the player interpret what the character sheet says. Why do you think you should play a normal day in Apocalypse World during your first session? Why do you think there are pauses in the game where the participants read about the world in Montsegur 1244?
    \nI take all of that on board. But let me take a stab at illustrating how central the character is to immersion, and how peripheral the world is. Once you have the character, you get to know them like an imaginary friend. They are then there for you to immerse into because you've created that construct for yourself in your mind. You don't need to think hard about the game-world in its different aspects. Simply close your eyes, imagine being your character in a vague, undefined environment and dwell on your character's burning drives and motivations. Sure, these drives/motivations exist in a context, but that context doesn't need more than the vaguest definition for immersion to begin.

      For me the point is this. OK so the word "immersion" literally connotes "being plunged into", and if we wanted to use the word purely in the broadest literal sense you could be "plunged into" the game in the sense of engrossment/flow or "plunged into" the fiction in the sense of suspension of disbelief. But when we talk about immersive roleplaying games, by and large we're all talking about games that focus on suspension of disbelief in being the character. For some people that suspension of disbelief may be very heightened, with deep engrossment not just in the game but in the suspension of disbelief itself, such as to lead the gamer to experience physiological reactions to fictional stimuli. (I've been there.) So that is a triple-plunging:- you're suspending disbelief; you're engrossed; you're engrossed in suspending disbelief. But we all know that suspension of disbelief in being the character tends, for many gamers, to be peculiarly likely to lead to that triple-plunging. And the whole reason why it's so important to so many gamers is that that likelihood is (for them, not for everyone) so keen. And the key ingredient in that process is the suspension of disbelief in being the character.

      The association between suspension of disbelief and engrossment is merely an association, though a close one.

      If a game delivers on suspension of disbelief (for Person A), then it will deliver (for Person A) what a person typically expects/seeks in a game of immersive roleplay. Person A might be the sort to experience deep or light engrossment in that experience of suspended disbelief, but in either event, will be able to find what they're seeking in the game. Because, as far as general game design goes, the same tools deliver suspension of disbelief, whether you are deeply or lightly engrossed therein. Those tools do vary somewhat from gamer to gamer, but the same tools that produce light engrossment in suspension of disbelief also produce heavier engrossment for the same gamer, if applied with greater intensity.
