    \n I read that article. I once designed a sport, which I called tagball. It was something like baseball played on a football (American-style) field. One team (offense) serves (volleyball style) a ball to the other (defense), then tries to send a runner to the other end of the field to score a goal. The other team tries to catch the ball then have the guy with the ball tag the runner out. I think the way I had it was that offense gets four tries to score a goal; there's some way one side or the other halts the current play to start another one, I can't remember. It's never been playtested, either.

    \n About 20 years ago at summer camp, I helped develop a game we called Oikos. You play on a basketball court with two teams of two players. Each team consists of one forward armed with a tennis racket and one keeper armed with a lacrosse stick. Each team must stay on their own half of the court. Behind each basket was one of those cushiony mats affixed to the wall (presumably to prevent injury on breakaway layups). The game is easy: You score a goal by hitting your opponent's mat with the tennis ball. No hands. Obviously, the forwards would try to smash the ball. But there was a really nifty surprise play, where the keeper lofted the ball at just the right angle over the opposing team's basket. If done just right, the ball would strike the back wall, bounce off the back of the backboard and carom onto the mat.

    Like I said, that was 20 years ago, and I still think about it fondly today. A lot of fun coming up with that game.

    \n In grade 3 or so me and my friends 'designed' a red rover/tag/en prison variant called 'Spider' whose rules I no longer remember -- I just remember that we played it for months, and that we were often making up new rules and settling disputes. We taught some of the lesser grades to play, and reserved a particular part of the schoolyard, where the older grades didn't tend to hang out.

    I'm sure the game itself was nothing special, but the feeling of having invented it was memorable and awesome -- even though I bet nearly identical variants already existed beforehand. Of course, playground games may not have the rigor necessary to qualify as 'sport design', but whatever. In retrospect hardly a week went by as a kid that didn't involve inventing some sort of sports game or new set of rules for existing games.

    \n Some friends of mine invented a game using croquet mallets, croquet balls & BBC-style commentary called "Not Gay Croquet," and we've been playing it for years. Essentially, you hit the balls around on the field and try to get them to touch the other balls to store points. It has its own terminology, such as scoring a point being called a "Poompf," to go after scoring being called a "Courtesy Thwack," and to score again on the Courtesy Thwack called a "Double Poompf." A "Gimme" is when your ball is within a mallet's width of another ball but not close enough to touch, and you score a Poompf then. We also have rules regarding "Gardening" (changing the landscape) and "Children and Pets" (in that if a toddler or pet changes the location of a ball, it is considered fair game wherever it lands -- this has lead to bribing of children to muck with the game in the past, though toddlers can rarely be replied upon to serve only you.)

    There are a few variants to play. English (or the default) involves Rock-Paper-Scissors to determine who goes second, third, etc. (Whoever has the black ball always goes first.) Australian rules involve dueling with the mallets as fencing weapons to setting who goes in what order and determines who scores on a Gimme. A couple years back, we invented Brazilian rules, which is where you use your bare feet instead of a mallet.

    This game's evolved over the last decade and probably was invented while my friends were drunk, but it's been a lot of fun for a silly, backyard sport.

    \n We used to play "Completely Stupid" in slow lunch hours. There were these great big sun umbrellas, with big plastic drums filled with water that held them up. The drums were circular, maybe two foot in diameter, and about ten inches thick. Filled with water, they were about as heavy as could be easily lifted and hefted around. The game involved standing about four or five yards apart, and simultaneously hurling the thing at the other player's legs, skimming it across the ground. The art of the game was to hurl the thing hard and accurately, but not to unbalance yourself enough to dodge out of the way of the leg-breaking doom-discus unleashed by the other player. I can't remember how we came up with the name for this game.
