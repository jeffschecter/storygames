    I had a similar experience in my group. I divide game-play into "action scenes" (framed by the GM) and "personal scenes" (framed by the players), but I set things up so that the personal scene had to end in some kind of conflict. The players complained that the personal scenes were too confrontational and sometimes they just wanted things to develop without using any mechanical resolution. We agreed to allow personal scenes to come to a "natural" resolution, if everyone involved felt no formal conflict was necessary.

    Natural resolution has a downside as well, though. A few of my players are good at talking themselves out of trouble, so they frequently ended their scenes "naturally" without any conflict at all. However, I am using the opposing stakes to drive the story forward. The "natural" resolution often meant I had no follow up and no real idea where the player wanted to take the story, since they never formally said what they wanted.

    I haven't found a way out of this impass yet, other than discussing the issue with the players and emphasizing the role of conflict stakes as a way of telling me (the GM) where they want things to go. To compensate, I've pushed them harder to frame scenes properly, so I get direction at the beginning of scenes. We are still using "natural" resolution, but I push harder on players that seem to be excessively conflict shy.

    Brand, Tony... brilliant.

    I also want to underline Tony's point that the issue of conflict doesn't have to be an issue of game mechanics. You can 100% have players engaging in game mechanics in scenes with no conflicts. The Wrestling game I'm working on has two types of scenes. Getting Over and Fighting. Getting Over is building investment in your character, their goals, feuds, and future fights. They often will have no conflict but still involve lots of game mechanic effects. Fighting is where the conflict explodes, informed by and properly invested in by the scenes that came before it.

    Andy, I've had most of these issues come up in PTA as well. Or rather, I should say I think conflict heavy scenes are often appropriate for PTA and for modeling certain types of TV. But often a lot of players who love PTA try to apply the same scene framing to other games where it may be less appropriate.

    In PTA, when a player frames a scene, they declare the scene's focus (to develop a protagonist or advance plot), agenda (what the conflict may be), and location. Then the GM (producer) takes this info and describes what the audience sees to start things off. Then let's the player take over. You are actively encouraged to push toward conflict. If there is no conflict, the GM should create one. The earlier the conflict the better. And if there is no conflict, players should put a halt to things and say, "what's the conflict?"

    But I feel like the second you say "what's the conflict", the momentum and emotional investment derails. If we have to ask that, then I don't think a conflict is worth having. And I suspect the issue is less with scene framing and more with buildup and investment.

    I agree on the need to find the balance between "foreplay" and "conflict", with the usual caveats that the right balance depends on the players, the group mood, etc.

    I also think it is useful to think on what each element contributes to the gaming experience. I believe my players enjoy the "foreplay" part of the scene because of the immersive experience of being their character. The conflict mechanics can definitely jar you out of that. Also, the conflict mechanics are often confrontational, and my players want some character-to-character interactions that are not confrontational.

    I find that conflict is necessary for the reasons other people identified: it drives the forward story and helps you decide "what happens next" when there is no clear resolution to an interaction. I think this is important because if all your interactions have a clear resolution, the story is likely predictable and uninteresting.

    For me, conflict resolution also gives the players an "out of character" way of expressing which direction they want the story to take. This is important, because it isn't always easy to indicate what you want in character. Also, sometimes player-interests and character-interests are not the same. That's very common for me as a player; I generally want my own characters to suffer.

    I also agree, as some people have commented, that conflicts don't have to be confrontational. Or more accurately, they can have cooperative elements as well as confrontational elements. My players complained that "We are spending all our time arguing with the NPCs", so I worked with them to craft conflicts that have them cooperating with some friendly NPC against another, unfriendly NPC. That way, whether they win or lose the conflict, their characters can still bond with the friendly NPC, which is what they wanted.
