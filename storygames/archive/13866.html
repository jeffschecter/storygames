\n For all of you who were certain that Microscope would never, ever get done, tear up your slip, because you just lost that bet.

Microscope is out. You can buy and download the PDF immediately. Books are on the way, and you can preorder one now.



Oh, and if right now you're saying "uh, dude, what the crap is Microscope?" let me give you the scoop: It's a fractal role-playing game of epic histories.\n
You start with the big picture, the grand scheme of your history, then zoom in and explore all the nooks and crannies. The more you play, the more complex the history becomes. Your once simple summary becomes a detailed tapestry, full of meaning and surprises. It\u2019s fractal gaming.

Want to leap a thousand years in the future and see how an institution shaped society? Want to jump back to the childhood of the king you just saw assassinated and find out what made him such a hated ruler? That\u2019s normal in Microscope. You have the freedom to move around and examine whatever you want, defying limits of time and space.

You have vast power to create... and to destroy. Build beautiful, tranquil jewels of civilization and then consume them with nuclear fire. Zoom out to watch the majestic tide of history wash across empires, then zoom in and explore the lives of the people who endured it.
\nI haven't talked much about Microscope around here recently because I've been heads down getting it done, but there are about a zillion people here who deserve thanks for playing it and helping me finally finish it.

    \n Johnzo pretty much nailed it on the head. On each player's turn they decide what part of the history they want to explore (aka expand) and then pick a scale to build: do they create a massive new Period, create a new Event describing something that happens in a Period someone else already made, or drill down into the nitty gritty of a particular Event and make a Scene showing us what really happened, right there, right then, with those people.\n
    Posted By: johnzoEach card must be judged as morally good or morally bad, which is very interesting, because it guides the fiction in surrounding cards and helps characterize the fictional people who are recording the history.
    \nI should point out that Microscope doesn't presume any fictional historians -- the only people looking down on the history are the players. It seems like a minor point but it's actually kind of critical. What you add to the history is real, not the subjective viewpoint of some fictional observer.

    Of course within the history there are plenty of cases where people misremember, distort, or flat-out make up facts. We see a teacher tell her students how colonists peacefully settled this planet a century ago, but then we can zoom back and see what really happened. Is what she said true? No? Then maybe we'll zip back to the future and see if she was lying or just clueless. Wait, she lied? Why would the teacher lie to her students? Let's zip to another part of the history and find out.

    \n Debt got the axe. It wasn't needed anymore. Now that Scenes use Push / finger-voting, the players can directly decide whether they think something makes sense. If they think it goes against the Tone, they can vote it down. If they think it makes sense even if it completely defies Tone, they can vote it up.

    This fixes the troublesome cases where Debt might say "there must be Dark!" and maybe the players agreed there should be more Dark but right now someone has a good Light idea that would fit perfectly, but they couldn't do it because the Debt blocked them. Not cool. Fiction is complicated, so there are always cases where seeming contradictions make wonderful sense to the players.

    I don't kid around when it comes to killing sacred cows.

    Thursday

      \n Yep, I advise against immortal characters in the book for exactly that reason. But as I said in the book, it's not always bad. For those who haven't read it yet, I'll save time and quote:\n
      MicroscopeImmortality has similar problems. It can work if lots of characters are immortal (like pantheons of gods), but if immortality is a special trait of just one or a few characters, they may hog the spotlight (\u201cnot Doctor Lazarus again!\u201d). Another good rule of thumb is never to have character lives span more than one Period since that starts to weld adjacent Periods together. Once you\u2019re thinking about lifespan, you start to estimate precisely how many years must have passed, which locks things down.
      \nI do find that Microscope games where part of the concept is immortality (gods, your Highlander game idea Tim) work great. The problem is usually when someone likes a character, and so they keep bringing them back, but you're kind of sick of them. The aforesaid Doctor Lazarus.

      So how do you handle it? The rules give you some solid protections from spotlight hogging figures, whether they're immortal or not. If you're making the Scene, you can outright ban a character you don't want ("and I ban the Sentient Sun..." True story. Somewhere Tony and Paul are weeping). The flip side is that if someone else is making the Scene, they _can_ include any character they want, but of course they'll be the last one to pick who they play, so you can also guarantee someone else plays that character if you're tired of hearing them do the funny voice for the King of the Frogs, or whatever. It is impossible for a player to maintain a monopoly on a character.

      If you think certain characters are starting to span too much history, you have another simple solution: kill them. Or show them being born/created. Either one bookends them in history, so you know they didn't exist before X, and they don't exist after Y. As always they're not out of the game (nothing is ever out the game in Microscope) so anyone who wants to explore them more can do so in the history they're already a part of, but they can't expand farther.

      And don't forget the Palette. If you don't want immortals, just say No at the outset. If someone does want long-lived characters that span multiple Periods, the Palette is the right time to ask if it's okay to have that in the Yes column.

      \n Smaller-scale histories can work. You just have less latitude to create broadly (of course) and some of the social steam valves built into the game will have less power to help you.

      Limited physical space is a bigger problem than limited time. If you play a game that takes place in a small area (like or a single town or a colony ship, which we've done) then big changes are inescapable, and likewise known facts in the future can prevent you from being able to make changes (if colony ship is around at the end, you can't crash it into the sun in the middle). But if you have a huge physical space to work with (like all the many theatres of WWII) then even with a small timeline you have latitude to jump around.\n
      Posted By: Risus MonkeyI get a sense that you still don't want to be specific about dates, but there will undoubtedly be associations with trends or events that place things (more or less) specifically in time.

Of course you could wind up with a bunch of Periods "in the 70s" or none in some decades. The distribution doesn't have to be even at all, but of course if you're paralleling real history you're going to know in the back of your mind that you have skipped over some spans of time.\n

      Posted By: Ben RobbinsIf you think certain characters are starting to span too much history, you have another simple solution: kill them.
      \nI should point out that this sounds like a fairly aggressive move ("Ha, that guy you like gets killed!") but in Microscope it isn't. On a huge timeline, death is a natural part of any character's story. You could even say you don't really know a character's complete story until you see how they die, because that's how it really ends.

      When players are really attached to a character, they'll often choose to jump ahead and declare how the character they like dies -- literally killing their darling. Maybe the character had a rough life, but they want to show them at peace, surrounded by loved ones, or satisfied that all their sacrifices meant something. No matter what else happens, we know that's how their story ends. No one can take that away.

      \n Saying someone disappears without a trace, leaving no body, practically _screams_ for them to come back later. I mean, really.
