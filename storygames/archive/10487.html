\n So Vincent introduced this idea of "IIEE with teeth". I think it's a great design technique that he's getting at, but I feel that it's ultimate significance is starting to get a bit exaggerated.

So here's an alternate challenge---rather than worry if so-and-so system has teeth to "force" particular player behaviors, why don't we try thinking about design structures that are flexible enough to handle multiple play-styles?

I'm feeling unimaginative, so I'm going to revert to talking about my own game. In The Mountain Witch, individual conflicts are fairly meaningless in-and-of-themselves. The real significance of isolated conflicts---generally speaking---is that they provide fodder for the stew of inter-party relationships.

What that means is that you can have a fictionally nuanced conflict followed by a shallow mechanicallistic conflict, and both will add meaningful content to the larger dramatic tension. Players can tailor the conflict experience to fit their individual preferences, and the larger game won't be affected (generally speaking).

There are certainly larger play techniques that will break the game if they're not followed, but because the mechanics only *indirectly* affect the larger dramatic tension, individual groups have quite a bit flexibility in how they interpret IIEE and such.

    \n After my last post, I had a lightbulb moment about how DitV works versus tMW. I even drew up a diagram:



    In DitV (and IaWA?), the on-going dramatic tension emerges *entirely* out of the game's moment-to-moment fiction. Because of that, there must be a strong interplay between mechanics and (moment-to-moment) fiction, otherwise things would feel disjointed.

    It's different with tMW, though. In tMW, a large chunk of the dramatic tension is caused by the interaction between the Trust mechanics and Dark Fates. Because of that, the game doesn't require a strong interplay of (moment-to-moment) fiction and mechanics the same way DitV does. Even shallow, mechanicallistic play (within reason) gets fed into the dramatic tension, which in turn gets bounced down into the moment-to-moment fiction, connecting everything together.

    Posted By: Dave LucasI don't know, Tim - when someone pulls a gun on you and you know, mechanically, that you'll be sucking down some d10 fallout, that seems like mechanical tension. Without that, the gun doesn't have as much fictional tension.
    \nDave,

    A gun pointed at your face certainly introduces "tension", but I would say that's a tension in the "moment-to-moment", not the "on-going".

    "Dramatic tension" is probably not the best phrase to use, but right now I'm having a hard time coming up with something better. ("Play tension"? "Story-" or "play-dynamic"? I don't know.) Here's what I mean:

    In DitV, on-going play is informed by an emergent sense of morality, right? What does the player think is right & wrong, and how far are they willing to pursue it? This sense of morality is generally informed by the events of the moment-to-moment fiction, but I don't see how mechanics (directly) play into that question at all. The Dogs have both the fictional authority and mechanical power to do whatever they want, so it seems to me that the decision of what's right/wrong/etc is totally up to the player.

    I am NOT saying that DitV's mechanics are divorced from the "on-going dramatic tension", what I'm proposing is that there's a chain of events. The mechanics inform the moment-to-moment fiction, which in turn inform the "on-going tension". (And the chain works in the opposite direction as well.)

    In tMW, there is a particular "play tension" caused by, or at least exaggerated by, the interplay of the Trust mechanics and Dark Fates: The ronin have to trust one another for mechanical reasons, but can't trust one another for fictional reasons. So unlike DitV, there is direct link between the mechanics and the "on-going tension".

    Now, the idea that has emerged from this thread (for me, at least) is that in order for a game to feel "coherent"---and I mean that in the normal sense, not the jargon-y sense---the game must link up those three bubbles in some sort of chain.

    Since DitV has a missing or weak link on the right side of the triangle, maintaining the bottom link is crucial, hence all the "IIEE w/ teeth" talk. (Is this also the case with IaWA? I'm not familiar with how exactly that game works.)

    Since tMW has a strong link on both the right and left, it can get away with a weak link on the bottom.

    (Are there games with strong bottom and right links, but a missing or weak link on the left? I haven't really thought about it, but I suspect some tactical RPGs might work that way.)
