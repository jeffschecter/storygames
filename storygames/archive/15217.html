    \n I'm not an expert. But this is pretty much my favorite aspect of American history (because I'm fascinated in a "watch a train wreck") sort of way.

    First off, the relationship between the natives of North and South America and the European settlers is long and complex. If you narrow it down to what occurred in what is now the United States, you're still talking about events occurring between the late 1500s to the late 1800s (yes, stuff happened outside those date ranges, but I'm trying to focus on what I'm assuming he wants to look at). So over the course of about 300 years (approximately 15 generations) relationships changed significantly. And even at any given point, a range of different opinions and attitudes were present on both sides.

    So any sort of answer to these questions are, by necessity, going to be summations and generalizations. (And I think that would be key to any sort of RPG portrayal of the subject matter. There are going to be differences of approach and opinion on all sides, which is good for making story stuff happen.)

    Probably the most significant factors in place the whole time were the fractionalization of American Indian cultures and the vast ongoing power differential.

    There are dozens and dozens and dozens of different tribes. I just did a quick count on a culture website and got to 106. And I think that number is probably quite low and represents some clustering. And those tribes had complex relationships with each other - they ofter raided or warred with each other. Many tribe names are actually the unfriendly names used by other tribes to refer to them.

    For a European equivilant, think about the celtic clans of Ireland and Scotland. When the Romans were invading, they'd make an alliance with one clan while attacking another. The same sort of thing happened with the Indians. Now imagine that the Romans not only had big organized armies but also had guns and much better medicine practices. How much easier would it have been for them to conquer?

    The biggest challenge for the Europeans was getting a foothold. So the earliest contact with Indians were on the eastern coast, like the Algonquian speaking tribes, not on the plains. Initial contact was relatively tentative, cautious, and generous. This time period is where we get things like the story of the first Thanksgiving. This is also the time period of things like Lewis and Clark and Pocahontas.

    Later, once a foothold was established, expansion began. That's where you get the treaty breaking. Every couple of generations the population of Europeans would double. And they'd need/want more space. So they'd break the treaties their grandparents made and expand. These were things that happened over periods of like 50 years, not 5, for the most part. 50 years is a pretty long time for a treaty to last in most any period of history (yes, some last much longer. But many many don't.)

    After a while, the expansion happened over and over again. Regarding this time period, you should look up the term Manifest Destiny. The term itself was only used for a relatively brief period, but it's a pretty good summation of what a lot of Europeans felt about westward expansion.

    Also, you might want to look at the book Guns, Germs, and Steel. Not everyone agrees with the theories in there, but they're certainly interesting. And it's a good look at conquering that took place over lots of history and points out how a lot of similar patterns show up.

    There's some thoughts to get started.

    \nThat's a very broad question, since the American Indians were in no way unified culturally or politically. There is a huge diversity of languages, culture, and customs. The main reason for breakdown of relations was generally over land use - the English colonials always wanted more land for herding and farming, and tended to view Indians as an obstacle to that. Treaties were frequently broken when the colonials wanted more land. That was why early on the French trapper colonials in Canada tended to have better relations, since the trappers did not want to push Indians off their land, and were usually willing to pay for use.

    The last question really depends on the specifics of the time and place. Basically, if a Prairie Indian tribe at that point has had bad relations with colonials recently, then they can expect suspicious treatment at best. However, if they have had few recent clashes, then they may be curious and positive. Relations got better and worse at various times, and differently for different tribes.

    \n Halito. Chim achukma? Achukma hoke.

    I will preface this by saying that I am Choctaw. My ancestors were the first tribe to walk the Trail of Tears. I grew up on the Coeur d'Alene reservation, and I graduated from medical school having earned both my medical degree as well as a Certificate in Indian Health. I cannot speak for Native America, and while grounded in a strong sense of history, my opinions will likely cause some who fancy themselves as historians to take umbrage, but I think that you do deserve an answer to your question.

    First, I think you need to narrow your question some. North America is a huge place, and as such, there were millions of Native Americans, and it was not at all a homogeneous place culturally. My ancestral people are way different than the people who I grew up with, and my Navajo friends are way different from my Ojibwe friends. Thinking of Natives as one people, especially in a historical sense, would be like considering all Asians throughout history to be the same. Huge mistake. As such, the only commonality in the history is Native people's interaction with a seemingly never ending invasion, and even this is a little too simplistic considering that England, France, Spain, as well as the US government all interacted with Native people in ways that would lead to prosecution for war crimes in the modern world. Not an invasion by a single political entity or united people. Just a bunch of different people, all with basically the same disregard for the rights of the aboriginal people of North America.

    As for why they went sour, consider hanging out in your neighborhood, when suddenly a bunch of new and highly armed and violent people show up and kick you out of your house, telling you that it is now theirs. We'll call these new people the White family. They tell you, "Don't worry, we just want this house. You take a different house, or maybe go live with your Aunt across the street." At first you figure that there are plenty of houses, and we can all get along, so you move two houses down the block. The White family practically starves that first winter, and after helping them out with some food and such, they make it through that year.

    Next thing you know, Mr. White calls his brother up and invites him to come live in the neighborhood. To prove that it is a good place, Mr. White sends the severed hands of several local kids to his brother. That gets everyone excited. You are just settling down to watch a football game (the Redskins are playing the Chiefs), when the door gets bashed in, your son is shot in the face, your daughter is raped, and you are thrown out of your house again. You are told that, in order for there to be any peace, you have to move all the way across the Mississippi river! Fuck! You don't really want to go, but next thing you know, the Feds have put a bounty on the skin the grows on your head! And then that stash of blankets that the White family gave you to say thanks for keeping us alive that first winter, it was coated in biological weapons, and now most everyone you know is sick or dead.

    I think that this is getting close to the point in history that you were most interested in, but consider that there subsequently was a national policy made to move Native people as far away from their ancestral lands as possible, children were taken forcefully from parents and enrolled in boarding schools where they were beaten for speaking their own language, Native spiritual practices were considered to not count with regards to the constitutional right to freedom of religion and were made illegal, and eventually entire tribes were told that they no longer existed in an effort to eliminate any Native cultural identity. It was only in the early 1970s that Native people were able to practice their cultural and spiritual practices without fear of being jailed.

    I think that in the end, I have to ask "Which tribe's experience with stolen ancestral lands, organized policies of cultural extermination, indiscriminate violence and concerted acts of war to include biological weapons, and repeated broken international treaties are you most interested in?" They are all similar, but happen at different times, were tolerated to different degrees, and had different results.\n
    - main reasons to why relations between white settlers and indians went sour?
    Probably because there was no regard for the fundamental human rights of Native people shown by the white settlers. Lying to, stealing from, killing, raping, or otherwise doing anything you want to Native people was not only considered OK, it was actually subsidized by and even perpetrated by the federal government!\n
    - what main elements was present in negotiations between whites and indians?
    The main element was that white people wanted Native land. So they offered peace in return for the land. "We will stop shooting you in the face if we never see your face around here again."\n
    - why was treaties broken, again and again?
    Because native people were living in places that white people wanted to. So white people ignored the fact that an international treaty was signed acknowledging the right of the Native people to live there, and so some people got shot over it. Again and again.\n
    - if I was a white man visiting a indian prairie-tribe, early in the settling of America; how would I be met?
    Depends on which tribe, when you showed up, what the tribe's experience with white people had been, how you acted, and how many friends with guns you brought with you.

    I should probably stop there before I get too emotional about it. I apologize ahead of time if anyone is offended by my treatment of the subject. It is too huge to get at the subtleties in a forum post, took me both a lifetime of personal experience as well as a formal program of education over 4 years to get at, and ultimately it comes down to racism and complete disregard of the human rights of an entire continent of people. I am also a little sensitive because of the recent observance of Columbus day, and having to explain to people why I do not celebrate it.

    Posted By: AndyTomas, as a point of curiosity, were there issues in Norwegian history that regarded indigenous people, or cultural sublimation/genocide of one people over another?
    Yes, there were.

    I'm partly sami myself, and have knowledge of how they were treated. As late as the 70s sami children were sent to boarding schools, were they were beaten for talking sami. They were forbidden to practice their beliefs. Whatever rights they had were undermined by an unholy alliance of state, church and greedy private entrepreneurs. Judges were as biased as the rest of society, so no justice were given to a sami man who was tricked or mistreated. Any small "rebellions" (victimized people protesting, amongst other things by boycotting official shops, and their liqueur) were met with guns and hangings.

    The same happened to the romani people in Norway. Children were taken away, and given to foster-families, to be "assimilated". They were often mistreated in their foster-families, and regarded as free labor on farms. At schools such children were systematically vilified and mistreated by teachers and peers. They were considered primitive and dumb, and "lucky to be given proper education" (yes, to beat and vilify a child is very proper, you know). Adult romani were forced to sterilize, to ensure that the "problem" would go away.

    In later years I've looked into the sami part of our modern history, and found that the hurt is still there. Anger and shame is still smoldering in many a soul, and in many a small village, especially in the north. Old men and women learned to be Norwegians the hard way, and some of them still hides the fact that they are sami. I talked with a sami man last year, up north. A friend told that the man was actually sami, and that he had experience with boarding schools, and that interested me. So I asked him about it ... Ouch! He stiffened and his smile died. He turned and walked away. No answer, and no word of goodbye. He just left.

    Healing is taking place, with pride and positive cultural identity building. But a lot of sami people are deeply hurt, and that makes it hard to talk about their experiences. It also makes some of them act with little empathy towards others, as well as their own. The healing of a mistreated society and a vilified culture is painfully slow.

    The same took place in Sweden, regarding both sami and romani.

    In Denmark you have the "problem" with inuits on Greenland. Them being so primitive, their children were taken in early age and sent over the sea to Denmark, to "be given the benefits of civilization". There they were placed in foster-homes, dressed up in strange clothes, not knowing anything about the culture, the climate or the language. Not knowing at all where they were. And they were met with little love by foster parents, those being instructed to be "very firm in raising these wild children". I've read some instruction-manuales from the time, and they had some interesting paragraphs on inuit hygiene. Imagine being 5 years old, having such an experience; what a trauma! I believe this particular practice ended in the early 70s. There are lots of really heartbreaking tragedies in that history.

    Normality has been a brutal tyrant in most parts of the world, at some time in history.

    \n Two books if you're interested:

    Lies My Teacher Told Me, this one is fairly brief and a pretty quick read. The book is about some of the historical inaccuracies taught in American high school, it isn't exhaustive though. Only a couple chapters deal with Native Americans, but the one on Columbus will have some really good historical info for you. It'll also give some great examples of how a people can be willfully ignorant of the crimes they or their fore bearers commit. There are a couple more sections on slavery and racism, which is tangentially related.

    A People's History of the US, is closer to a complete general history text, it covers a lot of the bad shit that happened in the US.

    In America, we like to envision that we forged this country from wilderness. White people arrived and "settled' the land. There is some speculation that the Black Death was carried to America by fisherman who would resupply before heading back to England. Whatever it was, it's estimated that it killed some 90-96 percent of the population. Shortly after the Mayflower landed, they sent someone to entreat with a tribe they had no contact with yet, about 40 miles away, all they found were skulls and bones, hundreds if not thousands. The pilgrims didn't settle land, they just moved in.

    That story got repeated time and again, though not always with the tribe being dead before the settlers arrived (as Nameless described quite well).

    The racism present I think has it's roots in two European events, or at least the ideologies that allow for racism. One is the history of conflict between the English and the Irish. The Irish for a long time have been treated as little better than slaves, not for individual failings, but usually just because they're Irish. It's part of the nature of conflict, you dehumanize the enemy, if you do it long enough, that dehumanization itself becomes a reason to continue the conflict.

    The other would be the Spanish Inquisition. There had been other inquisitions before, but there was a notable shift with this one. Previously, when a Jew converted, they were considered now to be Christian (baring relapses, secret rituals, etc). During the Spanish Inquisition, that never really happened, they continued to be prosecuted and even the children of Jews were seen as unable to be Christian, because they were born to Jewish parents, it became a matter of lineage.

    A lot of the influences that produce alcohol and drug addictions are cultural. Indigenous people had everything taken away from them, given shit land and looked down upon. Their children stolen from their homes, given demeaning educations and taught how their people are lazy and stupid. People born into poverty often stay in poverty, not because of genetics, but because they get crappy educations and told they'll never amount to anything. People in poverty have higher chances of drug and alcohol abuse, which certainly doesn't get you out of poverty. It's a very self-perpetuating cycle.

    The church isn't necessarily the cause of a lot of these cultural problems, but rather a tool used by people to perpetuate and perpetrate what they are doing. Against the native peoples, it was always God's will. The City Upon a Hill is a common theme in America. It was God's will that white people spread over this land and make it their own, it was their promised land, paradise on earth. We were special... but that "we" didn't include the native peoples.

    As an aside, this is a really good documentary series about America and our relationship with religion. God in America

    I haven't really discussed this topic much in the past 10 years, but I'll see if I can't refresh myself to help you out, as its a collection of topics I find extremely fascinating. A lot of it comes down to the process of dehumanizing "other peoples" and how it shapes the relations between people and the people who do the dehumanizing.

      So the goal is to explore the racism and xenophobia inherent in colonialism, in a somewhat generic situation, using fantasy as an analogy to remove people from the easily taken stance that "Humans are humans, duh... These humans have rights too." Tomas, you seem to have found what I have already stated is a perfect combination to basically trap your players into recreating events similar to the European Invasion of North America.

      Were these my goals, I would approach it slightly differently than you have. There are a few elements that I would insist were present, and I would avoid any traps that are easy to fall into that make your scenario problematic for me.

      1) Both colonizers and indigenous people need to be "human." This does not mean that they have to be human by fantasy definitions, it means that the things that we think of as humanizing elements in relationships must be present on both sides. It is absolutely necessary for the players to eventually come to the conclusion that both the colonizers and the indigenous people are understandable, and that there are no real villains in this scenario. When there are true bad guys, you eliminate the hard choices and difficult situation that is possible with this scenario. Know both sides well enough that you can sympathize with them before you play them. Make the orcs real to yourself, as a culture and as individual characters, before you bring the into play. Caricatures will not suffice when you want believable feelings. Allow the orcs to love, to be afraid, to be loyal, to have families, to have dreams and ambitions, to laugh, to cry, to have hope, and to be backed into a corner in despair. Make them real people, or the impact of the players killing them will be nil. If you do not make them real people, understandable people, respectable people, you are just perpetuating many hollow memes about indigenous people, and are furthering racism. You can do better than that.

      2) Utilizing players' inherent biases will enhance the impact of the scenario. Using orcs is a great idea. In this instance, you can use all that the players come to the table with to really add some punch to the situation. When players assume they know about orcs, then they are doing exactly what most colonialists do with the indigenous people. They bring tons of bias to the table and then justify horrible acts based on these biases. Orcs allow this. Use it. Do not give them easy outs by making them caricatures of easily recognizable real world cultures that have been oppressed. Just make them tribal orcs without the death cult evilness. Make them totemic or something, still scary to the players, but not evil or degenerate. The orcs have to not be degenerate or the truth of the scenario will be lost. The tragedy of colonization is the assumption by the colonizers that the indigenous people are degenerate and have no rights, somehow beneath the colonialists. The orcs have to be deserving of human rights, or there is no impact to the scenario.

      3) There is strong danger that you will make the players feel lied to or tricked. Don't allow them to deny the impact that this scenario can have by blaming their actions on you for tricking them. The orcs should not behave in ways that are in any way aberrant/immoral. The orcs do not need to piss on each other, they do not need to act in strange ways. This perpetuates the falsehoods and racism that indigenous people have had to deal with, and provides justification to the players for doing bad things. Explaining to them afterwards that this is how orcs behave in a friendly manner will not change the fact that you had them piss on the player characters. There is no way that anyone can see this as anything but aberrant and aggressive. Don't let the players have this easy cop out. Play the orcs as different, but not aberrant. Do not fall to the seemingly easy choice to create a feeling of "other" by making them degenerate. The players bring enough feeling of "other" with them when you call them orcs. Subtlety is better in this instance.

      4) Use lies and misunderstanding to further the animosity that will drive the situation. Make the orcs good and deserving of respect, but make the colonists tell stories of how they are not. Create situations where things are misinterpreted. Maybe a girl is missing at the same time as the orcs are in the area. Maybe a colonist beats his wife to death, then frames the orcs. Maybe some sheep escape and are legitimately hunted down by orcs. Maybe a berry patch is "raided" by the orcs because there is no concept of ownership of plants/animals. These can ramp up aggression between the two peoples. Eventually, some of the more aggressive factions on each side will want to take action. The girl is missing, lets go get her back! Your wife was killed by orcs, lets get revenge! They stole some of your sheep, we should go and arrest the sheep rustlers! These events can cascade. This can only have the tragic impact that you are seeking if it all starts out innocent, but is misinterpreted, or starts out sinister in the form of prejudice and lies that are told to the PCs. The PCs should be allowed to "do the right thing" based on what they hear and what they assume, only to find out that their assumptions lead them to actions of injustice. This will be a difficult trail to walk because you have to make the players feel that YOU have not lied to them, even if they have been lied to.

      5) Give the players the opportunity to see things as they are. Never paint the indigenous people as evil or degenerate. They can be aggressive or violent, but this has to be understandable violence or you are back to the tired trope of mindlessly aggressive and violent savages. This will ruin your scenario. The colonialists can see them as degenerate, but make these feelings believable. The colonialists view of the world must be "real" to you. You have to be able to play it straight faced based on what the colonialists "know". But make all this bullshit. The whole point is that racism is easy, believable, but wrong. Anything else reverts to business as usual orc killing.

      6) Reveal and play out this scenario over a long time. Too short a time and it will be hard to make it real to the players. It will seem a caricature of all the trite shit that has been talked about in this thread, both the racist shit and the oversensitive liberal PC bullshit. Only time in play will allow you to make this have real meaning, and not be a bunch of preachy crap. Use AW style Announce Future Badness moves to increase tension, to justify future stances and events, to make it real to the players. Make them feel like they have lived it, not that they were told it.

      These ideas should get you a long way toward what you want, without falling into the trap of perpetuating the racism inherent in the usual indigenous stereotypes. Use player bias, put the PCs in the middle of a conflict between two equally good peoples, create lies and misunderstanding to justify bad actions, build tension slowly and as a believable consequence of actions that are reasonable to the perpetrators from their perspective, present the truth about the situation as GM while presenting lies about it as the NPCs, and do not let the kettle boil over until the PCs are thoroughly confused about what is right or wrong in this situation. When the players can understand every side and they have interests on every side, make the situation erupt and make them chose. Give them a hard choice. See where things line up, and even give them a chance to argue amongst themselves. If you can pull that off, it will be a campaign of legendary status.

      The main pitfalls of this exercise will be creating "facts" in your world out of racist stereotypes, not tapping into real biases but instead leading players astray yourself, and not creating cultures that are truly sympathetic. I am pretty sure that your original presentation will fall victim to each of these.
