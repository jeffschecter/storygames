\n What games lack external opposition to the actions of PCs, and how do they set up whatever opposition there is?
Off the top of my head, the only games I've read like this are Lacuna (I believe players roll and try to beat a particular number and can keep pushing/rolling but it becomes more dangerous the more they do so?) and Otherkind (in my specific case, Psi-Run) and things based on it (players roll a number of dice and assign them to categories, which determines how they succeed and how they get hurt). What other games have rules that pretty much put players in charge of whether they succeed?

I ask because I've been thinking a lot about my nascent game idea last night and today, and I've been trying to decide whether it would be interesting to allow the PCs to "succeed" at anything they are capable of attempting, while having the dice determine in what way they succeed. For example, if an NPC attempts to mug the PC, and the PC is capable (as determined by his traits) of violence and the PC chooses to do so to stop the mugging, there would be no doubt that the PC succeeds. The dice would, however, determine whether the PC just roughs up the mugger and runs him off, or whether the PC, for example, takes the mugger's gun and shoots him 5 times in the head.
under this idea, choosing not to be violent, and thus allowing the mugging to occur, would have negative story effects (the PC is mugged!) but positive mechanical effects (accruing some form of player currency to spend to exert greater control over the outcome of other events).

    \n You might want to look at The Committee For the Exploration of Mysteries.

    It has opposition but success is guaranteed. It's hard to describe exactly how it works. Basically the opposition player rolls a bunch of d10s which sum up to a target number. The acting player then tosses narration back and forth between himself and the opposition. Each time the active player narrates he puts forward two d6s pulled various sources. Then the opposition narrates a set back. Back and forth.

    The idea is that the active player is trying accumulate enough d6s to be the TN rolled on the d10s... in under three minutes of real time. This is key.

    If time runs out one of two things happens. If the player has enough d6s in front of him to beat the TN and he simply didn't have enough time to play them then the obstacle is literately hand-waved away. You just assume that he did beat it... somehow. You just never say how.

    If on the other hand the player DOESN'T have enough d6s in front of him to beat the TN then every player in the group loses a resource. You go around the group and each player narrates how they contributed to the eventual defeat of the obstacle and why it cost them a resource. But again, success is guaranteed.

    Posted By: fnord3125How (roughly) does it work in that system?
    \nAll challenges, including facing off against non-Big-Monstar NPCs, are assigned a target number. PCs roll and try to beat the number. Drama Points may be spent by PCs to give themselves sizable bonuses. When NPCs act against PCs, the PCs roll a resistance attribute or skill and again try to beat the NPC/challenges' rating. No NPC ever rolls dice unless they are a Giant Monstar.

    This is more like Lacuna than the direction that the rest of the thread went in, tho. The possibility of failure or bad consequences even for minor challenges does exist.
