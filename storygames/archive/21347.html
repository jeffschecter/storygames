\n I\u2019m polishing my GMless Collaborative Story Game, House of Spiders, before I send it to one of the very best and most successful designers in the indie game industry, for its first studio critique. Needless to say, I want to work out any glaring issues before the first critique.

While the game\u2019s major mechanics have been created, I\u2019m looking for inspiration to improve upon the game and to make it better. The game is similar to Game of Thrones and produces stories like those in GoT. It has both elements of cooperation between characters and competition amoung characters, similar to how a game of Fiasco might\u2014however, of course, it has a different a tone, different characters, and different themes, as it attempts to emulate a Game of Thrones type story and not a Coen Brother\u2019s film.

I\u2019m looking for games that have some level of competition amoung the characters, where one player can be a Jon Snow type and another a Cersei Lannister type and another a Little Finger type\u2014game mechanics and procedures that could effectively introduce and handle elements of courtly intrigue and Machiavellian character verse character situations. When I say I\u2019m looking for these things, I don\u2019t mean I\u2019m looking for these specific elements; I mean that I\u2019m looking for mechanics and procedures that would be effective at telling these types of stories\u2014so, in short, mechanics or procedures to do with conflicts between characters. BTW, the major inspirations of the game thus far, mechanically speaking, are Fiasco and Archipelago.

What GMless Collaborative Story Games would fit the bill? That contain an element of cooperation but also character vs character play. If you could list any that would be awsome! Thank you so much!

    \n Off the top of my head, I might suggest looking into Polaris and Remember Tomorrow too, for (GMless) games where the general story structure allows for protagonists to both help and hinder each other at times - but I suspect you're already familiar with both.

    Have you played Spione? It's very focused on intrigue, of course, though perhaps of a different sort. There are a number of little things it does differently than the most beaten path.

    What about the sort of tight relationship drama found in Montsegur 1244? Incidentally medieval in setting, this is all about putting a tight relationship map through the double test of confined quarters and impending doom.

    The last incarnation of Fifth World I've played is a far stretch thematically, but it's fundamentally small-community drama. I can't help but think that the gentry and royalty of a quasi-medieval-Britain setting such as Game of Thrones' are fundamentally a small community, just spread a bit wider geographically. The lessons here are all about the fundamental act of putting characters together in pairs and threes through organic, but strict, mechanics.

    Speaking of small-community drama: I suspect there are lessons to be be taken from The Quiet Year, including its controversial Contempt Tokens (disappointing to a lot of players I've spoken to, I actually love that mechanism and think it's pure genius).

    Sebastian Hickey's Hell for Leather, in its standard setting, is about forced cooperation first, then fighting each other to the last one standing in a deadly arena. It may sound ham-fisted, but enforcing it as part of a multiple-act structure is actually a legit, effective way to introduce such a switch in the mode of play.

    Mist-Robed Gate is of interest in how the Knife Ritual has binding consequences on character behavior, making any conversation into a potentially deadly game of chess.

    Mobile Frame Zero: Firebrands, meanwhile, approaches a comparable thematic goal from the wholly different angle of a "casual" role-playing game. Here, it's simply your objective as a player to get your own character entangled in treacherous relationships and in over their head.

    Serpent's Tooth, by Ross Cowman, is explicitly a game about intrigue - specifically, about conspiring to seize power from its current holder. I haven't yet managed to get a fully satisfactory game of it, though.

    Durance, by @Jason_Morningstar , makes good use of a cast of characters including the representatives of two distinct, facing social hierarchies - which don't necessarily oppose each other at all times.

    Vincent Baker's mini-game Midsummer Wood.

    Elizabeth Shoemaker/Sampat's quintessential mess-it-up game It's Complicated, of course.

    \n You could turn the fish tank into a game procedure. I already done it with how the fish tank creates a mystery in a game I called The Murder of Mr. Crow.

    Another game that already uses the fish tank is Svart av kval, vit av lust, that needs to be translated into English. It's a game about intriguing vampires, and is basically what Vampire should had been.

    While the World Ends is a great game to be inspired from when it comes to competitive play in collaborative storytelling. The only game I played that actually successfully combine those two opposite play styles, and creates really good stories every time. This happens because the competitive part of the game must create fiction in order to work. It's ... just great game design.
