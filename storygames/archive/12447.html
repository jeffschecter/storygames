    \n I'd say that you don't have to write very much as long as what you write is business: tell the others consistently that you're not going to write a lot and stick to it, and the others will adjust their expectations. The problem with not writing much is not that the big writers somehow will drown you out, but rather that if you are always terse and never personalize the terseness, it comes off as untrustworthy: the other guy will imagine that you're talking more with the other players and not talking seriously with him. For instance, I don't consider terseness a problem if you tell me that you're going to be terse (thus nullifying the suspicion that your terseness is a subconscious signal) and then follow that up by going to the point and discussing the things we minimally need to: tell me what you want, tell me what you're willing to give, tell me what long-term cooperation we could have. Anything more is only necessary when you're trying to explain your strategy to the other guy, anyway. Heck, I'm all for bullet-points if the player is clear enough on his strategy to provide them.

    On the other hand, I am more prone to stab you if your terseness is combined with non-communication: if I don't know what you're planning and you refuse to commit to anything plan-wise, then I lose nothing by making prey of you.

    \n Spring 1901. It begins!



    Germany comes out of his corner swinging with a strong anti-'Russian opening! The fleet move to Denmark threatens to keep Eero out of Sweden, and the armies in Prussia and Silesia threaten Warsaw. England's opening is also strongly anti-Russian; expect a convoy to Norway supported by the other fleet. The French moves are temporizing, portending a rapid occupation of Iberia and an uncontested invasion of Belgium although one can imagine Germany sweating out the next 24 hours over Munich. All in all, it looks like England and Germany have agreed to throw in together for a smash-and-grab against Russia, with France either a silent partner or a fly in the ointment.

    Meanwhile, Italy's fairly traditional deployment suggests a convoy to Tunis as his most conservative fall move, since Austria's heavily anti-Turkish opening promises a supported attack on Greece; no opportunities for the Italian here! But there are some tricky judgments to be made. Russia's moves show him gunning for Rumania, but T:Arm-Sev, Bul-Rum conceivably puts Russia down to 3 SCs if Warsaw falls. To the extent that Austria is counting on Russia's big guns against Turkey, he may opt to support Ukr-Rum so that there are Russian boots on the ground pointing toward Anatolia. That being the case, Turkey might roll the dice and support an Italian convoy into Greece, since the worst that could happen from his perspective is that Austria would be denied his second build. A Greek gambit that failed would leave Italy out in the cold, though, and failing to get that first-year build would be a powerful psychological drag on the Italian player as well as potentially a flag for France to come on down to where the water's warm. In any event, the maneuvering suggests that Italy and Austria are working toward some kind of modus vivendi if not outright cooperation against Turkey from the get-go. So you might see Austria using F Adr to support Italy into Greece while A Ser supports Russia into Rumania--although both Russia and Italy would put Austria on the list as second to go after Turkey, so there's a limit to how accommodating you can expect Austria to be to his nearby rivals.

    Is it possible that Eero's reputation has caused his neophyte neighbors to gun for him in the early going? What arguments and expostulations can he deploy to forestall the deadly blow? Is the apparent Austro-Russian collusion similarly a product of the two board leaders getting each others' backs against a ravening horde of wannabes? Whose side is Italy on? Will France make an early break against his Western neighbors while they are marching eastward? The questions are many! Let the kibbitzing commence!

      The Western Triple

      In the East

      Italy and Turkey combine to stop Austria from picking up his second build in Greece. This is an interesting gambit on Italy's part, forgoing a move on Tunis (and thus a build) in order to influence events in the Balkans. Italy's northward march into Tyrolia puts pressure on Trieste, and so Austria has to consider how best to defend. Does he concentrate on defending Trieste, ceding Greece to the Turk? Does he make a play for the Ionian Sea, hoping to grab an unprotected Naples in the fall? Does he try for Greece again, knowing that Italy will probably want to grab Tunis before France gets within striking distance? A lot depends on the diplomatic interaction between Italy and Austria, and Italy's estimation of how far he can prosecute his attack on Austria before being forced to turn his attention to the West.

      Turkey's maneuvering suggests that he prefers a hamstrung Russia to a mutilated one; Chris's withdrawal from Armenia only makes sense in the context of some kind of deal with Eero. But Russia's maneuvering points him at Turkey, since that fleet in Rumania is useless for attacking Austria--except that the Turkish fleet in the Black Sea acts as a disincentive to Russian adventurism. This suggests that Russia's maneuvering is primarily defensive. What kind of deal did Eero make with Chris? More to the point, what kind of deal did he make with Jamey?

      \n Bill's in accord with my own experiences.

      Something to understand about growth is that you don't grow in terms of SCs, primarily. Rather, you grow in terms of stable territory and defensive synergy, and perhaps diplomatic clout (although that last one is debatable). Merely taking SCs is meaningless when those centers and units are situated in ways that do not improve your overall defensive synergy. The real goal in taking territory is to build one of the many stable configurations of SCs on the map, one that allows you to vacate a part of your possessions because units elsewhere keep the enemy out. This is the strength of the Turkish beginning position: it's one of the few that is perfectly defensible with less than the starting units. Of course adding to the three beginning SCs is a problem for Turkey, any SCs close by tend to be hotly contested and difficult to defend.

      Warsaw is actually a basic example of a "dead" SC gain for Germany in the early game: there is no way for Germany to take Warsaw and then vacate it, taking benefit from the gain. In actual truth Germany needs to occupy the place long into the middle-game, practically until he also has Moscow, before he can take any material advantage from the place. The actual power from taking a SC would be the ability to direct more units into wars, but in the case of Warsaw for Germany this ability is very limited: it's one more unit, but that unit is stuck defending Warsaw and doing little else, with the only exception being a war for Moscow, in which the unit in Warsaw will be able to help.

      \n Spring 1905: The Knives Come Out!



      Nathan grabs Vincent's takes Brest while at the same time supporting him in Belgium, albeit unsuccessfully--could he have been trying to slow Germany's advance into France while getting a piece for himself? Or did Vincent trade away a piece of France in order to secure Nathan's help?

Russell has to read Vincent's mind to figure out how to maneuver his armies next turn.

He's got to do it again in the fall, which gives Eero a chance to maneuver; Swe-Bal, Ska-Swe keeps up the pressure on Denmark while threatening Berlin and Kiel. An army built in Warsaw could result in the fall of Berlin in 1906 if Russell can't hold on to Belgium.

      Brian doesn't contest Vincent's conquest of Tunis, instead pushing into the Tyrrhenian from the Ionian. The ironic consequence of Brian's naval victory is that Vincent can retreat into Naples. It looks like he plans to take it back with TyS-Nap, Rom S TyS-Nap, but there's nothing stopping Jamey from ordering Ven-Rom to cut the support. On the other hand, why would Jamey want to help Vincent? He'd probably prefer to use A Ven to stand off A Ser Trieste, thus preventing Tyr-Tri. Maybe Brian can try to grab Munich, either standing off Bur-Mun or getting it and thereby denying it to France. Of course, that helps Russell but not Brian, long-term.

      Jamey taps the Ionian Sea, maybe to cut a possible Ion S Gre-Alb, and finds himself in possession of that crucial body of water. But he's got to worry about Budapest and Serbia. Vie S Bud keeps that city safe, and Ion-Gre, Alb S Ion-Gre might sacrifice the Ionian in order to cut Gre S Bul-Ser.

      Eero's pointing at Denmark and Budapest; expect attacks in that direction unless he decides that Chris in possession of Serbia and the Ionian is too formidable, in which case maybe he'll slide into Armenia and build a fleet in Sevastopol.

      Chris has to decide how to keep the pressure on Jamey and whether Eero needs to be taken down a peg or two. I'd expect him to try to convince Jamey to help him into Rumania, but failing that Eas-Ion, Gre hold, Aeg S Gre, Bul-Ser, Bla-Bul.

      The stakes are high next turn! Some real property is looking to change hands!

      \n Fall 1905: Russia's Great Leap Forward!

      Russell claims to NMR! Is he telling the truth, or was his all-forces-hold strategy part of a complex deception plan intended to make it look like Russia and Germany are falling out with each other? Watch the fleet retreat! Den-Hel bodes no good for England, whereas Den disband suggests a desire to rectify a mistaken fleet build. Den-Bal on the other hand, is convincing . . . but Russia's two builds represent a power-up for Eero that dramatically changes the situation. If he builds fleets in Sevastopol and St. Petersburg, then he intends to carry on against England and Turkey; army builds suggest intentions against Germany and Austria.

      Meanwhile, France takes the pressure off Italy to shore up his home defense; he's got the juice now to take back Brest in the spring . . . but he has to disband a unit before then. Assuming that Vincent needs to keep three units bearing on Brest, he can choose Bur, TyS, or Tun. I like how disband TyS leaves Beau Geste and the rest of the French Foreign Legion stuck in Algeria, a forlorn hope now cut off from their retreating brethren.

      But Vincent really wanted TyS; he even convinced Jamey to support GoL-TyS from the Ionian Sea. Chris's spoiling attack from Eas cut that support, of course, but it's the intention that counts.

      The Austrian is being similarly squeezed; he'll have to disband a unit as well, but unlike Vincent he doesn't have many good options. The one thing he might do is assume that Brian will disband A Tyr and so feel safe to similarly demobilize A Ven, since the fleet in Rome can't sail across the Apennines. But what if Brian throws the dice and leaves A Tyr on the ground, hoping that both F:F Tys and A:A Ven go away? Jamey has enough problems; maybe he'll just disband F Ion in order to get a shot at Greece (though he'll need Russian help to get it).

      Chris has been playing defensively but anti-Austrian; his withdrawal from Bla and Eero's simultaneous move to Armenia portend suggest that Eero is putting the pressure on Turkey as well. I'm not sure what that portends for events next year.

      In Italy, Brian's ambit is much reduced. He'll end the year with fleets in Naples and Rome, a regular Kingdom of the Two Sicilies. But he must feel fortunate in his enemies.

      The situation at the end of 1905:

      Austria goes down one SC to 5, giving up Rumania to Russia.
      England goes up one SC to 4, getting Brest from France.
      France goes down one SC to 5, giving up Belgium to Germany and Brest to England but getting Tunis from Italy.
      Germany holds steady at 5 SC, giving up Denmark to Russia but getting Belgium.
      Italy goes down one SC to 2, giving up Tunis to France.
      Russia goes up two SC to 8, getting Denmark from Germany and Rumania from Austria.
      Turkey holds steady at 5 SC.

      \n Fall, 1906: Italy Is Destroyed! (Delenda Est Italia!)



      Brian didn't stand a chance! Jamey agreed to give Vincent a piece of the Italian peninsula, and he leaped at the opening, charging in from the Tyrrhenian to take Rome. Meanwhile, Brian's last-ditch attempt to grab an Austrian SC hadn't a forlorn hope in hell, as Jamey covered Vienna from Budapest and stood himself off in Trieste from Albania and Venice.

      Meanwhile, Eero supported Jamey's army in Serbia against a Turkish attack so that the Turkish SC loss caused by his own attack on Ankara wouldn't be offset by Turkish gains elsewhere. Interestingly, Chris didn't bother to defend Ankara; instead, he moved Con-Smy, Eas-Ion. What could he have been thinking? I think Eero must have lied to him: "Keep attacking Austria; I'll help you. What, those fleets? Pay them no mind; they are a mere ruse, intended to convince Austria I'm on his side, not yours. Next year we'll ferry them through the Bosporus and they'll go fight France."

      In the north, Eero was not as successful, however. Piers, taking over as Germany from Russell, must have negotiated an at-least-temporary truce with Vincent to secure the Western front, enabling him to hold off the Russian forces charging toward Berlin. The French build in Marseilles can't stop Nathan from taking Spain in the spring, if he wants it. But the exchange seems likely to have been planned, and we may see that English fleet float into the Mediterranean to help out a French Navy that is likely soon to be hard-pressed by Austrian forces, which have local superiority in the Italian peninsula--especially if T:F Ion goes away.

      \n Huh, that's that. Thanks to everybody who played; despite the rather strange mid-game events in Turkey this was a very interesting game for me; part of this was because I think this is the first time I've ever soloed in a postal game that run its course. In Diplomacy the standard victory is a three-way draw, soloes are rare things and all the more so in postal games that allow the opposition to think things through and break the momentum off any solo attack. I am very happy with the level of dedication and responsibility seen in the end-game here, even the minuscule Austrian government-in-exile sent in moves like a trooper up until the end.

      Some notes on the game and performance from the Russian viewpoint:

      Early game

      The western Powers opened the game with a western triple alliance - a challenging move typical of American Diplomacy philosophy, and one that I've only seen once before in actual play. I didn't even know for sure that this was what we had in our hands before the fall moves. Entente or not, my own advice to Germany at the time was the same before and after the moves: a Russo-German war in the very early game is a horribly risky strategy for both parties, but especially for the offensive side, as a large number of units are committed to war against a non-conventional enemy; you're essentially taking away forces from other places to open up a war that you don't need to begin with. It takes a lot of trust to play this opening.

      My diplomacy at the start worked fine with Germany and Austria, while everybody else was more or less lukewarm: Italy didn't see any point in windy talks, France and England were hostile in retrospect (I should've almost expected the northern opening from England from the way he communicated) and Turkey was strangely grumpy (this behaviour continued through the game). For my part I did not have any grand plan for the game to begin with, except for the idea that I wanted to try a northern opening: this is a risky move for Russia, but I'd been thinking about the early game tactics and diplomacy, and I liked some of the synergies present in the implications of Mos-StP.

      In the fall I lost Warsaw, which is a pretty hard blow on Russia at first; Russia is a giant with feet of clay, and she's usually unable to maintain her borders without offensive momentum, and unable to keep up offensive momentum on all borders even without this sort of setback. I had enough presence of mind at this point to realize that Germany was likely playing a western alliance game, which was why I didn't even try to bounce in Warsaw - in Diplomacy it is often a good idea to allow those things you cannot prevent happen as they would so as to not waste your forces on futile struggle and to make your opponent over-commit their forces in the same. My plan at this point was largely to sit tight and wait for the inevitable pressures to do their job: Germany would find it difficult to occupy Warsaw on a permanent basis due to the fact that he still had strong early-game empires on his western flank, and two armies in Warsaw are still a loss of material for Germany elsewhere.

      Here we also see Turkey playing a fleet into the Black Sea for the first time. Now, I have to admit that I held a slight preference for anti-Turkish play from the very beginning of the game here, but that was just tactical, caused by my wish to not slip into a simple and expectable Juggernaut in the south. However, the way Chris played our relationship, my inclination soon blossomed into something of a dislike for the Sultan: he broke NMR, insisted on his peaceful intent and concocted paranoid scenarios of Russian plotting against him. I was already pretty thoroughly antagonized long years before I was ready and tactically willing to try for his throat.

      The spring 1902 was the diplomatic turning-point of the game in that Germany decided to play with me instead of against me. Russel and I had had some good discussions over the first days of the game, and apparently he decided that I was the more trustworthy option as an ally over the western Powers. The shift was quite dramatic and sudden, as can be seen on the map: Germany relinquished Warsaw and let me into Sweden even while England was preparing an attack on St. Petersburg. It is very easy to say in hindsight that Russia would have been dead if not for this turn; it'd be interesting to hear from England and France if they were really plotting to do Germany in next as I was speculating at the time. The French moves certainly seem somewhat ambivalent.

      My own play this year was rather tactical in nature; I was just trying to preserve my skin. Italy was playing an interesting Cha-cha game as far as I could discern, while Turkey and Austria were doing an admirable job keeping each other out of Russia. A very common weakness of northern openings for Russia (and of losing Warsaw early) is that it tends to trigger a feeding frenzy in Russia's southern neighbours who know very well that they're the ones who deserve the lion's share of Russia's bulk for themselves. In this case the southerners were much more placid, however; I suspect that this was because Chris was not making any great diplomatic strides with Austria, either, and thus Jamey felt that he had to gear up against the Sultan.

      In the fall a lot of things happened: both Austria and Turkey took a bit of a shot against me, while I arranged to fuck up England beyond all recognition - a suitable revenge for his very anti-Russian play so far. The dual attack on Holland and Norway was arranged with Germany, as was the bounce in Warsaw, all executed beautifully. England dropped back to three SCs at this point, which took much of the steam out of his system. I also hear that Nathan tried to retreat to StP out of Norway, resulting in a disband, while he would've actually wanted to keep the army in Finland. A stroke of luck for me, that.

      Overall the situation was once more rather grim for me: I was building out of Sweden and Norway despite the loss of Rumania, but Austria had also moved into Galicia while my southern front was all but empty. I was pretty active in diplomacy at this time, of course, trying to arrange for all seven sorts of difficulty for both Turkey and Austria. Especially an Italian attack on Austria's back was something I very much wanted to set up to bail me out of trouble.

      In 1903 I mostly rearranged my forces and brought material from the north to the south while allowing Turkey and Austria as much room as possible for fighting each other, which they admirably did. My German alliance was also going strong; Russel and I talked things over, and most of the time Russel found no fault in the advice I gave him on how to crush the westerners. Jamey in Austria was also a solid ally for me for a while at this point, while Chris in Turkey was grudgingly neutral. Just looking at the board, the game could have gone awfully wrong in the spring of 1903; it was solid diplomacy and knowing how the relations between the other players were developing that allowed me to sleep at night.

      It was around this time that I started to seriously notice how very strangely Vincent was conducting his diplomacy as France: I think that I only ever got one message from him early on, so I stopped writing him around this time, too. For the rest of the game I gave him nothing and got nothing from him. A pretty hardcore stance, that.

      \nMiddle game

      By 1904 the immediate pressure on Russia was off, which allowed me to make some strategic plans with my comfortable 6 SCs. Germany was proving a good ally against a stiff western resistance, so I had no trouble working with him indefinitely, especially if I could talk Russel into giving me Denmark at some point. In the south I was playing a hard line: I was firmly in the Austrian camp against Turkey, as that's the way diplomacy was pointing, but I also wanted to have a solid position against Austria - it was obvious to us both that Austria would have to be my next target after Turkey, after all.

      Italy met her doom at this point in time, as French and Austrian forces united to put him out of his misery. The Cha-cha did not serve him well, as it seemed to mostly annoy French and Austrian leadership sufficiently to amass the necessary forces against Brian. Might have been some flaw in diplomacy, or perhaps cha-cha is just not a very smart thing to do.

      After 1903 the game had slowed down considerably, excepting the Italian reduction going on in the mid-Mediterranean. In 1905 I used my love-hate relationship with Austria to my advantage to seize Rumania; I seem to remember that it was agreed that Rumania would have to belong to me, while we would use the seeming war between us to confuse Turkey further. Jamey was likely not happy at all with how the situation was developing, and he certainly skittered this way and that while waiting for the hammer in Galicia to drop. Chris apparently believed that I was fighting with Austria, and why not - I was, after all, no matter how diplomatically harmonious we were. My relationship with Jamey was very harmonious all through the game, in fact, as we discussed frankly the necessities of crushing each other.

      The western front had been slowly grinding an advantage for Germany for a couple of years, and in 1905 we finally got England to join in against France as well. All seemed to be going well until the fall, when Germany NMR:ed. This was a pretty funny thing, as the plan was for him to bounce me in a false war up in Denmark; without his support Denmark fell to me completely unintentionally.

      This was also the year when I finally got my southern front into order against Turkey. It was pretty funny that Chris, after acting paranoid through the whole game, finally believed in me enough to move out of the Black Sea only to find me in Armenia. That combined with the build I got from Rumania gave me a solid front against Turkey.

      End-game

      It was not immediately obvious in 1906 that we'd moved into the end-game, but the factors were all present already at this point from the Russian viewpoint: I couldn't get Russel to make a serious peace with me even as I outright promised him Denmark, which inspired me to send some armies his way to see if there would be any easy gains to be had; this war ultimately shaped out into the great end-game crunch. Meanwhile my Turkish assault began down south, and while nobody else could know it yet, Turkey had already started his suicide-by-Cossack which ultimately enabled me to overrun both Turkey and Austria, making me a fit candidate for a solo victory.

      Regarding the Turkish play here at the end, I have to be very clear in that I still have no idea why Chris chose to suicide by fighting against Austria while letting me take his SCs. Maybe he just felt so very betrayed by Austria? All I got in 1906 was a short message chastising me for my choice, and then a final silence that lasted until the end of the game. Perhaps Chris might tell us what he was thinking here? I considered stopping the game more than once and even discussed this with Jamey, as Chris's actions were verging on collusion: as it was, I ended up just making a careful advance on him while making it very clear to everybody who asked that I had no clue whatsoever as to what was going on.

      I'll write more about Piers in Germany, the Grand Alliance and the tactical niceties of the Russian triumph later separately. Although this latter half of the game dedicated to the end-game crunch was mostly strategic and tactical, it wasn't entirely devoid of diplomacy: first I got beaten by Germany several times due to my inability to believe that he was already completely dedicated to a western alliance, and then I turned the tables by talking Austria into aiding and abetting my rise to power. More on that later.

      \nJust you wait; I'm fully committed to riding the tiger myself whenever I next become the underdog myself. Jamey didn't get a happy ending this time, but that's just because theaters of war outside his control up north turned out unexpectedly well for me.

      Grand Alliance and Me

      In 1907 Piers took control of Germany. He quickly established an anti-Russian alliance with England and apparently France; I never had a good handle on the French play, as Vincent didn't talk to me - the moves seemed like he was giving up Spain to England voluntarily, which would've been a strong play if England proved a strong ally and I became a menace. At this point the threat of my solo was already able to be read from the board, as Turkey's back had been broken and Austria was tactically in a very difficult position regarding me: I was not officially in a war against Austria, but Jamey probably had even less illusions than I did about the course of events.

      Piers played a very, very strong game against me from the beginning. He ruthlessly talked me out of attacking him with promises of cooperation in a situation where he was in hindsight clearly building up a Grand Alliance - an alliance of the remainder of the board against myself. This was a pretty far-seeing action in 1907, as even I myself was not entirely committed to making the last push at this time; I could have imagined just taking Turkey and establishing a fleet presence in Anatolia, then trying to talk Austria into a war against the west. As it happened, Turkey was consistent about throwing his SCs to me, which in combination with my withdrawal from the German border led me into such a good position against Austria that I had no reason to wait; once I started to engulf Austria the need for a Grand Alliance became technically obvious: Russia in possession of the entire Balkans and Anatolia is just a couple of SCs away from victory, and even with just the northern Austrian SCs in my possession it was soon tactically clear that Austria could not prevent me from claiming everything east of Venice.

      England was still fully in the game at this point, which weakened the Alliance somewhat; although I couldn't make any particular headway in fracturing the allies after convincing England to attack France earlier, the mere presence of England on the backsides of France and Germany made the situation somewhat tense for them. England died out of commitment early into the end-game as it happened, changing the field entirely.

      In the fall of 1907 I'd made up my mind about attacking Austria while the Nordic fleet war got into a flying start. I don't think I knew what Germany was doing in Tyrolia at this time, but whether he had anti-Austrian ideas at first, it was soon pretty clear that his role would be to support the crumbling Austrian against me there just as he was cooperating against me with the Englishman up north. France NMR:ed here to relatively little effect in hindsight; his most important moves at the time would have just aggravated his western allies.

      That northern fleet war was at the same time important and somewhat optional for Russia; at the beginning I was at a massive disadvantage with three units against the English and German fleets, and had England continued to play, I would have probably had to concede that front altogether (not a massive set-back; StP is a dead-end for western Powers, I could've just held onto it in a static defense while trying to make a break elsewhere). The strategic meaning of the war in the north was mostly in me trying to maintain my hold on a couple of Nordic SCs that'd make it easier for me to reach a majority of the board later on; there is a defensively oriented trench on the Calhamer board that runs from the Baltic through Switzerland to Tunis, making large offensives over it geographically challenging; by keeping hold of the Nordic SCs west of St. Petersburg I could reduce the need for fighting the intense battles required to seize the likes of Berlin or Venice or Tunis from the western Powers. At this point I basically had permanent claim on 14 SCs in Russia, Turkey, Austria and the Balkans, so keeping a couple of Nordic SCs (or English - no matter, really) while I extended my claim to Italy was necessary. At this time I didn't believe in my chances to breach the extremely defensible Mun-Ber line, so my hopes were mostly directed at rolling over the Med and taking Tunis, Naples and Rome to complement whatever I could keep up north.

      At this point it was clear that I had nothing else to offer to Austria aside from a tiger ride. I did active and honest diplomacy towards Jamey, and while it took a while to take root, a couple of years later he would choose to vacate his difficult position in the Balkans area for the dubious safety of Italy. In hindsight it seems clear that France and Germany would have done their best to keep the faith and preserve Austria in a 4-way draw, had Jamey kept fighting me consistently. (Doing that would have been bad play on their part, objectively speaking, as a 4-way draw is a worse outcome than a 3-way; they'd have been fighting to lose, essentially. Perhaps this was why Jamey didn't believe them.) My own stance was rather clear, however: there would be no 4-way, they'd have to somehow manage to reduce my considerable empire into ruins before I'd even consider such a weak draw. The only route into a draw that I'd allow to Austria would be for him to support me in eliminating Germany, or for him to manage to deadlock me at 17 SCs with an unbreakable western defense. As I expressed this threat in those negotiations at the time: if I lost faith in my ability to take a solo, my very next act would be to sweep off Austria from the board, no matter the strategic cost on other fronts; losing SCs to Germany would be completely unimportant when and if I lost my soloing chance, as my next best option would be a 3-way draw, for which I would need do nothing but kill off Austria. I drummed this logic at Jamey consistently through the first years of the end-game in the hopes of getting him to see the light.

      1914 was Gencon time, both France and England NMR:ed. In hindsight this English NMR was probably the single most important strategic factor in my victory: I could have worked with a recalcitrant Austrian and a more efficient France all the way to the end, but I probably could not have survived a living and active England. Then again, having England around would've also meant more diplomatic pressure on the westerners, so it's distinctly possible that they would have had even more coordination problems than they had with two Powers in the Alliance.

      This is a good moment to mention a strategic factor in France's overall play: Vincent often surprised me with suboptimal moves through the last eight years of play. I didn't really understand his play, perhaps, due to us not communicating through the game, but even accounting for this there were some very interesting choices he made especially regarding builds: France built a humongous amount of armies all through the game, consistently having an average of 1-2 armies too many, with an equivalent lack of fleet force. His immediate tactical moves on the front were generally solid, but I never felt that France was using all of her strength as efficiently as possible to counter me; often forces would take an extra year to get to the front, and when they did they might have gone to the "wrong" fronts and be the wrong type. More on this later.

      In 1908 the Austrian fleet force was pretty credible down south, so it was not an inconceivable notion that he would keep me bottled up and defending the Aegean Sea for the rest of the game with French aid. I would've likely seized the Balkans regardless in time, but at this point I was definitely pouring the pressure on Jamey to turn back towards Italy. Tactical attrition helped, as the Austrian fleet force was mainly maintained by continental SCs that I was picking off one by one; in 1908 that fleet was enough to seriously threaten me in Anatolia, but a successful defense even for just a couple of years would see it reduced by necessity as I made gains in the Austrian homelands.

      The war with Germany was interesting at first in that we did not have a full trench in the Pru-Sil area, as is often the case in Russo-German wars. Rather, my armies were mostly fighting in Austria while a savage fleet war in the Nordic area took most of the German attentions. In 1909 I felt Piers's hand in the tactics of the board for the first time despite this relative lack of contact between our forces: in hindsight I have to say that he made heavy use of convoy tactics, often in ways that I did not fully consider myself due to the risks involved.

      The two convoys this year were both strategically solid and tactically brilliant. In the south I was somewhat blindsided by my hopes regarding Austrian, waiting for him to turn towards Italy; my argument for why he should do it had been so powerful that I couldn't myself imagine that he wouldn't go for it The Austro-French convoy from Tunis to Albania brought much needed respite to the Austrian forces in the area, providing Trieste in particular with sidelines support that would be very difficult for me to oust quickly. An unfortunate side-effect of this western offensive was that Germany and France seized Vienna and Venice from Austria; I understand that this was not entirely a cooperative maneuver, which probably influenced the Austrian decision to get out of the Balkans while he still had some force: it was this very fall that Jamey strapped on his tiger-riding boots.

      The northern Franco-German convoy from Belgium to Yorkshire was no less smart: by putting an army into England the westerners got a clear advantage in controlling the English SCs among which I was cavorting free of inhibitions at the time. This convoy I could foresee, but there was little to be done about it; I did seriously consider bringing in my own army from time to time to really lay claim to England, but ultimately I chose to remain strategically uncommitted there to optimize my presence in the Nordic area and the Baltic Sea: I knew that I needed the synergy advantages of holding the Baltic if I was to break into Germany later on, and giving up on Norway was a sure way to lose that chance. This choice made it a given that I would not hold English SCs long-term, which made my fleets in the area pure raiders: they were there just to fuck up with the integrity of the western defensive line.

      \nAs a result of the Austrian turn in 1909 the pretense of a war in the Balkans quickly quieted down as the westerners were forced to retreat to their primary defensive line in Italy. I was also bringing armies up to the front constantly, which meant that at the end of the year we would start to have some real pressure on the German home SCs; taking them was not really realistic, but keeping pressure on them was imperative so as to prevent a German advance and to prevent him from emptying any home SCs for building. Much of the tactical story of the last five years of play was about me picking specific move-sets that would prevent Germany from building should he make any gains.

      We had a pretty good working relationship with Austria this year, with frank and agreeable tactical play. I felt like I had a relatively good handle on Jamey's interests, and I did my best to fulfill them to keep him happy. We even got him a build in Trieste, which is a pretty good outcome for a minor Power latched remora-like onto a giant.

      Up north my fleet play was solid, but not brilliant. I was consistently forced to play the defense due to the larger forces the west had to bring into the war, but I managed to delay and confuse the lines sufficiently to prevent an overall offense from ever taking place. My tactical dictum was for the most part to ensure that any constructive plays by the west would have to be paid for in lost position elsewhere; for the most part this kept the situation stable and gave me time to build more fleets for myself. The tactical balance was in my favour in that the only position I needed to defend was in Norway, which freed up a couple of fleets to raid England, which in turn forced the west to keep a disproportionate amount of forces on duty second-guessing my raids.

      In 1911 I had the Alliance's number tactically - or so I thought. My plays on all fronts were finely tuned to make use of any logistical inefficiencies or misjudgments the opposition might make, and I did get what I hoped for when France held in Edinburgh and thus failed to oust me from London. However, this was also the year of Piers's finest play, a daring convoy from Kiel to Livonia: it's a testament to the clarity of my own move-set that this move, losing Piers the defensive strength of the single army, also immediately gave me access to Munich. Even then, though, the move was clearly advantageous to Germany in the short term: there would be inavoidable losses for me, he got a presence on the backside of StP, and while I did make some headway into the German homeland, there were plentiful French armies nearby to help plug the hole in his home defense. This was clearly the grimmest hour in my rise to hegemony.

      In the fall I made an amazing save against Austria; Jamey had apparently decided that I was doing too well, and I managed to intuit this from his diplomacy, leading to an unplanned and unauthorized bounce in Budapest. Had that move gone through, I would probably have lost the war and had to turn to a draw arrangement of some sort; with the German raider already in Livonia I could hardly afford an Austrian one as well. In hindsight Jamey chose a fortunate moment for his betrayal here, as I needed badly to straighten up the lines, and his presence in Trieste was a constant threat: I could not bring my army in Serbia onto the front with him ready and all too willing to stab me. Once he gave me reason to put him out in Trieste, I immediately did and thus freed up an army that I would very soon need in Sevastopol.

      France should definitely have built a fleet in the winter of 1911 instead of another army. This was in general a problem for the west, they never used their material advantage to simply bring on 2-3 fleets more than I could muster to crush my Nordic presence. They had something like five years in which to do this after England crumbled, but I was never down more than one unit in the area. Partially this was due to tactics, of course: my own army in Finland was consistently useful in offensive tactics, while the French army in England was such that I was able to render it useless simply by not attacking England; once I left the place, that army became dead weight for the Alliance.

      Piers over-committed himself with a second convoy to Livonia in the 1912 spring: not only are two raiders less effective than one due to the overlap in their threat zones, but Piers was also underestimating my strategic zeal: I knew that whatever happened in Russia proper did not actually matter as long as I could make a breach in Mun-Ber, so the constant convoys of force out of the area just redoubled my offensive efforts instead of forcing me to retreat and defend my home SCs as Piers perhaps expected. While my presence in Munich last year had been temporary, this second convoy made me a permanent guest in the Ber-Mun area. albeit one who regularly swung between the two as the tactics dictated. This meant that Germany would have to forget building in this game anymore: any losses he would sustain from now on would be permanent, and any gains he made would be meaningless for the west.

      This year the southern situation reached the end-game position, more or less. What we had there was a my defensive stalemate on the Ionian Sea and a very slow and unlikely siege against Venice, held by the Austrian. It is noteworthy that had Germany not played that second army into Livonia, I would likely not have brought my Serbian army up north, either; instead, that army would've gone to Greece and then convoyed via Ion to Italy, potentially breaking the stalemate in the area. In hindsight I'd say that this would not have been as good a play as the one I got from the German convoy.

      Probably the proximate cause for why I was able to crawl into a solo victory in this game was in the Nordic exchange we had in the fall of 1912. At this point I had an equal number of forces in the area with the west (discounting that army in England), and we'd been dancing for a while, so I thought that I had Piers's number. Moving Nth-Hol was a very deliberate move for me in that it was predicated on one of the basic principles of tactics in this game: don't defend weak positions when you can attack out of them. As I was losing Nth anyway (or rather, as I was unwilling to compromise other concerns sufficiently to defend the place), it was imperative for me to attack out of there in a way that would turn the loss of position into equivalent gain of position or (even better in this situation) material elsewhere. Holland was a good target in that the only realistic attack on Nth would be coming out of Belgium; therefore the only reasonable alternatives in the situation would be that I'd either gain Holland or retain Nth, a very strong tactical position. As it happened Piers stepped into the trap and allowed me Holland.

      \nFor me 1913 was all about Munich: France had taken up defense of the place, and Vincent had a realistic chance of locking it down by simply putting an army into Burgundy to help in the defense. The complicating factor here was that if I attacked and seized Munich while Vincent moved another army in there, that would result in disbanding the army currently in position; not a major catastrophe, but possibly motivation enough to instead leave Burgundy empty for Munich to retreat to. As it happened, Vincent made a clear tactical error by first leaving Burgundy empty in the spring and then outright bouncing himself out of the place in the fall, giving me plenty of time to choose my moment for attacking - as I ultimately did in 1914.

      A less egregious tactical error was Lon-Yor, Bre-MAO this same spring; our Nordic war was always a very delicate thing, and it was mostly controlled by small, individual force pressures. I doubt that I could've gained actual dominance in that war had the Alliance used their forces as effectively as possible, always ensuring that each and every unit does something proactive. This was my own principle: all of my units would always make moves that would require an equivalent exertion of force from the opposition to prevent me from gaining position or material. Moves like this strategic re-entrenchment France was doing to attack Norwegian Sea gave me breathing space in this, as they effectively kept the numerically superior Alliance force out of action while I crawled my way to domination of the Nordic area. I lost Norwegian Sea, perhaps, but gained a solid hold on Sweden and Denmark in exchange.

      In 1914 Germany should've moved his raider from Sevastopol to Anatolia; that would have split up his raiders and forced me to split up my internal security forces while giving him a good position from which the raider would have been very difficult to disentangle. I was pretty sensitive to mistakes like this at the time, as I was out for blood and very considerate of all the options the Alliance had available against me.

      1914 fall was a turn I planned with special care, as the game was running thin: the French decision to extend to seize the Norwegian Sea had given me an opportunity to seize the Nordic area, suddenly giving me some synergic attack options on Kiel and Berlin from the north. I was not worried about an attack on Norway from Norwegian Sea, as controlling Skagerrak and the additional forces that the Nordic area would allow me would make defense easy. However, the fact remained that the Alliance technically had more force than I did, and even in practice their immediately available force was near equal: I would have to make a clear break soon if I were to win this thing.

      We had something of a guessing game with the Alliance in that fall move over a number of issues: they could attack either Norway or Denmark but not both, while I would have to retake Kiel or Berlin or retain Munich to stay even. The German army in Warsaw also had a couple of options; a horrendously risky attack on Silesia or a more conservative play towards Moscow; The Silesian attack would be effective in cutting a support for Munich, but if I played Sil-Ber, Warsaw would not only fail to foil my German operation, but it would also lose its position in Warsaw. As it happened War-Sil was what Piers played - I did not count on this, though, which is why I chose a very carefully balanced set of moves that ensured that no matter what Warsaw did, I'd gain a SC out of it: as it happened, that SC was Sevastopol instead of Berlin.

      The real victory of the turn for me was not in Warsaw, though, but in Norway: the Alliance had two attacks on the Nordic area they could execute, and they chose the wrong one, causing me to regain Denmark, Kiel and Norway while Germany lost Belgium to France: a crushing defeat, putting me to 17 SCs. I was pretty surprised that I could outplay Piers and Vincent this much at this late stage of the game, as the assault they chose was clearly unlikely to succeed: my forces in Swe, GoB and Fin had a very limited number of useful plays to them, and the defense of Norway I opted for was the most obvious one of them as regards useful tactical positions for next year. I would have expected Ska S Bel-Nwy instead of the cut to Sweden, as that move would have succeeded certainly unless I used Denmark to cut Skagerrak - which would have been a very risky move with uncertain gain, and one that would have surely lost me Kiel.

      The last year was something of a formality in that there was no longer any question of my victory as long as I played a conservative game; it was just a matter of time. The Alliance outplayed me in Tyrolia (in hindsight a clear mistake on my part, caused by my relief over the entire game), but that did not effect the outcome. Germany could have delayed my victory for a bit by keeping hold of his raiders, though: those two were both guaranteed to keep a SC in 1915 for a net total of +1 for him, which could have off-set any gains I made in the west simply because my forces couldn't necessarily march quick enough to take ground that fast. It was clear that the Alliance had lost their will to live, though: even the dependable Jamey had mixed orders with Vincent in the fall. I won cleanly by taking Warsaw, Munich and Berlin while losing Kiel.
