    \n Pendragon should be part of the mandatory canon that all aspiring game designers should be familiar with. It also happens to be one of my favorite (and most played) games, ever.
    \n
    \nThat said, while the CONCEPTS behind the mechanics are genius and powerful, the actual mechanics themselves are pretty creaky by modern standards.
    \n
    \nThe core dice system is Chaosium's Basic Roleplaying (Modified to use d20s instead of %ile) and as such is subject to all the whiff-tacular whiffage whiffiness that plagues BRP. But it is quick to play and easy to house rule, so its still totally playable. One of my all time dreams would be to buy the license and team up with Jake Norwood to redo the game in v. 2.0 Riddle of Steel. That would be Hawt to the 10th power.
    \n
    \nThere is also a wonderful timeline of Arthurian events and suitable adventures (hooks, mini adventures, full fledged module-esque scenarios) that take you from the death of Uther through the death of Arthur year by year (season by season in some cases). So GM prep is relatively easy compared to most old school games.
    \n
    \nActual play looks likes this. 1) Spend some time at court interacting with famous NPCs and bragging about your achievements...witness or hear about and occassionally participate in key events of Arthurian canon. 2) comport yourself with the dignity due your rank, including the bonuses that come from spending loads of money on clothes and food and expensive gifts. 3) Get hooked into an adventure (perhaps a tournament is declared, some maiden comes to court desperate for assistance, the king asks for knights to right a wrong...whatever). 4) Go on adventure, have your virtue tested, be driven by your passions, succomb to your vices. 5) Return to your lands for the winter where you will hope to achieve sufficient prosperity to pay for your opulent lifestyle or else next year you will poor. Try to have babies, hope your horses survive the winter, go on mini adventures like hunting, or garrison duty. 6) Continue with step 1 earning honor and renown until you either die or retire old and famous. 7) return to play as the son of your previous character with all the benefits (and baggage) of beind a descendent of your first character brings. 8) repeat all this until your second character dies or retires...and hopefully (if you had children who survived) you can play the grandson (and maybe even great grandson) of your original character.
    \n
    \nThroughout the tone of the game, the events, and the adventures change. Initially the game is very gritty and historic (or at least pseudo-historic) with the Britons on the verge of being extinguished by the Saxons, raiding each other for cattle. Then in Arthur's early years its all about war. You can fight battle after battle (with a pretty cool battle sytem) against your fellow Britons, against Saxons, against the "Romans". Then after Arthur is triumphant the game gets more fantastical with merry feasts and adventures into faerie and romance and chivalry. Then after the Dolorous Stroke, things turn grim and start to fall apart, good knights start dying in droves Briton goes to shit Lancelot is exiled, Arthur dies and the magic dies with him.
    \n
    \n
    Not only does it have the generational aspect but it also has a slew of balanced Passions as traits that are used during play to compel the player to behave in certain way.
    \n
    \n
    \nI will note that this is a common misconception in Pendragon. Your Traits can only compel behavior if they are 16+ (on a 20 point scale) and that is pretty hard to do unless you really work at it, and its super easy to keep your Traits below 16 if you want (though there are rewards for going higher). Mostly the Traits are indicative of past behavior and used to represent what others think of you (people you meet have heard you're dishonest and lazy for instance). If you want them to think of you as honest and energetic, you simply act that way on a regular basis and over time the Traits will change to match your actual behavior. Only if your scores are 16+ can the GM compel you to lie or laze-away (and then you can roll to avoid it and call in other traits like your Loyalty and such to help you pass). Lots of players (including myself sometimes) like to go ahead and roll against their traits to let the dice decide how they react, that's totally not mandatory (although some of the published modules are unfortuneately written as if it was...the core rules are quite clear that its not).

    There are also times when you can use the traits to act as a resolution system. Like, if the hot fae seductress is trying to get you into her bower, you may have to roll Chaste to resist her regardless of your Chaste score.

    However, this is usually done as a mode of conflict resolution, and not simply the GM forcing a character to act in a certain way.

    And with that I fully endorse Ralph's post. Pendragon isn't the most modern or slick of games, but it is rock awesome and full of many, many concepts that it took other games 20+ years to catch up with.

    \n I fully endorse Jeff's endorsement of Brand's endorsement of Ralph's post, which is definitely the best concise description of Pendragon I've ever read.
    I'd add to Patty's point: if a character dies, and there are some years before his heir is of age, the best thing to do is to start up another family/ By the middle to late end of the campaign you might have several families, with familiy ties (marriage and affairs) with numerous other player character's families. Usually by this point, you shouldn't have a shortage of characters - you have a surplus! Many of which can be played in separate adventures, maybe even rotating the GM (Pendragon being one of those games that suggests this).

    It can be very lethal, as Patty says, so a surplus of characters is not a bad thing. Having said that, it does get less lethal as the game goes on, and technology (well, mainly armour) improves dramatically.

    \n My local game store (The War House in Long Beach, CA) had copies of the 5th edition rules and the Great Pendragon Campaign last time I checked.
    \n
    \nA few months age we finished a Pendragon game using the GPC in which we played through the Uther Period. I ran it for two of my friends and it was great fun. The GPC is just a big toolbox that Greg has set up, and I found it really useful to take what I wanted and ignore the rest. He's got great stuff in there. The Uther period is the most structured section and the least open, but this was intentional on Greg's part as the following section the Anarchy Period essentially leaves Salisbury in the hands of the PCs. The Anarchy Period provides for a much more open game where the PCs have much more autonomy and the training wheels come off. (THE KING IS DEAD! YOUR LIEGE IS DEAD! THE SAXONS ARE AT THE DOOR! etc.)
    \n
    \nThe game also has a pretty low prep time as I was spending maybe an hour or so the night before working on the game.
    \n
    \nGreg also often answers questions about the game over on the Arthaus/White Wolf Message boards for Pendragon in case anybody's interested.

    \n *The healing rate for a character is their Str+Con/10 HP per week. Which is going to work out to 1d3 or less per week. "Superhuman" characters could get up to a D4 I guess. . .
    \n
    \nIn play this ends up being less of problem than it might appear as several months will likely have passed between battles or adventures. The timescale on which the game is played is one of the best things about it. My group's PCs went from being young inexperienced knights to weary veterans in 10 years (game time) or so (there are LOTS of large battles during the Uther period). Nobody died, but they all were pretty grievously wounded at some point. The unconsciousness rules are there for a reason. Passing out can save your character from getting killed!
    \n
    \nI think one of the reasons the combat system works is precisely because it is pretty deadly. Combat is serious business here which breaks the game away from lots of other games where PCs can enter into cambat without batting an eye--which is fine if that's what you're looking for but this is a grittier approach.

    \n As a note: Pendragon hasn't changed much through its editions.
    \n
    \nPendragon 1st was just enough different from 2nd that the stuff isn't entirely compatable...but it doesn't take that much work to make it so. Same Pendragon 2nd to 3rd. Pendragon 4th is really nothing more than a reprint of a couple of Pendragon 3rd books into one cover (with like 1 chapter of actually new...but totally unnecessary (yet cool) material) thrown in. Pendragon 5th is really just a reprint of Pendragon 4th...possibly even removing a few things (though now I can't really remember). About the biggest difference between 3, 4 and 5 is who published it.
    \n
    \nPoint being, if you're having trouble finding it, you can pretty much pick up any edition you want off of ebay (though you'd pay a pretty penny for 1st or 2nd). The main difference is in how the core supplements are packaged.
    \n
    \nPendragon was one of the first RPGs to throw out the "your character can be anything" paradigm. You were a knight...period. Not a wizard, not a commoner mercenary, not a street rat...a knight...don't like it play something else. The core rules went even a step further, not only were you a knight but you were a newly made knight of Salisbury...not London, not Orkney, not Ireland...Salisbury...don't like it play something else. 3rd edition let you play ladies and made handwavy motions at allowing female knights (in that "if you really must violate the canon I guess you can" kind of tone)...I cant remember if that was presented in earlier editions. It also introduced Knights Adventurous, the supplement that did allow you to be knights from different parts of of the Arthurian world. 4th edition packaged Knights Adventurous right into the core book as I recall and introduced rules to let you be a Merlin-esque druid character (with rules that made for really cool NPCs but pretty not-fun PCs). If you're buying 3rd edition, The Boy King supplement gives you the campaign timeline. A beefed up version of this is what was rereleased as the The Great Pendragon Campaign for 5th (I think 4th just reprinted The Boy King, but I can't recall entirely). Everything else...Perilous Forest, Land of Giants, Savage something or other, are basically mini campaigns / adventure books for a specific geographical area and are pretty compatable accross any edition.

    \nMy experience of his games is second hand, but I've had plenty of discussions about the rules with him, and had the honour of playtesting stuff with him, if that counts. Basically, he does what all traditional GM's do (and it feels strange calling Greg a traditional GM, of course, since he is simultaneously an innovative games designer): in the moment-to-moment of play, he decides which rules to use and which rules to ignore. Over time, he might selectively drop certain rules often enough that he forgets about them and treats his current approach as the standard rule. When I've pointed out an official rule, he has occasionally asked for the page reference, which was the source of my "benign rules lawyer" comment
    Basically, he treats the printed rules as a toolbox, and decides whether or not to use them in the moment. An age-old practice.

    Also, as you suspect, he is an inveterate tinkerer: so he is always has a few house rules for different systems that he is trying out at any given time. Many of these are not replacements for existing systems, but additions to them. (For example, one house rule he has used: when PCs get married, another PC plays their wife, and is responsible for her training and actions when in a scene.) Some of these find their way into sourcebooks.

    It is clear as someone commented, that there are some things he does with the system that aren't in the rules. For instance, with the battle system, he treats the rules as a framework, and adds in lots of events geared to the players rather than simply relying on the tables. He's talked about expanding the battle system, and has playtested a new approach recently at a UK convention which gives players a lot more freedom of action, and ability to influence the course of the battle. It's likely this will be the core of a new supplement. It's an impressive overhaul (and I say that as a big fan for the current official battle rules).

    I'm hesitatnt to put words in Greg's mouth, but it may be that he believes there are certain aspects to GMing, some of which may be personal to each GM, that are more of an art than a science (deciding when to use or ignore a rule, for instance), and resist this kind of systemisation and therefore they can't easily be put into a rulebook. I don't think this is a controversial view, really, outside of the highly focussed games produced by some people hereabouts

    \n Hi Brand,

    And, thematically, I think that's something that's running through the whole game, of course. Everyone entering the game knows there will be a rise (of Arthur's realm, of splendor, of manners and customs) and a fall. And there's not a darned thing they can do about it.

    This is something I'm going to break out in a future thread, but I think there's something vitally different about this meta-plot and almost any other meta-plot I've ever seen published:

    Unlike Earthdawn, Tribe 8, Shadowrun, the World of Darkness metaplots and all the other games that had long-running metaplots pushed through months and or years of publishing, everyone knows the metaplot of Pendragon beforehand. In all the other games there's this tension (if not anxiety) for everyone playing the game (GM and Players): "We can go down this road, but in a few months game company X will be publishing a book and we might be crashing against what they're publishing."

    In Pendragon that's not the case at all. And I believe that the difference in application is huge.

    We all know the story of King Arthur and his knights to a varying degree. So, anyone who decides to invest in the game already knows the ground rules and says, "Yes. I want that."

    This is a completely different situation than not knowing. This is knowing in full. You know you're plugging yourself into the context of a pre-established background.

    Instead of questions like, "What will the metaplot bring, and how will it fit with what we're doing?" the questions become:

    "How do I stand in relation to Arthur. To his rise and fall."
    "How do I stand in relation to Lancelot? To romance and to adultery and duty vs. love?"
    "Where do I stand in the blood feuds that take place between the Orkney clan and -- well, everyone? Do I put family before the King's Law...? Or not?"

    Because the background is known, defined and fixed, the Players get to create characters and take actions that make a point in the context of the tale, using their knowledge of the ongoing tale to dramatic and/or ironic effect.

    This, of course, is what Mallory does as well... he gives us knights that put family above the law, or the law above love, and so on. And since he spins out yarns of many knights, all of which show different kinds of knights making different kinds of choices, the Players (with their knights) are simply folding more knights into the tales and becoming part of that tradition, creating the tales Mallory didn't hear about or didn't write down.

    The Battle System, in my view, reflects this tension in the game -- a tension that is echoed in all aspects of the game: your knight can fight like hell for Camelot, but Camelot is going to fall; you can fight like hell to keep your knight alive, but one way or another your knight is going to die; you can go into battle amid an army and slaughter till your skin is flayed, you won't be turning the tide.

    Just like the tension between the mundane and the fantastic, the bloody and the ideal -- which Greg lays out so clearly in his notes to the third and fifth editions -- the knights are part of something larger than themselves that they never will master and never determine. Inside that tension of the finite wrapped inside the infinite is some of the magic that I think makes the game spark.

    \n Chris,

    Yes, absolutely.

    Also, there is another thing that goes on with other metaplots vs Pendragon, that's around the same tension as the knowing/not-knowing one you describe so well. That's that the PCs in Pendragon aren't expected to fix it/ save it/ be it end all and be all.

    Like, in Tribe 8 the PCs (or two or three generations of PCs) are supposed to be the ones that save the world. They're supposed to start in one place, go to another, do all this stuff, and be the central pivot of a lot of the most important fights (if not the most important decisions) that will save the whole world. Its very heroic paradigm based, in which the PCs are the center of the whole world, not just the whole story of the game. (Two very different things that are often mistaken for each other in large-scope games.) And, as you say, you're supposed to do all this without knowing what is going to happen.

    In Pendragon, however, the PCs aren't expected to either save or kill Camelot. The rise and fall of civilization in the game is known and external to the PCs. Thus the PCs focus is cleared to be on themselves, their own story, their own choices.

    In the last full campaign of Pendragon I played there was a moment toward the end of the game that I'll never forget. The final battles are coming down, and the PCs are with Gawain when he comes back from France wounded after his battle with Lancelot. When the boats get attacked on the shore and Gawain rises from his bed to go and fight, everyone at the table knew he was going to die. There wasn't a moment of "oh shits, we need to save dude" as we all knew this was where he was going to bite it, and that there was no saving him. So one PC says "I'm going to go with him, the rest of you go to Arthur." And the other players say, OOCly, "But you'll die!" and he responds "So? We're at the end anyway. I'd rather die with Gawain and drown in my own blood in the surf than live in a fallen world fighting with lesser men."

    He knew what was going to happen, and knew that the expectation on him wasn't to fix it or change it, but to make a decision about how he felt about it. And he did.
