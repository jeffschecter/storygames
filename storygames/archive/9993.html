This isn't the start to an actual game. It's primarily intended to be something along the lines of a creative attempt at surveying possibilities, and secondarily an attempt to raise awareness for and interest. This 'game' will have 3 phases. The first phase is where you suggest an Endgame Goal. Post an idea, or a vote for someone else's idea. In a short time, I will see which idea has the most promise, and we'll move on to phase 2.
\n
\n
\nBy reading this thread, you're standing at the table. On the table is a book with the name "Mage Blade" written in fanciful writing across the front. At each seat around the table, there's a character sheet, a pencil, and a single twenty-sided die.
\n
\nBy posting to this thread, you're taking one of those seats. It's a big table, though. Don't worry about crowding. You can play along or just watch. It's more fun to play, of course...
\n
\n"So," I say to you, "The first thing we have to do is come up with the Endgame Goal. Think about it like watching a movie. You generally know what's going to happen by the end pretty early on, but you watch to see *how* it happens, and what happens to lead up to it. Endgame is like that. For instance, it doesn't take you long to find out that the Deathstar is bad news, so it's got to go. Destroying the Deathstar could totally be an Endgame Goal. Or even defeating the Empire, if you wanted to think longer term. The idea here is a broad-strokes statement of something you'd like to see happen in the fiction. Don't get too detailed or specific." I lean back in my chair, twisting the book around so you can read the name better. "Mage Blade is a fantasy game, pretty traditional in setting. Elves, dwarves, humans, orcs, all of that. Ancient wars, mysterious treasures and plenty of opportunity for adventure." Then I lean forward, take up a pencil with a smile, and look around at everyone gathered around. "So, what ideas do you have for Endgame?"

    \n Got some good ideas here. I realize I was a bit less than clear when I stated what Endgame was, though. The Endgame Goal is a group campaign goal, rather than an individual goal. On the other hand, if the group is fine with there being a 'main character' for the campaign, then you can totally rock out with an individualized goal.
    \n
    \nSo let's list out what we've got, so far:
    \n
    \n1. Orcish world domination, with elf punching
    \n2. Controversial marriage of feuding houses
    \n3. Ascension to Godhood, with vengeance (individual)
    \n4. Free the king from his delusions
    \n5. Become the leader of a new civilization (individual?)
    \n6. De-oppress the oppressed Whutevari people
    \n7. Defeat the lich lord and his empire
    \n8. Claim the Forgotten Sea navigational charts
    \n9. Die. Finally. (pretty sure this is individual)
    \n10. Claim the Mage Blade. Kill Ben. Or his character. Yanno, whatever. (individual)
    \n
    \nI think that's more than enough to get started with. Let's let this next round of posts be about hashing out which one we're gonna go with. I'm just the humble GM, servant to your collective will, but I'll weigh in with which ones I like best, and why.
    \n
    \n2 is awesome, because I'd never have come up with it myself. Having a bunch of characters who, one way or the other, end up working toward this goal sounds like it could be very cool. I see lots of politics and some cloak and dagger stuff. The Endgame could be about the wedding ceremony, and the desperate, last ditch efforts to stop it, which the PCs will likely attempt to foil.)
    \n
    \n4. is also pretty cool, because it requires some additional setup. Which king? What delusions?
    \n
    \n6 and 7 are bog-standard stuff I'd like to see played with Mage Blade. Grand, sweeping goals that will require exploration, combat, negotiations and travel, almost certainly with a nice big climax.
    \n
    \n8. is especially cool, because it acknowledges and takes advantage of the closed-form campaign structure.. This campaign has an end-point, but that doesn't mean we have to stop playing! I have to wonder how getting some scrolls will be the focus of an entire campaign, though. That would be interesting to see.
    \n
    \n10 and 3 are an interesting pair, because they could be mixed. In the setting (which I'm not going to bother with for this little game) the Mage Blade is an incredibly powerful artifact, and the last mortal to hold it is the closest thing to a god the setting had... Only now he's exiled to a prison dimension.
    \n
    \n5 is also interesting. Obviously you've got to discover a new/lost people along the way. It prescribes some of what has to happen, but not too much. It could be individual, or it could be that the character who becomes the leader isn't determined until the game is actually in play. Quite doable.
    \n
    \nSo round 1, part 2: Which ones do you like, or how might we combine some together to make an even cooler Endgame? Remember the idea is broad strokes. Too many details spoil the story!

Only at Endgame is his ruse discovered, and he takes on the mantle of his full power.

    \n Maybe one of the things that sucks about being a lich lord is that he doesn't get more powerful. He's either stuck where he was when he became a lich (can experience, but never learn), or regularly forgets anything more than a few decades ago. Sort of like Walker's character in the comic book "Powers", but with more ennui.

    Maybe the entire group that's been cursed with the same condition. The undying remember just enough of their past to understand their condition, and recognize each other. The game could include flashbacks (be they played-out scenes, or moments of "helpful" awareness).

    Why are they after the Mageblade? Because it's the only thing that can end their tiresome existence. But at least a few of the characters, including the orc, secretly believe that whoever controls it will be able to remember and use his or her entire past, and thereby ascend. And others may believe it's wrong for any of them to have that kind of power, so whoever would be lich lord/god must be stopped.

    The deluded king could be an undying who has forgotten too much, which has led him into a foolish marriage with a queen who's aging while he doesn't. And she, the court, and the torch-and-pitchfork peasantry are starting to get very, very nervous about him. He needs to be removed from his situation for his own good, and also because if they figure out his true nature, the other undying are in danger, too. (Just because you can't die doesn't mean normal people can't hurt you. A lot.)

    The charts are part of the kingdom's treasure, and point to the location of the blade.

    The feuding houses may be part of the kingdom, or may be in some other arc. Perhaps they're the descendants of two of the undying, and their feud is ironic. Or may have its roots in a forgotten conflict between the undying.

    The oppressed people could be subjects of the undying king, or could be part of another arc entirely.

    \n
    \nSo, I'll kick off phase 2. Again, the table is large, so there's no limit on the number of participants.
    \n
    \n"Alrighty then," I say, circling the choice we've made on my paper, several times for emphasis. "Next up, we get to make characters. Let's not get too in-depth yet. We're not going to worry about mechanics at this stage. All those stat blocks on your sheet, just ignore 'em. Matter of fact, flip over the sheet to the back." The back of the character sheet is blank. "Lets talk about character concepts. Keep the Endgame Goal in mind, too. Some rough guidelines is that your characters are probably in the 20-25 age bracket, though they can be a bit older, and even as young as 10. Write down some notes. If it takes more than 3-4 sentences to describe your concept, you're over-thinking it." Having given instructions, I look down at the page in front of me, glancing around at the people seated at the table, as if counting, and I take some notes. "Feel free to talk amongst yourselves, give suggestions and such. Let me know when you've finalized your concept."
    \n
    \n"Oh yeah, one more thing. Once you're done describing your concept, pick an element out of the 4 typical ones of Earth, Water, Air and Fire, or a 5th, Void. Your choice is kind of like a zodiac sign. It should be roughly descriptive of personality type. Don't over-think this either. Just go with what feels right."

    \n"A nicely mixed bag," I murmur, looking over the concepts. A few with reasons to be interested in the sea, and a few others just looking to get swept along in the next epic adventure." We move on into the mechanical bits of character creation, selecting lifepaths and getting skills and traits to fill out our character abilities. Once we're all done, I look around at you all again. "The final thing before we begin playing is to create Foundational Goals. I like to think of Foundational Goals as being the last step of character creation. These goals aren't intended to have anything to do with your character's ultimate destiny. They're intended to finish rounding out your character. They're intended to get the character in motion. That motion doesn't necessarily have to do with moving toward the Endgame Goal, especially considering that accomplishing these goals doesn't mechanically move the game any closer to Endgame. It's just the impetus that starts the ball rolling." I look over the characters, rubbing my chin thoughtfully. "Some of these concepts already have some good Foundational Goals inferred. LeMarque may want to escape those looking to stopper his ears and seal his lips. Maybe Roggwyn wants to find a companion. Ilona wants to find a patron so she can hire a crew. A good rule of thumb is that your goals should have some chance of bringing the characters into contact. We don't necessarily have to end up with an adventuring party, but an interwoven story is a better story."
    \n
    \n"You've got 50 points each to purchase Foundational Goals. Each 10 points you put into a Goal indicates roughly one challenge to overcome to accomplish it. The points you put in to each Goal are also what you'll get back to set Goals which will drive the story forward later, and to improve your character's effectiveness later. Oh, also... it's very important to understand that these goals aren't necessarily things your character wants. They're things you want your character to accomplish. You will drive your character to accomplish them, regardless of in-character motivations. Roggwyn doesn't necessarily know he's looking for a companion, but he'll likely manage to get one anyway."

    \nFor all goals, it's important that the GM and player see eye to eye on what accomplishing the goal means. This is doubly important for this sort of Goal. The worst case scenario is where I put an opportunity for LeMarque to see that he's worthy of redemption out in front of him, and you either don't recognize it, or aren't interested.       \n
      \nAt this point, it's more than safe to say that everyone is done. The next step would be to actually sit down and play. Over the course of the first few sessions, a majority of these goals would be resolved, and you as players would set others. I would take the goals, and weave them together into situations, and play would continue. Beyond the foundational goals, you'd be setting goals that either advanced the plot toward Endgame (Rising Actions) or detracted/distracted from Endgame (Falling Actions). The mix of these would end up with an organically paced game, where you could slow things down by setting Falling Actions, or speed them up with Rising Actions. These have the mechanical effect of incrementing a number called the EndCount, which when it reaches a certain value will trigger Endgame.
      \n
      \nI was a little dismayed at the beginning of this process, unfairly I believe now. There were some suggestions that seemed intentionally silly or over the top, and I had to pull back and realize that 1. silliness is going to happen at the table too, 2. these suggestions may have been attempts to test how far the game/process can go and 3. they may have been legitimate suggestions. Some of them, as we've seen, were less than ideal for the system. Others may simply seem that way to me because I'm a prissy bitch, but with this system and your individual groups may be perfectly viable.
      \n
      \nMy intentions with this thread were varied.
      \n
      \nFirst, I wanted to see what sorts of Endgames, characters and goals would appear. I've been surprised before by the creativity of the indie, small-press and story-games communities, so I wanted to have an idea what sorts of things might come up, and make sure the game can handle most of them. I didn't want to drown you all with system and setting, so what we got was a bit more free-wheeling.

Whether you want that guidance to come specifically from the built in structure of the process or from group discussion and negotiation, or both, is something to think about. If you want a lot of the fine tuning to be through group consensus/negotiation don't forget to put that front and center in the text. Giving this initial process a tight focus wouldn't hurt in my opinion, particularly since I don't perceive that in its existing form that it added much of anything to the creative end product.
