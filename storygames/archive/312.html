Directorial Power in Dogs

jhkim
March 2006 edited March 2006 in Story Games
I was going to comment on the Task Resolution thread, but I realized that it was prone to definitional problems. So I thought I'd start a specific mechanics thread. I've seen in a couple of places people saying that Dogs gives this huge amount of power over to the players. i.e. That if the players disagree with the GM, they can force the GM to accept something.

I'm confused by this. From my point of view, the Dogs mechanic only gives one narrow though important piece of directorial power to the players. As a player, you are allowed to narrate how you Take the Blow. i.e. So you can always describe how you are beaten. However, the GM narrates your successes (i.e. when someone else Takes the Blow), and has power over everything except the PCs.

As a Dogs GM, I feel like I'm faced with roughly the same difficult choices that I am in other resolution systems. First, how many opponents are the Dogs facing? I can toast them outright with a large unified gang, or effectively let them win if they can gang up on a single NPC. In other systems, I would have to decide both how many NPCs and how tough the NPCs are. (Whereas in Dogs the NPC stats are fixed by rule.) However, it has roughly the same effect on PC success -- I try to pick a number which will challenge the PCs but not overwhelm them.

The other question is, what Stakes do I accept? This is parallel to the question in other systems of "What do I let the PCs accomplish in a roll?" If I'm feeling generous, I can give them whatever they want with a success. If I'm not, then I have to decide how much to give them.

This isn't meant to be a dig at Dogs. I like Dogs, but I don't see it as being mechanically different in this aspect of resolution than other games with a GM. (As opposed to GMless games like Soap or Polaris.)

Do other people handle this differently? Am I missing something?

    The biggest power that Dogs gives the players is that they get to say when it's done. Seriously -- this is the biggest, fattest elephant in the room when you're playing Dogs. At any point, even as the Brother Hatfields are killing the Brother McCoys, the Dogs can say, "Alright, judgement time, this crusade between families is approved by the King of Life. We're heading off to the next town." It's an extreme example of a far more subtle point -- the GM sets up the town, sure, but the Dogs show up and make the conclusions about the town. The woman that the GM set up as a grieving victim of a tragic miscarriage gets labelled by the Dogs as a harlot. The Dogs aren't trying to discover "the solution" or even "what's real" that the GM has set up beforehand. The Dogs are just rolling in and laying down judgement -- and the players have complete control over that.

    What Stakes get used demands that both sides are kind of hard-asses about it. Sure, you can let the players get away with all sorts of stuff -- and the players can let the GM get away with all sorts of stuff, too. The game is set up so that both sides have resources (calling a conflict) to call on to prevent that (should they choose to do so).

    Mobs, on the other hand, are broken. This is the biggest problem I have with the game. There are no guidelines for how to put together a mob in terms of how much is enough and too much, and the fallout rules sort of fall flat when you're talking about ten guys with thirty dice fallout between them. Mobs would probably be done better by just rolling Demonic Influence for them or something.

    But Dogs doesn't give the players directorial power at all, or at least, not much. (In a sort of minor way, it doesn't give a shit about if there's a water bucket there if you want to use one, which I think is pretty healthy.) Dogs don't narrate their successes or introduce new characters or any of that. Mostly it gives the players the power to use resources to get their way (as opposed to using resources to prove to the GM that they've earned it) and the power to resolve the situation whenever they feel like it.

    I'm not talking exit, John, I'm talking ending the story. If some D&D PCs go down into a dungeon to save the prince(ss), fight some goblins and then high-tail it out of there, the story isn't really over; there is no closure. If some Dogs roll into town, don't uncover all the bits and pieces that are prepared for them, and declare that they, in their role as divinely-appointed judges, know what's wrong with the town, then whatever drivel they spout is what's wrong, and the story is concluded as they head out into the sunset. Am I making the distinction clear?

    Mobs aren't the only way to threaten a group of Dogs. A group of NPCs, each with their own die pools, are actually more dangerous than a mob of the same number of NPCs. Off the top of my head (and not having actually tried it) I'd suggest throwing three mob leaders at a group of Dogs and giving them "helping dice" from their third of the mob.

    As for setting stakes -- mostly it boils down to the "most discerning player" guideline. Yes, it can be abused, sure. But if you, as the GM, are dicking with the PCs and refusing to let them actually get the stakes they want, I'm thinking your most discerning player is not going to be pleased. Again, not perfect, and I'm not saying it is. I do think it is, however, an improvement over Fudge or similar, where the players have no recourse to set or even suggest stakes, and their task-actions do not concretely forward those stakes.

    Daniel -- you have a point. I'd question whether inventing the daughter out of thin air would really fly with most groups. "Dictating" that a guy has doubts registers around the same level as dictating a water bucket for me.
