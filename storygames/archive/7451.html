\n My group of about... wow, about 12 players... plays In A Wicked Age with a rotating roster.

One thing I'm finding in our games is that while we are getting the drama and the conflict, we're often a bit shy on the context. Because each player has only been there for at best 75% of the sessions (except me, I've gotten 11 out of 12), there's a timidity around things like what color the cobblestones are, whether it's raining, how people dress, and so on. That has lead to ... more often than not ... those elements getting missed.

I know we could sit down and world build, but it's so easy for that to be a waste of time or just plain un-fun.

How can we get over the descriptive hump, and get a more solid baseline to play with? This is especially crucial because we're all getting together on Labour day to play another chapter and do this kind of stuff... but I realized it will go so much better if I structure it.

How can this be fun and successful?

    \n While I LOVE the idea of people hauling out Universalis to augment their other games...for purposes of this issue, I'm with John...I think that will cause more confusion than it helps.
    \n
    \nBUT, you may get some mileage out of harvesting a piece of it.
    \n
    \nPart of IaWA (as noted repeatedly here) is that players must add a detail when they narrate. Its like a rule...so not doing it is actually breaking the rules. So I think step one is just to make sure the GM enforces this rule with a hard draconian fist, at least until people get into the habit of doing it without the whip.
    \n
    \nThe second part of the problem, however, is recording the details in a format that people who weren't there can benefit from. For this you may find it helpful to treat people, locations, cultures, etc, like Universalis Components. Create an index card for each thing that crops up during play and appoint one of the players to be "keeper of that thing" for the session. Whenever a detail is expressed (which should be frequently if the GM is cracking the whip) that detail gets recorded on the appropriate card.
    \n
    \nWhen future stories revisit existing locations its a simple manner to whip out the appropriate cards, read off the existing details to get everyone in the right frame of mind, and go.
    \n
    \nCombine with the Wiki and pictures idea above for even more impact.

    \n Some advice adapted from William Burroughs' comments on writing techniques:

    Make sure that in the course of a session, you've invoked all five senses at least once. If you want to be really disciplined, don't allow yourself to invoke any twice until each has come into play once.

    When you're imagining and describing an environment, go for some arbitrary quality. What's yellow in it? What's round? What do you find to the right of you but not on the left? Walk through the space, focusing on just one such thing, and remember what you find.

    \n We played last night - things came of it that are related to this (lots of other stuff too, including insights into the Iraq war and how damn challenging it is to see why a game is messed up until after).

    The game was much more textured, and I loved it.

    1) I brought up the detail rule right to start, and we did a lot more questions-asking.
    2) It's MUCH easier to bring people up to speed when you're on the knife's edge of the future, rather than playing in the past. Playing in the past needs so more explanation so people don't expect characters X, Y, and Z to die.
    3) I'm getting faster at explaining the rules, but 12 sessions with brand-new players in almost every one really does hinder the development of techniques at the table. That's a lot of time spent teaching the game, which also has clouded the details.
    4) The thing that went most wrong at the game was the loss of the detail at the end of a conflict. Towards 10:00 we were all getting tired and the descriptions started to wane. Two scenes were happening at the same time, one on the roof of a temple and one inside. The guys on the roof were fighting to the death and unfortunately started limiting conesequences to the mechanical in their duel... so the fight just dragged on and on and on without the situation developing. I call this "failing to take your victory." and I might going to have to enforce "winner describes damage to loser" so we can get it right. To me this is just another failure to apply the detail rule from the text.
