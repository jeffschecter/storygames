\n I'm interested in your experiences as a Game Chef participant who also haunts Story Games. Speak to me of the feedback you gave and received. Did you comment on designs outside your feedback group? Did you follow particular entries? Did you track down the games of freinds or people you were interested in following? What percentage of your group finished and submitted their games? What was the range in design techniques in your group?

I was in group Sigma, and it was a positive experience. I found that I couldn't give as much, or as rigorous, feedback as I would have liked, due to the volume of entries and the busy-ness of my life. I found myself answering specific questions rather than looking at designs holistically. Similarly, the feedback I got was encouraging but typically addressed specific mechanical questions I posed. I thought we had a very positive vibe and there was no drama at all.

I followed a couple of games outside my own group - a couple of friends and a couple of designs that caught my eye that I really wanted to encourage.

Our group will have entries ranging from fairly traditional to GM-less and a little experimental. I found it difficult to provide solid feedback for the designs that were outside my current enthusiasms, which is something I'd like to work on for next year.

It's looking like group Sigma will be running about 50% in terms of final submissions.

    \n I was in group Unicron and found that the energy level really shifted during the competition.     Overall I think the groups were a great idea. I have this feeling that going back to one week (perhaps with two weekends) might reduce the overwhelming factor and help people focus more on getting something down on paper.

    \n I was in Group Omega. My first game chef, and I finished, so that's gotta be something.

    It was tough to keep up with the feedback-giving -- I ended up giving quite a bit of feedback to some people, but then others would slip through the cracks of my attention. Basically, the earlier you got your concept up and decided on, the more likely people were going to stick with you and be able to offer truly comprehensive (I think someone used the word 'holistic', as well) feedback. And while both kinds of feedback are useful, I felt I was really being helpful when I could contribute some insight into the game as a whole, or the text as a whole, or whatever. And doing that for 12 other people is basically impossible. On the other hand the best single bit of 'feedback' I got was when a group member posted a 'Everyone write an example of play' thread -- I basically sat down and wrote out the core rules of my game in 'example' format and suddenly realised I might be able to finish a game after all. So sometimes instigation and encouragement are even better than analysis.

    I do think bigger groups might be an improvement, even though I've said that keeping up with 12 other games is impossible. I think the groups create this dilemma of responsibility that both pushes you to participate and finish your own game, and makes you feel bad when you can't keep up with feedback, etc. Of course that's something you can talk about in your group, but if a group was 30 people this expectation would basically go out the window. Is that better? I don't know. The problem with small groups is, well, what happens if you just don't 'get' anybody else's game? I felt like often that was a bigger obstacle to useful feedback than commitment, time, or energy -- sometimes you just can't think of anything to say.

    I was following other games at the beginning -- I had a long list of bookmarked threads -- but I fell off that wagon pretty fast once things started getting going in my group. It was always nice to see cross-group feedback, whether in my own threads or in other threads -- it helped remind me that there was more going on than just my own group's stuff.
