I'm working on a hack of Apocalypse World to use the concepts from the Avatar: The Last Airbender for my group. I'm trying to pull in ideas from other medias though, both from other things with heavy elemental casting systems and more traditional Asian media tropes (mostly thinking martial arts movies). Unfortunately, while I kinda have a handle on several things for the elemental side, I'm actually kinda clueless about martial arts movies and the like. So I figured I'd come by and ask!

I already started a little bit of work on the hack on my blog (link), but I'll summarize all my rambling. I've gotten kind of attached to my stats, Natural, Hot, Solid, Keen, and Fluid (representing the five elements/phases/steps), but am more looking to see if my preliminary basic moves and playbook lists make sense.
What I have for basic moves are: Speak With Honor (polite conversation, Manipulate an NPC), Speak Without Honor (impolite conversation, Go Aggro), Commit Open Violence (Seize Without Force - I'm not attached and am already probably going to axe this), Stand Fast (Act Under Fire), Observe Carefully (currently a combined Read a Sitch/Person), Meditate (planned to work along the lines of Open Your Brain/Gaze Into The Abyss), and Move With Intention (a move for deliberate and interesting motions since those are far more important in this). Bend The Natural Order, the bending move, is sort of a Basic Move, and sort of not.
My playbook set looks like: Firebender, Waterbender, Airbender, Earthbender, Monk (spiritual and wizened, not D&D), Samurai, Martial Artist, Aristocrat, Scholar, Ninja. I'm attached to having the benders as separate playbooks, but other than that nothing's in stone.

So my questions for you all:
What media would you consider required reading/watching for a hack like this?
What action types do you think define the genre? Is my list a good representation? Do I have too many?
What archetypes define the main characters of the genre? Am I missing any? Do I have any I don't need?

No need to do anything further than that, and the media question is really the most important, the rest would just be bonus. Things on Netflix are given priority!
Thanks for the help!

    \n What I would think about is, rather than calling it Karma, maybe 'Qi'/'Chi' and having the 'Karma Keys' as 'Chakra'.     Each Chakra represents a different form of motivation or physical attachment of the individual to the world around them, can be blocked by negative attachment(First in the list), and actually represents something neutral in nature. You could really use either depending on how you wanted to run it...
    1. What do you Fear? Actually represents the urge for survival.
    2. What do you feel guilty for? Actually represents the human search for pleasure.
    3. What are you ashamed of? Actually represents willpower and human strength to continue. (Might have trouble differentiating between the two for some.     \n Hey, I fixed my Meditate problem! It now does pretty much exactly what I think I want it to.

    MEDITATE
    When you spend time[!] in quiet[!!] contemplation, roll+Natural[!!!]. On a hit, you learn a detail relevant to the subject of your thoughts[!!!!]. On a 10+, declare what that detail is[!!!!!]. On a 7-9, the MC will declare what the detail is[!!!!!!].

    That's a lot of exclamation points, isn't it? Let's see:
    [!] Yup, you need to spend some time to meditate. This ain't no Epiphany.

    ...Oh my god, Epiphany would be a great move for something. I've got no use for it right now, but I gotta remember that one.
    [!!] "Quiet" is the word I'm going with, but my intention is that you're on your own basically. It's a lonely activity. Considering a change to "concentrated contemplation" but that's really more semantic than effectual.
    [!!!] Natural is the biggest difference I did to this. This is no longer a Keen move. It's about intuition and a connectedness to things and nature and stuff, and NOT really about observation and smart thinking. (you may have just guessed what I'm making Think It Through now) I kinda wanted the Monk to have a more Natural focus anyway, and this makes the Airbender, someone who already has meditation as a part of their culture, actually good at it.
    [!!!!] You pick the subject when you meditate. This is more of a Buddhist "reflection" type of meditation rather than the also-popular "clear-your-mind-completely" stress relaxation meditation. And you can't just Meditate and hope that SOMEthing comes to you, you DO need to have a topic in mind.
    [!!!!!] The most dangerous part of the move. This is an enormous placement of power in the hands of the player. I'm strongly thinking about adding a "(subject to MC approval)" but feel that violates the very spirit of the ApW game structure.
    [!!!!!!] This, on the other hand, is great. Yeah, they succeeded, they're learning something. This is a great place to complicate things, reveal things you've had in wait, or spring unhappy revelations. I really struggled with doing a 7-9 at first - you've seen the early draft of the move, and it's not that interesting. The second draft was just Spout Lore basically, and repeating moves doesn't please me (aside from Stand Fast - that one's simple enough and diverse enough to be in pretty much all the hacks. Even Speak Naturally gets a new context from Manipulate thanks to heavily engaging the Oaths mechanic). I'm actually very fond of this version, though possibly that's just because I only just now wrote it.

    So what was that I mentioned about Think It Through/Consult The Books? Here, the move is DAMN simple now.

    THINK IT THROUGH
    When you rationally Meditate on a subject, you roll+Keen instead of +Natural. If you spend 1 Material to consult your books in the process, you can Meditate quickly as well.

    The Keen instead of Natural bit was pretty damn obvious. The other bit I think is pretty neat though. It allows you to do so quicker than anyone else can Meditate, and this actually supports that Scholar character who DOESN'T fight but stays out of the fray, frantically looking through his books to find the solution to the monster, calling information out to the fighters. The Material cost is to tie it into his little gimmick, and gives a really good non-Inventor Materials use, but is mostly a last-minute idea of an addition, I haven't thought that one out very much. It feels good though.

    Man, this was exactly what I wanted for Meditate. I'm content.

      Unlike the Benders and The Scholar, The Ninja doesn't have a pre-selected move. The driving thing for me about the Ninja is that I need to cover a lot of ground. When I killed off the idea of a Martial Artist playbook, most of the characters who would have fallen into that instead fell into The Ninja. The Samurai is a pretty narrow concept of playbook - he's an armored, honorable warrior (though I've decided I'm gonna make it no longer solely sword-specific, despite a little hesitation on my part). The Ninja, on the other hand, is broad. The Ninja is a fast or stealthy warrior who doesn't rely on armor. That's a LOT broader. I want to be able to cover characters like Mai, Ty Lee, and the Dai Li, who are, in general, Ninjas who took Earthbending cross-Playbook, though I may write an unofficial sub-playbook for them as well. There's just way too much of that show for me to jump in and get a picture that would satisfy me. I know there's a pretty heavy supernatural element to the show's ninjas, but that's mostly covered indirectly by taking cross-Playbook bending moves - the Ninja himself isn't highly supernatural by default, though his actions aren't entirely grounded in reality.
      The big thing the Ninja needed to include when it absorbed The Martial Artist is that the D&D Monk is now a Ninja.
      Let's check out some move ideas.

      Speed: As I mentioned, this immediately makes me want to hijack the Airbender's Momentum, but that's actually easier said than done. However, I want to make it work. It'll make Airbender-Ninjas pretty damn tough, but I kinda like how the two synergize.

      Acrobatics/Athletics: This is a things Ninjas do. I'm tempted to call the move "Wire-Fu" and have it trigger "When you attempt a physically-implausible athletic or acrobatic act, roll+Fluid...". This lets you do all the crazy jump-kicking and ridiculous things ninjas do that make no sense. It also lets you fill in some of those fun urban legends about what historical ninjas could do - I'm thinking pretty specifically of water-running.

      Stealth: I have two moves in mind for stealth. One is a more general-purpose "Hiding in plain sight" type move, and the other is a Disguises type move. I haven't gotten into really thinking about the mechanism for the former, but I'll likely have some stuff from my Doppleganger work helping with the Disguises one.

It would apply to shuriken/kunai more than anything I guess, so probably the latter.

      Fighting: I want to have some kind of move about using a sort of street-level awareness type of move. Essentially, I guess I'm looking for a "Dirty Fighting" kind of move. Again, Mugen is large in my mind with regard to this, but the idea of fighting dirty is definitely the sort of thing historical ninjas did too. Dirty Fighting is probably what I'll end up calling it even.

      Supernatural: The ninja has a lot of supernatural force at his disposal in legend. A LOT.       \n Hmm, let's see how Chi Blocking could work... Keeping in mind that a Ninja is Fluid and Hot.\n
      Chi Blocking: When you assault your foes pressure points, roll+Hot. On a 7+, they're completely shut down, whether through paralysis, disabling their bending, or some other means, and they take a Tag to indicate this. Whenever they attempt to act contrary to the Tag, you may spend a point of Chi to negate their action. Additionally, on a 10+, you immediately gain 2 points of Chi.
      \nThe idea here is to leave it pretty open-ended in who it targets, so you can use it against fighters or benders or even support characters (as Ty Lee manages to aim at all three, though there's not a ton of explicit support happening in the show, more just fighters who occasionally do support-y things). I like the idea of spending your own Chi to keep them locked down. The 10+ difference gives you the chance to keep them locked down for free, while if you only got 7-9 you need to dip into your own supply to make it happen.
      My issue with this particular version (there was sure to be something, I did just write this out off the top of my head) is that they're even CAPABLE of acting contrary to the tag. I can't find where I actually said it, but even if I haven't, my intention is that tags are narratively binding as well as mechanically. Now, the latter helps inform the former, so that helps, but it leaves the idea of acting against your Tags. Some make sense to act against: the most obvious is Clumsy, caused by wearing Heavy Armor. Now, you can normally act with perfect grace as you narrate, you clumsiness only becomes an issue really when the MC triggers it. I guess I can call this a "passive Tag," as you can ignore (note: ignore as in has-no-game-effect, you ought to still work it into narration sometimes) them until they're triggered. Some, however, are kinda absurd to act against. Let's take On Fire. This should directly inform how you act, and as MC I'm gonna call bullshit if you try to do something like solve a puzzle without first dealing with the tag. Now, the MC doesn't actually have game authority to DO anything about it - if the player insists they're going to solve the puzzle while On Fire, the most the MC can do is trigger the Tag to impede the player. But it would stand to reason that something like this is going to restrict the place you can go narratively. This would be an "active Tag." It's worth mentioning that the passive and active distinction is a design thing, not a rules thing. They aren't formally implemented into the game.
      So then there's the trouble of a Tag that seems to forbid contrary action. Paralyzed. Stunted Bending. If you have these, it seems almost silly to try to take actions contrary to them, but that's what this move is about.

      Heck, now that I've rambled on about this, I've got a compromise point!\n
      Dim Mak[!]: When you assault your foe's pressure points, roll+Hot. On a hit, they take the Tag Addled [!!] [!!!]. Whenever an Addled foe attempts an action, you may spend a point of Chi to negate that action. Additionally, on a 10+, you immediately gain 2 points of Chi.
      \nThat's a little more concise too.
      [!] Dim Mak can be used both to refer to the use of pressure points as targets in martial arts, and the specific application of that technique to cause instant death. I mean it as the former.
      [!!] Addled is the best word I could come up with. Other ideas included Pressured, Dazed, and Stunned, but Addled was the most appropriate.
      [!!!] Yeah, I'm choosing to define precisely which Tag is being inflicted. It makes it easier to justify the effect if I have a specific one to key it off of. I ought to mention though - if you make someone Addled through other means, that's not the same. That's why I'm looking for another word more specific to the pressure point thing, but I don't have it yet.
      Now, Addled is one of those "passive Tags" I mentioned. That's a mind-state that you CAN act past, but it can be triggered against you. It's worth mentioning that it can both be triggered defensively by the Ninja to negate the action, but also by others just like any other Tag.

      \n For the most part, I hadn't really questioned playbook names since I knew you were working on something 'Inspired by but not necessarily Avatar' and was waiting for something more concrete about how you were pitching the setting to follow.
      When we look at cultural appropriation, it is often an important side-note to think of the images invoked in the audience's heads on hearing the word, much as J_Walton brought up with Ninja being such an already heavily appropriated term within the west. If you wanted to go for something more neutral, Wushi might be a nice middleground between getting across the setting's tone (Rather than going for something like 'Warrior' which feels a bit bland) and actually being an accurate word (In so much as it is merely warrior in Chinese, from the same root that we get Wuxia and Bushido, which both influenced the design). But Wushi is not a term used much in the English speaking world, and Samurai does in the West have a popular cultural image of "That dude who runs in and swallow cuts everything with an awesome katana". So in a sense it is a term that has, for the rights or wrongs of it, already been misappropriated, as similar things have happened with a number of European warrior terms (Cavalier, Paladin, etc.),

      In trying to name a general class for all these cultures at once is where you encounter the problem. If it was a more Japanese setting, Samurai would actually probably be fine. Though it has cultural connotations, the term Knight is often used in a similar manner in the west despite the two being roughly analogous positions in the feudal systems under which they lived. With a more general term, though, you might feel like you lose some of the specificity of the setting.

      The real problem here is that one of the settings you are drawing most heavily from, Avatar, itself drags a lot of inspiration from a lot of Asia and beyond,
      I think 'Agni Kai' is the best example here, being either a jumble of Sanskrit and Japanese (Fire Meeting), a legitimate fantastical Bengali word construction (Fire Argument) or a pun on a Bangladeshi sport (Agunali), depending on who you want to believe. So drawing too heavily from that source material there is probably going to result in some misappropriation. When faced with these decisions, check the etymology. Samurai means 'He who tends to the nobility'. If that fits what you want it to do, then use it. If not, maybe think more closely and use a different term.

      On the other hand, with the breadth of the influences, one might question whether there IS a specificity to the source material. If you used something like Wushi, would it be fair to be used with the Inuit influenced fighters of the Water tribe? Considering that the setting is your own, drawing inspiration from a source material, it is also something that you can tailor to more directly suit the culture you are naming from. If it were me, I would be tempted to use a term for Warrior that fitted the angle that I was aiming for culturally, and use Martial Artist for the Ninja playbook.

      So what did I learn about the rules? Well, here's the biggest things: Managing Chi feels a tad fiddly, especially any MC chi. Tags are significantly uncommon and only keying the Chi off of them makes it a rare thing. Reorganizing that system is top priority. An idea tossed around after the first game and tossed around to a much larger degree after the second game (they shared a person, Robert was a player in the first and ran the second) was the idea of bringing in a larger role for the Chakras.       \n Sorry it's taken me a bit to get back to you, it's been a crazy week but I've been using what you wrote to implement some changes! I'm really grateful for such a thorough look at what's there.

      The move limit is largely just game design symmetry (with the one violator, the Waterbender, having their extra move essentially just be a peripheral on top of Waterbending). I could break it, but after some stewing I decided that Bulldozer could replace Counteroffense. While that move was alright, I never saw it get taken and I think that, for all its mechanical viability, it's just not that interesting in play if I also have the modified Defender (which I basically just took from you).

      I'm deciding against an additional Fortune-restoring trigger for now. To start with most characters don't have any, so it's a bit unfair if it's easily refilled, but actually I'm doing so because of Tag frequency. It surprised me that, at least when I'm running the game (and I have yet to play in another's AvW game since player Tags came into being), players don't get tagged that often. Just like how dealing Harm was a very powerful MC move that didn't always add that much to the fiction, there are often more interesting consequences to failure, and most players will just accept their Mild Tag without spending their Fortune on it because Mild tags can often narratively be resolved quite easily. As such, having a lot of Fortune or the ability to restore Fortune without too much effort is very very good. There are a couple ways to get a lot of it, but they require dedication. For this reason I think I'll leave players with what they have.
      However, I did add a new entry to the upper-tier improvements (the ones you can access after 5 improvements) to raise your maximum Fortune by one, letting even the 0-Fortune playbooks get in on the action.

      For now I'm thinking a physical armor move is unnecessary. Actually wearing armor will just adjust fictional positioning of how you can be Tagged.

      I've fixed a great many typos! On top of the ones you pointed out, I finally found the dynamic spell-checking option in InDesign, so the only ones left are the ones that are actual words.

      The third suggested Airbender training move is brilliant and I'm using that. I had some thoughts about whether to make it trigger off of MWI or Airbending, but I like how MWI gives an incentive to occasionally have to roll +Fluid to get the ball rolling.

      I re-did Bloodbending, changing much of what you mention but leaving the no-PCs note. It is anomalous among the game's moves, but it's also the only one that even gives you the ability to, with a single move, completely hijack someone's agency and even with a cost I don't want to enable that. Some of this is driven by my experiences with MH's Vampire move Hypnotic, which I desperately wish had a no-PCs clause as both in discussion and later in-play (as a victim of it) I've really become quite uncomfortable with what it's capable of.
      Scary is a bit weak flavor-wise, I agree, but it incorporates a thing that I find essential to being a bloodbender. Besides, I already invented an aspect of the bloodbender whole-cloth and I'd like at least half of the design to be based on the show's characterization of bloodbenders. I think a better synonym or phrase would go a lot further to accentuate the move.

      With the Lightningbender's Charge, I think I'm going to keep it for now. If they want that +1 bad enough to hand over control elements to the MC constantly with that havoc, I'm comfortable with that. Plus as story gamers we're a little more comfortable with that idea than some gamers are - I get a remarkable amount of interest from primarily trad players with this game (I suspect because there are no other dedicated Avatar games), and I suspect they'd be much more hesitant about it. So I think there's a culture thing there.

      I replaced Impenetrable with essentially the same thing (but named different as it's about Fortune instead of Armor) but that's a temporary fix, I'm deciding if I want to do something more like what you suggested.

      Plantbending is something I've thought about for a while. For now, I'm leaving it as it is. Something I tried to do with all the moves is to keep them from being about specific tactical decisions (such as grabbing or cutting) and being more about the overall intentions of a move (hurting, impeding, etc). I think all of those are reasonably possible with plantbending, so instead I wanted to focus on HOW they do it and what that does to the fiction. The options are largely meant as imagination-drivers in this case, though each thing is somewhat desirable. I've yet to see it in action though so I won't say anything for sure until I've seen if it works how I want it to.

      Entry wording of the Avatar was adjusted.
      I'm actually quite fond of Spirit Within. Scene is poorly-defined, but that's somewhat intentional - scenes with the Avatar State don't share traits of being confined to a specific place, time, power limit, or even continuous action, it just goes until it's obvious that it's time to come out of it. You could change location, cross-cut to something else, introduce new elements, and still call it the same scene if it's appropriate. It's a little bit of a wobbly technical definition, but I'm comfortable with that for now. As for its power level, that's a fair point, but I don't want it to be truly excessively strong. In many ways, it's a character guide more than a mechanical advantage I think - the prompts of when Spirit changes will affect behavior a lot more than the actual use of Spirit warrants I think. (the entire mechanic's concept is inspired by my ongoing experiences with a MH Angel and the maxed-out track moves).
      That's an interesting take on Natural Mastery, but it seems odd to take a move and then have to fulfill another (difficult) condition before getting any benefit. I'm also not entirely dissuaded by the idea that awakening your nature as the Avatar suddenly fills you with these abilities you didn't have before.

      Thanks so much for such thorough feedback! It's gotten me working through a bunch of things, and I feel more solid about the sub-playbooks. I have a couple little things (art-replacement mostly) and I'll be posting v1.6.

I think with regards to a move for lavabending, I'd be tempted to lean towards something like: "When you isolate yourself and your allies using Lavabending, they you may give permission for them to spend your Fortune as if it were their own." It seems that primarily Lavabending is quite slow and defensive - the really common move is this ring to allow a group time to recover and rest. Perhaps something to do with removing allies tags or granting chi to isolate allies to give them a rest (Which would allow some tags to be removed fictionally)?

      The Spy seems really interesting, and has given me quite a few idea.

      The Avatar I must admit, I kind of glossed over since it isn't one I think I'm likely to use with the playgroup.

      I'm going to be keeping Equilibrium for a little, but mostly because I have a player taking Lavabending today who is interested in the move and if I get a chance to see it in play I can get a better sense for it. If it doesn't work out, something defensive definitely sounds like a good path.

      \n Re: the Chi economy, that is FASCINATING. Just earlier today I was going to make a post about my experiences with the Chi economy, and you've just thrown a crazy curveball into my thoughts.

      I completed my 4th session of my long-form game on Sunday and they spent their fourth move-boosting Chi in that session. Most of them (save one character) have four or five advancements, mostly due to aggressively pursuing their Chakras, some flexibility on my part with handing out Chi, and a couple players who've had the worst dice luck I've seen in quite a while, going like two sessions with only a few 7-9s even. The lagging player has the reverse - he rarely fails, though his occasional failures have been spectacular.
      For my game, given the way I've written the big plot I'm using, it's actually been perfect for the pacing. The first session none was used. The second had Chi spent that I used to kick things rolling fast. The third had Chi spent that I ended up making use of in the fourth, and the fourth session had a Chi spent that I used to set up the cliffhanger. They also spent another that I'm hanging onto to trigger the final stage of the Plot at an appropriate moment. At the same time, the advancement count has been very similar to Monsterhearts actually - if we were playing MH, the season finale would be next time, the 5th session.
      One phenomenon I've witnessed in this game was an increase in willingness to spend Chi for move-boosting as time went on. I expected this. At first, characters only have a couple moves - almost all players will have their eyes on a few moves they saw in the playbooks and sub-playbooks that they want to pursue (though my group has been rather character arc/development-focused in our various games, so they my have had more in mind that many players do). They'll spend their early Chi pursuing those things and developing their character. As they get comfortable that they like their current crop of moves (or just start running out of move advancements that they like) they'll be more comfortable letting that Chi go to the moment rather than the future. Essentially, as time goes on, the balance of "Do I spend Chi on this move?" goes from "I could do well now or advance myself later" to "I could do well now, but the MC will get to push harder later."
      I'm happy with that balance. It makes the desire to gain Chi steady, unlike XP in games like MH which stops to matter a bit after you've reached your desired crop of moves and stats.

      Your case is a fascinating counterpoint. I would expect regular Chi use from a one-shot, but the first part of a long-form game is surprising! Here's my thoughts on why I might expect that:
      ~ The players just happened to always fail on rolls they considered very critical. It happens, but five times surprises me.
      ~ The players are already comfortable with their crop of starting moves. Also surprising, but I guess it happens!
      ~ The players are not familiar with what failure actually means in PbtA games, and that it is often just as interesting as success. Alternatively, simply clinging to the narrative authority - not a problem, but they should understand the trade-off that comes with spending Chi to boost their rolls, that they give the MC a big chunk of more long-term narrative authority by doing it.
      ~ The players are used to one-shots and other systems where mechanical development over time isn't that important.
      ~ The players feel that they're receiving so much Chi that they can spare it. This is a concern I thought might come up starting with my second session - I had a player advance three times that game, but it actually balances out in the end. I think it's just kinda surprising to keep track of it like that.

      Any, all, or none of those! Do you have any additional ideas of why they might have spent like crazy? Ultimately it's not a problem - if the habit keeps up, you should adjust your Plots to either have smaller steps per Chi, many concurrent Plots to spread it out among, or higher costs per step in order to keep the game from escalating ridiculously fast (unless you want that!). They're making their choice about the cost, and the system is meant to handle that.
      The big thing I'm curious about is if they keep up that spending habit.

      One other note about the Chi economy from my own game that surprised me: I have a hoarder player. He gets his Chi, and doesn't spend it. He knows he can, but has a very clear intention: he wants to take moves when it's dramatically appropriate for his character to pull out this cool new thing. He had 15 Chi built up at one point last session, though I gave enough circumstance that he advanced a couple times with it. This is an interesting way to play, and while it's totally outside of my original expectations, it's also totally valid and the system seems to flex to accommodate it.

      \n It's been a while, so not sure if my advice on a kickstarter will be especially brilliant, but there's definitely a few things to make sure of before you start:
      - Know how much work there is left to be done.
      - Have people lined up for the extra things you need (art and editing in my case), and a plan for if any of them turns out to not be able to do the work.
      - Have a good budget: How much will each tier cost to fulfil, and how much will be profit? How much will you lose in fees? Have some extra room for things like shipping rates suddenly going up.
      - Having a playtest draft you can give backers (or allow interested people to check out) is a big bonus.
      - Don't make the tiers and rewards very complicated.

      It might be worth starting a thread about this? There's certainly a whole bunch more smart people who can give you advice from both the project owner and backer sides of things.

      \n Okay back to design stuff for a bit! It's been a while since I've had to write a whole playbook. Ground out the Spiritshaper last night. I think some of the moves are really creative, but some might be a bit complex. In general I wanted to play with some interesting concepts.\n
      THE SPIRITSHAPER
      You were once afraid of the monsters in your people's legends. They were ethereal and strange, like nature had special rules just for them. It was like they were from another world.
      You've learned those rules. You've visited their world and come back stronger. You're not afraid of the monsters any more.
      No, those monsters are afraid of you now.
      \n
      Stats: Add 1 to one stat.
      Natural +1, Hot -1, Solid +0, Keen +1, Fluid +1
      \nThe Spiritshaper's stats are thus more static than other playbooks, but is also flexible. It also has bonuses to the best three moves in the game (Speak Honorably, Observe Carefully, Move With Intention). Maybe I could be convinced to just do arrays, but I like the Spiritshaper being somehow weird and special, even among the other shapers.\n
      Chakras:
      ~ Principled and immovable,
      ~ Arrogant and zealous
      ~ Intimidating and straightforward,
      ~ Pacifistic and forgiving
      \nThe middle two are obvious in how they make conflict. The first is stubborn, and the last causes conflict with the more aggressive members of the group (and there always are some).\n
      Gear:
      You have 0 Fortune.
      A talisman against spirits. Choose its purpose: Sealing, Summoning, Destroying, Befriending
      \n
      Moves (Spiritshaping and one other)
      \nI'm teetering on just making this choose 2,\n
      * Spiritshaping: When you exert your will over a being's essence, roll +Fluid. On a 10 or greater, it accepts your command (perhaps with resentment). On a 7, 8, or 9, it contorts your command, fights you for control, or has some influence over you in return.
      If used against another player character, treat the command as an Oath.
      \nMind control/suggestion is a touchy subject in story games, especially when it's against players because of agency concerns. It's why I'm bothered immensely by the Vampire's Hypnotic in Monsterhearts. I think the Oath clause is a really good way to beat that; Oaths have a benefit if fulfilled, and a potential large penalty on a break.
      I'm still figuring out how I want the trigger to read.\n
      * Communion: When you summon a spirit to bargain for services, roll plus the number of conditions you fulfill:
      ~ You present a substantial offering,
      ~ You've met and established a price with the spirit in the past,
      ~ You are in a place of spiritual power.
      If you attempt to perform the summoning hastily, subtract 1 from the roll.
      On a 10 or greater, the spirit fulfills the bargain before payment is needed. On a 7, 8, or 9, payment is required up front.
      \nIt could be argued that this treads the same waters as the Monk's Spiritual. However, I think this is, while similar, more reliable in exchange for more costly.
      The roll +conditions is cribbed from a suggestion by Cneph over in The Shining Void thread. I think it works well here.\n
      * Astral Projection: When you step outside of your physical body, roll +Natural. On a 10 or greater, you can observe or converse beyond your own body. You may return instantly at any time, but can still be hurt and your physical form is unconscious and helpless while you are elsewhere. On a 7, 8, or 9, the same but you are quickly noticed, either in spirit or physical form.
      \nTransfer from the Spirit Medium.\n
      * Medium: When you channel the ghosts of the dead, roll +Solid. On a 7 or greater, you may ask 1 question that they must answer to the best of their abilities. On a 10 or greater, you may ask an additional question or choose to embody the ghost. If you do the latter, take a Chakra in line with the ghost's personality and you may appear as the ghost did in life.
      \nI wanted to play with Chakras a bit. I like this idea. Ghost-talking is a totally appropriate Spiritshaper move.\n
      * Whispering Spirits: When you Observe Carefully, you may choose to answer questions instead of asking them. If you choose to answer, the MC chooses the question, and you may answer however you desire (so long as it is appropriate to the fiction). You may mix asking and answering if you are allowed to ask more than one question. However, if you answer any questions, the MC instead places the Tag or Environment Tag when the move is resolved instead of you.
      \nThis idea was also pulled from The Shining Void, built upon the Survey A New Planet move. Do not underestimate how strong that Tag can be! Observe Carefully is actually the largest source of Tags in my experience.\n
      * Contracts: When you complete an Oath to a spirit, they are obligated to grant you an additional boon. When you break an Oath to a spirit, you subtract 1 from all Speak Honorably and Act Dishonorably rolls until you reconcile with the spirit.
      \nWould this be better if all of your Oaths were stronger?\n
      Backstory:
      You have the Respect of a Great Spirit, and thus its True Name. What is it?
      I Respect _______ and have shown them the Spirit World.
      What spirit have you promised to summon for ________? What has complicated this summoning?
      \nThe Backstory is the first place it's implied that you can actually visit the Spirit World. Maybe it was a one time thing, or you can do it almost casually. Up to you.\n
      Mastery Move:
      Once you've trained with a master Spiritshaper, you may use any spirit's True Name to summon it at any time, without a roll, though it may not be happy or forgiving of this. A True Name must come from the spirit itself to be used this way.
      \nI think it's kinda cool.\n
      Improvements:
      +1 Natural, +1 Fluid, +1 Hot, +1 Solid, Spiritshaper move, Spiritshaper move, another move, another move, subpb move, subpb move, gain a Chakra
      Advanced Improvements:
      +1 Any, +1 Fortune, Retire to safety, second character, change playbook, exchange one of your Chakras for another, Ethereal animal companion
      \nNothing crazy. The Exchange Chakras advanced improvement is universal now. I know the advancements list is pretty darn large, but I'm actually alright with that. I find the other games' a bit limiting, especially MH where Change Playbook is almost always the optimal season advance because you're almost out of advances by the end of the season.

      So how's it going? Keep in mind I wrote this all in one night, so if it seems like I was caught up on some idea, it might have just been that. Looking forward to hearing any thoughts!

      \n Hey, big fan of the system! (And your Shining Void system.. and Super-lite Heroes... but those are other topics... : P)

      First off, popping by to say I've started a campaign using your system to play in the world of Avatar 2000 years before Aang - here's a link, if you're interested in reading not only the story, but listening to a recording of our session, to see how it plays out in real-time. They're all brand new to RPGs, so I thought you might like it to hear some valuable feedback, and get a sense of how newbies play the system. (So far, they're really liking it!) I hope you enjoy it - we certainly are!

      Secondly, here's some feedback on the Spiritshaper:

      - I love the flexibility in the stats. Feels very exotic.

      - I dig the flexible talisman. Already, it feels like 'choice' or 'paths' might be a theme of the class?

      - Spiritshaping feels problematic to me. You did a great job of balancing an inherently problematic ability, but even on a 10+, just being able to command things with spirits to do anything seems a bit much. "Kill yourself"?
      These balance issues aren't serious - I think this is fine as an ability you have to earn with an Improvement - but what I do think is concerning is that the Spiritshaping ability feels very invasive. Yet, most of the Spiritshaping 'archetypes' you have here are more about respect, cooperation, and advice. (A glance over the other abilities: Bargain, Meet, Ask, Answer, Cooperate)
      No other abilities in the Spiritshaping class feel so hostile. Yet, this is the first move you get as a Spiritshaper? I feel like the starting move should be something that almost every Spiritshaper archetype would use frequently - not one that many characters would avoid out of principle. Compared to the other 'shapings', this feels a lot more controlling and domineering - which makes sense, given the subject matter, but it also makes it a lot less open to characters of all moralities and such. If it wasn't "command", but "influence" or something, that might help.

      A way to fix this is to indeed change it to "choose 2" rather than "Spiritshaping and one other". That way, not everyone is saddled with such an aggressive move.

      - Communion is amazing. "Plus the number of conditions" is indeed an excellent mechanic, I'll be stealing that too. The conditions and results all look great to me. This is my favorite move of the playbook.

      - I like the drawback of Astral Projection. That's gonna make for some great moments in the story.

      - Hm, not a fan of this being limited to ghosts of the dead. Why not be able to converse with any form of spirit? It doesn't really fit in well with the other, more broad to interpretation, definitions of 'spirits'. As a newbie, I'd wonder - are all the spirits in the Spiritshaper class meant to be ghosts? Or is this move just specific for some reason? etc...

      Taking a Chakra is amazing. Excellent usage of one of your core mechanics.

      - Whispering Spirits is amazing. Great way to embroil the characters in the game more.

      - Contracts feels weird with the wording "obligated". I also find it odd you can take Contracts AFTER you've gotten the Oath, and then get the bonus, when the spirit didn't know you had a Contract before you got it!

      If this modified the act of taking an Oath, rather than the completion of the Oath, it would make more flavorful sense.

      Regardless of that issue, the move itself seems fine. What do you mean by "all of your Oaths were stronger"?

      - Backstory: I love all of these.

      - Mastery Move: This one is so good, I'm sad it's a Mastery Move! What fun flavor and potential complications! Also, one small thing - I was a bit saddened that none of the Mastery Moves had names! It's not necessary at all, but I think it'd be a nice addition if you find the time for it. ( :

      - Exchanging Chakras is a perfect universal advanced improvement - helps reinforce character development.

      Alright, feedback over! Overall: This looks like a fun and flexible playbook, with lots of potential interpretations of such simple, but open, moves. A great example of the possibilities of the system. Nice work!

      Lastly, I have a question of my own: in the session, we were discussing what 'dodging' would be. Stand Fast? Move with Intention? I interpreted it differently based on the moment - if the dodging was under serious pressure, I made it Stand Fast, but if it was just a simple evasive maneuver, it was Move with Intention. What are your thoughts on this division? Do you have your own ideas of what a 'dodge' should be represented as?

      All in all, thank you so much for this system. Not only is it a delight, it's an inspiration! Seeing all the amazing work you've done with the *World system has inspired me to start doing my own explorations of it as well. ( :

      Thanks for playing, and writing it up and <i>recording it! I'm really looking forward to listening to it later tonight. The game sounded really cool, I really like how the story's unfolding.       I like obligated, or maybe obliged. I'll play around with the syntax, but the word is a really strong legal-sounding word.

      Naming the Mastery Moves might be fun! I'll take a look and see what I can think of. Probably not in this draft, but next one maybe!

      Alright, dodging! My take on dodging is that it would be Stand Fast. It's done in reaction to another acting against you. It would be Move With Intention if you were actively taking a position that's easy to dodge from or if you make the move in anticipation.
      Basically the distinction is: if you make the move before they actually make the attack, MWI. If you make it once they've already started attacking, SF.

      \n So I've been mulling over Animal Companions and talked it over with my group and think I have a decent solution: The Animal Companion sheet.
      It's not a whole playbook, and it's not a sub-playbook. Honestly, it's more like a pseudo-playbook, though I would never use that word in the book.

      The Animal Companion is a supplemental sheet that you get when you take an Animal Companion (whether through the Hunter's gear, the Friend Of Nature move, or the Advanced Improvement). The Animal Companion has one stat, Animal (clever, I know), and this starts at +1.
      The Animal Companion is generally an extension of its owner, and can be used to make Basic Moves using their stats. The Companion can be assumed to be loyal, and does not normally need to be convinced to follow intuitive or non-suicidal commands.
      Companions can be Tagged, but do not receive Mild Tags, only Moderate or Severe.
      In addition to the Basic Moves using player stats, there are five Animal Companion moves. One general move, two riding moves, and two hunting moves. Depending on how exactly you're gaining access to the Animal Companion, you get two moves, possibly with one of them being restricted to a Riding or Hunting Move. For example, if you are a Hunter starting with an Animal Companion, you get two moves, one of which must be a Hunting Move.

      Here are the five moves:
      ( ) Wild
      Your companion has an Animal score of +2 instead of +1.

      Riding Moves:
      ( ) Special Terrain
      Choose one type of terrain your companion can easily traverse: clouds, sea, solid stone.

      ( ) Speed
      When you ride with haste, roll +Animal. On a 7 or greater, you reach your destination swiftly. On a 7, 8, or 9, choose one:
      ~ trouble finds you there.
      ~ your companion is exhausted.
      ~ your arrival causes a big scene.

      Hunting Moves:
      ( ) Teamwork
      When your companion is an equal participant in your
      maneuvers, roll +Animal in addition to rolling your own move. On a 7 or greater, you may choose to use that result instead for your move.
      [I haven't entirely run the numbers on this, but I think it works out? If a 6- is rolled on Teamwork, it's a Hard Move, meaning the chance gives you an extra chance, always with at least a +1, to get a 10+, but also gives another chance for a hard move]

      ( ) Tracking
      So long as you have some recent physical link to a subject, you can spend 1 Chi to find them (though it may not be fast or stealthy).

      It is entirely reasonable for an MC to call for custom moves rolling +Animal, and Hard Moves can be used to break the "assumed to be loyal" clause.

      Regarding the idea of an Animal Playbook, I like the idea, but it's out-there enough that I wouldn't put one in the main book. However, given that "Limited Edition" playbooks are a bit of thing still (especially since I don't actually mean limited), the Animal might be a great first one of those. Alternatively, a Stretch Goal playbook if I eventually choose to Kickstart a physical run.
      Thoughts?

      EDIT 5/5 11:18: Oh, quick note, those move names are not final, they're just descriptive. I'll be coming up with more clever move names.
