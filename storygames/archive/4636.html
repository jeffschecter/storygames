\n I know this is entirely subjective, but I'm curious - where are the semantic edges of the activity of role-playing for you? I mean, there are angry guys who insist that "role-playing" means a powerful GM and a handful of players, full stop. Which seems oddly prescriptive to me, even if that is your personal preference. My definition is considerably more liberal, and I confess that I don't really know where the boundaries are. Are you playing a role? You play a role in Shadows Over Camelot - is that a role-playing game? What's the farthest you're willing to go and still identify the activity as a role-playing game? Do you have a definition? As a thought experiment, can you create a game that lives absolutely on your personal line between role-playing and not role-playing? What's it like?

    \n After several erased posts, I have my breakdown.


    It's becomes a roleplaying game when investment happens more into the imagination and immersion than the physical representation. For example, in Clue everyone has a character piece and a checklist and some rooms. It's all very evocative, and since you're doing guesswork and elimination work through the game there's a sense of role too.

    What would tip the balance for Clue being a roleplaying game in my mind is the moment that translating the board/game into our imaginations became more important than the actual physical board itself. Similarly, when our characters were more constructs of speech and action than they were plastic pieces.

    So, if we started acting in character and making a lot of off-colour period piece jokes, and taking on accents, and saying "A HA! I've got it! I've solved the mystery!", it suddenly feels like a roleplaying game to me.

    Inversely, if in D&D (3.5, say) people start focusing more on which model they are and on which 5 foot square they are on, rather than focusing on what that represents (ie, story and character and conflict), it ceases to feel like a roleplaying game so much as a miniatures game.


    Now, I don't know if this is a "rule", because I only just thought this answer up.

    \n A game with defined mechanics in which the mechanics are mediated by the imagined content. If the mechanics are not defined and repeatable, but are either absent or ad hoc, then Not A Roleplaying Game. If the imagined content is color, but never impacts the resolution of any mechanic, then Not A Roleplaying Game. If what a mechanical cue represents in the fiction changes how it interacts with other mechanical cues, then Roleplaying Game.

    My edge case: Games in which all the fiction:mechanics linkage happens at design time instead of during play. You design a character/setting/situation, instantiate that fictional entity through mechanics, and then set the mechanics in motion, but they behave deterministically from there. This is common in computer RPGs, for example, and is sometimes held out as the ideal for a certain strand of (non-GNS) "Simulationist" play.

    \n In August of last year, I posted this, after talking with Brand, under the title, "I Can Make You Do Anything":

    I recently began suspecting that the reason I love roleplaying as a medium has a lot to do with the infinite canvas. I can tell roleplayers to do anything, absolutely anything, and they will be game for it as long as it's part of an entertaining "game." That's really astounding.

    It's sorta like scripting a piece of performance art. I could write a game for, say, 1 mature female, 1 boy child, a pianist, and a puppeteer. The woman and boy are Mary and Jesus off buying groceries at a market in Egypt (they're still hiding from Herod). The pianist and the puppeteer collaboratively create the people and situations they encounter in the market, based on guidelines I lay out. Jesus and Mary react to these situations.

    AND PEOPLE WILL ACTUALLY PLAY THIS! Isn't that nuts?! And that's not even half the things I can get people to do, just by writing a "roleplaying game." And that's SO INCREDIBLY AWESOME.

    To reinterate, what does "roleplaying"\x9d mean anymore?

    I'd argue that it has come to mean people intentionally and collaboratively creating an experience.

    It's merely traditions and context and shared vocabulary that provides form to all this and makes it seem like a collective whole when really it's a host of very different activities. How many different ways can you "create an experience"? How many different kinds of experiences are there?

    Our river has run into the ocean.

    \n A roleplaying game, for me, is a game with a fictional element in which narrative input reliably and per the rules makes a difference to the flow of the game.

    Monopoly isn't an RPG even if people imagine the events fictionally, unless the players make new rules that allow their narrative to influence how the game goes.

    Playing a computer RPG becomes a roleplaying activity for me at the moment that I decide that the narrative I'm building up is going to influence my in-game decisions, even to the detriment of in-game efficiency.

    I'd really rather call this kind of stuff a Story Game these days, though. To avoid confusion, also with computer RPGs.

    \n So, Jason and I cleared up our confusion over "restrictive/prescrictive". With that I gotta say, for me "roleplaying" for the sake of an RPG is very much "pretending to be somebody else", and not just in the sense of putting on a show, but in the sense of imagining myself to be somebody else. (The two aren't completely disconnected, but they're not exactly the same either.) And yes, that's a pretty restrictive sense compared to what a bunch of other people, particularly hereabouts, seem to think of as "roleplaying".

    Now, putting that into "roleplaying game" is trickier since as a few here have pointed out, you can roleplay (by various definitions) in a game of Monopoly or as part of therapy or training, or in the course of writing a story, or whatever. I don't think of Monopoly or even various wargames as roleplaying games, though some of them come close, especially when they have multiple players, individual characters as pieces, a "world" as the board, etc. It's hard not to be prescriptive actually since we're now talking about a specific implementation of tools/rules to support or allow roleplaying.

    With that in mind, I look at "RPG" as games which pretty much don't work unless you have an element of "roleplaying" (as described above) in them, or to put it a little more positively, games whose primary purpose and attraction is "roleplaying".

    So I have at least two borderlines. Some of those wargames like Magic Realm or even Diplomacy have a lot of roleplaying attraction in them; what takes them over the line, though, is that they can be played completely functionally as abstract challenges with clearcut victory conditions. And then there are a number of "story games" which fail the "pretending to be" test, even if they might be great at what they do. Based mainly on reading, Polaris lies just over the border, because of all the out-of-character negotiating and no real "stance" restriction. The as-yet-unfinished Star Moon Cross probably lies on or just inside the border.

    \n For me, a role-playing game is a game in which you play a role. Similarily, a story game is a game to create stories. There are some things that are both role-playing game and story game (Primetime Adventures), there are some things that are story games but not role-playing games (Universalis, Once Upon a Time), and there are some role-playing games that are not story games (Arkham Horror, Dungeons & Dragons). And there are other, finer distinctions to be made: "narrativist" games, games to play with your kids, and genre-emulation games of whatever stripe.

    It's one of the reasons why my games are all labeled with something more than "Role Playing Game." FLFS is a roleplaying game of character. Sons of Liberty is a roleplaying game of freedom and badassery. Conquer the Horizon is a roleplaying game of discovery. Sure, those three are all roleplaying games, but they're all very different experiences, and the labels attempt to communicate that to consumers.

    Because I have that very strict definition of "roleplaying game," and because I'm not overly concerned about a given game being or not being an RPG, I can't think of an edge case. Either you're playing a role or you're not. That has nothing to do with whether or not you're having fun.

    \n Thanks for the thought question. It has me thinking about definitional issues and led me to a place I am not sure what to do with.
    \n
    \nFor me, role-playing happens when people act on the motivations of a character instead of their own. I guess that the implication of this definition would be that many story games are often not role-playing games for me. Many story games seem to focus on 'the story' over 'the characters' and my intuition tells me that it means they are sometimes caring as much about the characters as their desire to make the story be cool. I don't imagine many characters are motivated by creating a cool story for the players of other characters as much as their own immediate needs.
    \n
    \nMechanics in a game can encourage and discourage role playing as can individual interpretation of those mechanics in real life. My definition of role playing is probably an impossible ideal which players can only achieve in limited degrees. By this standard, games are simply rated on how much role playing they contain and any definition of a role playing game is arbitrary. So, I don't think that games can be on the line between role playing and not.
    \n
    \n
    \nThat said, this is only a definition of necessity. I have used and will probably continue to use another definition of utility which is parallel to my definition, but allows me to use the term role playing game with meaning.
    \n
    \nI guess that I know I am role playing when I care more about the character than I care about the mechanics of the game, though this may be colored by my experience with games in which the mechanics interfere with my ability to role play more than they enhance it. This leads me to the question of how to segregate mechanics between those which enhance my role playing and those which inhibit it. For me, a mechanic which enhances role playing is one which informs my understanding of the character without being an end in itself. If I am doing something simply to satisfy a mechanic, then I cease to role play, but if a mechanic reminds me of a motivation of my character then it isn't a problem. I guess this means that role playing mechanics reflect the motivations of my character.
    \n
    \nI am now confronted by a puzzle. Does my definition of role playing mean that I am somehow deferential to my character? If so, does that mean that games which give me greater narrative control over my character and the sources of that character's motivations are not as strongly role playing than those in which some external force defines those motivations?
    \n
    \nThanks a lot. I'm more confused now than when I started!

      \n A roleplaying game has two parts, to me, "roleplaying" and "game."

      Roleplaying is the easier of the two, cribbing from a lot of what people have already said here: it is where people intentionally and collaboratively create an experience through the playing of roles. This might not be the only thing you do in the process, but it's at least one of the things you do in the process.

      However, you can most certainly roleplay without 'game'. Longform improv, freeform roleplay, these are not roleplaying games, in my estimation, although they most certainly are instances of roleplay.

      To be a roleplaying game, you need 'game' elements. You need rules: mechanics. Concrete, repeatable methodical rules that are beyond social-contract in nature, and that are often used (or at least can be).

      As this is a roleplaying game, these rules need to interact with the fiction, and with the roles, ideally DURING the period of time one plays the roles. If all your rules are between-scenes, in the time nobody is actually roleplaying (like, only scene framing stuff), this is what I'd consider an edge case.
