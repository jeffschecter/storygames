\n Somebody mentioned Don't Rest Your Head on a forum the other day, and it got me thinking about all the pre-existing NPCs out there, such as the Tacks Man in the aforementioned DRYH and the Toymaster in Changeling. In fact, the characters in DRYH make me think about the ones in The Matrix, such as the Train Man, the Key Man and The Merovingian.

So, my question is: how do we feel about such NPCs? How do we make use of them? Do some folks tweak them, or do folks prefer to always make up their own lead NPCs/GMCs?

    \n I'm aware that I seem to be the only person here who believes that rails can apply to more than that abstract entity called "plot". But clearly it's impossible to railroad plot without stressing one fictive element or another. The experience, the story, is emergent and happens on many levels: the plot level is only one of them. Another is the thematic level, in which case (to get back to the point) the nature of a strong antagonist will absolutely determine or limit the nature of the theme. It's a thematic rail.

    Then again, let me quickly point out: There's nothing inherently wrong with that. I don't think Rails are Bad. They are tools, like you say. I don't necessarily hear a pejorative when I hear the word "rail" or even "railroad". The question involves artistic intent, grace, playstyle, player buy-in, and other factors. (Yes I am aware that I also seem to be a minority in this respect. But Catty asked for opinions, and that's mine.)

    \n Yeah I fail to see the difference between a GM doing an OSR sandbox or someone MCing apocalypse world and coming up with preexisting NPCs prior to play and the designers of a game including specific NPCs as part of a setting in and of itself.

    That being said, I think I get where Asif is going with this. Because "canon" NPCs tend to be movers and shakers in their respective settings, there can be pressure (implicit or explicit as with OWOD's metaplot) for the GM to "maintain the status quo" and railroad to secure the NPC's safety and agency rather than playing to find out what happens next.

    With that in mind, I would recommend that anyone considering putting "canon" NPCs in their game provide explicit instructions in the rulebook that canon details like that are meant to change, grow, and be destroyed in the course of play. Or as Vincent Baker put it, to be looked at through crosshairs.

    \n Ultimately my point (beyond this thread) is that railroading in its broadest sense is an Element of shared narrative realities. It is atomic, obsequious, and scattered across many levels of design and performance. Its ethics and practicality are simply matters of degree and artistic style. Like GM functions, it may be divided up amongst players, it may be obscured by dissolution throughout the system in more or less obvious places, it can be atomized and tucked away on certain structural levels such that its influences are not perceived as obviously, and any given rail can be made mobile or swappable with different rails, but the use of rails cannot be completely removed or the world will not cohere.

    An AW Front has a Countdown Clock which determines what the Front will do if the Players do nothing to affect it. It will be unavoidable when it happens. That is a conditional rail with a default setting of "On".

    \n In Das Schwarze Auge (The Dark Eye) there are lots of NPCs provided by the books and scenarios. Because the game has a shared world and a metaplot. So say the kings and queens of countries do have their set entries and personalities. Including them into your games of course drives towards a certain theme, I would agree, and limits what the players can do to remain in the shared world. If they murder someone who is important for the metaplot the world they are playing in is to a degree different from other players. It often can be worked around.
    In the newer books they are giving a perspective for the NPCs not just a snapshot where they are now but what will happen with them and how they will change.
    In campaigns they usually put a little sign next to the NPC's description that tells you if they are important for the metaplot later, if you can do with them what you want or if they are considered dead after the campaign. Which is kinda helpful, so you know when you are stepping onto something.
    I never hestitated including an NPC I thought fitting into one of my games. I try to make them match the description as I play them, because of the shared world, which is part of the DSA experience. But my players did not try to murder kings that weren't expected to get murdered yet. And some small local person really isn't changing too much.

    \n I think of NPCs as fantastic gateways into the mythology of the setting, if the setting has one. However, each NPC has to be concisely memorable for me to use them well, just like a player character. I'm not one for NPCs who have large blocks of backstory.

    Example of NPCs done in a really cool way: 13th Age's Icons, big NPCs who have a continuous presence in the game through their agents and associates, until you're able to come face-to-face with them at higher levels.

    I like NPCs that have just enough "hook" to give me an idea of what the setting is, and then let me take the game in some strong direction. I don't require a game to have those (see: Dungeon World), but they can be a great way to help me get into things.

    \n I like to have a half dozen loosely sketched out NPCs hanging around. Maybe I use them, maybe I don't. A clever trick I learned from Graham Walmsley is to provide three physical mannerisms; that can really bring a character to life. The Warren treats NPCs sort of like Fronts in Apocalypse World:

    AFFETIPOA
    Albino Canebrake Rattlesnake. Soft-spoken voice, a little bored. His name is Choctaw for \u201cOne who enchants and ruins\u201d. 5 feet long, milky white with bands of gold and red eyes. Known throughout the bayou as \u201cThe White King\u201d
    Trait: Enchanting
    * Humor his prey
    * Appear out of nowhere
    * Sink fangs into warm-blooded flesh and wait
    * Sun on a log
    * Dazzle with unearthly beauty

    That's more than enough to go on for me!

    \n I've never seen this done well (because all the attempts I saw were by immature teenagers, or horribly railroading GMs with pet NPCs), and I've never done this myself, preferring to make up NPCs which are imagined so as to play off the PCs' traits and themes.

    But in theory I can't see why it couldn't be done. I enjoy how in Apocalypse World there are recurring characters from game to game just because of the list of names ("What's your Roark like? Is he an asshole like ours?"). If you can find the right context for an iconic character like this in your game, it could be an interesting technique.
