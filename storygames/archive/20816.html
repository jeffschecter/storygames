Sort of like a more careful and complete actual play report saga.

    Eero, I'd love to hear more about how gaming theory informs your fiction-craft. Is it vague correspondences, or specific tools?
    \nHighly specific. Ron Edwards's theory of narrativism (influenced by, as we know, Lajos Egri) struck me in the mid-00's as an insightful literary theory, which was further reinforced by the well-known series of Forgite narrativist rpgs coming out at the time. Much play and study of the way these games work, how and why they succeed creatively, and in what ways they bring about stories (and how those stories differ from literary storytelling) has brought me much of my understanding in the matter. This isn't to say that I'm entirely ignorant of traditional literary craft, but it's equally clear that the mark of Forgite drama theory is indelible in my writing outside of gaming as well.

    I tried picking a specific example of influence, but it's proving a bit difficult, because I don't really have any idea of how common or obvious the way I perceive creative writing is among any of you, and consequently the odds are good that any single given detail will be incomprehensible without the entire context. Perhaps I'll just provide a list of basic artistic convictions, a dogma of sorts, and let you see for yourself - the implications and interconnections to the way narrativist roleplaying games function should be obvious:
    * Art is an act of communication. Literature consists of multiple simultaneous levels of transmittance, such as style and content, interpreted together in a momentary act of receipt. As communication, the meaning of art is in reception and inspiration of ideas.
    * Ideas in narrative arts specifically are embedded in diction, images of language and particularly the plot. It is not uncommon for the mode of communication to be round-about, instinctive and occluded, for human cognition apparently processes information actively, passively and at multiple levels; effective, powerful and inspirational art is therefore often dependent on more than merely clear and rational rhetoric.
    * Plot carries meaning by being a hypothetical scenario that makes claims about the nature of reality: whatever it is that "happens" in a story is, necessarily, a demand on the audience to accept the credence of the events. To accept a plot's credence is to accept - for the sake of the entertainment if nothing else - the implicit laws of nature exemplified by the plot.
    * As part of the plot, characters in a story are illustrated by their choices and reactions. Each statement of character is also a claim about the nature of reality - of humanity, specifically - and thus again judged for its credence by the audience. Therefore powerful plotting by necessity requires credible characterization.
    * Once we understand the nature of character, plot and style in the narrative arts, we can construct powerful stories intentionally, combining the appropriate elements. The characters need to speak credibly and truly of humanity; the plot needs to illustrate reality of existence; the style needs to bring a depth of expression that supports the narrative in non-verbal, implicit ways. Humans care about stories because they discuss issues important to them, so this is what the craft of writing is all about - effective communication and illustration of complex, sometimes pre-verbal ideas, often by the means of metaphor.

    So yeah, it's basically the Egri/Edwards theory of drama that I operate from while writing stories: introduce coherent characters, set up a plot situation that illustrates the desired premise, have the characters act upon the situation in whatever manner they may, and write down the resultant events with the appropriate style. I hope that clarified something.

    \n At this point I'm looking for anything, really - I guess I'm mostly curious about whether something like this is interesting to read as is, or if it's too rough without further editing. Also, does the meta-level process of gamifying the writing make for added value from the viewpoint of the reader, or would an equally good (or mediocre, or whatever) story written in a conventional way work just as well? And of course, both I and Petteri are interested in improving as writers, so I'm grateful if anybody who thinks they know what they're talking about sees some consistent flaw or art or craft and wants to point it out. (I'm less interested in feedback that boils down to "I don't like pulp fiction and think that it's dumb to invent new words for your imaginary cultures", as one might imagine - besides, I've heard that one already.)

    (My questions are really basic at this point, because as far as I know Paul is the only person who's read that story to the end so far, aside from me and Petteri, which means that I simply have not had much opportunity for critical dialogue yet. Sometimes projects fall flat in terms of achieving even basic critical interest from your circles, and this seems to be one of those cases for some reason. Probably it's just that the text's too rough, so everybody gets tired with reading it after the first couple of posts.)

    Regarding S/lay, there are big, fat creative payoffs that are only available with multi-episode play. For example, thematic contrast between multiple player characters (same genre and world and creators - presumably similar style - yet different characters may contain different wisdom for us as audience), as well as concrete crosses (as in, different characters encountering same or similar setting elements, such as NPCs, from each other's stories) are both interesting to me, yet only possible with further play. A single character's story also includes extremely significant maturing with multiple episodes: the character's life goals are only understandable after one or two development sessions, and the actual end of a campaign is, generally speaking, only possible to reach in a later session - most characters are not ready to settle down in their first story. The character's story will also necessarily include contrasting thematic elements when viewed as a sequence, as a cavalcade of Lovers and Monsters is viewed, with differing reactions from the hero illustrating his basic nature.

    In a nutshell I might say that while there is nothing new mechanically in multiple sessions of S/lay, it's by nature an extremely literary game, and all that literary meaning that accumulates through multiple episodes makes the simple mechanics come to life in a way impossible with just one episode. It's like a comic strip that only achieves its natural heights once the readers are familiar with the (sometimes surprisingly convoluted) character backgrounds and plot conceits underlying the simple 3-4 panel gags that each strip consists of.

    \n Well, it all depends how much you want to make narrative structure part of the game mechanics, yeah? This question is related to, but not necessarily synonymous with, author stance. It's also a necessary aspect of Genre Simulation.

    DayTrippers, for example, is a Genre Sim emulating science fiction short stories or episodic TV shows. When played with a strong GM, a DT session has a vertical arc which is controlled by the GM while the players control lateral movement in the gamespace, and the turning points in this arc are guided by the clock on the wall. Scene Framing is the GM's first job whenever there's been a break or change of location, although during the course of play the GM mostly uses Meg Baker's "backwards walking" method.

    When DT is played in collaborative mode ("GM lite") the same arc is used, but adhering to it is a group issue, a shared task. All Players are aware of it, and allow it to affect their actions. Likewise, Scene Framing is done as a group. Because - again - in DT we're not really simulating a "realistic" person's life; we are simulating a weird short story.

    ScenePlay goes even further (structurally speaking) by specifying not only the arc itself, but the individual beats which must be successfully hit before the story can move into the next act.

    In the first example, immersion in actor stance remains pretty consistent because the Players don't need to worry about shaping the arc; the GM handles it. In the second, there is a bit less actor stance and more author stance, as all participants at the table are aware of the the arc, and try to adhere to it. Finally, a game of ScenePlay is almost all author stance; there's very little "roleplaying" in it at all.

    From this we might draw a hypothesis that there's an inverse relation between the depth of immersion attainable in actor stance, versus the Players' direct involvement with the narrative structure as a guiding force or template (and this may be particularly strong in Genre Sims). This rings true to me: after all, we tend to see the narratives of our own lives only in hindsight.

    @AsIf,
    What does it look like in practice? Can you illustrate this approach with an example?
    \nIt comes down to two things: action and emotion. This is one of those things that's so basic (to my GMing style, anyway) that I had to think about it pretty hard when writing that section of the DT GMG. And just to be clear: I say "360-degree mirror" in order to indicate that I'm doing more than simply looking backwards; I'm actually looking all around, like a camera doing a 360-degree revolution around an actor who is simultaneously walking forward. (My style is pretty cinematic, and so - I gather - is Meg's and Vincent's). I take an auteur approach, sure, but that doesn't mean everything is decided by me. I take my most thematic cues - both action and emotion - from the Players. As a general rule: actions get resisted, while emotional tone gets reflected (and modulated, usually upward).

    Action cues are easy: the Players say what they're trying to do, and I say what gets in the way. (Depending on the game, of course, sometimes Players have a larger amount of creative agency, sometimes smaller; but basically: it is their story I am paying attention to, not my own.) This is all pretty standard GM business.

    It's the emotional cues that get truly reflective. Often these cues are not things the Players are explicitly or consciously communicating to me, but rather elicited, provoked by happenstance, or just blurted out. I call this stuff "Psychic Content" and it's actually the main thing I'm on the lookout for while running most games.

    In fact I often feel more like a cinematographer than a director, using my camera to depict all setting and action (including interactions with NPCs) which is visible/audible as the Player moves from point A to point B, in a way that supports or enhances whatever tone is emerging. Part of the GM's job is empirical description, sure. And a basic description will simply list all furniture, walls, exits, etc, but an auteur can go so much further than that. This is where reflection comes in.

    Because not only was it the Player (not me) who decided to move from point A to point B in the first place, it is also the Player who sets or influences the tone I'll be reflecting back at them. If they're angry and thirsty for revenge, for instance, my depiction will reflect a sense of irritation and discomfort. If they're weary and the session is drawing to a satisfactory close, my depiction will reflect things calming, relaxing, and settling down. If they're wary and entering an unknown situation, my depiction will include a few jarring or confusing details to prompt later examination.

    \n I think that's a fantastic example of taking the general GM principle of "pay attention to what excites the players" and making it real and meaningful.       There is a fine line for me between writing and role-playing. Somewhere along the line, a line that stretched to the 1980\u2019s, the two got strangely confused. I use the process of dice, dice-writing, to recreate, well, mimic, the act of throwing dice and seeing what the hell happens.

The major problem is the wish for writing gets in the way of rolling, or the rolling gets in the way of the writing.       Writer as role-player is nothing easy. A psychotic break between the game master and player must occur for the rolling dice to turn into ink. I think the key is a system that does not allow the entire picture to be revealed all at once, the curtain of mystery must hold up until the last possible moment. That where the thrill of the dice come in, waiting anxiously with a pen in one hand and a die in the other.

      There are two games that allow the player to breath, to freeze and let the ink flow while still holding that curtain of mystery.

      Oculus and Four Against Darkness

      \n My wife (a professional, NYT bestselling author) and I ran an epic fantasy tabletop RPG. It was amazing.

      She later tried to write it as a book. It was terrible--she thought, because it was her first real book. Later, when she became a much better writer, she realized that it had deep structural problems that were largely unfixable. While players will put up with a lot of weirdness and shenanigans at the table, readers are less flexible.

      She's had to scrap a lot of the actual plot of things that happened in-game, even eject some of the canon, to turn it into a story that works. And she's still not that happy with it.

      \n I keep wondering if I have anything to say about this subject...the two APs I did on this site are both easily novel length (I haven't done a word count on the whole thing, but probably they're both well past 100K words).

      Starting with the third session of the "Masks" writeup I recorded the sessions, which helped me do the write ups. So for that reason the dialogue is about 90-95% verbatim from the session. I compressed in some places, omitted in others, and occasionally invented dialogue to cover things that we handled in narration or via out-of-character discussion, or to compress a scene.

      The writeups however are neither summary nor transcripts; I wrote them formally, using third person omniscient for the most part. In play I don't tend to give a ton of details about scene trappings--certainly not as much as some more evocative GMs I've played with. (I differentiate my worlds via a large amount of distinctive NPCs, which are my primary vehicle of information transmission to the players; it's also why they tend to speak in speeches in the writeups.) When I went through the process of doing the writeups, however, I incorporated a lot more descriptive writing. I had to work through capturing action sequences that were easier to handle in the game but make no sense on the page...well, screen. (A couple of times in Eternal Lies I ended up creating handles for NPCs I never bothered to name or differentiate in play.)

      When I did the Eternal Lies AP, I very consciously adopted a more novelistic voice, especially in the early episodes where I was consciously echoing Hammet and Chandler (and in the latter case, inserted some passages verbatim from Chandler when I was bringing his characters into the game.) I even introduced a narrator...a very unreliable one. However, he tended to function a bit like the narrator in Brothers Karamazov--breaking into the text with observations from time to time, even while the narrative remained in a more neutral, observant voice.

      So there you have it...a longish body of fiction produced as the result of gaming. Is it a novel? Almost assuredly not. For one thing, I tend to model my campaigns on television writing rather than novels. For another, they're much too loosely plotted and shaggy to be real novels.

      But on the other hand, both in the campaigns and in my writeups, a lot of novelistic techniques are deployed: foreshadowing, reincorporation, symbolism, even allegorical action. I'm really not exactly sure what they are, other than very long Actual Play reports. They somehow seem more than just a summary. They're for sure not a description of what was happening moment to moment in the game; as I said, I edited them while writing them.

      But they do seem at least somewhat germane to this discussion; so I thought I'd discuss them at least a little bit.

      \n To be honest, I tend to expand more than contract :-| which is a bit of a problem as the damn things end up taking more of my time, and more time for other people to read. (Each time I start one I promise this is the time I'll do more summary narration rather than using all the dialogue and every time I hear too many things that are too cool to not use.)

      The biggest additions come in the descriptions, which as I mention I don't spend a lot of time on in play but expand out when doing the writeups, since people weren't in the room with me to pick up on my verbal shorthand or other cuing. Here's a couple of examples, from the first episode of the Eternal Lies AP, which may have the most application to this thread--when I did the first couple of episodes of that AP, I actually did multiple edits on the writeups to sharpen the descriptions and basically pack as much Chandlerania as possible into them. As the campaign progressed and I fell further and further behind in the writeups I tended to only do one draft and not work on description as much (with some exceptions, like the first Malta episode.)

      \nDraw your own conclusions, I guess, especially about the quality of my rewriting; it is what it is. I'm not proud. :-)

      The main point to take away, I guess, is that I'm pretty conscious of the need to change the way the events are presented. An outside reader, who wasn't there, needs the extra support that doing things in a traditionally narrative way allows. I make even more micro edits--like breaking dialog up with attribution tags to create dramatic expectation, sometimes attributing minor details the players didn't bring up but serve as characterization (lighting a cigarette, brushing hair, glowering, etc.) because I need it for the narrative to function.

      There are other solutions, of course. The (unfinished) AP I did of my Cortex + Drama Whitechapel game used screenwriting as the format (you can't see it here, since I rendered it in "book play" format; the original AP I gave the players was in screenplay form). In some ways, this is closer to how I actually run a game--the slug lines for the scenes are fairly close, as you might gather from the above, to how I actually set the scenes in my games, and the rest is dialog, which is by far my most common mode of expression in the games I run.

      I hope at least some of this is useful and or interesting...
