\n This is the crazyist idea I have ever come up with. The idea is this, "Build an MMO like environment with few people and a focus on story". Even as I write this down the idea sounds mad. The price you would need to charge for a game where only, say, 1,000 people can play is far more than the market can bare but I still think it might work. The problem is how do you deliver on the promise of story for every player and how do you do it enough to make the people playing the game feel like you have given them a value for the money they spent. The question of creating meaningful story leads to the equaly difficult question of how do you design the game in a way that the players can tell you what they want in such a way that they don't feel like they had to explain what they wanted.
\n
\nThe first thing that I thought of when these questions occured was to bring them here. The designers and want-to-be designers here are some of the best at getting their players to tell them what they want stories about. Many games even start this at character creation and build out wards with the player laying down the tracks where they want to go. This is also the community to talk to when you are thinking about drasticly changing the publication system of a game.
\n
\nI hope that you will tell me I am insane and give me the solid push I need to seek professional help. But if on the other hand my dimentia spreads to you through the intar-tubes I would be glad to dive into this delusion with you.

    \n Hi Josh. I don't think this is a wrong idea at all. I guess that means we suffer the same pathology.

    I think the idea that a story-focused limited-player MMO (let\u2019s call it a story-MMO for short) seems insane is that our expectations of an MMO are conditioned by the MMOs that dominate the market. These are mass appeal MMOs with high production values. As long as you're willing to abandon some of their assumptions, then I think the goal of a story-MMO is possible. In fact, there are several candidates out there already. I'm not sure any of them actually achieves the goal (though maybe someone will bring one up).

    The first candidate I can think of is MUDs. I know at least one company was offering a story-based MUD as one of its premium online offerings. I wish I could dig up the link, but the name escapes me right now. The tradeoff is there are no 3D graphics.

    Then there are platforms for multiplayer role-playing that can be used to create an environment for story. There are still lots of people who get together to role-play on private Neverwinter Nights II servers. Here it's largely the fan base that creates content. There might be some of this going on in Second Life too.

    Finally, there may be a convergence of tabletop role-playing and online tools. D&D Online probably isn't the story-MMO platform we're looking for, but it's a step in the right direction. I know there have been discussions on SG about online tools for playing PTA and other indie story games. Can anyone point us in the right direction?

    \nThis is a cool idea to expand the game beyond the 1000 players paying for the monthly subscription. Basically, Josh, you're looking to see if you can be the content provider for one very large roleplaying group. What would people pay for this service, to have the game world revolve around their characters? $2 a month? $5? $10? I imagine for such a premium and select service, you wouldn't have to worry about competing with the pricing of the big subscription MMOs.

    I think the audience exists for this product and would be more than willing to pay generously for the experience. The presentation would have to be attractive, but wouldn't need to be state-of-the-art. There would have to be some kind of underlying game system to handle what people wanted to do in the game. I have no technical expertise with which to even begin imagining what that side of this undertaking would be like. But if my full-time job was to provide content for the players, well, it certainly seems doable.

    And as far as that content is concerned, it seems that the easiest and most fun for the players would be to set up the situation (something like rival kingdoms besieged on all sides) and let the players decide what they want to do. Perhaps they play the political game in the former capitol. Maybe they fight orcs in the borderlands. Participate in intrigue in the college of mages. As long as a few players go after every hook, and spend more time interacting with each other than with the NPCs, the story will write itself.

    Posted By: jmhpfanThe price you would need to charge for a game where only, say, 1,000 people can play is far more than the market can bare
    I don't think this is necessarily true. Granted, I know little about business, but there's not reason, I don't think, that you'd have to limit the entire game to 1,000 people. Just like WoW has different servers that have their own characters living in them and don't interact, you could do the same sort of thing with this idea. The main difference would be that, over time, each server, or world or whatever, would start to become very, very different from one another.

    Not only do I think this should happen, I think it NEEDS to happen in order for certain games, or types of games, to be feasible. For example, ever since White Wolf and CCP merged (or CCP bought them, or whatever actually happened, I don't know) there have been rumors about an eventual WoD MMO. I'm not sure about much in the world, but I am sure that if a WoD MMO appeared as was basically like WoW in modern cities with vampires and werewolves it will be complete and utter shit. In order for a WoD MMO to work (or any MMO with a horror theme, in my opinion) it should be rather like you describe, with a much smaller number of players in each server or instance of the world, as well as a world that is, at least somewhat persistant.
    Ten years ago or so, I used to play on a few MUSHes, and basically, if you give people an interesting environment that can change and hold its changes, and people create interesting characters, and the number of players is both small enough that it's possible to meet and know at least a significant minority of everyone playing, story will happen. You may need "game masters" of some sort if you want to create large plots that affect everyone, but the interactions of players and the groups they form will create a lot of individual stories naturally.

    \n Word. Mind you, a lot of the MUSH community thinks that the way I do things is fucking insane, and I can't always disagree. If I had it to do again, it might not be a huge-codey thing cuz it's a lot of work and maintenance, but it was cool and few people had really done anything like it on the MUSH/MUX platform in 1996, so I forged ahead.

    A lot of the MUSH games are pretty low-code. They have some code for chargen, some code for dice rolling against people's character sheets, and not a whole lot else except utility crap like bulletin boards and 'who is online and where are they' stuff. That works, but it relies on the players not to be asshats and resolve things all on their own and figure out their own plots and stuff, or it requires a GM to step in and do those things. Firan substitutes code for the GM wherever it's feasible. We automate tons of stuff, right down to our infamous choice to write commands for having babies. Whee. (But it's a god-damned multi-generational game now in it's 4th or 5th generation, so childbearing stuff is important to the players' stories.)

    Players have a tool to roll dice against their stats. It's loosely based on old Storyteller attribute+skill vs. target dice pools, with the rough statistical edges filed off with a bad-ass dremel. They use it to resolve a lot of stuff so they don't have to call a GM for trivial shit.

    We still GM a fair amount. We get to focus our GM time on stuff that has the biggest impact to the most players instead of resolving every damned thing. The "plots" we run are things like important legal trials, kidnappings, angry deities (plagues of locusts, lightning strikes, etc.), and wars between Firan clans or between the Firan people and the evil humanoid race that plagues them.

    If you were gonna drop a GNS label on it, you might call it Simulationist drifting towards total incoherence. The game itself is so detailed that it has to be some kind of terrible fetishization of the original fantasy setting we created. But my wife Steph is a professional fiction author, and she wants the game and its players to tell cool moral stories, and that tends to push a Narrativist agenda. The code sometimes encourages players to min-max skills and other scoring indicators ("social points," for example) to be The Best, and those people seem to be pretty much anchored in Gamist territory. Occasionally, there are serious clashes where players get all up in each others' grills over what seems like a Creative Agenda war over "the right way to play." If we step in as staff, we tend to resolve those things with a Sim hand, relying on what is most believable in the setting. More often than not, though, we do whatever is most fun, as long as it doesn't violate Sim principles.

    Firan has a roster of several hundred pre-generated characters. You can browse the roster, pick one, and jump into play quickly. They come ready with backgrounds, personalities, stats, secrets, and relationships to other characters (often played, but not always). Sometimes other people played those characters before you. That can be weird, but also awesome as long as the character isn't way out of date. Still, there's nothing like stepping into a character who is already in trouble in a fun way. There's a very simple-to-use system to create your own character, too, but those characters tend to be hollow shells compared to the ones the staff creates. Really, there are only like two or three people (including Steph) who make roster characters that pass our muster on the first inspection. Shit, I can't do it without a couple revision passes.

    \n Coding stuff that doesn't do anything is dirt simple in MUSH. You just @create Cup of Mead and @describe Cup of Mead = It's a copper cup about a hand high, and it's full of mead. But you can't eat (drink) it. That takes code. But what's the purpose of that code, anyway? Why do you need a "cup of mead" object? Generally, you could just include that kind of dressing in your role-play without any problem. (But! If you were doing something like Waiting/Tea and you had an important prop like a Bucket, you should probably create and describe it.)

    Likewise, geography is pretty simple. You @dig "rooms" and describe them and you can open one- or two-way exits between any two rooms. People can generally travel freely between these areas. Each room is really just a localized place. When you use the SAY command to talk, only the people in the same room as you can hear you. It organizes conversations. Use rooms like Primetime Adventures "sets" -- that is, intelligently. Don't dig every damned spot you can imagine.

    So, imagine you have 5-7 "sets" for your Dogs game. You @dig and @describe them. You link them in appropriate ways. Maybe there are a handful of important props that you leave around: they're part of the story, not just window dressing. Window dressing belongs in the static room descriptions. You can pretty easily create NPCs that are programmed to spout off something whenever they hear a certain phrase (text matching).

    Here's what I'll do: Let's pick an evening and I'll give a demo/tour/tutorial to anyone who wants one on FoundryMUSH. Someone needs to pick a good date and time for this. Thursday 1/22 is out for me because I have an Annalise game (rock on). Maybe Friday or this weekend (I have a D&D game on Saturday, I think). It'll take at least an hour just to show you some basic stuff, cuz text is slow.

    If you're interested in seeing this, here's what you must do. Long before the actual thing, you need to figure out how to log onto FoundryMUSH. It might take you an hour to download a proper client, figure it out, and log in the first time. After the first time, it'll be a breeze. Create a character with a reasonable name (we MUSH folks like names, not "handles" -- this is more personal, more "real," than IRC or IM). Hop onto Foundry, say hello, and that's all you have to do. Then you'll be ready for the real thing, which I'd prefer to do only once or twice, not fifteen times over the next month.

    I can show you what we have on Foundry, too. Some tools for rolling generic dice, for example, and even a tool for rolling and handling dice for Dogs in the Vineyard (raises and sees and all that).

    \n Continuity is really important for story-driven games. That's one of the reasons that Firan went the pre-gen character route. Even if a player disappears, the storyline for that character doesn't have to die. The roster system isn't perfect (like poorly updated characters is a plague on our souls) but I think the game is better with it than without it.

    A game doesn't have to have staff running plots. It just seems to me like you'd want that, cuz you get a better game that way. Yeah, there are people who want to be left alone, like Daniel up there. Player-run plots (PRPs) have their own troubles, too. For example, enforcement of canon.

    See, in a tabletop story game with a lot of co-authorship, if you don't like something, you're at the table and can say, "What the fuck?" and correct it before it gets out of hand. A MUSH is more like a LARP. I had 170 characters logged in the other night, role-playing all over the game in small groups. I don't want to watch them (and generally don't) and I often have no idea what they're up to until it's done. I really can't give them any kind of co-authorship over the world outside of their character's choices and our interpretation of the consequences. I know for a fact that players make shit up that doesn't fit into Steph's and my vision of the game. Further, most of the players like that vision of the game, and they don't want a random player suddenly saying that the world they play in is radically different than anyone thought.

    Yeah, I suppose I could just let go and see where the players took the thing, but I'm not interested in spending time and money to run a free game that isn't something that moves me. The players have a ton of input into events on the game, but they have to result from in-character actions. They can't just invent flying ships or introduce aasimar characters because they think that'd be cool.

    Anyway, if we let players make stuff up, we wouldn't find out about it till it was too late for anyone (staff, players -- anyone) to have a say. We do entertain player suggestions on our web forum, and implement the ones we like when we have time.
