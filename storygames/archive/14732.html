    \n I think you're right that they can be both a style of game and a goal to strive towards (although Story Before is probably mostly pejorative in these parts, and I haven't seen it as much as the other two).

    Story Now is the tagline for Narrativist play. My current understanding is that the group sets up a thematically powerful premise and an unstable situation, and then plays out the resolution of that situation (which then leads to the next situation, and the next, etc, depending on how long you're playing for) without any predetermined agenda on anyone's part (usually this restriction applies especially to the GM, if there is one, but it can also be important for players with certain backgrounds to understand) about "how it's supposed to turn out." The story of the game is what happens at the table, and it is both entertaining and thematically (or literarily, if you will) interesting.

    It's interesting to note that many Story Now-supporting games have rules that prevent things like random and undramatic character death; contrast this with 1E D&D's "go through a stack of characters until you get one who survives to 2nd level" mentality. Those rules can help us to play honestly, without fear.

    Story After is where the actual play may not focus on themes or moral character choices, but when all is said and done we can look back and construct a narrative based on how things turned out in the end. That 1E D&D thing is definitely a good example\u2014if my first 5 guys die, then I wasn't, in hindsight, playing anyone central to "the story" with them, but if my 6th guy survives to 2nd level and goes on to continue adventuring, then it turns out this game is actually maybe mostly his story, but we couldn't have known that at the outset.

    Story Before, to me, means everyone puts a great deal of work into elaborate backstories for their characters, and then will focus on exploring the ramifications of that backstory in play. I've rarely seen this done well\u2014there are numerous pitfalls, including turtling, having too many secrets for the other players to meaningfully participate in your scenes, and being unable to modify your vision of your character to fit the current circumstances.

    The origin of Story Now\n
    Story Now requires that at least one engaging issue or problematic feature of human existence be addressed in the process of role-playing. "Address" means:\n
      \n
    • Establishing the issue's Explorative expressions in the game-world, "fixing" them into imaginary place.
      • \n
    • Developing the issue as a source of continued conflict, perhaps changing any number of things about it, such as which side is being taken by a given character, or providing more depth to why the antagonistic side of the issue exists at all.
      • \n
    • Resolving the issue through the decisions of the players of the protagonists, as well as various features and constraints of the circumstances.
      • \n
    \nCan it really be that easy? Yes, Narrativism is that easy. The Now refers to the people, during actual play, focusing their imagination to create those emotional moments of decision-making and action, and paying attention to one another as they do it. To do that, they relate to "the story" very much as authors do for novels, as playwrights do for plays, and screenwriters do for film at the creative moment or moments. Think of the Now as meaning, "in the moment," or "engaged in doing it," in terms of input and emotional feedback among one another. The Now also means "get to it," in which "it" refers to any Explorative element or combination of elements that increases the enjoyment of that issue I'm talking about.

    There cannot be any "the story" during Narrativist play, because to have such a thing (fixed plot or pre-agreed theme) is to remove the whole point: the creative moments of addressing the issue(s). Story Now has a great deal in common with Step On Up, particularly in the social expectation to contribute, but in this case the real people's attention is directed toward one another's insights toward the issue, rather than toward strategy and guts.
    \nTo my knowledge "Story Before" and "Story After" arose as responses to Ron's decision to call the process that identifies Narrativist play "Story Now", and mean pretty much what they sound like they mean.

    \n Story Now (narrativism, pretty much interchangeably) is one type of roleplaying. If it's the only kind you've ever done, then you might not recognize anything strange in its definition. The same actually might go for those who've never done anything like it: much has been written about the misleading rhetorical devices that roleplaying games use to convince the participants that their ouia board really acts on its own - that is, even the GM who preplots the game and fudges with the rules to arrive at his preplanned story often seems to think that for a given value of "creation" the story is, indeed, being created in play. "Story Now" and "narrativism" are useful terms for emphasizing it when in your game the dramatic impact of the story - the factor that makes the story interesting as a story - is genuinely created at the gaming table, as an end-result of the interaction between the players.

    How much pre-planning is required for a game to be considered Story Before?
    \n

    Well-- to answer that, you have to understand that, when Ron Edwards coined the term Story Now, he also asserting that the key part of Story is Theme. (Or what Ron's muse Lagos Egri called 'Premise'.)

    Therefore,

    Theme Now would mean that now, at the table, is when the question inherent in the theme gets addressed.
    For example-- "Will you save your child or yourself?"

    Theme Before: it's foregone before play even begins that the hero will sacrifice themselves to save the child. So if pre-planning pre-decides the answer, then it's Story Before.

    Theme Now: during play, we find out what the hero will do. Until then, it's an open choice.

    Theme After: The hero does all sorts of stuff. Afterward, we ignore the dungeon looting and murder and selectively recall the time
    he sacrificed himself and declare that it was, retroactively, the defining moment which makes him a hero.

    So Theme After may really be Theme Not At All Unless/Until Somebody Bothers To Think About It Later.

    \n Jocelyn,

    The key to understanding that phrase is in understanding what was meant by "Story". Under the definition of "Story" relevant to those terms this:

    Three guys go into a cave and slay a dragon.

    is NOT a story. It's not a story because there are no issues of human interaction *at stake* in the described events. Notice: It's real easy for us to infuse that with all kinds of issues. Do the three guys trust each other? Why are they there to slay the dragon, etc, etc. But look at what I actually wrote. None of that is actually present. The reader would have to imaginatively infuse it with those things. And so, again, given the idea of Story those phrases were developed under what is actually written above is not a story.

    So now looking at an instance of play, are there (a) issues of human interaction in play at all (b) are they ever *at stake* and (c) when?

    Story Now means those issues are alive and at stake, right here, right now in play. My Life with Master is Story Now because whether the Master dies or not is, not what's at stake. What's at stake is, "Am I worth loving?" Who kills, the Master and why is HUGE! What the Master represents and where the minions choose to stand relative to it is a vast, wide open question that is live from start to finish.

    Story Before doesn't have to be some crazy rail-road. Many investigative scenarios or heavily lore/exploration based adventures are Story Before. All the issues of human interaction are explored and resolved among NPCs in the scenario's back story. The PCs are simply uncovering or revealing that information and sort-of emotively reacting to it. There's nothing at stake, right here, right now (except perhaps some unresolved lingering danger from the back story). "Oh, my god,100 years ago NPC Henry murdered his NPC wife because she found out about the blackmail scheme he was running on NPC Alice. Now her ghost is running around killing people, she must be stopped!"

    Story After is largely a psychological phenomenon much like the "reading issues of human interaction" into my sentence above about three guys killing a dragon. After play a player might say, "It was totally important that I kill the dragon because in my backstory my parents where killed by dragons and so this was personal." But that was never actually, a live *at stake* issue during play. Or there might be one or two choice moments in play where perhaps there WAS a live issue of human interaction at stake in play and later we condense the game down to those key points while ignoring the hours of random encounters, shopping, and hanging around in taverns that filled up the rest of the time.

    So if this your definition of story: "A situation which raises issues of human interaction, develops and resolves them," then it becomes clear what these terms mean.

    If your definition of story is this: "a series of fictional events," then the phrases are a bit murky.

    Another thing that just struck me: part of what's necessary for Story Now is for the players to either allow the answer to the question posed by the theme to emerge from play or disagree about the answer and have that disagreement play out through character interactions. For example, in Dogs in the Vineyard, if everybody is convinced from the beginning that the Dogs are religiously deluded murdering psychopaths, the group isn't going to really get much Story Now because they've already judged their characters in their own minds. But sometimes it works to have one or two players doing the zealot thing if the other players are engaging with the theme and trying to make the right choices and do the best they can under dubious circumstances.     \n Well, surprisingly, I have time to go on break, so I guess I'll post my concrete application of Story Before, Now and After right now. Please keep in mind that I haven't been using the terms themselves, but the work method I'll explain here is something I've been using for a couple of years already.

    Before I go into the method itself, here is why I think my application of Story Before / Now / After is different than how it's being presented here. From what I can grok, I think every single RPG session is made up of Story Before, Story Now, and Story After. To me, they don't seem to be opposing concepts, but rather different aspects of how a Story is made up while playing an RPG. And I really think that, when designing a scenario and most likely even a game, it's probably useful to think about what you want to be Story Before, what you want to be Story Now, and what you want to be Story After... because all three will happen in your game whether you want it or not.

    Basically, this is a work method I developped when I was heavily into creating one-shots scenarios, which need to have a beginning, middle and end within the span of four hours. Player empowerment has always been pretty important to me as a GM, and as an end result the first one-shots I designed failed at being four hours long, because I left too much open space and the scenario never reached its end because I wasn't willing to railroad it into destination.

    Eventually, I decided that since a 4 hour game using an RPG meant for campaign play meant using it for a much smaller span than what it was designed for, I needed to limit the span of my player empowerment as well.

    Here is what I ended up doing. I decided on a specific area of the fiction where I wanted the players to have full control (the Story Now element), and designed the rest of the scenario super tightly on the rest of the issue (the Story Before) element. Finally, I picked the overall impression I wanted all of us to leave with, once the game was done, in order to color my scene framing in the course of play (this feels like Story After, but with foresight... or maybe not.)

    What I discovered as I did this was that my one-shots became super tight, and even though the plot became somewhat more railroady, the players felt more empowered because for the elements they did have control over, they could go full throttle on that power without fear of breaking the story.

    So here is how I think Story Before and Story Now (at least) can have concrete applications: by being clear that both of these will always be part of a RPG and by making sure the players are clear what parts are set in stone and what parts they'll be able to impact.

    Then again, maybe I'm misreading the concepts... like I said, I'm slapping them over a scenario design method I already had... maybe they fit, maybe they don't.

    (but he says it is suitable for the Epic form, and for Biography-- where the same themes repeat.
    Which is an awesome way to reconcile the dividing line between Narr and Trad approaches to theme.
    Narr = drama, Trad = epic and biographical.)

    "Captain Hook walks into a bar. The bartender looks at the hook, and says 'You should have been here yesterday, because'..."

      \n Back in post #55, I asserted that 'having an ending' might be the hallmark of Narrativism.

      Ralph and Paul, rightly, noted that non-Narr stories also have endings. Yup, I was speaking too loosely.

      But-- let's look at what makes a Narr ending. How do we know when a Narr story is over?

      Well, for Narr, we need there to be an active (dynamic) moment of thematic choice.

      For example, in 'The Third Man', Orson Welles has committed murder. Joseph Cotten plays his best friend.
      Will Cotten choose friendship or justice? Will he help the police catch his best friend?

      In Narr, the story is over when Cotten decisively makes his choice.

      Mamet says a dramatic ending is both "surprising" and "inevitable".

      Throughout the movie Cotten is steadfastly loyal to Welles. Time and again the loyalty is challenged. Each time, there's less & less good reason to be loyal. When Cotten finally betrays Welles it's a surprising change, but inevitable-- because every choice has a breaking point, or else it's no choice at all.

      Mamet atributes the "surprising and inevitable" formulation to Aristotle, but says Aristotle used very different words:
      "pity and fear". Pity because the audience sees the tragic protagonist reaching the surprising point of failure. And fear, because the audience identifies with how we-too might reach that tragic low point.

      The reason I'm posting all this, is I just got to the part of poetics where Aristotle mentions fear & pity:

      Part VI
      "Tragedy, then, is an imitation of an action that is serious, complete, and of a certain magnitude; in language embellished with each kind of artistic ornament, the several kinds being found in separate parts of the play; in the form of action, not of narrative; through pity and fear effecting the proper purgation of these emotions."

      It's funny that Aristotle says the basis of Tragedy is "action" and specifically-not "narrative".
      Oops-- so-much for the term Narrativism! Guess what Drama means in Greek: "To Act". Oh well.

      What does Aristotle mean by "narrative"? According to Mamet, "narrative" is a dirty word. Narrative is force-feeding theme to the protagonist*. Such as putting him in a thematic position with only one sane choice:
      Narrative is Railroading!

      Drama is putting the protagonist in a quandry, where he has a dynamic choice of action; and watching to see which he'll do. Drama is Story Now.


      (*And, to digress once-again into Epic and Biography, aren't those idioms where the the theme-- is a static thesis, to be hammered-away at ad nauseum. Such as "Grant was a drunk!" or "Hercules sure was strong!" In such cases, any ending is determined subjectively. It comes when the author runs out of bullet points with-which to Narrate the thesis. Aristotle says "Epic action has no limits of time".)
