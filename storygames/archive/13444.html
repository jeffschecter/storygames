    \n I had 9 players for the finals of a "twin saga" (two groups playing parallel for 250 sessions) in my classical frpg Fabula. The finals were one weekend in the woods, from friday evening to sunday night, 24 hours of effective gameplay. It worked due to all the players knowing the setting very well, and sharing in framing scenes during the finals.

    I had 400 players in the first test of my game Memorand, a very special role-playing and workshop game, where players are free to come and go, and immerse themselves in the game at exactly the level they want. It worked very well, due to very strong initial framing, a largely "undiscovered" setting created to be explored and filled in during play, and a game-method that enables the players to take over more and more as the game progresses. At the end I had players leading the game, and that is part of this games idea.

    \n I ran a two year long campaign in college using Ember Twilight (my fantasy heartbreaker that I eventually published) with a group that had between 8 to 12 people. I also published a game called Hierarchy that was playtested with 12 people.

    For Ember Twilight, familiarity with the mechanics as vitally important- especially during combat. As the GM, I had been playtesting the game for 5 years, so I knew it in and out. The other players picked it up quickly, so there was little lag time during combat. Spotlight sharing wasn't too much of a problem. I made sure that I proposed situations where every character, sooner or later, whould have a chance to shine. There were also about three people who were natural leaders in our group, so they took things over when necessary. Though, I think, some players were marginalized at times. If I had it to do all over, I would have topped the group out at five or six instead.

    In Hierarchy, the game breaks everyone out into pairs for most of the roleplaying. It worked for me and my group because, again, we knew the mechanics so well. However, I don't believe the game was very well written, and so duplicating those epxeriences would be very difficult in my mind.

    Peace,

    -Troy

    \n For a couple of years in college \u2014 in college, of course! \u2014 I ran a Shadowrun game that ranged from between 10 and 12 people. I had to approach things in a rigidly structured manner, making sure I made a full rotation of the table about once an hour. It played a lot like a soap opera with guns, featuring little snippets of scenes that would stop at mini-cliffhangers and then return only after everybody else at the table had their turns, singly or collectively, to shine.

    It was exhausting. I would go home with my voice hoarse from shouting just to be heard. I could never do it now.

    \n When I was in highschool, I ran sporadic sessions of Paranoia. People would come and go, and at one time I had 8 players at the table.
    \nOn one hand, lots of the game involved intrigue and secret bargaining between the players. On the other hand, I was almost constantly engaged in reading and writing notes and resolving secret actions, so that I neglected my other duty as a GM, what I now know to be scene framing.
    \n
    \nI don't remember much from that session, but I believe we had a fun game even though the PCs didn't get past the briefing room. Maybe the secret to running games at this size is making sure the players keep each other busy with little need for input from the GM or other group members.

    \n I've run 7-player games of The Mountain Witch that were really fun. Mountain Witch has a good combination of ultra-quick resolution and strong color that allows for a lot of people to do a lot of stuff in a short time. Also I've run InSpectres for groups of 6 more than once (and maybe for 7, once). InSpectres works really well for large groups - partly because of the low handling time of the mechanics, partly because it harnasses everyone's creativity roughly equally, and partly because there's a clearly-defined endpoint of the mission that you can all see coming and steer towards.

    \n Average number of players in my White Sandbox OD&D; campaign is 7-10, and it more often varies to the 12-14 range than the 4-6. It was interesting to talk to Eppy about why the awesome session of Swords Without Masters he ran at Anonycon required three players and was capped at 4, and a good comparison because that yielded a crack-like essence of the sword & sorcery pulp goodness I enjoy in old-school D&D.; My take-away was that Swords requires a group mind to create the fictional space, and races through it at the speed of thought; you can't get good consensus on the tone and grok the essence of other people's characters if you have too many minds pulling in different directions, and explaining and playing the game took less than three hours.
    \n
    \nMy OD&D; games for big groups use a dungeon to create the fictional space, the canon of '70s D&Dness; to set the tone, and we crawl through it at the speed of graph paper; six to eight hours of play, lots of joking around. Like ndp says it works because the mechanics are super low handling, because the "it's your turn, here's the dire situation, what do you do?" lets everyone step up about equally and ideally there are lots of different things to do to impact the situation or die trying, and because we have a clear endpoint (when the session ends you need to leave the dungeon, preferably laden with treasure).
    \n
    \nWith a group this big the focus is necessarily on exploration - where will we go in the external world, how can we integrate what we find there with what we know already, how can we use what we find to survive and get rich - with character emerging only through individual actions and reactions, or not. We do more personal stuff via carousing - a hybrid play-by-post where individual players describe how their characters seek to gain XP by spending their gold on things that matter to them, which shows us what they care about and sets up situations that feed into the action of the next group descent into danger. I think it's noteworthy that I've started using Apocalypse World moves for this essentially one-on-one aspect of the game, while finding that standard OD&D; rules work great at-the-big-table (aided by procedures instead of rules for individual narration, like "describe how you kill this guy" - the art gallery session where I talked about these procedures had something like 15 players at once.)
    \n
    \nThere are lots of GM-advice things I more or less give up on with a group this size, including scene framing - pacing happens through social negotiation between the over-eager and the over-cautious - but I find that some of the most enjoyable and surprising moments in this kind of play come from things that I wouldn't have thought worth framing a scene around anyway, like "gee I didn't realize you're four gold pieces short on that boat your plan requires, what will you do to earn the money?" or "hmm a wandering monster arrived en route to where we thought the action is, but it turns out to reincorporate something more interesting".
    \n
    \nNote that this style is not for everyone, but that in a big group this is less of a problem; it's easy for me to accomodate a steady flux of new players, and in fact that is as much the reason why I wound up playing this way as is wanting to follow the OD&D; dictum "At least one referee and from four to fifty players can be handled in any single campaign, but the referee to player ratio should be about 1:20 or thereabouts." (I know now that that probably meant a situation like New York Red Box has now, where two recurring referees plus occasional ones run open table games for a floating shared pool of maybe 10-15 regulars and 20-25 occasionals and one-time drop-ins; the campaign that meets more often than mine also has a smaller average group size, maybe 4-8).
