    \n I'd go for a new design intended to capture the genre of French comics in general. The same hypothetical game would work equally well for Smurfs, Asterix, Spatio-Temporal Agent Val\xe9rian and of course Johan and Peewit, the superset of the Smurfs universe. Tintin would be in the Belgian supplement. The game would fail utterly in capturing the short-form comedy like Le Petit Spirou, being geared towards adventure comics.

    I'd say that a game like that would be like the anime game the nerds were looking for in the other thread, except cool. I'd play Fant\xf4mas, or some other character with weird accent marks in his name. Fant\xf4smurf, rather, considering the purported game plan. Now somebody go design this.

    \n Seriously, though, I wouldn't pick any game I know for playing Smurfs. It'd be worth it to design your own. Something like this:\n
      \n
    • One player plays Gargamel, the rest play the smurfs. However, they don't play individual smurfs - instead, they play the smurfs collectively, suggesting ideas and actions and attributing them to individual smurfs as appropriate - whenever somebody says something smart, that's the brainy smurf talking, and so on. Invent new smurfs as necessary and write them down - although all players play all the smurfs, the individual smurfs each gain some sort of xp/screen time points each time a player acts through that smurf. Essentially, the group creates their own set of smurfs that they actually find pertinent to feature in the game each time they play.
      • \n
    • Gargamel plays some sort of alchemical subgame. He has a sheet of items he crosses off - whenever the smurfs choose to have a scene without Gargamel (which is always when they don't actively spy on Gargamel, and he doesn't attack them), he gets to cross off some rare and exotic ingredient to his magics. The amount of the item Gargamel manages to gather is randomized. The game has a spell list, the Gargamel player gets to combine what he has to cast a spell. Each spell is essentially an adventure hook Gargamel immediately uses to get involved with the smurfs - the alchemy subgame is there to allow the smurfs some character development time in between Gargamel attacks. The randomized ingredient amounts mean that Gargamel has left-over ingredients that with time allow him to cast more elaborate workings, which then lead to different (not necessarily more efficient) plots against the smurfs.
      • \n
    • Gargamel controls all of his workings against the smurfs, as well as his cat, hypnotized villagers and whatnot. Gargamel actions against the smurfs are resolved with some sort of drama resolution - he succeeds as long as it's interesting and starts to fail when the smurfs figure out the fundamentally new approach to the problem; perhaps the smurf players have to write down the lesson of the episode and implement it before they can start to succeed against Gargamel. Of course Gargamel ultimately fails, though - this is pure emulative Sim, after all.
      • \n
    • Individual episodes leave us with a more detailed image of the smurfs (more different ones and a deeper appreciation of the life-lessons to be learned from the artsy indie rpg smurf or whoever it was that saved the day), as well as some extra ingredients and ruined plans by Gargamel. Perhaps there is a campaign arc where new alchemical options open up for Gargamel as his desperation and shame mounts - the pace depends on how humiliating the smurfs make his individual defeats. At first alchemical options like "weeks spent without meeting other humans" open up for the reclusive wizard. Then Gargamel turns to more drastic and evil means when he gets frustrated enough, ultimately turning into a bitter lich willing to go to any lengths in black magic to destroy the smurfs. The smurfs stay fundamentally unchanged, as they are the axiom of the setting - whatever Gargamel introduces, the smurfs get by somehow. Part of the challenge for the smurf players is, in fact, to explain how. The campaign ends when Gargamel crosses some final line, such as human sacrifice (assuming an adult group playing for the giggles, rather than children), at which point the smurfs destroy him and his dark magics permanently.
      • \n
    \nYes, I think this game has legs. Who'll take Burning Empires and hacks it to do this?

    \n I'm utterly disgusted by this OneTrueWaySmurfism. Smurfs are the apotheosis of RPG calcification and conformism, soon inertia and apathy will take over the hobby and make it implode into pale copies of World of Smurfcraft. The obvious remedy of this evil is to play instead a game about the Scrameustache who, in total opposition to Smurf swines, incarnate the triumph of individuality and heroism.
