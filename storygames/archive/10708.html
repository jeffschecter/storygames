\n This follows on, to a certain extent from Matt Syder's post on a new publishing model.

I think people who publish under the COP model want ownership and control, control over all aspects of their publication from concept, through creation, and sometimes to the extent of getting the book into the customers hands. This control might amount to doing everything themselves, even the art and layout. Sometimes they originate the text, and get third parties to do the rest. Now, in some cases, I'm sure that the publishers enjoy every aspect of this process (Fred!), but I suspect in others, the desire for control of the project means that COP publishers end up doing a bunch of stuff that they don't like, and perhaps are not even good at.

To what extent do publishers enjoy the whole package and to what extent do you do it just to control the final output, or maximize revenue?

If another publisher or organisation took your print-ready manuscript and you had a final say over print quality, then printed and distrbuted it, taking a cut, (a la Mongoose) would you be concerned you were losing control, or just annoyed you weren't making as much as if you were doing it yourself? Would that remove the connection you feel with your customers?

Concept
Game Design
Writing
Playtesting
Editing
Art
Layout
Print Buying
Marketing
Sales

Note that I'm asking this out of personal interest; nothing to do with Pelgrane.

Edit: Added Concept, Game Design, Editing and Printing to the list of things.

    \n See, from my time hanging out with the Exalted crew, it seemed playtesting didn't get outsourced overly much-- if you were writing a thinsplat it was your job to playtest your material (which explains why a lot of the thinsplats sucked), and for fatsplats and new editions, you and other freelancers would take the new text home to playtest with your crew. If something was super broken, the person or people who discovered it would take their finding to the original author first, who would then change it or not depending on their sensitivity to critique. I imagine that the playtesters could also take it to the developer, who may or may not then demand that the original author change it, but I don't think that happened much.

    I enjoy art and layout, but if a publisher or organization wanted to do that crap for me, I wouldn't be mad at 'em. Same with sales and marketing. I'd probably do the thing that, again, freelancers like to do for Exalted and be extremely involved in the fan community to answer feedback and give clarification. I'd like to be kept in the loop about what the general feedback was from playtesting and also be able to do some myself for fun, but really, that's it.

    Before I ever even knew about indie games, I was giving away material I wrote, uncredited, to Exalted freelancers in the hopes that eventually one of them would put in a good word for me and I could make too little money for too large a wordcount as well. I'm not shy about delegating to people more prepared to do things than I am, or giving up control in exchange for increased revenue or visibility. I mean, most of my money comes from selling stock photography for people to do whatever the hell they want to with it. My "artiste" sensitivities are mostly long gone.

    \n Excellent question Simon.

    For me my primary interest and effort is in the Writing and Playtesting side of things. Secondarily I'm intensely interested in marketing and sales but lack the time to put the effort in. Translation...In the grand scheme of prioritizing what I am doing with the time I have available, I find a way to make time for Writing and Playtesting but the Marketing and Sales tend to get bumped by other things. They're not at the bottom of the list in the sense of "I hate that"...so if I had more available time they'd clear the hurdle, but with the limited time I have other things clear the hurdle first.

    Of course this has as much to do with opportunity cost of other things as it does level of interest in these things. Taken as a whole, their is a limit to the total amount of time that can be dedicated to this hobby...including the 6 items you list as well as things like play, internet forums, searching for and reading new games, attending conventions, prep, etc. All of those things have to fit in the box labeled "Roleplaying Hobby time". Which itself has to fit in the box labeled "gamer" with computer and console games and board games. Which itself has to fit in the box labeled "Entertainment" alongside movies, reading, hanging out, and other assorted recreational activities. And THAT box has to leave room for all of the rest of life's activities including maintaining relationships with loved ones beyond where they overlap with with the above boxes.

    Currently, this hobby occupies a fairly disprotionate amount of my time such that if I suddenly found myself with an extra 5-10 hours a week, it would probably go into expanding the size of the other non Gamer related boxes first, meaning there still wouldn't be the additional time available to hit the Marketing and Sales items effectively.

    As for Art and Layout...my skills their are marginal (i.e. pretty close to nonexistant) so there's no real question of doing it myself. Doing it myself would mean not only finding the time to do it, but finding the time to study and practice to get good enough to do it. Which given my current schedule is completely out of the question. I suspect that there's a high correlation between Indie-designers who do their own art / layout and those who have art / graphic design related jobs.

    That said, I'm not nearly as militantly indie as some in our extended circle are. That likely has something to do with the fact that I work with contracts (essentially) in my career alot and am thus familiar with the notion that there are few aspects of control (either financial or creative) that can't be spelled out effectively in an agreement and can't then be worked out effectively by reasonable people being reasonable. Similarly to my graphic design comment above, I wonder if their isn't an inverse correlation between people who are most militantly independent with those whose jobs more frequently touch contract law in some fashion.

Sales is probably the largest pain, which is why having a fulfillment service like IPR -- or even, for many folks, Lulu -- was such a god-send for many of us. The parts of Marketing that I like are getting out, meeting people, talking about games, and playing games with them. The parts I don't like so much are market research, tailoring projects for the market rather than just doing whatever I feel like, and worrying about larger issues like which conventions I should attend (even if I don't really want to) and whether to buy advertising and the like.

    \n Simon, as you note...there's a difference between control and responsibility.

    Ultimate responsibility, of course, resides with the person who has control. But that person can delegate.

    So if your question is at what point I'd be happy to *delegate* responsibility...pretty early on. Certainly after playtest at the latest, during playtest...surely, and even really part of the design.
    At which point am I happy to *hand over* responsibility...really never...which is why I'm indie rather than a freelancer.

    Which I guess its just another way of saying "I'm happy with Steve Jackson Games, as long as I get to be Steve Jackson".

    \nI'm far more interested in managing the final product than I am creating it at every step along the way. I wouldn't want the Mongoose situation you suggest; I want to be more like a film director (well, not a work-for-hire, big-budget blockbuster director), delegating a bunch of stuff but having power to fiddle with stuff at the end.

    Stuff I wouldn't want to ever have ayone else do: Writing.

    Stuff I'd be happy to have other people do along with me, simultaneous or in sequence: Editing, playtesting

    Stuff I'd be happy to delegate and have strong creative control over in the end but really really want help with: Art, Layout, Marketing.

    Stuff I feel like I ought to stay on top of even though I don't want to: Print Buying

    Stuff I am afraid if I delegated out, I'd never see money on, but that's ridiculous because I'm definitely going to do some delegation: Sales

    \n For me, one reason to do every step is to learn about the steps. Having laid out one book, I'm happier to pay someone else to lay out another, and it helps that I know a bit more about it now.

    Control is an important thing. It's got two sides, for me: the financial side and the actual "control". From the financial point of view: I get a bigger cut if I publish things myself. For example, I've made more from self-publishing Play Unsafe than I would if I'd gone via a company.

    From the non-financial side, it's just nice to know that nobody can republish or change my stuff without consulting me first. It'd annoy me if, say, I'd sold the text of Play Unsafe to someone, and they used it as a chapter of the Dungeon Master's Guide 4, without me getting the option to say no.

    What's not on your list is the design phase. Design is the hardest part. It's wretched really, if you're not just filing the serial numbers from some other game, or bolting mechanics from other games together into a workable design.

    So I'd outsource design if I thought it was possible for creative folks to produce brilliant and powerful results from my specifications; I just don't think game design greatness works that way.
