    \n The categories kind of blur for me.

    I've been running a superhero "Campaign" for a super duper long time (well over a decade) except... it happens only once a year. I was inspired by an old comic book special where mainstream supers were banding to save Christmas. So every year during the holidays, we run one session of supers on the Christmas theme. Many of the players and characters are the same but we add variety and some side storylines have emerged.

    But if I had to put numbers:

    One shots: 1 session
    Adventures/shorts: 2-4 sessions
    Campaigns: 5-26 sessions
    Long campaigns: 27+ sessions
    Big-ass campaigns three years+ of (roughly) weekly sessions

    Big-ass campaigns are extremely rewarding. I'll go for a blanket statement here and say everybody should at least *try* this experience once. Probably easier said than done.

    \n To me:

    1 session = Unique case, "The Roach", "It Was A Mutual Decision" Games that can and are meant to display their full range in just one sitting.

    2-3 sessions = short form.
    5-6 sessions = Average. This is where I think I like most of my "deep" gaming to lie. I want a good solid single story arc in that amount of time.
    6-18 sessions = About my limit for focusing on one game. But I'd like multiple story arcs out of it. Two seasons of PtA. All three phases of Burning Empire. A trillogy of Sorcerer stories.

    Long Term Dreams. (These are gaming projects I would like to undertake one day).
    A multi-story non-linear Sorcerer & Sword epic ala the original Conan stories.
    A multi-story arc run of Capes ala the actual comic book industry.
    A full game of Burning Empires.
    Three nine-episode seasons of Primetime Adventures. Three seasons is about how long a TV show can hold my attention.

    \n One-shot: 1-2 sessions, maybe 3 on the outside
    nameless, because it's the default assumption: indefinite. Historically, multiple years for those that went well and nobody strongly wanted to do something else, and as short as 4 sessions for games that petered out due to lack of interest or, more often, scheduling problems. This is the default not so much because there's anything wrong with on-shot games, as because it's always been the unspoken assumption that, if we're having fun with it, we'll keep at it.

    There's also a third duration that i've never needed a name for, because in most contexts it's identical to one of the above two categories. That's a game that is clearly and intentionally more than a couple sessions, but also clearly and intentionally has a definite end-point. To date, that has always been because the game rules enforce an endpoint--i don't think i or any of the people i play with would choose to self-impose a pre-ordained endpoint. That is, there's no planning ahead on that point--at the most structured it's "okay, after the big fight with the sorcerer, how about we wrap this game up, because i'd like to run ...", and it's usually more like "that last session seemed like a good ending point; want to try something else now?"

    Anyway, defined-endpoint games don't have a special name because lots of them end up playing out in a session or two, so they're just one-shots. And very few of them *prevent* you from continuing the game (a new villain/plot in With Great Power\xe2\u20ac\xa6; a new season of PTA; a new town in DitV), so the default assumption of "we can continue if we want to" still holds. So, hindsight ends up classing them in one of the other two categories, even if planning didn't. I guess if i had to distinguish these games as a third category, it would be "mini-campaign" with the longer category becoming "campaign"--the difference being that a mini-campaign is conceived of, from the get-go, having a forseeable endpoint, while a campaign is open-ended.

    These numbers have shrunk as age and responsibility has crept up on me. Growing up, bah.
