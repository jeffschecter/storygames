    \n Wushu is all about generating color, which it does very well. We discovered over time that the onslaught of narrative detail was cool, but failed to generate the kind of suspense and tension we were looking for. Eventually, my players just wanted to jump straight to the rolls without detailing in order to see how they did. They had come to see the requirement of supplying details as onerous task rather than joyful embellishment. The process eclipsed the result, you might say, but emergent story details and character growth hinged more on the outcome of contests than in the storm of details, which really didn't do much to highlight character-significant choices. To be fair, I don't think Wushu was ever intended to support long-term continuous campaign play. For short-term games or one-offs, I think it rocks.

    I have Mist-Robed Gate, but hadn't considered it for Exalted. My group prefers long-term play, so I've been searching for an Exalted port that supports that preference within a less burdensome set of mechanics. I've developed an unholy fascination with comparing how all these different designs grapple with the setting's parameters.

    Edited to answer the last poster: Setting details are sprayed through all the books. A lot depends on what splat interests you the most. I was always intrigued by the Dragon-Blooded and the Dynasty, because I wanted a "Lantern Bearers" style of game with political maneuvering, duels, and hidden conspiracies, so I have the books that detail the Terrestrial Exalted, their culture, and the Blessed Isle. The first edition D-B book is OK. The Blessed Isle book that came out a couple of years ago is quite good IMHO. Conversely, the Sidereal book offers this amazing gonzo look at playing amid Heaven, Gods, and Fate. If you want a game of divine bureaucracy and wonder, start with Sidereals.
