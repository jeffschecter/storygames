\n I'm taking a several week improv class near where I live. I wrote about the first one last week.

It's an intro class and most of the participants are new to improv. We have been doing several exercises each time, and are learning about related concepts a bit at at time. This week we worked a bit with two ideas: "Yes, and..." and having an audience.

Yes, and... We first did a straight up "yes, and.." exercise. We sat in a circle on the floor, someone started us off by offering the person next to her something, and we each had to accept the offer and add to it. By offer, I mean they described something, in this case hamburger meat to be made into a patty. The next person (the instructor) added in broken glass. This made it both more interesting to deal with, and harder to accept. The next person tried to block by saying the glass was really orange peels, but then ended up just adding orange peels instead.

We also did a circle of "No, buts...", describing what an object the instructor had brought was really used for, which gave good contrast. It was a small object on a braided rainbow string which got described as a tooth holder for the tooth fairy, fairy rescue boat, and a painting implement respectively. We'd been encouraged to hold expert authority on the object, so the last person got major points (so to speak) for saying that the object had been the subject of a scientific convention that had been held for 2 weeks and 3 days..after which they conclusively decided it had no use whatsoever. Very funny.

Graham talks about blocking and accepting on his improv blog. I thought there was a post about "yes, and..." but there isn't one specifically. That "and" is so important. It's what allows you to help eachother build a scene out of nothing. We did a series of two person scenes where this was the goal. The "audience" (the rest of us) gave a relationship between the characters, a place and a situation, then the players went with it, intentionally using the yes, and.. principle.

The first scene was a mother-daughter duo in a library, stealing books. Who played who was decided in the scene by an offer from one of the players. "Mom, why don't we just use a library card like everybody else?" The other player responded by saying it was a family tradition and asking the child to put another book under her jacket. So the other player mimed holding a huge pile of books under her coat etc.

The final sketch of these was hysterical. The relationship was a couple, the place the zoo, the situation: discussing their impending divorce. Hard to hold the tension between the characters and yet accept all the offers. Very interesting to watch. One partner eventually suggested that the other climb into the monkey cage, which the other accepted by saying it was just like another time in which they'd locked them inside and they'd stayed with the chimps for 2 weeks!

Playing to an audience - the instructor talked about "cheating out", facing your body at a 45 degree angle, so you can face your fellow player, as well as the audience and make eye contact easily with both. The intent being to remember to address what you say to the audience, since they are the real target, as well as to be able to keep engaged with your fellow player. Other issues, like speaking loudly and clearly, never turning your back on the audience.

It made me really appreciate the difference between role playing and improv. Having an audience changes things in many ways, simply from a logistics standpoint, and I am sure from an energetic and narrative standpoint as well. What is interesting to do may be dull as ditchwater to watch. And, of course, improv audiences are often involved in the process, giving ideas, cheering or being bored etc.

    \nExactly. As I understand it, the "yes" is saying "I accept that bit of reality you've just established," but is not agreeing to have exactly the same opinion of what to do with that bit of reality.

    For example,
    I'm your grandmother
    We used to go out
    We're on the run from the cops
    You stole my land, revenuer (including the part about being a revenuer)
    I'm throwing a punch at you

    Saying yes to that offer of tangible reality or past history doesn't necessarily mean that:

    you have to like what your grandmother is doing
    our opinions of our relationship match
    I have the same opinion about whether we should stop at that house up the way
    I need to give your land back, hillbilly
    I have to let you punch me in the face

    \nI've found that the big problem is that in free form or improv, things bog down when all parties are not looking for change and progress and complication. The punching and blocking tango only gets repetitive if both parties are bent on offering nothing new after the first "yes, and" exchange. This also stems directly from that lovely place where players are so focused on the idea of their character's "success" that they hijack the story and the scene. If no one is willing to offer their own failure or accept a setback, then it's just not interesting to me.

    One of the things that you'll likely discover is that good offers (sort of things that the other person says "yes, and" to) will often have a sort of open-ended shape to them. That is, you will often give the other person multiple ways to agree with you, depending on what they're most comfortable with.
    \nNow that makes sense. I can imagine it is partly because, you're helping the other person broaden the circle of ideas that you can play with in the scene, so if you offer something that they can only go one way with, then it is a narrow offer, and less likely to help them hit on the next good thing that they have in mind.

    It's funny how small offers can be really successful, though. There was one scene that came out of a game of Freeze Tag we played (starting from being placed in some arbitrary position with another person, you make up a scene that would fit it, then when someone else sees a potential scene in a new set of positions you are in, they call out "Freeze", tag someone out, and jump in to start the new scene with them as a part of it.)

    In this scene, one person had her hands spread out in front of her, and the other person said something about her having just done her nails. So she started blowing on them, and then asked the other person (a fellow) to blow on one of her hands. He said something about her having painted her toes too, and she *asked him to blow on her foot*. Oh my goodness. So he bends down, takes her leg in one hand and starts blowing on them like nothing was ever strange about that. It was a great position to switch to something else, but we all just wanted the scene to keep going. There was so much tension in it, because she had asserted her dominance, and held it ("so where are we going to eat?" she asks in a way that implies he's supposed to find somewhere good).

    This was a clear status play, and it was amazing how appealing it was, for how little actually happened.

    \n I have only done traditional improv once, actually at a gaming convention as a warm up for an all acted out freeform game. It was super fun. When it works (and it mostly did that day) you feel really creative and *present*.
    \n
    \nThere is a fun thing about about improv. At the university we were a couple of rpg nerds, always, always talking about roleplaying. At one time we started to play with a couple of female classmates, who had no roleplaying experience at all. Anna had done a lot of improv in her youth, like weekly for a few years I think. And when she started to play with us she was really enthusiastic, but she was always like "I don't understand why you guys call this roleplaying. I can't see any difference between this and improv."
    \n
    \nAdmittedly we didn't only play traditional games, we also played a lot of Nordic larp (and a tiny little bit of freeform) and when we play trad the gm might say "Play this out" and you have to stand up, look each other in the eye and play something out. There are of course some substantial differences. I don't think you ever do four hour scenes (or forty or hundred) in improv.     \n
    \nAlso, I feel the goodness in your 'nail' example. I have just been thinking about these thing: you can get powerful insights in character relationships from full physical play. While trad verbal play is a great tool for creating story lines and forcing players to character decisions there are things like this that actually matters that are just very hard to really get to in verbal games. I have been thinking about a text about this for the knife fight. Plus, the no-touching rule would really have screwed this scene up!

    \n No need to shut up. I think your friend's experience is very interesting, and the question of how and how much one can incorporate improv tecniques into rpg is one we're exploring right now as a community. Graham's just cutting on the edge, as usual.

    The two forms seem fairly distinct to me, although that may just be because of how they are applied, and because of underlying assumptions of the practitioners. Like you say improv is brief, with "long form" commonly being 20 minutes long, and some lasting bare moments. Also the continuity of plot and character of rpg are quite distinct from most improv. Until you start getting to very long forms, such as the improv soap opera that Paul Tevis' group has been doing. I think that has a 16 week run, plus workshopping prior. (Paul how's that going? Time for an update?) Paul and Remi talk about both the diferences and the similarities, or applicabilities of improv to rp on Have Games Will Gravel (shows 97&98, I think).

    Personally, I think that improv folk are doing interesting things. The exercises are fun, and hone your responses in ways that improve your ability to work collaboratively with others. I'm fond of live play--your point about the physical embodiment of a role making different things visible or possible is a good one, I think. Using body position to inspire a scene, as we did in Freeze Tag, is a great experience-it gets you looking at wholly different things than one would normally focus on in play.     Ars amandi isn't very good IMHO. Basically, you can touch each other's arms and shoulders as a way of simulating sex. Methods like that just doesn't cut it in my book. AA worked in the game for which it was conceived as the arms were diagetically sexual (which allowed for other nice things, but that's an orthogonal issue), but that's done. Let's move on.

    \n

    In A Nice Evening, we used dance improv to simulate sex, which worked so much better, even though I admit to being sceptical purely on account of me being a huge coward when it comes to dancing. We played sweaty tangos and danced the shit out of people. They even got "post-fuck hair" (don't know what English for "knullrufs" might be) afterwards, which was very in-game as they stumbled into the larp sweaty, with a pulse, and bad hair. Great.

    \n

    Dance improv is nice -- it pretty much forces touching, but it is a dance so touching is allowed according to social protocol. And you can really do nice metaphors with it -- who is leading, is it rough, etc. Very very good stuff. I'm happy I got to see it in action, and I'm hoping to use it in an upcoming jeep game (A Perfect Evening), but I see trouble using it outside larp. We'll see.
