    \n I was thinking of examples, and most of them came down to either some kind of railroading, or just a lack of skills (which isn't, per se, bad GMing so much as could-use-some-improvement GMing, if that makes sense to you - it's not a question of a technique that's being misapplied as just an area which could be improved with practice).

    However, then I realized that I've seen some pretty atrocious GMing which is the very opposite of railroading. (This can serve as an example of Crunch's post, above, as well!)

    The game was a science fiction GURPS campaign that had been going on for several years, with a pretty intense political situation that the characters had to try to control in order to save some people's lives and forestall conflict. However, from time to time the characters also carried out reconaissance missions, trying to find some of their enemies, new allies, or some kind of resources.

    We were flying through some kind of planetary system when we spotted an ice moon. I don't remember why, but for some reason we decided that we should investigate. The GM kind of hinted that there was nothing to see there ("Uh, are you guys sure that you want to go there? Your sensors are just picking up ice"), but it wasn't really clear whether we might find something there or not. (I'm guessing we didn't have anything better to do, as well - this game tended to drift aimlessly now and again, which is another, but related, problem.)

    So we flew down to the moon's surface to land. The GM made us roll Piloting checks for, among other things, making a safe landing. Sure enough, the roll was bad. Bad enough that we had to roll on a table for damage to the craft or something like that, and we discovered that we'd damaged the drive on the craft badly enough that it couldn't take off again.

    Now, there was no one and nothing around (in the system), the moon was a featureless ball of ice, and our communications systems didn't have the range to contact anyone further away than that (all this had been also established earlier). We didn't have the parts we needed to fix the craft, and we'd run out of oxygen in three or four hours.

    We kept playing for another three hours or so (!!!), but couldn't figure out anything we could do. We were stuck on a featureless ball of ice and doomed to die. The GM was a fair arbiter of causality here, making reasonable and realistic decisions and calls about "what would happen". In the real world, how many arctic expeditions took a wrong turn and then were lost, never heard from again?

    The problem was that it just wasn't fun... for anyone. The GM seemed to be more frustrated than anyone. I remember the evening ended with a lot of angry faces and a lot of shouting at each other. ("Why the hell did you guys just want to go there?" "What were we supposed to do?" "What am *I* supposed to do? You don't have enough oxygen to survive much longer; there's not enough time to fix the ship from scratch, without the proper parts..." and so on.)

    Everyone went home pissed off.

    \n I've mostly played with fun GMs, but almost all of them have been guilty of at least one of the following:

    1) Clarifying the characters' situation only after the players have made binding commitments (in the GM's eyes) to action. This includes "there's a lava pit behind that guy, so when you tackle him, you both fall into it and die" as well as "looks like you rolled an 8, which should beat the standard difficulty of 7, but oh by the way, this guy's standing on a toppled pillar, and high ground gives +2, meaning you needed a 9."

    2) In systems where there's significant GM discretion in interpreting outcomes: going by whim rather than principle, producing confusing chaos rather than reliable patterns. (As a player, do I concern myself with realism? Physics? Psychology? Tolkein-fidelity? Cleverness? Action movie kewlness? Dramatic oomph? Who knows!)

    3) Introducing nonsense into the fiction -- people, places, things and events which simply don't make any sense once the players interact with them.

    4) Not bothering with characterization; roleplaying every NPC as themself.

    5) Providing only content that requires railroading, and then not railroading.

    6) Handing the players a situation that doesn't demand action and passively waiting for them to make something of it.

    7) Failing to say "this is a dead end"; letting players spend infinite amounts of game time on hopeless endeavors.

    8) Putting an unacceptable possibility in an encounter they expect to go the other way. "Surely you'll easily defeat this monster I put in the Hall of No Escape! Whoa, you didn't; TPK in a game where we agreed on No TPKs."

    9) Creating "challenges" where it's not clear what the challenge is or how to even attempt it.

    10) Creating "challenges" that test skills the players don't value (such as "ability to read the GM's mind" or "thinking to look up rather than just left/right/down").

    11) Erring on difficulty. Making challenges way too easy or way too hard.

    12) Not prepping when the game requires it.

    13) Hurrying. Introducing decision points before anyone has invested in their subjects. "As the last orc falls and the battle is won, your long estranged dad who you haven't seen in 50 years runs up to you and says honor demands that he duel you! Declare stakes and roll!"

    14) Not being on top of the rules. Not knowing them, taking too long to find them, etc. (This actually just applies to whoever is responsible for knowing the game. But that usually winds up being the GM.)

    15) Using the fiction to respond to table-level issues rather than communicating directly (players can do this too, but GMs can often do more damage / disguise it better / get away with it longer).

    16) Acting like a cult leader. Appointing themselves the singular and omnipresent source of truth and doctrine, and refusing to compromise or back down. Playing up their power. I list this specifically, rather than something like "being a dick", because some texts and cultures actually encourage it, and well-meaning folks could wind up thinking this is simply how it's done.

    Plus the stuff mentioned upthread about playing favorites, showing off their NPCs or world, and false advertising.

    \n When I was a kid, I experienced one pernicious example of #2 (mixed with a bit of #11) on the list David_Berg posted: the GM who thinks everything the characters do must be challenging, and just scales everything up accordingly. Like if a character is kind of barely okay at picking locks, the locks will all be whatever difficulty they need to be for a character who is kind of barely okay at lockpicking to succeed, say, half of the time. Ten sessions later, if that same character is now a grandmaster of lockpicking, all of the locks will now be whatever difficulty they need to be for a lockpicking grandmaster to succeed...half of the time.

    A nastier variation on this is the Arms Race GM: when your character gets fire resistance, you can be assured that you will never be attacked with fire ever again. When you learn to cast lightning bolt, everything gets electricity resistance. When you have a sniper rifle, you will never see anything at long range. The GM reshapes the universe so that no time is "wasted" on anything that a character might be immune to or prepared for.

    Or is it some intensity thing, if so how would you try and scale it to a group?
    \nMy best intensity tip is to start small and try to read how scared the PCs or the players are.
    In one group of new, younger players, I had a lot of success with just a few drops of weird. Things like a wizard going down one set of stairs only to appear at a door on another floor.


    My other horror tip is this:
    There\u2019s a bit of a divide in horror roleplaying in that some games put it as a primary goal to scare the PCs (via fright checks, madness meters, sanity rating). I interpret Cthulhu Dark to be one of these games. Your character has feelings and reactions that you portray (and get swept up in\u2014of course the players are often scared as well in these PC-scaring games).
    Other games want to scare the players themselves primarily and do away with these mechanics.

    I think it\u2019s a good idea to figure out, both for yourself and with the group, which you like. One, the other, or a mix.

    I\u2019ve gone from prefering scaring the players with rules-light or no-rulesgaming, to try to do more of the first one, use mechanics to making the PCs themselves more real\u2014to have the PCs react to the horror rather than the players. It\u2019s like on theatre or a novel\u2014is it more important that the protagonist/character is scared or that the actor/author is scared?
    This change in my GMing style has come from my experience as a player in horror games. YMMV of course \u2014 the point isn\u2019t to GM or think like I do, but to make sure you\u2019re on the same page as the group.

    \n What a wonderful, painful story! I remember stuff like that happening when I started playing these games, too.

    Some people were asking about Dave's "dead ends" point, and I think I can explain that.

    When you're playing a game, just like in any sport event, any good film, great story, etc, the important part is that there is always something meaningful at stake. We're spending time playing and/or doing something because it creates the possibility to develop something we're interested exploring, something which we enjoy, and which enriches the game.

    Let's say the PCs are exploring a dungeon, and they've become convinced that a certain room is hiding a secret door. However, the GM knows that there is no such thing in this particular room.

    How do you play this out? In many styles of play, players are rewarded for trying new approaches, looking at every angle, and interrogating the GM. They could potentially spend an hour -- or even longer! -- testing for openings, rolling skill checks, asking the GM to describe the room in more detail, prodding at things, collecting resources and tools for further tests, making detailed plans about they will check for secret passages in this room when they come back tomorrow, and so on.

    How do you know if this is a good thing to do in the game? Well, there are a few ways:

    1. Perhaps the most obvious: observe the players (including the GM). Look at their body language, how they're talking to each other, their facial expressions. Are they having fun? Is there a sense of enthusiasm? It's usually not too hard to see if someone isn't having a good time so long as you don't forget to check in. And, if you can't tell, you can always just ask, straight up - almost always a good move, especially if playing with strangers.

    2. Is the current action creating material for future play, which will enrich the game? In some groups, and some games, there are arguably "boring" activities which take up play time, but are welcome overall. Making maps or detailed contingency plans, designing new moves/rules, creating game art, fleshing out background information... all this stuff can sometimes be welcome even if it derails the action of the game. Body language and/or direct questions are good here.

    Another example, though, is some point of relevance which is not immediately apparent. For example, let's say this group of dungeon delvers finds an interesting way to check for secret passages: let's say they want to start a magical fire which creates colourful smoke, and then look for drafts by following the tendrils of smoke. This would be a total waste of time if there were no secret doors to be found... but it might actually be worth doing if the GM realizes that the smoke will attract a certain type of monster, or get the characters in trouble with an important NPC. Imagine that there is a war going on on the surface, and green smoke is used as a signal to attack by the foreign invaders. Suddenly, the smoke the PCs are making in the dungeon, floating up to the surface, becomes an important event!

    3. Most importantly, however, I like to ask: is something at stake here?

    Is the current action going to have important consequences of some kind?

    Taking time to look for something that doesn't exist might be important for a variety of reasons:

    * The group values not being able to tell when a certain search is pointless, so they can't figure out where things are just by seeing how long it takes to find them. (I can't imagine many groups wanting to spend an hour looking for something that doesn't exist, but a nod towards sufficiently consistent behaviour from the GM can be a necessary context in some play styles.)

    * Taking the time to search for the secret door is an important strategic decision, and the group values the possibility of failure more than the potential for wasted time. Maybe the characters only have so much time remaining (their torches are burning low), and taking this time could mean that they will miss out on another challenge. In this case, though, it's important to separate the concept of "time spent" in the fiction from actual time we, the players, are wasting. Here it's probably best to abstract away the time spent searching and move on to the next thing: the important part is that the *characters* lost some time, not the *players*, so let's keep it that way.

    Old-school dungeon crawls deal with this nicely with wandering monster checks and similar mechanics which make taking time to do something a potentially life-threatening decision. We can decide how long we spend searching, roll those checks, and move on to the next part of the game.

    * There is another reason why this scene is important, orthogonal to the issue of the secret door. For example, there is an important conflict taking place between two of the PCs, and playing through the search for the secret door will allow us to resolve or escalate that conflict. Or the characters are having a really important discussion which has everyone paying attention at the same time.

    Generally, though, I think it's best if any group develops some simple method to cut out wasted time. Outside of the three categories I listed above, I can't think of any good reasons why the game wouldn't be improved by the GM saying, "There's nothing to find here. What do you want to do next?" Or even, "Ok, you spend X amount of time searching, you do your very best, you expend [whatever resources], but you don't find anything. You can tell that further searching isn't getting you anywhere, either. What do you want to do next?"

    This is what Dave was talking about with regards to a GM failing to identify a dead end. Think of it this way: a whole group of people is engaged in a totally pointless enterprise. Only one person in the group knows for sure that the activity is pointless. The onus is on that one person to speak up and let people know they're wasting their time.

      \nAll happy campaigns are alike, but each unhappy campaign is unhappy in its own way.

      I can detail my own terrible past GMing another time. But I'd like to talk about an interesting situation I once found myself in--a game that was often entertaining, even gripping, but also remained frustrating and a curious maelstrom of bad GMing techniques.

      Before I start, the GM I'll call F wasn't, in many ways, a bad GM. He created atmospheric, multi-sensory descriptions, had an enormous amount of care and history sunk into the setting, and was frankly a genius at finding emotionally grey situations. These were all qualities I appreciated at the time, and frankly I find sometimes lacking in my own games.

      Some background. I started RPing in grade school, back in the early '80s, but after a 2nd Ed D&D campaign I ran my senior year in college, I hadn't been gaming essentially at all for about...sheesh, 14 years. I went to the local D&D meetup one time, and decided to look for a game--and I found a campaign that was forming via their forums.

      F, the GM, had heavily--I might say, drastically--overhauled the 3.5 ed rules, changing things from initiative (based on weapon speed & using a d10) to hit location (including a chance to sever limbs). None of the races were standard, and human ethnicities even had their own stat mods. There was a plethora of custom classes, and the progression of the existing classes was radically altered. Crucially, he adopted a spell point system that included the ability to emulate spells not known by the caster.

      I didn't have a lot of system mastery at the time, so none of that raised a red flag.

      On the forums, F asked where we'd like to start in his world--Celtic setting, or Viking setting. There was a weak preference for Celtic, and so at the first session we began in the capital of CelticKingdom.

      And then were promptly whisked halfway across the world to a magically-devastated desert. Those of us who were more into actor stance avowed that they didn't have all their equipment.

      The environment was completely different from the Celtic world, and incredibly hostile--I quipped that the ecology was entirely composed of predators. There were some nice touches and reversals, though--the local orc tribe was being oppressed by the evil humans. There was a winged elf who basically wore Big Bad on her sleeve. There was a lot of stuff I liked, and we were RPing well.

      Bad things happened. My character got raped, but I decided to let it go to see the complications. One of which turned out to be that she was the victim of a dominate spell, and had her wisdom blasted. (That wasn't the effect of the rape, but the effect of the circumstances that allowed it to happen.) We spent an incredibly frustrating session at the end of the arc--we had found the place we needed to be to stop the Big Badness, but nothing we tried revealed any information, no matter what we did or how many checks we made. Finally my PCs rapist--himself under domination--showed up and sacrificed himself, and we concluded the arc.

      (I've talked about the rape here a lot, but it wasn't as big a deal to me at the time as I think it ultimately became.)

      After a lot of misdirection, we eventually returned to the Celtic region. And here the problems began in earnest. We lacked a clear rationale to operate as a group--something F frequently berated us for, in the name of party unity. In a different game, that could have been a rich source of interesting situations, as friends and rivals scarred by an intense experience operated with and against each other. But not this game, which was more or less rigged out to be standard D&D high fantasy, and deviation from that idea--or the idea that the PCs could meaningfully effect the world--wasn't on F's agenda.

      I'll note in passing that F seemed to want to run a game with a AD&D sensibility under 3rd ed rules. This is generally not a good mix, and wrecked what little system balance exists in 3rd ed.

      The flaws that were covered up by the initial survival portion of the game began to become more and more apparent. As my system mastery increased, I became dissatisfied by all the houseruling. d20 games often become all about the PC build--the huge carrot of competence hung out at the end of a long stick. But I couldn't plan my build at all; I didn't know what feats were legal, what houseruled feats were out there, and even the effects of spells.

      I also noticed that the changes to the magic rules meant that non-caster characters were worse than useless; all problems could be solved by the two casters in the party--most of them could be solved by the party druid on his own, the only PC consistently able to do anything, mostly by virtue of being able to do everything.

      The posts we wrote on the forums became more interesting than the game sessions, because they allowed us to fulfill a creative agenda that actually changed the world. In the sessions, we mostly ended up with very powerful NPCs telling to do things (that they could have easily solved themselves), and a deep metaplot mystery that we had no luck at coming to grips with.

      One time on the forums I wrote several posts where I played a game of asking every possible followup question over and over to any leads F gave us. These depressed me--they were as fun to write as a term paper, and seemed to indicate the kind of effort we were supposed to make during sessions.

      Once we defeated a circle of evil druids. The druid PC was happy, and immediately began sending settlers to the now cleared region. Until we checked in on them, and discovered the evil had returned. Our victory was meaningless.

      And despite this, F created evocative details, beautiful magical effects, and interesting emotional binds. Well, mostly interesting; sometimes he did stuff that in a different kind of game would call for the veil or the X card, or at least "that's not what I want to explore with this character."

      In the end, the game foundered. We continued to bitch at each other, mostly because interpersonal relationships were the one thing under our control. Various houserules continued to rankle (the worst was that new PCs could start at the average party level -1, but then had to earn back all that XP before they could advance.) I finally broke off about two sessions before the campaign ended, frustrated with my utter inability to make all the emotional turmoil and growth I had found in my PC have any meaning in session.

      I heard once that there are two ways to design a setting--either make it very detailed but somewhat static, like a museum; or give up on the detail and focus on making it a playground for the PCs, like an amusement park. F had found a third way: the campaign was like church. We weren't allowed to touch anything, sometimes gave ritual responses devoid of immediate meaning, and often got berated for our flaws. And the central mysteries were beyond our grasp.

      I think about this campaign a lot still, though. It changed the way I ran games--I immediately became a "player's GM" and swore I'd allow PCs to alter the setting based on their own actions. I still remember how rapt we could be sometimes, though, and how much the emotional conflict that the game had brought out some of my best RPing.

      But the game transcended railroading and illusionism. Not only didn't our choices matter; there weren't even any to make.

      \n Well, the impetus for the game and/or the story has to come from somewhere. If the GM is not providing it, who or what is? In some games, it's baked into the premise (e.g. the world is ending, and you're the only ones who can save it!). In others (like a "sandbox" game), the impetus has to come from the players. In yet another category are games where the impetus comes from the characters (character goals).

      This has to be made clear to the group, somehow, before you play. It's not complicated, but it requires a quick conversation about the style of play and how the game is going to work. You can't have the players sitting around waiting for the GM to throw them a "plot hook" while the GM is sitting there and trying as hard as possible not to do that. Of course everyone's going to go home frustrated.

      For instance, for a "sandbox" style game:

      "In this game, there is not set 'plot'. You can explore and do whatever you like. So, what do you want to achieve/find/destroy? Ok, now how are you going to go about it? What's the first step in your plan? Great, let's see how it pans out..."

      I have no issue with labeling a game or group which does not have this conversation as "bad GMing" (or bad playing!), to stick to the topic of the thread.

      \nThere are plenty of things I might try before it's clear that sandbox isn't for these players. Once that's clear, though, give up (and hopefully do some other fun thing instead)!

      Things I might try early on with players who aren't engaging: clarify their role, hype what I think is cool about their role, lead one of them into an example. So:

      "Guys, there are plenty of options out there in my world, each offering different rewards and risks. Your job is to decide which option appeals to you most, or forge your own that's even better. I think it'd be fun to see what's in the caravan, or offer your services to the embattled mayor, but you guys don't have to do what I think is fun! If you'd rather decide that the mayor seems like a dick and hatch a plot to murder him, you totally should! So, Brad, you've heard my spiel about what's going on in town. Your character Bloodbeard seems like he's out to make his fortune and earn wealth and fame, above all else. Is that right?"

      "Sure."

      "Okay, so, which of the things going on in town gives you the best chance to do that?"

      "I dunno."

      "Maybe Bloodbeard sees an opportunity to catch the eye of those in power? The mayor has plenty of money, and many friends."

      "I guess."

      "You don't seem excited about that. Would Bloodbeard rather win fame and riches by wresting them from the corpses of his enemies?"

      At this point, given a choice between "is your character more like A or B", almost every player will engage, offering some sort of motive or orientation that the GM can work with. If not, maybe pause for that player and look to see who else seems more interested. If the rest of the table engages, and you have one problem player, that's survivable.

      If everyone fails to engage, then give up on sandbox. You did your best! Time to play a module or something.
