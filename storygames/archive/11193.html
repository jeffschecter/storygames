    \n I've written a game that uses the Tarot as a kind of setting generation thing. It almost works. The pdf is here. The pitch is this: \n
    The time is the 12th century. The place is the Kingdom of Prester John, great and wise Christian monarch of the Far East, Lord of the Four Indies. Into this world it has been foretold by the apostle Judas Didymus that \u201ca star called Wormwood\u201d will fall, unleashing beasts of war, plague, famine and poison to devour the people. Heroes from far off lands will rise to oppose these beasts, at great cost to themselves, and ultimately to little avail, for both they and the kingdom of the Four Indies will perish. This game is the story of that time.

    \nTerribly Beautiful uses a tarot deck, albeit in not-so-mystically-inspired ways. The game isn't everybody's cup of tea, and horrendously unrefined, as it was built for a 10-day challenge. I'll be using a variant on that system as a core mechanic for a Houses of the Blooded larp in the new year.

    I've played Psychosis, and my experience was not-so-good. I found it was like playing several 2-person games of Toon, each randomly switching between a dozen scenarios every fourth action, completely destroying any sense of buildup or foreboding or shared experience. The boredom and frustration at the table was palpable. Psychosis holds a special place in my heart as the worst designed game I've ever actually played.

    \n The Tarot has always traditionally been used as a story-telling game; the fortune-telling thing may well have come a good deal later on. In effect, you don't really need a set of rules to use the Tarot as part of your evening's gaming, so long as your deck came with a solid book detailing the associations and meanings of each card. All you need do is shift your perspective from 'finding out what will happen' to 'deciding what does happen in a fictional sense'.

    -Ash
