\n In another thread @2097 mentioned, \n
I've been writing a lot of RPG theory on a small, local-language forum to an audience of half a dozen or so.
\nThis interests me. I'm a language worker/professional, you see. I know that there are RPG communities all over the non-English speaking world, and of course there are designers on this board whose first language isn't English. I'd be very interested in a discussion of non-English languages, language facts, linguistic ideologies, etc. in role-playing game play, and design. Whether it's about translating or working with translators, adaptation, or anything else that's language-related: barriers to entry, linguistic-related design challenges, finding a space for your non-English game in the market-place, or any other anecdotes about language use, I'd be thankful if people would share their stories and their thoughts about RPGs and non-English languages.

I will start things off with a few questions:

Sandra, without being too nosy, might I ask what local-language you are writing about RPG theory in? Is it you first language? Have you had to develop a glossary to allow the discussion to proceed? Or were the other discussants also role-players as well? I'm interested about how you've built a framework for understanding. RPG game design is loaded with a lot of domain specific terminology (especially since the Forge). Would you mind telling us a little more about this?

    Our recent heated discussions, started by me, have been about:
    - Hillfolk-style talking it out ("to say it, say it") vs rolling for it?
    - Finchian trap-searching ("to do it, do it") vs rolling for it?
    - Why game play examples suck
    - In praise of boring combat systems
    - In praise of hard stops and against fail forwards / fail sideways [Here we did come up with a new thing: to separate the "layers"; i.e. a stop in the diegetic character arc is fine [well, we didn't agree on that \u2014 some separated that layer further into a stop in the larger character arc vs a stop in a particular conflict or challenge], a block in the procedures of play is not.]
    - Modularity of 5e
    - The flaws of spell slots & spell points

    \n Yeah, the situation in Finnish is similar (although obviously less fruitful, being a smaller language area). I count about half a dozen Finnish rpgs that have international relevance, yet have never been translated.

    One wonders: if there is about one international-quality game in existence per million non-English people in countries that have had a rpg culture since the last century, does that mean that there are like 500 relevant games out there, untranslated? That's about as many "international quality" games as there are in English, if not more. Quirky thought, even as the English games are obviously more historically important due to having been more accessible and therefore more influential worldwide.

    I wouldn't be surprised if the Finnish numbers are skewed somewhat over the average, though; roleplaying is relatively popular here, it may be that we punch a little bit above our demographical weight class in the rpg arts. Still, we may nevertheless be talking of several hundred worthwhile games that even the more scholarly of us have never studied.

    \n English and some british games get translations into spanish usually after many years of their original launch, though the local communities consume the original products in their native language. In gameplay it turns into a chaotic mix of english, "spanglish" and spaniard terms, along with any local slang.

    In our peruvian community any veteran will get the phrase "\xa1Casteo fireball al orco!" (I cast fireball against the orc!) and newbies will soon get stuck to it, because in our own language "le lanzo el hechizo de bola de fuego al orco!" somewhat lacks the reaction speed and the strenght of the former expression.

    On instances where the spanish expression sounds better, it's easier to understand, it's shorter or plain funnier, players will often say it in spanish, or "spanglish", by conjugating english words as if they were spanish verbs.

    Surely rpg discussions in just about all European languages are pretty common? Even Eastern Europe is starting to have a noticeable "scene" these days.
    \nI am pretty sure that they are really common. I am Czech and there are multiple Czech and Slovak (Czech and Slovak are mutually intelligible and we understand each other without difficulty, so both countries create sort of one pool of gamers, also communities are intertwined) discussion servers focused on RPGs, not mentioning discussions on FB and other media. There is solid market for RPGs and a lot of Czech and Slovak releplaying games (very few if any at all are translated into English, my The Few is one of the exceptions). Same goes for Poland (Polish is Slavic language, so I can understand it to a degree and I took interest in few Polish RPGs before), where there are again multiple discussion servers and a lot of RPGs in Polish. In Germany, where I live now, it goes without saying - that is a huge market (between 90 and 100 million native speakers) and there are a lot of RPG discussions and really interesting games that are available only in German. DeGenesis is an example of stunning German RPG that was also translated in English.

    About Eastern Europe - as far as I know, there was a big boom for RPGs in Russia like five years ago and there were serious considerations about translating RPGs for the growing Russian market. But Crimean crisis and trade embargos put mostly stop on these activities. Not sure how is situation right now, maybe someone here knows better.

    Sorry, had to join.
    In my native Denmark, I find there's not much discussion as such in Danish, as most feel the language is too small and limited, so most communication is done in English.
    There are several reasons for that, but mainly it's to avoid misunderstandings with our Scandinavian languages. Norwegian and Swedish are easy enough for us to understand (written, not spoken, especially some obscure dialects), but reading it, to me, feels too much like Danish with loads of typos, and that annoys me to no end.
    Also, in my usual circles, there are quite a few foreigners, Finns, Swedes, Norwegians, USians, Belarusians etc, so English is used as a main language in order to allow people in.
    At Fastaval we have a large translator task force that will translate any scenario into English if the author merely asks (and has the game finished within spitting distance of the deadline), and many of us also act as simultaneous interpreters (there are badges to identify us) at various functions.

    Some random thoughts.

    Sweden got a thriving theory creating community, both in tabletop and LARP. I'm attending a local RPG theory discussion group every week, where we (read: I) write summaries about what we discussed. We also got a glossary of our own.

    Swedish games are starting to leak out on international markets (En World's 10 Most Anticipated RPGs of 2017 - top three are all Swedish).

    Language wise, it's easier to set a tone in the roleplaying book if it's written in my native language.

    It's easier to write in a gender neutral way in Swedish.

    It's easier to create new words to describe things in roleplaying game theory, because we can take two words and put them together to make a third new word. Words that people will get even if they haven't heard the word before. "Samber\xe4ttande" has more to it than "Collaborative storytelling" does.

    It doesn't matter if I write an English or a Swedish game for the Swedish audience.

    I can write in Swedish to beta test my game before I translate it.

    Translating a game is harder than writing it in English directly.
