\n Hi!

I hope this is the right category to start. And I hope this is the right forum to start. First of all I'd like to tell that this is not intended to be bitching. I started to play story games, because classic RPGs haven't met my needs and story games promised me all the stuff I missed. But I still miss many things after having played some story games. Until now I've played Dogs in the Vineyard, Prime Time Adventures, 3:16, Universalis, Trollbabe and some others.

My problem is, that most of these games give the adventure and the session a structure which is same every time. This can be good theoretically. I mean there are some TV series I love, and the concept of TV serires are that the structure is always the same and the conflicts are always the same type. I don't know why, but I dislike this. It was very much fun in classic RPGs that I could make up an adventure with any structure I liked. It could be an 'arena' with locations the characters could travel, or it could be a 'railroad', like a road movie. It could be totally different from session to session. And the system didn't want to tell me how it should be played, I could GM as it naturally came to me.

I know, classic RPGs had their problems. There is no real catharsis if the players are confined to a strict suleset, they should have the ability to create the fiction, not only play it from start to end. I mean I totally used the players' creativity for example to think up NPCs on the fly, and my adventures are based more on the characters than the world. And I don't even want to have the power to choose the structure of the adventure, let the players turn the tables, surprise me and start to chase their chasers, for example. I totally love to give the players what they want even if I didn't expect it. Especially if I didn't expect it.

So I'm inclined from now on to play classic RPGs with light rules that won't hinder me and use story game like effects that will make the game more awesom and GM as I feel instead of listening to story games that want to tell me how to play.

Does anyone else have similar problems?
Do you have any other solution for this problem?

    \n First, a possible solution to your problem: Burning Wheel. I think it'll hit your sweet spot for GM/player interaction and GM authority.

    Second, a little discussion.

    Part of the thinking behind the games you mention is that the system should tell you how it should be played. If the system doesn't tell you how to play it, there's no reason to change systems. If, choosing a random system, Chaosium really lets you play every type of game, why use any other?

    GMing as it comes to you is one mode of play, but some games ask you to change that to fit the game. That's part of what a game is: a set of rules that ask you to do things you wouldn't otherwise. Dogs has that structure because that's the structure the game is designed to support. Not supporting a different structure is no more a knock against the game than saying that a chair is not edible: it's not supposed to be.

    So my suggestion is two-fold: when you want to play the style you're used to, play Burning Wheel. When you try something different, accept the style of the game.

    \n This is an interesting conversation. Add me to the group of people who are confused about what "the problem" is. It sounds like there is something about these games that you wish you were getting out of them, but you can't achieve with most of the games you've tried. Can you tell us more about that, maybe give an example of what sort of thing you'd want to do, but can't using one of those games?

    It IS notable that the indie design mindset is one that's leaning very much away from One System you can use for everything and much more towards a variety of games that each do one (or two) things really well. I think the theoretical "hardcore indie gamer" expects to play a variety of games, not to find One System with which to run everything. The idea is very much that if you want a different experience, you play a different game.

    That said, I find a lot of "indie games/story games" provide such powerful tools for story creation that they can be used much more flexibly than the basic text often suggests. For instance, if you like Dogs in the Vineyard, there's no reason you couldn't use the same rules to play out a Victorian drama (ignoring the Town Creation rules).

    Yeah, some things might get a little funny (for instance, expect some violence in places you might not normally see it, and vice-versa) and you might run into some problems if you don't give it some thought first... but that's not that unusual. Playing out a Victorian drama with D&D would be a whole harder than playing out a Victorian drama with Dogs. Same with most other "traditional" systems I can think of.

    If you ignore the modules published for D&D, why can't you ignore the Town Creation procedure in Dogs?

    That said:

    The Shadow of Yesterday (specifically the Solar System version) is a great general-purpose tool that is genre-independent and feels very traditional in some ways. Same for the underlying engine for Lady Blackbird as well as FATE/Spirit of the Century. None specify a particular length of play or scene structure, or say too much about adventure design.

    None of these games are like, say, My Life with Master or The Mountain Witch, which really put you into a specific story, and might be harder to "hack" into different types of stories.

    There are also some great generic, free resolution systems like The Pool or Otherkind Dice, which can be used as-is or even combined with other rulesets.

    So, what are you missing in those games that you can do more easily in other games? Is it genre, or story conventions, or something else? Or is it that you just prefer the more "traditional" games to these "indie" games?

    \n Here's my stream of consciousness response:

    Literary Theory distinguishes between internal structure (matter) and external structure (form).

    Dogs is tight on both. Matter: sin, judgement, community, faith... Form: initiation-town-judgement-reflection-town-judgement-reflection...

    PTA is strong on form, but has no matter. Form is always the episodic tv show format. But matter can be anything. The Wire is unlike BSG is unlike Seinfield in a way no two Dogs games can be different.

    3:16 is pretty tight on both, too. Form is mission-downtime-mission... 3:16 also carries a decent thematic punch. Nothing deep but palpable.

    Universalis ok, here's where we start hitting problems. I don't really see any such structure in Universalis. I'm not super-familiar with it, but I'm at loss.

    Trollbabe, same. The matter is whatever your questions are, and the form is not really pre-supposed. I'm not seeing it.

    So yeah, it might be good to know what structure means in this context.

    I tried thinking about other games in this way.
    *oD&D...out of the box this is pretty darn structured. What you do is you get treasure, spend it, get treasure. Level up, zero to hero. Get a castle or church, retire. It is decisively picaresque in its progress.
    *mid-period D&D...a lot more loose, XP is awarded for killing monsters/overcoming the opposition which technically gives you a lot more maneuver space with your subject matter. There's is still the level-up form, but it's not that rigid. In almost all instances the level-related character positioning is also gone, so there's definitely less structure there.
    *4E D&D...quite tight in form. You can do other stuff but by the book each game is a series of encounters. You can see the same trend in late-period 3E & Pathfinder with adventure paths. Compared to earlier editions encounters are far more complex and in the forefront (instead of exploration), so encounters is where the game is at, which requires a pretty tight path.

    Vampire tM. The subject matter is implied but not explicitly supported by the rules (which is why the game caught a lot of flak in some circles), very loose there imo. The form is pretty loose but the book talks a lot about how the storyteller should build their chronicles, so there's definitely an underlying form to it.

    And to stop the threadjacking, Eppy Ravachol has a standard rant that may address Gabor's issues. He calls it the Archipelago and the River Delta metaphor. Archipelago play (as distinct from playing Matthijs' Archipelago specifically) deals with navigating through the ocean of free play, and touching down on mechanics when the mood and moment are right. Hopping from island to island, there are a plethora of rules that can be invoked, and they are (when things are working correctly) when it is both right and desired. There's a huge amount of room for Rule Zero here. It's raised event to an aesthetic philosophy, which may be how it was intended all the time (but can be used to cover a world of errors). A River Delta game is one where the rules focus your play like the river banks do. You are not left to wonder where you should be headed, the rules hand you off from free play in the water to the next set of procedures.

    Now the River Delta is likely to be very dependable. You're less liable to get lost, or hit uncharted rocks in the water. But it's a completely different experience, and may be suited to different skills. Playing a primarily free-play game you are susceptible to the storms of player discontent, or may let you be beached on the shoals of GM burnout. The River Delta makes it much easier for anyone to pick up the role of GM/navigator. And this kind of structure is well suited to more distributed, multi-GM play. If there is not just one hand at the wheel, then having rules for how the many hands work together makes sense.

There is a pleasure to, as a GM, be able to take the reigns and sculpt an endlessly varied campaign.     \nWell, Ribbon Drive is about going on a road trip and discovering yourself. Mechanically, one of the things that play centers around are the characters' Futures: statements about what they want and think will happen in the future. The way Futures work is that, over the course of play, Futures are woven into conversations and reflected upon.

    The game invites you to let go of your Futures, to realize that they don't really matter, to learn to live in the present. An important part of play, the climactic moment, is when someone let's go of all of their Futures.

    Now, in a way, that's about conflict, you could say. The conflict between goals and the present. The conflict between two visions of yourself. IMO.

    So, that's how Ribbon Drive does it. There aren't really opposing forces. There's just you, deciding what sort of force you are.

      \n Yes, exactly. I'm in total agreement with the desire for more character exposition, exploration of environments, etc. Playing conflict-conflict-conflict will, of course, get stale sooner or later, at least for some of us.

      What I'm not seeing (as Leonard's anecdote shows) is any coherent argument for why or how "indie/story" games facilitate that kind of thing less well than "traditional" games.

      In other words, if you miss having exposition scenes, long stretches where nothing happens (and I mean that in the most positive way), and similar slow-paced techniques of story-telling... why does that suggest a return to more traditional systems, as the OP is suggesting?

      I'm all for slow-paced stories. But what's the connection between that and some kind of dichotomy between "trad" and "indie" games? I definitely see a difference in terms of play culture, yes. For sure. But I'd love to hear more about what it is in terms of design that facilitates that kind of play. Because from my own limited experience I see no reason I can't have drawn-out free play in In a Wicked Age..., or Dogs, or pick-your-own-game-here, just as easily as I can in a game of D&D or Call of Cthulhu or GURPS.

      I see two related issues, and both interesting ones:

      1. How does existing game design push us towards faster story motion (more conflicts resolved per unit of time) or slower story motion (fewer conflicts resolved in the same span of time)? What components of, say, D&D facilitate one versus the other?

      (I suspect handling elements of escalation in various different ways is an important variable to consider here.)

      2. How can we design games that aren't based on the creation and resolution of conflicts? What areas for design exist here that are still untouched?

      \n Maybe games are just too lacking generally in gradations of time of play?

      I really like one-off, lots of stuff done games. That's because I play infrequently. Like, really infrequently compared to lots of you. I want to know that lots of mental space can get covered in a single evening. A long event is three sessions. A tremendously long story is 5-7 sessions.

      I can't wait for the slow burn. I definitely can't wait for the really slow burn, like the open-ended campaign.

      I really do think that having some time off improves play experience. It's kinda like the pilot of a show, especially a two-parter.       Too open-ended and too long and commitments fade. Sessions are missed. Characters become beloved stereotypes that don't really do too much or risk too much.

      \n I've been mulling over the OP's post for a while now, and there's this thing that's nagging at me.

      What constitutes a more or less "structured" RPG?

      Let's look at THE classic RPG, AD&D. Defined character classes, 20 levels, strict character progression, and a rewards system focused around killing monsters. That, right there, informs a specific story structure. AD&D was never really The One System at all; it did a handful of story types. D&D (the various boxed sets) was even more focused in its storytelling.

      I think the confusion with "classic" RPG's is that, since they were the only things available at the time, we used them for everything. How many games did you play where the DM said "In my campaign, Elves have these rules modifications" and so forth. We constantly made house-rules for things because the actual game did not provide guidance for the thing we wanted to do. We ignored the rules if they got in our way. But the thing is, the system was designed to tell a particular kind of story, so we had to hack in those rules to cover things for which there were no systems.

      The lack of structure in a game is not a good thing. The structure in an RPG exists to direct, inspire, and inform the storytelling that game is shooting for. If you break the structure of the game - as was often the case with classic RPG's - you're basically just making your own game entirely. The key is to find a game whose structure informs the kind of story you want to tell.

      tl;dr: Don't look for the Swiss army knife RPG. Become a Swiss army knife GM.

      Posted By: Paul T.What I'm not seeing (as Leonard's anecdote shows) is any coherent argument for why or how "indie/story" games facilitate that kind of thing less well than "traditional" games.
      \nSo, I think the main difference comes from a trend, however brief, toward less permissiveness about this topic in the game texts. Here are some examples.

      Primetime Adventures, 2nd Ed, page 3: "Each time you play a game of Primetime Adventures, you and the other players create... one episode of a fictional TV show... At the end of a game session, which usually takes a few hours, the characters on the show will have completed one episode of that show..." (This assumption is explicitly carried through the book - one session = one episode, period.)

      page 31: "Don't hesitate to get right into the conflict, especially the first few times you play the game. It can be easy to start talking and describing and maybe acting out dialogue (you know, however you like doing it), and the next thing you know, fifteen minutes (emphasis added) of play time passed and no conflict. Oops."

      Misery Bubblegum, back of box: "30-60 Minutes"

      In a Wicked Age, 1st printing, page 25: "End the chapter when... You've been playing long enough and are at a perfect cliffhanger. / You're plain out of time." "Everyone else, gather up the sheets, cards, and dice, clean up the snacks, and finish the wine. Wish your friends a dear good night; the chapter's done." (This is the softest of these injunctions, but the frame story that the text relies on implies at other points that all the stuff that happens in the book happens in one night.)

      The Dogs example is already above, re: pushing toward conflict.

      Shock:, page 17: "About 45 minutes to an hour per player"

      ...And so on and so forth, ad nauseum. Now, obviously, one might argue that absent of this provided context, the mechanics themselves don't enforce this kind of urgency or time limit the way that, say, Zombie Cinema or The Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries does. Perhaps nothing tangible actually forces you into that mode. But I think the context counts for a lot, enough that it lends validity to the perception that "traditional" games are freer or more facilitating in this regard. Yeah, there's structure, but there's less there telling you to apply that structure in a particular way if you don't much feel like it.

      For some groups, that's a feature, and for some, it's a bug. For Jason Corley, it's a way of life.

      Posted By: Paul T.How do Ribbon Drive and A Flower for Mara deal with conflict?
      \nWell, actually, A Flower for Mara handles conflict purely at the social level. Basically, any narrative details said by anyone are assumed to be true, and the Director (basically, the GM) has total veto power over anything, which he is supposed to use if there is a conflict between the players. Otherwise, the assumption is that everyone is going to roll with what happens and what is said. The text does provide instruction and guidance on how to do this, but there it is.

      So, oddly enough, A Flower for Mara relies exclusively on players "playing nice" and GM fiat to work.

And I realized that roleplaying is not controlling characters in an endless fantasy world, but it's functions that the GM uses to affect the characters and the players. So I started to classify these functions as mechanic ones (like the PCs have to fight) and dramatic ones (an NPC gives an information to a PC). And since then adventure writing consists for me of putting these functions into structures. When playing it's up to the players, how they move around the structure and activate the functions.

      For example there was a male character in the party who was fond of the ladies (and this is an understatement). You have to know, that tihs was a road adventure: the PCs were escorting nobles across the mountains to a city. So I made up a female NPC (the daughter of the head of the noble family) whose goal was to get him to trust her and after they arrive, to make him kill someone she didn't like.

      So I came up with stages: I made a thread of dramatic functions and each function consisted of something she did to get his trust. The first was that she got behind him when enemies attacked. The second was that she asked something (I wrote down beforehand what she asked). The third was that she invited him to her tent for some talking about trusting people and asking whether he killed anyone before. Etc.

      So while playing I had this list before me, and whenever an opportunity came, I activated the next item on the list, but I didn't push it. If the opportunity for it didn't arise, I didn't do it. And if the PC could anytime stop the progression down the list, but I knew the player enough that he will want to know what will be at the end (he hoped for sex, and his character got it after he gave his word to kill that someone). So it seemed natural, and it gave the feeling of progression without real awkward railroading. (This may be railroading, but it didn't feel like that.)

      And the adventure consisted of such lists: another list was that of the stronger and stronger enemies who attacked them on the road (mechanic functions which consisted almost exclusively of combat values), a third one was the declining health of the head of the noble family, etc. So there are lists enough to have a full adventure even if one of the threads won't work. So you can't say that this female character was a conflict, you can't even say that the first fight was a conflict. These were four or five long conflicts which played out I think under four or five session.

      And I can vary how these lists or threads are to each other and how they work intrinsincly.       So maybe I dislike concentrated games, because I can't use this method. It wouldn't be a problem, but mostly the GMing methods of these concentrated games don't work so well for me as mine above. I don't know, maybe I designed my own RPG and I don't even know it.

      Posted By: algiSo I'm inclined from now on to play classic RPGs with light rules that won't hinder me, and use story game like effects that will make the game more awesome, and GM as I feel instead of listening to story games that want to tell me how to play.

      Does anyone else have similar problems?
      Do you have any other solution for this problem?
      I like modern games. Some of them are great games, and all of them make something completely different from the classical game. Prime Time Adventures and Until We Sink ... are my favorites. But I see what you mean; lots of them do tell us to play this drama, in that way. If you find that bothersome, you should go classic. On the other hand; if you still find some beauty in the modern games you try, you should keep on trying them, not to find the one and only modern game you can play for the rest of your life, but to get the golden moments buried in them. Some of those moments are hard to find in classical games.

      I like classical games too. I have written my share of them; Muu, Fabula, Romanse!, Homuncle, Sirkumdo. I like it when a classical game delivers the very special treat of immersion, in some cases jointly experienced by a whole group in intuitive and free-flowing interaction. I love it when the game surprises us, with turns and quirks none of us is solely responsible for, building a drama that none of us can predict.

      I propose for you, algi, to continue your classical training; as a GM and possibly as a designer. The "classical role-playing game" (with GM and players) is still a form to investigate and improve on. Go game!

      Posted By: Paul T.2. In a D&D/GURPS/Shadowrun/other trad game one-shot, you probably won't see many meandering, reflective scenes. And the book doesn't really you get any, although it doesn't stop you from having them. The game text is focused on revealing and confront the sources of conflict in dungeons.
      \nYeah!!!! Something I was thinking about in the light of the most recent Recess, in fact: not only do many indie games suffer from always being played as one-shots because their overarching structure only becomes evident with repeated sessions, BUT it can become harder to tell the difference between modern and traditionally-structured games in that context as well.

      When people sit down to a con one-shot, they tend to be naturally predisposed to focus on a single, compelling situation and resolve it in the space of 4 hours or so. The characters were likely set up by the GM to be "pointed at each other" in interesting and conflicty ways, or to synergize well in a more cooperative game. In other words, you get some of the most important features of most indie games no matter what you're playing! However, to *set up* a good traditional game one-shot takes a lot of work\u2014I hear trad GMs talk about this all the time, in terms of not having time to prep games for cons. Meanwhile I'm like, "Dude what's your problem? I own like 12 games that I can run any time with no preparation except for running off character sheets."
