Can the GM lead by example?

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

So in Capes one of the best things I can bring to the table is my intent to play the game with passion and excitement, pursuing my own goals even when it gets me screwed.

This is leading by example. People see "Oh, wow, Tony is doing this stuff that looks like ten liters of refined liquid fun. The stuff he's doing is clearly working. He's having more fun than me, the bastard! Well I'll show him! I'll go out there and have TWENTY liters of supersaturated fun," and then they go out there any play for all their worth and we all have a ball.

But part of that is because I'm showing them how it can be done. I am doing what they could do. They know they can do it because they're in the same position that I'm in.

Can a GM do that? I'm not at all sure. I mean ... there are many ways in which the players are not in the same position as the GM. They can look at a GM having more fun than five barrels of monkeys, and not get the idea that they too could be having a lot of fun.

The more I think about this the sadder it seems.

    Outside of like, roundrobin GMing schemes, I'm thinking, no, categorically, a GM can't show a player how to better enjoy being a player by modeling that behavior. If the GM did so, he wouldn't be the GM, or he'd be running one of those GM-pseudo-characters, and even then players can easily assume he's doing awesome things because he's the GM and he's giving "his PC" unfair breaks.

    That's not to say, though, that there's no way for the GM to show players how to enjoy themselves in the game. One of the best examples I can think of this is your example from running Dogs, and enticing players to use their Book of Life 2d8 to beat somebody over the head. They're not doing the same thing you're doing, but you can still show/guide/lead/tell them how to enjoy what they're doing.

    Caveat: if you atomize the things happening at the table, as James pointed out, the GM can model some things, like performance standards and descriptions. In games that allow players to reward each other and recognize the GM as a player, this also happens -- giving the Director Fan Mail, for instance.

    I think you can define leadership as the moving people towards a shared goal. If the shared goal is only to have fun, and the players aren't having fun, then the GM isn't being an effective leader. To lead by example, a GM can be fair, a GM can stick to the rules, a GM can behave in all types of ways that lead to fun and effective play. And it doesn't have just to be for fun.
    Not all people are effective leaders, so it's probably not a GM leadership thing, but a who is the GM leadership thing.

    Huh. I'm a marginally-traditional GM, and I totally lead by example. I can't model exactly what I want the players doing, because I've got a slightly larger scope, but I can model what I find to be interesting situations (and then let them create situations that I have to react to), and what I find to be interesting characters (and then challenge them to play their characters in equally interesting ways), and what I find to be effective portrayal of scenes (and then give them fantastically juicy opportunities to throw the very same thing back to me).

    For me, a game is a collaboration between me, any other GMs, and the players - and our game scribe (who rocks!) and any observers that we happen to have (usually two to four). I can model appropriate collaborative behavior and narrative approaches without actually having to take the exact same roles as the players have. I can't model how to be a good player, because I'm not one, but I can demonstrate my expectations for our shared game, and offer opportunities for players to engage at the same level. I'm a GM because my players want me to do more of the conflict-creation and world-building work, but I see my role as an equal partner, just one with a bigger toolbox. I can't show the players how to use my tools, nor can they model for me how to use theirs, because we have different roles - but that isn't going to stop us from building a damn fine birdhouse together.
