\n Unknown Armies. A few weeks we ran Jailbreak with AW. I'd thought it might work pretty well for the particular scenario - it's nicely constrained and doesn't lean particularly heavily on the UA setting. As we were playing it out though, it became clear that not only was Apocalypse World a really good system for Jailbreak, but even moreso it would probably work really incredibly well for ongoing UA.

As an example, it's always bothered me a bit that high Mind characters are most resistant to Stress Checks - but the five stresses map really quite well to AW stats; well enough that I'd guess that anyone familiar enough with both systems would come up with the same mapping. So, the Stress move is: when confronted with a stressful situation roll+[stat] - 10+ no problem, 7+ clear reaction, miss: the MC's move can involve you taking action.

    \n I've been doing a Cthulhu hack based on BRP and UA. A miss on your percentile dice is a miss in AW terms. A hit is a 7-9 in AW terms. Cherry (ugh) is a 10+. Maybe impale is a 12+, if you're allowing for the possibility of transcendence like the expanded moves in AW. All skills give you questions to ask, costs to avoid paying, or hold to spend. You get one question/option/hold on a hit, and three on cherries. The GM gets extra guidance (and can be lazier), the players get to steer the GMing around a bit, and everyone wins.

    \n I wouldn't use anything else for UA. It's maybe the only system where the percentile system makes sense. The game's metaphysics have so much to do with probabilities that the dice system, with its clear basics and fiddly bits to make it wonky, fits the theme very well. What makes it fuzzy (in a bad way) is the "roll higher for better results", but it never says what the better results are for anything else but magick and combat. AW could be the way here.

    I'll also steal Fronts and Threats the next time I'll play UA.

    Posted By: halskiWhat makes it fuzzy (in a bad way) is the "roll higher for better results", but it never says what the better results are for anything else but magick and combat. AW could be the way here.
    I'm positive it is. Writing up each of your skills as a move, using guidelines like the ones I proposed:
    1. answers the question, "what does it mean that I succeeded better or worse?"
    2. helps get everyone at the table to agree on what the skill means, which sharpens our shared image of the fiction
    3. tosses out flags to the GM, who, if he's anything like me, will appreciate the help

    By the way, I think it's probably better to say something like, "for each 25% (or 20%, or whatever) you pass by, choose one:" rather than the precise thing I outlined above, which makes perfect sense in the game I'm writing but is less good in the full UA framework.
