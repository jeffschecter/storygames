    \n I think so; that said, it's not necessarily going to be what everyone wants out of narration.

    For me, the key trick to merging roleplay and tactical considerations is using tactical inefficiency to give teeth to your character's decisions. When everyone around the table knows what the right tactical decision is, but the character takes a different path because it's a) better for the story or b) more appropriate for the character, there's a weight to that decision. There is the potential for an immediate consequence. It's a sacrifice.

    You're thus privileging story (or immersion, as the case may be) above combat success.

    \n I think so.

    The key, for me, is to briefly narrate the results (and not restate the action.) Why restate the action if the player already said it? It's a common GM verbal tic and one you can avoid easily if you replace it with stuff that's much cooler and more engrossing:

    Restating the action (bad!)

    Player: "I hit him with my sword. Uh, 16. 8 points of damage."
    DM: "Okay, you swing your sword at him and hit him!"
    Player: "I just said that."

    Briefly narrate withour restating (good!)

    Player: "I hit him with my sword. Uh, 16. 8 points of damage."
    DM: "There's blood everywhere from the shallow cut. His knees go wobbly."
    Player: "Wow! Let's get him, guys!"

    Also, tie it to mechanics:

    Player: "After I see an opening, I'll leap into it, sword point first! At-will, careful attack. Uh, 16. 8 points of damage."

    OR

    DM: "There's blood everywhere from the cut. His knees go wobbly. He's bloodied."

    Last, if the players are going all descriptive but not tying it to mechanics, make that your job. If they describe a certain action, tie it back to the rules.

    Player: "Okay, so he's on the carpet, right? When he's not looking, I'll yank it out from underneath him!"

    DM: "Hmm, that's a bluff-in-combat check. Roll it!"

    \n I'll be the first one to say, "I don't think so!" In fact, I think the opposite can be true: Narrating combat can open up additional tactical options.

    When a player narrates a bunch of cool description, yes, it might distract her from tactics. What she says might put fiction-driven limits on further tactical options. I think that's why people think they're at odds.

    I, on the other hand, feel like narrating combat and really getting into the fiction helps me visualize what's going on. That makes my tactical play better, because I can manage more details in my head. I can see creative solutions that others might have missed because they're ignoring the fiction and focusing only on the character sheet. I can say, "He has a lot of little cuts from the blade barrier I just slammed him into. I know he disarmed me and I'm prone, but I'm lying in a salt plain. Can I scoop up some salt and fling it into his wounds?"

    \n I think that there are 3 factors, and only two can live in harmony:
    combat narration
    combat tactics
    handling speed

    If you're going to be creative and descriptive, and you're also going to have tactical considerations, the game will move slowly.
    Streamlining/downsizing one of those two will pick up the speed of combats.

    So, if you're into combats taking an hour and being entertaining on many fronts: yes, narration and tactics can sing harmoniously.
    If you want something snappier, gut one of those two factors.
