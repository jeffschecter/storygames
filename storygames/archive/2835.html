\n Hey all, I was kinda peeking into a recent discussion over at RPGNet (here) about historical RPGs. We've even had discussions on historical games over here, too.

I've been thinking about historical gaming more, because at some point I'd like to give it a chance again.

I was thinking, though: Many historical games that have been presented to me (like a vikings game I played) basically took a fantasy game with standard magic, races, and super combat techniques, and removed the magic, races and super techniques to make it historical (in my case, GURPS Fantasy).

At that point, you're left to enjoy the story to be sure, but the other cool elements that were in the game (magic, etc) have been suppressed, and replacing it is this need for the players to "enjoy the historic-ness of it all".

I guess my questions are:
* It's hard to enjoy the historic-ness of a setting when you know little and the GM knows a lot, without forcing all the players to read a book or two before playing. Is there a way to fix this?

* Is there anything that can be "put back into" a game (once magic and stuff is out) to enhance the historicness of the roleplaying? I dare say that I'm almost looking for a system tweak here. Like "freebie fate points if you make use of real historic events"?

In the end, I've had fun with roleplaying using something like Art Grace and Guts (Jason M ran us a Norse-themed two-parter that was fun, but the Norse stuff was basically trappings. It could have easily been Aztec or Ancient Russia) with the setting as nothing more than "stage props", with no real nods to historic events and the like.

But how about historical roleplaying, in a non-magical 'realistic' setting (any system, from D&D to MLWM), where real historic events emerge and are played out? Where the players meet, influence, or even are major players in the game?

How do you dig into that when all the players aren't at the same level of historical knowledge?
How do you make the history as something more than stage props and backdrop?

-Andy

    \n Andy, I wrestled with all these questions working on Grey Ranks, and you've been a playtester, so you know my answers.

    Inject the real history bits as situation somehow and give the players meaningful ways to interact with them. Provide the background on the fly and don't expect hardcore emulation or accuracy - tell your own story and accept departure from reality. Have a name list. In a long-term game, find ways to get people engaged and bringing their own discoveries to the table. One way to do this is to encourage people to find real world NPCs they'd like to interact with.

    I've never had a problem with the lack of sparkly bits - all conflicts are essentially human, so taking out the elves and spaceships just seems like focusing on the stuff that matters to me. Not that I have anything against elves and/or spaceships.

    * It's hard to enjoy the historic-ness of a setting when you know little and the GM knows a lot, without forcing all the players to read a book or two before playing. Is there a way to fix this?
    \nI think Bob has the right idea (i.e. basically giving the players a "relevant facts about the time period" sheet at the beginning).

    Another suggestion is to make sure that the players feel free to ask the GM "in brief, what does my character know about this situation?" so that he could be more grounded when necessary.

    In addition, just like if you were running a fantasy game in a setting that the players are not familiar with (like when I ran Exalted the first time) you should probably start things slow and locally contained. For instance, in a game about the Avignon papacy, you would not want to start the characters off as one of the major players who are required to understand the tangled set of alliances and enmities that confront them ("Wait, I forgot... was Pisa in the Florencian anti-papacy league?" "No, Pisa was the one that killed all those rioters at Cesena" "Nuh-uh, I am pretty sure that was us..." "Wait, isn't Pisa also the name of the papal legate?" "Uhmm... let me check my notes...")... rather you would start them off as emissaries of a minor player who are essentially "told" what to do until such a time as it looks like the players understand enough of the setting to start moving up the chain.

    The final big point is to not overwhelm players with lots of foreign names and places... focus more on the story that you are trying to tell (at least at first). Even set in the long-long ago, human motivations (power, greed, sex) don't really change and give an understandable and comfortable grounding for understanding things... and then begin to add in the subleties and eccentricities of the era as things go by.\n

    * Is there anything that can be "put back into" a game (once magic and stuff is out) to enhance the historicness of the roleplaying? I dare say that I'm almost looking for a system tweak here. Like "freebie fate points if you make use of real historic events"?
    \nI really think what is needed is a game specifically designed to "emulate" the historical period and reward period-accurate behavior (I think using a generic system is part of the problem)... depending on what period you are talking about.

    I would be wary of rewarding players by introducing 'historic' events because you run the twin risks of destroying any feeling of 'control' that the players have or you turn it into a bizzare Trivial pursuit session where people spout random facts and names in the hopes of getting a bonus.

    With that being said, I think you are on the right track. The ideal system would reward those who work to enrich the historical accuracy of the game.

    Posted By: AndyI was thinking, though: Many historical games that have been presented to me (like a vikings game I played) basically took a fantasy game with standard magic, races, and super combat techniques, and removed the magic, races and super techniques to make it historical (in my case, GURPS Fantasy).

    At that point, you're left to enjoy the story to be sure, but the other cool elements that were in the game (magic, etc) have been suppressed, and replacing it is this need for the players to "enjoy the historic-ness of it all".
    \nWell, that does sound pretty sucky, but then it would because it's a negative.

    The trick to historical gaming is the same as the trick to any setting, sell it on what it is, not what it isn't.

    There's no magic (no pun intended) to historical gaming, it's about taking a setting or situation that grabs you and playing that. If you start with thinking the middle ages is like a fantasy game but with no magic, well who'd want to play that? If however you pitch a game about warring BIshops fighting for supremacy directly and via their henchmen across medieval Europe, well hell, that sounds more fun. That latter game, Dictemus Papae in the current 24 hour competition, is a historical rpg with no magic but the game doesn't even talk about magic. Why should it? That's not what it's about, it's about being a scheming bishop.

    Enjoying the historic-ness, I don't even really know what that means and the vast bulk of my gaming is historic gaming. I sell games to my group, or have them sold to me when I'm playing, on what they are.

    Hey guys, do you want to play a Vikings game with a feel based on the Icelandic sagas? Marriages, blood feuds, some raiding, some trading, how's it sound?

    Hey guys, do you want to play a game set in the French wars of religion? You're part of the civic administration of a town being ripped apart by political and religious differences, trying to keep it together while advancing your own cause, how's that sound?

    Hey guys, do you want to play a game set in 1770s Naples? It's going to be all about con artists and adventurers trying to make a name at the Royal court and to gain their fortunes, how's that sound?

    Those are all games I've played in or ran. That 1770s one? I don't think anyone did any research besides me and I did mine so I could throw in some period detail from time to time for colour. But the thing is, none of them were about enjoying the historic-ness, they were about being Icelandic freemen or scheming politicians or fast talking con artists, they were about what the players actually do.

    \n As an addendum, I enjoy historical gaming because I enjoy most games about stuff that resonates with me. Greed, passion, revenge, fear, envy, intolerance, fanaticism, ambition, all that good stuff.

    Historical gaming is rich with that, because history is rich with that, because history is about people. All too often games with magic or cool stuff become about that magic or cool stuff, but I don't really care about that stuff, I don't find it that interesting.

    Taking out the magic, if done well, means focussing on something else that's cool. The something else I try to focus on is the human emotions, why were so many people trying to kill the Marquis (whom the PCs knew in fact to be an Irish fraudster)? The answer lay in a mistress he dumped when she was of no further use to him, who has since herself gained influence, the plot wasn't about the mystical mcguffin of mcgoo, it was about revenge. When you have no magic, you have to look to the mundane, and the mundane frequently contains real drama.

    I like gaming to be about human issues, and for that for me (and others will vary of course) I find it best to stick to the human.

    \n This is essentially the same as the worldbuilding thread, has anyone noticed?

    There really is no difference between not knowing much about the Forgotten Realms and not knowing much about Poland in 1796.

    Why did I pick Poland in 1796? Because that's my next game project - a Vampire: the Requiem game set in Poland in 1796. That's right folks, put on your bike helmets to prevent brain damage, JDC's going to talk about his Vampire game!

    But first I'm going to tell you about a D&D game.

    It was set in the Scarred Lands, a really kickass setting concocted by White Wolf. I don't want to get too far on a tangent but the reason I like the Scarred Lands is that it really really needs heroes. It's not like a lot of dark fantasy games where things are just dismal, in the Scarred Lands things are dismal but if there was just a ragtag gang of hot-cha adventurers in implausible armor who could come in with quips and derring-do, things might just start looking up.

    Anyway, the game was set in a city that at the time had not been detailed by White Wolf. (Eventually it would be but I liked mine better and nobody else cared at that point.) I went into considerable detail in detailing the coinage, calendar, daily life, etc. of this fantasy city and gave the packet to the players.

    Then I got really annoyed because all of the questions I was getting were answered in the packet and gps were still just "gold pieces", not the actual name of the coin. It was, in my view, killing the atmosphere I was trying to create. I was having these NPCs say all sorts of awesome stuff and the players might as well have been playing Greyhawk or some other D&D setting. I got pretty pissed off, felt like my contribution was wasted, etc. That is the feeling the setting buff gets when people blunder over something they tried to establish, whether it is a fictional setting or a nonfictional one.

    Afterwards I realized this was all happening because I was a fucking moron.

    I had just given these packets to the players without explaining my expectations or goals. What, were they just supposed to read my mind and know what I wanted? I put my attacks of opportunity FAQ in the same packet, for god's sake. I didn't expect their characters to say "hurrr, I really stabbed that guy with an attack of opportunity".

    Fast forward to today.

    After getting psyched about Vampire: the Requiem, I do what I do with all games - ruin it by doing something with it the authors wouldn't ever give a shit about and would think I was stupid or maybe brilliant for doing. In this case, I wanted to run a historical game, but in a setting that most American games more or less shit on and ignore: Eastern Europe (hats off to e23 for their GURPS WWII supplements for Poland and Romania, though...). So I took Napoleonic Poland (just after the last partition) and I wrote up a one-page precis on life in Poland in that time.

    When I presented the page to the players I said, "I expect you to know everything on this page, and to use the terms defined on this page in conversation, and when I refer to something on this page I expect you'll be able to have your character converse intelligently about it."

    Then I said, "If there is some historical question that is not related to something on this page, I don't care if you know it or not, I will be happy to answer it if I can, and if you forget the answer, that's okay, I'll answer it again. If I don't know the answer, I'll establish an answer as true for our game - after all, we're already positting that vampires exist, why not a little historical wiggle room?"

    And: "If you surmise something that historically turns out not to be true or there is some historical reason that something you're working on wouldn't work, I promise to intervene and explain things. You promise not to get upset at this. This arrangement of page and JDC knowledge exists in lieu of you knowing anything about Poland in 1796."

    All three of those things work in concert. The page satisfies me that there is something about the setting that they'll know and they won't stumble over a prop while they're popping about the stage. The use of me as historical fact repository/decisionmaker eliminates endless attempts to remember something they saw on the Discovery Channel. And the intervention guarantees that the players won't concoct a plan involving something that won't be invented/developed/widespread for fifty years... ("We'll catch him, we'll take the train!" "Um...no." ) ...while at the same time cautioning them not to leap right to the edge of the envelope of the setting and invest in a plan that might not be feasible in the setting, hopefully dampening disappointments.

    Posted By: BalbinusCould you remind us how Yet and Up worked?
    I wish I could. I've got a vague grasp of it at best, having only gotten second-hand info, mostly. Here's what little I can say with clarity... Up is chronological time (like going up the timeline) and Yet is your personal time (like "I have yet to do something"), right? But, sometimes, you know what's going to happen in your Yet. The game lets you make statements about what your future self did (hide a gun, write yourself a warning, whatever) -- this gives you some "frag" until you turn around and do the event.

    So, err, if you're trying to get events to line up with some prewritten timeline in a historical game, you're basically doing something where the players know that they must do something in the future in order for events to work out -- on the metagame level, you're trying to minimize the "frag" that comes from not making things happen as they're supposed to.

    That's all just talking out of my ass, of course. If I picked up a historical game, I'd probably either be going for the experience of a general period rather than specific events, or specifically for an "alternate history" kind of situation where I could change up something big and see where that led.

    \n Well, this question actually inspired a little side project for me, so here's a bit of what I'm working on.

    Take a counter-intuitive approach.

    Usually with a setting, there's the urge to hit the player with lots of info, and fairly complete info at that.

    Instead, give them short bits of info with key words on multiple topics related to the setting. One form might be a sheet with ten to twelve single sentences containing info. Decide on what info goes on each sheet all William S. Burroughs style. Cut the sentences in strips, mix-em-up and draw them.

    Alternatively, just write them on 3" x 5" cards and save yourself the hassle.

    Deal them out randomly, along with either a brief verbal or written background. Brief, like two paragraphs.

    The idea here is to criss-cross player info. No single player needs to have all of the info, as they will support one another's understand to build up a picture of the setting. It does mean allowing either discussion time or in-game pauses to relay info. OTOH, they could play a bit in-character- " No! Don't offer Saladin the porkchops, dumbass!"

    Anyway, that's what I've got for an idea, Andy.

    As for encouraging a bit of research, allow those interested to add their own data cards and deal them out as the game progresses. It might be a good indication of what parts of a setting are revving up the players' interest.
