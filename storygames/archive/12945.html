\n I'd like to investigate the idea of deliberately using speed and rhythm within role-playing games. How does speed and rhythm applies to your role-playing games?

Do you have experience with different speeds of conflict-resolution?
(like: slow deliberations, normal question-answer, stressed prompting)
The speed by which the game (and possibly; the GM) solve a conflict; does that affect how you experience ...
- the act of choosing your action/s?
- the happenings within the conflict?
- the consequences of it?

Are changes of rhythm part of your game-play?
(like: flowing in calmness, trotting forwards, driving the chase, exploding action)
- In which game, and how, have you experienced the deliberate use of rhythm?
- How does it affect the fiction (does it enhance the different conditions under which the characters strive and live)?
- Does it, or would it, enrich your game-play?

    \n I could see a really cool mechanic wherein people had a limited amount of real-world time to resolve a conflict (group conflict resolution). The catch is that everyone has "sand" chips (thinking sands of time) that they use to manipulate the total conflict time. All conflicts start at 5 minutes. Players go around the table bidding the time up and down by 1 minute per chip (min 1 minute / max ten minutes). Each minute you add gives you +1 to your rolls while each minute you subtract allows you to give someone else a -1 to their rolls. So, you can push for resolution and rush your opponent or take your time and work for a better outcome for yourself. When the timer goes off, the conflict ends.

    \n Here's a bit more context rather than a direct answer.

    Although the subject material is ostensibly the same, there's a big difference in the way Call of Cthulhu and Trail of Cthulhu run. The latter does not require dice rolls for investigation so the game progresses much more quickly, or you might say it leaves more time for character interaction and development of the fiction.

    One thing I've noticed is that players want their characters to be busy all the time. In RQ3, for example, if characters had any spare time at all, players would assign this to training so as to increase their skills. In Pendragon the winter season allowed no adventuring so players would engage in horse breeding to make money. I slowed this down by introducing tasks around the running of their estates such as court cases to be seen, dignateries to be entertained, children to be raised etc.

    In a recent game of Trail which I set in an Asylum, instead of adapting to the rhythm of the place, players wanted to know what their characters were up to all the time, any spare time was spent trying to escape. The best way I've thought of for slowing this down is to change the characters' drive to something about "getting better" rather than escape at any cost. It's something of an imposition but it would make the roleplaying experience much richer.

    Fiction doesn't happen all at once, or in an unremitting stream of actions. I think game developed fiction could be improved by slowing things down.

    \n I've got some simple tools of this kind in my classical frp-game Fabula. They are GM-tools, and is largely focused on setting the tone and speed of dialogue.

    Dialogue is a wonderful tool. Your voice works in very simple ways. By reinforcing it you may be a GM from hell (and heaven). Some examples from the top of my head:

    Calm voice = calm dialogue = calm players -> fits calm, reflective situations

    Pitched voice = pitched dialogue = stressed players -> fits critical situations

    To slow down tempo: describe details, focus on relationships, make food an issue, maintenance, reflection and moral qualms
    To support slowing down tempo: use calm voice, give players ample time to ponder and take action, ask follow-up questions on what they do, how they do it, and if they take this and that into consideration, be caring and thoughtful

    To speed things up: deliver effective descriptions, aim at intensity in interaction, press for conflict
    To support the change: be direct, clear affirmation of actions, matter-of-factly language, essential details, praise actions with direction, encourage pro-active stances (researching, guarding, bivouacking, investigating, etc.)

    Critical mode: use wild mimics and gestures, hard eye-contact, short descriptions, demand concrete answers NOW (punish delays in answer with: "Your character hesitates! NO action!")
    To crash into the overdrive of a crisis: stand up, slap the table for effect, give the sergeant-yell, act mercilessly with NPCs, give instant consequences, go from player to player in frenetic tempo, blast them with critical situations

    To end critical mode and kill the tempo: sit down, use tired, panting voice, talk slowly, rest your hands and slump your body
    To support drop-dead effect: give narrative and social affirmation of "crisis is over"; touch players, be empathic, smile, tell the players their characters are through it, lick their wounds, gather their wits, goes home ...

    - and so on ...

    You need a flexible and robust resolution-system to support this, simple enough for you to quicken resolution in critical mode. You may use it as slowly as you like in other modes.

    \nThe Committee for the Exploration of Mysteries uses real world time as part of the game mechanics. It is challenging at first, but really reinforces the pulp action feel once you get the hang of it.

    To give a short summary:

    The scene is set and when a conflict arises, opposition dice are rolled and added to give a total that must be beaten with the PC dice. The player rolls dice and sets them all aside. A timer is set. The player puts forward a die and describes their action that will attempt to neutralize the conflict. The opposition then describes a compication that keeps the conflict going. The player then puts forward another die and describes an action, only to be countered by a complication by the opposition. This back and forth action-complication only ends when the action dice total exceeds the opposition total or the timer runs out. If the total of the action dice does not beat the opposition total, then other resources can be brought in or other players may help out. If they still can't beat the opposition total or the timer runs out, then the game experiences a complication that the whole group must overcome.

    This style of play favors short narration until it is obvious that the conflict has been overcome, at which point there are rewards for extravagant narration. It reinforces the pulp action style of the game as well as the boastful, over the top style of the characters. It is quite fun, especially since it incorporates a drinking game in the game mechanics. Best beer and pretzels game I have yet played.

    \n I am a 4E dungeon master.

    Once, Kord, the Father of Battle, put our fighter on trial. The trial was held on the thin crusty ice over a fast-flowing river.

    Kord's priest invited all the fighter's comrades to testify in his defence. They had to come out on the ice to do so. Kord only accepts testimony from the bold!

    While they spoke, I was timing them with a sand timer from a Boggle game. Every time the sand ran out, I rolled d%. If I rolled < the number of people on the ice, the ice would break and everyone would fall in. I spent my speaking time trying to goad as many PCs out onto the ice as possible.

    I thought it worked great -- it was plenty tense -- but I forgot about the warlord's teleportation powers, and the ice breaking was just a stupid anticlimax.

    Posted By: jdfristromSo...there...a bunch of mechanical ways of slowing down or savoring important conflicts, none of which I've actually played.
    I know about this way of doing it. It has some merit. A lot of players find it very good to have this option, not the least due to the fact that they may choose to make unimportant conflicts in a hurry, and focus their game-play on the important ones. I like it!

    In my own design I am working with the finale of really important conflicts being played out in great tempo, to simulate the real world tempo of it (as is often the case, but depending on the conflict, of course). By emulating the real world tempo I try to help the players "live" the conflict. I am not fond of players managing conflicts in a game-tactical manner, so I work with tools to keep the conflicts from going in that direction. Tempo and rhythm are very central in that work.\n
    Posted By: jdfristromThe TSOY thing is kind of neat ... because if you're just smacking down a few orcs, boom, you can do it in one roll, but if you find yourself evenly- or over-matched you draw it out.
    Or; if you have a transitional conflict; one of lesser importance - you deal with it offhand ...
    - and the peak-conflicts, the important ones, are dealt with in ways that instill more energy and intensity in them.

    One way of instilling energy is to go closer to the character with the conflict (the stakes), and then to reflect on it more (playing out the aftermath in greater detail). In some games there is more detail to be had in the aftermath, when the characters retell or relive their conflict in one or more reflective scenes, than in the actual conflict, which is played out in one, explosive scene. This is, of course, a bit more sophisticated way of narrating such conflicts (jumping back and forth in time, letting details coming after the conflict, feeding the reflection rather than the conflict-scene). Sometimes this is the perfect way of doing it, and sometimes you need a big, blasting conflict-scene, and no reflective aftermath ... only brutal consequences.

    Posted By: TomasHVMSometimes this is the perfect way of doing it, and sometimes you need a big, blasting conflict-scene, and no reflective aftermath ... only brutal consequences.
    But; on the whole people tend to do the "brutal" variant all too much, and forget about the aftermath as an option for reflection. So I am more inclined to talk about the reflective aftermath, with its greater potential for showing the mental and social consequences of a conflict.

    And that is one effect of working deliberately with tempo and rhythm too; you get an overall better balance between the physical, social and mental elements in the characters, as they get more chances to shine in different scenes. A slow scene, reflective in content, is a much better arena for mentalities to be developed. And homely scenes of friendship, family and love are the best arena for social skills and emotional bonding to emerge, and fill out the character.

    You will most likely have more complex and "whole" characters in your campaign, if you let the tempo in each game-session go from reflective slowness, through homely conversation, and up through the layer of active preparation, towards the explosive finale ...
    - and then down again. Or varying the tempo, and changing it, in any order you deem beneficial to the fiction.
