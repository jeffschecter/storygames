Ok, so right here I got all crazy and said that if you aren't playing The Shadow of Yesterday in the world of Near, you aren't playing TSOY, which was a ridiculous idea said to provoke a reaction. But then we started talking about the assumptions behind the game.

\n

I'm not entirely keen on discussing the creative process in such detail that it kills the poor frog, but this part's important to the discussion: TSOY is intentionally, in every way, a game about a world in which the only thing that matters is human action, and human action is empowered to change the course of history. Any and every human in the game has the capability to change the world, and there is nothing that can override that. Storn asked if it wouldn't be too hard to add gods and true monsters, and it's a good question, but the answer is definitely no. Even if you found a way to make it mechanically work - which I think might be hard - it's not TSOY by my reckoning.

\n

But I want to back up. My original statement was if you aren't playing in the setting that comes with a game, you are not playing that game. That's a seriously auteur stance to take, and I'm not sure I can back it up, but it's an appealing position to me and I want to see if it's right. Before someone attacks the idea on the front that it's elitist and puts the power of creation into the hands of some rarefied game designer, know that my end goal for the RPG publishing hobby is for each group to make their own game for each story they play out, which this idea plays into.

\n

So let's look at some real cases:

\n
    \n

  • I ran a game of HeroQuest set in Glorantha recently, but simplified the setting, merging some gods and people that were similar, in order to make it manageable for my group.

    • \n

  • I am playing D&D, but have made up my own setting, which fits into an empty part of the canon setting. It is made for me to do this, and I'm not making up new monsters or races or anything: I'm just describing a cold place.

    • \n

  • I played in a game of TSOY with the Durham 3 set in a fantasy-punk Japan. We used only the rules from the book, and made species metaphorical: the street gangs were "ratkin" and had ratkin powers, and as a Buddhist monk, I had elf powers.

    • \n

  • I wrote and have played much of The Princes' Kingdom, which uses the basic rules of Dogs in the Vineyard in a totally different setting with different morality and different purposes of play.

    • \n
\n

Ok, so maybe some of these aren't playing the original game. I bet many of you would think that The Princes' Kingdom ain't Dogs, and I'd agree, although I'd say that's an easy conclusion to come to, since it's in a book. What if it wasn't? How about that game of TSOY? I count it as not-TSOY, but instead "Remi's unnamed game a lot like TSOY."

\n

This seems like a semantic issue - and that phrase is a great phrase to pshaw an idea you don't care to think of, so back off, haters - but it's an important one to me as a creative person. I love every person who talks about TSOY online, but man, every time I hear about "TSOY in this world where...", I think, is it TSOY? When I hear about My Life With Master, but at the North Pole, I think, is it My Life with Master? I don't think it is, but I also think that I may be slicing this bread a bit too thin, so I'm looking for your feedback.

    \n Well, as someone who has never played in the Near setting, but yet has a deep love for TSOY, I may not be qualified to answer. I'll try anyway.

    Obviously, I think that while we've played TSOY with so many non-Near settings, we're still staying true to the basic concepts of the game.* Each of our games has focused on essential human interactions, and their power to change everything. We just finished a long running game where my character transcended with Endure, sacrificing himself to save countless others. Despite the fact that my guy was from Kentucky instead of Khale, we're still using the same abilities and keys, making the same hard choices and dear sacrifices.

    So, yeah, I think we're still playing the same game, just like I think that Graham's Darker is still the Roach. I think it departs from being the same game when it becomes both mechanically and conceptually a different game, so maybe that includes The Princes Kingdom. I think you'd be the best judge of that.

    *okay, well, in this latest one we went off the wall a bit with super-powered humans, but still...

    \n I think Clinton's query can be summarized this way:

    What criteria is the best tool to make subjective judgements about the parameters of a given game?


    And that reminds me of Sim-type debates about whether the -true- essence of Star Trek is this or that or some other thing.

    Which is what spawned the pigeonholing of criteria into: Gamism, Drama, Simulationism; and the claim that all three are valid for those who prefer each.


    And, as in those debates, in this instance-- a question arises-- as to whether some Platonic Ideal version of a game exists, and what is it?...

    The printed-on-paper version? Or is it the author's perogative to define canon, beyond the rulebook? Or is the ultimate jury actual culture of active players?


    I mention all this, especially the comparison to Sim fundamentalism, in order to save us all the breath of retreading well-trod ground.

    Making coffee this morning, I got my own point: somewhere along the way, System Does Matter became Only System Does Matter, for a lot of people.     \n I think that, while you're technically right JD, the conversation really doesn't stop there. Just because you don't play 100% the game as written doesn't mean the game as written doesn't have a substantial impact on play. The same group playing according to the rules of D&D will have a different experience than the same group playing according to the rules of TSOY; this is not impacted by the fact that Group A playing TSOY will have a different experience than Group B playing TSOY. There are similarities between the difference among Group A's D&D and TSOY games that mirror the difference among Group B's D&D and TSOY games, and those are what we talk about when we discuss specific games.

    So when I say, "we're playing Game X," I'm not saying that our play experience and/or our whole System is exactly the same as anyone else who plays Game X. I'm saying that Game X has an impact on our System, and that's what we're looking at. Dismissing the discussion about games just because that impact is not 100% determinative of our play experience is unproductive.

    The questions then are: to what degree, and how, does Game X impact our System? Does it reliably do that for other groups? Is this impact largely kept intact if we change the setting or tweak the rules in certain ways?

    And it seems to me that those are the questions we're addressing in this thread, and they're very valuable at that.
