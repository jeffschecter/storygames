    I have three groups that I tend to game with, and they sort of represent different points on the "gaming as a social activity" spectrum, I think, running from "acquaintances with the potential to be friends" to "friends who I talked into gaming and who really quite like it."

    The "more acquaintances" group is my newest one, not coincidentally -- we've only had two sessions of the game. The GM is a casual friend, part of my larger friends network, but not someone I've spent a lot of time with on a one-on-one basis. I know the other three players even less well, although all three are good friends of my good friends, so I've seen them around. It's my hope, frankly, that I'll become better friends with everyone through our game, since I've always liked these guys but never quite took it upon myself to get to know them better.

    The second, "inbetween group" I tend to think of as the West Philly group. There's a core of 5-7 of us, with a lot of other people who're willing to get roped in for a short game, or who could easily participate in a longer campaign in the future. Starting out, I knew one of the guys really well -- he was the one who talked me into playing in the first place. I knew the GM less well, but still thought of him as a friend, and I was acquainted with the other four players -- I'd seen them at parties, probably chatted with them all at one time or another over a beer or a smoke, but I probably wouldn't have quite called them friends. Through the course of gaming together, I've definitely gotten closer to the two guys who were friends to begin with, and I also ended up cultivating a close friendship with Tim, who played the other ratkin in our TSOY game. He and I spent a lot of time scheming together over email and discussing what our characters were going to do next, and gradually started to talk about life stuff as well as gaming stuff. Now I hang out with him and his wife in non-gaming contexts quite regularly. The rest of the group I'm not as close with, but they've gone from nodding acquaintances to being people I invite to parties and who I'm generally happy to hang out with.

    The last group is, I think, possibly the classic "friends who game together, just like they'd go see a movie together or something" kind of thing. I actually met all of them through my knitting circle, although we also hang out in non-knitting contexts! One day whilst knitting, I mentioned how much fun I was having with the West Philly gaming crew, and it turned out that my friend Rachel's husband was an avid GM and game designer. Rachel and I convinced a bunch of other people from the knitting group, who all had little to no gaming experience, to try it out with us. Now we meet monthly for gaming, in addition to our knitting circles and other hanging out.

    This model also reached a critical mass. It worked really well when the community was 40-60 people. But when it hit 100+, small groups start to form that interact with larger group less and less and it becomes harder and harder for new people to break in. It also becomes a physical space problem. It's hard to find affordable spaces that fit more than a 100 people. And making dinner reservations for more than 15 people can be a nightmare.

    So I can understand why at some point it may make sense to separate groups based on activity (although I hate it).

    On the flip side, we've had gaming friends turn into friends we game with. Which meant we hung out more but gamed a bit less. One of our players mentioned that we've become excellent socializers but tardy gamers! And although we're having lots of fun I never want to let go of the gaming part.

    This is a very dear topic to me that I'm constantly trying to navigate.

We played together, we suffered together through sharing our crushes and heartbreaks and divorces and alcoholic parents and whatnot. And we played a LOT. Like three to seven days a week, for hours, sometimes 16 hours in a row.

    But we were also highly socially active. We were part of the local party crowd, and our annual joint birthday celebration (my two best friends' and my birthday fall within four weeks) was legendary among people up to several years above and below our own age, bringing together a few hundred people each year. When Marc and I went to college together, we joined the social science student council, whose main job was to arrange the four big parties each year, and to socially network among students, and we ran the new student orientation training several semesters in a row.

    In other words, we were total non-geeks, despite our incredible time and energy investment in RPGs.

    When I moved to the states, I joined up with a coworker and his friends. Aside from going to lunch with one or two of them twice a month, and playing once every five or six weeks, I did not hang out with them socially, and while I liked them, we didn't form much of a bond despite playing together for two years or so. For one, they live on the other side of town and getting out there for the game was hard enough, and for another we just didn't click in the sort of way that makes me seek someone out actively on my part. But these were all guys (and the GM's wife, at some point) who were married, in professional careers, with plenty of other activities going on, and the three of them frequently hang out doing other things together, like watch BSG. The GM and his wife lived in my coworker's garage for seven months after coming back from the Peace Corps, so there must be some true friendship there.

    My experience with the geek factor, therefore, has been pretty limited. For most of my gaming life, it's been one of the main activities, but by far not the only one, I've done with my closest friends.
