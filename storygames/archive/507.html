GM Obligation: Ignorance or laziness?

TonyLB
April 2006 edited April 2006 in Story Games

Ah, the fine art of the provocative title.

Anyway, I've heard the following (in one form or another) many times:

The GM participates in the game solely to serve the other players. They get their pleasure out of making other people happy, and selflessly sacrifice their own chance to directly make themselves happy in the name of this goal. If they want to pursue some agenda of their own, to directly make themselves happy through their own actions, then they have no place being the GM.

What's funny is that I can't recall ever hearing an argument for this position. It's always stated as self-evident. The sum of people's argument for it is "But ... dude ... if that weren't the case then you'd have the GM pursuing their own agenda. You see?"

So, yeah, given what I've heard to date, I think this is utter bunk. The GM is a player. A few systems allow that player to pursue goals (often different goals from the other players, but goals nonetheless) and to be rewarded and validated for reaching them. If other systems do not permit the GM to do this, that's a choice of the systems (or, I think, most often just an unconsidered default born of laziness).

But that's only what I've heard to date. Like I said, I've never heard an argument proferred for why the selfless GM, acting from an obligation to please the other players, is superior.

I am entirely virgin territory (in this one way ... get yer mind out of the gutter). If you actually have an argument for this, you'll be the very first person to present it!

Or we can all just get together and mock the people who unquestioningly accept this principle without ever putting their brains in gear to wonder why. I'm good with that too!

    You gotta know that I agree with you on this one. But I'll try to be a devi's advocate to spark some discussion.

    First, as Anders said, if you actually get your fun from making other people happy, then making other people happy is fun for you. I have had moments in RPGs where I've been happy because I had hit everyone else's emotional buttons just right -- not because of any other agenda than that.

    Maybe we should be thinking about a distinction between power and happiness. Being in charge of the story is not the same as being happy or as abdicating one's own happiness in favor of the players. (And, obviously, being the GM is not necessarily being in charge of the story.)

    For me, at least, being happy as a GM means abdicating quite a lot of narrative power. What makes me happy is the back-and-forth play between me and the other participants in the game, where we both come up with great characters and stories that none of us could have created alone. I don't want to make my players happy! I want to make them do interesting things, which makes them happy, and they want to be interesting to me right back.

    You could call me a self-sacrificing GM because I spend a lot of time thinking about what would be interesting for my players, but I don't think that would be accurate. Really I have a very strong agenda, and I do an excellent job of carrying it through. I want to be surprised by the story, to have it be awesome in ways I never would have expected. It's not the same pleasure as my players have, particularly the most immersively-oriented of them who really identify with their own characters' exploits, but it's a very strong pleasure nonetheless.

    My agenda is a meta-agenda, though. I take a lot of pleasure in a successful story, but not necessarily in success within the story. I don't (usually) get attached to the success or failure of particular characters. So I guess you could argue that I am meta-happy rather than directly happy, but my happiness feels pretty damn direct to me. I think that's what the quote that Tony brought up is about, though - the difference between in-story happiness and meta-story happiness (though I have no idea whether those terms will make sense to anyone but me).

    To pick up the volleyball metaphor, for me there's no net and no one's scoring points. My players and I are playing together to find out how long we can keep the ball in the air, and I find myself really enjoying it when the players set me up for a spike, or when I set them up for one. I may be the one who laid out the lines of the court, but once the ball gets into play, it's not at all clear what's going to happen. My job is to try to make it as challenging as possible for the players to hit that ball back to me, without setting them up for inevitable failure, and their job is the same. I just happen to have more narrative resources at my command to do it - which is good, because there's more of them than there are of me!

    It also helps that my group expresses their appreciation of each other and of the various GMs on a regular basis. I like knowing that they're enjoying it as much as I am, because it sucks to play with someone who's not enthusiastic. But that may be a different discussion.

    --Jess
