\n So, assuming you can't change the rules of the tactical roleplaying game you've got (say, D&D 4E), by what techniques does the scene/combat/dungeon "framer" make fights truly meaningful, heartfelt battles over something that matters and not just a win or lose life and death struggle?

Can entire dungeons be framed beforehand to hold particular meaning? What if, during the middle of the dungeon, the meaning is changed or defunct? Can abandoning the dungeon be just as good?

I'm searching for something here but am having a hard time saying it. How does a DM/GM string together tactical combats with strong meaning, making the fight worth fighting and not just murder and thievery?

Assume I am playing the game I want to play. I'm looking for technique.

    \n Hey Mark,

    There's ways to load anything with meaning, it's just about thinking about it from the opposite side- what kinds of meaningful things would you have a physical fight over in a story?

    Protecting refugees? Freeing prisoners? Undertaking a pilgrimage for a religious rite? Bringing back the body of a loved one for proper burial? Getting a magical something that can cure disease for your child? These kinds of things make good "quest level" motivations.

    For dungeon style play, I usually load the dungeon with a couple of groups in conflict, ala a Conan story or a DitV town. Here come the adventurers, of whom everyone is both suspicious and maybe looking to use, and the players get bits of story of what's -really- going on, and they do stuff and make decisions and get into fights. Folks who come at it with the usual D&D "Kill everything" mentality quickly find that they might not be making the right choices OR making good friends along the way.

    I think the big problem is that most tactical roleplaying has looked to other tactical roleplaying for the purpose of framing a "story", instead of stories which feature a lot of action and combat.

    \n Hey Mark,

    Do you remember the scene in The Two Towers where they're leading the people from their broke down town to Helmsdeep and they get jumped by goblins on wargs? That's meaningful, the fight is desperate.

    The problem with your example question is that the players -don't care- about the refugees, no connection has been made with any of them. It's different when it's, "Jodir, the father who just wants to get enough to buy back his farm from the landlord so his two kids can have a home of their own", or, "Mala and Siha, the teenage couple that's freaking out because she's pregnant and their parents don't approve", "Hala, the crazy old woman who taught me the Ritual of Healing because I look just like her grandson..." etc.

    You build meaning first, then you load up the combats about it. This is pretty much a classic tactical rpg problem "Oh, save the town", and the players go, "Why? I don't CARE about the town". Give the players something to buy into first, then you threaten it, after it means something to them.

    (A larger concern is that a lot of old school players have learned NOT to care, because usually if they care about something, it either gets taken away -like Jared's story of trying to own a bar which would always get bombed or burned down-, used a carrot to railroad them, or else something which inevitably betrays them. You know, because that's -so- awesome.)

    With the dungeon style set up, you have to throw the meaningful stuff at the players much like towns in DitV work- players have to quickly pick up that there's more than fights going on here or they won't look for it.

    \n I would not use D&D for meaningful combat. My own FRPG Fabula has the mechanical simplicity to cater for methodic tools that makes combat into a breathtaking and hearthrendering experience.

    EXAMPLE TOOLS:
    - Contrasting build up: relatives, friends, sweethearts -> love, foreboding and anxiety
    - Control of rythm: increasing and decreasing as situation demands it
    - Vacuum breaks: break off drive suddenly, to create vacuum of "no-thinking stance"
    - Encounter cascade: bombarding players with encounter-crisis
    - Forced point of view: players into character-shoes, split second decisions, killing shadows/faces/movement/innocents/your own
    - Delayed consequences: create end-zone to evaluate actions, or to suppress such evaluation
    - Contrasted consequences: relatives, friends, sweethearts -> hero-worship, estrangement and aggression

    These are tools used in Fabula, on and off and in different mixture (and with other tools), according to how intense the battle we want.

    The reason I explored this venue back in 2003-04, was this: I realized that my critique on combat being dull and dumb in RPGs, had to be followed up by me making something better. I made Fabula: Era of The Worm, a convention module with three stringed scenarios. The whole module focused on war, from duels and skirmishes, to the grand battle. It was made for the Norwegian convention Arcon.

    The first scenario made players say things like:
    - This was the worst RPG I've ever played, and the f***** best!
    - I'm so sad and hurt I have to go home and lie down, man!
    - This was breathtakingly good! No more RPGs for me on this convention!
    - I want to cry!
    - Wow! I have played D&D for many years, but I never knew RPGs could be so intense!

    As I said; I would never dream of using D&D for such gaming (or any other mechanics-heavy game), but then I'm an odd one with my own classical RPG, and my own design-philosophy about it. I'm astonished how little work there has been done with methodic tools within classical RPGs! There's OCEANS of possibilities to explore, and enormous advantages to be had for both gamesmith and games-master!

    Go do it, man! Explore the possibilities! Empower your game!

    \nNever, ever, ever destroy something a player cares about without giving them a legitimate chance to defend it.     \n John's right on. You can't -make- players care about anything.

    a) have them tell you outright what they'd like their character's stories to be about (Flags)

    Posted By: Mark CauseyI'm searching for something here but am having a hard time saying it. How does a DM/GM string together tactical combats with strong meaning, making the fight worth fighting and not just murder and thievery?
    \nOne advantage of a campaign-style game like D&D over other games is that you have the time to build tensions and layer the story over time. In my long-term (20 years, with infrequent play) D&D game that I run, I figured out a long time ago that great bad guys were one key to making great, meaningful fight scenes. At some point I stopped letting every combat be a charnel house- the heroes don't always get a conclusive win (or they don't win at all), and sometimes the bad guys get away. Those bad guys have names, and will come back later, much to the players intense irritation and delight. At this point, I have plots that have tied up years after they started, and villains who never fail to capture the players' attention. \n
    Posted By: bankuei(A larger concern is that a lot of old school players have learned NOT to care, because usually if they care about something, it either gets taken away -like Jared's story of trying to own a bar which would always get bombed or burned down-, used a carrot to railroad them, or else something which inevitably betrays them. You know, because that's -so- awesome.)
    \nI think this is a really interesting point as well- sometimes with short-term or one-shot games, folks tend to use up story elements too readily. "Okay, so lessee... you've got a sister that you love, and your family manor, and a sword the king gave you? Right! So, while you were out hunting, some bandits came and burned your house, stole your sword, and sold your sister into slavery! Awesome!!" I think the key here is, again, to let these elements mellow over time with the story- let the players develop relationships with the NPCs, and make sure their fancy rocket car gets them through a dozen adventures before you really threaten it. The more invested the player is- the more they depend on that plot element- the more it will mean when it's in danger.

    \n Hi Steve,

    I think the other thing is that people see "threaten" rather simplistically, usually as kill/destroy. What happens if the player thinks it would be cooler if their sister dies so they can go on revenge road?

    My usual trick for threatening something is to take a real life kind of situation that hacks me off and start applying it's equivalent to the game.

    "Hey, brother... I don't really want to ask but.."
    "What is it? Ask anything, I'll help out."
    "Well, we need 300 gold pieces"
    "Why?"
    "The landlord said he was going to loan us 50 gold last fall, but he says the interest means we owe 300 gold now, or else he's taking all our cows."
    "WHAT?!?"

    Since we're talking tactical combat gaming, I'd probably model the landlord off of some of the worst slumlord/strikebreaker types out of history and give him a band of crooked cops/bodyguards, etc.

    Just take the worst dickwad-ery you've heard of or experienced and apply it liberally to your game. Somewhere, the players are going to get hacked off and give someone their just due. And like life, since it's not "life or death", the problems can just keep coming.
