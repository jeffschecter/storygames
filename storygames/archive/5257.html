    \n Presumably the license itself places these restrictions on you, in one way or another. Notice that FAQ entry that says that they'll be "better defining" the new OGL license by "incorporating" elements from the D20 license into it. Presumably this could mean that the OGL will cease to become really open, and might become what the old D20 license was: a quick and streamlined way for you to arrange a marketing license for WotC's IP and limited use of their trade-dress, hand-in-hand with assigning over (to the D20/OGL licensor community) some rights on your IP and giving them the right to yank your license any time they felt like it.

    As for cost, software SDKs are often tiered in this fashion; premium "partners" must pay for that relationship, but get "rewards" in enhanced support or tools or cost breaks on development equipment purchase, etc, etc.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarI'm curious how they can restrict, by date, the creation and publication of OGL stuff. I guess I don't understand it very well. Anybody?
    \nFrom what I understand, the new OGL is fairly restrictive and hinges on two major things:

    1) The DnD 4e Players Handbook must be the basis for play in any product that participates in the 4e OGL. That means in order for your game to be under the 4e OGL, you have to reference (but not copy/plagiarize) all the new rules for Fourth Edition Dungeons and Dragons. Scif-fi, Horror, Cyberpunk, whatever is out unless it is directly compatible with the PHB4e

    2) Your game/product must say on it somwhere other than the front cover that it is compatible with Dungeons and Dragons 4th edition. WotC will develope some boilerplate, procedure, or symbol that publishers will indicate the fact.

    If your game/product does not meet both of those criteria, you're not under the guidelines of the 4eOGL. Which means 1 of three things:

    1- You are in violation of the OGL and may be subject to legal action

    2- You are using a 3.0 or 3.5 OGL and your product has nothing to do with DnD 4e

    3- You are completely making up your own content and are disassociated with DnD/D20 altogether

    Now, that's the best I understand it so far. I could be completely wrong. My guess as to the reasoning for all of this is to encourage and forster the boom that followed the initial D20 OGL release without the devastating bust that game soon after that. WotC is trying to manage the growth of DnD compatible products rather than letting it spiral out of control.

    Posted By: viktor_haagTo be more clear on my statement, they can't further restrict materials published under the existing OGL licensing scheme.
    \nActually, I'm not sure this is the case. I'm not an IP lawyer, so I don't know, but the text of the OGL license belongs to WotC, so presumably they can restrict its distribution, and in that case, they can effectively limit you from producing OGL products by forbidding you to distribute the text of the OGL license (as you'd have to do) inside your product.

    Given the widespread intention they've already established for free dissemination of this license, I suspect that point would get defeated if it came to legal action, but there's nothing to stop WotC from sending out a cease and desist order to a publisher and trying to get an injunction against them, is there?

    I suspect that what we have here is a bifurcation not unlike the old GNU Emacs/XEmacs software split. A bunch of publishers will presumably decided that they like the OLD SRD/OGL structure very nicely, thank-you (i.e. Green Ronin with True20/M&M, Crafty Games with Spycraft) and don't need to sign on to the spanky new system. Those who want to produce material compatible with 4e (Paizo?) may well decided to embrace the times and move their products along. And we'll have two big streams of slightly compatible stuff moving forward; presumably, with one being "mostly fantasy" in tone, and the other being "other stuff" in tone.

    Just a thought.

    Posted By: Robert BohlHow do they get around the fact that rules can't be copyrighted?
    \nFrom my naive reading of the original D20 and OGL licenses, by agreeing to the license you were basically agreeing live under the more restrictive world where the SRDs and their rules were property you'd agree to use only within the terms of the license. That is, you promised not to just put out a PHB with the serial no's filed off, and by making this promise you got access to the licensee community of IP and usage of the D20 branding. As the worth of that brand diluted, more and more publishers moved to operation solely under the (less restrictive) OGL licensing scheme and, no doubt, this whole move on WotC's part is (in part) a reaction against that general drift and an attempt to reassert their control over the central IP/brand at stake here.

    But that's just my (potentially very) naive understanding of the situation.

      \nSince ethics are about values--what you believe is right or good--they're always at stake. If you believe that the only values that matter are what's going to make you the most money in the short term, then all your decisions are easy. However, even most business decisions aren't based solely on the criterion of maximizing short-term gain: multiple criteria, including felt loyalties to different internal and external stakeholders (e.g.,employees, shareholders, "the hobby," the industry, etc.) as well as beliefs about the appropriate means to achieve valid ends (aka "values"). If all those loyalties and values complement each other--if they point in the same direction--then decisions are still easy. Decisions are hard when the decision-maker has conflicting loyalties and competing values.

      Leaving aside the issue of whether differential access to the SRD is an economically rational means to achieve some business objective, the ethical dimension emerges when one wonders if it betrays some value or loyalty that WOTC ought to observe. For example, someone might say, "Doesn't WOTC have an ethical [note: not legal] obligation to work with those 3rd party publishers who've proven themselves to be good custodians of WOTC's intellectual property?" And someone else might say, "Sure, but only to the extent that it doesn't mean taking money out of the paychecks of its employees." My point is only that saying that is by definition an ethical decision: it's deciding among competing values and loyalties.

      It turns out that most debates over ethical issues are between those who see them and those who don't: that is, between those who perceive the values conflict and those who don't.

      But all of this is a digression from the pragmatic question of whether charging $5K for preferential access to the SRD is "instrumentally rational": whether it will achieve the business purpose it's intended to serve, and what precisely that purpose could be. I'm putting in my two cents to expand on Malcolm's attempt to note the distinction between the ethical and the pragmatic dimensions of the discussion.

      \n Yep, like when people vowed to put Dark Ages Fae up immediately release to protest DRM. Didn't happen, because the community involved in RPG piracy really doesn't give a shit about the rest of the RPG community. There's no swarm-network that makes things bubble up from the ether. There are seeders and leechers (which is why Bitorrent is so amazing -- it functions within the actual network dynamic of filesharing). The latter mostly scan hardcopies, though there are exceptions in small press -- often when the author specifically encourages sharing. Because there are in fact a small number of people who provide source files -- and because the piracy community is based on prestige -- they can often be traced. For example, a lot of pirated White Wolf material comes from a guy named Ruud Dirven -- a guy in his early 40s who lives in Voorburg, in the Netherlands.

      It's not impossible but a leak of 4e from inside anyone with the documents is extremely unlikely. Aside from the fact that there's some secure-hardcopy scheme in the works, the fact is that it's just not hard to find the source of something in the industry. I remember one situation where an author broke NDA, someone on RPGNet commented on what this person said without naming them or giving any other clues -- and that person was found maybe three days after the incident occurred. This was not the result of a vigilant witchhunt, but maybe two or three emails.

      But never mind. Read lots of Cory Doctorow shallowly and believe that t3h Intarnets Kan Doo Anything. Whatever to want to pretend floats your leaky boat.
