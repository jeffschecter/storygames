\n One of the mechanics I use to guide games are scene cards. These are basically plot points that will guide play through a story without the players needing to make one happen. The players are free to ignore the scene cards if they wish and make any other story happen but if they don't want to work that hard this is an easy out.

There are four scene cards per game. They build on one another. Here is an example of the ones I used in a recent game:

What evil is the bad guy up to now?
How can you get to the bad guy?
Confront the bad guy and talk... don't kill him yet.
Get medieval on the bad guy.

Now I'd like to make a deck of scene cards that players can mix and match from to customize any game. The only requirements for the plots is that they be broken down into four different parts so there is a first scene, second one, third and fourth.

I would really appreciate people's ideas on plots. My brain is too limited on it's own to come up with the coolest stuff. You guys are much cooler than I am.

Please throw out your plot ideas!

Chris Engle

    I'm reading this as a fairly linear plot structure. That is to say: "here are Act 1 scenes
    to choose from, here are the Act 2 scenes to choose from, etc. Now let's put four in order
    and play it out."

    If that's the case, I would suggest it's not necessary (I suspect I'm reading it wrong but
    the end result will be the same suggestion. So, no harm, no foul. Just a little more text).

    The above four could simply be re-ordered to make a different story:

    Act 1: (Introduction/Discovery phase) "Get medieval on the bad guy." Perhaps the bad guy was
    someone the characters already defeated; a minion of the previous bad guy or just some NPC
    from a bar brawl. The characters made him the bad guy.

    Act 2: (further Discovery/Escalation phase) "Confront the Bad Guy and talk." The characters
    definitely know who it is by now, if not earlier. Perhaps they want to apologize; make
    reparations. The bad guy wants none of it, of course. "Talking" need not be face to face:
    messenger; phone call; note pinned to a dead loved one; loud-speaker from a gunship.

    Act 3:(further Escalation - kinda messing with the three-act structure but we'll improvise)
    "What evil is the bad guy up to now?". Foiling his nefarious plot. Pretty standard.

    Act 4: (Resolution) "How can you get to the bad guy?" to have the final showdown. Putting
    "What evil is the bad guy up to now?" as Act 4 suggests that he will be a re-accuring
    villain.

    Just to be clear (as I'm afraid I couldn't get this from your initial post), the "showdown"
    or "rescue the princess" or "recover the McGuffin" can occur in any Act (even the first) as
    long as things escalate from there. I like establishing a number of Acts as well (though I
    like the 3 Act structure), since it tells us the story isn't over yet. As long as everyone
    accepts that structure, there's no confusion/anger over why the villain gets away or why he
    wasn't the true villain. "Dude, it's the first Act; the story just started!"

    So what you want are plot twists to complicate and escalate things (which is probably what
    you meant in the first place; so, yeah - sorry).

    What are some common things that characters are called on to do? And how can we mix it up a
    little?

    Recover the McGuffin:

    -It's a fake but also a clue to the real one.
    -A competitor makes a better offer for it's return or an "offer you can't refuse".
    -Someone else is after it or already has it.
    -It's stolen from you.
    -It's not what you were led to believe (ie: the "God Flask" is actually a powerful weapon or
    a small child or a secret ritual song).
    -You have found something that should have remained lost and now must seek its destruction.

    Defeat the Big Bad:

    -The Big Bad is just the Harbinger of a greater evil.
    -It's not bad, just misunderstood. "Holy Crap! WE'RE the bad guys here!"
    -It's a potential ally against a common foe; "The enemy of my enemy is my friend"
    -In death, it transcends into something more powerful or becomes a martyr to an evil cause.
    -Defeated, the Big Bad offers you your heart's desire for its release.
    -Redemption of the Big Bad is the only way to truly defeat it.
    -A terrible sacrifice must be made to defeat it.

    I'm also thinking about "Discover a Place (tomb, derelict ship, lost city, etc)" and
    "Investigate a Mystery (murder, theft, disappearance, etc)" Now you can mix-match Who(villain), What(McGuffin), Where (place), Why (investigate).

    But I may be talking to myself, in which case I'll pursue my musings on my own.

    I should also mention media res ("in the midst of things") as a First Act situation twist.
    Start a game with the characters standing over the corpse of their nemesis; now we can begin.
