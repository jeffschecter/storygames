Something cool about Heart of the Rose is that the characters are unique in the setting. There's nobody else in the world that can do what the Rose does. Nobody else poses the threat to the world that the Shadow does.

\n

That's something I really like about HotR, and about fantasy fiction of the McKillip/McKinley/Harris/Keyes school in general, which is mostly absent from fantasy roleplaying games - they tend to classify and anatomize characters rather than making them unique and special entities.

\n

Supers games like to make characters unique and special entities.

\n

How do we fit those two tastes together?

    \nHmm.

    Using DRYH as an example, your Madness Talent is essentially "hey, this is what you narrate when you want more dice." From a mechanical standpoint, characters are the same -- my Madness Talent of "Mathemagician" and your talent of "Yahweh Fire a-GoGo!" are both invoked the same way, up to our ability to make our madness talents make sense narratively in a given scene.

    Now, are you looking for that level of crunch, or do you want each player to also feel mechanically different?

    If the latter, then you're probably looking for a game with simple-enough procedures, but with enough crunch handles that each player can have a unique "rule-breaker" power -- a way their mechanics are slightly different than everyone elses' in a way that's attached to their fictional unique ability. In other words, mechanically reinforcing the "I am unique in the world" feel, so that it's being supported by more than solely in-fiction. (I have a crunch-level that supports this in Mythender without being too overwhelming in procedure, I think, but I don't enforce uniqueness.)

    I don't necessarily have an answer for anything yet -- more thinking aloud. But I didn't want to wait until I had some idea to post, since the group-mind is way, way smarter than my going off to my tower.

    \n Another thing to look at is "the one" vs "the group" and "the group of ones."

    Like, in a lot of the fantasy we're talking about there is some element of the chosen one aspect to the protagonist. This works well for games with one player, or games with one protagonist. It works less well for group games, historically that is.

    One of the common ways around that is to do, basically, the superhero fantasy group. Justice League of Greyhawk, as it were. Doing that is possible, but it also does shift the emphasis a little. Justice League is different than Batman or Superman, even if both characters happen to be present. The shift from single protagonist does make a difference. In general, I'd speculate that the difference is between "I am a unique snowflake" and "We together, like Voltron, form a unique snowflake / blazing sword."

    So, either you're going to need to shift the emphasis, make a game with a single protagonist (easy with a single player, more complicated - but not impossible - with a multiplayer game), or do something cooler than what I'm able to come up with at the moment.

    Hmm, other random thoughs.

    Fantasy Buffy the Vampire Slayer? I mean, Buffy is basically a "dark" street-level superhero right? And we've seen all sorts of ways to do Buffy as an RPG. The Slayer / The White Hats split from the Eden game, the various folks who've taken shots at it with PTA, and so forth. All of that seems like good material to steal fro... get inspiration out of.

    I have to admit that my personal slant does head that way, anyway. Most superhero teams annoy me, for various reasons, so if I'm going to do a "unique snowflake" game, I'm going to want the majority of the uniqueness to stick to one character.

    Anyway, any of that hitting anything for you S, or am I talking about something different than what you're after?
