\n Marshall Burns' discussion about the different kinds damage sparked a similar subject in my mind, the different roles combat as a whole serves in tabletop play. A fight between a party of adventurers and a tribe of orcs in BXD&D if a very different thing from a gunfight in Dogs in the Vineyard.

Slugfest:old school D&D combat, silver age superheroes and 80's action movies. Straightforward contests of strength and endurance, the winner is the guy who can both take and dish out the most raw damage.

Melees: current editions of D&D, the big fight at the end of a kung-fu flick between the heroes and the villain and his chief henchmen. A big set piece battle with several combatants, each of whose movements and tactics have repercussions for all the other fighters on the field.

Obstacle: ninjas and zombies, hoodlums and fight montages, combats in T&T and mook fights in Wushu. Mechanically the combat is highly abstracted, handled as a single challenge to overcome, no different than climbing a sheer wall or fast-talking an inconvenient guard.

The Big Beast: Dragons and giant robots. Faced with an opponent that can overpower them utterly physically, the hero has to win (or just escape) via lateral thinking.

The Puzzle: Alien horrors and weird science beasties. Similar to the Big Beast, but its a matter of figuring out the opponents singular vulnerability and exploiting it. "He's repelled by silver!" "It has gap in the scales on its belly!"

The Duel: Most any swashbuckler film ever made. A one-on-one fight between evenly matched opponents, lots of maneuvering and back-and-forth, decided by a single telling blow rather than accumulation of lesser damage. Lots of finely detailed tactics like stance.

Firefight: modern crime thrillers, war movies. Extremely deadly ranged attacks, lots of positioning and diving for cover, frenetic pace, keeping a cool head more important than personal strength.

Thematic: Good westerns, gangster films, Dogs in the Vineyard. Fights that are not about the fighting, they're just a means to express the inner conflicts of the characters involved. Its not a showdown of gunplay its a duel between one guy's sense of justice and the other's sense of guilt.

What here can be changed, and what else can be added?

    \n There's a big difference here between the role of combat in an rpg and what it looks like/feels like. I'd say, of the examples given, only Obstacle and Thematic are roles that combat plays, the rest are just different 'qualia' of fighting. In terms of roles, I would add the following:
    \n
    \nAttrition: Most horror games, such as Dead of Night. The PCs have finite resources for playing the game and part of the GMs duties include wearing away at those resources, with combat being one tactic in their repertoire for doing so.
    \n
    \nSolution: Heroic fantasy & supers. Each session heads towards a big fight at the end where the PCs get a chance to physically battle the bad guys and save the day.
    \n
    \nHooking: Almost any game. Combat is used as a way of introducing new clues or plot elements that will involve the PCs further in the plot of the session.
    \n
    \nColour: Richly detailed games. Combat is a way of celebrating or reinforcing the setting of the game world, allowing players to get under the skin of their characters a little more comfortably and get the feeling of living in that world.
    \n
    \nShowing Off: Heroic fantasy, supers, martial arts, space opera, etc. The characters have gnarly powers and various cool combat tactics available to them, so combat gives them a chance to exercise these and provide some really cool narration about their character.

    \n You mentioned games as examples of some of the styles you mentioned here, like Dogs for "Thematic," and Wushu for "Obstacle," but do you know of any game examples of the other kinds? Especially "The Big Beast," "The Puzzle," "The Duel," and "Firefight?"
    \n
    \nI really loved a lot of the way some Legend of Zelda games dealt with combat, in that there was a straightforward hack-and-slash element, but on top of that there was a more important element where you had to explore your way around a dungeon, solve puzzles, acquire a magic item that gave you a new ability, and then use that new ability to defeat the boss. It wasn't so much about having the fastest gaming reflexes or about being the strongest character in the world -- it was about going through a series of puzzles that expanded your character's ability to solve other puzzles, and gave him just the tools he needed to succeed. I wished that kind of gameplay could be recreated in a pen-and-paper roleplaying game too, without being too scripted by the GM or designer ahead of time the way a video game is.

    Posted By: KayfallYou mentioned games as examples of some of the styles you mentioned here, like Dogs for "Thematic," and Wushu for "Obstacle," but do you know of any game examples of the other kinds? Especially "The Big Beast," "The Puzzle," "The Duel," and "Firefight?"
    \nFor "The Big Beast," the closest I can think of is Barony, a mostly traditional fantasy game which had a separate series of combat tables for fighting dragons. Its been a while, but if I recall correctly they were very descriptive and presented the fight as an epic encounter of very little things scrambling around a very big thing. Also, a smattering of very old semi-wargamish systems which would have completely different combat rules for different kinds of foes. "This is how you fight a knight, now here's how you fight a band of brigands, now here's how you joust, now here's how you fight a dragon..."

    For "The Puzzle," I dont' think I've ever encountered a system that specifically catered to it, except for spot rules in scenarios that say things like "this adamantium golem is completely immune to physical attacks and spells, but can't swim if tricked into the bottomless pool in the next room."

    For "the Duel," theres' PDQ# in Swashbuckler's of the Seven Skies, nemesis fights in Wushu, the duel rules in Castle Falkenstein, and the main combat system in En Garde! Also Fading Suns, but I don't think that system was very good. I'm sure there are plenty more, since swashbucklers is a popular genre.

    "Firefights" that actually feel like firefights (rather than D&D combat at range) can be found in Recon and Twilight: 2000, and sometimes Cyberpunk 20xx (if the fighters aren't too tricked out with armor and metal bits). Again, I'm sure there are others.

    \n I've both DMed and played in D&D 3.5 campaigns where the majority of sessions followed a fairly strict pattern of:

    1) The Warm-Up Fight - usually a random encounter early on in the session, put there mostly to get the players into rolling dice and adding up numbers mode.

    2) a bit of roleplaying/exposition to set up the

    3) Plot-Advancement Fight - which is tougher than the warm-up fight, but whose main purpose is to point the players in the right direction for the real fight of the session -- as well as serving to deplete some of the party's resources.

    4) A bit more roleplaying and/or exploring, possibly involving either a puzzle to solve or a mechanical yet non-combat oriented challenge such as a trap, a Bluff/Diplomacy/Gather Information scene, a chase scene, etc., and finally...

    5) The Boss Fight, where the PCs need to pull out all the stops and expend all of their major resources to survive. Sometimes success in the previous step confers an advantage in this step (someone gave the PCs a helpful magic item, they discovered the Boss's hidden weakness, or whatever), but other times it came down to just using good tactics.

    Winning the Boss Fight would then give the players the clue they needed to progress in the overall story arc, which would continue in the next session with the same basic structure.

    It was an outline I used a lot in games where we only had a limited amount of time to play (i.e., weeknight games for busy adults) because while it was pretty predictable, it meant that in each session everyone got to Do Stuff but there was also enough of a sense of "here's why this session is important to the overall campaign" to keep momentum going.

    In those games, the role of combat was that of timing mechanism. And, of course, an excuse to roll dice and geek out and say, "Yay! I did 31 points of damage!"

In a part where the knights Holger and his Moorish ally are fighting through a horde of foes, near the end they're described as weary and battered. Their mail has been torn by dozens of blows, they bleed from bruises and flesh wounds that though individually minor collectively sap their strength, and their swords have struck so many times their edges have been blunted away, leaving them little better than steel clubs. Viewed as an expression of that kind of attrition of prowess, classic D&D combat is an excellent model.

    \nYour list doesn't explain anything, none of that stuff is related to combat. About #1: you can be badass in other ways than in combat. You may think that badassery only relates in combat, but in that case you can as well say "a character good in combat". It's quite easy to see why people would like to play someone competent, but this is not explaining why someone wants to be competent specifically in combat. About #2: You can advance the story in other ways than combat. The question is, why would you use combat for it? About #3: Combat being the whole purpose of the game does not explain what purpose combat has in the game. Why did you make your whole game about combat instead of cooking?

    My take is that combat doesn't interest folks because it does something that leads to cool: it is cool in itself. That's why it's everywhere.
