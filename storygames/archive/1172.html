Testing the Nixon Principle

Ben_Lehman
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games

To the uninitiated, the Nixon principle is "Any role-playing game can be used to play Star Wars."

Let's test this with our new games:

Perfect: You play Jedi struggling against the oppressive lifestyle of your order.

Hero's Banner: So easy I cannot even begin.

Shooting the Moon: Two sith apprentices competing for their master's favor. Likewise, a Master and the Dark Side competing for an apprentice.

1001 Nights: In the temple of the Jedi there are many rooms. In these rooms are many knights, and the knights tell many stories. Most intruiging for our tale, none of the knights can leave their order... Tell stories about your exploits as an attempt to get away from the stifling environment.

Primitive: Ewoks and Wookies! (this is a bit of an edge case.)

For others: It was a mutual decision, untitled, cold city, mob justice, death's door, best friends, panty explosion, the mr lincoln experiment, anything else.

Shock: is surprisingly hard.

    Don't Rest Your Head -- let's make that Don't Rest Your Force:

    Discipline is the Light Side. You use it to keep yourself calm and under control.

    Exhaustion is the Dark Side. Bit by bit you grow closer to the Dark Side, as it tempts you and offers you greater power. If you ever go beyond 6 Dark Side, it destroys you utterly.

    Madness is Passion. Giving in to your emotions puts you in a bad way, and can erode your grip on the Light Side in time. Instead of Fight or Flight, you have Fear and Anger as your responses. When you've had three moments of Fear or Anger and your Passion takes you a fourth time -- you have a Crisis, your Light Side erodes by one, and you gain a permanent die of Passion. If you ever run out of Light Side dice due to this erosion, you turn fully to the dark side, and become a Sith Lord -- with all of your dice turning into your new (NPC) Pain rating.

    I think it works.

  • tony_dowler
    March 2007
    Sorceror!

    The force is a living energy that pervades you and obeys your commands. Yes, it's a demon. This isn't what the Jedi council taught. Why? Because many high ranking members of the council have demons that need lies. True, they've done their best to spin a myth of self-control, inventing the "dark side" as a boogey man to bolster padawans against the inherent dangers of sorcery, but in the end, they are nearly impotent, perpetually treading a find line between feeding the needs of their demons and falling completely under their sway.

    Each Jedi has a force demon. Force demons have needs like "perpetuate the fabric of lies that is the Jedi council", "find promising young proto-sorcerors and set them down the path", "foster a political environment that encourages stasis and stagnation", "quell new ideas", "maintain the mystique of the Force", "maintain the 'peace' that allows the demons to keep their galactic hegemony".

    If your Force demon has desires like "stagnation", "peace", and "obfuscation", you end up in the diplomatic corps. Desires like "corruption", and "lies" suit your for a role training padawans.

    Every Jedi goes into the desert and spends days in a complex ritual after which they return with a lightsaber. The lightsaber is a demon. It's need is to be used. Lightsabers don't like it when you solve problems without using your lightsaber.

    The Emperor? A demon. His need is to be obeyed.

    Don is right. You could use Ganakagok to play Star Wars, if you changed the tarot card motifs to make them more galactic and less icy. Shawn de Arment had the idea of using Ganakagok to play a God-Emperor of Dune game; I think he changed the tarot suits to Tears, Sand, Spice, and Stars (or maybe Space).

    For Star Wars, the rules changes have similarly to do with changing the color. For the deck, how about Planets, Suns, Hyperspace, and Starships? Instead of Good Medicine and Bad Medicine, you have Light Side and Dark Side for individual characters, the Jedi Order, and the Old Republic. During the game, your actions can cause those points to accumulate. If the Old Republic winds up with more Dark Side than Light Side, the Empire rises. If the Jedi wind up with more Dark Side than Light Side, the Order is undone by the Sith. And if individual characters have more Dark Side than Light Side...well, you know.

    So on your turn, the GM might throw the Six of Hyperspace for the current situation ("Bantha Tracks: An opponent leaves traces of his activities") and your action, whatever it is, might allow people to bring to bear their Gear and their Scars, their Loves and Hates, their Disciplines and their Fears, their Virtues and their Flaws, on the consequences.
