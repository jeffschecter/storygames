\n ...appeal to some people but not others. (Screw it, let's stir the pot.)

Bangs ask you to make a statement. They present you with a situation that is inherently unstable, and they ask you how you see it and what that leads you to do. From a certain point of view, this is perfect -- it asks for your interpretation, your perspective, on a thing that doesn't have inherent meaning until you construct it through your actions. They're fun, it's entertaining to watch how people deal with them, and they don't force anyone down a certain path.

From another point of view, that's an awful situation to be in. It's ambiguous, there's no clear right choice. Much of the time, they put your principles in conflict with each other. These situations are for people who don't know what they stand for, who want to "explore issues." You ought to just be able to do the correct thing and get it over with, move the story forward.

From a third point of view, bangs are still awful. They present you with a challenge that has no clear goal, that has no defined end point, that doesn't specify what resources you have available, and gives you little to no way to figure out what trade-offs you can make to resolve it. And even worse, sometimes they go so far as to violate the notion that the imagined universe operates according to observable principles, because creating bangs puts a higher priority on "interesting story" than on "how the world works"!

I don't believe any of those view points is right, but they're all valid. And they are, nearly verbatim, the Radical, Manager, and Strategist from over here (for the tl;dr crowd, scroll down to the table.) They're also the views I hear expressed the most, and the views I most often see in conflict, here and elsewhere.

I've seen it enough times now I can almost predict what those viewpoints would say about each other. And for that matter, what each of them will say about the idea of other viewpoints being valid...

(A hint: everyone is wrong, if you listen to everyone else.)

    \nOh, absolutely. Just as a disclaimer, understand that I'm not expressing my own preferences here, so you're really in no danger of offending me regardless of how you feel about any of these approaches.

    "Do what was planned so we can move on" is often exactly the sentiment expressed by someone thinking in Manager mode. The deal is though, that particular phrase has completely different connotations depending on which mode you're thinking about it in. From a Manager standpoint, moving along the planned track is what you're supposed to do, it's the correct approach. This, of course, is maddening to someone coming from a Radical standpoint, since it puts the GM's vision on a categorically separate plane from everyone else's -- it removes opportunities to contribute, to share your viewpoint, to make statements, to interpret, to relate as equals. And it's maddening to someone coming from a Strategist viewpoint, because if there's right way to do it, there's no fun in searching for the best available solution. It removes the opportunity to be a creative problem solver, to balance important concerns, and so forth. But to the Manager, it's just the way things are done: You are presented with choices, there are right choices and wrong choices, and you choose. Then you are either rewarded or punished. Nothing in that is conflicting with a preplanned story, which is why people with strong Manager mode preferences often express a fondness for what others might call "linear scenarios."\n

    \nExamples? I am not quite grokking how a bang can violate "how the world works."
    A frequent complaint is that something or other, bang or otherwise, isn't plausible. You may recognize this complaint if you've played with people who are into heavy Simulation.

    The viewpoint expressed in the above quote, the Strategist mode, wants things to operate on the basis of universal, observable principles of how the imagined world works. The problem with bangs, from that viewpoint, is that they're motivated not by the laws of the imagined universe, but by the dictates of creating an interesting game. In other words, when people thinking in Strategist mode complain about bangs, they mean that, from their point of view, the situation was not a natural result of what came before it, but instead imposed on the fiction from outside. And that's a problem, because if the world doesn't have observable principles of how it works, then the opportunity to strategize goes out the window. If you can't rely on the world operating predictably, then any strategy you come up with, any solution you devise, can always be invalidated whenever the GM feels like it. That removes the opportunity for a Strategist to have the kind of fun they want.

    A real example from my group: Someone with a strong Strategist preference is playing a character who is married and has children. The character comes home from a long trip, and, with no warning, discovers that his wife has become addicted to opium in his absence. From a pro-bang point of view, we're cool -- something has happened that forces evocative choices, and depending on how the character reacts, a ton of interesting things can happen. There are all kinds of interesting dynamics here, and lots of issues to make statements on. This is juicy one.

    From the Strategist's point of view, he just got fucked over big time. Since there were no clues that it was coming, there was nothing he could do to prevent it. And since it went completely counter to the sort of person he understood his wife to be, it didn't seem plausible -- no principles of the imagined universe he was aware of indicated that this sort of thing might happen. And even worse, there was no way to strategize about what to do now, since the situation itself didn't prevent any resources to work with and it was single-faceted.

    The natural Radical counter-argument, and the one we actually did see in real life, is that the player is free to make these things up. "If you need resources, make some up, and we'll happily run with that. You can approach it however you want. We're excited to see how your character's perspective plays out, and what sort of statement you make here. You're empowered to do these things." It didn't go over well, for reasons that should be obvious by now.

    Well, the obvious answer (and not so much useful, for now, but it's a start) is making sure that the initial situation (and characters, and setting) is prone to producing bangs. In a "natural" way, without having to force-fed them to the players.

    The usual way I have seen this done is using character creation. Many games have "flags" on the character sheet that are gold for a GM searching for something that already exist in the game that is problematic for the character. Why this is the usual way? I suspect it's because, this way, you get to have the players do most of the work, and you are more sure that they will find the bangs interesting. The drawback is that, if you have players who don't like this amount of "protagonism" for their character (don' want the bangs to revolve around them), and/or see any flag on the character sheet as a "weakness" and fill it with inanities that they don't really consider interesting, this methot can fall flat.

    Luckily, there is an alternative, even if it's less used because it's more work for the GM: to get a bang-bringing situation, you can complicate the characters, yes... but you can instead complicate the setting!

    You can have the most vanilla, undetailed, "bland" and bang-free character in the world, but if you put them in complicated world, they WILL have to make a choice. Two leaders, fighting for the domination of the clan, both with good reasons: who will the characters follow? They will follow one? Both? No-one? (many players will not recognize these as "bangs" because there is this idea that bangs are character-centric. But it's not so).

    Dogs in the Vineyard is build on this principle (as is the game that inspired it, Trollbabe). Shadows of Yesterday is less explicit, but work best like this, too. Some setting, like Glorantha, are rife with bangs-creating situations.

    I don't see the conflicts that Max puts forth. After reading, I think it's because I may see bangs differently than he does. It may be that I see bangs differently than Ron Edwards, who coined the term, does too.

    This is what I think of when I hear people talk about bangs. A bang is an event that happens that forces one or more people to make a choice, and to have things happen as a result of their choice. It doesn't have to be story-driven. Bangs, from my viewpoint, happen in real life.

    A grenade is tossed into your tent. You can choose one of many courses of action. None of them are dictated by the grenade being tossed. You can stand there and stare. You can run. You can throw yourself on the grenade. You can try to grab it and throw it away from you. You can grab a buddy and get him to cover. You can throw a buddy on the grenade.

    This is a simplistic example, but I thought of 3 of those choices as I was writing them, and I'm pretty sure I could think of at least a couple more. Now, I could tell you which ones are the right ones and which are the wrong ones, based on my values. Your values may be different, and so your perception of which ones are right and wrong will differ.

    Here's the important part: No matter what choice you make, something happens as a result of that choice. You get blown up, you maybe escape, you die saving everyone, you toss the grenade out of the tent and it blows up outside, you save your buddy. Your buddy dies while you live.

    The manager might have some problem with the open-endedness, but unless he perceives that there is absolutely no plan (see "You led us by the nose too much") then he'll react as he deems proper and see "reward" or "punishment" as appropriate. The radical will like the open-endedness. He can react however he likes, and it has the potential to be cool. The strategist has many built in tactical options. Depending on the circumstances, (the tent is in the Ozarks on a camping trip, or it's in the middle of a military camp) he might have problems with the event not "fitting", but that has nothing to do with the fact that it's a bang, it has to do with the manipulation of the fiction.     This is what I think of when I hear people talk about bangs. A bang is an event that happens that forces one or more people to make a choice, and to have things happen as a result of their choice. It doesn't have to be story-driven. Bangs, from my viewpoint, happen in real life.

    A grenade is tossed into your tent. You can choose one of many courses of action. None of them are dictated by the grenade being tossed. You can stand there and stare. You can run. You can throw yourself on the grenade. You can try to grab it and throw it away from you. You can grab a buddy and get him to cover. You can throw a buddy on the grenade.
    \nThis is my understanding of what a Bang is, too. Not story-driven, story-driving. A Bang is any fictional event that says "now what do you do?" in a way the character can't ignore. (Or if they ignore it, that too has meaningful consequences).

    The thing that makes a Bang is

    1. It demands a response, even if that response is inaction.
    2. The response has meaningful consequences that change the course of events, reveal character, or otherwise impact the fiction in a non-trivial way.
    3. The event is relevant to the character. (This last one might be optional. I'm not really sure.)

      Posted By: MelinglorSo lemme try n' explain my perspective. I'm seeing a lot of people saying they like/don't like this or that kind of play and attaching the word "Bang" to it, which is all fine and dandy. But every time someone tries to give a description (real or hypothetical) of Bang usage they didn't like. . .it doesn't look like it's any intrinsic quality of Bangs that's killing the fun, it looks like a whole other element is responsible. Basically, the thing introduced is a disconnect of some kind--from the established setting or situation, from the character concept, from the previous motives and behavior of NPCs, what have you.
      \nAnd now you've hit on one of the most fundamental things going on here.

      A bang does not exist in isolation. It necessarily depends on and interrelates with the other aspects of your game. Now, having read this thread and the one about modes of thinking, it should not surprise you that each one of these modes of thinking about roleplaying produces games that rest on different underlying assumptions. Those assumptions have effects on far more than just bangs. They affect nearly every aspect of the game, from how authority is distributed to what sort of procedures you use to the basic properties of the imagined world. In other words, Radical mode thinking produces not only Radical-style bangs, but a Radical-styled environment for those bangs to exist in. That's what makes their version of bangs work. Similarly, Strategist thinking will produce Stategist-style bangs, in a Strategist-styled environment. When you take Radical-style bangs and put them in a Strategist-style environment, the support structure is radically different, and they don't work as well. It would be like taking me and dropping me in Mongolia -- without the familiar landmarks and social institutions, I don't know if I'd even be able to feed myself. But I could either view that as a problem relating to my intrinsic qualities (I don't speak Mongolian, for one), or a problem related to some whole other element (Mongolian geography, for example.) Neither is wrong -- they're both true, but they're both partial.

      So when people start talking about bangs, it often goes like this:

      Radical: "This is what a bang is. You should do it like this."
      Strategist: "Nuh-uh. When I do that, all these other bad things happen."
      Radical: "All those bad things happen because you're not using bangs correctly. They should be based on XYZ."

      Manager: "Yeah, come on. Who really enjoys getting kicked in the nuts that much? Masochists."
      Strategist and Radical together: "Sigh."

      Strategist: "XYZ? Are you out of your mind? XYZ ruins the fun every time. You're doing bangs all wrong. What you need to do is JKL."
      Radical: "Oh no. I've seen what happens when you do JKL -- all sorts of fail."
      Strategist: "That's because you're not doing JKL correctly. It needs to be strongly based on RST."
      Radical: "RST? Dear lord no. You're still playing games with RST? Poor soul."

      Manager: "Clearly bangs are the wrong way to play."
      Strategist and Radical together: "Shut up, heathen."

      So...where do we go from there?

      A first step is to recognize that we're having that conversation. From the posts in this thread, I'd say some people do and some people do not. (That, of course, is one of the dangers of the internet.) A second step is to realize that there is no objective reality of bangs. There are a number of different perspectives, that, when taken together, form a cloud of bang-like concepts. Depending on which angle you look at that cloud from, different pieces of it stick out. So a third step is to begin to self-identify what perspective you're looking at it from. If your core values line up more with the Strategist than the Radical, that's helpful information for you and for everyone else. It tells us what sort of fun you're looking to get from bangs, and that also helps everyone spot what sorts of biases we can expect you to have. A fourth step would be to accept biases as a natural part of having a perspective. In other words, know that you're never going to get someone else to see what you see until they step out of their own perspective. You can tell them what you see, but as they listen, they'll be trying to match it up with what they're seeing. When it doesn't, you'll sound wrong to them. Conversely, when what they say doesn't match up with what you see, they'll sound wrong to you. The nature of this conversation will never change until one person relinquishes their perspective, stands up, and walks over to where the other person is. "Oh, that's what you were describing!" You're the only person under your control, so you can't make anyone else do this. Be the first is a fantastic step five. A sixth step would be to abandon the "let's have one monolithic conversation about bangs" approach, in favor of narrower conversations about each perspective. Maybe something like "let's talk about bangs in the context of Warrior thinking."

      That's the sort of process that the table is intended to help with, and that's what this thread is intended to be about.
