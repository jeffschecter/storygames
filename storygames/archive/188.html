    Teach me how to tell a story
    Um... how's this?

    First you can think about it as a chain of stuff that happens. I was in the store, then I bought some tortillas, and then I went home.

    But nobody wants to hear that kind of story. They want a story with conflict in it. So really why you'd be telling that story is more like, "Dude, I was gonna make tacos, but holy crap, I discovered I was out of tortillas."

    Cool. So now you're all, "dude, so did you end up getting tortillas? What happened?"

    So any story worth a tortilla gots to have a conflict. People with any sense about them already know this. They just might not know what to call it.

    Even better stories have a series of things that happen that keep the conflict from easily resolving. "The nearest store was closing in 15 minutes, and I was also supposed to walk the dog." And you're all, well, crap, so did you get your wife to walk the dog so you could go to the store? And then there's probably red lights and stuff to keep the suspense building, and maybe some old dude in the parking lot who won't get out of the way to let you park.

    And of course it all builds up to an exciting end, where I'm all, "dude, they were saying store closing in like two minutes, and there I am trying to find the goddamn aisle with the mexican food, and then I remembered that the tortillas are actually back by the cheese, refrigerated, and I'm almost knocking people over, and..."

    Everybody knows how to tell a story like that, I think, except the one guy at the party you end up stuck in the kitchen with, who proceeds to describe the stain on his new pair of pants.

    The best stories (IMO) are a result of the protagonists following their ruling passions. That's because I enjoy stories where the protagonists are active instead of reactive. So it's not, "This happens to the protag from the outside, then this happens to her, then this," even when there's conflict involved. Instead, it's "She sets out to do x (driven by her ruling passion), and gets in a conflict over it; in the resulting new situation, she is driven to do y, which escalates the situation into a more conflictual one and raises the stakes; until finally, she does z, which resolves the overall issue and proves that her passion leads to that outcome (in this case).

    Say, Veronica wants to find out what really happened to her murdered mother; that's her ruling passion, the force that drives her. So she tries to interrogate person A and has a conflict over getting the information. With the newfound info, she goes and breaks into her mother's huge corporate employer's record center, where a conflict will be whether she will be discovered after finding evidence against her mother's boss. Finally, she sets out to bait the boss by setting up a trap, and the conflict becomes about what it'll cost her to bring him down and how far she's willing to go.

    Compare this to Veronica whose mom was murdered. Her ruling passion is still to find the killer. But things are brought to her: a man comes to her and shares evidence. She waits around, and someone stalks her and she escapes. Then someone else approaches her and shares evidence. Then the killer thinks she has it figured out (maybe she has, but she's not acting on it) and tries to get her, too.

    There were conflicts in that second example, but I'm not going to root much for the protagonist. She's passive, reactive, not purposefully addressing the situation.

    That's my $.02.

    Incidentally, that's part of why many old-school games suck at creating good stories. They will tell you to present a plot to the players, to throw them into situations, to guide them from one clue to the other. They're reacting, not acting. (Not always, of course, but the basic GM advice is usually to simply "hook" the characters in some semi-logical way to give them an excuse to participate in the conflicts, instead of making them actively follow their own passions.)

    Damn. I wrote a follow-up post, and then my browser ate it. So here's a short version.

    As for techniques: see Kickers, R-Maps, Bangs.

    A Kicker gives an inciting incident, but then keeps on asking: What do you do now? Note that you don't keep writing new Kickers; that would lead you into passive territory in which the character simply always reacts. One Kicker, then the player needs to push it forward.

    The R-Map gives you the environment in which you always have choices to answer that question of what to do next. There are NPCs involved in the situation, they are interconnected, and if you want to get to one of them, you can do it directly or indirectly.

    Bangs are attempts to open up a venue of action for the character. Sometimes the player likes that venue and drives down it; other times they drive right by.

    That's how I understand it all, anyway.

    I can think of two things missed so far.

    1. Make the audience care about the characters in whatever way suits. If the villian is evil, work to make the audience hate them. If it's a comedy of errors, then everyone should be sympathetic. And so on.

    2. Pattern. Most audiences wants there to be a cadence to the story as a whole. The first son, the second son, the son-as-protagonist from folklore. The story that frames a story that frames a story, as in the Arabian nights. The actual form of pattern varies wildly, but an audience should be able to pick up on it without you showing it to them - if they can see the structure, and think ahead, predicting bits here and there, this can carry them through any required dry stuff; because they have a sense of the structure and where they're going with you... ...and because they know where they're going with you, they are with you.

    How do you tell a story? Well, I know of one way to answer that question...

    One of the first primary rules of storytelling: Know your audience.
    Period. If you don't know to whom your[sic] speaking a story will fall flat.

    This is one of the key elements of what the National Park Service (where I am a volunteer) calls Interpretation. The formal study of this stuff is fairly recent, and largely comes after nearly a century of scratching heads about how certain Rangers did better than others. One thing they realized quickly was that they didn't want either accomplished entertainers or lecturers.

    So NPS Interpretation usually involves a park or other natural/historic resource, and an attempt to get the visitor to really connect with it. So as I describe this, think of what it is that you want your audience or participants to connect to. Is it the kingdom you created on the map laid over the table? Is it the characters you had the players craft? Is it the legendary artifact that your story focuses on? Is it an NPC or Villain?

    So here's my rundown of the "Interpretive Process Model" that the NPS hammers to death:

    1. Select a tangible place, object, person, or event that you want the audience to care about. You may have many, but should pick one to be iconic for your story.
    2. Make a list or tree or other collection of intangible implications this resource has.
    3. Find Universal concepts in this set of intangibles. Concepts such as death, freedom, god, safety, family, anger, and so forth all exist in some form in all cultures and across age groups. These are your guaranteed hooks into any audience.
    4. Identify your audience. Often this advice is merely a bromide, and is easier said than done. Largely this is all about using audience reactions to determine what story they heard, so that you can cater to them further.
    5. Write a single sentence of your story's theme, and this must include one Universal idea in its statement.
    6. From this, lay out the lay of the tale in your head. As anchors, think of the associated emotional and intellectual connections you want your audience to make with the tangible object you chose in #1.

    ...and then step 7 is usually a stodgy way of saying "put it all together and just finish it."

    If this all sounds painfully bureaucratic to begin with... Hell, it's the US federal government we're talking about here. You'd be amazed at how many buzzwords I've sanded the edges off of.

    There are of course other associated techniques that go into this model thingummy. You have to balance the emotional connections and the intellectual connections: on Alcatraz we try not to get too technical about cell door mechanisms without also detailing how the prisoners felt when the doors slammed shut. You also have to decide what your objectives for the tale are, although that's usually more for the lecture-ish side of things.

    This sort of process is good for coming up with a 15-minute talk that keeps a crowd of all ages and varying English skills participating to the end. If you know how to use those simple universal concepts as hooks, you can keep just about anyone from losing the plot. The techniques also help compensate for varying levels of charisma or oratorical linguistic skill in the speaker.

    What you'll notice isn't in there is anything about dramatic structure, buildup, climax or denouement. Part of this is because the park service does naturalist interpretations as well as historical, so the system was made general enough to cover both. Part of it is just because that isn't the sort of thing that's worth formalizing to the point of forcing everyone to standardize.

    But the rest, in my opinion, is all delivery and characterization and linguistic skill. It's all embouchure and sense of pitch and tone and rhythm. I don't know that you can really teach that stuff, although it can be learned.

    There are two or three really good ways to learn oral storytelling, in my opinion.

    1) Listen to other people tell stories, especially if they are good storytellers and you really enjoy listening to them.

    2) Try to be like them. Maybe re-tell the same stories, doing exactly what they did, but also telling some things a bit differently, trying to put your own spin on things. This is, traditionally, how people learned to tell stories.

    3) Practice reading stories aloud. Pick ones that you love with all your heart. My favorites to read aloud are Salman Rushdie's Haroun and the Sea of Stories and Italo Calvino's Invisible Cities. Find yours. Develop a good speaking voice, learn to SLOW THE FUCK DOWN and pause at the right places, learn to have nice innotation, and a variety of character voices. These don't have to be goofy stereotypes, but can be subtle things you do to indicate differences of personality and tone.

    Finally...

    4) PAY ATTENTION TO THE WAY OTHER PEOPLE REACT WHEN YOU TELL STORIES. Watch where you lose their attention. Watch when you hook them good. Watch when they laugh or grin or frown or gasp. Figure out how to work with your audience so that they can anticipate where you're going and follow you there. [This applies to roleplaying too.]

    I teach public speaking, and so I watch people tell stories all the time, usually in the service of making a larger point. Here's what I tell my students:

    "Stories are the currency of human experience. We all tell stories all the time, and we listen to the stories that other people tell us. But we wrap them up in the back and forth of everyday conversation, so the stories we tell every day aren't as sharp as they need to be when we're in front of an audience.

    "Ultimately, every story boils down to this: an Introduction, where you bring in the central characters and their situation; Complications, where you give the characters a problem that they have to deal with; and a Resolution, where we see how the characters deal with the problem.

    "So this is a story: 'I was standing around one day, when this guy comes up and starts giving me a hard time. So I clocked him in the face.'

    "So is this: 'I was standing around one day, when this guy comes up and starts giving me a hard time. So I just walked away.'

    "Notice," I tell my students, "how you know something about the character of the person who'd tell the first story, and about the person who'd tell the second story. You know something about those guys!" Because I want students to recognize how stories are connected to character, in the sense of persona.

    So, my answer to Shreyas is "Introduce a character, give that character something to react to, and tell how the character reacted."

And both Jonathan and Spads suggest paying attention to language, to how others use words and delivery to create a specific effect. I want to think more about that, too.
