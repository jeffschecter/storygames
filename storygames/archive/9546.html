\n This is something I've been thinking (and podcasting) about lately, and which has become important to the two games I'm currently working on. An "archiving mechanic" is basically a rule that makes recording elements of the gameplay experience a part of the gameplay process.

So, for example, I'm adding a thing to Raspberry Heaven where after each session you sit down and write down the most memorable thing that happened, and that in turn becomes mechanically significant in future sessions. If you play the game a bunch, you wind up with a bunch of "memories" that you can invoke by playing certain cards.

Another example I heard on a podcast (and I suck and can't remember which one) was someone was running SotC, and applying new Aspects to the setting based on the PCs' actions.

Anyway, I'm wondering if there are any other such games (or hacks of games, or fragments of games, or whatever) out there, and how they work, and how well they work?

    \nThe Questing Beast is a relatively early title that incorporates a player diary as part of the play process.

    I've been working around these matters for a couple of years now. Last week I reviewed a commisioned design I made for an abortive one-player game wherein a big part of the game was the diary the player wrote during play. This was five years ago, so apparently I've been interested in this for a while now.

    In Solar System I specifically went out to resolve the amount of bookkeeping and format issues I'd want to have in a dramatic adventure game: all record-keeping in the game is formalized into something the players have to pay for to keep, specifically so the system favours forgetting things and, if necessary, recreating them anew later.

    Records are a major part of Eleanor's Dream as well - the players are in fact expected to write over the book itself as a part of the process of play. (My long-suffering game about adventures in a dreamland, for those who don't know.)

    \n Games that generate interesting ephemera:

    Grey Ranks, where situation elements pulled into play are paired with Radio Lightning broadcasts fixing them in time. This works as well as you want it to, really, since there's no procedural need to write your elements on the back of Radio Lightning sheets, but if you do it it forms a nice record.

    Primetime Adventures, where character sheets post-season are essentially a record of change combined with a relationship map. This might be a weaker example.

    \nI was thinking of "stronger" examples, though BW character sheets definitely do communicate more of what's gone before than most.

    I'm not totally sure what it was that led me to start thinking about this stuff, but some of it was probably the simple fact that whenever I've tried to run a game with an elaborate setting and story, it became very difficult to keep track of everything. In one campaign I tried having a wiki with setting info and session summaries, but the players largely lost interest in it after a while. To some extent, I've just taken this as a sign that my group isn't suited to elaborate campaigns, but it's also gotten me interested in rules that do that kind of archiving as a natural extension of play.

    Dictionary of Mu is a really neat example, and I really don't know why I didn't think about it. In hindsight, it's probably another of the things that got me thinking about this stuff. It does this "archiving" stuff partly by making the archive a central feature of the game in the first place.

    \n This is one of the main design goals of our The Wire style game. Those rules are a bit of a mess, but basically, the idea was to have a campaign be played on one of those big noticeboards that detectives use to refer to while working on a case, with pictures and notes stuck to them with lines drawn everywhere, etc. Nothing is ever removed from the board, but things may be stuck over or crossed out.

    \n Check out unWritten, which is sort of built on this premise. Your character's stats and the setting's "cues" are generated by what happens in play.

    And in Agora, you can archive your opportunity cards, which can create a 'book' of what has happened on your Agora. The obstacle library is also a persistent thing that gets modified as you play, and you see the same obstacles come back, slowly changing just as your character changes as they survive and build Agora. The library also overwrites itself, though, so I suppose it's a pretty crappy archive.

    However, I find that archiving mechanics are generally cumbersome beasts. unWritten works for me because you jot down a very punchy single line and move on. Agora's "book" is mostly a side-effect nifty thing you can do. I have seen a lot of games attempt to "create a record of the story you play" but in my opinion, they often fail because they require far too much handling time or do not distill the experience of play into the archive, whatever format it takes.

    Posted By: HiQKidIt's been getting a bit of noise around here already, but it deserves it... the whole point of "A Penny For My Thoughts" is filling out questionaires about the character's memories.
    \nTo expand on this for those who haven't read it: Each player is the focal point of a (ostensibly remembered) story three times during the game. The Questionnaire looks like this:

    1. Recall a pleasant memory
    When I think of [blank], I remember:

    What was pleasant about it?


    2. Recall an unpleasant memory
    When I think of [blank], I remember:

    What was unpleasant about it?


    3. Recall how you came to be here
    When I think of [blank], I remember:

    How did you lose your memory?


    Do you want to remember your past? [yes/no]


    So, basically, the Questionnaire is the procedure of play, and as you go through that procedure everyone ends up with a nice tidy record of what their character did, and the growth they come to (or not) at the end.

    Also, you can only go through each question when you have enough pennies (a mechanical element; you get one when you finish a story, and you can get others by saying an action for someone else's character that they then choose to have happen in the fiction). So, to begin your pleasant memory, you must have two pennies, to begin your unpleasant one you need three pennies, and to recall how you came to be at the Orphic Institute with no memories, you need four pennies.

    I think this one is a winning example of "archiving as the process of play", Ewen.
