    \n The only thing that's interesting about any transhumanist concept is what piece of humanity remains behind. So what you should do in all these situations is identify a core human story. Like Nathan's AI: it has a big Oedipal issue. Someone who wants to kill their father, cool, that's a thousand different sorts of stories right there. So now you have some being that wants to kill something that's in his father's place, and you work out why and that's your situation. Now you can put it in whatever transhumanist or utopian setting you want without it being boring because you already have your conflict cooked up.

    If you're looking at a transhumanist situation and saying "wow, that's fucking boring", then you're not looking at it wrong, you're looking at it right. It's only the humanity acting within the situation, acting as humanity has always and will always act, that allows us to relate to the story and find it interesting.

    Unless you're not worried about a story and just looking at what-would-it-be-like-if exploration, which is fine too, in which case if it turns out a little boring, that's okay, because not every "what if" has an interesting answer.

    That aside, transhumans would logically have some motives that would be incomprehensible to us. I'll leave those to others as for me if it's incomprehensible I can't do much with it.

    So, what do I do? What I'd do now, but with different tools and abilities.

    I'm a collective group mind, networked but retaining some individuality (the embodiness). Ok. can others join the collective? Does that dilute "me"? What about love? What if one of us has a reaction to a person, feels passion for them, but we're not getting it at group level. How do we feel about love anyway?

    Is there prejudice? Maybe I want to get rich so that I can be sure I can always protect my own interests. Trying to get rich fast can't help but breed drama.

    I'm an automaton with limited mobility, ok, but what do I want? Do I want mobility? Is that something I care about? Perhaps I want recognition? I'm composing music, but nobody wants to hear music created by a machine. Is there a way I can get it out there, past the prejudice? In the end that's not that different to the challenge facing someone sitting in their bedroom today, the transhuman bit just adds a bit of flavour.

    \n A spoke is an avatar that is a reserve of collective expert knowledge, but without authority over the community it serves, as a new spoke can easily be created to replace one which gets too uppity. Spokes represent their constituents as accurately as possible, or else. They gain or lose knowledge with each gain or loss of a constituent, but especially if they gain or lose a true expert of their field. As such, longlived spokes are also diplomats, weaving together the practices and ideologies of the traditional, egotistical, radical, and mainstream. Nevertheless, a new paradigm often means the downfall and death of one spoke and the meteoric rise of another, sometimes aided by the untimely death of a key constituent.

    Posted By: JohnstoneBut damn, could you imagine being told that you are teleported whole, and then everybody you know uses the teleporter and then you find out no, it actually destroyes the original and makes a copy at the other end? That would be so fucking creepy.
    \nYou have very nearly recreated the origin story of the Stored in Sufficiently Advanced.

    The definition of "self" is a real problem, more so at higher tech levels as philosophy drives technological adoption.

    If one believes in the soul as the true self, particularly that a soul might inhabit multiple bodies and serial bodies, and may even return from whatever afterlife exists to do so, then copying yourself and considering that as the same person makes perfect sense.

    If you consider "all the bits that are me" as yourself, you're in trouble. \n
      \n
    • Larger bits, like cells, fall off the body all the time, and others are created. Your brain cells stick around for your whole life, but they change substantially and everything else gets replaced, so you can't use "your original cells" to define you. Mere physical contact with other body cells is a lousy criteria, as it would include all your microfauna as "you," as well as including anyone with whom you're in physical contact.
      • \n
    • Smaller bits are an even bigger problem. All electrons are identical; there's no way to tell one from another. All the electrons in your brain, whose states encode your short-term memory, might spontaneously quantum tunnel to another location and be replaced by other electrons in the exact same states. (The odds are fantastically low, but non-zero.) You'd never notice because they're indistinguishable; in fact, stating that they're "other electrons" is fairly meaningless. Continuity of experience may well be an illusion at its core.
      • \n
    \nIt's fairly easy to show that a copy isn't the "original you," what's difficult is showing that anything currently in existence is the original you.

    I think about this kind of stuff a lot with SA.
