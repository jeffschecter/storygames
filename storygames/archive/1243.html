This is a follow on to this discussion, in the hopes that it won't get too horribly derailed.

Paul suggested that we needed a stronger mechanism to recognize well designed, fun games. My immediate thought was of a strong culture of cogent review, which would imply a shared critical vocabulary.

I'd suggest that various models of gaming theory provide a wealth of nouns to pick over. Mike Holmes suggested a long list of mechanical faults, most importantly "broken," "unbalanced," "inelegant," and "known."

I'd be quick to point out that the object here is not to produce jargon that finds it's way into game texts: I think that's a mistake. Instead, I'd like to see a shared language such that we can quickly address issues with a design, and relate the strengths and failings of games in order to better focus attention where it's deserved.

Points of contention: do we need a vocabulary at all? Do we need more of one? Over what should it be framed? Does "incoherent" have a place, and where?

    Posted By: DInDenverTake Simulationism, intuitively, you would imagine it has to do with simulating reality. About crazy rules and tables that determine how many calories you need to survive. Not about engaging a setting and adhering to a genre/license conventions.
    I'm not sure what you mean by 'intuitive' in this context, but I think you mean 'you can figure it out based on speaking English'? If you frame the issue that way, it seems obvious that jargon cannot ever be intuitive -- since generally the whole point of jargon is to ascribe a complicated, specialized meaning or series of meanings to a single term. That's why it's effective -- because it's shorthand, and because it's specialized, and consequently it cannot be understood by someone who has not learned what it means. Jargon must be learned, it cannot be guessed at, which is why it's important to clearly indicate when something is a specialized term (which is why people write Simulationism, not simulationism, most of the time.)

    This is why I am a bit skeptical of people suggesting that borrowing terms from other disciplines is the way to go -- those terms exist as specialized language for a separate discipline, and have a very specific meaning in those disciplines. The odds of this meaning mapping one to one with what we want the term to mean in RPG-land seems fairly slim -- as a result, you get a word that for some people (anyone who is familiar with the discipline from which the term is borrowed) has very strong specialized connotations that differ from the new way you want to use the term. This is likely to result in confusion. While on the one hand, it can in some cases reduce the learning curve for understanding the new jargon, on the other hand you may end up with some people who only think they understand it, based on familiarity with the original.

    In a sense, this describes the problem DInDenver brought up with 'Simulationism' -- because it's such a recognizeable English-rooted word, people are already familiar with some possible meanings, and when the jargon usage turns out to be different (but not different enough to be clearly at odds) that produces confusion.

    Now, I'm not suggesting that we need to rename all our jargon into nonsense words with no possible associations -- but it's definitely something to keep in mind, especially when the borrowing may be misleadingly imprecise. (For example, 'known' seems like a very straightforward and valid borrowing to me. Whereas I recall threads on the Forge about 'bricolage' that I just could not follow without severe cognitive dissonance.)

    One of the dangers of jargon, though, is the learning curve. You mention philosophers and physicists--both which require hefty training. Quite literally, years of intensive study and, more importantly, *training.* I feel like my ol' philosophy of science chops are getting a workout here--the jargon does facilitate further developments, but at the cost of requiring training to become an essential component of access to the community.

    And the distinction between training and study is important--it is veritably impossible for someone to enter into the community of physicists or chemists or biologists or academic philosophers without having an extensive amount of time in a lab/coursework/etc. The complexity that needs to be acquired demands a certain kind of training that study alone does not provide.

    Given that, so far, game design and game play is a voluntary activity, a proliferation of jargon will make it less and less accessible. The Forge already has a little of this--watch Ron when he really gets involved in an AP discussion. He's not just talking, he's *training.* I'm pretty sure Ron knows this--he gets in there and regulates the discussion in ways that seem too refined to be accidental.

    I think it makes a lot of sense to consciously counter this trend to the best of our abilities if we aim to keep the hobby open. That probably means being a little hyper-sensitive to jargon rather than acknowledging it as a necessary thing. Sure, jargon will still develop, but it should have a harder time than jargon in disciplines like physics and academic philosophy. In theory, we could reach a point where you need to 'go to a pro' to get your actual play analyzed or something, but I would like to hold that off as much as possible.

    Here's my point: I don't think that theory jargon necessarily aids immediate discussion of play. But I do think there are other fields in whatever academic approach we want to take to RPGs as art. I don't think that anyone needs jargon in day to day play.

    As a corollary, I'd recommend that jargon never appear in any game text. Credos about gaming at the back of games: fine. In the actual text: recommend against.

    The Rubicon here is stepping into theory. I happen to belong to a camp that believes that gaming theory can help us design better games. And possibly even to improve our gaming at the table. (I'm still of two mind as to whether it helps at the table in any other way but by helping us design our own home brew systems off of games we half-like.)

    And I'd specifically suggest that while theory and actual play should be related, I'd like to see theory relate more strongly to critique. And critique seems to me to be a very appropriate place to find (and refine) field-specific jargon. Furthermore, I think critique that included jargon would tend to provide excellent context for newcomers to learn by example.

    The flipside to all of that is that I expect their to be a somewhat insular, even if welcoming, community devoted to theory. It's not for everyone. There's plenty of folks who can't stand it, and are better off not dealing with it at all. And it's not that anyone is a better person. Or even that theory is useless - it finds its way to the light of day in games.

    But we can have a culture of theory, marked by its use of jargon, without excluding outsiders from a culture of gaming. By not using jargon when it's out of place: namely, when we're talking about play and not about theory.

The opposition to jargon outright is, of course, problematic. But the effort to keep jargon at a minimum is not.
