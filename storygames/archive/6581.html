\n I got a chance to play a session of Keep on the Shadowfell, which is the D&D 4th edition preview/quick-start module this weekend. I don't have a strong history with D&D. I've played third edition twice, so it was all pretty new to me and I ended up having a great time. Here are some observations:

1. Characters are fun. All of them. The puny halfling rogue kicked about 50% of all ass in our party. Everybody has powers they can call on to do fun things every round. There are choices to be made and the miniatures/combat aspect was pretty solid and engaging.

2. Complaints about first level guys being utter bad-asses are a little off the mark. I played the paladin, and my special powers were fun but not hugely unbalancing. My favorite power - I could choose to gain a bonus based on the number of enemy dudes adjacent to me, encouraging me to wade in there and slay. One easily-tweaked aspect that rang false to me - it seems almost impossible for a PC to die.

3. It seemed pretty streamlined. There are minion rules - one hit and they die; they do a set amount of damage, you can mow them down as though you were actually honoring fantasy tropes. The dwarven fighter could kill these guys even when he missed. Everything points at fun, which I definitely appreciated.

4. It seems like the non-combat stuff waseither deliberately hand-waved for the introductory module or is the same-old same-old. I hope the full rules allow for some fun and nuanced interaction akin to the combat stuff, but it wasn't there for the Shadowfell. My guys has +10 Intimidation though! W00t!

5. I'm a hopeless story gamer I guess - I looked at my dragon-born paladin fighting little dragon-y kobolds and thought "what the hell? What if these "brigands" are really the oppressed ones here?" I decided my guy was a xenophobic racist who couldn't tell "soft people" apart - dwarf, elf, human, all the same to me. It turned out they really were bad guys, though - who would have thought it?

6. So much math! Damn, +7 attack and a +2 bonus from the cleric's last thing, +1 because of this, then you roll a d20, man, I'm not used to it. It's just adding modifiers and stuff, but I'm not used to it, and there's a nontrivial state tracking component, too. Also, having a rigid initiative order and lots of guys with lots of options really slows things down.

I'll let my fellow delvers out themselves if they want to, but I'd love to hear your reactions as well.

    Posted By: Jason Morningstar4. It seems like the non-combat stuff waseither deliberately hand-waved for the introductory module or is the same-old same-old. I hope the full rules allow for some fun and nuanced interaction akin to the combat stuff, but it wasn't there for the Shadowfell. My guys has +10 Intimidation though! W00t!
    \nTrue. The scenario itself got a little tiring at the end, as one encounter strung into the next and the next, with no downtime in between to roleplay. But I totally buy off on the fact that this is because it is an INTRODUCTORY ADVENTURE. "Raid the Fucking Shadowfell", not "Go Talk to Some Elves about Loneliness and Ennui". The first thing that 4e-ers will want to do is get under the hood and test out that combat. I figure after about the first 1/3 of the adventure, you'll hit a stopping point:
    * At this point, you know all the rules you need to play.
    * You've got a handle on the tactics involved, and can now work on new combos and stuff.
    * Got that under your belt for future encounters? Cool, NOW go and roleplay.

    What happened in our group was, as Jason mentioned, we roleplayed in the tavern (there's always a tavern), we talked in character about what to do about these reptilian fiends, etc. We squeezed it in, and it totally worked. We even roleplaying walking into two combat pieces, basically negotiating with some draconians to rethink worshipping an evil god (Nope, but at least we reconfirmed out own intentions to burn them down), and investigating their lair pretending to be fellow worshippers... for half a second before we busted out the murder.

    In reflection, I totally have the idea in my mind that combat and roleplaying can totally happen side-by-side, and even started building a campaign world and scenarios in my head where you can bust down both with no issue.

    To go back to 2e, which I played for years: The rules? Kinda shitty. The core book doesn't even tell you how to properly roll to attack. Yet, there's a bare minimum of tactical crunch. So our games, which were primarily 75% roleplaying... we could get it on, and not be too distracted when combat happened.

    My problem with 3e was the fact that I always felt like I had to plan for my 12th level feats during 1st level character creation (career pathing). It was distracting. There were more role-play-ee elements to the core classes of 3e, but the combat rules (3.5 especially) were extremely distracting: It felt like I was being pulled in two directions. I want to roleplay some awesome scenes, and yet I'm also concerned with improving my attack shit as high as possible by linking feats and abilities. Attacks of Opportunity, and in Conan 5-foot-steps and armor damage reduction basically made it very hard for us to "stay off the battlemat".

    Now? Fuck it. The battlemat comes out. When it's combat time, it's motherfucking combat time. The combat is tactically sound, if a little weird at times (Cleric ability: "I hit my enemy so hard that my friend heals himself!"). I'm embracing the battle mat/minis (lego men, etc). The game feels like a much more complex version of Memoir 44.

    However, the game setting... it's gonna have to be a little gonzo'ed up. I can't see myself playing puny 3-hp warriors in a non-magical kingdom that run away at the sight of kobolds. Your Homebrew Setting will probably have to have lots of common magic, powerful heroes, crazy-ass villains/monsters, etc. I can see how Forgotten Realms might get converted, as well as my faves Dark Sun or Planescape, but something like Krynn or Living Arcanis (fantasy Rome) could probably not make the leap.

    But now, instead of playing something like Mongoose Conan and losing interest quickly because a Barbarian in armor is un-fucking-defeatable (without presenting a challenge that will kill all the other characters in 2 seconds flat), and having this schizophrenic "we're roleplaying, don't bring out the mat"/"we need a mat to utilize the rules of 3.5 and get the most out of the game" experience, I've come around to be totally cool with solid-ass roleplaying, and when it's time to bust ass, we bust ass proper.

    It felt good. This intro scenario is probably not the best "intro to roleplaying" in general, but it does a great job of introducing old D&D hands to the combat system, presenting it in waves (instead of all at once) like going through the scenario book of Memoir 44 or Battlelore.

    I ran this game, and I have to say: way fun. It's simpler than 3.5 for the GM by a long shot, and the monsters get to have way better tactics. Kobolds were actually kind of scary: they can dance around and avoid people like no one's business. (They get to take a 1-square step for free each turn, basically, which avoids attacks of opportunity and lets them be crazy annoying.) They also get bonuses for ganging up, which meant Jason's paladin and them both grew more powerful as they mobbed him.

    \n

    As for the roleplaying and other non-combat stuff, I bet we're going to see this turn into more of a Tunnels and Trolls-like thing. The skill list is pared down, and attribute rolls are back in as an emphasized thing as far as I can tell. This should let you pull off stunts easily, as the GM can just call for two contested attribute rolls for just about anything. I'm waiting to get the PHB before I am sure this is awesome, but I think it may be. I'm guessing the feel of this part will be more like 2E: that is, the amount of roleplaying is dependent on what you bring to the game and what you want out of it. I think we'll have better rules for it - and I know there's now "skill challenges," which are non-combat encounters - but I think the emphasis will vary from group to group.

    \n

    All the things Jason and Andy mentioned as awesome are awesome for the GM, too. Mook rules? Now I get to slam down 10 kobolds on a map and not think twice about it. They will mob the crap out of you - for a round or two, and then they get wiped out. It's incredibly satisfied. I also think character death may happen more than you think: the next encounter in the adventure will get nasty.

    \n I imagine that play without a battle mat would nerf a lot of character abilities. Certainly a lot of kobold abilities.

    The kobolds, for example, get to "shift" (one 5-foot square step) as a minor action instead of a move action. Some of the kobolds got to shift as a free action when threatened in certain circumstances. Those shifting abilities would be very hard to adjudicate without a map.

    Also, area of effect spells seemed to be pretty important. The various blasts and bursts really need a map to understand who is standing next to whom, and if there are PCs in the blast area, as well.

    You could play without a battle mat, but you'd be giving someone an advantage most of the time. Probably the players.

    I used cardboard counters instead of miniatures, and used the printed battle mats that came with the adventure. For something of my own, I'd use easel-sized graph paper with a one-inch grid on it and just draw out the map as necessary. It's not a large investment to play that way, compared to the miniatures route.

    And you don't need a mat with a grid on it. You can always just use a ruler and inches, like in the Old Days, ya know?

    \n In terms of why the map is needed, a great deal of the core powers and secondary power effects affect movement, range, and area.

    I use X power or X power is triggered based on my status or position which could cause any of the following to happen as a primary or secondary effect:

    - my character moves Y squares in Z pattern
    - my allies move moves Y squares in Z pattern
    - my enemies moves Y squares in Z pattern
    - my allies in Y range gain an effect (status, bonus, penalty, damage, healing)
    - my enemies in Y range gain an effect (status, bonus, penalty, damage, healing)
    - characters adjacent or in Z range from my allies in Y range gain an effect (status, bonus, penalty, damage, healing)
    characters adjacent or in Z range from my enemies in Y range gain an effect (status, bonus, penalty, damage, healing)
    - and so on!

    Imagine playing Battle Lore or Memoir 44 without a map!

    4E can also be run GMless or the GM can be the story guide or source of adversity while someone else acts as the rules arbiter. The map helps keep everyone on the same page in these distributed authority situations.

      \n I'll provide a few data points from my group (*** LIGHT SPOILERS ***):

      \n- We played one session, starting at about 5PM on Sunday the 25th and lasting until about 12:20AM, with a 30 minute intermission. So that was a roughly 6.5 hour gaming session. We had five players plus a DM, with every character but the Tiefling Warlord represented.
      \n

      \n
      \n- I wasn't too pleased that the social and exploration encounter rules are not represented in the module. I wasn't pleased with the fact that there were so many combat encounters littering the beginning of the module.
      \n
      \n- Overall play speed seemed good. I feel like with a little practice the game will run faster than 3.5 core.
      \n
      \n- There was a lot of good rock-paper-scissors action, like the fighter trumping dragonshields, the rogue dealing the megadamage (eek! but not Palladium megadamage...), the wizard rocking minions pretty savagely, etc.
      \n
      \n- We got through the first encounter ("road"), the second encounter ("ambush on road"), the third encounter ("fight outside the lair"), and the fourth encounter ("fight inside the lair"). The first fight was easy for the party, the second was tougher, the third was easy, the fourth was very nearly a TPK.
      \n
      \n- I was excessively happy about the fact that none of the characters seemed to be a weak link whatsoever. Everyone had a role to play and interestingly, the players all more or less figured out their roles just from reading their abilities. So I think that was some elegant design work there.

      2) Resource management. With healing surges, per encounter power, and the like, the focus of 4e has become the encounter, and there resource management is a much less important element.       3) Removal of risk. There's a lot of extended discussion that went into this but I'll boil down my attitude: threat of loss helps build emotional tension in the game. 4e's design philosophy seems to go out of its way to sanitize and normalize threats in the game, which IMO takes much of the sense of wonder and dread out of the game.

      Fortunately, Clark Peterson seems to agree with my philosophy on #1 and #3, and it seems very likely that Necromancer Games' products forthcoming for 4e will address some of these tastes, which will be useful if I find myself in a 4e game.

      Posted By: HituroDoes 4th Ed have rules and / or intentions that would encourage people to be roleplaying as opposed to just tactically fighting?
      \nThis smells like a new topic altogether.

      But if you buy into the "fruitful void" idea of design, then the answer is "fuck yes".

      Basically, the game is broken out into Combat stuff (all the rules) and Everything Else. Everything Else is very loosely defined. That's where the roleplaying happens. And since there are not any real deterministic rules (AFAICT) on how roleplaying affects the game, then that means the roleplaying should be able to take the story in any direction. The game tells you to roleplay. It tells you that that is the point of the game, which means that it's part of the game. But instead of making you roll dice when playing your role, it gives you a virtually rule-free sandbox to do it in. Complete freedom on anything non-combat-oriented.

      Further discussion (if you want) is probably best taken to a new topic, mind.

      Posted By: Callan S.Mark, you mean you decide on a XP budget for the dungeon rather than calculate, don't you?
      \nSorta. Here's a bit from the 4e DMG, p. 56:\n
      Step-by-Step encounters
      1. choose an encounter level. Encounter level is relative to the number of characters in the party. An easy encounter is one or two levels lower than the party\u2019s level. A standard encounter is of the party\u2019s level, or one level higher. A hard encounter is two to four levels higher than the party\u2019s level.
      2. Determine your Xp budget. multiply the number of characters in the party by the XP value of a monster of the encounter\u2019s level.
      3. Spend your Xp budget. You don\u2019t have to spend the exact amount. But if you go too high, the encounter level might increase, and if you don\u2019t spend the exact amount, you\u2019ll end up with a lower level encounter.
      \nThe DMG also talks about the total encounters you'd have for a given adventure, and Mearls has pointed out that you can basically total up the XP for the dungeon, and then just start buying monster to sprinkle throughout, with an eye on the ELs for each individual encounter.
