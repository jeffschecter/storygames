\n Did you guys know there is a Fighting Fantasy tabletop game? They called it an introductory roleplaying game. It uses pretty much the same mechanics from the books. I haven't played it, but I'm itching to run a short campaign using it.

There's also Advanced Fighting Fantasy TTRPG, which adds a bit more crunch, but it's still super simple.

There are a couple of guys who are trying to update Advanced Fighting Fantasy and turn it into a Fantasy Heartbreaker (the uninformed design bit, not the printing 5000 copies and having them rot in your garage bit). They're adding stats and crap like that...something you can't teach in 5 minutes. I'm choosing to ignore them.

Anyone here ever played it? Interested?

    \n The Dungeoneer game is unfortunately completely mangled by the Skill stat mechanics. The number of skill points you start the game with is determined by your Skill Stat. And how good you are at these skills? Add your skill level to your Skill stat. So having a Skill of 12 gives you 12 skill points, each added to your base of 12, whereas the guy with a skill of 7 gets 7 skill points, and if he spends them all on one skill his total is still only 2 points higher than someone who started with 12 skill and didn't put any points into that skill.

    I played it quite a bit, but we house-ruled chargen so the number of skill points you get is inversely proportional to the starting Skill stat - someone with 12 Skill gets 4 skill points, and for every point of Skill you have under 12, you get an additional 2 skill points. (This is from memory, the exact ratio we used was probably different)

    The game became the inspiration for the game I wrote for a 48 hour RPG challenge and posted on my blog - Adventures in the New Kingdoms.

    I also posted a sample character for Dungeoneer (the Fighting Fantasy RPG) - here.

    Dungeoneer was great for pick-up games in the D&D-like fantasy genre, but has been replaced in my repertoire for one-shots when players don't show up for a regular game by the 1PG fantasy game BroadSword.

    \n My brother ran me through the original FF game book when it first came out in '84 or '85. I still remember going down the well and mapping out the dungeon from there. I had a lot of fun with it as a 10 year old, and your thread brought up good memories of the system, but reading this thread did bring up many legitimate complaints about the system.

    I also played in the Riddling Reaver, when I was 14 or 15, and still had fun with it. Admittedly, I had very limited experience with a lot of RPGs at that point.

    I don't see the three stats as being a huge barrier to having a fun campaign of it, since there are so many awesome games out there that have as many (if not fewer) stats present in the game. One or two more wouldn't mess up the simpleness IMO.

    I still have all my books, and am now tempted to pull it out for my group as a one shot, but highly doubt there would be any interest in it.

    \n IIRC, the strength of Dungeoneer was in the background and setting. Which, given the number of kitchen-sink generic fantasy games around these days, ought to be easy to replicate. The nearest system/setting to it at the time was probably WHFRP, since it was a pretty low-fantasy mud and peasants setting. Titan (setting book) and Out of the Pit (bestiary) are probably the best sources to get, as well as Blacksand! (which purported to be a supplement but was really a dirty pirates-and-thieves port city setting book).
    \n
    \nAnd, for the record, Out of the Pit is a BRILLIANT monster manual, despite everything being defined by two pretty meaningless traits... a real triumph of fluff-over-crunch.

    \n Just to clarify, there is a little cross talk going on here:

    Dungeoneer, an elaboration of the system from the Fighting Fantasy game books into a full blown RPG: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dungeoneer

    ...is not he same thing as...

    Dungeoneer, a dungeon crawl card game created by Thomas Denmark, and published by Atlas Games: http://www.atlas-games.com/dungeoneer/

    ...which Thomas is independently elaborating into a full blown RPG, called Dungeoneer.
