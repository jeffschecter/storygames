\n By "affected your gaming", I mean:
* At the table, in character (or as the GM): do you find yourself pulling in elements from your job into your game?
* As a player, how does your job ("wage lifestyle") affect your gaming ("leisure lifestyle")?

So, I'm a senior engineer at a company that creates storage computers. I help when problems arise in client environments for whatever reason, even when (in 80% of the cases) there's actually a problem with some other piece of the environment or other computer/router/network that "isn't us". I do all that Linux, Windows, networking, NAS and SAN stuff, focusing on computer performance.

* It really doesn't affect my gaming style at all, except for two specific situations:
-- I need to describe a business environment or something in a game (as a GM or player): I'll often imitate mannerisms of some co-workers in the game to flesh out NPCs in such a business environment (happened in "Workplace Solutions" and a PTA game)
-- More commonly, any game involving cyberpunks or computer hacking, from modern PTA to Cyberpunk and Shadowrun: As a player or GM I always embellish on little side-details involved in a hacking experience that make for pure color and immersion. This really got me into a long-running Shadowrun campaign we were playing a few years ago, combining modern computer techniques and extrapolating them into "Teh Futur".
For example, my orc suited fixer/hacker wouldn't "hack their system for that file I need", I'd "get a look at the hardware they have, then do an op-net search for the tech reports on it, have an AIBot scan forums and message groups for problem issues reported. OK, Alpha Dragon reports back that there were stability issues on some old motherboard firmware, and this box doesn't look updated. I sweep in using a basic user password liberated through a spycam, run some options on the hardware that causes it to crash due to a bug in that firmware. Now we just wait for the admin to log in to see what happened... ... ...and ... ...ah, there he is. I grabbed his password in a v6 packet trace, so I reboot the hardware, jump in with his credentials, force a dump of the data I need to the other user's account disguised as a restorative measure... done. Log out, log back in as the first user, and push it over the net to my remote biscuit-sized server I have installed in the basement of the Harris Teeter data center. And hell, good because I'm feeling nice I'll upgrade their motherboard firmware for them, defrag their raid array and discreetly log out."

Admittedly, it's useless when playing fantasy or any game that doesn't feature computers or hacking, but when it happens it can be a nice embellishment on an otherwise boring series of skill rolls or result explanations.

* As a player, my job both does and does not affect my gaming. I don't (often) have to stay late, I hold a normal first-shift job, so when gaming is scheduled (Wednesdays, Thursdays, and occasional weekends) I can throw down knowing that I'm not gonna get a call or page at some bizarre time. I've actually gone as far as to avoid job paths (even slightly more lucrative ones) that lead to being on a 24-hour call or the like.
However, work has affected my tabletop gaming in a few ways:
--When I'm at work, I'm busy. Period. So I don't often have time to blog, write campaign notes, even post on this forum in any major way during work hours.
--And when 10 hours of work in front of a computer over, the last thing I often want to do is spend more time in front of a computer.
--This has basically savaged my previous ability to sketch out scenario or campaign notes during a free moment at work, design or talk about designing RPGs, and that sort of thing. I tend to favor games that I can run without doing a lot of research or "campaign notes/handouts" and the like these days.

How about you?

    \n I'm a writer. I've written novels in the past (and now wish I could go back and re-write them, cause I know a hell of a lot more than I used to know). But my focus now is dramatic narrative -- film and television or Internet.

    I tend to bring everything I know about storytelling to my RPGs. I see RPGs as a little laboratory to practice storytelling techniques and craft, and the fun of storytelling, in a non-pressure environment. When I'm stuck on a project, I often work up settings or think about how I'd construct situations in a game of Hero Wars or Sorcerer. I suppose it'd be a little like a baseball player going off to play golf. They're both sports, but the brain is processing the activities differently.

    I pull in everything I know from being a writer when GMing or Playing. It's not a 1:1 correlation, of course. But there are enough direct connections between story-telling techniques across different media that it's easy to re-apply knowledge. Kicker and Bangs are the first generation children of techniques from TV and Film, for example. When I create a character as a Player, I dive right into the conflicts and personal connections with wild abandon -- I want more complications for my character and shovel story opportunity to the GM. As a GM I keep the focus this way: "The camera stays on the Player Characters because they're the leads. Sure, there's a world around them, but the world around them only matters to the degree that it reveals who the characters are." Which is how it works in a movie or TV series. Sure, that's not how reality works. But then a still life of oranges isn't really about real oranges. And that's my point: my craft of being a writer is what I bring to the table as a player of RPGs.

    Most importantly, I think, I trust myself and other people to be creative and have a natural instinct for storytelling. Jesse Burneko once said to me, "You have this amazing ability to trust people will do interesting things!" And I replied, "Because they do!"

    As a GM I really try to create a place for people to trust their ideas and the things they care about will be honored. I often move at a slower pace than I used to as a GM, no longer trying to be The Entertainer, and no longer trying to hammer the Players with pacing that causes stress or panic in their choices for the sake of "immersion." Instead, I'll slow things down and ask questions: "So, how is your character doing that?" Or, "Do you need a moment to think about what you want your character to do? We can cut to Colin if you need a moment." Or, "Okay, before we go on, what does your guy think about his sister? What's the relationship been like?"

    I play RPGs as essentially a creative act. I look at the rule, techniques and procedures I used as the focusing tools for that act of creativity. They are the limited pallet and brush we've chosen to play with, and by using these specific tools we then use them in a new combination to create this play this night.

    I bring all this up because this comes very much from my job. My job is not to freak out when I am staring at a blank screen. My job is to mix craft with trust in the whateverthefuck the Muses are. I engage my fellow players and ask for more details -- I love details -- quick specific details that let me catch a glimpse of an image in my mind.

    Oh. And my job is to communicate -- and to communicate clearly. To share.

    Writing in the business I'm in isn't just sitting around Writing. Hollywood is a social place. You have meeting with executives. You have meetings with producers. You go into an office, spread your mat, put out the bowl for coins and tell stories. They either put coins in the bowl or not.

    I see RPGs as a very socially creative activity. As I GM I ask people to share details. As a Player I ask people to share details. I want to know what that cool image in their head was. I want to know what that gesture that I saw them think about that their character doing but that they didn't say out loud. I'll ask, "Hey, wait, I know your guy just said goodbye to her, but what happened. What was that like." Not all the time. Of course. Some moments are just incident to skip over. But the act of telling a story is the accumulation of details that hook us in to one level or another. When I'm at a table, I want us all sharing those details that matter to each of us.


    As for how my job affects my game time, it's getting tougher, honestly. The work of years is beginning to pay off now and I need more time to focus on several projects at one time.

    Moreover, I've learned, finally, that to get done what you want takes a lot of focused energy. I said to a friend a few months ago, "I had no idea how much effort it took just to stand still in Hollywood."

    The truth is, RPGs were the arena I played in for years because that's the arena I could play in. Now I'm playing in a different arenas (writing a feature film assignment; just sold a pitch for a TV pilot; setting up a feature to write and direct in 2011; setting up a mini-series to write about the history of Saudi Arabia...)

    Part of me isn't sure exactly how much time is left for RPGs, since what I got from them is being answered by my professional ambitions coming true. On the other hand, I feel a debt to RPGs. Through both positive and negative example, I know I'm becoming a writer people trust because of things I learned at the gaming table or struggled with once I stepped away from the gaming table. What I wanted from RPGs I've gotten, and now I'm getting it at work too. I'm curious to see what gaming will mean for me this coming year.

      -GM prep is only useful if it gives the players something interesting with which to interact; ditto for teacher prep.

      My RL-area of knowledge that I use most often with my characters, however, is music. I've often played bards or other musician types and have been able to fold references to actual music or musical principles into my play.
