    \n@Bedrockbrendan has already mentioned one of the more traditionally-structured games, now let me drop one on you that you might not have run into.

    Wushu.

    Now, you might say, "wushu" is a martial arts term. And you're right! But the RPG is a lot more broad than that, covering Pretty much anything where the action is assured, the stunts awesome, and the system is about whether you have an impact rather than whether the cool thing you just described actually happens. Of course it does.

    You want to pick up a ladder, swing it wildly, knock down six guys, then have two of them bum rush you against the wall? Done.

    You want to blow a hole in the floor to get the drop on the guys below you, spinning in mid air to plug five guys between the eyes, in the heart, or in the junk, before you land in a three point stance? Done.

    It's that kind of game.

    I might also suggest Capes if you want to experiment with being GM-less, but I always suggest Capes. It's fantastic for putting the attention and focus right on the conflicts that matter to the characters on screen, and those characters might not be the "protagonists" at all. It's pretty all 'round awesome and its a shame it doesn't get more attention.

    \nFeng Shui 2 is coming out soon. Die Hard, Hardboiled, any Sy or Arnold movie, Crouching Tiger; Hidden Dragon, Jackie Chan ... you name it.

    If Wushu is on the table, so is Cthulhu Dark. Just change "sanity" to "chi" and you got a system for hong kong action in a similar grade as Wushu, meaning it's a game that throws everything into the participants laps to simulate action movies. It works for a couple of sessions but then the imagination usually runs out (and I played a lot of player descriptive heavy games these last ten years so I know what works well and what to avoid).

    \n The interesting thing about Hong Kong Action Theater is you play Hong Kong Action Movie star. It is a very unusual concept (so much so I've had trouble selling it to my players in the past). But you can skip that aspect and play it straight. What most impressed me about HKAT (particularly when you consider the time it was written) is it really seemed to be the game that understood the subject matter, and in a way that wasn't poking fun at it. For the movie list/description alone the book is worth it (I had the second edition so can't comment on the first). Skarka definitely has a handle on Hong Kong Martial arts and action films. Absolutely had a huge impact on my viewing and how I approach martial arts in game systems.

    \n Oh man, I've had a ton of fun with Wushu, and wrote up my own little hack for Star Wars:

    http://schirduans.com/david/my-creations/jedi-wushu

    Wushu works well, but requires a small group familiar with kung-fu fight scenes. Also, as the GM, it can be exhausting. I can run 6 hours of Dungeon World no problem, but after about an hour of Wushu, I'm mentally wiped.

    It's fast and fun. But honestly...the mechanics don't really matter. They give us the incentive to keep playing, and a way to measure success. But after a while, we forget about the dice and just keep going back and for with fun fight scenes. And that's where the magic of Wushu shines.

    Wushu works well, but requires a small group familiar with kung-fu fight scenes.
    \nThey only really need to be familiar with the tropes of the kind of story that you're creating together, and honestly if you don't have your group on the same page as regards the tropes of the style of game you're playing, you're going to have way bigger problems than your choice of mechanics down the road.

    One of the best games of Wushu that I ever had the pleasure of running was for a completely random pickup group at the local game store RPG night. We weren't playing kung fu action \u2013 I actually picked the game up from a very, very traditional dungeon crawl in the D&D style, then changed systems in mid-game to Wushu. It was fantastic. Everyone was already invested in the tropes and understood the tropes of the kind of story that was going on, so when I turn them loose to actually make things happen that they expected to be able to happen \u2013 it was insane. People were having some of the best times of their lives, and I didn't actually mind that I was running a GM-full game.

    That the game is now free just puts the capper on an already awesome choice.
