\n One thing L&F, for all its greatness, doesn't quite capture the feel of, IME, is the silly technobabble of Star Trek, especially during ship to ship combat. So I was thinking, what if for a big setpiece encounter at the end of a session, you had everyone pull out their phones and play Spaceteam? Or, alternately, there's a card game version of it, that only takes like 5 minutes to play. You could then determine the in-game effect based on how well the group did at Spaceteam.

In short: Spaceteam!!!

    \n It's this weird game where you all get on your smartphones at the same time, and you each have a "console" full of strangely-named controls in front of you. Instructions come up on the screen, such as "turn off the phase inverters." The problem is, most of the instructions won't be related to anything on *your* screen. So the whole thing ends up with everyone shouting absurd Star Trek-esque technobabble at each other until their ship explodes. It's fucking great.

    \n There's also an analog, card-game version that plays similarly, and only takes about five minutes.

    I'm thinking that how far you get in the game could directly correlate to how much damage the ship takes in the battle. Like if you only make it to level 3, then you roll on a worse damage chart than if you make it to level 6. The GM could also set various battles to different levels.

    The real problem is it only plays two to four. I'd really want 6 PCs + GM for a Trek campaign game. You could split into two teams of three, I guess.

    But the cardgame plays up to 6... perfect. (I also think dealing with apps during an RPG, though fun to envision, could lead to a lot of problems in practice. "My wifi's not working!" etc.)

    \n When I ran "Star Trek: Voyager Done Right" I believe I had seven players at first, which sounds overwhelming but actually worked well; I generally had guest NPCs help me GM. So for instance there'd be a scene going on down on the planet's surface with an away team, which I'd GM, while the remaining PCs entertained a dignitary on board the ship, played by the guest. It was a bit chaotic, but reasonably fun most of the time.
