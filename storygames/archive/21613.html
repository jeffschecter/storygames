    \n I'm a big fan of Whitehack; its classes are more generic and open to interpretation than the traditional D&D archetypes, and (at least from my perspective) its big thing is encoding a level of improvisation in character abilities, which for me is a big part of OSR play alongside the more tactical kind of problem-solving. It may be a bit problematic if you want "no training wheels, tossed into the deep-end" style OSR play because of how much improvisation it encodes, but even if you don't play it I think it's a fantastic example of a rules text built to illustrate a concept.

    Not a game itself, but even if you believe you have a handle on what OSR-style play entails, I would recommend checking out A Quick Primer for Old School Gaming (which is available as a free pdf). For all that it strawmans trad gaming, I think it does a good job of doing what it says on the can, being an introduction to the core ideas of OSR play, particularly for people who are used to the norms of heavily rules-mediated play.

    Krippler said:

    The systems are pretty much all the same, if you want to spend money do it on good modules. Stonehell and Barrowmaze are very good and it's easy to cut out parts if you don't play the dungeon as a whole.

    \nAgreed, the rules-set thing is very much a red herring; only moderately experienced GMs in the style will benefit from extensive cross-comparison between texts. The texts for this style of game simply don't delve into the methodological issues that might justify caring about one text over another in the way you'd do with some other types of rpgs. For the occasional one-shot or a campaign starter you're best served by taking any one single one of these texts happens to please you the most.

    For the purposes of having a campaign-starter text (a chassis that will be developed further through play) I would recommend any one of the following. Just one:
    Any iteration of TSR Basic D&D
    LotFP
    Swords & Wizardry
    Backswords & Bucklers
    Dungeon Crawl Classics
    Stars Without Number

    For the purposes of studying clearly non-D&D old school texts (perhaps you're bored with the mechanical cornerstones of D&D or something), I recommend the following:
    Mazes & Minotaurs
    Tunnels & Trolls
    Traveller

    ... or one of the many titles on this list of retroclones and other retro games; the above picks are largely based on my arbitrary personal preferences and what I've happened to actually study. I know for a fact that there are like twenty rules glosses that I might use just as well as any of the above.

    It goes without saying that what you have is already more than sufficient. There are no OSR game texts that I know of that will make you any better at the game than your current selection does. It's simply not that kind of gaming scene.

    \n I think you have a solid base. We (our gaming group) starts new refs with the Moldvay version of B/X since it contains solid procedurals, a DM budget and story hooks, a solid focus on exploration (not adventure), and tells you how to play the game. The game also has a way to train new DM's using the Caller position. The Moldvay rules were restated recently as B/X Essentials. It's the only clone of B/X that actually gets Moldvay and I highly recommend it.

    Beyond the Wall is another great one. It's focused on a different kind of exploration \u2013 transitioning into and through being a young adult with all kinds of cool subsystems.

    Into the Odd is near modern occult-science with a light touch of class based socialist dystopia. There is Electric Bastionland which is a different version of Into the Odd.

    Lamentations of the Flame Princess is interesting but kinda lacks what do we do with this. The play culture is in the modules. That said if 15th-17th cent. human centered horror is your groups thing and you don't mind deciphering play through modules.

    I'd skip the majority of OSR rulesets since they lack the what do we do with this game and aren't doing much in the way of innovation or thoughtful reflecting on design.

    \n Yes! Agreed.

    And I think that is a big part of the typical OSR mindset, as well. For instance, if you go read an OSR blog, you'll tend to see things like "Look at the SuchandSuch Table from the 1977 edition; now look if we rewrite it with d6s, like in the Purple Version, which was published two ears later!" Very much a problems & tools approach, collecting random tables and little tricks for their own table.

    Very different from the "let me make a distinctive and unique set of procedures which become a new game, offering new creative insights, different from any other game out there" which we tend to see in story game land.

    \n I'm in an ongoing Beyond the Wall game and running a game of Godbound. Both use OSR-style ability checks but bolt a kind of freeform skill system onto it. In BtW, it's the skills that provide a bonus on ability checks when narratively applicable. In Godbound, it's that characters have a list of Facts that act as both their backstory and, when narrative applicable, provide a bonus to ability checks. They seem like remarkably similar approaches.

    But I've notice that my Godbound players are more likely to describe in fictional terms what they're aiming to do, see if I call for a check, and then say which Fact is relevant to give them a bonus. In the BtW group, a number of players are more likely to ask the DM for permission to roll a particular skill - before describing their character's actual attempt and even though you don't need a particular skill to attempt things in BtW (since skills just provide a bonus on the relevant ability check, which you could always make anyway). My working theory is that the difference in how each is express - that is, as part of a narrative about the character v. as specifically named discrete skill, impacts how players think about and approach them. If I'm right, BtW's way of expressing skills is subtly pushing players to think more in modern D&D-terms than in OSR terms
