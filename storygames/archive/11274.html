\n So after reading the original Dune in the late 90s, I was all over the thought of using poisons in a fantasy game. I mean, why wouldn't people use them? They're quick, deadly, and hella effective.

Many games (I'm thinking Forgotten Realms) wussed out by saying "only evil clerics and assassins use poison", but at the same time it was a clever patch to a problematic predicament with classic TRPGs:

* If PCs can use poisons, then the GM will certainly use them against the PCs as well
* Most folks control one character (often for weeks, months, even years, building them up over time)
* Most poisons consist of "one successful hit, and you're dead. Even if it takes a few minutes to kick in, you kiss your ass goodbye".
* A + B + C = Funkill

Also, something can be said for the sheer excitement of tooth-and-nail gladitorial blow-by-blow combat, vs "I STAB ONCE AND YOU DIE", which is a classic staple of RPGs.

Is there a way to implement poisons in your favorite game which is unique, or somehow breaks the above cycle?

One thought, for 3e/4e: If the players use poisons, It reduces the HP of all enemies by 75%. The remaining HP indicate "stress, maneuvering, close calls, near misses", and the last HP which drop the boss to 0 (or -10) is the actual killing blow that hits the monster and delivers the poison.

After that, the GM takes a "poison token". At some future encounter, the GM gets to do the same to the players, as the baddies use poison (and remove the standard "save vs poison" poison rules for these encounters).

Anyway, just wondering what other people thought. Pretty much anyone who has read Dune, Hamlet, Blade of the Immortal, etc nods vigorously at the thought of poisoning weapons (it just makes sense), but would be hard-pressed to open up the option in an adventure game which features PCs you own for more than one session...

    \n How about the realistic angle? In the real world you'd be hard-pressed to find poisons that are practical for the sort of environments we get in fantasy games, unless we assume that wizards can manufacture nerve toxins or something like that. The common medieval-type poison is really only usable mixed in with food or drink, and even then it's hit and miss how long the poison takes to work, if it works at all. I've never found it problematic to just decide that being able to make an useful poisonous blade is similar to a magical ability - if I can handle wizards capable of enchanting a sword, why couldn't I handle a poisonous one?

    That said, the standard conflict resolution approach that I've been using in TSoY works pretty well, no matter what sort of poison: if the poison is deadly, you just need to make sure that the delivery of the poison is the real contest in the situation. If that is properly proceduralized, then a deadly poison is no more problematic than a deadly sword. Both kill you dead, so the rules system just has to deal with both with an equal gravity.

    \n My 3e and 3.5e experience was more along the lines of "Sure, go ahead and use poison, but the only ones with a save DC high enough to work on the guys you actually want to use poison on are stupidly expensive." Except for monsters poisoning us; there, it was more along the lines of "Okay, you'd better rack a fuckload of Neutralize Poisons today, or we're going to get our asses handed to us." Which pretty much meant that while we basically never used poisons ourselves, we had 'em used on us all the damn time.

    Although to be perfectly honest, the most problematic thing in tabletop play in nearly any system you look at is knocking people out. Inevitably we'll want to do the perfect movie takedown -- one solid blow to the head, dude falls down -- but can't make it happen within the ruleset. It's a real bummer.

    \n It just takes more simulation:

    Player: "Roll for initiative ... OK, my body wins. First my liver's going to break down the poison molecules. Roll my liver's lysis score against the poison's molecular geometry \u2014 dang, it got through."
    GM: "The poison's using its Neurotoxicity trait. [Rolls] Critical hit! Zagmid's on the floor in convulsions!"
    Player 2: "I pry Zagmid's mouth open and pour antidote in."
    Player: "My cerebellum is casting Neuroplasticity to reverse the nerve damage."
    GM: "That's five HP restored, but only to your peripheral nerves. Unfortunately your heart's stopped."

    And so forth. I'm surprised no system's gone to this level of detail; you could make stepping on a rusty nail into an exciting internal melee that could play for hours.

    Posted By: TristanClassic D&D geeky question. Does poison use make your character "evil"?
    It depends on what your references are. The Book of Exalted Deeds highlights the neigh-incoherence of the 3.X approach to poisons: it both defines poisons as evil, except maybe the ones that the drow use, and introduces new "poisons that aren't called poisons but act just like poisons" for good characters to use. It is very weird. Ultimately, whether or not a hunter is morally reprobate for rubbing his arrows on frogs before shooting at a monkey is a straight GM call, since the attempted rules on the subject are so bad.

    That said, only non-good classes get the "poison use" class feature, which allows them to wield poisoned weapons with no chance of hurting themselves. Then again, druids get poison immunity, so a good-aligned druid could conceivably transform into a giant porcupine, roll around in snake venom, and hurl themselves at enemies. So yeah.

    \n The D&D 4E methods sound pretty cool. All I got to add, as a data point, is the Descent method: Each wound of Poison does damage AND gives you a Poison Token. You must heal Poison Tokens before you can heal damage to Health.

    Example
    - A PC with 12 Health is hit for 5 poisonous wounds.
    - The PC's Health is reduced to 7, and its player receives 5 Poison Tokens.
    = To get to full Health, the PC must heal 10 damage (5 to remove the Poison Tokens + 5 to increase Health back to a full 12 value).

    In essence, Poison is "double damage" BUT the second multiple of damage doesn't lead to Unconsciousness or Death conditions; it just soaks up healing effort (be that potions, spells, normal rest, whatever).
