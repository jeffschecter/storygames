Well, I think that in a archetypical D&D dungeon crawl, the players described actions are extremely important. The players completely control the pacing, for example. Further, their choices are critical to the outcome, and can mean doom or victory for the party.

I think partly this may be a clash of terms. I use narration in the literary sense of described action. So, for example, within a book if there appear the words, "And then Sir Tirnal drew his sword and tried to open the door" -- that is narration, aka narrated action. So even a pure miniatures game has narration insofar as people use words to say what is happening (as opposed to moving pieces on the board).

Now, the narration in D&D is highly constrained, I agree. It is far more constrained than in [u]Once Upon A Time[/u], for example. But narration can be highly constrained and yet still meaningful. However, I think I agree with Shreyas that the constrained narration of a dungeon crawl isn't necessarily important in learning less-constrained narrational games like OUAT.

    [Edited to note the cross-post with Adam, who seems to be getting it.]

    John, saying that going left or right is meaningful in such a game, doesn't make the description of the sword meaningful. What Shreyas is saying is that, yes, a player can dictate decisions about play that are meaningful in the sense that the game is, effectively, a miniatures game. That includes things like whether to go left or right - where you move your mini. It doesn't include any description of any other element of the game that's not related to the game as a miniatures game.

    You could say that choosing to buy Park Place in Monopoly is narration. But it seems to lack that explorative element that we assign to RPGs. "Narration" as colorful description of the in-game events. It's really about giving power to things like plot and color. Can the player affect the plot? Can his colorful description have a mechanical effect? If not, then he'll ignore those things.

    And this seems to match most, say, D&D play that one sees. Instead of saying, "I bring my sword down in a circular loping chop, intended to take his head off" the player will tend to say, "I attack. Rolled a 18. I hit. 9 Damage."

    That is, because there's no mechanical empowerment to create in-game events other than by use of these sorts of miniatures mechanics (including moving your character), you tend to get lackluster narration. When you have empowerment, you get more compelling narration.

    Or at least I think that's his theory.

    John, my impression is that we're talking about the difference between description and direction.

    What you're talking about is "direction"; "The king approves," "I choose the door on the left," or "I aim for its head."

    What Mike and Shreyas are talking about is "description"; "My sword is Glambring, the foe-hammer," "I make sure that the sheriff sees the scalps on my saddlebags," or "We thank the spirit of the Great Bear for allowing us to use its skull as shelter."

    The latter involves a lot of color, detail, and context. The former is focused solely on dictating actions. In, e.g., D&D, the latter are all irrelevant 99% of the time, as there are no mechanical bits that give any of them inherent meaning. Any meaning needs to be added on by the players at the table, so if they're not actively working at this, there's little incentive to add those descriptive flourishes.

    I see this all the time in the multiple D&D campaigns I play. In one of my groups, a couple of us call it "dinner theater." It's something we endure, as it's never, never, ever relevant to what happens when dice are actually being rolled.
