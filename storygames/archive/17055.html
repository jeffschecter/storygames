    \n I'm speaking at pax dev this is a blurb i wrote about my workshop. "advanced tabletop prototyping"

    If you want to know all the secrets of this batman's utility belt check out Advanced Tabletop Prototyping at Pax Dev. Open to developers and students pax dev is the double secret conference that is dedicated to the modern art of making games. a press restricted event that keeps the focus on the work and offers an open environment for devs to share what they otherwise could not.
    I will describe my event in detail

    While I don\u2019t think much of my prototypes I consistently get complemented on them. While I have a few new gadgets and techniques to share I also wanted to share the role prototypes serve in my game development process. I have a very odd skill set for a game designer and that has forced me into using different methods.

    For starters. writing is an incredible challenge for me. I can do it and do it well occasionally but it requires a huge investment of effort and time. Much of what game designers do all day is write. I assume writing is how most game designers think. Not me though I prototype, I sketch and write notes. Often times by hand and quickly as possible.

    I use conceptual prototypes that are not quite playable but allow me to solve problems and iterate well before a playable prototype is created. Often times I have a raw hand made playable prototype well before I write a draft rulebook. Of course I make the standard development prototypes with rulebook drafts as a project becomes more promising. But the most impressive thing I put together are pitch prototypes and I will be covering the design of a pitch document in detail

    About the image. One card deck is factory produced, the other i made in my office. the decks are from brawl (by james ernist) and can be downloaded here. http://www.cheapass.com/freegames/brawl
