    \n It's not a community for "everyone" if you don't let everybody in, but on the other hand it might not be a fun community if you do. I see your dilemma.

    Exclude Jimmie Joan, of course. Specifically, were I Billy Sue, I'd explain to Jimmie Joan why it's not a good idea for them to hang. Note that adult people have no problems with something like this: it is completely routine for us to be accepting and agreeable about the fact that I am not the one who decides who you invite in your project, birthday party or whatever. This goes double for when there are goals and reasoning involved.

    Not being willing to do this smacks of cowardice to me, of being unwilling to accept that somebody somewhere might potentially not like you. Billy Sue needs to accept that either they're friends with Jimmie Joan despite not sharing this community, or they won't be friends because JJ can't accept being excluded. It's not Billy Sue's responsibility to be the guardian of Jimmie Joan's feelings.

    I say this with full understanding of the fact that I am, for many potential communities, Jimmie Joan On the other hand, I am often also Billy Sue, and I've learned to exclude ruthlessly (yet hopefully nicely) in direct proportion to the importance of my goals. For example: the more serious my ambitions are for a given roleplaying game, the more ruthless I am in choosing a player base who can actually execute those ambitions. Jimmie Joan gets to play with me when I'm playing something that suits them, or when I'm playing something casual where I don't care if it'll work; however, they are not welcome if their participation would derail the entire reason we had for getting together in the first place.

    But, here is where it gets tricky in my view. Assume I'm Billy Sue in @Rafu's first example. Open door, not only my place, etc. If J.J. shows up and says/does things that are sexist/racist/oppressive in some way, then the door isn't equally open for all people. It's open widest to people who aren't offended/affected by that, and less so for those who are. Further, by pushing those people away, this event/community/place/whatever you call it, is contributing, in at least a small way, to the oppression (via inequality/non-equity) of those people.
    \nTo me this seems like a category error in analyzing the issue, frankly. Being "open to everybody" and being functionally egalitarian are different things. Rafu is discussing the former, and it is a matter of principle, not an endless series of iterative responsibilities over the mental states of other people.

    It's like this: you're saying that being "open to everybody" necessarily has to include handicap-accessibility. I think that this is an unsupported reading of the idea of public, open space, considering the cultural history we have for the concept - of course we can define words however we want, but handicap-accessibility, topic-sensitive censorship and other similar active inclusiveness measures are not traditional parts of public space, at least in European tradition. They may be nice to have, but saying that your space is not open to everybody if it doesn't fulfill these new requirements is essentially raising the threshold for something being called "open".

    (In case it's unclear what is part of the cultural history of open, public spaces, my own understanding is that we essentially have to look at the shared norms of civilized behaviour, as encoded in tradition and law. From this we see that things like non-aggression, individual rights and freedom of speech are given elements of the rules for public forums, while various types of actively egalitarian measures are not. Whether for good or ill, the cultural tradition understands "openness" as "open to equal participation by the average baseline person, even if this might shut out people with special needs".)

    Of course Matthijs might have originally meant something more radically egalitarian and inclusive by this hypothetical open community, but that's besides the analytical point. I just wanted to show how Rafu's viewpoint about principles-first openness can be entirely sensible, and how it doesn't make a community less "open" if it interprets the ideal of openness in that way instead of Quincunx's way.
