\n Death is pretty much as interesting as anything gets; we all have it in store for us, it's the still center of a storm of emotions, and we know next to nothing about it.

Death has been a part of playing rpgs since the origins. It has not been much talked about, as a fact in the lives of characters. So let's talk about it.

Here's some elements to consider:

Fields of death
- War
- Murder
- Accidents
- Malady
- Age

Dying
- The death-bed
- Fear
- Pain
- Remorse
- Anger
- Despair
- Acceptance
- Confessions
- Forgiveness
- Last rites
- The last breath

Rites
- Last rites
- Funeral
- Priest
- Family
- The corpse
- The shroud
- The coffin
- From dust to dust
- Sorrow

Aftermath
- Sorrow
- Revenge
- Repercussions
- The empty chair
- Inheritance
- Reminiscence
- Ghosts

Characters dying
- Death as a threat
- Death as consequence
- Player reactions
- Symbols of death
- Death scenes
- Problems of discontinuity

    \nThe Game at the Wake
    As laid out in my will, please have my daughter read these rules aloud to the guests who attend my wake.

    Darling, before you read this, make sure you have a glass of something dark in your hand. Take sips to keep your throat from catching. Tap the glass with something to get everyone to settle down and then read the quoted text.

    \u201cFriends, my father has passed away and you will never see him again, but all of you hold him in your memory. In your minds, there are facts and facets of him. Let\u2019s all play a game to share those pieces."

    Pause for emphasis, right?

    "I\u2019m certain that today, many of you have been telling stories about him to as many people as will listen. This game will be the opposite of that. For the next 45 minutes, we will whisper things about Morgan to each other. These will not be anecdotes from his life or funny opinions about what he would think about any given situation. Each of you must whisper two things to everyone else present:

    A thing Morgan would never do under any circumstances.

    A thing Morgan wanted to do, but never did.

    As you quietly relate these \u2018things that never were\u2019 to his friends, they will correct you if necessary. Perhaps they know that he actually did what you indicated. If that occurs, two things will happen. Your impression of him will change slightly and you must think of another thing to whisper to others. After everyone has whispered their two whispers to everyone else, we will meet back here and toast. Make sure your glass isn\u2019t empty.\u201d

    Now, Darling, allow everyone some time. Make sure you participate. You don\u2019t need to be too strict about the time, just make sure that everyone understands exactly what we are doing. You can wrap it up earlier if you get the sense that the task is accomplished.

    \u201cBy now, you should all know two things that Morgan definitely never did. It is his request that you make a promise to do one of those two things. As we raise our glasses, think briefly about which one you will commit to. Cheers.
    Thank you all for playing.\u201d

    You did good, Darling.

    Posted By: Mathalus\u201cFriends, my father has passed away and you will never see him again, but all of you hold him in your memory. In your minds, there are facts and facets of him. Let\u2019s all play a game to share those pieces."
    I find it very refreshing to see a game on this theme, to be played in such a circumstance! Very interesting response to the theme of death. Thank you!

    Saying something is a "game" gives a certain lightness to it. Having death treated with some lightness is ok, in my mind. There will be crying in a funeral anyway, so having people going about doing something else for a couple of minutes is good (they may whisper AND cry, of course). And playing a "game" over the life of the deceased is very much a proper thing to do, especially if he was an avid gamer.

    I made a game some years ago on the theme of life and friendships, in which death has a very strong presence. The translated title is "Autumn of Life" (Livets h\xf8st). It's a small game of the modern type, wherein the players depict a group of lifelong, old friends. Almost all scenes in the game takes place in the funerals of the characters. They die, one by one, but the players of dead characters continue to play them, as some kind of "whispering ghosts". They whisper "memories" to the players with living characters; shared moments of friendship or pain they had with the deceased, or simple minutiae the living recollect about the dead. It's a beautiful game to play, even though death is very much present in it, and treated with all the respect it deserves.

    \n In a classical fantasy role-playing game I arranged at a convention some years back, I instructed the leaders of the groups to be hard on the characters if the players did the wrong thing;
    - "Let them die if they deserve it. Standing up against a monstrously huge and fiery dragon is not something you survive! Let such a character be roasted, with a lively description"
    And then I went on to tell the leaders that players of dead characters should be given thanks for their participation, and asked to leave the room. No one should be given new characters, nor the chance to watch the rest of the game from the sideline. Dead character = game over for you.

    I also instructed the leaders, in our play-test of the scenario, that they should work their ass off to make each and every scene in the game a really memorable scene. Playing this game should be a top experience for the players, wether they got to play 30 minutes or 3 hours. The quality should be in the scenes, and in how the characters acted in them, not in how many minutes of character-life the players got. "Help the characters come alive in each and every scene", I told them; "so they may die and leave you with a good feeling".

    It worked very well. We had a blast playing the scenario (with a dragon), had some players die on us, with the heat of interaction steadily increasing through the whole scenario. The players really wanted to be in on the drama, as the end of it was fore-spelled in several exciting ways, and that made the game even more intense. Dying was a real threat to them ...

    The end of the drama was tragic, and beautiful, with one central, mentally-disabled NPC climbing an altar and demanding to be sacrificed for the good of their home-village. It brought tears to the eyes of players in most of the groups. Death was very much present in this game, both as a definitive reality of individual game-end, and as a symbolic and ultimate sacrifice, elevated to the sphere where it gives meaning to a life.

    The very fatalistic technique of "dead character = game over for you" was inspired by death itself, of course. It worked well with the theme of the scenario (a legendary trek to save the village, at any cost), to infuse it with a strongly felt, underlying acuity.

    Posted By: whduryeaThomas, how many players did you have when the scenario started? How many were left at the end?
    We were several leaders, with each our own group of 5-6 players. The numbers of fatalities varied. I had two characters die during the scenario, one of them very close to the end. And ended up with four players in the last stages. The other leaders had 1-2 deaths.

    The players were told that death for their characters would end the game for them, and still some of them were surprised by it. No one reacted badly, but there were some pouting from a couple of them (in total, in all the groups). They were given thanks for the game, and they had had a great game, so the pouting had more to do with them wanting more ...

    I could sympathize with that, and so could the other leaders, but we did not give in. Players of dead characters were asked to leave, politely.

    Death is fatal! ;-)

    \n Every story is about death. It's so big, you can't escape it.

    One of the things I like most about Werewolf/Mafia is that when you die, you get to see what was happening behind the scenes. It's a nice pay-off for not being able to vote. At least that's my take on it. I've heard people complain about dying in Werewolf/Mafia and I just don't get it. I've never felt that way. I mean sure, I'm bummed when I die, but it's still a nice game to just observe. I like the idea of games ending, like stories.

    Oh, and there's figurative death too!
