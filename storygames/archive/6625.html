\nI posted this on my LJ and on Knife Fight, but here I'm asking you guys specifically for play advice about how to handle this without going nuts, without bending the game just because one player is disruptive, and without clobbering her with "page 11" and tossing her from the game!

So, like, in the D&D campaign I started last night, using Paizo's second Pathfinder series of adventures, I've got this person I play with. Let's call her, um, "A."

Now, I'm not saying "A" is always a big ol' Jerky McDisruptopants, but she does have her moments, and last night was another of them.

She loves playing characters with stealth. Who are loners. Who don't interact with anyone in the party in any material way, and who go off by themselves.

All the fucking time. A ninja, a scout, a ranger, whatever. I don't mind the repetitiveness -- I tend to play the same rogue/bard over and over again myself -- but it's the anti-socialness, ICly, that I object to.

So last night, we're starting the campaign, and ...

MINOR SPOILERS FOR CURSE OF THE CRIMSON THRONE (Pathfinder #7) FOLLOW

...an NPC has arranged for all the players individually to meet at her house at sundown. The monk goes there early, sits down lotus style, and starts meditating. For hours and hours. Kinda cool, I'm good with that.

"A" -- playing a ranger -- shows up, goes across the street, hides in a bush, and watches the monk for hours. Uh okay whatever.

The halfling rogue shows up just about on time, rolls well on her Spot check, notices the ranger. Drops down on her from above to say hello; "A" basically shoos her off and doesn't talk, roleplaying being anti-social. Uh okay whatever. The rogue goes over to annoy the monk.

The war cleric shows up, talks to everyone (except "A" who is still hiding across the street); the sorcerer shows up. They all knock, nobody answers, the monk tries the door. There's a note inside, saying "come on in, I'll be along shortly." Everyone goes inside -- well, except "A", who sneaks up to listen at the door.

The NPC who owns the house and has gathered everyone comes down the road. "A" hides from her. She goes in, talks to the other players; there are 6 seats at the table -- one for each player and one for the NPC. "A" stays outside, listening at the door. Someone asks about the extra seat; the NPC says she invited someone else, but apparently she didn't show. Halfling pipes up, "Oh, she's across the street, hiding in the bushes!" "A" listens to this, still says outside.

NPC explains the plot, asks for the players' help; they agree to help. "A" is still outside, ear against the door, listening in.

NPC does a tarot card reading, for each of the four players who are in the house. "A" is still lurking outside. Players are told to write down the initial card drawn plus a number based on the card read. When all the card readings are done, then "A" decides she's going to come in.

    Posted By: KynnGood DMs are about maximizing the fun, not about snubbing someoneeven if they deserve it. Consequences which bring the whole game down for everyone aren't fun, are they? GMs have a responsibility to avoid knee-jerk reactions,even whenthe players are being annoying.
    I would recommend introducing "social death" to the game. If a character does something to alienate the other characters, and they don't want that character as a friend anymore, then the character is socially dead. Such a death works just like any other death: the player loose it, and must create a new character (hopefully with better social antennas).

    With this rule in place, you may invoke it anytime a player let her character do something violent or disruptive to the other characters, or makes her character stay out of the group. Invoking it should be tough enough to draw most players back in the group. Classical roleplaying games are about ensembles, so split party is not good.

    And that is a second advice: make it absolutely clear that any character out of the group, will have very little and crude playtime. Focus will always be on the group, you may tell your players, and it is your responsibility to make the characters function well as a group. I d\xf6n't mind how you players do it, but YOU have to do it. I will focus on plot and setting. OK?

    Posted By: KynnCan you unpack "obstructionist" some more? I would see obstructionism as preventing her from doing what she chooses to do -- "oh, the NPC sees you lurking outside and goes out to invite you in" -- rather than allowing her to do what she wants and allowing the consequences to happen.
    \nIn my opinion, when someone is gamemastering well, every player/character choice results in an interesting outcome. This doesn't mean that the players always get what they want, or that they always succeed, but it does mean that all their choices result in interesting play. That style of gamemastering is the opposite of obstructionism.

    What you describe with 'A', I would consider to be obstructionist; She comes in to get her reading, but the fortuneteller refuses; she runs off to break in to the building, but none of the doors will open, and no one reacts to her presence; she tries to swim around to the other side of the building, but a shark blocks her path. Each of these scenarios basically boils down to "nothing happens; you can't have what you want." That's boring and unrewarding for everyone.

    What if the fortuneteller agreed to tell A's fortune, but because doing so delays the fortuneteller's departure, soldiers who have been hunting her catch up with her, and she (the fortuneteller) is captured, requiring a daring rescue? Alternately, what if they just had a cool argument, and the NPC grudgingly agreed to the reading, but the negative emotions engendered by the argument colored the reading in a spooky way? What if guards saw A trying to break into the building, and it resulted in an awesome rooftop chase through the city? None of these scenarios result in A's character getting what she wants, and all of them involve negative consequences, but they're consequences that are interesting in play.

    \n Kynn, you did Punish "A." I want to state that clearly. You started to say you see it. But, then you backed off by justifying your behavior. The reality is, no one can force the words out of your mouth when you GM. There was no rule making you say "No" and no law of nature that forced your hand.
    \n Clearly, this is an out of game issue. Please, do not try and solve out of game issues in game. What I mean is, the character is in game. In game the character is not a problem. They are not breaking the law or acting in a way that is disruptive to the game world, right? In cases like this, it is appropriate to send in game consequences after them. If they break the law, send the guards. If they unmake the world, send a paladin or white mage. But, this issue has nothing to do with the character, character actions or setting.
    \n The problem does have to do with the person at the table. So address that problem with that person. But, before you do anything accusatory, etc. Find out how the other players feel. Maybe they think the lone wolf thing is fun or cute. Maybe they just don't care. If you are the only one who has a problem with this, that changes the whole dynamic, right? And if the other players agree that this is an issue (their behavior isolated from your reactions to it). Then that presents an opportunity to say, I was wrong, but I am not the only one affected. Don't immediately mention this issue about the other players. You don't want to leave the impression that you are talking about them behind their back or that you are campaigning against them. But, if the defense arises, you're the only one that has a problem, you have an answer.
    \n Also, try and escalate slowly.
    \n First, talk to them in private about this issue. Make sure to clearly communicate what the issue is as you see it. Don't just say, your Ranger was a loner 'til it stopped benefiting them or you went off to the BBEG's hideout alone. But state what you see the overall arch of the problem is like "I don't know what you want from the game" or "your character concepts/actions is messing with the group dynamics," or whatever the problem actually is. And be careful to use language that is not accusatory or leaves them no way to explain/redeem themselves. Like if you say, "you were a jerk," the only possible responses are "so what" or "ok, I guess I won't be a jerk anymore." There's no room for discussion or debate.
    \n If that doesn't work, then schedule time before the game session starts and discuss the issue as a group to see how we can solve it together. Make sure it doesn't turn into a "bitch session" and that "A" doesn't feel piled up on. But, let them know, in no uncertain terms, that it is affecting the entire group.
    \n After that, you might have to ask another group member to adjudicate between you two. Maybe the SO might be able to find a balance between the group and their SO, you know?
    \n Anyways, sorry that this player was so disruptive and I hope this advice helps!

    \n Okay so I won't cover the stuff that says communicate with your players about what they want from the game and what sort of stories they want to tell. If the player likes the loner character type ask her (and everyone else) what they like about it.

    Secondly, make two things explicit to the players because this stuff isn't obvious to many people that play certain games:
    1) Everyone brings their own fun to the table. If she's having fun lurking around then so be it. She has a responsibility to support the other players and they have a responsibility to support her. Introduce the idea of little color scenes where they get to make other characters look cool. Start off with "where is you character and how does your interaction with them make them look cool?"

    2) Everyone needs to understand that conflict is good and that they should look forward to having bad things happen to them as long as it moves story along (assuming story is important to them). Thus it's okay for the GM to levy crazy consequences on them as long as the consequences are part of a roll (many players hate GM fiat) and are stated ahead of time.

    Consequences should always move the story forward and enhance fun. Make your NPCs cower and whimper under the might of the PCs power. "Okay! Okay, I will read your cards!" and have it be something dark and forboding, maybe even to reflect the mood of the fortune-teller.

    How I run these games (because they don't really tell us how to):\n
    • Set a scene up based on locale, NPCs present, etc.
      • \n
    • Ask each player what they want from the scene - "what's at stake?" Do they have different goals? Do they want to support one another? Do they want to be in direct conflict and how?
      • \n
    • Ask how they plan on getting what they want from the scene. What skills or feats or powers do they want to use?
      • \n
    • Let them know what may happen if they fail their roll. Give them something to FIGHT for - to spend Action Points on, to help one another to avoid, etc.
      • \n
    • Wrap up the scene based on their stakes and the result of their roll. Ask them if there is anything they want to add.
      • \n
    • Move onto the next scene. Often this may be some followup item or reacting to fallout to the stakes. Never have scenes setup to negate previous stakes, only build upon them.
    \nI often even ask players "what is it you want out of this combat?" If it's to survive or kill everyone else I try and set things up to push more buttons such as "stop someone from using a powerful magic item or continuing a ritual or from killing an NPC."

    I have a lot of experience (as we all do) with loner characters. Having a little more scene-based game allows them to shine a little more and the other players to reflect on who the character is and what he's all about despite the characters' ignorance.

    I hope that is at all helpful,
    - Don
