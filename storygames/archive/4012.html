\n I'm reading Roleplaying Mastery by Gary Gygax, which I found at my parents' house during my last visit. Apparently I had bought about a million years ago but never actually read it. I was curious as to how this book - published in 1987 - had stood up to the test of time, and if there were any gems of wisdom in there that could be successfully salvaged.

The answer so far is - no, probably not. But as I read it, I will use this thread for commentary and will make sure I reveal any hidden gems I do find, plus poke fun at things I find especially wrong.

I'm only around chapter 2 right now, but from today's perspective, the book is a mess.

In the introduction, Gygax mentions he hopes that people interested in roleplaying games will read the book before playing, which will improve their experience greatly. His greatest disappointment seems to be the people who try get into the game, play it "wrong" or with people who don't know how to play it "right", figures it's not for them and quits. In this, he and I share a motivation - I too think that most everyone can find something worthwhile in roleplaying, but that playing a game "wrong" is a surefire way to get the impression it's not your thing, so this premise excited me.

He proceeds by introducing the concept of the "masters" - a "small group of enthusiasts" who have "mastered" the art of roleplaying. He assumes the reader is not part of this clique, but probably thinks they are. The book seems to be written from the standpoint of a member of an esoteric elite introducing a chosen ignorant follower to the Mysteries by way of vague allegories and subtle hints instead of explicit instruction, while assuring the reader that there is great wisdom to be learned if they follow some exact yet unspoken steps. This is somewhat off-putting.

Chapter 1 (Roleplaying: the Foundation of Fun) is about the concept of roleplaying, and covers things like the history of D&D, why roleplaying is fun and useful, and why all the terror-mongers are wrong about D&D causing death, suicide and satanism.

Notably, despite Gygax' claim in the intro of having written the book partly for those new to the hobby, he doesn't define what roleplaying is. He doesn't talk about the different roles, social dynamics, activities, techniques, immersion vs non-immersion, different playstyles, or whatever. This could be expected, I suppose - we didn't have the terminology to talk about a lot of these things back in the 80s, and furthermore - as far as Gygax is appears to be concerned - there is but One True Way to play the Great Game of D&D, and it is The Way of the Master and needs No Further Explanation. However, this lack of definition becomes a blatant flaw later in the book, as we shall see.

Another gripe in this chapter comes from this: Gygax rarely talks about D&D specifically, preferring to speak about "roleplaying" in general, and even offers examples of game genres like sci-fi, secret agent and horror. He never names names, but it's occasionally obvious what games he's referring to, since the selection was rather small back then ("You can choose between literally dozens of games!") - Call of Cthulhu is an obvious one, for example. However, he then introduces as gospel D&D specific tropes, like "Alignments" and "Classes", and how to deal with them in-game. This makes it sound like he's trying to be accepting of the existence of other games, while shoe-horning them into the mold of AD&D. This does the book a great disservice, since it's supposedly intended to teach skills universally applicable to roleplaying games as a whole.

The gripe torrent does not slow down with Chapter 2 (The Master Player). The biggest one I've encountered so far is that now, Gygax seems to use the word "roleplaying" to refer to the activity of play and the act of immersive character acting interchangeably. This is why a definition would have been a great idea, because I know a fair number of (A)D&D players who rarely enters Actor Stance yet still has plenty of fun with the game. "Make sure you play your role to the hilt", Gygax assures us, giving some examples of stereotypical acting based on class ("A magic user should act mysterious, while a cleric should be righteous and wise") but gives us no particular reason why we should do this or why it would make our game any better. Having played these games since 1989, I think his point is partly accurate but his presentation very imprecise and vague. Had this been 1987 and I was a newcomer to the hobby armed with this book, I'd be more confused than I was before reading it.

A very concerning contradiction is with regards to character and player separation. In multiple locations prior to Chapter 2, Gygax talks of how there is a clear separation between character and player, and that "a master will understand this intuitively" or some similarly vaguely insulting handwave. However, in Chapter 2, a checklist provided for players include making sure to pick a character type that will provide a useful set of skills and abilities to the player group - frex, if the party has no cleric, make a cleric instead of yet another fighter. The reason is not because you will appreciate carving your own niche, but because the group will want to maximize efficiency. This is fine and dandy, from a gamist Us-vs-the-DM perspective.

Here's the but. But, then he goes on to talk about roleplaying your character a certain way, and then reverts to stating that if you're playing a cleric, and you're not providing your healing services fairly to the party members, or demanding payment for your services, then this behaviour will cause inter-player strife! All these statements seem entirely incongruent to my mind: First, make a character for purpose of gamist efficiency. Then, ensure that your character has a personality and will act according to what the game world deems logically necessary. Then, ensure that you act according to the wishes of your fellow players. It's as if he invented an entirely new Stance (sorry, I'm on a Big Model trip) - Communist Author Stance, or something like that, where you are not allowed to decide what you want your character to do, but your character has to act in accordance with what the rest of the group's players want to ensure party cooperation.

So far, I think the book reads like GG's stream-of-consciousness on the subject, with many ideas that aren't well thought out or presented. I'm also put off by the esoteric tone. As a book for roleplaying beginners, I'm afraid that in this day and age the book would do far more harm than good.

I will keep posting observations and anecdotes from the book and if I find anything of use, I'll be sure to report it. In the meantime, feel free to comment as you wish.

    \n Actually, here's a nice one, though I'm not sure I'd quite call it a "gem".

    In the beginning of Chapter 2 (IIRC) GG talks about the "Spirit" of the roleplaying game, something which is critical that a player understands and which can only be grasped after you understand the interplay of the rules in action. This is a predecessor, I suppose, to the Forge-ish belief that deconstructing Actual Play sessions is the best way of understanding how a game works, why it works that way, and how it could be improved.

    As an example, Gygax offers the insight that in AD&D, a base assumption is that humans and the demi-human races (i.e. player races) are all generally good. There is evil in the world, but generally good, noble heroes have always been able to beat it back and ensure that order and goodness prevails in the end. You play these good, noble heroes.

    By stating this, it made a couple of things click for me. My great gaming curse was being introduced to White Wolf WoD and its world of moral ambiguities, playing the hell out of that system with some limited amount of "success", and then returning to fantasy-based hack-and-slash gaming mostly because I couldn't find any other local groups to join. I falsely assumed that my affinity towards more "Bang-oriented" play (though had we known the exact definition of bangs at the time, and directed play consciously towards them, our experience would certainly have been much better) was mostly a result of my maturation, and that others gamers my age or older would share my tastes. I now understand that when my ex-DM, who was going on 40 at the time, built a campaign world based around pure good-vs-evil and made a plot centered around the fetching of the holy McGuffin to save the world, he wasn't being immature and wouldn't be impressed by the shades-of-grey and internal strife of my pacifist half-orc monk. Instead, he'd be annoyed that I tried to take the focus away from his carefully balanced encounters by having internal doubts about the wholesale execution of an entire orc tribe while we were in the middle of miniature combat. Fact is, the kill-them-and-take-their-stuff model works best without moral ambiguity to get in the way.

    Thus, in the (for this example) wise words of Gary Gygax, assume that your dudes are the good guys. Other minis on the battle map are evil. They probably have stuff you'd like, and you're perfectly justified in killing them and taking that stuff. If you do that enough times you'll get more powerful. This is good.

    Similarly, alignment is a tool by which to keep consistency to your roleplay and a mechanical device for supernatural abilities, not a constraint to be slavishly adhered to - a DM that constantly puts your character in situations where they can choose between following their alignment or get cool stuff, then punish you for choosing not to go along with your alignment, is an abusive DM. These never-ending RPG.net threads discussing the nuances of alignments or the alien nature of elves are pointless - these things should not matter in a D&D game, and most games will only be worse for trying to include them.

    If you want to play moral ambiguity in a fantasy setting, run it in Burning Wheel or something - that system actually support it.

    \n Simon, thanks for answering Jason's question. You summed it up very well in your first response, I thought.

    Let's put it this way. Gygax states, in the book which this thread is about, that alignment is a mechanism that allows you to maintain consistency in your roleplaying, i.e. character acting. It is a crutch for you as a player to rely upon. The rules have also been modeled to make occasional use of this "Alignment stat", usually when determining results of divine magics. He makes this statement in the section where he discusses the "Spirit" of the game of (A)D&D, as being a game where the players portray good, noble heroes. His point is that this is what the game is, and to achieve "mastery" (i.e. maximize your fun, in my words) you must play a game according to its nature.

    Thus, in my interpretation and view, alignment is not a "flag" to the DM to centre play around issues and choices stemming from a player's chosen alignment. When I choose to play a Lawful Good Paladin in D&D, I choose to send a message to the DM that I want to portray a really good guy, righteous and steadfast in my beliefs. I am not sending a message to the DM that I want to explore how one can truly be good, divine and holy while still being the guardian of an absolute rule of law, especially under a corrupt lawmaker. Similarly, if I play a True Neutral Druid, that means I want to play a game where I am free to take whichever side I want and still have a perfectly valid justification for my behaviour - not that I want to be put in a position where I am forced to side with the forces of Evil because Good is growing too powerful, especially if that means I will be shunned by the rest of my party.

    In a different game, all these choices may lead to awesome. In D&D, these things aren't hard choices, they are choices between getting to play the character I created with the rules the game system offers, versus playing a character who is constantly being called into question and not getting to count on the system for support in the process.

    For Gygax, Bang-driven, hard-choice play is not what (A)D&D is about. Some may be successfully playing the game that way but I would suggest that you are using a whole subsystem of implicit social rules in doing so to ensure that players aren't getting fed up. In a setting where the DM is pushing for such play, against the Spirit of D&D and likely without coming to an explicit understanding with all the players, the DM is indeed being abusive. SHe is questioning the players' character choices and tastes and is constantly trying to make the game be about something other than that which he has been flagged for.

    It's much like trying to play Monopoly with some jerk whining you're not being fair by overcharging him for rent on Boardwalk, and why don't you think of his five starving kids at home before you try to extort him.

    Jason, if you take offense at the "abuse" statement, understand that I'm not saying the DM is necessarily malicious. Like Mike said, "accidental torture" is indeed a better term for situations when the DM doesn't know any better. I would not water it down any more than that though - like all participants around the table, the DM is there to give and receive entertainment. If the DM thinks hir role is to challenge the players to deeper, more meaningful character development by calling into question the choices they made when they first sat down to determine what kind of game they wanted to play, then SHe is in direct breach of a social contract very prominent in (A)D&D - the game is about killing things, taking their stuff, leveling your little dudes, and having light-hearted fun doing it.

    Take a game like Burning Wheel, with built-in mechanical support for Beliefs and Instincts with rewards associated with making hard choices regarding them, and lifepaths like "Thug" instead of "Rogue", and you have a totally different implicit social contract where such play makes perfect sense. The players are sitting down explicitly looking for such play, and it'd be abuse not to facilitate it.

      \n
      \n1) Character's passive behaviour (i.e. when the Ogre bashes me, do I get thrown backwards or stand my ground?)
      \n2) Character's active behaviour (i.e. when I bash the Ogre, do I do it in a flashy way, spinning around, or do i two hand over head whack him?)
      \n3) Character's speech (i.e. does my character say "Have at you, ogre!" or simply grit his teeth?)
      \n4) Character's observable emotion (i.e. Does my character look afraid, or angry, at the ogre?)
      \n5) Character's internal emotions (i.e. IS my character really afriad of the ogre, or not?)
      \n6) Character's internal beliefs (i.e. Does my character hate ogres?)

      \n
      \n* I kiss the barmaid (level 1)
      \n* I get the barmaid to kiss me (level 2)
      \n* I get the barmaid to tell me she loves me (level 3)
      \n* I get the barmaid all flustered with my flirtation (level 4)
      \n* I get the barmaid to fall in love with me (level 5)
      \n* I get the barmaid to feel I am the only one in the world for her (level 6)
      \n

It also helps to enforce the "us good guys against EVIL!" vibe that really makes D&D work well, as Gygax seems to be saying.       \nI wanted to comment on the presence of the big-gaping-hole in DnD of leaving wide swaths of interactions (social interactions, exploring the world) with no mechanics. One reaction to this is that we should try plugging the hole with mechanics. Another is that we should just try "not to be a dick". But expanding on that, maybe learning "not to be a dick" is something one learns, as an art form. You can get a little bit of it by reading examples of play in books. More from just experience. But I think maybe part of what Gygax was getting at is that it can also be learned from others with experience, by just playing with and emulating them. Between personal experience and apprenticeship, one eventually cultivates an intuitive sense of handling the "just roleplaying" aspect of DnD.
      \n
      \nHowever, like art, the skill of just winging it can't be categorized, summarized, analyzed easily. It's just something one picks up with time.
      \n
      \nI'm not sure I agree with this approach, but here is a thought on a more specific case. Consider the trade-off between perfect character fidelity and harmonizing with the needs of other players. One way to handle this is with a mechanic that automatically balances the two. Another way is to just... try to balance the two goals, using experience and instinct. The former method has some obvious advantages. But the latter one might also:
      \n
      \n1. The weight between the two is decided by the player, and the optimal amount of weight is a matter of taste. For example, in extreme cases some indy games solve the problem by abandoning character-fidelity entirely. For some that's a perfect solution. For others, its a catastrophe.
      \n
      \nOTOH maybe the answer here is to have a plethora of games, or mechanics with "sliders" that can be adjusted to taste.
      \n
      \n2. Maybe with sufficient "mastery" a human being can always, with just instinct and experience, do better than the cold mechanic, even holding values fixed.
      \n
      \nAnd maybe that's why Gygax puts so much weight on a vague, hard-to-describe and quantify, mastery of roleplaying skill.

      \n I'd like to insert a few facts here.

      Original D&D didn't have Chainmail anywhere on the cover of the box or the three manuals. What they said was "Rules for Fantastic Medieval Wargames Campaigns Playable with Paper and Pencil and Miniature Figures". (You can check that over at the Acaeum.)

      Note that this was the edition that was published in 1974 and thus preceded both Holmes Basic and the AD&D Monster Manual by 3 years.

      However, I think Mike is incorrect insofar as he's talking about the intent of those early rules. While the term "roleplaying" wasn't current at the time, D&D wasn't designed as a "wargame" in the vein of Chainmail or other miniatures rules sets. Instead it was a development, through Dave Arneson's Blackmoor campaign, of ideas which had originated with Dave Weseley's "Braunstein" games, which were basically RPGs by another name. Arneson used Chainmail as an adjunct to roleplaying, to handle combat (though he also developed the "alternate" combat rules which later became the standard AC/"to hit" mechanic).

      Sources:

      A Brief History of Role Playing Games by Victor Raymond. (In private correspondence, Victor explained to me that he got much of his information directly from Dave Arneson.)
      Reproduction of an account of the Braunstein -> D&D development (Bottom post on the page, drawn from an essay that was to accompany a D&D documentary.)
      And generally, other interviews with Arneson and Weseley, which are scattered around the net.

      Now, intent is of course separate from expression, and I don't have the original D&D texts handy at the moment; I do recall that they were pretty poorly presented, which might have led to some seeing the game purely as a wargame (in the sense that later was differentiated from "RPG"). However, I kinda doubt it, because frankly, D&D isn't a very good wargame, and if it was received and transmitted on those terms, it would have failed completely.
