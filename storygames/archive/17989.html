\n The examples of resources in the rulebook are all tangible and 'real' things (e.g. food, water, shelter etc.) Increasingly I'veheard of games using more abstract resources such as concepts and ideas (e.g. hope, faith, order).

These abstract resources sound really interesting, but I wonder what the risk is in having a game which uses too many. Does the game fundamentally change or break down if your abundance is 'liberty' and your scarcities are 'equality and fraternity'?

Has anyone had any experience with abstract resources or have any thoughts about their use/misuse?

    \n Here's the thing about gamers: they're a clever bunch, and they know they're a clever bunch. They like to push the envelope a lot, in terms of what's possible in a design.

    And so when I just listed tangible resources in the book, playtest groups sometimes introduced an esoteric resource. And when I just listed regular earthbound settings in the book, playtest groups sometimes placed their games on space colonies or weird surreal fantastical places.

    When I listed "hope" as an example resource and "the moon" as an example setting, the cleverest ones in the bunch then took that as the baseline and wanted to push the envelope past that.

    So, yeah. I love inserting a weird esoteric resource into the game. I think more than 1 makes the game unfocused and essentially not about scarcity any longer. I don't mention intangible/abstract/esoteric resources in the book because I want that to be where the envelope gets pushed too (just like how I say the game is for 2-4 when I know that it often works fine for 5, because folks are going to try to add in 'just one more player'). It was a measure of tinker-proofing and drift-proofing the game.

    \n I agree with Joe that one weird/abstract resource per game is ideal (if you want to have one at all.) If somebody else introduces an off-the-wall scarcity on their turn, I will almost always suggest something as concrete and immediate as possible -- even if I had originally intended to introduce my own unusual resource.

    But there is also an enormous and very fruitful middle-ground between 'drinking water' and 'love', and it's worth distinguishing between purely abstract concepts and things which are concrete but unusual/unexpected. I have played a game where 'obsidian' was a resource -- a scarcity, it turned out. Obsidian is an actual thing, but what its scarcity might look like in a community is pretty abstract (it turned out to have a primarily social/ritual function.) Similarly, I have played a number of games where something like 'rituals' or 'traditions' or 'historical knowledge' was introduced -- very abstract concepts, but their application is fairly straightforward.

    For me, the most interesting resources exist in the middle-ground -- they're unexpected, but as soon as they get introduced there are numerous, relatively obvious directions the group can take them. They have enough immediacy that the group can't just kind of hand-wave them away, and enough specificity that they don't just turn into a sort of generalized theme (this latter is I think a big problem with emotional states like 'hope' and 'love'.) And of course, they fit in somehow with the map and the landscape as it has been defined so far.
