\n How do you monologue well?

At what point in the game session is a monologue appropriate? Is it possible to do an opening monologue well? (I know it's the bane of some players, when done poorly.)

When you monologue, do you tend to do so in character, as a GM, or a mixture? Do you do cool things with your tone and volume to keep attention on your words? Is there a signal (verbal or non) at your table that says "Okay, guys, listen up for a while"? If you feel that a game *needs* a monologue, but you have players with short attention spans, how do you reconcile those two things?

Also, what are some cool monologue tricks? JDCorley, if you read this feel free to link to and talk about your Arrested Development voice-over faulty narrator trickz.

In summary: Do you monologue? Well? Please tell me specific details on how one monologues in game.

    \n I monologue generally at the begining of a session, as a recap and booster at the begining, its a sign to listen, and start the game proper as apposed to the stuff that happens before.
    \n
    \nI also occassionally monologue IC as a villian or something, with the priviso that if I'm interrupted the monologue ends. The monologue (Imho) should never be so important or necessary that the story can't go on without it , and if the players are interrupting its a pretty clear sign that the monologue is either too long or badly placed.
    \n
    \nThings I try to do with the monologue. (Mostly from what little I know of Public Speaking )
    \n
    \nKeep Eye Contact, and Vary Your Voice (In both terms of Volume, and Pitch)
    \n
    \nHave a point, tell people your point (its not a joke where you hold the punchline for last ) as quickly as possible
    \n
    \nIf necessary go into supporting/argumentative bits but keep it short and snappy.
    \n
    \nAt the End of it, ask for questions, even if it may not normally be appropiate (ie after the big bad's monologue), a monologue is a long period of one person talking by definition, if you lost someone at the second sentence they need a way to get back to basics.

    \n As GM, I try to keep "set up" monologues short. I prefer to have them delivered "in game" as in they are the contents of a letter (given as physical prop) or statements by an NPC (who can be questioned). I prefer to let Players ask questions, investigate etc. to flesh this out further.

    "Everybody knows" type information is distributed on-line via a message board outside the play at table.

    In game, again try to keep them shorter still. They should be rare moments when it's obvious that "we don't get to hear this NPC talk very often and we fought/bought/sweated our way here so we want to listen" This is in keeping with my table-survey of interests indicating Players' strongest preference is Action with Exploration/Investigation 2nd (they want to fight their way to discovering cool secrets).

    So details, in one session on an island they had worked hard to find (magically hidden), they at last have a chance to meet the reclusive mage who might be a villain and hear his side of story. They present "credentials", some in-character talks indicating their approach, some skill rolls (ties to mage's attitude, how forthcoming he is) and then I give monologue that explains his side of events and his decision on whether they get to leave island or not.

    They're interested (a) the information reveals a reinterpretation of commonly accepted truths; (b) their fate as PC's is at stake as ways off the island include such options as zombies, brain-wiped, bound-agents of mage etc.

    \n I use monologues in my TSoY/Solar System-based Transhuman Space-campaign - or rather my players do. We added it as a part of the game - and we tied it to the Refreshment-rules (sort of like healing-scenes, if you don't know TSoY - and if you don't shame on you). So whenever a player wants to do a refreshment, he can describe his or her character writing a blog, creating a videolog or whatever - and it is done as a monologue. Afterwards the other players and myself chip in as various commenters on the blog. It is a very popular thing and it is often done even without the need to Refresh, but simply to share the inner life of the character, as the character would express him- or herself in writing.

    \nRob, cool stuff. If I may paraphrase to ensure that I've sucked every ounce of knowledge from your writing:

    If the players work for a monologue, it gives them a chance to invest in what you're saying. Make information a target, and when they get to it they'll be eager for a monologue instead of bored.

    When you monologue, don't tell them a bunch of new information. Tell them information which changes things already in motion.

    \n I love monologueing. I like to play with a lot of atmosphere. I don't use it nearly enough, though. That's why I adore the Swedish game Ber\xe4ttelser fr\xe5n Staden (Stories from the City), written by a friend of mine. In it, play is divided into chapters, and between each chapter, GM role and characters switch owners ("Ooh, I wanna play this dude in this chapter!").
    \n
    \nAnyway, it's specifically written in the game that each chapter starts with a monologue. It's not just the GM framing the first scene ("Alright, so Johannes is in his room, right? And a tube arrives through the tube mail thingy."), it sets a lot of atmosphere ("The little room is dark and dank, and full different kinds of perfumes. Jasmine and Hyacinth leaks from the tiny bottles that Johannes the perfumer uses in his trade ..."). I've never had any trouble with players finding this boring. It's probably because they're all listening for interesting ways to get their newly chosen roles into the action. When the monologue starts, the stage is set, and everyone can start going "I burst in, sweaty and filthy. 'Johannes! You'll never guess what's happened!'".
    \n
    \nDunno if this was useful, but there you go.

    \n I actually tend to not have any problems signalling or doing monologuing (as a GM or a player, much to the chagrin of certain GMs!) because I have the skill of talking in such a way as to get the room quiet and listening - it comes from a lot of practice.

    Try doing a big deep intake of breath the next time you want to - that helps. Shift your body language. Stand if you are sitting, sit if you are standing, put a foot up on something, turn your chair around, do anything physical to change the situation and people will look at you. Take off your glasses if you have them, put them on if you have them off. Fold your hands if they are out, spread them if they are together.

    \n Interesting.

    As for myself, I don't use monologuing (if I'm understanding it correctly) very often. So what I am saying isn't about "how to do it", but rather "what to use it for".

    Anyway, I ran a game loosely set in the Night Watch world, of which only I had read the books (one other watched the movie, and the other two quickly picked up on how things worked).

    Instead of front-loading all of the knowledge about the world (although I did a little bit, to get people on the same page). I'd do something that came out, basically, like I was reading the Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy entry for that element.

    For example, someone was like "I look at him in The Gloom, do I see anything?"

    To which I would say:
    "Well, as you are aware, the Gloom will not reveal the true nature of a person's powers. For example, if they were a vampire or a werewolf, you wouldn't be able to tell that without using deeper scrying magic. However, everyone's "auras" can be read in the Gloom to see if they are basically light or dark or neutral.

    When you look at THIS guy, you see that he falls on the dark side, but not too deep. You suspect that he's just a grunt."

    The first part being a monologue to introduce new information about the setting, or to re-introduce information that might have been forgotten.

    -Andy
