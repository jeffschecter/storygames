\n Okay, so in some games, players are expected to make things harder on their characters. They are expected to make poor decisions, at least from the perspective of the character, that will create excitement, drama, pathos, or what-have-you. In IAWA, for instance, approaching a situation with your weak stats \u2014 trying to talk something out when you're much better at punching in the face \u2014 is incentivized over going with your strong stats. You get your name on the Owe list, and you can tap that for dice or appearances later. In our recent playtest of Fiasco (AP coming, Jason, I swear), I felt like I should be doing the same sort of thing.

I feel like this is a sort of common thing, but I'm having trouble coming up with further examples. I appeal to the good people of Story-Games for more.

Specifically, I'm curious how folks' experience with this phenomenon relates to the Czege Principle ("authoring and controlling your own opposition is not fun"). It seems to me, and this may be a personal perspective thing, that this sort of play borders on, but does not fall under, the principle. This isn't authoring and controlling outside adversity, but it is a sort of ratcheting adversity and introducing internal complications. Does this jive with others' experiences, or am I the crazy man on this one?

    \n For me there's a somewhat-fuzzy line between me stating a general preference for certain obstacles in a particular game and me having to specifically define those obstacles. Still, I generally know what side of the line I'm on during a gaming session...because one side is fun and the other is not.

    For example, if we're playing a roaring twenties game and I've defined my character as a playboy wastrel who never met a redhead he didn't like, I'm going to be just fine with all the grief that's sure to come from trying to bed the ginger-haired moll who usually toddles around on the arm of the local tommy-gun-toting rumrunner. If there are no dangerous redheads in the game, or never any consequences for chasing the wrong redheads, I may be disappointed. Similarly, if every girl is nothing but trouble, I will quickly get bored and stop putting any effort into that part of the character. I just can't stay interested in hitting the same beat over and over and over again. I want whoever's putting these dames in front of him to mix it up a little: mostly bad results are okay by me as long as at least a few are good, and I don't mind at all being surprised by which ones turn out to be which.


    What I don't want to do, and what I resist doing, is demanding or actively providing the obstacles for my own character. I'm not going to tell you "So, Tommy-Gun Marcello's best girl is a foxy redhead named Lola, and when I hit the speaks tonight, that's who I'm going to go home with." I'm not going to say "Oh, the DA's secretary should be exactly the kind of girl I go for." I'm not going to suggest that the random flapper I drank too much champagne with last night should come back to me the next morning with incriminating photographs and a blackmail demand. Me handing that to you just so that you can hand it back to me is not fun. And, I suspect, it means less to you than if you were to say "Okay, so I should put a redhead somewhere in this for the playboy wastrel to tangle with" and come up with your own scheme. (At least, that's how it works for me when I'm providing obstacles for someone else; the more input they have into what obstacle I should hit them with, the less invested I am in that obstacle.)

    \nThere's a problem I tend to run into in that sort of game, where the mechanics are telling me to do something that increases the chance of my character failing (leading with my weak attribute or what have you) in order to reap some kind of reward later on. I see what the system is trying to encourage me to do, but I'm also focusing on the actual conflict...and in most cases I'm only in that conflict because I want to win it. My usual style of play has me only going into conflicts where there's something I care about on the line, and as a player, I genuinely don't want to fail in those contests. I put my character into that situation because I wanted him to win, and so it really messes with my head when the game mechanics are saying "you should make things worse for yourself right now."

    What happens is that eventually I start trying to cheat the system. Instead of playing the way I usually do and only gunning for conflicts that matter to my character, I start looking for things I could turn into conflicts that I absolutely do not give a shit about. I look for ways to disguise what I really want as a player from everyone else at the table, so that I can B'rer Rabbit my way to a double victory. ("Oh no, I fail and now I guess I get thrown in the briar patch; what a drag, but since I lost, at least I get some bonus dice in my pool now.") I stock my character concept with trash that I want to see destroyed, and use them as an ablative shield to protect the few elements that I desperately want to preserve at all costs.

    And yes, that sucks. It sucks for everyone. I waste braincycles thinking about how to work the system to my benefit and end up not enjoying the game as much, and I'm just not a good enough liar to pretend that I'm enjoying it (nor do I want to lie to my friends about something like that). Worst-case scenario, my antics end up breaking the game: I rig everything to my advantage, selling dearly what I would give away for free, and I end up throwing off whatever economy is supposed to keep my character in check.

    There's obviously something important I'm missing when it comes to those games, some psychological gulf between "I want my character to face adversity and succeed" and "I want my character to be knocked around by adversity and then go looking for more" that I can't quite figure out how to cross yet. I don't know how to find the fun over there, not yet at least. For the most part, I just avoid playing that style of game.

    Posted By: Josh Roby
    Specifically, I'm curious how folks' experience with this phenomenon relates to the Czege Principle ("authoring and controlling your own opposition is not fun"). It seems to me, and this may be a personal perspective thing, that this sort of play borders on, but does not fall under, the principle. This isn't authoring and controlling outside adversity, but it is a sort of ratcheting adversity and introducing internal complications. Does this jive with others' experiences, or am I the crazy man on this one?
    \nThere is a clear line here, and some games fall on different sides of the divide. I discuss this issue nowadays with the useful term advocation (got it from Mike Holmes, I think): the Czege principle only holds for games that are a) narrativistic and b) based on the technique of character advocation. The "advocation" here is a very generic technique (a "technical agenda", certainly) of play wherein the game has been set up to address premise by assigning one or more players to specifically play certain characters in the story. Furthermore, the advocating player plays in a specific way: he strives to express the choices and passions of his character to the game group, and he makes these choices based on character integrity rather than other priorities. The movements of these characters withing the boundaries set by the rules of the game then produces drama in an advocation-based game without any single player necessarily having a major hand in plotting anything at all.

    Advocation-based games are a very important slice of narrativistic roleplaying, so much so that I'm not entirely unsympathetic towards claims of superiority by the proponents of this set-up. The Czege principle sort of touches on this: advocation is usually discarded by games that make the individual player responsible not for the character integrity and expression of individual characters (advocation), but for the well-being of the entire story. This could be seen as a more generic restatement of the Czege principle: advocation (fun) flies out of the window when you force an individual player to take responsibility for the perceived narrative quality of the story (to provide adversity for his own character). This seems to cause the creative agenda of the game to shift into simulationism, too.

    From this viewpoint it should be pretty clear why I find narrating your own character's defeat in Houses of the Blooded unfun on Czegean grounds while I don't bat an eye at choosing a suboptimal conflict venue in IaWA. One of those solutions forces the player to take responsibility for the perceived well-being of the "story" as an external entity, while the other just allows the player full freedom in advocating for his character. There are a multitude of reasons for the PC in IaWA to choose different conflict venues, and those venues are an important means of expressing the character (one half of the advocation role, as noted above): the fact that my character is bad at acting for others is, frankly, rather inconsequential when doing so regardless is sure to drive home the point about what my character is doing in the scene and why. This has nothing to do with providing external adversity or otherwise plotting story, and everything to do with advocating for my character.

    The reason for why I've been thinking on this is that it's very common for people new to Forgean narrativistic games to start creating their own, and the first efforts seem to more often than not run into a wholesale destruction of the advocation technique. For some reason people get excited by the techniques used in these games and then make their own with very similar techniques but with no believable basis for any sort of advocation, which then results in a lukewarm conch-passing exercise. I've had to develop vocabulary to explain why I don't like these games and what would need to change to make them work for me. Interestingly enough, not everybody agrees; some people specifically want a game with no advocation elements, one that resembles Once Upon a Time more than anything else.

    (Note also that games that use advocation do not necessarily have all the players in advocation roles, nor do the players necessarily stay in those roles constantly. Gift dice decisions in TSoY are made according to audience priorities, not advocation, for example. What matters is functional movement between roles, not that advocation is enshrined )

    \nHere's the thing, I've never actually been in a game where people resolve their own conflicts. But here's what I think he was referring to (apparently this happens in octaNe):

    I create my character, and play with friends.
    A few minutes into the game, I roll some dice and say, "Oh, this result means that my girlrfiend is mad at me! Oh no!"
    A few minutes later into the game, I roll some more dice and say, "Oh, I just resolved my problem, my girlfriend and I made up again! Yay!"
    A few minutes later, the dude next to you rolls some dice and says "Oh crap, ninjas jump out and attack me!"
    And in his next roll, he resolves this complication "...but I defeat them all! Go me!"

    That's the super-close view: If you author your own "complications" but don't have control over their resolution (leaving that to the GM, other players, etc: You can still make the combat/love die rolls, but you can't say "It's resolved now, for good!") then it's not the Czege thing.
    If the GM or other players author your "complications", but you are able to resolve them yourself (many games swing this way), then it's not the Czege thing.

    \n This may or not be a tangent, but I've been noticing lots of discussions about how Conflict can screw up games because sometimes we can have a scene that reveals something about a character without a life-shattering conflict and so on....

    And I want to point that for some players the point of play is to reveal who the character is. That's what scenes are for. Sometimes this is done in an "OhMyGODFuckSHIT!" climactic sort of battle, and sometimes its how a father takes the time to tuck his daughter into bed.

    Certainly it's what I play for.

    Thus, choosing between whether a PC uses his strongest trait or a weaker trait reveals who the character is. As Jesse says, it's not "do the best thing possible." It's "I could do the 300 version of Lord of the Rings and beat the snot out of Frodo to get him to do what I want, but I won't, because that's not who I am, so I'm taking the riskier approach by talking to him."

    The point of using the smaller dice (or the larger dice) is that it's tied to the fictional/color attribute that define who the character is. That's the part that matters when choosing the dice. Not the winning or the losing; its about revealing the character.

      The issue at the core of your question is, I think, one of conflict of interest, right? As in, "Why would I be expected to do something that doesn't get me what I want?" right?

      \n

      As Jason says,

      \n

      Sympathy is its own beast, and very important.

      \n
      \n

      Lemme throw down an example here.

      \n

      Dave Cleaver played with Dan Ravipinto, Ben Lehman, and me in a game of Shock: a couple of years ago at Dreamation. The setting was Europa a couple of centuries in the future. It was a society with very few people, a clan system of sorts, and an ethic of conservation over the wellbeing of individuals. His character was a proselyte, come from Earth to bring people Jesus once a dome had been built for comfort and faster travel for convenience. He was expecting to show up, eat people's mushrooms, take their electricity, and in return he'd give them Jesus. He didn't speak the language well, had a tenuous grasp on the rest of the culture, and was totally off-base about the sociosexual mores that had evolved over the last couple of centuries in this weird, lonely, freezing desert. Dave really didn't like this guy at the outset. He set him up to be sort of a city mouse in a complex society that he assumed was primitive and just waiting for him to save them.

      \n

      Over the course of the next couple of scenes (including, naturally, an apocalypse of sorts), the guy became a totally sympathetic character. He wound up being killed and his wife and erstwhile lover (one from Earth, one a Canny \u2014 a Europan native, living in a can) formed a new moral movement in his stead. If I recall, he piled those dice into his moral movement and didn't use any to save his own life. The character, in narration, tried to stay alive, but totally failed. He was sympathetic because he was trying so hard to not be such an ignoramus. In the end, though, Dave had to choose which he wanted to happen more in the story.

      \n

      There was no conflict of interest here. The character was acting in his own best interest. Dave might have even been hoping the character would be OK, but the system never gives you enough dice to make both outcomes likely. It's unlikely enough to get one of the things you want.

      \n

      Authority is distributed in Shock: so that you always have pressures on the character. It's assumed that, whatever you want, you'll have to make a choice, and that comes from the order of operations in the course of resolution. And even if your Intent is something like, "I look stupid in front of everybody" (coyly setting yourself up for failure), you still need to choose between that and "The people refuse to follow you." That is, you might succeed in looking stupid and the people follow someone stupid, and now you have to deal with that. Or you succeed and they refuse, or whatever. You still need to allocate dice and you need to overcome the dice and senses of humor of the Audience members.

      \n I just completed a campaign using a homebrew system (currently written in French, so I guess it's pointless to link) which uses weaknesses for players to screw up their characters without setting themselves for failure (thus probably avoiding Czege's principle trap):
      \n
      \nPlayers describe what they do, until they are stopped by the GM who requires a die throw under a certain type of expertise. The player can then indeed throw the die, using his character's best fitting Trait (or none at all, if non available). Otherwise he can invoke an appropriate weakness, which bypasses the die throw but forces the player to describe an epic fail due to the character weakness. In return the character earns an Hero Point, the basic game currency which can be traded for different advantages later in the game.
      \n
      \nSo in this case:
      \n
      \n- The player (and the character) were trying to succeed, and only later decided to use the weakness, when the GM required a die throw. This avoids players hogging attention and speaking time to describe narcissist problems and failures affecting their characters.
      \n
      \n- Although the character fails epically, the fact that the player has narrative control allows him to produce the most personally satisfactory type of failure (kind of the stylish way Indiana Jones often fails, instead of some lame failing).
      \n
      \n- The player can only invoke a weakness when it's appropriate to the type of die throw, which reinforces the character theme in the group narrative. If you don't have an appropriate weakness for the die throw, you just can't decide to fail.
      \n
      \n- One can't use this mechanic to "break" the game, since the die throws are always called by the GM, and the GM only calls for die throws when s/he's ready to manage the possibility of success/failure of the die throw.
      \n
      \n- It's often rational and desirable to play a weakness, specially when the odds are against the character, since although you fail for sure, at least you get more narrative control plus acquire significant bonuses. I find it an intuitive way for players to give color to their character's failings.
      \n
      \n
      \nNothing of this is original (it's a mix of Fate and Trollbabe failure systems), or perhaps even on topic of the thread, but I find it a good example of how to hose your character without annoying/boring the other players, and/or breaking the system.

      \n Another issue is the success of the overall game or story, which everyone might or might not be concerned about.

      I recently played one of my own games, Land of Nodd, which requires other players to name dangerous consequences for your character. One of the players, when faced with such dangers, would sometimes say, "Wait a minute. My character's leg is blown off? That's not so scary. How about my leg gets blown off AND it means his men no longer want to follow him? That's better. That's really scary."

      He was raising the danger for the character until the overall game and the overall story was interesting enough for him, even though doing that in this particular game could potentially screw his character AND his overall chances as a player.

      As a non-RPG example, have you ever played Chess and had your opponent make a really stupid move... and then asked them to take it back and make "their real move, already"?
