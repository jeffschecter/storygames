\n Ostensibly the appeal of randomisation is that it's impartial, and that the chance of failure coupled with it actually happening some of the time makes success actually feel good. There's probably something to this, as it matches up with operant conditioning.

Most systems I've seen* are based on average results, and when I play them in a "test" fashion, with no emmotional involvement, they always seem to go ok, with a mix of good and bad. However, when it gets serious, and I have a run of bad luck with rolls, it just starts sucking.

What can be built into a game to make failure still enjoyable? Or, what can be built in to a game to guarantee there won't be a string of failures forever?

*I realise this probably means I just need to see more systems

    \n When I Story Jam with my group, we don't roll dice. Options for randomization exist in the method, but I've discovered that except for trad gamers who are just starting to experience that kind of play, no one chooses randomization.

    And yet, the characters we create fail constantly. Sometimes we throw them into the darkest, most hopeless places for sessions and sessions, where they strive against tremendous adversity.

    When we do bring randomization into SJ, we do it by saying "Figure out two outcomes that the group can see*. Determine one by flipping a coin.

    My opinion: Failure can be wonderful. Loss can be narratively awesome.

    Taking the story somewhere that folks know is wrong because a random number generator says so, on the other hand, is something that I can't get behind.

    I'm curious why this one slipped me by. Would there be anything to saying this is an issue of conflict vs task resolution?
    \n
    \nIt is a prerequisite for effective conflict resolution, yes. Using conflict resolution without ensuring equally interesting pass / fail / other outcomes leads to unfun. But its equally effective for task resolution as well.
    \n
    \nConsider: You are a thief...ostensibly a good one...you are picking a lock. Does: "If you succeed you get in, if you fail you don't" sound fun? Not to me. How about instead (as one possible example out of many) "If you succeed you get in, if you fail you get in, but the alarm is now going off"...or..."if you fail you get in, there's no alarm, but you leave a bit of evidence behind that might get tracked back to you"...or..."if you fail you don't get in yet and now a nosy neighbor is looking your way and wondering what you're doing there." In other words...dead ends are boring...new branches are interesting.
    \n
    \nAlso note: it is entirely unnecessary for the GM to outline the "if you fail, then" part in advance. As long as they are doing something interesting and fun with your failure, you don't need to know which of the above they might choose. If the GM can't envision an interesting new branch to introduce on a failure...then that's probably a time to just not call for a roll at all.

    \n You could reward the player for failure in some way, thus making failure more appealing.

    In a larp I ran, conflicts involved cards. High card won the conflict, but then the players traded the cards they played. So if you played a high card, now you get a lower one. If you lost the conflict, you got a better card out of it, for use later on.


    You can make failure entertaining on a story level, regardless of mechanical success or failure. Playing a game like Otherkind involves everyone agreeing to interesting outcomes for success or failure, then rolling dice to see which one happens.


    You could create a system where repeated failure is unlikely or impossible. Awarding a bonus to future rolls when you fail does this, or you could have some sort of card/dice system where future results trigger off of current ones. Say you have a game where you draw cards, and whoever draws the "failure" card loses the conflict. But they then keep that card until the next one, and can draw from the conflict deck before reshuffling the "failure" card in.

    \n Talking to the people involved is wise, about any problem in game play. Some solutions if this continues though, on the mechanics side is to find games which don't rely so much on luck for their play.
    \n
    \nMarvel Saga (Marvel Superhero Adventure Game) uses the Saga system and is powered by cards. While you can get a hand of bad cards, the rules are set up to allow you to still make an impact on play by choosing which cards go where, or utilizing other rather nifty mechanics to shape play. Playing under "Edge" with low cards lets you play more than one low card, Edge is essentially "Experience" so a higher edge means you can play higher value cards under that mechanic, which means you see an impact on play that is significant.
    \n
    \nHearts & Souls (my own superhero game) allows you the player--in your "in character" voice, to monologue/pep talk yourself to reroll the dice without stress most of the time. The monologue is tied to your characters reason for being a hero and so as long as the two align you get re-rolls a plenty to make good. Even if you can't think of anything in the current situation, you can still get a reroll for a price. (Stress...which is what causes all bad things in the game to :fallout on your hero.)
    \n
    \n
    \nThere are other games with hero point mechanics (Marvel Superheroes Karma, Mutants and Masterminds hero points, or Buffy the Vampire Slayer's drama points) all which help shore up bad rolls. They're a patch for the randomness and work passably well for what is intended.

    Posted By: migoI've thought using a deck of cards would work for this kind of thing before, having guaranteed averaged results, but they seem very underused considering how beneficial they could be.
    \nOh yeah, the guaranteed averaged results could be used for much gaming goodness. Suppose you have a game where each player gets a single suit of cards, which is shuffled into a deck that they draw from, and that the game lasts exactly 13 scenes/conflicts/plays of a card. Then everyone ultimately has the same total effect on the game, but they may be picking from a randomized subset of that static total. More certainty here of balance than you would get with dice (where one player might roll high or low overly often).

    Not that good or bad dice luck are ever as bad as people think they are. We had a player constantly complaining of her chronic bad dice luck, until another player recorded her rolls and showed that they were dead average. The human mind is biased to find patterns, even in total randomness.\n
    Posted By: migoAnd also, strangely disliked. Are there any problems cards carry with them?
    \nHandling time, maybe. More difficult to do some existing dice tricks (dice pools, etc) and a slight increase in the difficulty calculating probabilities as each draw affects future probabilities. Legacy carry-over from dice based wargames and D&D. Nothing that can't be overcome, really.

    \n One thing that I personally find takes the sting out of a botch for me is a description that makes it not my character's fault.

    There's a temptation when a botch comes up to go with "You done fucked that one up good and proper, son." And if you've got a character concept that revolved around being excellent (or at least competent), that can suck. But when your failure is described as your opponent's success or an unavoidable twist of fate instead, it might feel better: that botched attack roll becomes your opponent's amazingly well-timed dodge. That botched bluff attempt becomes the unforeseeable fact that the guy you're conning is actually an expert on Nigeria and knows that not only are you not the exiled son of its king, you aren't even from Africa at all. The botched driving roll becomes you hitting an unexpected oil slick.

    Of course, if all you get are botches, you can't keep going back to this well. Everything has its limits. Mixing it up -- giving bennies as described above, not rolling the dice for some activities, finding fun/interesting consequences (you always break into the house, but do you set off the alarms?), etc. -- all seem like valid ways to avoid thinking "god, I really stink."

    \nYes, the thrill of risk is very real and obviously strong enough to be addiction-forming.

    But, I think that it is a mistake to convolute risk with randomness. When I narrate about and sincerely invest in a character in a collaborative story, I am playing a very risky game. I find that such a game is thrilling when my characters succeed, and it is thrilling when my characters fail. When I hand my control of the story over to the collaborative geist, if you will, I experience the thrill of risk-taking and the thrill of story. Having experienced that, it's hard for me (personally) to take pleasure in what feels like the emptier joy of chance-based games and stories.

    But, I do think I'm in the minority among story gamers in this outlook, and that's okay with me.

    \n That's a good point. From a play perspective I guess eventually someone will figure it out, but from a design perspective all the spells would have to have such rider effects at least.

    This thread and some sleep has really got my creative juices flowing. I just remembered a step dice system I thought up that determined how many attempts/rounds/seconds/minutes (depending on the action) it took to succeed. The better you were, the faster you got it, so someone really good rolls a 1d2, someone bad rolls a 1d12, eventually you'll succeed, but how long it takes is another question. There were some odds problems with this, but it still worked out to a degree.

    Another thing I just thought up is having skills increase based on failure. You learn from your failures, not your successes, so each time you fail you get a raise or a partial raise to your skill. If you really are having a lot of bad luck with your dice, eventually you'll hit a point where you're so good it's impossible to fail most actions, while other characters who succeeded a lot early on aren't as good and are failing more. It could work any number of ways, I was thinking of having a skill based on a tally, each time you fail you add one mark to the tally. Once you hit 5 (the cross mark), that constitutes an increase in your skill. In a dice pool system this could be doubly cool, especially if the successes determine how much you succeed - the character who fails more ends up being capable of much more impressive stuff later on than the character who fails less. If things are just a matter of perception, you'll be able to tell because everyone has roughly the same stats. If one character has every stat maxed to the limit, then you can really see their dice are cursed, and potentially do something about it.
