    \n Interesting, Filip, although I think perhaps you could be reading too closely to the imperialism angle. Remember, most Americans have no direct connection (or knowledge) of their country's foreign adventures.

    It seems to me that DnD addresses more "everyman" issues in American culture, such as:

    Racism is okay, because its all true!

    Violence is a meaningful, constructive solution to the problems the players must face.

    Players do not need to know anything about the outside world (the dungeon) before they go mucking about in it, but that's okay, because discovery is part of the fun!

    Also, I think the "points of light" approach supports these issues much better. You are right, dungeon exploration is very close to imperialism, but the new approach supports the heroic ideal in America, where one man with a gun big sword really can make a difference. This more closely represents the history of the game as played, and the culture surrounding it.

    \n The design goal of GURPS - one system for every game.

    Why? The designer was obviously, at the start of his gaming career, forced to learn new games at a break-neck speed, and then punished for his lack of system mastery. I can imagine the author as a young man, mercilessly defeated at a new tactical wargame by grey-haired fiends every week or so. He sticks with it, because this mirrors his childhood experience with board games - always being beaten by older siblings, aunts and uncles, who demonstrated a much higher mastery of the rules and used it to mock our poor author.

    Then he moves on to roleplaying games. This is back in the day when all games have more or less the same setup, but all games have various fiddly rules, charts and tables, that other people use to shame and humiliate the author. So he designs his own system which he can use for any setting, so he will never have to learn a new set of rules.

    [who wrote the original GURPS rules? Steve Jackson?]

    \n The Mountain Witch was a metaphor for the author's experiences at a technology startup. A small but capable team united around a single product, bound together by an initial infusion of venture capital, but as they worked towards getting the product to market, doubts crept in. One of them was thinking about trying to get the company bought up at the earliest opportunity, and jumping ship. One of them was recently married, and the long hours were making him consider a nice secure position writing accounting software. One of them was funneling internal communication to their competition. Cue the trust problems, the meetings that turned into shouting matches, etc.

    \n Basically take high powered weapons and armour, stick them on some guy who is the PC. Put him in a dangerous environment where something even more powerful could just happen along by chance even. Add in a village full of relatively fragile people in peril or under the reign of an evil protector.

    Press play. It's not terribly far from something like spidermans main structure - granted great power, fragile people mixed in liberally, villains never far away.

    The gung ho bit is to basically just take a war gaming framework and set this up in it. Just relying purely on the expressed (or trying to express) emotions in the set up of a pretty much straight forward table top wargaming system. Given how protagonism is kind of vulnerable, to have the guts to go for it in just a pure war game structure is...pretty gung ho. In an admirable, but slightly dangerous way. When people don't understand the emotions it raises, it's slightly dangerous. We have somewhat more of an understanding today. Back then I don't think anyone realised just how pissed off they'd get at 'No, your character wouldn't choose that' and suchlike until it happened, and when it did happen, it was mixed up with so many emotions and cultural expectations it'd take often take decades to straighten out. So...gung ho.
