    \n This totally depends on the game in question. Different games have different (often implied) methodology for scene framing. Many of them assume that the GM has a scene-framing duty that is fulfilled in service of player character intent; the GM's job in a game like this is to frame towards interesting events, but not to skip over any important choices. In a game like this the players have veto (often left out of the written game text because it assumes that the GM is not pushing this authority in the first place), so the correct procedure is to back off and allow the player his input; scene framing here only exists for the purpose of skipping towards the next important scene, not as an active method of wielding power.

    Often in a game like this you can get what you want (the player character in a compromising situation) by setting up a conflict of one sort or other in an earlier scene. Essentially you buy the right to compromise the character by establishing the character's inability to control himself. For example, if I want to have the player character wake up in a ditch in Dust Devils, I need to frame into the night before and request the player to run a conflict over his ability to keep from succumbing to his Devil, alcoholism. Obviously the player can give with no fuss if he doesn't have a problem with his character being a drunken sod, but the game's methodology requires me to give him a chance, so I'll have to establish the drunken binge in the game instead of just jumping ahead to the aftermath.

    \n What game are we even talking about here? Seriously, that kind of agency-shattering "scene framing" is not part of any of the usual games people name-drop around here. You don't do it in Primetime Adventures, Mountain Witch, Shadow of Yesterday, Burning Wheel, Sorcerer, Dust Devils, Apocalypse World, Polaris [draws breath] Dungeons & Dragons (well 4th edition you might, but still), or any but the most conceptual experimental proge designs that often for good reason are considered marginal. Games that utilize non-negotiable framing are often about powerless people to begin with; you might get something like this in My Life with Master, and even that game fails horribly if you don't have advance player buy-in for the fact that their character is beholden to a dysfunctional relationship.

    If your scene framing means that the players have to constantly be accepting that you play their characters off-scene, then we're not talking about mere scene framing theory-wise, but rather of a curious form of joint character ownership. That could be appropriate for some horror game where player characters have fugue episodes and alternate personalities. If players are getting the feeling that you're putting their characters into situations that they have no buy-in for, then you're using scene framing as a power tool.

    Now, I can get where a GM might come from and end up working like that. The game's point often is all about putting the characters into compromising positions and seeing what happens. It's seductively easy to come to the conclusion that your job is easiest to perform if the players don't get any say on what sorts of scenarios their characters end up in. However, the difference between a philosophical thought-exercise and a dramatic roleplaying game is in player buy-in, and you can't get that if you just tell the player that now he's gonna be playing a serial killer ("New scene, you've just murdered somebody, what do you do?") or whatever. You need to talk these sorts of things over with the players in one way or another, through game-play or explicitly in planning.

    In practice hard scene framing is not about players meekly accepting whatever, but rather about careful groundwork and conditional statements. I can frame a character into a fight with his father because it has been established earlier that their relationship is problematic, for example. Or I can frame the character into a drug-abuse orgy (to pick a famous example from TSoY) because I offer the frame in a suggestive tone, waiting for the player to affirm that it accords with his character vision before proceeding. If a player has a complaint about a frame, then I need to back up and work with the player to establish his terms.

    Scene framing is about recognizing the important fictional elements and going directly for them, not about putting player characters into spots they don't want to be in. Fluid scene framing can only grow among a group with trust: players can only let go of detailed fictional positioning when they truly believe that they're not going to lose the game because of it. For this reason, too, your scene framing needs to always be consensual; if players do not have choices about accepting your frames, then trust will never grow. There is no trust without freedom.

    What this process looks like in practice is, you always give way when players have problems with your scene framing. Yes, you redo the frame as many times as necessary. And specifically, you talk about the fictional positioning of the player character as long as the player needs to feel comfortable. Say that I wanted to frame a murder attempt on a character while he's in the middle of a shopping mall. "You're doing your shopping at the mall when..." I start, but the player interrupts. OK, doesn't your character ever go to the mall? Doesn't need to be the next day, could be a week from now. OK, let's say not the mall then, but he does get out of the house at some point? Would a movie theater be OK? Surely he doesn't just sit home all day watching the door? We've established that he's an adjusted and productive member of society, right? Sooner or later you'll get a baseline understanding of how the player sees their character, and then you can use this joint, common, consensual understanding as the basis for your framing. "After you come back from Joe's poetry bash..." will fly with the player because he participated in developing Joe and his character's poetry habit, and thus garnered buy-in for the fact that indeed, his character might well be walking about town at night because of something like that.\n
    Something like this:
    (calm down, breath deeply)"Oh, you must have some strong feelings about this. Do you want to talk about it? Should we take a break? And if you're ready, we can continue with the game as well."

You need to talk over the method of the game and come to an agreement on whether it's kosher for the GM to decide on scene framing unilaterally. (Assuming the game is not built on that premise to begin with - presumably this is not the case if we're having this discussion in the middle of the game.)

    \n Graham, here's what I mean:

    If we're playing a game and it's my turn to frame a scene of whatever I want and I say "I want to see a scene where your character has a conversation with the super-villain on his private yacht," and that's cool with the rules and the spirit of the game, but you still get tight about it, that's probably you being a big baby (I mean, you chose to play this game, right?)     \n I did what Graham did and read the City of Fire and Coin. Looking very good, Epidiah! (Epi's one of my favourite designers, he always pleases me.)

    Epi doesn't go into the details of how these types of games are played in the text, but Swords Without Master seems to be on the farther edge of the continuum between experiental and dramatic gaming: character experience is secondary to storytelling interests in many of the game's procedures. Characters are not set in stone, but rather discovered through storytelling. Also, the game is deliberately situated in trusting, intimate creative space by opting to provide players with wide authorities regarding e.g. scene framing.

    The important lesson to take from here is that there is no guarantee that this type of game, or any type of game, will work for all crews. Some games are more intimate and trust-based than others, and this is an important dividing line to know. In this case I'm reading the text and seeing a game that will only work if the players are consistently capable of creative cooperation: you need to be able to frame scenes that are fun for all, you need to be able to describe your character's actions and nature in a compelling way, and so on and so forth. The game text doesn't provide any real tools for what to do with creative discordance because it's the sort of game you would only play with a same-minded crew that is capable of dealing with these creative issues themselves. It's a game where I would stick close to genre conventions and not take many creative risks if I were playing with strangers; creative concord is built over time, we have to show others with small signs where we're willing to go and what we're capable of appreciating.

    Because we're discussing scene framing, it should be noted that the way SwM handles scene framing (aggressive GM control) is a model example of features that makes the game only functional in a trusting creative environment. Where the game text says that the GM gets to pick the next scene what it really means is that the GM should pick the next scene with an eye on player buy-in (which is a function of established plot and characterization), and ready to renegotiate if he manages to invent something unacceptable to the other players. That latter part is often not said in a game text like this because it already assumes a priori creative concord. You don't even get the total failure state (the one where the GM has to discard his idea and try again) in practice in a group like this, as everybody's riffing so strongly on the same creative wavelength and the level of trust is so high that it's a once in a blue moon event that you'll stumble on something that is not acceptable.

    In summation, what I think we have here is different assumptions about the underlying social context of play. Epidiah doesn't even acknowledge the possibility that the GM might flub a scene, so he doesn't address the event with a special rules procedure. The missing procedure is definitely not "players just need to suck it, bitches", though; my experience has always been that the natural and functional procedure that Epi leaves unsaid is consensual in nature.

Could this happen, he asks? What pre-established fiction or rationale would have to go into making this scenario coherent with your character concept? None, she says - under no possible circumstance could that situation occur to my character.

    Now, you and I and the Internet know this is not true, assuming the character is indeed of human origin and have a personality which could change over time, which is what drama-play is all about. What the player more likely was trying to communicate was I, the player, do not want such a scene to occur in this game, given my current frame of mind and the dynamics of the table. If this is true, she failed to communicate this fact twice: once when Peter asked about Lines and Veils, and again when asked about the test scene.

    Furthermore, you and I and the Internet know that in order for drama to be compelling, it has to touch us in unfamiliar and possibly uncomfortable ways. Player discomfort is a powerful though dangerous tool in a GM's toolkit, and the best way to use any such tool is to read the safety warnings, create a safe environment, and test it out carefully until you can wield it proficiently. It seems to me that's what Peter was trying to do - maybe not flawlessly, but this certainly doesn't read to me as an "RPG horror story" with an abusive, sexist GM. In fact, my guess is that Peter framed this test scene not because the player or PC was a woman, but specifically because the player had indicated discomfort with a certain kind of sexual content, so he tested out a different - lighter - form of sexual content to ensure that'd be both OK and possibly a bit discomforting - and therefore conducive to drama - to the player.

    I'm suggesting here that Peter didn't do anything wrong in framing this test scene for this player in this circumstance, although it may have been more conducive to ask for clarifications by private email ahead of the game instead. Rather, I feel Peter fulfilled his GM responsibilities - maybe even went above and beyond - but the situation broke down due to poor communication, mostly it seems on part of the player in question. If she had reservations about any sexual content involving her character, she should've been up-front about this during the Lines and Veils section.

    Reply: "No. My character could never, ever, have reached a point where she could possibly have done that, no matter the internal and external stressors in her life."
