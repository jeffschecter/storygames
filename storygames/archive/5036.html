    \n Bog-standard for general rpg books is one quarter-page illo for every four pages. BUT, there are a lot of variables.

    1) You can easily get away with far fewer but higher-quality/larger illos. See, The Mountain Witch, Shock:, etc. I'd go so far as to say this is a trend in the indie movement necessitated by economics but supported by an actual aesthetic.

    2) If you have a lot of charts and tables, you can get away with fewer illos. The idea is that one of the illo's functions is to break up long stretches of text. Charts and tables can serve this function as well.

    3) The nature and style of your layout can impact this as well. An "artistic" layout style can obviate the need for some of the art.

    4) Format and size play a role. Smaller books can necessarily get away with less art.

    Hope that helps.

    \n Robert, do you have a sense of the book's form factor yet? That might influence what kind of art you're looking for.

    Also, you might consider whether you want fairly homogeneous artwork or if you'd rather include a variety (different styles, media, colors, etc.). The Shadow of Yesterday uses art from several different artists, which serves to reinforce the differences in the cultures of the game. On the other hand, having a unified style (by using a single artist, or a very similar style) can pull the whole book together and give it a strong identity (such as with Spirit of the Century).

    \n Let me throw my informed 2 cents in here.

    The advice given has been pretty good. I don't have any problems with anything I've read so far. And I personally think that "indie" games can get away with less art than some of the bigger publishers... partly because focus is usually narrower... even the wide open world of The Shadow of Yesterday is a much narrower, tight setting than Forgotten Realms.

    Think about where the art can do the work faster than writing. It takes about 2 seconds to absorb about 90% of an illustration's info. How long does it take to absorb a paragraph? (not read, absorb, understand...). You can babble for a long time about the heights and weights of 8 races in each individual write up of those races, or I can show it to you in a height hierarchy illustration of all those races side by side in 2 seconds. That is just one example.

    As a playtester of your game Rob, I have more advice. Your game is about COOL and REBELLION and the CRAPPY FUTURE THAT OUR CHILDREN INHERIT.

    That is what the art REALLY should convey. I would look to Transmetropolitan GNs. 80s New Wave. Cutting edge graphic design in terms of the fringe cultures out there ... (graphitti, snowboarding, skatebording, street drag racing maybe?) and extrapolate 20 mins into the future from there. I would present the "corporate" part of the text ( the rules, the mechanics) in a real safe, sans-serif font... boring, by the book. Present the flavor text in something easily read, but a bit radical and fun, like

    Simpson font
    or
    BB on Mopeds font
    ... these two were with just a few moments of searching... and the trick is to print some of these out at like 8 or 9 pt and see if it is readable... because I would ALWAYS lean towards the readable over the 'oohh... its so kewl & evocative..." font.

    Something like Slammer Tag could be fun for the section headers....
    Slammer Tag font

    I would like to see the art be long, narrow verticals, instead of the more common 1/4 spot and the 1/2 page horizontal that you see in most fantasy stuffin's.

    Posted By: Robert BohlSome of the feedback I'm getting says write and layout first, then art. Jason, you seem to be saying art first. Am I right about that? If so, how do you make the decisions?
    \nIn my day job, manuscript goes to layout along with finalized art. Before writers are even hired, I have a bookmap that lays out how long each section is and what goes on which page. I make a list of necessary art (diagrams and illustrations that convey important information), place those in the bookmap, and then scan over the whole thing for visual interest. In general, I try to have one item of visual interest on each spread -- an "item of visual interest" is either a piece of art, a chart or table, a header, or some boxed text or sidebar. A spread with just text is boring. The eye needs someplace to go. So any spread that doesn't have something already gets incidental or spot art, and those get added to the list of necessary art.

    I write art specs (do I need to unpack that?) and send the specs to the artists about the same time I send out the writing assignments to the writers. The submissions and roughs come back about the same time. A few cycles of edits later, I have final manuscript and final art. These go to design together, so the layout folks have all the assets they'll need to lay out the book.

    I should point out that the final product NEVER matches the initial bookmap. The bookmap is a guide, not a dictate. Things shift around and get repurposed in edits, and occasionally in pages.

    Make sense? Any help?

    \n I'll reiterate what folks here have been saying about getting most of your writing done first. You'll likely be retooling your text as you go through layout (with many passes by your eyes and others), but it's best to get it pretty solid first. You can adjust the text around visual elements after the fact.\n
    Posted By: Robert BohlSome of the feedback I'm getting says write and layout first, then art. Jason, you seem to be saying art first. Am I right about that? If so, how do you make the decisions?
    \nI think the actual pattern we followed was Writing > Planning Layout > Art > Actual Layout, with a bit of rewriting at the end to pull things together. It's helpful to have a good idea of your probable layout before you try to contract your artwork, but you can't know that until you have the bulk of your text in hand.
