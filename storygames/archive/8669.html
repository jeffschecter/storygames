    \n I live in Los Angeles and I found players I love playing with... Not only for my own group, but folks who show up at local gatherings (local conventions, games people form up at local game stores and so on.)

    It took some time, but I attended several local conventions and got to know the people who played the games I liked playing. By the time I felt I knew enough people to build a group of regular gaming, I had a list that was too long. Is started going down the list. When I had three players, I stopped. If I needed to, I'm pretty sure sure I could put together another group pretty quickly.

    One of my players is my best friend in Los Angeles, another writer who years ago did writing for R. Talsorian RPGs. He stopped playing the games years ago for the same reason I did -- he found the games boring. He wasn't a part at all of the local gaming scene and knew nothing about what was happening online or with new games. But I talked the games up, and he came with me to one of the conventions. He played in a terrific game of Primetime Adventures, Sorcerer & Sword and a few other games and got hooked.

    The other two players I met at conventions. I loved their excitement for play and I could tell we lined up in terms of what we wanted out of games. I knew that if they were at my table I'd have a great time. So I gave them a call and asked if they wanted to play a game of Sorcerer. They said, "Yes." Bam. A group.

    I would say I'm very lucky in that the local convention scene had two great guys (Paul Tevis and Denys) who did a lot of ground work to make it more convenient to play the kinds of games I liked. (Quick example: most conventions assume, for whatever reason, that Six Players is the proper amount of Players to have at a game table. If you submit a game to the convention schedule and say that your cut off for the game is three players, it is assumed you are slacking. But, of course, if you're Playing HeroQuest in Glorantha or Dogs in the Vineyard or Pendragon or Gray Ranks with six people the quality of the game can sink fast. Paul and Denys worked hard to re-work the convention policy so that GMs could determine the ideal cut-off for their games, making the fun-value of each game much easier to maintain.) In this way, the folks who either wanted to try out new games or wanted to play more-more-more of these games could gather, meet, and exchange contact info.

    So, here's the thing, for me, in the context of your post: If I wasn't playing the games I wanted to play (we've got a game of Don't Rest Your Head coming up next) I wouldn't play the games. I wouldn't go retro. I'd do what I did years ago, which would be find something else to do with my time that I really wanted to do.

    There's not really an "RPG-thing" I want to be doing; there are specific games I like. I'm either doing that, or something else. (In the same way, I love Diplomacy and Monopoly, but I have no real desire to play Chutes and Ladders, even though they're all board games.)

    I'm just lucky that there happen to be games I like playing these days. I'm even luckier that the local convention scene is a hotbed of folks who like these games. Because, let me be clear about this, I didn't choose people who happened to like the games I liked. I chose people who, when I played games with them, I really liked playing with -- or, in the case of my friend Eric, someone I just liked being around.

    And, also... I only need three other people once a week or so to have the fun... "Ghetto" it may be, but how many people are we really talking?

    \n Well, I can throw in some second-hand anecdotes from the guy in our gaming group who really loves non-traditional RPGs; he doesn't play them in a regular group (instead getting his non-trad fix at conventions, since our group really never clicked with them), but probably could and would if the week had extra days in it.

    When he gets people together to play something like that, he says he tends to draw from two different groups:

    1. Non-gamers, ranging from people he works with to relatives to people he meets doing some other hobby (Nanowrimo or whatever). His pitch for that usually emphasizes "this is a fun creative thing to do for a couple of hours," talking up the story-making part heavily, and generally presenting the whole thing as a "I think you'd have a good time, and hey, it won't take very long" situation, the same way you'd pitch a game of Charades at a party or an impromptu game of cards on a train ride. He also tends to sell them on a setting or a concept ("Hey, I love Burn Notice too, let's play a game like that!") and keep the focus on that rather than stress the ruleset that they'll be using. Usually this turns out pretty well for him; I suspect that if he tried, he could assemble a solid weekly group of 4 (meaning people who really enjoy the game and will commit to a weekly session) within a half-dozen sessions or less.

    2. GMs. At conventions, he tends to zero in on habitual GMs rather than players, and he typically pitches games with shared narrative authority and/or distributed GM roles to them. He'll emphasize low-prep systems, too. Basically, he sells them on the idea of getting to have the fun of playing a game while still getting to have some of the fun of being a GM. Also, he goes after system wonks, wannabe designers, and inveterate tinkerers by emphasizing whatever unusual mechanics a game has. Again, if he had time in the week to fit in another night of gaming, I bet he could assemble a weekly group of 4 pretty easily there. To some extent, the other inveterate GMs he knows through conventions practically qualify as a group that meets 5 to 8 times a year. (I suspect from how he describes those convention games that -- although he hasn't said it -- he likes the non-trad games he plays with other GMs better than he likes the non-trad games he plays with non-gamers, or at least gets stronger and easier buy-in from them and therefore gets to the good stuff that much faster. Also, I think this only works because he happens to know or meet a lot of other people who tend to GM much more than they play. If he lived out in the sticks with no other gamers around, this probably wouldn't work out so well for him.)


    In both cases, he tends to avoid regular and devoted players of trad games, maybe because he's already getting his fix in that department from our own group, or maybe because the way that games like PTA flopped with us has made him think that it's just easier to look elsewhere, or maybe because he still enjoys getting to be a straight-up traditional GM and doesn't want to give that up. Which he does in fact enjoy a lot, he is quick to point out.

    (As a side note, I should probably point out that if a particular story game stays fairly close to the traditional GM/player arrangement, it generally goes over really well with our group. It's really only when that division is heavily blurred or entirely removed that half of us -- perhaps predictably, the ones who only like playing and either refused to try GMing a traditional game or tried and hated it -- stop having any fun at all. And if half of us aren't having any fun, all of us would just as soon play something else.)

    \n Loath as I am to fuel the Polish Inquisition, I will mention that my own gaming experience loosely parallels something like what he's suggesting. In my experience, the three ways you can get people to come to the table are:

    1. Super-casual social play. Hey, try this thing, it's cool. This one works really well with non-gamers and non-"geeks". The result: an amusing evening of light play of one of the small set of games that support that format. Fun, but rarely results in "recruitment" - at best, tends to make it easier to get a person back for another single-shot. Drawback: few games really shine in this format (or at least, few that tickle my particular fancy), and even the ones that do often don't show off a lot of their coolness on a cold first play-through... which is all you'll usually get.

    2. Joe Gamer. Pitch something that people already know and like - this can be either a system "let's play Mutants & Masterminds" or a high-concept "let's play Lost, only in space, with giant robots". I get a random assortment of people, mostly previously geekified, and then go through a period of attrition where people self-select out as the differences between the game on offer and the game in their heads become apparent. If we survive that process, fun will begin. Usually this only sustains a short-run game - the effort/reward ratio is too low for one-shots, and the durability of commitment is too low for a long-running game. D&D is surefire for this, but often comes with an expectation of long-form play that isn't actually sustainable.

    3. The "gaming group" - this is the group that plays a little bit of everything. If you have the social capital, you can get just about any short-form game up and going. However, this is usually largely based on social capital rather than genuine engagement with the specific game - so again, games that are off the beaten path don't tend to shine (since people just play them with the same techniques and expectations they bring to every other game). You can ride it out, just like you do with Joe Gamer... but the very fact that the group is not formed around THIS game means that it's usually easier just to move on to the next guy's idea.

    In all of these, there's a common difficulty in getting full-play-cycle, engaged play of most "off the beaten path" games. Your casual friends will play anything, but only once. Joe Gamer will play his favorite high concept, in any system - but will struggle mightily to sync up the concept as it exists in his head with concept as it exists in everyone else's head. And "the group" will play anything, but ooh-shiny will move you on before you get too far, and the ingrained social system of the group will usually utterly swamp the game's system.

    So I'm largely not trying to play anything other than various flavors of so-called "trad" these days. Coming to the game with that common set of expectations means that we can get to the fun, and that I can recruit from beyond my own immediate social circles with some hope of common expectations/interests. The regular "gaming group" that I'm a part of has a marked preference for longer-format, beer-and-pretzels escapism over just about anything else, so I'm not interested in expending my social capital with them on "weird games" that will not deliver the fun in the expected ways. The other groups that I recruit are single-purpose, recruited for THIS game, this concept - and in general I haven't had a lot of luck with off-beat concepts.

    One thing I am less and less interested in is getting to "play" a cool game for just a hint of its play cycle. That always leaves me feeling cheated.

    Which brings me around to the original topic, by the way. I expect that the Old School thing is more about low-footprint play than anything else. Everyone I know who's into it is either on a nostalgia trip, actively reactionary against "modern gaming" in general, or just likes that you can just roll some dice and GO, relying on the common D&D culture to make it work.

    My Old School tastes are pretty different, and I find them nicely served by 4E - since they're largely about the Gonzo High Weird, not the gritty Dungeon O Doom.
