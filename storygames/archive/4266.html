    Chris has never played the game and that shows in the review. He doesn't realize that the game is about people, not victims; the characters in SAJ (available over here) are the people who have a shot at defining their lives in a way that few humans do, ever.

    \n

    The game is a lot of fun. It's about doing what you can to become a free person. The mechanics make things tough for you, but they make things tough for everyone, including your owner and other oppressors. But you, unlike they, have a chance to do something heroic, to become free, to become a piece of history. Your owner just owns a plantation. You can live and die by heroic effort, helping your friends, defeating your enemies, and making it out despite all their efforts. Their interest in you is as cattle (or sometimes as a target of affection, but the power dynamic has more to say on that than you might like); your interest is in your humanity. Trying those against each other in fiction favors the humanity, and the whole game supports that.

    \n

    I had a good time when we played and I'd love to play more (I'd like to set it smack in the middle of the Civil War). I think my character, "Marshal" is in the book, so if you've got it, you can see what his bag was, at least in part. He was a Muslim blacksmith from Angola, a respected man in town, who'd been bashed on the head for getting on someone's bad side and illegally sold into slavery. His goal was to get a new family and become a tradesman again, and to eventually own a slave of his own (he'd had one back home). The target of his family aspirations was Ginny, who was pregnant with someone's baby, probably Emily's character's. Vincent was trying to get free with his brother (he did) and Meg's character was trying to run, as well, as he'd murdered a nasty, nasty man.

    \n

    Everyone got free in their own way but Emily's character, who wound up being taken down by the dogs chasing Vincent's character. Vincent's dude and his brother made it to Ohio, where they parted ways, unable to really address their past, including some of the things they'd had to do to get free. Meg's character escaped the death sentence, thanks to my framing someone else for the murder he did (the murder, by the way, was completely justified and the closest you could have gotten to justice for a slave), and Marshal got to give himself back his own name \xe2\u20ac\u201d Yusuf \xe2\u20ac\u201d and became a free tradesman with Ginny as his wife, having bought his freedom through ... well, basically fucking with the owner mercilessly. People called him "Joe".

    \n

    The mechanics are, in large part, about helplessness, but they're about doing whatever you can when you're helpless, not being helpless. You engage the mechanics as often as possible because you need a shot at good stuff, not because the dice are in your favor (which, oddly, doesn't seem to be something you can affect). So you do what you can to get free.

    \n

    The game is about freedom. There's not better way to address freedom than by making the protagonists slaves.

    I think that the game has a lot going for it mechanically. I like that the dice mechanic actually means that having more dice could be a penalty, because you can roll more ones. Personally, my favorite mechanic is that the GM names the character but the player has secret goals. I'm waiting to see how that works in play, but I have high hopes.

    I have some specific mechanical questions about the Root Doctor, but I may or may not include that in my game. Still haven't decided. I'd also love guidance on GMing this game, especially interacting with the secret goals that the players have. But maybe I'll just figure it out along the way.

I'd want a better thought flow from concept to concept. Currently it works okay, but I'd want it to be great! There are also some overlooked items.     \n I'm okay with constructive criticism. I have thanked both Matthijs and Chris for their reviews, and the appreciation was heartfelt. I don't have to agree with everything they say and still be gracious and appreciative that they took the time to review the game. When I sit down and work on Steal Away Jordan again, I'll have copies both reviews, AP reports, and all the other comments that people have put out there, to work with to improve the game. I may not use all the suggestions or comments because they may not be in line with my vision, and as such may not be helpful, but I'd be foolish to ignore them.

    When I was in college I took a writing class. I had to submit a writing sample in order to be let in. The professor accepted about 25% of all the students who tried to get in, so it was a big deal to be in the class. Some weeks my professor really liked my stuff. Sometimes he didn't. He was an honest, thorough, and occasionally, harsh critic. For our final project we had to go back and revise a story. I picked the one that got the harshest criticism (it dealt with a difficult, painful subject--see a pattern here?). I got an A and it was noted that I improved and revisited work that had a strong backbone but needed work, and I did the work. I thought it was some of my best writing the first time I handed it in. My professor thought so, too, and wanted to see me cut out the bullshit and make it publishable. So he was harsh and honest.

    Matthijs and Chris have no investment in me as a writer as my professor did. They're reviewers, they have opinions, they publish those opinions. It's up to them to be fair, but they don't benefit from being fair. It makes the criticism a little bit harder to take, especially since all eyes are on the writer to see what she and other people say next.. It doesn't make it less valuable. (so don't think that's what I'm saying!) I had a really fruitful conversation with Matthijs via email, which made the review a whole lot more helpful.

    Truthfully, a bad review sucks. Before the rational deconstruction of what is said, and the taking to heart and the noting where your blind spots were, there's the initial visceral sensation that someone just told you that you have an ugly, stupid child. And since you love your children despite their flaws, a bad review at first really smarts.

    I don't think anyone here is blindly defending the game.

    It would be foolish to take in a criticism and not examine it for its fairness and unfairness. It would foolish to ignore it altogether. We'd go nuts if we didn't listen to people say that our children are beautiful and smart when there's a spotlight on them and we first think others are saying they're stupid and ugly.

    So yeah, criticism is good. In addition to very constructive feedback, I keep hearing "I would never play this game,and I have no interest in telling that kind of story." That's not helpful to me when I revise, because it's not something I can really change. If anything, it helps me see who my audience is. If you don't want to play a game set in slavery in the US, where you are a black slave, you'll have to play a different game, because even after all the revisions and additions and improvements, the game is still a role playing game where you create a character who is a black slave in the antebellum US.

    Again, a review on rpg.net isn't my professor giving me feedback with his investment in me as a student. It's one person, sharing his experience with the text and/or with play. And the difference between a review and feedback is to me that the review is more for other players, not so much for the designer. So should a reviewer mark down a game because it's not his "thing"? For a player, that's a valid concern. For me, it's less so, especially since Steal Away Jordan is about something difficult, and maybe controversial. It does concern me if the reviewer considers the subject matter and how it plays to him and his friends as a negative issue.

      \nSo you can have no tangible magic and play straight historical narrative, or you can make a ghost story (there are NPC ghost characters), or do something in between. Beloved is a ghost story. Kindred is more speculative fiction than slave narrative. These two books were inspirations.

      We had a playtest where one of the PC's wrote as one of her attributes "I can fly." It was a one shot session at a game store, and we had players who were seasoned gamers, rusty gamers, and one woman who the store owner nabbed from a Star Wars game and said, "play this instead". I made it a ghost story. The PC who could fly was the Root Doctor, and her magical use of the herbs was serious magic. The story turned out to be more folk story than straight slave narrative. We still explored more adult themes, and I think the magical and supernatural elements diffused some of the tougher stuff. We also had a funny love story, and a bit of a power struggle between a slave midwife who knew herbs but not magic, and a mute, somewhat crazy Root Doctor who could fly and communicate with animals, and whose spells and potions worked. No one died. One PC escaped with the help of a young slave who was in love with him and the Root Doctor (who, I think didn't like the young slave). The Root Doctor worked for an undertaker. She procured a coffin, and the slave escaped in the coffin.
      One of the toughest issues we dealt with was with the midwife and her master, a very kind doctor who was actually in love with her, and kept promising to emancipate her, take her to a free state, and marry her. He never made good on his promise, and now he was on his deathbed (yellow fever). The ghost of his dead wife actually helped the slave realize that the only way she would ever be free was to get the doctor to sign the emancipation papers he'd drawn up, but never formalized. He signed the papers in a stupor (and also willed his estate to her), she waited at his bed dutifully until he died, and she left for a free state with his money. So there was the betrayal from her lover, but with the ghost, we kept it on the lighter side, which isn't to say we didn't have some good dramatic tension between slave and master. Perhaps his cruelty was that he held her freedom unnecessarily.

      The story was lots of fun and gleefully historically inaccurate. Women, especially free women of color were not allowed to own property. The yellow fever epidemic we created didn't actually come about until after the Civil War. Because the setting was a city, the slaves had more freedom of movement than plantation slaves would have, but these characters had more freedom of movement than most slaves in history. We played according to the mood of the players. No one really wanted a brutal game, and I wanted to test out playing a lighter, more fantastical game with supernatural elements beyond simple spells. I didn't know one of the players, one was my husband, who isn't a gamer, but played D&D as a kid. Another player was a good friend of mine whom I'd never played with before. She was the Root Doctor. She played herself and then some. And one player was someone I play with regularly, who has played one of the more brutal playtests.

      So you can put as much or as little magic in the game as is appropriate for the players. This is a trick I gleaned from Dogs in the Vineyard. Magic is a subjective thing. It can be a belief in magic or a true tangible element.

      Let me give an example from another game, basketball.       Similarly, the average happy gamer is quite capable of turning down decent games without too much introspection, and it neither makes them wusses nor cowards nor ahistorical assholes, it just means that it sounds different enough from what they're doing that they don't want to give up a sure-thing of a fun experience for a gamble of one that might be or might not be.
