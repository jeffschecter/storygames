\n So me and Graham have this beef going on - it's Bull City vs. North Side. It started out on the Game Chef forums but we're bringing it here and I'm going to settle his hash once and for all.

Remi's Game Chef entry, Young Adult RPG, is built to emulate Daniel Pinkwater novels. One feature of the game is that characters grow in a very regimented way - they exist in three sequential states, and their relationships to one another change in clearly defined ways. For example, the Bully starts out much like Graham in real life, but by the end of the game explicitly rejects violence and is a changed kid. Graham says:

Why can't the player decide how he grows: perhaps he channels his violence or uses it for good? The New Kidalways makes friends by the end. Why can't he learn to enjoy the way he's different and independent? ... I don't like this for various reasons. Firstly, it impacts replayability. Secondly, it means I can't decide things for my own character. Thirdly, it gets close to imposing a plot.

And I issue a savage rebuke:

Hey Graham, knowing the ultimate disposition of the characters isn't an impediment to gripping fiction, is it? It becomes a question of how the Bully comes 'round, just like it's a question of how The Roach ruins Pemberton. I don't see that as problematic at all.

Oh, snap! Anyway, there's more conversation in the thread I link to, including the requisite improv example and some John Ford name-dropping, but I think we're approaching the issue from remarkably divergent viewpoints for two guys who like the same kind of games. I'd like to talk about this more, particularly since there seems to be a disconnect between our thinking. Maybe more voices will help us find common ground! That usually works well, doesn't it?

Conversation starter: Does it help or hurt my case that D&D does pretty much the same thing that Remi does in Young Adult RPG? Character growth is regimented sequentially, and you enter specific, pre-defined states, right?

    \n Gaerik, it's a bit more than that. I think that prescribed story arcs take the fun out of roleplaying.

    The one thing that RPGs do really well is let us tell a character's story. We tell it, moment by moment, making decisions along the way. If you tell me, before the game, that my character will renounce violence and embrace peace, you've taken away a very fun decision. (What if I don't? What if I go evil?)

    Oh, I have a parallel argument: characters with story arcs are less fun. Contenders is fun because we don't know, until the end, whether the boxer will achieve his dreams or not. Every fight is exciting, because you can see those dreams receding or approaching. A character who follows an arc is duller, because you can see where he's going; a character who might change either way is more interesting.

    Let's deal with those examples.

    The progression in D&D describes how the character's effectiveness changes. It doesn't make decisions about the character's personality. If it did, it'd be crap. If you tell me "You're going to become an evil mage and scourge the world", it's kinda cool, but I'd rather decide for myself.

    The Roach analogy doesn't work, either. It's OK to constrict some bits of the plot. But not how the character develops.

    It's been a pleasure talking to you, Jason. It's OK that you were wrong. I'll await an apology.

    \n Interesting. Brennan, yes, I'd have no problem with a slide from naivete to corruption.

    Can I throw something out there? Let's imagine two games. First one:\n
    Graham's Awesome Game

    In Graham's awesome game, you play a character. At some point during the game, you must renounce violence! You decide when and how. Once you do, you become more powerful!
    \nNow, I think that's great, even though the game prescribes a choice for my character.

    Second game:\n
    Jason's Sucky Game

    In Jason's Sucky Game, you play a school bully. After your first conflict, you realise you love violence! Your second conflict must show this love of violence! But after your second conflict, you start to worry about violence. In your third violence, you still enjoy violence, but you decide you have to give it up. From then on, you mustn't initiate a fight. After your fourth conflict, you become a pacifist. Now you must run away from every fight.
    \nThis game sucks, because I'm thinking: why don't you just play the game for me?

    So why does my game RULE and Jason's SUCK? Obviously, it's partly because Jason's Sucky Game is more prescriptive.

    But it's not just that. The thing I like about my game, which ROCKS, is that I can still decide to have my character flip out and punch someone, at any time, as long as I renounce violence before or after it. In Jason's game, past a certain point, I can't have my character flip out.

    That's because my game has one discrete change in the character. For me, that's fun: it's a cool change but it doesn't prescribe the course of the whole game. Jason's game is more of a continuum: at any point, I know exactly how my character should be, and that takes the decision away from me.

    Does that make any sense? The game making decisions about my character is fine, because it leaves me room to make other decisions: but tying me to a character arc isn't okay, because that restricts me.

    \n Vaxalon & I have been trading emails for a week, trying to conceive of a mechanic which would reflect
    how people wear metaphorical 'masks' in social situations.

    One way that we weren't quite seeing eye-to-eye, is that he envisioned the mechanic empowering players via
    offering a Free Choice: between having one's character stay true to their self image/
    or else conform to an opposing-character's conception of them.

    Either decision would result in a positive reward. But
    the reward would be different depending which option was chosen.

    That's a nice, solid hippie mechanic; like Keys in TSOY.


    I, on the other hand, envisioned a mechanical decision-maker; rather than a reward-offerer.
    Player1 would consult the mechanic to see whether the way Character1 presented themselves would prevail/
    or whether the opposing Character2's impression of Character1 would overpower & 'win'.

    The system would determine the outcome, rather than letting any player choose (and then impose consequences
    to that free choice.)

    Vaxalon's idea hardly needs defending. Any number of recent games work that way, and function well.

    Whereas my idea feels out of fashion.

    I would like to believe the axiom that "players will tolerate unwanted things happening to their characters,
    as long as they feel those outcomes were arrived-at fairly; not personally directed at them."

    But I don't know whether that applies to filthy hippies. I think there's a belief around here-- something-like,
    "why should I tolerate being hosed when I don't wanna be, if I could be playing a game where I don't have to be hosed?"

    This isn't the first time I've linked to Narrativists -- A new breed of munchkin?
    but the response has always been silence.

    I'm not surprised that folks would be wary of the name-calling. But the questions raised feel legitimate to me.
