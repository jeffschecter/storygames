\n Just wanted to let all the interested folks here know that I'll be re-releasing a revised version of Story Engine called "Classic Story Engine, Plus Edition."

It features a complete reorganization of the rules for better clarity, optional rules, expanded health levels, and a few new sample plug-ins. Apart from some updated terminology to match my other games, it remains virtually the same, focusing on scene-based resolution rather than turns. This is not a third edition, rather an update (and an homage) to the classic rules, basically 2.5E. I also plan on releasing an add-on for the genreDiversion 3E rules, which will essentially be the third edition.

For those unfamiliar with Story Engine, here's an old review by Ron Edwards.

Let me know if you have any questions or would like to see optional rules that worked well for you in the past.

    \n Thanks. It's a question of new vs old. As per feedback of late, I'm not making a new edition as a stand-alone game, because it works better as an add-on for the much-in-common genreDiversion 3E Manual. At the same time, I think the classic version is a great game, but in need of updating. So I decided to keep the Classic game around in an official capacity... and while editing and reorganizing, I came to the conclusion that it needed a good refit. For instance, the different types of affinities are now broken down into Pursuits (trait affinities), Cultures (cultural affinity), and Gimmicks (prime affinities). This should make it easier to understand for those new to it and especially those familiar with my other games.

    No release date yet, but I'm aiming for next month.

    \n It's been a while but I'll see what I can recall.

    One thing was the idea about using words to convey the difficulty of something instead of exact feet. Instead of saying it's 10 feet wide and then arguing about whether it was humanly feasible for a person to "really" jump that distance, just say "it would be difficult but not impossible" and leave it at that. The idea that rather than establish a "reality", you could establish a feeling or emotion and let everyone at the table fill in what was appropriate for them. For one guy that might be 50 feet and another guy that might be 10 feet but the number doesn't matter, only that we agree on what the emotional impact of the world feature was.

    The scene resolution stuff also began to give me an inkling (though full understanding would come much much later) that "danger" and "action" was about much more than just who lived and died. The idea that we might have a fight OVER something and that it didn't have to end with one side or the other completely obliterated was new to me. The idea that who was hurt and how badly was separate from "did we protect the magic sword from the orcs" was pretty revolutionary.

    Those were the two big things I recall.

    Jesse

    \n From a design standpoint, contrast is important, so having every element in the same style is not always a good idea. Likewise, you wouldn't want to use the same font throughout the book for everything. Using a different font for headers is a good example of that contrast -- a sans-serif header when serif is used for the body text.

    A cogwheel could be steampunk, but I don't equate the two. A cogwheel could also be cognition, construction, or rudimentary. I'm just saying that there are different ways of looking at things. While some people may get the steampunk vibe, I don't.

Also, all the earlier releases of Story Engine had gears on their cover.
