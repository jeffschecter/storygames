\n Yesterday I finally tested an impro trick for the start of our D&D 5e campaign. Basically, when PCs are on the tavern or Inn (or well, really, any place at all) the GM asks the players: "What kind of rumours of interest you hear?" providing them with a couple of words for inspiration for each player to come up with a really basic premise for a quest. Then all PCs regroup and decide which quest sounds more interesting, and the GM just implements it.

It's working wonders so far. One PC heard a town was being attacked and it should had something to do with the evil god of the setting (which the players created themselves too). Another heard of a wondrous artifact, a mantle of some sort and rolled to see how easy would be to find a buyer if they went and stole it. The last player went to the bartender, who told him about an old battleground where he might still find magical weapons. The group decided to visit that place first. They noticed they were being followed by someone and tried confronting the guy, which ended up turning into another quest again.

Among the things I like the most from this trick is that the players come up with quests that actually interest them, that they can provide infinite ones and even collaborate to make another PCs quest more interesting. No longer railroading, asking them to collaborate or otherwise coax them into following specific quests. I love it that I don't know what is going to happen next and I'm as much surprised as them by whatever comes up.

Now that I don't have to guess what kind of choices the players would take nor otherwise influence their choices, I'm relaxed enough to deal with other parts of the game, like the encumbrance and hexcrawling. About the encumbrance, I found this amazingly simple way to handle it in lastgaspgrimoire.com. Now each player has a separate sheet for their items, which now I handle them as small post-its with their weight listed and it's up to them to decide if they carry it or not and deal with the encumbrance, or pass it to a friend with more space in their pack, or plain drop some other less important stuff.

Combined with hexcrawl stuff, now even the weight of the rations matters. They haven't even heard of tresure yet and they are already wondering how are they going to carry it with them once they get it.

So far I've got tricks to make up interesting NPCs and dungeons on the go; I can do monsters from scratch too but I'm not so sure I can make these too well while playing. Whenever I'm using monsters from the MM I prefer to prep in advance, as some monsters are more appropiate than others for different types of encounters. My players had fun, but I found a group of Manes I pulled randomly to be somewhat uninteresting. Then there's this "You see a nicely mounted reception hall with a red carpet and an old suit of armor in standing position holding a sword on it's hands" encounter I want to try, where all three are animated objects that work amazingly well as a group (carpet grapples, armour can tank a bit and both the armour and the sword can hit the poor bastard who got grappled by the carpet without damaging the carpet too much.

I'm thinking I'll still have to properly prep combat scenarios, to pull them and spice up whenever players come up with a quest where these can apply.

I'm also making prep from the consequences of whatever the party does, to show them the impact their character's actions have on the world.

How do you do to make up interesting stuff on the go? Any tricks you wanna share?

    \n I've used a similar trick once:

    One of the characters was alone in his car and I asked "You are listening the news on the radio, what slightly strange thing happened today in your city?"

    The player answered with a short story about some dogs dissapearing from a given neighborhood and then I reincorporated this fact into the story when the players ran out of clues to find the dangerous serial killer they were looking for.


    I think that the key for this trick to work is to ask many questions and then reincorporate only a few of them (the ones that fit better into the story).

    \n The mountain witch trick works in two singular ways:
    -the GM receives feedback from the players which acts as inspiration to further progress the game.
    -Players get into the creativity flow of the game, as they get rewarded everytime the GM or other players build upon their input.

    I like to think of it as an advanced random table whose results consider whatever the player has perceived from / expecting from / imagined upon the game so far. To make it flow better, the amount of information required should be as small as possible. I found that it all works better and faster when you:

    -Ask a player to connect two or three things.
    -Set a subject and ask the player for details about it.
    -Ask the player what could X character be thinking given the situation.

    At some points you don't even need to ask, perhaps players themselves will give their input as a joke, like "wouldn't be funny if Y happens right now?"

    The thing is to embrace player input as long as it's barely possible / logical / follows the consequences from anything that has happened before. But for this to work, it's best if the player's input is kept short. You can easily put a strange event into the world through the radio, a rumour of something that can be either false, truth or just deformed. Anything that suggests things instead of imposing them. That way the GM can keep things under control, as all we need is some inspiration.

    About identifying the important stuff, well, what matters most here for the GM is "what's next?". And I mean like, inmediatly next. Do the players decided where to go? Who's going to show next in front of them? They have a lot of information now, but what could it all mean?.

    If you've got ideas and inspiration enough from the players to answer that, the next question is "how can I make this better?". Detail, cinematic narration, plot twists, everything can be used and improved at least a bit, even if it's just by mixing it with something previously done.

    Use player input sparingly, oh, and if you're in the middle of the session, it's a lot better to ask things addressing the characters, not the players, to prevent breaking their immersion.

    \n Oh yeah, it's one of those moments where as hard as it may be for the GM one must humbly acknowledge the players creativity and say "you know, you're right, f*ck my prep, let's redo the session using that"

    One of the first times it happened to me, I hadn't time to prep well and was railroading players along a setting supposed to be a sandbox. I was even using a randomly generated dungeon from the web, one sad thing I'll never ever be doing again. In the middle of the dungeon they half-jokingly say: "frag the factions (who were railroading them) let's start our own cult!"

    And instead of blocking them as any other GM would do (I've never heard too many accounts of players starting their own religion in the game, now seen too many adventure modules or supplements based on this concept, so I suppose GMs normally block players who dare go that way) I went for it and made some rulings on the spot to enable them to make their own religion. It went so good that soon it wasn't them being chased by the factions around the setting, but standing their ground and challenging them. They became another important faction in the setting and forced a better situation than if they had confronted each faction on their stronghold or made all kind of diplomatic moves on them. This was one of the things that made that entire campaign memorable.

    The other was a hack I made of Microscope. Out campaign had kept all along the mistery of what had happened with humankind a thousand years ago, as it seems they were more developed than now but went through an apocaliptic event that came to define the current status quo. So, as the PCs visited the ancient ruins of a long lost human city, they faound what had happened. Except that instead of me prepping that and telling them through a long recording making lots of exposition, we played through it.

    I had the players make new PCs. Their stats didn't matter, we just stated they were epic top tier characters. They got to make their own equipment. Any sci-fi technology could go. They had giant mecha, orbital lasers, nanobot swarms, etc. We listed them all on separate cards and that was it. Then I randomly handled one behavior condition for each character, more of a "modus operandi", like "you prefer a pacific solution", "you won't accept a pacifical solution", "Your people comes first", "Science and development comes first" and such.

    Then they all faced an invasion of interdimensional aliens using whatever they had made up, and rolls informed us about how successful it was against the aliens. They barely stopped the first wave and after the aliens sent an ultimatum, all the PCs gathered through video-conference to coordinate a solution. Here's where their modus operandi (which they kept secret until then) came into play and to our surprise, it went as we figure any international peace conference goes, with people agreeing to disagree, some plans got coordinated and some never came to be, which it all ended up further dooming the whole setting.

    It was awesome, better than anything I could have written at that moment. Since then I've been amazed with the potential of content creation the players have when it's properly guided, and the positive effect this has on the game Flow.

    \n I've built a lot of this style of play into my OSR rules, including a mandatory, randomly determined, open-ended question put to each new character to answer about their place in the world. But best of all is now we have two high-level clerics with the spell Commune which means that pretty much every week one of them is drawing a new island onto their world map or \u201cdiscovering\u201d a powerful magic artefact in a distant land, once they run out of immediate questions to ask about things that I've already prepped. One of them basically wrote an adventure outline for the whole spring by inventing five locations relevant to another player's Quest (as in the spell where you have to do something or die, which also calls for this same kind of play).

    I don't usually do on-the spot dungeons beyond a simple cave system or something like I would for a random encounter, although I do add things to my existing maps. But I make a lot of notes to put things on the edge of my prepped areas, relying on their travel time to buy me a day to do full prep, and now (gosh I've been running this game for five years) I think a majority of the big world-shaking NPCs or enemies are ones that someone made up when they were rolling up a level one years ago.

    \n That's great adamwb! And yes, player creations are always the best. Usually all you need is some filtering, mostly to adjust the campaign to what the players expect/want to do.

    It might be useful to stall players a bit sometimes. Like, when they come up with something and go to that place, you can always let them waste a bit of time buying things for the travel and throw some obstacles or a random encounter at them along the way, so they reach the place right at the end of the session. That gives you until the next session to prep the place properly if you want to surprise the players and work on lore/tactics a bit more. Otherwise you can count on the players to come up with most of the rest.

    Making dungeons on the spot can be tricky depending on what you want. Randomizing rooms and connections may prove chaotic in terms of the function and history of the dungeon. However if you keep at least thematic coherence for the whole dungeon things can go smoother. I'm currently working on the third version of my ResourceFull deck that features 4 random tables that I've found really helpful when improvising dungeons: general shape of the room/corridor, exit, triggers for traps and traps themselves. Add a theme, a random encounter and maybe a puzzle and you can get a really interesting dungeon from these variables.
