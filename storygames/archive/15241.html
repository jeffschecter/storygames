    \n Piers,

    Do you have a sense of what *kind* of competitive element your friend is looking for?

    There is a big difference between these three styles of play, for example, and not all may interest the same person:

    * We're playing D&D. We complete to see who can make the best character build by optimizing her character's abilities, and then we compete to see who can defeat the challenges thrown at us by the DM first. Whose mastery of the rules is best?

    * We're playing Baron Munchausen. We compete to see who brings the highest level of theatrical flair and excitement to the table! Who will impress the most?

    * We're playing a tactical wargame: we match our wits against each other to find out who is the best strategist. Who is smartest? Who can outwit the others?

    Most of the games suggested so far... might help scratch one itch, but fail entirely at another. I think you need to talk to your friend some more and see if you can get a little more information!

    \n 3:16 isn't PVP by default, but Hutton puts a lot of carrots in there to provoke players into fucking with each other. The Corporal kicks a lot of ass, and will almost always get the most kills (you automatically 'level up' by having the most kills in a given mission) due to his crazy weapon. But the Sergeant outranks him--nothing is stopping the Sergeant from ordering the Corporal to turn over his weapon. Nothing is stopping the the Troopers from forcing the Corporal to make poor tactical choices (via overzealous use of grenades, or their special ability 'Force Weakness').

    So there is PVP there, but it's sort of subtle and you're not really allowed to be open about it ("Do you know what the chain of command is? It's the chain I beat you with..."). If you're playing a short one-shot type game, most players won't think to fuck with each other. As the game progresses, though, it becomes more obvious that you should maybe cock the other players about.

    \nBeast Hunters by Christian & Lisa Griffen is a role-playing game designed for real competition, with honor and good sportsmanship in mind.

    For contrast, the amount of so called "pvp" in a Fiasco or In a Wicked Age game is not really competition, bur rather a particularly aggressive attitude you take within an assumed and well-established context of cooperation. It's, to a degree, a mock competition, with lots of drama and posing as we show off how cutthroat we can play, but really we're cooperating at making story and staging said mock competition and everything, or the game would break.

    \n Simon:
    The "cloud-to-dice" thing is not an issue, IMO. Judgments based on fictional considerations have been part of hardcore wargaming since its invention. This is what Judges did in the classic German military training exercises, and many tactical tabletop wargames employ a Referee to make mechanical rulings based on fictional circumstances.

    "Cloud-to-dice" doesn't inhibit competitive play at all.

    Also, consider the importance of proper role-play in those scenarios. Even if it's possible, it's not proper for a player to issue guerilla warfare commands to his Napoleonic army during a battle. Doing so might increase his chances of winning in the absolute sense, but would be out of bounds for play, whether or not a "no guerilla actions" rule was in place.

    I think, (too often among gamers) "competitive" is taken to mean, "exploit everything to your advantage with no other considerations," which is, of course, ridiculous. No competitive contest in the real world is carried out that way.

    This is where "playing to win makes the game suck" comes from. That person who is making the game suck isn't playing the game. They're playing some kind of simplified, Xs and Os abstract, instead, and ignoring or minimizing the game elements that give context and structure to the tactical play (because that structure and context require their tactical play to be more nuanced and challenging, which they want to avoid).

    \n A bit off track, but I did want to mention something I've seen now a few times repeated at wargame conventions I've attended (since the subject of wargames has been brought up and I just got back from a small local wargame con last night and it's on my mind for design reasons).

    Very often, at the end of a wargame session at a con, or at the final ceremonies if there are game match ups that play into one another ( like Games Workshop events), there will be multiple types of awards.

    There's usually the obvious one: Who won the battle(s) overall?

    But there's also generally a second one, a social recognition one: Who made this game fun to play?*

    It's a sort of MVP/Consolation prize. I've won one of this sort in two different big play wargames now (nice medal for the one last night actually) and once in a Wodehouse-themed LARP co-GMed by John Kim.

    It's a social nicety, and something designers might want to consider if designing competitive RPGs. John Harper brings up some of the reasons why in his post.

    I know that RPGers in particular seem to have a bit of a rough time when faced with the thought of judging other players on their play ( and, more importantly, being judged themselves!). Still, if grognardly old wargames can and do recognize how important this aspect is to successful/enjoyable gaming, it might be something worth considering adding in.



    * Actually, wargamers, especially of the minis using variety often offer awards in multiple categories beyond battlefield victor, including recognition awards for the hobby aspects ( whose toys looked the coolest) and scenario design (mostly for the GMs, so that they finally get some recognition as well). As for the MVP award, that's kinda there to recognize that sometimes, even with weighted victory conditions, some players will be badly out-classed almost from the get-go. Playing well,possibly even in character when it means utter defeat as opposed to rules mongering for a squeaky win and general helpfilness is considered as important for a functional ame experience. You may not be able to win, but your attitude in the face of that does make a difference in the games enjoyment by all players.

    \n Agon and Paranoia are the only rpgs I've played that functioned well when played competitively between players. 3:16 is fun as a player v. GM thing, and old-skool dungeon crawls with the GM as impartial arbiter of the players' attempts to overcome the traps and monsters are great - a lot of my competitive friends find this kind of challenge-oriented play a lot of fun.

    The key element is that the game should minimize the need to choose between creating cool fiction and increasing your chances of winning. I am aware that every game has some degree of sportsmanship, but I really think it's a perverse set of incentives to continually pit the thing that makes story games unique vs. the competitive part. If you're really playing Shab-al-Hiri roach to win, you'll bring in so many elements each scene to help you that the fiction will lose coherence. If you're playing IAWA to win then every scene is someone bludgeoning (physically or otherwise) someone else to get what they want. If you're playing Baron von Munchausen to win, you will never make a challenge. If you're really playing Burning Empires to win, not only will you be endlessly bumping up against the lack of specificity about how different forces are outfitted, but the choices the players and GM make will be boring as they continually attempt to all gang up to put important npcs permanently out of commission.

    All of these scenarios I've described have happened in games I've played in. They're all games I like, in some cases games I love. The fact that there's a winner and a loser focuses and motivates play. But if the players are too interested in winning in these games, they don't function right. If your friend wants to play rpgs that are competitive because he's a competitive gamer, don't show him games that offer victory as a goal to move play in a certain direction; look for ones where strategic play contributes positively to the fiction.

    \n Murderous Ghosts has a skill component, similar to blackjack. It does depend on your draws, but you can mitigate the effect of bad draws by timing your discards well and managing when you bust. Also, the actions you choose to take play a big role.
