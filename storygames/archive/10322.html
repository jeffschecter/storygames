    \n The inference in this question is that there is something pathological about gaming. I am my own therapist (analyse that). I'll admit that I have had feelings at times that roleplaying may fulfill certain drives that I may be better off trying to fulfill through some other more "real" means.

    However I get a lot of enjoyment out of roleplaying and I think that it is healthy enjoyment. I do have a propensity for escapism but I'm not sure if my gaming came first or my tendency to enjoy escapism came first and/or whether escapism is a societal symptom anyway.

    Bottom line is I'm gonna keep gaming no matter what my therapist thinks about it (see above).

    Interesting question though.

    \n Not completely related to the core question...but...

    I've been a part of a court case in which the legal counsel for the defence used roleplaying as a part of the case.

    Using a claim that the defendant's ability to relate the to real world was often seen as a pale shadow of the fantasies engaged through roleplaying, the defence counsel sought to negotiate a reduced sentence (or even acquittal) on the grounds of mental competence (without blatantly using an insanity plea).

    The defendant recieved the minimum sentence, a good behaviour bond.

    In this instance, the legal counsel was actually a gamer himself, and was using the prejudices of society to his client's advantage.

    \n Fascinating and frightening story, vulpinoid...

    I saw a shrink in high school. At the time I was playing an online diplomatic role-playing game: raise armies, negotiate treaties, declare emergency police actions, and, of course, support terrorism. There were two interesting reactions. One was the school: the librarians learned to recognise the web site (it had stark red text on black background) and banned it. I later created a new game using the school's colours for the web site so we could play on.

    My shrink used to get me to talk about the game as an icebreaker, then switch to talking about all of the rubbish things I was dealing with. After a while this got kind of transparent and I stopped going. In retrospect, some of the skills I was using in the game could have been the basis for better coping skills in my life. I think the educational value of games (including social and emotional learning) is really underestimated.
