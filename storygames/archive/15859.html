    \n I've only played modern OSR modules, never an original TSR one. Tomb of the Iron God is one I like for its minimalism and finely-tuned challenge level for 1st-level parties. Dyson's Delve is a free minimegadungeon with potential for a very compact, but also very complex tactical spinning. Jim Raggi adventures are easy to recommend in general for how they break the mold while remaining playable with the D&D method - Death Frost Doom is, of course, the classic not to be missed here.

    I haven't played Ursined, Sealed & Delivered, but on the basis of reading it's one of the best OSR adventures out there in terms of challenge, both strategic and tactical. Potential for interesting consequences abounds, too. Highly recommended.

    \n My historical favourites that have something to do with some flavour of D&D:

    \u2022\xa0U1 Sinister Secret of Saltmarsh -- (probably my all-time favourite introduce-you-to-the-game adventure for D&D branches of the tree)

    \u2022 Sunless Citadel -- (a close second to U1 for the introductory adventure)

    \u2022 The first two instalments of the first Paizo adventure path Rise of the Runelords were fun: not as fun as the previous two bullets, but fun nonetheless -- a lot more fun, imho, than the sections of the path that followed them.

    If you're willing to drift a bit to old-school WFRP (which would involve conversion, but probably not onerously so), then you can admit my all-time fave fantasy-genre adventures, the first two instalments of The Enemy Within campaign: Mistaken Identity and Shadows over B\xf6genhafen. Typically I've swapped out the first two encounters in MI with an inn-based adventure from White Dwarf (found in The Restless Dead), but I've also run it as is. Either way, the Kastor Lieberung hook is probably one of the most effective I've ever run across for simply fly-casting at a party, and it leads to considerable hilarity throughout the early part of the campaign.

    It's hard to think of any fantasy module/campaign material that matches TEW up through to Power Behind The Throne: after that, things get a bit wonky, but it's still a pretty fun ride.

    \n There's a 4e revisit of Keep on the Borderlands that I really really like. It follows 95% of the original, but it crosses the i's, dots the t's and makes it all feel more cohesive. And it slightly redraws the map to make it scale nicely to map focused combat.

    http://www.dndcorner.com/downloads/Module B2 The Keep On The Borderlands - Revised for 4th Edition.pdf

    I spent a year running a sandbox campaign made up of the best old D&D modules I could find, just tweaked a little to make them more cohesive and to fit with the slightly different setting I was using. (A fairy tale setting - so werebears were actually just talking bears looking for a blonde thief, etc)

    I used Village of Hommlet as the "home town". With the moathouse just upriver, of course.
    The Secret of Saltmarsh alchemist's haunted house was down the river a ways toward the bay. One of my players joined in while they were exploring the house, so he was the tied up thief upstairs.
    The Caves of Chaos from Keep on the Borderlands were in an abandoned mine nearby, and the region was running low on metals as trade routes were breaking down.
    The Dwellers of the Forbidden City location was in the nearby mountain range, though they never made it there.
    The lizardman caves from Danger at Dunwater was ready to show up just down the coastline, if needed.

    The cultists from the moathouse and the Caves of Chaos were tied together. I probably would have used the Forbidden City as a tweaked version of the Temple of Elemental Evil, if they had made it that far through the possibilities.

    The dungeon crawly type stuff started after I did a big time shift. At first they were in a pseudo-late-medieval setting, but with tons and tons of tiny kingdoms everywhere - no large countries or empires. Because in fairy tales, there's always kings and princes and princesses coming out your ears. But I was having a hard time driving adventure. So I had a jilted fairy (a la Sleeping Beauty) send them back in time by about 1000 years, to the era of the fall of the pseudo-Roman-Empire. So basically, they were in England in the 400s, when the Romans were losing their stronghold and falling apart. Being on the edges of collapsing empirical civilization made for a great setting. Plus, they came through time nearly broke. The Cinderella character had to sell off her fancy ball gown for room and board money. This gave them tons of motivation to look around actively for ways to make money. I found a pretty sweet price list that helped me drive the game with economics - and I backed off the treasure dumps considerably - no 500gp stashes on minor bad guys - they were pawning silver combs and such to get by, and scrounging together used armor from fallen bad guys.

    Oh, and I ran the whole thing using Savage Worlds, which I find much more fun than D&D as a system. Though I tweaked that a bit, as well.

    \n Coming in at #1 on my list of "Modules I'd Love to Run" is Bruce Cordell's epic boxed set, Return to the Tomb of Horrors. It builds beautifully and organically on the original Tomb of Horrors module; in fact, it even comes with a facsimile of the original module, that you play through as part of the adventure! It's a great fantasy horror adventure. It is horror, though; you'll need players who love fighting against terrible odds, and who love having terrible things happen to their characters.

    #2 on my list is The Red Hand of Doom. It's a great "PCs vs. Army of Evil" adventure. #3 is Castle Amber. I think it would be fun to run it using dream-logic; everything is real and is happening, but time, perception, and logic operate more like they do in dreams. (As in "Synecdoche, NY", for instance.) I'd also love to run the boxed-set sequel, Mark of Amber. Mark of Amber comes with an audio CD of dialogue that you play during the adventure, with deliciously awful faux-French accents.

    #4 on my list is Dead Gods, but I'm not sure I'd play it as a linear adventure. I'd definitely steal large chunks of it, though.

    #5 on my list is Keep on the Borderlands, and its various sequels and spin-offs. I rather enjoy Return to the Keep on the Borderlands, and Kenzer & Co's "Little Keep on the Borderlands" is fun, too. I can highly recommend Kenzer's "Frandor's Keep"; it's oozing with flavor and adventure potential.

    #6 is Hellbound: The Blood War, and #7 is The Eternal Boundary. Are we noticing a Planescape theme, here?

    #8 is the Temple of Elemental Evil, but maybe that's just because I really enjoyed the PC game so much. I mean, reading through it, it's kind of boring. Also, it's not obvious at all where all the secret passages and such are, and what the politics inside the temple are. But I played as a character through it years ago, and really loved it, so I think maybe it's worth giving a look.

    #9 is Fantastic Locations: Fane of the Drow, by Gwendolyn Kestrel. It comes with four poster-sized battlemaps (2 sheets, double-sided), and a sixteen-page adventure that uses those maps as set pieces. It squeezes a lot into those 16 pages, though! I especially appreciate that they took the time to give names to all the monsters.

    #10 is The World's Largest Dungeon, because I am insane, apparently. Well, actually, since I own it (it was a gift), I feel obligated to get something out of it. Maybe it'll be fun? Who knows!
