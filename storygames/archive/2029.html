Posted By: Judson LesterAs to an RPG for yer gramma?
I didn't want to derail another productive thread, but I want to share anecdotes about playing games with tolerant/curious/drunk relatives.

1. When I was, like, ten I wrote a tabletop version of Hunt the Wumpus and made my Grandparents play it with me. They were a little confused but they rolled with it like grognards.

2. First edition Traveller with my Mom. She and I were merchant scout types on a ship, and my brother was the GM. We were boarded by pirates, captured, tied to chairs, taunted, and murdered via airlock. Good times. Given her interest in playing to "find out what we were up to", I'm surprised she was so supportive after that.

3. The Morrow Project with my Dad, also GMed by my older brother. My Dad's sort of a right-wing survivalist type so he got into it, insisting on playing a character who had natural night vision. That was strictly not in the rules but he was our Dad, what could we do? There was a lot of shooting things and some blowing up of things for added spice.

How about you?

    I have two experiences doing role-playing stuff with parents.

    My first RPG ever was the old red Basic D&D box set. Pam, a friend of the family, thought I (11) and my brother (8) would be interested and brought it over. My dad and Pam and her husband tried to read the rules as we went and make up characters on the spot, as if it were some board game that you could do that (and why can't you, dammit?). An hour or two later, we agreed to get back together at some later date and try to actually, you know, play. In the weeks before we played, I read the rules a bunch of times but didn't really get it. We finally reconvened. My dad was the DM. He had not read any of the rules and expected me to tell him and everyone else how to play. I didn't understand stuff well enough to DM a game. We fumbled for about fifteen minutes and it disintegrated. I eventually figured enough out to run stuff for my brother and a friend of mine and the rest is history.

    Last year, my wife and I hosted a 'How To Host a Murder Mystery' party. We sent invitations to her parents, her sister Jen, Jen's husband Chris, Chris' sister and her boyfriend. We did the full dinner party thing with Twenties music to suit the story. It was a blast and my in-laws got into it by dressing appropriately (dad as a ship captain and mom as a 1920-era European heiress). They talked in funny voices without anyone suggesting it and in general hammed it up more than Jen and Chris, veteran role-players, did.

    When I was again around 8 or 9 or so, my dad and I saw a commercial for Star Frontiers on the TV. Didn't know what it was, but it looked fun. So we were at a bookstore about 3-4 weeks later and they had it, so we bought it. It was like, what, $15.00?

    Anyway, we too tried to 'figure it out', but weren't too successful. Man, those old games were great as long as you had someone there to tell you what the hell you were supposed to DO with it. We played it like a kind of board game for a while, moving our pieces around the fold-out map, shooting guys, playing poker (my dad made up houserules for poker on the fly). We tried it a few times but it kinda fizzled. I still kept the books, and read them often... Cut to about 1, maybe even 2 years later: After church, I was reading the books in the car again (I usually just read the world/race/tech stuff)... and suddenly I *got it*. I figured out that you were supposed to engage in the adventures and 'do stuff', not just wander around the provided play board like it was an Open Monopoly or something.

    We never played after that, although my dad's been interested and I've been meaning to run something for him sometime. Maybe I'll throw down The Shadow of Yesterday for my dad and stepmom this Christmas...

    In BC, at least, there is a school course called Navigator. The basic pitch is: you pick a field of interest, create a learning proposal, learn about and explore that field of interest, and host a presentation at the end of the semester.

    People used Navigator to study random subjects of interest (ex. Mayan civilization, woodworking, genocide), improve skills in a certain sport/activity (ex. hockey, golf, biking), or learn new activities (ex. knitting, drumming.)

    First semester of my Grade 12 year, I did RPG Design. It was in this semester that I first learned about newfangled indie games, and joined The Forge. I created a post-apoc, war-torn, near-future game called Point of Collapse. (available at my site, www.incitefulentertainment.com, for those interested.)

    For my end of term presentation, I presented on how designing the game went, and ran a short session. I had pre-gens. The scenario was a simple one: You all work for Coca-Cola's Armed Forces (Corporate armies, w00t!), and you are delivering cargo to a bottling plant. Your subway train picks up a signal from another train, which has crashed and left many injured and dead. What do you do?

    So, I played this with my mom, my teacher (also the school counsellor), and three friends I'd invited to play (two had gamed before). The game used pre-gens like the doctor addicted to painkillers, the army sergeant who needs money bad, and the driver who's bitter about being a widow. The session was a SMASHING success, and completely validated what I do to both my mother and school, while at the same time earning me school credit.
