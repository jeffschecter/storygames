Opening/Closing Ritual Roundup

ColinC
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
When I read Polaris one of the things that really struck a note with me was the use of little rituals to open and close the game. Lighting the candle and saying the key phrase "Long ago, the people were dying at the end of the world" is a signal to all the players to get in the game and focus. Xenopulse's Beast Hunters starts with a martial-arts style handshake between the participants to indicate that while they may push each other to their limits, it's still just a game. My friend ran a Babylon 5 themed game where someone would read the opening blurb of our fictional TV show at the beginning of every session.

I'm thinking of using something like these in games I run in the future, and I'd like to see some more examples.

Can anyone provide other examples of these kind of rituals, either from published games or from their own groups?

    Full Light, Full Steam starts with everybody announcing who they are playing and listing off their thematic batteries and describing a part of what happened last session.

    Joe: I'm playing Al Flannagain, a Veteran Sharp-Shooting guy with a Dark Past. Last time we investigated the ancient ruins buried in the jungles of Aphrodite.
    Maria: I'm playing Jari Tashdurk, a Curious Balinese Flyboy. We found dead venerians in the first few chambers, and heard something big slinking through the darkness beyond.
    Gwen: I'm playing Melissa Cooper, a Hot-headed Firecracker who's good at Saying the Wrong Things and Tinkering. We fell down a trap into the temple's lower levels, but managed to turn on the lights of the secret Old Martian lab we found ourselves in.

    Each session always ends with a Feedback phase where everybody (a) spends XP, (b) swaps out thematic batteries, and (c) goes around the table saying at least one thing about the game, even if it's just "I had fun."

    My Life with Master has a very powerful ritual pattern.

    At the smaller level, there's the "command, resist, act, return" ritual for every player's scene. More important, though, is the "create Master, suffer Master, kill Master" ritual. The "create" is the initiation ritual that gets the players hooked. The "suffer Master" ritual is this tension of mounting orders that make you hate him more and more. Finally, the "kill Master" ritual gives you closure and lets you wash away the terrible sins you committed in his name. The epilogue is part of that, too, and you have the option to destroy your own character if destroying the Master wasn't enough to absolve you.

    In my current long-term game the official "be there" time is half-an-hour before the official "start play" time. We gather and chat, as the time wears on the chat is more likely to turn to role-playing or the game itself. When it's time to start the GM hands out the character sheets, often with slightly extravagant gestures.

    This repeated rhythm at the start of sessions really works. It's subtle, simple, and feels very natural because games often have this structure without intending it. Interestingly, the group is pretty promptly there at the "be there" time.

In order to protect those that no one else would protect and ensure the future's bright promise could come to be, she gave up a normal life and became Deja Vu!"

    "This is New York: (episode appropriate scene, statement or descriptive)
    This is Manghattan: (episode appropriate scene, statement or descriptive, narrowing in) ,
    This is Alphabet City: (episode appropriate scene, statement or descriptive, tight and evocative.)

    In both cases, there is also a theme song (I love Myself Today, Bif Naked and The Kinghtrider/LoseYourself Mix Panjabi MC) that we play at the beginning of the eposide. Together, with ritual elements that help to deliniate the game space (lighting, scent, etc). It really works to put the game directly on track and moving, and to create safe space in which hard subjects can be wrestled with.

    Even in games that we don't have ritual phrases we always always have a theme song that starts things off, and most of our games have specific soundtracks.
