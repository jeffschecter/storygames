\n So what was the first RPG you fell in love with?

I think mine was Shadowrun. I remember seeing it on the shelf of a comic store when I was a kid. Up to that point I'd played a handful of RPGs - Toon, Bughunters, Marvel Superheroes - but the big troll with a machine gun on the cover kind of made my brain pop. I was like, "Wow someone took Lord of the Rings and gave everyone MACHINE GUNS" and it was this revolutionary, mind-blowing thing to me. Keep in mind I was like 11.

How about you?

    \n Nothing super revolutionary when I say Advanced Dungeons and Dragons (2nd Edition). I bought the Player's Handbook from a friend of mine for 10 dollars and the first time I tried to read through it (age 9) I was like "Wow, this is complicated", but I still knew this roleplaying thing was for me. I ended up having a disproportionate exposure to the little monstrous compendiums, as those were the only D&D things cheap enough to convince my parents to buy for me/I could save up for on a regular basis (until one day I conned my older friend into loaning me the money to buy Planescape). Since that was all I had, I *really* got into reading the little monster descriptions and imagining situations involving these fantastic creatures. I skipped those annoying stat blocks and skimmed "how this monster fights" and read the juicy ecology and behavior sections. This is why the 4E Monstrous Manual makes me sad.

    Oh, and I also loved the experience of looking at the campaign setting supplements listed on the inside of the covers and trying to extrapolate what the hell could be going on there so that we could play adventures without buying all that expensive stuff.

    \n Planescape.

    I mean, shit, I'm over ten years older than I was when I first met it. I'm a different person. I can see the flaws, not just in the system, but it's like... not the flaws, per se, but it's like a magic trick once the magician's explained how it works to you, metaphorically speaking: you just can't get that same sense of wonder. Ether gaps are just magic black holes, in the Ethereal Plane. Dust mephits are a parody of Vampire LARPers. Sigh.

    But going through the books, and here and there there's one of Diterlizzi's illustrations, or a description of one of the many countless wonders or strange dangers to be found lurking on the planes, like that bit about how the real library of the yugoloths isn't made of books, it's contracts between them and other fiendish races tattooed on the skins of mortal petitioners, kept in countless cages in vast hallways beneath the surface of the Tower of the Arcanoloths, and they've got dudes whose only job is to stir the vats of blood used to write these contracts, to keep it from scabbing over, and these poor souls sit there with nothing to do or think about other than the contracts of evil permanently written on their flesh...

    And I will admit it. There's romantic music threatening to play in the background. Metaphorically speaking.

    \n For me, it is D&D 3E.

    I had been playing AD&D 2nd for some years by then, and I was starting to hate some aspects of it. Then the third edition came out, and I fully fell in love. My, oh my. They pretty much had solved every single one of the problems I was so bitterly complaining about.

    When the 3.5 came out, I felt betrayed. What could they possibly do to improve my beloved? Well, I gave it a chance. And it did pay out. It was more of a make-over. Still the same sweet lover, only better fit and sexier.

    Then I played the heck out of my D&D. (By that time, out group got to play weekly for almost three years in a row.) And by the end of it I felt heartbroken. There were some places the game was failing me, and I was growing more and more unsatisfied with it. Was that all there was into it?

    Finally, I discovered the Indie thing, and my eyes started shining again. I tried to use some new tricks and moves to rekindle our dwindling love, but then it finally came to me. System matters. The rules would not support the kind of game I was looking for, and the group's social contract was too stale and full of bad habits and passivity. And so our love was over...


    Some months ago I got to play a couple of sessions again. I was curious about the experience, now that I had been into all this hippie stuff for almost 3 years.

    And it was kind of sad, actually. The nostalgia was big, but it was exactly as going with an ex for a nice coffee to catch up on each other, and find out how apart your lives have drifted.

    \n I first played The Fantasy Trip for years. It would be easy to say that. For a long time that and D&D made up almost all of my role-playing experience. Even though I had a lot of fun with those games I rarely felt like I was emulating the R.E. Howard or Edgar Rice Burroughs fiction I adored. Mostly just a goofy sort of Tolkein-esque vibe except where the heroes spent a lot of their time drowning and falling in the mines of Moria. I loved these games but I was maybe too young to be in love with them, y'know?

    So I think my answer has to be R.Talsorian's Cyberpunk. First the 2013 and then the 2020 version. Gibson's fiction had blown me away and then this came along and it felt like me and my friends could be the hackers, street samurai & razor girls. We could tell stories that made us feel like Deckard or Roy Batty.

    I think it was the intersection of being old enough to want to tell stories that weren't straight heroics and experienced enough to be able to help tell them even where the system wasn't giving us everything we needed. Going down in a hail of flechettes to a pack of Arasaka corp mercs while pretending to be someone like Bobby Quine was so much more satisfying than being mowed down by orcs while pretending to be someone like Conan. My hacker's destiny was written the first time he caught that femme's eye while hunting down new 'ware in the Sprawl; my barbarian was supposed to have kicked those orcs butts and gone on to eventually become king, dammit.

    As long as I was dying in the rain. At night. In slow motion. For all the wrong reasons. While wearing my black leather pants. As long as those things were true, I was in love.

    \n Traveller. Late November 1977. I had just turned 21, and my mother had bought me whatever version of D&D was current for my birthday, and I was really juiced by the *idea* of RPGs, but D&D was not IT. Before I ever played D&D, or any other RPG, I walked into my game store - I was a veteran wargamer, and had patronized this store for years - and saw this new RPG called Traveller. I had been weaned on SF, not fantasy. Back then, fantasy was nothing. My dad was a big fan of SF, and we had tons of it dating back to the forties around the house. I bought the little black box, read it, and fell in love.
    \n

When I looked at Traveller, it just wasn't the same. The Traveller fandom got locked into a downward spiral of who could be the biggest Grognard. The system was nailed to the Third Imperium. Trav got to be like my first girlfriend, recalled with fondness, but from whom I had long since moved. I wrote my own SF and never looked back. I haven't played Trav since the early eighties.

      \n I was a polygamist, without a doubt. The process and experience got me--system didn't much matter. But my earliest harem included:
      * Blue Book D&D, followed by all AD&D1 hardbacks - The Old Lady Who Taught Me The Way
      * Gamma World - Wait... now I can make my own race? Oh, hell, yeah!
      * Toon - Wait, now I can make my own powers?!? **mind-fuse pops**
      * Call of Cthulhu - When I first learned playing insanity could be interesting and even creepy, not just madcap or incoherent.
      * James Bond 007 - The modules for the movies were in boxes. BOXES, man!!!
      * Champions - All the other supers games didn't know how to do HALF of what she knew. To this day, I still visit her.
      * Chill - The first time I was truly, seriously frightened and a bit panicky during play. (I've only ever got that again in LARPs.)

      If I had to pick ONE.... I would punch the person forcing me to do so. Then I'd mutter, "D&D" as I shuffled away, embarrassed by my commonality.
