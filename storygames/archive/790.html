Wierd little thing that requires a little context. I love the anime series Nadia, Secret of the Blue Water. It's good-natured fun. The problem is, its title heroine is pretty young, very dishy, and spends most of the TV series in a skimpy circus-acrobat outfit that highlights her dishiness. Also, personality wise, she is ... well ... occasionally bitchy and not immensely smart.

Now like I said, I enjoy the series, but when I think about the idea that it's me, or even more uncomfortably a male animator in some Japanese sweat-shop somewhere, constantly envisioning this fictional girl in this particular way ... it's not a great feeling. I like the series despite this.

So I was watching previews on another anime disc, and there was this wonderful little girl voice and a warm and loving old-lady voice, and they have the following little exchange:

Little Girl: Gramma? Mommy says you have a secret ...
Grandma: Oh ... do you want to know the secret?
Little Girl: Oh, Gramma, pleasssse ....
Grandma: Well I suppose you're old enough now.

And then they shift into ensemble shots of Nadia. And here's the awesome: They open with one of the most cheesecakey of scenes (Nadia swinging through the air, bare mid-riff prominent) but it struck me as totally okay ... because now it was the grandmother that I envisioned as narrating. I could totally hear her in my head saying "Yeah, you might not think it, but back then I was a dish!" and then chuckling.

So I totally don't want to get into the question of cheesecake and fan service in a general sense. I know there's opinions, but it's a side issue. What I find fascinating is how the exact same sequences strike me as completely different, depending on how they're framed and who I attribute the narrative voice to. I feel like there has to be some application to RPGs, though I don't see what it is immediately.

    OK, so there's the issue Jonathan is pointing out, which we could call the "it's different when you do it" issue. But I'm more curious about this business of in-character narrators that Tony raises.

    Has anybody done much with in-character voice-overs and narration? The only time I've done this was when playing The Mountain Witch - all the scenes were framed by me, the GM, narrating as the Witch. I didn't have any particular reason for doing it: it was just a way to make the Witch a presence before he appeared on the scene, plus I enjoyed doing the Witch's voice as Mako/Aku from Samurai Jack. But (imho) it actually had a weird and neat effect on the game: did the Witch's past tense narration mean he had survived? was he watching everything the samurai did? just who was he telling this story to?

    I agree with Tony, there's got to be some applications for both scene framing and actual game design.

    Tony, this is a prose-based game, so the issues may be a bit different, but I'm playing in a PBEM right now which explicitly works to try to keep scenes rooted that way. All of the scenes are kept within a single narrative voice. Only the player who "called" the scene (it rotates among the players, a bit like in PtA) is playing the narrative voice; the scene is told from her character's subjective point-of-view. The other players called into the scene are "support:" they're there to contribute external details of what their characters are doing and saying - no internal states and as little reflections thereof as possible permitted in their prose.

    So far it seems to work quite well at keeping scenes rooted within a single narrative perspective - but then, that's rather specifically what it was designed to do.
