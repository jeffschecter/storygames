    \nSweetwater Crossing

    Alice - A charismatic widow, who has against odds, held onto and runs her late husbands prosperous orchard.
    Samuel - A widower, runs the day to day labor of the farm, and is having a relationship of mutual comfort with Alice.

    Lavinia - Older sister to one to the PC Dogs, Fiery and spurned
    Amos - Lavinia's husband, and laborer on the Orchard. Loves Alice and desires her, ignoring Lavinia

    Eli - The Branch Steward, meek and frightened of losing control of the Branch
    Matthias - Laborer on the Orchard, reformed bandit and Faithful convert. Chafes under the authority of appointed judges, sees Alice as a true authority.

    The PC Dogs are summoned to Sweetwater Crossing after the last party of Dogs assigned there failed to report in. (This is the part I started with, the missing Dogs, but after I made the town by the creation rules, I'm wondering if it no longer fits as nicely as I'd hoped. I welcome all thought and feedback, I'm not married to any of this. )

    1a Pride - Alice and Samuel are having a relationship without thought of marriage, and Alice is defensive of her status as Landowner. Amos desires the widow Alice, at the expense of Lavinia. Matthias sees Alice as the true authority of the town, not Eli

    1b Injustice - Lavina is left alone with their teenage son, as Amos spends all his time near Alice's Orchard. Eli's authority is degraded by Amos, Samuel, and Matthias's reverence for Alice.

    2a Sin - (Here's where it get's hairy) Two weeks prior, Caleb and Carmine, the meanest Dogs in Deseret arrived in town. It didn't take long for them to talk to Eli, and decide Alice was the linchpin of the whole burgeoning heresy, and decided to kill her. Matthias overheard this conversation, and warned Amos and Samuel. When Caleb and Carmine cane to the Orchard one night, Samuel, Amos, and Matthias were waiting on the road. One thing led to another, and wanting to protect Alice, they killed both the Dogs. They buried them in a box canyon, sent the horses free, and vowed never to speak of it. But that didn't happen. Matthias first, and Samuel second realized that the Steward and the Dogs were in the wrong to judge Alice, and their trust in the appointed leaders of the Faith is gone. Alice knows nothing about the murders.

    2b Demonic Attacks - Eroding trust in the Appointed of the Faith. In the weeks since, Brother Eli has been made to look a fool by both fate, and the talk of Matthias and Samuel. It's gotten far enough that they openly mocked him after a sermon for some minor offense. Alice on the other hand, is well liked by all but Lavinia, and people are talking about her strength.

    3a False Doctrine - "Just because the Temple appoints you, don't mean your worthy. True authority comes from noble and faithful individuals." Matthias and Samuel. And very soon, Amos. We haven't got to outright corrupt worship, just the disruption of Eli's authority, but it's oh so close.

    Wants

    Alice wants the Dogs to restore harmony to the town, under Eli. She respects Eli, and she wants Lavinia to stop hating her, or at least stop giving her the evil eye. But she does not want to be judged for her relationship to Samuel, which she views as completely acceptable, or to have her position of relative power judged just because she's a woman.

    Steward Eli wants the Dogs to restore his authority, and to at the very least, embarrass Samuel and Matthias. He suspects they're involved in the Dogs disappearance, and although he did not want Alice to be killed, he'll tell the PCs his suspicions if it will rid him of Samuel and Matthias.

    Lavina wants the Dogs to judge Alice, and judge her hard. Wants them to return Amos' affection to her.

    Samuel wants the Dogs to leave. Right now. He doesn't want to kill again, but he will protect Alice at all cost. He does not them to even think about judging him. What he did was hard, but right. Caleb and Carmine were thugs masquerading as authority.

    Amos wants the Dogs to find out about the murders, but does not want to tell them directly. He wants them to tell him he did a good thing and wants Eli, and the late Caleb and Carmine judged as sinners by the Dogs (Who else would conspire to kill Alice?), and a new authority established.

    Matthias wants to drive off or if need be, kill the Dogs. They're all one in the same, false authority figures who poison the true Faith of individual righteousness. He's on a hair trigger, and once the Dogs are gone, he can continue to undermine Eli.

    The Demons want the Dogs to act foolish, embarrass each other and the Steward, but most importantly, they want the Dogs to disagree. In public. Undermine authority.

    If the Dogs never came...
    Matthias and Samuel eventually bring Amos over entirely, and to a certain extent, Alice, because she knows she deserves more respect. False Priesthood, with Matthias or Alice at the head. They'd drive out or kill Eli for condoning Alice's murder, and establish a new authority based on personal righteousness. Inspired by the success and righteousness of the charismatic Alice, the town would follow, and would become a very dangerous place for future Dogs, or authorities of the Faith.

    \n I like your town, and I think going all the way to hate and murder on your first town is a good idea.

    My main suggestion would be to omit the previous Dogs thread. I don't think it adds anything that plain old hate won't, and it will cause your players to roll into town defensive and ready for some murderin'. If you feel like you need murder in the mix make it a local.

    The guys who just want the Dogs to go away - that's weak tea. There's no interesting choice to be made about a guy who comes at you immediately with a gun. What if they want to be forgiven, or supported by the Order Set Apart? They don't think they are wrong. If you remove the previous Dog murder it gives the players more moral ambivalence to work with.

    Remember that they are going to make some choices, and the very very best gift you can give them (and yourself) is a lot of reasonable potential choices. Could a reasonable person side with every position in this town? If so, that's gold.

    \n Please feel free to ignore. Here's what I would do:

    - I wouldn't make Lavinia the older sister of one of the PCs.

    - Instead I would give the PCs a list of all the town's NPCs (with brief descriptions) and ask them: Who do you know? How? Maybe require everyone to have at least 1 relationship with any NPC. Maybe instead of asking "who do you know" ask provocative questions like: Who did you judge harshly but were wrong about? Who's your older sister? Who trained you? Who do you love? Who do you hate? Who do you respect? Who do you owe a favor? Who knows your secret? The faithful is a very small community. It's not uncommon for people to be tightly connected.

    - You describe Lavinia as "fiery, clever, and spurned" but many of the other NPCs lack these descriptions. I would give each NPC 3 words or phrases that describe them. Maybe 1 positive, 1 negative, and 1 quirky. Maybe grab a picture from IMDB to represent each NPC.

    - I would start off the first scene with Matthias beating Timothy. But you probably need some sort of reason that keeps the Dogs from immediately siding against Matthias as the situation is a bit one sided.

    - At the same time, have some sort of commotion occurring at the church, maybe a mob of people questioning the Stewards authority. If you want to go even crazier, at the same time show smoke and fire emanating from the orchard. Maybe someone tries (but fails) to burn down Alice's property.

    Posted By: entropybluesMy major concern now is driving conflict. I see a bunch of reasonable proto-protagonists, and I'm not sure how or when they're going to come into conflict with the dogs...
    \nI think you will do great. You already have what you need! Just keep the list of what every NPC wants from the Dogs in front of you and use that as your guide.

    But how do wants turn into conflict?

    - At the first chance possible, have your NPC confront the Dogs.

    - They want to be the first to ask / bed / demand that the Dogs give them what they want.

    - Conflict will arise naturally if your NPCs are afraid, desperate, angry, panicking, or paranoid. But what make your NPCs act this way?

    - Time is running out. They need to convince the Dogs before someone else does. Mobs are starting to form. The town is splitting into sides. Demonic attacks are increasing. People's livelihoods are threatened. But most importantly...

    - NPC know who the Dogs are. They've heard what Dogs do. Maybe not these specific Dogs but others. Maybe sometimes the Dogs judge fairly. Maybe they judge the wrong person (or you think they were the wrong person). Maybe innocent people get hurt in the Dogs path of unleashing judgement. Maybe, given how powerful the Dogs are, they will take away what's yours. Take away your job, your family, your respect, your life. They have that power. And they WILL USE IT! Maybe Dogs in general are fine people. But maybe this is these Dogs first Town. Maybe they don't know what they are doing but think they do. Who will pay for their mistakes? YOU WILL! That's enough to make any NPC act crazy around the Dogs.

    - Go the dice early and often. Trust the mechanics, they were specifically engineered to encourage and motivate you to escalate. Or give up, take your 2 highest dice, and then escalate in your next conflict. The dice will help you!

    \n You've got the conflict cooking pretty well. Have the NPCs push their agendas hard, and it shouldn't be a problem. If the Dogs arrive, and don't have a clear direction (e.g. "Go visit my sister" or "Pass out the mail"), have an NPC come running up and tell them about their very urgent problem and desires.

    If the Dogs do have an immediate goal, even if it's one that will drive story (e.g. "Go check in with the Steward"), have an NPC come running up to them anyway!

    I like jenskot's ideas of asking questions. But go ahead and force them if you want. You can always ask "Who's a sibling to Lavinia?" Set up the relationships you want to exist. Don't ask open ended questions if you want specific answers. Though, those are great when you want to make more relationship connections, and don't have any specific needs (or they've been filled). Another way to ask would be "Who's got someone they hate in this town?" And then you can either tell them the NPC, or give them some more ability to refine it like, "What did they do to you? What did you do to them?" Or let them get closer to choosing, such as "Was this person a bandit,* or a farmer?"

    (A great possible relationship seems to be someone who knew Matthias from his days of banditry - especially since it looks like he is truly reformed)

    Anyway, you've got a lot of meat there. You will probably make a few mistakes the first time. This is perfectly fine. You've got a better set up than my first time, where I failed to make any meaningful relationship connections to the PCs, so the whole story ended up rather lackluster.

    \n When I'm nervous GMing I do 1 of 2 things (sometimes both):

    1. Tell the players you're nervous and want their help making the game ___________ (insert goals).

    2. Insert a nervous NPC into the game that allows you to play out your nervousness. A bumbling guide that keeps apologizing for their nervousness or how their "new to this" does wonders for expelling your own nervousness.

    \n One comment:

    You've got only two players?

    Then you probably don't need the mobs. I mean, if the story calls for a mob, that's great. But you've got to feel really comfortable with the conflict rules: they can feel a little wonky in a "mob" situation (especially for Fallout and who takes the blow and all that kind of stuff).

    With just two players, I would imagine that sticking with single NPCs for the players to interact with will make the game easy to play.

    It's also difficult from the players' side: taking on a mob can be a difficult challenge! They get a lot of dice.

    So, for simplicity's sake, I would stick to individual NPCs for a while. Pick interesting conflict stakes and push hard and things will getting interesting plenty quick.

    If you need good conflict stakes, and "what does this NPC want?" isn't giving it to you right away, you can always ask the players something like:

    "What do you really wish the Steward wouldn't do next?" or maybe "What are you most afraid of right now?"

    Finally, if you feel lost or confused or unsure, just go into a conflict. You can just say, "OK, this NPC wants [you to validate their adultery]!" (insert what they want there) and then go to the dice. The game will get plenty interesting on its own, based on the mechanics!

    You can have a great Dogs session just going straight from conflict to conflict to conflict, with almost no "free roleplaying," and that could be a good tool if you feel unsure about what to do next.

    Let is know how it goes!

    \n Well, we had our game yesterday, and unfortunately due to the pressures of two of us having babies in the next room, we didn't get a chance to play for more than two hours.

    The characters they created were:

    Ben Coldstone, a Mountain Person orphan and convert, whose Dog's coat was given to him by the trainer, the man that rescued him when his family was killed. The coat belonged to the trainer's son, and still bore the fatal bullet hole. When asked for a relationship with either Matthias or Samuel, he picked Matthias and decided that Matthias had killed his brother when he was orphaned. This through me for a loop.

    Joseph Stapleton, a sickly coughing youth (and Lavinia's brother), whose village was decimated by a fever that left him week. His coat is stitched together plague blankets from his village's dead. Awesome.

    We had a couple of good initiations, that they both failed nicely. One of my players had read the book, and the other was still on the fence, but when he first realized he could get more dice by raising his fists, and the implications of the ease of escalation, his face lit up. He got it.

    They rode into Sweetwater, where smoke still lingered, and a crowd of orchard hands had gathered outside Eli's house. They broke up the mob, and Matthias came up and demanded that they hear the orchard hands side first. Conflict! This worked out so so well. Ben Coldstone immediately recognized Matthias, and rolled his powerful 2d8 relationship dice, and narrated in his 2d4 Quick to anger. But Joseph had taken Matthias side in the conflict wanting to hear him out. So right away, player vs. player. Matthias had a trait for "seeking forgiveness" so he stopped Ben cold with a crazy high raise, where he recognized him and begged forgiveness, and asked to be treated like any Faithful. With Joseph on Matthias side for the stakes of "You will hear Matthias' side of the story" Ben had to give, holding over some dice for his next face-off with Matthias. They listened to Matthias, then to Eli, then rode on to meet Lavinia and Timothy, where they learned of the beating.

    I still had a hard time forcing conflicts. Actually, I knew exactly when to start them, but had a devil of a time setting stakes. When they went to the Orchard, Samuel started a conflict when the Dogs were talking ill of Alice (Stakes: "You will address Sister Green with all due respect.") Joseph was most interested in getting Amos to come home and attend to his familial duties. So when Joseph suggested he be given the right to pursue Alice as a second wife, we launched into a real natural conflict. After some fiery scripture quoting, Amos lost his temper at some of the presumptuous things the Dogs (and a mountain person to boot) were telling him and he pushed Ben over. Ben chose to give, without escalating, but Joseph escalated all the way... and shot his brother in law in the foot, with all the orchard hands surrounding them. Matthias approached, helped Ben up from the ground and asked permission to take Amos to see a doctor.

    At that point, the babies demanded our attention, so we broke for the afternoon. But boy howdy, do I have two new converts to "weird little games". After the game, they looked at my bookshelf, asking what this "Fiasco" and "Dungeon World" were, and could we try them next?

    I'm really pleasantly surprised to see what happens when you plan nothing. It felt so damn natural, to let them drive the action. I think all three of us were shocked when sickly Joseph shot his brother in law in the foot, and the Dogs seem to be really siding with Lavinia and Eli, naturally assuming Alice is a sorcerer. But they haven't met Alice yet....

    So, it was a success, albeit brief. Thanks to everyone, and thanks to Jason for his Wisdom from the Book of Life, which I printed out. We had a few conflicts that consisted of scripture-counterscripture that added a lot of flavor to the whole affair. Can't wait to finish the town!

    \n That sounds awesome!

    Here's a piece of advice for going in to your next game: Don't let the players be fooled for long. Your GM instincts may tell you to preserve the "trick" that Alice is the Sorcerer, but don't! Actively reveal the town in play. Make it clear as quick as you can (by playing your NPCs) that Alice isn't who they thought she was. It sounds like that's your plan, but it can be a powerful temptation to let the players be fooled.

    It can be hard to set stakes, and sometimes there's a nasty pause while you try to think of the right thing. Follow the book's advice: push for small stakes. Also, think about what the characters are doing, what they want, and who's involved. Remember stakes aren't "this happens/ or this happens" they're "this thing". That leaves you more scope to figure out the details as the conflict develops.
