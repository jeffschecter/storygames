\n So, there's this thing happening in the Over the Edge game that I run, which has been gnawing at me: nearly all the players are taking their cues for "what to do next" from me, the GM, when the game should ("should" meaning "what I want out of the game, and the goal we've discussed as a group") be about the goals and actions of the PCs driving the story forward. And while it's not (usually) outright killing the fun, it's definitely impeding it. I can totally see how I'm contributing to the problem, too! I'm totally giving marching orders and providing plans and stuff. I just don't know how to break out of the loop. I'll try to explain with an example:

Setting: a fictional island dictatorship full of conspiracies and psychics and crazies. One PC, Frank, was taken in for questioning at Peace Force HQ, because he checked in to the State hospital with bullet wounds from UN Peacekeeper weapons and the cops put 2 and 2 together and figured he had something to do with the UN facility that was blown up the other night. In fact, he WAS there with his pal Delcus, trying to warn the base personnel about the other PCs who did infiltrate and blow the place up. Only they "warned" them rather belligerently, and got thrown in the brig then later Frank was shot as they escaped.

So he's in custody, and the cops are trying to soften him up and get him to spill before they hand him over to the UN guys, who won't ask so. . .nicely. Meanwhile, Delcus tries to get in to see him, but the Peace won't let him in, so he returns to Frank's apartment to weigh his options. Now, so far, the players got here through their own choices. . .they found out about the UN raid through player interaction, decided what to do about it, decided to go to the Govt. hospital even knowing the risk (getting shot is practically a confession on this island that you were interfering with state business), decided what to do about the arrest. But at this point, the players seem to be out of ideas. Frank's player is just going with a belligerent "hey, I'm innocent, you got nothing on me" stance, and not giving any info on thew real infiltrators, and Del's player is just, "eh, couldn't get in to help him, I'll just check on him in the morning." As it stands, Frank's gonna get tortured for information, then summarily executed. Delcus will poke around and maaaybe find out where they dumped the body, but will never see his friend alive again. The end.

Soooo, I stepped in. Suddenly the setting is crawling with helpful NPCs suggesting courses of action to save poor Frank's ass. When Del arrives at Frank's apt Aleksandr, apprentice to the sorceror Frank had dealings with, is waiting, seeking Frank on his master's behalf, and suggests a plan to get in to save him. Another PC, James is contacted by the organization he works for and told that his friend Frank has been arrested and helpfully contacts an inside man on the Peace Force to help James infiltrate HQ. And when the UN interrogators come in, Frank does spill the beans on the infiltrator PC, but then sits in relative silence as I have the two interrogators argue about whether the information is legit (one of them KNOWS it's legit, because he tipped off the PC about the facility, and is trying to distract from that avenue of investigation). And everyone's "playing their character," and seems to be having a good time more or less, but it strikes me when I'm about two-thirds deep into it that nobody's really doing much of anything except me, as I furiously spin and weave this elaborate web of situation.

The causes of this dynamic aren't exactly mysterious:\n
  • I know all kinds of crap about the setting that nobody else does (OTE is a book crammed THICK with weird and wild setting bits), and the game really hinges around discovering that setting, therefore I often end up doing a lot of exposition, introducing characters and locations, highlighting little vignettes and snapshots of what the city is like.
  • I tend to try to support the PCs' character concepts by having those around them treat them like important people--hey, MR thief, can you steal this for me, hey Mr Assassin, can you take somebody out?--but this leads to a lot of NPCs doing a lot of the talking and planning.
  • When PCs are in a fix, I've often had NPCs come to their aid, to avoid senseless PC death and such, so now it's a habit. The aid is generally pretty plausible in terms of PCs' contacts and relationship, but still.
\nWhat I don't know is how to break out of it. It seems like we're stuck in a rut; the players are low on solutions to problems or initiative to solve them, probably because I keep doing it for them, but if I stop doing it for them, they're screwed, so I keep supplying the plans. . .I totally don't wanna be that "Come and listen to my cool GM story and roll some dice occasionally" guy, but at these sessions I'm just talking so damn much. Even when I try to draw the players in, solicit or wait for their input, it still ends up defaulting back to me. Like, when the cops turned Frank over to the UN guys, there was some cool tension because Frank DID spill the beans about Ai'li, the infiltrator PC, and one of the interrogators tried to discredit Frank's claim, because unbeknownst to the other guy, he was the one who leaked Ai'li the info on the facility. Ai'li's player even pointed that out, confused, and I said "yeah, but THIS guy doesn't know that!" and she wen't "O-oh," and suddenly everyone was interested in this little exchange. People started to go, "Hey, maybe Frank could figure out that this guy's lying," but Frank's player was like, "uh, no, he's not psychic." I suggested that he roll his "Keen insight into Homo Sapiens" trait to figure out that he's hiding something," but the player insisted that that trait was only for discerning what bugs a person so he can get under their skin, not for any kind of lie detecting or motive sensing application (which isn't the way OTE traits are supposed to work, but hey). And so boom, it's kicked back to me, as I proceed to talk to myself for 10-15 minutes as the two interrogators argue. Bleh.

The whole game doesn't necessarily proceed this way; a couple of players are pretty consistent with driving the action forward proactively with their characters. And most of the players have some initiative; they'll at least know what their characters want, and pursue that. But there's this sort of uneven lurching start-stop pace to play as they take decisive action toward whatever, then don't know what to do when they get there, so their momentum grinds to a halt and I end up taking over, until finally they get to a point where they know what they want to do it, until the next time they draw a blank, so I step in. . .it's kinda frustrating and wearing.

Can anyone relate? Any advice and ideas are welcome, or just personal impressions and such. One thing to note, though: we're actually working on bringing the game to a close--not with any kind of deadline, just a general directive to move toward completion and closure of individual characters' storylines. This makes hangups in the pacing even more frustrating, and also means that any solutions have to work within the timeframe of oh, 6-10 sessions. Any thoughts?

Peace,
-Joel

    \n OTE's Al-Amrja is a really gonzo setting, IIRC; it's Hunter Thompson in Duvalier's Haiti mixed up with Burrough's Interzone. It seems like you want to remain faithful to that, but at the same time run it in sort of a Sim way, i.e. the Peace Force would torture and murder the PC and that's the only plausible option.

    This seems to be a pattern -- you also say that the PC's aren't able to figure out what's going on so you help them with NPC's, because otherwise they'd be screwed.

    I mean, it seems like you're playing tennis on both sides of the net here, which leads to you working way too hard and the players getting disengaged and confused. The scene where you feel obliged to dramatize an arguement between NPC's because the PC in the scene won't bite any of the hooks you offer is probably the ultimate example of this.

    That's the key dysfunction from what you wrote; there are probably many ways to respond to it.

    Here's one way that's worked for me as GM:

    If you have a wheels-within-wheels plot that the players can't keep up with and which is not working very well, why not jettison it and go with something much simpler? You know, like come up with a film noir sort of backstory that's consistent with what's gone before but helps both you and them make sense of what is to come. Let the plot move forward, getting more and more obvious as time goes on, until the PC's figure it out and start engaging it.

    Another gimmick from film noir: reveal at some point that one of the NPCs has been using the PC's as catspaws all along, and that's why they keep getting manipulated, assisted, and rescued from all angles -- this guy is trying to get them into position to make his enemy vulnerable, or to make them act as fall guys. If the players are feeling confused and paranoid, it might be good for them to figure out who's been screwing with their heads this whole time.

    \nI can relate.

    Here's some things that we did in our With Great Power game to basically focus it on the PCs, with the adventure moving with them (it's in line with Danny's observation above). Not sure how much of this you can do now that the campaign is winding down though. But the steps below were directly responsible for the players running the game with me as a facilitator (and yeah, having some "world secrets" to reveal, some of which changed as the PCs invented new shit):

    1) Create a stable of NPCs that the players regularly interact with. I did this all myself as the GM, setting up the world so that we didn't have to have a totally hippie "lets make all characters together" session. Still, I left it open for if the players were aiming to create a new NPC or two. Didn't happen, though, we just used my stable of about a dozen characters: 4 "baddies" (who weren't out to directly kill the NPCs, they could have meals with them and stuff; they just weren't friendlies), 6 "coworkers/friends", and about 1-2 characters per PC which were personally attached to them: Best Friend, Fiancee, and Ex-girlfriend.

    2) The important part: Make the players take on the roles of the NPCs. Tell them like two things about that character, make them take the role, and let them go nuts. If you have a scene with one PC and one NPC, hand the NPC over to another player. Let them go at it. Maybe tell the player the NPC's motivation and maybe 1-2 things about them so they can "take on the role", but let them go at it. If you have a "GM secret", only step in and "take back control" or "guide the conversation" when the player-run NPC starts to move into that territory: "Great job, Rick, I'll take it from here". But for the most part you let them take on the roles on NPCs.

    In the above, one the players take on responsibilities that lay on "both sides of the screen", you'll see them become more like GMs themselves, authoring their own, and others', stories.

    I can't theoretically explain the solid connection between #1 and #2 and how it directly led to the players calling their own shots, developing their own storylines, and most importantly doing it themselves without waiting for the GM to force them. But that's Exactly what happened. It worked. More than the backbone rules of With Great Power, those above two bits of WGP game advice really drove the players into carving their own stories with the material provided.

    -Andy

    \n I can relate, though I don't necessarily have any useful suggestions for you.

    My recollection of OtE was that as settings go, it was aggressively cryptic -- you couldn't count on anything necessarily being like it is in real life, unless you came from some bizarro Earth where Naked Lunch is an anthropology textbook. And given that, I can't say I'm surprised to hear that your group tends to flounder when called upon to make useful plans for what to do next, and even when simply deciding what (if anything) they ought to do next. They probably feel like they only understand enough about the setting to know that they don't understand nearly enough about it, and so you're probably right to feel like doing a lot of exposition might be doing more harm than good. You're trying to throw a lot of light onto the setting, but because so much of it is unrecognizable, all it's doing is making bigger and scarier shadows.

    (Seriously, I love Al Amarja, but...holy kickboxing jesus, I don't know that I'd want to run a game set in it. Certainly not for a group of people who aren't already familiar with it and with the mind-boggling assortment of source material it's swiped from.)

    So the suggestion to simplify sounds like a good one to me. Pick out one or two bits of the weirdness that you think really click with you and with the group, and then "normalize" the rest so that A.) you don't have to spend time on exposition to familiarize everyone with it, and B.) everyone can feel like their normal everyday knowledge of human motivations/society/technology/whatever is applicable in this game. Trim the story's complexity down -- fewer NPCs, simpler plots, more straightforward agendas -- until you hit the sweet spot where there's enough material to keep the players engaged without there being so much that it buries them.

    Of course, that'd be a lot easier if you were, say, two sessions in...but since you're actually 6-10 sessions from the end, the time when you could have reasonably simplified the setting without anyone noticing is probably long past. But you still have time to cut out anything that you haven't already shoved at the players and come up with something cleaner and more straightforward. And maybe instead of doing exposition and having NPCs jump into the mix, you can fall back on asking the players how they think something works (like what the cops are going to do with a re-captured prisoner) and reassure them that you will be their "common sense" and jump in if they're making an incorrect assumption -- although, again, this late in the game they may have already decided that they just don't know enough to even guess at how things work and will be totally gunshy about suggesting anything.

    Oh, and as always, this is exactly the kind of thing you should sit down and discuss with your group. Asking people on the internet is fun and sometimes interesting, but it's nothing compared the results you get when you actually address your concerns with the people who share them. Probably you've already mentioned it to them, but if you haven't (or if you just haven't gone into the same kind of detail you have here), that really should be your next step: any ideas you and your group think up to make your game better will automatically be fifteen trillion times better than the best idea you'll read here.

    \n I'm a little surprised no one has mentioned this before...

    It might sound cruel, but if the players make bad choices and get into a position where they will be screwed, then let them be screwed. Overprotectiveness isn't going to help your players from taking bad choices, not taking the initiative and not even bothering on thinking what to do next. They need to learn that their choices have consequences (even choosing not to do anything at all), instead of letting the GM lead the way once they run out of ideas.

    As a GM wanting to tell a story, that will be frustrating. But think on how frustrating it already is for you. Do you really want to keep saving their asses over and over again?

    Of course, this is not the only thing you could (or should) do, but it relates to part of the problem. You have tried talking with them, it didn't work... But think of them as teenagers. Since when do teenagers really act differently by just talking to them? You have to make them realize they are doing wrong, and face the consequences to their actions.

    Else, they will remain the same no matter what you do, how much you make them relate to the story and characters, how easy the problems get, how cool and detailed the setting is...

    \n I can totally relate. I remember this one D&D game where I set up an adventure which involved escorting a diplomat through miles of forests filled with hostile tribes. The problem? The diplomat had far better social skills than anyone in the party. Every time they met some hostiles, they'd sick the diplomat on them, to talk their way out. All the complex inter-tribal politics I'd worked out for them to negotiate their way through became a session worth of me talking to myself.

    It seems like the players have gotten used to having answers provided for them, and have retreated into "I'm just playing my character" disengagement. The solution, I think, is to present the characters with tough, obvious choices to make. For example, in your situation, I'd have the UN guys make the PC an offer. They'll execute him, or he can work for them as a double agent. They'll install a magi-tech surveillance device in his head, and he can go about his day. Also, if he tells anyone, it explodes.

    For the other PC, maybe a sorcerer comes to him to offer not advice, but help, at a cost. A terrible cost. Of course, it's best not to have these choices be between doing something or doing nothing, because then it's kinda no choice at all.

    \n I can relate to your situation as well. I was in an OTE campaign a while back that ran aground after only about four sessions (I was a player). The biggest impediment was that all of us players tended to sit back and wait for the GM to give us interesting things to do, while the GM was much more interested in having us drive things forward.

    As I recall, the things that kept us from doing that were pretty clear, at least in retrospect:
    - We were small fish dumped in a really big, weird, pond and everybody we ran into had tomes full of secrets. We each had our one little secret. So we didn't feel that special.
    - Everybody in the setting had connections and plots - it was very well worked out. We had each other, and we didn't even really know each other, we just happened to come in on the same plane.
    - We each had personal goals (find my missing son, find and take over a mysterious ninja cult, stuff like that). But we didn't have any idea how to take little steps toward that goal, so we looked to the GM to provide them.

    Some of this was because of the setting. It was impossible to tell what sorts of actions would work in this weird setting, and reality wasn't a very good guide. I could look for my missing son by putting up posters, or hiring a detective, or talking to the cops. But maybe talking to the cops gets is a good idea, or maybe its a really bad idea - there was no way to know. Maybe putting up posters is reasonable, but maybe it leaves traces of your DNA around and you'll get cloned by aliens or something - there was no way to know. It was all very frustrating.

    Now, it sounds like this all might not be directly relevant to your situation - you've been playing for eight years here. Frankly, I find that pretty impressive, and feel a little strange trying to give you advice. But the sorts of problems that you're describing seem like the same kinds that we ran into at the very start of our campaign, so maybe there's some overlap.

    As far as your immediate situation, I would recommend giving the PCs broad categories of actions that they could take, but not spelling out specific plans. That might be a way of meeting them halfway and getting them to contribute more. For example, any given problem could be attacked by one of the following methods:
    1) Physical assault. (But then how will you do it? What's the plan, and what weapons can you bring to bear?)
    2) Socially manipulating someone who has power in the situation. (Who do you go after? How do you investigate what sort of blackmail/threat/bribe/persuasion will work against them? How do you get into a situation where you can interact with them closely?)
    3) Calling in allies. (Who do you call? What do you expect they might do?)
    4) Something Weird. (Your buddy's trapped - maybe you can transfer his soul/mind out while leaving his body trapped? Maybe you can dig up a time machine and keep him from getting captured in the first place?)
    5) etc., etc. You can probably come up with other broad categories.

    The idea is to have a semi-formalized list of Ways to Attack Problems. Once the PCs decide on a Way, you can question them to pull out specific details of their plan. When the question is too open-ended, it's possible for players to get frozen; by channeling things a little maybe you can get them to start being more proactive without having to have one of your NPCs come up with the whole plan.

    The second bit of advice is to let them succeed. No matter what plan they come up with. Now you don't have to do this forever, but at least when you're starting to get them used to coming up with their own plans, let any wacky idea they have work brilliantly. Later you can ramp this down. But there's nothing more frustrating than being told you have to come up with a plan, spending an hour on it, and then having it fail miserably (often because the GM's assumptions of how reality works aren't the same as the players'). If that happens two or three times in a row, then you'd better believe the players will start waiting for the GM to spoon-feed them a plan of action, because at least there's the hope that there's fun down that road.

    \n You need to back away from throwing things at the PCs.

    One of the unfortunate things about looking for a story-games style answer is that wacky doctrine says that a traditional game is all about making the characters follow a story directed by the GM, so in a self-fulfilling prophecy, all advice ends up being about how to push the players around. I believe this community has difficulty visualizing other kinds of traditional gameplay outside of, say, the dungeon crawl.

    There is no need for you to make a story and give chunks of it to the PCs. Rather, Al-Amaja is already set up as a large network where even visiting a particular barrio triggers meaningful activity in the environment. It's enough to let the PCs know that something is happening and make *them* invest in revealing it through their own efforts. The setting is an engine that produces stories based on what the characters put into it. The typical structure for this kind of game (which, incidentally, is also the way Vampire works) is that while there is a GM-generated plotline, the players relate to it by making use of their own network within the setting. That means, for instance, that if there's some mystery about a psychic that the GM starts up, the players are the ones who, for instance, connect it to the Mr. LeThuys because of their contacts. The basic site and situation based entry points to player contact are fairly well-coded into things, so it is not difficult to make this happen.

    So as I said: Back off and give the PCs time to proactively build relationships in Al-Amarja, so they can draw on these to take true ownership of the setting and storyline.
