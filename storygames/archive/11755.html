\n
\n

\n Thinking back to Ron Edwards' essay on Fantasy Heartbreakers, and every one of them using a new magic system - because the D&D Magic System is pretty weak for representing anything other than D&D Magic, I'm wondering why so many of them stuck with ability scores.

When I think about what I'd want to take out of AD&D, it would be the ability scores. There are so many problems centred around them, and so much work to fix it (of course, sometimes that's part of the challenge and fun in a game, but in the same sense so is working around memorized spells). If ability scores have any sort of influence on the mechanics, and they're randomly rolled, you run into problems. If they have an influence on mechanics and aren't randomly rolled you still run into problems. Problems that somehow are less present without them.

Now I'm sure there are some games which work just fine with them, but they strike me as a rarity. I can think of A Dirty World off hand, and that uses only ability scores, and in a rather different fashion. Pretty much every game from the 80s and 90s uses them, to the point that they're almost as omnipresent in games as dice. Even a lot of newer games have them.

So, why ability scores? Why is that something that's sticking around on RPGs so much?

    \n Because we like to think that there are things about ourselves that are hard to change, and things that are easier to change. You can learn how to fight with a sword. It's harder to become more intelligent. At least, we like to think certain things are intrinsic and very hard to change.

    At a much more meta level, though, I think designers use abilities because they are an abstraction for "things you want to be good at." When you put an 18 into Intelligence and dump your 9 into Strength, you're saying that you want to be good at "smart" things and bad at "strong" things, and you'll probably play a Wizard, right? Abilities also give you fixed categories to organize skills or other traits (e.g., Diplomacy is a Charisma-based skill). All of these things aid niche protection.

    Look at how Dogs in the Vineyard uses abilities (called "stats," if I remember right). You have Acuity, Will, Heart, and Body. Different pairs of those are used for the four different kinds of conflicts. Like if you're fighting but not with a gun, you total Body + Will, but as soon as you start shooting, you are using Will + Acuity. Since you never get to add dice for a stat more than once in a conflict, that means escalating from fighting to guns lets you add your Acuity dice, but not if you started the conflict by talking (cuz talking is Acuity + Heart).

    My Life with Master has a weird sort of ability score system. You rank your character's Weariness and Self-Loathing, and these influence (up and down, according to clever little formulas) how well you can do terrible things to people, but succeeding and failing at those terrible things makes your Weariness and Self-Loathing go up.

    My problems with ability scores in AD&D are:
    * the funky way they handled Exceptional Strength (e.g., 18/00)
    * the derivative effects of each ability score (e.g., Dex 14 could mean a -2 to AC)
    * the individual scales of such effects and how they differ between abilities (a 14 Dex might be better than a 14 Int).

    These are problems they solved in 3E and 4E.

    There are plenty of games that have no abilities (just skills, or not even skills). FATE-based games, for example. Or carry, a game about war, which has no traits at all. Shock: has some numeric traits, but I hesitate to call them ability scores in the sense used here.

    What do you believe are the problems with ability scores? How do you even /define/ "ability score," to make sure we're talking about the same thing? At the highest level of design, if you want dice, you'll need a way to determine what dice to roll (or how to interpret them) and if you want it to be tied to the character somehow, you need some kind of number associated with the character. Ability scores are just one kind of number.

    \n In my case, while developing Do: Pilgrims of the Flying Temple, I had to ask myself "Does it matter whether character A is more likely to succeed in a feat of strength or intelligence or dexterity than character B?" The answer was "no." And that begged the question, "Does success or failure matter to me in this game?" And that answer was "Maybe."

    What mattered was a character getting into trouble, yes. But failing? No, that wasn't interesting. So the basic mechanic assumes that success is guaranteed, but trouble is in question.

    Consequently, characters do not have "ability scores" per se, simply two possible states (In Trouble/Out Of Trouble) that have different effects on narrative freedom.

    \n It seems that ability scores - whether as presented in D&D (whatever editions they may be), or as roll modifiers only (True 20), or as a base for rolling (GURPS, Warhammer Fantasy RPG), or dice (Dungeon Squad) - offer a quick and easy way for any player to be able to not only define their own character, but understand the measure of other characters. I mean, think about how we often define ourselves and others in real life; Joe is strong, Larry is smart, Betty is charming, etc. And then when we compare; Larry is smarter than Joe; Joe is stronger than Larry, etc.
    While it would be great for characters to be defined by more descriptive, story-based elements, I think there would be a steep learning curve for some people.
    What might be better is boiling down abilities into more thematic concepts (such as the abilities in Danger Patrol), or making everything a "skill," and assume that unless a skill or weakness says otherwise, the character is average in a particular area (Pirates and Privateers did a good job with this, I think).
    I've been toying around with the idea of thematic abilities (such as Effort, Action, Saavy, etc.) where they represent more HOW you do things, rather than what you use to do things, as sort of a middle ground between the old-school mechanics, and story-based concepts.

    \nBrian: I just wanted to tell you that I feel your pain. My solution in this has been something rather clever: I just use the entire score as the ability modifier in checks, actively refusing the layer of complexity added by a separate modifier. This might seem to give you too much variance (after all, characters could have even 15 points of difference in their abilities!), but it actually so happens that if you drop out the degenerate ability score matrices from later editions of D&D and use only 3d6, there is no problem: the great majority of characters will have abilities in the 6-15 range, which corresponds rather nicely with the -5 to +5 range of modifiers from 3rd edition D&D. It's still a bit more than the -3 to +3 in basic D&D, but for my purposes it's quite nice; abilities have an impact on how characters approach things, just as they should, but most characters will only have one exceptional (over 13 or under 8, say) ability, at least to begin with. This also means that when you have a ridiculous strength score like 18 (representing what, top 3% of humanity?), it also matters hugely in terms of what the character can expect to accomplish with the ability. Cinematic and tidy, nowadays I couldn't imagine going back to a separate ability modifier.

    As for the more general topic, isn't this just the same as asking whether characters should be differentiated from each other based on their capabilities? The answer is obviously going to hang on what the game is trying to accomplish. Many games go for D&D-like team adventure, in which character differentiation is nice because it makes different characters do different things. This could be achieved without numerical abilities, but those have tradition and utility on their side, so alternative solutions are not that common.

    Posted By: stupidgremlinIt seems that ability scores - whether as presented in D&D (whatever editions they may be), or as roll modifiers only (True 20), or as a base for rolling (GURPS, Warhammer Fantasy RPG), or dice (Dungeon Squad)
    \nSome other ways I have used ability scores:

    In one game I was designing, instead of the ability score directly affecting success, ability scores just affected the point cost of skills. This a high dexterity character would probably be better at dexterity type skills, but it wasn't a given. They might just have broader skill.

    Another way is that ability scores are primarily used for things other than success rolls. The way I have started to run RuneQuest, the ability scores don't contribute to skill bonuses. Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, and Size all affect combat by allowing use of certain weapons, allowing higher action speed, and granting higher hit points. Intelligence limits how many spells can be memorized. Power is spell points and spirit combat hit points. Now ability score rolls can still be made (and this is the primary effect of Charisma). When building a character, you get so many points to use on ability scores. And then you assign an array of percentage bonuses to the skill modifiers. The one thing is that I asked players to consider their ability scores in applying modifiers, but about the only placements I would ever veto would be a low dex, large PC putting +25% into their dodge, or a low intelligence PC having a high knowledge skills modifier. But even then, a player could probably talk me into a justification.

    I'm trying to think if I've ever played an RPG without ability scores. I really can't think of one. I do see the problems that ability scores present. In fact, RuneQuest really shows this. Intelligence is so important that I couldn't figure out a way to do point buy and steer players away from always maximizing intelligence. Yet, when I considered just removing ability scores entirely, it just didn't work for me. Pure strength is useful to know, but doesn't seem like a skill, although it is trainable (and RuneQuest provides rules that do allow strength training).

    I've also seen problems with casting everything as a skill. Invariably something gets left out and something that should have been part of the character concept isn't, either because the player didn't understand the skill list, or got lost in a huge skill list, or because there aren't enough points to buy a decent set of "everyman" skills and purchase some specialties also. This issue can be alleviated by having a good system of defaults, though often such systems are based on ability scores.

    In the end, I keep coming back to ability scores being useful, at least for the types of games I tend to play.

    Now Dogs in the Vineyard is interesting, because the stats aren't used quite the same way as other games use ability scores, however, they are a good example of how useful it is to have some common ground. If you just had traits in DitV, how would you handle escalation? How would you know which trait to roll to start a conflict (sure, the initiator can probably pick a trait, but how about the other guy?). So even a system that is very different from D&D, RuneQuest, and GURPS still depends on ability scores.

    \n As to having both ability scores and skills, I like having ability scores to cover those broad strokes, and things that don't do necessarily well as skills.Taking RuneQuest for an example: How much you can lift, how fast you are in combat, hit points, how many spells you can memorize, how many spell points you have. The skill bonuses (which actually are ability scores in a way) are nice because they give a broad generalization of ability, and the way I use them with the experience system means that someone who is good at "attack" will always have an experience advantage over someone not as good in "attack". One thing keeps all "fighters" from having the same skill abilities. You have to distribute one +5, two +10, two +15, two +20, and one +25 amongst a bunch of abilities, several of which are relevant to combat. So one guy might be +25 attack while another is +25 parry. If one wants to be good outside combat, then the variation comes in even more. One guy might be +25 knowledge, +20 parry, +20 manipulation, +15 attack, +15 dodge, etc. While another guy might put all his high scores into non-combat areas. There was always some variation on the more normal ability scores, depending on how much the player wanted to concentrate on magic use, though I accidentally gave a bit too large a point pool, so players didn't have to sacrifice too much. If I start up again, I will reduce the point pool (the point pool actually originated from reverse engineering a couple players totally arbitrary attribute picks when I decided to jump start the game without coming up with a point system - that was what also led to the very simple system for picking skill abilities).

    Perhaps some of the advantage of having both skills and abilities comes from having a crunchy system. OD&D works quite nice with a simpler system, though it still does have abilities and skills, it's just that it's skill list is in very broad strokes - the character classes). Tunnels & Trolls provides an example more heavily weighted to just abilities, though it still has character classes.

    I really can't imagine a system with only abilities being any more palatable than a system with only skills or traits. I guess part of the appeal for me comes from a set of abilities that usually are not as easy to change as skills/traits, or at least are changed differently (and perhaps this is part of where GURPS and Hero actually fail for me, abilities are just as or more easily changed that skills). Somehow having the two different types of ratings adds some depth to the system.
