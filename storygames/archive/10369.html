    \n The Secret Of Blackstone School, a game set in a boarding school, where the investigators are pupils. Along the lines of The Three Investigators, The Famous Five and Daisy Pulls It Off.

    Your character sheet is your report card and your grades translate to a skill level. For example, if you've got an A in Physics, you essentially have Physics 3, which lets you rig up yoghurt pots and string to overhear conversations. If you've got an B in English, you have English 2, which lets you decipher coded messages. Imaginative use of your academic skills is encouraged. Your A in Physical Education is your fighting skill.

    For each skill, the current number of points available to spend on Clues is marked by tokens. For example, if you have an A in Physics, you start with 3 Clue Tokens for Physics. Every time you attend an appropriate class, you refresh your tokens in that skill.

    Graham

    \n How about:

    * Judge Dee styled wuxia detective game. Chinese magistrates and maybe some kung-fu as well...you know, since you have Robin Laws RIGHT THERE! Ad copy - "Feng Shui was just the start..."

    * Something kinda post-cyberpunk mixed with Tom Clancy. International intelligence network or Interpol-esque agency that handles cyber-crime, next-level tech crimes/thefts, etc. Emergent Tech can be just as tricky to use in mysteries as magic can, so consistency is a boon here as well. You could even have one emergent tech that you give special properties to (like the mysteries in 'I, Robot') that serve as the basis for the mysteries. (This may step a bit on the MCB/Sci-Fi in terms of overlap).

    * Anime-styled Gumshoe - Steam Detectives, Future Boy Conan, QED, etc... (Nope, Japan...)

    * Military Police show - NCIS, JAG, etc.

    I have to second the idea of A Kid Detectives genre - that gets a lot of resonances there, from Scooby Doo to Encyclopedia Brown.

    \n How about a game that doesn't have magic or horror or anything else? Just a classic Law & Order game with plenty of accurate and interesting emphasis on police procedure, department politics, modern forensics (and budgetary limitations), and other elements?

    My side mechanic would be the Case mechanic. The Case rating impacts the throw of the dice at the end of the session to determine how things went in court. It has a base modifier based on the affluence and notoriety of the suspect, with high enough values resulting in even a minor mistake in police procedure allowing the suspect to evade justice. Case is increased or decreased during play based on found clues, good/bad rolls, and errors in police work brought into play through character flaws. For example, Elliot on Law and Order: SVU allows his temper and sense of justice to override his police work at times to the detriment of the case. Lenny on Law & Order sometimes slips up by playing things old school, or by focusing on "getting the guy" instead of building a case. Ray's turbulent homelife and overactive penis cause his work to become more difficult. All of these can ultimately impact the throw of dice at the end of the session to see how things turned out once the detectives have done their part.
