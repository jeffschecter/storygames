    \n (I wrote these notes in 2005)
    \n
    \nStar Trek Works Like This:
    \n==========
    \n1. Human cowboys/adventurers/dreamers
    \n2. A bunch of cool alien species embodying human tendencies
    \n3. Nify gadgets galore
    \n4. Smooching up green-skinned space hoochies
    \n5. Punching God in the nose.
    \n6. All of which is done in an optimistically secular humanist way.
    \n
    \nIt's actually kind of hard to do Numbers 4 and 5, which owe something to the pulp adventure serials. If you could have combined Jules Verne, Harlan Ellison, Isaac Asimov, and Robert Howard to work on a TV show, you'd end up with Star Trek.
    \n
    \nNow--I can change all kinds of funky color and silly stuff! But those are the invariable essentials of what makes Star Trek so wonderful and enduring. Note that *thinking about it too hard* endangers several points--notably 2, 3, 4, 5, and arguably 6.
    \n
    \nNote that over the years Star Trek has really downplayed the Space Hoochie angle; the last you really heard about that was the allegations that Riker was a ladykiller in Season 1 of TNG. The sexual revolution ended thirty years ago in the Federation, too!
    \n
    \nStar Trek has also downplayed the Aggressive Atheist too. Kirk had a chip on his shoulder: he punched God in the nose, obliterated world-governing AI systems with a sneer, and generally scorched all available earths once the fire of his skepticism had been ignited. Kirk was a bad ass! Kirk would roll up to a planet, laugh at its idiotic belief system, humiliate an entire culture, and then fly away. TNG, at least, moved to a "softer, gentler" humanism, where it's okay to believe stupid things so long as it doesn't hurt anyone: Picard never angrily shows a benighted culture the error of its ways.
    \n
    \n(Damn! "Firefly" really *was* a Star Trek rip-off--- right down to the two-fisted atheist!)

But only part of this is the "discuss the theme" style of writing, it's also a style of story presentation that puts the key plot moments foremost -- noteably at add breaks as if they were little cliffhangers.
    \n
    \nI do think that an awesome Trek rpg needs the Captain and crew and ship and their relationships, then throw out the rest of the since-established Trek universe. I'm thinking of the way Ron handles "the world" in Sword and Sorcerer or Trollbabe -- we start with some sketchy assumptions and then put whatever is useful for the story (or session) into the blanks on the map.
    \n
    \nI think too that a situation generation system -- like Dogs town creation -- is essential. The obvious starting place is "the planet where such and such a present day issue has been taken to extremes." (This suggests using current day issues that interest players and technological shocks just as Shock does.)
    \n
    \nBut I think it would be worth looking at TOS for the types of conflict in an episode, then creating a situation generation system that allows for each type. The story types I can think of for TOS are:
    \n
    \n- Enterprise arrives at a planet with a situation/issue [there's lots of these!]
    \n- Enterprise encounters superhuman beings (that represent the awesomeness or perversity of the universe)
    \n- Crew-focus episode -- a main character gets in trouble
    \n
    \nThe first step of situation generator might be to choose between 3 or 4 overall types like these.
    \n
    \nRemember the episodic nature of the show, too. One episode almost never follows directly on the actions of the previous one -- they stand alone as stories. Also, it's okay to graft new issues into PC lives between episodes.

      \n When I think Red Shirts, I think of the Gloating mechanism from Capes.

      1. In the fiction, the Alien Bad Guy wants to kill someone. Let's say he wants to kill Kirk.
      2. The rules require that Kirk lives. Therefore, if the ABG wins a conflit that would cause Kirk to die, the Red Shirt Rule applies instead.
      3. The Red Shirt Rule tells us that someone else is killed instead of Kirk. However, the ABG gains a bit of game-currency which will give it oomph in future conflicts.
      3b. Since the Red Shirt Rule states that the ABG can only gain the Dead Red Shirt bonus once per planet, it never bothers trying to kill anyone else. One poor enlisted guy bites the dust to save the upper class officers and the ABG has made it's point.

      It's also interesting how some of the ideas here about the Kirk Problem got reflected into it.       I was wondering, though, if there needs to be a Kirk problem. Given that we're AWESOMIFYING things, there's no reason we can't take a mighty page from the BSG reboot and tone down Kirk as the viewpoint character.

      I was thinking of doing something like this...for each of the Command Crew from TOS (whether they are the actual characters or archetypes) have a pair of somewhat opposed issues they express. Like:

      THE KIRK

      EXPLORATION: You seek out new worlds, people, experiences (including sexual ones!) You're the positive side of the Federation--the outward-looking, peacefully expanding culture

      EXPLOITATION: You also have the dark side of exploration, namely the impulse to meddle, change, or exploit people, worlds, relationships and cultures for your own understanding of morality or ends.

      THE SPOCK

      RATIONALITY: You can use logic and your supreme command of science to understand the universe and come up with the best course of events. When others let their vision become clouded with emotion, you can still act decisively and make the right call.

      THE SHADOW: Your struggle against your human side leaves you prone to all the darkness of human emotions: pain, longing, lust, anger. Sometimes you retreat into cold inhumanity to escape them; other times you give in to these impulses, with invariably destructive results.

      THE McCOY

      COMPASSION: You can cure just about anything, even relationships, given time and a lab. Your strong sense of humanity pushes you to do so, even when others would give up.

      BITTERNESS: You can't save everyone, though. And in an increasingly mechanized universe, you react strongly to people who try to transcend or deny their humanity. Sometimes you lash out at them, or act in ways that are self-destructive.

      THE SULU

      ROMANTICISM: Your passions are deep and varied, and you pursue them deeply. You have a deep sense of yourself as a noble warrior-poet, who will make the tough choices and sacrifices even to his own destruction. You love your job and being out there where history is being made.

      DISAPPOINTMENT: Not everyone understands your passions, or is put off by them. Sometimes you have to hide them from the world, which is a source of bitterness for you. And the world resolutely refuses to work out the way novels and epic poems tell you it should.

      THE UHURA

      COMPETENCE: You're utterly competent at what you do. You speak over a dozen languages, including several AI programming languages. Sure, it's the The Scotty who can fix the warp drive, but it's you who can tell him about the account from an Andorian tech manual way back in 2104 that found a way to warp with fractured dilithium crystals.

      FEAR OF FAILURE: You know you're a fragile thing in the universe and you fear running into the limits of your abilities--whether technical, emotional, or even the inevitable fading of your physical beauty. Ironically, you have difficulty communicating your deepest self to the world, especially to people you love.

      THE SCOTTY

      INGENIOUSNESS: If it's broken, you can fix it. You regularly transcend the boundaries of human knowledge, and sometimes the physical limits of the universe itself. No matter what, if you have a tool, you can come up with a solution to your problems.

      ASOCIAL: You can change the laws of physics, but not of human society. You don't know how to act, and have to resort to drink or displays of what you think are gender-appropriate behavior in order to fit in. You'd rather spend an evening with your technical manuals, because they're what you understand. When your passions are aroused, they tend to be inappropriately aimed. Sometimes you forget the human factor.

      THE CHEKHOV

      INNOCENCE: You experience everything like its the first time; it's what drove you to this mission of exploration and tends to align you strongly with The Kirk. You want new experiences, to gain in knowledge of the world.

      BRASHNESS: You're less experienced than everyone else but have a desperate pride that drives you to try and fit in. Sometimes you come off as brash, or aggressive. Your inexperience causes you to misjudge situations and respond inappropriately.
