I like Charisma

\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nFebruary 2009 \n edited February 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n We played some AD&D recently, which was thoroughly good fun. Creating a thief, I rolled 5 for Charisma.

Obviously, my first thought was that my character was rude. But I thought that would become boring. I wondered, briefly, about body odour and aggressiveness, before settling on tedium. My 5 Charisma meant that I was boring, I decided. I was a geeky thief, a lock obsessive.

And, God, that was the best roleplaying tool in the world. I spent the game talking about the intricacies of locks. "Oh, yeah, that's a mock Dwarven design". "That's going to be difficult, it's got horizontal tumblers". Every time I picked a lock or found a secret passage, I explained how. "The sliding mechanism meant it was flush against the rock, but you could see the cracks around the outline of the door. They should have disguised that with some fake moss."

I can't think of much, in indie games, that beats Charisma as a roleplaying tool. Firstly, it's random. I love that. You suddenly find yourself with 5 Charisma, or 15, and must explain it. Secondly, it's unspecific. It's not like a freeform trait: "I am obsessed with locks". That's much more dull. Charisma gives me some constraint, but also some freedom of imagination.

If there's one other roleplaying tool I like as much as Charisma, it's Intelligence. Playing a character with low Intelligence is equally fun.

Graham

    \n Can't any low stat be a role-playing opportunity?

    Strength 5: "Gah, I can't open this potion. Can you open it for me, Conan?"
    Intelligence 5: "Attack the orc? Which one is the orc?"
    Wisdom 5: "Hey, guys. I met these nice orcs and they wanted to meet you guys."
    Dexterity 5: "Oops!" tink, tink, tink! "Sorry. At least the potion of healing didn't break."
    Constitution 5: "Can we stop and take a break? I'm tired. I'm totally sleeping in tomorrow."
    Charisma 5: "Oh gawwwwwds, not more monsters. I'm so sick of monsters. All we do is fight and fight and fight. Well, it's not my turn."

    (Presented in Olde School order.)

    When I play a character with some kind of disability, I try not to make the disability hurt other PCs. Like, dropping the potion is funny, unless you actually break it. in doubt, ask the players first: "Hey, is it okay if I say a bunch of kobolds talk my low-Wisdom rogue into leading them back to the group? We're gonna end up fighting them anyway."

    Don't let your GM be a dick to you, either; making you make Dex rolls or something not to drop your stuff just discourages you from role-playing your low stat. When

    \n Adam (which is not to dismiss anyone else's contribution, you understand), yes. What fascinates me is that there's a choice in how you play it. Is a low Wisdom stupidity or naivete?

    I wonder if there's a difference, too, between stats that define what you are and those that define what you do. Strength seems, generally, to define something you can do. Charisma and Wisdom are more interesting, to me, because they define something you are. I realise the division isn't perfect, but it makes some sense. I feel we generally design for what characters can do, rather than what they are.

    \n I think any one low stat is a great oportunity for roleplay. I rolled up a character with 16 Dex and 4 Con. Clearly the 16 Dex put me in thief territory (or I suppose Illusionist, as the Int was higher than 9). I thought about what the 4 Con might be. I decided that the Thief had some prior experience and ran into a trap that he failed to remove properly, and it poisoned him, severely weakening him. The end result was that he'd be willing to do normal Thief things, but when it came to traps he'd search for them, but if it was necessary to disarm them he'd go "'To hell with this, if this is the only way on, I'm going home." With the potential that if there was a very clear goal (rather than let's see what's further on), that was actually important (ie, save the world, or a good chunk of it type stuff), the other characters could cajole and bribe him into dealing with that one trap.

    Come to think of it, it is like games that have quirk and backstory characteristics, but there's no way I'd think of "sprung a trap and was poisoned, badly weakening health" and "refuses to disarm traps" as Thief character concepts in a system like that. That's only something that comes up with the specific combination of High and Low scores.

    I also rolled another one in BD&D which was 9 across the board and 18 Wisdom. I was trying to think of how to deal with that, and then decided that I was a very old Priest, not really a fighter type, but the kind who has been around, talked to a lot of people and has experience. He hasn't actually been out adventuring himself, but he has heard a lot of stories from many adventurers, so he's not the type to make mistakes. Any description quirk that tips me off as a player that something is up, due to some meta-gaming (and I have to, 'cause there's no way I as a player have what would be considered 18 Wisdom), the character has heard about it and recognises something is different. So he doesn't make stupid decisions. For everything else though, he's not particularly competent. He's not strong, fast, tough, smart or well spoken, so his character role wasn't as much that of a leader as much as an advisor for the rest of the party.

    Other times the rolls end up really boring and I can't think of a good back story. In that case, I like to completely re-roll everything and see if something better turns up. There's not much you can do with average across the board unless you have one high and one low score (only one high score can also work, average plus low score has some serious suck to it that makes me want to re-roll as well).

    Posted By: Adam DrayCan't any low stat be a role-playing opportunity?
    \nYes! Any stat score at all, in fact. I made this point in my "Story Made Simple" thread project (that I should really get back to, but don't have the energy for).

    The trick is to think of the character as a person first, and a functional avatar in the SIS second. He's got 18 Strength? Why? Is it genetic? If so, what was his family like? Is he following in the footsteps of his forefathers, or breaking from them? Or is it through training? Or did he work a heavy-labor job? Miner? Dockworker? And so on.

    You can do this with attributes, skills, classes, alignments -- everything.

    So I had a thing to say a ways back, and the thread ran away from me. It's this:\n
    Posted By: Marshall BurnsThe trick is to think of the character as a person first, and a functional avatar in the SIS second. He's got 18 Strength? Why? Is it genetic? If so, what was his family like? Is he following in the footsteps of his forefathers, or breaking from them? Or is it through training? Or did he work a heavy-labor job? Miner? Dockworker? And so on.
    \nI've always found this to be super-hard to achieve in any long-term group I've been in. As a teen I seemed to be the one guy who was assigning scores based on what my guy is like. And it never did me any good; I'd put all my high rolls into intelligence and charisma, only to have the more socially-dominant player out-influence me with his dumb-as-rocks barbarian.

    ANd when I found a new group in adulthood, it was much the same. D&D 3E was out, and everyone was going hardcore on the 'builds," where you have a high Cha because your Sorcerer needs a high Cha, and that's that. I remember I made a con-artisty Rogue with high Int and Cha, and low Wis--figuring he's too clever and charming for his own good. I really wanted a "talks his way into trouble, then talks his way out" deal for him. But I quickly figured out that the low Wis was just making it hard to spot guys sneaking up on me, or tel when someone's lying--so, with the pressure and approval of the group, I rebuilt him with a nice modest Wisdom like 12 or something.

    But Jaden Augurfuris still has 8 Wisdom in my heart.
