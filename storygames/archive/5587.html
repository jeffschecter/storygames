\n I've been struggling with this for ages.

How do I do a game where

a) there are these innocuous portals like in the more lighthearted parts of planescape (hey, this well goes straight to that fey demiplane! That must be where my grandfather found his magic carpet!)

b) large-scale conflict is still fought with fleets of ships / armies / tanks / airships

Because I've been thinking about it for a long time and every time I see it I think "man, there's really nothing more important than controlling portals." Large scale warfare would turn into a matter of commando raids and such because portals create incredible vulnerability. But I like big airship battles.

    \n Portals are strategic chokepoints. But you want them to be like beachheads in amphibous warfare: the important thing is to break out once you've arrived. Maybe for some reason, you can't build anything near them (I know: because they're hanging in the air and they float around). So once you get through the portal, you can expect defending fliers to arrive to try to push you back through or shoot you down, and you want to get to the bases where the defenders muster in order to disable their capability to do that.

    Now you're not attacking and defending these fixed points; instead, you've got zones in which invaders will likely appear, that you have to monitor with your own ships or vehicles, rather than fixed installations. And having them hang in mid-air means that portal commandos can't really secure the portals by sneak attack; nobody can.

    There you go: the best of both worlds.

    \n Portals as fuel, munitions and conduits?

    Portals lead to Another Place. Another Place has materials that can be refined into fuel to generate lift for the air ships and highly destructive weapons. As such, it is in a nation's best interests to find and control these portals. Portals are much too small for warships to fit through, so in This Place, wars can be decided by huge war machines, but these war machines need Stuff from Another Place. So there're also raids into Another Place, where it's discovered that portals in This Place are connected in Another Place; an elite team can go from their headquarters to enemy headquarters. And all the while, denizens of Another Place are on the sidelines saying, "WTF?!"

    \n Portals only exist on non-attached constructions. That is, things that can move. We can build them, yes, but it's not easy. It's costly in time and resources. And we still don't understand how to make a portal for a specific place. It appears to be a physical law that each must be unique. And also a portal will change destinations if the construction changes for anything but repairs (it's rumored that some of the kingdoms have an ancient canoe or wagon in its vaults, hopelessly antique, but with a very useful active portal). But where they will lead we can't determine until the construction is complete and the portal has power.

    In ancient days, portals were crafted into the earliest wagons and river boats. Wars were fought over control and defense of these. But as humanity progressed, we took to the high seas and later the sky. Now, one of the best ways to ensure to safety of a portal is to build it into something that can defend itself, a sea vessel or skyship. But as always, the powerful try to one-up their rivals, so you find these Portalships only with their masive fleets to defend them.

    A popular debate among portal engineering strategists is whether you build a portal ship with weaponry or without. The "with" camp says that it adds to the defense, and because of the cost and time to build a ship, adding several dozen cannons and lightning generators is a pittance. The "without" camp likes to point out that these costs could be moved to the defending fleet, and by keeping the Portalship from being a threat, it reduces the chance for accidental destruction, and add to do otherwise is short-sighted. The "with" camp counters by claiming they are "designing in surrender".

    Elite squads get sent into the portals, sometimes leading work crews, to extract useful things from the world on the otherside. More than one squad over the centuries has returned to the entry point only to find their portal gone, the ship that generated it, destroyed. For this reason, we must be cautious not to send too many through at once, or as they say in the north, too many that you hope to see again.

    \n What a topical thread.

    The "Two Worlds" campaign world my players created last spring starts next weekend and it has both portals and airships. The portals are generally small, inconstant, and located and opened only by those who are arcanists or of both worlds, "night-blooded." Humans have to enter the "otherworld" and then find their way back, generally having to travel some distance by conventional means in between, so it's not exactly teleportation most of the time. Passing though a door is also extremely stressful, so they're not exactly the kind of thing you'd want to drive an army through for a surprise attack...

    Those factors solve some problems, but it's not entirely defined yet and I like the idea that some areas could become tactically important for a period of time, maybe with some kind of priestly astrologist factions whose predictive powers are militarily important.
