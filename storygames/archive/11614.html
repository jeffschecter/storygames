    \n A few ideas...

    > Text Messaging

    Works well with a small number of people. It would be fantastic if you could text message a number to subscribe to specific updates. Text messaging is great because most phones can do it. But it also costs money.

    > Twitter

    Twitter with #hashtags works well. Not all phones have twitter. But it's incredibly easy to use.

    > Posterous

    Posterous.com could be used to updated a shared blog. People could email, text message, or tweet game updates to Posterous and it would post everything to 1 place for easy access. The downside is you have to have a phone with a basic web browser (although nothing fancy). And you have to give game organizers permission to contribute (vs twitter where anyone can use the same #hashtag). It also has the benefit of being versatile. You could output the updates to twitter for example.

    > Wiki

    Are there wiki's you can update via twitter or text messaging? This could work similar to Posterous above.

    \n if you're talking about organizing pickups among a community, a text-tree? like, text (or call) people that you know would be interested, and they have people who they know would be interested that they can text if they can't/don't want to play, etc. which could be set up before-hand without necessitating that everyone know everyone else.

    hrm. example: jenskot starts a GenCon pickup-game text-tree thread where he says "hey, if you're going to Gen Con and are interested in a pickup game, whisper me your number so I can text you. When I have 5 numbers (or whatever), I'll post the names and people can whisper those people their number, and so on. If you want to run a pickup, post in this thread, same rules." So as to create a tree where you only have to text 5 people at a time, but your drawing from a known community pool.

    Which is probably more front-end organization than you're looking for, but hey, a thought.

    \n I'm sure some folks here have been to a few techie "unconferences"; does anyone know the specific kinds of techniques they use?

    I think one important thing is to not get muddled into a consensus. You do want to fish for general interest, but I think a pickup game (given diffuse enough organization as-is) needs one person to say "I will do this if I get N others to jump on board".

    So I think you want a place to broadcast "here's my idea / standing offer" and a response-channel for "ok, I'm interested". A twitter hashtag for #genconpickup could work for broadcast (and DMs or cell text messages would get back to the key person to organize). Maybe a bulletin board with index cards (and text-message contact info) to propose ideas?

    \n It sounds like you are looking for a broader, more technical solution, sort of a matchmaking service many people would use at once. Here are the hurdles I see:

    Variance in aptitude and technology. If the solution requires, say, twittering from your phone, a certain segment is out because they can't or don't know how.

    Aggregation and organization. This is an interface design issue and not a crushing one, but people need to be able to parse the incoming data quickly and easily, make connections, commit, and get full sessions closed, etc.

    Just-in-time. All this has to go down in half an hour or something - at least that seems like the model now. You aren't going to plan a few days in advance for your down time.

    To me all these potential hurdles speak to a very low-tech solution, like a centralized physical bulletin board that serves both as information hub and meeting point. The lobby of the Embassy Suites at Gen Con serves this purpose informally - people mingle, GMs round people up, and a small cohort just stay to chat all evening while others make matches and go game.

    Just some thoughts!

    \n It depends entirely upon your goals.

    Do you want to game with friends, scenesters and those who're already connected to the Story Games community?
    Use Twitter, or word of mouth at a popular "scene hangout" (Embassy Suites, Games on Demand, IPR booth, Burning Wheel booth, etc).

    Do you want to do just that, but be much more selective?
    Don't use Twitter, just go to the popular scene hang-outs and actually pick out and ask the people you want to play with.

    Do you want to meet new people, and invite people outside the scene?
    Play in a random semi-indie game (Savage Worlds, something homebrew, something old-skool, something "wacky"), and then afterwards invite the people you enjoyed playing with to play another game, this one being impromptu.

    Do you want to meet hot chicks?
    Play in a random semi-indie game that has hot chicks playing, and then afterwards invite them to play another game, this one being impromptu.

    \n Jiawen, I could definitely see that!

    At NerdNYC's Recess event, when we have pickup games I usually will walk around for people announcing that games are open. It works well because I have a staff badge and am making general announcements anyway so people are already tuned in. It also works both ways, players will then start to come to me and ask what games are still open even when I'm not making announcements.

    In terms of the low tech route, we can have a giant bulletin board with black forms people could fill out. 1 form for people wanting to run games and another form where people can list all the games they would like to play and the times they think they will be free. Then potential GMs could look through the lists to inform them of what games they may want to run. This way it's not all GM -> Players one way communication.
