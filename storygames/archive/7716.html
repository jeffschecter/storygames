\n So we've got a lot of good techniques for seeking out intensity ... especially for using explicit out-of-character discussions in order to figure out what direction to take things in order to maximize intensity.
\n
\nI feel that this is becoming the proverbial hammer that makes every problem look like a nail.
\n
\nI recently played two playtests of Misery Bubblegum with my friends, in the same story-arc. It was about some kids who were in a School for Spies, and here's my point about it:
\n
\nGame #1: We had big, big, big dreams. Raven wanted her (storied) grandfather to think she was a worthy successor to his legacy. She planned to pull a big, complicated double-cross on some of her classmates to make it happen. She feared that if he found out that she needed his approval, he'd stop loving her as a grand-daughter. Dustin wanted Raven to see him as more than just the muscle-head of their group. He planned to run an operation behind her back to make that happen. If she found out too soon, she might never trust him again.
\n
\nGame #2: We had one small dream: Raven wanted (adversary) Tom Hawke to be interested in her. She planned to pull a desperate operation without resorting to dirty tricks, so that he could see that her (increasingly decent) tactics could work. She feared that if he found out that she wanted his attention, he'd cease to have any lingering respect for her.
\n
\nGame #1 was good, but felt very driven and frantic. Game #2 was awesome, and felt natural and comfortable in pacing.
\n
\nIn Game #1, we sometimes froze up ... unable to figure out how to address something BIG ENOUGH, we addressed very little of any consequence.
\n
\nIn Game #2, we always had more ideas than we knew what to do with, and skipped from one to the other according to which was the most important right now.
\n
\nAt the conclusion of Game #1 we addressed the big, big dreams, and it was heart-warming but felt very inexorable and final. It was unclear what either of the characters could do next, having resolved the one thing driving them.
\n
\nAt the conclusion of Game #2, Raven confessed that she secretly "likes" Tom (and, yes, it was that ambiguous) ... which was a total surprise, but felt completely natural, and leaves us desperately wanting to know what comes next.
\n
\nSo I would suggest (with actual play to back it up) that there is great fun to be had by aiming for much less than the most intense thing you can do just this moment. I would go on to suggest that there are a wide panoply of techniques (like, for instance, framing scenes that have no point except to show the characters being themselves) that are well suited for this goal and poorly suited for the goal of seeking out high intensity ... and that those techniques have been given short shrift by our design community, precisely because of the focus on high-intensity play.

    \nWell, see, here's the thing, at least from my own experience. Shooting for high-intensity gets you something that is very intense and frantic. But, when that rush wears off, it's gone. Calming down, chilling out, slowing down a bit, and letting the characters be characters (framing scenes for characters to just be themselves is a wonderful thing), that leads to something that is calmer, but more nuanced and resonant. Intensely resonant, sometimes.

    What I'm saying is that the real intensity is when you don't Shoot For Intensity. Shoot For Intensity gets you a nice, sweet rush, but it's empty calories. It's a candy bar. The slower, calmer kind is a steak. (I also think that the slower, calmer kind is the one that's referred to on the cover of Sorcerer)

    I figured this out the hard way with The Rustbelt.

    \n So: What techniques have you seen that are particularly well suited for low-intensity play, but that get short shrift because they aren't suited for high-intensity play?
    \n
    \nFor instance, in another threat I suggested that the act of ending a scene can be viewed as not merely the necessary adjunct to starting the next scene, but also as a way to add emphasis to an action or statement (by making it the last one in the scene).
    \n
    \nIf Jackie says to Jason "I love you ... I've loved you for a long time," and we all wait patiently for Jason's player to craft a response ... well, that's one thing.
    \n
    \nIf Jackie says "I love you ... I've loved you for a long time," and we say "And we cut away from the scene there. Let's check in on how Molly's doing with the demon-summoning ritual," then that emphasizes the heck out of Jackie's confession.
    \n
    \nDoesn't really work in a game where the meat of what you're looking for is to see Jackie and Jason resolve their relationship, but works great in a game where the confession is a bang that's meant to fuel lots of later angst, with the relationship still being up in the air.

    \n I can relate somewhat--I'm currently working on a game that's nothing groundbreaking or too exciting, but is just aiming to combine a few elements of the tried-and-true that I've enjoyed. I was inspired to write it because of a game I played a few years ago and still look back at fondly.

    I had four players: a disgruntled roleplayer (he had stopped having fun with RPGs; but he stilled had some hope, evidently, as he joined this game), a long-time-ago-I-think-I-kind-of-remember roleplayer, and two total newbies.

    This meant that I wasn't keen to lean on any heavy stuff. I wanted to let things develope naturally. We also weren't using any sort of system that would drive behaviour in any given direction--it was a light, stay-out-of-the-way kind of system.

    Also, everyone (including myself) was learning as they went along. Two people were totally new; the others (including me) were trying to play in a style completely different from anything we'd tried before, without any clear guidance or goals--we were all just aware that we wanted something _different_. Having to clarify the procedures of play slowly and carefully meant that the game itself proceeded at a very leisurely pace.

    And the result was really, really wonderful. A heart-warming, moving story everyone enjoyed, which mostly consisted of people talking to each other and some occasional light conflict (some arguments, a minor theft, a friendly sparring match which became important because onlookers showed up and one of the parties involved wanted to impress them). Unfortunately, the game had to end just as we were hitting our groove (three sessions in)... and what was interesting is that, just then, I felt like we were ready to go somewhere, and at the point I started really wishing we were using a system that would give us all a kick in the butt and some more hardcore conflicts and issues. I'm not sure the game, as it had started, could have continued indefinitely and still been fun.

    One of the keys was that no one (including me as the GM) was pushing much, fiction-wise or pacing-wise. Also, whether by coincidence or not, the two new players created characters that had similar issues to their own, and that made them more sympathetic and/or funny--we all wanted to cheer when they went to overcome their issues.

    Roleplaying, as an activity, can have this wonderful feeling of "exploration", whether it's the fiction being explored or just the process itself. I could see the players thinking, "OK, but what if I do this... what will happen? Ok, gently..." I personally have played many wonderful games which consisted largely just of exploring a fictional milieu and enjoying discovering things about it. Kind of similar to the computer game Myst.

    I don't know if that helps.

    But I've also been rereading Eero's "Solar System". And that has "refreshment scenes" in it. That seems to belong in this conversation, somewhere. I dunno.

    I think a lot of this stuff gets squashed if you require that the players know what the connection will be for later before they spend a scene on it to start.

    You can create meaning by throwing out real, human, stuff without knowing how it will all go together: We're all really good at drawing the threads together, later ... honest.
