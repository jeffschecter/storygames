    Thinking on this, the baseline AW playbooks (they're the few I know about) would work, with the Apocalypse numbers filed off and a coat of Traveller paint; Gunlugger, Chopper, Driver, Angel, Hardholder.     Traveller wasn't post-apocalyptic (until a later edition), but a system could be. The characters would have to be able to get along almost anywhere, or fail to do so in enjoyable ways.

    Playbook, schmaybook. Traveller character generation as it stands is such that each term is itself a set of moves that you make (you're even rolling 2d6, for heaven's sake).
    When you try to enlist in the Scouts, roll +Str and +Int. On a 10+, they accept you and your training proceeds normally; you serve a term in the Scouts. On a 7-9, they accept you but your training is particularly hazardous; you face death in the line of duty. If you survive, you serve a term in the Scouts. On a 6 or less, the Scouts reject you and you are subject to the Draft.

    When you serve a term in the Scouts, roll +End. On a 10+, you are sent on a Scout mission that proceeds without complication; choose one personal development benefit, service skill, or advanced education training [these are not in themselves moves but instead bonuses to other moves you may make] and you may serve another term in the Scouts. On a 7-9, the mission experiences difficulties; choose a benefit as above but you must either leave the service and muster out or face death in the line of duty. On a 6 or less, choose a benefit as above and you face death in the line of duty. In any case, advance your age 4 years.
    ...and so forth.

    As far as getting a ship is concerned, there are actually two different Traveller games; one where the PCs have a ship, and one where they don't and have to buy or work for passage between systems. Both are interesting, but they're hard to mix. One PC "owning" a ship on which other PCs are passengers is worse than when other PCs are long-term crew or better yet co-owners, so I'd institute some kind of "ship share" mustering out benefit the way that Mongoose Traveller does. The PCs can then choose whether to have a ship or not, and if anyone "has" a ship, they all do.

    thinking further in terms of exception-based design, here is a move I came up with for system generation (as the CT system takes a lot of die-rolling and generates a lot of unexceptional worlds, despite the gold it can throw up).
    Basically it is assumed that you are generating a cluster or sub-sector and the "hold" is used to reduce the number of really exceptional worlds. It is designed to incorporate the tropes of CT-style world generation.

    When you generate a new worldconsult library data about a world or scan it for the first time:
    Roll + 0hold
    On a 10+ choose 3 from list A below and take -1 holdor take 4 from A and two from B:
    On a 7-9 choose 1 from list A and one from list B:
    On a 6-, take +1 hold and pick 1-3 from list B, if you take 3 then also pick one from list A:

    List A
    - it is a rich world with plenty for all
    - there is great personal freedom and little bureaucracy
    - high tech is freely available
    - it is a centre of extreme high tech (counts as 2 picks)
    - it is teeming with people
    - there are excellent starport facilities
    - local industry is booming
    - there is an imperial navy base
    - the government is representative and/or open-minded
    - food and other crops thrive here
    - the world is balkanised

    List B
    - the atmosphere is unfit for humans, pick: vacuum, trace, tainted, corrosive or exotic
    - there are few people, if any, on the planet
    - the starport facilities are primitive or non-existant
    - individual action is repressed, red tape is everywhere
    - no high tech is found on this planet
    - the locals are primitives, living in a preindustrial society
    - the world has an extreme environment, pick from: desert world, water world, arctic world
    - it is poor
    - there is little local industry
    - the world is unsuitable for agriculture
    - there's a local scout base
    - the government is elitist and/or regressive
    - no gas giants exist in the system for refueling

    Then choose other details like world size (remember asteroid belts are possible) to suit your picks. Contradictory picks are available, either don't pick these or do and rationalise them!
    By default a world can support humans, has less than a couple of million inhabitants and has low interstellar technology, an adequate startport and police harassment of the PCs on every 2nd day :-)

      Some Starship Combat Thoughts

      Ranges: Escape, Outside weapons range, Missile range, Laser range, Boarding range

      Relative Ship Performance dice roll modifiers for moves:
      Comp = Difference in computer number between the ships,
      Gs = difference in maneuver-drive number between the ships

      Ship Weapon Harm Values
      Beam lasers 1
      Pulse lasers 2
      Missiles 1d6

      To escape or avoid contact: roll + pilot + Gs
      10+ you are safely outside weapons range and if the ref decides it you have escaped entirely
      7-9: either:
      - you successfully dodge and jink to avoid fire, hazards or a dangerous enemy maneuver but the range remains about the same, or
      - you floor it and increase the range by one band but you either need to avoid incoming fire or make emergency repairs on a malfunctioning sub-system (perhaps the drives, powerplant, computer or sensors) to complete the maneuver.
      6 or less:
      The ref will decide what happens

      To make emergency repairs to partially destroyed or malfunctioning equipment: expend a unit of spares and roll + engineering
      10+: the item returns to service, for the moment. Pick 1 from the list
      7-9: the item returns to service, for the moment. Pick 2 from the list
      - it operates at drastically reduced efficiency, maybe 50%
      - it needs your ongoing undivided attention and care to continue to work
      - it will fail again soon
      - you must spend additional spares and time to complete the repair
      6 or less:
      The ref will decide what happens

      To avoid incoming fire: roll + pilot + comp, or
      + gunnery + comp if performing anti-missile point defence with lasers, or
      + gunnery + comp if defending against lasers or missiles with sandcasters, or
      + computer + comp if defending against missiles with ECM
      10+ you suffer explosions, shaking and maybe cosmetic damage
      7-9 the ref picks a number of the following equal to the ship harm inflicted:
      - Maneuver drives are hit, reduce rating by 1, if now 0 it is disabled
      - Jump drives are hit, reduce rating by 1, if now 0 it is disabled
      - Power plant is hit, hold 1 brown-out that the MC may use to delay or cancel future actions
      - a Turret is hit, disabling all weapons there
      - Hull penetrated in one compartment or zone, explosive decompression if not already decompressed. Anyone there must don a vacc suit in a hurry
      - Hold/cargo hit and partially destroyed
      - Fuel tank hit, now leaking fuel into space, unless a special design it is unlikely that the ship has sufficient fuel left to jump
      - Computer hit, hold one malfunction that the MC may use to delay or cancel future actions
      - Bridge or crew hit, one or more characters there are down, they need to make the Harm move with 1d6 harm AP
      - Your defensive sand screen is evaporated or dispersed
      - Defence takes all your concentration and you miss your next chance to target the enemy
      On a 6 or less:
      the ref picks from the 7-9 list but the item is completely destroyed, sufficient harm may vaporise the ship

      To close with and potentially board a hostile enemy: roll + pilot + Gs
      10+ you make it to your desired range
      7-9: you close by one range band and the ref may choose one of:
      - you draw fire or attention
      - a new threat becomes obvious
      - you miss something important
      6 or less:
      The ref will decide what happens

      To disable or destroy an enemy ship:
      - go aggro, seize by force?

      You can choose to work a number of gigs+obligations equal to your number of qualified crew for each day in-port. You have obligation gigs as per your spaceship or last commitments/load.
      The captain rolls + deal, on 10+ get profit from all, on 7-9 get profit from 1, if you picked more than 1 then you get catastrophe from one, on a miss the referee will pick many catastrophes. Unworked gigs give neither.
      Starship Gigs:
      Sell speculative cargo (+credits/ripped off)
      Locate passengers (+credits+obligation/complicated passengers)
      Find cargo (+credits+obligation/complicated cargo)
      Find speculative cargo (locate something potentially profitable/press ganged)
      Go sight-seeing (learn a fact/cornered by hostile locals)

      Robb - your move setup is a lot like were we started with engagement moves in the Regiment. We are moving away from that concept in favor of a single move, where the trigger is "When you start a new engagement" rather than a set of parallel of fictional triggers like "Ambush," "Defend," etc. We made the switch because the 1) Regiment already has so many moves 2) the six or so engagement types we started with, left gaps - that is, "I want to do this other thing; wait, there's no trigger in the list of engagement moves" and 3) there were a lot of overlaps in the fictional outcomes - both ambush and travel (I think) had "you get ambushed instead" as a miss result. I think we made the right choice in consolidating the engagement moves for the Regiment; but, as a result, the move has lost a lot of it's narrative grab. Our approach for the Regiment may not be the right approach here; but, do consider the following:

      Like Noofy says, the 10+ result should be fiction-based. If you are using this like the operator move, where you can cut in at any point in the mission, it may not make sense to give them their reward with the move (e.g. if you cut to the beginning of the mission and you're playing to find out what happens); but, after they've established it in the fiction. In the Regiment, the engagement move never resolves the conflict, it only establishes the conditions when play starts. If that's what you're going for, the architecture should be (major advantage / complications / disaster) rather than (reward / complications/ disaster). If you set up your moves this way, then you can either resolve the mission (like the Operator move) as good, bad, or ugly or you can cut in (like the engagement move) at the beginning with "things are looking good, no one's sounded the alarm;" in the middle with, "you've got eyes on your prize, there are three shabby-looking scavengers sifting through the wreckage looking for salvage - but you have the drop on them;" or at the end with, "You've got the goods in hand and made a clean getaway - time to hit the pub!"

      Play test the move a bunch to see if you have irritating gaps or overlaps in your triggers and outcomes. I thought we had a pretty good set for the Regiment when we started; but, found the list lacking in play.

      Play to find out! :-)
      More seriously it is just a hook. I've not defined what any of the tags mean. They are just meant to trigger you to think of stuff at the table. In Classic Traveller the main advantage that a young character has is that their latent psi talents, if any, have not yet dwindled without training. Otherwise they are just worse than the older guys+gals. I know it is not very AW to discriminate based on age but I thought that it was an important enough trope of CT (young PCs are outshone by the older ones) to include it in some form.

      In my own games, when it has been important, I've got players to roll 2d6 for their latent Psi talent with 10+ being something interesting, 7-9 having complications + a glimmer and 6- being trouble. Characters without the psi potential tag have shifted down results one tier. However that's just a wild stab that worked at my table at the time... Please post and tell me what you do with it!
