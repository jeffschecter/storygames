Describe a room in a dungeon

\n
\n
\n
\n\nGraham \n\n\n
\n
\n\nDecember 2009 \n edited December 2009 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n Hey, try this out for me.

You're going to describe a large room in an underground dungeon. Do this aloud. Here are some guidelines.\n
  • Be obvious. Describe things that you naturally imagine would be in a dungeon.
    • \n
  • Picture the dungeon.
    • \n
  • Don't be afraid to make things up.
    • \n
  • Let one thing lead to another: let your description of the walls lead to describing the shadows on them.
    • \n
  • Expand on details: let your description of the walls lead to describing the brickwork. Don't worry about going into too much detail: if you spend 30 seconds describing the brickwork, that's fine.
    • \n
  • Don't censor yourself.
    • \n
  • Describe things you find unpleasant.
\nKeep going for at least 30 seconds: again, you're describing aloud.

Did you find that difficult or was it easy? If it was difficult, what was difficult? What was your dungeon like?

Graham

    \n It was pretty easy.

    It was a room that was crumbling into itself and in which a variety of big mushrooms were growing. There were some creatures camped underneath them, and you could just see the light from the torches of some adventurers coming round a corner. In parts the ceiling was very high. There were echoes and a musty smell. The bricks of the wall were damp and the grouting crumbled to the touch.

    In part, I guess, it might have been inspired by Ultima Underworld and the various games for which I created dungeons over the years, underground cities being a speciality. Also, creative writing has taught me to hit all the senses. Jack Vance may also have something to answer for. But I did wonder about the audience. Who was I describing this for, how much should be obvious to them, and how much secret and how much should hint at some deeper truth or mystery. The room exists for a reason. Is that reason dependent on some plot, or some story or some clue?

    How about people record these and send them in?

    \n I'm trying to figure out how to explain why describing a room in a dungeon is hard. It's not because it's difficult. I can riff on any number of stereotype subterrenean environments at will. It becomes hard when you want to describe a room that's not stereotypicaly. I try to surprise with every dungeon locale I represent. I want my dungeons to surprise even me to a certain extent. In that way it's not unlike running a character. If I don't feel like I'm taking some risks, I'm not doing my job as a dungeonmaster.

    \n I think the difficulties that come are the classic problems with creativity and brainstorming: It's hard to create and judge and broadcast all at the same time.

    Here's something that I think would be easier: take the above scenario and:

    1. Spend 30 seconds just thinking about the dungeon room. Make notes or a mindmap or whatever if you like.

    2. Spend 30 seconds editing what you have so far; delete things you don't like, expand on things you like, organize everything a bit.

    3. NOW spend 30 seconds talking about the dungeon room.


    Or another approach, based on the 'constraints are liberating' concept:

    1. Spend 30 seconds thinking and making notes about the dungeon as a whole. Something like "It's a kobold warren, clawed out of the clay soil. Moist, hot, stinky. Most tunnels 3' high. Fungus."

    2. Pick a random room, and: spend 10 seconds talking about what the party sees; then 5 seconds about what they can hear; then 5 seconds about what they can smell; then 5 seconds about what they can feel; then 5 seconds about any monsters or other threats.


    I'll hazard a guess that the most successful of us in Graham's exercise are the ones who carried a lot of #1 in with us, consciously or not.

    \n I didn't time myself. I think I talked for over thirty seconds, but not by much. It started out easy. I was scanning the bullet points in your opening note as I did so. I detailed the source of light in the room for quite a while including the flickering yellow and the complementary shadows. I totally stumbled when I got to the last bullet. I felt pressured to add something unpleasant. I ended up with it being quite cool and then fumbled for bad odors. It didn't start out as an unpleasant room and then I had to retrofit unpleasantness into it.

    I first imageined a big room -- you told me to! Then it seemed weird to be big, so I put a big sloping pit in the center. Then, I was going around the room looking for details as I was reading your list, so I talked about the walls (hewn from the living stone, not masonry at all) and the iron pots hanging around the perimeter with burning oil in each which lead to imagining the yellow light cast and the darkness in the center of the room. But since I'd already established the sloped pit, I made sure the edges of the slope were visible and the darkend center was creepy and alluring. Then there was sand on the floor and some lichen growing on a broken barrel in the corner, stuff like that. So I guess I was panning around the room and serially zooming in on details.

    I think you could make it easier by asking the subject a bunch of questions instead of providing directions, at least once you're past the "do this aloud and visualize the chamber as you speak" bits. What is the focus of the room? Is it currently being used? By whom? For what? What do the walls look like? Are there signs of life? How is the room lit? What do the features that you have thus far elaborated say about the state of this place? Is this place unpleasant? How? What does it smell/feel/sound like?

    \n What I meant was: don't try to consciously make your dungeon interesting. Just describe whatever would naturally be there. If you think whirring blades would naturally be there? Cool. But don't think: oh, I need to make this interesting, what can I do?

    By "things that make you feel unpleasant", I just meant that you shouldn't be afraid of creeping yourself out. In my dungeon, I described an unlit ceiling, way above, from which a clear slime dripped, coating the floors. That idea makes me grimace, but that's a good thing.
