Leisure, Hobby, Craft, or Art?

James_Nostack
December 2006 edited December 2006 in Story Games
In one of the Geek Sociology threads, someone (I forget who!) said something smart: "There's a big difference between approaching something as a leisure activity, and approaching it as a hobby."

Damn right. (Was that you, Mark W.? Because it definitely needed saying.)

Here's the deal: if gaming is hobby, then I think it's hard to "draw new people in." Hobbies attract people who are simply interested in the activity in its own right. I do martial arts as a hobby (or, did) because I like that stuff for its own sake. The chief appeal of the hobby, is doing the hobby. And I suspect that most hobbies are very goofy to non-hobbyists, perhaps inherently so. (Can you imagine a stamp-collecting message board, wondering how to attract fresh new blood to stamp-collecting?)

If gaming's a leisure activity, I think the mindset changes. The point of the leisure activity is to have fun; the "how" isn't usually very important. (On a typical night, I might wonder if I want to watch a movie, read a book, or watch TV; it's usually a toss-up.) If someone can be convinced that a role-playing game is a fun leisure activity, it's just one more thing to do, and it doesn't make a statement about you as a person.

For me personally, I tend to approach games as a leisure activity, and I'm leery of the hobbyist aspects (collecting many books, mastering complex rules, playing for years on end). Gaming stores, for example, creep me out a little bit because the hobbyist focus is so strong.

I don't know where I'm going with this, but I think that mindset makes a difference to the experience of play, to design philosophies, and to the social role of gaming in peoples' lives and in society at large.
