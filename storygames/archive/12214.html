    \n I don't consider myself a great GM, but I think these are the tools for improvement:
    -talk to your players, create an atmosphere of creativity and sharing, dissolve passive-aggressive behaviour and break down firewalls
    -know the rules, help others get on board, know what others like and dislike
    -facilitate and guide, don't rule and command
    -be enthusiastic, enjoy what you're running and know when to call it quits
    -read and play lots and lots of games
    -read books about play and GMing like Graham Walmsley's Play Unsafe and Robin Laws' Laws of Good Gamemastering
    -don't use the game as a vehicle for your expression but rather ensure it's like a music jam
    -make handouts, draw maps, bring stuff to the table, give players cool and tangible things
    -make your NPCs human, give them names and desires
    -describe a lot, don't use flowery prose but use all the five (and sometimes six) senses, paint the scene, make sure the group has a coherent SIS
    -ask leading questions to prompt roleplaying "How does that make you feel?", "What are you thinking right now?", "Will you let that stand?"...

    Of course different approaches work for different games.

    Anyway, if I had to summarize, it would be:

    Play games with other GMs. Watch the things they do that make you more interested in the game, your character, or just the act of gaming. Copy those things, and do them yourself.

    My GMing went from "pretty good" to "really awesome" in around 2002 or so? That was when I sat in two really awesome, fun sessions that Rafael Chandler ran. It was a game I never played before. I totally got into the game, the play, the excitement due to tricks and feedback from the GM. I immediately incorporated those tricks and tips into my GMing, and improved dramatically as a result.

    Also interesting: Mark Causey picked up tips and tricks from me (many of the same techniques I picked up from Raf and others), and went on to run awesome sessions. As he did so, he developed new techniques, either totally new or based on those other techniques. I had played in a few of Mark's sessions later, and learned new tips/tricks/skills from him, based on stuff he learned from me, and so on.

    It's kinda weird, but hey: I'm a visual and tactile-kinesthetic learner. I'm not an audial (book) learner. So for me, reading tips and tricks is only a little useful, seeing them with my eyes and incorporating them into my "routine" is huge in my world.

    \n I love the fact that there are a bunch of games out there that effectively show you how to be a good GM.

    Try playing games like Dogs in the Vineyard, Apocalypse World, Lady Blackbird, The Shadow of Yesterday, and Primetime Adventures, and apply the lessons they teach you to any game you run.

    For instance, Dogs teaches you how much fun it can be to do things like "reveal your [backstory] in play", the whole Say Yes or Roll the Dice thing (useful in many many contexts), preparing all your NPCs to want/need something from the PCs, make your NPCs escalate beyond what you'd consider "reasonable", etc.

    \n Read books on creative and dramatic writing. Setting description should engage the five senses. All NPCs have an agenda when interacting with the character, and conversations are about maintaining or increasing status or power.

    When the scene comes to a pivotal point, I imagine three possibilities: what the weirdest thing that could happen? The coolest thing that could happen? The worst thing that could happen?

    It's been mentioned already, but if you haven't read Play Unsafe by Graham Walmsley, you really should.

    Don't let it be something that feeds into, or harms, your ego. Whether or not you run a good game has no reflection on your value as a person. If you're worried about impressing people, or what they'll think of you, then you aren't concentrating on the task at hand. Steve Giordano really pushed this when I was studying jazz guitar with him. I teach it to my own students, and their playing improves immediately.

    \n I'm actually beginning to suspect -- which I probably should have realized before -- that there's no such thing as a GM who's consistently awesome, playing every game, with every group of people, under every circumstances. Just doesn't happen. Roleplaying is so contingent on a variety of factors, including the behavior and expectations of the other people at the table, that you can't make it awesome all by yourself, even if you are super talented and experienced.

    The real question, I think, is how to use the social authority legitimately or illegitimately entrusted to you, as GM, to make the entire table or the entire game more awesome than it otherwise would have been. And most of the advice given here gets at some of that.

    My current #1 suggestion would be: realize that different games ask GMs and players to do really different things, and many of them don't even clearly state what those different things are. You can't merely get by on your previous experience and talent at improvising, especially if you want the game to be really awesome instead of just enjoyable. What you need is fluency both with the game you are playing and in interactions with the other players at the table.

    Fluency with game mechanics and pacing and such comes from reading, playing, and talking about the game, watching other people run it, running it extensively yourself, hacking it and seeing how that changes the results (for better and worse), playing a bunch of games that inspired this game or later games that were inspired by it, and that kind of thing. Fluency with the players at the table comes from playing games with them a lot and talking about those games, but also from hanging out with them, living or sleeping with them (in some cases), going to the movies with them, arguing about game theory with them on the internet for 8 years, sharing the same background in games played, etc.

    If you don't have that fluency with either the game or the players, awesome sometimes still happens, despite that. Sometimes everything just comes together, either with mechanics that really click, or people who you really engage with or fiction that turns out feeling "just right." And sometimes playing with people or mechanics that are new and unfamiliar is necessary to push your group into playing more actively and attentively, instead of relying on their shared familiarity and having a fun but easy time of it. Still, in my experience, you can't really beat fluency with mechanics (in their broadest sense, "what the game desires and requires you to do") and your fellow players in creating the most solid foundation for awesome.

    If you can't or aren't willing to invest in that fluency and intimacy, then you have to try to get as close as possible to it in the brief amount of time you have before and during the setup and play of the game. You have to really try to connect with other players and build connections between them as quickly and as deeply as you can, without being a creepy fuck. You have to be able to convey to the players what the game is asking them to do, both on a macro and micro level, which means the text either has to help you do that ("Do XYZ under circumstances ABC") or you have to do all the work yourself, expressing to the other players your internal understanding of how the game works.

    You're not going to do this every time -- just last week I was having trouble connecting with the other players at the table, for weird personal reasons -- but I do think you can get better at it. However, it's something you have to be aware of and actually have the energy to engage directly, especially if it's being a problem or there are obstacles towards achieving fluency. It doesn't always take hard work, in the sense of it not being enjoyable, but it does take sincerity, I think.

    Posted By: Jason MorningstarCare about the people you play with.
    \nThis is such a fabulous answer. If you are tempted to say "Yeah of course, but give me something more specific and useful" then you need to re-read it over and over until it really sinks in.

    Being a good player (GM or otherwise) is about connecting with people, understanding what engages them in the fiction and working to make sure they get it. (Generally they will reciprocate.)

    It's also about understanding the systems, which only comes through experience. Games have emergent properties that you will not see or predict when you read the rules, you have to see the system in motion to figure out what it's patterns of behavior are. It's hard to move the game in a direction that will provide optimal fun if you don't understand how it works.

    So the best advice is to seek out every play opportunity you can and, when you are in the game, be as fully aware of others as you can be. Try to do things that engage them more in the game and openly respond to things that engage you more in the game. (Say "I like that, that's awesome!" when people add stuff to the game you like. Try to get this response from others too. Watch their non-verbal cues.)

    \nGeneral:
    Be enthusiastic about what you're doing. If you aren't excited, why should your players be? And it's not enough to be excited; you have to convey excitement. Get the players excited, too!

    Specific advice for "be enthusiastic":
    * Don't play a game you don't enjoy. Or a setting. Or a situation / set-up / whatever.
    * Find out what parts of the game make your players excited, and let their excitement feed yours.
    * If something is killing your enthusiasm, talk about it with the players during the next break. Nip it in the bud.
    * Make a list of 3-5 items that make you excited about this game. Refer back to it. Try to touch on each one during play.
    * Share that list with your players. Reward them for touching on those points. Enthusiasm feedback loop!

    Specific advice for "convey enthusiasm":
    * Things to avoid: mumbling, ending a lot of sentences with a falling pitch, and monotone speech. Vary the tone of your voice.
    * Don't hide behind a GM screen. Body language is important!
    * Stand up a lot! Move around! Standing and moving convey energy; energy conveys excitement.
    * Keep things moving from player to player. Don't let any player sit too long. This is standard pacing advice.

    Specific advice for "get the players excited, too":
    * Players love hand-outs, but they don't like to read. Pictures are awesome. Props are awesome.
    * Unless the players are clamoring for it, the written stuff should be saved for between games, and don't tie your own enthusiasm to it.
    * Create setting and situation as a group. Buy-in leads to enthusiasm which leads to excitement.
    * At the end of each game session, ask each player, "Tell me one thing you liked and one thing you didn't like." Make them answer, no cop-outs. Revise your GMing strategy as best you can to do more of the things they like and less of the things they don't.

      \n There are various general things that GMs can do that will always make them better at their job. Depending on the game and the social dynamics of your group, you will have to do these things in greater or lesser proportion, but they will always be necessary at least sometimes to some extent. There are also specific techniques that are widely successful in pursuing these broad goals of good GM-craft.

      Create enthusiasm:
      * I give immediate verbal reinforcement and high fives whenever I think something is awesome. Lame? Perhaps, except that after a couple of sessions all my players spontaneously started doing it (thereby transporting ourselves back to fifth grade), and suddenly we had an explicit system for encouraging the awesome (to the point that even the most ego-tastic player in my group started actually trying to work with instead of against the other players, just because he got tired of being left out of the high fives for being an asshat. Where talking to him [multiple times!] failed, high fives succeeded. Go figure.).
      * Prep the players before they narrate so that they don't miss any chance to be awesome. Personally, I try to make it clear that they should not hold back from being awesome: all players are excited when their character gets to do something awesome. "Nice! That was awesome! Now the villain's completely at your mercy-- what do you do to crown your moment of glory?" Enthusiastically invite players to realize their ends in a triumphant way.
      * Corollary to the previous: cede to the players opportunities for being awesome at every turn.

      Listen to your players not as an end in itself, but always at the same time as a means. (From anti-Kantian ludology. )
      * When my players speak, I always immediately reincorporate their contributions. If they mention setting details, I add to them or use them ("The thug grabs the fire extinguisher off of the wall-- the one that Eric mentioned a couple of minutes ago-- and swings it at your head!"). If they adopt a certain accent, I use a complementary one (hmm... he's a dwarf with a lowland Scottish accent, so these dwarves from near his homeland will have highland accents!). If they mention goals or motivations, I make reference back to them ("... so that by overpowering the guard quickly, we can stop him from raising the alarm." *Rolls.* "As you tackle the rent-a-cop to the ground, he opens his mouth to shout for help. A surge of adrenaline and alarm races through your body as you barely manage to snap his jaw shut, all the time aware of how close he was to undermining your mission and endangering your friends..." etc.).

      * Disclaimer: Examples are contrived; my games, thank god, are not nearly this banal.

      \n David Berg and Adam Dray, your advice is really practical and thoughtful! You win this thread.

      I've just recently (in the past few years) started GMing long term games. This is so much more complicated than one-offs! Here's some things I did:

      *Kept all my notes in one place in a giant word file on my laptop. Run the game directly from this laptop, with various things in the document highlighted, notated, and in different colors. Have a PDF of the game I'm running on the laptop too. This is AWESOME because of the SEARCH option to look back over my notes in 0.00001 seconds to find a name of a place or something that happened in a previous session.
      *In said notes, I write notes to myself all the time. Things like "player A put worms in the ship's ducts!" This way I remember all the details! (which I'm horrible with and would forget otherwise)
      *I also put things at the top of my game document. Things like "Include a new alien, new technology, and at least one fight in every episode" to keep my story consistent through every session with the tone I want to keep. I put themes in this too, like "betrayal, dark and moody, pulpy".
      *Also notes like "focus on intrigue! what do the players want? what do the NPC's want?" These help me focus on the mechanics of running the game.
      *The style of GMing I've found I prefer is to approach it as a player. I consider what I want as a player "colorful settings, unique and interactive NPCs, meaningful conflicts, tough decisions, fun fights" and I let this be the things that color how I run the game because I enjoy doing them. Then I take what my players like "autonomy from the big plot, ability to change things meaningfully, control over their characters and some of their character's NPCs, relationships and realistic interactions, being silly not serious, winning fights awesomely" and try to do those things too. That way, we all get what we want no matter what game we're playing. I kinda GM like I make art. I like to make things that people understand and enjoy.
      *Constantly try to improve! If something doesn't work one session, I note it down and change it for next session. Around session 5, we get into a groove. I'm of the opinion you can never stop improving in anything. This is fun!
      *Keep everyone's character sheets in a folder with all other maps/aspect/story sheets/badges of honor/specshul weapons. That shit gets lost.
      *Come prepared! However that is for you! I tend to just write out specific scenes I have in mind, a general plot idea, some pretty descriptions of scenery and settings and objects, and NPCs that I want to focus on. The rest I improvise. That's just how I like to do it. My husband writes monologues and paragraph text. That's just how he does it.
      *Two of the best GM advice things I've ever read (paraphrased): "Always say yes and" and "Don't keep the story from your players! Tell them everything and then let things happen." I'm still working on both of these, because a lot of the time I say no when I should say yes but lets negotiate that, and I also sometimes still think my story is the sacred thing whose secrets will be revealed when I want them too. I say if you have story, share it, then create more.


      Hope that was some concrete stuff!
