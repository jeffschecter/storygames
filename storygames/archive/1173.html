Shock: for Fantasy?

Andy
August 2006 edited August 2006 in Story Games
I'm wondering if, deep down, good fantasy doesn't rely on the same "Shocks/Issues" breakdown of SF? That is to say, I'm wondering if Shock can be used for Fantasy?

On the surface, it looks like it would work, when I think of books like the first of the Coldfire trilogy (Black Sun Rising), and other fantasy books here and there that don't involve twin rotating scimitars and the like. Magic is usually pervasive, weird, and has its own internal logic (even if humans don't completely understand it or have it mapped out).

Any thoughts? I'm thinking of taking my group on another fantasy romp where this time, we all put the setting togehter, and we had a lot of success with an SF game when using Shock: to build the world (admittedly Universalis could be used too, but that one is more of a case of bidding for control of the game elements and the like, whcih I don't want to do in this instance).

-Andy

    There's a discussion about Shock: with fantasy over at glyphpress' Forge forum, here: http://www.indie-rpgs.com/forum/index.php?topic=17481.0

    That said, I think that Shock: would be good for some fantasies, but not all, just like it's good for some science fiction, but not all. In a game that's less about exploring the characters and more about the world might be served well by the worldbuilding part of the game, but when you get to the character-driven part, it might fall apart.

    This goes hand in hand with my notion that not every game in a genre is perfect to model every kind of story that can be told in the genre.

    To get back to the topic, I'd gladly use Shock: in my the fantasy setting of the novel I'm finishing up. Issues: Individuality vs. Nationalism, Imperialism Shocks: Flying Islands and ships, Somatic Magic.

    And it'd probably be even more useful to model a fantasy world that's specifically constructed to interrogate specific issues using a sociological speculative fiction approach.

    The trouble here might come from the orientation of the genres. I think writers and readers come/go more frequently to Science Fiction asking the kinds of questions Shock: appears to be designed to ask and investigate. However, I think that to a certain extent, the framework of Shock: could be used for any setting where you could imagine novum colliding with a familiar world and shaking assumptions and prejudices.

    ...Whatever Shock: can do is, in fact, science-fiction.

    I endorse Paul's definition of science fiction and I think the Wikipedia entry about it should be changed to that single sentence.

    I've been kind of explicit about this elsewhere: what makes a setting work in Shock: is that you follow the rules on creating the setting. I don't care if people use swords or lightsabers, or if their magic is based on nanotech they don't understand or whatever. It doesn't matter. I don't think it even matters in terms of whether or not it's science fiction. It would still be admissible to Amazing, it would still show up in Sci Fi at Blockbuster.

    I'm quite content if you make a story about barbarians and chainmail bikinis that confronts Nationalism through the lens of War of the Frost Giants so long as you come to that setting by using the World Creation tool. Stick to the World Creation rules and it will work fine. Don't push a setting agenda until you've got your Grid worked out, be open to others' ideas, and make sure everyone's bought into your setting. Then see what you've got. Don't try to retrofit a setting to the game and you'll come out with something unique and accessible to your fellow players, whatever the color.
