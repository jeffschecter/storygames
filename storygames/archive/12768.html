\n As a general rule I prefer rpgs where there's a clear distinction between GMs and players, where GMs create a world and players roleplay characters within it, I tend to prefer an absence of metamechanics but I do like room for a healthy dollop of GM fiat. This is irrespective of whether I'm playing or GMing.

So far, so trad. But there's something else I like. The quotidian. And that's something trad doesn't do so well.

What do I mean by quotidian? I mean stuff without cool powers, I mean stuff that's about people.

The people don't need to be accountants and lawyers working their daily grind, I can't see any way to make that fun. But I find something distracting in superpowers, magic, aliens and star-spanning empires because none of that is what really interests me.

Zombies don't for me make everything cooler.

What does interest me is people. Crime dramas, political dramas, romantic dramas even. Clients of a Roman senator doing his dirty work. 14th Century mediterranean traders trying to make a living. 1940s private eyes digging into Hollywood's seamier secrets.

Hm, I really didn't mean quotidian. Oh well. I still like the word.

Trad games struggle with these kinds of games because trad mechanics are (horrible generalisation coming up) about doing stuff. Physical stuff. But conflicts between real people mostly aren't about that, they're mostly about motives and talking people into doing what you want.

What's interesting in a game about real people isn't how strong they are, or how fast they can run. It certainly isn't how much damage they can take from a fall and still walk away. What's interesting is what they want, why they want it and what they're willing to do to get it.

And that's storygames territory.

So, if anyone's still with me, two questions.

1. Does anyone else know what I mean by quotidian gaming here? Does anyone else feel the lure of no cool powers, no fantastic setting, just people?

2. What elements in a game for you help deliver that kind of story?

    \n I agree with 2, but some games do make it easier.

    For example, I've run and played Dying Earth and while I thought it had some bugs (combat was a total drag to be precise) the emphasis on persuasion and succumbing to vices really drove a different sort of play. Solutions were much less about violence (except the system slightly undermined that) and much more about talking people round.

    Also, Heroquest I found people using relationships a lot more. I didn't like the game's bidding mechanic, but I did play a con game once where my granny used her nagging mastery to defeat two spearmen from a rival tribe by shaming them out of attacking. That's cool. It's also the sort of thing actually happens on occasion in real life and drama both, with the risk being if you don't shame them you really are toast.

    UA is a good suggestion though. It's Ellroy the rpg in part, and the emphasis on obsessions and so on really helps underline the importance of psychological elements. And yes, the madness meters do I imagine help. I sold my copy because most of it was about avatars and magic and stuff that didn't interest me but the core mechanics are very nicely done.

    I've heard good things about Dirty Secrets too. I also own a copy of A Dirty World but haven't got to grips with it yet.

    \n I think there's a strong trend toward the quotidian in a lot of small press games, or at least the capacity to focus on the prosaic and mundane amid extraordinary circumstances.     Primetime Adventures is magnificent for dealing with regular people in ordinary but compelling situations.

    \n ndp,

    One issue I have sometimes is I get tired of the violence in most games.

    I don't mind violence in games. Violence creates drama. But the level of it is wearying. Billy the Kid killed I think six men and is still famous. Jack the Ripper was in single figures I think. The average rpg character kills hundreds and hundreds of people. I struggle to imagine what that would do to a person. Plus, frankly, in most games nobody wants to as it would be out of genre and social contract.

    One issue I have with shared authorship games is what I tend to call gonzo creep. Someone introduces a gonzo element, someone else does, things get gonzo. Most games have veto mechanics, but gonzo creep tends to be gradual and anyway vetoing something can harsh the buzz. That said, once a psychic crops up in your PI game it's now set in a world with psychic powers and that one cool element just changed the whole setting.

    One thing therefore I look for in shared authorship games is elements that will help create a shared vision. Spione, although I didn't take to its core mechanic, was good at this. Introducing a zombie or whatever would just be lame, plainly out of genre.

    I'm familiar with Carry, it's one I'd happily play but the Vietnam war isn't one that appeals to me (it's not really my history). Good call though, it's another way of addressing the human which is what this thread's really about.

    Posted By: TomasHVMPosted By: Bret GillanPrimetime Adventures
    Aboutnormal people? Must be the "normal people" in a TV-show ...

    Not really. The TV show in PTA is the metaphor, the way you look at the world described in play: with a viewer point of view and a soundtrack. They you use it to play everything you want, from a D&D-like dungeon crawl to a human drama.

    Did you ever play it?

    --------------
    About the OP request, I find that a lot games not usually associated with the "real" can work well, if the group can keep the Gonzo effect in check. It's something that people lean, the first times a player play a game where he/she doesn't have to ask for anything from an all-powerful GM, they almost always go gonzo, like a child kept at school for too much when he/she can finally go out and play: he/she run screaming, and do things without any sense just because he/she finally CAN. After a while they calm down and play improve.

    Annalise can be played with a totally mundane "vampire" (a mobster, a corrupt politician, etc... A group of people I know played it with Nazism as the Vampire). Thou are but a Warrior turn Polaris into a game set in Spain during the Reconquest, that can be played without any fantastic element. PTA work much better with everyday drama than with superpowers, etc.

    If you like playing normal people in historical situations, you should really check "Montsegur 1244", too.
