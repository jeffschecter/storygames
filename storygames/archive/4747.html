If I had a lot of time to game, I'd definitely start up a long term HeroQuest or Spirit of the Century game. But I don't, so I keep my games small and focused so we can try something else in the pile of books that I have yet to play (or want to play more of).

    We tend to play about six sessions of a given game before picking up something else to do new stuff. Right now, we're playing PTA in a series called One For The Road, about a highwayman in 18th century northern England. We'll play out the season and resolve those characters and issues over the next four sessions. Keeping going would be pointless; the story will be over. The characters would just be standing around saying, "So, waddaya wanna do?" "I dunno. Wadda you wanna do?"

    \n

    And that, right there, is I think the key to this discussion: many of the indie crowds' games are based on the structure of fiction. Fiction has an ending. In fact, it's the most critical part! So you can't play it forever. Playing in the same setting is a different matter. But that's not really a "campaign" if the characters change out and the players are addressing new issues.

    \n

    Go games that don't have that structure are the way to go for campaign play. The Shadow of Yesterday is, I think, a particularly brilliant system for that (though I've never played it long-term myself \u2014\xa0campaign play isn't really my thing).

    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanAnd that, right there, is I think the key to this discussion: many of the indie crowds' games are based on the structure of fiction. Fiction has an ending. In fact, it's the most critical part! So you can't play it forever.
    \nSay, I've got a novel for you, it's call The Wheel of Time.

    But seriously, I don't think the end of the fiction requires to have the character's standing around going "What do you want to do?" For some, yes, but not all. Just take the notion of playing in a setting again and apply it to characters too. A lot of times setting has elements left open to be explored. So do characters. Sometimes characters end up with a complete resolution that forcing a continuation rings false. Sometimes setting are so fleshed out there's no room to explore without breaking it (like Middle Earth, I'd say).

    And then there's a difference between open ended and a 3, or 5, or 7, or 12 novel series. You can still aim for closed fiction form, and play a game that lasts a very long time (how many sessions in a novel is also something to consider.)

    I think Ben has a good point, though. RPGs have been generally set up to be open ended serials (or perhaps a long series of novels). So it's nice to have ones that don't try to do that.

    \n We played more than 30 sessions of my Red Cow game for HeroQuest.

    We have tended to move away from games that last that long though, but its nothing to do with indie games per se, but changes in our perception. When I ran Red Cow we deferred resolution a lot at the beginning, in order to stretch out what we could from a storyline i.e. if a player had a story about loving a girl from the wrong clan, we created a lot of situations around that story without ever moving it to a conclusion. Later on we began to resolve conflicts more quickly as they occured.

    We found that we preserved resolution over deferral. As a result we are moving to play shorter games. Instead of 30 session campaigns we are much more likely to play 3 games of 10 sessions using the same background over several years.
