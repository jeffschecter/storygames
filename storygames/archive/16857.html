\n Okay, I'm looking for feedback. You've got a cleric, he is about to cast Bless on a fighter. The situation dictates that success isn't automatic, so the cleric has to roll. Should this be an opposed roll [even though the fighter is willing] or a difficulty number? If I go opposed roll, what does the fighter use to determine dice?

In the system there is a rule [the fighter just wants to use the least amount of dice possible]:

'Want to try something but don\u2019t have the trade? No problem, pick up two dice and add them to the pool. Unfortunately, one of these will be a penalty die so the highest number rolled will have to be dropped.'

This means the fighter would roll 1D + 1P, and drop the highest die. The low score is what the cleric needs to meet or beat for a successful roll.

or the GM could assign a TN for the Bless spell. If the cleric rolls equal to, or higher than, the TN then the spell is successful.

Which method do you prefer, or is there better way to handle it?

    \n That depends on the basic motivation.
    If you want the Cleric to succeed, then don't roll.
    If you want a simple test of the Cleric's ability, TN.
    If you want to use the dice in an oracular fashion, and then interpret the results backwards for story purposes, opposed Roll.

    Sanguine systems do this rather well. In fact, you often roll multiple dice for opposition. If the die used for Cover is the highest result, then the bullet hits the wall. If the die for Concealment is the highest result, then the shooter didn't even understand where the target was. If the die for Dodge was highest rolled, then some super cool acrobatics, or some innate sense of incoming fire kept the defender safe. etc.

    Opposed rolls are more work for everyone, but they can give the narrative some fuel and flavor. Maybe the fighter is distracted by the goblin's sword, maybe he accidentally brought his right palm higher than his left during the spell being cast, maybe his eyes looked South, maybe he recently disrespected a minor god who is paying him back. If you want to work in flavor (on the fighter's side), throw some opposed rolls in it.

    Then the dice show you the interplay between the Cleric's skill and the Fighter's fortunes.

    \n So, I played the heck out of Cyberpunk 1st Ed (sometimes called CP2013) and CP2020.
    Everything was the same, except CP2013 was always Roll vs. Roll and CP2020 was always Roll vs TN.
    The game play did produce a difeerent feel.
    For instance, the CP2013 with the ability to crank up your roll produced more anime-like characters. Without even trying, just by getting Reflex boosters, etc. I could make a character that could dance their way through a firefight untouched. It was cool and over-the-top, but was not gritty at all.
    When CP2020 came out, the TN was a set number and a skill marksman could hit anyone, but you couldn't make a character that could reliably survive a fire fight without investing heavily in personal armor. These games were grittier, and in combat-heavy games, everyone ended up investing heavily in skinweave, subdermal armor and Kevlar trench coats.
    Hope that helps,

    Bad example on my part. I'm thinking instead something like:

    Cleric attempts Bless. The spell works automatically, unless the cleric has Doubt. Doubt is a negative condition which results in a Penalty Die [1P]. A penalty die is rolled along with the rest of the dice, but the highest die is removed.

    Lets say the cleric rolls 3D + 1P and it comes up 6,4,3,2. The penalty die of 6 is the score to beat. In this case the cleric fails with a 4.

    This reflects the nature of faith more accurately, and in a narratively pleasing manner as well, showing the cleric overcoming his own doubt to invoke his faith.
