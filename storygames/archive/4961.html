\n Hi! Stop me if you've heard this one before.

So, we play story games, right? And the idea with story games is to create stories, right? So, if everyone's supposed to collaborate to make a good story, why do we need "conflict resolution"? Where do these conflicts come from, in the first place? Are they conflicts between characters in the narrative? Well, in that case, why don't we just agree on whichever outcome is more interesting for the story? Or might they be conflicts between the participants? If so: where do these conflicts stem from? Weren't we supposed to be collaborating, right now?

Couldn't we just get rid of resolution rules altogether and, y'know, agree upon things?

/Kalle, who's got his own answers but would like to hear the ideas of others.

    \n You don't need to have conflict resolution; what you're doing without it, in the way you describe, is basically collaborative storytelling (or writing, depending on your medium). It can work well. Stephen King has done it with plenty of people, and many other writers, too.

    Personally, I like to have a conflict resolution system to get me out of my comfort zone. Allow for some unexpected outcomes. Feel like there's suspense there. Fire up my energy levels and focus my creativity.

    Like, in our Contenders game, when two PCs were fighting each other. I had much fun doing that not knowing who'd win. It made for story material either way, but it was nice to have some system there to play through, rather than us agreeing on the outcome.

    But I also like collaborative writing. So it's a mood thing, much as anything.

    \nThese are not mutually exclusive. We collaborated and arrived together to use resolution at that moment, not found conflict and agreed to use resolution to handle it. Does that make sense?

    \n I think you absolutely can play with no rules conflict resolution system.

    However, sometimes people disagree. Sometimes, in playing a character, you have desires that are contrary to another character's wishes, or, as a player you're resistant to seeing your character go down a certain path. That's where a conflict resolution system is helpful. Bringing fruitful outcomes at decision points.

    Further, if someone burns resources to ensure an outcome, it says, "THIS IS IMPORTANT" in a very real way to the other players. It can heighten drama, as well as say something about the resolution.

    For example, in Sign In Stranger, if you lose during a conflict (which can only happen after 5 investigation scenes), you can make another card pull by risking something. So, while you get another shot at success, you may end up destroying a valuable item, or being wounded, in the process. This makes the struggle really lively, and has a huge influence on the resolution narration.

    I'm also going to second Christian's point on 'getting out of the comfort zone'. It's helpful to have an impartial, an unquestionable, outcome.

    \n Here's my personal answer.

    When I use the phrase "conflict resolution" I strictly mean conflicts between the fictional characters of the narrative.

    I dislike consensus building for conflict resolution because it inhibits committed passionate play. I don't mean immersion. When I'm GMing I play to have my NPCs villains and otherwise succeed. When I'm playing a PC I play to have my guy succeed.

    This to me is not the same as playing to "win." Winning and Losing is a social construct among players. I'm not trying to show off my skills. I'm not trying to be the better player. I'm playing my characters (NPC and otherwise) with committed emotional passion.

    I'm concerned about consensus techniques leading to prioritization of STORY STRUCTURE, which is cool if that's what you like to do. I don't. I don't want to be thinking, "Well this is an appropriate time for the villain to lose" or "this scene should really be about the down turn of Joe's relationship with Mary." I want the SYSTEM to reveal those things to me through play so I can stay focused on playing my character's passions and for those plot twist and turns to shock me when I botch a roll. And believe me despite my commitment to character success I relish unexpected failure. Because it's just that: unexpected.

    This is why I love Sorcerer so much. It doesn't matter how many bonus dice you stack on, the odds are never guaranteed, and the odds are rarely good.

    \n My answer would be that a story is helped if one person can push the agenda forward with all their heart and soul, and another person can push back with all their heart and soul. If there is noone to push back, the story risks going off into ridiculous overthetop direction, unless the agenda-pusher throttles back. If she throttles back, then she has to do two things at once, push forward and provide her own adversity. This is hard at best, results in muddled, wimpy stories at worst. If there is someone who can push back, but they have to operate solely on consensus all the time, then either there will be standoffs if people really push heart and soul, or again both people will have to throttle back in order to negotiate compromises. Conflict rules other than pure consensus allow both parties to push forward or push back with no wiffle-waffling.

    \n Another wonderful thing conflict resolution does is allow players to safely put their trust into the game, so that they can assure themselves that what they themselves think of as the best thing to "happen next" is not, in fact, the best thing.

    Basically, I'm saying that conflict resolution helps players surprise and delight one another with ideas they didn't think they wanted, and then found out it was better than anything they imagined at first themselves.

    For me, it's one of the best damn reasons to play story games. Sometimes, other people just knock me out with their ideas! More of that, please.

    EDIT: And, to clarify -- yes, you could do something close to this with "just collaboration," but I find that when you, as an individual, get vested in something, and then lose that thing, very often you're surpised with how much you like it when you "lose."

    EDIT, THE REVENGE: And, every sort of "just collaboration" I could possibly imagine has implicit conflict resolution. If it does not, play does not proceed. Either you, as a group, agree to what is going on, or you don't. If you don't, play ceases. That sucks! Now, very often this "resolution" is just people talking, grabbing onto each other's ideas. But, it's still assent. Which is to say, it's still resolution of fictional events.

    \n Lots of good points. I would also like to add (Part of what I put up on this post, but I thought it might add to the discussion here): Resolution rules also separate out play from actual human relationships, in essence creating a buffer of sorts. Without resolution the entire process of collaboration depends on human interaction between the participants, which is pretty dangerous territory. In my experience people ended up pulling their punches or stepping on toes, so I think that's another reason resolution is necessary even when people collaborate. It facilitates collaboration and creates a bit of distance between people (the players) and the issues (story direction).

    (And yeah, that last bit is from Getting to Yes. Dispute resolution theory is absolutely fascinating in the context of roleplaying.)

    \n ...mechanical resolution and similar rules can also force people to do stuff they really don't want to do, but is actually good for the story.

    You could all be rooting for the hero to win, but if the system tells that no, there's yet another setback, there's a whole lot of tension and uncertainty created... even if everyone at the table wasn't feeling strong enough or imaginative enough to push that hard themselves. Not only can that create story above and beyond the ability or will of the story-creators, but it can also free the story-creators to care deeply, root for, and fight for their favorite side. That's [i]liberating[/i]. You don't have to shoulder all the responsibility any more: you can just have fun, care deeply, fight all out.

    Finally, an interesting set of rules can create outcomes that no one at the table could actually have come up with.

    \n Another thought - My post above was in reaction to the term "conflict resolution" meaning something very broad. Sometimes the term "conflict resolution" refers to a particular procedure, where one step of the procedure is "negotiate stakes" and sometimes this step in the procedure is handled by consensus.

    This particular approach muddles the difference between using formalized procedures/rules to resolve disputes and "total consensus." It just so happens I dislike this hybrid-approach (or at least, I disliked it when using it in PTA). I dislike it so much I am sympathetic to the notion that pure consensus might be better than hybrid mechanics.
