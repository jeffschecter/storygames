\n In the "The Forgotten Indies" thread, James wrote:\n
Posted By: James_NostackThis is one of the things that blows my mind about this community! Here are all these great, wonderful games--which, due to resource limitations, will probably never reach a huge audience beyond the existing internet communities--and yet [i]people are designing new games non-stop[/i]. So that a whole bunch of great games basically get forced under the waterline until they drown, because all these new games are clambering over them to get to the surface.

Which, like, is great and all... but as someone who doesn't design, I feel bad for those old games. It's like everyone is so obsessed with innovation that no one wants to appreciate the fine stuff we've already got. Like, I'm still learning to play [i]Sorcerer[/i], [i]Dogs[/i], and [i]The Pool[/i]. I'm [i]interested[/i] in all the wacky stuff that's come out since 2003, but I'm still assimilating and appreciating the stuff from five years ago--and these are just the games that got a lot of attention, to say nothing of the under appreciated games from that era.
\nI agree with your sentiments. In fact, there was a big bull session last night at Orccon. (It started at midnight and went till four in the morning! I have to add Paul Tevis did a great job of moderating.)

Much of the discussion that took place was about the issue of games competing for attention in a market with so many games.

I'm not a designer, however, so my take was different than many of the people present. As Paul put it, "CK is here speaking as a GM." I'd add, especially in the context of this thread, I'm a consumer of games, not a maker.

At the con this weekend, I ran Primetime Adventures, HeroQuest (set in Glorantha), and Pendragon. I played Don't Rest Your Head, a Jeep form game ("Upgrade"), Unknown Armies, and In a Wicked Age.

I hadn't played Pendragon in years, had never played HeroQuest before, and had run and played Primetime Adventures several time over the last two years.

It occurred to me during the conversation last night that while the designers were working hard to figure out how to get people to buy play their games, I was a content man. See, I have Primetime Adventures, Sorcerer (and supplements), HeroQuest and Pendragon. With those four rules sets I have four very different games that deliver everything I want from games. (I've loved Pendragon for years, think Sorcerer just rocks, fell for Primetime Adventure while running it at cons, and yesterday HeroQuest did everything I was told it would do and it's now on The List.)

I'm not saying there aren't other games that delivery what I want. I'm saying I've got four right now. Why work hard to find them when I've got four rock solid games that deliver the goods I want and deliver them very well?

Note also I'm not saying I won't play other games. I had never played Don't Rest Your Head, Unknown Armies, Jeep form, nor In a Wicked Age until this past weekend. I'm saying going out to master new games and information when I already have four games I've invested doesn't make much sense. This is all trumped by the fact, of course, that not many games apparently have grabbed me and said, "Yes, i want to find people to play that game with!" I know what I want to play. I got them. I want to gather them to play them.

All of that is in turn trumped by the fact that reading games and learning how to play them is a pain in the ass. I check out new games. The mechanics and intent of the mechanics are often obscure; the game mechanics after have terrible flaws in them; I cannot escape the feeling that most games require the person who wrote the book to sit down with me for a couple of sessions as the GM so I can see how the thing is supposed to work. I would put, by the way, Sorcerer, HeroQuest, and Primetime Adventures in this camp. However, I've done my work on these games. The thought of having to go through it again for another game just doesn't seem worth it -- especially since I have four games I could run till the day I die.

Does this mean I'll never play a new game? Or that I'll never buy a new one? No. But like James, I'm content with the older games right now.

    I find it interesting that the games I love are old to older: Pendragon is 25 years old; Sorcerer 7 years old. Only Primetime Adventures and HeroQuest are relatively new. And even then, once you figure in that HeroQuest was 25 years in the making is actually the full expression mechanically of Stafford's Glorantha setting, my tastes are pretty fucking old -- and that surprises me.

    I guess this is my way of saying I don't feel bad for the older games -- simply because I want to play them. I'll bring them to Cons (they always fill up) and I'll gather people to play them. I mean, I think frankly we need more playing.

    I'm saying, the indie field might ask, "What do people need?" I don't think we need more games. There's nothing wrong with more gams. I just don't think we need them right now.

    What I do think we need is to learn to play better.

    As Joshua Newman wrote:\n
    Posted By: Joshua A.C. NewmanRyan just reminded me: I started thinking about this a couple of months ago. I was starting to see abstract "sales" and "con slots filled", and I was grouchy about shipping books. Then I remembered that I never got toplayShock: because it was always overbooked. I wasn't saying no to people showing up to play, and it was making me really crabby.

    So I decided, OK, I'm gonna only run games with three other people. That means that I get full participation (I usually play Audience, which means I get stuff to do, but I don't get a Protagonist) and I get to have a lot more fun with the world and I get to make moral statements in the way the game's designed to support.

    All of a sudden, more fun! And more fun has led to more sales! I can honestly and enthusiastically recommend the game, and I know this is weird to say, but Iunderstandthe game better. Like, I had all these parts that theoretically did stuff, but I was so wrapped up in the cogs that I couldn't play with the whole machine.

    So, if publishing or selling is getting in the way of your play, drop them down a notch and play your damn game. Then do the next part that will add to your satisfaction.
    \n
    It'd be sweet if we could sidestep the "play better" issue by saying, "The new games help people play better." But that's not been my experience. Because the new games (often met with great praise by people who -- as far as can tell -- have never played them) often have huge gaps in them and sometimes leave the players stranded in matters as blunt as conflict resolution.

    So, that's pretty much what I ended up saying last night and wanted to say here: I think we need more work on playing well -- and the newness of a game, as far as I can tell, has little influence on us playing better.

    Playing better means paying attention to techniques that we've learned from many games, learning how to communicate effective techniques and habits of play to new players, being aware of our habits and techniques. Issues of pacing. Getting description and color comfortably out of our mouths to evoke more detail and pleasure from words and images. Picking away at what good scene framing is, if appropriate. What tools help Players get on a narrative track from the get-go, and so on....

    Craft, really, I guess is what I'm talking about. The craft of play, not design.

    I'm not saying don't design games, or don't publishing games. That's between you and God. I'm talking what is going to make the experience of play better at the table. ("Better" being the bugaboo of "Quality." Sorry, but there it is!)

    I thought I'd note directly to a bunch of designers that the answer to that question for me is to play and to player deeper with games I'm already satisfied with rather than get on another wheel to figure out how to play (and probably have to fix) another game.

That's a good mindset to adopt at times, I think. You don't have to be all "Why God Why don't people play my game!     Posted By: VBWyrdeHow do I perfect the game I already love?
    \nActually, I came back (instead of going to bed) to discuss this very thing, and there you went ahead and posted it!

    Here's what I love about discussing new ways to play and run games: Sometimes you end up looking back on the games you have and see them in a new light.
    Now, a lot of the time these developments in new ways to play and run games are attached to a specific new hippie super-focused game. But so what? I can play that game to suck up the idea, else I can read about the game and extract the idea that way.

    Here's a real example of how my gaming go turned on its ear. I was talking to... I forget, I think it was Ron Edwards (talking about his spy game Spione). The idea of "situation" had been out there a little bit, in a game here or there. But it was specifically hearing him talking about the fact that in most role-playing games, they tell you to make up characters first. Then it's the GM's job to put all those disparate characters together and make a story out of it. It TOTALLY WORKS in squad-based games like D&D or Dread: Pandemonium easily. In games where the job isn't to get the PCs to get together and kick ass while chewing bubblegum... well, things become more complicated.
    Anyway, his point was "when an author writes a story, she doesn't make up characters first (although she might for that old in-house TSR schlock fantasy crap). Instead, she comes up with a situation. From there, she starts creating characters that, y'know, fit in that situation. Protagonists, Antagonists, people with freedom, people trapped by the situation, etc". The idea is, why don't we try that with our tabletop roleplaying games? Y'know, just to see what happens. To see if that makes them more fun or interesting to play (it will depend on the person, of course).

    I ran a game of L5R a few years ago, but it flopped because I couldn't get the Crab and Scorpion and Crane and Dragon to do anything together. And when they joined forces to slay an evil wizard, it felt really contrite, like they turned off their drama and backstories in order to go all D&D on some wizard's ass. Same thing happened with a game of Aberrant: We had three characters that were so different from each other, I couldn't really do anything with them together that seemed fun or interesting. I thought at the time that the game flopped because one player totally min-maxed his character to be a psychic combat badass, but in truth I didn't give them much direction, or a campaign setup, with which to build their characters.

    Cut to Last Year. The most successful game that we played, hands down, in our bi-weekly group was a game of With Great Power set in the world of the Night Watch. The players were all awesome and into their characters. And in fact, you build your characters first in that game. However, the whole scenario and enemy generation component of With Great Power basically sticks a needle into the spine of each character, pulls it out, dumps it into a petri dish, and from there creates a big fat capital-S Situaion. Things were afoot that were intimately related to the characters. The characters were all pawns in an inter-woven conspiracy that the players were helping define through their actions as they went along. It was great.

    Likewise, I went back to L5R again. At one point I owned the core book for 1st edition and just about every splatbook for it (which I skimmed, ran, flopped, skimmed again for ideas, then sold). Now I simply own the core book. I came up with a Situation for the game first, an idea of the Big Problem that will be afoot, before even talking to the other players (in some hippie games the players all define the situation together): The Crab Wall falls, and the Shadowlands creep into the world of Rokugan. That's a big problem to deal with, and I have no clear idea of how the players will "win it" (or even if they can). After telling the players about the situation, they created characters that can easily fit in this situation, each with their own motivations and paths that will intersect or split due to how this whole Wall Falls situation progresses. In two days will be our first play session (last session we decided to play L5R, I rolled out my situation, we made characters, and tested the combat engine briefly). I am excited as shit not only because I get to play what looks to be a wicked-sick campaign of a game I've had on my gaming shelf for effectively 9 years, but because the situation really hits my buttons, and L5R is a game that really can deliver (I think, anyway) when used in this way.

    Situaion before Characters. A simple fucking little idea, but it hammered hard into my skull, and now I can pretty much go to any game book on my shelf that I played or haven't played, from Blue Planet (aka "Awesome game, but What are We Supposed to Do?") to Unknown Armies to Conspiracy of Shadows to Dungeons and Dragons to Shadowrun. New and interesting ways of looking at play is helping me get back into the games I've already had, without waiting for the New Hotness to come out.*

    -Andy

    But as I mentioned elsewhere, I'm addicted to the New Hotness. Probably now more than ever. Because I can drink those new gaming ideas up, and perhaps use those new idea-gems to make my Blue Planet or Fading Suns game even better!

    \n I'm going to jump in here because I think I have a highly specific take on this subject. I'm thoroughly a Story Now player as The Big Model defines it. I pretty much need a game to be about addressing the problematic nature of humanity for me to really enjoy it. Here's the thing, I've seen two strong approaches to this.

    The first one I call the Fill In The Blank method. Primetime Adventures and The Pool are in this category. Basically at some point during setup you write down "what the game is about." In Primetime Adventure's that's the character's Issue. In The Pool that's whatever you pull out as traits. Then the procedures of play allow you to hammer whatever it is you've written on the blank line.

    The other major approach is the highly specialized approach in which the game picks a particular topic and then the entire design resolves around addressing that topic. Dogs in the Vineyard, My Life with Master, It Was A Mutual Decision, and Spione are examples of this. I personally much prefer this approach because I'm fundamentally selfish. In PtA everyone might have a different Issue representing a particular slice of a larger whole. I'm much more satisfied when everyone at the table is addressing the *same* issue and thus through play we ultimately learn something about where we all stand on that issue.

    This leads in me a desire for more and more and more games because every time I notice a particularly interesting facet of the human experience, I want a whole game that just address *that* one thing and addresses it well. I want the mechanics themselves to raise key questions. I want my decision to cross off that trait, roll the d8 instead of the d10, and choose the red die over the blue die to carry just as much thematic and emotional punch as the narration that's attached to the application of those mechanics.

      \n I was also in the late-night conclave, and while I tend to lurk around here I feel like there's another perspective that is a blend of the two extremes, a perspective that happens to be mine.
      \n
      \nI have a list of games that make me happy, that satisfy my itch. As someone who is driven to make stories, however -- so much so that I chose it as my profession -- a *separate* need I have is finding new ways to tell those stories, which is why I am interested in new games. So, Ryan, I say to you that the effort is very much worth it, for consumers like me if not for CK.
      \n
      \nBut here's the key word, one that I think you and I both agreed on Sunday night: EFFORT. I work more than one job, I have a long commute; my time is very precious to me. And in the glut of indie game publication the last couple of years, there has been a decline in the quality of both texts and mechanics. NOT together, mind you: some texts have great mechanics that are not explained well, and some games have great explanation with frustrating mechanics. And given how precious my time is to me, there is nothing that can kill my interest in new games more than a game that is hard, confusing, or unsatisfactory to play. And, for the record, I see those things as distinctly different from games I simply don't *like* to play.
      \n
      \nSo back to the effort. Designing and writing a game are two very different skill sets (if not more, as suggested by Paul at the con), and if you do not excel at both, you *must* be willing to put in the effort to make up for the area in which you lack. There is a distinct air of blind support in this community, with little push for or acceptance of constructive criticism (something which is understandable, given that one of the origins of the indie community was as a refuge), but as the creation of independent games is clearly moving from a Golden to a Silver Age, keeping the interest of someone like me in new product is going to require dedication to the craft.
      \n
      \nI don't design. I play, and I playtest, and I edit. And I've seen, more than once, how a game that someone thought was nearly perfect was blown up by just one more playtest. It's a fine line between "It's almost there and I just need to edit the rules myself" and "It's almost there and I need more people to play it." One is easy, the other requires effort. Then on top of that, the writing itself. It's an equally fine line to give the latest draft to someone who's already played it to see if they understand, and someone who's never seen a single word. Again, time and effort.
      \n
      \nBut believe me, it's worth it.

      Posted By: Mike HolmesAs a business analyst, we talk about data and "intelligence." That is raw information in the first case, and data presented in such a way as to be actionable in the second. What you seem to have right now is a single data point. By itself... not much use. Now we can even extrapolate that you are not unusual, and that others, too, are satisfied for the most part with what they have in terms of RPGs.       \nIt was pointed out that the question asked was about what designers can do with it. The answer (as was also pointed out) might be nothing. Yes, System Matters, yes, design is cool. It's one of the triad of theory, design, play, and they're all good and valuable and important.       None of that blunts the point: there are things that can be done to make a specific game work better. Compare a good D&D game from back in the day with a bad one. See? And when we, as players, go flitting from game to game, it's very possible never to find the balance necessary to make any particular game work well. Designers can't change that really -- making less games wouldn't even do it. If something changes, it's got to be on the play end, and Chris is telling you what change he's realized he can make. It's also possible that without playing a variety of games, we'd never find the way to make any of them work. So, again, it's a balance. All things in moderation.\n
