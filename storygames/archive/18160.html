    \n G+ lies somewhere between forums and Twitter. Great for catching snippets of thought and discussing them, it gets unwieldy after too long. The big plus (pun unintended) is that G+ is immediate. It makes me feel far more connected to folks, especially when prominent RPG designers like Vincent Baker, Fred Hicks, or Brad Murray are actively dialoguing with people.

    Kotodama Heavy Industries is one where a lot of Tenra Bansho Zero people are hanging out in, so you can start by circling some of them. When you do, make sure that you have some info in your profile so that they know (or can guess) why you've circled them. Something like "Interest: RPGs".

    There's also an official Burning Wheel community, too, along with an Apocalpyse World community, a Fate Core community, a Tabletop community...well, you get the idea.

    Like I mentioned, the best way to use Communities is to mine them for posters whom you find interesting. Then you just start slowly accumulating posters, circling people, getting circled (and circling back if you can figure out what their schtick is), and shortly...you've got a bunch of interesting posts.

      \n Okay, so here was my last-iest thoughts on this topic, the topic of the potential of moving S-G to G+ at some point in the future:

      * I was contacted by someone who works in the entertainment industry. It's a cutthroat world where googling people's names to see what they do and what their footprint is is super common. They mentioned that for them, G+ is problematic because in G+ you can't use pseudonyms, and that means that everyone in those industries can't post. I also imagine the same would apply to people in jobs or communities (or genders) where stalking is a concern.
      -- However, it appears that this one might be debunked. Apparently while in the beginning G+ did not allow pseudonyms, they do now. Here are tutorials:
      http://www.quikstarts.com/internet/google-plus/how-to-change-your-display-name-in-google-plus/


      * Someone at PAX East (Joe, maybe? I was running on little sleep, I remember the discussion but not people) was talking to me about how they like the fact that S-G topics can go on for days, weeks even. In his words, "Because once it hits 11:59, the thread is DEAD." He clarified by saying that once the day ends, any discussion in a thread is pretty much over on G+ in personal feeds and communities: New topics or threads emerge, and it's always the Newest Topic (not the Most Active) that hits the top of the feed. By the time the next day dawns, people log in and look at topics... New topics, to post in. Ones that scroll down more than 2-3 topics' worth are basically dead. No one new looks into them. Only the people who originally posted to them will respond (because they have email notification of replies). Or "/sub"ed people. But no one new. Topics dry up extremely quickly.
      Which made me consider my own posting habits, both here and on G+: G+ is great for instant communication, feedback, gratification, but for many people it's failing them for the long term for lengthy discussions with the potential for lots of eyes. It's awesome for asking simple questions or posting transitory information/feedback, but it doesn't feel very lasting.
      -- It might be that "Hey, that's just the way things are now" and I need to get over it. Could be!
      -- But on that note, after Pax East, when that discussion was on my mind, I poked around a few communities I was in. I looked into the Dungeon World Tavern, which is indeed FUCKING AWESOME (a wonderful, enthusiastic crowd) because I wanted to see what people were doing with it, just wanted a general overview of what was going on in DW-land, because over the course of that weekend I fell in love with the game all over again. I loaded up the community, started skimming threads. Scrolled all the way to the bottom of the page (which can be around 20-30 topics or so depending on size), each page scrolldown being about 1-2 times the entire length of the front of the S-G forum topic list, which prompted G+ to load the next page. I skimmed again, went to the bottom of the page, prompted a load, scrolled down again. 3 times. G+ stalled while loading the fourth page, which led me to consider that I had scrolled down perhaps "24-30 PageDowns"... and the topics were still only three days old. It kinda blew my mind, because that's a lot to keep track of, especially in that format (where you have to read not a "topic line", but the first 2-3 sentences of each post, to determine what the post was about). D-W is really well organized into sections (classes, monsters, magic items, general discussion, etc) but I was pretty overwhelmed.
      -- Which might be again, just me, and the whole "I need to just get over it, it's the way things are". But in that conversation, other people are having the same thoughts, too.
      -- However, after picking Jenskot's brain, he suggested that there may be ways around that. Perhaps instead of "one S-G G+ community", that there may be a few, inter-connected, each with perhaps different policies as needed (private posts for members only vs open for all to see depending on the threads; etc). That might be a way out of that bind, to some degree.
      -- But in the end, the "at 11:59, all discussions effectively die" (plus followup discussions I've seen where people note that folks in Europe and Asia adopt posting styles and times on G+ in order to maximize interaction with Americans in order to participate in discussions and get feedback, rather than having their comments and posts dry up) is a troubling thought.

      Anyway, just some thoughts I had as I considered possible futures. At the very least, I don't plan on turning S-G into a Facebook group or Twitter hashtag... :-)

      As for G+ going away someday--maybe, but not until something subsumes the whole social networking model. They're not going to give up such a rich source of data (tastes, trends, demographics) and they're not going to leave Facebook standing alone in the space. Plus, G+ is actually a different paradigm than FB, in that G+ 'socialifies' the whole web, while FB wants to be the portal or lens through which one encounters the web. Like the difference between a forest with trailblazes and a walled garden with windows.
