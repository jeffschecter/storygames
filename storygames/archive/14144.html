    \n Yes, it's too many. Run it as a LARP?

    Fast-paced action-y scenes could work okay, especially if you have multiple groups engaged in mechanically-distinct fights that are united by some ongoing event. Eg. a volcano erupting, a large-scale battlefield, a countdown to some sort of missile launch.

    The problem is, fast-paced action-y scenes are not really what your first session should be about, or most of your other sessions for that matter. I would probably go insane trying to run AW for seven people.

    (Cross-posted with Graham, who also said 'run it as a LARP' in so many words.)

    \n I've run with smaller groups 2+MC up to 5+MC. AW is very "say it to do it" driven so as the numbers go up the opportunities to act go down which waters down the experience.

    I'd split in two and run two parallel 3+MC games. Maybe holds connected only by the maelstrom.

    If you really want to go with 7+MC I'd recommend adding these:
    - As Framweard says, get them to make 2 or 3 closely connected groups of characters so you can say: "now what are you guys doing" and get more than one person involved.
    - An MC move "Look around and ask some who hasn't said anything for a while";
    - Some structure to the combat so people don't miss the chance to act.

    \n I was in a game with five players, and although the MC seemed a little harried, we all managed to bop around to two or three different subplot things, which kept things interesting for me even when I was off-screen.
    I should add, though, that our poor Angel ended up having a lot of scenes by himself, and this was sub-optimal simply because only two out of the six people at the table were "on" during those times.
    It seems like the best thing would be to make sure there's at least two players and the MC active in each scene, making it easier to throw popcorn without overwhelming the active participants.
    Drafting players to portray NPCs can help, but I think the more fundamental thing to do is to really get more than one player engaged with each thread of the story. Solo questing was the death of this 7-player Exalted game I was in back in college, no doubt about it.

    \n My current game has ten players. We've never had everyone at the game table at the same time, but we've definitely had seven. There are issues with quiet players fading into the background when you have this many people looking for scene-time, but most of my crew are pretty good about not hogging the limelight. It helps when you can count on the players to notice something like this, and they're like "We haven't seen Boo in a while... I go over to her clinic and see what's happening there."
    \n
    \nWith so many PCs crammed into a medium sized hold, it gets to the point where any ambitious move by one character is simply going to step on another PC's toes. I've been much more laid back with my fronts and threats because sometimes it seems like there just isn't room in the action and intrigue for them.
    \n
    \nAlso, with so many characters, our relationship map has become... ornate, let's say.

    \n I've run quite a few session with 5 players now, and it works great for us. The players are all socially minded and wants to share the fun, and I encourage them to pull other players into their scenes when possible.
    \n
    \nI also keep it in mind when setting scenes in general; "so Baby, you and Sorrow are wading knee-deep through the sewers, what are you doing there, and what the fuck is that creepy noise?" Or "Alan, A.T. just walked in the door, carrying something of yours, god damn, what did she break this time?"
    \n
    \nSetting the scenes so they involve 2 or more players, and encourage interaction will help you a long way, but 7 players seem a quite a handful.
    \n
    \nGood luck :)

    \n I'm running a 5-6 player game and it's always rough starting out but tends to smooth out after about 20 minutes. It helps that most of their gaming experience is with mainstream games, so they naturally congregate into having their characters together. My one-and-a-half fronts seems to be the right amount for keeping those things going. Any more and there would be too many directions for individual characters to go and I've got a pretty good pressure cooker going with a small holding and an incredibly active Hocus cult subverting the locals like there's no tomorrow.

    \n Apparently I can't count, because I have no idea why I said I would have seven players. There were only six, but that was still a few more than is suggested. I ran the game earlier today, and things went pretty well! We mainly just created the characters and established the relationships between them, and created the world. That took up most of the time, but I gave them some chances to use some of their moves with the time they had left so everyone could get a grasp on how it worked. Everyone seemed to latch on to the story and the game, and everyone is eager for the next session.

    One of the players, who is totally new to tabletop RPGs was particularly awesome. I didn't expect her to pipe up much, but she added in a bunch of great stuff about the back story on the fly. I was impressed, and I feel like having that many people added to the game a little, but I can definitely see how it could be beneficial to have a smaller group. I didn't give each character as much face time as I would have liked, but everyone seemed okay with how things went.

    I also got some decent threats out of them, so I have plenty to work with for the next session.

    Overall, I think it was great having the number of players we did, and everyone else had a blast as well. It seems like the only problem we're going to have is getting all the schedule's to jive.

    I would like to thank everyone for their input! It definitely helped things along.

She was the first to find the Quarantine, and she ended up as his mistress and using him to get out of the cult. "Well, why do you want out of the cult so bad?" "Because I was born into it and raised this way, and the cult leader killed my parents. Now I finally have a chance to escape."

Pity slipped up and Violetta found out she was being stalked, so she decided she wanted him dead. When I tried to steer her away from murdering the other characters in the first session, she said that she wanted to try to manipulate him into marrying the Dust, as she was about to have to enter into an arranged/forced marriage with him, but Pity could take her place. Pity's player hasn't decided whether Pity is male/female yet (chose ambiguous and concealed as his look), so he jumped at the idea as a chance to figure out his character, and the Hocus went for it as well.

    Then there's the big dumb oaf (the character, not the player), Big Fucker, who acts as the muscles for the cult and is smitten with Pity, so he's feeling abandoned by Pity and betrayed by Dust.

    This is going to be fun!
