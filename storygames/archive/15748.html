\n What stuff should we watch, read, play?

1.Feel free to respond here in-thread to another link posted.

a. If you're looking to start a conversation, perhaps take your comment and create a new thread with it in the Stuff to Watch category.

b.Otherwise, to prevent too many comments in the thread, number your response (first response: [1]; second response [2]; third response [3]). If you see that you're the second person to post, "Wow, that looks cool!" to a post here, then put a "[2]" at the top of your post. Three posts ([3]) is the limit: The fourth person to post should just take their comment and create a new thread, to carry the discussion over there further.

Why?

Because these threads are great: We can link to little things being talked about on the web in general, on blogs, or other forums that might be noteworthy, without having to dedicate a new thread to each and every one (although that, too, is fine if you want to discuss the item further or "open up a discussion").

Responding to these posts with a comment or two is understandable (other times there might be no comments, just cause the link was self-explanatory: nothing to say, lots of heads nodding in agreement). But we don't want to create a huge discussion in the middle of the thread: Some folks get a lot out of these threads, and don't want to look back to see the "10 latest posts" just to see that it's a heated discussion of something from a previous page. Cool? Cool.

    \n The Dungeonslayers team is teasing out some previews of the English translation of the 4th edition. Apparently the full release has been delayed a bit for undisclosed reasons, but the've uploaded PDFs of the chapter on character generation, the list of talents, and the character sheet.

    I've only played DS once and it was a while ago, so at first glance I can't tell what's changed in the rules since version 3, but the text looks like it's been rewritten and is now easier to follow. I still think the system is a bit math-y (I actually made myself a character sheet in a spreadsheet app so it could compute the stats for me) but it's one of the more interesting OSR games IMHO.

    \n [DungeonSlayers 1]\n
    Posted By: snejI still think the system is a bit math-y
    \nI've been anticipating the 4th edition of DungeonSlayers for quite a while. I ran a 9-10 session minicampaign with it a year or so ago. Combat was quick and easy, spells were no longer one-shot wonders. It was fun, for free, and was the main impetus for me to give up all of my D&D 3e+ books (and almost sold all my Pathfinder stuff, too).
    I don't really think it's that mathy. There's a bit of record-keeping when you're tracking the cooldown of spells, but hey, if warriors have to track their arrows, why not have wizards track how much they've been casting?

    \n Last night, instead of a date, Terry and I listened to Luke and Jared talk to us about love.

    \u2665 Jared Sorensen & Luke Crane: Roll for Romance: Gender, Crossplay and Romance in Roleplaying Games.

    \u2665 Dylan McKenzie and Max Sebela: Do You Love Me? A Talk About Love, Trust, and Data Collection'

    \u2665 Lizzie Stark: LARP Love, Not War: Scandinavian Methods for Sexy Roleplaying

    If you're friends with me on G+, you can check out photos!

      \nDid The Sopranos do more harm than good?: HBO and the decline of the episode
      \n
      \nIt's about television, but once you get halfway through it suddenly becomes applicable to RPG campaigns:
      \n
      \n"The idea of having a fixed point toward which a show inevitably builds is fine in theory, but false in practice. There are too many variables at play when producing a television show that slavishly adheres to a predetermined finish line. All those breadcrumbs have to lead somewhere. But what if that destination changes along the way? How can one account for the clues already left behind? Assuming that a superior idea won\u2019t arise later is simply arrogant thinking, and counterintuitive to any collaborative process. A television show is a living, breathing entity that represents a synergy of creative, cultural, and social forces that simply can\u2019t be predicted five weeks out, never mind five years out. It\u2019s not a book that can be rewritten before anyone can read it, or a film that can be reshot/re-edited before it screens in theaters. The cat\u2019s out of the bag, for better or worse. Laying in groundwork for a massive payoff down the line is a terrible risk, one that comes with so little control as to be almost laughable."
