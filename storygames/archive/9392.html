\n
\n For the last year or so the game I've been working on has completely stalled as my goals and basic design premise has changed. Now, this isn't really much of an issue, it's a personal project, I have no desire to publish or anything. It's just something I've been doodling on for a long time.
\n
\nToday I made the decision to throw it all away.
\n
\nI've spent so much time I feel kind of lost in it. re-reading it I can see where my thoughts evolved as I went along. It just took too long. I guess there's something to be said for that whole "perfect is the enemy of Good" thing.
\n
\nSo tonight I re-read all my notes, went through everything I've written one last time, and deleted it all. After a year stalled out I just don't think I have it in me to work back up momentum. It just feels right to start over, knowing what I know and hope that it pours out from a fresh start.
\n
\nSo, any advice? Anyone else felt the need, or had a positive experience trying? Was it the right decision? Because suddenly it feels kinda scary.

    \n No effort is ever wasted. I have learned from all my failures, everything I've dropped or discarded, everything. Damned Anonymous started as a more trad-style horror game years ago, and turned into this jeepform experience about someone who abuses his family (which I'm more satisfied with), so you're definitely in the company of folks who have seen their goals move beyond their works.

    We fall so we learn to walk. You just metaphorically skinned your knees. To that, I say "hell yes."

    Though, I never throw anything away. I just re-label folders things like "OLD CRAP" and "MACKLIN THE FUCK YOU SMOKING", things like that. And sometimes they don't make it to the next computer I'm on. But then, maybe the catharsis was necessary for you -- in which case, awesome.

    (Also: "Mantras are the enemy of progress.")

    Posted By: Master of the GameI know it sounds weird, but I thought it might help to cleanse the pallet a bit.
    \nSure, definitely. I wouldn't reference them in creating something new. I have a few drafts of various character creation for games, and each time I do new one, I don't consult the old ones unless I get a nagging "I know I've said this right somewhere before." I enjoy working from scratch because it forces me to re-confront the same issues, and forces me to gain a sense of consistency with them.

    \n Yep, it's a good strategy sometimes. When I was trying to put together the first playtest draft of microscope I archived everything I had already written and started over with a blank document. I had too much "well what if I did it this way?" baggage.

    There's the fear that you'll forget something important, but let's face it, if you don't remember it probably wasn't that important in the first place.

    \n Last October I put to bed a game I had been working on for two years. By March, I had a new complete and playable game. Since then it has been professionally edited, polished and illustrated. I'm now almost done with typesetting and I'll hopefully get my first proof copy in a matter of weeks.

    Starting from scratch has helped immensely. It also really helps to define what you want to accomplish and set tight, defined goals and milestones.
