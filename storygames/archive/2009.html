    My game Primitive is 4x6. I selected this size because i knew i wanted to make the books by hand in a very particuluar fold/sew/slice way. There is a lot to think about.

    why do you want it to be small, does its diminutive stature have anything to do with the content or flavor?

    4x6 doesn't hold a lot of type on the page, so unless the game is quite short its going to get thick. If you are perfect binding it, it simply won't stay open on a table or even in your hand, and that can be a pain when your referencing numbers, charts, rules, etc. Also, a book that small doesn't have a lot of room for a gutter or margins. I sacrificed these with primitive and threw type right into the gutter (because i used other type tricks to break up spreads, and because the way the book was stiched i didn't lose anything in the fold space). I sometimes regret that decision. Gutter space is important, good margins are important. and don't go smaller then 9.5 point type in a book this size or it will feel like your just holding a miniturized version of a "real" book.

    In a big wall of books a little guy has just as much chance of getting lost as it does of standing out. The cover really needs to set it apart if its going to jump out at you.

    People associate small with not-worth-it. they will want the game to be cheeper, and the game will have to offer immediate signs that it's production value are up to snuff.

    full color will help.

    Good news is the books are easy to store, move, take on planes. Bad news is the books get lost on a bookshelf. You can't just stick it in with paperbacks or other games, it slides back and you can't see it, or worse, it gets damaged.

    From one small-booker to another. I hope this helps.

    Just to weigh in with my own experiences as a consumer. This is just what I like to buy, own and hold in my hands for easy reference and reading.

    Basically, it's down to word count.

    The format Jason used for Roach and Drowning and Falling (21.5cm by 14 cm or 8Â½" by 5Â½") is the format I want to see all book below a certain word count printed in. This is also the format (IIRC) for The Mountain Witch, Dogs in the Vineyard, InSpectres, Primetime Adventures and a great many more in my collection. I love this size, it's just right to hold and to read easily, is transportable and there's enough information on each page.

    Above a certain word count, I want to see the page size shift to the format used for Sorceror, Dictionary of Mu and Legends of Alyria. That is, US comic size (25.5cm by 16.5cm or 10" by 6Â½"). If a book is this size though, latout and artwork is much more important. The minimalist design used in PTA looks great in 21.5 x 14, but a similar design looks cheap and crappy in the Sorcerer books (IMO etc etc). Look at Dictionary of Mu for example, that design is wonderful but I can't see that layout working at all well in a 21.5 x 14 book.

    23cm by 15cm or 9" by 6" is a kinda middling size. Generally, I'd prefer games to be 21.5 x 14 and perhaps have a slightly more efficient layout. The only book I own which I don't feel that way about is Spirit of the Century, which seems to work quite well at that format but weighs in at over 400 pages and would have to be even bigger if it was a smaller page size and has a layout that suits a smaller size.

    Other sizes annoy me, some more than others. Covenant and Shock: are square 19cm by 19cm or 7Â½" by 7Â½". I would like both books better if they were 21.5 x 14. Covenant has a hell of a lot of white space and I have several issues with the layout of Shock that I won't go into here, but I'd have liked to see that as a 21.5 x 14 book instead. Any book that's landscape annoys me. Yes, even Agon. They're awkward and don't fit on the shelf properly. I've skipped a couple of books because they're landscape.

    Spiral bound books are irritating too. They don't open right and can't be put on a bookshelf. Also, they feel cheap. I don't really think that they represent nice artefacts and to be hones I'd rather have a PDF than a Spiral bound book.

    Speaking of PDFs, I cannot stress how important it is for a PDF to be 28cm by 21.5cm or 11" by 8Â½". I actually have to print onto A4, so while not perfect, this format is fine. PDFs in other formats are not printable easily. All of John Wick's PDFs are left unused on my PC because they cannot be printed. The PDF of Agon was printed, but then binned largely unread because it was landscape and therefore does not fit into a binder. I went through merry hell to create a usable copy of Mechaton. PDFs need to be 28 x 21.5 portrait so they can be printed and put into a binder. That's what happens to 90% of PDFs so that needs to be possible.

    Anyway, that my 2p worth.

    What you need to get on paper and across to the audience is the main controller of the layout of your book. If you were forced to choose between the 6Ã—9 and 4Ã—6 versions, I'd have to recommend the 6Ã—9 for various reasons mentioned above (information density per page, ease of reading, not getting lost on the shelf, etc.). But if free to choose (and do shop around. The various printing companies offer varying size options), definitely play around with different layouts to see what works best aesthetically for you. This will be a PITA to some extent, as you'll have to reformat and relayout everything for each size option, or at least several pages. And, with the ease of desktop publishing, you can have multiple layouts for the various publising venues.

    My humble suggestions: Try to keep the thickness around an inch or less, to save wear on the spine. Try to ensure the book can be read one-handed, or placed on a table and held open with one hand. Preferably, it should lie nearly flat on its own, but that's what those paperweight thingies are for. I think they call them d100s. Keep the binding margin large, to avoid wear on the spine. Stick with larger fonts, like 12pt or even 14pt, if possible, for ease of quickly referincing information.

    Some thoughts on the PDF issue. An idea Colin and I have discussed for upcomming releases, like Permafrost and Stories from the Shelter, is to bundle a PDF package with one screen-view aspect ratio (11"Ã—8.25" gives you a 4:3, the standard for monitors for a while, though 16:9 may soon replace it.) and one home-printing aspect ratio (8.5"Ã—11"). It's only a little more effort to switch from landscape to portrait, and we hope it will be a nice selling point. Then we'll have to decide on a POD layout, too. And I am definitely a fan of buy-the-print, get-the-PDF-free.
