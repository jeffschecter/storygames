    \n Is this for a typical dungeoneering situation? Then, maybe the OP's list can be further condensed as:

    1. Grant advantage in a fight (from quickening or giant-growing ally/self to weakening or dazing opponent or putting them to sleep, etc.)

    2. Harm opponent(s) directly (fold into the above if you're not using hitpoints in your system)

    3. Acquire temporary ally (that's what Charm spells are for, as well as animating the dead, summoning demons, etc. - but this can also be subsumed into the other categories based in what the temporary ally is for)

    4. Gain access (unlock door, shrink self to diminutive size, fool guards with illusions or mind control, whatever)

    5. Prevent access (lock door, ward, force wall, a number of illusions\u2026)

    6. Counteract effect/Restore ally to a previous state (including, but not limited to, HPs healing)

    7. Information gathering (crucial to at least some styles of play)

    If your dungeon crawling is heavy on resource-management, maybe you need a "Save or replace a resource" category: HPs healing can also be interpreted as a subset of this one. And if you're really hardcore, do away with the "Counteract effect" category entirely (replaced by resource-saving).

    \n I tried my hand at this thought experiment once. The results:

    Connection \u2013 Connection magic involves offering, seeking, forcing, taking, or exchanging information between two or more people, places, or things. Connection effects include identifying magical properties, sensing distant places, telepathic communication, reading minds, forcibly changing people\u2019s attitudes, and speaking with the dead.

    Creation \u2013 Creation magic involves bringing a person, place, or thing into being or returning a person, place, or thing to a natural state of being by introducing ordering energy. Creation effects include generating figments, healing the sick and wounded, producing an item, and conceiving of new forms of life or existence.

    Destruction \u2013 Destruction magic involves damaging, demolishing, or annihilating a person, place, or thing by introducing disordering energy. Destruction effects include dispelling another magical effect, causing sickness or injury, ruining an item, taking a life, and erasing existence.

    Motion \u2013 Motion magic involves movement through time-space by a person, place, or thing. Motion effects include flying, telekinesis, summoning, teleporting, and modifying the flow of time.

    Protection \u2013 Protection magic involves defending, guarding, securing, or sheltering a person, place, or thing. Protection effects include evoking magical barriers to reduce physical harm, deny entry or observation, and negate another magical effect.

    Transformation \u2013 Transformation magic involves changing the current or natural state of a person, place, or thing. Transformation effects include increasing or decreasing abilities, manufacturing a new thing from raw materials, and changing from one form to another.
