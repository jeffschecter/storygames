\n This weekend Remi and I were in DC for A Taste of Fiasco, and there was a gaming "after-party" of sorts. About a dozen people, plus us, were there. It occurred to me that Remi and I could probably rock two tables of Dungeon World on the spot, despite having zero prep. It further occurred to me that if we had two tables of six, we could swap players periodically. So we put our heads together, created a vague backstory (the evil and teleport-happy wizard-king Radithor dispatches two groups of adventurers to deal with problems in the north and south of his kingdom), and jumped right in.

So in the north there was a mysterious plague and in the south there were menacing supernatural giants. Every so often Radithor would teleport adventurers into the opposite group and we'd swap players - usually it'd be Ranger for Ranger or whatever, but early on I ended up with two wizards - both elves, who Sam and George immediately decided were brothers. It was so much fun. The wizard's "oftener right" move is totally killer.

The GM end was pure improvisation, which Dungeon World handles well. There's so much player agency that you can't plan much anyway. The character/player swapping was pretty fun, and introduced unexpected elements - I lost a peaceful, nature-loving bard and got some kind of insane chaos rock star in her place, for example. I ginned up a menacing frog god and a glamorous witch and Remi had his party doing really crazy stuff (at least the fragmentary reports from the characters plucked out of his adventure were crazy).

It was light-hearted and made for a great evening.

    \n I had a blast running this game. It was especially nice to be able to play with almost everyone at the party. Also, the occasional pauses to reset and explain what was happening to the newly switched people helped keep the fiction at least somewhat sensible. I was letting my imagination go crazy, so this occasional sanity check was very good for keeping things from flying off into Cloud Cuckoo Land.

    As for the TPK, well . . . the characters had ice-amulets, given to them by the Air Nomad, that kept them protected from the flaming apocalypse around Firesson the Giant. They inserted said amulets into the star in the Firesson's head to kill the giant, freeing Stormsson's son's soul from Crom Cruach, and saving the valley, the village and Radithor. Unfortunately, this left them exposed to the incredible heat of a star for a moment before it went out, charring them all into their component atoms. I was happy to see no one held back their amulets, plunging them into the star with little hesitation. True Heroes!

    I feel like the biggest mistake I made was giving -1-continuous to the party for blowing a Perilous Journey roll. It just slowed things down and frustrated the party, so boo on me for that. Occasional bad effects from being tired (say, on other blown rolls) would have been better than a super-boring -1.

    I loved Chris's not-a-dick Paladin. That said, he lit up with bloodthirsty glee when he finally found some evil bastard goblins to slay.

    \n Musing out loud and trying to get a more clear definition...how about something like:
    \n
    \nEvery PC is in either 1 of 2 states: Threatened or Not Threatened.
    \n
    \nWhen a PC is Not Threatened they can make any Move they want and they won't suffer any Harm UNLESS they choose a Move that indicates Harm as one of its results.
    \nWhen a PC is Threatened they will ALWAYS suffer Harm that Move (pending applicable armor) UNLESS they choose a Move that indicates avoiding Harm as one of its results.
    \n
    \nPCs can become Threatened by:
    \n1) making a Move that indicates they are now exposed to danger (IIRC, this is one of the Help results)
    \n2) by the MC "announcing future badness" during the Monster turn
    \n
    \nFor example, in our Dreamation game, the volleys of incoming spears would put all of the PCs in range in a "Threatened" state where on their turn each PC would eat some Harm unless avoided by the Move they chose. Narratively that Harm could be from eating a spear or from spraining something while diving for cover, or even something more abstract like being forced out of position (depending on how abstract one wants to treat Harm).
    \n
    \nThat would make things, I think, pretty clear and also make Monster's key thing being how they put PCs into a Threatened state (how many PCs, what range, what harm, and what accompanying narrative color).

    \n That seems about right to me, Ralph. The DM starts the round by framing the scene and making a general Move or two (announcing badness being the most likely). Then the spotlight goes around the table (using a regular rotation or by rolling initiative, if you must) giving each player a chance to take action and make moves. Those moves might also result in an additional DM Move, depending on their outcome (a failure, for example).

    I think the point is that damage from monsters and traps doesn't need to be directly connected to a DM Move- it can and should happen as a matter of circumstance, a repeatable consequence from a previous DM Move.
