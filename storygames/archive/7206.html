    \n Hey David,

    the only time i've played Zo was at a con and Ed, the GM, provided toys for us to pattern our characters after (we were members of The Playful Watch)
    it was really quick to make characters based on our interpretations of ideas handed to us.

    with that in mind, i'd say take three avenues:
    1) make a character or two, if you've got the time to prepare
    2) have some ideas prepared that fit into your vision of the world, for them to draw from or riff off of.
    3) be open to their ideas.

    also, i don't know if it was in the book or not but Ed made a cool list (that the Players all contributed to) about what we wanted to have in the story.
    whenever we incorporated an element (an awkward kiss, the sinking of a ship, running after someone etc) it was crossed off the list and we got an xp bonus. that was hella fun, to have different things we were all gunning for, and we could just do.


    have fun!

    \n Too late to be any use now, but -

    With any of the PDQ games, what I wouldn't do is just tell the players they can make any character they like for a single few-hour session. The system is so open that this can lead to paralysis. It'd be better to do guided creation, where the choices go into slots or you have lists or something (eg see QMR Character Assistant).

    \n I agree with Tim: Though I haven't played ZoZ, I've played Fudge-based games, and one shots of Dead Inside and the like (same system). In those, for a one shot it's always easier to make characters in advance than to have the players come up with them. The paralysis thing is very real. Also, with that hour of chargen you could have been playing like 45 minutes of it.

    When I have players make their characters together at a one-shot game, it means that either we're playing "Dread: The First Book of Panedmonium", or we're playing anything else, and I'm stalling for time and ideas, willingly sacrificing 1 hour plus to give me some time to come up with an adventure. Sweating, perhaps.
