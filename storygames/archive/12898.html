Dear designer, what currency do you get paid in?

\n
\n
\n
\n\nMatthijs \n\n\n
\n
\n\nAugust 2010 \n edited August 2010 in Story Games
\n
\n
\n
\n
\n I was just answering Christopher over here, and I almost added "...whether or not you buy it - that's beside the point, really".

Because to me, "getting paid" in attention (from people playing and discussing my games) and getting paid in cash (from people buying my games) are two different things. They're both valuable to me - but if I had a choice, I would go for attention over money any day. I have a day job that pays well. I've mentioned earlier that if I could trade my games for AP reports, one game for each thread, I'd do so in a second.

How about you? What is it you want when you put your games out there?

    \n I'm in it for the money. Game design is probably under 30% of my income at the moment, but it's supposed to be growing. More significantly, money is respect, which is really what I'm after: I am only considered a responsible and respectable adult in my community insofar as I'm able to maintain my lifestyle and contribute to the well-being of my community; I can call myself a "game designer" all day long, but that won't matter to anybody whose opinion I care about unless this occupational identity also translates into a credible economic arrangement. I don't even need that money that much, conservative as my lifestyle is, so it's pretty much just that I want to be respected as a contributing member of the economy by my immediate friends and family. I want to be able to call what I do (including but not limited to game design, note) a career and not just a hobby.

    This is not to say that I don't care about what Internet strangers say about my design; of course it's heartwarming, and I'll go a long way for the appreciation of somebody whom I admire myself. But if I had the choice between money and Internet fame, there's not one in ten among y'all whose opinion I wouldn't trade for more credit in my "Eero is really a professional writer" fund. In fact, anybody wants to show their appreciation for me, there's a tip jar on my website somewhere I would still take Matthijs's deal of games for AP, but only because that's very likely to be a net win for a game of sufficient quality, at least if we could figure out a way to make that AP somehow non-shilling once it's mixed with pecuniary compensation like that. I'd have offered 20 \u20ac gift tokens in exchange for AP a long time ago if I didn't consider paid AP a form of Internet community spamming.

    Of course it goes without saying that the reason for why I don't choose an easier line of work is out of the tremendous respect and interest I have in the field of culture in general and game design in specific. It's one of the most important, challenging and rewarding fields to work in, which is why I want so much to feel like I'm successful doing it. So I suppose I could say that ultimately I'm paid by the satisfaction of knowing that I do a good job.

    \nSome people get paid in Tweets.\n
    The Tweet Paywall
    Aug 27 2010, 10:30 AM ET
    If it's true that attention is a scarce resource, drawing attention to something ought to be worth something, right?

    That's the idea behind a service that soft-launched this summer called PayWithATweet. It allows you to stash a piece of content (say, a website or an e-book) and only allow access to it after a user has tweeted something about it. In other words, it's like a paywall in which you pay by tweeting about something.

    I encountered for the first time today in accessing Steve Daniels' book about Kenyan craftsmen, Making Do. Tweeting about the book (with my own verbiage) got me a digital copy of the beautifully illustrated book for free.

    The "social payment system" was developed Leif Abraham and Christian Behrendt of the firm Innovation Thunder, who released the system in June when they put out their own book, Oh My God What Happened and What Should We Do?

    The stats they compiled about using their own system are pretty good. After eight weeks, their book had been downloaded 113,000 times and they'd sold 1,300 copies on Amazon. It became a Twitter trending topic twice, and the only promotion they did for the book was Tweet a single time, "This Book helps you move into the Digital era of awesomeness. Download it for free" with a link to their site.

    So far, 2,500 people have signed up to use Pay With a Tweet, but their own book remains the most successful use case. Abraham and Behrendt said that outside of authors, DJs and musicians appeared to be having the most success with the system.

    So, cool idea? Yes. I love transactions that occur outside the money economy; your non-currency resources are shown to have a real value. On the other hand, if everyone put their content behind Tweetwalls, Twitter would really start to look like a mess.

    \n I've literally had millions of people play my (computer) games, which is almost overwhelming to think about. And certainly seeing the first few game boxes on the store shelves was a real reward. But it all starts to become numbers after awhile.

    Strangely, I've gotten just as much satisfaction from creating a Fiasco playset because I can actually talk to people I know about it and hear about their play experiences. The same satisfaction I feel when I get a letter from someone saying that my video game helped them through a particularly difficult time.

    The personal connection is what does it for me.
