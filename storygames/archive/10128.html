\n Nick Novitski posted an actual play report of Happy Birthday, Robot on his blog!\n
Last night, at the Compleat Strategist's weekly get together, I played a game of Happy Birthday, Robot!

I enjoyed it a great deal. Choosing and arranging words was fun, and that play produces a poetic artifact, a kind of picture-book libretto, pleased me immensely. I would absolutely play it again, and would especially love to try it for a longer series of sessions. I wonder if it would adapt to campaign play with a varying pool of players?

Two of the players were young, and invested in making the story incoherently silly, or just maximizing their ability to negate whatever was being claimed by the storyteller. I joked at one point about only adding words like "not," "didn't," and "couldn't," but on the whole, it was surprisingly disruption-resistant. When you pull tangled threads in different directions, they mesh and knot to make something unexpected, but strong. Or some other metaphor.

I see that the playtest draft has been altered to only let you roll three dice at one time. That's probably for the best. Some of our players were rolling 16 at a time, resulting in much longer sentences, resulting in coins getting flipped sooner than they would have, resulting in the game ending sooner than it would have.

Here's the story we came up with:

Happy Birthday, Robot!
by Dee, Chris, George and Nick


Happy Birthday, Robot!

Robot wakes up and checks his fluffy pink cellphone, but the battery is dead.

Robot sighs and makes coffee, but the coffeepot is broken.

The sun shines warmly on red Robot, and he feels at peace, but radioactive bunnies fall from the sky!

Robot braces against their scolding words, but falls down hard.

Rising to his feet, Robot laughs a cheerful laugh and shoots the bunnies down with his happiness-inducing laser-vision.

Robot and bunnies start to break-dance maniacally, but the bunnies begin to bite him.

The epic fight was soon over, and the voracious bunnies were exhausted for days.

Robot looks upon his work and is glad, but still feels despair for the enslaved bunnies.

Robot cries as he finds a single dead bunny nearby, so he brings it back to life, but Bun-Bun was changed!

[Ed: endgame triggered here.]

Bun-Bun bit other bunnies, changing them into creatures that fed on brains.

Satisfied, Robot went home. "I should charge my cellphone now."

Robot received seven birthday messages; how auspicious!

Meanwhile, the bunnies plotted Robot's massacre.

THE END
\nAwesome. Nick later mentions in comments that he isn't sure if he should identify the bits he contributed. That's kinda cool, I think. An artifact so inherently collaborative in its creation that you don't want to take it apart.

    \n I really love the Happy Birthday Robot! rules. They're easy, so young-ish kids should be able to grok them easily. I have a five year old who is probably just a year away from being able to successfully play it (he's a bit on the hyperactive side, so I don't think he's ready just yet.)
    \n
    \nThe story above seems to be proof that the rules work, and work well.
    \n
    \nMaybe now you ought to start thinking about playing the game to develop a nice kid friendly story (i.e. no dead bunnies - even if they do come back to life), get it illustrated, and then produce and market a kids book for kids aged 4+ complete with the rules at the end of the book, and a set of three fudge dice.
    \n
    \nThe neat thing about the game is that it can easily be shifted from Happy Birthday Robot, to any other kind of creature or thing, such as happy Birthday Pony, or similar.

    \n I'm thinking along the same lines, too. If I were to publish this game, I'd probably include a sampling of starter sentences for different stories, based on other well-known robot stories.

    Robot lives alone in a dump, but wants to fall in love.
    Robot collects from the past.
    Robot has a visitor.

    Robot is a gift.
    Robot's brother is a real boy.
    Robot wants to be a real boy, too.

    Robot is a giant from outer space who fell on Earth.
    Robot doesn't want to be a weapon.
    Robot is found by a little girl.

    Robot loves the good Doctor.
    The Evil Doctor wants Robot back.
    Evil Doctor makes new machines to fight Robot.

    Aside from that, I dunno how to really prevent inappropriate content from coming up in the game aside from parental supervision, I guess.
