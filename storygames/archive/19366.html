    \n Just copy/pasting:

    Once you have used up all of your Strength and Weakness cards (including any Wild cards), you get to refresh them when the next scene starts. This means picking three new Strength cards and three new Weakness cards, from among the cards you most recently played. You also receive fresh Wild Strength and Weakness cards.

    The refresh process gives you the opportunity to shift your character\u2019s focus over time. You can do this by choosing to retain Wild cards you played previously, dropping some of your original Strength or Weakness cards to make room.

    For example, if your character began the game with three \u201cStealthy\u201d Strength cards, and then you played a Wild Strength card called \u201cWell-connected\u201d, during the refresh process you could choose just two \u201cStealthy\u201d cards and replace the third with \u201cWell-connected\u201d card. Your character has now changed in terms of the ways they can overcome challenges in the story.

    Since your cards refresh when used up, it is always in your interest to play all of your cards, including your Weaknesses.

    --

    So each "refresh", you have 3 premades and one wild, but the premades shift over time.

    Because of what I've been playing on the tabletop for the past decade, the Strong and Weak outcomes on Obstacles look like 10+ and 7-9 results in Apocalypse World...
    \nInteresting, that's not how it's working for us. The general shape of things is that the narrator will set up a situation, then you can respond to it with a Strong, Weak or Neutral card, which defines both how well it turns out for you and who gets to say what happens. Strong is generally success, weak is generally failure, though we've definitely been set some challenges where failure is by far the more interesting option.

    Massively over-simplified example:

    The narrator establishes that you've walked into a room with an ogre in it. You can pick:

    Swordsman (strong card) - You decide you want to stab it with your sword. You describe what you intend to do, and because you played a strong card you also describe how that plays out for you, presumably reasonably well since you have a strength for this specific situation.

    Scares Easily (weak card) - You decide your character freezes in place at the sight of the ogre. You describe your failure to react well, and because you played a weak card you're in charge of listing out how that goes badly for you. It lets you control how much of a mess your character ends up in, but it means you can't wriggle out of consequences for the entire game because everyone has weak cards and everyone has to play them before they can refresh the strong cards they've used up.

    Monster Hunter (neutral subplot card) - Your character is a monster hunter and is lacking ogre from her collection, so you decide to try and drop it in a pit trap. You describe how you set that up and maybe what you're hoping will happen, but because you played a neutral card it's up to the narrator to say how well that goes for you.     \n I think the *World analogy works reasonably well, because while the weak outcome might be framed as a failure in the challenge the narrator sets up, achieving the weak outcome still give the player who beats the challenge (i.e. plays the last card on it) control of the story.

    It's only if you get the 'uncertain' outcome that the narrator retains control - in *World terms, gets to make as hard a move as they like.

    Of course a lot depends on how the narrator frames the outcome options.

      I'm not claiming they're evil. They're free to do whatever they want.       \nFreeform with a very particular (and mechanically-elegant) structure. Mechanics that are managed by the site, as well. Mechanically, it's rather spiffy. There's a few different nuts and bolts that work in tension with one another so that you get the advantage of a game that steers the narrative and encourages a degree of strategic thought.

      For instance...

      Resolving a subplot (by making moves which play Subplot cards) gives you a little boost: a wild Strength. It's like a simpler version of Marvel Heroic's Milestones/TSoY's Keys. It's a short power burst that won't stick around

      Burning through your Strengths and Weaknesses lets you slowly redefine your character, because you pick three Strengths/Weaknesses from your most recently-played Strengths/Weaknesses (as appropriate). Resolve subplots, and that lets you redefine your character faster, by writing in another wild Strength.

      Subplots and Assets let you pad a challenge, conserving your Strengths to win a challenge, or letting you dilute your Weaknesses. (For instance, let's say a challenge has 4 slots. That could be one Strength and three Assets/Subplot cards, or two Strengths, an Asset/Subplot, and a Weakness. Either way, you win. If you were only playing Strengths and Weaknesses, you'd have to play three Strengths to win that Challenge.)

      It's narratively tactical. Which is really cool. And the mechanics are elegant. They lead to some interesting emergent behavior. You've got all sorts of levers to monkey around with, and the whole thing is an incredibly-accessible story-based RPG. Which integrates with a smooth online platform. It's "plug-and-play".

If you order a drink in a scene and the other person on stage says "Oh, I thought you were recovering", now you're a recovering alcoholic. You accept this and don't block it, in fact you gladly accept it, because it's much easier to play a scene with someone who creates content than someone who waffles ("What kind of drinks do you like?", "Sour drinks, and you?", "I like sweet drinks.", "Uhu.").

      In tabletop RPGs I've always encountered a much stronger sense of ownership from players over their character. Unless the system explicitly gives another player or the GM the right to give the character the "alcoholic" trait, the player probably won't accept it from someone else. People are totally blocking all the time in tabletop RPGs ("my character would never do that!"). I tried to talk to one of my groups about this, but they were really reluctant to let anyone else, even (or especially?) the GM, add things to their character without prior out-of-character consent. On the other hand, I agree that a lot of tabletop RPGs let you push quite hard in conflicts against other players, perhaps because the player are right there and can express immediately if a line is crossed.

      In Storium, you could let players select how much they want others to influence their character at character creation. Perhaps there's a "character ownership" card that can say things like "My character is mine, don't add anything that I haven't established or approved" or "Include my character in your moves, but keep it friendly" or "Create my character together with me, transform it with your moves". In my current game I want to be included in other players' moves, I think it's great, but I'm hesitant to add others to mine, because don't know if they'll like it. Perhaps there should be a built-in way of coming to an understanding apart from the comments?
