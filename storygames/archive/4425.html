    Posted By: Christopher Kubasik
    For me, I just want players to have more input into the creation of the events. That just seems like a good, productive idea. Yet it always presented as "taking power away from that big, bad GM." It isn't. It's more creativity for everyone. The GM is still very much a GM. There's must more fun spread around the table.
    \nOn the one hand I totally agree with this. Games like Dogs in the Vineyard, Sorcerer and My Life with Master all fall flat without a strong central GM pushing situation through strong NPC agendas. But there's a part of me that actually disagrees with this because sometimes those of us who play these games forget just how much story organization GMs often do.

    The most obvious is social conflict. Any game without social conflict resolution will come down to someone's fiat. If two PCs are arguing it will simply come down to which Player fatigues first (the real human). And often this is lauded as "good hard successful roleplaying" because clearly the player who caved was simply out argued in character. This goes doubly so for a GM who wants a particular argument between PC and NPC to go a certain way. And Hell, sometimes PC to PC social conflict is resolved by GM fiat and often through subterfuge like having a particularly significant NPC step in on the side of the PC he wants to see win the conflict.

    Less obvious so is the GMs ability in most games to simply state what is and is not possible. For example, say the PCs walk into a house just in time to see a man pocket something and run out the door. The PCs say they want to give chase! With Golden Rule style games it's perfectly legal and even expected for the GM to say, "No, he's just too fast for you."

    This can't happen in Dogs in the Vineyard. If the PCs want to give chase and call for a conflict and state their goal as "We catch the guy" and they win, then they catch the guy. The GM just has to deal. Which I can see as frustrating for GMs who like to use such scenes as "set up" for another scene which gets utterly destroyed if the guy doesn't get away.

    So on the one hand, yeah a lot of "Story Games" require strong GMs that feel like "GMing as always" but at the same time these games do eliminate "Magician Force" tactics that many GMs use without even being conscious of it. And so yes, in that they remove such techniques from the GM they DO remove power from the GM.

    Jesse

    \n I understand how "Power" became the bugaboo word that gets tossed around like monkey shit when talking about the changes some of these games have made in the re-definition of play for Players and GM. However, I think it's utter nonsense and diverts conversation from fruitful results. (Hi, Malcolm!)

    When I wrote that "Interactive Toolkit" for White Wolf Magazine back in '94, I brought up the same ideas we're talking about now, and I can guarantee you concerns about "power struggles" (as some folks on RPG.net and theangryRPGsite like to babble on about on occasion) were not on my mind at all. Nor was it a matter of "re-distributing" power in some sort of Marxist, revolutionary fable.

    It was about having everyone participate more in the making stuff up part of the game -- which always seemed to me to be where a lot of the fun of RPGs lies. It was also about letting everyone get stuff on the table that mattered to them. That might be defined as a power issue, but I can guarantee everyone it's not how I saw it, it wasn't the principle framing of the matter for me.

    The principle framing was adding more stuff to the game that you thought was cool or interesting to share it with your fellow players.

    Some people around the Internet have taken a few conversations in theory-talk that about re-distribution of narrative authority and ran with it, like a flag that says Power Struggle -- as if all this work people have been doing for years has always been a fight about taking power away from the GM. It's isn't. It wasn't. It has always, in my view at least, been about letting everyone at the table PLAY more.

    Now, I understand some people don't want to Play More. Some people want the GM to carry these burdens so they don't have to add anything to the game in public view. Got it. And although this whole thing might sound snarky, all I can say is, "Good!" I want them to play what they want to play, and I don't want them suffering through games they're not liking and writing lonely missives to Malcolm by candlelight when the other players have gone away where they reveal their frustrations with having to play in ways they don't like.

    All I can say is, I want to play with the Players who want to add more. It's not just a matter of the game mechanics, it's a matter of the temperament of the Players. And in ever fun game I've had over the last few years, I've NEVER once sensed that a "Power Struggle" between the Players and GM was in any way -- at all -- an issue for the Players or the GM.

    What I often do see is conversations by everyone at the table as we openly and like creative, cooperative human beings, sort out imaginative elements at the table. (This is a spike in the heart of another common bugaboo I see crawling around the Internet: "Those Story Gamers don't know how to cooperate! It's always about a Power Struggle with them that the have to have the rules adjudicate!" Um... No. We talk just fine. And cooperate all the time. Thanks.)

    Again, there are Issues of "power" involved, of course. If someone suggests something at the table that strikes the wrong note for the group at my table (and the GM might be the one to do this) there will be a discussion that resolves the issue, the key for me has never been an issue of Power. It's always been more creative input from everyone and everyone feeling as if they could bring up ideas and fictional elements that turned them on so they could share them with their friends and make more soup out of the things they cared about.

    \nWhen we created Universalis, we had, as a goal of the design, giving players the power to create the setting. Over time we found less and less reason to have a GM, and the position faded from the design. The last version of the GM was called "First Player." And we only had that, because we were assuming that you had to have some such role in a RPG. Eventually even that went away, as we found tools to give to the players to do everything we'd assigned to that position.
    \n
    \nWe didn't eliminate it intentionally, or as some design goal. We just couldn't think of any reason to keep the role, for the rules we had written. It has nothing to do with "power," and everything to do with what made sense for the game that we'd created.
    \n
    \nMike
    \n
    \nP.S. so called "Story-games," heck I'd go so far as to even say Narrativism as a movement, have come about, in my opinion, largely due to the existence of GM's who never really wanted to be in sole control of the game, and wanted to play themselves. That is, we didn't want to give up the tools we had as GMs, but we also didn't want to have to "perform," but, rather, get to participate the way the players participate. So it's no surprise that we ended up with games that facilitate this.

      \n I do like the idea that one of the essential functions of story game mechanics is to help people who aren't so good at collaboration collaborate.       \n I think "Conservative Games" sounds terrible. And I don't see how it's in anyway descriptive. And it could be seen as placing a value on a category/style of games and I don't think you want to do that.
      \n
      \nI don't know why "Traditional Games" was considered a "clever" term in the past. I only learned and started using the term recently because it's descriptive and gets to the point quickly, not to be clever or make a joke of some kind. So I don't really understand the comment. Generally, everyone knows what is meant by the term when it's used so I've found that it's very effective. Why would you want to leave all that behind because the term isn't "clever" anymore?
      \n
      \nIt's the "Indie" and "Story" games terms that are ill-defined and misunderstood. That's not a critisicm, everything new takes time to understand. But in a hobby where everything not published by one company in particular can be considered independent and some segement of players in every RPG considers their games story-driven both terms have an uphill battle.
