\n Man alive, theorists in all branches love to load their terms with heavily biasing phrases. I was looking at insuring our big screen TV (because, y'know, kids) and I pointed out that with the rapid turnover in models, such insurance must provide powerful problems to the insurance agency ... not being able to monitor whether people keep taking care of their precious (but now accidentally refundable!) hardware. My wife handed me the term moral hazard, and I immediately thought "Oh! I get an immediate sense of what that term is ... there's a danger in incenting people in some way that they'll act in ways counter to the original purpose. Happens in RPG design all the time. But OH, what a shame it's so provocatively phrased. It's not necessarily about morality at all, after all."
\n
\nThen I went and found out that it's not actually about incentives (directly) either, but a special case ... anyway, it's the wrong term, and I was momentarily excited because I thought "Maybe the right term isn't so freighted! It'd be nice to have a way to talk about how games with certain incentives don't force anyone to do wierd stuff, but can create an environment that powerfully influences them in one direction or another."
\n
\nSadly, the correct term (I think) is perverse incentive.
\n
\nDo these people WANT to make it impossible to use these terms to a layman without insulting them? Sheesh.

    Posted By: Joe Beasonwhat do you mean by "weird stuff"? And what do you mean by "influences them in one direction or another"? Weird stuff vs. not-weird stuff?
    Just anything unexpected, like if you told your kids that they weren't allowed to hit each other with their fists, and so they decided that meant that they were allowed to hit each other with rocks. Sometimes rules systems don't actually end up encouraging the sort of behavior their designers intended for them to encourage.

    Example: I tested out a game (long ago) where people could not gain happiness for themselves, but rather could only be given happiness (and empowerment) by other people who sympathized with their plight. Sounded good enough, was intended as a whole uplifting "Look to each other for strength" thing, but the players took the incentive to its ultimate extreme, and many of the characters very rapidly set out to be radically self-destructive (bordering on the actively suicidal) because they were incentivized for getting people to notice how much they were hurting.

    Now if I were to discuss such a ruleset with people who are using it and who are NOT going in that direction, should I say "Well, this game will make your characters suicidal"? Nah ... it hasn't made their characters suicidal. But there's a perverse incentive there ... and it'd be nice to have a clean term to discuss that.

    \n "Unanticipated consequences" doesn't quite cover it, since there's a difference between an incentive which is a motivator or cause and a consequence, which is an effect. "Perverse" in its literal sense of "twisted" works fine to describe the sort of incentive that motivates behavior that it is intended to reduce. But if the sexual connotation is a sticking point, why not say "unintended incentive"? That is, the rules reward a behavior that the designer didn't intend for them to reward. What could be plainer?

    \n I think "perverse incentive" is certainly catchier, which makes all the difference in whether a slightly loaded or not-entirely-accurate term catches on or not.
    \n
    \nIt's hard to work up the desire to use the term "unintended incentive," although that's probably the most accurate.
    \n
    \nMeanwhile, "perverse" subtly implies sado-masochism, which in turn titilates the listener and rather underscores the whole idea that what's happening here is just plain wrong. In fact, I'm probably going to go away from these boards using the term "perverse incentive" to describe every instance of a game mechanic which either undermines the system or takes some negative aspect of the system to extreme, but logical, extremes.
    \n
    \n(For instance, in the old White Wolf LARP mechanics, you could take "Shy" as a drawback, thereby making your character more powerful. The perverse incentive this created was a desire in players to avoid interacting with one another, because they were so into their Shy But Powerful characters. Since LARP rather requires a great deal of interaction in order to fly, well, for most people, anyway, this made for some dull games. I remember walking into a room once where six unimaginably-powerful, city-controlling overlords were all sulkily facing different directions, unable to even make eye-contact, let alone work out an age-old delimma or order their minions about. I felt like I should have a big round button on my lapel with a penny-farthing and awning, it was so surreal... and dull. Yes. I'd say "Perverse Incentive" does it for me, accurate or not.)
